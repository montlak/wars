2. Confidentiality of Settlement Process

Clauses that require the terms and conditions of settlement be kept confidential should not be confused with the confidentiality extended to settlement negotiations. As the Commission noted in Mulkey v. Secretary of Defense, 05990894 (2001):

We find that the previous decision correctly determined that complainant had failed to state a claim with regard to the phone conversation he had with the AGC in an attempt to settle his other pending EEO complaints. "Settlement negotiations, including any statements or proposals, are to be treated as confidential and privileged to facilitate a candid interchange to settle disputes informally." Harris v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05941002 (March 23, 1995). To allow a new complaint based on a settlement offer would defeat this purpose. See Millea v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05980235 (May 21, 1998); Montague v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920231 (May 2, 1992); see also Green v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05980207 (June 25, 1998) (agency's withdrawal of an offer made in settlement not actionable). Regarding complainant's allegations of conflict of interest, we find that the internal agency processing of this complaint, and any involvement by the AGC, did not affect the processing of this case, and that the agency correctly concluded that complainant had failed to state a claim.

See also Nutter v. Secretary of Navy, 01A24324 (2003); Gribcheck v. Postmaster General, 01A12030 (2002).

Similarly, in Flotildes v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A60423 (2006), the Commission upheld dismissal for failure to state a claim where the complainant alleged that she was told during settlement negotiations that she could not have a contract specialist position because she was from another country and the agency was worried that she did not communicate well enough to perform the job.

The confidentiality extends not only to settlement negotiations, but also to ADR processes such as mediation. In McMurtrey v. Postmaster General, 0120055430 (2007), the Commission found that the agency could not base an order for a fitness-for-duty examination on vague allegations by the mediator that the complainant had engaged in threatening behavior. The Commission also expressed concern about the mediator violating the confidentiality requirement of ADR in McMurtrey noting:

After a careful review of the record, we disagree with the AJ 's determination that this case was appropriate for summary disposition. In finding no discrimination, the AJ credited as legitimate the agency's stated rationale for placing complainant in emergency non-duty status and requiring him to take the fitness-for-duty examination--that it relied on concerns allegedly expressed by a mediator about complainant's potential for violent behavior. First, complainant, during the investigation, disputed any suggestion that he acted inappropriately during the mediation. Complainant said that shortly after making his opening statement in the mediation session, the agency's Labor Relations Specialist called him "asinine," and that when complainant reminded her that they were not to call each other names or threaten each other, the Labor Relations Specialist purportedly stated "I haven't threatened you yet," which prompted complainant to leave the room. Given the present record, we find it unclear precisely why the mediator allegedly reported to agency management that complainant "openly displayed anger, belittlement, and a belligerent attitude."

In addition, we are troubled that the alleged conduct that was the precipitating factor in the scheduling of the fitness-for-duty examination occurred during a mediation discussion between complainant and agency management regarding an-EEO complaint he had filed. These circumstances raise a strong inference that retaliatory animus was at play in the agency's actions. While the Human Resources Manager referred, in a very general way, to complainant's past display of "traits" that included a resentment of authority and questioning supervisory instruction, we note that until the EEO mediation no action had been taken against complainant for this alleged behavior.

The Commission notes that provision 5 of the "Agreement to Mediate" in this case expressly states that the mediator will not "report anything said during this mediation UNLESS one of the participants makes a genuine threat of physical harm, reports criminal activity occurring on postal property, reports fraud or abuse of postal property or suspected child or elder abuse [emphasis in the oriGINAl]." The record does not support a determination that complainant's participation in the mediation process reflected any of those matters. Instead, based on the evidence of record, the Commission cannot discern the precise reasons why the mediator broke the duty of confidentiality articulated in provision 5, i.e., whether agency management solicited the information from the mediator; and whether complainant ever made a genuine threat of physical harm during the session. We do note, however, that in the referral letter from management to the psychiatrist selected to conduct the examination, there is no mention of threatening language or actions on the part of complainant.

The confidentiality of the settlement process also extends to statements made during settlement negotiations. Such statements cannot be basis for an EEO complaint. See, e.g., Wolff v. Secretary of Energy, 0120083727 (2009), where the Commission found that statements allegedly made during a resolution attempt could not support a retaliation complaint. The Commission, in Wolff, noted:

We find that the AJ properly determined that he could not consider complainant's claim of retaliation with respect to claim (B)(2). In reaching this finding, the AJ properly noted that the meeting was held to discuss settlement of complainant's case. The record, for example, contains an attachment to complainant's formal complaint in which complainant states that the meeting was held to discuss settlement of her informal EEO complaint. In EEOC's Management Directive (MD)-110, Chapter 3 at 3-3 (November 9, 1999), the Commission has indicated that nothing said or done during attempts to resolve a complaint through alternative dispute resolution can be made the subject of an EEO complaint. The instant claim concerns such a matter and, as such, is not the proper subject for an EEO complaint. Settlement negotiations, including any statements or proposals, are to be treated as confidential and privileged to facilitate a candid interchange to settle disputes informally. Harris v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request N o. 05941002 (March 23, 1995).
