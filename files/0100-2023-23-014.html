D. THEORIES OF DISCRIMINATION

There are four separate theories that support a claim of disability discrimination: 1) the theory of intentional discrimination, which applies to the extent an agency discriminates against a qualified individual with a disability because of his or her disability; 2) the theory of disparate impact, which applies to the extent that facially neutral policies of an agency may have an adverse impact on disabled persons; 3) the theory of hostile environment harassment based on an individual's disabled status; and 4) the theory that an agency failed to make reasonable accommodation of a disabled person and the accommodation would not place an undue burden on the agency. In some instances, the Commission described the Rehabilitation Act as presenting only two separate theories of discrimination: the "intentional discrimination for reasons of social bias" theory and the "surmountable impairment barriers" theory. See, e.g., Reilly v. Postmaster General, 01881666 (1988). Most often, disparate impact cases would fall within the ambit of the "surmountable impairment barriers" theory. The theories of intentional discrimination, adverse impact discrimination and hostile environment discrimination are discussed in Chapters 10 through 12, respectively. Those theories are not distinctly different when applied to disability discrimination than they are when applied to other bases of discrimination. The primary difference between disability and other bases of discrimination with respect to those theories is that a complainant must affirmatively demonstrate that he or she is a member of the protected group. Rarely is this an issue in cases involving allegations of discrimination based on race, color, sex, national origin, religion, or age. Reasonable accommodation, however, is a theory that is unique to disability discrimination cases, except to the extent that reasonable accommodation of religious beliefs is required by Title VII. Disability discrimination and the accommodation of individuals with disabilities is a vastly complicated and rapidly changing area of the law and warrants its own separate discussion.

In cases involving disability discrimination, it is imperative to determine the theory of discrimination that is being invoked by the complainant. For example, in Cooper v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 01913514 (1991), the Commission rejected the agency's argument that it was not required to provide reasonable accommodation to a female employee in a wheelchair to allow her to participate in low cost breast screening provided at the agency work site. The agency argued that it was not required to provide a requested accommodation that does not assist an employee in performing the essential functions of her position. The Commission rejected the argument on the basis that the complaint was not over failure to provide a reasonable work accommodation. The complaint alleged that the complainant was treated differently because she was not provided equal access to agency-sponsored activities and, thus, was a disparate treatment claim under the theory of intentional discrimination.

The distinction in the theories of discrimination becomes more important given the 1992 amendments to the Rehabilitation Act. Under those amendments, the standards set under the Americans with Disabilities Act for nonaffirmative action employment discrimination are now applicable in Rehabilitation Act cases. See 29 USC 791(g). The applicable sections of the ADA are codified at 42 USC 12111, et seq., and 42 USC 12201-12204 and 12210.

In addition to the theories discussed above, participating in the EEO process with a disability discrimination claim, requesting reasonable accommodation, and opposing practices made illegal by the Rehabilitation Act are protected activity. As a result, a complaint alleging reprisal for engaging in protected activities under the Rehabilitation Act states a claim for relief. 29 CFR 1614.103(a).
