29. Service on Non-Attorney Representative

Service of the notice of right to file a complaint on a non-attorney representative does not start the running of the 15-day period. See Wilda M. v. Postmaster General, 2020004212 (2020); Paoletti v. Postmaster General, 01944177 (1994). The Commission regulations only speak to service on an attorney representative. See 29 CFR 1614.605(d). In cases where the employee is represented by a non-attorney, the concept of constructive notice does not apply and the time period does not begin to run until receipt by the complainant. See Carrigan v. Postmaster General, 01943649 (1994), aff'd on recon, Carrigan v. Postmaster General, 05950264 (1995); Carrigan v. Postmaster General, 01945295, 01945299 (1994).

The Commission reversed dismissal in Wilda M. v. Postmaster General, 2020004212 (2020), for example, where the agency counted the 30 days from the day the non-attorney representative received the notice rather than complainant's receipt:

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(f) requires that the Agency provide Complainant with a copy of the Report of Investigation (ROI) and a notice of the right to request a hearing from an EEOC AJ or a decision from the Agency within 30 days of receipt of the ROI and notice. Here, the record shows that the Agency sent the notice of the right to request a hearing by letter dated January 8, 2020. The record further establishes that the notice was received by Complainant's designated non-attorney representative on January 10, 2010. However, the record further shows that Complainant did not actually receive the notice herself from her post office box until February 22, 2020, and she filed her hearing request within thirty days from that date.

In response to the appeal, the Agency contends that the ROI and notice were available to Complainant on January 10, 2020, in her post office box, but she did not pick them up until February 22, 2020. It argues that the date of receipt by Complainant should be considered January 10, 2020, when the package containing the notice was place in her post office box.

We have previously held that evidence of actual receipt by a non-attorney representative is insufficient to establish constructive receipt by a complainant. See Hittle v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120080588 (May 7, 2013), citing Scroggins v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05901153 (June 19, 1991) (declining to hold that the complainant had constructive notice of a final agency decision when the decision was sent to the complainant's nonlegal representative but not to the complainant). Here, Complainant designated a non-attorney as her representative, which means the time frame for filing her hearing request did not begin until Complainant herself received the notice. As already noted, postal tracking information shows that Complainant did not retrieve the notice from her post office box until February 22, 2020.

We have previously utilized the date of retrieval from (rather than availability in) a complainant's designated post office box as starting the regulatory timeframe. See Doyle S. v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120172037 (September 25, 2017). As such, the record is clear that Complainant received the ROI and notice on February 22, 2020, when she retrieved them from her designated post office box, and the 30-day deadline was March 23, 2020. Hence, we find Complainant's March 21, 2020 hearing request timely as the record does not show that Complainant failed to pick-up the notice in bad faith. Id. Although we cannot state that a complainant has an indefinite amount of time to retrieve an EEO process-related package, based on the circumstances here, we find no evidence of bad faith in the delay. On appeal, Complainant has explained that she has medical conditions that affect her reading and understanding so she has to wait until assistance is available to her to process official documents. When her post office informed Complainant that the package containing the notice would be returned to sender, she picked up the package before that occurred. Complainant requested a hearing within 30 days of her actual receipt of the notice.

In Naveiras v. Postmaster General, 05960625 (1997), the complainant notified the agency that he would be on vacation, but the notice of right to file a complaint was sent to him anyway. The agency dismissed the formal complaint as untimely since the complainant's non-attorney representative also received the notice. The Commission in Naveiras found that the agency's decision was erroneous, and that the complainant had 15 days from the date he received the notice to file a complaint:

In this case, appellant has presented proof that he was on vacation when he was sent the notice of right to file his complaint, and that he did not return to work until May 30, 1995. Therefore, appellant has provided adequate justification for tolling the time limit. Accordingly, as appellant received the notice on May 30, 1995, he had until June 14, 1995 to file his formal complaints. Consequently, appellant's complaints were improperly dismissed for timeliness reasons.

If the complainant's representative is not an attorney, all required notifications must go to the complainant. This does not prevent the agency from also serving the notice on the non-attorney representative. The agency, however, may not rely on receipt by the representative as constructive receipt by the complainant. Moreover, it is not enough that the notice sent to the representative contains a statement that a copy is being sent to the complainant. In order to demonstrate that a copy of the notice actually was sent to the complainant, the agency must submit some supporting evidence beyond such a statement. Burris v. Postmaster General, 01950684 (1996).

The concept of constructive notice of right to file a complaint does not apply even where the complainant's non-attorney representative is her husband and has the same mailing address. Johnson v. Postmaster General, 01960014 (1996).
