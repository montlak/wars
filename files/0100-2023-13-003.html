A. METHODS OF INVESTIGATION

Most agencies provide their own resources for investigation. Some agencies contract with outside organizations to conduct EEO investigations. In certain circumstances, an agency can execute a memorandum of understanding with the EEOC to conduct investigations. The EEOC criteria for accepting the responsibility of conducting an investigation had been set out in Chapter 5 of the Complaints Processing Manual. MD-107 (September 1987). There is no parallel provision in MD-110. It is not clear whether the omission is deliberate or simply an oversight. Under MD-107, the EEOC would consider conducting the investigation if the allegations raised sensitive issues involving high-level officials or presented the agency with a potential conflict of interest, the agency was too small to process its EEO caseload (generally 450 employees or less), or the complaint involved more than one federal agency.

The general requirements of the investigative stage of complaint processing are explained in MD-110, at 6-4 to 6-5:

An investigation of a formal complaint of discrimination is an official review or inquiry, by persons authorized to conduct such review or inquiry, into claims raised in an EEO complaint.

The investigative process is non-adversarial. That means that the investigator is obligated to collect evidence regardless of the parties' positions with respect to the items of evidence.

A copy of the complaint shall be provided to the investigator prior to the commencement of the investigation.

Models for the analysis of common types of discrimination cases appear at Appendix L to this Management Directive.

A. Methods of Investigation

Investigative inquiries may be made using a variety of fact-finding models, such as the interview or the fact-finding conference, and a variety of devices, such as requests for information, position statements, exchange of letters or memoranda, interrogatories, and affidavits. The inquiry/review process may also incorporate some of the features of a dispute resolution plan.

B. Purpose of the Investigation

The purpose of the investigation is 1) to gather facts upon which a reasonable fact finder may draw conclusions as to whether an agency subject to coverage under the statutes that the Commission enforces in the federal sector has violated a provision of any of those statutes and 2) if a violation is found, to have a sufficient factual basis from which to fashion an appropriate remedy.

C. General Investigative Requirements

The investigation shall include a thorough review of the circumstances under which the alleged discrimination occurred; the treatment of members of the complainant's group as compared with the treatment of other similarly situated employees, if any; and any policies and/or practices that may constitute or appear to constitute discrimination, even though they have not been expressly cited by the complainant.

Usually, investigations will begin with some form of interview with the complainant, and either an affidavit will be obtained from the complainant or a transcript of the interview will be taken. Many agencies require that witnesses provide a "preliminary" affidavit prior to their interview with the investigator. The preliminary affidavit consists of written questions submitted to the witness which require written answers. Preliminary affidavits are designed to give the investigator some general background information on the witness and his or her area of knowledge with regard to the complaint prior to any formal interview.

Another popular form of investigation is to require that the complainant and all other witnesses prepare their own affidavits in response to written questions submitted by the investigator. This saves agencies the expense of an on-site investigation and also greatly reduces the amount of time required by the investigator. It does, however, have the disadvantage of allowing witnesses to make summary responses that are of marGINAl value.

Some agencies routinely conduct investigations through the use of factfinding conferences. The Department of Defense (DOD) frequently utilizes this type of investigation, and has set up the Office of Complaint Investigations (OCI) to oversee factfinding conferences. Factfinding conferences resemble traditional hearings in many of the more superficial respects. The witnesses are given oaths before their testimony. They are questioned by the investigator and a court reporter transcribes the conference. The purpose of a factfinding conference, however, is radically different from that of a hearing. The factfinding conference is simply another way to extract the factual information necessary to investigate a case. The conference is not aimed at coming to any conclusion about whether discrimination occurred. The atmosphere is less formal than that of an EEOC hearing, and the role of representatives is less central to the process.

A complainant is entitled to representation throughout the EEO process and that includes participation in the investigative stage. Technically, witnesses, including the responsible management official, have no regulatory right to representation in the investigative process. However, nothing requires that an agency deny witnesses the right to representation.

In addition to giving testimony or an affidavit, the complainant also should be prepared to submit any documentary evidence in his or her possession and to suggest to the investigator other documentary evidence that may be relevant. Many investigators also will request that the complainant provide a list of witnesses whom he or she believes have relevant testimony.

Because the Commission gave agencies great latitude over the manner in which investigations are conducted, a complainant must show that an agency abused its discretion in order to mount a challenge to the chosen investigative techniques. For example, in Jackson v. Secretary of Air Force, 01972082 (1998), the Commission found the agency was justified in dismissing the complaint for failure to state a claim where the complainant refused to respond to lengthy interrogatories during investigation of the complaint. Although the complainant challenged the interrogatories as "highly technical and legal," the Commission noted the complainant did not challenge the relevancy of the information sought or demonstrate that providing the information would have posed an undue burden on the complainant. As a result, the Commission found that the complainant had not demonstrated that the agency abused its discretion in investigating the complaint.

An agency need not necessarily use the same investigative techniques with all witnesses. In Taips v. Secretary of Labor, 01993712 (2000), the complainant alleged investigatory bias because the investigator interviewed the agency witnesses in person but corresponded with the complainant in writing only. The Commission in Taips found that absent any independent indication of bias, the use of different investigative techniques was within the investigator's discretion:

Even assuming that the investigator chose a different approach in obtaining information from the complainant than he did from the agency officials, this action by the investigator does not necessarily show bias. The complainant has not demonstrated how he was harmed by the investigator's alleged face-to-face meeting with the SO and the complainant's supervisor and the investigator's choice to work with the complainant only through written communication.

The Commission noted that the complainant was given a full opportunity to respond to the agency officials' articulated reasons for the nonselection at issue.

MD-110, at 6-6 to 6-7 sets out a number of methods of investigation:

The investigator may accomplish his/her mission in a variety of ways. The investigator may function as:

1. a presiding official at a fact-finding conference;

2. an examiner responsible for developing material evidence;

3. an issuer of requests for information in the form of requests for the production of documents, interrogatories, and affidavits;

4. a face-to-face interviewer in on-site visits;

5. and/or, any other role so long as appropriate investigative techniques/methods are utilized.
