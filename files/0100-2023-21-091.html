broida eeo Chapter 12: Theories of Discrimination--Hostile Environment Chapter 12: Theories of Discrimination--Hostile Environment, Subchapter 05: Overview of Liability for Harassment, Section D: Hostile Environment Harassment--Supervisors and Coworkers, Subsection 01: Prompt and Effective Corrective Action, Part k: Preventing Harassment
k. Preventing Harassment

Prevention is not limited to measures designed to stop sexual harassment before it happens. An agency is required to take immediate and definitive action to prevent further sexual harassment once it has reason to believe that sexual harassment has occurred. In Velez v. Postmaster General, 01902746 (1990), the Commission explained this obligation in the context of an agency's failure to fully investigate the complainant's allegations of sexual harassment:

[T]he agency wholly failed to take appropriate action to fully investigate appellant's allegations of sexual harassment or to take appropriate corrective action regarding such harassment. The agency has an affirmative obligation to take all steps necessary to prevent sexual harassment from occurring. According to the Commission's Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment Law:

An effective preventative program should include an explicit policy against sexual harassment that is clearly and regularly communicated to employees and effectively implemented. The employer should affirmatively raise the subject with all supervisory and non-supervisory employees, express strong disapproval, and explain the sanctions for harassment. The employer should also have a procedure for resolving sexual harassment complaints. The procedure should be designed to "encourage victims of harassment to come forward: and should not require a victim to complain first to the offending supervisor." See Vinson, 106 S. Ct. at 2408. It should ensure confidentiality as much as possible and provide effective remedies, including protection of victims and witnesses against retaliation.

EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment Law (October 25, 1988) at pp. 27-28.

Our policy statement also requires that:

When an employer receives a complaint or otherwise learns of alleged sexual harassment in the workplace, the employer should investigate promptly and thoroughly. The employer should take immediate and appropriate corrective action by doing whatever is necessary to end the harassment, make the victim whole by restoring lost employment benefits or opportunities, and prevent the misconduct from recurring. Disciplinary action against the offending supervisor or employee, ranging from reprimand to discharge, may be necessary. Generally, the corrective action should reflect the severity of the conduct. See Dornhecker v. Malibu Grand Prix Corp., 828 F.2d 307, 309-10, 44 EPD 37, 557 (5th Cir. 1987) (the employer's remedy may be "assessed proportionately to the seriousness of the offense"). The employer should make follow-up inquiries to ensure the harassment has not resumed and the victim has not suffered retaliation.

EEOC Policy Guidance, pp. 29-30.

The agency should not wait for a formal finding of harassment prior to taking such actions. As the Commission explained in Velez:

The agency, in this case, wholly failed to discharge its obligations by failing to fully investigate appellant's allegations and by failing to reassign appellant and the offending supervisor until the AJ's RD was received by the agency. The minor investigation conducted with regard to appellant's initial allegation of sexual harassment utterly failed to take into account appellant's testimony and resulted in no further action being taken by the agency. In fact, appellant was encouraged to confront the harasser with her concerns rather than pursue further channels of investigation into such conduct....

In Buckley v. Secretary of Navy, 0120062739 (2008), the Commission found that the agency took prompt and effective remedial action where she alleged that a Gunnery Sergeant had threatened to bite the complainant while preparing for contract negotiations, and later the same day actually gnawed on her shoulder during negotiations. The Commission upheld the administrative judge's decision to grant summary judgment to the agency on the liability issue where immediately upon learning of the incident, the complainant's supervisor reported it to the sergeant's second-line supervisor. The sergeant was counseled about the incident that same day. A few days later when the complainant reported the incident to the Criminal Investigation Division, a restraining order was issued prohibiting the sergeant from coming within 1,000 feet of the complainant and he ultimately received a punitive letter of reprimand and was docked a month and a half of pay.

Prompt remedial action must include measures designed to bring the harassment to an end, but must also include measures designed to ensure the harassment does not recur. For example, in McKinney v. Postmaster General, 07A00049 (2003), the Commission found that the agency had failed to establish an affirmative defense where it reached an agreement with the offending supervisor where he was placed back on the complainant's shift with the same days off. Although the supervisor was advised he would be terminated if the harassment continued, the Commission found that "[r]the Manager to complainant's supervision was not designed to ensure that further harassment would not recur." Id.
