5. Circumstances Beyond Complainant's Control

In addition to the complainant's physical or mental incapacity to timely file a complaint, the time limit may also be set aside when circumstances beyond the complainant's control result in an untimely filing. For example, in Petersen v. Postmaster General, 01A50990 (2005), the Commission set aside the 15-day filing deadline where the complainant was prevented from timely filing a complaint due to hurricanes in Florida.

In an interesting, if somewhat unique case, the Commission found that the complainant presented adequate justification for setting aside the 15-day filing deadline where the untimely filing was caused by a death and lapse on the part of the U.S. Postal Service. In Heater v. Postmaster General, 01A63556 (2006), the Commission found:

Complainant's attorney acknowledges receipt of the Notice on April 13, 2006. Complainant's attorney states that the complaint documents were prepared, signed and meter-stamped April 28, 2006 for mailing. Complainant's attorney stated that he had intended to mail the formal complaint that afternoon but was called away from his office, to attend to a woman who had lived with his family for approximately six years, and who had suffered a heart attack. Complainant's attorney states that the woman died in the arms of him and his wife, while they waited for paramedics to arrive.[1]

Complainant's attorney states after the agency's dismissal of the formal complaint, he conducted an inquiry relating to the hospital mail box. Complainant's attorney states that, evidently, the United States Postal Service had given the hospital a key to the collection box; that the mail was collected by a hospital employee on week days; that a day could be missed; and that mail was not taken on week ends. Complainant's attorney argues that the United States Postal Service failed to follow its own policies in ensuring secure and timely handling of the mail, which was deposited in good faith on April 28, 2006. Consequently, complainant's attorney requests that the Commission extend or toll the fifteen-day time limitation for filing a formal complaint.

Based on a review of the current record, the Commission finds that complainant has provided adequate justification, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c) for extending the filing period.

[1] Additionally, complainant's attorney states that he had been unaware that his wife noticed the envelope addressed to the EEO Manager by the door, as they prepared to go to the hospital; and that she picked the Notice up en route, and mailed it at the hospital in an official U.S. Mail Letter Box, on April 28, 2006.

In Gordian-Kenney v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120101147 (2010), the Commission reversed a dismissal on timeliness grounds where the complaint was filed eight days after the 15-day deadline even though the notice of final interview had been served on the complainant's attorney by Federal Express. On appeal, the complainant's attorney explained that a burst pipe in his office building and the resulting city order to vacate had caused an immediate relocation of his office and that the original notice must have gotten misfiled in the ensuing confusion.
