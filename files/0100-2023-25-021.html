2. Discrimination Within Protected Group

The EEOC stated that the "notion of a protected class has a narrower application in age discrimination cases than in the Title VII context. Age is relative rather than absolute. It is age discrimination that is prohibited, not merely discrimination based on membership in the class of persons more than age 40." Thomas v. Department of Army, 01842249 (1986); see also 29 CFR 1625.2. This is a significant departure from other bases of discrimination where a failure to show that a comparison employee is outside the protected category may, in some cases, prevent the establishment of a prima facie case of discrimination.

An employee may establish a prima facie case of age discrimination within the protected group by showing that the selectee, although over 40, was 18 years younger than the complainant. See Haughness v. Department of Interior, 01832887 (1985). The guidelines for establishing age discrimination within the protected class are not clear. In the Thomas case, a difference of three years was sufficient to demonstrate a prima facie case, but a difference of four years in age between the selectee and the complainant was not sufficient to establish such discrimination in Scarpelli v. Department of Navy, 01810408 (1981). The differences are resolved on a case-by-case basis depending upon whether the agency advances a legitimate reason for its action and whether the employee can demonstrate pretext. The Commission stated that for discrimination to occur within the protected group, the age difference must be "significant," but it has not defined just how significant. In Miller v. Borden, 168 F.3d 308 (7th Cir. 1999), the court stated that a 10-year difference in age was significant.

The determination of whether an age difference is significant, however, is not a function of age alone. When the complainant was only five years older than the selectee but was the only similarly situated employee of retirement age, this was significant enough to establish a prima facie case of age discrimination. See Cates v. Secretary of Army, 01942773 (1994); see also Pirko v. Postmaster General, 07A20036 (2003) ("While there is no bright-line test for what constitutes 'substantially younger,' that term has generally been applied to age differences in excess of five years.").

Regardless of age differences, the employee still must be able to allege that he or she is treated differently, in some respect, than younger employees. See Lance v. Department of Energy, 01842871 (1986). The probative value of the age disparity increases with the difference in the ages of the comparison employees. See Sloan v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01862442 (1987). The disparity in age should also be measured against other factors such as the relative qualifications of the complainant and the selectee. See Soto v. Postmaster General, 01903375 (1990) (a five-year age difference was significant where the selectee had less experience and less education than the complainant and also had no veterans preference as did the complainant).

A similar view has been adopted by the courts. For example, in Showalter v. University of Pittsburgh Medical Center, 190 F.3d 231 (3rd Cir. 1999), the court held that the district court applied the wrong legal standard in finding the plaintiff had not alleged a prima facie case of age discrimination where two comparable employees were also over 40 years old. The court found the proper standard to apply was whether comparables were "sufficiently younger" and not outside of the protected class. The court in Showalter found that the plaintiff demonstrated a prima facie case of age discrimination where one comparable was eight years younger and the other was 16 years younger than the plaintiff.

It is unlikely that the Commission will ever establish a bright-line rule on discrimination within the protected category. On the practical side, the less significant the difference in age, the less likely that age will be a determinative factor.

When there is more than one position being filled, it is not fatal to the complainant's prima facie case that some of the selectees were within the protected group. As the Commission explained in Nevin v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01992795 (2002):

Applying this analysis to the present case, we cannot agree with the agency that complainant failed to establish a prima facie case of unlawful age discrimination. The evidentiary record shows that the following facts are uncontroverted: that complainant is a member of the class of individuals protected under the ADEA; that he applied for and was qualified for the TSA position; that he was not selected for the position; and that persons outside his protected class were selected for the TSA position. The agency's contention that complainant could not establish a prima facie case because of the existence of three selectees who were within the class of persons protected by the ADEA and who are near complainant's age is incorrect as a matter of law and expresses a misunderstanding of complainant's burden. Complainant need not, as the agency's analysis would suggest, show that all the selectees were outside the protective scope of the ADEA, or all were significantly younger than himself, in order to establish a prima facie case. To establish the fourth element of a prima facie case of age discrimination, complainant need only prove that someone substantially younger than himself was selected while he was not, or present noncomparative evidence which would support an inference of unlawful discrimination. See Reeves, 530 U.S. at 142; O'Connor, 517 U.S. at 312-13. Complainant has met this burden, and has established his prima facie case.
