10. Limited Liability Defense

It is, of course, entirely possible for an agency to discriminate against an individual in a selection or promotion case and still select the best qualified candidate. For example, a selecting official may eliminate all young females from consideration because he believes there is too great a chance they will get married, have children, and leave their positions. This would constitute discrimination. However, it is possible that the male chosen for the position was the best qualified candidate in any event.

In such cases, the agency is not required to award the position to the victim of discrimination if it can prove, by clear and convincing evidence, that the complainant would not have gotten the position even absent discrimination. Under the 1991 Civil Rights Act, this is not a complete defense because the complainant still is entitled to declaratory relief and attorney fees.

In Beland v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01994590 (2002), the Commission held that such a defense focuses on the respective qualifications of the selectee and the complainant:

The Commission's regulations provide that where an agency finds that an applicant for employment has been discriminated against, the agency shall offer the applicant the position unless clear and convincing evidence indicates that the applicant would not have been selected even absent the discrimination. Day v. Mathews, 530 F.2d 1083 (D.C. Cir. 1976); 29 C.F.R. 1614.501(b). Once discrimination has been established, the agency bears the burden of producing clear and convincing evidence that complainant's qualifications were such that s/he would not have been selected absent discrimination. Id. "Clear and convincing proof" is defined as "that proof which results in reasonable certainty of the truth of the ultimate fact in controversy." Black's Law Dictionary 251 (6th ed. 1990). See Mahran v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Request No. 05940973 (June 20, 1996).

In nonselection cases, a comparison of the respective qualifications of the candidates is usually dispositive. Thus, if the record demonstrates that complainant's qualifications are as good or better than the other candidates' qualifications, the agency has failed to sustain its burden of proving by clear and convincing evidence that complainant would not have been selected absent discrimination. Similarly, if another candidate possesses measurably better qualifications than complainant, then the agency has met its burden. Here, given the evaluation of the candidates by the review panel and the review of Applicant A's application, we agree with the agency that Applicant A would have been the clear choice. Specifically, the record contains evidence that Applicant A, and possibly other candidates, possessed measurably better qualifications than complainant. Thus, we find that the agency has demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that complainant would not have been selected for the position, even absent discrimination. Tucker v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05910099 (April 12, 1991).

In this case, the record indicates that the credentials of Applicant A were far superior to those of the complainant. In the first instance, we note the assessments by the individual members of the review panel, who independently scored her application with the maximum number of points of 18 and scored complainant five points lower, at 13. Also, the record plainly shows that the panel members considered that the qualifications of Applicant A and the other unidentified 18-point candidate were markedly superior to complainant or the others on the BQL. Given the assessment of the qualifications of the candidates and a review of the record, we find that the agency has demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that even absent discrimination, complainant would not have been selected for the position.

While complainant criticized the attention given to the qualifications of Applicant A as not being relative to the position at issue, we disagree and find that Applicant A's qualifications, answers to the KSAs, and breath of experience in EEO are very applicable to the posted job. Complainant also contended that two of the panel members were not qualified. We find that complainant's assertion is not supported by the record and find no irregularity in the selection process.

Accordingly, we accept the agency's finding that it discriminated against complainant. Nevertheless, clear and convincing evidence exists to demonstrate that complainant would not have been selected for the position even absent discrimination. Consequently, complainant is not entitled to individual relief in this matter. The Commission orders the agency to eliminate any discriminatory practices and ensure that such discrimination will not recur. 29 C.F.R. 1614.501(b).

In Bedynek-Stumm v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720060054 (2008), the Commission, in an age discrimination case, found that the agency had failed to establish that it would have selected another candidate even absent discrimination finding:

We now turn to the matter of relief. When an agency, or the Commission, finds that an applicant for employment has been discriminated against, the agency shall offer the applicant the position that the applicant would have occupied absent discrimination or, if justified by the circumstances, a substantially equivalent position unless clear and convincing evidence indicates that the applicant would have not been selected even absent the discrimination. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501(b)(ii).

During the hearing, the agency argued that Applicant A or Applicant B would have been selected before complainant. However, we are not persuaded that there is clear and convincing evidence to support this position. Applicant A scored substantially higher than the selectee, and if she had been a clear choice, she would have been selected first. Furthermore, since we have already found that the selection process was tainted by age discrimination, the fact that Applicant A was over the age of 40 also does not support the agency's conclusion that she would have been selected if the selectee was not. Finally, the interview notes for Applicant A reveal that the agency was concerned that she would not "fit" into the facility after having been out of the facility for some time. Accordingly, Applicant A was not a clear choice. As for Applicant B, this individual actually scored the highest in his outreach answer, but was not chosen for the position. Information on his age is not contained in the record. Interview notes for Applicant B reveal that he was going through personal issues at the time. Accordingly, he was not selected for the position.

Because of its findings that the agency did not present clear and convincing evidence that it would not have chosen the complainant even absent discrimination, the Commission did not address an agency argument that such a showing would be a complete defense under the ADEA, which was not amended by the 1991 Civil Rights Act.

Without significant analysis, in Lawthers v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120060987 (2008), the Commission awarded no relief to the complainant who was the victim of age discrimination where the agency produced clear and convincing evidence that the complainant would not have been selected even absent age discrimination. The Commission, in Lawthers, did find:

We find that the agency demonstrated, by clear and convincing evidence, that complainant would not have been selected for any of the subject positions in the absence of discrimination and that, therefore, placement in the position with back pay and benefits is not an applicable remedy. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501(c)(1).

Where unlawful discrimination is a determinative factor in the denial of an employment opportunity, the agency is required to take all steps necessary to eliminate the discriminatory practice and ensure it does not recur consistent with the remedies set forth in 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501(a). The agency's order did not provide for notification to all agency employees, training, or consideration of discipline. Therefore, we hereby modify the order of relief.

The Commission, in Lawthers, specifically ordered the agency to provide four hours of remedial training for managers and supervisors at the facility where the complainant worked, post a notice of violation, and to consider discipline of the offending employees.
