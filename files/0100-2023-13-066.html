XIII. NOTICE OF HEARING RIGHTS

As noted earlier, the agency has 180 days from receipt of the formal complaint to complete its investigation, unless the time frame is extended by mutual agreement. At the conclusion of the investigation, the agency must notify the complainant that the investigation is complete, provide the complainant with a copy of the investigative file, and further provide the complainant with a notice that within 30 days of receipt of the report of investigation, the complainant may either request a hearing or a final agency decision based on the investigatory record. 29 CFR 1614.108(f). In the absence of such a notice, the complainant may request a hearing any time after the complaint has been pending with the agency for 180 days. Id. If the complainant fails to timely respond to the notice, the agency must issue a final decision on the basis of the record and may not dismiss the complaint for failure to prosecute.

Effective September 24, 2012, 29 CFR 1614.108(g) provides:

If the agency does not send the notice required in paragraph (f) of this section within the applicable time limits, it shall, within those same time limits, issue a written notice to the complainant informing the complainant that it has been unable to complete its investigation within the time limits required by § 1614.108(f) and estimating a date by which the investigation will be completed. Further, the notice must explain that if the complainant does not want to wait until the agency completes the investigation, he or she may request a hearing in accordance with paragraph (h) of this section, or file a civil action in an appropriate United States District Court in accordance with § 1614.407(b). Such notice shall contain information about the hearing procedures.

Hearing requests are submitted directly to the EEOC office that will hear the case. The agency is responsible for notifying the complainant of the proper EEOC office in its acknowledgment letter. Under the 1992 version of the regulations, hearing requests were submitted to the agency and the agency, in turn, was responsible for transmitting those requests to the EEOC. The new regulation certainly makes more sense, as it expedites the process by eliminating the agency as an unnecessary middle-man.

It is incumbent upon the agency to prove proper receipt of the notice of hearing rights. In Williams v. Secretary of Transportation, 01951686 (1997), the Commission found that the information submitted by the agency to prove that it sent the complainant the notice of right to request a hearing contained insufficient detail:

Appellant has asserted that he was not given notice of his right to request a hearing following the investigation of his complaint. The agency stated in the FAD that the investigation was completed June 9, 1993. The Commission notes that the Report of Investigative summary is dated June 11, 1993. The record contains a letter dated June 24, 1993, advising appellant that the investigation has been completed and that a copy of the investigative file was enclosed. The letter, which contains the notation "certified mail return receipt requested," also informed appellant of the right to request a hearing before an administrative judge or an immediate final decision. The record contains a copy of the green card of an article addressed to appellant and annotated with: "completion of investigation." The green card does not show a street address, only appellant's name, the annotation above, and the signature of another individual with appellant's last name, dated July 1, 1993. The receipt card indicates that it is for article no. 423-172-620. The record contains insufficient evidence that the article described above consisted totally or in part, of the investigative file with or without a written explanation informing appellant of his right to an administrative hearing or an immediate final agency decision. The Commission cannot determine what the contents were of article 423-172-620. The complaint file contains insufficient documentation showing that appellant was informed of his right to a hearing. The Commission finds that the agency has not complied with EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.108.

An agency cannot prove proper delivery of the notice of hearing rights where the notice is sent to an incorrect address and there is no other evidence of actual receipt of the notice. Kiely v. Secretary of Labor, 01984839 (2001). The notice of hearing rights must advise the complainant of where to send the request for a hearing and the time limit for doing so. When the agency cannot establish that it properly advised the complainant of the time limit for requesting a hearing, the case should be remanded for issuance of a new notice of the right to a hearing; Staley v. Attorney General, 01A541675 (2006).

As with other notices, such as the notice of final interview, the Commission will apply the doctrine of constructive notice to the notice of election rights. Under the doctrine, constructive notice of the right to a hearing is presumed if the agency can demonstrate that the notice was delivered to a family member of suitable age and discretion. In McCabe v. Postmaster General, 01963495 (1998), the Commission held that the mentally retarded son of the complainant was not of suitable discretion where the son was able to sign the return receipt with only his first name. See also Chapter 2 on "Constructive Receipt of Notice."

When an agency does properly deliver a notice of hearing rights and the complainant does not respond, it should issue a final agency decision within 60 days of the end of the response period. Kaiser v. Postmaster General, 01971995 (2000), request for reconsideration denied, 05980414 (2000).

The notice of hearing rights must also include the report of investigation, if the investigation is complete. In Burton v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A20431 (2003), the Commission found that the time frame for filing the request for a hearing did not begin to run when the complainant's attorney received the notice of hearing rights. The time frame did not begin to run until the attorney subsequently received the investigative file, which was previously provided to the complainant with the notice of hearing rights.

Hearings are covered fully in Chapter 6 and Final Agency Decisions are covered fully in Chapter 7.
