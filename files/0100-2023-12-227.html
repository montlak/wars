4. Hearings

A failure to cooperate at the hearing level will not result in dismissal of the complaint absent egregious circumstances that reflect a clear record of delay and contumacious conduct on the part of the complainant. For example, in Cheatem v. Postmaster General, 01964546 (1997), after the appellant failed to respond to the administrative judge's requests for confirmation to proceed, the judge remanded the decision to the agency to issue a final agency decision. Instead, the agency dismissed for failure to cooperate. The Commission in Cheatem then ordered the agency to issue a final decision on the merits of the complaint:

The AJ directed appellant to provide the AJ with a written statement that she intended to go forward with this matter. The AJ also ordered appellant "to include her current mailing address and telephone number for herself and that of her representative, if any." The AJ's Order further averred that "[f]ailure to provide a confirmation statement as requested will result in the file being remanded to the Agency for failure to proceed, where upon the Agency may issue a final decision on the merits." (Emphasis in oriGINAl; footnote omitted). The record reveals that this Order was sent by certified mail to appellant at the address provided by appellant in her January 3, 1995 hearing request letter. The return receipt acknowledgement form ("the green card") shows that appellant's address was crossed out and another address substituted. The green card was signed by a named individual (who had a surname different from appellant's) on June 16, 1995.

The AJ, by letter dated July 6, 1995, in relevant part, remanded appellant's file to the agency on the grounds of "failure to proceed or to cooperate." The AJ noted, inter alia, his unsuccessful attempts to contact appellant by phone and mail, and the failure of appellant to provide a written letter of intent to go forward with her case. The AJ also remanded the file for the alternative reason of "failure to locate the complainant." The AJ concluded as follows:

Under the circumstances, rather than dismiss the complaint, we recommend that the Agency issue a Final Agency Decision on the merits of the case based on the existing record.

We find the FAD erred in dismissing appellant's complaint for failure to cooperate. We find no evidence that appellant engaged in either delay or contumacious conduct, although we also find no evidence appellant properly advised agency officials or the AJ of changes in her address or responded to the AJ's Order regarding her intent to proceed in this matter. 29 CFR § 1614.605(e) states that "[t]he Complainant shall at all times be responsible for proceeding with the complaint whether or not he or she has designated a representative." We further find, from our review of the EEO Counselor's report in this case that the EEO Counselor properly advised appellant of her duty "to immediately notify the EEO office if [her] mailing address changes." Further, having reviewed the entire record in this matter, including the investigative report and accompanying exhibits, we agree with the AJ's recommendation that the agency issue a FAD on the merits of appellant's complaint.

See also Casias v. Postmaster General, 01965651 (1997) (agency erred in dismissing a complaint after the judge remanded due to failure to prosecute at the hearing stage); Botelho v. Postmaster General, 01971830 (1997) (it is error to dismiss the complaint when the complainant fails to appear at a hearing if the record is sufficient for decision on the merits).

The Commission noted that "sufficient information is likely to be available in a case processed to the hearing stage to permit an adjudication on the merits." Gordon v. Postmaster General, 01976510 (1998). Of course, the Commission's generalized statement does not account for those cases in which the agency failed to complete an investigation prior to the request for a hearing.

Under the 1999 regulation amendments, there is no longer a need for the administrative judge to remand the case with a recommendation the agency dismiss for failure to prosecute. The administrative judge can simply issue a decision imposing dismissal as a sanction. 29 CFR 1614.109(b). However, that dismissal is generally construed by the Commission as only a cancelation of the hearing and remand to the agency for a final decision on the record. See Chapter 6 for further discussion of "Dismissal of Complaints."
