E. INTEREST ON FEE AWARDS

Under Title VII as originally passed, there was no interest on fee awards. In Cole v. Postmaster General, 05910450 (1991), the Commission found that Loeffler v. Frank, 486 U.S. 549 (1988), holding that Congress had waived sovereign immunity for the Postal Service with respect to payment of interest on back pay awards, does not constitute a waiver of sovereign immunity for the purpose of interest on fee awards involving the Postal Service.

In the 1991 Civil Rights Act, Congress specifically provided that interest is payable on awards of attorney fees and costs. See 42 USC 2000e-16(d). Although in Landgraf, the Supreme Court held that the substantive provisions of the 1991 Civil Rights Act are not retroactive and, thus, awards of compensatory damages were not applicable to cases pending on the effective date of the Act, at least one court has held that Landgraf does not apply to the interest provision for fees and costs.

In Brown v. Marsh, 868 F. Supp. 15 (D.D.C. 1994), the district court held that the interest provision on attorney fees and costs was a collateral provision and applies to any case pending on the effective date of the 1991 Civil Rights Act. The court further determined that the proper rate of interest is determined under 28 USC 1961(a) to be "a rate equal to the coupon yield equivalent (as determined by the Secretary of Treasury) of the average accepted auction price for the last auction of fifty-two week United States Treasury bills settled immediately prior to the date of judgment." Id. However, in Brown v. Marsh, 78 F.3d 645 (D.C. Cir. 1996), the court of appeals reversed the decision and held that the provision in the 1991 Civil Rights Act that allows interest on awards of costs and fees was not retroactive.

After a three-year delay in payment, the Commission had occasion to revisit the Cole decision. In Cole v. Postmaster General, 04950009 (1997), the Commission held that interest on attorney fees was proper where the agency delayed payment for three years:

[T]he agency did not actually pay the award until October 3, 1994. By this time, more than three years had passed since the Commission's decision authorizing the award had been issued. Interest on attorney's fees is awarded in order to compensate the attorney for the loss in value of money resulting from delay in payment. See Missouri v. Jenkins, 491 U.S. 274, 282-83 (1989); Pennsylvania v. Delaware Valley Citizens' Council, 483 U.S. 711, 716 (1987); Dean v. Veterans Administration, 1995 WL 605838 (N.D. Ohio March 29, 1995). Accordingly, we find that the agency's three-year delay in payment resulted in interest accruing during that time frame. The Commission will therefore enter an order directing the agency to pay petitioner's attorney for the interest resulting from its three-year delay in issuing the fee award.

The Commission's citation to Delaware Valley is interesting because that case specifically did not involve compensation for delay in payment, but instead involved whether an adjustment in the lodestar was proper to compensate for the risk of receiving no payment if the case was lost. In any event, it does appear that there are occasions where the Commission will use an award of interest to compensate for delay in payment.

In Swailes v. Secretary of Army, 01963436 (1997), the Commission, following Cole, found that the agency's five-month delay in paying attorney fees was unreasonable and that the attorney was therefore entitled to interest:

The Commission has held that a payment of interest on an award of attorney's fees is appropriate in order to compensate an attorney for the delay in payment. See Cole v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 04950009 (February 19, 1997). In Cole, the Commission noted that in August 1991, when the Commission denied the agency's request for reconsideration of its decision concerning appellant's request for attorney's fees, appellant became entitled to a specific sum. The agency, however, did not make the payment until October 1994, or more than three years thereafter. The Commission ruled that the agency should bear the loss in dollar value of the attorney's fees that occurred over the three-year period.

The Commission determined in Swailes that, where the parties agreed to cap attorney fees, a five-month delay in payment was unreasonable:

In this case, the settlement agreement required the agency to pay attorney's fees not to exceed $2,500.00. The agreement thus did not establish a specific fee, but rather placed a cap on appellant's entitlement to fees. The agreement further required Counsel to submit a request for fees in accordance with Commission and agency regulations. We note that Counsel submitted his request for fees mid-October 1995. We further note that thereafter the agency was shut down for one week in November 1995 for a government-wide furlough.

The Commission finds that in this case, where the parties placed a cap on appellant's entitlement to attorney's fees, a five-month delay between submission of a request for attorney's fees and the issuance of a check was unreasonable. We find, rather, that the agency should have been able to process appellant's request within sixty days of its receipt of the request. Factoring in the November 1995 furlough and the end-of-the-year holiday period, we find that the agency should have issued appellant a check for attorney's fees no later than the first week of January 1996. Accordingly, we find that the agency should pay appellant interest on his award of attorney's fees for the period January 8, 1996, to March 25, 1996.

It is unlikely the Commission will establish any bright-line rule for how much time must pass before interest will be awarded for delay of payment. Instead, the Commission will probably continue to decide on a case-by-case basis and consider, in addition to the length of delay, the agency's excuse for the delay. However, the Commission will probably not require an exceptionally lengthy delay because the Commission's attorney fee regulations provide an agency with only 60 days to issue a final decision on fees. Generally speaking, a delay in the vicinity of 90 days--60 days to decide and 30 days to process the payment--would be reasonable, absent unusual circumstances.
