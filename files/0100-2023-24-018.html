10. Discipline as Accommodation

Although the Merit Systems Protection Board indicated that it believes discipline can be a form of reasonable accommodation, Hougens v. USPS, 38 MSPR 135 (1988), the Commission continues to eschew any notion that discipline can play this role. It has also consistently held that a request for reasonable accommodation does not insulate an employee with a disability from discipline for misconduct where the same or similar misconduct by an employee without a disability would result in discipline. In Gibson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 02920018 (1993), the Commission explained its position:

Petitioner continues to argue in his RTR that the admonishment should be set aside as a reasonable accommodation because the incident giving rise to the admonishment was the resultant effect of his mental disability. The agency issued the admonishment in accordance with its policy on patient abuse. Reasonable accommodation is intended to assist the individual with a disability in meeting the performance and conduct standards of the agency, rather than requiring the employer to forgive violations of those standards. Therefore, an agency does not have a duty to excuse an employee's misconduct as a form of reasonable accommodation to that employee's disability where such misconduct would result in discipline or discharge if committed by another employee. See Floyd J. Stallworth v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01893705 (January 29, 1990) Request to Reopen Denied in Floyd J. Stallworth v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05900416 (August 2, 1990) (appellant was not a qualified disabled person because, by assaulting a supervisor, he had engaged in conduct for which the agency customarily terminated employees and the agency was not required to accommodate the appellant by excusing his violent conduct).

In Wilber v. Brady, 780 F. Supp. 837, 840 (D.D.C. 1992), the court stated that the Rehabilitation Act is designed to put individuals with disabilities on equal footing with non-disabled people in regards to the hiring, promotion, and discharge decisions of the federal government, but not to insulate them from disciplinary actions which would be taken against any employee regardless of his status. Thus, if an employee's misconduct would normally result in discipline, an employer is free to discipline the employee without consideration of reasonable accommodation.

It is apparent that the agency took abuse seriously because it required all employees to sign a statement evidencing that he or she read and understood its policy on the prohibition on patient abuse. Therefore, the agency may impose the same sanction on the disabled employee because agencies have the right to hold disabled employees to the same standards of conduct and performance that are imposed on other employees. There was no evidence in the record that the agency applied its patient abuse policy differently when the employee was not a disabled person.

With respect to both court cases cited by petitioner, we find that Walker v. Weinberger is factually distinguishable and Teahan v. Metro-North is analytically distinguishable and, therefore, both are not applicable to the present matter. Petitioner cited Walker v. Weinberger for the proposition that the agency should forgive his misconduct. In Walker, the government employee was discharged by the agency for his attendance infractions, not the type of misconduct engaged in by the petitioner in the instant matter. With respect to Teahan, petitioner, beyond his assertion, did not provide any analysis to support his contention that the previous decision was at "odds" with the case. Teahan involved a cause of action arising under a different section of the Rehabilitation Act (i.e., section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act). The standard enunciated in that case (i.e., prohibition against disciplining an individual "solely" because of his or her disability) is not applicable to the present matter.

The Commission further explained its position in its Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Other Reasonable Accommodation Issues," at Questions 35 & 36:

An employer never has to excuse a violation of a uniformly applied conduct rule that is job-related and consistent with business necessity. This means, for example, that an employer never has to tolerate or excuse violence, threats of violence, stealing, or destruction of property. An employer may discipline an employee with a disability for engaging in such misconduct if it would impose the same discipline on an employee without a disability.

...

An employer must make reasonable accommodation to enable an otherwise qualified employee with a disability to meet such a conduct standard in the future, barring undue hardship, except where the punishment for the violation is termination. Since reasonable accommodation is always prospective, an employer is not required to excuse past misconduct even if it is the result of the individual's disability. Possible reasonable accommodations could include adjustments to starting times, specified breaks, and leave if these accommodations will enable an employee to comply with conduct rules.
