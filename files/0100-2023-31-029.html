4. Customary Rate

In conjunction with a fee application, counsel should state the rate at which other clients actually were billed during the time period in question, and not just the rate he would have liked to receive. See Rivas v. Department of Air Force, 01821058 (1983). Likewise, statements from local counsel that they find a certain rate to be reasonable are not persuasive when there is no evidence that the attorneys actually charge that rate for similar services. See Amaro v. Postmaster General, 01A43288 (2005) (counsel's request for $300 per hour not justified where evidence showed she customarily received $200 per hour).

The burden is on the appellant to show that the hourly rate requested is reasonable. Although the attorney's qualifications are a factor in establishing the customary rate, where no other evidence is submitted those qualifications may be insufficient to justify the requested rate. For example, in Daniels v. Secretary of Army, 01951767 (1997), the Commission found that the mere submission of the attorney's resume did not support his claim for a rate of $175 per hour, and therefore disallowed the time billed at this rate:

As noted above, appellant had the burden of producing satisfactory evidence to support the attorney's fees request. A review of the record showed that there was no evidence to establish the reasonableness of the $175.00 hourly rate. That is, there was no evidence to show that this was the attorney's customary billing rate for fee-paying clients, nor was there any corroborative evidence of billing rates from one or more attorneys in the same community who were engaged in a practice similar to his own. In this regard, the Commission finds that the one page resume submitted by appellant's attorney is insufficient to establish that a rate of $175.00 an hour is reasonable. Based on the foregoing, the Commission finds that the agency properly disallowed the 8 hours expended in April 1993 and the requested rate of $175.00 an hour.

The customary rate is "determined by the 'prevailing market rates in the relevant community.'" In Hayes v. Secretary of Air Force, 05880813 (1989), the Commission explained:

Most circuits have found that an attorney's customary billing rate for fee-paying clients is the most reliable evidence of the prevailing market rate. See Grendel's Den, Inc. v. Larkin, 749 F.2d 945 (1st Cir. 1984); Cunningham v. City of McKeesport, 753 F.2d 262, 265-68 (3d Cir. 1985); vacated and remanded on other grounds, 106 S. Ct. 3324; Vaughns v. Board of Education, 598 F. Supp. 1262, 1280-82 (D. Md. 1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 1244 (4th Cir. 1985); Canasa v. City of New Orleans, 619 F. Supp. 39, 46 (E.D. La. 1985); Kelly v. Metropolitan County Board of Education, 773 F.2d 677, 683 (6th Cir. 1985) (en banc), cert. denied, 106 S. Ct. 853 (1986); Ohio-Sealey Mattress Mfg. Co. v. Sealy Inc., 776 F.2d 646, 654 nn. 7 & 8, 664 (7th Cir. 1985); Reid v. New York, 584 F. Supp. 461 (S.D.N.Y. 1984). However, in Laffey v. Northwest Airlines, Inc., supra, the D.C. Circuit went even further and established a presumption that a private attorney's normal billing rate is a reasonable rate for computing a fee award. The court in Laffey noted the Supreme Court's language in Blum that "the rates charged in private representations may afford relevant comparisons." Blum, 465 U.S. at 895, n.11. It also relied on its prior decision in National Association of Concerned Veterans, which held that "the best evidence would be the hourly rate customarily charged by the affiant or by his law firm." 675 F.2d at 1325. The court in Laffey cited several reasons for presuming the attorney's own customary billing rate is a reasonable hourly rate: it avoids the difficulty of judicial ratemaking; it prevents arbitrarily picking a fee; and, it avoids engendering a "second major litigation" over the scope of attorney's fees. Laffey, 746 F.2d at 18-22. Thus, the court determined that the fee applicant must provide evidence of the rates charged by the applicant or the law firm for similar work in private representations. Next, a "bracket" is established by the fee applicant which shows the prevailing low and high market rates charged by other firms for similar work in the same community. If the customary rate charged by the fee applicant falls within the rate brackets, it is the market rate for purposes of calculating the lodestar. Laffey, 746 F.2d at 24.25.

In Hayes, the Commission rejected evidence of past fee awards received by the appellant's attorney because the "best evidence" of the customary rate was the fee actually charged the complainant. The fee was within the "bracket" for the prevailing market rate in the area and was consistent with rates charged by other sole practitioners in the community.

It is appropriate to consider evidence of the hourly rate used by an attorney to bill other private litigants, even where the legal services rendered are for matters not involving employment discrimination. See Douglas v. Secretary of Air Force, 05890186 (1989). First, there was no indication that the attorney's customary billing rate differentiated in the types of legal services provided to his clients. In addition, such rates are indicative of the "opportunity cost of what appellant's attorney turned away in order to handle federal sector EEO claims." Id.

In Mareno v. Secretary of Veterans Administration, 01943104 (1996), the Commission reaffirmed its position that the proper customary hourly rate is the reasonable hourly rate in effect at the time of the award and not the time the services are provided. As the Commission emphasized in Mareno, this rule was not changed by the 1991 Civil Rights Act:

The goal of the fee award should be to place the attorney in the same economic position as if the matter had been billed and paid like any regular component of the attorney's practice for a paying client rather that having to wait for payment at the end of the litigation. In Pennsylvania v. Delaware Valley Citizen's Council, 483 U.S. 711, 716 (1987), the Court pointed out that in setting fees for prevailing counsel, courts have regularly recognized the delay factor, either by basing the award on current rates or by adjusting the fee based on historical rates to reflect its present value. In Missouri v. Jenkins, 491 U.S. 274, 282-283 (1989), the Court stated that compensation received several years after the services were rendered is not equivalent to the same dollar amount received reasonably promptly as the legal services are performed. The court held that an adjustment for delay in payment is an appropriate factor in the determination of what constitutes a reasonable attorney's fee. The Commission, consistent with prevailing case law, finds that an adjustment for delay in payment is properly measured by compensating AR at the current, rather than historical hourly rates for all work performed on appellant's complaint.

The Commission notes the agency's argument on appeal that, pursuant to provisions of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, appellant's attorney-representative should be compensated at her historical, not current, rates. The agency, however, cites no specific provision of the 1991 Act in support of its argument. The Commission therefore finds that the agency's argument is without merit.

See also Layman v. Postmaster General, 01995568 (2002) (an attorney was entitled to an hourly rate of $275, in effect at the time of the fee petition, and not the $250 rate in effect at the time services were rendered).

In Safford v. Postmaster General, 01952348 (1997), the Commission found that because the appellant's representative did not set forth an hourly rate in his petition, nor justify with sufficient specificity why he was entitled to the amount requested, the agency's rate of $150 per hour was reasonable. In rejecting a request for $200 per hour, the Commission in Safford also noted that the agency had made specific inquiries of hourly rates in the appropriate geographic area in determining that $150 per hour was reasonable:

Regarding the hourly rate at which fees in this case are to be awarded, the Commission notes that AR did not set forth an hourly rate in his fee petition. When queried by the agency, AR claimed an hourly rate of $200. The agency's further inquiries in the geographic area, however, led the agency to determine that $150 per hour was a reasonable rate. AR set forth in his fee petition that he had "personal knowledge of the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions, the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly..." without providing any of the particulars, such as what was novel or difficult about a case in which the chief contention was that appellant had been made to work outside of her medical limitations. Neither has AR presented any evidence of the prevailing hourly rates in his geographic area. Because AR has failed to present sufficient evidence to establish the hourly fee to which he is entitled, the Commission finds that the $150 hourly rate offered by the agency is reasonable.
