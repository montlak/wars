B. WITNESS CREDIBILITY

The EEOC has issued conflicting decisions on whether the credibility of agency witnesses should be considered in evaluating whether the agency has articulated a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action. On the one hand, the Commission has held that even though an agency offers an explanation for its actions, it does not sustain its burden of production where the administrative judge determines the testimony of agency officials is not credible. See Holmes v. Postmaster General, 01940014 (1994). In that case, the Commission upheld the credibility findings of the administrative judge, as well as his finding that the agency had failed to sustain its burden of production. See also Hollis v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01934600 (1994).

However, in Thompson v. Postmaster General, 01971189 (2000), the Commission held that it was legal error for the administrative judge to conclude that the agency failed to meet its burden of production based, in part, upon the judge's assessment that the agency official's explanation for not promoting the complainant was not credible. In Thompson, the Commission attempted to distinguish Hollis on the basis that the agency's failure to preserve the promotion package in that case contributed to its decision. However, the agency had also failed to preserve the promotion package in Thompson.

The reality is that the Commission's previous holdings in Holmes and Hollis are simply incorrect. Credibility is properly considered as part of the analysis of pretext. See Thompson, supra, see also Jones v. Postmaster General, 07A20004 (2003) (holding administrative judge erred in finding that agency had not met burden of production because its proffered reason was not credible, but affirming finding of discrimination because credibility findings went to the issue of pretext). In fact, in Herskowitz v. Secretary of Transportation, 07A50031 (2006), the Commission went to great lengths to explain its affirmation of the administrative judge's credibility determination with regard to the agency's failure to sustain its burden of production:

Furthermore, the Commission finds substantial evidence in the record supports the AJ's finding that, in this case, the agency failed to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the complainant's non-selection. The AJ appropriately determined that the credibility of the RMO was critical, especially in regards to his subjective reasons for not selecting the complainant. The AJ specifically pointed to the lack of consistency and truthfulness in the RMO's testimony, leading to his impeachment on several significant points. The AJ found that, while on cross-examination, the RMO denied his personal preferences and ability to work with the selectee was a factor in his determination, and when confronted with previous contradictory testimony from his direct testimony, he "became flustered and began to ramble in an attempt to clarify his present and past testimony." (HT 405, ln 9-14; 406-407, ln. 7).

Additionally, the evidence of record confirms the AJ's finding that the RMO falsely informed the agency's headquarters that both the peer and subordinate panels had affirmatively endorsed the selectee when in fact only the subordinate panel in plurality decision did so. The record shows the peer panel was never asked to make a recommendation but made determinations strictly by scores, and reported that the complainant received a higher test score based upon her responses to interview questions. (HT 235-36, ROI D-1B). The AJ noted that when confronted with the falsehood between his testimony and written evidence of the letter sent to headquarters, the RMO back-tracked in his reasoning and stated the panels were insignificant and not required by regulations, although the record showed his practice was to consistently use them in the selection process. (HT 411, ln. 5-10, ROI Dl-A). The AJ correctly noted the RMO's candor and credibility problems in his determinations.

Due to the judge's first-hand knowledge through personal observations of the demeanor and conduct of the witness testifying, an AJ's credibility determinations are entitled to deference. Grant v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01985972 (Aug. 2, 2001). Here, the AJ specifically pointed to the RMO's evasive tactics during cross-examination and his method of oral sparring in an attempt to manipulate the questions to his advantage. The RMO engaged in a repetitious practice of creating "artificial pauses" between questions and answers to consider his response in length. This practice extended past the point of "carefully listening to each question," as the agency argued he had been instructed to do, to such a point the AJ was forced to intervene and instruct the RMO that no further questions would be repeated. (HT 402-03).

. . .

The selection decision was made by the RMO, and the RMO's credibility was so compromised that his reasons for the selection decision could not be relied upon. Accordingly, without the RMO's testimony, the agency could not establish legitimate and nondiscriminatory reasons for why complainant was not selected, especially in light of the fact that one of the panels the RMO allegedly relied upon scored complainant higher for the position than the selectee. We therefore discern no basis to disturb the AJ's decision in this regard.

See also Rice v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A52575 (2006) (finding that the agency failed to sustain its burden of articulating a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the complainant's rating where the supervisor's testimony was found not credible).

The use of subjective criteria in a personnel action in combination with evasive testimony by the official responsible for taking the action can easily lead to a finding of discrimination. For example, in Rodriguez v. Social Security Administration, 0720100032 (2011):

Upon review of the record, we find no objective evidence that undermines the AJ's determination that AM's testimony was not worthy of belief. Contrary to the Agency's argument, an accurate reading of the AJ's conclusion is that AM refused to admit Complainant performed duties that were outside his job description or that the SLC duties he performed were "special." It is clear from the record that AM had some issues with Complainant 's performance as well as what AM described as excessive use of his cell phone while at work. While it is possible that those issues created a blind spot for AM with regard to the fact that Complainant was the only Claims Representative who moved heavy equipment and crawled under desks, they do not excuse AM's refusal to admit Complainant was performing needed work in the office that other Claims Representatives were not having to perform.

Subjective criteria are subject to heightened scrutiny because they are inherently vulnerable to unlawful motivation. We find that the AJ correctly characterized the criteria AM used to decide who deserved an award as very subjective. For example, one of Complainant's colleagues was given an award because she contacted the relative of a potentially suicidal client and expressed empathy The record suggests that this individual had good skills for handling stressful and sensitive situations and assumed responsibility for them voluntarily. While this conduct may in fact be worthy of recognition, we agree with the AJ that it does not appear to be outside the normal job duties of a Claims Representative. Complainant, on the other hand, performed technical duties and some physical labor for the office, yet this was not acknowledged or recognized, and it is unclear why AM valued it less than he did interpersonal contact with the clients. We find that from this example the AJ reasonably inferred that the Agency's explanation for who deserved an ECSA award and who did not was false and was more likely than not, intended to cover up gender based discriminatory animus.
