WEINGARTEN MEETINGS AND FORMAL DISCUSSIONS

Union representatives have the right to be present at two specific types of meetings between agency managers and bargaining unit employees:

  1. 1. Investigative or Weingarten meetings.
  2. 2. Formal discussions.

An investigative or Weingarten meeting happens when:

  • • An agency representative meets with a bargaining unit employee in connection with an investigation;
  • •The employee reasonably believes that the examination may result in disciplinary action; and
  • • The employee requests union representation.

When the employee makes such a request, the union must be given the opportunity to be represented. The term Weingarten is drawn from a 1975 private sector decision, NLRB v. J. Weingarten, Inc.

"Formal discussion" is a term contained in the labor relations statute. It refers to meetings held by representatives of management with bargaining unit employees concerning grievances, personnel policies or practices, or general conditions of employment. Management must notify the union in advance if a formal discussion is going to be held and give it an opportunity to attend and participate.

Managers need to understand the rules on Weingarten meetings and formal discussions because the job of notifying the union and observing the union's rights will often fall on their shoulders. The union's right to be given the opportunity to be represented exists in the labor relations statute, so a violation of that right will result in an unfair labor practice.

Note: Union officials may also have the right to be present at other meetings under the terms of their bargaining unit's labor agreement. For example, the labor agreement may provide that employees are entitled to have a union representative present when discussing development of a performance improvement plan.

The Weingarten right

Managers must be aware of the extent of the Weingarten right because a violation could subject the agency to an unfair labor practice charge.

The relevant statutory provision says that an exclusive representative shall be given the opportunity to be represented at an agency examination of a bargaining unit employee in connection with an investigation if: 1) the employee reasonably believes that the examination may result in disciplinary action against the employee; and 2) the employee requests representation.

The idea behind this right is that individual employees may be too frightened or intimidated to adequately present their side of the story in an investigative interview. Presumably, a union representative may help them to better present helpful facts.

In order to extend the Weingarten right to employees properly, you must:

  • • Know how to identify and recognize such situations.
  • • Understand the options available when employees request representation.
  • • Understand the general guidelines governing the conduct of both agency and union representatives during such meetings.

Note: There is no general requirement that an employee is informed of the right to ask for a union representative, but some labor agreements contain this requirement. Check with your LR office.

In order to qualify as a genuine investigative Weingarten situation, five specific elements or conditions must be met:

  1. 1. A bargaining unit employee is being -- or is about to be -- questioned.
  2. 2. The person doing the questioning is a management representative.
  3. 3. The nature of the discussion is, or will be, investigative (not counseling sessions, performance reviews, etc.).
  4. 4. The employee being questioned could reasonably fear that the discussion will or could lead to disciplinary action.
  5. 5. The employee involved asks for union representation.

Note: An employee does not have to specifically request "union" representation. A request for "an attorney" or "somebody to talk to" is sufficient to put the agency on notice, triggering the union's right to represent the employee.

A meeting or discussion qualifies as a Weingarten meeting only if all five elements are present.

Limits on union representation

At a Weingarten meeting, the union rep plays an active role in advising and assisting the employee. However, there are limits as to what the union rep can do. For example:

  • • Union reps are not allowed to impede, disrupt, or prevent an investigative interview.
  • • The union rep may not answer questions on the employee's behalf. The agency is entitled to answers directly from the employee.
  • • While the union rep may object to certain questions, the management representative decides whether the question will be changed or dropped, or whether an answer will be required.
  • • The union rep may not require the management representative to ask any specific question. It is the agency's right to determine what information it needs.
  • • The agency can reject a particular union rep under certain special circumstances, such as situations in which the choice of representative might compromise the investigation. This may be the case if the union rep is to be questioned as part of the investigation.
  • • An employee does not have the right to refuse to answer questions in a non-criminal investigation. If the union rep advises the employee to remain silent, the employee should be told that failure to answer questions could result in disciplinary action.

If the employee is eventually disciplined, and the union believes the management representative conducted an improper investigative meeting or otherwise failed to allow the employee's side of the story to be told, the union could file a grievance. It could also choose to file a ULP charge if it alleges that it was not allowed to fully represent the employee during the meeting (e.g., union rep wasn't allowed to say anything, etc.).

Note: If the employee's valid request for representation at a Weingarten meeting is denied, the employee's refusal to participate is protected by the statute. In such cases, the employee does not commit insubordination.

Formal discussions

When a union is voted in, it legally becomes the "exclusive representative" of all employees in the designated bargaining unit. This means it has the sole right to deal with agency management in establishing or modifying employees' conditions of employment and personnel policies.

To protect that right, the labor relations law contains the concept of "formal discussions." Agency officials must give the union an opportunity to be present any time they will be discussing personnel policies, practices, or conditions of employment, as well as a grievance or appeal, with employees.

Consequently, managers must be able to spot potential formal discussions before they occur and notify the union. The union will then choose whether to attend the meeting.

Identifying formal discussions. Ask yourself these questions to help you to identify potential formal discussion situations:

  1. 1. Will the meeting or discussion include both management officials (supervisors, managers, personnel specialists) and at least one bargaining unit employee?
  2. 2. Will there be discussion of a new or changed personnel policy or condition of employment, or a detailed interpretation of an existing one? Or will the meeting involve discussion of an active grievance or appeal?
  3. 3. Will the discussion be "formal" in nature, rather than an unplanned, casual conversation?

If the answer to all three questions is "yes," the meeting satisfies the requirements of a formal discussion.

It's important to remember that the formality of a discussion is determined by the totality of circumstances. However, there are specific factors to consider -- known as "indicia of formality" -- that will indicate whether the meeting was formal. These indicia, or indicators, include the number and level of management representatives in attendance, whether the meeting was scheduled or unplanned, whether a formal agenda was used, how long the meeting lasted, the location of the discussion, whether attendance was mandatory, and how the meeting was conducted (for example, notes were taken).

A meeting may have some of the characteristics of formality and still not be a formal discussion. On the other hand, conversations conducted by telephone may still qualify as formal discussions. If you're unsure whether the discussion qualifies as "formal," ask for help from your LR office.

Note: OPM guidance regarding Executive Order 14025 states that new-employee orientation sessions may also be considered formal discussions under certain circumstances. It also asserts that during periodic breaks in such orientation sessions, the union may be allowed to solicit bargaining unit employees to join the union. Check with your LR office about how to navigate to such situations.

Mistakes to avoid

Many avoidable labor disputes start because individual supervisors and managers make errors in dealing with the union. Here are tips to avoid mistakes in the areas of Weingarten meetings and formal discussions.

Do not:

  • • Improperly exclude union officials from Weingarten meetings or formal discussions. The right to attend belongs to the union and is protected by law.
  • • Allow a union representative to take over a meeting. Union representatives are not allowed to impede, disrupt, or prevent an investigative interview, or to answer questions for the employee.
  • • Continue an investigative meeting after an employee requests union representation. Wait until the union rep arrives. Questioning the employee while the union representative is still in transit could open you up to charges of violating Weingarten rights.

Ask FMG

Q. Who qualifies as a "management representative" in connection with an investigative meeting?

A. All supervisors, managers, personnel specialists, and agency security personnel engaged in investigative work.

Q. Are employees entitled to their representative of choice?

A. Possibly. Some agreements specify the union official who will represent a particular part of an organization. In most cases, however, it will be up to the union leadership to determine who will handle a Weingarten meeting. An employee cannot unduly delay an investigative meeting to obtain his representative of choice if other representatives are available.

Q. What if an employee is being questioned about possible wrongdoing but doesn't request a union representative?

A. In that case, it is proper to commence the investigation without a representative present.

Q. Do I have to inform an employee of the right to representation before beginning the investigative questioning?

A. The law does not require you to do so, but your labor agreement might. Check to make sure.

Q. If I'm asking questions but have no intention of disciplining an employee, is it still necessary to allow a representative to participate?

A. Yes, unless you are certain you will not recommend discipline and you clearly inform the employee that there will be no discipline, regardless of the employee's answers to your questions. Remember that it is the employee's reasonable belief that discipline could occur that triggers the right to representation. Unless you are prepared to guarantee the employee that no disciplinary action will result no matter how he answers the questions, you should allow representation if all other elements of a Weingarten meeting are present.

Q. Do routine staff meetings qualify as formal discussions?

A. Usually no, unless you routinely discuss personnel policies and how to interpret and apply them, or use the meeting to announce new or changed policies.

Q. Are performance counseling sessions considered formal discussions?

A. No. Presumably what you are discussing there is the employee's performance relative to an established standard, not the establishment or change of general personnel policies.

Q. What if an employee doesn't want a union representative present while discussing the grievance with me?

A. Since the right to be present belongs to the union -- not the employee -- the union still must be notified and allowed to attend if it wishes to do so.

Q. If an employee asks me a question about how a policy is applied -- for example, how to request annual leave -- is that a formal discussion?

A. No. Casual, informal responses to routine questions about existing policies do not qualify as formal discussions.

Q. Would a verbal warning to an employee that continued tardiness will result in discipline qualify as a formal discussion?

A. No. In this kind of situation, you are merely advising the employee that conduct relating to an existing standard is inappropriate. You are not establishing or changing a personnel policy.
