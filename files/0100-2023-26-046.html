B. PRIMA FACIE CASE

In Dickerson v. USPS, 01822242 (1983), the Commission adopted the McDonnell Douglas formula for analyzing reprisal allegations. See also Hochstadt v. Worcester Foundation for Experimental Biology, Inc., 425 F. Supp. 318 (D. Mass. 1976), aff'd, 545 F.2d 222 (1st Cir. 1976). The employee in Dickerson established a prima facie case of reprisal by showing that her performance evaluation was downgraded after she initiated informal counseling, naming her rating supervisor as the RMO and calling him a "racist." The agency failed to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason when it admitted that the RMO probably overreacted when informed of the allegation that he was racist.

An appellant may establish a prima facie case of reprisal by showing: "(1) that she opposed unlawful employment practices or participated in Title VII proceedings; (2) that her employer was aware of her protected activity; (3) that subsequently her employer took adverse action against her; and (4) the adverse action followed her protected activity within such period of time that retaliatory motive can be inferred." Downing v. USPS, 01822326 (1983). In that case, the appellant rebutted the agency's proffered explanation that she was terminated because the quality and quantity of her work product fell short of the agency's expectations. The appellant demonstrated that the agency terminated her shortly after the filing of her EEO complaint. The agency later reinstated her because it found the poor performance rating was not based on an official evaluation. Six days after her reinstatement, the agency conducted an evaluation and again rated her performance as poor and terminated her. The Commission found the second termination was in reprisal for filing an EEO complaint based on evidence that the agency intentionally increased appellant's duties beyond those normally scheduled for similarly situated employees and made no allowance for the increase. The evidence also showed that the appellant was monitored more closely than other employees, was given less assistance than other female employees, and that her supervisor had not observed her work long enough to fairly assess the level of her performance.

The EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Retaliation and Related Issues, No. 915.004 at II. (2016), breaks retaliation claims into three essential elements:

1) protected activity: "participation" in an EEO process or "opposition" to discrimination

2) materially adverse action taken by the employer; and

3) requisite level of causal connection between the protected activity and the materially adverse action.

In this formulation of the elements of a reprisal case, knowledge is addressed as part of establishing a causal connection. However, knowledge remains an element of the complainant's prima facie case and failure to establish knowledge on the part of the agency official taking the action is a failure to establish a prima facie case. Peltier v. Secretary of Treasury, 01983060 (2001). The converse, however, is not true. Knowledge of previous EEO activity, alone, is not sufficient to establish an inference of reprisal. See Campbell v. Secretary of the Army, No. 19-2395 (4th Cir. 2022) (proof of supervisor's knowledge of complainant's EEO activity was not enough to save the claim from summary judgment dismissal).

In Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 01910116 (1991), the Commission found that if there are no comparative employees for the purpose of establishing a prima facie case, it will look to other evidence, such as the temporal proximity of the protected activity and the adverse action:

Accordingly, appellant may establish a prima facie case by showing that: (1) he engaged in protected activity under Title VII; (2) the agency was aware of the protected activity; (3) appellant was subsequently subjected to adverse action by the agency; and (4) absent other evidence tending to establish retaliatory motivation, the adverse treatment followed the protected activity within such a short period of time that retaliatory motivation can be inferred. Hochstadt v. Worcester Foundation for Experimental Biology, 425 F. Supp. 318, 324 (D. Mass.), aff'd, 545 F.2d 222 (1st Cir. 1976).

In another case, the appellant failed to demonstrate a prima facie case of reprisal where a period of 17 months elapsed between the filing of her first EEO complaint and the next incident of alleged discrimination and there was no evidence to show the RMO was aware of the previous complaint. Frederick v. USPS, 01830361 (1983).

Although a prima facie case of reprisal often is stated as including a requirement that the complainant demonstrate he was meeting the legitimate expectations of his employer, this is not a bright-line requirement. On a request for reconsideration, the Commission clarified what is required to establish a prima facie case of reprisal discrimination in Stephan v. Secretary of Treasury, 05950162 (1996):

The previous decision found that appellant had failed to establish a prima facie case of reprisal because he did not show that he was "meeting the legitimate expectations of his employer or that a comparative employee was treated more favorably in circumstances similar to his." The Commission, however, has held that appellant can establish a prima facie case of reprisal by showing that: (1) he engaged in protected EEO activity; (2) the agency was aware of his protected activity; (3) he was subject to an adverse action; and (4) the adverse action followed his protected activity within such a period of time that a retaliatory motivation may be inferred.

Where the complaint is based on the opposition clause, the employee, as part of a prima facie case, must demonstrate that he or she had a good faith belief the practice opposed was discriminatory. It does not suffice to simply allege reprisal for complaining about working conditions, unless those conditions are allegedly discriminatory. See, e.g., Brown v. Secretary of Energy, 01973113 (1998).

A prima facie case of reprisal must be established by a preponderance of the evidence. See Cannon v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01831390 (1983). As with other bases of discrimination, the complainant is not required to meet this burden until the investigation stage. An agency errs in dismissing a complaint prior to investigation on the basis that the employee has not engaged in protected activity. See Contreras v. USPS, 01850542 (1986). The dismissal is tantamount to a decision on the merits.

The employee, as in other Title VII cases, is not held to formal pleading rules in alleging reprisal. The fact that an employee does not specifically identify her conduct as protected does not permit an agency to reject the complaint. The complainant repeatedly stated, in precomplaint counseling and in her formal complaint, that she believed she was terminated for visiting the EEO office to discuss the possibility of filing a complaint. This was sufficient to raise the issue of reprisal. Brazell v. Secretary of Army, 01902793 (1990).

Because the Commission follows the McDonnell Douglas formulation in analyzing cases of reprisal, the agency must proffer a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions. This frames the issues of the case with sufficient clarity so the complainant receives a full and fair opportunity to show that the proffered reason is a pretext for reprisal.
