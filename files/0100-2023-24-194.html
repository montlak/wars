IX. THE GENETIC INFORMATION NONDISCRIMINATION ACT--OVERVIEW

In 2000, President Clinton issued Executive Order 13145 (Feb. 8, 2000), prohibiting discrimination in federal employment based on protected genetic information and also containing restrictions on the use and dissemination of genetic information. The EEOC had no enforcement authority under the Executive Order. However, it was responsible for coordinating the government's policy against discrimination based on protected genetic information.

On May 21, 2008, Congress passed the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act of 2008, Pub. L. 110-233 enacting the first statutory prohibitions on discrimination based on genetic information. Much of GINA involves prohibiting health insurance companies from using genetic information to discrimination against individuals. However, Title II of GINA does contain prohibitions on employment discrimination based on genetic information and the EEOC has been given enforcement authority over those provisions. 42 USC 2000ff-6(a)(1), (e)(1).

On March 2, 2009, the Commission issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking under GINA. It published its final regulations, which are codified at 29 CFR Part 1635, on October 11, 2009. 75 FR 68912.

In many ways, particularly with respect to the federal sector, GINA is a prophylactic statute in that it seeks to prevent a problem before it become a widespread occurrence and it also seeks to strike a balance that encourages genetic research while protecting individuals from the potential abuse of genetic information about those individuals and their families.

As the Commission explains in the Introduction to Part 1635:

Experts predict that the twenty-first century will see tremendous strides in the new field of genomic medicine, bringing it into mainstream medical practice. The National Human Genome Research Institute (NHGRI), the institute within the National Institutes of Health responsible for the mapping of the human genome, notes that "by identifying the genetic factors associated with disease, researchers may be able to design more effective drugs; to prescribe the best treatment for each patient; to identify and monitor individuals at high risk from disease; and to avoid adverse drug reactions." NHGRI, The Future of Genomic Medicine: Policy Implications for Research and Medicine (Bethesda, Md. Nov. 16, 2005), available at http://www.genome.gov/17516574 (last visited July 7, 2010).

Many genetic tests now exist that can inform individuals whether they may be at risk for developing a specific disease or disorder. But just as the number of genetic tests increases, so do the concerns of the general public about whether they may be at risk of losing access to health coverage or employment if insurers or employers have their genetic information. Congress enacted GINA to address these concerns, by prohibiting discrimination based on genetic information and restricting acquisition and disclosure of such information, so that the general public would not fear adverse employment--or health coverage--related consequences for having a genetic test or participating in research studies that examine genetic information. Scientific advances require significant cooperation and participation from members of the general public. In the absence of such participation, geneticists and other scientists would be hampered in their research, and efforts to develop new medicines and treatments for genetic diseases and disorders would be slowed or stymied.

75 FR 68912.

Although Title II is designed to protect individuals from employment discrimination on the basis of genetic information, the very nature of genetic information requires that its reach extend to family members in the sense that an employer is prohibited from discriminating against an individual because of genetic information about family members.

To a large degree, GINA is patterned after Title VII of the 1964 Civil Rights Act in terms of its structure. As a result, the Commission has made clear in Part 1635 that it will look to its Title VII jurisprudence in interpreting GINA.
