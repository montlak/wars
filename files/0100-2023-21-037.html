4. Context in Which the Alleged Harassment Occurred

The determination of whether alleged harassing conduct is actionable or merely trivial should take into account the context in which the conduct occurred. Though a reprisal case, the Commission's discussion in Leatherman v. Secretary of Navy, 01983615 (2000), applies to all bases of claimed harassment:

While it is well established that a group of isolated incidents will not be regarded as creating a discriminatory environment, we find that management engaged in a pattern of offensive conduct during the early months of 1994 by taking every chance to belittle and criticize complainant in an attempt to discredit and humiliate her. See EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment, N-915-050, No. 137 (March 19, 1990) ([a] 'hostile environment' claim generally requires a showing of a pattern of offensive conduct). Our belief that complainant was subjected to a hostile environment is strengthened by the background information provided by some of her co-workers. Statements made in several affidavits make it clear that many workers in this office felt that management was hostile toward complainant. As previously noted, CW6 testified that when she began working for the agency, S2 told her not to associate with complainant. CW5 noted that she was forced by S1 to write a memo which reflected poorly on complainant's performance. Based on a review of the entire record, we find that a reasonable person in complainant's shoes would have found the conduct engaged in by S1 and S2 to be humiliating and harassing. We therefore agree with complainant that S1 and S2 subjected her to a hostile work environment.

See also Louvenia S. v. Secretary of Navy, 0120160699 (2016) (supervisor's comments to complainant that "nobody likes her," "telling her in a meeting in which she was the only non-Caucasian employee to 'cease talking and shut up,' excluding her from budget meetings, taking away her responsibility for completing the daily report and giving this responsibility to a Caucasian employee, and not including her in regular meetings with colleagues" sufficiently severe to set forth an actionable claim of harassment); Wild v. Secretary of Defense, 01984101 (2000) (reference to complainant's penis in a performance evaluation, combined with supervisor's regular use of sexual profanity, humor, and sexist remarks about men was sufficiently severe or pervasive).

Of course, there are cases which are just not that difficult to decide. For example, the Commission found a sexually hostile environment in Waskewicz v. General Services Administration, 01A00965 (2002), where

the record clearly establishes that swimsuit calendars, posters and other pictures of women in swimsuits, specifically bikinis, had been displayed in the cubicles since 1994. Complainant gave the Commission and the agency copies of pictures which were hung on the cubicle walls. The pictures depict women in bikinis or with clothing in suggestive poses. Complainant also provided documentation to show that the cubicles within the office did not have high walls and that she could easily see over the walls while passing by. The pictures were on display in Co-worker 1's cubicle until December 1996 and Co-worker 2's cubicle in May 1997. Furthermore, it is not disputed that other pictures began appearing within the office on cubicle walls near complainant's workstation in 1996 and into 1997. Complainant also averred that catalogs for women's lingerie and sex toys were placed on her desk. The record also shows that an agency official played a prank in which a training video was replaced with a sex exercise tape. Based on the continued display of scantily clad women and the sex-based pranks in the workplace during a period of two and a half years, we find that complainant was subjected to a hostile work environment.

In Roy v. Secretary of Interior, 01971341 (1999), the Commission found the incidents, while not particularly egregious standing alone, constituted a hostile environment when viewed in their totality:

We find that the incidents in support of appellant's harassment claim support a similar conclusion. In particular, the incident set forth in Issue 9, i.e., the RO angrily confronting appellant about a file, appears to have been typical of his behavior towards her, and there was testimony indicating that the RO frequently yelled and swore at appellant. Similarly, regarding the incident set forth in Issue 2, the RO not only offered conflicting reasons why he refused to assist appellant in investigating the homicide, but there is evidence indicating that he readily assisted male investigators who made requests for assistance. The most compelling piece of evidence in support of appellant's claim is the birthday card she received from the RO and two other male employees. The card stated, "We would like to wish you a professional, dignified birthday by slapping the tune to 'Happy Birthday' on our butts with our pants around our ankles." Evidence of record revealed that the RO did not routinely give his male subordinates birthday cards, and that, in effect, he singled appellant out in giving her this card. Based on the foregoing, the Commission finds that appellant was subjected to sex-based harassment by the RO.
