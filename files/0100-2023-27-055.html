1. Unfair Advantages in Selection Process

In some cases, the complainant does not allege that there was an actual preselection, but alleges that the process was skewed in a way that gives an unfair advantage to a certain group or groups. One such case was Markuns v. Office of Personnel Management, 01965969 (1997). In Markuns, a candidate for an administrative law judge position alleged discrimination in the selection process when certain information regarding the selection process, including information in a judge's handbook, was given to some candidates but not others. In Markuns, the Commission found the complainant stated a claim for relief.

In Dow v. Office of Personnel Management, 05980404 (2001), the Commission affirmed its decision in Markuns finding that a claim that a candidate for a position was denied material information alleges a sufficient personal injury to be aggrieved. The Commission in Dow held:

On request for reconsideration, complainant argues that the prior decision involved an erroneous interpretation of material fact or law. Specifically, complainant alleges that he did suffer a personal injury when he was not provided with the handbook and not told of its existence because this lessened his chances of scoring well on the ALJ examination as compared to the female and minority applications [sic] who were provided with the handbook. Complainant also argues that the previous decision is inconsistent with an earlier decision involving the same issue, citing Markuns v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Appeal No. 01965969 (June 23, 1997).

After a careful review of the entire record, we find that complainant's complaint was improperly fragmented by the agency. Correctly defined, complainant's claim is that, due to his race/color, sex, and national origin, he was denied an equal opportunity to compete for a position as an ALJ when the agency failed to provide him with a copy of the handbook and placed an SSA ALJ on the panel that interviewed him. See Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 CFR Part 1614 (EEO-MD-110), as revised, November 9, 1999, at 5-5 (agencies must avoid fragmentation of EEO complaints).

Commission precedent holds that an agency shall accept a complaint from any aggrieved employee or applicant for employment who believes that he or she has been discriminated against by that agency because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age or disabling condition. 29 CFR 1614.103; 1614.106(a). The Commission's federal sector case precedent has long defined an "aggrieved employee" as one who suffers a present harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. See Diaz v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994).

In Markuns, the complainant alleged that he was discriminated against when the agency denied him material information regarding the agency's method for evaluating ALJ applicants, such as that contained in the handbook. The Commission, noting that the complaint raised the issue of whether complainant was discriminatorily denied a handbook, found that the complaint stated a claim. The previous decision in the case at hand offered no grounds upon which it could be distinguished from Markuns. The agency attempts to offer such a distinction in its response to complainant's request for reconsideration, noting that in Markuns, the complainant was "denied" the handbook, whereas in the case at hand, the agency "did not provide" complainant with the opportunity to obtain it. This minor difference in language does not alter the fact that both complainant and Mr. Markuns alleged that they were not given the handbook due to protected bases and that this negatively affected their chances of becoming ALJs.

Although the previous decision found that complainant failed to allege an injury resulting from this alleged action, it is clear from the record that complainant alleged that the agency's failure to provide him with this assistance, along with the placement of an SSA ALJ on the interview panel, lessened his chances for selection. We find, in agreement with Markuns, that this states a claim under our regulations.

Another example is provided by Banks v. Social Security Administration, 0720100014 (2012), where the complainant alleged that the agency added a bilingual requirement to a position announcement to preclude her from qualifying for the position. On appeal, the agency argument that posting a vacancy announcement for a position that the complainant did not qualify for was not an action that sufficiently aggrieved the complainant to state a claim. The Commission disagreed in Banks finding:

The Agency argues that posting a vacancy for a position requiring a qualification Complainant does not possess does not constitute an adverse action. We note, however, that the Commission has held that a Complainant may establish an inference of discrimination where the Complainant can show that, based on her protected bases and/or retaliation, Complainant was prevented from applying for a position by the Agency's actions. See Guerrero v. Air Force, Request No. 05900912 (October 25, 1990); see also Simms v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A00869 (June 20, 2000) (when a complainant expressed an interest in a position, his supervisor told him it was already filled. This stated a claim). In the instant claim, Complainant alleges that the Agency inserted the Spanish language requirement into the position description for the sole purpose of preventing Complainant from applying for the position and hence she states a claim.

The Agency next argues that the AJ improperly used a "disparate impact analysis" that was unsupported by the evidence. Following a review of the AJ's decision, we disagree with the Agency's conclusion that the AJ used a disparate impact analysis. A claim of disparate impact alleges that an Agency practice or policy, while neutral on its face, disproportionately impacted members of the protected class, and generally requires a showing of statistical disparities between various groups. See Watson v. Fort Worth Bank & Trust, 487 U.S. 977 (1988). In the instant case, however, while Complainant did present some statistical evidence, a fair reading of her complaint and arguments before the AJ indicates that she was not alleging that members of her protected classes generally were disproportionally impacted by an otherwise neutral Agency policy. Nor did the AJ make such a finding. Instead, Complainant alleged, and the AJ found, that the Agency specifically discriminated against her by deliberately preventing her from applying or qualifying for a position, and that the Agency's argument that its actions were for legitimate reasons is a mere pretext to mask intentional discrimination. Such an allegation is a standard disparate treatment claim, not a disparate impact claim.

The Agency argued before the AJ that it needed a Spanish-language Claims Representative in order to handle claimants who only spoke Spanish. Once the Agency has articulated a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its action, the burden then shifts to Complainant to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the Agency's explanation is pretextual. Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products, Inc., 530 U.S. 133, 1120 S. Ct. 2097 (2000); St. Mary's Honor Ctr. v. Hicks, 509 U.S. 502, 519 (1993). The AJ found that Complainant succeeded in establishing pretext, but the Agency argues that the AJ cited no basis for his conclusion that the Agency's articulated reason was unworthy of belief. A review of the AJ's decision, however, indicates that the AJ found that the Agency took no action to determine if there was a need for a bilingual claims representative in the Madison area. The AJ next determined that the Madison office conducts an average of three Spanish-speaking interviews per month, that 19 percent of the office employees were bilingual, that Agency employees had access to a tele-interpreter service, and that "most tellingly, the bilingual Claims Representative that was hired has not been conducting interviews with Spanish-speaking clients." AJ's Decision, pps. 8 & 10.

A review of the record shows that the District Manager (DM: male, age and race unspecified) testified that the number of times it was necessary to conduct interviews in Spanish varied from once a week to three times a month. See Hearing Transcript, pp. 493-500. The record does not show that DM or any management official conducted any objective studies to determine the need for a Spanish-speaking representative. Instead DM testified that he decided on the need for a bilingual representative "based on my observations." Id., p. 475. DM further testified that the Agency had access to a tele-interpreter service, see id., p. 509, and that six out of 32 "production employees" could speak Spanish. See id., p, 499. Finally, a bilingual Claim Representative and colleague of Complainant's (CW: female, age and race unspecified) testified that the bilingual Claims Representative who was hired for the position in fact was conducting no bilingual interviews. See id., p. 189. The AJ's findings are thus supported by substantial evidence and we discern no basis to overturn the AJ. In so finding, we note that we are not ruling that, in general, the hiring of bilingual employees constitutes discrimination. Instead we find that, based on the unique facts of this case, Complainant successfully established that the Agency's legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its action was a pretext to hide discrimination and/or retaliation.
