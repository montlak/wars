2. "Me Too" Evidence

Similar to evidence involving comparative employees though somewhat different is the use of so-called "me too" evidence. "Me too" evidence is evidence from other employees who, although not similarly situated, allege that they have been subjected to discrimination. At issue in determining whether the evidence is admissible is whether the probative value of any such evidence outweighs the potential prejudicial value of the evidence. The problems with "me too" evidence most often arise in jury trials since judges are presumed to be able to ignore the prejudicial value of evidence and weigh only its probative value. However, the Supreme Court has decided one case involving "me too" evidence, it is discussed here to the extent that it has some bearing on whether such evidence should be deemed relevant in an administrative hearing.

In Sprint/United Management Co. v. Mendelsohn, 552 U.S. 379 (2008), the plaintiff was terminated during a company-wide reduction-in-force and brought suit claiming the termination was based on age. In support of her claim, she proposed to introduce testimony from five witnesses who were former Sprint employees. Three of the witnesses would have testified that they overheard one or more Sprint managers make disparaging remarks against older employees. One would have testified that an internship program was used as a pretext for age discrimination and that she had seen a spreadsheet suggesting that age was a consideration in termination decisions. The final witness was a former supervisor who would have testified that he had to seek permission before hiring an employee over 40 years of age and had witnessed harassment of an employee because or her age.

Sprint filed a motion in limine to exclude the testimony and, in a decision that contained only a brief discussion of the issue, the district court granted the motion and the testimony was excluded. On appeal, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit reversed based on its belief that the district court had applied a per se rule that such testimony was never admissible. The Supreme Court in Sprint reversed on the narrow ground that the 10th Circuit had erred in finding that the district court imposed a per se rule. Because of the lack of explanation behind the ruling, the Court remanded the case to the district court to further explain the evidentiary ruling. In remanding the case, the Court noted that per se rules on the admissibility or inadmissibility of such evidence were improper. However, it gave little guidance on the parameters for admissibility of such evidence noting only that "an individual ADEA case is fact based and depends on many factors, including how closely related the evidence is to the plaintiff's circumstances and theory of the case." Id.
