broida eeo Chapter 13: Bases of Discrimination Under Title VII Chapter 13: Bases of Discrimination Under Title VII, Subchapter 03: National Origin, Section A: Citizenship

A. CITIZENSHIP

It is not unlawful under Title VII for an agency to discriminate against persons on the basis of national origin if they are not United States citizens. "Aliens are protected from illegal discrimination under the Act, but nothing in the Act makes it illegal to discriminate on the basis of citizenship or alienage." Hipona v. Office of Personnel Management, 01861791 (1986) (no discrimination on basis of national origin where OPM failed to hire citizen of Philippine Islands for competitive service job).

Executive Order 11935 (Sept. 2, 1976) effectively barred noncitizen aliens from employment in the competitive civil services. This general rule has been codified with regard to federal employment. Under 5 CFR 7.4:

(a) No person shall be admitted to competitive examination unless such person is a citizen or national of the United States.

(b) No person shall be given any appointment in the competitive service unless such person is a citizen or national of the United States.

(c) OPM may, as an exception to this rule and to the extent permitted by law, authorize the appointment of aliens to positions in the competitive service when necessary to promote the efficiency of the service in specific cases or for temporary appointments.

The Commission weighed in on citizenship issues as they relate to national origin discrimination in Section VI of its Enforcement Guidance on National Origin Discrimination. However, most of that guidance is not relevant to federal employment because:

Federal law requires U.S. citizenship for most federal civil service employment. When U.S. citizenship is required by federal law, the failure to hire an individual because he or she is not a U.S. citizen does not constitute national origin discrimination in violation of Title VII.

Complaints from aliens over their exclusion from the competitive selection process are not within the EEO purview because it is not a matter over which the agency has jurisdiction. Hipona v. EEOC, 01861598 (1986). See also Wright v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120072987 (2007). The Commission in Raquel T. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120141502 (2016), upheld the agency's disqualification of the complainant for a position where it learned that the complainant's mother was not a U.S. citizen. The at-issue position required a Top Secret security clearance, which necessitated that the incumbent's immediate family members be citizens of the United States. The Commission flatly rejected the complainant's argument, in Raquel T., that the agency "seems to be hiding behind the subtle differences between national origin and citizenship, because both are the same in this instance," instead holding:

We note that with respect to Complainant's claim of discrimination based on national origin, the term "national origin" does not include citizenship. See Espinoza, supra. We also note that the Commission defines national origin discrimination as including, in pertinent part, the denial of equal employment opportunity because of an individual's, or his or her ancestor's, place of origin. 29 C.F.R. § 1606.1. While Complainant argues that national origin and citizenship are the same, we find that the Supreme Court has clearly decided that there is a distinction between the two. In this case, the Agency's decision to cease Complainant's application process was based on her mother's citizenship. Therefore, because citizenship is not a protected basis under Title VII, we find that the AJ properly found that the Commission lacks jurisdiction for Complainant's complaint.

It is not violative of Title VII to ask foreign-born applicants for employment for proof of resident alien status or citizenship where the policy is applied across-the-board to all foreign-born applicants regardless of national origin. See Loeza v. Postmaster General, 01891770 (1989) (citing Espinozo v. Farah Manufacturing Co., 414 U.S. 86 (1973)). Although the Commission noted the requirement that foreign-born applicants claiming resident alien status present a "green card" was overly restrictive, it was not discriminatory because the policy was applied equally to all resident aliens.

However, in Stamatiades v. Secretary of Navy, 0120112425 (2011), the Commission held that the agency's dismissal of a complaint of national origin discrimination by a Greek citizen was an improper determination on the merits of the claim without an investigation where the agency asserted that the citizenship was not a protected category under Title VII. In remanding the complaint for investigation, the Commission, in Stamatiades, noted:

In his formal complaint, Complainant claims that he was subjected to discrimination based upon his national origin. The Agency, however, contends that the formal complaint is based upon citizenship. Citing the Supreme Court's decision in Espinoza v. Farah Manufacturing Co., 414 U.S. 86 (1973), the Agency reasoned that the basis of citizenship is not covered by Title VII. While the Supreme Court found that in the case before it the basis of national origin did not encompass citizenship, it nevertheless noted that it "ha[d] no occasion here to question the general validity...of the Commission's belief that there may be many situations where discrimination on the basis of citizenship would have the effect of discriminatory on the basis of national origin." Espinoza at 92.

Therefore, we find that the Agency's determination inappropriately goes to the merits of the complaint. The same holds true for the Agency's reliance on the NATO treaty. The Agency's statement that Complainant is a Greek citizen and therefore prohibited from employment with the U.S. Forces in Greece, goes to the merits of the complaint and is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether he has stated a justiciable claim under Title VII. See Osborne v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Request No, 05960111 (July 19, 1996); Lee v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05930220 (Aug. 12, 1993); Ferrazzoli v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05910642 (Aug. 15, 1991).

For purposes of stating a claim, a complainant properly distinguishes himself from other candidates for a position on the basis of national origin when he demonstrates that he is a native of the United States mainland and the other candidates were natives of the Virgin Islands--a U.S. territory. Jackson v. Secretary of Interior, 01924572 (1993).
