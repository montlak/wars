8. Prohibited Personnel Practice

In February 1994, Congressman Barney Frank (D.-Mass.) announced a new policy by the administration which prohibits discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation. Though no specific regulations had been drafted, in a January 26, 1994 letter to Congressman Frank, OPM Director James B. King set forth his agency's position on the subject:

You asked that I clarify the legal remedies available to Federal employees who believe they have suffered discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation. Discrimination is specifically included as a prohibited personnel practice (PPP) in 5 USC 2302(b). One of the listed PPPs, 2302(b)(10), provides that no employee with authority to take or direct a personnel action shall

discrimination for or against any employee or applicant for employment on the basis of conduct which does not adversely affect the performance of the employee of applicant or the performance of others.

OPM has long taken the position that this prohibition applies directly to discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation. OPM Director Alan Campbell addressed this point in a memorandum to Heads of Departments and Independent Establishments, issued May 12, 1980. While the memorandum outlined the Government's overall policy on PPPs, it included the specific statement that "applicants an employees are to be protected against inquiries into, or actions based upon, non-job-related conduct, such as religious, community, or social affiliations, or sexual orientation."

Allegations of PPPs may be raised in several different ways, depending on the circumstances. If this allegation results from an agency action against the employee that is appealable to the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB), the employee may raise it as a challenge to the agency action in the appeal to MSPB. Actions appealable to the MSPB include major discipline such as removals or lengthy suspensions.

In addition, an employee who has been subjected to a PPP may file a complaint with the Office of Special Counsel (OSC) whether or not the agency's action is otherwise appealable to the MSPB. The OSC is an independent agency authorized to investigate and seek corrective action from MSPB concerning allegations of PPPs.

Other remedies may be available within the employing agency. In general, a bargaining unit employee may grieve through a Negotiated Grievance Procedure (NGP) "any claimed violation, misinterpretation, or misapplication of any law, rule, or regulation affecting conditions of employment." This language from the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 is intentionally broad. Occasionally, some contracts name specific matters (e.g. short suspensions) that the parties have agreed to exclude from the NGP. In such cases, the bargaining unit employee may take a dispute concerning excluded matters to the Administrative Grievance System, as discussed below. If the matter is grievable through the NGP, but it also appealable to the MSPB, the employee must choose one forum or the other.

Most agencies also have a formal agency Administrative Grievance System (AGS) to resolve disputes that fall outside their other formal review channels. In general, these systems will attempt to achieve a relatively informal resolution between the immediate parties before elevating the dispute to higher levels of management. Specific procedures vary greatly from agency to agency, and an employee contemplating such a grievance will want to read the rules for his or her agency's system.

Finally, an employee's allegation of discrimination based on sexual orientation could be reviewed by the agency Office of Inspector General (OIG) if the alleged conduct were part of an overall pattern of being disclosed as fraud, waste, and/or abuse.

Note that none of the remedies listed by OPM involves the EEOC, nor are any of the rights or remedies based on any interpretation of Title VII.
