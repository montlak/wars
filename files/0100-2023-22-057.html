h. Recruitment and Retention Pay

Matching a private sector salary where the agency is having problems recruiting and retaining qualified personnel is a reason other than sex that constitutes a defense under the EPA. In Antosz, et al. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 07A30031-07A30036 (2004), the Commission found that the agency was justified in hiring a male LPN at a higher rate of pay than some of female LPN's were receiving. The Commission in Antosz found:

Complainants have raised a claim under the Equal Pay Act. The U.S. Supreme Court articulated the requirements for establishing a prima facie case of discrimination under the EPA in Corning Glass Works v. Brennan, 417 U.S. 188, 195 (1974). To establish a violation of the EPA, a complainant must show that she or he received less pay than an individual of the opposite gender for equal work, requiring equal skill, effort and responsibility, under similar working conditions within the same establishment. Id. at 195; Sheppard v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, EEOC Appeal No. 01A02919 (September 12, 2000); see also 29 C.F.R. § 1620.14(a).

Once a complainant has met her burden of establishing a prima facie case, an employer may avoid liability only if it can prove that the pay difference is justified under one of the four affirmative defenses set forth in the EPA, namely: (1) a seniority system; (2) a merit system; (3) a system which measures earnings by quantity or quality of production of work (also referred to as an incentive or piecework system); or (4) a differential based on any other factor other than sex. 29 U.S.C. § 206 (d) (l); Corning Glass Works, 417 U.S. at 196-97: Kouba v. Allstate Insurance Co., 691 F.2d 873 (9th Cir. 1982). Job classification systems qualify as a "factor other than sex" only if the systems accurately reflect job duties and/or employee qualifications. The requirement of "equal work" does not mean that the jobs must be identical, but only that they must be "substantially equal." Id. (citing Corning Glass Works, 417 U.S. at 203, n.24; Homer v. Mary Institute, 613 F.2d 706, 714 (8th Cir. 1980); Laffey v. Northwest Airlines, Inc., 567 F.2d 429, 449 (D.C. Cir. 1976)).

In analyzing whether complainants have established a prima facie case under the EPA, we must first determine which set of grades and salaries will be considered for purposes of this decision. The record reflects that Comparator was hired in September 2000, and the grades and salaries of the agency's LPNs have since been adjusted pursuant to a salary survey in the private sector. We find that, because complainants filed their complaints based upon their grades and salaries in September 2000, when Comparator was hired, the appropriate grades and salaries to consider for purposes of our analysis are those as of the date of Comparator's hire.

The Commission finds that three of the complainants, complainant-1, complainant-3, and complainant-5, have failed to establish a prima facie case under the Act. First, complainant-1 has not shown that she received less pay than Comparator. Her annual salary was $33,945 at the time of Comparator's hire, whereas Comparator earned $33,238. Therefore, we find that complainant-1 fails to establish a prima facie case under the first prong of the Act.

Complainant-3 and complainant-4, GS-5s during the relevant time, have failed to show that their work required equal skill, effort, and responsibility as compared to Comparator's GS-6 position. The record reflects that complainant-3 worked as a GS-5, Step 4. Moreover, she worked in the Medical Ward, whereas Comparator worked in Ward 7. Complainant-3 testified that she believed that her duties far surpassed those of Comparator, and she noted in Complainants Statement in Opposition to Appeal that responsible management officials (RMOs) testified that she was qualified for the position. We find, however, that she failed to put forth sufficient evidence that her job as a GS-5 in the Medical Ward constituted equal work to that of a GS-6 in Ward 7. Furthermore, we note that, while complainant-3 points to one RMO's testimony that all GS-6 LPNs have the same job description, complainant-3 was not in fact a GS-6 during the relevant time, and while she met the professional experience requirement for the GS-6 level, she did not meet the nursing practice requirement in September 2000. Similarly, complainant-4 worked as a GS-5, Step 4, and she has failed to provide sufficient evidence to show that she was performing the same work as a GS-6. Moreover, she met neither the professional experience requirement for the GS-6 level, nor the nursing practice requirement. Accordingly, we find that complainant-3 and complainant-4 failed to put forth sufficient evidence that their GS-5 positions constituted equal work to that of Comparator's GS-6 position within the meaning of the Act.

The Commission finds that complainant-2, complainant-5, and complainant-6 established a prima facie case under the EPA. The record indicates that these three complainants received less pay than Comparator did in September 2000. Specifically, complainant-2 earned $32,531 annually, complainant-5 earned $29,703 annually, and complainant-6 earned $31,824 annually, as compared to Comparator's salary of $33,238. In addition, these complainants, like Comparator, worked in Ward 7 as LPNs with a grade level of GS-6. Their job descriptions were identical to Comparators, and they performed substantially the same duties under similar working conditions. Therefore, we find that Comparator and complainant-2, complainant-5, and complainant-6 hold jobs of substantially equal skill, effort, and responsibility; and therefore, these three complainants have established a prima facie case under the EPA.

We now turn to whether the agency has proved by a preponderance of the evidence that the salary difference between the three complainants and Comparator fell into a permissible statutory exception, namely, here, that the salary difference was due to a reason other than sex. The agency contends that, because of a shortage of LPNs over the past several years, it was experiencing difficulty attracting well-qualified LPNs; therefore, after Comparator provided proof for his appointment at the GS-6 level, the agency hired Comparator as a GS-6, Step 9 to match the salary he was making in the private sector.

The Commission finds that the agency has met its burden of proving that its decision to grade and compensate Comparator at a higher rate than complainants was based upon a factor other than sex. Although the AJ found that the decision to pay Comparator based on his prior experience was a "post-hoc" rationalization, the evidence establishes that the shortage of LPNs caused the agency to recruit qualified applicants aggressively. AJ Decision, p. 5 (citing, EEOC v. White & Son Enterprises, 881 F.2d 1006, 1010 (11th Cir. 1989) (Employer must show that it knew of employee's experience at the time the starting wages were set.). The record reflects that at the time Comparator was hired, there were five LPN vacancies at the facility, and the facility had been recruiting aggressively for LPNs for about a year and a half. Warren Transcript, p. 28; Hall Transcript, p. 7. The record further reflects that the facility had gone through staffing changes with the number of Registered Nurses decreasing and the number of LPNs increasing, and that there had been an increase of LPN positions from 30 in 1998 to 40 in 2001. ROI, Exh. C-2; Hall Transcript, p. 14. Finally, complainant-2 indicated that she believed there was a shortage of LPNs; and complainant-6 admitted that, at the time of Comparator's hire, Ward 7 was experiencing an increased workload. Cook Transcript, p. 28; Press Transcript, p. 34.

Complainants maintain that, in both January 2000 and January 2001, management informed them that they would not receive a COLA because there were no retention and recruitment problems. The agency admits that LPNs did not receive such adjustments in 1995 through 1999. However, the record reflects that they did receive a COLA in January 2000 and January 2001. Agency's February 10, 2003 Brief, Attachment 1 (shows increase in January 2001 only).

Complainants also argue that Comparator had no special qualifications. However, the agency states and the record reflects that the RMOs did not appoint Comparator at the GS-6, Step 9 grade because of his "superior qualification," but instead, they relied on a salary match to recruit Comparator during the LPN shortage. See generally, ROI, p. C-9, VA Directive 5103.7. Moreover, Comparator did indeed meet the minimum requirements for the GS-6 grade once he provided additional information in his application. Warren Transcript, p. 12.

We also find that, although the AJ found that the C-l and C-2 were improper comparators, the evidence indicates that the agency made offers to these two female applicants above the minimum rate of the grade due to the same LPN shortage and pursuant to the VA Directive. Finally, we note that, once complainants were hired at their respective grades, they were subjected to time-in-grade requirements before they could be promoted to the next grade. Based on the foregoing reasons, the Commission finds that the agency has proven that a factor other than sex was the reason for the existence of the salary differential between complainants and Comparator. See Kouba v. Allstate Insurance Co., 691 F.2d 873 (9th Cir. 1982) (An employer may consider prior salary when it is used 'reasonably' in view of a business objective.); Earner v. Mary Institute, 613 F.2d 706 (8th Cir. 1980) (Prior salary may sometimes be used as an indicator of market demand, thus excusing an employer from EPA liability under the factor other than sex defense.).

Similarly, in Smith v. Postmaster General, 07A50037 (2005), the Commission found that the agency based its determination of salary on a factor other than sex where two female lawyers who made more than the complainant had more experience and had been paid at a higher salary in their previous job in the private sector. By contrast, the complainant worked for the agency prior to becoming a lawyer and was subject to an agency regulation that provided that nonbargaining unit employees could not receive more than an 8% increase upon promotion. See also Crim v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A60301 (2006), where the Commission found that a male medical records technician recruited from the private sector was paid more than an in-house female technician for "reasons other than sex"; namely, the agency had difficulty finding qualified record technicians and the male was paid the higher salary based on what he had been earning in the private sector.
