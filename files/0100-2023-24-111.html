2. Leave Offenses

In order for consideration of reasonable accommodation, the complainant must establish a nexus between the absences and his disability. Moreover, the Commission takes the position that a disability is not an excuse for misconduct and seldom is there a nexus between a disability and conduct issues such as failure to follow leave procedures. The Commission set out its position rather succinctly in Howerton v. Secretary of Army, 0120113177 (2013):

An Agency does not have a duty to excuse an employee's misconduct as a form of reasonable accommodation to that employee's disability where such misconduct would result in discipline or discharge if committed by another employee. See Martinez v. General Services Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122326 (Nov. 15, 2012); Stallworth v. Dep't of Veterans Aff., EEOC Appeal No. 01893705 (Jan. 29, 1990). req. to reconsider den. EEOC Request No. 05900416 (Aug. 2, 1990). A complainant who has an excessive amount of absences must show a nexus between the absences and the disability. See Martinez, supra; Tsai v. Fed. Deposit Insur. Corp., EEOC Appeal No. 0120035347 (June 14, 2007); Roscoe v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01974138 (Sept. 21, 2000); Southerland v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05930714 (July 15, 1994). Here, we agree with the AJ that Complainant has not established that a nexus existed between his excessive absences and his disability. A review of the record reveals that of his 41 absences and tardiness in 2008, only 9 were linked to his disability. Complainant provided his supervisor with other reasons for his other 32 attendance issues in 2008, including: power outages; personal reasons; a carbon monoxide leak in his home; gastroenteritis; the flu; another assault; his alarm clock didn't go off; he forgot his access card; traffic; car problems, etc. Additionally, the record reflects that Complainant's attendance issues began before the assault in October 2007, which was the trigger for his PTSD.

Further, we agree with the AJ that Complainant did not provide evidence that would establish that the accommodation he requested, a flex time schedule, would have been effective in accommodating his attendance issues. The AJ pointed out that flex time would not have addressed Complainant's 28 unscheduled absences in 2008, nor would it have assisted him on the numerous days that he arrived at work after 9:30 A.M. As a result, we agree with the AJ that the requested accommodation would not have been effective. Therefore, the Agency did not fail to reasonably accommodate Complainant's disability.

When an employee is a put in an AWOL status and disciplined for that AWOL, the complainant is entitled to have those actions reversed if she establishes that the AWOL and discipline would not have occurred if the agency had accommodated her. In Harden v. Social Security Administration, 0720080002 (2011), the complainant suffered from a sleep disorder that resulted from depression and had difficulty arriving at work on time. She requested that the agency accommodate her by extending by 30 minutes her flexible starting time and allowing her to take leave when she could not report during that time period. The Commission found that such accommodations would not have imposed an undue hardship, in part, because by the time of the hearing the agency was providing those accommodations. The Commission, in Harding, found that "[g]iven that the record shows that Complainant's AWOL charge and discipline for attendance would not have occurred if the reasonable accommodation had been provided, the AJ's findings that these acts were related to her disability, is supported by substantial evidence in the record."
