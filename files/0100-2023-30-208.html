O. INTEREST ON COMPENSATORY DAMAGES

The Commission has held that when an agency delays payment of a compensatory damage award, it must make a further award of interest to compensate for the delay in receipt of the award. In McCann v. Secretary of Air Force, 01971851 (1998), the Commission explained:

The first final agency decision, which was issued on November 15, 1996, ordered an agency component to pay the appellant $51,790.80 in compensatory damages within 30 days of that unit's receipt of the decision. The second final agency decision, which was issued on March 18, 1997, ordered the agency component to pay the appellant $8,103.31 in compensatory damages within 30 days of that unit's receipt of the decision.

In an appeal brief by the appellant's attorney filed in connection with a subsequent final agency decision on attorney's fees, the attorney avers that the agency did not pay the above amounts until on or about November 3, 1997. The agency has not contested this.

A recent case by the Commission concerned a final order by the Commission for, a federal employer to pay a specified sum of attorney's fees that was not paid for three years. The Commission ordered the employer to pay post-judgement interest to compensate the attorney for the loss in value of money resulting from the delay in payment. Cole v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 04950009 (February 19, 1997). In the instant case, as in Cole, there was a final award(s), albeit by the agency itself, requiring it to pay specific sums to the opposing party by a specific date. While the appellant claimed that she was entitled to more damages, the amounts awarded by the agency were uncontested, and hence final. The delay by the agency in paying the amounts awarded was excessive and unexplained. Accordingly, as in Cole, the agency must pay interest to compensate the appellant for the loss in value of money resulting from the delay.

In April v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01963775 (1997), the Commission found it was appropriate to award interest on a compensatory damage claim where the agency had delayed payment of the damage claim for almost two years. In that case, the agency agreed that the complainant would be paid up to $30,000 in compensatory damages after submission of objective evidence of damages. The agency then took almost two years to process the claim and awarded the complainant less than $10,000 in damages. In holding the complainant was entitled to interest on the damage award, the Commission, quoting DeLaCruz v. Pruitt, 590 F. Supp. 1296, 1309 (N.D. Ind. 1984), noted interest was available "'to make the wronged party whole by compensating the wronged person for being deprived of the monetary loss, i.e., the money and the use of the money.'" See also Rao v. New York City Health and Hospitals Corp., 882 F. Supp. 321 (S.D.N.Y. 1995); Evans v. Ford Motor Co., 768 F. Supp. 1318 (D. Minn. 1991); Cordero v. De Jesus-Mendez, 922 F.2d 11 (1st Cir. 1990).

In Chow v. Secretary of Army, 01981308 (1999), the Commission expanded the period of time during which a complainant can collect interest on a compensatory damage award. In that case, the Commission noted the complainant became entitled to a damage award in August 1995 and submitted evidence of damages in October 30, 1995. The Commission held the complainant was entitled to interest running from December 1, 1995 up until the damage award was actually paid. It also is worth noting that in Chow, the Commission ordered the agency to pay interest on not only the emotional distress award, but the entire award of compensatory damages, including pecuniary damages. The Commission affirmed its decision in Chow v. Secretary of Army, 05991106 (2001).

In Vashi v. Postmaster General, 0420060009 (2007), the Commission denied interest on the damages award distinguishing the case from Chow. The Commission, in Vashi, explained:

Petitioner requests interest on the $35,000.00 non-pecuniary compensatory damages. The Commission has previously held that a petitioner to entitled to an award of interest only where an agency delays in paying proven compensatory damages. In Chow v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05991106 (February 13, 2001), the agency adopted the AJ's finding that the petitioner was entitled to compensatory damages, and stated that it would issue a decision in the matter ninety days after receiving relevant proof in support of the claim. Nevertheless, the agency then failed to issue a decision for more than two years. Citing April v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01963775 (June 5, 1997), the Commission found that the petitioner was entitled to interest as of the date on which the agency was to have issued its final decision addressing the issue of compensatory damages. Chow, supra.

In contrast to the facts in Chow, however, petitioner has not shown that the agency acted with willful disregard or willful delay in the implementation of the Commission's order. Instead, the record reveals that the agency issued a check on September 23, 2005, which is well within the appropriate time of the Commission's July 12, 2005 order in EEOC Appeal No. 07A50056. We find that the agency was in compliance with our decision and Order related to the payment of non-pecuniary compensatory damages. Therefore, we find that petitioner is not entitled to interest on her non-pecuniary compensatory damages because she has given no persuasive reason for additional interest to be paid on her award.
