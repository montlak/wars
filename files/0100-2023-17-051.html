1. Credibility Determinations

In terms of final agency decisions, a credibility determination is like any other factual determination--the Commission reviews those findings de novo. The fact that in the absence of a hearing things such as witness demeanor cannot be determined does not mean that credibility findings cannot be made and, indeed, to resolve cases, they must be made.

When determining credibility based on the written record, the Commission will look to the nature of the statements that give rise to the need for a credibility determination, the statements of other witnesses, and documentary evidence in the record. The case of Vincent v. Postmaster General, 0120072908 (2009), provides a good example of how the Commission resolves credibility determination when reviewing final agency decisions:

Before conducting a legal analysis, we must first determine whether S in fact made the statement attributed to her. Where consistent and inconsistent statements and documentary evidence help establish the truth or falsity of affidavits, the Commission will resolve questions of fact based on a preponderance of evidence. See, e.g., Whidbee v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal 01A40193 (March 31, 2005). Here, we weigh complainant's allegation that at the end of a September 10 presentation on EEO rights, S told employees "some of you have stuck up for each other who have filed and that's good, but. . .what goes around, comes around" against a denial in S's affidavit that the agency characterizes as "unequivocal." We find that S made the statement in question, as complainant avers, for the reasons that follow.

First, there is nothing in the record that impugns complainant's credibility. Second, when S was presented with her alleged statement in a list of questions from the EEO investigator, she responded by affidavit dated September 25: "I do not recall making this statement after the service talk. After the service talk, I probably said Thank you." (Emphasis added.) A failure to recall is patently not a denial. Third, in supplemental investigatory questions, S was asked: "Was [complainant's] prior EEO activity a factor when on September 10, 2005, after a stand-up talk on EEO, you allegedly made a statement that was considered retaliatory for those engaging in EEO activity? If so, why?" On September 29, S responded: "No. No known prior EEO activity by Ms. Vincent. I did not make the alleged statement. I read verbatim from the service talks." S's initial failure of recall renders this subsequent denial, at best, equivocal. See, Whidbee v. Department of the Navy, supra. Fourth, CW2 and CW3 submitted affidavits dated October 23, 2006 in response to a list of questions that did not include a quote of the purported threat and did not identify the supervisor alleged to have uttered it. Yet, CW2 and CW3 both identified the gravamen of the statement at issue which was "what goes around, comes around." They also identified S as the individual who made the statement. Thus, the Commission finds that a preponderance of evidence establishes that S made the remark attributed to her at the close of an EEO rights presentation for employees. We now address the legal significance of the remark.

The fact that counsel has assisted witnesses in drafting affidavits does not, standing alone, indicate that the statements of the witnesses are not credible. In Coopwood v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120083127 (2012), the agency argued that affidavits of witnesses submitted in support of a damages claim were not credible because of entries the complainant's attorney's fee statement that reflected time spent on drafting affidavits. In responding to the agency's contention in Coopwood, the Commission noted:

As a preliminary matter, we determine that the Agency has not sufficiently shown that the credibility of Complainant's supporting affidavits has been fatally compromised by select "drafting affidavit" entries in Complainant's attorney's fee petition. A closer inspection of the fee petition shows that Complainant's attorney interviewed the witnesses; took notes; prepared first drafts of the affidavits; sent those drafts to the witnesses for review and edits; incorporated the witnesses' comments and changes; and sent the final drafts to the witnesses for review and their signature. The actions described in the fee petition, in and of themselves, do not raise dispositive questions about whether the signed and sworn statements accurately reflect the views and observations of the witnesses.

Nor has the Agency identified any instance in which the affidavits contained identical language, thereby suggesting that the testimony more accurately reflects the attorney's views instead of the witnesses.' The Agency merely stated in its final decision that "some of the affidavits have similar language and refer to the same incident." This is unremarkable; one would expect supporting witnesses to talk about shared incidences in the course of providing corroborating testimony. As such, the Commission finds it reasonable to place greater weight on the affidavits than the Agency did in its final decision.

The Commission will examine the statements in the existing record to make credibility determinations with an eye toward statements that are inconsistent. For example, in Sanchez v. Attorney General, 0120122273 (2012), the Commission reversed an agency finding of no retaliation explaining:

The burden now shifts to the Agency to articulate legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for denying the training to Complainant. The Warden stated that he responded to the Union President's request. He indicated that he consulted with the Associate Warden and the Supervisor regarding the request. He was told that Complainant was eleven weeks behind in her medical file copying and filing. Therefore, it was determined that, because of this backlog, she should not be allowed time away from the office to participate in the EEO training. The Associate Warden confirmed that he was consulted on the request for Complainant to participate in the EEO training. The Associate Warden stated that Complainant was ten months behind in her work. He also noted that this was determined in consultation with the Supervisor. The Supervisor stated that he had only one conversation over the phone with "his supervisor" about Complainant's request for training. He stated that he only indicated that the medical records were not caught up. He also noted that this was the extent of the conversation pertaining to the training request.

Upon review, we find that Complainant has proven, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the reasons proffered by the Agency were pretextual, and that it is more likely than not that retaliatory animus for her prior EEO activity motivated the responsible management officials. The evidence gathered during the investigation establishes that in May 2009, Complainant's department was scanning medical files. The assignment of files was made in May 2009, and Complainant was assigned to work on 588 files, while all other employees were given approximately 10 to 178 files each. The record included emails from management to Complainant in July 2009, showing that Complainant has been doing a "great job" working on the backlog.

The Warden and the Associate Warden had inconsistent statements about the extent of Complainant's remaining backlog, ranging from 11 weeks to 10 months respectively. Neither Agency officials provided any support for their assertions that Complainant was behind on her assignment. Further, their statements are inconsistent with the emails praising Complainant for her progress on the backlog. In addition, while the Warden and the Associate Warden indicated that they consulted with the Supervisor when they denied the training, their statements are somewhat inconsistent with the Supervisor's affidavit. The Supervisor said he was only asked if a backlog remained. He indicated he was never asked about how much of a backlog remained in Complainant's assignment. As such, we find that the Warden and the Associate Warden have not substantiated their bald and inconsistent assertion that Complainant had an extensive backlog which would prevent her from going to the three-day EEO training. We also note that when the Supervisor was asked if he recommended that Complainant should not be sent to training, he stated "no." Based on this evidence, we find that it is more likely than not that Complainant was subjected to unlawful retaliation when the Agency denied her request to attend the EEO training.
