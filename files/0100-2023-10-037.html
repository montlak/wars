3. Appeals to the EEOC

An agency that does not intend to fully implement a decision of an administrative judge must file an appeal with the EEOC. See 29 CFR 1614.110(a). Two routes are open to the complainant who is dissatisfied with the agency's final action on the decision of an administrative judge or final agency decision. One involves appellate review and the other involves trial de novo in U.S. district court. The respective processes for both complainants and agencies are discussed below.

Within 30 calendar days of receiving the agency's final action, the employee may file a notice of appeal with the EEOC Office of Federal Operations. See 29 CFR 1614.402. The complainant must also submit a copy of the notice to the agency. See 29 CFR 1614.403(b). The EEOC requires that any supporting brief or statement be filed within 30 calendar days of the notice of appeal. See 29 CFR 1614.403(d). The brief also must be served on the agency. If the agency wishes to submit an opposition brief, it must do so within 30 days of receipt of the complainant's brief, or if the complainant did not submit a brief, within 60 days of receipt of the complainant's appeal. See 29 CFR 1614.403(f). The agency also is given 30 calendar days from the time it receives notice of the complainant's appeal to submit the complaint file to OFO. See 29 CFR 1614.403(e).

OFO then conducts an appellate review of the case. The review of the decision on appeal is de novo with the exception of factual findings of administrative judges, which are subject to a substantial evidence standard of review. See 29 CFR 1614.405(a). The substantial evidence standard grants the findings of administrative judges far more deference than under the previous regulations. Essentially, OFO will affirm the factual findings of an administrative judge unless they are clearly erroneous. Conclusions of law are reviewed de novo. Under the 1992 version of Part 1614, the EEOC could engage in its own factfinding, even absent a determination that the findings in the decision were clearly erroneous. The current standard represents a more traditional standard of appellate review.

The employee also has the option of filing a civil action in the appropriate U.S. district court within 90 calendar days of receipt of the agency's final action. See 29 CFR 1614.407(a). This reflects the change in the statute of limitations brought about by the 1991 Civil Rights Act and represents a significant expansion of the former 30-day period for initiating a civil action. The previous time limit of 30 days was particularly onerous for complainants who had proceeded pro se in the administrative process. It is often difficult enough for a lawyer to file a complaint in U.S. district court within 30 days of receipt of a decision in a case where he or she has represented the complainant and is familiar with the facts. For pro se litigants, it was far more difficult to find a lawyer and for the lawyer to become familiar enough with the facts of a case to file a civil action within 30 days. Some courts have held that the 30-day period for filing a civil action still applies to cases involving federal employees in actions that have been appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board. If the agency has not taken a final action on the complaint within 180 days from the date it was filed, a complainant also may proceed to district court without final agency action on the complaint. See 29 CFR 1614.407(b).

In U.S. district court, the employee is entitled to a trial de novo of the complaint. A complainant does not preclude the option of proceeding to federal district court by filing an appeal with OFO. The complainant still has the option of proceeding with a civil action within 90 days of the EEOC's final decision, or after the expiration of 180 calendar days from filing the appeal if no decision has been issued: see 29 CFR 1614.408(c)-(d).

Agencies that wish to appeal an administrative judge's decision have 40 days from receipt of the hearing file and decision. See 29 CFR 1614.402(a). The agency must submit the complaint file to OFO within 30 days of filing the notice of appeal. See 29 CFR 1614.403(e). If the agency wishes to submit a supporting brief, it must do so within 20 days of filing the notice of appeal. See 29 CFR 1614.403(d). A complainant then has 30 days from receipt of the agency's brief to file an opposition brief. See 29 CFR 1614.403(f). If the agency did not file a brief, the complainant has 60 days from the date of the appeal to file its opposition brief. See id. OFO then conducts a review, using the same standards as it does for appeals from complainants, as discussed above.

The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 also conferred on the Commission the power to sanction parties for failing to comply with appellate procedure. See 29 CFR 1614.404(c). This is the same authority that investigators and administrative judges have to sanction parties. See 29 CFR 1614.108(c)(3) and 1614.109(f)(3).
