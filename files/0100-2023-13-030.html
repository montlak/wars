1. Sanctions Against the Agency

If the complainant requests a hearing, 29 CFR 1614.108(a) requires the administrative judge to oversee development of the record if the investigation is not adequate. Beginning with the 1992 version of MD-110, agencies can be sanctioned by the administrative judge for inadequate investigations. The most likely sanction was to grant the complainant costs and attorney fees for the discovery necessary to correct the deficiencies in the investigative record. See, e.g., Genaro G. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2020004241 (2022). The Commission's sanction of choice has been to draw an adverse inference against the agency in instances of inadequate investigations. See, e.g., McDuffie v. Secretary of Navy, 05880134 (1988). Default judgment is reserved for the most egregious conduct, and inadequate investigations don't normally rise to that level. See, e.g., Genaro G. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2020004241 (2022).

In conjunction with changing to the reasonable factfinder standard, the Commission also revised its policy on sanctions against agencies for inadequate investigations. Section 1614.109(a) provides, in relevant part:

When a complainant requests a hearing, the Commission shall appoint an administrative judge to conduct a hearing in accordance with this section. Upon appointment, the administrative judge shall assume full responsibility for the adjudication of the complaint, including overseeing the development of the record....

If it is clear that the agency did not develop an appropriate investigative record, the administrative judge may impose sanctions on the agency as described in MD-110 at 6-27:

Where it is clear that the agency failed to develop an impartial and appropriate factual record, an Administrative Judge may exercise his/her discretion to issue sanctions. In such circumstances, the sanctions listed in 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3) are available. See Petersel v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0720060075 (Oct. 30, 2008) (Administrative Judge properly drew an adverse inference against the agency when the investigative report failed to include any comparative data on other employees); Royal v. Dep't. of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0720070045 (September 25, 2009) (finding that the agency's delay in completing the investigation within the 180-day regulatory period is no small noncompliance matter and warrants a sanction). Even when an agency eventually completes the investigation during the hearing stage an Administrative Judge may issue sanctions in appropriate circumstances.

Before an Administrative Judge may sanction an agency for failing to develop an impartial and appropriate factual record, the Administrative Judge must issue an order to the agency or request the documents, records, comparative data, statistics, or affidavits. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3). Such order or request shall make clear that sanctions may be imposed and the type of sanction that could be imposed for failure to comply with the order unless the agency can show good cause for that failure. See Rountree v. Dep't. of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 07A00015 (July 17, 2001). The notice to show cause to the agency may, in appropriate circumstances, provide the agency with an opportunity to take such action as the Administrative Judge deems necessary to correct the deficiencies in the record. This may include curing the defects in the investigation caused by improper interference by the agency's general counsel, if possible; and/or disqualifying counsel from continuing to represent the agency before the Commission. The Administrative Judge also shall provide the agency with a reasonable period of time within which to take the action that the Administrative Judge has deemed necessary. Only on the failure of the agency to comply with the Administrative Judge's order or request and the notice to show cause may the Administrative Judge impose a sanction or the sanctions identified in the order or request.

Note that the guidance does not specifically mention remand to the agency to supplement the investigation. However, the language of Management Directive 110 does provide that the administrative judge "may provide the agency with an opportunity to take such action as the Administrative Judge deems necessary to correct the deficiencies in the record." As a result, some administrative judges will remand the complaint to the agency for supplemental investigation. Sanctions are also discussed in Chapters 6 and 8.

An agency may not be sanctioned for conducting an inadequate investigation unless it has been informed by the administrative judge of the specific inadequacies, given an opportunity to correct the deficiencies, and also notified of the consequences of failing to correct the deficiencies. Such notification was not required under the 1992 version of MD-110. In adopting this rule, the Commission balanced several competing concerns. Complainants now have the right to amend complaints for like or related claims without going through the counseling stage of the complaint process. Although by regulation, the agency has additional time to complete the investigation, the complainant retains the right to request a hearing after the original complaint was pending for 180 days. The Commission balanced relieving the complainant of counseling with giving the agency more time to investigate. It balanced the extension of time to investigate with the complainant's interest in a prompt hearing, by allowing the complainant to elect to proceed with a completed investigation. However, it chose to offset that interest with a concern that agencies not be unduly punished for failure to timely complete investigations, particularly when complaints are amended late in the investigative stage.
