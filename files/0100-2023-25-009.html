4. Mandatory Retirement Age

The Foreign Service Act, 22 USC 4052, provides that members of the Foreign Service shall be retired at age 65. In Strawberry v. Albright, 111 F.3d 943 (D.C. Cir. 1997), the court held that the provision also applies to members of the Foreign Service who had been converted to civil service positions. The court in Strawberry, 111 F.3d at 947, further held that the provision did not violate the ADEA:

Finally, the district court correctly held that the mandatory retirement provisions of the 1980 and 1986 Acts do not run afoul of the ADEA. As the district court noted, Congress maintained but increased the mandatory retirement age in 1980 and applied the mandatory retirement age to FSPS participants in 1986. Both actions occurred after the ADEA was made applicable to federal employees. It is well established that "[w]here there is no clear intention otherwise, a specific statute will not be controlled or nullified by a general one...." Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535, 550 (1974). Thus, the district court correctly held that the ADEA's general prohibition of age discrimination does not prohibit enforcement of the mandatory retirement provisions. We therefore grant the Secretary's motion for summary affirmance.

Similarly, the Commission held that 10 USC 10128 creates a statutory exception to the ADEA by permitting agencies to implement mandatory separation of non-dual status military technicians who are eligible for an unreduced retirement annuity. Barto v. Secretary of Army, 01A04149 (2001).

In Complainant v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120141529 (2014), the Commission found that an allegation that the agency refused to grant a waiver from the mandatory retirement age of 56 for an air traffic controller stated a claim, but the evidence demonstrated that the complainant was not a victim of age discrimination:

While the Commission has consistently held that complaints generally challenging statutorily created exceptions to the ADEA fail to state a claim (such as a mandatory retirement age), it has ruled that a claim that an Agency engaged in discrimination by not granting a complainant's request for a waiver to the age requirement under its procedures states a claim. Campbell v. Department of Justice, EEOC Request No. 059605550 (April 17, 1997); DiLuglio v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05930890 (Aug. 22, 1994). Accordingly, the Agency's dismissal of Complainant's complaint for failure to state a claim is REVERSED.

...

All of the comparative employees who made waiver requests were, like Complainant, very close to the retirement age of 56. Complainant, in fact, was younger than those who had an additional waiver requests pending after their prior waiver request(s) were granted and expired. Complainant argues that the real reason his waiver request was denied was because it came within a window when all waiver requests by air traffic controllers (presumably non-supervisory) were denied. But applying denials to everyone does not disparately treat anyone because of their age, and is consistent with the Agency's authority to mandate retirement. Complainant failed to show he was disparately treated based on his age, and has failed to establish a prima facie case of age discrimination.

In Complainant v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120131080 (2014), the Commission found statements by a supervisor to a 41-year-old complainant that he was too old and could not physically handle the work of a firefighter were not indicative of age discrimination:

With regard to issue (2), Complainant alleged that on May 7, 2010, S1 told him he was too old and cannot physically handle firefighter work. S1 stated she did not recall making those comments, but acknowledged she might have made them. We find a preponderance evidence shows that the comments were made by S1. The record reveals that the comments at issue were made in reference to Complainant's expressed desire to apply for a firefighter position. S1 stated that she gave Complainant a reference when he applied for a position in fire. However, S1 stated in her view Complainant did not meet the Maximum Entry Age (MEA) for a fire-fighter position, which was 37, since there was a mandatory 20-year retirement. The record contains a Forest Service--Human Resources Management document from October 1988 entitled "Firefighter Retirement" also noting that "All rigorous/primary firefighter positions have a Maximum Entry Age (MEA) of 37." Even if, as alleged by Complainant, the Maximum Entry Age of 37 did not apply to the position for which he was seeking, we find S1's statement was not based on discriminatory animus based on age. Moreover, we note that the present complaint does not concern the actual selection process for the firefighter position for which Complainant applied.
