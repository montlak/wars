B. ECONOMIC FACTORS

Hiring a younger employee because of economics does not constitute age discrimination where the employer's motivation is saving money rather than discriminating against older workers. This may be true even where an agency hires a younger, less qualified employee, as the Commission found in Cimini v. National Mediation Board, 01942369 (1995):

The Commission further notes that appellant's argument that the Commission's regulations prohibit the agency from considering appellant's salary in its hiring decision is without merit. This case involves the agency's decision to hire a well-qualified applicant at an affordable salary over a very well-qualified applicant at a salary which appeared unaffordable. Both the regulation and the Federal case law cited by appellant are inapposite to this case. Section 1625.7 of the regulations, cited by appellant, prohibits differentiations based on "the average cost of employing older employees as a group" (emphasis supplied). 29 CFR § 1625.7(f). Similarly, the case of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power v. Manhart, 435 U.S. 702 (1978), cited by appellant, dealt with a situation where the employer impermissibly considered the supposed higher cost of providing pension benefits to women as a group to justify treating women differently as a group. There is no evidence in this case that the agency treats older employees differently as a group based on cost.

Finally, the Commission notes that where there are two equally desirable candidates competing for the same position, the selecting official may exercise his prerogative in choosing between the candidates, and a trier of fact should not substitute his judgment for the legitimate exercise of managerial discretion. Bennett v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01893757 (April 20, 1990); see Burdine, 450 U.S. at 259; see also Bauer v. Bailar, 647 F.2d 1037, 1048 (10th Cir. 1981). In this case, appellant and the selectee appear to have been desirable for different, but equally compelling reasons: appellant was admittedly the best-qualified applicant for the position, while the selectee, who was less qualified than appellant but still considered very well qualified, was willing to accept the position at a substantially lower salary. Considering the overall circumstances of the case, the Commission is not persuaded that the agency discriminated against appellant based on his age.

Note that a pivotal fact in the Cimini case was that the younger selectee was well-qualified for the job. A different scenario would be presented if a younger selectee was unqualified or, perhaps, only marginally qualified for the position. See also Jenkins v. Secretary of Army, 01941299 (1995) (relying on Hazen Paper Company for the proposition that "because an employee's age is analytically distinct from his years of service, it is incorrect to say that a decision based on years of service is necessarily age based").

However, in Varley v. Attorney General, 01972338 (1998), the Commission found a supervisor's reluctance to place employees near retirement age in positions that required training does constitute age discrimination. As the Commission explained in Varley:

It is undisputed that the agency has a mandatory retirement age of 57. It is also undisputed that the Chief disapproves of any person near retirement who seeks a position which requires training. The statement made by the Chief is very different from the Hazen case. Pension benefits were at issue in Hazen which correlated with years of service, not age directly. In the present case, retirement is directly linked with age and there is no way to take into account mandatory retirement while ignoring age. Accordingly, we believe that the comment made by the Chief clearly makes an assumption, based upon age, that older workers have lost their motivation, drive, and utility which makes training them a bad investment. This assumption is based upon unfounded stereotypes. At the time of the non-selection, appellant was 53 and indicated his desire to request an extension of the mandatory retirement age of 57. Appellant could have entered the three-month training program, applied for an extension of retirement and continued to make positive contributions to the agency for many more years. Accordingly, the Chief's bias is unfounded.

But the Commission's view does not square with most courts' interpretation of Hazen. For example, in Dilla v. Secretary of Army, 179 F.3d 1348, 1348-49 (11th Cir. 1999), the Eleventh Circuit found there was no age discrimination where:

After a bench trial, the district court found that the appellants were rejected because (1) they would be eligible to retire much sooner than Nolan and (2) they had achieved a higher pay grade than Nolan in previous employment, and therefore would require a higher salary than Nolan in the air traffic controller position. See Dilla v. West, 4 F. Supp.2d 1130, 1141-44 (M.D. Ala. 1998), corrected by 31 F. Supp.2d 1347 (M.D. Ala. 1999). Both of these factors are directly correlated with age--older workers are generally (but not necessarily) closer to meeting federal retirement criteria and further advanced in their pay grade than younger workers. The district court, however, relying on Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, 507 U.S. 604, 113 S. Ct. 1701, 123 L. Ed.2d 338 (1993), concluded that "the mere fact that there exists a perfect correlation, or even a direct link, between age and the factor purportedly relied upon by the employer does not perforce mean that the employer has impermissibly relied on age." Dilla, 4 F. Supp.2d at 1142-43. Consequently, the district court ruled in favor of the defendant.

We agree with the district court's analysis of Hazen Paper: Reliance on factors correlated with age does not by itself constitute age discrimination. To be sure, purported reliance on such factors may be a pretext for discrimination; if so, the defendant has violated the ADEA. See id. at 1142. The district court, however, concluded on the basis of substantial evidence that no such pretext was involved in this case.
