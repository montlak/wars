52. Union Activities

Discrimination against an employee because of union activities it not within the jurisdiction of the EEOC because it is not prohibited by the statutes enforced by the Commission. However, an allegation by a complainant that she is subjected to restrictions not imposed on male or black union stewards states a claim within EEO purview. Cox v. Postmaster General, 01A11802 (2001).

An allegation that the complainant was denied union representation during a meeting to discuss a proposed removal failed to state a claim. Moore v. Postmaster General, 0120120324 (2012). The Commission found that the complainant failed to allege a personal harm in a term or condition of employment. The Commission did not separately analyze the complainant's claims of discrimination and reprisal, which is somewhat surprising since denial of union representation may dissuade a reasonable person from filing or pursuing a complaint.

Moreover, allegations that a union official harassed the complainant by "bullying him by making false allegations and bringing unfounded claims against him" failed to state a claim in Roland T. v. Postmaster General, 0120163209 (2017). The EEO counselor in Roland T. noted that on each occasion the agency determined that the union officials' accusations against the complainant were unfounded, but that the agency never took action to stop the union official's allegedly harassing behavior. The agency dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim, noting that the National Labor Relations Act forbids employers from interfering with employees from working together to improve the terms and conditions of their employment. The Commission, in Roland T., affirmed the final agency decision, explaining:

In Gavin v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 01955161 (Oct. 15, 1996), a supervisor filed an equal employment opportunity (EEO) complaint alleging discrimination when he was notified by an EEO investigator without prior notice that he was a named official in an EEO complaint, and the investigator denied him access to the allegations against him. In affirming the agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to state a claim, the Office of Federal Operations found that the complainant in effect was challenging how the agency investigates another individual's complaint. We found that allowing such a claim would have a chilling effect on the filing of EEO complaints by aggrieved individuals.

While not directly on point, we find Gavin is analogous to the situation here. Complainant's claim is that a union official, while acting in the capacity thereof, is harassing him by repeatedly bringing unfounded claims and false accusations against him, and he asks that the Agency take steps to stop this. Allowing Complainant's complaint to go forward would have a chilling effect on the exercise of the union official's right, in his capacity as a union official, to bring claims, and the Agency has an obligation to look into the allegations. We find that Complainant's complaint fails to state a claim.

In Doria R. v. Postmaster General, 2022004594 (2022), the complainant's allegation that her union steward sent a text message to a coworker using the n-word failed to state a claim upon which the agency could grant relief:

While Complainant is understandably deeply offended by the racial slur alleged to have been used by her union steward, we must conclude the Agency properly dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim because Complainant's remedy lies with the union, and not the Agency. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.103; Complainant v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 0120142257 (November 7, 2014); Barbara C. v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 2020000348 (May 20, 2021).

The Agency has no control over this matter.

The relief Complainant requested in her complaint accentuates that her claim is against the union as she is seeking the replacement of union steward as her union representative. This can only be accomplished by the union, not Agency management. If Complainant believes the union is unresponsive to her concerns in this matter, the appropriate place to raise such a claim is with the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB).
