29. Partially Prevailing Complainants

Because complainants frequently bring multiple claims, it is not unusual for a complainant to prevail on some claims but not on others. Such a result creates problems in terms of calculating damages. The complainant continues to believe that he or she was harmed by all of the agency's actions, but the agency is liable only for the harm caused by its discriminatory acts. Essentially, the Commission in such cases apportions the harm between the discriminatory and nondiscriminatory acts, just as it does in cases where the agency demonstrates that other external factors also contributed to the harm suffered by the complainant.

The Commission articulated no real formula or standard for apportioning damages in cases where the complainant only partially prevails. For example, in Jones v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A13671 (2002), with virtually no analysis, the Commission awarded the complainant one-quarter of her medical expenses where it found the agency had engaged in reprisal but not in race or sex-based harassment. The Commission also rejected the complainant's claim that her resignation was forced. Because of the latter finding, the Commission also denied the complainant's claim for expenses related to a new job search and her claim for future pecuniary damages in the form of loss of income.

In Lois G. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120151972 (2017), however, the Commission employed a "greater harm" analysis. In that case, the complainant brought two claims--a sexual harassment claim and a reprisal claim. She prevailed only on her sexual harassment claim. Based on the damages evidence presented by the complainant and Commission awards in comparable cases, the administrative judge determined that $57,000 in nonpecuniary damages was appropriate, but cut that award in half, attributing half of the complainant's emotional distress to the sexual harassment and half to the alleged retaliatory harassment. The Commission in Lois G. held that a 50% reduction in nonpecuniary damages was not appropriate because the "greater harm" was caused by the agency's sexual harassment rather than the complainant's unsuccessful retaliatory harassment allegation, explaining:

The AJ awarded Complainant $55,000 citing the harm suffered by Complainant, the length of time she experienced the harm and Commission case precedent. The AJ then reduced the award in half citing to Complainant's assertion that the retaliatory harassment contributed to the emotional harm she experienced. We note that the AJ correctly indicated that Complainant claimed that her emotional harm was in part due to the alleged retaliatory harassment. However, we find that the greater harm was caused by the sexual harassment Complainant experienced. The retaliatory harassment alleged involved isolated events which, according to Complainant's testimony demonstrated of lack of civility on the part of CW2, CW3 and CW4. However, the sexual harassment caused by CW1 included sexual assault, kissing, and physical touching. It is more reasonable to find that Complainant's experience with anxiety, exaggerated startle response, avoidant behavior, hypervigilance, and thoughts of suicide were related to the conduct of CW1 rather than the slights caused by CW2, CW3, and CW4. In addition, the cases cited by the AJ in which the Commission awarded $45,000 to $65,000 involved similar harm and types of harassment as engaged by CW1. Therefore, we find that the AJ's award of $55,000 is supported by the record without a reduction. See Viers v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A14246 (June 20, 2002) (awarding $60,000 to Complainant who experienced fatigue, insomnia, loss of marital harmony, apprehension, anxiety, loss of self-esteem, feelings of isolation, fear, depression, worry, humiliation, embarrassment, became withdrawn due to sexual harassment).

Where the complainant has a preexisting condition, the agency is still responsible for damages even if the triggering event is not discriminatory where the agency's discrimination contributes to an exacerbation. For example, in Tan v. Postmaster General, 01A46030 (2006), the complainant suffered from Post Traumatic Stress Disorder. Although the administrative judge found that a hostile environment existed, he also found that an incident in which a coworker allegedly called the complainant "Viet Cong" was part of the hostile environment. The "Viet Cong" incident triggered the complainant's hospitalization. The Commission still awarded 75% of the hospitalization costs in pecuniary damages where it determined the harassment seriously undermined the complainant's chance of recovery from PTSD.

There is an inherent danger for complainants in bringing multiple claims of discrimination with regard to proving damages; namely, assigning the alleged harm to specific acts of the agency. This is particularly true where the complainant only partially prevails. For example, in Landrum v. Secretary of Transportation, 0720080043 (2009), the Commission reversed the award of $9,250 in damages where the complainant prevailed on a nonselection claim but failed to prevail on hostile environment and retaliation claims. The problem for the complainant, as the Commission noted, was conflicting evidence about when the alleged distress began. The Commission determined that the emotional distress did not begin until four months after the nonselection and attributed it to the alleged retaliation. Since the complainant did not prevail on that claim, he was not entitled to an award of nonpecuniary damages because he failed to establish any causal connection between the distress and the nonselection.
