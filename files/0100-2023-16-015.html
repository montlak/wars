A. APPEAL RIGHTS

The agency is required to give a notice of appeal rights in its final action. 29 CFR 1614.110(a)-(b). The precise notice of appeal rights required will depend upon the nature of its action. A full discussion of appeal procedures is included in Chapter 8.

If the agency does not fully implement the decision of an administrative judge, it is the agency that must file an appeal with OFO. See 29 CFR 1614.110(a). But, the regulation also requires the agency to give the complainant notice of his or her appeal rights. Although the regulation is less than clear, when the agency files an appeal it must advise the complainant of his or her right to also file an appeal within 30 days of receipt of the final agency action. See MD-110 at 9-5.

If the agency does fully implement the decision of the administrative judge or a final agency decision issues without a hearing, the agency must advise the complainant of his or her right to file an appeal within 30 days of receipt of the agency's final action. 29 CFR 1614.402(a).

The failure to issue such a notice will prevent an agency from asserting that an appeal is untimely. For example, in Ballentine v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 05950598 (1996), the Commission found that the agency erred when, subsequent to issuing a final agency decision with a finding of discrimination, it issued a final decision regarding the amount of back pay to be awarded that did not include appeal or compliance rights. In overturning the agency's dismissal of a compliance petition as untimely, the Commission noted that the agency had not made the complainant aware that the same parameters that applied to the final agency decision on the merits also applied to the back pay award.

Although the failure to issue the requisite notice will likely extend the time period for appeal, errantly issuing such a notice does not confer EEOC appeal rights on a complainant. For example, in Jackson v. Postmaster General, 05930381 (1994), the Commission found that the agency erred in issuing a final agency decision with EEOC appeal rights because the complainant, who had been removed, was a preference eligible veteran with MSPB appeal rights. Where OFO improperly accepted jurisdiction, as it did in Jackson, and issued a decision, that decision should be vacated and the case remanded to the agency with instructions to issue a new final agency decision with MSPB appeal rights:

Following a thorough review of the record, the Commission concludes that the previous decision improperly entertained appellant's appeal, as the Commission did not have jurisdiction to consider the merits of appellant's discrimination allegations at this stage in the administrative processing of his complaint. Accordingly, as the previous decision improperly addressed the merits of appellant's complaint, the Commission is exercising its authority, pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.407(a), to reconsider the previous decision.

EEOC Regulations at 29 CFR § 1613.401 et seq., in effect at the time the agency's decision was issued, provided for the processing of mixed case complaints and appeals. The Regulations stated that a mixed case complaint is a complaint of employment discrimination related to, or stemming from, an action taken by the agency against the complainant when that action may be appealed to the MSPB. 29 CFR § 1613.402(a)(1). Removal is an action which permanent, competitive service employees may directly appeal to the MSPB. 5 USC § 7511(a)(1)(A) and § 7512; 5 CFR § 1201.3(a). However, Postal Service employees are generally excluded from this provision pursuant to 5 USC § 2105(e), and do not have MSPB appeal rights, unless they have a veterans preference and a year of continuous service. 5 USC § 7511. Appellant, as a preference eligible veteran, was entitled to appeal his removal to the MSPB, as indicated by the rights provided in the Letter of Decision on his removal. Therefore, appellant's EEO complaint was a mixed case complaint, and should have been processed as such. Accordingly, the agency's [proposed disposition] should not have informed appellant of his right to request an EEOC hearing, as such a hearing is not available in mixed case complaint processing. Instead, appellant should have been informed of his right to request a decision by the agency without a hearing, and of his right, thereafter, to file an appeal of the agency's decision to the MSPB. 29 CFR § 1613.405(e). Likewise, when the agency subsequently issued its FAD, after appellant failed to respond to the [proposed disposition], the FAD should have informed appellant of his right to file an appeal with the MSPB, not the EEOC. 29 CFR § 1613.405(e)(4).

The Commission finds that the previous decision should have remanded this case to the agency for further processing as the Commission did not have jurisdiction to entertain an appeal from a final agency decision on appellant's mixed case complaint. Such an appeal must properly be filed with the MSPB rather than the EEOC; therefore, the FAD should have contained rights notifying appellant that he could initiate an appeal to the MSPB. 29 CFR § 1613.401 et seq. Accordingly, since this matter should have been raised first with the MSPB in accordance with 29 CFR § 1613.401, the Commission is required to remand appellant's complaint to the agency for issuance of a new FAD with appeal rights to the MSPB. The agency is advised that should appellant decide to pursue an appeal to the MSPB from the new FAD, the agency must advise the MSPB that the delay in bringing this matter to the MSPB's attention was the result of the agency's failure to provide the appropriate appeal rights.

Although the decision was issued under the regulations of Part 1613, there has been no change in its position with the adoption of Part 1614.
