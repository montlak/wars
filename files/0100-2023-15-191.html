1. Ex Parte Communications

As a general rule, ex parte communications between a party and the administrative judge are prohibited. In order for an ex parte communication to be prohibited, it must relate to the substance of the case. There is no prohibition against a party contacting an administrative judge outside the presence of the opposing party to discuss procedural matters and, indeed, those type of communications occur with some frequency.

The Judges' Handbook at 1-3 explains the EEOC's policy toward ex parte communications:

EEOC Order Number 690.001 (January 30, 2002) sets forth the policy and procedures regarding ex parte communication during rulemaking and decision-making on federal sector cases. An ex parte communication is an oral or written communication between decision making personnel of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission and an interested party to the proceeding which does not provide for the participation of the other interested parties.

Except when he/she is engaged in alternative dispute resolution, an Administrative Judge is prohibited from engaging in ex parte communication concerning the merits of a case from the time he/she learns that the complainant has requested a hearing until complainant has no further right of administrative appeal. If, for example, one party telephones the Administrative Judge and attempts an ex parte communication, the Administrative Judge should terminate the conversation. If appropriate, the Administrative Judge may convene a conference with both parties to discuss the matter raised. Another example of an ex parte communication might occur when the Administrative Judge receives a document that has not been properly served upon the other party. In this circumstance, the Administrative Judge should return the correspondence or send a copy to the other side. The Administrative Judge may wish to memorialize the ex parte communication.

Administrative Judges are permitted to engage in communication that does not concern the merits of the case but only references procedural or ministerial matters. Permissible communication includes, but is not limited to, inquiries regarding the status of the case, the date of a hearing, or the method for transmitting evidence. An Administrative Judge may also contact a party to request copies of documents that are illegible or miscopied.

When ex parte communications occur, the decision of the administrative judge will not be reversed without a showing that the communication resulted in some error. Leonard v. Secretary of Navy, 01A12006 (2002).

It is not an impermissible ex parte communication for the administrative judge to contact the agency representative and indicate he is issuing a summary judgment decision and, therefore, the witnesses do not need to appear for the hearing. Rountree v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A20129 (2002). Similarly, there no prohibited ex parte communication where the administrative judge contacted the agency representative for the purpose of determining if he had received a copy of a response to a summary judgment motion that the complainant claimed to have filed. Cartwright v. Secretary of Navy, 01A51267 (2006).

Contact between the administrative judge and a pro se complainant is not prohibited where the discussions are limited to procedural matters, particularly where there is no evidence that the agency was prejudiced by the discussions. Bowers v. Secretary of Defense, 0720070012 (2010).

Similarly, although both the complainant and agency should be included in prehearing conferences, merely showing that an ex parte communication took place is insufficient to require reversal of the administrative judge's decision. The excluded party must also establish that there was some harm that resulted from the exclusion. For example, in Park v. Postmaster General, 01983948 (2001), the Commission found there was no harm in an ex parte conference between the administrative judge and the agency representative where the discussion involved whether the complainant has established a prima facie case of discrimination. The Commission based its finding in Park on the fact that the judge accepted that the complainant was qualified for the position in question and proceeded directly to the issues of whether the agency articulated a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for her selection and whether the complainant could establish pretext.

While usually the issue of ex parte communications comes up in the context of one party attempting to overturn a decision as biased, it can also come up as an issue of sanctionable misconduct. In Flack v. Postmaster General, 0120060535 (2007), the Commission upheld sanctions against the complainant and her counsel where each had engaged in ex parte communications with a supervisory administrative judge and also failed to proceed at hearing because the agency representative was late. In Flack, both the complainant and her counsel separately contacted the supervisory administrative judge after the presiding judge denied motions to amend the complaint and compel discovery. In addition, the agency representative was some 24 minutes late for a telephonic hearing due to confusion over whether the starting time was 9 A.M., Central Standard Time or Mountain Standard Time. The administrative judge, as a sanction, canceled the hearing and remanded the case for a final agency decision. As a sanction against the complainant's counsel, he was barred from representing the complainant in any further EEO proceedings. The Commission upheld both sanctions on appeal.
