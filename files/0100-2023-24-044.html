2. Timing of Submission of Medical Information

There is no specific time period for submitting medical information to support a request for reasonable accommodation, although because the ADA requires only that an agency accommodate the known limitation imposed by a disability, 42 USC 12112(b)(5)(A), the agency's obligation to provide accommodation does not arise until it has sufficient information to determine the employee is an individual with a disability and in need of an accommodation. However, that does not limit the scope of medical information that may be provided or requested during the complaint investigation or hearing stage.

In Harden v. Social Security Administration, 0720080002 (2011), the Commission held that the administrative judge did not err in taking evidence on the issue of whether the complainant was an individual with a disability at the hearing:

We reject the Agency's contention that a fact finder, when determining whether a complainant is an individual with a disability or a qualified individual with a disability, is limited to considering evidence related to the medical documents actually submitted to the Agency before the discovery process. When the nature of an impairment is not obvious, it is permissible during the investigatory or discovery process to ask for information that was not necessarily in the agency's possession at the time of the events in question. See Executive Summary: Compliance Manual Section 902, Definition of the Term "Disability" (Mar. 14, 1995), at 902-6, 902-7. Investigation or discovery is not limited to only obtaining the medical documentation concerning the complainant's condition that the agency has in its possession. Id. at 902-7. A complainant can provide medical documentation that describes the condition or that contains a diagnosis of the condition. Other information, such as the complainant's description of the condition and statements from friends, family, or co-workers, may also be relevant to determining the nature of the impairment. Id. Such statements or documents may not necessarily have been presented to the agency at the time it declined to provide a complainant's request for a reasonable accommodation. But they constitute relevant evidence that serves the purpose of the investigatory and discovery process: to help the fact finder determine whether Complainant is an individual with a disability.

The Commission did not reach the question of whether a finding of failure to accommodate could be based on evidence that the complainant was an individual with a disability that was not in the agency's possession at the time of its decision because it found that, even in the absence of the evidence submitted at hearing, the agency had adequate information in its possession when it denied the complainant's request for accommodation. Presumably, a finding of failure to accommodate could not be based on such evidence, but would impact on the agency's future obligation to provide accommodation.
