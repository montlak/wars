6. Experience of Counsel

Just as the experience of counsel is factored in setting a reasonable hourly rate, the experience of counsel also is considered in setting the number of hours reasonably expended in representation. The experience of counsel is a factor in terms of such things as research and preparation. Experienced counsel are not expected to expend large amounts of time researching relatively routine issues and are expected to be reasonably efficient in terms of preparation time. For example, in Toy v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 07A20122 (2004), the Commission applied a 60% reduction in the requested fee, in part because of claims which were withdrawn prior to hearing and in part because the number of hours claimed were deemed excessive for an experienced counsel:

Here, the AJ determined that complainant's counsel was entitled to the entire amount he requested because complainant succeeded on a level "equal to any success complainant may have anticipated had he elected to press further the inter-related claims." The AJ indicated that a review of the entire record in this matter reflects that "the claims which complainant withdrew prior to hearing on which complainant failed to prevail are related to the claims on which he prevailed" because these claims "are bound by a common core of facts."

The agency mainly argues that the attorney's fees awarded in this matter are not reasonable because complainant's counsel spent unnecessary time and attention on issues which were not novel, but were rather redundant, requiring many times continuous duplication as the EEO process progressed for his client. The agency maintains that a seasoned employment lawyer, based in San Francisco should not have required such numerous hours to assist complainant through the federal sector EEO complaint process. Further the agency maintains that a sixty percent reduction is in order because complainant dropped most of his issues on the eve of the hearing. In summary, the agency argues that complainant's attorney is entitled to $21,393.75 (57.05 hours x $375) for the period (2002) spent on complainant's case before and after the hearing. Coupled with the award of $8,347.50 (55.65 hours x .40 x $375) for his representation of complainant in 2000 and 2001, the agency asserts that a reasonable fee in this case would be $29,741.25, as opposed to the amount of $42,965.62 awarded by the AJ.

The Commission finds that the agency's argument here is persuasive. Complainant's counsel claimed 55.65 hours for his representation of complainant in 2000 and 2001. As most issues (approximately 60%) were withdrawn before complainant's hearing, compensation for many of the hours spent by complainant's counsel preparing are not justified. Thus, $8,347.50 (55.65 hours x .40 x $375) would be appropriate considering the 60% reduction. Complainant's counsel also claimed 26.55 hours in preparation for hearing, which is excessive given his employment law experience. We find 20 hours to be reasonable in light of counsel's expertise. Complainant's counsel claimed 11 hours for the hearing before the Administrative Judge, and neither the agency nor the Commission take issue with this claim. However, the 23.8 hours spent by complainant's counsel after the hearing to prepare his closing is excessive. The issues addressed at hearing primarily involved performance ratings, which, while arguably related to the multitude of previously withdrawn issues, were the only claims that complainant prevailed upon. Also, these issues were not novel and complex. We find 4 hours to be reasonable in light of counsel's expertise. Finally, complainant's counsel claimed 26.45 hours for more or less reviewing agency arguments, legal research on redundant issues, and generating a memorandum for complainant's appeal with an accompanying request for additional fees. We find 10 hours to be reasonable in light of the record herein and counsel's expertise. In total, complainant's counsel claimed that he spent 86.75 hours in 2002, beginning with hearing preparation through preparation of complainant's closing, and 26.45 hours after the hearing for a total of 113.20. The Commission determines that the time spent should be reduced by 42.80 hours. Therefore, complainant's counsel is entitled to $26.400 (70.4 x $375) for this period. Coupled with the award in 2000 and 2001, complainant's counsel is entitled to $34,747.50. Thus, the Commission determines that the total amount warranted, including for work done on complainant's appeal, to be $34,747.50 in attorney's fees.
