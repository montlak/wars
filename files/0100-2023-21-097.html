q. Pre-Burlington Industries and Faragher Caselaw

In a case decided before Burlington Industries and Faragher, the Commission held that prompt remedial action will not excuse an agency from liability for sexual harassment where a supervisor used his authority to aid him in accomplishing the harassment. In Taylor v. Secretary of Army, 01942699 (1996), the Commission found that although the agency took prompt action against the supervisor, it was still liable for his harassment of the complainant. It is unclear whether and how Taylor applies in the wake of Burlington Industries and Faragher, though Taylor suggests there may be circumstances where a supervisor was aided in the harassment by his supervisory role to such a degree that no affirmative defense would be viable. The Commission in Taylor found:

On June 22, 1992, appellant and Supervisor A, who were assigned to the agency's Albuquerque office, travelled by car to Hobbs, New Mexico on a TDY trip. On the evening of June 23, a Tuesday, appellant and Supervisor A went to a lounge across the street from their motel and drank four or five beers over a period of about three hours. At the hearing, appellant testified that they returned to the motel, Supervisor A parked in front of her room, and they got out and approached her room. Appellant stated that as soon as she unlocked the door to her room, Supervisor A grabbed her, turned her towards him, and, while "gyrating his hips," kissed her. "The next thing I knew, we were both sitting on the edge of the bed, and he still had his arms around me." Appellant testified that she told Supervisor A to stop and said, "Look, neither of us need this. You're married. We have to work together." Appellant further testified that they got up from the bed, that Supervisor A kept his arms around her as they walked to the door, and that as he left the room, supervisor A stated to her, "If you change your mind, come to my room."

...

The Commission finds that the agency took prompt remedial action reasonably calculated to end the harassment. The record indicates that Chief B met with Supervisor A on June 29 and, after listening to his version of events, decided to terminate Supervisor A's supervisory responsibility over appellant as well as over other employees. He also decided that it would be in appellant's best interest to remove Supervisor A physically from the premises for a period of time and approved a TDY trip to Sacramento, which began early in July and extended into August. The record also indicates that Chief B counseled and cautioned Supervisor A concerning his behavior on the June TDY trip. When Supervisor A returned from Sacramento, the agency moved him from his eighth floor office to the seventh floor, in order to separate him physically from appellant.

In her recommended decision, the AJ held that the agency did not take appropriate action because it failed to discipline Supervisor A or "take any action against [Supervisor A] to insure that the sexual harassment would stop." Although the AJ noted correctly that the agency did not reprimand, discharge, or otherwise formally discipline Supervisor A, her observation that it failed to take actions to insure an end to harassment ignores the removal of Supervisor A's supervisory responsibilities over appellant and other employees, his removal from the office for over one month, his assignment to a different floor, and the counseling and cautions that he received from Chief B. We further find that although disciplinary action against a harasser is an appropriate remedy in sexual harassment cases, it is not the sine qua non of prompt corrective action.

Finding that the agency took prompt remedial action does not end our inquiry, however. Under agency principles, an employer may be liable for the actions of its supervisors, despite taking prompt remedial action, where "the (supervisor] purported to act or to speak on behalf of the principal and there was reliance upon apparent authority, or he or she was aided in accomplishing the tort by the existence of the agency relation." Restatement (2d) of Agency, § 219(2)(d); see Hirschfeld, 916 F.2d at 579; see also Karibean v. Columbia Univ., 14 F.3d 773, 779 (2d Cir.), cert. denied, 114 S. Ct. 2693 (1994). The issue thus is whether Supervisor A was aided in his harassment of appellant because of his supervisory relationship over her.

Based on the record in this case, we find that Supervisor A used his supervisory relationship over appellant to require that she accompany him on the TDY trip and join him for drinks in the lounge, and that these actions facilitated the harassment that ensued. Chief B and appellant testified that the TDY trip did not involve matters within her job duties and took her away from them. Nonetheless, appellant, who only had been working under Supervisor A for two months, felt compelled to accompany him as he requested because of his position as her supervisor. Supervisor A relied on his supervisory position for much more than mere access to appellant; rather, he was able to command her to travel with him on an overnight trip away from the office. For this reason, we find that the agency is liable for Supervisor A's sexual harassment of appellant.

Likewise, in Muller v. Secretary of Army, 01972435 (1998), a case of hostile environment harassment based on reprisal, the Commission found that the agency's prompt remedial action did not insulate it from liability where the harassment was carried on by supervisors acting under apparent authority. The Commission in Muller explained:

It is undisputed that the agency took prompt corrective action in response to appellant's initial complaint regarding the harassment. According to the AJ and the agency, the prompt remedial action taken by the agency insulates it from liability. We disagree. Contrary to the agency's contention, a finding that the agency took prompt remedial action is not an automatic preclusion to agency liability when supervisors engaged in the harassment. See Parker v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01943227 (June 8, 1995), recon. denied, EEOC Request No. 05950787 (April 17, 1997); Taylor v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01942699 (March 7, 1996), recon. denied, EEOC Request No. 05960405 (August 7, 1997); Green v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 01955142 (February 5, 1998).

Under agency principles, an employer may be liable for the actions of its supervisors, despite taking prompt remedial actions, where "the [supervisor] purported to act or speak on behalf of the principal and there was reliance on apparent authority, or he was aided in accomplishing the tort by the existence of the agency relation." Taylor v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01942699 (March 7, 1996), recon. denied, EEOC Request No. 05960405 (August 7, 1997).

To avoid liability for the hostile environment, the agency must show there is no basis for imputing liability to the employer under agency principles. Moreover, in the absence of a showing that the agency has a strong, widely disseminated, and consistently enforced policy against harassment, the Commission has generally found that employees could reasonably believe that a harassing supervisor's actions will be ignored, tolerated, or even condoned by upper management and, accordingly, find an agency liable for hostile work environment. See Parker v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01943227 (June 8, 1995), recon. denied, EEOC Request No. 05950787 (April 17, 1997); Taylor v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01942699 (March 7, 1996), recon. denied, EEOC Request No. 05960405 (August 7, 1997); Green v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 01955142 (February 5, 1998).

In the present matter, appellant's own first and second-line supervisors contributed to the hostile work environment. Moreover, the record shows that S3, appellant's third-line supervisor, also contributed to the hostile work environment by spreading false information to his co-supervisors that a monetary award by appellant in his EEO complaint could ultimately cause transfers and layoffs. The evidence shows that SS, S1, and S2 all referred to appellant as the "EEO man" and mocked him by pretending to be wearing a listening device when appellant was around. In addition, supervisors and co-workers treated appellant with animosity and S2 attempted to intimidate appellant with a threatening statement. In addition, while it appears that the agency has a policy on reprisal discrimination and hostile work environment, the agency presented no evidence that it has a strong, widely disseminated, and consistently enforced policy against such discrimination.

Accordingly, although the agency took prompt action to end the harassment after appellant's opposition to said harassment, we find that SS, S1, and S2 were all acting under apparent authority of the agency. In addition, we believe that the record supports that S3 had actual or constructive knowledge of the harassment and did nothing to correct the situation prior to appellant's express opposition. We, therefore, find the agency is liable for the hostile work environment and acts of reprisal.

Under Burlington Industries and Faragher, the proper basis for imputing liability to the employer is the aided in accomplishing the harassment standard and not the apparent authority standard. Still, the Commission's rationale in Muller, although relying on the "apparent authority" standard, could easily apply to the "aided in accomplishing" standard.
