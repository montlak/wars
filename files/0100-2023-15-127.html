5. Sanction Procedures

The procedure for imposing sanctions can be initiated by either party through the filing of a motion for sanctions or by the administrative judge sua sponte. The procedures differ slightly, depending on whether consideration of sanctions is prompted by motion or on the judge's own initiative. However, the underlying premise in both situations is that sanctions should not be imposed unless the offending party had a specific opportunity to respond to the possible imposition of sanctions.

If the administrative judge is considering the imposition of sanctions sua sponte, the Judges' Handbook at 6-2 provides that an order to show cause must issue:

An Order to Show Cause directs a party to explain why a potential sanction should not be imposed. In the event that a party did not receive sufficient notice that its conduct could be sanctioned and what potential sanctions may be imposed, and did not have an opportunity to explain its conduct, an Administrative Judge must issue an Order to Show Cause, addressing these three factors. In lieu of a formal Order to Show Cause, notice and opportunity to respond could occur at the hearing or during a telephone conference. An Order to Show Cause is not necessary where a party has filed a motion for specific sanctions and the non-moving party has had an opportunity to respond.

An order to show cause is not necessary where consideration of sanctions is prompted by a motion, since the motion itself gives the offending party notice. For example, in Hailey v. Postmaster General, 0120110260 (2011), the Commission found that the administrative judge did not err in failing to issue a show cause order where the complainant had already been warned that a failure to respond to an order to compel discovery could result in sanctions and had also failed to respond to the agency's motion for sanctions. In rejecting the complainant's arguments that the administrative judge abused her discretion in not issuing a show cause order in Hailey, the Commission found:

We first address the AJ's decision to dismiss complainant's hearing request and remand the matter to the agency. The Commission's regulations afford broad authority to AJs for the conduct of hearings, including the authority to sanction a party for failure without good cause shown to fully comply with an order. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109 et seq.; Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (EEO-MD-110), Chapter 7, § III(D) (November 9, 1999). Where a party fails to respond to an order of an AJ, the AJ may, as appropriate, take action against the non-complying party pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3), i.e., an AJ may: (1) draw an adverse inference that the requested information would have reflected unfavorably on the non-complying party; (2) consider the requested information to be established in favor of the opposing party; (3) exclude other evidence offered by the non-complying party; (4) issue a decision fully or partially in favor of the opposing party; or (5) take other action deemed appropriate. Before sanctions are imposed, the Commission requires that the AJ notify the parties of what sanctions or other actions may be imposed for failure to comply with the AJ's order. EEO-MD-110, Chapter 7, § III(B).

The AJ did not abuse her discretion in imposing a sanction against Complainant. In her dismissal notice, the AJ stated that on May 24, 2010 she issued an Order Granting Agency's Motion to Compel, directing Complainant to provide the Agency with answers to interrogatories and requested documents. The Order also advised the parties that failure to obey the Order could result in sanctions, including the dismissal of a hearing request. The AJ noted that Complainant did not comply with the order, and that he did not provide an explanation for his inaction or seek an extension of time. Further, Complainant did not submit a response to the Agency's July 1, 2010 Motion for Sanctions. Therefore, we find that the AJ's imposition of a sanction in this case, as a result of Complainant's repeated failure to respond, was proper. The Commission also agrees that in light of these facts, the AJ's decision to dismiss the hearing request and remand the matter for an agency decision was appropriate.

As for Complainant's argument on appeal, that the AJ erred in issuing the sanction without first issuing a show cause order, the Commission is not persuaded. As noted above, Complainant was previously placed on notice of the sanctions that could be imposed for failure to comply with the Order Granting Agency's Motion to Compel in a timely manner. Moreover, Complainant did not request an extension of time or demonstrate good cause for failing to comply with the AJ's orders. Complainant and his attorney also did not provide good cause for their inaction on appeal, stating vaguely that Complainant was "hindered by an unfortunate series of events that left him out of touch...."

Consequently, we find that Complainant has failed to present sufficient evidence to support his contention that the AJ's action constituted an abuse of discretion. See Sanders v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A00214 (February 10, 2000) (finding that the AJ acted within her discretion when she cancelled a hearing and remanded the matter to the agency after complainant failed to submit a timely pre-hearing statement); see also Grant v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120064456 (January 7, 2009) ("dismissing a hearing request is an appropriate sanction for failure to comply with an AJ's Order").

Similarly, the administrative judge is not required to issue a separate notice to show cause why sanctions should not be imposed where the acknowledgment order issued in response to the request for a hearing adequately puts the parties on notice that sanctions may be imposed for failure to respond to discovery requests. Campbell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120112704 (2011).
