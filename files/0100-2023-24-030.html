8. Threatening or Violent Behavior

In Casem v. Postmaster General, 01972115 (1999), the complainant was unsuccessful in contending that the agency acted in discriminatory fashion by placing him on emergency leave following his threat to kill his supervisor, despite his contention that he was merely seeking psychiatric help. The complainant also failed to rectify the situation by his "good will" gesture of handing over the pistol he had in his pocket. The agency found that the complainant had failed to establish a prima facie case of race discrimination because he presented no evidence that similarly situated individuals not in his protected class were treated more favorably under similar circumstances. The Commission in Casem found:

As noted above, complainant argues that the agency deliberately misconstrued this statement as a death threat and placed him off duty as reprisal for his prior EEO activity. Contrary to complainant's contention, we find that the agency's interpretation of this statement and its response were reasonable. First, the statement was referred from OWCP to the Postmaster because it, an impartial actor in this matter, viewed the statement as a possible death threat against S, thereby lending credence to the agency's interpretation. Second, our review of the record discloses that complainant's treating psychiatrist submitted a letter to the agency, dated November 28, 1995, describing complainant's high stress level and the possibility that he might injure S. We view this letter as evidence further legitimatizing the agency's interpretation. Third, we find that the agency acted responsibly when it placed complainant in off duty status while conducting an investigation given the immediacy of the "threat" communicated in the statement to OWCP. Therefore, we conclude that complainant has failed to show a nexus between these actions and his prior EEO activity.

Regarding complainant's removal (incident 3), the FAD found that the agency's action was not motivated by reprisal because the investigation showed that complainant made a written death threat against S. We concur with this finding. Review of the "Assault & Threat Speciality Report" shows that complainant was interviewed and admitted to having feelings about killing S, but stated that he did not intend to make a death threat against S, and was only describing the urgency of his need for psychiatric help. The report notes that he relinquished his personal revolver to the inspectors, apparently as a good will gesture. However, the record also shows that during the course of his employment, complainant had attended numerous training sessions conducted by the agency regarding the seriousness of threats against supervisors, and its zero tolerance policy towards this type of conduct. Accordingly, we find that complainant had knowledge about the agency's policy regarding threats towards supervisors, and should have realized that the OWCP would alert the agency regarding his statement due to its startling content. Even absent these assumptions, we find that given complainant's continued "feelings" to kill S, as admitted to the investigators, the agency acted appropriately in construing the OWCP statement as a death threat toward S, and that his removal was fully consistent with the agency's policy. Therefore, we conclude that appellant has failed to show a nexus between the agency's decision to remove him from employment and his prior EEO activity.

In Knill v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01984220 (2001), the Commission found that the complainant's unpredictable and potentially violent behavior could not be reasonably accommodated by training other employees to handle the complainant's agitation:

Based on the record, the Administrative Judge found that complainant often became agitated at work, that his behavior was not predictable, and that on two occasions, he used potentially violent weapons, a car and a knife, in an unacceptably threatening manner on agency property. The Administrative Judge noted that in recommending complainant return to work, both psychiatrists recommended that complainant receive stress management counseling but that complainant's father testified that complainant had not followed the psychiatrists' recommendation. The Administrative Judge was not persuaded that training other cemetery employees as to how to handle complainant's agitation would reduce the risk of similarly threatening incidents recurring and found that there was no evidence in the psychiatrists' findings to support such a conclusion. The Administrative Judge further concluded that the proposed accommodation requiring the agency to hire or transfer a supervisor for the sole purpose of creating trust with complainant in order to diffuse his agitation was not a reasonable accommodation that would help complainant perform the essential functions of his position. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, March 1, 1991, at Question 32. The Administrative Judge concluded, based on the facts presented herein, that complainant posed a real, not speculative, direct threat and there was no accommodation available to reduce or eliminate the risk he posed. The Administrative Judge thus determined that complainant was not a qualified individual with a disability within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act. See Coffman v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Petition No. 03A00028 (July 27, 2000).

After a careful review of the record, the Commission finds that the Administrative Judge's factual findings are supported by substantial evidence in the record and that her decision properly referenced the appropriate regulations, policies, and laws. We find that the agency's termination of complainant was not based on generalized fears about his ability to safely perform the essential functions of his job but on its individualized assessment of complainant's unpredictable unwillingness to obey his superiors and his brandishing of a weapon, albeit small, in an unacceptable manner on agency property. We discern no basis to disturb the Administrative Judge's decision. Therefore, after a careful review of the record, including complainant's contentions on appeal, we affirm the agency's final decision.
