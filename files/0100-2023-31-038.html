1. Downward Adjustments

When the agency proposes a downward adjustment in the customary hourly rate, it has the burden of proving that the adjustment is justified. See Toliver v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01890107 (1989). An agency may not reduce a properly documented hourly rate on the basis of its "experience in evaluating attorney fee petitions." See Pollack v. Postmaster General, 01931105 (1993).

The EEOC adopts a result-oriented approach to any downward adjustment of fees. See Williams v. Department of Health and Human Services, supra. The fact that a complainant does not prevail on certain bases of discrimination does not diminish the actual results obtained in prevailing on any other basis. In Williams, the Commission held:

In other words, the degree to which appellant prevailed, and the remedy to which she thereby became entitled, would not have increased one iota had a finding also been made that she had been discriminated against on the bases of her race and/or sex. Appellant fully prevailed on her allegation that she had been discriminated against in not being granted a reassignment. Whether she was discriminated against on one basis (handicap) or two or three (race and sex) is irrelevant to whether she fully prevailed. There was only one issue in this case (the failure to be reassigned). Although appellant raised three bases of discrimination, it was necessary for her to prevail on only one of these bases to fully prevail and to be entitled to a full remedy.

The Commission disallowed the downward adjustment of the agency on the grounds that the complainant had not prevailed on her claims of race and sex discrimination.

An agency is not justified in arbitrarily reducing the hourly rate of an attorney on the basis that the work claimed could have been done by a paralegal. Lopetrone v. Secretary of Air Force, 01923930 (1993). The Commission rejected the agency's argument that services such as legal research, meeting with the client and telephone conferences were "the type that a paralegal could perform." Id. The Commission noted that the burden was on the agency to sustain any deviation from the reasonable hourly rate and, without analysis, rejected the agency's argument about the nature of the services provided.

Although it did not directly do so in terms of adjusting the hourly rate, the Commission did find it possible to reduce a fee based on only partial success and to enhance the fee based on quality of representation in the same case. In Simmons v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A60798 (2006), the Commission upheld the decision of the administrative judge to reduce to total fee award by 25% because the complainant did not prevail on both his claims. The judge reduced the award by only 25% because both claims involved proof that the complainant was a qualified individual with a disability. The judge then enhanced the fee by 25% because the competency of the complainant's counsel significantly reduced the hearing time in the case. See section below on "Partial and Fractionable Fees."
