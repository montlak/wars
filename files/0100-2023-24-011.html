3. Constructive Discharge

The opposite situation also arises; that is, the agency refuses to provide reasonable accommodation and the employee declines to report to work. Even though the agency has not provided reasonable accommodation, this does not per se mean the employee is justified in not working. The Commission will analyze the case as it does any other allegation of constructive discharge.

For example, in Greer v. Postmaster General, 01976756 (2000), the agency accommodated the complainant by transferring her from a transitional job to a causal appointment. Although the Commission found this did not constitute reasonable accommodation, it also found in Green that the employee had not been constructively discharged when she failed to report for work:

Accordingly, the question raised is whether an employee with a disability who is reassigned against his or her wishes when there is an accommodation is available which will permit that employee to perform the essential functions of his or her oriGINAl position may refuse to report for work in the reassigned position.

In essence, therefore, complainant contends that her reassignment to a less desirable position was tantamount to a constructive discharge. The central question in a constructive discharge case is whether the employer, through its unlawful discriminatory behavior, made the employee's working conditions so difficult that any reasonable person in the employee's position would feel compelled to resign. Irving v. Dubuque Packing, 689 F.2d 170 (10th Cir. 1982). The Commission has adopted a three-pronged test for establishing a constructive discharge. A complainant must show that: (1) a reasonable person in his or her position would have found the working conditions intolerable; (2) conduct which constituted prohibited discriminatory treatment created the intolerable working conditions; and (3) complainant's involuntary resignation resulted from the intolerable working conditions. See Taylor v. AAFES, EEOC Request No. 05900630 (July 20, 1990). While we find that complainant has established that conduct which constituted prohibited discriminatory treatment (i.e. her reassignment) created the allegedly intolerable working conditions and that her failure to report was the result of the reassignment, we decline to find that a reasonable person in her position would have found the working conditions intolerable. In so finding, we first note that the record reflects that the duties of a Casual Clerk position were within complainant's physical abilities. See Meyer v. Brown and Root Construction Co., 661 F.2d 369 (5th Cir. 1981) (constructive discharge established where the employer, upon learning that plaintiff was pregnant, reassigned her from a desk job to a warehouse job which involved heavy lifting) Second, complainant had contacted the EEO Counselor on January 17, 1996, with respect to her contention that the reassignment constituted discrimination. We find that a reasonable person in complainant's situation would have reported for duty while pursuing her EEO claim and, therefore, complainant cannot justify her failure to report for duty from January to March 19, 1996, based on the agency's failure to accommodate her within her Transitional Clerk position. Inasmuch as the record supports the AJ's finding that complainant failed to establish that she was subjected to disparate treatment when the agency placed her off the clock and thereafter refused to renew her appointment based on her failure to adequately document her absence, we find that complainant failed to establish that these agency actions constituted discrimination.

However, in Arnold C. v. Postmaster General, 0120093856 (2015), the Commission did find a constructive discharge where the failure to accommodate led the complainant to retire. The complainant in that case suffered from depression and could no longer perform the essential functions of his job, but did point to two lower-graded jobs that were vacant that he could perform. The Commission, in Arnold C., noted:

Notwithstanding the Agency's contentions, we find that the "voluntary" determination by the MSPB in dismissing Complainant's appeal does not preclude the EEOC from adjudicating the merits of a constructive discharge claim, nor is the Commission required to adopt this finding Instead, under such circumstances, the Commission has held that MSPB findings are not res judicata with regard to a complainant's claim of constructive discharge. See Holland v. Social Security Admin., EEOC Appeal No. 01A01372 (citing Borghese v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920734 (Nov. 27, 1992)) (noting that the MSPB's standard for determining whether a complainant's resignation or retirement is voluntary is different from the Commission's standard regarding constructive discharge). In the instant case, based on our review of the record, we find that Complainant's disability retirement is tantamount to a constructive discharge.

With regard to forced retirement, constructive discharge occurs when an employer deliberately renders an employee's working conditions so intolerable that the individual is forced to retire from his position. The Commission has established three elements which Complainant must prove to substantiate a claim of constructive discharge: 1) a reasonable person in Complainant's position would have found the working conditions intolerable; 2) the conduct causing the intolerable working conditions is an EEO violation; and 3) Complainant's resignation was caused by the intolerable working conditions. See Taylor v. Army and Air Force Exch. Serv., EEOC Request No. 05900630 (July 20, 1990); see also Perricone v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05900135 (June 11, 1990).

Here, we determine that the record supports a finding that, rather than engage in the interactive process with Complainant, Agency management compelled Complainant to apply for retirement by presenting him with only two options: (a) retirement or (b) reporting for work, thereby placing Complainant at high risk for difficulties with respect to his major depression. In finding that Complainant's choice was limited to these two options, we note that the Agency never considered reassignment or continued sick leave. See Jambora v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 07A40128 (May 16, 2006). In Jambora, complainant alleged that he was forced into disability retirement and constructively discharged when he was denied reasonable accommodation for his disability. We found that the complainant in Jambora was denied reasonable accommodation and constructively discharged because management failed to engage in the interactive process and compelled her to apply for disability retirement by only presenting her with a position that was harmful to her disability.

Further, in Holland, EEOC Appeal No. 01A01372, we found that a complainant who had obsessive compulsive disorder and depression was also constructively discharged when management failed to provide him reasonable accommodation. In Holland, we found that, due to the agency's failure, the complainant's only choice was to retire because his position negatively affected his disability.

In the instant case, the Agency requested that Complainant return to work and denied his further requests for sick leave even though Complainant and his psychiatrist explained that he could not return to work at that time due to his major depression. Not believing Complainant or his psychiatrist, the Agency submitted Complainant to a FFDE, which it used to deny him further use of sick leave for his disability. The Agency did not provide him with any form of reasonable accommodation after it denied him sick leave. As a result, Complainant took annual leave for one month then retired. During this time, the Agency presented Complainant with no accommodations. We find that due to the Agency's failure, Complainant was left with no other choice but to retire. Therefore, we find that Complainant's retirement amounted to a constructive discharge.

Note that when the MSPB finds that an appellant was not constructively discharged, it dismisses the case based on lack of jurisdiction. Since jurisdiction is not a determination on the merits, that decision essentially "unmixes" the case and vests merit jurisdiction in the EEOC.
