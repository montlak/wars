2. Service of Hearing Request

Separately from filing the request for a hearing with the EEOC district office, the complainant must serve a copy of that request upon the agency EEO office. 29 CFR 1614.1089(g). Service should be made simultaneously with the filing of the hearing request. The hearing request should specifically state that a copy of the request was served upon the agency, and indicate the date and method of service.

Failure to serve a hearing request on the agency means the request for a hearing is "legally deficient and rendered ineffective, such that the agency [has] jurisdiction" to render a final agency decision in the case. Gallo v. Secretary of Labor, 05A01085 (2002). However, the agency must give the complainant adequate notice that failure to serve the hearing request may result in forfeiting the right to a hearing. In Cerisano v. Postmaster General, 01200416291 (2006), the Commission explained:

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(g) provides that a "complainant shall send a copy of the request for a hearing to the agency EEO office." Furthermore, in Gallo v. Department of Labor, EEOC Request No. 05A01085 (October 9, 2002), the Commission held that failure to provide an agency with notice of a hearing request may render the request legally deficient and, therefore, ineffective in transferring jurisdiction of the complaint to the EEOC for the purpose of conducting a hearing.

However, in the case before us, we find that the agency did not provide complaint with adequate notice that he could actually forfeit his right to a hearing by neglecting to send the agency notice of his request for a hearing. Instead, as set forth above, the agency advised complainant that his failure to provide notice of his request for a hearing could result in delay, non-processing the request, or placement on an inactive docket, the latter which could then be remedied by complainant subsequently notifying the agency of his hearing request. As such, we find that the agency's notice here is fundamentally insufficient to put complainant on notice that he risked forfeiting his right to a hearing, and, in fact, would lead him to believe that he risked far less dire consequences for this omission. Moreover, the record is otherwise devoid of any evidence to show that complainant had notice that he risked forfeiting his right to a hearing by failing to provide the agency notice of his request.

Because complainant did not have adequate notice that he risked forfeiture of his right to a hearing by neglecting to provide the agency with notice of his request for a hearing, we find that the AJ improperly issued a Dismissal Order in this matter. Furthermore, lacking this notice, we find that complainant's October 7, 2003 request for a hearing at the EEOC's New York District Office effectively transferred jurisdiction of the complaint to the Commission, such that the agency had no jurisdiction to issue a final decision on December 1, 2003.

Notwithstanding this determination, we note that complainant failed to notify the agency of his request for a hearing, which has now resulted in significant administrative processing delay and cost. We acknowledge that the agency's correspondence, as described above, was too confusing to put complainant on notice that he risked forfeiture of his right to a hearing. We nonetheless find that the agency acted properly by issuing a final decision in this case. Specifically, we find that because complainant made no response whatsoever to the agency's September 13, 2003 notice, the agency could only assume that complainant desired a final decision rather than a hearing. Under these circumstances, we cannot fault the agency for taking this action.

We hereby advise complainant that the Commission's regulations and/or Management-Directive 110 (available at www.eeoc.gov), in pertinent part, mandate that a complainant must provide the agency with a copy of every document he files with the Commission, whether it be at the hearing level or on appeal. We further advise complainant, that by virtue of this decision, he has clearly been put on notice of the requirement to provide the agency with a copy of all documents he files with the Commission. Finally, we advise complainant that any further omissions in this regard could result in sanctions against his interests in this case.

The Commission remanded the case to the New York District Office for a hearing.
