PERFORMANCE APPRAISALS AND STANDARDS

Conducting a proper performance appraisal -- with all the preparation and follow-up that should go into it -- can be one of the most challenging aspects of your supervisory position. If you do it right, it can also be one of the most rewarding.

Improve performance through the appraisal process. Managers are evaluated based on the performance of their employees and on their ability to use their agency performance appraisal system to accomplish performance goals. Your own career -- and your ability to make the case for why your unit should receive the share of scarce budget dollars you think it needs -- may depend on the real performance of your employees.

It's not in your or your employees' best interest to give everyone good performance reviews -- unless, of course, they've all earned them, and your careful appraisal process confirms that they have. It might be easier in the short term to give every employee a favorable review, but it's likely to cause you headaches in the long run.

Giving everyone top ratings, without regard to whether they've been earned, doesn't improve employee performance or even morale. If every employee is "recognized" with a top rating merely for showing up, no one is recognized for genuine top performance. In agencies where incentive awards are linked to performance ratings, even the awards will not serve their intended purpose.

Generally, your employees have a pretty good idea who the top performers are -- and who they are not. In the long run, even your most highly motivated employees may be tempted to slack off if these differences in performance are not recognized in ratings and awards. Inevitably, your unit's bottom line -- and your own reputation as a manager -- will suffer.

Go beyond filling out the appraisal form. Performance appraisals should go beyond simply filling out the performance appraisal form. If you don't sit down with your employees and talk to them seriously about their performance, the appraisal process will come up short.

Even your best employees will grow frustrated if you don't give them specific feedback. And even your very best employees have room for improvement -- we all do. Even the best appraisal form won't give them the feedback they need to improve -- but talking with you probably will.

When you're dealing with problem employees or workers who are struggling, they definitely need your personal attention. If their performance warrants a low rating that could hurt their chances at pay raises or promotions, they need to hear about these problems from you. While they may have earned the poor evaluation, you need to discuss their situation with them so they have an opportunity to improve.

If you have to remove an employee for poor performance, whether you use the disciplinary system or take a performance-based action, the employee is likely to appeal. The third party reviewing the appeal or grievance will want to know that you gave the employee full information about the performance problems that led to removal and offered advice and assistance toward improvement. The annual appraisal forms will be scrutinized for just this sort of information, and you may be asked for supplementary documentation on your performance discussions. Without it, the removal may not be upheld.

Skipping sit-downs with your staff at appraisal time may make you come off as distant, even aloof. Your employees may think you don't want to take the time to talk with them about their performance. If this impression persists, it could hurt employee morale and make it more difficult for your employees to motivate themselves.

At a minimum, you should set aside enough time to discuss with your employees individually their performance during the past year and what you expect from them in the coming year. The discussions should cover areas where you saw them excel and any difficulties they may be having.

That annual meeting is a minimum and may well be a requirement of your agency performance appraisal system (some agencies also require a mid-year interim appraisal and meeting). You and your employees will be much better off if you provide them with specific performance feedback throughout the year -- positive whenever possible, negative whenever necessary. In fact, every work-related conversation you have with an employee should in some sense be a conversation about that employee's performance.

Ideally, no employee should ever be surprised by the content of the annual conversation and rating because you will have been discussing these matters throughout the year.

Performance plans

Your performance appraisal cycle is based on the performance plan you develop for each employee at the start of the annual rating cycle. You may develop the plan only after consulting with the employee (some agencies require you to solicit employee input before the annual rating is determined), and this is usually a good idea, but it is your plan, for which you are responsible.

Formally, the most important things in the plan are the performance elements, the duties and tasks on which the employee will be evaluated in the coming year, and the performance standards, the measures by which you will conduct the evaluation. Your rating at the end of the cycle will not stand up to scrutiny or serve any useful performance management purpose unless these two components are there and validly expressed.

But there are other things that may be in the plan, aspects you may develop after consulting with the employee, HR, your colleagues, and your boss. These may make your plan an even more effective performance management tool:

  • • Your employee's training needs, and how these may be met over the coming year or long term.
  • • Other employee development opportunities
  • • Specific employee performance goals.
  • • An explanation of how these goals fit into your unit's mission.
  • • Specific commitments to mutual feedback and procedures to track performance and deal with any issues that may come up.

Performance elements

Performance elements are the building blocks of which performance standards and overall performance plans are built. They are the individual tasks, functions, processes, and duties that an employee must accomplish. They are generally of two types:

  • • Critical elements. Those tasks, functions, processes or duties that are so essential to the successful performance of the position that failure to meet them would justify an overall rating of unacceptable.
  • • Noncritical elements. Tasks, functions, processes or duties that, although important, are not essential to the successful performance of the position.

Identifying performance elements. Most positions involve a variety of functions, some that are centrally important to the successful accomplishment of the work involved in the position, and others that -- while assigned to the position -- are not vital.

Only important functions should be included as elements in a performance standard. And only the most essential of them should be identified as critical elements. Here are several things to consider in determining whether a task or function is sufficiently important to include it as a performance element:

  • • Frequency. How often is the task or function performed? If the answer is "seldom," this might not be a good element.
  • • Percentage of time spent. The amount of time spent on a task or function is often a good indicator of which parts of a position are most important.
  • • Inclusion in the PD. If a task or function is included in the position description, it might make a good element; if not, probably not. If it seems necessary anyway, the PD should probably be revised to include it.
  • • Considered in classification. If the task or function was considered in setting the grade and pay for the position, it is important enough to consider as an element.
  • • Employee control. If the task or function is not under the employee's control, it isn't a good element.
  • • Impact. What good or bad things happen if the identified task or function is or is not successfully accomplished? This is the most important consideration when you determine whether an element is critical or noncritical.

Remember: Your agency may assist you by offering (or requiring) certain elements and standards for specific jobs, especially those widespread in your agency. Also, remember that your agency appraisal system may specify whether or not noncritical elements are to be used. Be sure to consult HR.

Whistleblower protection as critical element. The National Defense Authorization Act of 2018 made whistleblower protection a job requirement. Agencies are directed to develop performance criteria that add promoting whistleblower protection as a critical performance element for supervisors. Supervisory performance appraisals must cover principles for protecting whistleblowers, such as:

  • • Whether the supervisor responded constructively to whistleblower disclosures.
  • • If the supervisor took responsible actions to resolve these disclosures.
  • • Whether the supervisor fostered an environment where employees felt comfortable approaching the appropriate officials about disclosures.

In addition, performance evaluations must also address whether the agency entered into an agreement to resolve prohibited personnel practice allegations on the supervisor's behalf, and how many times these agreements were made for that supervisor.

Value of noncritical tasks. There may be aspects of performance that, while not crucial, can nevertheless add to the quality or timeliness of your agency's work. How can these nice-to-have aspects be measured and rewarded? That's where noncritical elements come into play.

Noncritical elements may include, but are not limited to, objectives, goals, program plans, work plans, and other means of expressing expected performance. Noncritical elements may be included in appraisal systems for ratings at a higher level. In other words, a person cannot be rated "unacceptable" based on a noncritical element. However, the difference between "fully successful" and "outstanding" can be how a person performed in these nice-to-have aspects.

For example, a supervisor might not want to remove experienced employees for choosing not to spend time away from production to mentor new employees, but the supervisor might really like to encourage and reward employees if they mentor others because it's a good long-term investment. This mentoring could become a noncritical element. If employees don't do it, they can still be fully successful. But if they do it and perform well, that can constitute outstanding performance.

Some work may not lend itself to noncritical tasks. There, the work is everything that needs to get done and there is nothing optional about it. However, if your work unit has positions with space for optional tasks, the noncritical element can be a way to measure and provide rewards for them. It's one more tool supervisors can use to constructively manage performance.

Performance standards

The performance ratings you issue -- and any disciplinary or performance-based action you may have to take because of unacceptable performance -- will stand or fall based on how well the performance standards are written and applied. Performance standards link performance elements to the explanation of how well they must be performed to attain a particular rating.

Your agency performance appraisal system may specify certain standards for classes of positions, which you will have to use. For example, your agency has specific elements that apply to your position as a manager, and to all managerial positions, and may also provide generic supervisory standards. Consult HR to see if generic elements or standards apply to positions you manage.

A position should have three to five standards that concentrate on the most important aspects of the job. If you have any more than that it will just make your own job -- monitoring your employee's performance in accordance with each standard -- more difficult to no real purpose.

Focusing on a few standards increases your performance plans' credibility. It makes it easier for your employees to accept that you really do intend to monitor their performance and that you can do it.

Standards usually focus on one or more of these methods to measure performance levels:

  • • Quantity. The amount of work an employee produces within a specified period of time.
  • • Quality. The degree of accuracy with which an employee performs a task or the level of errors in a completed task.
  • • Timeliness. The accomplishment of tasks or functions by certain specified time limits. For example: "Delivers financial reports within five working days after the end of the month."
  • • Manner of performance. An explanation of how a particular task, process, or function must be accomplished. For example, the specific order in which a computer file back-up process will be done.

These principles offer further guidance:

  • • Performance standards must be achievable to be valid.
  • • "Absolute standards" require perfect performance to attain a fully successful rating. This standard should be avoided and is acceptable only in very limited circumstances such as when a single failure to perform under a critical element could result in loss of life, injury, breach of national security, or great monetary loss.
  • • "Backward standards" describe the type or level of performance that will result in an unsuccessful rating. They should not be used. Performance standards should be stated in positive -- rather than negative or backward -- terms. That is, they should tell the employee what the employee has to do or to accomplish to obtain a successful rating.
  • • Performance standards should set forth a minimum level of performance in each objective and should include information about the requirements for achieving ratings at each level.
  • • Performance standards should be practical. They should not require an excessive amount of time or effort on the part of managers to determine whether they are being met.
  • • Performance standards may be amended or adjusted during the performance period.
  • • Performance standards may be supplemented through verbal instructions and feedback.
  • • Although employee participation and input are desirable in developing performance standards, the final decision is your prerogative as a manager.

Measurability and precision. Performance standards should be precise, although they may not be measurable numerically. You should include numerical measures whenever appropriate, but this isn't always possible or even desirable.

It may be more appropriate to include the manner in which a task is to be done, rather than the amount of output required. For example, the standard "Greets members of the public in a polite and professional manner," is not numerical, but it is precise. If the employee has trouble understanding the standard, explain it, and document your explanation.

Writing performance standards. To write effective performance standards:

  1. 1. Identify the key components of an employee's position. Pinpoint the most important functions, tasks, processes, or duties the employee performs -- the performance elements.
  2. 2. Identify the specific tasks that are essential to the successful performance of the position. These are usually referred to as the critical elements of the position. They are sometimes called the "what" component of performance standards.
  3. 3. Determine how well you expect the employee to perform each of the key tasks, functions, or processes that you intend to include in the overall performance plan. "How well" can be expressed in terms of quantity of work performed, quality of work output, the speed or timeliness with which it is produced, or the manner in which particular processes will be performed. The combination of "what" and "how well" constitutes a performance standard.
  4. 4. Obtain employee input in the identification of performance elements and standards.
  5. 5. Check draft performance standards to make certain that they are clear, achievable, do not require perfection unless this is both reasonable and necessary, and are stated in positive terms.
  6. 6. Communicate the final performance standards to the employee through your performance plan.
  7. 7. Review performance standards regularly to make sure they still reflect the employee's duties properly, especially in light of mission changes.
  8. 8. Change performance standards as required. This may be done within the rating period, provided the employee is only rated on the new standard after it has been properly communicated through an amended performance plan and the employee is allowed a reasonable time to adjust.

Remember: Because standards are so important, and because the legal and regulatory requirements are tricky, you should always seek the assistance of your HR office, where employee and labor relations specialists can help you write standards that are both useful in managing performance and defensible in the event of appeal. They may also be able to supply you with tested standards for positions you supervise.

Warning: Avoid making sudden changes to performance standards without thorough preparation. If you want to enforce long-ignored performance standards, or write and enforce new ones, and give accurate performance ratings in a unit where this has not been done for a long time, first consult with HR, and with your own colleagues, and boss. Be sure all your employees are properly informed of the changes in the appraisal process and given time to adjust, along with any help they need. Also, be aware of and conform to any relevant provisions of a labor agreement.

Provide continual feedback

Too often, managers and employees talk specifically about performance only once during the rating cycle, during the annual performance appraisal discussion, or twice, if a midyear interim discussion is required by the agency's system. This is a performance management mistake.

Every work-related private discussion you have with your employees should be, in some sense, a discussion about performance. Your feedback should be:

  • • Specific. What did the employee do that met, exceeded or failed to meet your expectations (which should have a close relationship to your performance plan and its standards)?
  • • Timely. Give feedback as close to the event as possible.
  • • Constructive. Both negative and positive feedback should be constructive. Focus on "how we can avoid this" or "how can we repeat this."
  • • Performance-oriented. Stick to performance and avoid any reference to the employee's worth or value.
  • • Private. No one else needs to hear your performance discussion.
  • • Ongoing. Feedback should be regular and recurring.

Your goal is to link performance discussions with the performance plan. The genuine, mission-based goals of the work unit should be built into the plan and standards so they can serve as continual points of reference.

If you provide proper feedback, you will make your annual performance discussion much easier because whatever rating you give, your employee likely will not be surprised by it.

Warning: Do not store up negative feedback to spring on an employee in a performance appraisal discussion in the hopes that continued poor performance will justify an annual rating of unacceptable so you can get rid of the employee. Not only is playing "gotcha" like this fundamentally unfair, it usually doesn't work. Employees who appeal can point to a lack of feedback and often show successful performance ratings from past years to demonstrate they were not offered a proper chance to improve.

Provide an accurate, sustainable rating. The annual employee appraisal process ends with the annual rating. If you have provided continual feedback and documented performance by keeping records and notes for each employee, determining the appropriate rating should not be difficult, and should be sustainable if the employee appeals.

Here are a few tips to assure your ratings are accurate and will be sustained if they are appealed:

  • • Consider performance at the beginning of the appraisal period as important as performance at the end of the appraisal period.
  • • Remember your goal is to give accurate ratings, not to win friends.
  • • Review performance against each standard and element, and base ratings on the result.
  • • Provide concrete illustrations of how the employee has earned the rating.
  • • Make sure you understand your agency's system going into the process. Seek advice from HR. HR won't do your work for you, but specialists will provide you with the guidance you need.

Positive and negative. One reason why managers avoid performance discussions is the assumption they must be negative. But positive reinforcement works better than negative reinforcement. Positive reinforcement is an action that stimulates people to do things because they get something of value in return. This reward then encourages them to repeat the behavior. Your performance management strategy should be based on positive reinforcement, which is far more effective in performance management than negative reinforcement -- the punishment of undesirable behavior.

As much as possible, use positive reinforcement in your performance discussions. Be specific. It is not sufficient to tell employees "You're doing a great job" and "Keep up the good work." These statements are too general. Tell them what they have been doing right, and how they have improved (even if the improvement is slight).

Remember: Positive reinforcement encourages the repetition of desirable behavior (good performance). Negative reinforcement merely stops undesirable behavior (poor performance) for a time -- when it works at all. Negative reinforcement may be necessary, but be sure you build a performance management strategy around positive reinforcement. Use monetary rewards, informal rewards, and your own system of informal recognition.

Warning: Avoid "sandwiches." Managers are often told to use praise when they must criticize in a sort of sandwich in which the real point of the discussion, criticism of poor performance, is stuck between "First of all, you should know I think you're doing a great job," and "But despite this problem, I still think you're doing a great job." This sends mixed signals, and in the end neither praise nor criticism may be effective.

Giving low ratings. Employees have been known to file grievances -- and even EEO complaints -- when they receive the lower ratings they earned, rather than the higher ratings they expected. But part of being a manager is dealing with difficult employees and the complaints they file.

You may be able to avoid complaints by issuing unearned top ratings, but when you need to take an adverse action against an employee for poor performance, you will have a hard time making it stick.

The first thing an HR specialist will do when you request an action will be to pull the poorly performing employee's most recent annual performance appraisals. If you've been giving that employee top ratings all along, it will be much harder for you to justify any adverse action. You may still be able to do it, but it will almost certainly take longer and require more effort.

Mistakes to avoid

Performance appraisals should not be dismissed as paperwork exercises to be dealt with once a year. To use your agency's performance appraisal system as an effective performance management tool, avoid the following mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Rely on negative reinforcement. Negative reinforcement may stop poor performance -- for a time. It may be warranted, but by itself it won't improve performance.
  • • Be afraid to give negative appraisals. Although these may result in grievances and EEO complaints, they can be defended with proper documentation.
  • • Make sudden changes without thorough preparation. If you intend to provide accurate performance ratings in a unit where this has not been done in a long time, consult with HR, your colleagues, and your boss. Properly inform your employees and give them time to adjust, along with any help they need. Respect any relevant labor agreement provisions.
  • • Offer your feedback in "sandwiches." Supervisors are often told to use praise when they must be critical in a sort of sandwich in which the real point of the discussion, criticism of poor performance, is stuck in between. This sends mixed signals, and in the end neither praise nor disapproval may be effective.
  • • Give ratings distorted by unusual events or the employee's most recent performance. Rate the employee's typical performance throughout the year.
  • • Play "gotcha" at the performance appraisal meeting. Not only is it fundamentally unfair, it usually doesn't work.
  • • Use vague or impractical standards. The point of standards is to pin down what an employee is expected to accomplish and how well. If the standard doesn't say that, or if you cannot practically determine it, it has no real value.
  • • Use backward standards. If your standard portrays performance you don't want to see, chances are it's a backward standard, and not permissible.
  • • Use too many elements or standards. There are no legal or regulatory limits, although your agency may have guidance. It is usually best to limit the total number of standards to no more than five.

Ask FMG

Q. Why should I consider employee input in performance plans?

A. Employees may know their jobs better than anyone else, and they are more likely to take plans seriously if they had a voice in creating them. However, performance plans and ratings remain a management responsibility.

Q. Do I need to have continual performance discussions, even when nothing is wrong?

A. Yes. In fact, every discussion you have with an employee about work should be a performance discussion. This is the way you provide continued feedback to reinforce your performance standards. It's the best way to keep employees motivated to improve. Even your best, most motivated employees appreciate and need your recognition of their performance, which you provide by talking about it.

Q. Can I change a standard before the rating period is over?

A. Yes. However, you must communicate the change to the employee and must allow a reasonable amount of time for the employee to meet the changed standard before rating on it.

Q. Do I need to develop performance standards for different rating levels?

A. It depends on your agency's policy. If it has a two-level (i.e., pass/fail) rating system, only standards that specify an acceptable level of performance are needed. In other systems, agency regulations may require standards at several levels of performance.

Q. Does a performance standard have to be tied to a specific duty reflected in the employee's position description?

A. No, nothing requires a direct link to an item in the PD. However, in general the performance elements on which standards are based reflect the duties and functions in the PD. If you have and need performance elements -- and standards based on them -- that are not in the PD, you may want to check with HR about rewriting the PD.

Q. Can I address attendance in a performance standard?

A. It's not a good idea. Attendance is a conduct issue, not a performance matter, and therefore should not be addressed in a performance standard. Workers who don't show up when they are supposed to are candidates for discipline, not a poor rating.
