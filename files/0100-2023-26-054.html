6. Actions by Non-Employers

In determining whether a complainant is aggrieved for purposes of stating a reprisal claim, the Commission is not confined to considering only actions taken by the employing agency. Extending the same rationale that has led it to conclude that a former employer can be held liable for retaliation when it interferes with a complainant's right to current employment, in Mares v. Secretary of Army, 01960499 (1998), the Commission held that an allegation that an agency made disparaging remarks to the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs in retaliation for filing an EEO complaint stated a claim for relief.

In Mares, two of the complainant's supervisors specifically commented on past EEO complaints in responding to an OWCP claim. The Commission in Mares held:

The agency has erred in dismissing appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim. The Commission has held that an employee of a federal agency who is negatively affected by an action taken by another agency with regard to a term, condition or privilege of his or her employment has standing to file a complaint of discrimination against the acting agency. In such a case, appellant is required to file his complaint against the particular agency which has taken the discriminatory employment action by which he is allegedly harmed. See 29 CFR § 1614.106(a); Buchhagen v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05940948 (June 3, 1996); Colantuoni v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Request No. 05950136 (September 14, 1995); and Pion v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Request No. 05880891 (October 18, 1988). Moreover, the Commission has held that former employees who can still be affected by the outcome of a complaint may be considered "aggrieved" within the meaning of Commission regulations. See Sternberg v. Department of Defense (Dependents Schools), EEOC Request No. 05890976 (January 8, 1990).

Thus, appellant, as a former employee of the SARPMA, a organization under Army control, has standing to file an EEO complaint because he is "aggrieved" with regard to the actions of two of his former supervisors, who were and remain employees of the Department of the Army. The Commission is unpersuaded by the agency's argument that the two supervisors were not acting on behalf of the Army. The record is clear that the statements were requested from these individuals in their capacity as appellant's former supervisors, not simply as individuals who were acquainted with him. The Department of the Army is the only agency with control over these two supervisors and the only entity in a position to provide appellant an appropriate remedy for the unlawful actions of two of its employees.

It is worth noting that, under Mares, it would appear that a claim lies against both the employing agency and the agency that ultimately takes action on the claim. To avoid duplication, or even conflicting results, the complainant should request consolidation and the agencies should cooperate in a joint investigation of the complaint.

A negative job reference, when motivated by retaliation for protected activity, is a sufficient personal injury to sustain a claim of reprisal whether or not the negative reference resulted in nonselection by a prospective employer. For example, in Akinyemi v. Secretary of Air Force, 07A10070 (2003), the Commission rejected the agency's argument that the negative reference was not the cause of the complainant's failure to be hired by the Department of Veterans Affairs. "Complainant need not establish a causal connection between the negative reference and nonselection at the VA, because the dissemination of the negative job reference is an actionable employment decision in and of itself." Id.
