4. Summary Judgment

One thing that an inadequate investigation record precludes is summary judgment. Not only is it important for the agency to develop an adequate investigative record, but it is equally important that the investigator document the reasons for any inadequacies in the record. In Graham v. Secretary of Energy, 01A46187 (2006), the Commission found that the administrative judge erred in granting summary judgment to the agency where the investigative record did not contain an affidavit from the complainant and the record was not entirely clear as to whether that failure was due to the complainant's medical condition or recalcitrance:

In this case, the record reflects that the investigator failed to obtain an affidavit from complainant during the investigative stage of the complaint. The investigator's notes to the file indicate that she tried to obtain a statement initially but was not successful because complainant was undergoing eye surgery. There does not appear to be any dispute that complainant is blind in his right eye and that he was reassigned to his present position for medical reasons. The investigator postponed the investigation for three weeks but still could not obtain complainant's statement because he asserted he needed to get new reading glasses in order to read any statement she took. The investigator documented that there were various delays related to complainant's attempts to get new eyeglasses, but in the end, the investigation does not contain a statement from complainant.

The record also contains a statement from complainant to the effect that because of a "breakdown in communications between [him] the investigator and his representative, [complainant] felt the need to request a hearing." This statement was directed to the agency's EEO complaints manager.

The AJ did not address the absence of a statement from the complainant during the investigative stage or whether, in light of this circumstance, the record was sufficiently developed to allow for summary disposition. This was error. Although the investigator's notes are probative of efforts to reach complainant, they are not dispositive and should have been addressed on an independent basis. Specifically, the AJ was required, at a minimum, to explore the reasons for the absence of complainant's statement, whether it was due to his recalcitrance or his medical inability, or some other such reason. Without any definitive explanation for the omission of complainant's statement, which may or may not place factual issues in dispute, it was error to conclude that summary judgment was appropriate.
