A. REQUIREMENTS OF FEE PETITION

An agency is specifically required to inform the complainant of the evidentiary requirements for fee petitions, as the Commission noted in Clayborne v. Secretary of Air Force, 01890269 (1989):

The agency's argument is incorrect; pursuant to Management Directive (MD)-105, the Commission does impose such a duty on the agency. MD-105 read in pertinent part:

The notice to the complainant and his or her attorney that attorney's fees will be awarded shall include notification that the attorney should submit the following information.

1. A statement of the number of hours spent in the preparation and presentation of the case. This must specifically include the dates and a statement of exactly what was done. It must also describe the training and experience of each individual who worked on the case and the number of hours spent by each individual.

2. A sworn statement of the attorney's usual and customary hourly charge, as well as the usual fee for each person who worked on the case.

3. A sworn statement explaining whether the fee for the case was fixed or contingent, whether the handling of the case precluded other employment, the nature and length of the professional relationship with this client, and any other factors which might affect the amount of the award.

4. Sworn statements by other attorneys in the relevant geographic area, who work in the field of employment discrimination, setting forth the customary fee for such work, the desirability of the case, awards in similar cases, and the reputation, ability and experience of the attorney requesting the fee....

If the agency fails to include this notification in its final decision, the Commission will consider any information submitted in support of the application on appeal. Presumably, the notification requirements also apply to an administrative judge.

The Commission has restated the requirements for a fee petition in MD-110 at 11-17:

A statement of attorney's fees and costs must be accompanied by an affidavit executed by the attorney of record itemizing the attorney's charges for legal services. A verified statement of fees and costs shall include the following:

a. a list of services rendered itemized by date, number of hours, detailed summary of the task, rate, and attorney's name;

b. documentary evidence of reasonableness of hours, such as contemporaneous time records, billing records, or a reasonably accurate substantial reconstruction of time records;

c. documentary evidence of reasonableness of rate, such as an affidavit stating that the requested rate is the attorney's normal billing rate, a detailed affidavit of another attorney in the community familiar with prevailing community rates for attorneys of comparable experience and expertise, a resume, a list of cases handled, or a list of comparable cases where a similar rate was accepted; and

d. documentation of costs.

National Ass'n of Concerned Veterans v. Secretary of Defense, 675 F.2d 1319 (D.C. Cir. 1982). A fee award may be reduced for failure to provide adequate documentation. If seeking an adjustment to the lodestar figure, the fee application shall clearly identify the specific circumstances of the case that support the requested adjustment. Id.

The evidence in support of the hourly rate must be submitted at the time the fee petition is presented to the agency and not on appeal. In Totten v. Secretary of Interior, 01A21880 (2003), the Commission upheld the agency's determination that $170 rather than $185 was the proper hourly rate where the attorney did not submit evidence of her 14 years of experience until the case was on appeal with the EEOC.

When an agency contests a fee request, its submission "must provide equally detailed documentation in support of its arguments." MD-110 at 11-18.

Failure of the EEOC to include its pro forma notice of eligibility for attorney fees does not amount to a denial of fees. Haddock v. Department of Air Force, 01830822 (1983).

Preparation of the fee application is an important aspect of ultimately obtaining the award. The Commission's regulations and guidance on the subject should be followed meticulously. Failure to follow the proper procedure is sure to result in a substantial delay in finally obtaining the fee and may result in a denial of all or part of the fee award. The requirements are not difficult. Some lawyers assume, however, that submitting a fee application is like submitting a bill to a client; all that is really necessary is an accounting of the hours expended, the fee charged and the costs incurred. Somewhat more is required. For the practitioner who does not routinely handle EEO cases, the requirements may be viewed as burdensome. For those who practice regularly in the area, the requirements should pose few problems.

When affidavits from other practitioners in the area are provided, they must do more than state a general "belief and affirmation" of the prevailing community rate. The affidavits should relate the hourly rate and fees actually charged by those lawyers. It is also insufficient to provide information on the prevailing rate for "federal and state administrative proceedings." The rate evidence submitted should be on the prevailing community rate for EEO administrative work. See Clayborne v. Secretary of Air Force, 01890269 (1989); Ewers v. Department of Air Force, 01842010 (1986). When no evidence on the prevailing community rate is available, the Commission may examine alternative evidence, such as past administrative and court fee awards.

The importance of keeping daily timesheets in EEO cases should be obvious from the above. Whatever counsel's billing practices with respect to other cases, there is no substitute here for being able to demonstrate the actual time spent, when it was spent, and for what activities it was used. For anyone who keeps adequate timesheets, it will not be necessary to prepare a separate statement on the hours spent. It will be sufficient if copies of those timesheets are attached to the fee affidavit.

With the use of computers in the legal community, preparation of a fee affidavit should not require extensive time. It is a good practice to keep a copy of a standard fee affidavit in the computer system, which can then be amended and edited to suit the particular case.
