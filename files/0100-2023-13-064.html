XII. CONCLUSIONS OF INVESTIGATORS

The Commission's regulations do not explicitly require or prohibit an investigator from including conclusions in the complaint file. However, it would appear that the former 29 CFR 1613.216 envisioned the investigation to be a factfinding exercise, the results of which were to aid the appropriate agency personnel in rendering a decision. The current regulations, at 29 CFR 1614.108(b), merely provide that an appropriate record will allow "a reasonable finder of fact to draw conclusions as to whether discrimination occurred."

MD-110, at 6-22, specifically provides that the report of investigation will include a summary that states the issues and delineates the evidence on both sides of each issue: "The summary should state facts (supported in the complaint file) sufficient to sustain a conclusion(s). The 1992 version of MD-110 added that the summary "should not state the conclusion(s)." This provision was deleted from the 1999 version of MD-110.

Through its case law, the Commission has indicated that an investigative summary should not state a conclusion because to do so goes against the requirement that the investigator be unbiased and objective. In Goodwine v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01991479 (2000), the agency submitted an investigative summary and analysis ("ISA") from an earlier complaint as evidence that no discrimination occurred in the instant complaint. Aside from the fact that the Commission found the earlier report not relevant to the present case, it also noted that the ISA "improperly rendered a conclusion that complainant...had failed to demonstrate that the agency had discriminated against him." Id. The Commission concluded:

We further find the ISA's recommendation of "a finding of no discrimination" contrary to the need for EEO personnel, including investigators, to be neutral and impartial. See, e.g., the Commission's revised EEO Management Directive (EEO-MD-110) for 29 CFR Part 1614 (November 9, 1999), Ch. 6, § VI (C) ("Investigator Must Be Unbiased and Objective").

(Footnote omitted).

An agency is free to simply adopt as its final decision the recommended findings of fact and conclusions of law issued by the administrative judge. However, the Commission's regulations do not permit an agency to adopt the factual findings and conclusions of an EEO investigator as its final decision. As the Commission explained in Jefferson v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 01944529 (1996):

EEOC regulation 29 CFR § 1614.110 further provides that the "final [agency] decision shall consist of findings by the agency on the merits of each issue in the complaint...." In cases where there has been a hearing before an EEOC Administrative Judge (AJ), EEOC regulations provide that the agency may adopt the findings and conclusions of the AJ. See 29 CFR § 1614.109 (g). There is no provision in the regulations, however, that allows the agency merely to adopt the investigator's summary as its FAD. Unlike the AJ, whose role is to issue findings of fact and conclusions of law on the merits of the complaint via a recommended decision, the investigator's responsibility is to prepare an investigative summary that states facts sufficient to sustain a conclusion but does not actually state a conclusion. See EEO Management Directive-110, chapters 5 and 6. Here, rather than state its findings and conclusions, the agency simply agreed with the investigator, by reference to the investigative summary, that it articulated legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for its actions which appellant failed to prove were pretextual. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the agency failed to meet its duty under EEOC regulations to render a sufficient final decision on the merits of appellant's allegations.

See also Dorsey v. Secretary of Air Force, 01941767 (1994) (agency abdicated its duty to make its own finding based on the record when it adopted the findings and conclusions of the investigator).

In Bearce v. Secretary of Army, 01881355 (1988), the Commission remanded a complaint for failure to conduct an adequate investigation. Because the investigator previously assigned to the complaint had concluded that the allegations were without merit, the Commission ordered the agency to assign a new investigator upon remand.
