28. Service on Attorney Representative

When the complainant is represented by an attorney, the Commission's service regulations require that all notices be served on the attorney. See 29 CFR 1614.605(d). Time frames are computed from receipt by the attorney, not receipt by the complainant. Id. It is not sufficient to address those notices to the complainant in care of his attorney. In Porter v. Postmaster General, 01952979 (1996), the Commission held that an EEO complaint would be deemed timely, even though not filed within 15 days of receipt of the notice of final interview, where the notice had not been specifically addressed to the attorney. See also Carroll v. Secretary of Army, 01964894 (1997) (dismissal for failure to timely file was improper where agency failed to show if and when the complainant's attorney received the notice of right to file a complaint). Similarly, when the notice of interview is sent to the right address but the wrong attorney, the time period does not begin to run until actual receipt by the attorney of record. See Townsell v. Postmaster General, 01A22218 (2003).

When an agency dismisses a complaint on the basis of untimely filing it has the burden of establishing the facts necessary to demonstrate that decision is valid. Failure to produce evidence that the notice of final interview was served on the complainant's attorney will result in a reversal of the decision to dismiss for untimely filing of the formal complaint. Jackson-Bostic v. Postmaster General, 01A13948 (2002).

If the agency establishes that the notice of final interview was served on the complainant's attorney, the burden then shifts to the complainant to demonstrate good cause for any untimely filing of the formal complaint. For example, in Garcia v. Postmaster General, 01A55791 (2006), the complainant established good cause when the agency demonstrated that it served the notice on her attorney with a private mail box service. The complainant's attorney did not, however, receive the notice until after expiration of the 15--day period for filing the formal complaint. The attorney submitted an affidavit, under oath, stating that he did not receive the notice until after expiration of the filing period due to an error made by the private mail service. In addition, the manager of the mail service submitted a letter to the effect that the notice was misdelivered to another patron and was not received by the complainant's attorney until after the 15--day period expired. Based on that evidence, the Commission found good cause for waiving the filing period where the formal complaint was filed three days after receipt of notice by the complainant's attorney.

When the complainant chooses not to have her attorney present for the final interview, this does not relieve the agency of its obligation to deliver the notice of final interview to the attorney. In Barron v. Secretary of Air Force, 01954987 (1996), the agency gave the complainant written notice of her complaint rights at the final interview. It then rejected her formal complaint when it was filed 22 days later. On appeal, the Commission reversed, finding that the time period for filing a formal complaint only runs from receipt of the notice by the attorney. In Barron, the time period had never started to run.

An agency cannot assume that an attorney who represents a complainant in a grievance also represents him in his EEO matters. Maimone v. Secretary of Navy, 05960037 (1996). If the complainant has not specifically designated an attorney representative for her EEO case, service of notices on an attorney representing her in another matter does not begin the running of any limitations period.

If the agency's EEO office is notified that the complainant is represented after it has issued the notice of right to file a complaint, it may be required to reissue the notice directly to the complainant's attorney. In Randall v. Postmaster General, 05960822 (1997), the Commission waived the time period to file a complaint where the complainant's representative sent notice that he was representing the complainant to the complainant's plant manager, and the letter was forwarded to the EEO office within days after it issued notice of right to file an EEO complaint. As the Commission in Randall explained:

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.605(d) provides that when a complainant designates an attorney as representative, service of documents and decisions on the complainant shall be made on the attorney, and time frames for receipt of materials by the complainant shall be computed from the time of receipt by the attorney.

Here, appellant's attorney notified the Plant Manager where appellant worked that he was appellant's representative by letter dated June 15, 1995, which was received the next day. The letter was forwarded to the EEO office on June 19, 1995, and probably received a day or two later. It would have been appropriate for the notice to be sent directly to the EEO office. However, given that the EEO office likely received the forwarded representation letter within days after issuing the notice of right to file an EEO complaint to appellant, and it knew the letter was directed to the agency prior to the notice, the EEO office should have reissued the notice to appellant's attorney. 29 CFR § 1614.605(d). Given these circumstances, appellant's one day of untimeliness is waived, and the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint for failure to timely file the complaint is reversed.

In a similar vein, in Tarlow v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120113673 (2011), the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to file within the 15-day period where the complainant notified the agency that she was represented by counsel the day after it issued the notice of final interview. The complainant had orally notified the EEO counselor that she had retained counsel, but the counselor said she had to issue the notice to be within the 30-day period for concluding counseling. The attorney sent an email the agency that day notifying it that he had been retained by the complainant. He followed that up with an email a few weeks later asking if the notice had been issued. The Commission, noting that the complainant also had a medical condition, found there was sufficient grounds for extending the filing period.

Although the regulations provide that time limits begin to run upon receipt by the complainant's attorney, the regulations also provide for service upon the complainant. Where the agency serves the attorney, but not the complainant, the time limit for filing the formal complaint does not begin to run. As the Commission explained in Singh v. Postmaster General, 01A12091 (2001):

In its dismissal, the agency found that complainant failed to timely file her formal complaint, noting that complainant's attorney received notice of the right to file on October 30, 2000, but complainant failed to file the complaint until November 20, 2000. On appeal, complainant asserts that she did not receive a copy of the notice, and only learned of the attorney's receipt by directly asking the agency for the status of her complaint. Complainant contends that she then requested a copy of the notice, and fired her attorney in order to file her complaint.

Complainants must file their formal complaints within fifteen (15) days of receiving the right to do so. See 29 CFR 1614.106. The regulations require the dismissal of claims that fail to comply with this time limit. See 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(2). Where complainant is represented by an attorney, time frames for receipt of materials shall be computed from the attorney's receipt. See 29 CFR 1614.605(d). However, the agency must serve correspondence on both complainant and her attorney. See id.

Ordinarily, the Commission holds a complainant responsible for her attorney's failure to meet applicable time limits. However, the Commission's insistence on adhering to the time limit is based on the complainant's notice of the agency's correspondence. In the current matter, the agency never sent a copy of the correspondence to complainant. Where complainant has no notice of the correspondence, she should not be held responsible for her attorney's failure to abide by the time limitation.

A similar result was reached in Dark v. Postmaster General, 01A12357 (2001), where the agency did not serve the notice of final interview on the complainant and the complainant's attorney did not timely file a formal complaint because he had a stroke. "Although time frames are calculated from the attorney's receipt, it would be inequitable to require complainant to respond to a document she did not receive." Id.

Lawyers' offices are not usually open on the weekend. Thus, in Estwick v. Postmaster General, 0120072758 (2007), the agency erred in counting the 15-day period for filing the formal complaint where the notice of final interview was received in her attorney's office on a Saturday. The notice should not have been deemed as received until the following Monday when the attorney's office reopened.
