2. FOIA and Privacy Act Requirements

The Freedom of Information Act, 5 USC 552, requires the federal government to make available to the public, upon request, many records and documents subject to certain exemptions. The Privacy Act, 5 USC 552a, imposes certain requirements on federal agencies with respect to the collection, use, maintenance, and dissemination of information concerning individuals, kept within a system of records. Agencies are required to make complainants aware of the proper Privacy Act requirements, but the Privacy Act does not impede an agency's ability to use information collected in an EEO investigation in further processing the complaint. Privacy Act considerations can be further complicated by virtue of the fact that government employees often work with records that contain information on private citizens. When their specific work tasks become the focus of an EEO investigation, precautions must be taken to avoid unwarranted disclosure of information about those citizens.

In most cases where complainants seek records that are related to their complaint outside of formal discovery during the hearing stage, the request is properly made under the Privacy Act. However, many people, and occasionally the Commission, use FOIA and the Privacy Act interchangeably.

In Sofio v. Secretary of Treasury, 05880633 (1988), the Commission addressed all of these issues and explained the relationship between the Privacy Act and the use of information collected in the EEO process:

We find that both the agency and the appellant have misinterpreted the Privacy Act of 1974, and the regulations of the Commission. Under the regulations of this Commission, a complainant has no right to confidentiality in regard to his or her complaint once a written complaint is accepted for processing by the agency. 29 CFR 1613.213, supra. The agency, when asking a complainant to supply information, is under a duty imposed by the Privacy Act, 5 USC 552a(e)(3), which states that each agency that maintains a system of records shall:

inform each individual whom it asks to supply information, on the form which it uses to collect the information or on a separate form that can be retained by the individual--

(A) the authority (whether granted by statute, or by executive order of the President) which authorizes the solicitation of the information and whether disclosure of such information is mandatory or voluntary;

(B) the principal purpose or purposes for which the information is intended to be used;

(C) the routine uses which may be made of the information, as published pursuant to paragraph (4)(D) of this subsection; and

(D) the effects on him, if any, of not providing all or any part of the requested information.

5 USC 552a(b) reads in relevant part:

Conditions of disclosure.--No agency shall disclose any record which is contained in a system of records by any means of communication to any person, or to another agency, except pursuant to a written request by, or with the prior written consent of, the individual to whom the record pertains, unless disclosure of the record would be--

(1) to those officers and employees of the agency which maintains the record who have a need to know for the record in the performance of their duties;

(2) required under 552 of this title (Freedom of Information Act);

(3) for a routine use as defined in subsection (a)(7) of this section and described under subsection (e)(4)(D) of this section;

...

(7) to another agency or to an instrumentality of any governmental jurisdiction within or under the control of the United States for a civil or criminal law enforcement activity if the activity is authorized by law, and if the head of the agency or instrumentality has made a written request to the agency which maintains the record specifying the particular portion desired and the law enforcement activity for which the record is sought.

[The a]gency form...is worded so as to perform the function mandated by 5 USC 552a(e)(3), to inform the appellant of the facts as required by that section. Nothing under 5 USC 552a(e)(3) requires that a complainant, or anyone else similarly situated, sign an acknowledgement of receiving such information. Even if such a requirement existed, it was satisfied by appellant's letter of July 16, 1987, wherein she informed the agency that she had read and understood the paragraphs which were required under 552a(e)(3). We note that the agency cannot argue that it sought the appellant's signature on [the] form...as a clarification of her ambiguous alteration of the statement on her affidavit, as she was asked to sign the form before her affidavit was taken. The appellant's confusion concerning [the] agency form...arose because of the agency's insistence on appellant's signing what it referred to as a "disclosure authorization." The appellant was correct when she asserted that such authorization was unnecessary. The agency is authorized to use the appellant's employment file and any information gathered through the EEO investigation for the purpose of investigating and settling the complaint, as such a use falls under the exceptions to the Privacy Act. See 5 USC 552a(b)(1), (3) and (7), supra. We find, therefore, that the appellant's failure to sign the agency's Privacy Act Notice provided no basis for canceling her complaint.

While we find that the agency's request to appellant that she sign a disclosure authorization to have been unnecessary, we also note that the agency's request carried none of the dangers the appellant apparently feared. The signed form would have given the agency no more disclosure authority than it already had. The agency was simply, and unnecessarily, asking the appellant to authorize disclosure authority already granted by the exceptions to the Privacy Act noted above. The agency could only disclose facts gathered in the investigation to necessary agency personnel, the MSPB, and this Commission. The agency is constrained, with or without the signed acknowledgement sought, from revealing the appellant's employment file or facts from the investigation to the general public, which is what the appellant apparently feared. In the interest of thoroughness and to avoid further controversy, we will briefly address the appellant's arguments concerning disclosure.

The appellant's second and fourth arguments, that the information sought by the agency is protected by the Privacy Act and IRS taxpayer confidentiality, is misguided. The appellant's employment record and any information gathered by the EEO investigator is privileged under the Privacy Act, except for uses which fall under the exceptions defined in 5 USC 552a as discussed above. The exceptions cover the agency's use of such information in processing her EEO complaint. Her protection under the Act against unauthorized disclosure, to the general public for example, continues. The information sought for processing of her EEO complaint, however, is not protected by the Privacy Act from being used for that purpose.

The appellant is wrong in attempting to assert that her release of information encompasses the release of any taxpayer's confidential information. The appellant has no authority to assert that confidentiality or to release it. The information sought by the investigation, and in which the appellant had a privacy interest, concerned the appellant's record, which is her employment record and any information gathered in the investigation itself. See 5 USC 552a(b). To the extent that a complete investigation of her complaint might have entailed an investigation and discussion of her handling of a particular taxpayer's case, those records could have easily been sanitized by the agency (names, addresses, and any other identification removed) such that there would be no breach of taxpayer confidentiality.

In most cases, any Privacy Act considerations will not be nearly as complex.

An agency may not be required to disclose otherwise confidential records in the course of an EEO investigation, but it may be required to extract relevant information from those records and provide it to the investigator. In Simmons v. Postmaster General, 05910643 (1991), the employee alleged that his supervisor referred him to an Employee Assistance Program because of his race and religion, and in reprisal for EEO activity. The Commission remanded the case with instructions to the agency to include in its investigation comparative information about other employees that the supervisor referred to EAP. On reconsideration, the Commission noted the agency's valid interest in preserving the confidentiality of actual EAP records. However, it found the agency could review the records and provide the comparative information in affidavit form without breaching confidentiality.

When an employee believes that an agency violated his rights under the Privacy Act, the proper remedy is provided under the Act. Privacy Act violations cannot be cured through the EEO process. See, e.g., Bucci v. Secretary of Education, 01883733 (1988). The Privacy Act provides the "exclusive statutory framework governing the disclosure of identifiable information contained in federal systems of records and rests in the United States District Courts exclusive jurisdiction in matters brought under the provisions of the Privacy Act." McGraw v. Postmaster General, 01923062 (1993) (agency properly dismissed allegations that it disclosed medical information on the complainant to three creditors without the complainant's permission in retaliation for filing an EEO complaint). It is worth noting, however, that the complainant in McGraw may have had a claim under the ADA for disclosure of confidential medical records. See Chapter 14, subheading "Medical Disclosures and Confidentiality of Medical Information."

In Kirk v. Administrator, Environmental Protection Agency, 05930122 (1993), the Commission held that an allegation that a supervisor has discriminatorily failed to provide an employee with a copy of his "private" notes does not fall within the ambit of Title VII:

Appellant asserted that the supervisor's failure to provide her with a copy of his personal notes involved the terms and conditions of her employment. However, there is no indication in the record that the notes maintained by the supervisor constituted "personnel" documents which should have been placed in appellant's Official Personnel File (or made available to appellant). Moreover, appellant has not demonstrated how she was harmed by either the keeping of personal notes by the supervisor or by the agency's failure to disclose the personal notes maintained by the supervisor.

The parties in the present matter disagree over the applicability of the Commission's decision in Bucci. The appellant in Bucci requested through a FOIA request a copy of an EEO complaint file in which he had been named as the responsible management official. The agency refused to provide the appellant with the entire case file. The agency maintained that the appellant was only entitled to copies of documents which had been previously sent to him. The Commission in Bucci determined that the appellant had been provided with all the information he was entitled to receive. The question of divulgence of EEO records is clearly within the Commission's authority and appellant, unlike the appellant in Bucci, has not demonstrated that the personal notes she was requesting were EEO records to which she was entitled under Commission guidelines. See Management Directive 110 (MD 110) Chapter 5, VIII, D-E., p. 5-20 (October 22, 1992).

The Commission further distinguishes the present matter from its decision in Fred M. Rose v. Anthony M. Frank, Postmaster General, United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01910937 (June 4, 1991). In Rose the appellant directly challenged the Postal Service's processing of his FOIA request (the agency did not respond to his request within the regulatory ten (10) days). The Postal Service rejected the appellant's complaint asserting that the appellant did not demonstrate how he was harmed. The Commission found that appellant was aggrieved as to the actual processing of his FOIA request (i.e., the delay) and that the Postal Service's reasoning for rejecting the appellant's complaint went to the merits. The Postal Service request for reopening was denied. Fred M. Rose v. Anthony M. Frank, Postmaster General, United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910670 (August 29, 1991). In this case, however, appellant is not challenging the processing of her FOIA request, but rather attacking the result therefrom. As we pointed out in Bucci, the more appropriate forum for challenging an agency's denial of requested information under FOIA is through "FOIA's own established procedures for violation of the Act." Bucci, EEOC Appeal No. 01892837, at p. 3.
