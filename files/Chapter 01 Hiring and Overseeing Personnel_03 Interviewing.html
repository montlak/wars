INTERVIEWING

Recruiting happens on the front end. Hiring is the final result. Sitting between them is the all-important candidate interview. This part of the process is designed to identify the strongest candidates. It is critical for you to become skilled at the various facets of interviewing. The future of your agency and success of your work unit depend on it.

There is a ton of information about the technical and legal aspects of the hiring process. Although you should be familiar with these areas, your agency HR specialists are the experts as well as your most valuable hiring resource. However, the key to successful hiring boils down to your preparation, management of the interview process, and your skill as an interviewer.

When it comes to filling a vacant position, the face-to-face interview, either in-person or via video, remains the most widely accepted means of evaluating job candidates. You can't just show up at an interview and wing it, in the hopes that the ideal candidate will walk through your door. There is a great deal of prep work involved, as well as -- you guessed it -- rules to follow when selecting and interviewing potential candidates.

Get started

Get off to a good start by doing the following things now:

  • • Schedule strategic hiring meetings with HR at the very beginning of the hiring process so the HR specialist knows exactly what sort of person you're looking for. The HR specialist is your ally in this process, so it pays to expend some extra effort to cultivate that relationship.
  • • Educate the HR specialist on what people in your unit actually do, so the specialist has a feel for the functions to be performed.
  • • Analyze positions under your supervision, and determine the competencies, skills, educational background, behavioral skills, and personality characteristics needed for each (such as writing, verbal communication, multitasking, analytical, etc.).
  • • Examine your agency's hiring process, and ask which steps are needed to comply with the law and which ones can be eliminated or abbreviated to expedite the process.
  • • Be open to changing hiring practices and procedures. Processes evolve and laws change, so it helps to be flexible.

Your HR office has a complete understanding of available hiring flexibilities. In any given situation, the HR specialist should be able to offer you three or four ways to fill a position, instead of just one, to ensure you get the type of candidate needed. This may affect the interviewing process, so consider all options before moving forward.

Warning: When interviewers believe they need to make a decision quickly, they tend to make decisions based on a limited sample of information or on a small number of candidate interviews. Adhere to the established interview procedure and timeline with each candidate to avoid making bad decisions.

Preliminary work

Some managers would like the hiring process to go this way: Candidates are selected by HR, interviews are scheduled, you show up, interview the candidates and select one, game over. The problem in this scenario is that your role is not comprehensive enough. Without involvement in the preliminary work, you have no input on many of the critical elements that go into the interview process. To make sure you see the best candidates, you need to invest time doing the legwork prior to the interview. Your ability to identify the best candidates through the interview process is a skill you should develop.

Review and analyze the position. Review the position description and qualification requirements of the position for which you are conducting interviews. Also, you should:

  • • Familiarize yourself with the critical elements of the position.
  • • Identify the specific knowledge, skills, and abilities that the candidate should possess in order to succeed in the position.
  • • Seek feedback from your colleagues and subject matter experts about the qualities they view as being critical for the position.

Review the application package. The next step is to review the candidate's application. Prior to your receiving the applications, human resources has already determined which candidates meet the minimum qualification requirements. It now becomes your job to determine which applicants have the maximum potential to meet your needs. As you review each resume or application, ask yourself:

  • • What are the strengths and weaknesses of this candidate?
  • • What are the candidate's relevant skills and experience?
  • • How do their qualifications fit with the necessary skills of the position?

Scoring guide. Along with HR, subject matter experts can also help you develop a scoring guide to assess candidate responses. Make sure to document and evaluate each candidate against the scoring guide. This will help you measure candidates and their responses so that you can identify your top candidates.

The basics of question construction

Because the questions drive the interview, it is essential that you construct them carefully to reveal the best candidate. Each question you ask during the course of the interview must be job-related. You are trying to elicit information from the candidate that reveals whether or not she is able to do the job, is willing to do the job, and if she will make a good match for your agency. The questions you ask will draw this information out and paint the picture of who is the best candidate.

The following are sample questions that will help you to draw out valuable information. Modify them as they apply to the particular requirements of the position, but keep them open-ended for maximum information.

  • • Attention to detail. Describe a project or task that required your exacting attention to small points and issues in order to keep the project on track and turn out a quality product. How did these matters come to your attention? How did you handle them?
  • • Clerical skills. Describe the type of routine office procedures that you have had to follow. What volume of paperwork have you been responsible for? What experience have you had in handling confidential records? How do you ensure that confidentiality/security of information is maintained in the office?
  • • Position description. Looking at the position description, are there any tasks, skills, or duties that cause you concern? Why? How would you adapt?
  • • Interpersonal skills. Tell us about a time when you disagreed with the actions or decisions of your supervisor. How did you approach the situation? Was the situation resolved to your satisfaction or did nothing change?
  • • Teamwork. Provide an example of when you worked effectively as a member of a team. What was the task? How many people were involved? What was your role? Would you rather lead a team or be a member of a team, and why?
  • • Avoid prohibited questions. In general, avoid questions that are not linked to one of your specific objectives. In other words, stay away from questions that are not geared to bringing out legitimate information regarding a candidate's knowledge, skills, abilities, or relevant experiences. Violating EEO law and the rights of protected groups can land you in trouble.

High on the list of questions to avoid, therefore, are those that could be viewed as eliciting information that could be used in an improper, discriminatory manner. A question as simple as, "What kinds of organizations do you belong to?" has the potential to violate the rights of a protected group because the candidate's answer may lead to topics involving religious or ethnic background.

Pilot test the interview questions. Prior to using your interview questions in an actual interview, give the questions to colleagues and position subject matter experts for a trial run. This pilot test will ensure questions are clearly worded and draw an appropriate range of responses. The test run will indicate if and where revisions need to be made.

After you receive the feedback and tweak the questions, provide the questions to your HR office for a final review and stamp of approval. Getting HR's endorsement will ensure that the questions are proper and none are discriminatory under EEO law.

Situational and behavioral questions

Interviewing techniques come in a variety of flavors. Your experience and HR's input will determine what works in an interview and what doesn't. But two types of questions seem to consistently get at the heart of the interview and provide valuable information to you -- they are situational and behavioral questions. You can tailor your interviews around one type of question or a combination of the two. Either way, developing a skill in preparing them will serve you well during future interviews. Keep the following in mind as you prepare your situational or behavioral interview questions. They should be:

  • • Reflective of competencies derived from a job analysis.
  • • Realistic of the responsibilities of the job.
  • • Open-ended.
  • • Clear and concise.
  • • At a comprehension level appropriate for the candidates.
  • • Free of agency jargon.

Warning: Some agencies have their own rules on interviewing, which may be quite detailed on the types of questions and use of interview panels. Be sure to check with HR.

Situational questions. These questions are based on future-oriented behavior. They give the candidate realistic job scenarios or dilemmas and ask how he would respond. The underlying premise is that a person's intentions are closely tied to his actual behavior.

Try to construct questions that are reflective of the competencies needed for the position. For example, a situational interview question for the competency "integrity/honesty" is: "You and one other employee are assigned to take payments at the lobby counter. You notice that your colleague is placing some cash payments in a separate envelope and not the cash drawer. How would you handle this situation?"

To develop situational questions, you should work with subject matter experts with extensive knowledge of the job to be filled who can help identify needed competencies and effective behaviors that lead to success. It's best to use hypothetical situations that are as real as possible and reflective of the job.

Behavioral questions. The primary purpose of behavioral questions is to gather information from job candidates about their actual behavior during past experiences, which demonstrates competencies required for the job. The underlying premise is that the best predictor of future behavior on the job is past behavior under similar circumstances.

Traditionally, you may ask, "How would you deal with an angry customer?" and the candidate responds with the answer you want to hear. The answer can be rehearsed or even come from a book.

The behavior-based approach is to ask how the candidate handled a nasty customer in an actual work situation. This forces her to describe real situations rather than to show you how cleverly she handles questions. And you can ask follow-up questions that probe into the situation the candidate described -- you can even ask for another example if the first was unsatisfactory.

If, for example, the competency "flexibility" is required of the position, construct a behavioral interview question that focuses on the candidate's past behavior, such as: "We often face many changes in the workplace. Describe a specific situation in which you feel you were especially effective in adapting to an unanticipated change."

Or if the competency "decisiveness" is required of the position, construct a behavioral interview question such as: "Describe some examples of decisions or recommendations you are called upon to make in your current or past position," or, "What has been a stubborn or recurring problem area you would like to solve in your current job? How would you solve it?"

Again, subject matter experts who possess knowledge of the job to be filled will be invaluable to help you write behavioral interview questions. SMEs should develop more questions than are actually needed to allow for subsequent discarding of questions during review and tryout. Each question should be written to elicit specific details about a situation, task, or context; the actions the person took or did not take; and the impact of these actions.

Use superlative adjectives in the questions (e.g., most, last, worst, least) to help the candidate focus on specific incidents.

Structured interviews

OPM strongly recommends that managers use structured interviews to assess competencies and qualifications. Given its inherent fairness to all candidates, this type of interview has demonstrated a high degree of reliability, validity, and legal defensibility.

In a structured interview:

  • • All candidates are asked the same questions in the same order.
  • • All candidates are evaluated using a common rating scale.
  • • Interviewers are in agreement on acceptable answers.

By following the consistent model of a structured interview, you will be able to compare "apples to apples" when assessing candidates. Check with your HR office to devise your rating scale.

Probing questions. A structured interview does not mean that all other questions are off limits. You can ask probing questions to help clarify answers or to ensure the candidate has provided enough information.

When probes are necessary, interviewers should use very similar questions for all candidates to ensure the same opportunities are given. While probes may need to be tailored to address each candidate's specific response, the general meaning of the probes should not change.

Prior to the interview, determine if probing questions will be utilized. If so, decide how many questions will be appropriate and establish the specific probes for each question the interviewer is allowed to use.

Probing questions usually surface during your pilot test of the interview questions with colleagues and HR. While they may seem like "off script" questions, they, too, need structure and consistency.

The panel interview

During the interview process, an abundance of information is exchanged between the candidate and the interviewer. A panel of two or three interviewers may be better able to document and interpret the information. A panel also reduces the risk of biases in ratings and allows for a diverse range of interviewers, indicating to the candidate that the organization values diversity and fair treatment.

Interviewers may conduct the interview together at one time or individually in a serial fashion in which the candidate progresses through multiple interviews. When feasible, the same interviewers should be used (either in a panel or serially) across all candidates, to ensure consistency in ratings.

In a panel interview, each panel member should individually observe, record, and evaluate the responses of the candidates. After each candidate, panel members should discuss their individual ratings.

Having panel members also enables a more thorough interview. Others may observe behaviors or pick up on information that may have slipped by you. Final scores or ratings should be based on the consensus of the panel.

Although the interview panel works as a team, the hiring manager is typically designated as the chairperson or coordinator and he is responsible for the administrative and logistical arrangements of the interview and for documenting the process.

The interview setting

Don't underestimate the importance of the interview setting. The last thing you want is to put any candidate at a disadvantage by an unforeseen distraction or interruption. There are a number of factors to consider when setting up the interview. Several are provided below for an in-person interview:

  • • Select a location that is somewhat removed from your regular work area. This will reduce the likelihood of disruptions, such as ringing telephones or visitors to your office.
  • • Choose a comfortable environment. Be sure the temperature is adequate and the interview is in a quiet, non-threatening, and private place.
  • • Establish seating arrangements for the candidate and panel members. They should be the same for all interviews.
  • • Ensure the interview room and facilities are accessible to candidates with disabilities.
  • • Reserve a separate area for those waiting to be interviewed.
  • • Plan the logistics so that individuals who have been interviewed cannot communicate with those waiting to be interviewed.
  • • Schedule the interviews far enough in advance to provide adequate preparation time for the interviewers.
  • • Verify all panel members understand the procedures to be followed and have all necessary materials.
  • • Allot the same amount of interview time for all candidates.

Conducting the interview

All of your preliminary work leads up to the actual interview. Try to maintain the same level of enthusiasm with each candidate, both before and after lunch. Go into each interview thinking that the next person will be the one you hire. This will lead to a better rapport with each candidate and set a positive tone during the interview.

The greeting. Whether in-person or by video, a first impression is irreversible. That's why your initial greeting is so important. Be enthusiastic, make eye contact, call the candidate by her name, and introduce all the panel members.

You may want to chat informally for a few minutes. Try to keep your topics limited to commuting, your office's location, or the weather. Breaking the ice like this will help you both relax prior to the interview, and more importantly, it will help you to build rapport with the candidate.

Warning: Interviewers tend to make rapid decisions about the qualifications of a candidate within the first few minutes of the interview based on minimal information. Reserve your judgment until sufficient information on the candidate has been gathered.

Beginning the interview. Now that everyone is settled in, get down to business:

  • • Thank the candidate for her interest in the position and for coming to the interview.
  • • Describe the job and relevant organizational characteristics briefly to allow candidates to become comfortable in the interview setting.
  • • Explain the interview process in a standardized way. This explanation may also be provided to applicants in writing.
  • • Advise the candidate that she will be asked the same questions that will be asked of all the candidates (for structured interviews).
  • • Inform the candidate that notes will be taken throughout the interview.
  • • Ask if the candidate has any questions before beginning.

The formal interview. As you begin the formal interview, begin asking the candidate the questions you have prepared and brought with you to the interview. You may want to begin with a few warm-up questions to put the candidate at ease before working up to more substantial situational or behavioral questions. A few opening questions are:

  • • What interests you most about this position?
  • • Why do you want to work for this agency?

If you don't understand a response, ask the candidate to elaborate on or clarify what was just said. While it is important that you have a list of the same questions you will ask each candidate, you are not prohibited from asking additional, clarifying questions.

Note taking. Taking regular and detailed notes of observable behaviors and verbal responses during each interview is crucial. Notes will reduce the burden of having to remember details about multiple candidates. Additionally, these notes should:

  • • Summarize the content and delivery of each candidate's answers.
  • • Document the candidate's grammar, body language, and other nonverbal factors.
  • • Help interviewers focus on pertinent information during the interview.
  • • Be of sufficient quality and quantity to document the interviewer's reasoning for each rating on each competency.
  • • Serve as documentation to support the employment decision.

Nonverbal behavior. Your body language, such as facial expressions and body movements (e.g., nodding, raising eyebrows, frowning), communicates a lot to the candidate. For example, an interviewer can communicate disinterest by slouching, regularly looking at the clock, leaning back, or doodling with a pen. Avoid these negative impressions during both an in-person interview and one done by video. Additionally, don't bury your head in the notepad. While taking notes, look up periodically and make eye contact with the candidate to show your interest and to provide opportunities to observe the candidate's nonverbal behavior.

The candidate's nonverbal cues can also provide some helpful insight. While your selection is not to be based solely on nonverbal behavior, take note of the following:

  • • Eye contact. While answering your questions, is she making eye contact with the interviewer asking the question, and the rest of the panel? If she is, it's a good sign of self-confidence.
  • • Posture. Is her posture upright and poised? If so, it's a positive sign of confidence and self-assurance.
  • • Hand gestures. Does she use hand gestures while she speaks? Interviews are usually high in anxiety, and hand gestures could signal a relaxed, comfortable state of mind.
  • • Legs shaking. This sign of nervousness can only be detected during an in-person interview. And while it's normal to be nervous during an interview, observe if the shaking leg eventually settles or continues throughout the interview.
  • • Arms crossed. Whether it's one arm or both crossed, this is a sign of defensiveness. A candidate's statement that she's "good with people" will be contradicted by this nonverbal indicator.
  • • Fiddling with hands. Again, this may only be detected during an in-person setting but it's a sure sign of nervousness.

Interviewers should base their evaluation of the candidate on her past performance and current behavior as it relates to the competency being evaluated and not exclusively on how she acts during the interview. Questions and probes relating to the competencies of interest will usually direct the interviewer to the important information.

The silence strategy. Most interviewers and candidates fear dead air. Give the candidate enough time to think over her answers, but not so long as to create discomfort for all. It may feel awkward, but allow her the necessary time to think about her answer or recall a situation that fits your question.

Take note of how she responds to silence. Is she quick to fill the vacuum with poorly thought-out remarks? Or does she have the confidence to take the time to formulate a rational and articulate answer to your question? Accept the silence, and use it to learn a bit more about your candidate.

If you are silent for a few moments after the candidate provides a response, it may allow her time to think of additional things to say. Or, you may use neutral phrases, such as "I see," or "Oh," that may prompt her to elaborate further. This indirect probing method is an effective way to elicit more information.

Listen. Perhaps your greatest interviewing asset is your ability to listen. When you listen, you learn things you otherwise would not learn, and it encourages your candidate to engage himself fully. Pay attention not only to what he says, but also to what he doesn't say. This can give you additional perspectives of the candidate.

During answers, express your listening with nonverbal gestures such as nodding, maintaining eye contact, and leaning forward.

Closing the interview

After you have finished asking the candidate questions, allow the candidate time to ask questions of you. This is a great opportunity for you to elaborate on the agency, your work unit, and/or details of the specific job. You should be prepared to answer a variety of questions from the candidate. The following list provides a few examples of questions that a candidate may ask:

  • •What would a day on this job be like?
  • • To whom would I report?
  • • What is your management style?
  • • How big is the workload?

You should answer these questions as thoroughly as possible. Just as you are trying to determine if the candidate fits your organization, the candidate is also making a determination about your agency being a suitable employer for his needs.

Explain the selection process. Advise each candidate what will follow if he moves forward in the process, and what he can expect if he doesn't. Whether it's a second interview by higher managers, or a standard letter informing the candidate that he did not get the position, transparency of the process will prepare him for the possibilities.

Also, inform the candidate of your anticipated time frame for decision-making. Be realistic. If you expect to make a final decision on the job in the next two weeks, say so. If the time horizon is longer, be sure that the candidate leaves with realistic expectations: "It may be the end of next month before we make a final decision on this vacancy."

Evaluate and document. Immediately after each interview, all interview panel members should evaluate the candidate against the scoring guide you developed earlier. Each candidate should be assessed independently. Avoid comparing and contrasting to other candidates. Panel members should individually observe, record, and evaluate the responses of the candidates. Afterward, the panel should discuss their scores.

Once all candidates are interviewed, the scoring guide will help identify top contenders. Final scores should be based on the consensus of the panel.

Note: After the interview, be sure to keep your interview notes and score cards in a secure location, or store them in accordance with your agency's policy. They may be needed later if your hiring decision is questioned by a disappointed candidate.

Mistakes to avoid

The interview process is multilayered and requires your attention. Selecting the right person to fill a position in your work unit is arguably one of your most important duties. The time you invest in the various aspects of interviewing will make the difference in hiring.

Do not:

  • • Rely on first impressions. Reserve your judgment until the interview is concluded. You don't want to make an erroneous decision about a candidate within the first few minutes of the interview based on minimal information.
  • • Succumb to pressure to hire. You may feel pressured to fill a position quickly to ease the burden of your work unit or for other reasons. Do not make hasty decisions in the acquisition of personnel. Adhere to the established interview procedure and timeline with each candidate to avoid making a bad hire.
  • • Ask prohibited questions. Be careful to avoid questions that violate EEO law and the rights of protected groups. A candidate's answer may even lead to topics involving religious or ethnic background. Run your questions past HR to stay out of trouble.
  • • Ignore nonverbal cues. While you may not evaluate the candidate exclusively by how she acts during the interview, nonverbal cues can provide some helpful insight.
  • • Underestimate the importance of the interview setting. The location and environment of the interview are critically important to avoid any unforeseen distraction or interruption, which may cause a disadvantage to one or more candidates.

Ask FMG

Q. Shouldn't the questions I ask each candidate be based on their application materials?

A. Application materials will determine qualifications for the position. It is not advisable to tailor a set of questions for each applicant based on that information. To be fair, all applicants should be asked the same questions based on the position competencies and qualifications needed. That way, your rating system is more reliable and legally defensible.

Q. At times, there occurs an uncomfortable silence following an interview question. I try to ease the tension by making a witty remark or steering the candidate toward an answer. Is this advisable?

A. Silence is not a bad thing during an interview. It's best to let the candidate find her way without your assistance. By coaxing an answer, you can be giving her an unfair advantage. Accept the silence and observe how your candidate handles it.

Q. Isn't it important to know if a female candidate plans to get pregnant? Maternity leave really impacts the rest of staff.

A. Since this type of inquiry has nothing to do with the candidate's knowledge, skills, abilities, or relevant experiences, it would likely be viewed as improper and discriminatory. Speak to your HR office to find out which subjects are prohibited.

Q. Since I'm the one to whom the selectee will report, I see no point in including others in the interview. Makes sense, right?

A. Consider the advantages of a panel interview. By having two or three interviewers, you will better be able to document and help interpret the extensive information being provided by the candidate. You will also reduce the risk of ratings biases and allow for a diverse range of interviewers with a panel. Having panel members just makes for a more thorough and inclusive interview.

Q. I like to wait until all the interviews are finished before scoring them. That way I can compare candidates to each other so that my top contenders emerge. Is this a viable method?

A. Each candidate should be assessed independently, and this means avoiding comparing and contrasting to other candidates. Immediately after each interview, all interview panel members should evaluate the candidate and discuss their scores. Your top candidates will shine either way, but this independent assessment method gives each candidate the chance to shine on their individual merits.
