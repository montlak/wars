F. REVIEW OF REPORT OF INVESTIGATION

As discussed more fully below in the section on "Discovery Sanctions," over the past few years the Commission has become far more aggressive about imposing sanctions on the parties for dilatory conduct in the hearing process. Most of these sanctions have occurred in the context of discovery disputes and follow a growing trend in the federal courts to reduce, if not eliminate, discovery shenanigans by punishing the offenders. This is as it should be. Discovery was never intended to be an exercise produce fair trials. Representatives, who intentionally thwart those goals, and the parties they represent, should be punished for misconduct. Without indicating any retreat from its trend of sanctioning dilatory discovery conduct, the Commission made a significant change in its posture on sanctioning agencies that fail to complete complaint investigations or produce inadequate investigations.

One of the first duties of the administrative judge is to review the report of investigation. The Judges' Handbook at 1-2 to 1-3 provides:

If the Administrative Judge reviews the investigative report and finds that the agency did not sufficiently comply with its obligation under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(b) to develop an impartial factual record from which a reasonable fact finder could determine whether discrimination occurred, or if no investigation has been conducted, the Administrative Judge retains jurisdiction over the complaint. In order to develop the record, the Administrative Judge may order the agency to complete an investigation within a particular time period; allow the parties to develop the record themselves through discovery; issue orders for the production of documents and witnesses; or consider appropriate sanctions. The parties shall initially bear their own costs with regard to discovery, unless the Administrative Judge requires the agency to bear the costs for the complainant to obtain depositions or any other discovery because the agency has failed to complete its investigation as required by 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(e) or has failed to investigate the allegations in gamesmanship. It was designed to facilitate the exchange of information prior to trial, to eliminate "trial by ambush," and, ultimately, to adequately pursuant to EEO MD-110, Chapter Six.

As discussed fully in the section below on "Discovery Sanctions," in January 2003, the Department of Justice, Office of Legal Counsel, issued an opinion memorandum indicating that it did not believe the EEOC had the authority to award attorney fees against a federal agency as a sanction for discovery misconduct. That memorandum concerned the authority of administrative judges under 29 CFR 1614.109 and not the Commission's authority to order agency investigations under 1614.108. It is not clear how, if at all, the Justice Department opinion affects the Commission's guidance regarding the authority of administrative judges to cure inadequate investigations by allowing the complainant to conduct discovery and imposing the costs upon the agency. Such authority would seem to be precluded by the Justice Department opinion because it was premised, in large part, on the doctrine of sovereign immunity.

Under the current regulations, if the agency fails at the investigative stage to develop an adequate record, the administrative judge assigned to the case may impose sanctions. However, the administrative judge must first identify the inadequacies in the record, request that the agency provide the missing information, and notify the agency of the consequences for not abiding by the request. MD-110 at 7-7 to 7-8 provides:

The Commission has the authority to issue sanctions in the administrative hearing process because it was granted, through statute, the power to issue such rules and regulations that it deems necessary to enforce the prohibition on employment discrimination. See Waller v. Dep't. of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 0720030069 (May 25, 2007), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 0520070689 (Feb. 26, 2009). In this respect, the Commission has determined "that delegating to its Administrative Judges the authority to issue sanctions against agencies, and complainants, is necessary and is an appropriate remedy which effectuates the policies of the Commission." Id.

However, before an Administrative Judge may sanction an agency for failing to develop an impartial and appropriate factual record or for not cooperating in the discovery process, the Administrative Judge must issue an order to the agency or request the documents, records, comparative data, statistics, or affidavits. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3). Such order or request shall make clear that sanctions may be imposed and the type of sanction that could be imposed for failure to comply with the order unless the agency can show good cause for that failure. See Rountree v. Dep't. of the Treasury, Appeal No. 07A00015 (July 17, 2001). In appropriate circumstances, the order or request may provide the agency with an opportunity to take such action as the Administrative Judge deems necessary to correct the deficiencies in the record within a specified reasonable period of time. Only on the failure of the agency to comply with the Administrative Judge's order or request and the notice to show cause may the Administrative Judge impose a sanction or the sanctions identified in the order or request.

Under the 1999 regulations, the administrative judge may not remand the case to the agency for a supplemental investigation. However, the judge, while maintaining jurisdiction over the case, may order the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation. Simmons v. Postmaster General, 01A21416 (2002).

As the Commission explained in Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120133391 (2015):

Complainant essentially argues that the AJ erred in issuing a decision by summary judgment because the investigation was untimely and inadequate. Complainant argues that the AJ should not have ordered the Agency to complete its investigation since the 180 days had passed. She also argues that the AJ, in making the decision by summary judgment, was relying on information which the Complainant did not have an opportunity to rebut.

We are not persuaded by Complainant's arguments on appeal. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(g) authorizes a complainant to request a hearing where the agency has not completed its investigation within 180 days. This is what occurred in the instant case. However, EEOC Management Directive (MD)-110 makes it clear that the AJ has a variety of options available to develop the record, including, as here, ordering the agency to complete its investigation within a designated period of time. MD-110, Ch.7, Sect. III(B) (Nov. 9, 1999). We find that the AJ in this case did not err in exercising his discretion to order the Agency to supplement the record by completing its investigation. While Complainant argues that she did not have an opportunity to rebut the evidence gathered during the fact-finding conference, the record shows that the investigator holding the conference notified Complainant and her representative of the time and place of the conference and that Complainant was scheduled to testify. However, the investigator noted in the record that Complainant and her representative did not appear in person at the fad-finding conference, and further declined to participate by telephone. She also did not provide a list of witnesses for the investigator to interview or provide any rebuttal to management testimony. Therefore, we find that Complainant failed to avail herself of the opportunity provided during the investigation to rebut management's testimony.

An agency is not excused from its responsibilities to conduct an adequate investigation within the regulatory timeframes because it has engaged a contractor for the investigation. Those responsibilities remain with the agency. Cox v. Social Security Administration, 0720050055 (2009).

Previously, administrative judges were permitted to impose sanctions for inadequate investigations in appropriate cases without the notice and opportunity to respond now required. Although the Commission does not specifically explain its change in policy, the change likely reflects adjustments necessary because of other 1999 amendments to Part 1614. For example, under the revised regulations, complainants may now amend complaints at any point during the investigative process. If the complainant amends a complaint and then requests a hearing shortly thereafter, the record on the amended issue will obviously be inadequate. MD-110 at 7-8, footnote 6, notes that sanctions would not be inappropriate in such a case. The new guidance reflects the regulatory changes by allowing the administrative judge to request any necessary information from the agency and then sanction the agency if it does not comply. The change in policy may also reflect an implicit recognition by the Commission that it is not always possible for agencies to complete investigations within the regulatory time frames, even absent amendment of the complaint, given the large volume of federal sector EEO complaints. By relaxing its position on sanctions for inadequate investigations and retaining the right of the complainant to proceed to the hearing stage after 180 days regardless of whether an investigation has been completed, the Commission has tried to reach a balance that does not unduly punish agencies for missing the regulatory deadline.
