I. FITNESS FOR DUTY EXAMINATION

Agencies have the authority to order fitness-for-duty examinations in limited circumstances. See 5 CFR Part 339, Medical Determinations Related to Employability. The examination itself and its potential use qualify it as a personnel action within the scope of the discrimination laws.

Under 339.301, an agency can order fitness for duty examinations in the following circumstances:

(a) An agency may require an individual who has applied for or occupies a position which has physical/medical standards for selection or retention, or which is part of an established program of medical surveillance related to occupational or environmental exposure demands, to report for a medical evaluation:

(1) Prior to an appointment or selection (including reemployment on the basis of full or partial recovery from a medical condition);

(2) On a regularly recurring, periodic basis; and

(3) Whenever there is a direct question about an employee's continued capacity to meet the physical or medical requirements of the position.

(b) An agency may require an employee receiving workers compensation benefits or assigned to limited duties as a result of an on-the-job injury to report for medical evaluation when the agency has identified an assignment or position (including the employee's regular position) which it reasonably believes the employee can perform consistent with the medical limitations of his/her condition. If the medical information (consistent with generally accepted medical principles and practice) indicates that the employee is capable of performing the duties identified, the agency will promptly return the employee to corresponding duty and pay status.

(c) An agency may require an employee who is released from his/her competitive level in a reduction-in-force to undergo medical evaluation if the position(s) to which the employee has reassignment rights requires specific physical capabilities to perform the duties of the job, and those physical capabilities are different from those required in the employee's present position. The agency shall be aware of the affirmative obligations of the provisions of 29 CFR 1613.704, which require reasonable accommodation of a qualified employee who is handicapped.

(d) An agency may offer a medical examination when an individual has made a request for medical reasons for a change in duty status, assignment, or working conditions or any other benefit or special treatment (including reemployment on the basis of full or partial recovery from a medical condition) and the agency, after it has received and reviewed medical documentation, determines that it cannot grant, support, or act further on the request without verification of the clinical findings and current clinical status.

The outcome of such an examination is not determinative of whether ordering the examination was discriminatory.

The agency in Lilla B. v. Attorney General, 2019001462 (2020), made a legitimate and defensible request for the complainant to submit to a fitness for duty examination. The complainant in Lilla B. had been in a modified assignment for two years as a reasonable accommodation. When the complainant submitted a doctor's note indicating that she was partially recovered and should be reassigned to a different department, the agency referred her for a fitness for duty examination to better understand the complainant's ability to perform the essential functions of her position:

Regarding claim (4), Complainant being referred for a fitness for duty exam (FFDE), we find that Complainant did not establish that the Agency violated the Rehabilitation Act. Employers may require a medical examination such as that conducted here only if the examination is job-related and consistent with business necessity. See Enforcement Guidance: Disability-Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations of Employees Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) (July 27, 2000) (Guidance), at B. This requirement is met when the employer has a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that: (1) an employee's ability to perform the essential job functions is impaired by a medical condition; or (2) that an employee poses a direct threat due to a medical condition. Guidance at Question 5.

In the instant matter, we find the Agency's referral for Complainant to attend a FFDE was job-related and consistent with business necessity. Complainant had been working desk duty since December 2014 as a reasonable accommodation for her medical conditions and had not performed the duties of Nurse Aide. The record contains an email from a Human Resources Specialist to Complainant regarding the FFDE. Therein, the Human Resources Specialist states that "the doctor's note that [Complainant] gave to the [Human Resources Manager] does not indicate [she] can return to full duty, no restrictions...[Complainant] has been on a modified job assignment since 2014 and the Agency is trying to determine if and when [she] can return to full duty..." ROI at 354.

We find that the Agency's referral of Complainant for a FFDE was job related and consistent with business necessity. Complainant had been in a modified assignment for two years as a reasonable accommodation for anxiety. Complainant's physician indicated that Complainant could return to duty but be reassigned to a different department. Complainant's physician stated that "return to work...should be started gradually and monitored closely." ROI at 341. Thus, we find that the Agency was seeking clarification of the information provided by Complainant's physician regarding her ability to perform the essential functions of her position. See Calicott v. U.S. Postal Serv. EEOC Appeal No. 01A01169 (July 2, 2003) (finding Agency not in violation of Rehabilitation Act when it sought clarification of information provided by a complainant's physician regarding whether the complainant could perform the essential functions of his position). Accordingly, we find that the Agency referring Complainant for a FFDE was not a violation of the Rehabilitation Act.

An appellant who claims that he was discriminatorily ordered to take a fitness-for-duty examination may state a claim. In Ricciardi v. Postmaster General, 01963211 (1997), the Commission held:

On February 22, 1996, the agency issued a FAD dismissing appellant's complaint, which was filed on April 6, 1995, wherein appellant alleged that the agency discriminated against him as referenced above when appellant was the only employee ordered to take a fitness for duty examination on March 2, 1995, and a psychological examination on March 27, 1995. The FAD indicated that appellant failed to state a claim as both examinations were conducted while appellant was on the clock and at the agency's expense. Consequently, appellant suffered no loss to a term, condition or privilege of employment. 29 CFR § 1614.107(e).

The Commission finds that the agency incorrectly focused on the fact that the examinations were conducted on the clock and at its expense and ignored that appellant alleged disparate treatment as he stated in the addendum to his formal complaint that he was the only employee ordered by the Postmaster (RMO) to undergo the examinations. Contrary to the agency's conclusion, we find that appellant is aggrieved as disparate treatment is a present harm which impacts on a term, condition or privilege of his employment.

In Little v. USPS, 01853250 (1987), the Commission found that subjecting the employee to a fitness-for-duty examination was discriminatory where the examination was totally unrelated to a concern for the employee's safety or the safety of others. The Commission found there was no link between the alleged unexcused absences of the employee and the purposes of the exam. In reaching this finding, the Commission noted that there was no evidence that the employee took excessive leave, was advanced such leave, or was counseled about using excessive sick leave.

In Zuniga v. Postmaster General, 05950709 (1997), the Commission found that the employee stated a claim for relief where he alleged that the agency submitted letters he had written to a behavioral analysis by a private consulting firm. The Commission found that, particularly where the agency kept records of the results and there were indications that the agency was motivated by retaliatory animus, this sufficiently aggrieved the employee.
