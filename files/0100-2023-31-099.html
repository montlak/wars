2. Expert Witness Fees

The statutory $40 witness fee was also applied to expert witnesses. As explained in Mathers v. Postmaster General, 01902612 (1990), relying upon Crawford Fitting Co. v. J.T. Gibbons, Inc., 482 U.S. 437 (1987):

We find that our regulatory authority and the Supreme Court's decision in Crawford Fitting Co. restrain us from awarding more than the $30-per-day-witness fee provided for by 28 USC 1821. Appellant's counsel erroneously asserts that Crawford Fitting Co. did not involve Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, when two cases were consolidated in that decision, one of which was brought under Title VII. Further, lower federal courts have followed the Supreme Court's holding in Crawford Fitting Co., which held that the reimbursement of non-court appointed expert witness fees for a prevailing party in federal court, is limited to the 28 USC 1821 $30.00-per-day rate absent contract or explicit statutory authority to the contrary. See Denny v. Westfield State College, 880 F.2d 1465 (1st Cir. 1989); Gilbert v. Little Rock Arkansas, 48 FEP Cases 1039 (D.C.E. Ark. 1987)....

Section 113 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, codified at 42 USC 2000e-5(k), now authorizes recovery of expert witness fees.

The recovery of an expert witness fee depends on the complainant prevailing, but does not depend on the success of the expert in obtaining damages for the complainant. In Young v. Social Security Administration, 01955120 (1998), the Commission approved an expert witness fee even though the expert was unable to establish that the complainant was depressed:

[E]xpert fees in this case totaled $2,000.00, and copying costs totaled $220.00. The Commission finds that appellant is entitled to the total amount of these costs. Although the AJ found that appellant's expert's opinion was of limited probative value, she did so in the context of his opinion on whether or not appellant was entitled to damages resulting from depression. The credentials of appellant's psychologist were never brought into question, nor was any other evidence presented to disqualify him as an expert in the case.

In addition to the time spent on a hearing, an expert witness is entitled to a reasonable amount of time to prepare for hearing testimony. Abbate v. Secretary of Navy, 01971418 (2000). Perhaps more significant in the Abbate case, however, is the Commission's finding that the complainant's treating physician was entitled to be reimbursed for his time as an expert witness instead of a fact witness. Approving the doctor's reimbursement at the higher expert witness rate, the Commission, in Abbate, noted:

With respect to Physician 2's preparation time and hearing testimony, the agency does not dispute that these charges were incurred in connection with complainant's EEO complaint. Instead, the agency contends that two hours for testimony preparation was unreasonable. In addition, the agency contends that Physician 2, as complainant's treating physician, was called to testify solely as a fact witness and accordingly, complainant should only be reimbursed $40.00 for Physician 2's testimony. We disagree with the agency in this regard. While it is clear that Physician 2 testified on factual issues, he also rendered medical opinions and recounted his diagnosis and prognosis of complainant. Accordingly, we find that complainant is entitled to reimbursement of the costs incurred in calling Physician 2 to testify at the hearing in this matter. In addition, we find that 2 hours to prepare for the hearing is not unreasonable.

An expert witness need not testify in order for the complainant to recover costs for the time the expert spent on the case. The complainant must, however, show that the use of the expert was reasonably necessary, and present the expert's bill. In Darrell v. Secretary of Navy, 01992288 (2001), the Commission explained:

The agency also refused to reimburse complainant for an expert witness fee charge of $425.00. A prevailing complainant is entitled to expert fees as part of recoverable cost. 29 CFR § 1614.501(e). Recovery is generally limited to testifying experts, but fees may be awarded for non-testifying experts if the complainant can show that the expert's services were reasonably necessary to the case. See Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 CFR Part 1614 (EEO MD-110), 11-4 (November 9, 1999). Here, the agency contends that the record did not show that the expert provided any testimony in this matter. Further, the agency states that complainant did not submit a billing statement from the expert verifying the charge. In reviewing the record, we find that complainant submitted a report from the expert in question with a billing statement for his services. In fact, the consultation of the expert was required because of an information request by the agency. As a result, complainant is entitled to reimbursement for the services of the expert consultant even though he did not testify in this matter.

Cf. Cobb v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A24465 (2003) (denying fees to an expert who did not testify at the hearing because no information was provided on the need for the expert). See also Wilson v. Postmaster General, 01A528831 (2005), denying costs for non-witness consultant because there was no demonstration that the consultant was "reasonably necessary to the case."

Expert witness fees are not confined to physicians. The fee is payable to any witness who qualifies as an expert. See, e.g., Castillo v. Secretary of Transportation, 01970175 (2000) (approving an expert witness fee for an economist).

The fact that the complainant's representative has expertise in a subject matter area does not entitle the representative to fees as an expert witness. Estate of Bose v. Secretary of Navy, 01961912 (1998). In that case, the complainant's representative, a mechanical engineer who was not an attorney, attempted to collect fees as an expert witness. Noting that it was irrelevant if the expertise assisted him in his representational capacity, the Commission found that the representative never functioned as an expert and, as a result, could not qualify for fees.
