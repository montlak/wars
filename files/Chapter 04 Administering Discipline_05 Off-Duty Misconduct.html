OFF-DUTY MISCONDUCT

As a federal manager, the off-duty conduct -- or misconduct -- of your employees is generally none of your business. But there are some exceptions, when off-duty misconduct adversely affects the performance of one of your employees, the performance of that employee's coworkers, and the accomplishment of your agency's mission.

Cases may be complex, unique. When infractions take place on duty, you have fairly clear guidelines on how to handle it. However, when one of your employees does something wrong during off-duty hours, things get a lot more complicated.

Off-duty misconduct cases often involve unusual facts that arise in a unique context. The particular circumstances and context may be so distinctive that you aren't aware of any similar cases in which action has been taken. But if the off-duty conduct adversely affects your agency's mission, you should be prepared to take adverse action against the employee.

When it comes to any type of misconduct -- on duty or off duty -- you can take adverse action against the employee only if you can demonstrate three things:

  1. 1. The employee engaged in the misconduct with which he was charged.
  2. 2. There is a nexus between what the employee did and disciplining the employee to promote the efficiency of the service.
  3. 3. The penalty is reasonable.

Nexus

Nexus is a logical connection between an off-duty incident and the negative impact it has on the agency's operations or mission, known in legal language as "the efficiency of the service." As you consider taking adverse action against an employee for off-duty misconduct, your agency must be able to prove a nexus between the specific misconduct and the efficiency of the service.

Proof of nexus is required for all disciplinary actions, whatever the circumstances of the misconduct. Essentially, there is really no difference between off-duty misconduct and misconduct on duty.

A determination of nexus requires a highly technical legal and factual analysis. The good news is that you don't have to undertake this analysis; and you shouldn't try. When any instance of off-duty misconduct comes to your attention as a possible occasion for discipline, your first step should always be to alert your chain of command and to consult your servicing HR employee relations specialist. The legal office may also be involved. However, the decision to take adverse action is a management responsibility, and you should understand the principles involved.

There are three ways an agency may show a nexus linking an employee's off-duty misconduct with the efficiency of the service.

1. When the misconduct adversely affects the job performance of an employee or coworkers, or management's trust and confidence in the employee's job performance. This may happen, for example, if the employee is forced to miss work. The employee's own performance will be affected, as will that of the coworkers who have to cover for their missing colleague. The off-duty misconduct may also distract your employees, adversely affecting their performance.

If your employee's off-duty misconduct involves actions that could call into question their honesty, integrity, or character, it's likely this would adversely affect your trust and confidence in the employee's job performance. This is especially true if the employee performed their duties with little oversight, since you would need to trust their judgment and integrity.

Off-duty use or possession of illegal drugs or DUIs also may cause you to lose confidence in an employee's job performance. If the employee's duties include driving, operating machinery or heavy equipment, or other activities that could endanger others, you may lose confidence in the employee's ability to perform these duties correctly and safely.

2. When the misconduct interferes with or adversely affects the agency's mission. If you supervise law enforcement officers, for example, off-duty criminal activity will likely interfere with your unit's law enforcement mission. If your employee engages in off-duty misconduct that coworkers witness or get involved in, it could have a chilling effect upon relationships at the workplace. Both these situations could adversely impact your unit's ability to fulfill its mission with proper efficiency.

There also may be an adverse impact on an agency's mission if the employee's off-duty misconduct has caused bad publicity for the agency. If the story has been covered by the media, or is available online, that tends to increase the adverse impact on the agency. Negative publicity can cause the public to lose faith in the agency, making it harder to accomplish its mission.

3. When "certain egregious circumstances" are present. In very serious cases, the agency can presume a nexus based on the nature and gravity of the misconduct. This includes:

  • • Criminal convictions.
  • • Convictions for the use of deadly force.
  • • Incidents of egregious sexual misconduct.

However, the employee may be able to refute that presumption on appeal, with presentation of sufficient evidence.

Managers and supervisors. As a manager, you already know that you are "held to a higher standard." That's as true when it comes to off-duty misconduct as it is in other areas. It's easier to show nexus when a manager gets in trouble for off-duty incidents. Off-duty misconduct may compromise an agency's trust in a manager's ability to function as a manager or supervisor.

Here are a couple of examples:

  • • An employee held a high-level management position in which she had fiduciary responsibilities for up to $25,000. She switched price tags on merchandise and attempted to purchase it at reduced prices. This compromised the agency's trust in her ability to function in that supervisory position. The agency proved a nexus between "engaging in criminal misconduct -- criminal conversion" and her management position.
  • • A chief of fiscal operations held a high-level management position in which she was entrusted with oversight of billions of dollars in payments each year. Her off-duty misconduct consisted of facilitating the commission of a crime, accepting and using stolen furniture, appliances, and computer equipment in her home, and later selling a stolen computer printer to an agency employee. The nexus was evident, even though the stolen property was not government property. The employee seriously compromised the trust the agency had in her to function in her position.

Criminal charges

If you learn that criminal charges based on off-duty misconduct have been filed against one of your employees, your first reaction might be to suspend them without pay until the charges are resolved. This would probably be premature. For an indefinite suspension to be valid, as for all adverse actions, your agency must show a nexus between the criminal charge and the efficiency of the service. Don't assume a nexus is established just because an employee was arrested for an off-duty offense.

You have some immediate options available short of suspension, including:

  • • Barring an employee from a facility under administrative leave (excused absence).
  • • Limitation of access to classified or sensitive material.
  • • Changes in duty assignments. For example, it may be reasonable to ban an employee accused of driving under the influence of alcohol from government vehicles, pending an investigation.

If your employee is indicted for a crime and could be imprisoned, then you have to decide whether to allow him to continue working. An indictment is a formal written statement framed by a prosecutor and found by a grand jury charging a person with an offense.

Indefinite suspensions. The agency cannot simply assume or speculate that an employee charged with criminal offenses poses a danger to the workplace community or that their presence at the workplace would be disruptive. The agency must be able to show a nexus between the charges and efficiency of the service.

To suspend an employee, based on a contention that he may face imprisonment, the agency must be able to prove:

  • • It has reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime that could result in a sentence of imprisonment.
  • • It has provided a suspension terminating event (e.g., completion of its investigation) in its notice of suspension.

Usually, an employee against whom an action is proposed is entitled to at least 30 days' advance written notice. However, an agency may use a notice period of less than 30 days if it has reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime for which imprisonment may be imposed. This is referred to as the "crime provision" or "crime exception."

If an agency imposes an indefinite suspension because it believes the employee has committed a crime that could require prison time, the agency doesn't have to prove the underlying misconduct on which the belief is based. It only has to prove that it had a reasonable belief that the employee has committed a crime that may result in imprisonment.

Often, a criminal indictment is used to establish this reasonable belief. However, an indictment is not specifically required to invoke the crime provision. An agency is not required by law to wait for an indictment.

Keep in mind that this reasonable belief is the foundation for the suspension action, not the merits of the case proving the alleged crime. Once that belief is resolved through the conclusion of the criminal matter, the agency must act within a reasonable time period to either return the employee to duty or take further administrative action (such as removal).

If the employee is found guilty or pleads guilty to the charges or to a lesser offense, the agency can then take action to remove the employee. However, if the employee is found not guilty, the agency must place the employee back at work immediately.

Removals. If the employee is found guilty, the charge in the removal document must match the exact criminal charge so that the criminal conviction papers prove the charge in the event of an appeal.

The employee may use the appeals process to contest any presumption of nexus by presenting sufficient evidence, so care is always needed in investigating and drafting charges. Check with your HR office to determine your best course of action.

If an employee goes to jail for criminal behavior, the easiest way for the agency to remove the employee is for absence without leave. The agency does not have to grant leave as long as it establishes that it needs the employee at work.

Law enforcement and criminal charges. If you work in a law enforcement agency, you will find it easier to discipline an employee for off-duty criminal misconduct, because criminal misconduct is usually inconsistent with the law enforcement mission. This is especially true if your employee is a law enforcement officer.

Here are some cases in which nexus was established when law enforcement personnel engaged in off-duty misconduct:

  • • A law enforcement officer allegedly threw a vase at his then wife, threw her down on the floor, choked her, pointed a gun at her and then pointed a gun at himself. The employee pled guilty to criminal possession of a weapon in the fourth degree and was sentenced to three years' probation.
  • • While off duty, a law enforcement agency employee was involved in a traffic accident and was charged with driving under the influence of alcohol or a controlled substance. Nexus was established based on the nature of the misconduct and the nature of the agency's mission of law enforcement.
  • • A law enforcement agent associated with a known or suspected law violator when he was off duty, in violation of agency regulations. Associations between agents and suspected criminals, especially those suspected of felony drug offenses, undermine the public's confidence in the agency's ability to fulfill its mission.

On premises, or in presence of coworkers

An employee gets into a drunken fight off duty. There are no serious injuries, no legal consequences. These things happen. Usually, this is the end of the story, but not always.

Sometimes an employee engages in misconduct while off duty, but on agency premises or when coworkers are present. Although the off-duty misconduct might not be enough to establish nexus on its own, the fact that it occurred at an agency facility or in front of other employees may be sufficient to show nexus.

Here are some examples:

  • • After her agency proposed to reprimand her, an employee was off duty when she entered the canteen on her agency's campus. She approached a supervisor, who said hello. The employee responded: "You know I didn't get my law degree out of a crackerjack box. You're going to wish you hadn't started this." Based on this conduct, the agency suspended her for one day for disrespectful conduct. The employee argued that the suspension was improper because she was off duty. However, a nexus was established since the incident happened at her employer's facility and involved a supervisor.
  • • An employee and a coworker became involved in a fight involving deadly weapons at a union picnic. The situation was enough to demonstrate nexus. The employee's coworkers complained about the incident to their supervisor. The employee's misconduct against his fellow employee, in front of many coworkers at a union event, had a chilling effect upon relationships at the workplace.
  • • While off duty, an employee was intoxicated and staggering near the sidewalk adjacent to a busy street when he was picked up by the security police and transported to the police headquarters on the base where he worked. His supervisor was called to the police headquarters to assist with the processing. The incident took place on the agency's premises, presented a possible danger to the employee and others, and involved the use of agency personnel to deal with his misconduct.

Warning: Remember that supervisors and managers are held to a higher standard. Any off-duty misconduct they engage in on or off agency premises, or that in any way involves one or more of their employees, could land them in serious trouble.

Domestic issues and sexual misconduct

What happens behind closed doors between employees and their family members or sexual partners is normally a closed book to you, and that's the way it should be. Unfortunately, it doesn't always work out that way.

If there is egregious misconduct or the circumstances affect your confidence and trust in an employee, a nexus between the conduct and the efficiency of the service may be established.

Domestic issues. Domestic misconduct often involves criminal violence. Here's one example:

  • • When off duty, a worker engaged in a heated argument and physical struggle with his former girlfriend. In an attempt to stop the altercation, the former girlfriend's 22-year-old daughter struck the employee. The employee fired two shots at the women as they fled. He pleaded guilty to domestic violence and assault to do great bodily harm and served six months in jail. In light of the violent, life-threatening nature of the crime, nexus was established. The conduct for which the employee was convicted -- shooting at and injuring a human being -- conflicted with his agency's mission of providing medical care to the sick and injured.

But domestic misconduct does not have to be violent or criminal for a nexus to be established. Consider this example:

  • • A program manager at a military facility had an affair with the wife of an officer assigned to a unit supported by the employee while the officer was deployed overseas. The employee held a managerial position in an office that was responsible for providing support to military families, including the families of service members who were deployed overseas. The necessary trust and confidence of the families and commanders were undermined by the employee's conduct.

Sexual misconduct. Off-duty sexual misconduct, particularly involving minors, is usually enough to presume the existence of a nexus, but the underlying facts must be considered.

Here are cases in which the employee's off-duty sexual misconduct established a nexus:

  • • A law enforcement agent videotaped two female coworkers and another woman, without their knowledge or consent, during off-duty sexual encounters. Rumors spread around the office, which upset the women. The agent's conduct had an adverse impact on the job performance of both the female coworkers and other employees, since one of the female coworkers had discussed what had happened at work.
  • • A supervisor pleaded no contest to child molestation after he had a sexual encounter with a 12-year-old boy to whom he paid $10. The mission of his agency is to administer health and social services for the disadvantaged, elderly, indigent, and children, and to oversee the administration of those services by others. The employee's supervisor testified that the victim of the employee's offense was among those the agency tries to help. The employee's conduct compromised agency programs and adversely affected the employer-employee relationship.

This seems obvious enough. But avoid any assumption of nexus. When the sexual misconduct involves a low-grade felony offense, it may not be serious enough for nexus to be established. Here's an example:

  • • An employee was indefinitely suspended because the agency had reasonable cause to believe he had committed a crime for which he could serve prison time. The employee was charged with having carnal knowledge of a 14-year-old girl. He pled guilty to statutory rape. However, there was no evidence that any of the employee's coworkers objected to his continued employment. There also was no evidence that the employee's duties brought him into contact with children in a way that would establish a nexus.

Security clearances and debt

Keep in mind that off-duty misconduct can lead to consequences other than discipline. Even in cases where no discipline results, off-duty incidents can adversely affect an employee's security clearance. This can be a major consideration for employees in the departments of Defense or Homeland Security, and many other agencies.

If an employee's off-duty misconduct makes you question their reliability or trustworthiness, you may restrict their access to classified information and take appropriate disciplinary action. But even if the conduct is not deserving of disciplinary action -- for example, indebtedness or a DUI -- and has nothing to do with the employee's job, it could still trigger a review of the employee's security clearance.

If the security clearance is taken away as a result of the misconduct, and the position requires access to classified materials, then the employee may be removed for failure to meet a condition of employment. During the adjudication process, the employee may be suspended indefinitely.

Debt. Private debts are usually a private matter. But we've already noted that indebtedness may cause the loss of a security clearance. Even when security clearances are not involved, there may still be a problem.

If your employee has not promptly paid money owed to your agency or when arrangements with outside firms are jeopardized because of employee debt, a nexus can be established since the efficiency of an agency's operation is adversely affected.

Creditor calls at work may disrupt the workplace if coworkers have to answer and handle those phone calls. It also may be an embarrassment to the agency that one of its employees is not meeting debt obligations.

Mistakes to avoid

Here are tips to help you decide whether an employee's off-duty conduct warrants adverse action:

Do not:

  • • Act too quickly in deciding to discipline. You need to gather the facts about what occurred, and you should never simply assume nexus, especially with off-duty misconduct.
  • • Impose an inappropriate indefinite suspension. An indefinite suspension is an adverse action. It cannot be imposed on an employee who has been charged with a crime if there is no nexus with the efficiency of the service.
  • • Ignore off-duty misconduct that disrupts the workplace. Even if the misconduct doesn't directly affect the employee's duties, if coworkers know about it, there may be serious disruption at work.

Ask FMG

Q. As a manager, how can I determine if there is a nexus between my employee's misconduct and my agency's interests?

A. Generally, this is not your job. A nexus is a logical connection between the misconduct and the negative impact it has on the agency's operations or mission, the "efficiency of the service." Determination of nexus requires legal analysis by your servicing HR employee relations specialists or agency legal office. If you believe or suspect there is an adverse impact, check with HR immediately. However, the decision to take adverse action is a management responsibility.

Q. Are my employee's duties relevant when it comes to determining nexus?

A. Yes. An example of a clear nexus is shoplifting by a law enforcement officer or a postmaster. There may not be a nexus if the shoplifting was undertaken by a clerk without law enforcement, managerial, or financial responsibilities. But there may be a nexus if the clerk worked in a PX or a warehouse.

Q. Criminal charges based on off-duty conduct have been filed against one of my employees. Can I suspend him without pay?

A. To indefinitely suspend an employee based on a criminal charge, your agency must show a nexus between the charge and the efficiency of the service. You cannot just assume that the employee poses a danger to the workplace or that his presence would be disruptive. Consider administrative leave while the matter is investigated.

Q. An employee was sentenced to a year in prison for a DUI. She has a desk job that doesn't involve driving. Will I be able to remove her for her off-duty misconduct?

A. Possibly, but it might be easier to remove her for absence without leave, since she won't be able to come to work while she is imprisoned. An agency does not have to grant leave to a jailed or imprisoned employee as long as it establishes that it needs the employee at work. Contact your HR office to discuss the best course to take.

Q. One of my employees defaulted on a personal loan. He needs a security clearance to carry out the duties of his job. Is this going to be a problem?

A. Off-duty misconduct can lead to consequences other than discipline. If his security clearance is taken away as a result of the conduct, the employee can be removed for failure to meet a condition of his employment. During the adjudication process, you can suspend the employee indefinitely. Check with HR on how to take these steps.
