III. PROCEDURES FOR APPEAL--AGENCIES

The procedure for an agency to file an appeal with OFO consists of a two-step process. The agency must issue a final action in the form of an order advising the complainant that it will not fully implement the decision of the administrative judge. The agency must simultaneously file a notice of appeal with OFO. 29 CFR 1614.110(a). Failure to both is fatal to an agency appeal. See Kauppila v. Secretary of Interior, 0120111917 (2013) (agency indicated it would appeal damages decision in final action but never filed appeal).

Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.403:

(a) The complainant, agency, agent, grievant or individual class claimant (hereinafter appellant) must file an appeal with the Director, Office of Federal Operations, Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, at P.O. Box 19848, Washington, DC 20036, or by personal delivery or facsimile. The appellant should use EEOC Form 573, Notice of Appeal/Petition, and should indicate what is being appealed.

. . .

(d) Any statement or brief on behalf of the agency in support of its appeal must be submitted to the Office of Federal Operations within 20 days of filing the notice of appeal. The Office of Federal Operations will accept statements or briefs in support of an appeal by facsimile transmittal, provided they are no more than 10 pages long.

(e) The agency must submit the complaint file to the Office of Federal Operations. . .within 30 days of submission of an appeal by the agency.

(f) Any statement or brief in opposition to an appeal must be submitted to the Commission and served on the opposing party within 30 days of receipt of the statement or brief supporting the appeal, or, if no statement or brief supporting the appeal is filed, within 60 days of receipt of the appeal. The Office of Federal Operations will accept statements or briefs in opposition to an appeal by facsimile provided they are no more than 10 pages long.

As with complainants, agency appeals should be sent to:

Equal Employment Opportunity Commission

Office of Federal Operations

P.O. Box 77960

Washington, DC 20013

Appeals may also be faxed to the Commission, or hand-delivered to the Office of Federal Operations. The Commission has proposed to amend its regulations to require that agencies submit all appeals, complaint files and appellate filings electronically. See 74 Fed. Reg. 67843 (December 21, 2009). The amendment would also permit but not require complainants to file electronically.

The agency must inform the complainant when it decides not to fully implement an administrative judge's decision and files an appeal. This notification must contain appeal rights. MD-110 at 9-10 states:

The agency must certify to the Commission that it has provided the appellant with a copy of the order in which it advised the appellant that it did not intend to fully implement the Administrative Judge's decision, that it informed the appellant of his/her right to file an appeal of its decision and provided the appellant with information as to how s/he may file an appeal, and that it provided the appellant with a copy of EEOC Form 573, Notice of Appeal/Petition (Appendix P).

Although most agency appeals involve cases with a finding of discrimination by the administrative judge, an agency can appeal a finding of no discrimination. See Murdock v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 07A10085 (2002). In fact, even when an agency rejects the decision of an administrative judge with findings unfavorable to the complainant, the agency and not the complainant must initiate the appeal. For example, in Pugliese v. Attorney General, 01A51412 (2006), the administrative judge granted the agency's motion to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction on the basis that the complainant was not an agency employee. Although the agency adopted the judge's decision, it noted that the complainant had not been given an opportunity to respond. The complainant appealed and the Commission reversed and remanded the case to the agency with an order to supplement the record on the complainant's status as an employee for Title VII purposes. The agency issued a new decision noting that the complainant could be an employee for Title VII purposes. However, it did not file an appeal, but instead advised the complainant of her appeal rights. In issuing the second decision, the Commission noted that the agency was essentially rejecting the decision of the judge and, therefore, the onus was on the agency to simultaneously file an appeal with OFO. The Commission found that the error was harmless, however, because neither the agency nor the employee was challenging the jurisdictional finding. The Commission remanded the case for further investigation and a determination on the merits of the underlying claims.
