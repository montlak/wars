1. Notice of Remand

EEOC administrative judges are under time limits for processing cases. Under 29 CFR 1614.109(i), administrative judges are required to issue decisions within 180 days of receipt of the request for a hearing. In order to avoid running afoul of those time limits, administrative judges, under the previous version of Part 1614, would remand the complaint to the agency with instructions to resubmit a request for assignment of a judge when the parties were ready for hearing. When the delay was due to the complainant, the judge would instruct the agency to give the complainant a reasonable amount of time to resubmit the request for a hearing. If such a request was not forthcoming, the agency could not dismiss the complaint for failure to prosecute but was required to issue a final decision based on the complaint record. See Todd v. Postmaster General, 01892616 (1989). The 1999 version of Part 1614 did away with remands by administrative judges. However, Todd expresses the current policy insofar as it stands for the proposition that an agency cannot dismiss a complaint due to the complainant's failure to proceed to a hearing in a timely fashion, but must instead issue a final decision on the merits based on the existing record.

In Stahl v. Postmaster General, 01961248 (1998), the Commission implicitly approved of the standing practice of remanding a case to the agency for a period of time and giving the complainant an opportunity to submit a request for a hearing when he or she is ready to proceed. In Stahl, some two weeks before the scheduled hearing, the complainant's representative submitted a request for a continuance because the complainant's mental and physical health had been affected by the recent deaths of two family members. The administrative judge, finding the complainant unable to proceed at that time, remanded the case to the agency and gave the complainant 90 days to request a new hearing. The Commission implicitly approved this procedure when it found the agency erred in issuing a final decision on the case after the complainant timely submitted a new request for a hearing. Certainly, such a procedure makes sense and is not unlike a procedure used by the MSPB. In Board cases where there is good cause for delays that are likely to be lengthy, the administrative judge can dismiss the case without prejudice for refiling. Generally, the order of dismissal will set the time limits and any other conditions for reinstating the appeal.

There are many instances where judicial economy will favor postponing a hearing on a complaint. For example, it generally makes sense for a complainant to have related complaints heard together. If one case is in a different procedural posture than the other, however, this may prove difficult given the administrative judge's internal time limits. If a case before an administrative judge, for example, is related to a case that is up on appeal, the administrative judge will not want to have the case sitting dormant on his or her docket while OFO decides whether the related complaint will be heard. One way around such a dilemma is to have the agency and the complainant enter into a settlement agreement which provides that the complainant will withdraw his or her initial hearing request pending the resolution of the related case. When the decision of the related case issues, the agency will provide the complainant with the right to submit a new hearing request within a certain number of days of the decision. If the hearing request is submitted, the agency will forward the request and the case file to the administrative judge who was initially assigned to the case. This scenario could be applied to a number of situations in which complaints by one complainant are in different stages of the process.

Under the previous version of Part 1614, prior to remanding a case to the agency for a final decision on the basis of failure to prosecute, the administrative judge was required to give the appellant written notice that failure to proceed in a timely fashion would result in such a remand. See Hill v. USPS, 01840591 (1985). If the administrative judge failed to give such a notice, the Commission would remand the case to the agency to issue a notice that renewed the employee's right to a hearing.

A failure to follow the instructions of the administrative judge may not, standing alone, provide grounds for dismissal for failure to prosecute. The Commission also will consider any other actions taken by the complainant that exhibit an intention to proceed with the complaint. If such actions are present, even if they do not fully comply with the orders of the administrative judge, then dismissal is improper. In Sapp v. Secretary of Energy, 01945362 (1996), the Commission discussed the type of actions that would exhibit an intention to pursue the complaint:

...Although in this case appellant did not submit a witness list or a letter stating her intention nonetheless to go forward, the Commission finds that, under the unique facts of this case, appellant adequately manifested her intention to go forward, so that the AJ should not have canceled the hearing.

The record reflects that when appellant's case oriGINAlly was assigned to AJ 1, appellant was informed that if she did not submit a list of proposed witnesses within 14 days, she could be limited to her own testimony at the hearing. Although AJ 2's April 13, 1994, letter to the parties did advise appellant to reply whether or not she intended to submit a proposed witness list, and did mention possible sanctions, two events transpired which persuade the Commission that the hearing nonetheless should not have been canceled. First, AJ 2 advised the agency that it need not resubmit the proposed witness list submitted to AJ 1, and sent a copy of this advisory to appellant. The Commission finds that AJ 2's advisory reasonably may have led appellant to believe that actions taken by the parties when the case was assigned to AJ 1 would continue in effect now that the case was assigned to AJ 2. Second, and more importantly, during the 15-day period in which appellant was supposed to advise AJ 2 whether she intended to proceed with the hearing even if she did not request witnesses, appellant sent a letter to AJ 2 concurring in the agency's request for a new hearing date and suggesting a time frame; this letter clearly manifested appellant's intention to proceed with the hearing, despite the fact that she had not requested any witnesses. Accordingly, the Commission finds that AJ 2 erred when she canceled appellant's hearing for "failure to cooperate," in other words, failure to prosecute. The Commission therefore will vacate the FAD and remand the case for a new offer of a hearing.

Even in cases where the complainant demonstrated a lack of intent to proceed with the complaint, dismissal for failure to prosecute may be improper. The Commission has a reluctance to have complaints dismissed on procedural grounds. As a result, under the 1992 regulations, even when the hearing was canceled and the complaint remanded to the agency, it was necessary for the agency to issue a decision on the merits of the complaint if the record was sufficiently developed through investigation. Under 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(7), an agency is required to issue a decision on the merits, in lieu of dismissal, where the record is adequately developed. Because the administrative judge's dismissal powers also derive from 1614.107(a), the same prohibition against dismissal for failure to prosecute when there is an adequate factual record for a decision on the merits would appear to apply.
