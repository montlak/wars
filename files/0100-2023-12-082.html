m. Mission Critical Work

Not all of the factors have equal weight in a given situation as is demonstrated in Baiamonte v. Postmaster General, 01A61526 (2006), where the Commission highlighted the fact that the work performed by the contract employee was "an integral part of the business of the agency." In Baiamonte, the Commission determined that the complainant, who had a private contract to deliver mail, was an employee for Title VII purposes:

The Commission has applied the common law of agency test to determine whether complainants are agency employees under Title VII. See Ma v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 01962390 (June 1, 1998) (citing Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. v. Darden, 503 U.S. 318, 323-24 (1992)). Specifically, the Commission will look to the following non-exhaustive list of factors: (1) the extent of the employer's right to control the means and manner of the worker's performance; (2) the kind of occupation, with reference to whether the work usually is done under the direction of a supervisor or is done by a specialist without supervision, (3) the skill required in the particular occupation; (4) whether the "employer" or the individual furnishes the equipment used and the place of work; (5) the length of time the individual has worked; (6) the method of payment, whether by time or by the job; (7) the manner in which the work relationship is terminated, i.e., by one or both parties, with or without notice and explanation; (8) whether annual leave is afforded; (9) whether the work is an integral part of the business of the "employer"; (10) whether the worker accumulates retirement benefits; (11) whether the "employer" pays social security taxes; and (12) the intention of the parties. See id.

In Ma, the Commission noted that the common law test contains, "no shorthand formula or magic phrase that can be applied to find the answer.... [A]ll of the incidents of the relationship must be assessed and weighed with no one factor being decisive." Id. The Commission in Ma also noted that prior applications of the test established in Spirides v. Reinhardt, 613 F.2d 826 (D.C. Cir. 1979), using many of the same elements considered under the common law test, was not appreciably different from the common law of agency test. See Id.

Therefore, we review the record in light of the factors listed above. As to factor (2), the record shows that the work conducted by the contract employee is under direct supervision and even noted that contract employees may be followed without notice. As to factor (4), the contractor provides the vehicle and fuel, however, the place of work is at the agency's facility. As to factor (5), complainant has been under contract with the agency for over two years and the term of the contract is for three years. The record shows that complainant, as the contractor, was paid directly from the agency's St. Louis Accounting Service Center based on the annual amount awarded on the contract. In this case, the relationship was terminated by the agency. With regard to factors (8), (10), or (11), there is no indication that the contractor receives any other benefits such as retirement or leave or that the agency pays social security taxes.

The most striking factors are factors (1) and (9). As to factor (1), the record contains documents containing the means and exact manner complainant's work must be performed. The agency even breaks down the exact route the contract carrier follows and the amount of time the stops along the route should take. The agency provides training which is similar to the training provided to its own rural carriers. The agency controls the type of vehicle the contractor may use including the age of the vehicle. The agency also dictates a uniform for its contract employees. Further, as to factor (9), the contract is for the "carrying and/or handling the United States Mail." This is most clearly work which is an integral part of the business of the agency. Based on the information within the record and the factors above, we find that complainant is an "employee" of the agency for purposes of Title VII.2 Accordingly, we find that the agency's dismissal pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(l) was inappropriate.

See also Complainant v. Secretary of Energy, 0120130998 (2014); Complainant v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120110146 (2014) ("[D]uring their almost two-year working relationship, the Agency controlled the means and manner of Complainant's performance, supervised his work, and furnished the equipment used and the place of work. Additionally, Complainant worked as a Clinical Psychologist who provided mental health counseling to Agency employees which was an important part of the Agency's goal of providing health care to its employees."); Randolph v. Attorney General, 0120110412 (2013) (agency controlled virtually every aspect of performance including right to terminate and complainant performed normal agency business--security during handling and transportation of detainees); Pietsch v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120090933 (2009) (finding sufficient control where the complainant had been on the agency premises for eight years, used agency space and equipment for work, and performed services that the Commission deemed "the regular business of the agency.").

However, the fact that the employee is not performing work that is part of the agency's mission does not preclude a finding that a contractor was an employee for Title VII purposes. For example, in Rachael F. v. National Intelligence Agency, 0120151373 (2015), the Commission found the complainant was an agency employee for Title VII purposes, explaining:

Factors 1, 3-7, 9 and 11 Indicate That the Agency Jointly Employs Complainant

Complainant processed FOIA cases. In a sheet completed with assistance from the Agency's contracting officer's technical representative (COTR), the Agency conceded that Complainant's work was done under the review of a government branch chief (Chief of the Information Review and Release Branch). While the Chief relayed to the EEO counselor that Complainant received guidance and work assignments from a contract lead as outlined in the contract statement of work, she did not illustrate what this meant in practice. On appeal, Complainant wrote that the above Agency Chief assigned cases to her, and that while her staffing firm offered a compressed work week schedule, the Chief would not permit her to work on a compressed schedule. She also wrote on appeal that the Chief asked contractors (meaning herself and co-workers) to complete performance appraisals to be conducted in the same manner and time period as government employees, and would then give feedback on their performance to the staffing firm (factors 1, 6, 7). Complainant worked on Agency premises using Agency equipment and supplies (factors 3 and 4). She started serving the Agency with a previous staffing firm on April 1, 2013, and continued to serve when another staffing firm won the contract until her termination in September 2013, and the record does not show she would not have continued working but for the termination (factor 5). There is no indication in the record that Complainant had any role in hiring or paying assistants (factor 9). The Agency is in government business (factor 11). In its FAD, the Agency wrote that the Agency requested Complainant's staffing firm to cease Complainant's service to the Agency because of poor performance. Complainant wrote that when the staffing firm terminated her employment, it explained she was being removed per the Agency's request. Given that this case involves a removal, the Agency's role in Complainant's removal is a significant factor in finding that it jointly employed Complainant.

Factors 2, 8, 10, 12 and 13 Indicate That the Agency May Not be Complainant's Joint Employer

The record suggests that Complainant's job required a high degree of skill--she suggested her salary was $110,000 annually, a professional rate (factor 2). The staffing firm took care of Complainant's compensation (factors 8, 12, and 13). Complainant was involved in processing Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests, which is not the mission of the Agency (factor 10).

Based on the legal standards and criteria set forth herein, we find that the Agency exercised sufficient control over Complainant's position to qualify as her joint employer for the purpose of the 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 EEO complaint process.

But see Tassy v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120112472 (2012) (finding that the complainant, an aircraft mechanic, was not performing regular agency business for the Coast Guard and noting that "the record herein does not contain evidence indicating that the maintenance and repair of Government aircraft is a vital or express part of the Agency's mission."); Powell v. Secretary of Army, 05930076 (1993) (insufficient agency control where, among other things, that the contractor's work was not an integral part of the agency's business).

But when the contractor controls most of the working relationship and the contract employee provides services not directly related to the agency's mission, the contract employee will not be an agency employee for Title VII purposes. As the Commission found in Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120142415 (2014):

Complainant's job as a counselor requires a high level of expertise (factor 2). The staffing firm was responsible for and administered all of Complainant's compensation (factors 8, 12, and 13). Complainant submitted her requests for leave with the staffing firm, which were acted upon by it (factor 7). While Complainant plays an important role in supporting soldiers and their families, this is not the mission of the Agency, which is to keep the peace and engage in foreign conflicts (factor 10). The staffing firm hired Complainant, played a robust role in her daily activities and operations at Fort Dix, and Complainant does not indicate she believed she was creating an employment relationship with the Agency (factor 15).
