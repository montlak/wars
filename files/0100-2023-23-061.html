f. Shifting Burdens of Proof

In McCullough v. Postmaster General, 05950539 (1996), the Commission reinforced its position that absence does not go to the issue of whether the employee is a qualified person with a disability. If the complainant establishes a nexus between the absences and his disability, the question then becomes one of whether the agency can, given the number of absences, reasonably accommodate the disability without imposing an undue hardship on its operations. As the Commission explained in McCullough:

Yet the previous decision has failed to recognize Commission precedent making it clear that in a case involving excessive absences from work, a complainant may prove that he or she is a "qualified disabled person"--in spite of such absences--by first showing that there is a sufficient nexus between the absences and the purported disability. See Southerland v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930714 (July 15, 1994); Omar v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920207 (May 14, 1992); Ruiz v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05880859 (May 21, 1990); Rivers v. Department of Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 03920152 (September 2, 1993); Shipp v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01912739 (December 19, 1991). The burden then becomes the agency's to demonstrate that it would pose "an undue hardship" for the agency to tolerate or excuse an employee's absences. See Southerland, EEOC Request No. 05930714, at 7-8; Omar, EEOC Request No. 05920207, at 8-9; Ruiz, EEOC Request No. 05880859, at 6. As we have previously stated, the agency's concern that an employee maintain regular attendance is understandable, but it creates a circular argument that would ultimately prevent any disabled employee who is frequently absent from being considered a "qualified person with a disability." Id. For this reason, then, a federal agency could always avoid the issue of reasonable accommodation and undue hardship in such cases. Id.

As authority for its statement that absences kept appellant from meeting the essential elements of her position and thus kept the agency from accommodating her, the previous decision cited Moody v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900177 (July 13, 1990) and Bankston v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Petition No. 03940162 (October 13, 1994). In the Moody case, however, there was no finding that there was a nexus between the purported disability, hypertension and diabetes, and appellant's frequent absences. Further, there was some suggestion in the decision that appellant's sicknesses in Moody, primarily bronchitis and viral illnesses, did not warrant the amount of time that appellant took off. For these reasons, then, we find Moody to be distinguishable from the facts in this case. Moreover, in Bankston, EEOC Petition No. 03940162, at 2-3, the decision explicitly states that there was no nexus between the absences at issue and the complainant's medical condition. Bankston too, then, is inapposite.

The record is therefore insufficiently developed on the issue of disability discrimination and failure to accommodate, and the previous decision erred by making a finding of no discrimination in light of all the foregoing precedent. On the issue of disability discrimination and the failure to accommodate, we will therefore reverse and remand for supplemental investigation according to the standards set forth in Southerland v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930714 (July 15, 1994); Omar v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920207 (May 14, 1992); and Ruiz v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05880859 (May 21, 1990). Primarily, the agency must investigate whether appellant's absences were in fact related to the medical condition that is the stated basis for her disability and whether it would have posed "an undue hardship" for the agency to tolerate or excuse appellant's absences that resulted in her discipline. On the remaining bases of discrimination, we will affirm the finding of no discrimination.

Likewise, in Allen v. Postmaster General, 01954091 (1997), the Commission held that the burden is on the employee to establish a nexus between his disability and his absenteeism. However, once the employee does so, the burden then shifts to the agency to demonstrate it would be an undue hardship to accommodate the absenteeism. In Allen, the Commission found:

In this case, the Commission concurs with the AJ that the evidence of record establishes that appellant has both physical and mental disabilities which substantially limit his ability to perform manual tasks and successfully deal with even minor stress. The agency argues, however, that appellant is not a "qualified" person with a disability because of his excessive absenteeism.

Commission precedent makes it clear that in a case involving excessive absences from work, a complainant may prove that s/he is a "qualified disabled person"--in spite of such absences--by first showing that there is a sufficient nexus between the absences and the purported disability. See McCullough v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05950539 (April 25, 1996); Southerland v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930714 (July 15, 1994); Omar v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920207 (May 14, 1992). The burden then becomes the agency's to demonstrate that it would pose "an undue hardship" for the agency to tolerate or excuse an employee's absences. See McCullough, EEOC Request No. 05950539 at 4; Southerland, EEOC Request No. 05930714 at 7-8; Omar, EEOC Request No. 05920207 at 8-9.

As authority for its statement that absences kept appellant from being considered "qualified," the agency cited Moody v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900177 (July 13, 1990). The Commission notes that the decision in the Moody case was based on a determination that there was no finding of a nexus between the purported disability and the complainant's frequent absences. By contrast, appellant in the instant matter has testified that at least two-thirds of his absences were the result of disabling pain from his physical disability. In addition, appellant has also asserted that the stress created by his struggle with the agency for accommodations to his physical disability resulted in a resurfacing of compulsive behaviors associated with his mental disability. The agency asserts that appellant's testimony on this matter is insufficient to establish a nexus between his absences and his disabilities. However, the Commission notes that the supervisor who issued the Notice of Removal corroborated that, on a number of the dates cited in the notice, appellant did tell him that his neck was so stiff and he was in so much pain that he could not even get out of bed. The Supervisor, however, testified that he was not aware that the agency had a duty to accommodate non-job-related disabilities, and that he never really made an effort to ascertain how many of appellant's absences were due to his disabilities.

In addition, the Plant Manager, while acknowledging awareness of his affirmative duties under the Rehabilitation Act, indicated that he never considered excusing appellant's absences as an accommodation to his disabilities. Significantly, he admitted that he knew about the October 4, 1990 letter from appellant's physician which stated that appellant's cervical disc syndrome would continue to become "symptomatic" and might occasionally keep him from working. In light of this, the AJ correctly found that the Plant Manager failed to exercise his affirmative duty to make proper inquiries about the relationship between appellant's absences and his disabilities to discern whether it was possible to accommodate the absences. Rather, it appears from the evidence in this case, that once appellant's claim for worker's compensation was denied (a denial which was eventually overturned on appeal), the agency became much more reluctant to accommodate appellant's disabilities.

Therefore, the Commission finds that the record supports the AJ's finding of disability discrimination in this matter. The AJ found that appellant credibly testified that two-thirds of his absences were directly related to his disabilities. This testimony was corroborated, at least in part, by the testimony of appellant's supervisor and appellant's physician's statement that his cervical disc syndrome would continue to become symptomatic and could keep appellant out of work. In the context of the agency's full awareness of appellant's disabilities, and his continuing dialogue with management concerning a variety of accommodations, the agency, prior to issuing a Notice of Removal, had a duty to conduct an inquiry into the relationship between appellant's absences and his disabilities and the possibility of accommodating the absenteeism. It is significant to note that the agency has not advanced an argument that it would have created an undue hardship on the operation of the Bridgeport facility for appellant's disability-related absences to be excused. For these reasons, the Commission concurs with its AJ's finding that the agency failed in its affirmative duty to accommodate appellant's disabilities under the Rehabilitation Act.
