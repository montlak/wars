AWARDS

Do you have an employee who is doing exceptional work but you don't know how to reward her?

Do you have an employee who has come up with a great idea to help your agency save money?

Good news. You can recommend both of these employees for awards.

Under your agency's incentive awards program, you can provide your employees with the recognition they deserve. This is particularly important now, when pay raises and promotions may not be feasible.

Awards programs provide incentives and recognition to employees for their positive performance, and acknowledge their contributions to the agency's mission. Behind them is a basic principle of management: If you want employees to repeat superior performance, reward them for it. Good employees have a certain degree of internal self-motivation to do a good job, but it's even better to receive some kind of recognition.

Your agency's incentive awards program is governed by specific statutes and regulations. Programs generally allow managers the flexibility to recognize employees for a wide variety of accomplishments, as well as to create incentives. Awards usually take four forms:

  • • Monetary awards. Awards of cash and cash surrogates.
  • • Non-monetary awards. Symbolic recognition.
  • • Time-off awards. A period of time off from duty without a loss of pay or charge to leave.
  • • Suggestion awards. Recognition for suggestions that save money.

Awards should be:

  • • Specific. There should be a direct connection between the reward and the employee's actions.
  • • Meaningful. The achievements rewarded should provide an important return on investment to both the performer and the organization. There is no point in providing rewards that are not valued by the recipient, or in rewarding behavior that has no clear benefit to the agency.
  • • Achievable. The employee's or group's goals should be within the reach of the performers.
  • • Earned. The awards program should operate according to its principles and purpose. Too often they are allowed to degenerate into routine bonuses for merely acceptable performance (however highly it may be rated in annual appraisals).
  • • Timely. The rewards should be provided soon enough after the achievement to make performers feel valued for their efforts.

Monetary awards

Agencies usually allow supervisors to grant small "on the spot" cash awards on their own authority for specific, limited achievements (such as a particularly good example of customer service). Larger cash awards generally require at least one additional layer of management approval. The payments are usually made through electronic funds transfers.

The larger awards are generally reserved for achievements requiring a more sustained effort over time. Check your agency's specific policy and with HR for details.

By law, federal agencies may grant cash awards up to $10,000 for an employee contribution, without prior approval of the Office of Personnel Management.

The $10,000 limitation applies to each member of a group in cases of group awards. Agencies must receive approval from OPM to grant awards between $10,001 and $25,000 per individual. Awards of more than $25,000 per individual require approval from OPM and the president. Awards of this nature require advance consultation with HR.

Cash surrogates. Agencies may present their cash award recipients with "cash surrogates." Examples of cash surrogates are "award vouchers" created by the agency that can be exchanged for currency and "debit cards" that are purchased through a vendor and that are easily and widely redeemable for cash, rather than simply for merchandise.

Cash surrogates are an appropriate option for delivering cash awards. Recipients of cash surrogates have the same freedom and control over how the award may be used as they would have over any currency.

Cash surrogates must meet these criteria:

  • • They are subject to all the limitations and requirements that apply to cash awards.
  • • They must be easily and immediately convertible to cash.
  • • They may not be limited to redemption only where purchased, at a few selected sites outside the agency, or through specific vendors.
  • • If purchased from a vendor or financial institution, they are subject to all relevant procurement regulations.

Warning: Cash surrogates should not be confused with merchant gift certificates and other common merchandise vouchers. Such items usually cannot meet a cash surrogate's criteria because of limitations on where, how, and for what they may be redeemed. The only circumstance where a gift certificate or voucher may be used to recognize an employee contribution is as an as an on-the-spot recognition award..

Performance awards. In addition to incentive awards, some agencies use performance awards. Awards based on the performance of assigned duties are usually linked directly to the annual performance rating. For example, a rating of 4.5 or "exceeding expectations" translates to an award of XX dollars computed either as a cash amount or as a percentage of salary. With this kind of procedure, the supervisor has no discretion regarding the award. Rather, the discretion is exercised when doing the rating.

In another variation, once employees have attained a certain performance level, either based on a numerical score or adjective rating, the supervisor decides, within budget limits, which is to be recommended for an award and approximately how much the award will be. This process is more work (writing up the recommendations), but provides more discretion to the supervisor. In either case, your HR advisor is the best source of expert guidance.

Non-monetary awards

As cash awards budgets tighten, interest in the use of non-monetary awards may grow. There is no reason you can't exercise your own imagination, and your knowledge of your employee's personal interests, to come up with "cool stuff" they'll want to have.

Non-monetary awards can include:

  • • Plaques.
  • • Mugs.
  • • Medals in presentation cases.
  • • Hats, T-shirts, and jackets.
  • • Career service certificates.
  • • Engraved crystal sculptures.
  • • Pen-and-pencil desk sets.
  • • Paperweights.

Non-monetary awards to federal employees have taken these and many other forms under broad agency authority. The law gives general authority to agency heads to "incur necessary expense for the honorary recognition of an employee." While there is no set dollar amount, these kinds of awards must be of nominal value.

Medals. Medals are a specialized form of non-monetary recognition with a high return. They may be appropriate for:

  • • Specific actions of great importance, such as saving lives in emergencies or demonstrating personal courage in dangerous situations, especially if the performance goes "above and beyond" normal expectations.
  • • Sustained service under hardship or hazardous conditions.
  • • Superior performance of regular duties over a period of some years.

Although medals cost relatively little, they have very high recognition value with colleagues, family members, and members of the public. It is important that they not be devalued through overuse, and they are usually subject to tight agency controls. But if you have extraordinary employees, learn what you need to do to obtain this level of recognition for them. If you are successful, their recognition will reflect positively on you as well as on them.

Benefits. Honorary, informal recognition can be a powerful tool to promote organizational and team goals and objectives. Non-monetary recognition can often be more effective than cash awards.

Non-monetary awards generally have four major advantages over cash awards:

  • • Memory value. The value of an honorary or informal recognition award is longer-lasting than cash since cash is spent and gone, whereas the recognition items remain on employees' desks or in their homes.
  • • Trophy value. Non-monetary awards can be displayed to coworkers and friends as trophies given in appreciation of good work.
  • • Flexibility. The type of non-monetary recognition given can be designed to emphasize particular organizational or team goals. The award recognizes good performance and promotes teamwork. And you may be able to tailor the award to the particular circumstances or tastes of the employees you are rewarding, so they will value both it and you, as the person who took the trouble to give the right thing.
  • • Value for money. Cash awards cost more.

Non-monetary items presented as honorary awards must meet all of the following criteria:

  • • The item must be something that the recipient could reasonably be expected to value, but not something that conveys a sense of monetary value.
  • • The item must have a lasting trophy value.
  • • The item must clearly symbolize the employer-employee relationship in some fashion.

Warning: The item must take an appropriate form for use in the public sector and purchase with public funds. Use common sense here and control your sense of humor. Gag items in questionable taste are definitely out. So is anything that suggests religion, partisan politics, or anything else your agency would not want to be identified with.

Recognition. Non-monetary awards go further if they are widely recognized. They have little or no cash value, but the recognition they bring to top employees can be priceless. Wide publicity increases that recognition, and, therefore, the value of the award in the eyes of the recipient. It also encourages other employees to value the award and improve their own performance.

The form and extent of the recognition depends on the nature of the achievement being recognized. How important is it? To whom is it important -- members of the unit only, members of a wider employee group, members of the public?

Group and individual awards can be publicized through:

  • • Print and electronic newsletters and special mailings.
  • • Press releases to the general media.
  • • Internal award ceremonies.
  • • Public award ceremonies.

Warning: Don't overdo it. There is a difference between a logo mug or ball cap and a lifesaving medal. Too much recognition for a relatively small achievement or minor award can devalue your whole awards program.

Time-off awards

Many agencies allow an award of time off to be used as an alternative to monetary or non-monetary recognition for superior accomplishments. This award grants the employee time off from duty without a loss of pay or charge to leave.

The period of time off granted is usually based upon the value of the employee's contribution, not on her grade or salary. And employees may not convert a time-off award to a cash payment.

Your agency's awards policy probably limits the amount of time off that may be approved for any single contribution, as well as the amount of time off that an employee may receive in any leave year. Check with HR.

Advantages

  • • Equity. Since the "currency" for these awards is time, you can award the same number of hours to employees in different grades for the same, or comparable, achievements. This can help eliminate perceived inequities associated with grade differences.
  • • No additional taxation. Time-off awards do not have explicit cash value and do not change the employee's income. As a result, these awards are not subject to additional tax withholdings.
  • • Immediate reinforcement. Time-off awards often can be granted quickly and so are linked to the time and place of the accomplishment.
  • • Value. Some employees may value paid time off more than a cash award. For example, new employees traditionally have lower annual leave balances and may prefer additional paid time off.

Disadvantages

  • • Loss of productivity. Your employee won't be working during her time-off award period.
  • • Value. Employees with a lot of available leave -- especially "use it or lose it leave" -- may not appreciate an award of more time off.
  • • Non-transferability. If your employee takes a job with another agency before using the time off award, he can't use the award at his new agency. In other words, the award does not transfer to the new agency.

Are time off awards appropriate for your employees? Think through the issues and then contact HR to discuss any specific concerns.

Suggestion awards

Suggestion awards recognize ideas to improve government efficiency and save money. The amount of the award is often based on the amount of money saved.

Decisions on these awards are often made by a designated agency official, rather than the manager. But you still have an important part to play in the process. As a manager, you need to keep an eye on your agency's bottom line, and that's what suggestion awards are all about. Be sure to know the details of your agency's program, and encourage your employees to be inventive and put their ideas forward.

The major benefits of suggestion programs are:

  • • They save the agency money.
  • • They promote internal communication, involve employees beyond their normal duties, and empower them to work for change.
  • • They improve operations and efficiency.

Not every employee idea is a good candidate for a suggestion award. Explain to your employees that suggestion awards are based on ideas that go beyond an employee's assigned duties. If an employee is tasked with developing money-saving ways to improve a particular process, then that's the employee's job. There are ways to reward superior performance in that job, but suggestion awards are not the way to go.

Suggestion awards are appropriate for ideas that affect processes that are either entirely outside the employee's area of activity, or at a level above that with which the employee normally deals. For example, an aircraft maintenance technician who comes up with an idea for saving money in the agency's building maintenance program may be a good candidate for an incentive award. The same may be true of the technician's ideas for reducing the costs of aircraft parts storage or acquisition, since neither of these comes under the normal duties of the position.

Remind your employees there must be a clear cash savings or other tangible benefit to the government. A good suggestion, made on your agency's form, will contain:

  • • A statement of the problem or the present situation.
  • • A proposed solution to the problem or improvement in the situation.
  • • The possible benefits of the idea to the government.

Many good suggestions are of the simple "Why didn't I think of that?" category. But others -- especially those of a technical nature, where powerful savings may be achieved -- may require a good deal of work before they are properly developed. Should the employee be permitted to use duty time for this purpose?

That's mostly a judgment call. You are the supervisor, so it's your judgment. This calls for common sense. A lot depends on your workflow at the moment, and your immediate needs may have priority. You don't want to offload work unfairly onto other employees while your resident genius writes up the idea of the century. It makes sense to encourage employees to participate in a suggestion awards program by allowing them a certain amount of duty time, as long as it doesn't get out of hand.

Mistakes to avoid

Awards programs are a great way to get the best performance possible out of your employees. But they have their pitfalls.

Do not:

  • • Reward routine performance. As much as possible, avoid awards programs that reward everyone who shows up and doesn't screw up. And don't give employees awards because it's "their turn."
  • • Overvalue achievements. There is a difference between a logo coffee cup and a distinguished service medal. Keep the reward in proportion to the accomplishment.
  • • Use gift certificates as incentive awards. Certificates redeemable only for merchandise cannot be used in incentive award programs. Merchandise of significant value should also not be used. Cash surrogates, such as vouchers, must be readily convertible to cash. However, gift certificates of nominal value may be used in informal awards programs.
  • • Fail to publicize awards. Recognition works better if people know about it.
  • • Fail to reward any improved performance. It doesn't matter that much how you do it--and you don't want to overdo it--but you should find at least informal ways to recognize any improvement in the performance of an employee in training, struggling with a new task, or genuinely trying to improve poor performance. A simple "that's better" or "I think you are getting it" may be all you need.

Ask FMG

Q. Can I use agency funds to buy gift certificates as incentive awards for employees?

A. No. Only cash and cash surrogates (vouchers readily exchangeable for cash) can be used in incentive awards programs. However, gift certificates of nominal value may be used in informal awards programs.

Q. What is a cash surrogate and how does it work?

A. Cash surrogates are "award vouchers" created by your agency that can be exchanged for currency and "gift checks" that are purchased through a vendor and that are easily and widely redeemable for cash, rather than simply for merchandise. They must be easily convertible to cash and cannot be limited in when and where they can be used.

Q. Can I overdo awards?

A. Yes you can, and it happens all the time. Don't cheapen your program by over-rewarding small achievements, or by handing out awards to everyone who shows up for work on time. Employees will see no reason to improve their performance, which is the point of awards programs.

Q. Can I refuse to nominate undeserving employees for incentive awards, even though other supervisors are handing them out freely?

A. You may have little choice if your awards program is governed by an agency policy or labor agreement linking annual ratings with awards. Check with HR. But if you have the discretion, recommend awards for those who deserve them.

Q. Why would some top employees value a medal or certificate over a cash incentive award?

A. Many incentive award programs have been cheapened in their recognition value by overuse. A medal may not be worth much in cash, but it provides a memento of special achievement that may mean something to the recipient, and brings ready recognition from colleagues, family members, and even the general public.

Q. Can my employee convert a time-off award to cash?

A. No, time-off awards may not be converted to cash or salary. This is true even if the employee cannot use the time off before leaving the agency.

Q. Do I need to worry about EEO issues when I'm recommending my employees for awards?

A. Yes. Award decisions must be made without regard to race, color, religion, national origin, sex, age, disability, or other protected categories.
