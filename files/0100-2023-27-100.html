O. POSITION CLASSIFICATION

To establish a prima facie case that a position was reclassified based on discriminatory motives, the employee must show that she belongs to a protected group, that the job was audited, and that other persons not within her protected group but who were similarly situated remained classified at higher grades. Smith v. Department of Air Force, 01832869 (1985).

In a case where the employee alleges he was denied a promotion by virtue of the agency's refusal to reclassify his position, the focus is on the duties performed, not the actual performance of the employee. Employees are not entitled to have their position reclassified on the basis of good performance. Reclassifications are effected because of the nature of the duties performed. Lyddane v. EEOC, 01840916 (1985).

When an employee demonstrates that her position is properly graded, but similarly situated male employees are employed at a higher grade, she is entitled to back pay from the time of her assignment to the position until the time her comparison employees are downgraded. See Wewers v. General Services Administration, 01880297 (1988). Stated another way, the issue in such a case is not whether the position itself is properly graded, but whether the employee "performed duties that other employees were performing at a higher grade." See Gamble v. General Services Administration, 02880007 (1988).

As a general rule, an employee is entitled only to the rate of pay of his assigned position and not to the rate of pay that would be commensurate with the level of the duties actually performed. In addition to permanent promotions to a higher grade level, agencies also have discretion to make temporary promotions to higher grade levels. In those cases, the employee is paid at the higher grade rate for the period of the promotion and then reverts to the pay rate of his or her prior grade at the expiration of the temporary promotion. In a case involving temporary promotions, the Commission has defined the proper inquiry as being: "(1) whether appellant was assigned duties outside of his position description which were performed by other employees at [higher] grade levels; (2) whether the duties were performed to such an extent and with such regularity that he was subjected to a de facto detail or promotion to a higher graded position; and (3) whether other employees performing these same duties in the same manner were temporarily promoted to [higher] grade levels." Gamble, supra. Where the employee fulfills the first two requirements and then demonstrates that white employees received temporary promotions for similar work, the agency cannot prevail by focusing on whether the position was properly classified.
