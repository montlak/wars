3. Maximum Entry Age Limits--Law Enforcement Officers and Firefighters

Under 5 USC 3307(d), an agency head may establish a maximum age for an "oriGINAl appointment" to a law enforcement position. It allows an agency to determine minimum and maximum age requirements for law enforcement and firefighters, thereby carving out a statutorily-created exception to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act. Id.

In Garrett v. Secretary of Treasury, 01955214 (1997), the Commission held that 3307(d) establishes an exception to the ADEA. However, the Commission could not determine whether the appellant's application for a criminal investigator position should be considered as an "oriGINAl appointment" for purposes of deciding whether the agency was exempt from the requirements of the ADEA. The appellant, who was 50 years old at the time of his application for a GS-11 Criminal Investigator position with the Department of Treasury, was already employed as a GS-6 Police Officer with the Department of Veterans Affairs. In remanding the case for further briefing by the parties, the Commission in Garrett noted:

The Commission has held that a law enforcement officer is defined as an employee--an officer appointed in the civil service, who investigates, apprehends, or detains individuals suspected or convicted of federal crimes. See 5 USC § 2105(a), 8331(20); DiLuglio v. Dept. of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05930890 (August 22, 1994). The Office of Personnel Management has been designated by Executive Order as the entity to concur with a federal agency when it makes determinations fixing the age limits within which oriGINAl appointments may be made for law enforcement officers. See Exec. Order No. 11817 (November 5, 1974), 39 Fed. Reg. 39427 (1974); Exec. Order No. 12107 (December 28, 1978), 44 Fed. Reg. 1055 (1978). The legislative history of 5 USC § 3307(d) demonstrates that Congress sought to create a special age entry rule--an exception to the ordinary prohibition against age-based determinations under the ADEA--where a young work force was necessary due to the nature of the work, and where law enforcement officers would receive especially generous retirement benefits as an offset to a shorter work life. DiLuglio, supra.

In the case before us, appellant, a GS-6 Police Officer at the Department of Veterans Affairs, applied for a GS-11 Criminal Investigator position at the Department of the Treasury, Internal Revenue Service. Appellant was fifty years old at the time. While we agree with the agency that 5 USC § 3307(d) is an exception to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, we find that there is a significant question as to whether there was an "oriGINAl appointment" in the case before us. The Commission finds that because there are factual determinations yet to be made, we will remand this case for a supplemental investigation to give the parties an opportunity to explicitly address in detail whether appellant's application to the Department of the Treasury for a law enforcement position in a different job series, and at a higher grade, was an "oriGINAl appointment" for purposes of the exception to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, found in 5 USC § 3307(d). DiLuglio, supra.

In Campbell v. Attorney General, 05960550 (1997), the Commission, relying upon Stewart v. Smith, 673 F.2d 485 (D.C. Cir. 1982), upheld a policy of the Bureau of Prisons which applied the statutory age exception for "law enforcement officers" to all employees in its correctional facilities. The Commission in Campbell, however, found the complainant could challenge whether the exception was applied on a uniform basis.

In Hall v. Attorney General, 01A00528 (2000), the administrative judge issued a summary judgment decision in favor of the agency based on the statutory exception at 5 USC 3307(d). The Commission in Hall remanded the case, however, because there was an issue as to whether the complainant qualified for an exemption from the maximum age requirement:

After a careful review of the record, we find that the AJ erred when she concluded that complainant failed to state a claim. In making this finding, the AJ failed to recognize that complainant did not allege that the general application of the maximum age requirement for law enforcement officers violated the ADEA. Rather, complainant alleged that he was denied consideration for the position even though he was eligible for the exemption to the maximum age requirement due to his prior service in covered law enforcement. Therefore, because complainant challenged the actual processing and denial of application, and not the general applicability of the maximum age requirement, we find that complainant did in fact state a claim of age discrimination.

In Burbey v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A43103 (2004), the Commission upheld section 3307(d) as it applies to federal sky marshals.

Reentry to a law enforcement or firefighter position is also covered under 5 USC 3307(d), which allows individuals who have previously encumbered such positions to be reinstated as long as their tenure does not put them over the mandatory retirement age. In Ryan L. v. Secretary of Navy, 2019005521 (2020), for example, the Commission found that the agency did not violate the Age Discrimination Act when it rescinded a tentative job offer to a 52-year-old candidate because he exceeded the maximum reentry age.

[W]e find that the Agency rescinded the offer because Complainant exceeded the maximum reentry into a covered position age as provided by Federal statute. 5 U.S.C. § 3307. 5 U.S.C. § 3307(d) allows an Agency head to determine minimum and maximum age requirements for appointment to Firefighter positions.

In pertinent part, Department of Defense Instruction 1400.25, Volume 336 states:

a. Firefighter Positions. The maximum entry age for covered primary firefighter positions is 37. Persons not appointed by the last day of the month in which they become age 37 shall not be oriGINAlly appointed or assigned to these positions.

...

c. Reentry into a Covered Position

(1) Individuals who are past the maximum entry age limit (37 for firefighters and law enforcement officers) but who previously served in a covered primary position may be reinstated to a covered primary position if they:

(a) Meet the applicable qualification requirements for the position and;

(b) Will be able to complete a total of 20 years of covered service as a firefighter or law enforcement officer by the last day of the month in which they become age 37.

Complainant stated that he was 52 when he was given a tentative offer for the GS-7 Firefighter position. He stated that he consistently worked as a Firefighter with the Agency between June 1996 and October 2011, but had an OWCP injury-related absence between May 2006 and October 2006.

The Agency stated, in September 2016, it made a tentative job offer to Complainant. It stated that it sought Complainant's OPF to determine his eligibility under the maximum reentry age requirement pursuant to DoDI 1400.25, Volume 336. Sometime between the job offer and January 2017, the Agency calculated Complainant's covered service time without his OPF. It used personnel actions obtained from Complainant and another Agency and calculated creditable service time at 14 years, 9 months, and 4 days. The Agency determined that Complainant exceeded the maximum reentry age requirement and issued a letter of rescission of the job offer. The Agency stated, in early 2017 it obtained Complainant's OPF and confirmed his ineligibility. The Agency stated that Complainant would have had to reenter the covered position by April 21, 2016 to be eligible. The Agency stated that discriminatory factors did not motivate its actions.

While age-based discrimination is generally prohibited, the Agency has statutory authority, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 3307(d), to set age restrictions for firefighter positions under an exception to the ADEA. The Commission has previously recognized the validity of a statutory exception to the ADEA in its federal sector cases and has consistently held that complaints challenging statutorily-created exceptions to the ADEA fail to state a claim. Campbell v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05960550 (April 17, 1997).
