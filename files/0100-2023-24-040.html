III. INTERACTIVE PROCESS AND REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION REQUESTS

Although the burden is on the agency to provide reasonable accommodation, the process of selecting a particular accommodation is intended by the Commission to be an "interactive" process that requires equal participation on the part of the agency and the employee. Quite simply, the agency cannot conduct an individualized inquiry without the cooperation of the employee requesting accommodation.

The Commission provided extensive discussion on the reasonable accommodation process in its Technical Assistance Manual, "Disability Discrimination." In addition, on July 26, 2000, President Clinton issued Executive Order 13164 requiring every federal agency to establish written procedures for processing reasonable accommodation requests. To assist in implementing that Executive Order, on October 20, 2000, the EEOC issued Directive No. 915.003, Policy Guidance on Executive Order 13164: Establishing Procedures to Facilitate the Provision of Reasonable Accommodation. The Policy Guidance is available on the Commission's website at www.eeoc.gov.

The purpose of establishing written procedures for reasonable accommodation requests is explained in the Policy Guidance at 2:

An accommodation is a change involving the workplace that enables a person with a disability to enjoy equal employment opportunities. Many individuals with disabilities can apply for and perform jobs without the need for an accommodation. However, where workplace barriers exist, such as physical obstacles or rules about how a job is to be performed, reasonable accommodation serves two fundamental purposes. First, reasonable accommodations remove barriers that prevent people with disabilities from applying for, or performing, jobs for which they are qualified. Second, reasonable accommodations enable agencies to expand the pool of qualified workers, thus allowing the agencies to benefit from the talents of people who might otherwise be arbitrarily barred from employment.

Effective procedures for processing reasonable accommodation requests will advance both these goals. They will enable agencies to handle requests in a prompt, fair, and efficient manner; they will assure that individuals with disabilities understand how to approach the system and know what to expect; and they will be a resource both for individuals with disabilities and for agency employees, so that all parties can understand the legal requirements of the Rehabilitation Act.

The agency's failure to engage in the interactive reasonable accommodation process can have two results. First, the agency may be found liable for failing to provide reasonable accommodation if the complainant is a qualified individual with a disability who was entitled to accommodation. If the failure to accommodate results in a loss of pay or benefits, the agency, in addition to providing accommodation, will be required to reimburse the complainant for the loss as part of the make-whole relief. The second result will likely be a finding of liability for compensatory damages. The 1991 Civil Rights Act provides a "good faith" defense to a claim of damages in a failure to accommodate case, but only if the agency, in consultation with the complainant, has made a genuine effort to provide the legally required accommodation. The good faith defense is discussed in detail, above, and in Chapter 21 under the subheading "Good Faith Exception."

The employee's failure to participate in the interactive process can relieve the agency of any liability for failure to make reasonable accommodation. In Faconti v. Postmaster General, 01976463 (1999), the Commission found that the agency had engaged in good faith, reasonable efforts to accommodate the complainant but, due to lack of communication on the part of the complainant, never understood precisely what accommodation he was seeking. As the Commission explained in Faconti:

The dispositive issue in this case is whether the agency had sufficient information from which it should have provided appellant with the accommodation of either working at home or at a worksite within walking distance of his home while his application for disability retirement was pending. The AJ agreed with appellant's assertion that the agency was provided such information in his application for disability retirement. The agency, on the other hand, argues, in essence, that it never understood exactly what accommodations appellant was requesting, and that it provided the best accommodations it could given its limited understanding of appellant's needs.

The Commission has held that the responsibility for fashioning an appropriate reasonable accommodation is shared between the employer and employee and is best determined through a flexible, interactive process. 29 CFR Appendix to Part 1630--Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans With Disabilities Act ("Guidance"), Section 1630.9. See also, Hupka v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 02960003 (August 13, 1997). In this case, the evidence establishes that the agency and appellant did engage in an interactive process. Appellant presented the agency with letters from his doctors outlining his medical restrictions, and the agency accommodated those restrictions, providing appellant with light duty assignments, as well as approving extended periods of sick leave when appellant was in need of bed rest. In March 1993, the evidence shows that the agency and appellant engaged in a discussion over appellant's request that he be permitted to come in to work for two or three days to assist a contractor in updating a computer software program. Finally, the record is also clear that during the agency-wide reorganization, the agency and appellant went back and forth examining a variety of positions to which he could be assigned which would best accommodate his back condition.

However, it is equally as clear from the record that at some point the interactive process broke down. Appellant asserts that he expressed the need to work at home or at a location near his home in his application for disability retirement. However, he concedes that he never raised this option with his management. There is no indication in the record that any agency official in a position to consider such an accommodation was aware of the contents of appellant's application for disability retirement. While it is clear that management knew appellant was seeking a disability retirement, the application was processed outside the agency by the Office of Personnel Management. Applying the concepts of good faith and reasonable effort to the facts of this case, the Commission finds that the responsibility for the failure to isolate the specific accommodations appellant believed would have enabled him to continue working rests on appellant. Management could not even consider his request to work at home if he did not effectively communicate that request to them. This is not a case where appellant failed to specify a necessary accommodation because of a lack of knowledge. Instead, the record indicates that appellant, in his own mind, was clear about what he needed and how it could be implemented. However, he failed to communicate that information to management for their consideration.

This failure to communicate led to the breakdown in the interactive process. Management was left to guess at what actions it should take and did so by attempting to assign appellant to a variety of positions, and appellant was left frustrated that his disability was seemingly not being accommodated. Liability for the failure to provide reasonable accommodations ensues only where the agency bears the responsibility for the breakdown. But where, as here, the agency did not obstruct the process, and instead made reasonable efforts both to communicate with appellant and provide accommodations based on the information it possessed, a violation of the Rehabilitation Act does not exist. See Beck v. University of Wisconsin Board of Regents, 75 F.3d 1130 (7th Cir. 1996).

Once an accommodation is in place, if the complainant continues to experience difficulties completing work within the established medical restrictions, it is incumbent upon the complainant to reengage with the agency about additional accommodations. See Selene M. v. Postmaster General, 0120171824 (2019) ("there is no evidence indicating that management officials assigned Complainant work outside of her restrictions. If Complainant experienced difficulties completing her duties within her medical restrictions, it was incumbent upon her to reengage with the Agency about additional accommodations rather than self-accommodating.").

Even though the agency is absolved of past liability for failure to provide accommodation if the complainant does not cooperate in the interactive process, the agency is not released from its obligation to provide reasonable accommodation if the complainant ultimately provides sufficient information to establish entitlement to accommodation. See, e.g., Hupka v. Secretary of Defense, 02960003 (1997).

The written procedures established by agencies should not be a means of imposing technical requirements on employees seeking reasonable accommodation such that failure to comply with the procedures results in denial of accommodation to an employee who is otherwise entitled to accommodation. As the Commission notes in its Policy Guidance at 3-4:

Reasonable accommodation procedures should be designed to expand employment opportunities for people with disabilities, not to create new bureaucratic requirements. The Rehabilitation Act requires that individuals be given substantial leeway in the ways in which they can make requests for reasonable accommodation. Additionally, agency procedures must permit flexibility in the processing of requests and assure that agency officials act expeditiously in providing reasonable accommodations.

Agency procedures must also inform individuals with disabilities and agency employees about their rights and responsibilities under the Rehabilitation Act. The procedures must be written in plain language so that they can be understood by those not familiar with the law. They must also explain relevant terms (e.g., essential functions, undue hardship, etc.).

An agency may permit different components of the agency to adopt their own procedures as necessary to expedite the processing of reasonable accommodation requests. However, all procedures must comply with the requirements of the Order and the standards set forth in this Guidance.

(Emphasis in oriGINAl.)

The Commission's Policy Guidance also requires agencies to engage in efforts to educate employees about the reasonable accommodation procedures, including making the procedures available in alternative formats, such as Braille or large print. Id.

The reasonable accommodation process can be separated into three stages:

1) The agency's knowledge of the disability and need for accommodation.

2) An individualized inquiry into the nature of the disability and possible accommodations for the position in question.

3) Identification and, if necessary, implementation of a reasonable accommodation.

Although the process consists of three stages, it is intended to be flexible and more than one stage of the process may be conducted simultaneously.
