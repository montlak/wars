8. Fees in Counseling Stage

The issue of payment of fees for work done during the precomplaint stage has been the subject of much debate. The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 reflect the controversy inasmuch as they represent a compromise between the position taken by most agencies--that fees should not be awarded for work in the precomplaint stage, and the position of most complainants--that fees should be awarded for all work in the precomplaint stage.

Prior the 1999 amendments, attorney fees could be awarded only for services provided subsequent to the filing of a formal complaint and after the agency has been notified that the employee is represented by counsel. However, a successful complainant also could recover fees for a reasonable amount of time spent by the attorney to review the case and to determine whether to represent the complainant. See Owens v. Department of Air Force, 01820525 (1983). The Commission has determined that it is reasonable for an attorney to take up to two hours to review a case and determine if he or she will accept it. See Doherty v. Attorney General, 0720170027 (2017); Childs v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01994951 (2002); Taylor v. Secretary of Air Force, 01924488 (1993). Previously, the Commission had held that hours expended by an attorney considering whether to take a case prior to the filing of the formal complaint were not compensable. See Ewers v. Department of Air Force, 01842010 (1986).

In a draft notice of final rulemaking for Part 1614 submitted to the Office of Management and Budget for review in early 1999, the Commission sought to revoke its prohibition on fees in the informal stage. The draft regulations would have allowed for recovery of fees for time spent in the precomplaint process. Because of opposition, the Commission adopted a compromise at 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(1)(iv):

Attorney's fees shall be paid for services performed by an attorney after the filing of a written complaint, provided that the attorney provides reasonable notice of representation to the agency, administrative judge or Commission, except that fees are allowable for a reasonable period of time prior to the notification of representation for any services performed in reaching a determination to represent the complainant. Agencies are not required to pay attorney's fees for services performed during the pre-complaint process, except that fees are allowable when the Commission affirms on appeal an administrative judge's decision finding discrimination after an agency takes final action by not implementing an administrative judge's decision. Written submissions to the agency that are signed by the representative shall be deemed to constitute notice of representation.

See also Trina C. v. Postmaster General, 0120150407 (2017) (eliminating from the complainant's attorney fee award those claimed for the period of time prior to the filing of the formal complaint).

Prior to adopting the 1999 amendments, the Commission had retreated from its earlier holding in Ewers and found that an attorney is entitled to a reasonable amount of time for discussing the complaint with the client and determining whether to accept the case prior to the filing of a formal complaint. Ponce v. Secretary of Air Force, 01903196 (1990). However, the attorney must show the time spent discussing the case with the client is reasonable. In the same case, the Commission declined to award fees for the time counsel spent preparing the formal complaint.

It is not unreasonable, even for an experienced EEO lawyer, to spend a minimal amount of time researching basic statutes and regulations before deciding whether to take a specific case. See Park v. Secretary of Treasury, 01933482 (1993) (allowing 1.5 hours of research time and rejecting an agency argument that an attorney accepting an EEO case should be familiar with basic EEO law). But, when the complainant's counsel notifies the agency that he is representing the complainant prior to conducting an initial consultation, there can be no fees awarded for deciding to take the case. See Atienza v. Secretary of Navy, 01872727 (1988).

As a general rule, the Commission allows two hours for an attorney to evaluate a case prior to deciding whether to accept it. However, it does make exceptions to that rule. See, e.g., Hedgepeth v. Secretary of Interior, 01A52869 (2006) (awarding eight hours in a complex case of failure to accommodate); Savage v. Postmaster General, 01A32565 (2004) (awarding 15.1 hours of evaluation and research time); Koehler v. General Services Administration, 01A02276 (2002) (awarding seven hours of evaluation time).

Attorney fees incurred at the precomplaint stage due to agency failures may also be compensable. For example, in Leticia W. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 2019002510 (2020), the Commission awarded reimbursement of 7.5 hours in attorney fees spent at the precomplaint stage where the agency concluded that the complainant could not pursue her sexual harassment claim because the alleged harasser had retired. The Commission in Leticia W. explained:

Complainant claims that the foregoing information from ORM created confusion regarding the validity and timeliness of her sexual harassment claim, and thus, it was proper for her attorney to expend a reasonable amount of time to assess whether these issues undermined Complainant's legal recourse. The record supports Complainant's assertions and thus, we find that Complainant is entitled to 7.5 hours for pre-complaint work performed by Senior Attorney 1 as a result of ORM's actions.

Also prior to the 1999 amendments, the Commission held that an appellant is not entitled to attorney fees pursuant to a settlement agreement entered into prior to filing a formal complaint. In Rice v. Secretary of Energy, 01950397 (1997), the Commission noted that it was well-settled that attorney fees may be recovered only for services rendered after a complainant had notified the agency that he has filed a formal complaint in the matter, the only exception being that fees may be paid for time expended by counsel to determine whether to represent the complainant on the complaint. The 1999 amendments specifically provide that an agency may agree to pay an attorney for time spent in precomplaint counseling as part of a settlement agreement.

The 1999 amendment providing an agency may pay attorney fees for work done during the precomplaint counseling stage extends to work done during alternative dispute resolution conducted in lieu of counseling. Green v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01973651 (2000).
