B. ELECTING THE FORUM

From the employee's perspective, the first step in electing a forum is to make an informed decision about the forum that will best advance the particular complaint. Election of the forum is entirely up to the employee.

Although from the employee's perspective the election of forum should be informed, neither the Statute nor the Commission's regulations require an informed election of forum. In determining whether a complainant elected the negotiated grievance procedure, the MSPB, or the EEO complaint process to seek redress of alleged discrimination, the Commission does not decide whether any such election is informed. Pruitt v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01971730 (1998). The Commission's regulations do not require an informed election; they require only an election be made. Because election decisions are irrevocable, even if they are not informed decisions, it is particularly important for the employee to determine what his or her rights are in each forum.

To make an informed decision, the employee should compare the EEO process to the procedure available under the collective bargaining agreement. In many cases, the grievance procedure will provide for a speedier disposition of the complaint. In the grievance procedure, hearings usually are not provided unless the grievance proceeds to arbitration. In most cases, it is the union as exclusive bargaining agent, not the employee that has the right to elect arbitration. In the EEO process, the right to elect a hearing rests solely with the employee.

The employee also should consider the nature of the complaint. It is especially important to note that in pursuing an EEO complaint, alleged agency contractual violations may only be used as evidence of discrimination. There is no relief for contract violations under the EEO procedure unless it is shown that the provision is applied in a discriminatory fashion. In the grievance procedure, it is possible to prevail on contractual remedies even if the claims of discrimination are rejected.

Selection of a representative is another important factor. In the grievance process, the union is the employee's representative. The union must represent the employee, so he or she is guaranteed representation without charge. There is no requirement that the representative be a lawyer. The representative may or may not be knowledgeable in both the areas of contract and discrimination law. In the EEO process, the employee has the right to select the representative of his or her choosing. The union has no right of representation, although some unions do provide EEO representation to its covered employees. Unless the union provides legal representation, legal representation is not provided in the EEO process, however, and must be undertaken at the employee's expense. As discussed in Chapter 22, attorney fees are recoverable for prevailing parties in cases under Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act.

Timeliness is another factor that should be considered. Under the former regulations, an employee was required to contact an EEO counselor within 30 calendar days of the alleged discriminatory personnel action to preserve the timeliness of the complaint. Under Part 1614, this period has been extended to 45 days. In most cases, the grievance procedure will have to be activated in a more timely fashion. The election of the forum should be considered at the outset. Otherwise, the forum may be elected by default.

In the past, there was a distinction between initiating an action under a statutory scheme, e.g., Title VII, and filing a grievance in writing. (Many grievance procedures only require an oral grievance to be submitted at the first step.) An employee was deemed to have initiated an action under Title VII by contacting a counselor because this is the required first step. See Ramsey v. Department of Energy, 01831215 (1983). The Commission has changed this stance, and its current regulations specifically provide that contact of a counselor is not an election of a forum. See 29 CFR 1614.301(a). In order to elect a forum the employee must either file a written grievance or a formal complaint of discrimination. The fact that the first stage of a grievance procedure is designated as "informal" is not dispositive of the election question. If the informal grievance must be filed in writing, its filing constitutes an election of the forum. See Bradley v. Veterans Administration, 01851947 (1986). In addition, even if the grievance procedure does not require a written grievance, if the employee chooses to put the grievance in writing, this constitutes an election of the grievance procedure. In Norfolk v. Attorney General, 01A31617 (2003), the first step of the grievance procedure could be invoked orally, but the contract gave the employee the option of putting the grievance in writing. Because the employee elected to file the step one grievance in writing prior to filing an EEO complaint, she was barred from proceeding with her EEO complaint.

Where the employee first files a formal grievance before filing an EEO complaint, the Commission will uphold dismissal of the EEO complaint even though the agency did not inform the employee of his election rights. The employee is entitled to be advised of the right to proceed through either the grievance procedure or the EEO process. But, an agency properly dismisses a timely filed complaint when prior to contact with a counselor, the employee initiates a written grievance over the same incident. See Brown v. Defense Mapping Agency, 01830155 (1983). The regulation, 29 CFR 1614.301(a), requires dismissal irrespective of whether the agency has informed the employee of the need to elect a forum. See Hicks v. Secretary of Treasury, 01965117 (1997); see also Hackworth v. Secretary of Defense, DCA, 01952867 (1996).

To assist in clarifying the confusion, the Commission has provided detailed guidance at Chapter 2 of MD-110. This guidance is discussed in detail in Chapter 3, above, on EEO counseling.

The right to make an informed election is significant, and the agency has a clear obligation to see that the complainant is properly informed if he seeks advice from the agency EEO office. Despite regulations that require the dismissal of any EEO complaint where a grievance has previously been filed, 29 CFR 1614.301(a), the Commission has held that an agency may accept the processing of an EEO complaint over a personnel action that has been the subject of a grievance where the complainant contacted the EEO office prior to initiating the grievance. The EEO office dismissed the complainant's allegations, refused to assign a counselor and advised her to file a grievance. See Bass v. Secretary of Air Force, 05900670 (1990). In these circumstances, the Commission found that the employee had not made an informed decision to elect the grievance procedure and should not be barred from pursuing an EEO complaint. Based on the dismissal of her allegations by the EEO Chief Counselor, the complainant did not pursue her allegations of discrimination under the collective bargaining procedure. It is unclear to what degree, if any, Pruitt v. Secretary of Agriculture, supra, overrules Bass. Although the Commission held in Pruitt that an informed election is not required by its regulations, the likely difference is that the complainant in Bass attempted to make an informed election but was misinformed by the agency's EEO office and, as a result, failed to pursue the allegations of discrimination in any forum.

A proper election of forum is made where the collective bargaining permits a request for arbitration of removals without first proceeding through the grievance process, and the employee makes such a request and further asks for the removal arbitration to be consolidated with pending arbitration on another matter. The Commission rejected the appellant's argument that she had not elected the collective bargaining procedure over the EEO process because she never filed a grievance in writing. The Commission found the appellant "consciously chose a forum provided under a collective bargaining agreement rather than the EEO process...." Smith v. National Labor Relations Board, 05910640 (1991).

Even though the complainant does not raise discrimination in his grievance, if the grievance procedure permits allegations of discrimination he may not subsequently raise the matter in an EEO complaint. Veloz v. Secretary of Labor, 01962329 (1996).
