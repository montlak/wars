C. DISMISSAL PROCESS

Not all complaints of discrimination are accepted by the agency. The agency may dismiss a complaint for any of the reasons set forth in 29 CFR 1614.107(a). All of the dismissal bases are discussed in "Bases for Dismissal of Complaints," later in this chapter. The process by which the agency must dismiss a complaint is set forth at 29 CFR 1614.110(b):

When an agency dismisses an entire complaint under 1614.107...the agency shall take final action by issuing a final decision. The final decision shall consist of findings by the agency on the merits of each issue in the complaint, or, as appropriate, the rationale for dismissing any claims in the complaint and, when discrimination is found, appropriate remedies and relief in accordance with subpart E of this part. The agency shall issue the final decision within 60 days of receiving notification that a complainant has requested an immediate decision from the agency, or within 60 days of the end of the 30-day period for the complainant to request a hearing or an immediate final decision where the complainant has not requested either a hearing or a decision. The final action shall contain notice of the right to appeal the final action to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, the right to file a civil action in federal district court, the name of the proper defendant in any such lawsuit and the applicable time limits for appeals and lawsuits. A copy of EEOC Form 573 shall be attached to the final action.

When an agency dismisses a complaint, the agency bears the burden of proof on appeal to "have evidence or proof to support its final decision." Holt v. Secretary of Army, 01A12781 (2001); Pruitt v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01934793 (1993). In Pruitt, the EEOC found that it was not necessary for an agency to produce a copy of the first complaint to support its decision to dismiss a second complaint on the basis that it raised the same issue. But, "the Commission requires presentation of sufficient evidence from which it may determine that the earlier complaint raises the same claim or claims as the later one." Id. In dismissing a complaint, the agency has an obligation to issue its decisions in a manner deemed "consistent with acceptable legal standards." 29 CFR 1614.104(b). In Delalat v. Secretary of Navy, 01963847 (1997), the Commission held that this obligation requires the agency to do more than simply cite the correct legal standard. The agency must further "adequately and fairly describe the factual record" on which it relies. In Delalat, the Commission reversed a conclusory decision that an allegation was untimely under the continuing violation theory. A proper factual analysis under the correct legal standard, the Commission concluded, revealed that there was a clear nexus between dismissed allegations and those accepted as timely.

Whenever an agency dismisses a complaint, it must issue a written final decision. In Wright v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A10542 (2001), the agency failed to provide the Commission with the decision dismissing the complaint, despite several requests. The Commission stated that it would not affirm a decision where the agency fails to provide a copy of the decision, and ordered the agency to investigate the complaint on its merits.

An agency cannot dismiss claims in a complaint simply by not addressing them. The agency must include the rationale for dismissing any claims in its final decision. Stewart v. Postmaster General, 01996570 (2001). In an ironic procedural twist, when an agency does not address claims in its final decision, the Commission regards this as "tantamount to dismissal of those allegations." Id. Of course, if the claims were not regarded as dismissed, they would still be before the agency, and the Commission would have no appellate jurisdiction over them. The academics of the point aside, the proper remedy is for the Commission to remand the complaint to the agency for processing of the claims that were not addressed. See, e.g., Plante v. Office of U.S. Courts, 0120061807 (2010).
