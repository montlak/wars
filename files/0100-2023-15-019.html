5. Waiver of Hearing

Even where a complainant does not specifically withdraw his or her hearing request, actions of the complainant may constitute a waiver of the right to a hearing. Failure to request a hearing within the 30-day period constitutes a waiver of the right to a hearing. Ingram v. Postmaster General, 01996867 (2002); Guinan v. Postmaster General, 01997167 (2002).

The administrative judge does not abuse his discretion in canceling the hearing and remanding the case to the agency for a decision on the record when the appellant fails to attend a prehearing conference and subsequently fails to provide adequate justification for his absence. Easley v. USPS, 01830961 (1985).

Waiver of the right to a hearing does not constitute a waiver of the right to an impartial investigation. Gilmore v. Railroad Retirement Board, 05880105 (1988).

An administrative judge is justified in deeming a complainant to have waived the right to a hearing if the complainant refuses to proceed because the judge permits an agency witness to testify by telephone. The Commission noted there is no prohibition against the taking of testimony by telephone. The witness had to be out of state on the date of the hearing, and the complainant was advised of his right to cross examine the witness. Freeman v. Postmaster General, 01924204 (1993). The complainant's refusal to proceed with the hearing and his request for a final decision on the record was a waiver of his right to a hearing.

A complainant does not waive his right to a hearing when he tells the administrative judge that he will request a decision on the record if the agency does not pay his travel expenses to the hearing. In Thomas v. Secretary of Army, 05960077 (1996), the Commission found there was no waiver of the request to a hearing in those circumstances where the agency then dismissed the case for procedural reasons. Moreover, the Commission found the agency should have returned the case to the administrative judge for hearing when it later withdrew that dismissal and issued a final decision on the merits.

A complainant has a right to rely on the oral representations of the administrative judge. In Harsch v. Postmaster General, 01932321 (1993), the Commission found it was error for the administrative judge to cancel the hearing and remand the complaint to the agency where the complainant failed to attend the scheduled hearing after being told by the judge that the hearing would be rescheduled. In part, the Commission was persuaded by the fact that there was no indication that the agency representative had appeared on the hearing date. This supported the complainant's contention that the hearing was, in fact, canceled.

When a complainant is unable to proceed to hearing due to health reasons, the administrative judge has the discretion to remand the complaint to the agency with an order to hold the complaint in abeyance. However, it is an abuse of discretion for the administration judge to order such a remand without specifying a time frame. For example, in Kukal v. Postmaster General, 01A51010 (2006), the Commission found that the judge erred in remanding the case without a time frame for the complainant to reassert her right to a hearing. The Commission upheld a final agency decision issued on the record some six years after the remand where the complainant was still, due to health reasons, unable to proceed to hearing.
