g. Strength of Corroborating Evidence

The Commission does not require that the agency investigation into harassment allegations produce certainty before taking corrective action. In Logsdon v. Secretary of Agriculture, 07A401120 (2006), the complainant received an anonymous letter that contained allegations about her own sex life. The agency promptly initiated an investigation concerning who wrote the anonymous letter and, although it suspected a co--worker, he denied sending the letter. Because the agency considered its investigation inconclusive, it took no action. In addition, it made the mistake of focusing at least part of its investigation on whether the allegations about the complainant's sexual conduct were true. Ultimately, two more anonymous letters with allegations about the complainant's sexual behavior were sent to her and distributed to others. The Commission rejected an argument that because two of the letters were sent when both employees were on furlough, there was no term or condition of employment involved. In finding that the agency had not acted promptly to avoid liability, the Commission in Logsdon wrote:

The AJ determined during the hearing that the agency's actions were insufficient. It mattered not what status complainant and the co-worker harasser were in when the second letter was circulated. The point, according to the AJ, was that the agency became aware of an alarming situation and, although it promptly started an investigation, it did not stop it and prevent further harassment. This ineffective response caused a second and later a third offensive letter to surface. By not ending the harassment of one of its employees, the agency did not completely fulfill its obligation of taking "corrective action." After reviewing the evidence and evaluating witness testimony, the AJ concluded that the agency had become, or should have become, aware of who the harasser was by December 1997. The AJ further found that the investigation did not aim to put an end to the sexual harassment. Instead, the investigation turned into an inquiry into whether there was employee misconduct because the agency pressed complainant into admitting whether or not the sexual allegations contained in the offensive letters were true. The agency's investigation thereby became an inquiry into complainant's private life, exacerbating the humiliation she felt.

Having reviewed the record, we find that the evidence substantially supports the AJ's findings of fact. When an employer becomes aware of alleged harassment, the employer has the duty to investigate such charges promptly and thoroughly. See Rodriguez v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01953850 (Aug. 29, 1996). By "thoroughly" the Commission means "effectively," taking whatever action necessary to end the harassment and prevent the misconduct from recurring. See Voigt v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01931799 (Dec. 20, 1994). The mere fact that the agency took some remedial action by initiating an investigation does not absolve it of liability where that action was ineffective. Clearly here the agency's remedial investigation was ineffective because more offensive letters later surfaced. The agency argues that it could not do more to prevent the harassment because the letters were sent anonymously and so it did not know who the perpetrator was and could not punish anyone. The AJ however made credibility determinations on this point at the hearing and concluded that by December 1997 the agency constructively knew who the co-worker harasser was but did nothing, did not even warn complainant of its suspicions, and the harassment continued. We find this inaction unacceptable. Therefore, we agree with the AJ and conclude that the investigation was clearly ineffectual because it did not take corrective action.
