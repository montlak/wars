1. Reprisal

The Commission has been inconsistent in its treatment on the issue of an award of attorney fees in cases involving reprisal under the ADEA. The current rule is that fees are not recoverable.

In Terracina v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01912460 (1991), the Commission indicated that attorney fees were recoverable in reprisal claims, even though the original complaint was brought under the ADEA. In that case, the Commission rejected the agency's argument that it was not obligated to pay fees under an express provision of a settlement agreement because the underlying complaint was based on age discrimination. The Commission held that nothing in its regulations or policy prohibited the payment of fees because the reprisal complaint is based on a separate charge arising under a provision independent of the ADEA. The Commission, by regulation, prohibits reprisal. See 29 CFR 1613.261. But see Numrich v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01911429 (1991) (noting, in dicta, a previous Commission decision holding that fees were not available in reprisal cases based on ADEA).

The issue of whether an agency can agree to pay attorney fees in a case involving a claim of reprisal under the ADEA was again sidestepped in Franchi v. Postmaster General, 01922456 (1992). In that case, the agreement was silent on the issue of attorney fees. However, the Commission did not decide whether such fees could be awarded in a settlement agreement. Instead, the Commission voided the agreement because the EEOC administrative judge assigned to the case had incorrectly advised the agency that it was not liable for fees unless they were explicitly provided for in the agreement. As a result, the Commission determined there was no real agreement on the issue of fees.

However, on reconsideration in Terracina v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05910888 (1992), the Commission clearly held that attorney fees are not available on reprisal claims where the underlying claim of discrimination is based on the ADEA. It further held in Terracina that an agency cannot provide attorney fees in settlement of an ADEA claim:

The Commission acknowledges that the voluntary resolution of discrimination complaints is favored under both Commission and public policy, and that settlement agreements will not lightly be set aside. See, e.g., Rogers v. General Electric Co., 781 F.2d 452 (5th Cir. 1986). There are, however, instances when an otherwise valid agreement may be void, voidable, or reformable, depending on the circumstances: for example, due to coercion, misrepresentation, or mistake.

In the present case, appellant alleged that the agency breached the settlement agreement when the provision for the payment of attorney's fees was unilaterally rescinded. The agency stated that the provision was severed from the agreement because attorney's fees could not be authorized for the administrative processing of a complaint filed under the ADEA. The previous decision inexplicably found that appellant's reprisal complaint was not filed under the ADEA and therefore that the payment of attorney's fees was not precluded. Consequently, the Commission found that the agency had breached the agreement by refusing to pay the provided for fees.

Initially, we find that the previous decision in EEOC Appeal No. 01912460 erred in stating that appellant's reprisal complaint was not filed under the ADEA. Appellant clearly alleged that she had been subjected to discrimination in reprisal for having previously filed her age discrimination complaint. As noted by the agency, the ADEA has its own provision against retaliation for pursuing a claim under the ADEA, which is clearly the claim raised by appellant's reprisal complaint. See 29 USC 623(d). Further, we find that the agency correctly determined that the ADEA does not provide for the award of attorney's fees for Federal sector complaints at the administrative level. See Palmer v. General Services Administration, 787 F.2d 300 (8th Cir. 1986).

In a settlement agreement, an agency cannot incur a financial liability that the agency is not legally obligated to incur. Therefore, an agency is prohibited from agreeing in a settlement to pay attorney's fees for the administrative processing of a complaint filed under the ADEA and to the extent an agency does agree to such a provision, it would constitute a nullity. Albert D. Parker, 64 Comp. Gen. 349 (1985). Consequently, the Commission finds that the agency could not agree to award appellant attorney's fees for the administrative processing of her reprisal complaint filed under the ADEA and the provision was properly removed from the agreement.

We note, however, that at the time when the settlement agreement in the present case was executed, both appellant and the agency officials involved mistakenly believed that the payment of attorney's fees was permissible. Appellant clearly stated that in reliance on the agency's offer to pay attorney's fees, she agreed to forgo pursuing a higher performance appraisal rating and performance award. Therefore, the Commission finds that due to a mutual mistake, appellant detrimentally relied on the erroneous provision. Since the attorney's fees provision was the only term relating to appellant's reprisal complaint, we find that upon removal of that provision there is no consideration for appellant's withdrawal of her reprisal complaint. While generally the Commission is not concerned with the adequacy or fairness of the consideration in a settlement agreement, as long as some legal detriment is incurred as part of the bargain, when one of the contracting parties incurs no legal detriment, the settlement agreement will be set aside due to the lack of consideration. In the present case, the Commission finds that the May 23, 1990 settlement agreement resolving two of appellant's complaints only provided consideration for appellant's withdrawal of her age complaint, i.e., through the provision for appellant's promotion. The agency incurred no legal detriment in exchange for appellant's withdrawal of her reprisal complaint. Therefore, the Commission finds that the settlement agreement remains valid and in effect with respect to appellant's age complaint; however, there is no settlement agreement pertaining to appellant's reprisal complaint, which therefore must be reinstated. Accordingly, the agency's decision of April 26, 1991, is hereby vacated in part and appellant's reprisal complaint is hereby remanded to the agency for reinstatement and further processing from the point processing ceased.

See also Jacobsohn v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01922308 (1993) (an agency may not grant, through settlement, relief such as liquidated damages that are not authorized by the ADEA).
