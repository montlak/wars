3. Compensatory Damages

In cases of alleged discrimination occurring after the effective date of the 1991 Civil Rights Act--November 21, 1991--an offer of full relief must account for any compensatory damages claimed by the complainant. In Jackson v. Postmaster General, 01923399 (1992), the Commission held that a complainant under the Rehabilitation Act was entitled to an offer of compensatory damages as part of full relief where the complainant could present objective evidence of the harm suffered and objective evidence of a causal connection between the agency's action and the harm suffered. Note that the Commission oriGINAlly held that the compensatory damages provision of the 1991 Civil Rights Act is not retroactive and only applies to discriminatory actions occurring on or after November 21, 1991--the effective date of the Act. It later reversed its position on retroactivity. However, the Supreme Court has concluded that the 1991 Civil Rights Act is not retroactive. See Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U.S. 244, 114 S. Ct. 1483, 128 L. Ed.2d 229 (1994). A full discussion of the Jackson case is provided in Chapter 21 under the subheading "Authority to Award."

Although an order of full relief must take into consideration compensatory damages, the complainant still has the burden to prove that the harm for which compensation is sought is causally related to the alleged discrimination. Compensatory damages, then, are an exception to the general rule that in making an offer of full relief, the administrative judge must accept all of the complaint allegations as true. The administrative judge is not required to accept as true the complainant's allegation that he or she has suffered emotional harm, the harm is causally connected to the alleged discrimination, or the complainant's assessment of the level of harm. For example, in Herring v. Secretary of Navy, 01960797 (1996), decided under the previous provision covering offers of full relief, the agency specifically requested that the complainant provide objective evidence of damages, including evidence linking the damages to the alleged discrimination. Although the complainant submitted a copy of a bill for psychotherapy, that document did not include a diagnosis, and did not relate the treatment to any particular factors. Further, given the date of the treatment and complainant's statements, it appeared that the treatment related to incidents that were not at issue. Consequently, the Commission found that the complainant was not entitled to compensatory damages.

Even when the discriminatory event occurred after the effective date of the 1991 Civil Rights Act, the Commission initially held that a complainant was not entitled to compensatory damages as part of an offer of full relief if such relief had not previously been requested. In Carlson v. Secretary of Navy, 01930284 (1993), the Commission upheld a certified agency offer of full relief that did not include compensatory damages. The Commission noted that the complainant had sought compensatory damages in precomplaint counseling, but had not raised the issue of compensatory damages in her formal complaint. The Commission found that its holding in Jackson was specifically limited to cases "where the complainant has asked for such damages prior to the agency's making the offer [of full relief], and the complainant has shown some objective evidence that she has incurred damages that are related to the unlawful discrimination." Id. Again, the decision in Carlson appeared to carve an exception for compensatory damage cases in offers of full relief. Despite clear case law, discussed above, that the proper scope of relief is determined by the legal scope of relief supported by a complaint and not what the complainant requests, the Commission was making an exception to that case law for claims of compensatory damages. The Commission has now entirely retreated from the Carlson decision. See Chapter 21 under the heading "Compensatory Damages."

In Hicks v. Secretary of Air Force, 01965315 (1997), the Commission held that a request to the complainant for evidence to support a compensatory damages claim must precede an offer of full relief:

In this case, the agency noted in its offer that appellant requested compensatory damages as relief and requested that appellant provide evidence of his losses in response to its offer. Upon review, we find that the agency's request should have preceded its proffer of relief. The agency's conditional offer of compensatory damages, conditioned upon appellant's presentation of proper objective evidence of his losses, did not provide appellant with the full relief to which he may be entitled. An offer of full relief must unconditionally provide the complainant with the relief to which he would be entitled if he were to prevail on the merits of his complaint.

The same requirement would apply to an order of full relief. The administrative judge will request this information prior to issuing an order.
