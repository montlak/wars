J. HEALTH BENEFITS

A claim that a health benefit provider is discriminating against an employee is properly brought before the Office of Personnel Management and not the employing agency. Polifko v. Office of Personnel Management, 05940611 (1995). In Heittner v. Office of Personnel Management, 01955445 (1996), the Commission reaffirmed its holding in Polifko that health benefits for employees and their dependents are a fringe benefit available by virtue of employment, and as such, denial of health benefits for discriminatory reasons is within the ambit of the employment discrimination statutes enforced by the Commission. In Heittner, the employee first complained to his employer, the Department of Energy, when his health care provider refused to pay for a bone marrow transplant that was recommended to treat his wife's cancer. He then was advised to file his complaint with OPM because it administers the Federal Employees Health Benefits Program. The Commission reversed OPM's decision to dismiss the complaint because the wife was not an employee or applicant for employment with the agency. OPM further tried to reargue the Polifko case, contending that any relevant sections of the ADA had no bearing on the case because the ADA did not apply to the federal government. The Commission in Heittner, relying heavily upon the "EEOC Interim Guidance on Application of ADA to Health Insurance," held:

In its first argument presented in opposition to appellant's appeal, the agency claims that appellant's complaint fails to state a claim because "an allegation that a benefit extended to one category of [disabled] persons must also be extended to all other categories of [disabled] persons is not cognizable under the Rehabilitation Act." As noted previously in this decision, the Guidance provides that a "disability based distinction" in a health insurance plan, i.e., one which singles out a particular disability, discrete group of disabilities, or disability in general, is unlawful unless it is justified. Guidance at p. 2. Pursuant to Footnote 1 of the Guidance, the analysis contained in the Guidance, including the discussion of disability based distinctions, applies to federal sector complaints of discrimination brought pursuant to Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 by virtue of the recent amendments to that Act. Given that appellant has raised a claim which is recognized under the Guidance, and given that the Guidance applies to Section 501 claims, the Commission finds that the agency's first argument averring that appellant has failed to state a claim fails.

Next the agency argues that because the distinction between benefits for various illnesses is within the agency's statutory discretion, and not in conflict with the Rehabilitation Act, appellant's complaint should be dismissed. The Commission notes that appellant is alleging that the distinction between benefits is in conflict with the Rehabilitation Act, and, as we stated above, such a claim is cognizable under the Rehabilitation Act. The Commission recognizes that the agency has been granted broad discretion by Congress to decide what benefits should be available to employees, and it is not the intent of the Commission to disturb this authority. Nonetheless, safeguards against such a 'boundless notion' are established in the Guidance. Specifically, Section 501(c) of the ADA provides that such distinctions will be upheld if it is shown that; (1) the health insurance plan is a bona fide plan which is not inconsistent with state law, and (2) the disability based distinction is not being used as a subterfuge. 42 USC § 12201(c); 29 CFR § 1630.16(f).

In its last argument raised in opposition to appellant's appeal, the agency contends that the Commission's findings concerning the application of the ADA to the Rehabilitation Act are "wrong," inasmuch as the federal government is exempt from ADA coverage, and the term "employer" as it is used in the ADA does not include the United States. In addition, the agency contends that the 1992 amendments to the Rehabilitation Act were an attempt to provide consistency regarding "standards," which the agency defines as "what is a reasonable accommodation and what is a major life activity." Finally, the agency argues that the United States is immune to claims such as this one because it has not waived its right to sovereign immunity under the ADA.

While the agency is correct in its assertion that the federal government is not included in the definition of "employer" in the ADA, and that the United States has not waived its right to sovereign immunity under the ADA, the Commission reminds the agency that this claim was brought pursuant to Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act. The United States has waived its right to sovereign immunity under 501 of the Rehabilitation Act, and it is included in the definition of "employer" under this Act. As to the agency's argument that the association provision of the ADA and the Guidance do not apply to the federal government, the Commission deems it important to emphasize that on October 29, 1992, the Rehabilitation Act was amended to ensure uniformity and consistency of interpretations between the ADA and the Rehabilitation Act. As such, the standards used to determine whether this section has been violated in a complaint alleging nonaffirmative action employment discrimination under this section shall be the standards applied under title 1 of the Americans With Disabilities Act of 1990 (42 USC § 12111 et seq.) and the provisions of sections 501 through 504, and 510 of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1900 (42 USC 12201-12204 and 12210), as such sections relate to employment. 29 USC § 791(g).

The agency has provided no support for its assertion that "standards" is limited to defining what is a reasonable accommodation and what is a major life activity. Such a narrow interpretation would undermine the purpose of the 1992 amendments, that is, ensuring uniformity and consistency of interpretations between the ADA and the Rehabilitation Act. Accordingly, the Commission will not disturb its finding that the 1992 amendments ensure that the association provision of the ADA and the Guidance apply to cases such as this one.

Consequently, the Commission affirms the holding that health benefits for employees and their dependents are a fringe benefit available by virtue of employment, and as such, denial of health benefits for discriminatory reasons is within the ambit of the employment discrimination statutes enforced by the Commission. Therefore, claims alleging unlawful denials are properly made against the agency under those statutes and pursuant to 29 CFR Part 1614 of the Commission's regulations.

Accordingly, the Commission finds that appellant has standing to raise a claim of disability discrimination on the basis of his relationship with his wife, an individual with a disability, when his wife was denied insurance coverage for HD/ABMT.

Office of Personnel Management, 0120161068 (2017), where the complainant alleged that he was subjected to discrimination when AETNA denied him preauthorization for health insurance benefits to cover nipple-areola reconstruction as part of his gender reassignment surgery. AETNA granted the complainant preauthorization for a simple mastectomy, but determined that the nipple-areola reconstruction was purely cosmetic and therefore not covered. AETNA denied the complainant's level 1 appeal of its decision and advised that he had the right to further appeal the denial to the agency. Instead, the complainant filed an EEO complaint, which the agency dismissed for failure to state a claim because the complainant failed to appeal the decision to the agency. On appeal, the parties in Darin B., made the following arguments:

On appeal, Complainant maintains that the Agency improperly dismissed his complaint, which deprived him of an investigation of his claims. Complainant argues that his complaint "plainly states a cognizable claim" under EEO regulations, and Commission precedent establishes that claimants who are denied health benefits by an FEHB plan administrator are aggrieved. Complainant further argues that he notified the Agency of the denial of his claim for benefits in his EEO complaint, and the issue of whether he notified OPM of the denial is an issue regarding the merits of his complaint that is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether he has stated a justiciable claim. Complainant maintains that he was not required to exhaust the FEHB claims review process before being able to assert a claim in the EEO process against the Agency. Complainant argues that the Agency is responsible for ensuring FEHB plans comply with federal laws, and therefore is culpable for FEHB plan discrimination in this case even if it does not directly administer the plan.

In response, the Agency requests that we affirm its final decision dismissing Complainant's complaint. Specifically, the Agency contends that Complainant's complaint fails to state a claim because it (OPM) has not acted in the matters at issue because Aetna exclusively made the decisions denying Complainant coverage. The Agency maintains that FEHB grants it statutory authority to order carriers to provide benefits, but Complainant failed to avail himself of the disputed claims process, which would have allowed the Agency to consider Complainant's claim. The Agency notes that the applicable Aetna health insurance plan provides for coverage of transgender transition surgery.

The Commission in Darin B. sided with the complainant, reversing the agency's final decision and remanding for further investigation of the complaint:

In this case, Complainant alleges that he was subjected to unlawful discrimination and harassment on the bases of sex and disability when Aetna denied his request for pre-authorization for nipple-areola reconstruction surgery. We note that the Commission has long held that insurance coverage is a fringe benefit of employment, and the denial of insurance coverage concerns a term, condition, or privilege of employment. See Polifko v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Request No. 05940611 (Jan. 4, 1995) (Commission held that, based on the association provision of the ADA and the Commission's "Interim Guidance on Application of ADA to Health Insurance," Complainant had standing to bring a claim of discrimination on the basis of his relationship with his wife, an individual with a disability, who had been denied specific treatment for breast cancer by an insurance carrier).

The Agency argues that it has taken no action to harm Complainant because he failed to appeal the matter to the Agency through the process set forth at 5 C.F.R. § 890. However, the Commission has held that employees' failure to use or exhaust the process set forth at 5 C.F.R. § 890 does not preclude them from asserting viable claims in the EEO process. See Lawrence, et al. v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Appeal Nos. 01996217, 01996553 (Aug. 11, 2000), req. for recons. denied, EEOC Request No. 05A01233 (Mar. 15, 2001) (Commission rejected OPM's assertion that denial of coverage for in-vitro fertilization treatment failed to state a claim because complainant did not request OPM review of insurance carrier's decision as provided for in 5 C.F.R. § 890). Moreover, we have held that the Agency administering the FEHB program (OPM) is responsible for an insurance carrier's denial of benefits. See Polifko v. Office of Personnel Management, supra; Barta v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Appeal No. 01991959 (Aug. 17, 2000), req. for recons. den., EEOC Request No. 05A01232 (Nov. 30, 2000). Therefore, we find that Complainant's complaint states a claim of discrimination under EEOC regulations.
