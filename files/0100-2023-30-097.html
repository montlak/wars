V. COMPENSATORY DAMAGES

Compensatory damages are available only in cases of intentional discrimination under Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act. See Sec. 102, Civil Rights Act of 1991, codified at 42 USC 1981a(b). In addition, compensatory damages can be awarded under the Rehabilitation Act for failure to make reasonable accommodation, provided the agency has not made a good faith effort to provide accommodation. Compensatory damages are not available in cases involving adverse impact discrimination. The omission of the ADEA from the provisions of the 1991 Civil Rights Act on damages means that there are no such damages available in age discrimination cases. See Taylor v. Secretary of Army, 05930633 (1994).

The EEOC initially held that compensatory damages are available under the Rehabilitation Act in cases involving federal employees but, for a while, that holding was in doubt. In Lane v. Pena, 867 F. Supp. 1050 (D.D.C. 1994), the district court held that compensatory damages were available against federal agencies. In a suit involving a claim by a student with diabetes who was dismissed from the Merchant Marine Academy, the district court held that he was entitled to compensatory damages under the Rehabilitation Act. The court rejected an argument that the doctrine of sovereign immunity bars an award of damages in cases involving the federal government. However, in Dorsey v. U.S. Department of Labor, 41 F.3d 1551 (D.C. Cir. 1994), the court of appeals reached a contrary result. The district court then vacated its earlier decision in Lane. The decision of the district court was summarily upheld by the court of appeals in Lane v. Pena, 1995 WL 418635 (D.C. Cir. 1995). In Lane v. Pena, 116 S. Ct. 2092 (1996), the Supreme Court found that federal employees are entitled to compensatory damages under the Rehabilitation Act. Lane is discussed fully below.

In Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U.S. 244, 114 S. Ct. 1483, 128 L. Ed.2d 229 (1994), the Supreme Court resolved the issue of retroactivity of remedies under the 1991 Civil Rights Act, holding that the remedies were not retroactive. Therefore, compensatory damages only are available for acts of discrimination occurring on or after November 21, 1991--the effective date of the Act. As a result of the Supreme Court's decision in Landgraf, the Commission voted not to award compensatory damages for any act of discrimination occurring before November 21, 1991. See Warner v. Postmaster General, 01932293 (1994); see also Pearson v. Secretary of Army, 01932378 (1994). For a full discussion of Landgraf, see Chapter 13 under the subheading "Retroactivity of Remedies."

Although compensatory damages are not available for acts of discriminatory conduct that occurred before November 21, 1991, such damages may be available where a course of conduct continued after that date. In Adesanya v. Postmaster General, 01933395 (1994), the Commission noted that the complainant was not entitled to compensatory damages for the discriminatory denial of light duty prior to the effective date of the 1991 Civil Rights Act, but remanded the case for consideration of compensatory damages for denials that occurred after the effective date. The fact that a complaint is not investigated until after the effective date of the 1991 Civil Rights Acts does not entitle a complainant to compensatory damages. Under Landgraf, the focus is on the date the act of alleged discrimination occurs. See Bradley v. Secretary of Navy, 01942590 (1995).

However, pre-Act conduct is relevant to a determination of post-Act damages. The agency is not liable for any harm inflicted upon the complainant prior to the 1991 Civil Rights Act, but it is liable for all post-Act damages even if the harm suffered is greater due to the earlier harm. Adesanya v. Postmaster General, 04950026 (1996).

In Lewis v. Postmaster General, 01943048 (1995), the Commission held that relief in a case can be bifurcated such that compensatory damages are not available for acts of discrimination occurring prior to the effective date of the 1991 Civil Rights Act, but are available for acts subsequent to November 21, 1991. In Lewis, the Commission explained:

There is no dispute in this case that appellant is a victim of sexual harassment, and that she is entitled to relief for the discrimination she endured. The question presented is whether or not the relief includes compensatory damages. The agency correctly notes that the Supreme Court held that the compensatory damages provision of the Civil Rights Act of 1991 is not to be retroactively applied to conduct that occurred before the November 21, 1991 effective date of the Act. See Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 114 S. Ct. 1483 (1994). As a result of this decision, and as a result of the Commission's vote on May 23, 1994 not to seek compensatory damages for any violation involving pre-Act conduct, the Commission has held that compensatory damages are not available in such cases. Nonetheless, it is clear that compensatory damages are available for violations which occurred after November 21, 1991. As noted by the AJ in this case, some of the incidents of sexual harassment occurred before the effective date of the Act, and some occurred after November 21, 1991. As such, the Commission determines that appellant is entitled to an award of compensatory damages for the pecuniary and non-pecuniary losses she suffered as a result of the incidents of discrimination which occurred subsequent to November 21, 1991. Inasmuch as the record does not clearly specify which incidents of discrimination occurred after November 21, 1991, and what pecuniary and non-pecuniary losses are attributable to these incidents, the Commission finds that a remand for a supplemental investigation is warranted.

Compensatory damages only apply to intentional acts of discrimination. There are no compensatory damages that can be awarded for an agency's improper handling of an EEO complaint. Appleby v. Secretary of Army, 01933897 (1994). In the Appleby case, the complainant alleged that the agency submitted several false answers in response to interrogatories and also submitted a brief to the administrative judge that contained false statements:

[C]ongress added compensatory damages to federal EEO statutes in order to make the perpetrators of intentional employment discrimination liable for non-wage economic consequences of their acts, to the extent necessary to provide full relief to victims of discrimination. See 137 Cong. Rec. at S 15, 484 (daily ed. Oct. 30, 1991). Such damages were not added to the EEO statutes to address how an agency litigates an EEO complaint alleging employment discrimination, but rather, to address how an agency treated an employee or applicant in an employment-related context. For this reason, the Commission finds that appellant is not entitled to recover compensatory damages in this case.

See also Kanta v. Postmaster General, 01963376 (1998) (compensatory damages are not available for delays in the administrative processing of a case); McGill v. Secretary of Navy, 01955718 (1998) (no compensatory damages available for symptoms occurring as a result of complainant's "pursuit of remedies"); March v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01940975 (1994). But see Santiago v. Secretary of Army, 01955684 (1998) (future pecuniary damages were awarded for the full cost of treatment despite testimony that the complainant was "still depressed because of 'manipulations, intimidations' of the EEO case," and that the litigation caused her to relapse).

Compensatory damages only are available on complaints when the underlying allegation is one of discrimination. Compensatory damages are not available as a remedy for breach of a settlement agreement. See Martin v. Secretary of Defense, 05940745 (1995). As a remedy for breach, the Commission only may order reinstatement of the complaint or enforcement of the agreement. As the Commission explained in Child v. Secretary of Transportation, 01952080 (1996):

[C]ongress added compensatory damages to federal EEO statutes in order to make the perpetrators of intentional employment discrimination liable for non-wage economic consequences of their acts, to the extent necessary to provide full relief to victims of discrimination. See 137 Cong. Rec. at S 15, 484 (daily ed. Oct. 30, 1991). Compensatory damages are not available for allegations of breach since such allegations do not involve a determination of whether discrimination has occurred. See 29 CFR 1614.504 (c). Based upon a review of the record herein, we find that any alleged breach was promptly cured by the agency in accordance with the applicable regulations. Accordingly, the agency's decision not to reinstate appellant's complaint was proper, and is AFFIRMED.
