C. DAMAGES UNDER REHABILITATION ACT

Compensatory damages are available to federal employees under the Rehabilitation Act when an agency intentionally discriminates against an employee on the basis of his or her disability, or the agency fails to make a good faith effort to reasonably accommodate an employee's disability. To some degree, this proposition was placed in question by lower court rulings in cases involving claims against the federal government under the Rehabilitation Act for disability discrimination in programs and activities funded by the federal government. However, any question regarding the availability of compensatory damages to federal employees was resolved in Lane v. Pena, 116 S. Ct. 2092 (1996).

In Lane, a cadet was dismissed from the Merchant Marine Academy after a physical examination revealed that he had diabetes mellitus. The cadet brought suit in federal court and his reinstatement to the academy was ordered. Also, the district court found the cadet was entitled to compensatory damages under the Rehabilitation Act. See Lane v. Pena, 867 F. Supp. 1050 (D.D.C. 1994).

Shortly thereafter, however, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals found that a Job Corps participant who tested positive for HIV was not entitled to compensatory damages under the Rehabilitation Act. See Dorsey v. U.S. Department of Labor, 41 F.3d 1551 (D.C. Cir. 1994). The court determined that there was no clear waiver of sovereign immunity in the Rehabilitation Act as it related to federally funded programs and activities. As a result, the district court in Dorsey vacated its earlier decision with respect to compensatory damages in Pena, and the D.C. Circuit summarily affirmed the case.

The Supreme Court granted certiorari in Pena and upheld the finding of the lower courts that compensatory damages were not available in claims against federally funded programs and activities under the Rehabilitation Act. In finding there was no express waiver of sovereign immunity for programs and activities funded by the federal government, the Supreme Court relied heavily upon the 1991 Civil Rights Act amendments that applied to federal employees. Although the question was not directly in front of the Court in Pena, it held that compensatory damages are available in Rehabilitation Act cases involving federal employees, 116 S. Ct. at 2097-98:

The lack of clarity in § 505(a)(2)'s "federal providers" provision is underscored by the precision with which Congress has waived the Federal Government's sovereign immunity from compensatory damages claims for violations of § 501 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 USC § 791, which prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability in employment decisions by the Federal Government. In § 505(a)(1), Congress expressly waived the Federal Government's sovereign immunity against certain remedies for violations of § 501:

"The remedies, procedures, and rights set forth in section 717 of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 [which allows monetary damages]...shall be available, with respect to any complaint under section 501 of this Act, to any employee or applicant for employment aggrieved by the final disposition of such complaint, or by the failure to take final action on such complaint." 29 USC § 794a(a)(1).

Section 505(a)(1)'s broad language--"any complaint under section 501"--suggests by comparison with § 505(a)(2) that Congress did not intend to treat all § 504(a) defendants alike with regard to remedies. Had Congress wished to make Title VI remedies available broadly for all § 504(a) violations, it could easily have, used language in § 505(a)(2) that is as sweeping as the "any complaint" language contained in § 505(a)(1).

But our analysis need not end there. In the Civil Rights Act of 1991, Congress made perfectly plain that compensatory damages would be available for certain violations of § 501 by the Federal Government (as well as other § 501 defendants), subject to express limitations:

"In an action brought by a complaining party under the powers, remedies, and procedures set forth in...section 794a(a)(1) of title 29 [which applies to violations of § 501 by the Federal Government]...against a respondent who engaged in unlawful intentional discrimination (not an employment practice that is unlawful because of its disparate impact) under section 791 of title 29 and the regulations implementing section 791 of title 29, or who violated the requirements of section 791 of title 29 or the regulations implementing section 791 of title 29 concerning the provision of a reasonable accommodation...the complaining party may recover compensatory and punitive damages as allowed in subsection (b) of this section...from the respondent." Rev. Stat. § 1977A, as added, 105 Stat. 1072, 42 USC § 1981a(a)(2).

The Act's attorney's fee provision makes a similar point. Section 505(b) provides that, "[I]n any action or proceeding to enforce or charge a violation of a provision of this title, the court, in its discretion, may allow the prevailing party, other than the United States, a reasonable attorney's fee as part of the costs." 29 USC § 794a(b). This provision likewise illustrates Congress' ability to craft a clear waiver of the Federal Government's sovereign immunity against particular remedies for violations of the Act. The clarity of these provisions is in sharp contrast to the waiver Lane seeks to tease out of §§ 504 and 505(a)(2) of the Act.

The Commission held in James R. v. Environmental Protection Agency, 2019003256 (2020), that the scope of compensatory damages in that reasonable accommodation case was from the agency's formal denial of accommodation until the agency removed its own determination that the complainant was not medically qualified to perform the essential functions of the job. The complainant in that case had a phobia of flying, and the agency permitted him to use other modes of transportation to get to and from his work destinations. After 10 years of this arrangement, the agency issued a new policy that required air travel. The complainant requested to maintain his traveling arrangements with the agency. After reviewing the complainant's medical history and doctor's notes, the agency denied his request, noting that flying was essential to the job and deeming the complainant "not medically qualified to perform the essential functions of the job. The agency in James R. argued that the scope of compensatory damages should end on the day that the agency notified the complainant that he did not have to fly, but the Commission disagreed:

Complainant seeks compensatory damages for the Agency's actions between 1999, when he was rendered unable to fly due to his disability, and his retirement in December 2017. The Agency determined that Complainant was entitled to compensatory damages beginning with his formal denial of accommodation on February 3, 2010, and ending on October 22, 2013, when the Deputy Director, CID, testifies that he spoke with Complainant and assured Complainant that he would not be required to fly, and that the Agency was "not interested" in firing him. We find the proper time frame to calculate compensatory damages from February 3, 2010, through October 15, 2015.

Based on our prior decision and the record, the February 3, 2010 denial of reasonable accommodations triggered Complainant's anxiety and fear that he would be forced to fly or lose his job because it was when he became aware that he would be subject to the CID travel requirements under July 27, 2009 Memo. Moreover, the issue before us is compensatory damages arising from the Agency's discriminatory act found in Claim 1, the February 3, 2010 formal denial of Complainant's request for a reasonable accommodation.

Following the February 3, 2010 formal denial of accommodations, Complainant received a 2010 EPA-CID Medical Review deeming him "not medically qualified to perform the essential functions of the job," because complainant's medical conditions prevented him from flying. Despite his high blood pressure and phobia, Complainant was deemed "medically qualified" in his EPA-CID Medical Reviews from 2001 to 2009 with the exception of a year when he had a knee injury, when he was provided "recommendations" rather than deemed "not medically qualified." Complainant received a status of "not medically qualified to perform the essential functions of his job" in his 2011, 2012, and 2013 EPA-CID Medical Reviews.3 These reviews were essentially re-issuances of the February 3, 2010 formal denial of a reasonable accommodation, creating the same implications for Complainant's job and prolonging anxiety related to the February 3, 2010 formal denial of reasonable accommodations.

The Agency appears to argue that Complainant had an informal accommodation starting October 22, 2013, so that Complainant's anxiety related to denial of reasonable accommodation should have ceased on that date. It is well established in matters of reasonable accommodation that the alleged discrimination is considered ongoing. See Burris v. Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01200645531(Jan. 10, 2007). Even if the conversation between the Deputy Director, CID and Complainant took place on October 22, 2013, which Complainant categorically denies, any assurances he provided were undermined by the EPA-CID Medical Reviews. While the Agency points out that Complainant did not receive a "proposed removal" and was never "forced" to fly, we still find Complainant's anxiety related to the February 10, 2010 formal denial of accommodation to be present until concrete evidence was provided supporting his job security. Based on the record, the first EPA-CID Medical Review in the record to notify Complainant that he was once again "medically qualified" was issued on October 15, 2015.
