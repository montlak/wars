2. Applicants for Employment

The requirement that selection criteria and qualification standards not screen out, or tend to screen out, individuals with disabilities extends to applicants for employment and an agency may have to provide reasonable accommodation to an applicant for employment. In Jones v. Social Security Administration, 0720070002 (2011), the Commission found that the agency violated the qualification standard provisions of the ADA when it failed to accommodate an applicant with a head injury who was applying for a job as a contact representative. The agency had applicants take an oral "Meet and Deal" assessment test. The applicant had requested that he be provided with the questions in written form at the time he took the test. In finding the agency's failure to provide the questions is written form constituted a failure to accommodate, the Commission, in Jones, explained:

The agency asserts that Complainant is not qualified for the Contact Representative position because he is not able to perform the essential functions of the job, as measured by the Meet and Deal Assessment. Specifically, the agency maintains that the Meet and Deal assessment is meant to measure the applicant's ability to immediately process information from a caller, and respond to the question posed. We find that the agency's use of the Meet and Deal Assessment was an employment test as described in 29 C.F.R. § 1630.10 "Qualification Standards, tests and other selection criteria."

We note that it is unlawful for an Agency to use qualification standards, employment tests or other selection criteria that screen out or tend to screen out an individual with a disability or a class of individuals with disabilities, on the basis of disability, unless the standard, test or other selection criteria, as used by the Agency, is shown to be job-related for the position in question and is consistent with business necessity. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.10. See Pointer v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 07A10057 (April 24, 2003) (agency's reinstatement/re-hire policy screened out an individual with a disability and was not shown to be job related and consistent with business necessity when medical condition had been stable for years).

Furthermore, 29 C.F.R. § 1630.11 states: It is unlawful for a covered entity to fail to select and administer tests concerning employment in the most effective manner to ensure that, when a test is administered to a job applicant or employee who has a disability that impairs sensory, manual or speaking skills, the test results accurately reflect the skills, aptitude, or whatever other factor of the applicant or employee that the test purports to measure, rather than reflecting the impaired sensory, manual, or speaking skills of such employee or applicant (except where such skills are the factors that the test purports to measure).

The Interpretive Guidance on Part I of the Americans With Disabilities Act, Section 1630.11 "Administration of Tests," also provides guidance and clarity on the testing of applicants.

The intent of this provision is to further emphasize that individuals with disabilities are not to be excluded from jobs that they can actually perform merely because a disability prevents them from taking a test, or negatively influences the results of a test, that is a prerequisite to the job. Read together with the reasonable accommodation requirement of section 1630.9, this provision requires that employment tests be administered to eligible applicants or employees with disabilities that impair sensory, manual, or speaking skills in formats that do not require the use of the impaired skill."...

For example, "a written test may need to be substituted for an oral test if the applicant taking the test is an individual with a disability that impairs speaking skills or impairs the processing of auditory information.

Accommodation Request

The AJ in this case found that Complainant's request that as an accommodation, he "see the questions in writing, as the questions were being orally asked." (AJ Decision 5-6). On appeal, the agency contends that Complainant requested to modify the test by seeing the questions in advance. This request, the agency argues, was not reasonable as it would render the skills test useless.

Pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.405(a), all post-hearing factual findings by an AJ will be upheld if supported by substantial evidence in the record. Substantial evidence is defined as "such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion." Universal Camera Corp. v. National Labor Relations Board, 340 U.S. 474, 477 (1951) (citation omitted). An AJ's credibility determination based on the demeanor of a witness or on the tone of voice of a witness will be accepted unless documents or other objective evidence so contradicts the testimony or the testimony so lacks in credibility that a reasonable fact finder would not credit it. See EEOC Management Directive 110, Chapter 9, at § VI.B. (November 9, 1999). Although the agency cites to some conflicting evidence in the record, we find there is substantial evidence in the record to support the AJ's finding that Complainant requested that written questions be provided simultaneously with the oral questions, and not in advance.

Agency's Burden

Accordingly, the next question is whether the agency has shown that the use of the test, without the accommodation, is job related and consistent with business necessity, such that it was not required to modify the test. In other words, could the agency use an oral test without providing the written questions at the same time as an accommodation? The agency states that it could not. We disagree. Here, the agency argues that the Meet and Deal Assessment was intended to examine whether the applicant could immediately process verbal questions and interact with callers, which it claims are essential functions of the job. However, the Interpretive Guidance states: "the results of such a test could not be used to exclude an individual with a disability unless the skill was necessary to perform an essential function of the position and no reasonable accommodation was available to enable the individual to perform that function, or the necessary accommodation would impose an undue hardship." (Emphasis added).

We do not find the agency has satisfied its burden of showing that the use of the test, without an accommodation, was job related and consistent with business necessity. There is insufficient evidence that the provision of questions in both written and oral form was unreasonable or would undermine or change the nature of the task being assessed. Likewise, there is insufficient evidence that auditory processing of information alone is an essential function of the job. Indeed, there are multiple ways that Complainant could communicate with the public. The AJ found, and we find substantial evidence to support, that providing Complainant the written form of the questions posed by callers via voice to text computer software, was plausible and would enable him to perform the essential functions of the job. The agency admits that it has used voice to text technology in the past, however, in this case, the agency didn't attempt to provide Complainant with the questions in written and oral form.

Had the agency provided Complainant with the questions in both written and oral form, the agency would have assessed his ability to perform the essential functions of the job, if accommodated. However, instead, the test was done in a way that did not accommodate Complainant, and thus, it failed to measure what it was supposed to measure; the ability of the applicant to absorb and process information. Accordingly, we find that the agency discriminated against Complainant on the basis of his disability because it utilized a test which operated to screen an individual with a disability out of a job, and failed to modify the test so that it could assess the proper abilities. See 29 C.F.R. § 1630.10, 11. The agency did not establish that providing Complainant his requested accommodation would render an undue hardship on the agency. We also find that allowing Complainant to take notes was not a reasonable accommodation as it did not provide Complainant with the ability to read the questions at the same time as hearing the questions. Rather, allowing all applicants to take notes simply was not an effective accommodation for Complainant.

In Milford R. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2019001037 (2019), an applicant for a transportation security officer position argued that the agency should waive its "negative drug test" hiring requirement as an accommodation to his medical marijuana use to treat epilepsy. The agency countered that the complainant was not qualified for the position because he did not meet the medical qualification requirements mandated by the Aviation Transportation and Security Act, namely, "that security screeners 'demonstrate daily a fitness for duty without any impairment due to illegal drugs, sleep deprivation, medication, or alcohol,' 49 U.S.C. § 44935(c)(2)(A)(v)." The agency's written medical qualification guidelines for the at issue position required the complainant to pass a drug test screening, including for marijuana. Since the complainant could not pass the test, the agency argued, he did not meet the ATSA-mandated qualification standards for the position. The Commission agreed in Milford R., holding:

Although the Rehabilitation Act traditionally requires that agencies using qualification standards that screen out individuals on the basis of disability show that the standards are job-related and consistent with business necessity, our precedent dictates that qualification standards developed under the ATSA are not subject to this requirement. Id. Accordingly, in this case, Complainant must show that he can meet the Agency's standards related to drug testing. Complainant has not made this showing. A review of the record clearly reflects that he failed the drug test as a result of his use of medical marijuana. Accordingly, he has not shown that he met the qualifications for the TSO position. Because the ATSA is not subject to the same requirements under the Rehabilitation Act, Milford may be limited in application. It is worth noting, though, given the increased legality of medical marijuana use.
