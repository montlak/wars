1. New Evidence

Although the Commission may order supplementation of a record that it considers to be deficient, it generally does not allow the submission of new evidence once the hearing is adjourned. In Huddleson v. Postmaster General, 0720090005 (2011), the agency attempted to submit two videos of the complainant playing a guitar--an activity the agency contended contradicted his assertion that he had a lifting restriction that constituted a disability. The Commission upheld the finding of the administrative judge that one of the videos existed at the time of hearing and could have been discovered and admitted into evidence had the agency exercised due diligence, and that it would be improper to reopen the hearing record to admit a video purportedly taken a month after the hearing adjourned. The Commission, in Huddleson, explained:

The Agency also asserts that the AJ erred in denying the Agency's request to admit into evidence two CDs depicting Complainant playing the guitar. We disagree. The AJ in an Order dated July 9, 2007 noted that evidence was closed in this matter on May 8, 2007. Specifically, the AJ states that:

[t]he evidence consists of two CDs containing videos of the Complainant, one purportedly recorded on October 7, 2006, and the other purportedly recorded on June 15, 2007. . .. The Agency's counsel states that she did not obtain a copy of the video until June 28, 2007. While I have no reason to doubt the accuracy of that assertion, the fact remains that the video existed at the time of the hearing and deals with an activity that was the subject of testimony at the hearing. The fact that counsel was not in possession of this video, created more than six months earlier, does not provide an acceptable justification for reopening the record. The second video purports to record events occurring on June 15, 2007, more than one month after the hearing concluded and after evidence was closed. It would be entirely improper for me to reopen the record to consider evidence created after the fact, and I decline to do so.

Based on the foregoing, we find that the AJ did not abuse her discretion by denying the Agency's request to admit into evidence the two CDs in question.

The same principle holds true for final agency decisions. Moreover, the agency, in issuing a final decision, cannot rely on evidence that was part of the record. As the Commission stated in Complainant v. Attorney General, 0120132393 (2015), "[t]he Agency cannot just assert that evidence exists somewhere in the universe; it must place such evidence the record." In that case, the agency relied upon a videotape that was not part of the complaint file.
