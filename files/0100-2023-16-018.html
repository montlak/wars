B. ATTORNEY FEES

The Commission's regulations and its case law contain a conflict on the proper procedure for dealing with attorney fees decisions. The regulations suggest that the agency must take final action on a complaint and, if there is a finding of discrimination, advise the complainant of his or her right to recover attorney fees. The complainant then has 30 days to submit a verified statement of fees and costs.

Under the Commission's 1999 regulation amendments, in cases decided by an administrative judge, the verified statement of fees and costs is submitted to the judge who issues a decision on fees. In cases decided by agencies without a hearing, the verified statement is submitted directly to the agency and is subject to a final agency decision.

Although the regulations suggest a two-step process in which the agency issues a final decision on the merits of a claim and subsequently issues a separate final agency decision on attorney fees in appropriate cases, Commission case law suggests that an agency decision is not considered final until the agency has addressed all issues raised by the complaint, including issues related to relief and attorney fees. It is not sufficient for the agency merely to grant relief and award attorney fees. It must issue a final decision on those matters. In Brooks v. Secretary of Air Force, 05910615 (1992), the Commission held:

[P]ursuant to EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.233(a), "the decision of an agency shall be final only when the agency makes a determination on all of the issues in the complaint, including whether or not to award attorney's fees or costs. If a decision to award attorney's fees or costs is made, the decision will not be final until the procedure is followed for determining the amount of the award as set forth in 1613.271(c)." EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.221(c) provides that "When discrimination is found, the agency shall. . .(2) Require remedial action to be taken in accordance with 1613.271." Because the remedy is an integral part of appellant's case when discrimination is found, no decision can be said to be final until the relief is precisely determined.

The Commissioners have previously held that when an agency attempts to implement the relief pursuant to a finding of discrimination, it should issue a final decision and provide appeal rights to the Commission. For example, in Ludell J. Porter v. Anthony M. Frank, Postmaster General, United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900151 (May 10, 1990), the complainant questioned the amount of backpay and annual leave awarded by the agency pursuant to its previous finding of handicap discrimination. When the agency verbally informed her that she had received all leave and pay due her pursuant to the award, the Commission held that the agency actions were improper for the following reasons:

At this point, instead of merely verbalizing the finality of its award, the agency should have issued a final decision pursuant to EEOC Regulations 29 CFR 1613.221(c) and 29 CFR 1613.271(c) delineating the total relief granted including backpay and benefits, with appeal rights to the Commission if appellant was dissatisfied with the final decision. The agency, however, failed to issue a final decision on the award, and, as a result of this omission, it appears that appellant filed the instant complaint to preserve her EEO rights and to express her dissatisfaction over the award.

In Matter of the Request to Reopen by Esther J. Bogda, EEOC Request No. 05870137 (June 8, 1987), the Commission held that an agency letter, which was written approximately eighteen months after the agency issued a FAD finding discrimination and contained the agency's computation of backpay, leave balances, and attorney's fees, "did not constitute a final agency decision initiating the time limit for filing an appeal." According to the Commission, the agency's letter "cannot be considered a final decision because it failed to meet the technical requirements of 29 CFR 1613.221(b)(1) (in that it was not sent by certified mail and a copy was sent to appellant's representative, but not to appellant) and 29 C.F.R. 1613.221(e) (in that a statement of appellant's right to appeal to the Commission was not included.)"

Subsequent cases of the Commission have followed the holding of Bogda. See, e.g., Minda Thrasher v. Edwin J. Derwinski, Secretary, Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01900306 (March 5, 1990) ("agency has apparently failed to inform appellant through an FAD, of its determination of the precise remedy (including, e.g., the amount of backpay to be awarded), to be provided to her"); Carol Fairweather v. William L. Ball, III, Secretary, Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01890523 (May 23, 1989) ("original FAD finding discrimination did not specify the particular grade level in which appellant would be placed, or the amount of backpay. . .," and a subsequent letter did not satisfy the requirements of 29 CFR 1613.221(b)(1) and (e) and thus was "a continuation of the negotiation process, rather than an FAD"); Om Kalra v. James H. Burnley, IV, Secretary, Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01882569 (September 16, 1988) (letters issued subsequent to a finding of discrimination "detailing the grade level in which he would be placed and the amount of backpay to be awarded, also cannot be considered final decisions because they failed to meet the technical requirements of 29 CFR 1613.221(b)(1). . .(e)"). (Footnote omitted.)

In the present case, the agency issued a final decision on November 20, 1989, and adopted the AJ's finding of discrimination. As a result, the agency reinstated appellant with "all attendant pay and benefits" and reconstructed the selection process for the position of Custodial Worker, Regular Full-Time, NA-3566-01. Because the agency's decision did not specify the exact amount of backpay and benefits, the decision was not a "determination on all of the issues in the complaint." Subsequently, the agency notified appellant of her reinstatement by letter dated January 16, 1990, issued her a lump sum payment for her annual leave on February 8, 1990, notified her on or about April 5, 1990, of its computation of backpay, and on August 1, 1990, issued a check in the amount of $9,600 in settlement of appellant's attorney's fees.

The Commission finds that the agency, after it took the various remedial actions implementing the finding of discrimination and made a determination on all of the issues in the complaint, should have issued a final decision delineating the total relief granted and providing appeal rights to the Commission. The Commission finds no merit to the agency's contention in its Request to Reopen that the Commission's previous decision "implicitly requires every Federal agency to issue a new FAD each time it takes an action to implement a FAD." The Commission is mindful that the implementation of remedial action by an agency often involves a long negotiation process. Requiring the agency to issue a FAD each time it attempts to implement corrective relief would result in protracted and piecemeal litigation. The Commission's decision requires the agency to issue a new final agency decision only after it implements all the remedial relief and makes a determination on all the issues in the complaint. Consequently, the danger of protracted and piecemeal litigation is avoided. Accordingly, the Commission denies the agency's Request to Reopen.

In Campbell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01970890 (1998), the Commission found that the agency's adoption of the administrative judge's recommended decision was not a final agency decision because the issue of attorney fees had not been resolved by the administrative judge. The Commission in Campbell therefore found that, since the complainant filed a civil action, dismissal on that basis was proper:

Initially, we find that the agency's adoption of the AJ's recommended decision did not constitute a final agency decision because the agency had not yet made a determination of an appropriate attorney's fees award. With the implementation of the new Regulations at Part 1614, the Commission eliminated the provision for severing the issue of attorney's fees. In the present case, since a finding of discrimination was rendered, appellant would be entitled to full relief, including attorney's fees as a prevailing party. Therefore, absent a specific agreement to sever the issue of attorney's fees in this case, we find that the issue of attorney's fees was outstanding and the agency's June 11, 1996 letter adopting the AJ's decision was not a final decision until that issue was addressed. Therefore, the complaint was still being administratively processed at the time that appellant filed her civil action. The record reveals that appellant filed a civil action on September 9, 1996. A review of the civil action demonstrates that the allegations set forth in the administrative complaint are encompassed in the civil action. Thus, the agency acted appropriately in dismissing appellant's administrative complaint. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint on the grounds that the complaint formed the basis of a pending civil action was proper and is AFFIRMED.

The Campbell decision is somewhat confusing because of the lack of factual detail provided in the written opinion of the Commission. It is clear from the opinion that the complainant prevailed on one claim in her administrative complaint, but did not prevail on other claims. It is unclear, however, whether the complainant's civil action related only to the claims on which she was unsuccessful, or also sought trial de novo on the claim on which she prevailed.

Although the Campbell case involved the recommended decision of an administrative judge, it also applies to final agency decisions on the record. The agency is no longer required to make any findings with respect to attorney fees in cases that were decided by an administrative judge. Under the 1999 amendments, in such cases the administrative judge is responsible for issuing a decision on attorney fees. 29 CFR 1614.501(e).

Although the Commission's decision does not expressly indicate, presumably a notice of appeal rights was included in the agency's "final" decision with a finding of discrimination as well as a notice of the right to file a civil action and the applicable time limits for each. If that is the case, then the complainant in Campbell was faced with a choice between proceeding with an appeal, proceeding with a civil action, or doing nothing and risk losing those rights.

The logical extension of Brooks and Campbell is that, when an agency enters a finding of discrimination it should do so in the form of a preliminary decision that does not include appeal rights. That preliminary decision should, however, include a notice of the right to attorney fees. After receiving the petition for fees, the agency should then issue a final decision with both its finding of discrimination and its determination of a fee award. The regulations, however, do not provide for preliminary decisions.

Under Brooks and Campbell, the Commission adopted a procedure which is markedly different from that followed in the courts and before the MSPB. A court will not entertain a petition for attorney fees until a decision on the merits is final. That means all appeals must be exhausted. Likewise, the Board does not entertain fee petitions until a decision is final. See 5 CFR 1201.203(d). The reason for such a procedure is to eliminate the possibility of needlessly deciding fee petitions. Obviously, if a court of appeals or the Board overturns a favorable decision on appeal, there is no basis for a fee petition. At least one reason for the difference in procedure is that an agency cannot challenge, on appeal, its own finding of discrimination.

In practice, Brooks and Campbell are rarely followed. Instead, a decision is issued on the merits with notice of appeal rights included, as well as a notice of the right to attorney fees. The decision on attorney fees is then processed separately. There is nothing wrong with either procedure. The real problem is that the Commission needs to make clearer to agencies when a decision becomes final, so that a notice of appeal rights and civil action rights accompany only a final decision.
