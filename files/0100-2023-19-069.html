6. Preselection

An appellant does not necessarily rebut the proffered agency explanation by demonstrating that the successful candidate was preselected. The appellant must show that the preselection was based on a prohibited motive. Sumrell v. Department of Justice, 01840190 (1984). The Commission determined that the evidence displayed the agency engaged in preselection of the younger candidate, but also found that appellant's nonselection was based on a personality conflict rather than age, as alleged. See also Nolette v. Secretary of the Navy, 01920154, 01912035 (1991).

When preselection is at issue "[e]vidence relevant to such a showing of pretext includes. . .disturbing procedural irregularities. . .and (2) the use of subjective criteria." Caldwell v. USPS, 01880601 (1988) (citing Williams v. DeKalb County, 577 F.2d 248 (5th Cir. 1978), modified on other grounds, 582 F.2d 2 (5th Cir. 1978); Mohammed v. Callaway, 698 F.2d 395 (10th Cir. 1983); and Rowe v. General Motors Corp., 457 F.2d 348 (5th Cir. 1972)). In the Caldwell case, the selecting official made a significant departure from regular procedures by deciding not to employ a promotion panel, but to conduct the entire selection procedure himself. The selecting official then inflated the qualifications of the female candidates and, at the same time, minimized the qualifications of the complainant. Finally, the interview results were based entirely on subjective criteria.

"Even if the Commission found evidence that the selection was based on friendship, that alone would not violate Title VII." Montoya v. Secretary of Commerce, 01941532 (1995) (citing Hawkins v. McPherson, 42 FEP 699, 703 (D.D.C. 1986)).

Even where there is strong evidence that the selectee was preselected and is not qualified for the position, the complainant must still prove that the preselection was based on discrimination. Hawkins v. Secretary of Navy, 01956446 (1996).

In McKithan v. Secretary of Interior, 01950123 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant proved discrimination based, in part, on evidence that the selectee was preselected. But the Commission also found there was other evidence of discrimination in McKithan:

After a thorough review of all the evidence of record, the Commission finds appellant has met his burden of establishing, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the reasons offered by the agency for his nonselection were a pretext masking racial discrimination. First, the Commission finds that the weight of the evidence indicates that, despite the agency's claim that a competitive selection process occurred, the selectee, in fact, had been preselected by the Washington office. Both the staff from Washington and the Selecting Official admitted that the selectee had been initially chosen to be detailed to the position, and that appellant had not even been considered in this process. In fact, the Selecting Official stated he first learned about the creation of the position from the selectee. It was only when personnel regulations prohibited a noncompetitive promotion of this length, that agency officials were forced to issue a vacancy announcement and solicit applications from candidates other than the selectee. Despite the Selecting Official's assertions that he tried to fairly consider all the applicants, it is clear that the selectee had a considerable advantage in the selection process due to his insider's knowledge of the position and its requirements. While preselection is not a per se violation of Title VII, it is an indicator that heightened scrutiny of the selection process is needed.

When more carefully examining the relative qualifications of the appellant and the selectee, the agency's claim that the selectee was the plainly superior candidate is called into question. It is undisputed that appellant had many more years of supervisory experience, and an academic record which far surpassed that of the selectee. In addition, the record indicates that appellant had some of the relevant computer skills focused on by the agency as he had been instrumental in developing a database program for the National Historic Landmarks Program and had attended a computer training course on database manipulation for the Historic Preservation Program. Regarding planning skills, appellant stated that as a manager he was consistently involved in planning, and strategic, comprehensive planning was part of his state program review responsibilities. Specifically, appellant pointed to the fact that he wrote the implementation plan for the Secretary of Interior's Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCU) initiative. Of particular note, appellant had developed a cultural resource management plan for the historical resources at several Air Force bases and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, exactly the type of task required by the position in question. The Selecting Official, as appellant's first-level supervisor, was well aware of appellant's accomplishments in these areas.

Finally, although not dispositive, we find that appellant's evidence with regard to a pattern of promotions of white employees to the GM-14/15 grade level relevant to the issue of pretext in this matter. The workforce profile of the Southeast Regional office indicates that of the 20 employees at grades 14 and 15 during the relevant period, all but one was white (95%). Appellant asserted that during the four-year period preceding the selection at issue, at least four white employees were noncompetitively promoted to the GS-14 level. The record indicates that the Selecting Official was instrumental in at least one of those upgrades. Yet the agency has failed to explain why appellant's January 1993 bid for a similar position upgrade was met with little support from management. Clearly, the selectee in the instant matter was destined to be the fifth white employee noncompetitively promoted to grade 14 had personnel regulations not required advertising this particular position.

In summary, the record presents strong evidence of a preselection and other procedural irregularities; appellant with qualifications at least equal, if not superior, to the selectee; and statistical evidence of a failure to promote non-white employees to the grade 14/15 level despite their presence at the grade 13 level. While none of these factors, standing alone, establishes discrimination, in combination they are sufficient to prove that it was more likely than not that appellant was not selected for the position in question because he was black.

A demonstration by complainant that the successful candidate was selected before his interview effectively rebuts the agency's assertion that the complainant's poor performance at the interview was the reason for his nonselection. Howard v. Secretary of Defense, 01A51092 (2006).
