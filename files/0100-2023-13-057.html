B. UNTIMELY INVESTIGATIONS

The agency is required to complete its investigation of a complaint within 180 days of the filing of the complaint, unless the complainant agrees voluntarily to extend the time period for investigation. Once the 180 days expire, in the absence of an agreement to extend the time period, the agency may not compel the complainant's continued cooperation in the investigation. Koch v. Securities & Exchange Commission, 01965642 (1997). Under Koch, it is improper for the agency to dismiss the complaint for failure to cooperate after the 180-day time period has expired.

A complainant is not harmed by an agency's delay in completing an investigation where there is no evidence of intentional delay and the complaint is ultimately investigated and adjudicated. Conyers v. Secretary of Air Force, 01966816 (1998).

When an agency fails to complete an investigation within the 180--day regulatory period and the complainant exercises her right to request a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge, the agency loses jurisdiction over the case. It may not, subsequent to the request for a hearing, initiate an investigation and then dismiss the case because the complainant fails to cooperate with the investigation. In Jones-Sims v. Postmaster General, 01A50251 (2006), the complainant requested a hearing after 180 days elapsed with no investigation. The Commission found that it was error when the agency then initiated the investigation and subsequently dismissed the case for failure to cooperate when the complainant refused to provide an affidavit. However, the complainant later withdrew her request for a hearing and requested a final agency decision on the record. The Commission in Jones-Sims found that, upon withdrawal of the request for a hearing, the agency had an obligation to conduct the investigation prior to issuing a decision on the case, noting:

After complainant withdrew her hearing request and asked for a FAD, the agency was required to develop a complete and impartial factual record. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(b); EEOC Management Directive 110, November 9, 1999, Chapter 6, page 6-1. Unlike a court proceeding, which is an adversary process in which the plaintiff is required to adduce a certain quantum of evidence before the case proceeds, the EEO process is intended to be a neutral fact finding process that recognizes that the agency is far better situated than the complainant to obtain information and explain the reasons for its decisions. Thus, the agency is not excused from obtaining whatever information is available to it.

As a result, the Commission vacated the agency's final decision and remanded the case for investigation and a new decision.

Over the past several years, the EEOC has become more vigorous in imposing sanctions against agencies, including default judgment, for failure to timely complete investigations. See, Chapter 8, section on "Default Judgment."
