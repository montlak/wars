X. CONSOLIDATION

Consolidation of similar complaints from different complainants is permitted. Consolidation of two or more complaints by the same complainant for investigation is required at the investigation stage. Title 29 CFR 1614.606 provides:

Complaints of discrimination filed by two or more complainants consisting of substantially similar allegations of discrimination or relating to the same matter may be consolidated by the agency or the Commission for joint processing after appropriate notification to the parties. Two or more complaints of discrimination filed by the same complainant shall be consolidated by the agency for joint processing after appropriate notification to the complainant. When a complaint has been consolidated with one or more earlier filed complaints, the agency shall complete its investigation within the earlier of 180 days after the filing of the last complaint or 360 days after the filing of the original complaint, except that the complainant may request a hearing from an administrative judge on the consolidated complaints any time after 180 days from the date of the first filed complaint. Administrative judges or the Commission may, in their discretion, consolidate two or more complaints of discrimination filed by the same complainant.

Under the 1992 version of Part 1614, consolidation of multiple complaints by the same complainant was merely permissible. The 1999 amendments, for the first time, mandate consolidation of complaints by the same complainant. Note, however, that consolidation of complaints by the same complainant is discretionary at the hearing or appellate stage. "Consolidation" is discussed more thoroughly in Chapter 3.

Although the precise wording of the regulations speaks in terms of the agency or the Commission taking the initiative to consolidate complaints, this does not prevent the complainant from making such a request. See Bacon v. Secretary of Commerce, 01911313 (1991). Even before consolidation was mandatory, the Commission had held that it was error not to consolidate claims that were intertwined. For example, in Bacon, the Commission also found that the agency abused its discretion in failing to consolidate complaints where there were factual issues common to both complaints and the issues were intertwined such that disposition of one complaint would impact on the other complaints. Likewise, the Commission held that an agency improperly failed to consolidate cases where the agency ignored attempts to consolidate by the complainant's attorney. In Doedhar v. Secretary of Navy, 01950824 (1996), the Commission observed:

[W]e find that appellant's attorney (the attorney) made considerable efforts to have appellant's complaints consolidated, and to thereby preserve both appellant's and the agency's resources. Specifically, the attorney made numerous telephone calls to the Director, EARB which were not returned, and hand-delivered appellant's request for consolidation. For some as yet unexplained reason, however, the agency chose to ignore these attempts and issued a FAD on Case No. 93-00253-003, despite appellant's request to have this case consolidated with Case No. 94-00253-004.

The Commission ordered that the cases be consolidated.
