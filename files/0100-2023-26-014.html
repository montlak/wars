1. Participation in Proceedings Involving Others

The scope of the participation clause is broad enough to protect employees who provide information to fellow employees on their discrimination complaints, even on an informal basis.

In Bell v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01853377 (1987), the complainant relayed information overheard in relation to a fellow employee's MSPB proceeding. The Board proceeding involved issues relating to race discrimination. The complainant was denied conversion to a permanent position on the basis that she told the employee involved in the Board proceedings of a conversation she overheard in the personnel office regarding the Board case. "When an employee relays personal knowledge on an informal, ex parte basis to a party to a proceeding involving Title VII issues, his participation in the pending investigation and proceeding has begun." Id. (citing Smith v. Columbus Metropolitan Housing Authority, 443 F. Supp. 61 (S.D. Ohio 1977)).

The Commission in Bell dispensed with the agency's contention that the removal was taken because the complainant breached a confidentiality rule by engaging in a balancing test:

In order to determine whether conduct such as that allegedly engaged in by appellant is protected activity, we must balance the setting in which the activity arose with the interests and motivations of the appellant and the agency.... In Hochstadt, the court stated that "the requirements of the job and the tolerable limits of conduct in a particular setting must be explored."...

In United States v. City of Milwaukee, 390 F. Supp. 1127 (E.D. Wisc. 1975), the Milwaukee Police had a written rule treating as confidential the official business of the Department. Department officials interpreted that rule to bar employees from talking with Justice Department representatives regarding the terms and conditions of employment pursuant to an investigation of the Department's alleged discriminatory practices. The court granted an injunction against enforcing the rule, holding that "the threat or 'chilling effect' posed by [the Department's] interpretation of the rule cannot be allowed to frustrate plaintiff's discovery efforts in this lawsuit." Id. at 1128.

The Commission found that the employer's interest in confidentiality was outweighed by the relevance of the statements to the discrimination issues in the Board proceeding. It further noted that the agency had no written policy of confidentiality and that the office door was open when the complainant overheard the statements.

The Commission contrasted its holding in the Bell case with that of Jefferies v. Harris County Community Action Assn., 615 F.2d 1025 (5th Cir. 1980). In the Jeffries case, the plaintiff was terminated for surreptitiously copying and disseminating personnel records. The court found there was no need for such activity where there was no indication that the employer would have otherwise destroyed the records. See also O'Day v. McDonnell Douglas Helicopter, supra.

Giving a statement to a complainant's representative is protected activity, whether the statement is voluntary or involuntary. Garner v. Postmaster General, 01881064 (1988).

A federal employee is protected not only for participation in the EEO complaint process that applies to federal workers, but also for participation in other discrimination complaint processes, whether or not they are based on Title VII, including one designed to eradicate discrimination against military employees. In Causus v. Secretary of Air Force, 01920888 (1992), the Commission found:

Section 704(a) of Title VII provides in part:

"It shall be an unlawful employment practice for an employer to discriminate against any of his employees...because he has opposed any practice made an unlawful employment practice by this subchapter, or because he has made a charge, testified, assisted, or participated in any manner in an investigation, proceeding or hearing under this subchapter."

This section has been read to provide employees two avenues for protection from discriminatory retaliation by an employer--the "opposition clause" and the "participation clause." The opposition clause refers to an employee having opposed any discriminatory practice that violates the prohibitions of Title VII. The participation clause refers to employee participation of some kind in a proceeding "under this subchapter"--thus excluding from the section an employee's right to invoke this section pursuant to some other statute or regulation. (footnote omitted). "Under this Subchapter" does not necessarily refer to participation in an ongoing proceeding before the EEOC. See Hicks v. ABT Assoc. Inc., 16 F.E.P. Cases 802 at 809 (3d Cir. 1979).

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.261, which implements 704(a) for federal employees, similarly provides in part that it is unlawful to restrain, interfere, coerce, or discriminate against an individual because he has "filed a charge of discrimination, testified, assisted or participated in any manner with an investigation, proceeding or hearing" under this part. (footnote omitted).

In deciding whether appellant's conduct was protected under the "participation clause," the issue on appeal is whether Section 704(a) was intended to protect civilian employees, such as appellant, who come forward as witnesses in discrimination proceedings under the armed force's internal discrimination complaint procedures. We hold appellant was protected under the "participation clause" of Section 704(a).

Section 704(a) was enacted to encourage employee assistance in furthering the primary purpose of Title VII--eradication of racial, ethnic, religious and gender based discrimination in the workplace. In the Equal Employment Opportunity Act, Congress specifically extended the protections of Title VII to include civilian employees in military departments of the federal government by adding Section 717 to Title VII. See Johnson v. Alexander, 572 F.2d 1219 at 1221 (1978). Uniformed members of the military, however, were excluded from the protections of Title VII. Id. Consequently, non-civilian military employees bring discrimination complaints, similar to those brought under Title VII, within an internal complaint system.

Although the legal system, including the legal avenues for combating discrimination, is distinct for members of the armed forces, civilian and non-civilians work side by side in various areas of the military. It is easy to envision many situations, such as here, when civilians feel compelled to take part in a discrimination proceeding within the military court system. Title VII should be liberally construed to protect its policies, and there is nothing in Section 704(a), or in the Commission's Regulations implementing the section, which compels a statutory interpretation that would paralyze such participation by civilian employees in the military. (footnote omitted).

Consequently, we find that the "participation clause" of EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.261 protects employees who choose to serve as witnesses in discrimination proceedings within the military's internal complaint system....
