D. ALCOHOL AND DRUG DEPENDENCY

Alcoholism and drug dependency by federal employees are areas that have been the subject of constant turmoil in the past. As a society, we have come from the days of treating alcoholism and substance dependency as willful misbehavior to properly regarding them as illnesses. They are now recognized as illnesses that are treatable, if not entirely curable. On the other hand, the pendulum clearly has swung from the liberal days of regarding alcoholism and substance abuse purely as diseases that are visited upon unsuspecting victims. Because, at least at the outset, there is an element of conscious choice involved in alcoholism and substance abuse, these conditions are not treated the same as other illnesses.

There is perhaps no other area where the employee's entitlement to reasonable accommodation and the interests of the agency in maintaining a safe and efficient workplace come so directly into conflict. Congress has addressed this conflict, in part, in the Civil Rights Act of 1991 and the Americans with Disabilities Act. The Civil Rights Act provides that it shall not be unlawful to impose rules barring the employment of any person who currently and knowingly uses or possesses certain drugs defined under the Controlled Substances Act, unless the rule discriminates on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. The Americans with Disabilities Act specifically exempts from its provisions any employee who is currently engaged in the illegal use of drugs. The Commission addressed this requirement in its regulations. See 29 CFR 1630.3. The ADA also makes it clear that drug addiction or alcoholism is not an excuse for misconduct or poor performance. Employees who suffer from drug addiction or alcoholism are subject to the same conduct and performance standards as other employees. See 42 USC 12114(c)(4).

The Commission articulated a general formula for analyzing cases involving alleged discrimination on the basis of alcoholism. "Generally, to make out a facial showing that s/he is qualified, an alcoholic employee must show that s/he is able to perform the essential functions of the position when not drinking. In addition, the employee must show that there are plausible reasons to believe that the disability of alcoholism can be accommodated.... Thereafter, the burden is on the agency to show that it cannot provide reasonable accommodation." Beck v. Secretary of Army, 01924140 (1994).

It is difficult to articulate a formula for analyzing substance abuse cases. In part, this is because the first part of any formulation is necessarily a demonstration that the employee is a qualified individual with a disability. But, if the employee's condition involves current drug use and the agency action is based on that use or on misconduct related to that use, the employee is not a qualified individual with a disability. Any inquiry ends without advancing to the next stage. Indeed, this is where most substance abuse cases end, except for a few involving past substance abusers claiming an agency action is based on their record of a disability. Such cases are unlikely, however, to involve issues of reasonable accommodation; invariably they involve allegations of disparate treatment. For example, in Ferrell v. Postmaster General, 0120064642 (2008), the complainant did not even allege disability discrimination when he was suspended for seven days after failing a blood alcohol level test. Instead, he alleged and prevailed on a race discrimination claim showing that he was disciplined more harshly than similarly situated white coworkers who had failed the test. As demonstrated by that case, nothing in the ADA precludes an employer from disciplining an employee for alcohol-related misconduct as long as does not do so on a discriminatory basis.

Another principle that also often comes into play in cases involving alcohol and substance abuse is that a disability is never an excuse for past misconduct or performance problems even if the misconduct or poor performance is caused by a disability. The general rule is that an agency may treat an employee with a disability in terms of misconduct and poor performance the same as it would treat a similarly situated employee without a disability in the similar circumstances. A recent example of that principle is Gatseos v. Postmaster General, 0120090902 (2010). In that case, the complainant, a mail carrier, claimed that the agency discriminated against her on the basis of her alcoholism when it terminated her for purchasing and consuming alcohol on the job in several instances. The complainant contended that she should have been referred to EAP instead of being disciplined. In affirming a grant of summary judgment, the Commission, in Gatseos, wrote:

The agency articulated legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for its actions. The record contains a copy of complainant's Notice of Removal (Notice) dated July 6, 2007. The Notice provides that complainant is being terminated for "unacceptable conduct." Specifically, the Notice provides that complainant purchased and consumed alcohol on numerous occasions while on the clock. The Notice also sets forth that complainant extended her lunch period on various occasions and that complainant made Managed Service Point (MSP) scans while at a bar. Specifically, the Notice provides that "[b]y making MSP scans while [at a named bar], [complainant] gave the impression [she was] at a particular address delivering mail when in fact [she was] not." The record also contains a copy of an agency Office of Inspector General Report pertaining to complainant dated June 18, 2007 (Report). The Report provides that complainant and another carrier were consuming alcoholic drinks and driving an agency vehicle after consuming these drinks. The report also provides that complainant made several MSP scans while inside a bar.

Complainant did not establish that the agency's articulated reason for issuing the Notice was pretext for discrimination. Complainant asserts that the agency should have provided her with a chance to complete a rehabilitation program prior to disciplining her. However, the Commission has stated that an employer is not required to excuse past misconduct even if it is the result of the individual's disability. EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, No. 915.002 at question 36 (October 17, 2002). Complainant's asserts that male employees who committed similar offenses did not receive the same type of discipline that she did. However, the AJ properly found that these male employees were not similarly situated to complainant. Specifically, the AJ properly noted that the male employees in question were not cited "with a combination of extending their lunch breaks, consuming alcohol in uniform while on and prior to returning to duty and making MSP scans while at a bar making it appear as if the mail was being delivered at various sites when it was not."

In this area in particular, the reader is cautioned that current research is critical. Both statutory and case law have been subject to rapid change. As in most areas where the law is subject to change, it is generally helpful to know how the law has progressed over time. Refer to Chapter 14, "Drug and Alcohol Abuse."
