1. Purpose of Mixed Case Legislation

Prior to discussing, in detail, the procedures that apply to mixed cases, it will be helpful to have some understanding of the congressional purpose in providing for the processing of mixed cases.

In Gubisch v. Department of Treasury, 05870075 (1987), the Commission gave a detailed account of the legislative history of the Civil Service Reform Act vis-a-vis mixed cases and the respective roles of the MSPB and the EEO process. Because mixed cases often can create a good deal of confusion, the account in Gubisch is reproduced here to give the reader an idea of how the mixed case process came to pass:

The legislative history of the Civil Service Reform Act establishes that the MSPB has primary responsibility to initially adjudicate "mixed cases." The Presidential message of March 2, 1978, transmitting the draft legislation on civil service reform to Congress, outlined the objectives of the proposal; one of which was to promote equal employment opportunity. Legislative History of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, 96th Cong., 1st Sess. (Comm. Print 1979) Vol. 1, 640 (hereinafter cited as Legislative History). The functions of the Civil Service Commission were divided between equal and independent agencies: the Office of Personnel Management, to act as the central federal personnel office; MSPB, to adjudicate appeals from aggrieved federal employees; and, this Commission, to enforce the principles of equal employment in the federal work force.

It was intended that the MSPB handle both the personnel aspects of an appealable action as well as issues of discrimination raised by the employee. 5 USC 7702(a)(1). As Senator Bayh stated during the Senate debate on the bill: "I do not think any member of this body wants civil service reform to be viewed as an effort to shortcut and lessen our efforts to wipe out the last vestiges of discrimination in public service." Legislative History, Vol. 2 at 1716. The Senate Committee report stated that, "The [MSPB] will assume principal responsibility for safeguarding merit principals and employee rights" and "the appeal procedures...should also improve the handling of discrimination complaints for Federal employees." Legislative History, Vol. 2 at 1470 and 1474. As the Senate noted, the Civil Service Reform Act placed on federal agencies for the first time an affirmative mandate to adhere to merit system principles, one of which was the elimination of discrimination. Legislative History, Vol. 2 at 1483. 5 USC 2301(b). Moreover, the Act specifically states that it should not be construed as extinguishing or lessening "any effort to achieve equal employment opportunity through...and right of remedy available to any employee in the civil service under Section 717 of the Civil Rights Act of 1964." 5 USC 2302(d).

The Senate Committee reported that the appellate procedures set forth in Section 7701:

will allow the Board [MSPB] to consider, as related aspects of the same case, allegations that there had been violations of the merit system principles implemented by Title V, as well as anti-discrimination laws. In such cases, questions of the employee's inefficiency or misconduct, and discrimination by the employer, will be two sides of the same question which must be considered together. Any provision denying the Board jurisdiction to decide certain adverse action appeals because discrimination is raised as an issue would make it impossible for the government to have a single unified personnel policy which took into account the requirements of all the various laws and goals governing Federal personnel management. Legislative History, Vol. 2 at 1517.

Congress also stated in discussing the appellate procedures at § 7702(a): "It is expected that the Board [MSPB] will make adequate training and resources available for training and supervision of these appeals officers provided for in Section 7702(a) to avoid the possibility of inadequate preparation for the processing of those appeals matters which involve allegations of discrimination. Legislative History, Vol. 2 at 1982.

MSPB's regulations setting forth its organization and procedures became effective June 29, 1979, 5 CFR 1201.1 et seq. [44 Fed. Reg. 127]. The policy of the Board regarding discrimination allegations is stated as follows:

It is the policy of the Board to adjudicate impartially, thoroughly and fairly all issues raised under this subpart in the course of an action brought before the Board. In doing so the Board will allow appellant an opportunity to raise allegations of discrimination during the appeals process and to present fully evidence in support of the charges raised. 5 CFR 1201.151(b).

Subsequent sections provide procedures for processing "mixed cases" i.e., allegations of discrimination in appealable actions. If an aggrieved employee has been affected by an appealable action and timely files a formal complaint of discrimination with the employing agency, the employee may not file an appeal with MSPB prior to the expiration of 120 days unless he has received a final decision from the agency. He may file an appeal at any point after the expiration of 120 days but within a year after the filing of the formal complaint if the agency has not issued a decision. 5 CFR 1201.154(a)(1) and (2) (1985). The Board, in discussing its reasons for the one year limitation, stated that this time frame "is necessary because in its absence, the information necessary to adjudicate the case might be unavailable." Preamble to MSPB Regulations, 44 Fed. Reg. 38348. The Preamble and structure of this regulation indicate that the purpose of this provision was (1) for the agency to process the EEO complaint, and (2) that this information be available to the Board, although the employee was given an opportunity to appeal to the Board if the agency failed to respond.

Section 1201.155 concerns allegations of discrimination not raised in a petition to the Board. It provides that an aggrieved employee may raise such allegations at any time if he did not know of the existence of a basis for the allegation at the time the appeal was filed. 5 CFR 1201.155(a). According to the Preamble, "[t]he legislative history makes it clear that the Board is to be liberal in receiving discrimination complaints...." 44 Fed. Reg. 38348. The section also specifies that if the issue of discrimination is excluded from consideration because it was raised after the filing of the appeal, the allegation must be remanded to the agency for consideration. This provision indicates that while the Board is deciding under these circumstances not to adjudicate the allegations of discrimination, the individual's right to raise the issue is not lost.

Subsection (b) directs, in mandatory language, that the presiding official use his discovery authority "to develop the record sufficiently to make a determination on the merits of the allegation." 5 CFR 1201.155(b). The Preamble explains that Subsection (b) "has been expanded to provide that the presiding official must exercise his/her authority liberally in developing the record in cases when the issue of discrimination is raised during a proceeding under 5 USC 7701." 44 Fed. Reg. 38384. (emphasis added).

The Commission concludes from a review of these regulations that the Board has recognized its responsibility in developing a record when discrimination has been raised; information about discrimination allegations is relevant and pertinent to its policy of adjudicating appeals impartially and fairly; and, information developed in the agency's processing of a related EEO complaint should be and would be available to the Board.

Although the mixed case procedure is complex, the overall goal is to consolidate certain appellate actions within the Board's jurisdiction while accounting for the primacy of the EEOC on matters involving employment discrimination.

The complexity of the system means that it is subject to abuse. Particularly, for those who actually understand the system there is the potential of taking advantage of those who do not understand it. In Luellen v. Postmaster General, 01951340 (1996), the Commission found that the agency tried to manipulate the system through a request to remand allegations to the MSPB for hearing. The motion to remand, the Commission found, was done for the purpose of escaping the administrative judge's recommended finding of discrimination. The Commission in Luellen observed:

We also note that, during the hearing, the agency's representative [asked] for a remand of the case on the grounds that the matter was a "mixed case." He argued that the complaint raised the issue of constructive discharge or suspension that had to be appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board. The AJ denied the agency's motion. She noted that the complaint concerned the agency's decision to reassign appellant to another area, not constructive discharge or suspension. Moreover, she also noted that even if constructive discharge or suspension had been raised, the complaint was "so enmeshed" in the EEO process that it should remain. The AJ cited a Commission precedent, Simon v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910112 (January 17, 1991), to support her position. The AJ stressed that at the time the motion was made, a full evidentiary hearing had been held, in which five witnesses were called, including an expert witness for appellant. She also noted that a preliminary ruling indicating a violation had already been issued. The agency, in the FAD, repeated its contention that this complaint was a "mixed case" that should have been remanded.

At the outset, we find that the AJ was correct when she determined that, even if the issues of constructive discharge or suspension were raised, this matter was too enmeshed in the EEO process to remand to the agency for submission to the MSPB. We agree that a remand at this stage would be a substantial inconvenience to appellant, and would not promote judicial economy because it would result in the duplication of efforts. The agency's motion appears to have been designed to circumvent the AJ's finding of discrimination.
