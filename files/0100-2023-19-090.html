N. ALTERNATIVE, NONDISCRIMINATORY REASONS

"Where the agency cites two or more ostensibly legitimate reasons for its actions, appellant cannot prevail unless he proves that all of those reasons are pretext." McGuire v. Postmaster General, 01883494 (1989) (citing Sims v. Cleland, 813 F.2d 790 (6th Cir. 1987)); see also Thomas v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01891407 (1989) (appellant discredited three out four reasons for his nonselection; the agency decision upheld on the basis of its fourth proffered reason).

A similar result was obtained in Emery v. Secretary of Interior, BIA, 01942802 (1995). In Emery, the Commission found that "while one of the reasons proffered by the agency was not sufficient to justify appellant's termination, i.e., the delay in arresting the suspect, nevertheless appellant's being insubordinate in refusing to give up his police vehicle keys in a emergency situation, together with his failure to report the child abuse of his daughter were sufficient legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons in themselves to justify his termination." See also Logue v. International Rehabilitation Associates, Inc., 837 F.2d 150, 153 (3rd Cir. 1988) (holding that where an employer articulates more than one reason for its challenged action, the falseness or incorrectness of one reason does not impeach the credibility of the remaining reasons).

Although the Commission itself has held that a complainant must demonstrate that all proffered reasons are pretextual, in Burns v. AAF-McQuay, Inc., 96 F.3d 728 (4th Cir. 1996), the EEOC joined the plaintiff in arguing that there are cases where a plaintiff need not demonstrate that all of the proffered reasons are pretext in order to prevail. The court in Burns, 96 F.3d at 732, sided with the EEOC:

A showing of pretext demands the same type of proof--affirmative evidence of discriminatory intent--required under traditional principles, but not necessarily the same quantum. Affirmative proof that alone does not suffice under the traditional scheme may, when combined with the weakness of the defendant's asserted nondiscriminatory reasons, create a prima facie case of pretext.

In the Burns case, the complainant offered the following proof of age bias: her supervisor told her that she did not fit into his group, he accused her of walking around like a 10-year-old even though she was 65 years old, he questioned her about her plans to retire, and he threatened to demote her. The supervisor also expressed concerns about the quality of her work, including typographical errors. In all, the defendant cited approximately 18 incidents that allegedly motivated its demotion of Burns, but the district court found that Burns cast into doubt all but two of those reasons consisting of a few typographical errors and an alleged failure to schedule a meeting. The district court noted that a jury could find that those two incidents truly motivated AAF-McQuay's demotion of Burns, or it might disbelieve Burns's contradictory evidence and conclude that all eighteen incidents contributed to the decision. The court, in Burns, 96 F.3d at 733, held:

However, if the jury accepts Burns's evidence contradicting sixteen of the eighteen incidents, and her proof that Horney made ambiguous comments about her age, it might reasonably conclude that AAF-McQuay used the two remaining incidents as a pretext for age-based discrimination.

Although the court found that the complainant's evidence of age discrimination was not particularly strong, it was sufficient to withstand summary judgment given the weakness of the employer's proffered reasons. See also discussion on "Direct Evidence or Mixed Motive Cases," below.
