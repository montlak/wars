o. Work Performed on Agency Premises and Equipment

In Shorter v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120131148 (2013), the Commission found that the agency exercised sufficient control where the work was performed on agency premises, the agency controlled many of the work assignments, she had served fulltime at the agency for approximately two years and the agency had substantial input into the contractor's decision to terminate her employment). See also Pietsch v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120090933 (2009) (finding sufficient control where the complainant had been on the agency premises for eight years, used agency space and equipment for work, and performed services that the Commission deemed "the regular business of the agency.").

However, the fact that the contract employee works on agency premises and uses agency equipment is not sufficient to establish an employee-employer relationship where the contractor maintains control of all other aspects of the work. Allen v. Secretary of Army, 0120131268 (2013). Similarly, while the agency's control over the contractor's employment is a significant factor, it can be overcome by other factors. See, e.g. Complainant v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 0120131991 (2013) (agency could ban contractor from premises which was tantamount to termination but other factors warranted determination that the complainant was not an agency employee).

Mere provision of work space, supplies, and equipment to perform a job is not a sufficient indicia of control where the contractor otherwise controls all aspects of the employment relationship. For example, in Floro v. Secretary of Army, 01A12454 (2002), the Commission found:

In this case, while we find that complainant performed his contract duties at the agency's facility, using the agency's supplies and equipment, the record confirms that complainant was not on the agency's payroll and derived no employment benefits, such as paid leave, medical insurance or retirement benefits. We also find that the record fails to reflect any documentary evidence that the Program Manager set complainant's hours and schedule, required him to request leave, conducted routine performance evaluations, or administered discipline. We also find that the type of services complainant provided were not particularly conducive to supervision, being not only highly technical in nature, but commensurate with executive upper level management responsibilities. Furthermore, we find that provision of these services necessarily included close consultation and coordination with the Program Manager, as well as other contractors, and that all of the contractors, including complainant, made status reports at weekly meetings with the Program Manager. We find that this working arrangement suggests a collaborative working relationship as opposed to one of close supervision by the Program Manager. Moreover, despite complainant's contentions to the contrary, we find that the record is devoid of any evidence to demonstrate that the Program Manager exercised day-to-day control over complainant's routine work. While complainant provides the names of numerous witnesses who he asserts can attest to the level of supervisory control maintained by the Program Manager, he provides no statements from any of these witnesses.

Furthermore, based on our review, we find that the evidence of record largely belies complainant's assertion regarding the degree of control the Program Manager had over his work. Specifically, on January 25, 1999, the Program Manager issued complainant a "Cure-Notice" specifying his purported non-compliance with his contract, providing him with 10-days to submit a proposal to address the problems. We find that this demonstrates that while the Program Manager had knowledge of complainant's performance, she did not issue "discipline" or set out an improvement plan for him, but instead she left it to his discretion to propose and carry out a cure for the noted problems. Furthermore, we find that e-mail correspondence dated in early 2000, reflects that numerous problems were reported to the Program Manager regarding complainant's failure to obtain certain documents, and that as the matter grew critical, the Program Manager had to "constantly monitor" complainant's progress, but otherwise merely directed complainant to contact certain individuals to obtain these documents, and to get "hands-on" involved to expedite the process. We find that both of these examples reflect instances where there were problems with complainant's work, requiring the Program Manager to intervene, and that even in these instances, the Program Manager largely left it to complainant's discretion to deal with the problems within the parameters of his contractual duties.

See also Jones v. Secretary of Army, 01A15256 (2002) (finding no joint employment where the agency had no control over the complainant's work and provided only space and equipment). Similarly, the provision of computer equipment for the employee of a contractor does not convert the individual to a federal employee for complaint purposes where the contractor retains day-to-supervision of the employee, pays the employee, and retains the right to discipline the employee. Hall v. Secretary of Army, 01A31890 (2003).

Another example is provided by Kaissi v. Secretary of Army, 01A53101 (2006), where the complainant was a language instructor employed by Berlitz to teach the Iraqi language to agency employees. The Commission found that the complainant was not an agency employee even though she instructed agency employees on agency premises. The Commission noted that Berlitz hired and paid the complainant, provided all work materials and controlled the methods of teaching. In addition, her hours were set by contract and not by the agency. See also Kereem v. Secretary of State, 032302070 (2010) (placing heavy emphasis on fact that complainant had to submit an invoice to receive payment).
