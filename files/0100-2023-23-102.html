6. Agency-Induced Misconduct

Mental illness can bring stigmatization of the employee who suffers from it. It also can affect the way supervisors and coworkers interact with the employee. In fact, the reactions of supervisors and coworkers can trigger behavior or conduct that would otherwise be treated as disciplinary offenses.

"[T]he Rehabilitation Act is designed to prevent discrimination based on 'myths and fears about disability and disease.'...It is designed to replace 'reflexive reactions' to handicaps, borne of prejudiced attitudes and ignorance with 'actions based on reasoned and medically sound judgments.'" Bradley v. Department of Commerce, 03870059 (1988) (quoting School Board of Nassau County v. Arline, 480 U.S. 273, 107 S. Ct. 1123, 94 L. Ed.2d 307 (1987)). In the Bradley case the Commission found that the complainant's supervisor precipitated an assault by the employee based on her "unwarranted fear and misapprehension" of the employee's psychiatric illness.

The employee in Bradley suffered from acute paranoid psychosis caused by a chemical imbalance in the brain. The illness was controlled through medication and therapy and the agency had, on several occasions, granted the employee leave to receive treatment. The employee's supervisor called the employee's wife several times about his "strange behavior" and indicated she might have to take disciplinary action if his behavior did not improve. The supervisor later attempted to have the employee suspended, but was told by the personnel office that an adverse action would take weeks. The following day, the supervisor confronted the employee over a minor pencil-throwing incident. Eventually, the incident led to the employee shutting his supervisor's arm in a door.

Noting that the employee had never before exhibited any violent or dangerous behavior, the Commission, "under these peculiar circumstances," found that to permit disciplinary action for the assault would thwart the purpose of the Rehabilitation Act. It should be noted that nothing in the Commission's decision suggests that a disabling condition is an excuse for violent behavior. Rather, the entire focus of the Commission's decision was on the actions of the supervisor in responding to the employee's condition through irrational fear instead of an understanding of the condition.

After finding that the removal of the complainant in Bradley constituted disparate treatment, the Commission went on to find that it also constituted a failure to make reasonable accommodation of the complainant's illness:

The question then becomes whether the agency fulfilled its obligation of reasonable accommodation prior to deciding to remove petitioner. Between 1981 and 1985, the record shows that the agency had accommodated petitioner by providing him with sick leave, when needed, and by providing him with a supportive and tolerant work environment during the acute periods of his illness. In 1986, however, petitioner was not similarly accommodated. Although petitioner's psychotic behavior prior to the...confrontation was comparable to his previous psychotic episodes, his new supervisor failed to acknowledge the medical nature of petitioner's problems. Acting out of fear, the supervisor mistakenly treated petitioner's psychotic behavior as willful misconduct warranting discipline, rather than as the product of a handicapping illness requiring reasonable accommodation. As a result, the supervisor set about finding a disciplinary means to, at least temporarily, remove petitioner, rather than an accommodation to enable petitioner to maintain his employment.

The Commission does not mean to suggest that the agency was required to sit idly by and simply "wait out" petitioner's acute episodes. To the contrary, the agency could have pursued any of a number of alternative courses of action which would have adequately addressed the agency's concerns, while reasonably accommodating petitioner. The agency, for example, could have contacted petitioner's wife and psychiatrist, informed them that petitioner was too ill to be at work, and requested that petitioner's wife come to the agency to take him home. Alternatively, the agency could have escorted him from the building and placed him on enforced sick leave, and they ultimately did. Additionally, in view of the frequency and predictability of petitioner's recurrences, the agency could have established a mutually agreed upon protocol for handling petitioner's annual acute episodes that addressed the business needs of the agency while accommodating petitioner and enabling him to remain employed.

Had the agency thus accommodated petitioner, it is likely that the confrontation with the supervisor that led to the removal here at issue would not have occurred. Inasmuch as the agency approached petitioner's behavior, instead, from a disciplinary perspective, and inasmuch as the agency has not shown that it would have suffered undue hardship had it accommodated petitioner, the Commission concludes, therefore, that the agency did not fulfill its obligation of reasonable accommodation prior to removing petitioner.

The goal of protecting individuals with disabilities from the prejudices of myths and stereotypes was reaffirmed in the ADAAA:

in enacting the ADA, Congress recognized that physical and mental disabilities in no way diminish a person's right to fully participate in all aspects of society, but that people with physical or mental disabilities are frequently precluded from doing so because of prejudice, antiquated attitudes, or the failure to remove societal and institutional barriers;...

Id., Sec. 2(a)(2).
