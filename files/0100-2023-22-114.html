15. Schedules

The most likely accommodation to be requested is flexibility in the regular work schedule to participate in some religious practice. The Commission has outlined three alternatives in its regulations, but notes the alternatives are not meant to be all-inclusive. 29 CFR 1605.2(d).

Probably the least intrusive means is to allow the employee to engage in a "voluntary swap" of work schedules with another employee who performs the same or similar functions. Of course, in components of an agency that work a strict 9 to 5, Monday through Friday schedule, such swaps will not be possible. Other components of an agency will have much more flexibility in this regard. The Commission interprets the obligation to accommodate to require somewhat more than mere allowance of this practice. The agency should facilitate such swaps by publicizing policies that approve of such swaps, promoting an atmosphere in which the swaps are viewed favorably, and even assisting in identifying the pool of potential substitutes.

Flexible work schedules are another means by which an agency may accommodate religious practices. See, e.g., 5 USC 5550a. This can include flexible work schedules, floating holidays and the use of compensatory time.

Finally, if the agency cannot accommodate the religious practices of an employee through any other means, it should consider reassignment or lateral transfer to a position where it can make such accommodations.

The Commission's Compliance Manual, at Section 12-IV, C, I, provides:

An employer may be able to reasonably accommodate an employee by allowing flexible arrival and departure times, floating or optional holidays, flexible work breaks, use of lunch time in exchange for early departure, staggered work hours, and other means to enable an employee to make up time lost due to the observance of religious practices. However, EEOC's position is that it is insufficient merely to eliminate part of the conflict, unless eliminating the conflict in its entirety poses an undue hardship.

EXAMPLE 41--Break Schedules/Prayer at Work

Rashid, a janitor, tells his employer on his first day of work that he practices Islam and will need to pray at several prescribed times during the workday in order to adhere to his religious practice of praying at five times each day, for several minutes, with hand washing beforehand. The employer objects because its written policy allows one fifteen-minute break in the middle of each morning and afternoon. Rashid's requested change in break schedule will not exceed the 30 minutes of total break time otherwise allotted, nor will it affect his ability to perform his duties or otherwise cause an undue hardship for his employer. Thus, Rashid is entitled to accommodation.

EXAMPLE 42--Blanket Policies Prohibiting Time Off

A large employer operating a fleet of buses had a policy of refusing to accept driver applications unless the applicant agreed that he or she was available to be scheduled to work any shift, seven days a week. This policy would violate Title VII if applied to discriminate against applicants who refrain from work on certain days for religious reasons, by failing to allow for the provision of religious accommodation absent undue hardship.
