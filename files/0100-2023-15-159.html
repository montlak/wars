D. CREDIBILITY DETERMINATIONS

Credibility is an issue with almost any witness. Credibility is simply a determination as to whether the testimony of the witness should be believed, especially if it is in conflict with the testimony of other witnesses or evidence. It is the role of the administrative judge to make findings on the credibility of witnesses. The general rule is that the Commission will not disturb the credibility findings of the administrative judge on appeal "unless documents or other evidence so contradict the testimony or the testimony so lacks in credibility that a reasonable fact finder would not credit it." Johnson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720120023 (2013) (upholding credibility findings based on demeanor and tone of voice).

Many factors go into determining the credibility of a witness, including the demeanor of the witness, whether the witness is evasive or direct in the answers given, whether the testimony of the witness is consistent internally, whether the testimony conflicts with the testimony of other witnesses, and whether the testimony conflicts with other evidence in the record. Most often, credibility is discussed in terms of whether or not the witness is being truthful. But there are other reasons why testimony, even though truthful, may be of suspect credibility. For example, the witness testifying about his or her perceptions of an event may be influenced by factors such as vantage point, or the mere passage of time between the event and the testimony. In such cases, it is not the willingness of the witness to tell the truth that is being called into question. Rather, it is the ability of the witness to accurately observe or recall that is being questioned. Because credibility determinations can be based on many factors, the amount of deference to be given to a specific determination is a function of how that determination is reached.

Preparation of witnesses is an essential part of getting ready for a hearing. There is absolutely nothing improper about preparing a witness for testimony provided, of course, that the witness is not instructed to testify falsely. Far too little preparation takes place for most administrative hearings. Preparation of a witness also is a good way to test the credibility of a witness before putting him or her on the stand under oath. In presenting testimony before an administrative judge, a party's representative should do sufficient investigation to check at least basic facts. For example, when a witness testifies that specific events occurred on certain days, a simple glance at the calendar could reveal problems with the testimony. In Schonfeld v. NASA, 05950922 (1996), the complainant's supervisor testified that he circulated a memo advising employees to clean out their workstations in preparation for renovations and left a copy on the complainant's desk. The complainant countered that he was on leave that week and the supervisor knew that fact. Not wanting to be outdone, the supervisor testified that he assumed the complainant would get the note because he had been on leave the previous day but was in the office for five hours. In finding the supervisor's testimony was not credible, the Commission in Schonfeld observed:

We note in this regard that appellant's supervisor testified that he only left the note because he assumed appellant would be coming to work at some point. He also said that he based his assumption on the fact that appellant had been in the office the day before, which he said was also a leave day, for five hours. We note, however, that the supervisor left the note on January 4, 1993, a Monday; therefore, "the day before" would have been Sunday, January 3, 1993. Although appellant may have worked on Sunday, January 3, 1993, we find that it is unlikely that it would have been a leave day."

While a simple glance at the calendar may not have saved the agency from a finding of discrimination, it would have prevented the agency from placing the incredible testimony before the administrative judge.

There is sometimes a fine line between a finding that rests on the credibility of witnesses and one that rests upon an administrative judge substituting his or her judgment for that of the agency officials taking a particular action. Nonetheless, there is a line. Although credibility findings are properly within the realm of the administrative judge, second--guessing the business judgment of agency officials is not. An illustration of the difference between making a credibility determination and second--guessing business judgment is provided in the case of Glass v. Postmaster General, 07A50068 (2006). In that case, the administrative judge issued a finding that the complainant's termination was based on her sex. The complainant had been terminated for threatening coworkers and the judge's decision rested on his determination that agency officials had placed too much weight on the statements of the coworkers. The Commission in Glass reversed the finding of the administrative judge explaining:

The AJ's decision focuses on the agency exaggerating the veracity and weight of the co-workers' statements. However, we find that the AJ erred by substituting his judgement (regarding the amount of weight that the agency should accord to the co-workers' statements) for that of agency management with respect to the agency's personnel decision, and by not focusing his analysis on the motivation of A1 and A2 for terminating complainant. Employers generally have broad discretion to set policies and to carry out personnel decisions and should not be second-guessed by the reviewing authority absent evidence of unlawful motivation. Texas Department of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 259 (1981). The trier of fact must understand that the focus is to be on the employer's motivation, not its business judgment. See Thomas v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01945798 (December 12, 1996) (citing Loeb v. Textron, Inc., 600 F.2d 1003, 1012 n.6 (1st Cir. 1979)).

In addition, while the AJ stated that the inconsistencies in the co-workers' statement should have caused agency management to discount the veracity of the statements, the AJ in his decision also acknowledged that the statements from the co-workers could lead one to determine that complainant, at some point during her employment, made threats of violence with respect to other employees. Specifically, the AJ stated "[i]f believed the statements also suggest that at some time in her postal career, Complainant stated she would beat two co-workers." For these reasons, we find that the record does not support the AJ's finding that complainant established by a preponderance of the evidence that the agency's articulated reason for complainant's termination was pretext for sex discrimination.

The distinction in Glass is that the administrative judge did not find that the statements of the complainant's coworkers lacked credibility--a finding entitled to deference, but on the agency officials' evaluations of those statements.
