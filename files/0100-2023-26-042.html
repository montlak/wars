D. GRIEVANCES

When an employee files a grievance alleging discrimination, she may not also file an EEO complaint raising the same allegations. However, the filing of a grievance raising such allegations qualifies as opposition to discriminatory practices, and a subsequent complaint that the employee has been subjected to harassment as a result of the grievance is within the EEO purview. Bansavage v. Veterans Administration, 01861953 (1987).

An employee need not be precise in opposition activity. All that is required is that the employee explicitly or implicitly express his or her opposition to a discriminatory employment practice. For example, in Carter v. Postmaster General, 01A53912 (2006), the employee filed an EEO complainant alleging her termination was in retaliation for filing a grievance in which she protested a prior termination notice. In that grievance, she maintained that she had a chronic medical condition, that her supervisor made demeaning comments about her condition, and that she had been required to work outside of her medical restrictions. The arbitrator ordered the agency to give the complainant a functional capacity examination and to give the complainant a job with the restrictions established by that exam. Thereafter the complainant was removed a second time for failing to cooperate during the examination. The Commission, in Carter, referring to the former EEOC Compliance Manual, Section 8, found that the complainant's grievance was opposition activity because she had implicitly raised an alleged violation of the Rehabilitation Act:

A complaint about an employment practice constitutes protected opposition only if the individual explicitly or implicitly communicates a belief that the practice constitutes unlawful employment discrimination. Because individuals often may not know the specific requirements of the anti-discrimination laws enforced by the EEOC, they may make broad or ambiguous complaints of unfair treatment. Such a protest is protected opposition only if the complaint would reasonably have been interpreted as opposition to employment discrimination. An example is a complainant who complains to co-workers about harassment of a disabled employee by a supervisor. Id. at page 8-5.

A person is protected against retaliation for opposing perceived discrimination if she had a reasonable and good faith belief that opposed practices were unlawful. Thus, it is well settled that a violation of the retaliation provision can be found whether or not the challenged practice ultimately is found to be unlawful. Id. at ¶ 8-II.B.3.b, page 8-8.

Applying the above principles, we find that complainant's arbitration grievance constituted opposition EEO activity under the Rehabilitation Act. The grievance asserted in part that the agency should not have taken action against complainant because of her medical condition, which was described as severe and chronic. The arbitration decision indicated that complainant described a period of debilitating back pain with weakness in the legs, and that an upper level manager verbally indicated his distaste for employees in complainant's situation by referring to them as sick, lame and lazy. While complainant's grievance arbitration claim ambiguously communicated a belief that the action taken against her constituted unlawful employment disability discrimination in violation of the Rehabilitation Act, we find it was sufficient to constitute oppositional activity under the Act. Moreover, complainant's claim in the grievance process that her absences should have been excused because of her ongoing serious medical condition was in effect a request for reasonable accommodation under the Rehabilitation Act. Requesting reasonable accommodation is EEO activity.

The Supervisor of Customer Services who removed complainant was aware of the above grievance activity, and referred to it in the June 2, 2004 removal letter. The removal was adverse action. We find that there was a nexus between the grievance arbitration activity and the removal, i.e., it closely followed the activity, and referred to it.

The above supervisor indicated complainant was removed for the reason stated in the notice of removal. She also wrote that she was following polices regarding failure to maintain a regular work schedule.

Complainant contends that this explanation is pretext to mask reprisal discrimination. She avers that the action was actually taken because the agency was upset she prevailed in the grievance arbitration which reinstated her. We agree. Given the language in the FCE report, a removal action was unduly harsh, making the action suspicious. The FCE report did not state complainant refused to cooperate. Instead, it indicated that she did not apply maximum effort. Complainant gives very credible reasons for not doing so. There is no indication in the record that the agency contacted the medical personnel who conducted the FCE to inquire whether they believed complainant did not take the FCE in good faith.

The Supervisor of Customer Services also explained that she followed policies regarding failure to maintain a regular work schedule. The notice of removal, however, did not charge complainant with such a thing, the supervisor does not specifically explain what she means, and the record contains no supporting documentation of this. We find that complainant proved by a preponderance of the evidence that she was subjected to reprisal discrimination when she was removed.
