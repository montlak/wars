B. SAME-SEX HARASSMENT AND ONCALE

Same-sex harassment can violate Title VII, regardless of the sexual orientation of the perpetrator or the victim. Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, 523 U.S. 75, 118 S. Ct. 998, 140 L. Ed.2d 207 (1996). That is the current state of the law. As for whether sexual orientation and transgender are protected bases under Title VII, in 2020, the Supreme Court ended the debate simply, but definitively holding that "[a]n employer who fires an individual merely for being gay or transgender violates Title VII." Bostock v. Clayton County, Georgia, 590 US ___, Nos. 17-1618, 17-1623, 18-107 (June 2020). This section focuses on analyzing same-sex harassment claims. For a fulsome discussion of sexual orientation and transgender statuses as bases of discrimination, see Chapter 13.

Claims of same-sex harassment whether they involve individuals who are heterosexual, gay, lesbian, bisexual or transsexual have confounded both the courts and the Commission. Decisions in cases involving sexual orientation have been confusing and conflicting. Insofar as a claim of discrimination is based on Title VII, the prohibition against sexual harassment is not grounded in the sexual nature of the harassment. The prohibition is grounded in the fact that but for the employee's sex, he or she would not have been harassed. Title VII prohibits discrimination based on sex. It is not an attempt to legislate for or against sexual activity, whether the activity is heterosexual or homosexual. Making the analysis of cases even tougher is the fact that not all cases of same-sex harassment involve gays or lesbians. In many such cases, both the victim and the perpetrator are heterosexual males. (For whatever reason, there are not many cases of same-sex harassment involving heterosexual females.)

The pivotal legal question in cases involving same-sex harassment has been whether there is really any distinction to be made based on conduct directed at a person because of his or her sexual orientation and conduct directed at a person because of his or her sex. If the question is answered in the affirmative, i.e., that there is a distinction, then it follows logically that conduct directed at a person because of his or her sexual orientation does not violate Title VII. Title VII simply does not deal with sexual orientation. If the question is answered in the negative, i.e., that there is no distinction, then all such conduct potentially violates Title VII. For example, the Commission and most courts to examine the question found that gender is implicated where a male supervisor makes a promotion contingent upon sexual favors from a male subordinate. In such a scenario, the majority holds that the male subordinate would not be asked for such favors but for the fact that he is a male. Of course, there are cases to the contrary. The Commission and the courts, however, have been entirely in disarray over how to analyze a case where, for example, a male supervisor teases a male homosexual subordinate. Under one view, such conduct would not violate Title VII because the subordinate's gender is not implicated, only his sexual orientation. But another viewpoint holds that but for the subordinate's gender, his sexual orientation would not make him the target of harassment.

Same-sex harassment cases also can present a difficult factual question, particularly in cases involving a heterosexual victim and heterosexual perpetrator. How does the factfinder determine in such a case whether the harassment is directed at the victim because of his sex, as opposed to simple personal animosity having nothing to do with the victim's gender? The difficulty is demonstrated by the following example. When a homosexual supervisor makes sexual advances toward a heterosexual male subordinate, it is reasonable to infer the advances are based on the subordinate's sex. But for his sex, the male subordinate would not be subjected to the advances. This is the same reasonable inference that can be drawn where a heterosexual male supervisor makes sexual advances toward a heterosexual female subordinate. But what inference is a factfinder to draw when both the perpetrator and victim are heterosexual males? Is it reasonable to infer that the harassment is directed at the victim because of his sex? Or is it more reasonable for the factfinder to infer that the situation merely entails two males who do not like each other? And, what if the sexual orientation of one of the individuals is the source of that dislike?

As discussed in detail below, three major schools of thought emerged in same-sex harassment cases. One school of thought was simply that same-sex harassment did not violate Title VII. Another school of thought was that same-sex harassment could only violate Title VII if the perpetrator was homosexual. Finally, a number of courts believed that same-sex harassment could violate Title VII, regardless of the sexual orientation of the perpetrator or the victim. As noted above, the Supreme Court granted certiorari in Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, 83 F.3d 118 (5th Cir. 1996), and ultimately ruled that same-sex harassment can violate Title VII, regardless of the sexual orientation of the perpetrator or the victim. Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, 523 U.S. 75, 118 S. Ct. 998, 140 L. Ed.2d 207 (1996).

Prior to the Supreme Court's Bostock decision, the EEOC had already issued three recent decisions that obliterated any real distinction between discriminatory harassment based on sex and discriminatory harassment based on sexual orientation or transgender status. The EEOC used a gender stereotyping analysis based on Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989), in two cases that appeared to involve sexual orientation discrimination finding that the complainants, who were gay, were subject to harassment for failure to adhere to traditional stereotypes--i.e., an essential part of being male is being sexually attracted to females and an essential part of being female is being sexually attracted to males. Veretto v. Postmaster General, 0120110873 (2011) (claim that male employee was harassed for wanting to marry another male states a claim); Castello v. Postmaster General, 0520110649 (2011) (claim that female was harassed with comments about her relationship with females states a claim). In 2012, the Commission carried that premise to its logical conclusion finding that discrimination on the basis of transgender status was protected under Title VII as a form of discrimination based on sex. As a result, all theories of discrimination, including harassment, under Title VII are applicable to a claim of discrimination based on transgender status. Macy v. Attorney General, 0120120821 (2012). Although the Commission did not mention sexual orientation in the Macy decision, there was no reasoned basis to draw a distinction between discrimination based on transgender status and discrimination based on sexual orientation. Finally, in July 2015, the Commission issued a decision in Baldwin v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120133080 (2015), finding that discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation constitutes sex discrimination within the meaning of Title VII. For more on sexual orientation and transgender status as bases of discrimination see Chapter 13.
