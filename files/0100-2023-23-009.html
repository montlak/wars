1. Legislative and Judicial Employees

Unlike Title VII and the ADEA, the Rehabilitation Act does not state on its face that it applies to competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches of the federal government. By regulation, in the former Part 1613, the Commission included competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches within the coverage of the Rehabilitation Act. See 29 CFR 1613.701(b). However, as a result of the case law discussed below, competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches are no longer covered by the Rehabilitation Act. See 29 CFR 1614.103(b)(4).

In Faucett v. Government Printing Office, 01873052 (1988), the Commission, relying on the fact that the Rehabilitation Act incorporated all rights, procedures and remedies under Title VII, found that it did apply to competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches. See Chapter 3, for further discussion. However, on reconsideration, the Commission reversed itself in Faucett v. Government Printing Office, 05880886 (1989), and found that the Rehabilitation Act does not apply to competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branch agencies:

The United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia recently issued a decision concerning application of the Rehabilitation Act to the legislative branch in Judd v. Billington, ___ F.2d ___, Civil Action. No. 86-082; slip op. (D.C. Cir. Dec. 20, 1988). The Court of Appeals determined that the two sections concerning federal employment in the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (as oriGINAlly enacted) applied only to departments, agencies and instrumentalities of the executive branch. See 29 USC 791(b) and 794. Thus, the Court of Appeals stated, instrumentalities of the legislative branch were not included under the oriGINAl Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

The Court of Appeals then considered appellant's argument that the 1978 amendments expanded the scope of the Rehabilitation Act to include employees of the legislative branch. According to appellant's argument 29 USC 791(a) provides that employees who are aggrieved under 29 USC 791 have available the remedies, procedures and rights set forth under Title VII of the Civil Right Act, specifically, 42 USC 2000e-16. That statute...expressly includes employees or applicants not only in executive agencies, but units in the Government of the District of Columbia and in the legislative and judicial branches of the Federal government having positions in the competitive service as well as the Library of Congress....

However, the Court of Appeals rejected this argument. The Court of Appeals reasoned that the rights in Title VII which the 1978 amendment incorporated into the Rehabilitation Act were only concerned with complaints pursuant to 29 USC 791...and applies only to employees in the executive branch....

The Commission considered a regulatory change to extend Rehabilitation Act coverage to competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches. However, in its adoption of Part 1614, it expressly rejected such coverage. Pursuant to 29 CFR Part 1614.103, effective October 1, 1992, coverage of legislative and judicial branch employees was excluded. In adopting the final rule, the Commission noted:

In a change from § 1613.701(b), § 1614.103 eliminates coverage of units in the legislative and judicial branches and restricts the coverage of part 1614, for purposes of the Rehabilitation Act, to military departments as defined in 5 USC 102, executive agencies as defined in 5 USC 105, the U.S. Postal Service, the Postal Rate Commission and the Tennessee Valley Authority. This definition of EEOC's charge processing jurisdiction is based on the plain language of section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act, which limits coverage to departments, agencies and instrumentalities in the executive branch, and brings the regulation into conformity with a recent decision of the United States Court of Appeals. In Judd v. Billington, 863 F.2d 103 (D.C. Cir. 1988), the court held that section 791 of the Rehabilitation Act "applies only to employees in the executive branch. See 29 USC § 791(b)." 863 F.2d at 105. A couple of commenters urged us not to abide by this decision. The Commission, however, already acknowledged and adopted the Judd decision in Faucette v. Kennickell, Request No. 05880886 (March 1, 1989). Because the Commission already decided this issue, we are not reversing that decision by regulation.

The former Civil Service Commission had authority to issue regulations covering competitive positions in legislative and judicial branch agencies, 5 USC § 7153, however, that authority passed, not to EEOC, but to the Office of Personnel Management in 5 USC § 7203. EEOC has requested that the Office of Personnel Management issue a regulation under § 7203 extending regulatory coverage of the Rehabilitation Act to competitive positions in the legislative and judicial branches. The Office of Personnel Management has responded by stating it does not believe it has authority under the Rehabilitation Act to issue such a regulation. In addition, EEOC has asked the Interagency Committee on Handicapped Employees to recommend a legislative change to section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act to provide competitive employees of the legislative and judicial branch agencies with a remedy under the Rehabilitation Act.

The 1991 Civil Rights Act did extend some statutory coverage to employees of the U.S. House of Representatives and the U.S. Senate. The House legislation, however, only extends the protection of Title VII to its employees. The Senate did extend the protections of 501 of the Rehabilitation Act and 102-104 of the Americans with Disabilities Act to its employees, but enforcement authority is vested in the Senate itself instead of the EEOC.

Congress subsequently addressed the rights of legislative branch employees under Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, and the ADEA in the Congressional Accountability Act of 1995, Pub. Law No. 104-1, codified at 2 USC 1301, et seq. For the most part, enforcement was vested in the Office of Compliance and not the EEOC. However, the Act did bring the General Accounting Office and the Government Printing Office within the jurisdiction of the EEOC for Title VII and ADEA but not Rehabilitation Act purposes. See, e.g., Rocha v. Government Printing Office, 0120055092 (2008) (claim that GPO violated the Rehabilitation Act not within EEOC jurisdiction but could be brought against head of GPO under ADA).
