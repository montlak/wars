3. Unsuccessful Election

In Young v. General Services Administration, 01832204 (1984), the EEOC intimated that an employee who untimely files a grievance before timely starting the EEO process will not be denied relief based on the bar against using both procedures. However, the Commission did not decide the issue because it concluded that the subject matter of the grievance and EEO complaint were not identical and, therefore, 7121(d) did not apply. The decision is consistent with the Commission's regulations and the Reform Act because the intent is to prohibit the employee from utilizing dual forums and not to deprive the employee of any forum.

The issue has since been resolved. A grievance must be timely filed in order for it to constitute an election of remedy. An agency errs where it dismisses a complaint due to an election of the grievance process where the complainant timely initiates EEO counseling and the prior grievance is dismissed as untimely. Ball v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05930261 (1994). See also Complainant v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, 0120132869 (2014).

Use of the grievance procedure does not bar initiation of an EEO complaint on the same personnel action where the negotiated grievance procedure does not provide for the acceptance of allegations of discrimination. Pitts v. Secretary of Navy, 01883035 (1988). The complaint was dismissed, however, on the basis of timeliness because the employee did not initiate counseling within the applicable time limit.

When an employee files a grievance containing an allegation of discrimination and the grievance is dismissed subsequently on the basis that the subject matter is not grievable, this is not a true election of remedies. See Holmes v. Secretary of Navy, 05931010 (1994). In such a case, the time limit for filing an EEO complaint is tolled. The employee must contact a counselor within 45 days of receipt of the decision dismissing the grievance. See Chai v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 05970016 (1998). The employee is not required to take the agency's word that the subject matter is not within the grievance process. The time limit continues to be tolled while the case goes to arbitration. Id.; see also Goddard v. Secretary of Treasury, 01974986 (1998).

No election of forum takes place unless the grievance procedure is properly initiated. In Bainbridge v. Secretary of Army, 01966852 (1997), the Commission determined that the appellant did not knowingly invoke the grievance process, and thus the agency's dismissal of the complaint for raising the matter in a grievance was erroneous:

Upon review of the record, we find that it has not been established that appellant knowingly invoked the grievance procedure. Although appellant raised the matter of the letter in his supervisor's working file initially with his first-line supervisor and second line supervisor, and then subsequently reduced his complaint to writing for submission to the Chief, Maintenance Section, we find that those actions do not necessarily prove that appellant elected to pursue the grievance process. We base our finding in large part on appellant's failure to complete the Grievance Form Appendix A. As noted by the agency, Article 9, Section 7 of the negotiated agreement states in part that the procedure for resolution of a grievance at Step 2 is that if the grievant is not satisfied in Step 1, the employee will reduce the grievance to writing by completing the Grievance Form Appendix A. Appellant did not utilize this form and he also did not refer to this matter as a grievance when he raised it with his first-line supervisor and then subsequently with the chief, Maintenance Section. We therefore find that appellant did not make an election to pursue this matter through the grievance process. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint was improper and is REVERSED.

In Bainbridge, there was no clear evidence that the complainant intended to invoke the grievance process. It is unclear how the Commission would rule in a case where the complainant clearly tried to invoke the grievance process, but simply failed to do so properly.

The Bainbridge case also must be distinguished from the Commission's cases holding that an election of remedy is irrevocable even if it was not an informed election. In Bainbridge, the issue was whether the employee intended through his actions to invoke the grievance procedure and not whether his invocation of the grievance procedure over the EEO complaint process was based on an informed decision.
