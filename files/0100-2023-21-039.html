6. Corroborative Evidence and Demeanor Evidence

Harassment, in general, and sexual harassment in particular, often takes place in private. That means there are no witnesses other than the alleged perpetrator and victim. More so than in other cases of discrimination, corroborative evidence can be important in harassment cases. What the complainant told others at the time of the alleged events can be especially important.

In Henderson v. Postmaster General, 0120083298 (2008), the Commission relied on several pieces of corroborative evidence in finding that the complainant was subjected to sexual harassment:

Here, there is no question that complainant is a member of a statutorily protected class. Further, the record supports complainant's allegations that she was subjected to unwelcome conduct and harassment based on her sex. The record reveals that some of the alleged harassers verified the alleged incidents to a certain degree, but claimed that complainant took them out of context. For example, CW2 admitted to hugging and touching complainant in April 2004, however, he said he did it only to get her attention so he could say good morning and talk to her. CW3 admitted to engaging in very sexual conversations with complainant, but claimed that complainant was an active participant. S1 admitted to calling complainant at home, but denied making sexual advances to her. The co-workers who were named as harassers all claim that complainant is retaliating against them for various reasons, such as one refused to corroborate her allegation of sexual harassment against a co-worker, and another did not find her sexually attractive, resulting in her concocting these grandiose allegations of sexual harassment well after they allegedly occurred. However, these assertions are disputed by complainant's female co-worker, who stated that complainant confided these incidents to her as soon as they happened, and therefore complainant did not make them up after-the-fact as alleged by the male co-workers. Additionally, records show that complainant immediately reported most of the incidents to management, not long after-the-fact. Further, a supervisor confirmed complainant's allegations and told the EEO Counselor that complainant had many relationships with male co-workers that ended badly, which resulted in the co-workers making inappropriate comments to complainant. The statements of other female co-workers reveal that sexual harassment is prevalent amongst male co-workers and supervisors at this facility, lending further credence to complainant's allegations. Therefore, complainant has established by a preponderance of the evidence the second and third prong of the prima facie case.

The fact that there is no corroborative evidence does not mean the complainant cannot establish the existence of a hostile environment. The claim can be established solely on the basis of the complainant's testimony. Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120111865 (2014). In that case, however, the Commission also noted that even though there was no corroborative evidence as to the actual interactions between the complainant and the supervisor, there was corroborative evidence of the complainant's reaction to those interactions:

In appropriate cases, the Commission may make a finding of harassment based solely on the credibility of the victim's allegation. See EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment, EEOC No. N-915-050 (Mar. 19, 1990). A victim's account must be sufficiently detailed and internally consistent so as to be plausible, and lack of corroborative evidence where such evidence logically should exist would undermine the allegation. By the same token, a general denial by the alleged harasser will carry little weight when it is contradicted by other evidence. Id.

Upon review, we determine that there is sufficient evidence to show that the supervisor engaged in unwelcome verbal and physical conduct towards Complainant in that:

--Complainant's testimony was more plausible and believable than the supervisor's testimony.

--Complainant's testimony was corroborated by testimony from student interns and the Director, who either saw Complainant soon after the alleged incidents, or discussed the incidents with Complainant around the time that they occurred.

--These corroborating witnesses testified that Complainant had a visibly upset demeanor around the time or soon after the alleged incidents occurred, which suggests that Complainant was telling the truth.

--Complainant, the student interns, and the Director did not have motives or reasons to lie....

Overall, we find there is ample evidence in the record to show that the supervisor subjected Complainant to unwelcome verbal and physical sexual conduct that was sufficiently severe to constitute sexual harassment. Complainant's testimony was sufficiently detailed and internally consistent, and her demeanor and reactions to the incidents were observed and corroborated by multiple student interns and the Agency's Director. In contrast, the supervisor's accounts of various events were implausible on their face, and unsupported by testimony from other witnesses.
