ALTERNATIVE DISPUTE RESOLUTION

If you are a federal manager for any length of time, you will find yourself involved in an employee-management dispute. It may be a minor one, like who gets to be off the day after Thanksgiving, or it might be a formal legal case involving millions of dollars of back pay under the Fair Labor Standards Act. EEO complaints, union grievances, and unfair labor practice charges -- you name it -- at least one of these will likely happen to you.

One strategy to deal with such disputes is simply to litigate everything, all the way to federal court if necessary. You'll win some and lose some. Anyway, that's what all those lawyers are for, correct? Another strategy is to just give in and avoid all the hassle -- you'll have a happier workforce if you give them what they want, right? But experience tells us that neither of these answers is correct.

Certainly, there are some cases that because of the issues, or the merits, or the money involved should be defended all the way to the Supreme Court if necessary. And there are others that are such losers that they should be settled early before they become a public embarrassment.

But most disputes fall somewhere in between -- both sides have some good points to make in their favor, but neither is a "slam dunk." In other words, both sides have some incentive to settle the case short of binding arbitration, an administrative hearing, or court proceedings. Alternative dispute resolution, or ADR, is a formal mechanism that offers the parties a methodology for attempting to find grounds for a mutually acceptable settlement.

ADR authority

In 1990, the Administrative Dispute Resolution Act (ADRA) required each federal agency to adopt a policy on ADR use. In 1996, ADRA was reenacted as the Administrative Dispute Resolution Act of 1996. In 2000, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) required all federal agencies to establish or make available an ADR program during the pre-complaint and formal complaint stages of the EEO process.

Additionally, federal regulations require agencies to make reasonable efforts to voluntarily settle EEO discrimination complaints as early as possible and throughout the administrative process.

All parties to a dispute in the ADR process should have a right to be accompanied by a representative of their choice in accordance with relevant collective bargaining agreements, statutes, and regulations.

It is vital for complainants and management officials to understand and participate in the ADR process. Every agency has the discretion to create its own unique ADR program that is best suited for its particular office environment.

Benefits of ADR

There are many benefits to ADR, including:

  • • Complaints are processed more quickly and resolved earlier.
  • • The process leads to more creative solutions.
  • • Savings in time of attorneys, staff, and parties who are federal employees.
  • • Quicker resolution than a hearing would offer and less time spent by the parties under the cloud of pending litigation.
  • • Creative resolutions acceptable to the parties, but which a third-party reviewer could not impose.
  • • A durable and voluntary agreement.

ADR provides an excellent opportunity for win-win solutions, and it can enhance employee morale and trust by showing that management is open-minded and cooperative about resolving disputes. ADR can also save agencies money if complaints are ended early in the process rather than proceeding through litigation that can stretch on for years.

Best practices

If you are involved in a dispute that has been referred to ADR, you can ask your HR, ADR, or EEO office for information on how it works and what your role is within it. Generally, you can prepare for the ADR process by thinking about your interests and what you would like to see as an outcome.

When participating in ADR, you may reach a better result if you:

  • • Arrive at the meeting with the expectation that all parties are acting in good faith.
  • • Listen respectfully to all the individuals involved.
  • • Ask for clarification rather than make assumptions if you don't understand what is being said.
  • • Keep an open mind.
  • • Acknowledge the legitimacy of viewpoints that are different from your own. Refrain from making personal or otherwise provoking remarks.
  • • Try to refrain from getting angry. Ask for a break if you feel your emotions rising.

Don't try to discuss the matters at issue with the employee directly. This could be intimidating for them and could also lead to a claim of retaliation.

If a mutually acceptable resolution is not achieved, participants in the ADR process retain their right to have their claim adjudicated in another forum.

Types of ADR

What is ADR? Another way to frame the question is to ask, what is it an "alternative" to? The short answer is a mess and an expensive one at that. A more formal answer is litigation. Litigation can take the form of a hearing before an administrative law judge, an arbitrator, or a federal judge. It can involve depositions, discovery, requests for admissions, testimony, exhibits, and legal briefs. For you non-attorneys, don't be fooled by the word "brief" -- they never are. While all this is going on, the meter is running, and not just for the lawyers -- you're losing productivity, morale is suffering, and a final outcome is being delayed.

Which leads to the next question: What's so great about ADR? Does it mean we agree to settle everything? No, we don't settle everything. The decision to settle a particular case is a tactical one based on the specific facts of that dispute. ADR provides a framework for the parties to try to settle.

ADR isn't any one thing. It's a collection of options and methods selected by the parties which they agree to employ, either singly or in combination. Some are defined below:

  • • Conciliation is a trust-building process. The conciliator assists to establish communication, clarify misconceptions, promote openness, and break down barriers, so parties can build trust and engage in cooperative problem-solving.
  • • Facilitation focuses more on the process of resolution rather than on the substance of an issue. It is used when the parties have a common goal and some mutual trust, and need procedural assistance from an outsider.
  • • Fact finding is a form of investigation by an impartial expert to establish the facts of a dispute. Fact finders do not resolve issues, but they may provide an evaluation and make recommendations if requested by the parties.
  • • Interest-based problem-solving or bargaining is used in collective bargaining in lieu of traditional, position-based bargaining. It attempts to define the problem, separates the person from the problem, identifies the issues, and explores possibilities and opportunities in an effort to reach a solution. The focus is on the problem, not on what each party wants.
  • • Mediated arbitration involves a mediator or other neutral third party. If an impasse is reached after mediation, the third party may issue a binding decision on either the cause of the impasse or on the remaining issues, if that's what the parties agreed to. Two third parties may be involved, a mediator and an arbitrator. The process narrows the issues, so if impasse is reached, the remaining issues are more clearly defined for arbitration. (This is usually the only ADR process that involves a binding decision by a third party.)
  • • Mediation is by far the most common form of alternative dispute resolution. Mediation involves the use of a professionally trained neutral third party with no decision-making authority. A mediator meets with the parties to explore alternative resolutions and becomes a catalyst to enable the parties to arrive at their own effective resolution.
  • • Minitrials are similar to real trials (or hearings) but with less stringent rules. A third party often heads the trial. Minitrials help the parties form their case in summary format, so that a settlement may be reached to head off formal litigation. They also sometimes highlight weaknesses in one's position that had not been previously evident.
  • • Non-binding arbitration is a formal process in which material is presented to a neutral third party, who issues an advisory decision, usually after a hearing.
  • • Ombuds are individuals (usually agency employees) who employ counseling, conciliation, mediation, and fact-finding techniques to resolve disputes. They usually have no power beyond persuasion.

Control of outcome. In addition to the potential to settle disputes, ADR has a feature that is attractive to managers: A party cannot be forced into settlement terms that it finds unacceptable.

Anyone with experience in handling workplace disputes knows it is infinitely better to have some control over the final outcome of the case. Once an arbitrator or administrative law judge is in charge, who knows what the final decision might be? Sometimes it's something neither party wanted or expected.

If you and the employee fail to resolve an issue and the neutral determines that further proceedings would be futile, the ADR proceeding will be terminated. The employee will be able to pursue other remedies and processes, such as a formal complaint or litigation.

Is it voluntary? Well, sort of. At many agencies, managers are required to consider ADR if an employee wants to use it. In other words, an agency's ADR policy, EEO policy, or labor agreement might require that the parties agree to discuss ADR, but it doesn't mandate that a particular procedure be employed or that it dictates the outcome.

ADR does not produce a legal record or create a precedent. This means it is not suitable when important legal or contractual issues must be settled or when a broad workplace precedent is involved.

How it works

Employees have several forums to address workplace conflicts, and ADR is a voluntary option that has the potential to resolve conflicts at the earliest opportunity and the lowest possible level. ADR programs offer employees and managers the opportunity to confidentially resolve workplace conflicts through this process.

Any staff member (managers, supervisors, or employees) may refer disputes to the program, and participation by all parties is discretionary. However, at some agencies, once an employee has opted to try ADR, management is expected or required to participate. If the dispute is not resolved through the ADR program, employees may still have the option to pursue the matter through other forums.

Intake process

Employees who contact their ADR program will complete the ADR intake process. During the intake process, ADR staff or special counsel will:

  • • Explain the process. Core principles of mediation, the role of the mediator, and the role and responsibilities of the parties will be explained.
  • • Collect information related to the workplace conflict. The ADR office will open an official case file and review the requesting party's intake form containing relevant information about the workplace conflict. All information collected during this process will remain confidential.
  • • Provide information about other dispute resolution processes. See the Other forums section below.

Determining ADR eligibility

The decision to accept or reject a dispute for ADR is made on a case-by-case basis by the ADR office, who will consider factors such as the nature and complexity of the dispute, the relationship of the parties, and the relief sought by the parties. The ADR office will not accept matters that are clearly brought to the ADR program for a purpose other than to make a good-faith effort to resolve a genuine workplace conflict.

Generally, an ADR program is suited to address most workplace conflicts involving:

  • • Interpersonal conflicts or miscommunications.
  • • Discussions related to performance reviews.
  • • Perceived discrimination or unfair treatment.
  • • An employee's working conditions.
  • • Terms and conditions of employment.
  • • Personnel actions.
  • • Allegations of employment discrimination.

Disputes that are generally inappropriate for ADR and are not eligible for resolution under the ADR program include:

  • • Issues under investigation by the Inspector General, the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), the Office of Special Counsel, or state and federal police agencies.
  • • Issues over which the agency does not have jurisdiction, such as workers' compensation (Department of Labor), disability retirement (OPM), suitability determinations (OPM), and Thrift Savings Plan issues (Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board).
  • • Cases of egregious misconduct (e.g., threats of violence).
  • • Issues that are the subject of an agency class complaint.
  • • Disputes involving applicants for employment, except when the applicant has requested ADR during the EEO complaint process under federal regulations.
  • • Other issues where compelling circumstances convince the ADR office that ADR would not be appropriate or worthwhile.

Dispute accepted for mediation

If the ADR office accepts a dispute for resolution through the ADR program, it will:

  1. 1. Notify the parties named in the dispute and advise that the dispute has been accepted into the ADR program.
  2. 2. Notify the agency settlement official(s) who have the authority to resolve disputes and recommend that they attend the mediation sessions or be available.
  3. 3. Remind participants of their obligation to sign a mediation agreement and a confidentiality agreement before proceeding to mediation.

Other forums

The ADR office will inform all employees who seek ADR that using the ADR process to resolve a dispute does not suspend the filing requirements for other forums. While ADR is an expeditious process, there is no guarantee that the process will be completed prior to the expiration of the filing deadlines for other forums.

Most ADR offices provide employees the filing requirements for other forums and their rights and responsibilities under the following processes:

Equal employment opportunity process. Applicants, employees, or former employees who believe that they have been discriminated against on a protected basis, such as race, color, religion, national origin, gender, age, disability, genetic information, or in reprisal for opposing or participating in protected EEO activity, must contact an EEO counselor within 45 days of the date of the alleged discrimination.

Employees alleging discrimination are usually given the opportunity to choose between EEO counseling and alternative dispute resolution. Once an agency decides to offer EEO ADR, an accused manager has a duty to cooperate, like any witness, in the EEO ADR process but may not be the agency official that has settlement authority.

Unlike counseling, which must be completed by the agency in 30 days, ADR may take up to 90 days. To be clear, participating in the ADR program does not satisfy the requirement to contact an EEO counselor within 45 days of the date of the alleged discrimination.

Administrative grievance process. Many agencies permit non-bargaining unit employees to file grievances on matters affecting their terms and conditions of employment. However, seeking assistance through the ADR program does not suspend the time frames for filing a grievance under the agency's administrative grievance procedures.

Negotiated grievance process. Collective bargaining agreements permit bargaining unit employees to file grievances on matters affecting their terms and conditions of employment. Similar to the administrative grievance procedure, seeking assistance through the ADR program does not suspend the time frames for filing a grievance under the CBA.

Unfair labor practices. Employees eligible to file unfair labor practice charges with the Federal Labor Relations Authority may attempt to resolve the disputes through the ADR program prior to filing a ULP. However, participation in the ADR program does not suspend the time frames for filing a ULP under the FLRA regulations. To preserve their rights under the FLRA, employees must file a ULP within six months of the alleged incident.

Merit Systems Protection Board process. Use of the ADR program to resolve a dispute does not automatically suspend the deadline requirement(s) to file an appeal with the MSPB within 30 calendar days of the effective date of any action, if any, or within 30 calendar days after the date of receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later. However, individuals may participate in ADR prior to the expiration of that deadline.

If the parties wish to continue ADR efforts beyond the 30-day deadline for filing an appeal, they must submit a written notice to the MSPB prior to the timely filing of an appeal, which explains that the parties are engaged in ADR efforts. The MSPB will then extend the time limit for filing an appeal by 30 days -- for a total of 60 days.

Neutral third parties

ADR sessions are overseen by neutrals, who act as mediators, conciliators, or facilitators to help the parties come to resolution. They may be federal employees or contractors. They must be acceptable to all parties to a dispute and must have no conflict of interest, unless it is disclosed in writing to all parties. Your agency's Office of Dispute Resolution or ADR program office will help you and the employee to select a neutral professional to run the ADR session.

Components of ADR

In ADR, the use of a neutral third party with no decision-making authority is the catalyst for dispute resolution. The ADR process usually has five components:

  • • A trained third-party neutral. A neutral mediator or other facilitator whom the parties can trust, who ensures civility, and who (while supportive of the parties) is not an involved party.
  • • Confidential, undocumented proceedings. Confidentiality is key to establishing equality between the parties. No permanent records are kept of the proceedings. The absence of record puts the parties at ease and makes arriving at resolution much more feasible. It avoids one-upmanship "for the record." Notes taken by the third party are destroyed immediately after resolution. The only written documentary record is the final agreement prepared and signed by the parties.
  • • Good faith from the parties. The parties must enter the process intending to work toward resolution. ADR rules preclude legal interruption and bar attorneys and others from abusing the process. The focus of the process is to seek solutions.
  • • The presence of the parties. Those with full authority to act for the parties must attend, so that they can work toward resolution. The process often involves reconciling the parties themselves as much as resolving their problems.
  • • An appropriate venue. The site must be neutral, or at least mutually agreed to, a place where neutrality, confidentiality, and inclusiveness are assured and where there is no intimidating appearance of management control.

The manager's role in ADR. The full participation of a manager with the authority to settle the case is a key ingredient to a successful ADR experience. As the manager, your mindset is also a key component. If you come to an ADR session with the preconceived notion that it will fail, a self-fulfilling prophecy will likely result.

Take note that through the ADR process, you surrender none of your "management rights," and there's really nothing to lose by trying the ADR process, especially given its advantages. You have to agree to the process as well as the outcome.

If you choose to enter into ADR, you should try to approach it with an open mind. Let the neutral third party guide the proceedings. Remember that ADR is the last chance you'll get to resolve the dispute short of formal litigation. If it becomes a formal case, the dispute becomes an agency legal matter and your role will be diminished.

What happens in mediation

Knowing what to expect in mediation will help you relax and focus on trying to resolve the dispute with your employee. The mediator will take the lead and run the session. Your job will be to listen, answer questions when asked, keep an open mind, and stay engaged in the process.

  • • The mediator usually begins with an opening statement in a joint meeting with the parties. They will explain their role as a neutral and a facilitator. The mediator will make clear they do not represent or advocate for or against either party. The mediator also will set out the ground rules for the mediation session.
  • • Next, the mediator will ask the employee to explain, in their own words, the nature of the dispute and their version of the events. The mediator will ask the employee to state what remedy they are seeking in the mediation. Stay calm during this phase of the mediation and let the employee have their say. Your turn will come.
  • • Next, you will be asked to explain, in your own words, the nature of the dispute and your version of the events. Then, the parties will discuss the situation and potential ways the dispute could be resolved. The mediator will lead the discussion and will ask clarifying questions along the way.
  • • Usually, the mediator will meet privately with each side to explore possible resolutions and discuss confidential matters. Information discussed during these private meetings is confidential and will generally not be shared with anyone else. The mediator may give each side a "reality check" -- a frank assessment of the situation from a neutral, third-party viewpoint -- during the private meetings. You will be given an opportunity to obtain technical or administrative guidance from HR or the Office of General Counsel if you need it. The mediator may go back and forth between the parties, trying to negotiate different resolutions to the dispute.

Attempting settlement

  • • Next the mediator will reconvene the joint session and determine if there is any area of agreement on any issue. If not, the parties may continue to negotiate, possibly meeting again privately with the mediator, until it is clear if a settlement is going to emerge. If a settlement is reached, a settlement agreement is written up and signed before the session is ended. Depending on your agency's policies, check with HR before signing a settlement agreement.
  • • If settlement is not reached, and resolution doesn't appear likely, the mediator will end the session.

Be aware that the provisions written into a settlement agreement must adhere to the law. If the settlement agreement involves reducing discipline proposed or imposed against the employee, make sure that the reduction in penalty doesn't violate any statutes, regulations, or rules that govern the misconduct at issue.

In one case, an agency suspended an employee for 30 days for willful misuse of a government vehicle for nonofficial purposes. The statute requires an agency to impose a penalty of no less than a one-month suspension for an offense of willfully using a government vehicle for other than official purposes. The employee and agency settled the matter, with the agency agreeing to reduce the suspension to five days, but the charge stayed the same. The MSPB later set aside the settlement because it was not lawful on its face. Since the statute mandates a minimum penalty for the employee's offense, the agency could not lawfully agree to reduce the penalty for the charged offense below a 30-day suspension.

Mistakes to avoid

Alternative dispute resolution can be daunting to a newcomer, but you can go a long way toward success by following a few simple principles.

Do not:

  • • Assume you are going to lose your "management rights." The ADR process can serve as a win-win for both the employee and the manager. The process is about problem-solving, and management should not fear the immediacy of an informal or formal settlement agreement. A quick resolution to a problem can save relationships and lead to a positive working environment.
  • • Overlook your responsibility in the process. Managers play an important role in the process, and they shouldn't delegate their responsibility to someone else or the HR or ADR staff. Having a positive attitude about the process can only help make it work more smoothly.
  • • Be afraid to ask questions. If you are not sure what you can and can't do or say during the ADR process, make sure you speak to an ADR, HR, or EEO specialist. Confidentiality rules apply.
  • • Feel locked into the process. While understanding that a manager plays an important role in the ADR process, it is equally important to note that it is a process that can be ended at the request of either party. Don't make it more difficult than it has to be.
  • • Use ADR inappropriately. ADR won't work when the parties are too far apart to reach a settlement and may simply delay resolution. It is also unsuitable when a legal record or workplace precedent is required.

Ask FMG

Q. What are the advantages of ADR?

A. It tends to be faster as well as cheaper than formal hearings and arbitrations. It also results in more mutually satisfying dispute resolutions. It tends to avoid the hard feelings often involved in more traditional dispute resolution methods.

Q. Is ADR mandatory?

A. The decision to choose ADR is usually discretionary. At some agencies, however, once an employee has opted to try ADR, management is expected or required to participate. Check with your HR or ADR office and your agency's ADR policy. In EEO cases, agencies are required to offer an ADR option, although they have the discretion to decide if ADR should be attempted in a particular case. An agency also may be required to try ADR by a collective bargaining agreement.

Q. Do confidentiality rules apply?

A. Yes. Both disputing parties and neutrals are bound to maintain the confidentiality of all ADR communications unless all parties to a communication agree in writing to forgo confidentiality.

Q. Will there be a running record of what happened at mediation?

A. The ADR process does not produce case documentation, as formal adjudication does. Any records kept of mediation sessions and other ADR activities are confidential during the ADR effort and destroyed thereafter. Only the settlement agreement, if any, remains.

Q. Will my employee have a representative at the ADR session?

A. All parties to a dispute in the ADR process should have a right to be accompanied by a representative of their choice in accordance with relevant collective bargaining agreements, statutes, and regulations. Check with your HR or ADR office if this is a concern for you.

Q. Will I have to sign something before the ADR session?

A. If you agree to participate in ADR, you and the employee will probably be asked to sign an ADR agreement, documenting your willingness to participate in the process and identifying the dispute that is to be addressed.

Q. What types of situations are appropriate for a facilitator or mediator?

A. A mediator or facilitator is often appropriate:

  • • When the two sides have reached an impasse.
  • • When direct communications are complicated because of past conflict and distrust.
  • • When both sides could benefit by clarifying strategies and positions.
  • • When better outcomes can be achieved than through traditional litigation.
  • • When litigation is likely to be costly and protracted.
  • • When both sides will benefit from preserving and repairing ongoing relationships.

Q. When should ADR not be used?

A. ADR may not be useful:

  • • When the parties are so far apart or so emotional that a settlement is unlikely.
  • • When there are serious legal or contractual issues in dispute.
  • • When there are numerous parties involved with conflicting interests.
  • • When it is important to set a workplace precedent through a full legal record.
