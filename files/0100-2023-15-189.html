K. CLOSING STATEMENTS

Generally, each side will be permitted to make a closing statement. There is really no guidance from the Commission on the form or content of such statement. Parties should take advantage of the opportunity to make closing statements. It is an opportunity to explain to the judge what the evidence proves and how that evidence should be construed in light of the underlying issues.

It is important to remember that the statements of representatives are not evidence. In closing statements, a representative can summarize evidence or argue as to how that evidence should be interpreted. Evidence cannot be introduced via such statements.

In most cases, the parties do not submit either prehearing or posthearing briefs. A closing statement, in particular, is the place to make legal arguments in a party's favor. If there are cases that should be brought to the attention of the administrative judge, give a full citation for the case, explain the holding of the case, and tell the judge how that holding should be applied to the facts of your own case. Otherwise, the case may be overlooked in the judge's own research.

Some representatives like to write out closing statements. Such a practice results not in a spontaneous and animated argument on behalf of a client, but rather in a lifeless reading. Also, closing statements written the night before often do not account for the actual evidence introduced at the hearing. By all means, use notes to help organize thoughts and make sure that points are not forgotten or overlooked. Most judges, especially if requested, will give the representatives several minutes at the close of the hearing to organize closing statements. This does not mean that closing statements should be ignored until the end of the hearing. Advance preparation will mean that the appropriate case references are available and also will demonstrate that the representative gave the time and attention to the case that it deserves.

In some cases, judges will ask the parties to waive closing statements and, instead, submit posthearing briefs. Generally, such briefs are ordered without the parties having an opportunity to review the transcript, as discussed above. The administrative judge, however, does have the discretion to order closing briefs after the parties have been provided with a copy of the transcript. In submitting posthearing briefs, the parties should be careful not only to address those issues the parties desire, but to address any issues on which the judge has requested specific argument. Unless the administrative judge specifically authorizes it, no new evidence should be submitted with a written closing argument.

Finally, a representative should not assume that the judge will accept a posthearing brief in lieu of closing arguments and allow time after the hearing for submission of the brief. Unless the legal or factual issues are complicated, a posthearing brief will not usually be any more enlightening than a closing statement. If you believe that posthearing briefs are appropriate, this issue should be discussed with the opposing representative and the administrative judge prior to the hearing. More than one representative, operating on the assumption that posthearing briefs would be permitted, has been forced to make closing arguments with little or no advance preparation.
