1. "Logically Connected"/Wrong Agency Officials

From a purely literal perspective, 29 CFR 1614.105(a)(1) requires that contact must be made with an EEO counselor within 45 days of an alleged discriminatory personnel action or incident. Previously, Part 1613 also required contact with a counselor, except the time limit was 30 days. The Commission has moved from interpreting the counselor contact requirement quite literally to giving the requirement an expansive interpretation.

The Commission now adheres consistently to the rule that a "complainant may satisfy the criterion of Counselor contact by initiating contact with any agency official logically connected with the EEO process even if that official is not an EEO Counselor." Weinberger v. Secretary of Army, 01912568 (1991) (contact with a personnel counselor could logically be connected to the EEO process, if contact was timely). In Nia G. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120170943 (2017), for instance, the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of a complaint where the complainant made timely contact with an EEO Manager, who told her to contact a counselor and provided her with an instruction letter explaining that an EEO Manger is not a counselor. The Commission in Nia G. held that an EEO Manager is logically connected to the EEO process.

Furthermore, the Commission has held that when a complainant contacts someone "logically connected" with the EEO process, complains of discrimination, and indicates an intent to pursue a complaint, the agency must direct the complainant to an EEO counselor. As the Commission found in Davis v. Secretary of Navy, 01941910 (1994), an agency will not later be heard to argue that a complaint is untimely when it fails to meet this obligation:

Appellant alleged that she was discriminated against when she was sexually harassed by the Supply Officer on four different occasions. The agency dismissed allegations (3) and (4) occurring on February 7, 1993 and January 26, 1993, as untimely since appellant contacted an EEO Counselor regarding the matters on March 26, 1993. The record, however, reveals that on March 7, 1993, appellant brought her sexual harassment allegations to the attention of the Captain and the EEO Advisor. The record also reflects that appellant purportedly submitted a written statement regarding her sexual harassment allegations to the Captain. During the interview with the Captain on March 7, 1993, appellant clearly expressed her intention to pursue the sexual harassment allegations to a formal stage of complaint processing. The record reveals that the Captain understood appellant's intention, and told the Supply Officer that the matters, if not resolved at his level, would be turned over to the agency headquarters as a formal complaint. Finally, we note that in response to the appeal, the agency emphasizes that EEO posters were on display on the ship on which appellant was serving at the time in question; the EEO Advisor with whom appellant spoke was not an EEO Counselor; and the EEO Counselors were "only located at the Agency's headquarters in Bayonne, New Jersey." Under the circumstances, we find that the Captain and/or the EEO Advisor should have referred appellant to EEO counseling regarding the matters when they were informed of her intention to pursue an EEO complaint. Based on the foregoing, we find that appellant's EEO contact was March 7, 1993, when she brought her sexual harassment allegations to the attention of the identified agency officials, who were the only officials available logically connected to the EEO process with whom appellant could meet, with an intent to pursue the complaint process. See Kemer v. GSA, EEOC Request No. 05910779 (December 30, 1991). Therefore, we find that appellant's EEO contact was timely.

When a complainant does contact an official "logically connected" to the EEO process and exhibits an intent to initiate counseling and the agency responds to that request, the date of contact with the official "logically connected" to the EEO process determines the timeliness of the complaint. For example, in Cordero v. Secretary of Navy, 01A31455 (2003), the Commission found the complainant's contact with a human resources office constituted timely counselor contact where, as a result of her contact, an EEO counselor sent complaint forms to the complainant.

Whether the complainant has previously initiated the EEO process can be indicative of whether the complainant is exhibiting an intent to proceed with a complaint. In Harris v. Secretary of Navy, 01991170 (2001), the complainant sent an email to management, the union and the EEO office about coworkers who made negative comments about Black History month events. In the email, the complainant specifically inquired, "'Now who can I speak to that can make something happen about these comments?'" The Commission found the complainant did not exhibit the requisite intent to initiate the EEO process, noting that she had previously filed a complaint and, therefore, was aware of the 45-day requirement.
