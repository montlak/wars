3. Smoke-Free Environments

Public opinion has done more to resolve the issue of a worker's right to a smoke-free environment when he or she alleges that a disabling condition is aggravated by smoke in the workplace than any application of reasonable accommodation laws. Both the MSPB and the EEOC wrestled with the question, arriving at somewhat inconclusive results. While smoking indoors is no longer allowed in federal building, the analysis employed over the years regarding smoke inhalation and reasonable accommodations is still instructive.

In Pletten v. Department of Army, 03810087 (1983), the Commission reviewed a decision of the Board holding that an accommodation of an employee suffering from acute asthma irritated by tobacco smoke would impose an undue hardship on the agency. In Pletten, the agency placed the employee on involuntary sick leave after receiving a letter from his physician that it would be harmful for the employee to return to work in anything but a smoke-free environment. The Board's presiding official dismissed the appeal on the ground that the Board lacked jurisdiction over the employee's placement on sick leave since it did not constitute a suspension.

On appeal, the Board presumed, without addressing the issue, that the appellant was an employee with a disability within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act. Pletten v. Department of Army, 7 MSPR 13 (1981). Since, under its treatment of constructive suspension cases, the Board requires an employee be ready, willing and able to work, the MSPB analyzed this element in conjunction with the agency's efforts to improve the employee's working environment. In upholding the decision of the presiding official, the Board in Pletten noted:

The agency response to the petition for review states that its action was reasonable and proper under the circumstances. The agency's efforts to accommodate appellant's allergy condition included conducting an air content study of appellant's immediate work area to determine toxic substances present; prohibit smoking in the entire Civilian Personnel Division; relocating his office to improve air quality; initiating an educational program to discourage smoking within the general workforce; posting notices banning smoking in areas such as elevators, auditoriums, appellant's office and cafeterias; conducting periodic air quality surveys of appellant's immediate work area to insure compliance with health standards; and advising fellow workers and visitors not to smoke in appellant's presence.

The agency contended however, that it could not provide appellant a "smoke-free" environment ordered by his physician, and, for that reason, it placed appellant on sick leave. The agency's duty to accommodate is not unlimited. All that is required by statute is a "reasonable" attempt to accommodate appellant's handicapping condition. 29 CFR 1613.704. Furthermore, even if the agency's argument of impossibility of appellant's suggested accommodation cannot be substantiated, we conclude that performance of the requested conditions would constitute an "undue hardship" not legally required to be provided by the agency. Therefore, the Board finds that the agency made reasonable efforts to accommodate appellant's allergy condition by improving his surrounding working environment.

Id. at 16-17. Based on the finding that the agency could not accommodate the employee without undue hardship, it found he was not ready, willing, and able to work and upheld the dismissal on jurisdictional grounds.

In its review of the case, the Commission was extremely critical of the Board's findings of fact. The Commission noted that the accommodations listed in the Board decision were never attempted by the agency and the agency further ignored its own regulations providing that smoking was permitted only to the extent it did not cause "discomfort or unreasonable annoyance to others." The EEOC further noted the agency never complied with the recommendations of a grievance examiner on the same issues. In Pletten, on review, the Commission found:

Clearly, the agency had the authority to ban smoking from its buildings but the Board decided that such a ban was impossible and, even though not substantiated by the agency, an undue hardship on it.... In a footnote, the Board summarily dismissed other accommodations such as restructuring, reassignment or retraining, stating, "The record is silent as to the viability of these alternatives since petitioner never raised them for consideration and therefore the agency was unable to respond."...The Commission's interpretation of the applicable regulation [29 CFR 1613.704(a)] is at variance with the Board's and would place a much greater burden upon the agency....

The clear intent of the mandatory language of this regulation requires an agency to make such accommodation unless it can prove that such actions would constitute an undue hardship. The record does not support an assertion that the agency made any accommodation, much less, as the Board even admitted, prove that the banning of all smoking in its buildings would have imposed undue hardship....

Based on its finding that the agency had failed to submit any proof that accommodation would place an undue hardship upon it, the Commission referred the case back to the Board. The Board then reaffirmed its own decision, finding that a smoking ban at the entire installation would be too difficult to enforce. See 23 MSPR 682 (1984). The Commission did not have the opportunity to review the Board's determination, since the appellant filed a civil action in U.S. district court following the Board's affirmance of its earlier decision. The court found that the appellant was not a qualified individual with a disability. See Pletten v. Merit Systems Protection Board, Civil Action No. 84-CV-05360-DT (E.D. Mich. 1988), aff'd, 908 F.2d 973 (6th Cir. 1990) (unpublished).

In White v. USPS, 01853426 (1987), the Commission found that the agency made only minimal attempts to reduce the employee's exposure to smoke by offering to move his desk as far as possible from smokers and providing him with a facial mask. The agency had refused to place a "No Smoking" sign in appellant's work area and enforce the ban. The Commission noted that the burden of proving an undue hardship on the agency's mission rests with the agency.

Providing the employee with an opportunity to accept a reassignment in lieu of imposing a smoking ban in the employee's work area constituted an offer of reasonable accommodation. Hall v. Veterans Administration, 01880230 (1988). The Commission noted that the recommended ruling of the administrative judge that the agency was required to accommodate the complainant in her current position "presupposes that a reassignment offer cannot be a reasonable accommodation, and such a ruling would be contrary to established precedent."

Failure to provide the complainant, who suffered from asthma and restrictive airway disease, with a smoke-free truck was a found to be a failure to accommodate in Barnett v. Postmaster General, 0720060019 (2008). The Commission found that the agency had agreed to give the complainant, a driver, a smoke-free truck, but then failed to enforce the ban on smoking in the complainant's truck. The Commission further found that management's repeated failures to respond to complaints that other workers were smoking in the complainant's truck constituted retaliation.

In Crawford v. Secretary of Army, 01883270 (1989), the Commission considered an argument by the complainant that the agency's failure to accommodate her allergies to tobacco smoke, animal danders, and musk scent culminated in her resignation and constituted a constructive discharge. The Commission in Crawford found the complainant could establish a case of constructive discharge by showing:

(1) a reasonable person in her position would have found the working conditions intolerable; (2) conduct against appellant which constituted handicap discrimination or reprisal created the intolerable working conditions; and (3) appellant's involuntary resignation resulted from the intolerable working conditions....

However, the Commission found no constructive discharge based on the facts of Crawford because the complainant gave the agency only sketchy information about her allergies and, thus, her protests about the agency's smoking policy could not be considered "to be more than general protests...." For more on "Constructive Discharge" generally, see Chapter 18.

On the other hand, in Lach v. General Services Administration, 01965885 (1997), the Commission found that it lacked the jurisdiction to determine if the agency failed to accommodate the appellant's nicotine-addicted personality in light of its smoking regulations. The Commission in Lach determined that it lacked the authority to review the GSA Administrator's regulations:

Appellant has alleged that the agency has failed to reasonably accommodate nicotine-addicted persons. Appellant alleges that he has been diagnosed with chronic colitis which can become acute due to stress, and he alleges that short term withdrawal of nicotine can generate stress, causing the condition to become acute. Appellant is attempting to allege a personal loss or harm to the terms, conditions or privileges of his employment sufficient to constitute aggrievement as a result of the agency's policies and rules on smoking. However, the agency is not the appropriate party to process his complaint. GSA has promulgated regulations pursuant to its statutory mandate to provide or arrange for all services to house occupant agencies. Title 40 486(c) requires the GSA Administrator to issue regulations "as he deems necessary to effectuate his functions under this Act." In 41 CFR 101-20.105-3(a) smoking is controlled by designating areas where smoking is prohibited and where smoking is allowed. The Commission does not have authority to approve or change the Administrator's regulations. Therefore, appellant's complaint is not within the purview of the Commission, and fails to state a claim within the meaning of 29 CFR 1614.103(a).
