I. ATTORNEY FEES

Issues relating to attorney fees are covered in detail in Chapter 22. Prevailing on a class complaint entitles the plaintiffs to attorney fees. This includes the attorney fees expended in attempting to identify the individuals who have suffered from discrimination and in presenting evidence on individual claims to the administrative judge. See Hansen v. Department of Justice, 01860947 (1987). The Commission rejected the notion that the plaintiffs were not entitled to attorney fees in the relief phase of the action because not all those identified were awarded relief. Id.

The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 provide that attorney fee requests should be submitted to the administrative judge in cases where a hearing has been requested. See 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(2). However, the provision is unclear on the process with regard to class complaints. Because it is the agency that issues a final agency decision on the merits, the regulation appears to require that the fee request be submitted to the agency, and not the administrative judge, in class action complaints.

When determining whether an agency is justified in reducing the hours expended in pursuing individual relief for class members, it must consider not only the degree of success, but the amount of general research included that is applicable to all members alike. See In re Department of Justice, 05870512 (1988). In rejecting any mathematical reduction based on the percentage of success in that case, the Commission gave a lengthy explanation of the basis for awarding fees in the relief stage:

First, the Commission notes that the reduction sought by the agency is reminiscent of the "mathematical approach" rejected in Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424 (1983)] and Foster v. Board of School Commissioners, 810 F.2d 1021 (11th Cir. 1981)]. In Foster, the reduction was not based merely upon the hours spent briefing and presenting unsuccessful claims. Rather, those hours were reviewed to determine whether they related to general research and investigation applicable to all the claims and whether the evidence adduced at the hearings on unsuccessful claims contributed to the cases presented by victorious claimants. The agency here has made no claim that all of the hours expended on the briefs of the unsuccessful claimants entailed isolated research having no bearing on the successful claims. Given that the record reflects that the agency made no inquiry of this nature, the Commission declines to find that class counsel should now be penalized for their careful recording of time. (footnote omitted). Second, the agency's argument is based upon the belief that "once a claimant whose claim has been denied by the agency [at the negotiation stage] insists upon a formal briefing and hearing before the Complaints Examiner" then the claimant must ultimately prevail before a fee may be awarded under the "extent of success" standard espoused in Hensley. However, this condition amounts to an argument that individual class members must pass a second "prevailing party" test in order to be entitled to an award of attorneys fees. Such a "second prevailing party determination" was rejected in Foster. Because the class members have already prevailed, a reasonable fee award is not determined by mere reference to the success of each claim presented at the relief stage. Rather, consideration is given to the relationship between the amount of the fee awarded and the results obtained as a whole. See Foster, supra, at 1024. Thus, while the Commission does not necessarily concur with the McKenzie Court's holding that a fee award at the individual relief stage of a class proceeding may be reduced only for frivolous or vexatious claims, the Commission does not find that a reduction is mandated simply because certain hours included in a fee request can be traced to an unsuccessful contention pursued before the CE or an agency official.

...

Similarly, the Commission does not find that the previous decision adversely impacts on the "incentive to negotiate and settle claims before the agency in good faith." As noted in appellant's response to the agency request, claimants face substantial delays of up to one year if they decline to settle on the terms proffered by the agency and instead request referral to the CE. The prospect of such delays, and the unavailability of any interest on the backpay awards against the agency, are sufficient incentives to negotiate and settle claims in good faith.

Finally, the Commission is not persuaded that the prior decision creates such a "possibility and opportunity for the exploitation of fees in the relief phase of any class action" as to "render it against the pecuniary interest of the attorney to abandon an unworthy claim." In the instant case, the record clearly establishes that class counsel assumed, for various reasons, that fees would be awarded for all claims briefed before the CE. Nonetheless, class counsel withdrew the claims of 16 individual class members without requesting a hearing. Further pursuant to McKenzie, no fees should be awarded for time spent on frivolous or vexatious claims. Moreover, insistence on briefing obviously unmerited claims before the CE may result in a finding that the hours were not "reasonably expended" under Hensley. In addition, under Foster, counsel who chooses to pursue questionable claims before the CE would inevitably achieve such a limited level of success before both the CE and the agency that a reduction of fees would be proper.
