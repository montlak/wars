A. PROPOSED DISCIPLINARY ACTIONS

In the past, the Commission allowed complaints over proposed disciplinary actions. This led to many dual complaints where the same employee filed a complaint when the disciplinary action was first proposed and later filed another complaint when the proposed action became reality. Often, the complaints were consolidated at some stage of the administrative process and at the time the two complaints basically merged into one complaint over the actual discipline imposed on the employee.

In 1987, the Commission revised its regulations to provide that an agency shall dismiss a complaint filed over a proposed disciplinary action. See 29 CFR 1613.215(a)(2). This is clearly the better practice. It eliminated the processing of complaints for which there is no relief. Also, it is consistent with the Commission's treatment of the issue of standing, which requires the complainant to allege some personal injury-in-fact. See discussion in Chapter 3 under the heading "Standing: Failure to State a Claim." Under 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(5), agencies are required to dismiss complaints involving proposed personnel actions.

Under the former 29 CFR 1613.406, the Commission established a detailed framework for the handling of allegations that proposed adverse actions are discriminatory:

(a) Where the Agency Decision is Appealable to the MSPB. (1) Any complaint which is filed, pursuant to this part, in connection with any agency proposal to take an action that is appealable to the MSPB, shall be consolidated, pursuant to 1613.251, of Subpart B of this part, with any subsequent mixed case complaint filed in connection with that agency decision to take such action, either as proposed or as modified during the processing of the proposal, and shall be processed in accordance with this subpart.

(2) If, following a complaint on a proposal, the complainant subsequently files an appeal with the MSPB on the subsequent agency decision resulting from that proposal, the agency shall cancel the complaint on the proposal. The complainant shall be advised by the agency that any allegations of discrimination contained in that complaint should be raised with the MSPB, in connection with the pending appeal.

(3) If a complaint is filed on a proposal, and no appeal is filed with the MSPB on the subsequent appealable agency decision resulting from that proposal, and no complaint is filed with the agency on that appealable decision, the complaint on the proposal shall be deemed to include the final decision as an issue (as of the effective date of such final decision and shall be processed as a mixed case complaint, in accordance with 1613.405.

(b) Where the Agency Decision is Not Appealable to the MSPB. Where a proposal does not result in an agency decision which is appealable to the MSPB (e.g., a proposal to remove which is modified by agency decision to a 5 day suspension), such a complaint shall be processed pursuant to Subparts B, E, F and/or G of this part, as appropriate.

For a detailed discussion of mixed case complaints, see Chapter 19 under the subheading "Mixed Cases Before the Merit Systems Protection Board," below. These sections have been eliminated from Part 1614, and the section on mixed case complaints merely notes that an agency "may" dismiss a complaint for any of the reasons set forth in 29 CFR 1614.107. See 29 CFR 1614.302(c).

The Commission's revised regulations removing proposed actions from the purview of the complaint process should not be construed in such a way as to elevate form over substance. Noting that the EEO process "is designed for utilization by laymen without the assistance of counsel," the Commission held that where the employee filed a complaint over a proposed adverse action and subsequently was suspended for 14 days, it would be "unjust to suggest that appellant committed a fatal error in neglecting to note that she was challenging the ultimate agency decision to discipline and not merely the discipline which was imposed." Bowen v. Postmaster General, 05880553 (1988). Although the Commission noted that in the Bowen case the regulation had yet to become effective, the language of the decision suggests that the regulation's intent is to bar separate complaints on notices of adverse action and not to bar employees from challenging adverse actions for failure to comply technically with the regulations.

Even though proposed disciplinary actions are now more difficult to challenge through the EEO process, the agency may not use proposed disciplinary actions as a lever to extract settlement from a complainant. See Roberts v. Secretary of Treasury, 05900097 (1990). In the Roberts case, the complainant alleged that the agency physically attached a proposed 14-day suspension to a proposed settlement agreement of two pending EEO complaints:

In one respect, in arguing that the agency proposed her suspension solely as a means to induce her to settle complaints upon which she had received a favorable AJ recommended decision, appellant is attempting to show the agency did not have a legitimate basis for the suspension, and therefore it was based on discriminatory animus. In the alternative, appellant is alleging that, even assuming the agency had legitimate grounds for proposing her suspension, it used the proposed suspension in an impermissible manner in that it introduced the proposed suspension during settlement negotiations in an attempt to coerce her into signing a settlement agreement which she believed was deficient.... An allegation that an agency has coerced or attempted to coerce a complainant to accept a settlement is an actionable claim.

The distinction here is that the appellant was challenging the proposed suspension only insofar as it was used as an instrument of coercion and not as an action standing alone.

If a proposed adverse action is rescinded in its entirety, there is no adverse action resulting in harm. See Tajuna v. Secretary of the Navy, 01911407 (1991). However, the proposed action can still be included in a complaint that alleges continuing harassment where the proposal is one of the incidents of harassment relied upon by the complainant. See also Werle v. Postmaster General, 01912666 (1991). In Flores v. Postmaster General, 01954546 (1996), the Commission held that the complainant was aggrieved by issuance of a notice of removal even where the removal was later rescinded and never effected. The issuance of the removal notice itself aggrieved the complainant.

When an employee files a complaint over a proposed suspension and later files a complaint alleging the proposal led to her involuntary resignation, the agency errs in dismissing the first complaint because it involves a proposed action. Under the circumstances, the Commission viewed the proposed suspension as part of a pattern of harassment and, thus, dismissal of the complaint was improper. Moreover, the Commission determined that the complaints over the proposed suspension and involuntary resignation were "inextricably intertwined" and as a result, the agency also erred in refusing to consolidate the complaints. Capers v. Secretary of Treasury, 01955160 (1996).

As with other discrimination claims, a layperson is not required to state his or her case with any legal precision. The Commission will examine the underlying factual allegations to determine the precise nature of the claim. For example, in Cogen v. Postmaster General, 01912926 (1991), the agency dismissed the complaint as an improper challenge to a proposed disciplinary action where the complainant alleged her supervisor had threatened to force her from the agency. In support of the complaint, the employee had alleged that her supervisor treated white employees more favorably than black employees and had a tendency to harass females. The Commission found the essential nature of the allegation was one of ongoing harassment.

Under its current regulations requiring the dismissal of proposed adverse actions, the Commission tends to view midyear reviews as a preliminary step to taking a personnel action. As a result, such a review will not ordinarily aggrieve an employee. Alva v. Secretary of Treasury, 01932048 (1993). However, when a midyear review is cited as one example of an overall pattern of harassment, the agency errs in dismissing the complaint on the narrow grounds that the midyear review is not a final personnel action.
