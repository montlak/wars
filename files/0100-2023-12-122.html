8. Office of Workers Compensation Programs

The Commission reversed its position that an agency does not have jurisdiction over a complaint when an employee alleges that the agency sent a letter to the Office of Workers Compensation Programs disputing his claim of reimbursement for medical expenses and leave, even though the employee alleges the letter was reprisal for filing a previous EEO complaint. Initially, the Commission rejected such claims, holding that matters involving work-related injuries are solely within the province of the Department of Labor. See Salazar v. USPS, 01831177 (1983). There was some doubt about the soundness of the reasoning in the Salazar decision since, if the employee was able to establish that the agency had prohibited motives in opposing the claim and that the agency's opposition was a significant factor in the OWCP decision, then the employee should prevail on such a claim. For example, in Walters v. USPS, 01832749 (1984), in dicta, the EEOC commented that an employee could not prevail without showing significant agency involvement in the OWCP decision.

The Commission now holds that such allegations state a claim for relief, and a complaint may be filed with both the employing agency and the Department of Labor. See Mares v. Secretary of Army, 01960499 (1998). In Mares, the employee alleged that his supervisor made disparaging remarks about his past EEO complaint activity in responding to his OWCP claim. The Commission found such remarks were clearly retaliatory and, in addition to ordering relief with respect to the employing agency, noted that the complainant could have filed an EEO complaint against OWCP. See also Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120150830 (2015) (to the extent that the complainant was alleging the agency's modified job offer was discriminatory, she failed to state a claim, but to the extent she was alleging the offer failed to provide reasonable accommodation, she had stated an EEO claim); Brizenzo v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A14841 (2002) (an allegation that the agency willfully failed to provide information to OWCP on a discriminatory basis states a claim); Villa v. Secretary of Treasury, 01994737 (2000) (an allegation that a supervisor used the worker's compensation process in a discriminatory manner when compared to other employees states a claim).

However, the general rule is that "agency opposition to an OWCP claim does not affect a term, condition, or privilege of employment so as to render a person aggrieved." Corcoran v. Postmaster General, 01A20013 (2002) (an allegation that a supervisor lied in opposing an OWCP claim is a "collateral attack" on the OWCP process and does not state a claim). See also Alonzo L. v. Dept. of Labor, 0120160981 (2016) ("a complaint involving an agency's controversion of a complainant's OWCP claim constitute[s] a collateral attack and fail[s] to state a claim."); Gustafson v. Postmaster General, 01A00698 (2001). The complainant must identify a specific omission that influenced the OWCP decision in order to state a claim. By contrast, the failure of the agency to submit the required paperwork to OWCP does state a claim. See Williams v. Postmaster General, 01A10999 (2001); see also Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120141683 (2014) (allegation that agency delayed submitting paperwork for continuation of pay stated a claim).

The mere fact that the complainant has a claim pending with OWCP is not a legitimate basis for dismissal of a complaint. Granger v. Postmaster General, 01A54495 (2005) (OWCP claim had no bearing where complainant has alleged that she was subjected to disparate treatment due to disability when her hours were reduced); Corrina M. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120140413 (2016) (the complainant's harassment complaint concerning the agency's revocation of her modified work assignment based on her medical restrictions was not a collateral attack where the complainant had, seven years earlier, filed an OWCP claim based on those same medical restrictions). Moreover, the fact that a complainant may be eligible for workers compensation benefits from an incident, such as being sent home from work, is not a collateral attack on an EEO proceeding involving the same claim brought on the basis of disability discrimination. Thompson v. Postmaster General, 0120063745 (2008).

Moreover, the fact that a disabling condition was the result of a work-related injury and gave rise to a workers' compensation claim does not necessarily deprive the Commission of jurisdiction. In Zenia M. v. Postmaster General, 2021001220 (2021), the Commission found that, though the disability condition arose at work, the complainant's denial of reasonable accommodation claim was not foreclosed:

The Agency erred in determining that Complainant was lodging a collateral attack on the OWCP process. While Complainant's asserted disability was allegedly the result of a work-related injury which gave rise to a workers' compensation claim, in her EEO complaint, Complainant is alleging the Agency failed to reasonably accommodate her disability in violation of the Rehabilitation Act. Among other things, she asserted that on August 17, 2020, she was required to wear an open-toed splint by her doctor. She was initially allowed to report to work wearing the splint until September 1, 2020, when she was sent home by management, who asserted wearing the splint on the workroom floor was a safety violation. She then asserts on September 10, 2020, she was ordered back to work still wearing the splint, but placed in an unsafe location and required to sit on a stool and given no work to do. She also asserted she was located too far from the restrooms without consideration for her walking impairment. These allegations are sufficient to state a viable failure to accommodate claim under the Rehabilitation Act that requires investigation and further processing. Simply because her workers' compensation claim was also being adjudicated does not mean Complainant is not entitled to pursue a denial of reasonable accommodation claim under the Rehabilitation Act through the EEO complaint process.

An allegation that the agency does not provide promotional opportunities to disabled individuals who are placed in permanent rehabilitation duty positions as the result of an on-the-job injury does not constitute a collateral attack on the OWCP process and states a claim for relief. Glover v. Postmaster General, 01A04428 (2001). An allegation that work assignment is outside of the medical restrictions set by the Office of Workers Compensation Programs is a collateral attack on the workers compensation process and does not state a claim. See Garcia v. Postmaster General, 01A44785 (2005).

In Emelda F. v. Postmaster General, 0120162786 (2016), the Commission held that an allegation that the agency modified its job offer by assigning the complainant to a different work schedule based on OWCP's approval of the modified offer was not an impermissible collateral attack of the OWCP adjudicatory process:

The Commission has held that an employee cannot use the EEO complaint process to lodge a collateral attack on another adjudicatory proceeding. See Wills v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05970596 (July 30, 1998); Kleinman v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940585 (September 22, 1994); Lingad v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930106 (June 25, 1993). However, in the instant complaint, Complainant alleged discrimination when Agency management attempted to reassign her to a new modified position that placed her on a different tour of duty. While OWCP "approved" the position, it appears that it was only for the purpose of indicating that the position met Complainant's work restrictions resulting from her work-related compensable injury. OWCP did not decide that her tour of duty should be changed--that was a decision of Agency management. Therefore, we do not regard this as a collateral attack on the OWCP adjudicatory process. By alleging that Agency management attempted to force her to accept a position on another tour of duty, Complainant has alleged an injury or harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy.

However, there is a distinction that the Commission draws between proceedings that involve another agency, such as OWCP, and internal agency procedures. For example, in Carter v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A44372 (2006), the complainant took a preemployment medical examination and filled out a medical questionnaire. Based on her responses, the agency's medical consultant recommended that she not be hired because she could not meet the physical requirements of the position. The complainant appealed that determination to the agency's Medical Review Board, which denied the appeal. The agency dismissed the subsequent EEO complaint as a collateral attack on the MRB process. The Commission reversed finding that "[t]his situation is distinguishable from the allegations that make up a collateral attack where the proceedings take place in another forum, such as the Office of Workers' Compensation."
