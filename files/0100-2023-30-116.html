2. Damages Hearings

The Commission's administrative judges have the discretion to take evidence on compensatory damages at the hearing on the merits, or to bifurcate the proceeding and take evidence on damages only if a finding of discrimination ensues. However, if a complainant requests a decision without a hearing on the underlying merits of the claim, she is not later entitled to a hearing on the issue of damages. In Baker v. Postmaster General, 05960598 (1997), the Commission ordered the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation on damages and then give the complainant a notice of her right to a hearing or a decision without a hearing. On reconsideration, the Commission determined that it was error to order the agency to give the complainant the hearing option. The Commission found that the agency, after its supplemental investigation, should proceed to issue a final agency decision on the damages issue.

Whether to bifurcate the hearing on the merits and on damages is apparently a matter within the sound discretion of the administrative judge. In Finlay v. Postmaster General, 01942985 (1997), the Commission found that there is nothing requiring an administrative judge to bifurcate a hearing in which compensatory damages are an issue. The Commission in Finlay noted that agencies must come to hearings prepared to resolve the issue of compensatory damages:

As a preliminary matter, the agency contends on appeal that the AJ should have bifurcated the hearing to allow the agency to present evidence regarding compensatory damages. The Commission notes that AJs are not expected to bifurcate hearings in which compensatory damages are raised, nor has the agency drawn the Commission's attention to any support for such a requirement. Given the known potential liability for compensatory damages, the agency should have appeared for the hearing prepared to address the issue of compensatory damages, to include presenting relevant documentary or testimonial evidence. Accordingly, the Commission finds the agency's contention on appeal to be without merit.

As a practical note, there is no reason why an agency, or a complainant for that matter, should come to a hearing unprepared to address the issue of damages. Either party, or both, can and should seek clarification from the administrative judge well in advance of the hearing as to whether evidence on damages will be taken at the time of the hearing on the merits.

It is also within the discretion of the administrative judge to change his or her mind about bifurcation. In Hardman v. Postmaster General, 05990591 (2001), the administrative judge determined the hearing on damages would be bifurcated, but changed his mind at the hearing and took evidence of damages. The complainant's representative made no objection, nor did the representative raise the issue on the agency's initial appeal of the finding of discrimination. When the issue was raised on reconsideration, the Commission quickly rejected the complainant's argument that he was denied an opportunity to present evidence of damages because he had not come to the hearing prepared to present such evidence. Though not articulated in Hardman, it is likely the appeal would have had a different outcome had the complainant objected to the judge's change of mind at the hearing and also raised the issue during the initial appeal.

In its Judges' Handbook at 7-10, the Commission included the following guidance on taking evidence of damages:

1. Taking Evidence on Relief at the Hearing on Liability

The Administrative Judge has the sole discretion to order the parties to present evidence on relief as part of the hearing on liability but must advise the parties prior to the hearing as to whether they should be prepared to do so. Factors the Administrative Judge may consider in exercising this discretion include: impact on the length of the hearing; number of witnesses to testify about relief; amount and kind of relief sought; and the effect on docket management. If attorney's fees and costs are requested as part of the relief, the Administrative Judge should set a schedule for submission of the petition for attorney's fees and costs and the agency's opposition. The Administrative Judge should make clear to the parties that the agency cannot take final action and appeal rights do not attach until the Administrative Judge has issued a decision that encompasses liability, relief, attorney's fees, and costs, if appropriate.

2. Bifurcating the Hearing

An Administrative Judge may choose to bifurcate the hearing between the liability phase and the relief phase. If the Administrative Judge bifurcates the hearing, the Administrative Judge should inform the parties as early as possible and ensure that they understand the process. Factors to be considered when deciding whether to bifurcate the hearing include: expected length and complexity of a bifurcated hearing; the interests of the parties in minimizing expenses prior to a decision on liability; and docket management considerations of the Administrative Judge and the District Office. The same procedures described above should be followed for the hearing on relief.

3. Determining Relief Based on a Written Record

If the circumstances warrant, the Administrative Judge may determine relief based on written submissions. The Administrative Judge should not make this determination on a written record if the credibility of the complainant or key witnesses is material to the determination of relief.

Although the administrative judge has the discretion to determine when and how evidence of damages will be taken, he may not deprive the complainant of an actual hearing if, at the hearing on the merits, he indicates a separate damage hearing will be held if there is a finding of liability. In Sullivan v. Secretary of Army, 0120055812 (2007), the Commission found it was error for the judge to determine damages based on paper submissions after leading the complainant to believe there would be a hearing on damages. The Commission remanded the case for a hearing.

In recent years, the Commission has issued a number of default judgments as sanctions against agencies. See discussion in Chapter 8. When the administrative judge issues a default judgment decision, he or she must give the complainant a full opportunity to present evidence of damages. Suit v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120082737 (2010).
