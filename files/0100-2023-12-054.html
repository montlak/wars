2. Injury in Fact

Most of the Commission's cases on standing focus on whether the complainant has alleged an injury in fact, because virtually everyone is within the zone of interests protected by the antidiscrimination statutes, particularly Title VII. For example, in Patel v. Postmaster General, 01963070 (1997), the Commission held that an allegation that the appellant was discriminatorily assigned duties because of her race, color, national origin, and/or disability failed to state a claim where there was no evidence that she was ever made to perform those duties. The complainant was within the zone of interests protected by Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act, but she had not alleged an injury in fact because she had never been required to perform the disputed duties.

The complainant must allege a specific harm that has already occurred. The employee must "suffer direct and personal deprivation at the hands of the employer." Prater v. USPS, 01832284 (1984).

The alleged injury is not required to be economic. Title VII protects "psychological as well as economic well-being." Staten v. USPS, 01872557 (1987). An employee who claims that an order to take a psychiatric fitness for duty examination was issued on a discriminatory basis has alleged a sufficient injury in fact to have standing under Title VII. The Commission often refers to this requirement as "injury in fact." The injury can take several forms, including feelings of embarrassment and harassment. See, e.g., Moore v. Postmaster General, 05880665 (1988). But, the Commission has also held that the mere allegation that some action of the agency has caused the employee mental anguish is not sufficient to establish that the employee is aggrieved. Dogans v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 05920996 (1993) (an allegation that an employee was aggrieved by the agency's placing her position into "overage" status was dismissed).

The fact that the complainant requested specific relief does not establish that she has suffered sufficient harm to be aggrieved. As the Commission explained in Carpenter v. Postmaster General, 01941917 (1994):

Appellant alleged that she was discriminated against when her acting supervisor told her to go back to her work area in front of other employees on two occasions. Appellant was not asked to do anything that she was not already required to do. The acting supervisor's comments were not accompanied by any disciplinary or other action. We find that appellant was not aggrieved since a term, condition, or privilege of her employment was not affected by the acting supervisor's statements. See Simon v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05900866 (October 3, 1990). Thus, we find that appellant's complaint failed to state a claim.

We note that appellant asserted that she was humiliated and threatened by the alleged discriminatory actions, and as a result of the alleged incidents, she had to go to see her doctor, and had to take two days of sick leave. The Commission has held that, when an allegation fails to render a complainant aggrieved for purposes of Title VII and the EEOC Regulations, it will not be converted into a processable claim merely because the complainant has requested a specific relief. Larotonda v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 01933846 (March 11, 1994). Citing Czecha v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Request No. 05890929 (November 13, 1989), the Commission noted that the relief requested by a complainant is irrelevant to whether a complaint states a processable claim. Id. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's allegation for failure to state a claim was proper.

See also Nelson v. Secretary of Army, 01941702 (1994) ("[T]he relief requested by a complainant is irrelevant to whether a complaint states a processable claim.").

The Commission construes the injury in fact requirement very broadly. In Meredith v. Postmaster General, 05890714 (1989), the Commission found that an allegation that a negative recommendation for a detail was discriminatory sufficiently aggrieved the complainant. This was true even without any showing that the complainant was denied a detail as a result of the recommendation. "A fair and unbiased recommendation, when one is deserving, is in and of itself a term and condition of employment, independent of any right that appellant may have had to [a] detail assignment." Id.

On the other hand, the allegation that an agency action may, at some future point in time, prove detrimental to the employee has been deemed an insufficient injury in fact to confer standing on a complainant. See Quinones v. Secretary of Defense, 05920051 (1992) (a confidential referral to an employee assistance counselor, alleged to have future impact on performance appraisals and promotion opportunities, presents no cognizable claim); cf. Hansen v. Postmaster General, 05980707 (1999) (an allegation that an employee was ordered to the employee assistance program, which was 20 miles away, states a claim).

Even this brief review of Commission cases involving whether a complainant has alleged sufficient harm to be aggrieved underscores the comments of Justice Douglas in Association of Data Processing Organizations, supra, that "[g]eneralizations about standing to sue are largely worthless as such...." The determination is highly individualized and dependent upon the specific facts involved in a particular complaint.
