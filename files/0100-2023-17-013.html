5. Agency Failure to Issue Final Decision/Order

Agencies have 40 days to issue a final action either agreeing to implement the judge's decision or not implement the decision. The complainant then has 30 days to appeal that final decision to OFO.

If the agency fails to issue its decision in 40 days, the decision of the administrative judge becomes binding. 29 CFR 1614.109(i) ("If the agency does not issue a final order within 40 days of receipt of the administrative judge's decision in accordance with 1614.110, then the decision of the administrative judge shall become the final action of the agency."). The Commission has held, in that circumstance, "there is no regulatory provision that sets a specific time limitation for filing an appeal." See Avery S. v. Secretary of Treasury, 2020000221 (2020). Rather, the time for a complainant to file an appeal in this instance is governed by the doctrine of laches--"an equitable remedy under which an individual's failure to diligently pursue their actions can bar their claims." Id., citing O'Dell v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05901130 (1990).

In Iris D. v. Secretary of Army, 2020000407 (2020), for example, the AJ's order became binding when the agency failed to issue its decision within the allotted 40 days. The complainant appealed to OFO 46 days later. The agency moved to dismiss the appeal arguing the appeal was untimely. Relying on the doctrine of laches, the Commission found that the complainant's 16 day did not justify dismissal:

EEOC regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i) provides that if an agency does not issue a final order within 40 days of receipt of the AJ's decision in accordance with 29 C.F.R. § 1614.110, the decision of the AJ shall become the final action of the Agency.

In the July 26, 2019 decision, the AJ provided notice that our regulations require the Agency to take final action on the complaint by issuing a final order within 40 calendar days of receipt of an AJ's decision and order. The AJ's decision also provided notice that Complainant may not directly appeal to the Commission from his decision unless the Agency failed to issue its final decision within 40 calendar days of its receipt of the AJ's decision.

The Certificate of Service in the Order notes that for timeliness purposes, receipt of the Order and Decision would be presumed as received on July 26, 2019, by electronic mail. 2 The AJ's Order and Decision is therefore presumed to have been received by both the Agency and Complainant on July 26, 2019.

The 40-day countdown therefore started on July 26, 2019. The Agency then had 40 days to issue a decision in accordance with 29 C.F.R. § 1614.110, i.e., by September 4, 2019. Because the Agency did not issue a decision by September 4, 2019, the AJ's decision became the decision of the Agency by operation of law, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i). Ela O. v. Nat'l Sec. Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 0720130021 (Oct. 30, 2015) (AJ's finding of discrimination became agency's final decision by operation of law where agency failed to take action during the 40-day period); Complainant v. Dep't of State, EEOC Appeal No. 0120141065 (May 15, 2014) (complainant's appeal untimely where AJ's decision became agency's decision by operation of law).

At that point, Complainant, per the AJ's July 26, 2019 Notice, had to file an appeal directly to the Commission within 30 days. The Agency argued that, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.402(a), Complainant had to file her appeal by October 4, 2019. Complainant's appeal was not submitted until October 20, 2019. As such, the Agency requested that we dismiss the appeal as untimely.

However, on November 20, 2019, the Agency decided to issue its FAO. When the Agency issued its November 20, 2019 FAO, it provided Complainant appeal rights to the Commission. Therefore, in this circumstance, this made Complainant's October 20, 2019 appeal timely. Moreover, contrary to the Agency's argument, the Commission has held there is no regulatory provision that sets a specific time limitation for filing an appeal in circumstances such as this wherein an AJ's decision becomes final by operation of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i). Avery S. v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 2020000221 (Jan. 22, 2020). Rather, the time for Complainant to file an appeal in this instance is governed by the doctrine of laches--"an equitable remedy under which an individual's failure to diligently pursue their actions can bar their claims." O'Dell v. Dep't of Health and Human Serv., EEOC Request No. 05901130 (Dec. 27, 1990). Here, we conclude that laches does not apply to a 16-day delay.

See also Stella K. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2019005673 (2020); Avery S. v. Secretary of Treasury, 2020000221 (2020). For more discussion, see "Failure to Issue Final Agency Action" in this chapter.
