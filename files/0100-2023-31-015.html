1. Fees as Sanctions

Although fees are not available for prevailing in procedural matters, the Commission has held that fees may be available in some procedural cases as a sanction against the agency's conduct. In several cases, the Commission found it had the authority to award complainant's attorney fees as a sanction against agency misconduct. The sanctions could be imposed against an agency even if the agency ultimately prevails on the discrimination complaint. See Salter v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01993997 (2000) (an administrative judge awarding a nonprevailing complainant fees and costs incurred during discovery was appropriate where the agency failed to comply with a discovery request).

In January 2003, the practice of issuing an award of attorney fees as a sanction came to a halt, at least temporarily, when the Department of Justice, Office of Legal Counsel, issued an opinion that the doctrine of sovereign immunity barred such sanctions because Title VII provided only for an award of attorney fees to prevailing parties. The Commission then rejected the Justice Department's position in Waller v. Secretary of Transportation, 0720030069 (2007). The Justice Department memorandum and Waller are discussed in Chapter 6 under the subheading "Justice Memo Halting Fees as Sanctions."

Prior to January 2003, in Terrell v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 04950018 (1996), the Commission determined that the complainant was entitled to be reimbursed for attorney fees and costs incurred in processing a petition for enforcement because the agency was late in complying with the Commission's previous order. Relying upon its decisions providing for attorney fees as sanctions for agency misconduct in discovery, the Commission in Terrell held:

The Commission has previously awarded attorney's fees and costs as a sanction for an agency's bad faith conduct in failing to comply with discovery orders during the administrative hearing stage. See Comer v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, EEOC Request No. 05940649 (May 31, 1996); Stull v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01941582 (June 15, 1995). The Commission finds the rationale set forth in those decisions to be equally applicable to the case herein. Specifically, had the agency complied with the previous Order to complete the supplemental investigation within 90 days, petitioner need not have filed the instant petition for enforcement. Further, since the petition for enforcement was a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the agency's untimely compliance, the Commission finds it appropriate to award attorney's fees and costs in this case.

The Terrell case was a marked departure from the Commission's previous holdings in this area and, for complainants, it certainly was a positive development. Prior to Terrell, agencies were simply free to ignore any Commission order with virtual impunity. True, the Commission might have harsh words for an agency that blatantly stood in noncompliance with a Commission order, but that provided little comfort for the complainant who was forced to needlessly expend attorney fees just to get the agency to comply with procedural orders. The Commission subsequently awarded attorney fees in similar cases. See Ortega v. Postmaster General, 01956818 (1998); Velasquez v. Attorney General, 04960018 (1997); Williams v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04960024 (1997).

In Korchnak v. Postmaster General, 04960028 (1996), the Commission declined to impose attorney fees as a sanction in a case under the ADEA. Noting that the ADEA does not allow for the recovery of fees for a prevailing party, the Commission specifically declined to consider the issue of its authority to impose the fees as a sanction finding the complainant had not established that the conduct of the agency warranted a sanction. The Commission in Korchnak found:

Petitioner seeks payment of attorney's fees, arguing that the agency's delay in complying with the Commission's back pay order necessitated the hiring of counsel. The agency denied his request on the grounds that the statute under which the Commission found discrimination, the ADEA, does not provide for the payment of attorney's fees. Petitioner counters that he does not seek attorney's fees as a remedy for discrimination under the ADEA, but rather as a sanction for the agency's dilatory behavior in complying with the Commission's back pay order.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.501(e) provides for the award of attorney's fees or costs in cases brought under Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act. The regulations do not provide for the award of attorney's fees to complainants bringing cases under the ADEA. Moreover, as the agency stated in its Compliance Report, courts have held that a successful complainant has no legal entitlement to attorney's fees in administrative proceedings under the ADEA. Palmer v. General Services Administration, 787 F.2d 300 (5th Cir. 1986); Kennedy v. Whitehurst, 600 F.2d 951 (D.C. Cir. 1982). In this regard, we find that petitioner is not entitled to an award of attorney's fees as a remedy herein. Whether the Commission has the inherent authority to award attorney's fees in an ADEA matter as a sanction for agency misconduct is a separate issue and one that we need not address herein: petitioner has not established that the agency's actions in this case were sufficiently egregious to warrant imposition of a sanction for misconduct.

However, in Gray v. Postmaster General, 01965101 (1998), the Commission did award fees as a sanction for the work required to have an adverse inference drawn against the agency in a case that involved age discrimination, as well as discrimination under Title VII.

Under the previous version of Part 1614, when an administrative judge issued a decision awarding fees as a sanction, the agency was required to issue its own final action within 40 days or the decision became binding on the agency. See, e.g., Rockwell v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01956593 (1997). The Rockwell case involved a recommended decision, but would appear equally applicable under the current regulations.

Fees could not be awarded as a sanction for a conflict of interest. In Bartner v. Secretary of Transportation, 01966904 (1998), an agency attorney who was previously disqualified from representing the agency reviewed the fee request. Ultimately, the agency relied on that attorney's advice and reduced the requested award. The Commission rejected an argument that full fees should be awarded because of the involvement of the agency counsel.

But fees could be awarded to a complainant for an agency's bad faith conduct in settlement negotiations. See Epstein v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05970671 (1998). In that case, the agency entered into an agreement providing for a letter of apology. However, the agency's attorney had no authority to agree to the provision. The agency refused to implement that portion of the agreement and the Commission awarded fees as a sanction. As the Commission explained in Epstein:

The previous decision mischaracterized the basis for the attorney's fees award by referring to Commission case law and regulations regarding prevailing parties. Record evidence in this case showed that there was a dispute as to whether appellant knew that the agency's attorney had not received formal permission to agree to the letter of apology. The previous decision found that regardless of what appellant knew, "the evidence demonstrates that the agency's attorney purported to have the authority to promise a letter of apology" and that appellant appeared to enter into the agreement in reliance thereon. We find that the agency attorney's representation that she could provide the letter of apology--when she clearly knew that she had not yet received formal authorization therefor--constituted bad faith dealing. Consequently, we find that the award of attorney's fees properly was made as a sanction for the agency's actions during settlement negotiations. See generally Stull v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01941582 (June 15, 1995) (no prevailing party requirement in regulation pertaining to sanctions).

The Commission held that it could award fees as a sanction for dilatory discovery conduct on appeal, even when the administrative judge had denied such fees below. Moreover, when the Commission found the agency's discovery conduct was dilatory, it could award fees for work performed in seeking sanctions against the agency at the hearing level despite the judge's denial of sanctions. As the Commission explained in Shine v. Postmaster General, 01972201 (1998):

Based on our review of the record evidence, we find that award of attorney's fees is appropriate in this case in light of the agency's consistent failure to cooperate with appellant's discovery requests until the AJ issued her order compelling the agency to comply with discovery. We further find that the agency's objections in response to appellant's discovery requests were untimely and properly overruled by the AJ. Furthermore, we find that the fact that the AJ may have mis-characterized appellant's June 24, 1994, correspondence as a motion to compel is immaterial in light of the fact that the agency was placed on notice that the AJ was construing the correspondence as such during the August 25, 1994, prehearing. Thus, we find that the agency was aware of appellant's motion to compel discovery but failed to pose any objections at that time. In accordance with our decision, we further find it appropriate that the attorney's fees sanction include those fees incurred in connection with appellant's attempt to have an adverse inference drawn despite the fact that appellant ultimately did not prevail on this issue. See Stull v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01941582 (June 15, 1995). The Commission notes that appellant is also entitled to reasonable attorney's fees incurred in connection with the instant appeal, but only to the extent that such fees pertain to the scope of attorney's fees awarded as a sanction. See Smith v. Dept. of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01940320 (February 24, 1994); and Work v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 019202029 (May 8, 1992). Based upon a thorough review of the record, and for the foregoing reasons, it is the decision of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to MODIFY the final agency decision to reflect the appropriate attorney's fees sanction, and as modified, to AFFIRM agency finding that appellant did not prove discrimination as alleged.

Attorney fees could also be imposed as a sanction for the failure of an agency to cooperate during the investigation of the complaint. Graham v. Secretary of Transportation, 01986987 (2001). See also Emelda F. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720170024 (2017) (awarding as sanctions the additional attorney fees incurred by the complainant when the agency improperly delayed processing her complaint by five months).

Fees as sanctions also are discussed in Chapter 6 under the heading "Discovery Sanctions."
