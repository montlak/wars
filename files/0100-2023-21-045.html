12. Offensive Looks

The Commission has been somewhat inconsistent in cases considering whether offensive looks alone can rise to the level of creating a hostile environment. For example, in King v. Department of Labor, 01830894 (1983), the Commission observed that a hostile environment must be evaluated in light of the totality of the circumstances, but declined to decide the issue of "offensive looks" finding there was insufficient evidence to conclude the "looks" had, in fact, occurred.

In McManus v. Postmaster General, 05880625 (1988), the appellant alleged that his supervisor engaged in sexual harassment by staring at him while striking a sexually suggestive pose. While indicating that nonverbal conduct was included within the definition of physical conduct that could be considered sexual harassment, the Commission found the actions of the supervisor did not rise to a level of being sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile environment.

Once again, in Westfall v. Postmaster General, 01922687 (1993), the Commission, in dicta, noted that staring could be a form of sexual harassment where the complainant alleged her supervisor stared at her "backside." But, again, the Commission found there was insufficient evidence that the staring was severe or pervasive enough to create a hostile environment. In Nolen-Frisby v. Secretary of Transportation, 01950312 (1997), the Commission, noting the subjective nature of the complainant's allegations that her supervisor had looked at her with "lusting" looks and "elevator eyes" also found the conduct was not sufficiently severe or pervasive as to create a hostile environment. Similarly, in Bejar v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A20721 (2002), the Commission found that several incidents of staring, hostile and intimidating body posture and display of a hostile attitude were not sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile work environment.

It seems that in theory the Commission approves of the notion that offensive looks can create a hostile environment, but in practice there is little chance that such conduct will be found severe or pervasive enough to create such an environment. However, in Harasi-Doi v. U.S. Communications, 61 F.3d 777 (10th Cir. 1995), the court reversed a summary judgment decision in favor of the employee and held that whether threatening, provocative and intimidating looks created a hostile environment was a question of fact for the jury.

In Yonkers v. Secretary of Transportation, 01964675 (1998), the Commission found a hostile environment, based on reprisal, where harassing looks were combined with the perpetrator's unexplained presence in the complainant's work area. The perpetrator specifically began using a phone in close proximity to the complainant and, while talking to others, stared at her in a harassing and intimidating manner.

By contrast, in Lynch v. Postmaster General, 01981027 (1999), the Commission found a complaint that a coworker looked at the complainant's breast while talking to her did not create a hostile environment because the complainant did not establish that the conduct was based on sex, and the conduct was also not sufficiently severe or pervasive as to create a hostile environment. The Commission in Lynch held:

With respect to allegation 1, the record shows that OIC1, a male, worked with appellant for only a three week period. During this time, several co-workers and supervisors observed that OIC1 did not make eye contact during conversation, instead averting his gaze to the chest area. Although appellant did not inform OIC1 that she found this habit offensive, she did inform PM about the situation, threatening that OIC1 would have a "problem" if he persisted in this "unprofessional" manner. PM attributed OIC1's habit to his Native American Indian culture, made light of the situation, and did not discuss the matter with OIC1. In his affidavit testimony, OIC1 stated that he was not aware that he had offended appellant, and indicated that he does not make good eye contact. We concur with the FAD that appellant failed to establish a prima facie case because she provides no evidence to show that the OIC1's habit of chest "staring" was based on sex. Instead, the record shows that OIC1 engaged in this behavior in all of his conversations, and that it was a well known trait of his among his associates. Furthermore, the record fails to reflect that appellant found this to be sexually offensive, but rather was annoyed by OIC1's "unprofessional" conduct. Although appellant contends that PM should have discussed the matter with OCI1 after she brought it to his attention, under these circumstances, we do not find that his failure to do so is evidence of a hostile work environment based on sexual harassment because appellant communicated only that she was annoyed, and that she intended to address the situation herself, never mentioning that she found OIC1's chest "staring" sexually offensive.

One way to establish that offensive looks can create a hostile environment is present evidence from females other than the complainant and their reaction to the same conduct by the supervisor. See, e.g., Schmid v. Postmaster General, 0120101575 (2012).
