6. Drug Rehabilitation Programs

If an agency requires an employee to participate in a drug addiction rehabilitation program, that program must accommodate the employee's religious beliefs. In Harmon v. Secretary of Transportation, FAA, 01950755 (1998), the Commission found the complainant's right to accommodation was violated where he was agnostic and the agency required him to undergo a religious-based drug treatment program. The agency was not required to stop using the program entirely, but it was required to make alternative arrangements for those who objected on religious grounds.

The Commission in Harmon found:

Employers are liable under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 for failure to accommodate the religious practices or beliefs of their employees, absent proof that such accommodation could not be made without imposing an undue hardship upon its operations. 42 USC 2000e(a)(i), (29 CFR 1605.2(c); Transworld Airlines Inc. v. Convair, 589 F.2d 397, 400-101 (9th Cir.), cert. denied. 442 U.S. 921 (1979). Furthermore, any reasonable accommodation is sufficient to satisfy the employer's obligation under Title VII, and if an attempt at accommodation is deemed reasonable, the employer need not show that alternative accommodations would result in undue hardship. Ansonia Board of Education v. Philbrook, 479 U.S. 60, 68-69 (1986). An employer's duty of reasonable accommodation includes efforts to eliminate any conflicts between an employee's religious beliefs and employment requirements, and to preserve the employee's employment status. Hudson v. Western Airlines, 851 F.2d 261, 266 (9th Cir. 1988); EEOC Policy Statement No. N-915-025 (May 9, 1988). The reasonableness of an employer's attempt at accommodation must be determined on a case-by-case basis, taking into account the unique circumstances of a particular employer-employee relationship. Smith v. Pyro Mining Company, 827 F.2d 1081, 1085 (6th Cir. 1987).

Because the AJ had ordered, in part, that the agency cease the practice of requiring employees to participate in the religious based 12-step drug rehabilitation programs, the agency, apparently reading the AJ's order as requiring it to cease entirely from using the 12-step programs, contends that an appropriate remedy lies in providing alternative programs for employees who object to religious-based drug rehabilitation programs. For his part, appellant objects to any use by the agency of religious-based 12-step NA programs in the agency's efforts to rehabilitate employees.

After considering the record and the arguments of the parties, we find that a reasonable accommodation may be provided for appellant's religious beliefs (and for other employees with similar beliefs and objections) by offering an alternative substance-abuse program for employees who have religious objections to participating in the NA 12-step drug rehabilitation program, unless doing so would place an undue hardship on the agency.

Providing an alternative for employees who do not wish to participate in 12-step meetings would, in accordance with Hudson, effectively eliminate the religious conflict faced by appellant and others like him who have religious objections to participating in the 12-step program, and would also preserve the employee's employment status. Preventing the mandatory nature of the government's utilization of the 12-step program (without completely enjoining the agency's use of a tested addiction therapy) would also serve to further the purpose behind the religious accommodation prescription of Title VII, which the Commission has stated is to prevent a situation where an employee is forced to choose between his job and his religious beliefs. See Anderson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01890285 (April 2, 1990) (Commission ordered the agency to modify its Oath of Office to accommodate an employee whose religious views were in conflict with certain aspects of the Oath).

On reconsideration, in Harmon v. Secretary of Transportation, 05980433 (2000), the Commission reaffirmed its decision and held that the agency must advise the employee of any religious affiliation of the rehabilitation program so the employee can make an informed choice about rehabilitation. See also Chapter 15, under the subheading "Programs with Religious Affiliations."
