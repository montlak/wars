20. EEO Complaints, Complaints About

Perhaps only in the federal sector could one find EEO complaints filed about other EEO complaints. Still, it happens with some frequency. Managers file complaints because subordinates have filed complaints against them; subordinates file complaints because their managers have filed complaints.

In Blinco v. Secretary of Treasury, 05940194 (1994), an employee filed an EEO complaint alleging that the agency encouraged her manager to file an EEO complaint against her. In upholding the agency's dismissal of the complaint, the Commission in Blinco noted:

Appellant is alleging discrimination when the agency encouraged her manager to file a complaint against appellant as a form of retaliation and harassment in response to appellant's prior EEO complaints against the agency. Appellant asserted that her manager's EEO complaint was authorized and approved by management, and that the agency's actions were intended to be "an additional tool in keeping subordinates in line and under rigid control." Appellant stated that in response to her prior EEO complaint, which was settled, and her subsequent allegation that the settlement agreement had been breached by the agency, her manager began to prepare an EEO complaint against her.

The previous decision found that appellant had not shown herself to be aggrieved, as the filing of an EEO complaint by appellant's manager did not constitute an injury by the agency to a term or condition of appellant's employment. The previous decision further stated that to allow the processing of a complaint by an employee wherein the employee challenges the filing of another EEO complaint by a coworker or other agency employee would have a chilling effect on the filing of EEO complaints by aggrieved persons.

Although the Commission acknowledges its right to oversee the EEO process and to take action when the process is being abused, the Commission agrees with the previous decision that appellant does not have standing to file a separate EEO complaint challenging the actions of her manager in filing his complaint. Accordingly, the Commission finds that appellant's complaint does not state a claim for which relief can be granted, and the agency's rejection of the complaint on that basis is affirmed.

See also Davis v. Secretary of Defense, 01994609 (2002) (citing Blinco); Smith v. Secretary of Army, 01A0535 (2002) (citing Blinco); Shackleford v. Secretary of Army, 01A11483 (2001) ("The processing of a complaint by an employee, wherein the employee challenges the filing of an EEO complaint by a coworker or other agency employee, would have a chilling effect on the filing of EEO complaints by aggrieved persons."); Owens v. Secretary of Interior, 01964819 (1997) (an allegation that a subordinate supervisor instigated an employee to file an EEO complaint against a manager does not state a claim); Schlesinger v. Secretary of Treasury, 01965630 (1997).

In Coats v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A11276 (2002), the Commission explained its rationale for not allowing complaints about EEO complaints:

The Commission has previously held that to allow the processing of an EEO complaint by an employee, challenging the filing of an EEO complaint by another agency employee, would have a chilling effect on the filing of EEO complaints by aggrieved persons. See Blinco v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05940194 (May 25, 1994). We have also held that such a complaint constitutes a collateral attack on another EEO matter. See Smith v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 059506945 (April 4, 1996). Moreover, there is no remedial action available to complainant when another individual files an EEO complaint, as the agency has no authority to restrain an employee from raising EEO violations through the EEO complaint process. See Calloway v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01943406 (July 15, 1994); Sherwood v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01941846 (June 3, 1994). To establish that a settlement agreement between the employer and a third party was an independent act of discrimination, complainant must show that the settlement was a device to bestow unequal employment benefits under the guise of remedying discrimination, rather than a bona fide effort to settle a claim. See Carey v. United States Postal Service, 812 F.2d 621, 624 (10th Cir. 1987); Faison v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05900956 (October 12, 1990). Complainant has not shown that the agency's settlement with the coworkers was anything other than an effort to settle EEO claims. Complainant has produced no credible evidence of any other motivation on the part of the agency. The Commission concludes that the agency did not retaliate against complainant when she was reassigned as part of the settlement.

But in Currin v. Secretary of Treasury, 05960188 (1997), the Commission found the complainant had stated a claim for relief when he alleged that the agency had taken action against him because of a sexual harassment complaint filed by another employee. But see Soria v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120081473 (2008) (employee not aggrieved in being subjected to fact-finding meeting as a result of harassment complaint by coworker). However, when an agency reassigns an employee because he has been accused of harassment, this constitutes a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the reassignment. Sims v. Postmaster General, 01A54115 (2006). See also Chapter 12, subheading "Complaints by Alleged Harasser."
