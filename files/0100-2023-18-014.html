B. DESIGNATION OF REPRESENTATIVE

As soon as the complainant selects a representative, he or she should notify the agency of that choice, in writing. The Commission does not require a written designation of representative, though it has stated that such is advisable. Jones v. Secretary of Army, 01881619 (1988); see also Chapter 22, in the section on "Notice of Representation."

The complainant's notification to the agency that he or she has retained a representative serves at least two purposes. First, it authorizes the representative to act on behalf of the complainant. In the absence of such authorization, an agency may not be required to acknowledge or respond to the actions of the representative. Second, as explained more fully in Chapter 22, an agency generally is not liable for attorney fees in a case in which the complainant prevailed unless it was on notice that the complainant was represented.

From a practical standpoint, it is important for the complainant to file a written designation of representative because the EEO process is closed. See 29 CFR 1614.109(e). A non-party to the case who has not been designated as a representative may properly be denied access to any phase of an EEO proceeding. For example, if a representative who has not been duly designated shows up to take the deposition of an agency employee, the agency representative may refuse to allow the deposition to proceed. There is nothing that prevents a complainant from having a representative who is not the representative of record participate in an EEO case for a limited purpose, such as taking a deposition. If this is done, however, the proper thing for the complainant to do is to have the representative file a written entry of appearance with the agency and the administrative judge, with a description of what the representative will be doing in the case.

However, while filing a formal designation of representative is preferred, it is not required when the agency otherwise has knowledge of the complainant's representative. For example, in Complainant v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120130459 (2014), the Commission rejected an agency argument that the Commission should not entertain an appeal where the lawyer filing it had not filed a formal designation of representative signed by the complainant:

Referring to Complainant's appeal on complaint 1, the Agency argues that the attorney who tiled the appeal for Complainant does not have authority to represent him because the record does not contain a designation of representative form appointing him as his representative. We disagree. Complainant's attorney has represented Complainant prior to the appeals in his cases. For example. the attorney filed complaint 2 on behalf of Complainant and the Agency directed the FAD thereon to him, and actively represented Complainant in his related MSPB case. Complaints 1 and 2 are related. While a designation of representative form is preferable. we don't require such formality when there is other evidence of representation.

A complainant may designate more than one representative for his or her case. It is important for the complainant to note that, except in unusual cases, only one attorney may generally recover fees for appearance at the hearing and at any prehearing conferences or meetings. See Chapter 22, subheading "Multiple Attorneys." Furthermore, the administrative judge has the authority to regulate the conduct of the hearing. If a complainant chooses to have more than one representative, and problems arise because the representatives are filing conflicting pleadings or are otherwise creating confusion in the case, the administrative judge is within his or her discretion to place restrictions on the representatives, such as requiring that all pleadings be signed by one representative.

Selection of a representative is a right that is personal to the employee. An employee's right to choose a representative is not restricted by a collective bargaining agreement granting a union the right of exclusive representation. This is true even when the union has initiated a grievance over the same issue on which the individual employee files an EEO complaint. Before a representative can elect the forum in which to proceed, there must be a clear designation of representation. Pulido v. Department of Air Force, 01850459 (1985).

An employee is entitled, by regulation, to be represented at all stages of the complaint, including on appeal to the EEOC. There is no requirement that an employee be represented and the Commission does not have the authority to appoint a representative for an employee who cannot afford one. Allen v. USPS, 01842095 (1986).

The agency may not order an employee to select a new representative because the representative is unavailable for a scheduled counseling session. See Green v. USPS, 01830305 (1983). The employee's request to schedule the session on his representative's day off was reasonable and the refusal of the employee to designate a new representative or attend the session without representation did not warrant dismissal of the complaint.

When the agency is aware that the employee is represented by counsel, it is error for the agency to attempt informal resolution of the case over the phone without including the employee's representative. Goolsby v. USPS, 01830156 (1983). However, in the same case, the EEOC also decided that it is not error when the agency fails to notify the representative of the right to request a hearing, since that election can be made only by the employee and not his representative.

As applied to complainants represented by attorneys, it is doubtful that Goolsby is still good law. When a complainant is represented by an attorney, the agency is required to serve all official correspondence on the attorney and any time frames that begin on receipt of an agency notice do not begin until the attorney receives the notice. 29 CFR 1614.605(d). There is no similar regulatory requirement for complainants whose representatives are not attorneys.

Once an attorney representative is designated, the agency does not err when it dismisses a complaint for failure to prosecute if the attorney fails to respond to requests for information. In Ochoa v. Postmaster General, 01975240 (1998), the Commission rejected the complainant's arguments and the attorney's representations that he never represented the complainant, where the complainant had, in writing, designated the attorney as his representative.

Even when the complainant is represented by an attorney, it is the complainant who remains responsible for proceeding with the complaint. 29 CFR 1614.605(e). In simple terms, this means that neglect by the attorney is the equivalent of neglect by the complainant.
