6. Preemployment Inquiries

There is a need to balance the legitimate interests an agency may have in knowing, in advance, of a prospective employee's need for accommodation, and the possibility that awareness of such a need will make an agency reluctant to hire or promote that employee. The rule adopted by the Commission, at 29 CFR 1605.3(b)(2), is that preemployment inquiries presumptively violate Title VII, unless the employer can show that the inquiry:

(i) Did not have an exclusionary effect on its employees or prospective employees needing an accommodation for the same religious practices; or

(ii) Was otherwise justified by business necessity.

The Commission has suggested that a less intrusive means of determining whether the employee's requested work schedule can be accommodated is, without reference to religious accommodation, to inform the employee of the normal work hours and inquire if the employee is available for those hours. The employer can then, after the position is offered but prior to actually hiring the employee, inquire whether any religious accommodation is necessary and make any decisions in accordance with undue hardship guidelines. Most federal job applications make a detailed inquiry into an applicant's availability without reference to religious accommodation. Under 29 CFR 1605.3(3):

The Commission will infer that the need for an accommodation discriminatorily influenced a decision to reject an applicant when: (i) prior to an offer of employment the employer makes an inquiry into an applicant's availability without having a business necessity justification; and (ii) after the employer has determined the applicant's need for an accommodation, the employer rejects a qualified applicant. The burden is then on the employer to demonstrate that factors other than the need for an accommodation were the reasons for rejecting the qualified applicant, or that a reasonable accommodation without undue hardship was not possible.
