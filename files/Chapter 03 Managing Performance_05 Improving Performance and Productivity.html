IMPROVING PERFORMANCE AND PRODUCTIVITY

Are you developing your staff to function at peak performance and productivity levels? To get them to such levels may prove challenging, but with the right approach, it's very attainable.

You can expect to be evaluated by your agency on how well you improve performance and productivity. Are you able to turn acceptable employees into good employees, and good employees into great performers? Your ability to do this will define your reputation as a manager, both in the eyes of your employees and your superiors. Here's a good gauge -- if your weakest employee is just as good or better than the strongest employee in another unit, then your efforts to improve performance and productivity have been successful.

Performance improvement is for everybody. With tight agency budgets, you can't afford to ignore employees who are underperforming. Improving their performance is an investment in your agency's mission.

  • • Keep these principles in mind as you form your strategy to improve employee performance:
  • • Performance improvement is a process, not an event.
  • • Your own performance and the performance of every one of your employees can always improve.
  • • Each employee, even your most outstanding "self-starter," needs your personal attention and recognition.
  • • Continual performance improvement means increased unit productivity and a better bottom line and may therefore have a positive influence on your own career.
  • • If you neglect these principles, the results will likely have a negative impact:
  • • Employees lose their motivation to do the best job they can, leading to stagnant or declining productivity.
  • • The worst employees drag down the best, and they either find jobs elsewhere or give up on the mission and go through the motions.
  • • The unit's productivity and morale enter a downward spiral.
  • • Poor employee performance may adversely affect your career.

Recognition vs. negative reinforcement

Performance improvement does not depend on spotting and criticizing unacceptable performance. This approach has been out of favor with both successful business leaders and management experts for many years, and your own agency's management training is apt to warn you against it.

Recognizing those employees who deliver the performance you are seeking should be a prime tool in your performance improvement strategy. What is it? It is any action that stimulates people to do things because they get something of value in return. This reward then encourages them to repeat the behavior that earned it. It has the advantage of actually modifying behavior, so that employees become continuously improving performers.

On the other end of the spectrum is negative reinforcement, which consists of punishment for behavior you wish to stop. This may be necessary to break a pattern of declining or unacceptable performance, but by itself it does nothing to improve behavior. It simply stops undesirable behavior, when it works at all, and then only for a time.

When you must use negative reinforcement, also find balance by recognizing desired performance.

Competencies for improving performance

Good performance management requires proficiency in certain competencies. Setting goals, measuring performance against those goals, providing feedback on goal achievement and rewarding it improves performance significantly. There are a multitude of performance management competencies, but those that all managers should develop and demonstrate include:

Communicating. Establishing and maintaining effective communications with each employee not only requires good oral and written communication skills, but it also includes the ability to establish good working relationships. Providing specific positive and constructive feedback will reinforce desired work or behavior and ensure employees are clearly focused on goals. Check on them daily to make certain each employee feels connected and valued. Explain how their individual performance links to the agency mission. Employees who feel they are vital to the agency and its mission are more likely to want to improve their performance. Ideally, you want to establish an environment that promotes an open-door atmosphere, the sharing of ideas, and employee involvement in decision-making processes.

  • • Communication with employees is most effective when you:
  • • Incorporate active listening.
  • • Show respect for them and their perspectives.
  • • Ask probing questions.
  • • Provide specific positive and constructive feedback.
  • • Collaborate on solutions.
  • • Give clear instructions.

Setting goals. Setting and achieving goals can create self confidence in employees, pride in their achievement, and a willingness to accept future challenges. When goal setting is done correctly, employees strive to accomplish those goals and feel satisfied in achieving them. It also ensures that their efforts are targeted to necessary work elements, avoiding unproductive deviations. Research indicates that productivity increases in organizations that practice systematic goal setting.

When goal setting is done poorly, work does not progress as desired. Knowing how to set goals effectively is an important part of performance management and employee improvement. To do this well, you need to be able to clarify expectations and to set realistic standards and targets.

Measuring employee performance. Credible measures of performance that employees understand and accept are critical for achieving high level performance. Measuring employee accomplishments, using both qualitative and quantitative measures, provides the information that you and your employees need in order to monitor and develop strategies toward performance improvement.

Giving feedback. Feedback should inform, enlighten, and suggest improvements to employees regarding their performance. You should describe specific work-related behavior or results you have observed as close to the event as possible.

Adopt a routine that includes frequent, in-depth discussions about performance with employees at least once a quarter. These informal conversations should include open-ended questions to engage the employee in assessing their performance and for you to give them appropriate feedback. Share your observations and any performance data collected. Be clear on the purpose of this feedback which again, is to inform, enlighten, and suggest improvements where needed.

When employees receive feedback that their goals have been reached, it gives them a sense of confidence, pride, and satisfaction with their performance. Never pass up an opportunity to acknowledge that an employee achieved a goal.

Coaching and developing. Coaching is observing an employee at work and providing feedback and collaborative problem-solving to enhance performance or correct deficiencies. Using coaching skills, you can evaluate and address the developmental needs of your employees and help them select diverse experiences to gain necessary skills. Together, you and your employees create development plans that might include training, new assignments, job enrichment, self-study, or work details.

Recognizing. Being able to genuinely acknowledge improvement or a job well done is critical for strengthening employees' commitment to do their best. For some managers, giving praise and recognition is difficult. Yet various studies indicate that employees value personalized, spur-of-the moment recognition for their contributions.

When recognizing employees:

  • • Determine the most effective way to deliver it based on the needs of each individual. Some may respond better to private recognition, while others may relish an expression of appreciation in a staff meeting.
  • • Recognize them close to the time of the achievement. Waiting six months to recognize good work will lose its intended impact and may even cause resentment by your employee. For maximum effect, be timely.
  • • Establish a clear connection between accomplishments and recognition. This endorses the behavior or work you want repeated.
  • • Use your agency's performance awards program.

Recognize desired performance

Whether it's a slight improvement in performance or your employee hits a home run, your recognition will serve to reinforce the performance you desire. Recognizing desired performance is a skill you should develop. It is based on the observed psychological fact that people tend to repeat behavior that is rewarded.

But what is a reward? Basically, it is anything the recipient values. Money is certainly a reward for most people, but everyone values recognition that is done either verbally or in writing. Telling an employee their efforts paid off and they did well on a specific task will reinforce their performance, and it costs you and your agency nothing. The same holds true for written recognition. Since it is a documented acknowledgement of desired performance, it will reap even greater dividends.

In some incentive award systems, employees have come to expect that routine performance will receive inflated top annual performance ratings. These in turn will be linked to an annual monetary award. The award becomes, in effect, just part of the pay employees expect for showing up at work. It loses its link to specific behavior, which renders it ineffective in performance improvement.

A special act award for a specific achievement might be more effective, so long as it was genuinely deserved. A medal or other non-monetary award -- even a coffee cup with the agency logo for an appropriate achievement -- might also be effective.

Recognition. On a day-to-day basis, the most important reward in your employee performance improvement strategy is recognition, by you, by coworkers, and by others as appropriate. In a way, any form of reward works because it is a form of recognition by others that the employee's performance is superior -- or even simply improving.

How does it work? To give your employees recognition for the desired performance -- to encourage them to repeat and improve it -- follow these steps:

  1. 1. Know their jobs. Find out what your employees are supposed to do. This may be difficult if you are new to your position and you have not done -- and may not be qualified to do -- some of the jobs of the people you supervise. But you need to try. Your own employees may be your best source of information, especially if they've been around for a while. Most people like to talk about what they do for a living to someone who really wants to know -- especially their boss.
  2. 2. Know their performance. Learn what they are actually doing and how well they are doing it. You don't want to micromanage, but you do need the information. In some cases, automated tracking systems may be in place, or may be introduced, but these aren't always possible or appropriate. You may have to read detailed reports, follow up with customers, and ask for specific input. "No news is good news" is not a motto to follow.
  3. 3. Let them know you know. Find ways of communicating your knowledge through your work assignments, guidance, and feedback. Every work-related conversation you have with your employees should be, in some sense, a discussion of performance.

Once you have the information you need, the next step is to recognize desired performance, the kind you want repeated. General acknowledgements that folks are "doing a great job" will not do the trick. Remember what we've just said. Your employees need to know that you know what they are doing. That means your recognition needs to be specific.

Instead of saying "you're doing a great job," say something along the lines of "the financial section of the report you turned in on Friday was very thorough! Those figures really helped me in my meeting with the committee this morning. I think we're going to get what we need." What does this sort of recognition do? It tells the employee:

  • • What specific behavior is being rewarded by recognition (and therefore should be repeated).
  • • Why it is being rewarded.
  • • How it is connected with the larger picture of the unit's work.

Also notice that this feedback is timely. Feedback given soon after the achievement makes the recipient understand that you made the effort to evaluate that report very quickly, because it was important to you and the unit. Also, the recipient still remembers what was done to create that particular section of the report and knows what sort of behavior produced the reward.

If you do it this way, your employees will not only understand what they have done to get your recognition, they will understand why their behavior was recognized, and they will be more alert to new opportunities to do the same sort of thing -- opportunities they may recognize before you do.

When you set up a recognition practice like this -- and you give regular feedback -- you can lift the morale and productivity of your unit within a short time. And it won't cost your agency a cent. A good formal awards program can add something positive to the mix, but your success always depends on your personal recognition of your employees' good or improved performances.

Recognize all improvements

A performance improvement strategy based on positive recognition in conjunction with your agency's performance appraisal system and formal awards programs is the best way to make sure good employee performance stays at a high level and keeps on getting better.

But what about those employees who are not your best performers? Will recognition work with them as well? Generally, it will. Even you can overcome your hesitation to praise work that is far from perfect.

Some of your lower performers are simply new. We all understand this. New employees need training, careful instruction, mentoring or performance coaching, developmental assignments, and closer supervision than more experienced employees. When they do things right -- or even almost right -- these modest steps should receive appropriate recognition. If your employee onboarding program works well, this positive reinforcement should be built into it.

Even more experienced employees who are not performing well -- if they are at all capable of or willing to perform acceptably -- will benefit from recognition whenever they do something right, or almost right, or at least better than what they did the last time.

But don't overdo it. It is appropriate to tell an employee who finally wrote an acceptable report and got it in by the deadline that you have noticed the improvement, but recognition in a staff meeting or an employee-of-the-month award would be going too far. It would not allow room for further improvement and would only annoy the employee's better-performing coworkers.

Performance coaching

As a federal manager, you wear many hats and one of them is that of coach. Performance coaching is a problem-solving mechanism that is part of the daily interaction you have with your employees. It is intended to improve individual performances. In addition to providing positive feedback about performance, you also discuss performance issues needing attention and collaborate on steps to fix them.

This approach encourages employees to own their performance. It is not a technique where you, as manager, discover problems, direct the employee to correct them, and provide ways to go about it. You set specific performance goals in a cooperative manner rather than a dictatorial one. Think of it as a way to guide your employees in a way that will maximize their performance. It also gives you a platform to build trust in your leadership abilities with your work unit.

The most effective coaching techniques don't provide the answers; they provide a process for helping the employee discover the answers. In this way, the employee is more likely to own and buy into the solution.

Other benefits:

  • • Improved productivity and employee skill levels. Employees learn how to work and discover new skills in the process.
  • • Improved morale. A collaborative approach conveys the ownership aspect for your employees and inspires them to want to raise their performance level.
  • • Improved performance communication. During the course of the year, you are communicating and addressing performance issues with your employees. There should be no surprises during the annual performance appraisal.
  • • Improved retention rates. When employees feel comfortable that their manager is seeking to improve their performance rather than blame them for shortcomings, retention rates increase.
  • • Improved leadership skills. The more you coach to draw out improved performances, the more you hone your own skills as an effective leader.

Is it perpetual? While it can be argued that performance coaching is an ongoing process, logic tells us that it should decline as employees gain experience and their development reaches a level requiring less coaching.

This does not mean that experienced employees do not need performance coaching. Each situation is unique. It is obvious that new employees will need more attention, but those who transfer to your unit, with years of agency experience, may need coaching during the acclimation process.

The goal of coaching is to improve the work of your employees, which will impact the performance of your work unit and agency. So, if you're presented a performance problem by one of your more experienced employees, it is always appropriate to incorporate coaching to find a resolution.

If your agency doesn't already have a performance coaching program available to you, discuss with your boss to either start one or send you to training to learn the finer points. It will benefit your organization and improve the performance and productivity of your employees.

Improving productivity

Every manager has a diverse mixture of employee productivity levels within their work units. Some employees are content that their work is being produced at minimum compliance levels. Some employees strive to produce above and beyond minimum goals while others struggle to raise their performance to acceptable productivity levels. Regardless of the type of employees comprising your work unit, productivity, like performance, can always improve.

The work your unit produces is a direct reflection of you. Results are expected by your boss and the public and low productivity may not be the complete fault of your employees. Have you done all you can do to ensure efficiency and productivity?

Productivity improvement is usually best accomplished by a mixture of tactics driven by the work environment and the skills of your employees. In addition to techniques such as recognition, consider other ways to improve productivity.

  • • Examine processes. Your first step is to evaluate whether you or agency operational processes are impeding productivity. Are instructions from department or agency leaders reaching your unit? Are they clear? Do certain agency policies paralyze productivity? Do your own pet practices hinder the production of your unit? Is there an operational process that always seems to cause problems? Examine these processes and make the changes within your authority. Send the others up the chain for examination.
  • • Poll staff. It's the most basic approach, but also the most effective. You may not be aware of how your staff can improve their efficiency or productivity until you poll them. Schedule annual staff meetings to brainstorm ways productivity can be improved and explore each suggestion. For the ideas that are most viable, you may want to delegate further research to staff members. Be sure to follow up on their suggestions and implement those that show the most promise.
  • • Maintain accountability. Make sure everyone understands that they are accountable for achieving results that support the agency's goals and objectives. Be clear that excuses such as "I tried my best" are not acceptable in light of realistic goals. Productivity is goal-driven and if you do not tolerate excuses for unattained results, then your staff will adopt an appropriate work ethic. Keep in mind that for your employees to buy into your accountability requirement, goals must be realistic and attainable.
  • • Alternate work sites. Many employees are able to produce more work at higher quality levels if they can focus on the task at hand, uninterrupted. The office setting may not enable such an environment. Consider alternate work sites or a telework option. Depending on the employee, either may improve productivity.
  • • Upgrade equipment. Insufficient or outdated equipment may hinder productivity. This is especially true with electronics. Be sure your employees have the best equipment and current electronic devices to perform their jobs. Equipment that breaks down or causes access or connectivity issues will result in work stoppages -- not good for productivity.
  • • Encourage training. Perhaps staff can benefit from different skill sets to improve their productivity. Provide training courses or learning opportunities to your employees so they can broaden their skills and apply the training to their jobs. Cross-training is another area you should consider. Training your employees in other areas will not only enhance their skills, but they can also pitch in when workloads become too heavy in those other areas.
  • • Rotate assignments. Everyone can get bored with their core duties. If you can, consider rotating assignments at regular intervals. This will keep employees fresh, expose them to other tasks, and give them a broader understanding of agency operations -- all good for productivity.
  • • Form teams. The old saying that "two heads are better than one" is true. Teamwork increases productivity since more employees are putting their minds to work to generate more ideas. It can also create a competitive environment thereby bringing out the best in your employees.
  • • Monitor goals. Simply setting deadlines and targets for your employees is not enough. Perhaps the most important action you can take to improve productivity is to follow up and regularly monitor the goals you set. This keeps productivity on track, lets your employees know that you're involved, and it gives you an opportunity to adjust goals as change dictates. Do this without the temptation to micromanage, and you will see an improvement in productivity.

Mistakes to avoid

Improving employee performance and productivity should be a part of your daily activities, whether planned or unplanned. Simply handing an employee a set of written standards, then handing them a list of their deficiencies at the end of the performance period, is neither fair nor effective. Such a strategy is likely to invite rating appeals. When done properly, you can transform employee performance. To keep moving forward, avoid the following mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Dismiss the value of performance coaching. This daily interaction connects you to your employees and establishes a helping, collaborative work environment.
  • • Wait for problems to go away. Providing timely constructive feedback and performance coaching enables you to identify shortcomings with your employees and discuss ways to improve their performance. Problems may get worse if you wait for them to go away on their own.
  • • Pass up an opportunity to recognize. Recognizing good or improved work is the linchpin to improving performance. Never pass up an opportunity to let your employees know when they've done something right.
  • • Provide partial support to employees. You must fully invest. It is up to you to do everything reasonable to help employees perform as well as they can, whether they are top performers, performance problems, or somewhere in between.
  • • Neglect your own improvement. When you actively pursue your own performance improvement, you sharpen or add to your skill set. Your work unit and agency benefit and you also improve your prospects for career advancement.

Ask FMG

Q. Should I recognize an employee when performance is not outstanding?

A. Yes. Recognize improvement, even if an employee still has a long way to go. Improvement, after all, is what you want to see, the behavior you want repeated, and recognition does that. Just don't overdo it. An employee who finally manages to get a report halfway right and in by the deadline should get a "yes, that's getting closer to what I'm looking for," not an employee-of-the-month award.

Q. I tell my employees they are doing a great job all the time, but they don't seem to care much what I say. Am I doing something wrong?

A. Probably. There are two likely explanations:

  1. 1. They may not be doing a great job and know it. When you praise them, they know that you have no clue about their job and what really constitutes great work.
  2. 2. Or they may be doing a great job, but general praise doesn't really tell them you know what they are doing or that you care. They need specific, timely recognition for their achievements. That's the kind of meaningful recognition that works.

Q. I have an employee who is performing acceptably, except that they keep missing deadlines. Is the employee a lost cause?

A. Don't be too quick to cast your employee aside. There are a number of possibilities you should look into and do something about as appropriate. To name some, your employee may:

  • • Need time-management training.
  • • Not understand your expectations and priorities.
  • • Be overloaded.
  • • Be caught up in poor workflow design.
  • • Suffer from poor coworker cooperation.
  • • Be a victim of harassment or other workplace difficulties.
  • • Have a medical impairment, and perhaps require a reasonable accommodation of a disability.
  • • Be distracted by personal problems.

Q. I have an employee who makes all their deadlines, but their work reflects only the bare minimum needed for an acceptable rating. I know the employee is capable of much more. What should I do?

A. Your employee may not have made the connection between the importance of their job and the big picture of your agency. Be sure your employee understands how their individual performance contributes directly to the agency mission. Once your employee gains that line of sight, they will become more engaged and operate with a sense of ownership.

Q. I really don't have time to spend with each employee when performance issues surface. Instead I keep a very detailed file of their issues which I share with them during the annual rating process. Is there any problem with my method?

A. Plenty. Besides setting yourself up for a constant flood of rating appeals, you're doing nothing to improve performance. You need to change your management style. You can start by using feedback and performance coaching. During the course of the year, you should communicate and address performance issues with your employees as they occur. This will improve their performance and ultimately make your job easier. There should no surprises during the annual performance appraisal.

Q. For the employee who has improved and maintained their performance, can I give them more recognition than others who don't perform as well? If so, isn't that discriminatory?

A. Yes, it is discriminatory. But it's OK to discriminate on the basis of performance. If some people do better work than others, then they should get the recognition. They should be allowed more flexibility in their schedules and get first choice when interesting new projects arise. They will have earned these advantages because they make a more valuable contribution to your agency.
