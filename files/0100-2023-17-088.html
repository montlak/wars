broida eeo Chapter 08: EEOC Appeals Chapter 08: EEOC Appeals, Subchapter 07: Review of Sanctions, Section F: Sanctions Against Complainants, Subsection 06: Failure to Comply With AJ's Orders/Requests/instructions, Part a: Hearing Dismissal Upheld
a. Hearing Dismissal Upheld

The harshest sanction usually imposed on complainants for failure to comply is a cancelation of the hearing request and a remand to the agency to issue a final decision on the existing record. See, e.g., Complainant v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120140776 (2015) (AJ did not abuse his discretion in canceling the hearing and remanding the complaint for a final agency decision where complainant failed to comply with an order to present the agency with a written settlement demand); Dotson v. Postmaster General, 01A40291 (2005) (sanction of cancelation and remand appropriate where complainant did not establish good cause for missing prehearing conference); Kelley v. Secretary of Defense, 01A45412 (2005) (cancelation and remand appropriate where complainant failed to submit proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law, proposed witness list and list of damages). The Commission in Sheila O. v. Secretary of the Army, 2021002224 (2022), wrote:

[W]e have recently reaffirmed that there must be a showing that Complainant either willfully disobeyed the AJ's orders or unjustifiably failed to respond to those orders to warrant dismissal of the hearing request as a sanction. Nina H. v. Dep't of Labor, EEOC Appeal No. 2021000962 (Mar. 15, 2022); Clarine L. v. Dep't of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 2020004391 (Feb. 8, 2022); Wilma B. v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 2020003672 (Dec. 2, 2021). See also e.g. Linda D. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 2019004909 (Sept. 23, 2020) (the complainant's failure to respond to the AJ's orders impugns the integrity of the EEO process as a whole).

The complainant's failure in Sheila O. to respond to the AJ's acknowledgment and show cause orders rose to that level:

Factors pertinent to "tailoring" a sanction, or determining whether a sanction is even warranted, include: (1) the extent and nature of the non-compliance, including the justification presented by the non-complying party; (2) the prejudicial effect of the non-compliance on the opposing party; (3) the consequences resulting from the delay in justice, if any; (4) the number of times the party has engaged in such conduct; and (5) the effect on the integrity of the EEO process as a whole. Id.

In applying the first and fourth factors, we find that Complainant's non-compliance consisted of failing to submit her PCI in accordance with the AJ's acknowledgement order and her failure to respond to the AJ's show-cause order. Rather than try to justify her non-compliance, Complainant tried to argue after-the-fact that neither she nor her representative received a copy of the September 18, 2020 AO. However, none of the orders issued by the AJ in this matter were ever returned as undeliverable. Complainant's contention that she never received those orders despite providing that same email address several times throughout the process appears disingenuous. As to her representative, Complainant did not formally designate her as the representative until December 7, 2020. Therefore, the AJ could not have provided the representative a copy of the AO in September 2020.

The record reveals that Complainant's representative did in fact sign the hearing request form; however, the form contained only Complainant's email address and no contact information for the representative.

In considering the second and third factors, we find Complainant's failure to respond to the AJ's acknowledgement order and order to show cause effectively halted the proceeding and prevented the Agency from engaging in the discovery process or otherwise moving forward. Finally, in taking the fifth factor into account, we have recently reaffirmed that there must be a showing that Complainant either willfully disobeyed the AJ's orders or unjustifiably failed to respond to those orders to warrant dismissal of the hearing request as a sanction. Nina H. v. Dep't of Labor, EEOC Appeal No. 2021000962 (Mar. 15, 2022); Clarine L. v. Dep't of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 2020004391 (Feb. 8, 2022); Wilma B. v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 2020003672 (Dec. 2, 2021). See also e.g. Linda D. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 2019004909 (Sept. 23, 2020) (the complainant's failure to respond to the AJ's orders impugns the integrity of the EEO process as a whole). We agree with the AJ that there is no justification for Complainant's failure to respond to her AO and show cause orders. Consequently, the AJ's dismissal of Complainant's hearing request as a sanction was reasonable and well within the bounds of her discretion. We now move on to the merits of Complainant's appeal.

The Commission in Paul F. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2019005369 (2021), provided a good summary of the circumstances that would warrant hearing dismissal:

We have consistently held in recent decisions that dismissal of a hearing request as a sanction is only appropriate in extreme circumstances. One such circumstance is when the complainant engages in contumacious conduct, not merely negligence. Cassey B. v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 2019004838 (Sept. 24, 2020); Cecile T. v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 2019002373 (Sept. 22, 2020); Carolyn M. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 2019004843 (March 10, 2020). Examples of contumacious conduct warranting dismissal of hearing requests include: Charlie K. v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 2019002293 (Sept. 22, 2020) (failure to provide investigative affidavit during agency investigation and failure to provide answers to interrogatories during discovery despite being granted multiple extensions in both phases of the proceeding, as well as failure to appear at pre-hearing conference); and Cleo S. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120181406 (Feb. 28, 2020) (failure to participate in email communications being sought by the Agency and to produce documentation ordered by AJ in a manner demonstrating disregard for administrative process and unwillingness to comply with AJ's orders despite warning of consequences). Absent a showing of contumacious conduct, hearing requests may be dismissed where the complainant fails to pursue his or her claim with due diligence. Alice S. v. Soc. Sec. Admin., EEOC Appeal No. 2019002475 (Sept. 22, 2020) (failure to respond to emails from AJ that included initial conference order and order to show cause due to overlooking those emails); Robert A. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120182698 (Feb. 21, 2020) (failure to respond to order to show cause despite having received order from AJ via email, and failure to provide evidence that he was incapacitated and unable to comply with the order). In other words, there must be a showing that Complainant either willfully disobeyed the AJ's orders or unreasonably failed to respond to those orders in order to justify dismissal of the hearing request as a sanction. In the absence of either circumstance, we cannot as a matter of law find that the integrity of the administrative EEO complaints process had been so compromised as to warrant the most severe sanction.

Cancelation of the hearing and remand to the agency for a final decision on the record was deemed an appropriate sanction where the administrative judge granted the complainant two extensions to either retain an attorney to proceed or elect a final agency decision. After the last extension, the complainant failed to respond to the request of the judge to provide, within 15 days, a report from the complainant's physician addressing his medical and emotional ability to proceed with the hearing or written verification that the complainant had retained an attorney to proceed. See Reis v. Postmaster General, 01982617 (2001).

In Heckstall v. Postmaster General, 01A20172 (2002), the Commission found that dismissal of the complaint was too harsh of a sanction where the complainant, on appeal, presented evidence that he suffered from an "anxiety disorder" and maintained he was in a psychiatric unit during the prehearing conference that he missed. The Commission determined that cancelation of the hearing and remand for a final agency decision was the appropriate sanction. Given the circumstances, even that sanction seems overly harsh but may have been premised on the failure of the complainant to produce evidence, beyond his own assertion, that he was actually hospitalized at the time of the conference.

Cancelation of the hearing and remand to the agency for a decision on the record was also an appropriate sanction where the complainant failed to respond to the judge's order to submit a prehearing statement. Although prior to the time the statement was due, the complainant's father died and she had to leave the country, she did not convey this information to the judge or otherwise seek an extension of the due date. See Patel v. Postmaster General, 01994440 (2002). A similar sanction was imposed in Oshiver v. Secretary of Interior, 01A04959 (2002), where the complainant failed to show good cause in responding to a judge's order to explain why her medication rendered her unable to testify at the hearing but not unable to report to work. In Turner v. Secretary of Navy, 01A23074 (2003), the Commission approved the sanction of cancelation of the hearing and remand for a final agency decision where the complainant and his counsel submitted inadequate prehearing statements. The administrative judge specifically instructed the complainant on what was required in the prehearing statements after the initial statement was deemed inadequate. See also Byers v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120082542 (2008) (when judge did not rule on complainant's motion for extension to file prehearing statement, counsel should have assumed motion was denied).

In Broughton v. Secretary of Energy, 01A14837 (2002), the Commission did uphold dismissal of the complaint as a sanction where the complainant requested a continuance because medication he was taking affected his ability to concentrate. After the hearing was rescheduled, the administrative judge instructed the complainant to consult with his doctor on whether he could proceed. The complainant did not contact the judge until the evening before the hearing and, the next day, showed up unprepared, without his hearing materials and stated he was unable to proceed. Finally, the complainant did not respond to the judge's order to show cause why his complaint should not be dismissed.
