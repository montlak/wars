L. PREMATURE COMPLAINTS

An agency improperly dismisses an EEO complaint on the basis that counseling was not timely initiated where the employee contacts a counselor prior to the effective date of the action, but the agency never issues a notice of final interview. Newton v. Secretary of Army, 01944889 (1994). In Newton, the complainant initiated counseling over a proposed reassignment. The complainant was advised that her complaint could not be accepted because it was premature. The Commission found that since the agency had not issued a notice of final interview, counseling was still ongoing at the time the reassignment became effective. The Commission further noted the agency's decision to dismiss the complaint over the reassignment was premature itself because a formal complaint had not been filed.

Likewise, in Siegel v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05960568 (1997), the Commission found that the complainant's premature counselor contact prior to the time a nonselection occurred, constituted timely counselor contact when the nonselection later did take place:

Appellant alleged in his complaint that he was not selected for the position of chaplain services chief because of his age and because he is Jewish. However, at the time appellant sought counseling, no selection had been made. Appellant's claim therefore did not mature until the date that the agency actually filled the position on a permanent basis. We therefore agree with the agency that appellant's complaint was prematurely filed.

However, we disagree that appellant's complaint should be dismissed for untimely counselor contact, as the agency argues in its response to appellant's request for reconsideration. The Commission has the discretion to extend the 45-day time limit for contacting an EEO counselor for reasons that it considers sufficient. 29 CFR § 1614.105(a)(2). Where an individual received EEO counseling on a proposed action and the agency ultimately carries out the proposed action, the otherwise premature allegation merges with the effectuated action. Rose v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05950240 (July 27, 1995); Charles v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05910190 (February 25, 1991). Appellant did not sit on his rights. Rather, he contacted a counselor as soon as he began to suspect that he was being discriminated against. Since the nonselection that appellant contemplated actually took place, that action merges with his allegation of discriminatory nonselection. We will therefore order the agency to process appellant's allegation that he was not selected for the position of chief of the chaplain services because of his age and religion.

The Commission has reached a similar result in a case where the complainant's initial allegations of discrimination were untimely, but a third event occurred during counseling. In Charles v. Postmaster General, 01A55742 (2006), the complainant' requests for a limited duty assignment were denied in March 2003 and December 2004. He did not initiate counseling until late February 2005. During counseling, he made a third request for a limited duty assignment, which was denied. He did not, however, initiate counseling over the third denial. The Commission held that because the complainant believed the discrimination was ongoing, "he cannot be expected to seek counseling on this third denial of Limited Duty Status because he viewed it as part of the same incident." As a result, the Commission found that the complainant reasonably anticipated the third denial would be part of the complaint pending in counseling.
