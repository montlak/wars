4. Repeated Incidents

Though generally uncivil conduct that lasts for a short period of time will not rise to the level of creating a hostile environment, in Edwards v. Secretary of Defense, 01A32863 (2003), the Commission found that name-calling and insulting remarks that lasted for a two-week period, along with allegations that coworkers threw trash into areas the complainant just finished cleaning and that he was required to continue performing his work assignment even though it caused him pain, was sufficient to state a hostile environment claim.

However, alleged comments about "taking a knee," the Black Lives Matter movement, repeated references to "you people" and "you all" were not so inappropriate or racially charged to rise to the level of harassment in Jacqueline L. v. Secretary of Defense, 2019001449 (2020). The complainant alleged the following incidents as harassment:

1. On one occasion, A1, another supervisor who was a friend of Complainant's first line supervisor, S1, allegedly stated to Complainant: "You people," in the presence of S1.

2. A coworker, C1, allegedly used racially offensive language, including an incident when he allegedly stated: "I took a knee," and on other occasions talked about the Black Lives Matter movement, and the removal of the confederate monuments.

3. S1 allegedly stopped just short of Complainant's cubicle, and allegedly stated: "Now that I know what you are all capable of, the beatings will continue"; and, on October 4, 2017, he stated that the problem is that Complainant does not respect his authority.

Finding no discrimination, the Commission considered the context in which each alleged comment was made. First, the supervisor denied having said "you people." As to allegation 2, after the complainant complained about the coworkers racial insensitivity, the supervisor did send an email requesting all employees to be "respectful of the political and social climate within the workplace." As to allegation 3, the supervisor acknowledged having said that the beating would continue, but explained that it was not directed at the complainant or racially charged as it was a reference to Mutiny on the Bounty.

However, in Rusty C. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 2019005662 (2020), the Commission had little trouble finding that repeated comments to "speak English" among other harassing behavior created a hostile work environment for the complainant:

We find that Complainant belongs to a statutorily protected class based on his race, and that he was subjected to unwelcome verbal and physical conduct due to his race. For example, W1 witnessed CW refusing to open the gate for Complainant, claiming that he did not understand Complainant. W1 also stated that CW "constantly invades [Complainant's] personal space," and when Complainant requests that CW back off, CW responds that he cannot understand Complainant, and states that he should "learn to speak English."

The Commission has found that comments about a complainant's English language proficiency reveal a discriminatory motive. See Genny L. v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122795 (Feb. 23, 2016) (citations omitted).

Another witness (W2) stated that he saw CW invade Complainant's personal space, on several occasions, while stating "speak English," even though Complainant was "being very clear." W2 noted that he witnessed the events that occurred on April 26, May 17, June 10, June 13, and June 20, 2016. A third witness (W3) stated that he saw CW had "physically pinned" Complainant, while yelling, "learn to speak English! What are you doing in this country? This is America--speak English!" W3 stated that he intervened and convinced CW to leave.

In assessing whether the Complainant has set forth an actionable claim of harassment, the conduct at issue must be viewed in the context of the totality of the circumstances, considering, inter alia, the nature and frequency of offensive encounters and the span of time over which the encounters occurred. See 29 C.F.R. § 1604.11(b); EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment, N-915-050, No. 137 (Mar. 19, 1990); Cobb v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05970077 (Mar. 13, 1997). Generally, "simple teasing, offhand comments, and isolated incidents (unless extremely serious) will not amount to discriminatory changes in the 'terms and conditions of employment." Kozak v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A63021 (Aug. 23, 2006); Battle v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0120083387 (Feb. 4, 2010). Such conduct "must be both objectively and subjectively offensive, [such] that a reasonable person would find [the work environment to be] hostile or abusive, and...that the victim in fact did perceive to be so." Id.

In looking at the totality of the circumstances, we find that the complained of incidents rise to the level of a hostile work environment. Specifically, we find that CW's repeated disparagement of Complainant's English-language skills, and his invasion of Complainant's personal space, including a time when CW "physically pinned" him, were severe and pervasive. In addition, the record contains evidence that CW's conduct unreasonably interfered with Complainant's work performance and created an intimidating, hostile, and offensive work environment. W3 noted that CW "terrorized" Complainant, and that the "constant intervention of his coworkers was necessary to keep [Complainant] safe and able to perform his duties."

In the case of co-worker harassment, an agency is responsible for acts of harassment in the workplace where the agency (or its agents) knew or should have known of the conduct, unless it can be shown that it took immediate and appropriate corrective action. See Enforcement Guidance: Vicarious Liability for Unlawful Harassment by Supervisors, No. 915.002 (June 18, 1999). Here, S1 stated that learned of Complainant's claim of a hostile work environment based on his race after Complainant filed the instant EEO complaint. However, we find that S1 should have known of the harassing conduct based on the repeated reports from Complainant and his coworkers.

The record shows that witnesses corroborated that Complainant immediately reported the incidents to S1. For example, W1 stated that he was there when Complainant called S1 to report CW's harassment on May 17, and June 10, 2016.

W1 stated that he heard Complainant inform S1 that CW was "making fun of his Asian heritage," and that CW was "harassing him still and it's all because of his race." Regarding the May 17, 2016 call, W1 noted that S1 responded that Complainant should "try to stay away from" CW. W2 stated that Complainant reported many of the incidents to S1, and that Complainant stated that these events occurred due to his race.

W3 stated that after the incident in which he intervened (when CW allegedly pinned Complainant and yelled "Learn to speak English!"), Complainant immediately called S1 to report it "word for word." Another witness (W4) stated that he recalled an incident when CW lined up Chinese food takeout containers in the breakroom, which remained for a few days. W4 stated that he personally reported the incident to S1, stating that it was an attack on Complainant "as an Asian person." W4 stated that he did not recall the exact date, but that it was prior to Complainant filing his formal EEO complaint. While S1 asserted that he was not aware of Complainant's harassment allegation until he learned of his EEO complaint, we find that S1 should have known based on the repeated attempts to inform him that CW's conduct towards Complainant was racially motivated.

An agency can raise an affirmative defense when it shows that it took immediate and appropriate corrective action. Id. What is appropriate remedial action will necessarily depend on the particular facts of the case, such as the severity and persistence of the harassment and the effectiveness of any initial remedial steps. See Taylor v. Dep't Of Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05920194 (July 8, 1992). In this case, the Agency 1) added a camera surveillance system; (2) detailed police officers to patrol; (3) moved S1's office to better supervise CW; and (4) offered to change Complainant's shift, which the AJ noted that Complainant rejected "without participating in the interactive process."

However, we find that these actions were not appropriately corrective. CES noted that CW would "circle back" after the police left to "surprise and intimidate" Complainant, and that CW "does this outside of the coverage areas of the security cameras." In addition, S1 stated that when he was in the vicinity with CW and Complainant, CW was on "his best behavior," and it appeared that CW simply waited for a time when there were no witnesses to his conduct.

S1 also stated that "a lot of the confrontations" occurred in the locker room, and that there were no security cameras installed in the locker room. S1 added that the police were there "as often as they could be," approximately 7 or 8, out of the 10 times that they were supposed to be there; and that while CW was well behaved "most of the time" in the presence of the police officers, CW would occasionally raise his voice and the officers would tell him to calm down.

Regarding the offer to change Complainant's shift, which Complainant rejected, we find that it cannot be considered an effective remedial action because it was not voluntary. See Complainant v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01985285 (June 28, 2001). We further note that, unlike where there has been a request for reasonable accommodation for a disability, in the case of harassment there is no requirement for the employee to engage in an "interactive process." It is the Agency's responsibility to take immediate and appropriate corrective action to address harassing conduct.
