6. Disparate Treatment

Just as with circumstantial evidence in intentional discrimination cases discussed in Chapter 10, disparate treatment is a commonly used as a form of evidence to demonstrate pretext. A good example is provided by McFadden v. Attorney General, 0720110034 (2012):

Complainant has demonstrated that the Agency's articulated reason is unworthy of belief because S2 took action to ensure that Complainant's comments were investigated, and did not take similar action regarding the profane comments of CW2 and CW3 (both Caucasian). The Agency argued that S2 was justified in distinguishing Complainant's comments because they were "racially tainted." The AJ rejected this argument. We agree with the AJ. The Agency, according to its own policy statement cited by management in this case, is required to investigate all profane, obscene, and otherwise abusive language. S2 was notified on at least two occasions that other employees, CW2 and CW3, used profanity. CW2's statement was recorded in the memorandum S1 provided concerning Complainant. S2 declined to investigate either. In fact, with respect to CW3's actions, S2 testified:

[CW1] came to my office and stated [CW3] was talking about her--saying [CW1]--on the telephone, and that if she was going to talk to--[CW3] was going to talk about her, she needed to get her facts straight. Those two continued to have a disagreement that day. By the end of the day [CWI] was very agitated. I went down to her office and walked into her office and she said--you know. "She's cursing at me and saying things about me and I'm tired of it and I have had enough of it." And she was very emotional, and she was calling [CW3] names. She was stating that if [CW3] didn't keep her mouth shut that she would shut it for her, and making those types of statements. And 1 tried to calm her down I was trying to tell her, you know, "Just relax, let it go. You know, if [CW3] is saying things that are inappropriate, I will talk with her about it."

S2's testimony demonstrates that she was aware other employees were using profanity to the point where it disturbed the workplace. However, S2 took no action, except with respect to Complainant's statement. Moreover, as argued by Complainant on appeal, the justification proffered by the Agency in its final decision and on appeal concerning the "racial" aspect of Complainant's comment was not mentioned by any of the management witnesses in this case to justify the actions taken against Complainant. In sum, the record provides substantial evidence to support the AJ's determination that the Agency retaliated against Complainant when it initiated an investigation into her comments, and did not investigate similar profanity by other employees.

Evidence that a supervisor singled an employee out for scrutiny can be relevant to showing retaliation for protected activity. See Thomas v. Secretary of Air Force, 01954054 (1996) (evidence of heightened scrutiny plus questionable comments by a supervisor are sufficient to show pretext).

An employee does not have the right to engage in disruptive activities, such as advocating a work stoppage, even in response to agency actions that the employee believes to be contrary to law. The agency, cannot, however, single out a white employee as a "leader" of such disruption when the evidence clearly demonstrates that several black employees engaged in the same or similar conduct. See Benston v. USPS, 01820581 (1983) ("There is nothing in Title VII that compels an employer to absolve an employee for his participation in illegal or disruptive actions against the employer unless he is a member of a protected group and received less favorable treatment than others"). Properly analyzed, a case involving the situation in Benston is less a reprisal case than it is a case of discrimination based on race and color.
