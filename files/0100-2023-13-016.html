A. UNCLEAR ALLEGATIONS

Because many complainants proceed pro se, and because many EEO investigators are not adequately trained, one of the biggest areas of dispute in the investigative process is whether a complainant is raising factual information as evidence of discrimination, or as an actual incident of discrimination for which relief is requested. To the extent that the investigator acts as an arbiter of the information that is included in the report of investigation, the debate is not an academic one. For example, in a case involving a specific failure to promote, the complainant may raise factual information about past promotions. It may not be clear to the investigator whether the complainant is raising that information because he or she now wishes to seek relief for those past incidents of alleged discrimination, or because the complainant believes that information is somehow relevant to the contested selection.

The distinction is critical because it is determinative of how that information should ultimately be used in resolving the case. But in either instance, it is improper for the investigator to summarily reject such information. It is error for an agency to reject matters for investigation on the premise that they have not been raised with an EEO counselor where it is unclear whether the complainant is raising the matters for investigation or as evidence of discrimination. In the past, if the matters were intended as additional allegations on which relief could be based and had not been raised with a counselor, the proper course was remand to the counselor. This was the case even if the allegations on their face appeared to be untimely because it was premature for an agency to dismiss a complaint which had not been through precomplaint counseling. McCoy v. USPS, 01830809 (1983). If the matters were intended to be evidence of discrimination, they were required to be referred for investigation, and the investigator would determine if the proffered evidence was relevant to the complaint. Id.

This process changed under the 1999 version of Part 1614. Now, if the additional allegations are like or related to the complaint under investigation, then the complainant should request that the oriGINAl complaint be amended. 29 CFR 1614.106(d). The agency must accept the amendment. Of course, amendments, like any other type of complaint, can be dismissed for the reasons set forth in 29 CFR 1614.107(b), as discussed in Chapter 3 under the subheading "Substantive Amendments." New claims that arise during the investigation that are not like or related to the oriGINAl claim or claims of discrimination should be consolidated with the oriGINAl complaint to be processed together after counseling. Whether amended to or consolidated with the underlying complaint, new claims are investigated in the same manner as the oriGINAl claims. A factual record should be created from which legal conclusions may later be drawn. If later claims are raised, the investigation must be completed within 180 days of the later claims, or within 360 days of the date the oriGINAl complaint was filed, whichever comes sooner. 29 CFR 1614.106(e)(2); 1614.606. Time frames for investigating complaints are discussed above in the section on "Investigative Procedures."

When the agency notifies a complainant of acceptance of the complaint, it also should specifically define the claims that have been accepted for investigation and permit the complainant an opportunity to dispute the agency's definition of the claims. It violates the spirit of cooperation between the parties for an agency to deny a request for a 10-day extension in order to respond to the definition of claims. Sternberg v. Department of Defense Dependent Schools, 01881922 (1988).

Agencies zealously try to limit investigations to the specific claims accepted in the formal complaint. However, the Commission has stated that "the initial charge in any complaint is merely a starting point for a reasonable investigation and that the investigation may ultimately include the development of other issues that are discovered during the course of the investigation." Bachman v. Secretary of Air Force, 05920014 (1992) (agency should have investigated an involuntary resignation issue where a complainant resigned after the initiation of the complaint process).

When a complaint alleges disability discrimination but fails to identify the disability in the formal complaint, the agency should accept the case for investigation if it states a claim. The agency may then determine the specific disability claimed during the investigation. If the complainant cannot sufficiently define the disability during the investigation stage, then the agency can issue a final decision on the merits of the claim, finding the complainant has failed to establish a prima facie case of disability discrimination, provided the complainant has requested a final agency decision rather than a hearing before an administrative judge. Torres v. Postmaster General, 01983394 (1999). In cases where the complainant has requested a hearing, in a scenario such as Torres the agency should consider a motion for summary judgment.
