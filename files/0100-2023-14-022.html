D. WAIVER OF RIGHTS

In virtually every settlement agreement, the complainant is required to waive some right. Indeed, there would be very little incentive for an agency to settle if the complainant did not at least waive his or her right to proceed any further with the complaint being settled. Indeed, unless the agency has extracted some other promise from the complainant, a settlement agreement that did not waive the complainant's right to pursue the complaint that is the subject of the agreement would fail for lack of consideration. Most of the problems regarding waiver of rights in settlement agreements relate to the waiver of future rights, or the waiver of rights in some other forum.

Though it may seem elementary at first blush, an agency must ensure that its written settlement agreement provides for withdrawal of the pending complaint. A memorandum of understanding between the agency and the employee that did not reference the EEO complaint or state that it settled the complaint was not a bar to further pursuit of the complaint, even where the memorandum stated it was the "full and final agreement regarding [appellant's employment],..." Sternberg v. Secretary of Defense, Office of Dependents Education, 01941428 (1994).

If an agency offers the complainant some alteration in the working relationship and does not extract from the complainant a promise to withdraw his or her complaint, there is no settlement agreement, at least from the EEO perspective. This point was driven home in Rickey v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A31447 (2003), where the Commission noted:

The record further reflects that by memorandum to the EEO Counselor dated June 4, 1998, the same management official again offered to place complainant in a permanent midnight shift in his current position, but at the St. Albans location. The record further shows that the management official issued a memorandum to complainant, dated June 16, 1998, which states the following:

As per our discussion, in order to reasonably accommodate your medical condition, I am offering you a permanent [midnight] shift at St. Albans Extended Care Center with your current days of Saturday and Sunday off.

If you accept this offer, please sign on the line above your name.

The Commission notes that the memorandum is signed by complainant, the management official, and a witness.

In a breach claim, dated October 22, 2002, complainant contends that the agency requested updated medical documentation concerning his reasonable accommodation which he provided, although stating that it was not required of him by the "settlement agreement." Complainant further averred that after reviewing this documentation, the agency notified him that his shift and off-days would be changing, which he claims violates the "settlement agreement" in that the schedule specified therein was to be permanent. Complainant claimed breach, and requests specific enforcement.

In its final decision, the agency determined that the June 16, 1998 memorandum did not constitute a settlement agreement, but instead merely constituted an offer to provide a reasonable accommodation to complainant. Specifically, the agency stated that its records fail to show that complainant withdrew his pending EEO claim in exchange for this accommodation, or that he filed a complaint that was administratively closed because of a settlement agreement. The agency further determined that there is otherwise no evidence of "mutual consideration" to support the formation of a settlement agreement, also noting that the June 16, 1998 memorandum did not contain the mandatory language to create a settlement agreement. Therefore, the agency concluded that because no settlement agreement was formed, no breach could have occurred. Nonetheless, the agency advised complainant to timely contact an EEO Counselor should he wish to pursue the matter identified in his breach claim as a separate complaint of discrimination.

In its response to complainant's appeal, the agency additionally argues that although the notice of final interview had been issued prior to the June 16, 1998 memorandum, in recognition of its legal duty, the agency nonetheless continued to engage in the process of providing complainant with a reasonable accommodation. The agency argues that its June 16, 1998 offer was not intended to be a settlement agreement, and was not styled as such, making no reference to an EEO complaint, let alone complainant's agreement to withdraw the complaint in exchange for this particular accommodation.

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. 1614.504(a) provides that any settlement agreement knowingly and voluntarily agreed to by the parties, reached at any stage of the complaint process, shall be binding on both parties. The Commission has held that a settlement agreement constitutes a contract between the employee and the agency, to which ordinary rules of contract construction apply. See Herrington v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05960032 (December 9, 1996).

In the instant case, after careful review, we concur with the agency that the June 16, 1998 memorandum merely reflects complainant's agreement to accept the reasonable accommodation offered by the agency. We note that this same offer was made to complainant during the course of EEO counseling, which he initially declined. Furthermore, we note that all offers of accommodation, both during EEO counseling, and immediately after the Notice of Final Interview, were made through the EEO Counselor to complainant. We note that there is no evidence of when complainant received the Notice of Final Interview, nor is there any evidence that complainant wished to cease counseling and instead engage in mediation or some other alternative dispute resolution process, designed to terminate with a settlement agreement if successful. We note that the agency indicates that its records show no administrative closure of complainant's EEO claim.

Moreover, viewing the memorandum itself, we agree with the agency that there is nothing to suggest that the "offer and acceptance" therein were intended by either party to be a contractual obligation to provide the accommodation, or withdraw a complaint. Instead, we are persuaded that the agency tendered this offer as a legal obligation under the Rehabilitation Act, making no demand upon complainant to withdraw his complaint. We find that this interpretation is supported by the fact that the June 16, 1998 memorandum makes no reference whatsoever to an EEO complaint. While we find that it is reasonable to assume that complainant did not follow through and file a formal complaint because of the June 16, 1998 offer, there is nonetheless no evidence to show that his forbearance was incurred as a contractual obligation, or that he was mislead into believing that he could not file a formal complaint after accepting the accommodation offered by the agency. Under these circumstances, we find that there was no "meeting of the minds" necessary for contract formation, such that the June 16, 1998 memorandum cannot be construed as a binding settlement agreement. See Brown v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05940628 (November 3, 1994); Mullen v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05890349 (May 18, 1989).

In conclusion, for the reasons set forth above, we find that the parties here did not execute a settlement agreement, and we AFFIRM the agency's determination finding no breach for this reason. Furthermore, we find that the agency properly viewed the breach claim as a separate complaint, and properly advised complainant to contact an EEO Counselor should he chose to pursue this matter in the EEO forum.
