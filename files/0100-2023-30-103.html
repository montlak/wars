2. Past Pecuniary Damages

Past pecuniary damages are reimbursement for out-of-pocket costs the complainant has already incurred as a result of discrimination. An employee is entitled to reimbursement for those expenses that are proximately caused by the agency's discriminatory action or inaction. See, e.g., Brinkley v. Postmaster General, 01953977 (1998).

Beyond the obvious, the Commission in Brinkley held that the complainant should be reimbursed for an automatic car starter in a case where she was harassed with both sexually and physically threatening conduct. The Commission found the complainant's desire to get away from the workplace quickly was understandable given the agency's inaction. Likewise, the Commission found it appropriate to reimburse the complainant for certain expenses related to her husband's return from a job assignment in Korea in order to take care of their diabetic daughter. The complainant demonstrated that her inability to properly care for her daughter's diabetes was directly attributable to the psychological injury she sustained because of the agency's discrimination. However, as a matter of "public policy," the Commission declined to reimburse the employee for the purchase of a handgun for protection. Id.

There is no requirement that a complainant first prove actual out-of-pocket damages prior to asserting a claim for emotional distress. In Bensing v. Secretary of Navy, 01945741 (1995), the Commission stated:

[W]here a complainant has requested compensatory damages for emotional distress, the agency must request information concerning the non-pecuniary damages prior to issuing an offer of full relief. Mitchell v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Appeal No. 01934120 (March 4, 1994). Therefore, because appellant in this case made a claim for compensatory damages, the agency must request that appellant provide some objective evidence linking those damages to the adverse actions at issue herein.

A complaint demanding relief in the form of reimbursement for expenses incurred to receive approval of a workers' compensation claim presents a claim for compensatory damages where the complainant alleges the agency delayed processing of her claim on a discriminatory basis. Roberts v. Secretary of Treasury, 01942291 (1994).
