3. Bad Faith and Mistaken Advice by Agency

It is inappropriate for an agency, either through ignorance or bad faith, to induce an appellant to delay the filing of a formal complaint due to a possible pending resolution. Bullock v. Department of Navy, 01830565 (1983). An agency cannot dismiss a formal complaint on the basis of timeliness in the "face of its own improper actions."

A complainant demonstrates good cause for setting aside the time limit for filing a formal complaint when she presents evidence that she was given erroneous advice by the counselor as to the meaning of 15 calendar days. In Spencer v. Postmaster General, 01A53614 (2005), recon. denied 05A6004 (2006), the complainant relied on a little divine intervention by presenting an affidavit from her reverend supporting her allegation that the counselor had given her erroneous advice about the meaning of 15 calendar days. See also Buck H. v. Federal Aviation Administration, 0120152830 (2016) (complainant received notice with only eight more days to file due to his failure to update his address with the agency; late filing excused, however, where EEO counselor advised complainant that he had "15 days from the time [he] received the paperwork" to file his formal complaint); Cook v. Attorney General, 0120091964 (2009) (counselor told complainant she had 15 business days to the file the formal complaint).

The fact that the complainant reasonably believed that her complaint had been settled served to toll the 15-day period in Price-Gaines v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120111001 (2011). In that case, the complainant was given a notice of final interview on September 10, but did not file her formal complaint until October 2. However, on September 13, the chief of the agency's Office of Financial Management agreed to settle the complaint. The complainant signed the settlement agreement on September 17. On September 24, the chief decided not to sign the agreement. Because the complainant was out on sick leave until September 27, the date she learned the agency was not settling the case, and filed her complaint on October 2 was sufficient indication for the Commission that "she was diligently pursuing her complaint."

Where the EEO counselor admitted telling the complainant that the process would be burdensome and time-consuming but denied telling him he would not get his job back, the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the case. In Rush v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0210113868 (2012), the Commission found:

In his appeal Complainant states that he did not "have faith and lost trust when [he] first made contact and spoke to an EEO counselor." Complainant stated he felt discouraged from continuing his complaint because the EEO counselor told him he would not get his job back and that the process would be lengthy and burdensome. However, Complainant contacted his senator and informed the Senator of issues relating to his job as well as the EEO information he had been given. As a result of an inquiry from the Senator, the Agency looked into his claims and the EEO counselor admitted telling Complainant that the EEO complaint process can be burdensome and time consuming, although denied telling Complainant that he would not get his job back because he was an intern.

Under the circumstances of this case, we exercise out discretion to excuse Complainant's untimely filing. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c). Complainant did not sit on his rights in this matter. Rather, having been discouraged from filing a complaint by the EEO counselor, he contacted his Senator and sought assistance. Thereafter, he was contacted by another EEO counselor. Complainant was sent another formal complaint form on April 8, 2011, and was told he must file it with the Complaints Processing Center. Complainant did so on April 11, 2011. Given that Complainant did not sit on his rights, promptly sought assistance from his Senator, and when instructed to file with the Complaints Processing Center, promptly did so, we find adequate grounds to reverse the dismissal of his complaint.

Where the agency's actions contributed to the late filing of the complaint, the Commission determined that the complaint was timely filed. In Williams v. Secretary of Defense, 0120130153 (2013), the agency sent the complainant an extension of time form to extend the counseling period and gave five days to respond. He agreed within the five days, but the agency issued the notice of final interview before the expiration of the five days. In addition, the Commission noted that counseling continued after the notice of final interview was issued. See also Roy S. v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120160559 (2016) (reversing dismissal for untimely filing where management told complainant that they were looking into getting a temporary position for her and misled her into "believing that she should wait and be 'patient.'"). The Commission waived the time limit for filing a formal complaint where a mediator told the complainant that the matter would be resolved in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120150288 (2015):

The record discloses that Complainant received the notice of right to file a formal complaint on July 30, 2014. Although the notice indicated that Complainant had to file a formal complaint within fifteen (15) calendar days of its receipt, Complainant did not file her formal complaint until August 18, 2014, beyond the limitation period. In response to the Agency's inquiry as to why she filed in an untimely manner, Complainant explained that she had gone to mediation on the matter and during the mediation, the mediator represented to her that the matter would be taken care of and not to worry about it.

Based on the unique facts of this case, we determine that this is an appropriate situation to excuse the brief delay (four days) in filing her complaint. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c) provides that the time limitations in the EEO complaint processing regulations are subject to waiver, estoppel and equitable tolling. In reaching this decision, we have considered Complainant's statement regarding her misunderstanding of the mediation and being told "not to worry about it." Additionally, given the nature of the claims and the allegation that the same individual was the subject of sexual harassment complaints by other female employees, we find that waiver of the time limits in this situation is appropriate.

Similarly, the fact that the complainant reasonably believed that her complaint had been settled served to toll the 15-day period in Price-Gaines v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120111001 (2011). In that case, the complainant was given a notice of final interview on September 10, but did not file her formal complaint until October 2. However, on September 13, the chief of the agency's Office of Financial Management agreed to settle the complaint. The complainant signed the settlement agreement on September 17. On September 24, the chief decided not to sign the agreement. Because the complainant was out on sick leave until September 27, the date she learned the agency was not settling the case, and filed her complaint on October 2 was sufficient indication for the Commission that "she was diligently pursuing her complaint."
