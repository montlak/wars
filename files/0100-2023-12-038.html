24. Representative

A complainant is entitled to be represented by counsel if he so chooses. There is no requirement that a complainant be represented by anyone and an inability to find counsel will not excuse the untimely filing of a complaint. Syed v. Department of Treasury, 01831444 (1983).

In a case involving a sole practitioner, the Commission found that the filing of the formal complaint was timely where the complainant's counsel went on vacation, stopped mail to the office, and filed the complaint within 15 days of returning and having mail service restored. In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120121587 (2014), the Commission explained:

We find that Complainant's appeal was timely filed. Complainant's attorney asserts that she "filed an active Authorization to Hold Mail with the U.S. Postal Service with an effective date January 9, 2012 through February 1, 2012...since [Complainant's attorney] operates as a sole practitioner, when time off is taken and the business is closed, it is imperative to stop the delivery of time sensitive mail by filing a PS Form 8076 with the Post Office. After the [Complainant's] attorney returned from her time off away from work, the Post Office resumed delivery as scheduled, causing the apparent delay at issue. [Complainant's] attorney received the article of mail in question on February 2, 2012, which is the date it was date stamped by [Complainant's attorney]. Thus, when [Complainant's] Notice of Appeal was filed on February 21, 2012, it was timely filed...." We note that the record contains a copy of an Authorization to Hold Mail for Complainant's attorney for the period of January 9, 2012 through February 1, 2012. The form indicates that all accumulated mail will resume normal delivery on the ending date. The record also contains a copy of the Agency's final decision from Complainant's attorney date-stamped received on February 2, 2012. Based on the foregoing, we find Complainant's appeal filed on February 21, 2012, to be timely.

By contrast, in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120130181 (2014), the Commission rejected an argument by a sole practitioner that his late-filed complaint should be accepted because he was not in the office the day it was delivered:

On appeal, Complainant's attorney explains that he does not doubt that the package was delivered on June 7, 2012, but he was working at home and did not receive notification of the right to file until Complainant emailed it to him on Sunday June 10, 2012. He explained that he has a solo practice, and although the person who signed for the letter had previously signed for items, that person was not employed by his office. The attorney stated that the occupants of his business address "sometimes accept packages for each other sent by overnight, express mail." We find that Complainant's attorney has not offered adequate justification to warrant an extension of the time limit for filing the complaint. The Agency was given the attorney's business address as Complainant's official address. Complainant's attorney, at a minimum, should have checked to see if any mail was delivered to his business address when he was working at home, especially since he was aware that others accepted packages for each other.
