5. Temporary Appointments

When the job the complainant is denied is a temporary appointment, make-whole relief consists of a comparable temporary appointment. However, when the complainant shows that other employees given temporary appointments were subsequently renewed for additional terms, the complainant also is entitled to those renewals as relief. Davis v. Secretary of Army, 07A20023 (2003). The agency has an obligation to provide information about the subsequent reappointments of temporary employees. See, e.g., Lee v. Postmaster General, 0120052097 (2007) (remand for supplemental investigation where record not developed on subsequent rehires for temporary positions).

In Talukdar v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0420060042 (2007), the agency terminated the contract of a physician on a temporary appointment, which the Commission found was based on national origin and reprisal. In part, those findings were based on evidence demonstrating that the facility had never terminated or failed to renew the contract of a physician based on budgetary reasons and that the only two physicians terminated were both Indian nationals and had engaged in prior EEO activity. The Commission ordered the complainant reinstated with back pay. The agency paid back pay, but again terminated the complainant's appointment based on his noncitizenship status. The Commission, in Talukdar, found that the agency was not in compliance with its remedial order:

In our Order, we ordered the agency to place petitioner back into the position of Staff Physician, and provide all back pay, plus interest and benefits permitted under the Back Pay Act, as well as all seniority rights attributable from June 30, 2002. While our order did not specifically state, it should have been obvious to the agency that petitioner was to be placed into the Staff Physician position at the agency's Fargo Medical Center (VAMC) as that is where the act of discrimination occurred, unless petitioner declined the agency's offer. Further, although the position petitioner was terminated from was temporary in nature and was designated not to exceed April 28, 2002, our order stated only that petitioner would be placed back into the position of Staff Physician and did not state that the agency could place him in a temporary position with a not to exceed date which could then not be extended as a means for terminating petitioner from the agency. We find no evidence in the record to support the agency's contention that it is consistent with our order that petitioner's reappointment to the agency not to exceed June 30, 2005. We remind the agency that our initial decision found that the agency discriminated against petitioner when it did not renew his temporary appointment and terminated his employment with the agency in June of 2002. As noted above, neither agency management officials, nor the VAMC Chief Financial Officer, could identify any other physician who was ever terminated or not had their appointment renewed for budgetary reasons, despite the fact that the facility had experienced prior budgetary crises. The record clearly indicated that petitioner would have remained employed by the agency at the Fargo Medical Center (VAMC) even with a temporary appointment were it not for the act of discrimination by the agency. Absent clear and convincing evidence that petitioner's appointment would not have been renewed after June 30, 2005, we find that the agency failed to fully comply with the Commission's order.
