11. Overtime

A policy that denies overtime to all employees placed on limited duty because of their physical condition is discriminatory. The burden then shifts to the agency to show that the complainant would not have received overtime even in the absence of such a policy. Houston v. Postmaster General, 01871433 (1989).

When an agency assigns overtime, it may not routinely exclude a disabled employee. In each instance, it must conduct an individualized review to determine if sufficient work is available within the complainant's limitations. In Grier v. Postmaster General, 01956853 (1998), the Commission found that the agency discriminated against the complainant when it excluded him from overtime work on numerous occasions without determining each time whether there was overtime work compatible with his disability:

After a careful review of the record in its entirety, including the information gathered during the agency's supplemental investigation, the Commission finds that the AJ's recommended decision finding discrimination on the basis of disability was correct. It now appears clear that, despite their denials, appellant's supervisors automatically excluded appellant from overtime work because of his disability, rather than excluding him, on each occasion, only after conducting a good faith assessment into whether sufficient work was available within his limitations to call him in for overtime.

Even after the supplemental investigation, there is a dearth of evidence in the record to corroborate the agency's assertion that insufficient work was available that appellant could perform with or without reasonable accommodation within his limitations to justify calling him in to work on his off-day. It further appears that no consideration was given by agency officials to affording appellant a combination of duties within his limitations in order to fill out an 8-hour time period. There is evidence that such work was potentially available, but provided instead by the agency only to non-disabled employees. Moreover, there is no evidence that the agency explored the possibility of seeking a waiver of the eight-hour guarantee from appellant and the union in order to provide appellant with at least some overtime opportunities. At the hearing, there was testimony that at least one other employee was permitted to work less than eight hours of overtime per day.

Accordingly, for the reasons stated above, it is the decision of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to REVERSE the agency's final decision which rejected the AJ's finding of disability discrimination.

See also Earl v. Postmaster General, 01994514 (2002) (affirming decision of administrative judge that complainant was not covered by Rehabilitation Act but cautioning agency that its policy of denying overtime to individuals who cannot perform all duties of their job assignments may violate the Act by penalizing those who receive reasonable accommodation). However, in Camp v. Postmaster General, 01A51024 (2006), the Commission found that the agency did not discriminate against the complainant where the collective bargaining agreement provided that as many employees as possible should have holidays off and, as a result, the employees who did work were required to perform multiple jobs. Because of his medical restrictions, the complainant was not able to perform multiple jobs and this constituted a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason that the complainant failed to demonstrate was pretext. The Commission in Camp also found that the agency had not failed to accommodate the complainant because it did not deny him overtime generally and it was not required to create work for holidays. Similarly, in Hensley v. Postmaster General, 01A45810 (2006), the Commission found that the agency did not discriminate against the complainant on the basis of disability when it did not assign him overtime work. The Commission found that the agency articulated legitimate reasons that the complainant failed to demonstrate were pretext when it demonstrated that it could not assign him overtime work within his medical restrictions and consistent with the collective bargaining agreement. The Commission noted that three comparables relied upon by the complainant were in the clerk craft while the complainant was in the carrier craft.
