K. MEDICAL EVIDENCE

If there is a message that has not gotten across to agencies since the effective date of the ADAAA on January 1, 2009, it is with regard to the medical evidence, or lack of medical evidence, required to prove the individual has a disability under the Act. This is particularly true in light of the Commission's new regulations, discussed in detail above, with its rules of construction for interpreting and applying the term substantial limitation and its "predictable assessments" provisions that will "virtually always" result in a determination that certain impairments are substantially limiting. The case law discussed below must be read in light of those provisions and the lower standard for establishing that an individual has a disability.

The burden is on the employee to establish that he is a qualified individual with a disability. However, the agency must assist the employee in fulfilling this burden by providing the employee with the requirements of the position so that the employee can submit specific medical evidence on his ability to perform the essential job functions. Franco-Alejandro v. USPS, 01811188 (1983).

It is appropriate for the agency to dismiss a complaint for failure to prosecute if the complainant refuses to provide adequate evidence of the existence and nature of an alleged disability at the investigation stage. See Larger v. USPS, 01831950 (1983). In disability discrimination cases, the issue is the complainant's condition at the time of the disputed personnel action and not some later time in the processing of the complaint. See Burt v. USPS, 01851770 (1986).

Although the ADAAA did not directly change any of the medical evidence requirements to establish a disability, as a practical matter, it significantly altered both the amount and degree of medical evidence that can be required by an agency to establish that an individual has a disability. Most significantly, the amendments altered the degree of limitation required to establish that a medical condition substantially limits a major life activity. Section (2)(b)(5) of the ADAAA specifically states that one purpose of the amendments was:

to convey that it is the intent of Congress that the primary object of attention in cases brought under the ADA should be whether entities covered under the ADA have complied with their obligations, and to convey that the question of whether an individual's impairment is a disability under the ADA should not demand extensive analysis;...

It is not sufficient that the medical evidence merely establish the existence of a condition. The medical evidence must relate that condition to the performance of the job functions involved. See, e.g., Stuart v. Veterans Administration, 02850024 (1987). Moreover, a mere diagnosis, unsupported by medical evidence that the condition substantially limits a major life activity, is insufficient to establish that the complainant is a qualified individual with a disability. See Spangle v. Postmaster General, 01910130 (1991) (complainant diagnosed as schizophrenic).

For example, a diagnosis of depression does not guarantee that a complainant will be deemed a person with a disability. The Commission still will examine the underlying facts to see if the condition substantially limits a major life activity. As the Commission noted in Adams v. Attorney General, 03940156 (1995):

We find that petitioner does not have a physical or mental impairment which substantially limits one or more of her major life activities. Petitioner's somewhat increased risk for suicide does not meet this test. Further, petitioner's symptoms of anxiety, sleep disturbance, exaggerated startle response and so forth, by themselves, do not rise to the level of a disability. However, they would do so if they substantially limit one or more of her major life activities. The fitness-for-duty report stated that petitioner's memory was intact, her speech was coherent, she was not affectively restricted or inappropriate, and she did not exhibit the profound psychomotor retardation typical of depressed persons.

While a diagnosis of depression, standing alone, is insufficient to establish that a complainant is a qualified individual with a disability, depression can qualify as a disability. In Stander v. Postmaster General, 01930152 (1994), the Commission found the complainant established depression as a disabling condition:

In his note dated August 22, 1989, the psychiatrist indicated that appellant was incapacitated from work because of a major depression. In his October 13, 1989 report, the psychiatrist again indicated that appellant suffered from major depression, as well as dysthymia, extremely disorganized thinking, anxiety, and realistic fears of being killed if she returned to work, and noted that her cognitive abilities were not such as to be conducive to working. Appellant reported feeling crazy, anxious, extremely scared, helpless, hopeless, weak, unable to concentrate and remember, and barely hanging on. Based on the medical evidence of record, the Commission finds that appellant suffered from an impairment that rose to the level of a disability within the meaning of the regulations. The Commission further finds that appellant established a causal connection between her disabling condition and the agency's reasons for her removal.

Having met the above-cited regulatory definition, appellant also must show that she is a qualified individual with a disability to trigger the protections of the Rehabilitation Act. A qualified person with a disability is one "who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the position in question. In appropriate circumstances, a determination may be required as to whether appellant can perform the essential functions of the position without constituting a danger or risk to herself or others. 29 CFR § 1613.702(f).

It is undisputed that appellant was able to perform the essential functions of her position. Consequently, the Commission finds that appellant is a qualified individual with a disability and that the agency had a duty to reasonably accommodate her disabling condition. The issue, then, is whether the agency reasonably accommodated appellant under the facts and circumstances of this case.

In his affidavit dated May 16, 1991, the supervisor stated that "[d]uring the entire time [appellant] was off I did not specifically know what her medical condition was. She did not provide documentation which stated what her condition was. Without knowing what her condition was, we could not offer accommodation." In his supplemental affidavit, the supervisor further stated that "I took no additional actions to obtain adequate documentation," asserting that he had specified in his letters what constituted acceptable medical documentation.

The psychiatrist's note dated August 22, 1989 stated that appellant was "suffering from a major depression and is disabled from work." It further indicated that appellant had been disabled since March 14, 1989, and that the psychiatrist did not expect her to recover before January 1, 1990. The Commission finds that this document was sufficient to put the agency on notice of petitioner's disability and her requested accommodation. The record also shows that the supervisor accepted the August 22, 1989 note as adequate medical documentation. Consequently, the Commission finds unreasonable the supervisor's determination that appellant's subsequent medical documentation dated September 19, 1989 was unacceptable given the information contained in the previous note and the fact that he accepted the previous note as sufficient to support her absence.

The Commission also finds that appellant made a good faith effort to comply with the supervisor's requests for medical documentation. That is, when the supervisor informed her that the documentation submitted was inadequate, appellant provided new documentation in response thereto. If the supervisor believed the documentation was inadequate, he should have explained why he found it to be so and specified what information he wanted the psychiatrist to include therein. Instead, the record shows that he merely cited the same section of the ELM in each of his letters even after it should have been apparent that neither appellant nor the psychiatrist understood what he was looking for.

The Tour Superintendent stated that he removed appellant because she failed to report for duty within the timeframe specified in the prearbitration settlement agreement between the agency and the Union. The record shows that appellant reported to the agency's medical unit on May 23, 1990--before the date on which she was to return to work. At that time, she presented the agency's physician with a letter from her psychiatrist stating that she was medically incapacitated from work until January 1, 1991, and referring to his medical evaluation of appellant dated October 13, 1989. As a result, the agency's physician did not release her for duty at that time. It appears that the Tour Superintendent may have exceeded his authority by removing appellant for failing to report for duty when the agency's physician had not released her for same.

Because the medical evidence submitted by appellant was sufficient to establish her disability, and because the agency failed to show that granting her leave requests would result in an undue hardship, the Commission finds that the agency discriminated against appellant by failing to accommodate her disabling condition when it denied her leave requests and removed her from her position.

The important distinction is that establishing the complainant suffers from a medical condition is not sufficient. It also must be established that the condition substantially limits a major life activity.

Providing medical evidence sufficient to establish that the employee is an individual with a disability does not mean the employee has established entitlement to any particular accommodation. Particularly where the symptoms of an illness are not apparent, the medical evidence must also establish how the condition affects the employee and recommend suitable accommodations. Jensen v. Secretary of Defense, 01970621 (1998).
