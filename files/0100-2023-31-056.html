15. Legal Research

Legal research is a compensable service for an attorney to provide to a client in the course of representation. However, one of the biggest problems with legal research is the failure of the attorney to adequately document the reasons for the research. General notations of legal research on time sheets may well be insufficient to justify the hours spent. This may be particularly true if the attorney is claiming an expertise in discrimination cases, which often will be the case in attempting to justify a higher hourly rate. For example, in Lee v. Secretary of Defense, 01962241 (1997), the Commission noted there were 21 entries involving legal research but

it is impossible to ascertain from the billing statement how much time specifically was spent on that task. The Commission agrees with the agency that it appears that the time spent researching the issues of continuing violation and the failure to promote, as well as the time spent researching procedural matters such as the admissibility of witness testimony was excessive, given the attorney's stated expertise in the area of employment law. It is noted that the attorney offered no further explanation regarding the specific aspects of the issues which required research.

As a result, the Commission reduced the amount of time spent on legal research by 50%.

However, the fact that the complainant's counsel has expertise in discrimination cases does not mean that he or she is not entitled to do any research in a case. For example, in Southerland v. Postmaster General, 01A05403 (2002), the Commission approved over 37 hours of legal research even though the agency considered the complainant's counsel to be an expert in employment discrimination law. The Commission noted the case involved a psychiatric disability and had taken nearly nine years to resolve. "Like all attorneys, counsel needs to educate herself about the particular circumstances of her client's situation." Id. See also Oest v. Attorney General, 0720110002 (2011) (citing with approval finding of administrative judge that "'while some knowledge of the law is presumed and accounted for in the hourly rate, it is not unreasonable for an experienced lawyer to spend four hours researching and updating the law.'").

Employment discrimination law is by no means stagnant. Indeed, the Commission has indicated that the field of employment discrimination is constantly changing. As a result, in Goodwin v. Postmaster General, 01960485 (1998), the Commission found that the hours spent on research were reasonable, noting the evolving nature of employment law:

Initially, the Commission rejects the agency's conclusions regarding the thirteen hours of legal research it disallowed. The agency's conclusion that the legal research was spent in the education of counsel and his law clerk in general areas of the law does not address the applicable legal standard, i.e., whether the hours billed were reasonable in light of the circumstances of the complaint. See Johnson v. Georgia Highway Express, Inc., 488 F.2d 714 (5th Cir, 1974) (outlining twelve factors to be considered in assessing the reasonableness of a fee award). Appellant argues that, due to changes in the law and in our regulations, the research time expended was reasonable. We have noted in the past that employment law constantly evolves and the courts and the Commission continually issue decisions that impact on employment law. Denver Woods, Jr. v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01940710 (September 20, 1994). Under these circumstance, we find the thirteen hours of legal research reasonable and allow $975.00 for the September 15 and 16, 1992, legal research.
