VII. MEDICAL INQUIRIES AND EXAMINATIONS

The ADA's restrictions on medical inquiries and examinations, incorporated into the Rehabilitation Act, apply to all stages of employment and the application process: preoffer, postoffer, and during employment. Courtney v. Postmaster General, 01981316 (2001). Moreover, unlike other parts of the ADA and the Rehabilitation Act, the restrictions apply regardless of whether the complainant is a qualified individual with a disability or even an individual with a disability. In other words, the restrictions apply to all applicants and employees. The ADAAA did not directly change any of the provisions with regard to medical inquiries and examinations. Still, the ADAAA and the Commission's implementing regulations do indirectly impact the amount and degree of medical information that agencies can seek from complainants in making medical determinations. This is particularly true in the area of evaluating requests for reasonable accommodation where the amount of medical information, if any, to determine if an individual has a disability has been greatly reduced because of the lower threshold for a substantial limitation determination and the "predictable assessments" regulation, discussed fully above, that designates several impairments as "virtually always" substantially limiting a major life activity.

As the Commission noted in Courtney:

The ADA's restrictions on inquiries and examinations apply to all employees, not just those with disabilities. However, only disability-related inquiries and medical examinations are subject to the ADA's restrictions. Any employee has a right to challenge a disability-related inquiry or medical examination that is not job-related and consistent with business necessity. The ADA's restrictions on disability-related inquiries and medical examinations apply to individuals both with and without disabilities at all three stages: pre-offer, post-offer, and during employment. EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Disability-Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations of Employees Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), No. 915.002, at 5-7 (July 26, 2000) (Disability-Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations Guidance). See also Gonzales v. Sandoval County, 2 F. Supp.2d 1442, 1445, 8AD Cas. 1337, 1340 (D.N.M. 1998) (plaintiff need not establish disability to state a claim for a prohibited inquiry under the ADA); Fredenburg v. Contra Costa County Department of Health Services, 172 F.3d 1176, 9 AD Cas. 385 (9th Cir. 1999) (requiring plaintiffs to prove that they are persons with disabilities to challenge a medical examination would render 12112(d) (4)(A) of the ADA "nugatory"; thus, plaintiffs need not prove that they are qualified individuals with a disability to bring claims challenging the scope of medical examinations under the ADA).

Prior to discussing the restrictions on medical inquiries and examinations, it is worth noting that an "agency may make disability-related inquiries and require medical examinations when an employee requests a reasonable accommodation...The agency may disclose medical information to health care professionals when seeking advice in making reasonable accommodation determinations." Gomez v. Secretary of State, 01A45373 (2005).

Making an improper medical inquiry or requiring an improper medical examination is a violation of the ADA. However, when an impermissible inquiry or examination is made in the context of hiring or promoting an individual, this does not mean that he or she automatically gets the position as relief. Whether the inquiry or examination resulted in the individual not being hired or promoted is a separate matter that is subject to analysis under McDonnell Douglas. See, e.g., Huckelbery v. Social Security Administration, 0120071255 (2007) (complainant failed to show that inquiries into sick leave usage and need for reasonable accommodation were a pretext for failing to hire him).
