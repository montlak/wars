6. Same-Sex Marriage and Communications With LGBTQ Content

An email congratulating an employee on his same-sex marriage and inviting employees to a voluntary informal celebration of the announcement did not create a hostile work environment on the basis of religion for a Pentecostal employee. In Walker v. Environmental Protection Agency, 0120112853 (2011), the complainant alleged a hostile work environment when the acting director of his unit sent an email to employees on the unit's global-serve list that said:

[Employee A] and his partner [named] are getting married this Sunday. The IO is sponsoring an informal celebration to congratulate [Employee A] on this happy event. Please feel free to drop by the IO conference room on Thursday, October 7 at 4:30 P.M. to wish them well.

Some two weeks later, the complainant sent an email to the acting director with a copy to everyone on the global-serve list stating:

I feel your message announcing the celebration of the "union" of [Employee A] and his "Partner" was offensive and insensitive to my religious faith as a Christian. I think it is general knowledge that the Christian faith only condones "marriages" between men and women, not men and other men. As acting Office Director, I feel you could have been more "sensitive" and "neutral" with regards to this issue.

Several employees then sent emails on the global-serve list to Employee A congratulating on his marriage. None of the emails mentioned the complainant or his email, but two employees did personally email the complainant stating his email was insensitive because it was sent to everybody. The Commission, in Walker, found the complaint did not state a claim of hostile environment harassment:

In the instant case, we find no cause to reverse the Agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to state a claim. The initial email sent by the Acting Director was sent to all employees and not just to Complainant, and invited them to a voluntary social gathering. The record shows that the email comported with the Agency's long-standing practice of celebrating milestone events in the lives of its employees. Moreover, the initial email was a one time occurrence that could not be considered either sufficiently pervasive or severe to constitute a viable hostile work environment claim.

Complainant expressed his objection to the email nearly two weeks later and copied the NCEA global list-serve. While it is likely that the 15-20 congratulatory emails sent to Employee A that followed were provoked by Complainant's email, none of them specifically mentioned Complainant or challenged his religious beliefs. Therefore, we cannot find that these emails were sufficiently severe or pervasive to constitute a viable claim of unlawful retaliatory harassment because of religion. As already noted, the record does indicate that two employees personally emailed Complainant and raised objections to the fact that he sent his email to everyone, including Employee A. It is true that at least one of these emails questioned Complainant's religious beliefs on the subject of same-sex marriage. However, we do not find that this isolated incident of a coworker expressing his opinion in direct response to Complainant's email is sufficiently severe to state viable hostile work environment claim.

Similarly, communications referencing the LGBT community that are insulting or upsetting to a religious person are not sufficiently severe to be considered harassment and do not impose the kind of burden on one's religious practices to constitute intentional discrimination based on religion. See Humberto P. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120170528 (2017) (involving allegations of religious discrimination when an announcement concerning an LGBT pride program was made over the loud speaker and when the complainant received a "dismissive and hostile" response from the agency Director to his letter that raised concerns about "celebrating and promoting the sexuality of certain individuals in our workplace").
