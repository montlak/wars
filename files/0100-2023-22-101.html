2. Agency Investigations, Cooperation in

As a general matter, employees are required to cooperate in agency investigations. It is insubordination for an employee to refuse to cooperate in a legitimate agency investigation. For the most part, because insubordination is a serious offense, cases involving insubordination and the resulting discipline go before the Merit Systems Protection Board. Usually, the Commission's only concern in such cases is whether there has been disparate treatment in the discipline imposed.

However, in Perry v. Secretary of Army, 01966044 (1997), one enterprising soul argued that the agency's attempt to compel her to cooperate in an investigation constituted a failure to accommodate her religious beliefs. The agency sought the employee's cooperation as a witness to a dispute between two other employees. When she refused to give a statement, the agency issued her a letter of reprimand. The Commission, in Perry, determined that the appellant failed to show that her refusal to cooperate with an agency investigation was because of her religious beliefs:

In November 1991, one of appellant's coworkers reported that she had been verbally abused by another coworker, who had used profanity and curses. In statements provided to management, both coworkers placed appellant at the scene of the altercation. Accordingly, the Branch Chief instructed appellant to provide a statement as to what she had observed. Appellant did not do so, and the Division Head personally met with her to request a statement. In a written response, appellant declined to comment on the basis that she felt that a meeting should be conducted with both parties [to resolve the matter] at the management level." The Division Head met with appellant again to inform her that he expected a statement. By memorandum dated November 18, 1991, appellant cited scripture and dictionary definitions which prevented her from being a "talebearer" and indicated that she would not comment on religious grounds. After another meeting with the Division Head, appellant received a memorandum from the Branch Chief which informed her that he was not persuaded that she held a religious belief which precluded her from providing a truthful statement as to her observations on the day in question. After several other meetings, appellant submitted a written response stating that she "was involved in [her] work and not aware of what was being said [and, thus,] cannot give a statement as to what" occurred between the coworkers. Thereafter, appellant was issued a one day suspension for impeding an official investigation by being deliberately evasive and uncooperative by not stating at the beginning of the inquiries that she had not heard anything.

The Commission in Perry then determined that the appellant was not constructively discharged because of the agency's alleged failure to accommodate her religious beliefs, based partially on evidence that showed that she was already looking for a new job when the incidents at issue occurred:

In the RD, the AJ found that appellant failed to establish that the agency's instruction that appellant submit a statement constituted a failure to provide reasonable accommodation for her religious belief. In order to establish a prima facie case of failure to provide reasonable accommodation, appellant must show that: (1) she as a bona fide religious belief that conflicts with an employment policy; (2) she informed the employer of this belief; and (3) she was adversely affected in some way for failure to comply with the conflicting employment requirement. Upon reviewing the evidence as a whole, the Commission agrees with the AJ that appellant fails to demonstrate that she held a sincere religious belief that precluded her from informing management at the outset that she did not observe the altercation and, therefore, had no information to provide. The Commission further finds that the AJ properly determined that appellant failed to establish that she was disparately treated because of her religion or that management's persistence in requiring a statement from appellant constituted harassment because of her religious beliefs....
