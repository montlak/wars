47. Settlement Negotiations

Settlement negotiations are confidential and privileged and may not serve as the basis for an EEO complaint. As the Commission held in Gribcheck v. Postmaster General, 01A12030 (2002):

As a matter of law, a settlement offer does not give rise to a viable allegation. Such offers do not adversely affect a term, condition or privilege of employment. Further, settlement negotiations, including any statements and proposals made therein, are to be treated as confidential and privileged in order to facilitate candid interchange to settle disputes informally. To allow complainant to base a new complaint on a settlement offer would defeat this purpose. See Montague v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920321 (September 16, 1997).

See also Klauzinski v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A20863 (2002).

The same principle extends to mediation. See Sacramone v. Postmaster General, 01A52251 (2006) (allegation that postmaster made insulting remarks during mediation does not state a claim); Connors v. Postmaster General, 01A21585 (2002) (an allegation that inappropriate comments were made during mediation sessions does not state a claim).

Similarly, an agency's failure to engage in ADR in a particular case does not give rise to an EEO complaint. Mulligan v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 01A21675 (2003).

An offer made during the course of settlement negotiations cannot form the basis of an EEO complaint. In Figueroa v. Postmaster General, 07A30088 (2004), the complainant alleged that an offer of a transfer in lieu of discipline made during settlement negotiations was retaliatory. The Commission found the allegation in Figueroa failed to state a claim:

The agency argues that issue (4) was based on settlement discussions and therefore, cannot be raised as a claim. Upon review of the record, the Commission agrees with the agency. The record indicates that complainant was informed that the agency sought to transfer him from Corpus Christi. He testified that he spoke to an agency official who informed him that another employee who was also going to be transferred had the transfer cancelled when she filed an EEO complaint. Based on that advice, complainant sought EEO counseling. Complainant had not filed an EEO complaint when three agency officials contacted him about his EEO contact. The three agency officials included complainant's Corpus Christi supervisor and two other Inspectors. They offered complainant a voluntary transfer to another location rather than disciplinary action if he dropped the EEO matter. Complainant testified that he was given three days to decide whether or not to accept the offer by the agency officials. Complainant also indicated that the officials specifically told him to "go home" and "to talk to [his] mom and [his] dad and [his] wife because this is very serious." In addition, the officials wanted the response in writing. Complainant provided a written response stating that he could have chosen a voluntary move to San Antonio in order to avoid discipline on the condition he withdraw his EEO complaint. Complainant chose not to withdraw the EEO matter.

Upon review, we find that the substantial evidence within the record does not support the AJ's findings as to issue 4. In particular, the Commission determines that the actions alleged in issue 4 were in fact made during settlement negotiations between complainant and agency officials. Clearly, agency officials were offering complainant a voluntary transfer over disciplinary action and, in exchange, complainant would withdraw his EEO complaint. We further note that complainant testified that he made EEO contact in hopes of negotiating a settlement. We find that although the officials did not offer complainant exactly what he desired, it does not negate the fact that the officials and complainant were involved in settlement negotiations. Therefore, we find that the events alleged in issue 4 fail to state a claim. See Millea v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05980235 (May 21,1998) (claim alleging harm from settlement offer dismissed); Montague v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920231 (May 2, 1992) (citing Olitsky v. Spencer Gifts, Inc., 843 F.2d 123 (5th Cir.), cert, denied, 488 U.S. 925 (1988)); see also Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (MD-110), 3-16 (November 9, 1999). As a result, the Commission concludes that the AJ was precluded from amending the complaint to include issue 4. Hence, the Commission affirms the agency's rejection of the AJ's finding as to issue 4.

See also Ron W. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120133055 (2015) (upholding dismissal of claim alleging discrimination in privileged settlement communications between agency counsel and complainant's counsel).
