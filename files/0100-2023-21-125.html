VI. PROOF PROBLEMS

Proof problems abound in discrimination cases. But they can be particularly acute in sexual harassment cases where the only witnesses to events may be the actual participants. The Commission can enter a finding of discrimination based solely on the uncorroborated testimony of the employee. In Scott v. Secretary of Navy, 01903661 (1990), the Commission set forth the criteria it would use to evaluate such testimony:

[T]he Commission recognizes that sexual conduct may be private and unacknowledged, with no eyewitnesses. In appropriate cases, the Commission may make a finding of harassment based solely on the credibility of the victim's allegations. As with any other charge of discrimination, the victim's account must be sufficiently detailed and internally consistent so as to be plausible, and lack of corroborative evidence where such evidence should logically exist will undermine the allegation. EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment (October 25, 1988), pp. 11-12. See also Henson v. City of Dundee, 682 F.2d 897, 912 n.25 (11th Cir. 1982). For example, witnesses may not be available to the alleged conduct itself, but testimony may be obtained from persons who observed the victim's demeanor immediately after an alleged incident of harassment. A contemporaneous complaint by the victim to co-workers, supervisors, a friend or anyone else may be persuasive evidence that the conduct occurred and it was unwelcomed.

In the present case, the Commission, after very carefully reviewing all the evidence of record, can simply find no evidence at all to corroborate appellant's allegations and is unwilling to rely solely on appellant's word in this case because of a number of internal inconsistencies which cast doubt on her credibility. Appellant alleged that the Director solicited sexual favors in the early morning when a majority of the staff had not yet arrived. She maintained that two female employees arrived within a short period of time after her second conversation with the Director where he solicited sexual favors.... Appellant's allegations that she was asked by the Director to have sex in exchange for job protection is called into question by this employee who held a conversation with appellant within a short time of her being appalled by the Director's request for sexual favors. Appellant testified that the Director's actions shocked her, but her demeanor within thirty minutes after the conversation does not substantiate the allegations. Furthermore, appellant never reported any of the incidents to anyone, except a friend whom she had lunch with on one occasion, but the friend could not recall when she had lunch except that it was two years before the date of the hearing....

The Commission also noted several internal inconsistencies in the appellant's version of events and noted that the allegation of sexual harassment was not brought to the attention of the EEO counselor until two days after she received a notice of proposed termination. By contrast, the Commission found the alleged perpetrator's testimony to be internally consistent and indirectly corroborated by the testimony of workers who never observed any inappropriate comments or behavior on his part.

Evidence to corroborate the testimony of the alleged victim in sexual harassment cases may be supplied by other than direct evidence regarding the specific incidents in question. In Frick v. Postmaster General, 05920272 (1992), the Commission, in the absence of corroborating testimony, nonetheless found sufficient evidence in the fact that the victim made contemporaneous complaints and that there was also evidence that the supervisor engaged in similar conduct with other employees.

Because incidents of alleged sexual harassment most often occur with no witnesses, it is important that agency investigations of sexual harassment complaints consist of a thorough search for corroborative evidence. See Fotina v. Postmaster General, 01920290 (1992). The search should include interviews with any witnesses to whom the complainant allegedly reported the allegations of sexual harassment while the harassment was occurring. This should include interviews with coworkers and personal friends, if necessary. See also Kaplan-Vetter v. Attorney General, 01987002 (2001) (corroborative evidence consisted of complainant telling coworkers about incident when it occurred and a witness who said complainant was in shock after the incident and thereafter avoided the perpetrator).

While corroborative evidence is not necessary, the lack of such evidence, when combined with the questionable credibility of the complainant, can be fatal to a sexual harassment claim. For example, in Hamilton v. Postmaster General, 01951362 (1997), the Commission noted that, given the pervasive nature of the complainant's allegations and the number of alleged perpetrators, it was significant that there was no evidence to corroborate the complainant's allegations.
