1. Fitness-For-Duty Examinations: Performing Essential Functions and Direct Threat

Fitness-for-duty examinations are permissible under the Rehabilitation Act. However, in order to conduct such an examination consistent with ADA requirements incorporated into the Rehabilitation Act, the agency must have a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that the employee's ability to perform essential job functions is impaired by a medical condition. Collier v. Postmaster General, 01982758 (2001). In the alternative, the agency can demonstrate that the employee poses a direct threat to himself or someone else. 29 CFR 1630.2(r). See Chapter 15 under the heading "'Direct Threat' of 'Elevated Risk' Defense."

There is currently a conflict between the EEOC and the MSPB regarding an agency's authority to order a fitness-for-duty examination. First, a discussion of the EEOC's view under the Rehabilitation Act and ADA is appropriate. The Board's view and how it conflicts with the Commission's view is discussed in the section immediately following.

Because the restrictions on preemployment and postappointment medical examinations apply to all applicants and employees, the complainant is not required to establish that he or she is a qualified individual with a disability to prevail in a claim. See, e.g., Ike D. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120162134 (2018); Edmonds v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01A53467 (2005). Likewise, the complainant is not required to establish any discriminatory intent. In Fabish v. Postmaster General, 01981273 (2001), the Commission explained both of these principles and also warned the agency about a fitness-for-duty examination policy that was triggered by absences for mental and nervous conditions:

However, we further find that the FAD failed to analyze complainant's claim that he was improperly ordered to submit to an FFD as a possible violation of the Rehabilitation Act which, like claims regarding denial of reasonable accommodation, does not require any showing of discriminatory intent. An employer may not make disability-related inquiries or require a medical examination of any employee unless the examination is job-related and consistent with business necessity. See 29 CFR 1630.14(c); see also EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Disability-Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations of Employees Under the Americans With Disabilities Act (July 27, 2000) at "General Principles" B & n.3 (noting application of ADA standard to Rehabilitation Act claims). Moreover, a complainant need not establish that he is an individual with a disability in order to raise such a claim under the Rehabilitation Act; the provisions regarding when an employer may require a medical examination apply to all employees, not solely to individuals with disabilities as defined under the Rehabilitation Act. Id.

Under this standard, disability-related inquiries or medical examinations are permitted only where the employer "has a reasonable belief that an employee's present ability to perform essential job functions will be impaired by a medical condition or that s/he will pose a direct threat due to a medical condition." Id. at question 17. In the instant case, the record indicates that there existed a sufficient basis for complainant to have been required to attend an FFD examination. As noted above, complainant states that he advised the agency on November 26, 1996, that he had to leave work in the middle of his tour in order to seek psychiatric care, and was thereafter absent for one week due to what his doctor termed "severe stress." The agency was aware that approximately one month before, complainant had been hospitalized for psychiatric care. Based on these circumstances, the agency was permitted to require that complainant attend an FFD examination prior to returning to work. In discussing this standard in the EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Americans With Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities at question 14, example D (March 25, 1997), the Commission observed that an employer would be justified in ordering an employee with depression to an FFD examination where she sought to return to work after a leave of absence during which she was hospitalized and her medication was adjusted. In the instant case, although complainant asserts that he had returned to work for at least one month following his October, 1996 psychiatric hospitalization, he himself notes that he advised the agency he needed to leave in the middle of his shift on November 26, 1996 in order to seek emergency psychological care, and then required a one week absence due to what his doctor described as "severe stress." These circumstances were sufficient to support a reasonable belief, based on the objective evidence, that complainant's ability to perform essential job functions may have continued to be impaired, and therefore to justify the FFD examination which was ordered.

Notwithstanding this conclusion, however, we note that to the extent the agency also applied its 21-day absence policy as a trigger for ordering an FFD examination, such policies cannot be relied upon in lieu of an individualized assessment of an employee's circumstances. In order to require an FFD examination, the agency must possess a reasonable belief that an employee's present ability to perform essential job functions will be impaired by a medical condition or that s/he will pose a direct threat due to a medical condition. Moreover, as noted above, the agency's policy singles out absences attributable to particular conditions, such as mental or nervous conditions, as warranting an FFD examination regardless of the nature or duration of the absence, the physician's diagnosis and prognosis, or any other information. Again, this may lead agency officials to order FFD examinations where there is no reasonable belief that the employee's present ability to perform essential job functions is impaired, or that a direct threat is posed. In addition, by singling out absences attributed to particular conditions as warranting an FFD examination, the policy may result in agency officials treating individuals with certain disabilities differently because of their disabilities. See Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581, 598 n.10 (1999) (ADA prohibits intraclass disability discrimination against individuals with particular disabilities); Anderson v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01931604 at n.3 (February 17, 1994) (citing Shirey v. Devine, 670 F.2d 1188, 1204 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (same principle noted under the Rehabilitation Act)). Although these concerns do not affect the disposition of the instant complaint, the agency may wish to reconsider the policy at issue, and to instruct its managers accordingly regarding the foregoing standards for disability-related inquiries and medical examinations of employees.

With respect to complainant's additional contention that the agency improperly denied his requests to have a representative present in the proposed psychiatric examination and to tape record the examination, we note that complainant does not contend that he needed this as a disability accommodation, or that the agency's refusal to permit a representative or tape recording at the FFD constituted disparate treatment on any prohibited basis....

Although there is no intent requirement for a complainant to establish a violation of the ADA through an improper fitness-for-duty examination, the complainant can convert such a claim into one that does require a showing of intent by alleging the examination itself was based on a prohibited motive. As an example, in Stenken v. Postmaster General, 0120055305 (2007), the complainant alleged that the agency directed her to submit to a psychiatric fitness-for-duty examination because of her sex and in retaliation for protected activity. The ADA claim is analyzed by determining if the agency had a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that the complainant posed a danger to herself or someone else or was unable to perform the essential functions of her position. In other words, the agency must demonstrate that the examination was job-related and consistent with business necessity. With respect to the claims that the examination was based on sex or reprisal, the complainant bears the burden of proof just as in any other intentional discrimination claim. The complainant must demonstrate that the agency's reasons for the examination are pretext for discrimination or retaliation. As the Commission noted in Stenken:

Here, we will assume arguendo that complainant established a prima facie case of discrimination and retaliation as to the issues herein. The agency has articulated legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for its actions; namely, complainant was sent for a fitness-for-duty examination and subsequently placed in a non-duty status and forced to take sick leave, because she had exhibited behavior which caused management to have a good faith belief that she was a danger to herself or to her co-workers. The agency asserts that the manager's memorandum (contained in the record) describes the series of events that led to the request for the evaluation. In an attempt to establish pretext, complainant contends that the real reason for the agency's actions is that she had complained about sexual harassment. Complainant also contends that the manager's memorandum that is contained in the record might have been fabricated by management after the fact. We find no evidence however, to indicate that the document is not authentic. The AJ's finding that complainant has not proven pretext, by a preponderance of the evidence, is supported by substantial evidence of record. Therefore, after a careful review of the record, including complainant's contentions on appeal, the agency's response, and arguments and evidence not specifically addressed in this decision, we AFFIRM the agency's final order.

Similarly, in Hightower v. Postmaster General, 0120070560 (2008), the Commission found that the agency had articulated a legitimate, nonretaliatory reasons for ordering a psychiatric examination where the complainant told his supervisor to shut up, stated he was leaving work because of stress and that he was afraid of what he might do, stated that he might hurt someone or "could do the ultimate to somebody." Id. The Commission found that the complainant had failed to demonstrate that the agency's reasons were a pretext for retaliation. It also found that the reasons presented the agency with objective evidence that the complainant might be a threat to himself or someone else and thus the examination was job-related and consistent with business necessity.
