C. MARITAL STATUS

The Commission has no jurisdiction over claims based on marital status. Nor does it have jurisdiction over claims based on having or not having children. It only has jurisdiction over claims that discrimination is based on sex. In Duplantier v. Secretary of Navy, 05920609 (1992), the Commission attempted to explain the difference in a case involving an allegation that the complainant, a military spouse with a child, was treated less favorably than a civilian spouse with no children. Finding the complainant failed to state a claim, the Commission distinguished the situation where a married female employee alleges she is treated less favorably than a married male employee, or alleges that an employer treats a female employee with children less favorably than a male employee with children. Both of the latter situations raise claims of discrimination based on sex.

Likewise, in Thompson v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01954193 (1997), the Commission found:

In this case, appellant does not allege that he was treated differently on the basis of his sex, but that he was treated differently based on the fact that he was not married to an agency employee while the selectee was. Where, as here, the claim of discrimination involves only the "plus" portion of the sex-plus concept, such as marital status, then it does not fall within the purview of Title VII or any other statute that the Commission enforces. Benson v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05910427 (May 16, 1991).

Rules that forbid or restrict the employment of married women, but not married men, violate Title VII. "It does not seem to us [the Commission] relevant that the rule is not directed against all females, for so long as sex is a factor in the application of the rule, such application involves a discrimination based on sex." 29 CFR 1604.4(a). The regulation is directed to actual rules proscribing the employment of married women, but would be equally applicable in the absence of a formal rule. For example, the same rationale applies to the selection of a married male candidate over a married female candidate on the assumption that the female would be likely at some point to leave the position in order to raise a family.

Title VII also is broad enough to extend coverage to unmarried mothers. See Tribu v. Department of Army, 01862755 (1987) (citing Phillips v. Martin Marietta Corp., 400 U.S. 542 (1971)).

Title VII does not cover marital status itself and does not prevent rules that bar the employment of married persons, male and female alike. But see 5 USC 7202; 5 USC 2302(b). Without expressing an opinion on whether a marriage status rule could be a bona fide occupational qualification, the Commission notes that any such qualification would have to be related to the particular requirements of a particular job. See 29 CFR 1604.4(b).

The Commission will infer bias against women when a selecting official asks questions of a female candidate about divorce and how her spouse or children feel about her working, but does not ask the same questions of male candidates. See Homan v. Department of Navy, 01862911 (1987) (citing Anderson v. City of Bessemer City, N.C., 557 F. Supp. 412 (W.D.N.C. 1983), aff'd, 470 U.S. 564 (1985)).

In Huth v. Secretary of State, 01954155 (1997), some selectees were given security clearances based on their spouses' agency clearances, and selectees with spouses employed outside of the agency were required to obtain their own. The Commission held that this does not establish discrimination based on sex, but rather discrimination on the basis of the spouse's employment.
