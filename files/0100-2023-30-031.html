broida eeo Chapter 21: Forms of Relief Chapter 21: Forms of Relief, Subchapter 03: Types of Equitable Relief, Section A: Back Pay, Subsection 03: Calculating Back Pay, Part d: Overtime
d. Overtime

An employee is entitled to overtime as part of a back pay award. "The gross back pay determination must reflect fluctuations in working time, overtime rates, penalty overtime, Sunday premium and night work, changing rates of pay, transfers, promotions, and privileges of employment to which [the] petitioner would have been entitled but for the discrimination." Allen v. Secretary of Air Force, 04940006 (1996).

Although entitlement to overtime is sometimes an issue, it is more likely that computation of the overtime will lead to a dispute. Computation of overtime awards can require a degree in higher mathematics. Questions of fair determinations can especially arise in situations where the employee who has been discriminated against has been denied overtime.

In Carroll v. USPS, 01850071 (1987), the Commission was called upon to resolve a dispute between the agency and the employee on the method of calculating the overtime award. The appellant prevailed in alleging that he was improperly denied a comparable amount of overtime with two other employees. The agency proposed to average the overtime worked by all three employees and then deduct the amount of overtime actually worked by the appellant. The Commission found this formulation left the employee less than whole. The correct method, the Commission ruled, was to average the overtime worked by the two similarly situated employees and then deduct the amount of overtime actually worked by the appellant. For those with more analytical minds, the Commission in Carroll expressed the competing formulas as follows:

Total overtime worked/3--Actual overtime appellant worked

or

Overtime hours of non-discriminated employees/2--Appellant's overtime

The latter formula is the one adopted by the Commission.

In other cases, the Commission has found that the agency is not required to award back pay for denied overtime, but the agency must give the complainant the opportunity to make up the overtime on days of his choosing. See, e.g., Phillips v. Postmaster General, 01921161 (1992). The Commission analogized the denial of overtime to the denial of annual leave and noted that its remedy in those cases is to grant the employee annual leave on the days of his choosing. However, the Commission noted there is a difference in overtime in that, unlike annual leave, there must be sufficient work for the agency to justify the overtime. The Commission found that the complainant should have been given a right of first refusal on overtime to make up the time he lost. It further noted that the make-up hours should not interfere with new opportunities for overtime.

The Phillips case, however, seems to be an anomaly. Back pay awards for the denial of overtime should be calculated based upon the average amount of overtime worked by similarly situated employees. See Zito v. Social Security Administration, 04970012 (1997).

In Johnson v. Postmaster General, 04A20010 (2002), the Commission set forth its general guidance on calculation of overtime:

The Commission will next address petitioner's contention that the agency erred in calculating the total number of overtime hours he would have worked during the period he was off the clock when it calculated his overtime award. The Commission has held that the precise amount of overtime back pay a complainant is entitled is to be calculated by taking the average amount of overtime worked by similarly situated agency employees. The Commission has consistently used the pay of similarly situated employees in making overtime rate determinations. This amount must then be reduced by earnings petitioner received from other employment positions, if any, held by petitioner during this period. Sanders v. USPS, EEOC Petition No. 04990018 (April 23, 2001); Sanchez v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01975022 (October 28, 1999); Hanns v. United States Postal Services, EEOC Petition No. 04960030 (September 18, 1997); Allen v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Petition No. 04940006 (May 31, 1996).

In Sanders v. Postmaster General, 04990018 (2001), the Commission rejected the agency's argument that the complainant's overtime award should be premised on the amount of overtime she worked prior to the discrimination:

In calculating the ordered back pay, the agency first determined the salary and pay adjustments for petitioner's position for the back pay period. The agency then examined petitioner's overtime history during the 33 pay periods prior to her termination and determined that she averaged 6.97 hours of regular overtime and 1.89 hours of penalty overtime per pay period. After combining these amounts, the agency paid petitioner $141,380.11 in gross pay for the back pay period. Petitioner contends that the agency's calculation failed to include calculations for interest and Sunday premium pay. Petitioner states that the more accurate method of determining back pay requires examining the pay of similarly situated employees during the back pay period. Using five similarly situated comparison employees, petitioner contends that the correct amount of back pay totals $210,641.20, plus $21,065.58 in interest totaling $231,706.78.

After reviewing the parties' submissions and the policies, regulations and case precedent of the Commission, we find that the agency's back pay calculation is incorrect. We find that the appropriate method of determining the overtime portion of petitioner's back pay is to examine the pay of similarly situated employees during the relevant period. The Commission has consistently used the pay of similarly situated employees in making overtime rate determinations. See Sanchez v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01975022 (October 28, 1999); Hanns v. United States Postal Services, EEOC Petition No. 04960030 (September 18, 1997); Allen v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Petition No. 04940006 (May 31, 1996). As such, we find that the agency did not calculate petitioner's back pay rate correctly. The agency should have calculated the average overtime earned by employees who actually worked during the relevant period. These individuals' pay history represents the best evidence with respect to the overtime hours petitioner would have worked during the period in question. To remedy the unlawful discrimination, the Commission orders the agency to calculate the average overtime compensation provided by similarly situated employees during the period in question and within sixty days issue petitioner an additional back pay award, with interest, reflecting that amount.

However, in Holmes v. Postmaster General, 0720090025 (2010), the Commission upheld the overtime award of an administrative judge that was based on the amount of overtime worked by the complainant prior to the discrimination. The Commission found that judge's determination was an "appropriate estimate" of the overtime the complainant would have worked but for the discrimination.

It is proper for the agency to use overtime compensation received by a complainant as an offset against a back pay award when the new position is one where overtime compensation is not paid. See White v. Department of Navy, 01852251 (1987). The Commission rejected the complainant's contention that the overtime should be recomputed at his new grade and step level, because that would have placed the employee in a better position than if the discrimination had not occurred.

However, an agency may not benefit from its failure to provide reasonable accommodation to an employee through a shift change by calculating back pay based on the shift the employee would have worked if the accommodation had been granted. Back pay calculations, including overtime and premium shift pay, should be based on the shift the employee was working at the time the accommodation was denied. Williams v. Postmaster General, 04990033 (2001).

In determining compensation for missed overtime, the agency may not limit itself to the overtime earned by comparison employees. In Blackwood v. Postmaster General, 01960538 (1996), the Commission noted:

It appears that in proving his claim of disparate treatment discrimination before the AJ, appellant primarily pointed to the agency's more favorable treatment of the Comparisons, who were identified by name in the recommended decision. However, the Commission agrees with appellant that he was not required to establish that each and every employee on Tour 3 was treated more favorably than he with respect to overtime opportunities. The Commission finds that the agency erred in limiting its inquiries to the overtime earned by the Comparisons. Rather, as reflected in the final agency decision, the agency should look to the overtime earned by all "other Tour 3 employees" during the applicable time period.

In Henry v. Postmaster General, 01952601 (1997), the Commission reversed the administrative judge's recommended remedy of providing the complainant the amount of overtime worked by a comparable employee. Instead, the Commission in Henry provided instruction as to how to determine the amount of overtime an appellant would have earned but for discrimination:

As remedy, the AJ recommended an award of overtime pay equal to the overtime worked by the other employee in tour 2, along with interest. Further, the AJ recommended that the agency should investigate to determine whether the appellant is entitled to the requested compensatory damages for reprisal.

In awarding compensation for all the overtime performed by the other tour 2 employee (without prior EEO activity) on the overtime desired list, the AJ however erred. Overtime is not guaranteed to all employees on the overtime list, nor is the overtime made available without any regard for agency needs. Therefore, in determining the amount of overtime pay the appellant would have earned but for discrimination, it is necessary to determine how much overtime work was in fact called for on tours 1, 2 and 3, for which the appellant was qualified during the fourth quarter of 1993,1 and what proportion of that overtime would have been assigned to the appellant but for discrimination. On remand, the agency shall make that determination and award the appellant an appropriate amount with interest.

Though it was a nice try, in Zito v. Social Security Administration, 01954183 (1996), the Commission found that, in calculating a remedy, it is not relevant that the complainant did not work overtime. The relevant question is whether the selectees worked overtime. If so, the complainant is entitled to an appropriate amount of overtime as part of his remedy.

Similarly, in Milder v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A11753 (2002), the Commission rejected an agency argument that had it hired the complainant, it would have had an extra police officer and therefore would not have had to assign overtime. The Commission instead sided with the complainant, who argued that had it hired him instead of the selectee the number of police officers would have been the same.

The agency should fully explain the basis for any overtime pay calculations. If necessary, the agency should seek input from the complainant. See Carney v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04970018 (1998).
