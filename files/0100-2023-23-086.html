3. Evidence of Disability

Because many cases involving drug and alcohol abuse result in removal of the offending employee and many of those employees have Merit Systems Protection Board appeals rights, it is virtually impossible to discuss discrimination law involving such abuse without addressing the views of both the Commission and the Board. As in many areas, the EEOC and the MSPB have somewhat divergent views on what is required to prove the disability of alcoholism or drug addiction. The Board has taken a rigid view that the employee must present evidence from a medical or diagnostic expert on alcohol or drug addiction. See Loveland v. Department of Air Force, 37 MSPR 554 (1988). The Commission has vacillated on the question, sometimes tending to agree with the Board's position on medical evidence and other times retreating from that position. Certainly, the safe course for any complainant pursuing a claim based on alcohol or drug dependency is to provide expert medical evidence. However, the Commission's latest view is that such evidence is not required.

Mere drug or alcohol use or abuse does not qualify an employee for protection under the Rehabilitation Act. See, e.g., Russell v. Secretary of Navy, 01982942 (2001) (employee not regarded as disabled where he was considered drug abuser, but not drug addict); cf. Pugh v. City of Attica, 259 F.3d 619 (7th Cir. 2001) (evidence established that city regarded plaintiff as an alcoholic). The employee is required to make a showing that chemical dependency substantially impairs a major life function to be a qualified individual with a disability. In the alternative, he can show that despite the absence of such impairment, the agency regards him as being disabled. See Davis v. Postmaster General, 03890071 (1989). In Davis, the Commission found there was insufficient medical evidence to support a finding of chemical dependency. The suggestion in Davis seemed to be that such evidence was crucial to establishing such dependency:

First, there is no testimony from petitioner's coworkers or management verifying that petitioner displayed signs of addiction or dependency. Moreover, the medical evidence in the record is meager, and does not conclusively establish that petitioner has a substantially limiting condition which affects a major life activity. The fact that petitioner attended a rehabilitation program is not dispositive, inasmuch as it was ordered by a court as part of petitioner's sentence. Furthermore, although the rehabilitation counselor opined that petitioner was a drug addict and alcoholic, she conceded that her opinion was based upon petitioner's statements which could have been exaggerated.

In Hunt v. Postmaster General, 01901199 (1990), the Commission further elaborated on the medical evidence requirement:

As a threshold matter, one claiming disparate treatment on the basis of a handicapping condition must show that he/she is a handicapped person within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act, i.e., that he/she has, is regarded as having, or has a record of having an impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. 29 CFR 1613.702(a). Accordingly, while alcoholism is widely recognized as a handicapping condition (see, e.g., Rodgers v. Lehman, 869 F.2d 253 (4th Cir. 1989)), an employer does not have to accept an employee's mere assertion that he/she is an alcoholic but, rather, has a right to question the employee about the extent and effects of his/her alleged abuse and/or to request other objective corroborating evidence. Thus, an applicant seeking employment or reinstatement, with a past record of absenteeism or other misconduct, has a burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence, first, that he/she had a dependency on alcohol and, second, that his/her poor attendance or work performance resulted from that dependency.

In this case, appellant attempts to establish that he is handicapped simply by submitted letters from vocational rehabilitation counselors and attendance sheets from an alcohol treatment program; he does not, however, offer any actual proof of his addiction (i.e., an affidavit from himself or his family, friends, and/or coworkers concerning his pattern of alcohol abuse; objective medical evidence based on test results or physical examinations; or an evaluation and assessment by a qualified employee assistance program counselor)....

The Commission remanded the case to give the appellant an opportunity to present such evidence.

In Tapia v. Postmaster General, 01912722 (1993), the Commission appears to have abandoned any requirement for expert medical evidence. The Commission in Tapia overruled the finding of an administrative judge that the testimony of the complainant and the opinion of a rehabilitation counselor were not sufficient to establish that the complainant suffered from alcoholism:

In the Commission's view, the evidence herein--specifically, appellant's testimony and that of the rehabilitation counselor regarding his alcohol dependence and participation in various recovery programs--is sufficient to establish that appellant is an alcoholic and, thus, a person with a disability within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act. In this regard, we disagree with the conclusion of the AJ that appellant's own assertions and the opinion of the rehabilitation counselor based on appellant's treatment after the events leading to his removal were not reliable evidence. We also find that the AJ's reliance on the lack of evidence of work deficiencies is misplaced; it is not necessary for appellant to show a substantial limitation in this particular major life activity to establish that his alcoholism is a disabling condition. Furthermore, with regard to the agency's awareness of appellant's disability, we find that whether the agency was aware prior to the events of February 28-March 2, 1989 is irrelevant, since it was put on notice prior to the issuance of the Notice of Proposed Removal, i.e., by virtue of appellant's March 15, 1989 letter. Accordingly, we find that appellant is a person with a disability within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act.

It is significant to note that in Tapia, although the complainant presented no medical evidence of his alcoholism, he did testify that a physician made a formal diagnosis of alcohol dependency and that he participated in several rehabilitation programs, including programs through the agency's EAP.

The Commission also held, however, that the fact that a complainant sought treatment for an alcohol or substance abuse problem is not sufficient to establish that she has a disability. In Latimer v. Postmaster General, 03950024 (1995), the Commission ruled that such evidence, standing alone, is insufficient:

The Commission has held that alcoholism is a recognized disability under the Rehabilitation Act. While the burden of proving that one is an alcoholic is not onerous, some evidence establishing the disability must be presented. In the present case, there is insufficient evidence in the record showing that petitioner is an alcoholic or substance abuser. The only medical evidence presented by petitioner on the issue of her alleged alcohol and substance abuse is a letter from the VA and one from a community program verifying that petitioner sought treatment. There is no diagnosis of petitioner's condition given, nor does the record provide information describing the nature and extent of her condition. As such, the Commission finds that petitioner is not entitled to the protection of the Rehabilitation Act because she has not met the first threshold step of establishing that she is a person with a disability....

Tapia and Latimer probably are not inconsistent. The distinction, ultimately, is that the complainant in Tapia, while not presenting expert medical testimony, did present testimony from a rehabilitation counselor and other evidence establishing that his alcohol problem was sufficient to rise to the level of a disabling condition that substantially limited a major life activity. But a referral from the agency's employee assistance program to a treatment facility for alcohol and substance abuse is not sufficient evidence to demonstrate the employee suffered from an alcohol or substance related condition. Wardleigh v. Secretary of Air Force, 01921869 (1993). Even where the referral may have occurred after the employee's arrest for driving under the influence, there was no real evidence of an alcohol or substance related condition.

Testing positive for drug use on one occasion does not suffice as evidence of drug addiction. Sonn v. Immigration and Naturalization Service, 019000971 (1990). "While the Rehabilitation Act is intended to provide assistance to addicts who, because of the long term effects of drug abuse, have lost the ability to control behavior, it was not intended to protect those who misuse drugs occasionally, but who retain control of their actions." Id. The fact that the appellants failed a drug screening test was legitimate grounds for refusing to hire them where there was no indication that appellants were viewed as drug addicts.

To make a facial showing that he is a qualified individual with a disability, an employee who is an alcoholic must show that he is able to perform the essential functions of his position when not drinking. Percy v. Postmaster General, 03920057 (1993).
