broida eeo Chapter 15: Reasonable Accommodation and GINA Chapter 15: Reasonable Accommodation and GINA, Subchapter 06: Types of Reasonable Accommodation, Section M: Interpreters and Reading Assistants, Subsection 01: Interpreters and Signers

1. Interpreters and Signers

"[T]he Rehabilitation Act requires that an agency reasonably accommodate hearing-impaired employees by providing effective interpreter services during work-related activities where hearing-impaired employees are expected to be present." Darius C. v. Postmaster General, 2020000613 (2021); see Ortiz v. U.S. Postmaster General, 05960270 (1998). Provision of such services should happen at the activity itself. It is not enough to hold a make-up session with an interpreter for only the hearing-impaired. See Lamar M. v. Postmaster General, 2019005929 (2021) ("the "make up" meeting which subsequently ensued was, by the admission of the Agency's own HR Manager, insufficient to remedy the situation."). Nor is it sufficient to provide the employee with notes from the event. See Darius C. v. Postmaster General, 2020000613 (2021) ("We remind the Agency that the Commission has previously found that providing written materials does not excuse an agency from its obligation to provide a deaf employee with an interpreter simply because the employee can otherwise understand written material. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05880750 (Apr. 18, 1989).")

In Cassady v. USPS, 01841367 (1986), the Commission noted a distinction between "signers" and "interpreters." Signers are persons who know sign language and can communicate with deaf persons. Interpreters are certified specialists who can interpret the spoken word into sign language. In finding that the agency had not fulfilled its duty of accommodation to the deaf by providing signers for safety briefings, the Commission stated that Congress envisioned more than de minimis cost when it required employers to establish that accommodation would create an undue burden. The Commission further took note of the fact that the appellant could be hampered in carrying out her duties in a safe manner due to a failure to properly understand the briefings.

Similarly, in Green v. Postmaster General, 01990477 (2000), the Commission found that an alleged failure to provide an interpreter during a work discussion could be a failure to make reasonable accommodation. The Commission, in Green, in reversing an agency decision dismissing the complaint, held:

After careful review, we find that the agency improperly framed and dismissed the instant complaint. Instead, we determine that the instant complaint is properly framed as a claim of discrimination due to the agency's purported failure to provide a reasonable accommodation for a disability. Here, complainant contends that she is an individual with a disability and must use sign language to communicate with others, and that she requires a sign language interpreter to communicate with those who cannot use sign language. Therefore, by claiming that her supervisor "counseled" her for using her hands excessively to communicate to co-workers, and then refused to provide a sign language interpreter to communicate with her about this matter, we determine that complainant is stating an actionable claim of discrimination due to the agency's purported failure to provide her reasonable accommodation for her disability.

In Reiley v. Postmaster General, 07A10019 (2002), the Commission backed off its stance that interpreters need to be certified and, instead, held that the interpreter must be qualified. Moreover, in Reiley, the Commission held that an agency is permitted to use a volunteer interpreter and not required to have a staff or contract interpreter:

An employer must provide reasonable accommodation to enable an employee with a disability to have equal access to information communicated in the workplace. EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans With Disabilities Act (March 1, 1999) at question 14. We have previously held that "for a severely hearing impaired employee who can sign, reasonable accommodation, at a minimum, requires providing an interpreter for safety talks, discussions on work procedures, policies or assignments, and for every disciplinary action so that the employee can understand what is occurring at any and every crucial time in his employment career, whether or not he asks for an interpreter." Feris v. Environmental Protection Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 01934828 (August 10, 1995); see also Funk v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01984772 (July 27, 2001); Thomas v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01982321 (February 2, 2001). Inasmuch as the agency concedes that it did not provide an interpreter on numerous occasions which would have fallen within this range of situations, the agency's argument that complainant's claim was not proven in the absence of testimony regarding the specific occasions on which she was denied an interpreter is unavailing.

To the extent the agency contends that it was not on notice that the alternative means used by the PM at the Warrington Post Office to communicate with complainant were ineffective, and that the use of the custodian as an interpreter or the use of note-writing for communication between complainant and the PM was in fact effective, we find that the AJ's factual findings to the contrary are supported by substantial evidence in the record. In particular, the AJ credited complainant's testimony that she often did not understand the sign language interpretation done for the PM by the custodian or the written communications or notes given to her by the PM, and that her dissatisfaction was communicated to the PM. The AJ also noted that the PM conceded that early in her tenure she wrote to complainant that she was not permitted to spend the money for an interpreter.

We further note that while an agency is permitted to use a volunteer rather than a staff or contract interpreter, and while an interpreter need not be certified, under any circumstances the interpreter must be qualified. See 29 C.F.R. 1630.2(o)(2)(ii). We note that serving as a reader or interpreter is not always a simple task, and agencies have an obligation to ensure that any volunteers who are used are providing effective accommodation. The Commission has previously found agencies liable for violating the Rehabilitation Act where the interpreter provided was not qualified. See, e.g., Ortiz v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 05960270 (October 16, 1998); Marcum v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01892554 (January 22, 1990).
