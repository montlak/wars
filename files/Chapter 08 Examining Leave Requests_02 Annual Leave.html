Federal Managers Guide 02/09/2023 Chapter 08: Examining Leave Requests Chapter 08: Examining Leave Requests, 02: Annual Leave

ANNUAL LEAVE

Annual leave can be used for any purpose the employee desires, and it can be accrued to allow for vacations of two weeks or even more. A significant amount of it can even be carried over from one year to the next.

However, annual leave entitlements may challenge you. You want your employees to enjoy the full benefits of their federal employment, but it is your job to see that your unit's work gets done and productivity is maintained. You must juggle your staffing needs and your employees' annual leave entitlements. You also need to take other forms of leave into account, such as sick leave and leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act.

If you don't pay attention, you may have no one around to do the work. Inevitably, you will have to make some hard decisions about whether to approve annual leave requests.

Although HR will assist you, you have the primary responsibility for administering your employees' annual leave. Generally, this includes:

  • • Approving or denying leave requests.
  • • Scheduling leave to ensure adequate staffing.
  • • Preventing avoidable leave loss caused by "use it or lose it" rules.
  • • Preventing misuse of annual leave to cover attendance problems.
  • • Requiring adherence to appropriate leave request procedures.
  • • Applying labor agreement provisions pertaining to annual leave.

Federal employees earn annual leave at different rates, depending on their length of service or type of position:

  • • Employees with less than three years of service earn four hours of leave per pay period (two weeks), or 13 days per year.
  • • Those with between three and 15 years of service earn six hours per pay period, with the exception of 10 hours in the last pay period, or 20 days per year.
  • • Those with 15 years or more of service earn eight hours per pay period, or 26 days per year.
  • • Employees in the Senior Executive Service, and in positions designated senior level, scientific or professional, also earn eight hours per pay period, 26 days per year.

Employees newly hired into the federal civilian service may be given credit in their accrual rate for military or nonfederal work experience that relates to their new position.

Check with HR, and make sure you know how much annual leave each of your employees has accrued, so you can look at your expected workflow and plan your staffing levels accordingly.

Advanced leave. If an employee doesn't have sufficient accrued annual leave to cover an absence, they may request that the agency advance them annual leave before it has been earned. By law, an agency may advance annual leave to an employee up to the amount the employee would accrue within the leave year.

Agencies have discretion in deciding whether to advance annual leave. Annual leave should not be advanced when it is known (or reasonably expected) that the employee will not return to duty, such as when the employee has applied for disability retirement.

When an employee requests an advance of annual leave, managers should consult their HR office on agency policies and labor agreement provisions that govern advanced annual leave. Before granting advanced annual leave, a manager should consider the expectation of return to duty, the need for the employee's services, and the benefits to the agency of retaining the employee.

A Jan. 15, 2015, presidential memorandum directed agencies to ensure that their policies offer the maximum amount of advanced annual leave permitted by law for foster care placement in their home or bonding with a healthy newborn or newly adopted child. This benefit must be provided irrespective of existing leave balances.

Leave carryover. Encourage and enable your employees to use as much of their leave as possible in the year it is accrued. But it's not always possible or desirable to use all of it, either because of the workload of your unit or your employees' personal situations. However, federal employees can accrue annual leave up to a maximum of 240 hours (30 days), and 360 hours (45 days) for overseas assignments, to be carried over from one year to the next.

In most cases, leave in excess of the maximum allowable accumulation is wiped off the books at the end of the year ("use or lose"). Under limited circumstances -- the leave was forfeited due to administrative error, or due to an exigency of the public business or sickness of the employee when the leave was scheduled in advance -- forfeited annual leave may be restored. You should do everything possible to prevent the loss of your employees' earned leave. However, if the loss of excess leave is inevitable, programs such as the Voluntary Leave Bank or the Voluntary Leave Transfer program may be viable considerations for your employees. Consult with HR to find out more about these programs.

Purpose. Employees are entitled to use annual leave for whatever purpose they choose, and you are not allowed to deny requests for annual leave simply because you do not approve of the purposes for which it will be used. This is a major difference between annual leave and other forms of leave, such as sick leave, FMLA leave, or leave without pay, which may require the employee to demonstrate that the purpose of a leave request meets certain conditions.

Leave request procedures

Although employees are entitled to use annual leave, they must receive prior approval from the appropriate management official. Your agency has specific request procedures, and you should enforce them.

Remember that employees are entitled to use annual leave for any purpose. Even if a request is made at the last minute, you should approve it if workload and staffing requirements permit, regardless of its purpose.

You are not required to grant requests for annual leave that are filed after an unexpected absence, although you may do so if you feel circumstances justify it. For example, you may permit an employee who comes in late to use annual leave to cover the absence, if you feel that the employee has a reasonable excuse.

Note: HR can tell you the shortest amount of time your agency allows to be logged as annual leave. In most cases this is one hour, although periods as short as six minutes are allowed by some agencies.

Unplanned absences

Failure to request leave in advance is the most common violation of request procedures. "On the spot" requests for annual leave -- particularly when raised well after the workday has begun or when the unit would be understaffed -- can present a major challenge, as can call-in requests by employees who unexpectedly do not report to work. You are not required to approve such requests if they would result in workload or operational problems.

But you may approve them, and often you should. Emergencies do come up. Annual leave can be approved in increments to cover last-minute requests for short periods of leave, as well as to cover employees who come in late. Retroactive requests for annual leave can be approved for good reason, such as when an employee who usually comes in on time reports that serious traffic problems or a family emergency caused the lateness.

Consider this scenario: One of your employees with an excellent attendance record, calls you early one morning and says, "Boss, I'm sorry. I know this is no way to request annual leave, but I need the day off to take care of a personal matter that's just come up." What would you do?

The one thing you should not do is ask, "What's so important that you can't come into work?" The employee has no obligation to share with you the reason for the leave request, and in this instance, you should not ask.

You would be well within your rights to deny the request and order your employee to come to work. You could log the late time as leave without pay, or even absence without leave.

But why would you? Your employee has a good attendance record, which means this late request is not part of a pattern of leave abuse. The employee has acknowledged their request is not a usually acceptable one. Chances are they have a good reason to request the day's leave. If the employee is absolutely needed at work, at least part of the day, you could explain the problem and ask them to come in at that time, granting annual leave for the rest of the day. But in most cases the best course is simply to approve the leave.

If you do not, what is likely to happen the next time an emergency comes up in that employee's life or in the life of any coworker they talk to about the incident? Your employees will call in sick for the day, instead of requesting annual leave, and you won't generally have any choice but to approve their requests. A generous approach to annual leave approval may make your employees less inclined to abuse sick leave.

Need to ask. Because you must balance the work of the agency against the interest of the employee in using annual leave, you may find it necessary on occasion to ask employees how they will use the requested annual leave so that you can make informed decisions about scheduling the leave. Again, employees are not required to provide you with this information but should understand that in the absence of such information, their request for annual leave may be denied based on project-related deadlines or the workload of the agency. However, you should not make it a standard practice to require that employees inform you how the annual leave will be used.

Warning: Just be careful that you do not allow last-minute and retroactive requests to be used as a cover for chronic attendance problems.

Leave without pay can also be used to cover lateness. You may have the option of allowing employees to make up time lost, rather than charging any form of leave. And short periods of justified lateness or absence from a workstation can simply be written off as excused absences. If lateness becomes chronic, you may have to find the employee absent without leave and begin disciplinary procedures.

Note on overtime: The law does not prevent you from approving both annual leave and overtime for an employee in the same week, or even on the same day, but some agencies frown on or prohibit the practice. Check with HR. If your agency has these prohibitions in place, unexpected use of annual leave may complicate your overtime schedule.

Warning: If the unplanned absence situation is getting out of hand and you need to tighten up procedures, warn your employees before you start finding them AWOL or placing them on LWOP. And, if you are more lenient with some employees, be prepared to justify this differential treatment (e.g., some employees have better attendance records). Otherwise, you may face successful grievances or discrimination claims.

You are not required to approve annual leave retroactively. A denial of a request after the fact amounts to a finding that the employee was AWOL. The employee should not be paid for the period of absence, and you may consider beginning disciplinary procedures.

However, if you do approve an annual leave request (or any other form of leave), regardless of your misgivings, you cannot later discipline the employee for being AWOL. Under some circumstances you may be able to discipline for failure to follow request procedures, but this should be reserved for serious leave abuse cases.

Workload and staffing

Leave requests tend to cluster around certain popular times of the year, such as holidays and peak vacation periods. Often labor agreements contain specific procedures for determining the priority in which employee leave requests must be considered if they cannot all be approved. These provisions must be observed.

If there are no labor agreement provisions, adopt and stick to a fair procedure for determining which requests are approved. There may be a local or agency policy. Check with HR.

Warning: Because the end of the year, a popular time to request leave, is also the time when the entitlement to unused leave in excess of the permitted number of carryover hours (240 hours, 360 hours for overseas assignments) expires, some employees may be in a "use or lose" situation. It is your responsibility to keep this from happening by tracking leave entitlements and working with your employees to schedule leave throughout the year.

Sometimes workload or staffing requirements simply do not permit approval of annual leave. Managers must apply good faith and good judgment in making such determinations, but once these determinations are made, employees are required to abide by them.

Leave cancellations. It may be necessary to rescind approval of annual leave to meet a workload or staffing emergency. Cancellation of approved annual leave is not popular. Nor is it something that should be done frequently or without very good cause. But you are empowered to cancel leave approvals.

Slack periods. What if there is not enough work for everybody? Generally, you cannot force employees to take leave when they do not want to, although you can certainly encourage it during slack periods. Some industrially oriented operations actually shut down for a week or two each year. Because there is no work available for employees during such periods, they are usually given the choice of taking annual leave or leave without pay or are found to be AWOL if they will not request leave.

Leave situations to heed

Usually, an employee requests annual leave, you approve or reject the request, and that ends the matter. But if the employee is not satisfied with your rejection decision, the result may be a grievance or EEO complaint. For that reason, as well as for the sake of employee morale and your own credibility as a manager, you should not reject a proper request for available annual leave without a very good reason. Communicate that reason to the employee and try to obtain the employee's understanding whenever possible.

However, in some cases, employees may be entitled to your approval of their leave requests, no matter how good your reason for denying them. Leave may be required as a reasonable accommodation for an employee with a disability, or it may come under the leave entitlement of the Family and Medical Leave Act.

Reasonable accommodation. Under the Rehabilitation Act, qualified employees with disabilities are entitled to reasonable accommodations so they can effectively perform their duties. Some accommodations may require absences from work, and the employee may wish to use annual leave to cover all or part of these absences.

In these circumstances, your refusal to approve leave could lead to a successful EEO complaint. Whenever an employee mentions a possible disability or requests leave because of that disability, you should contact HR immediately. The law on reasonable accommodation is complex, and you should not try to interpret it on your own.

FMLA leave. If you reject an annual leave request and the employee shows that the leave is required for a purpose that qualifies under the FMLA, you will have to approve it. HR will require the employee to provide appropriate medical certification.

The FMLA permits federal employees to use up to 12 weeks per year of LWOP when incapacitated by serious health conditions, to care for family members (parent, spouse, or child) with serious health conditions, and for the birth, adoption, or fostering of a child. Available annual leave may be substituted for LWOP.

You may request that scheduled medical procedures be rescheduled to better accommodate your unit's staffing requirements, but this is only possible if the health care provider agrees to the rescheduling. Otherwise, the original leave request must be approved.

Sick leave for annual leave. An employee may become ill or be injured while on annual leave. In this case, the employee may substitute available sick leave for annual leave for the days the employee was ill or incapacitated by injury.

Should the employee make this request, it should be treated like any other request for sick leave. You have the right to request administratively acceptable proof that the employee was in fact ill or injured and incapacitated on the days in question. In most cases, the employee's word is good enough for incapacitation of less than three days.

Leave during incarceration. Remember that annual leave can be used for any purpose. This means an employee who cannot come to work because of incarceration can request annual leave. You are not entitled to deny leave simply because the employee is in jail. You may deny it, however, if you would have done so regardless of the purpose for which it was being requested. In other words, if the reason for denial is workload or staffing issues, you may do so -- and charge the employee's absence as AWOL.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Allow employees to routinely disregard request procedures. This is a good way to lose track of who has used leave and who will be in on a particular day. And if you are lax in administering leave requests, you will find it difficult to discipline those who most seriously violate procedures.
  • • Approve annual leave to cover chronic attendance problems. If an employee is occasionally late for good cause, there's nothing wrong with either excusing it or allowing the employee to use annual leave. However, you shouldn't allow an employee to routinely use annual leave to cover chronic tardiness or other attendance problems.
  • • Allow "use or lose" leave to accumulate to year's end. Managers who allow this to happen find themselves in the uncomfortable bind of either rejecting leave requests and forcing employees to lose leave or granting the requests and lacking sufficient staff to get the job done. It's better to encourage employees to use leave earlier in the year.
  • • Disregard employee entitlements under FMLA leave and EEO law. Employees may be entitled to annual leave even when you don't want to approve it, as a reasonable accommodation for a disability or for an FMLA-qualifying purpose. Consult HR.

Ask FMG

Q. Can I disapprove a request for annual leave?

A. Yes, but leave should only be rejected for good reasons; for example, workload or staffing requirements. Dislike of the employee's reason for taking annual leave is not a valid reason.

Q. Can I cancel previously approved leave?

A. Yes, but this should only be done for very good reasons, such as major unforeseen workload demands or an emergency situation.

Q. Can I require an employee to take annual leave?

A. No. You can, however, encourage an employee to take annual leave, or offer the options of annual leave or LWOP in appropriate circumstances, such as when a unit is shutting down for a short period.

Q. Do I have to approve leave requested after an employee has already missed the work period?

A. No, but you may use your judgment and approve it. If the employee has a good reason why the leave was not requested in advance, you may approve it. If you don't see a good reason to do so, you don't have to grant the request. In that case, the employee must be considered AWOL for the period of absence and not paid. You may consider discipline.

Q. Can I approve leave requests for small increments of time?

A. It depends on your agency regulations or labor agreement. Most agencies allow leave in increments of one hour, though some permit increments as short as six minutes.

Q. Can I allow an employee to take annual leave before it is earned?

A. By law, an agency may advance annual leave up to the amount the employee would accrue within the leave year. Consult your HR office on agency policies and labor agreement provisions because as part of the determination, you will need to consider whether the employee is expected to return to duty, the need for the employee's services, and the benefits to the agency of retaining the employee.

Q. Can an employee take annual leave and work overtime in the same day or week?

A. Although there's nothing in the law that prohibits doing so, your agency may have a policy that discourages or prohibits mixing annual leave and overtime in the same day or week. Consult HR on this issue.

Q. Can an employee retroactively substitute sick leave for annual leave?

A. Yes, if the employee was incapacitated by illness or injury for the days of the incapacitation. You have the right to require administratively acceptable evidence, as for any sick leave request.
