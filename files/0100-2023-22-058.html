broida eeo Chapter 13: Bases of Discrimination Under Title VII Chapter 13: Bases of Discrimination Under Title VII, Subchapter 02: Sex Discrimination, Section N: Equal Pay Act, Subsection 02: EPA Analysis and Burdens of Proof/Substantially Equal Work, Part i: "Bureaucratic Inertia"
i. "Bureaucratic Inertia"

In Jacobsen v. Secretary of Navy, 01200529571 (2006), the Commission found that its administrative judge erred in finding that "bureaucratic inertia" was a defense to an EPA claim. In addition to questioning there was such inertia, the Commission in Jacobsen, explained:

Upon review of this matter, we find that the AJ erred in her legal conclusion that "bureaucratic inertia" is a valid defense to complainant's EPA claim. The AJ cited Cayce v. Adams, 439 F. Supp 606, 608-609 (D.D.C. 1977) for the proposition that bureaucratic inertia is a valid EPA defense. However, in Cayce, the court merely found that no Equal Pay Act violation occurred until the agency was aware that the plaintiff was performing work equal to employees paid at the higher level, but a violation occurred from the time "there was a knowing failure to pursue a classification investigation designed to put male and female employees doing 'equal work' on an equal footing."

In this case, there is ample evidence that the agency was not merely asleep at the bureaucratic wheel, but was aware of the fact that complainant was doing work equal to the work male Level IV employees were doing but was being paid less. The Director of the Security Assistance Directorate from 1990-2000 testified that he repeatedly expressed his concern that complainant was not being paid the same salary as Level IV CPDs for equal work beginning in 1997 and cautioned management that "we could wind up having problems because of that." He further affirmed that a corporate review group made up of directors, counsel, and a personnel officer discussed complainant's salary differential. Similarly, a Deputy Director testified that complainant approached him in 1997 about the wage differential, in Telford v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01973892 (November 2, 1999), we held that a classification system offers no defense when the employee performed work beyond the job classification with the employer's knowledge and consent.

Further in Werners v. General Services Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 01880297 (May 9, 1988), we rejected the agency's purported defense that it was prevented from remedying a sex-based wage differential because of the agency's classifiers and the Office of Personnel Management. We found that "if an agency is aware of, but does not timely correct, a pay discrepancy resulting from its classification system, then the system is no longer a valid affirmative defense." Similarly, in this case, the agency cannot use bureaucratic inertia or its payband system as a valid affirmative defense because the record clearly reflects that at least since 1997, it has been aware that complainant was receiving less pay than male co-workers doing equivalent work. Neither the misclassification of complainant's payband nor bureaucratic inertia can serve as a valid defense where the record shows that complainant performed work equal to that of her higher paid male counterparts. See EEOC v. Maricopa County Community College District, 736 F. 2d 510, 515 (9th Cir. 1984); see also Telford v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01973892 (November 2, 1999). Even if the agency is vaguely asserting that complainant was erroneously paid less than men for substantially equivalent work, such an error would not constitute a valid defense under the EPA. EEOC Compliance Manual: Section 10 Compensation Discrimination (Compliance Manual), Section 10-IV, F.2.i. (December 5, 2000). Moreover, the agency has not linked complainant's lower pay for equivalent work here to any reasonable business purpose. Therefore, we find that the AJ erred when she found that misclassification and bureaucratic inertia were valid defenses.
