11. Retaliation Exception to Collateral Attacks Rule

The Commission makes an exception to the collateral attacks rule when the complainant alleges retaliation for protected activity. See Gully v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A55464 (2005) (allegation that agency issued request for information letter making false accusations about complainant to set the state for disciplinary action due to protected activity stated a claim). Although the Commission has been somewhat inconsistent in recognizing its own exception to the collateral attack rule, the Supreme Court's ruling in Burlington Northern & Sante Fe Railway Co. v. White, 126 S. Ct. 2405 (2006), makes it far more likely that claims based on retaliation will not be dismissed as collateral attacks on other proceedings. But see Thomas v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01200640121 (2006) (being subjected to an OIG investigation does not aggrieve the employee for purposes of stating a claim for relief). See also, subsection on "Standing: Failure to State a Claim--Reprisal Complaints," above and Chapter 17, subsection on "Adverse Treatment Requirement."

An example, without specifically referencing Burlington Northern, is provided by Wills v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A63185 (2006). In that case, the complainant alleged that he was interviewed by a civilian aviation security officer ostensibly about an anonymous complaint involving two other employees, who were witnesses in his pending EEO case. The complainant alleged that during the interview he was "brow--beaten" about his EEO activity. The agency dismissed the complaint as a collateral attack on a security investigation. The Commission reversed dismissal of the complaint in Wills finding that "Complainant's allegations, if true, would support a claim of interference with complainant's protected participation in the EEO complaint process by involving him and his two witnesses in a 'bogus' investigation that included an examination of their EEO activity. Therefore, we conclude that these allegations are sufficient to state a claim of reprisal which the agency should investigate." However, there is still a great deal of inconsistency in the Commission's handling of reprisal complaints alleging a chilling effect. In contrast to Wills is Eason v. Secretary of Navy, 0120064566 (2007). In Eason, the complainant alleged that he was subjected to reprisal when, in an investigation into damage to a police vehicle, he was ordered to unload his weapon, required to attend an investigative interview, not read his rights, denied a union representative, and was questioned about his body marks and tattoos. The Commission found that the allegations failed to state a claim for relief.

The Commission's current position is set forth in Finn v. Postmaster General, 0120113481 (2012):

Complainant alleges that the investigation of which he was the subject was initiated in retaliation for his having filed an EEO complaint. The Agency contends that Complainant is not aggrieved by being the subject of an investigation because no concrete action against him resulted from the investigation. The Agency's position is not well taken. Complainant has alleged an injury or harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. See Diaz v. Dep't of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994). In recent cases presenting similar circumstances, we have found that the initiation and processing of internal investigations for retaliatory motives can state a viable reprisal claim. See Santiago v. Department of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 0720100038 (March 2, 2011); De Vore v. Department of Justice (Federal Bureau of Prisons), EEOC Request No. 0520100546 (January 27, 2011); Murphy v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0120102787 (October 26, 2010). We find Complainant has alleged sufficient harm to warrant investigation into his claim and further processing of his complaint.

Similarly, in Lamar D. v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120161539 (2016), the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of a reprisal complaint as a collateral attack on an investigation conducted by the agency's Accountability Board. The complainant in Lamar D. maintained that his complaint was not a collateral attack but, rather, that the agency used the Accountability Board process to harass him. He further defended his complaint with the following details:

Complainant noted that the actions by management followed actions taken by Complainant with respect to his prior EEO Complaints. Complainant asserted that, with claim (1) [that the management used to the Accountability Board process to harass and retaliate against him for prior EEO activity], management ignored his complaints with the Accountability Board. However, following Complainant's prior EEO activity, the Agency decided to direct the investigations into old complaints filed with the Accountability Board against Complainant by a coworker.

Still case law exists that being ordered to participate in an agency investigation does not aggrieve an employee where there is no disciplinary action taken. Vanderbout v. Postmaster General, 01A05349 (2002). Despite the inconsistency, the more accepted approach appears to be that expressed in Santiago v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720100038 (2011), where the complainant alleged he was subjected to an internal investigation by the Office of Professional Responsibility in retaliation for EEO activity. In reversing the agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to state a claim, the Commission, in Santiago, explained:

At the outset, we address the Agency's contention that Complainant's claim that SAC 1 retaliated against him when she made a referral of his misconduct to OPR should be dismissed for failure to state a claim.

The Commission has held that merely conducting an investigation into purported improper or illegal conduct does not cause any injury without more, for example, resulting disciplinary action. Shelly v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01996655 (Oct. 27, 2000). It has also applied this rule to the initiation of an internal investigation. Martin v. Dep't of Justice (Federal Bureau of Prisons), EEOC Appeal No. 01A32934 (Sept. 17, 2003). The Commission has held that an employee cannot use the EEO complaint process to lodge a collateral attack on another proceeding. See Wills v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05970596 (July 30, 1998); Kleinman v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940585 (September 22, 1994); Lingad v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930106 (June 25, 1993).

The Commission has a policy of considering reprisal claims with a broad view of coverage. See Carroll v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05970939 (April 4, 2000). Under Commission policy, claimed retaliatory actions which can be challenged are not restricted to those which affect a term or condition of employment. Rather, a complainant is protected from any discrimination that is reasonably likely to deter protected activity. See EEOC Compliance Manual Section 8, "Retaliation," No. 915.003 (May 20, 1998), at 8-15; Carroll, EEOC Request No. 05970939.

Here, Complainant alleged in his EEO complaint that SAC 1 contacted OPR to initiate an investigation against him in reprisal for his participation in protected EEO activity. The Commission has found in similar cases that such a claim states a claim of reprisal. See De Vore v. Dept. of Justice, EEOC Request No. 0520100546 (January 27, 2011); see also EEOC Compliance Manual Section 8, "Retaliation," No. 915.003 (May 20, 1998), at 8-15, footnote 42 (citing Berry v. Stevinson Chevrolet, 74 F.3d. 980, 986 (10th Cir. 1996) (instigating criminal theft and forgery charges against former employee who filed EEOC charge found retaliatory)). Thus, we decline to dismiss Complainant's complaint for failure to state a claim.

The mere fact that another agency may have jurisdiction over some of the claims involved does not make it a collateral attack on another proceeding. In Anava v. Postmaster General, 0120111835 (2011), the agency dismissed several allegations by a deaf employee, including that the agency had not installed safety lights in the facility, as being a collateral attack on a matter within the jurisdiction of the Occupational Health and Safety Administration. The Commission, in Anava, reversed finding the complainant was alleging that he was being discriminated against based on his disability and in retaliation for protected EEO activity.

A claim that the complainant was discriminatorily arrested and given a parking citation by agency police is a collateral attack on the agency's parking regulations. Tibbs v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A54451 (2005).
