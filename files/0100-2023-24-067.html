C. AGGRAVATION OF EXISTING CONDITION

An agency does not fulfill its obligation to accommodate an employee by virtue of the fact that it offered the employee an acceptable accommodation when the employee's condition becomes aggravated and the accommodation no longer allows the employee to perform his duties. Sabella v. USPS, 01840090 (1986). In that case, the agency already accommodated the employee's back problems by placing him in a special program jointly sponsored by the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs and the U.S. Postal Service to accommodate disabled employees. The employee subsequently aggravated his existing back condition under the limited lifting requirements of his accommodation. The Commission held the agency was required to make an additional accommodation for the employee which eliminated the lifting requirement entirely.

In some instances, the aggravation of an existing condition can occur through the unwillingness of the agency to offer accommodation to an employee with a disability or through withdrawal of a previously provided accommodation. For a severely disabled person, an agency's failure in this regard can become so stressful as to be totally debilitating. In Watson v. Veterans Administration, 03860058 (1986), the agency reassigned a severely disabled employee to a new position and proceeded to remove all previous accommodations provided. The employee, who always received satisfactory performance appraisals in the past, could not function in the new position without the accommodations. As a result, the employee developed hypertension that rendered him unable to work. The employee was removed because he could not meet the mental and physical requirements of his position. The Commission reversed the finding of the MSPB that the removal was not based on disability discrimination noting that it was "only because he was placed in an untenable position...that petitioner was forced out of his job into leave status and a disabling condition of stress." Id. The employee's claim was bolstered by the fact that his workers' compensation claim for a stress-related condition was approved.

In some instances it may not be unreasonable for an employee to fail to report to work as a result of the agency's failure to afford reasonable accommodation. In Tampakes v. Postmaster General, 01913030 (1992), the complainant presented a prima facie case of disparate treatment by demonstrating that employees without disabilities were allowed to take prescription and nonprescription medication while at work, but she was denied the opportunity to use a breathing machine for treatment of bronchial asthma. As part of its legitimate reason for not permitting the complainant to use the breathing machine, the agency argued that the complainant was absent from work over 50% of the time and, when at work, she constantly left her work station to use the breathing machine. The Commission, noting that the appellant's physician cleared her for a full return to duty contingent upon being allowed to use the breathing machine at work, found that the complainant had no choice but to remain home from work when the agency denied her use of the breathing machine. The agency was ordered to reinstate the complainant from the time of her removal until the time of her voluntary retirement a year later.
