V. OVERVIEW OF LIABILITY FOR HARASSMENT

As noted in the introduction to this chapter, the questions of whether harassment occurred and whether the employer should be held liable for harassment are distinctly different questions. It is entirely possible for an employee to establish that he or she has been harassed on a protected basis and yet be unable to establish that the employer should be held liable for that harassment.

The rules of liability, as discussed in detail below, were established in sexual harassment cases. However, in Long v. Attorney General, 01984213 (2001), the Commission made clear that those rules apply to all harassment cases, regardless of the protected basis:

[R]ecently the Supreme Court and the Commission have departed from the commonly used categories of "quid pro quo" and hostile work environment harassment, in the context of supervisory liability, finding it more useful analytically to distinguish between harassment that results in a tangible employment action and harassment that creates a hostile work environment. See Burlington Indus. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 760-65, 118 S. Ct. 2257, 141 L. Ed.2d 633 (1998); Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775, 807, 118 S. Ct. 2275, 141 L. Ed.2d 662 (1998); EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Vicarious Liability for Unlawful Harassment by Supervisors, EEOC Notice No. 915.002, at 2, n.7 (June 18, 1999). It is this dichotomy that determines whether the employer can raise the affirmative defense to vicarious liability.

In Ellerth and Faragher, the Supreme Court made clear that employers are subject to vicarious liability for unlawful harassment by supervisors. The standard of liability set forth in these decisions is premised on two principles: 1) an employer is responsible for the acts of its supervisors; and 2) employers should be encouraged to prevent harassment and employees should be encouraged to avoid or limit the harm from harassment. In order to accommodate these principles, the Court held that an employer is always liable for a supervisor's harassment if it culminates in a tangible employment action. However, if it does not, the employer may be able to avoid liability or limit damages by establishing an affirmative defense that includes two necessary elements: (a) the employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct promptly any harassing behavior; and (b) the employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of any preventive or corrective opportunities provided by the employer or to avoid harm otherwise.

While the Faragher and Ellerth decisions addressed sexual harassment, the Court's analysis drew upon standards set forth in cases involving harassment on other protected bases. Moreover, the Commission has always taken the position that the same basic standards apply to all types of prohibited harassment. Thus, the standard of liability set forth in the decisions applies to all forms of unlawful harassment.

The Commission considers prevention to be the most effective tool in eliminating sexual harassment and encourages employers to engage in education and other measures designed to develop sensitivity to the issue. See 29 CFR 1604.11(f). Although prevention may be the best way for agencies to avoid liability for sexual harassment, it is not the only means. The EEOC has delineated five separate bases on which an agency may avoid liability under Title VII for hostile environment harassment. The bases were set forth in Crane v. Postmaster General, 01924585 (1993):

In order to avoid liability for hostile environment sexual harassment, an agency must show that: (1) the acts or conduct complained of did not occur; (2) the acts or conduct complained of were not "unwelcome"; (3) the alleged harassment was not "sufficiently severe or pervasive" to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment; (4) immediate and appropriate corrective action was taken as soon as the employer was put on notice; and/or (5) there is no basis for imputing liability to the employer under agency principles. See Vinson, supra.

The first two defenses are, in reality, factual defenses. Since the complainant bears the burden of proof in a Title VII case, the agency is not required to prove that the acts did not occur, or that they were not unwelcome. The agency need only present sufficient evidence to prevent the complainant from carrying her burden of proof. The third defense is a legal defense. Although it is listed as a defense, it is again important to bear in mind that the complainant has the burden of proof. That is, under Harris, the complainant must show that a reasonable person would have found the environment hostile, offensive or abusive, and that the particular complainant did so find the environment. An agency succeeds in defense when it prevents a complainant from making either showing. Of course, even though the complainant has the burden of proof, nothing prevents an agency from affirmatively demonstrating these defenses.

The fourth and fifth defenses are ways for the agency to escape liability even though sexual harassment has occurred. It is worth noting that they are phrased in the alternative. They are not so much two separate ways of avoiding liability as they are two ways of stating essentially the same thing. Under Crane, in the first instance, the agency can show that it took prompt and effective remedial action upon learning of the alleged harassment. However, this defense only applies to harassment by coworkers or third parties who are not agency employees. If the harassment is engaged in by management officials, the agency would have little chance of demonstrating that knowledge of the harassment should not be imputed to it. In that case, the burden shifts to the agency to avoid liability by demonstrating that it took prompt and effective corrective action as soon as it knew or should have known of the harassment, and that the complainant failed to take advantage of preventive measures or otherwise avoid harm. It is this latter defense that was the subject of Burlington Indus., Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 118 S. Ct. 2257, 141 L. Ed.2d 633 (1998) and Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775, 118 S. Ct. 2275, 141 L. Ed.2d 662 (1998), discussed below. In order to avoid liability, an agency must do "'whatever is necessary' to end harassment, to make a victim whole, and to prevent the misconduct from recurring." Maxine C. v. Postmaster General, 0120162531 (2018). "Taking only some remedial action does not absolve the agency of liability where that action is ineffective." Id.

Under Title VII, the term "employer" is defined to include "agents" of the employer. See 42 USC 2000e(b). Cases in which the perpetrator of the sexual harassment was the owner of a business created little difficulty for the courts. The employer could be held liable for his own conduct. But the basis for liability was much less clear when the perpetrator was a supervisor, manager or coworker. Virtually every court agreed that liability in those cases was to be determined by applying the principles of agency law. Agreement evaporated, however, on which of those principles applied and how.

Some early cases rejected sexual harassment as a violation of Title VII, in part because there was no action on the part of the employer that was implicated. Except in cases where the owner was the perpetrator, courts reasoned that there was no basis for imputing liability to the employer based on actions that were motivated by the personal desires of a supervisor and furthered no work purpose. For example, in Corne v. Bausch & Lomb, Inc., 390 F. Supp. 161, 163 (D. Ariz. 1975), rev'd on other grounds, 562 F.2d 55 (9th Cir. 1977), the court, after reviewing some of the working conditions that violated Title VII, observed:

In all of the above-mentioned cases the discriminatory conduct complained of arose out of company policies. There was apparently some advantage to, or gain by, the employer from such discriminatory practices. Always such discriminatory practices were employer designed and oriented. In the present case, [the supervisor's] conduct appears to be nothing more than a personal proclivity, peculiarity or mannerism. By his alleged sexual advances, [the supervisor] was satisfying a personal urge. Certainly no employer policy is here involved; rather than the company being benefitted in any way by the conduct of [the supervisor], it is obvious it can only be damaged by the very nature of the acts complained of.

Several other courts also found that sexual harassment was more in the nature of a personal romp by the supervisor. It was not conduct that the employer authorized; in most cases, it was conduct the employer prohibited, or at least that was detrimental to the economic interests of the employer. Many courts had difficulty finding that such conduct was within the scope of the supervisor's employment. Adhering to the general rule of agency that an employer is not liable for the torts of its employees committed while acting outside the scope of their employment, these courts rejected the notion that sexual harassment was a violation of Title VII.

The EEOC proposed to resolve the liability problem with some very simple rules at 29 CFR 1604.11 (1980):

(c) Applying general Title VII principles, an employer, employment agency, joint apprenticeship committee or labor organization (hereinafter collectively referred to as "employer") is responsible for its acts and those of its agents and supervisory employees with respect to sexual harassment regardless of whether the specific acts complained of were authorized or even forbidden by the employer and regardless of whether the employer knew or should have known of their occurrence. The Commission will examine the circumstances of the particular employment relationship and the job functions performed by the individual in determining whether an individual acts in either a supervisory or agency capacity.

(d) With respect to conduct between fellow employees, an employer is responsible for acts of sexual harassment in the workplace where the employer (or its agents or supervisory employees) knows or should have known of the conduct, unless it can show that it took immediate and appropriate corrective action.

The rules sounded simple, but they were not. The rules had to be applied to various situations involving managers, supervisors, and coworkers, as well as to both quid pro quo and hostile environment claims. After Burlington Industries and Faragher, the Commission rescinded 1604.11(c) because it no longer correctly stated the rule of liability.
