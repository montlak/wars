3. Contents of Decision

The administrative judge's decision will set forth the facts of the case, and will make conclusions of law. Under the Commission's previous regulations, findings of fact and conclusions of law were specifically required. The Commission has not included findings of fact and conclusions of law in the 1999 amendment to its regulations, but presumably those remain required elements of a judge's decision.

The AJ is obligated to address all properly raised claims in the decision. The Commission, for instance, reversed an AJ's decision addressing only three of the complainant's seven nonselection claims in Ginger N. v. Secretary of Treasury, 2019005182 (2021):

On July 2, 2019, the AJ issued a decision addressing the discriminatory non-selection claims. While the AJ addressed Claims 1, 3, and 7 in detail, the AJ did not address Claims 2, 4, 5, and 6. The AJ noted that while Complainant was qualified for the positions in Claims 2, 4, 5, and 6, she was not going to address those claims. The AJ repeatedly stated that lack of discussion was not the same as a determination finding.

Regarding claims 1, 3, and 7, the AJ determined that while the Agency presented legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for the non-selections, Complainant had provided sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the reasons provided were mere pretext for discrimination. The AJ concluded that the Agency discriminated against Complainant with respect to those claims.

Since the AJ determined that the Agency discriminated against Complainant regarding the positions raised in Claims 1, 3, and 7, she determined that Complainant was entitled to appropriate remedies and relief as delineated in 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501 and the Civil Rights Act of 1991, 42 U.S.C. § 1981a. The AJ ordered the Agency, among other things, to promote Complainant to a substantially equivalent permanent senior manager position, with back pay and benefits; to compensate Complainant $60,000.00 for non-pecuniary damages and $2,750.00 for the cost of deposition transcripts; to compensate Complainant's attorneys for fees and expenses; to post written notices of the discrimination finding; and to train management officials on employment-discrimination law.

The AJ heard all seven claims, but did not address four of them in her decision. The Commission found this was improper in Ginger N.:

Regarding the AJ's failure to address Claims 2, 4, 5, and 6, we find it improper. The Commission's regulations afford broad authority to AJs for the conduct of hearings. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109 et seq.; Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (MD-110), Ch. 7, § III.D. (Aug. 5, 2015). Generally, an Administrative Judge will conduct a hearing on the merits of a complaint unless: 1) the parties mutually resolve the complaint and the hearing request is withdrawn; 2) the hearing request is otherwise voluntarily withdrawn; 3) the Administrative Judge dismisses the complaint; or 4) the Administrative Judge determines that material facts are not in genuine dispute and issues an order limiting the scope of the hearing or issues a decision without a hearing pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(g). The Administrative Judge will issue a decision on a complaint and shall order appropriate remedies and relief when discrimination has been found. MD-110 at Ch. 7, § I.

In this matter, Complainant requested a hearing and decision regarding her four complaints alleging discrimination on seven non-selections. An Administrative Judge from the Commission adjudicates claims of discrimination and issues decisions. Id. The AJ held a four-day hearing regarding all of the accepted claims, but then chose not to address Claims 2, 4, 5, and 6 in her decision. Complainant did not withdraw those claims, and the AJ did not issue a prior summary judgment on those four claims. Instead of addressing said claims, the AJ repeatedly stated that no determination was to be made as to Claims 2, 4, 5, and 6 and did not specify why.

The Agency took the silence to mean that the AJ had determined that Complainant was properly non-selected. However, the AJ repeatedly noted that there was simply no determination made, and that silence on the matter was not the same as a finding. The refusal to address the other four claims was improper as Complainant requested a hearing and decision on the entirety of her complaint.

Moreover, the AJ found discriminatory non-selection in three of the seven claims. Based on this finding, the AJ proceeded to detail remedies owed to Complainant. However, the calculation of remedies is incomplete as there was no review regarding the other four claims. Additionally, as the Agency appropriately argued, attorney's fees and costs should be calculated based on the successfulness of the claim and/or complaint. Therefore, we find that a calculation of remedies owed cannot be properly assessed without a determination of discrimination regarding all claims at issue.

Based on the lack of a determination regarding Claims 2, 4, 5, and 6 and its impact on remedies, we find that the matter should be remanded in its entirety for a full discussion on all claims.

In addition to issuing a decision, the judge also is required to transmit the hearing record to both parties. The hearing record consists of the hearing transcript and all exhibits offered at the hearing and accepted into evidence by the judge. Also included in the complaint file are exhibits that were offered into evidence and not admitted. The administrative judge must retain those exhibits, clearly mark them as not admitted and include them in the complaint file. See Judges' Handbook at 7-8. Under the former regulations, the failure of the judge to transmit the record with his recommended decision extended the time period for the agency to issue a final decision. The time period did not begin to run until the agency had received the entire hearing record. See Wilson v. Veterans Administration, 01831535 (1984). There is no reason to believe that the time period for final agency action would not be similarly extended under the 1999 amendments.

It is error for the administrative judge to base her conclusions that the complainant was discriminated against on issues not raised in the complaint. Couch v. Postmaster General, 01881653 (1988). The Commission found that the judge erred in her conclusion that the complainant would have been selected for the position if the agency had not discriminatorily denied her training and work assignments consistent with promotional opportunities. Past training and assignments were not the subject of the complaint and had not been raised in the complaint process.

In cases where attorney fees are awarded, the administrative judge is responsible for issuing a decision on the amount of fees that will be awarded. Although it is unorthodox for an administrative judge to issue a precise determination on the amount of back pay owed a victim of discrimination, nothing precludes a judge from doing so. Harrison v. Postmaster General, 05920078 (1992). In that case, the Commission found it was proper for the judge to address the issue of back pay in his recommended decision where it was clear at the hearing that the amount of back pay was in dispute and both parties introduced evidence on the issue. Moreover, the Commission found that when the agency accepted the recommended decision as its final decision, it was precluded from later challenging the amount of back pay awarded in the recommended decision. The agency decision became final in all respects when it was adopted by the agency.
