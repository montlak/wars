CONFLICT MANAGEMENT

In the federal government, everyone shares the same essential goals -- defending the nation or advancing the interests of the American people. Even with clearly defined agency missions, conflict is still as much a part of the federal workplace as it is of any other human activity.

Conflict management is a competency on which your bosses may evaluate your management skills. You are expected to:

  • • Encourage creative tensions and differences of opinion.
  • • Anticipate and take steps to prevent counterproductive confrontations.
  • • Manage and resolve conflicts and disagreements in a constructive manner.

Consider that first point: "Encourage creative tensions and differences of opinion." It tells you something important right off the bat: Conflict is not necessarily a bad thing. Lack of conflict may not mean everyone agrees on how to meet important organizational challenges. It may simply mean they aren't actively engaged or aren't aware there are challenges to be met. The good conflict manager may have to start by stirring things up -- posing questions precisely to encourage people to disagree about the answers.

To understand this better, consider why people are likely to disagree with one another at work. There are two broad categories of dispute you are likely to encounter: conflicts about work and conflicts between people. Let's look at them separately.

Conflicts about work

Conflicts about work deal with the actual substance of people's jobs, which is why we call them substantive conflicts. This type of conflict is about what should be done and how to do it. It includes disagreements about:

  • • Data and its interpretation.
  • • Policies to be adopted.
  • • Interpretations of existing policies.
  • • Resource acquisition and allocation.
  • • Workflow, including assignments and process.

Substantive conflicts can be positive, especially when they are properly managed. It is through these types of conflicts that creativity is encouraged and innovation becomes possible. On the other hand, when they are not properly managed, they can destroy morale, paralyze policy, create inefficiencies, and hurt an agency's reputation.

Conflicts between people

Some people don't like one another, for whatever reason or no discernible reason at all. And others grow to dislike one another as they work together. These conflicts are less about the work itself than about the people doing it. The resulting disputes are grouped in the category of interpersonal conflicts. Causes may be harder to discern than with substantive conflicts, but they can include:

  • • Simple "bad chemistry" -- another way of saying two people just don't like each other.
  • • Irritating personal habits.
  • • Lack of interpersonal skills.
  • • Prejudice, including illegal discrimination.
  • • Professional rivalry and competition.

Interpersonal conflicts almost always have negative results. The goal of conflict management is to head off and resolve conflicts before they become disruptive with the goal of helping people to get along (the most constructive resolution). When this is not possible, it may be necessary to invoke alternative dispute resolution or more formal dispute resolution processes and even to use progressive discipline where appropriate.

Warning: Conflicts may be "over-determined," that is, they may have more than one cause. What appear to be substantive conflicts may have strong interpersonal elements. The greatest challenge to conflict management is to distinguish the substantive from the interpersonal, so the substantive conflicts can be resolved positively while the interpersonal conflicts are mitigated.

Preventing conflict

While conflict can serve the purpose of engaging people and presenting challenges that will improve your agency, you don't want your work unit to be defined by it. You cannot afford to spend every workday consumed by managing conflict. Taking preventive measures can alleviate some of this.

Consider the following to prevent counterproductive confrontations:

  • • Provide employees with the accurate and complete information they need to do their jobs effectively.
  • • Communicate impending changes to your employees and the reasons behind them.
  • • Meet with employees individually to discuss their concerns before they become conflicts.
  • • Meet with staff at least semiannually to address their concerns in an open and honest forum.
  • • Implement changes to ensure a fair work environment.
  • • Don't ignore problematic behavior that you observe. Address it immediately so that all employees are treated by others with respect.
  • • Promote collaborative approaches to problem-solving.
  • • Coach and motivate employees toward greater mutual respect and understanding.
  • • Encourage debate and open discussion.
  • • Seek opportunities to improve your conflict management skills.

The ability to recognize, resolve, and manage situations that can potentially grow into conflict is an essential element of this competency. An effective approach is to endorse a culture that through disagreements, learning opportunities can be gained. This will promote the basic rules of civility and encourage divergent thinking.

Conflict and consensus

At its highest skill level, conflict management is about using substantive conflicts constructively to improve agency and unit mission performance. Very often this is seen as a process of reaching consensus. Consensus is a general overall agreement among members of a group or parties to a negotiation on what should be done. Unfortunately, this misleads some managers into thinking that the goal of conflict management is to produce consensus.

The goal of conflict management is not consensus. After all, if the goal were consensus, why would managers be urged to encourage creative conflicts, which may well disturb the existing consensus? Whether conflict resolution is the result of formal negotiation, informal brainstorming sessions, or one manager's decision, the point of conflict resolution is to respond effectively to shared challenges.

Resolutions are effective when:

  • • They are practical.
  • • They do not violate law or regulation.
  • • Those who need to implement them have a strong motivation to do so.
  • • They will achieve their intended objectives efficiently.

A genuine consensus is a wonderful thing, so long as it produces an effective conflict resolution. A consensus process may be useful if it is understood as a process and not as a goal. Unfortunately, managers who are determined to produce consensus may wind up with something nobody really wants instead of something everyone wants. This happens when people are convinced to "go along" for the sake of consensus or when everyone involved becomes so exhausted by continual conflict and unending meetings they will agree to anything just to get on with their lives.

"Consensus" conflict resolutions produced in this way are usually not very effective. Things may limp along for a while, but eventually, these agreements tend to break up with recriminations and pronouncements of "I told you so" all around.

Top conflict managers avoid the trap of consensus for its own sake by bringing out the creative aspects in substantive conflicts and using them to produce exciting, innovative solutions.

Conflict management and leadership

There may be times when you prefer a quieter life to the challenges of conflict management, but these challenges are also among your greatest career opportunities.

If you can convince the parties to a substantive conflict to set aside their interpersonal conflicts and various agendas, to concentrate on the substantive, and to produce a genuinely effective, even innovative, resolution, you show yourself to be a leader with the potential to work at the highest management levels.

And you can begin at any time (even if you are a first-line supervisor) by using conflict management in your own unit to encourage creative conflict and to produce innovative resolutions that will improve morale and your unit's bottom line.

Conflict management tools

To effectively deal with conflict management, you'll need to call upon an array of tools and competencies. Practice also helps. Situations can become unstable or even destructive if you "wing it" without proper preparation. Conflict management is one competency in which you cannot afford to be weak. The following will help you become a stronger conflict manager.

Brainstorm

One of the best tools available for conflict management is the brainstorming session, whether it happens spontaneously or is set up in advance. Brainstorming is a particularly valuable way to encourage conflict for creative purposes.

A brainstorming session is a meeting in which a facilitator -- frequently a decision-maker looking for insight from employees, colleagues, or other stakeholders -- asks for ideas on how to approach a particular situation or challenge. All ideas are welcome, even those that do not seem feasible. Initially, no response to these proposals, positive or negative, is permitted. The facilitator writes down the ideas and groups similar ones. After the initial brainstorming session, the facilitator leads a winnowing process until the outlines of a plan emerge. The meeting may end at that point or be continued until a draft or final plan is adopted.

Brainstorming gets ideas out where everyone can see them, and unworkable ones may spark workable alternatives. The process also generates creative energy and a sense of common purpose that may overcome any interpersonal conflicts.

Brainstorming focuses on the challenge, not the conflict. It does two things that assist the conflict manager:

  • • It allows conflicting parties to consider and weigh all potential resolution elements independently of those who advocate them.
  • • It engages the parties to work together constructively to meet their common challenge, whatever personal agendas they may have.

Separate the substantive from the interpersonal

Part of the challenge of conflict management is that parties to conflicts may get attached to their positions to the point where they "take disagreements personally." They may view a disagreement through the lens of professional rivalry or agency politics. Or they may be so sure of their own case and their own devotion to the agency's mission that they ascribe disagreements to nefarious motives, ignorance, or simple stupidity.

And if the conflicting parties have a history of previous conflicts, that only makes it more difficult to separate the substantive from the interpersonal.

To do this successfully, you need a whole range of cross-functional skills ranging from influencing and negotiating to interpersonal skills to oral communication to political savvy. These are all skills that can be learned, and the rewards are worth the effort.

Listen actively

One effective tool is active listening, a technique to make the listening and responding parts of your oral communication more effective by adopting alert body language, avoiding interruption, showing you understand the speaker's point, and responding in a way that moves communication forward.

The point of listening actively is to learn what it is your conversation partner considers important and to convince that partner you want to know. Active listening may be the first step in engaging the partner in creative conflict management and defusing disruptive interpersonal conflicts. Equivalent techniques can also improve your written communications.

Harness diversity and conflict

As a federal manager, you are encouraged to leverage diversity in your unit and your agency, including employees of all backgrounds at every level of policymaking and mission accomplishment.

The very differences in the backgrounds of diverse employees and managers can itself be a powerful creative motor. A good conflict manager leverages diversity by harnessing its potential for creative conflict while mitigating the interpersonal conflicts that diverse backgrounds may engender.

Practice

Whether you're a seasoned manager or are newly promoted, you can never have too much experience in managing conflict. Since conflicts come in all shapes and sizes, practicing ahead of the event can really pay off.

Try practicing with your boss. Before handling a conflict, explain the situation to your boss and ask if they'll role play a side of the issue. Your boss will be familiar with agency processes and jargon to bring realism to the practice run. In their career, your boss has likely encountered a number of interpersonal conflicts so they can effectively play "devil's advocate" to prepare you in those situations. The feedback you get from your boss will prove valuable. The more you practice, the better you will become in handling these conflicts at work.

Workplace conflicts and workplace disputes

Unfortunately, you are apt to find that the employees you supervise get involved in disagreements that have less to do with substantive conflict and more with interpersonal conflict. We generally call these workplace disputes.

Many workplace disputes turn into substantive conflicts. For example, conflicts can start off as interpersonal issues between two or more employees or an employee and their manager. Because of those personality conflicts, employees may then raise substantive concerns about areas such as:

  • • Work schedules.
  • • Workflow.
  • • Work assignments.
  • • Working conditions.

The best way to prevent these concerns from becoming workplace disputes is to address points of conflict swiftly, openly, and frankly, and to provide as much information as possible. Where you see a genuine substantive conflict that can be managed to produce a positive resolution, handle it that way.

A staff brainstorming session can be very effective, or a small meeting with the parties involved can also be a good way to hammer out a resolution.

Many employee conflicts and concerns involve aspects of labor relations. If you manage employees in a bargaining unit, union rights must be considered before you engage in any discussions about working conditions. Check with your ADR office or HR's labor relations specialists before you deal with these issues.

Even if the employees concerned are not members of bargaining units, many workplace issues are governed by employee relations regulations and agency policies. HR can advise you here as well.

When conflicts become disputes

Unfortunately, it isn't always possible to resolve workplace conflicts in ways that leave every employee feeling good about things. Workplace conflicts may become workplace disputes when employees believe they have been treated unfairly in violation of their rights -- often when they decide to "take it personally."

When this happens, check in with HR immediately. Some of these disputes can be resolved very informally with a bit of positive communication on your part. Others may be amenable to alternative dispute resolution. Inevitably, some will result in formal grievances, even EEO complaints.

Not every workplace dispute starts out as a workplace conflict. Some are the result of real misconduct by employees -- or managers -- against others. Misconduct can include improper favoritism, illegal discrimination, sexual or other forms of harassment. Even when no real misconduct has occurred, it may be perceived by those who believe themselves to be victims.

What do you do when faced with a workplace dispute? Your first rule should be: Don't take it personally. The author Robert Townsend once wrote, "A good manager doesn't try to eliminate conflict; he tries to keep it from wasting the energies of his people. If you're the boss and your people fight you openly when they think that you are wrong -- that's healthy."

You are the manager. Employees will complain to you and about you. That is the sign of a healthy organization -- one that fosters a culture where staff can express themselves freely and without fear of reprisal. If you do take it personally, and especially if you attempt to retaliate against employees for raising issues or bringing complaints, you are apt to turn a healthy environment sour.

Again, your first response to a serious workplace dispute should always be to reach out to your ADR or conflict resolution office as a first step, and if they are unable to help, they can assist you with determining the appropriate office that can. Let the experts explain your options and follow their advice.

When serious misconduct has occurred, you will be responsible for correcting the problem. Working with HR, consider ways to redress any damage done to employees, prevent repetition, and apply progressive discipline to offenders.

Warning: A workplace dispute may be the result of a failure in your conflict management approach, but this is not always the case. It is a mistake to try to avoid workplace disputes at all costs. You don't want to be so fixated on avoiding conflicts or formal disputes that you wind up with unreasonable resolutions that will only hurt morale and productivity in the long run.

People are people, and interpersonal conflicts are inevitable in every workplace. You can do your best to resolve work conflicts before they become workplace disputes, but in the end, some disputes will have to be resolved formally. It is your job to respond properly, limit any disruption to your unit, and keep your employees focused on the challenges of their work.

Mistakes to avoid

Conflict management may be a challenge but avoiding these mistakes can help your efforts.

Do not:

  • • Avoid or discourage all conflict. Conflict encourages creativity and innovation in response to shared challenges.
  • • Make consensus your goal. When this happens, the result may be a conflict resolution that nobody really supports.
  • • Take conflicts personally. Taking offense and retaliating against others only makes things worse -- sometimes much worse.
  • • Try to avoid or quickly settle all workplace disputes. Unreasonable settlements only hurt morale and productivity.
  • • Leave HR out of the loop when dealing with workplace conflicts and disputes. Your labor and employee relations specialists can help you with options and warn you of pitfalls.

Ask FMG

Q. I always aim for consensus in the meetings I run, but it seems to take forever to achieve it. How can I run a more effective meeting?

A. The problem may not be with your meeting skills but with your goal: consensus. Achieving consensus through exhaustion is rarely a good idea. It would be a better idea to clarify all points of conflict and then work for an effective resolution -- even if not everyone present accepts it. This is why it might be beneficial to ask your conflict resolution or ADR professionals to assist you by facilitating challenging discussions (individual or group).

Q. What do you do if one manager tells another manager that they just don't like them? Do you tell them they don't have to like each other but they still have to work together?

A. Telling them that they may never like each other but they must work together is a handy way to summarize the conflict. But it does nothing to manage it toward a positive outcome. Conflict management starts when you get the parties together to explore the "why" of a conflict so that options can be examined to resolve it. People usually have cause for disliking another person. It would benefit everyone to sit down and talk about the reasons. You can't force people to like each other, but at least they will both have an idea of the underlying issues. From there, challenge them to find some middle ground and how behaviors or perspectives may need to be changed. One expectation you should make clear is that any discord between them should not be observed by staff or spill over into operations or procedures. Managers are held to a higher standard of conduct and that includes being an example of professional decorum.

Q. I don't see the point of brainstorming. Is encouraging everyone to come up with wild ideas really an efficient way to meet challenges?

A. Even if brainstorming takes some time, it can be very efficient in the long run. It gets ideas out where everyone can see them, and unworkable ones may spark workable alternatives. The process also generates creative energy and a sense of common purpose that may overcome any interpersonal conflicts.

Q. My employees and I get along just fine. Why should I complicate our relationship by involving HR or ADR whenever workplace conflicts come up?

A. "Involving HR or ADR" may not be necessary in every case, but even if you are an experienced manager, federal employee and labor relations are governed by complex regulations and agency policies. Consequently, it is easy to make a misstep without expert guidance from HR or ADR. This is especially true when bargaining unit employees and their unions are involved.

Q. My employees seem to run to the union to file grievances and make EEO complaints whenever something happens that they don't like. What am I doing wrong?

A. Good conflict management skills can never prevent all formal workplace disputes, but they should be able to resolve most conflicts before they get to this level. Your best bet is to address points of conflict swiftly, openly, and frankly, and to provide as much information as possible. A staff brainstorming session may be the best way to work out a resolution, or you can hold a small meeting with the parties to a conflict to reach an effective resolution. As long as substantive issues predominate over the interpersonal, creative conflict resolution can be explored.

If you find that the conflict is a little more challenging or more complex or that the individuals involved are more complicated or difficult, you should reach out to your ADR or conflict resolution office for a consultation. Your agency put them in place to assist you with all conflicts, large or small, so you should not hesitate to discuss the situation with them for guidance or support.
