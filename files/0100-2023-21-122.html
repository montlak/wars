K. AGENCY-WIDE CORRECTIVE MEASURES

When there are indications that failure to address complaints of harassment is widespread, the Commission may order an agency to take actions to eliminate harassment on a broader scale. For example, in Ayers, supra, the complainant complained about harassment, and management's failure to address her complaint led to escalation of the harassment that ultimately resulted in physical and emotional injury to the complainant. Noting that agency management officials were well aware of the harassment yet failed to take actions to stop it, the Commission determined that the problem was institutional. The Commission in Ayers ordered the following relief:

We are disturbed by the widespread failure of management officials to identify and properly address complaints of sexual harassment in the workplace. Given the fact that at least five management officials had knowledge of such harassment and, either failed to take prompt remedial action, or worse, encouraged complainant to continue working in such an environment, suggests a lack of institutional control by the agency. We find that an order by the Commission requiring mere training of the management officials would fall short in preventing similar failures by management in the future, because it appears likely that the agency lacked an effective plan to remedy sexual harassment in its workplace. Accordingly, as set forth more fully below, we order the agency, inter alia, to implement an effective instructive management plan[9] which will provide practical guidance on the proper action that it must take in addressing complaints of sexual harassment.

Footnote 9 The Commission has recently become aware of a newly developed IRS reference guide for managers on the prevention of sexual harassment which may sufficiently meet our requirements set forth below. However, as set forth, infra, the Commission requires the agency to provide such information in its report of compliance, along with proof that such plan is being effectively implemented and enforced.

4. The agency shall take corrective, curative and preventive action to ensure that sexual harassment does not recur, including but not limited to the following:

a. Develop, implement and enforce an effective instructive management plan which will provide practical guidance on the proper action management officials must follow when presented with complaints of sexual harassment;

b. Develop, implement and enforce a plan to effectively ensure the instructive management plan is followed; and

c. Develop and implement a plan to effectively notify and provide training to management officials of their responsibilities under the instructive management plan.

5. The agency shall provide a minimum of 40 hours of training to the responsible official(s), including S1, S2, S3, S4 and TL, at the Internal Revenue Service, Finance Division, Washington, D.C. facility about the law against employment discrimination and the proper protocol to follow when alerted to harassment in the workplace.

See also Horkan, supra (agency ordered to "immediately issue strong written policy" to all employees at the facility and provide all employees with sensitivity training, including reference to the policy).

In Sebek v. Attorney General, 07A00005 (2001), the Commission ordered training based on the agency's retaining and hiring a Director that it knew treated employees in a hostile manner:

Finally, we find that the Director's behavior towards complainant in this case was particularly egregious. In its final order, the agency acknowledged that it had notice of the Director's history of treating employees in a hostile manner. We note that despite the agency's knowledge of the Director's previous hostile treatment of employees and the egregious behavior by the Director towards the complainant in this case, the agency rehired the Director within one day of his voluntary retirement from the agency and less than four months after his physical attack on complainant. Accordingly, we find that the agency should provide EEO training, as specified below, in addition to the remedial relief recommended by the AJ.

In Ulloa v. USPS, 01972750 (2000), supra, the Commission ordered that the notice of discrimination be posted at the agency for 180 days rather than the usual 60 days, given the agency's inaction in responding to the complaint and the resultant nine-year delay in processing the complaint. The Commission did not explain why it changed the administrative judge's order that the notice be posted for two years.
