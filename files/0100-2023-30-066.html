8. Failure to Qualify

One question that arises with respect to remedies is what happens when the complainant demonstrates that he or she, as the result of discrimination, has not been qualified for a position or sufficiently qualified to warrant an interview. In many of those cases, the complainant simply cannot make the additional showing that the discrimination actually resulted in his or her nonselection.

The basic premise of relief in all discrimination cases is to put, as nearly as possible, the victim of discrimination in the position he or she would have been in but for the discrimination. Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405, 95 S. Ct. 2362, 45 L. Ed.2d 280 (1975). However, granting the complainant the interview he or she was denied is no longer possible since a selection was made long ago. In Edwards v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 07A50067 (2005), the Commission agreed with its administrative judge that the proper remedy for denial of a job interview was to be guaranteed interviews for future openings. However, the Commission put a two-year time limit on its remedy in Edwards explaining:

We find the AJ correctly determined that given the agency's assessment of the applications of the candidates selected for an interview, complainant should also have been certified by the agency for an interview. The Commission, however, modifies the relief granted by the AJ. First, we agree with the AJ that complainant should be granted interview opportunities for future Accounting Technician, GS-6 vacancies. The Commission adds the time limit of two years to this interview remedy. The Commission disagrees with the AJ that complainant should be provided training to allow complainant to successfully perform the duties of an Accounting Technician, GS-6. The agency was not found to have discriminatorily denied complainant training and such relief is beyond make whole relief. Furthermore, we add the remedies of: (1) EEO training for the agency officials responsible for the discrimination; and (2) the requirement that the agency consider disciplining the agency officials responsible for the discrimination.

The Commission, however, has been inconsistent in granting remedies in cases where the discrimination occurs in the application process. For example, in McCarroll v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720070060 (2008), the Commission held that the complainant was entitled to the position where she was not referred as a best qualified candidate due to her race and the agency could not show by clear and convincing evidence that it would not have selected her absent discrimination. The Commission, in McCarroll, explained:

Turning to the issue of remedies, a job applicant has a right to be free from discrimination throughout the selection process. If the process is discriminatory at any phase, the applicant must be awarded full relief, such as the position retroactively, unless the agency shows by clear and convincing evidence that even in the absence of discrimination, the applicant would not have been selected. See DeReyna v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal Nos. 01980077, 01980078, 01980079 (January 19, 2001). Thus, complainant is entitled to retroactive placement in the position unless the agency can show by clear and convincing evidence that, even absent discrimination, complainant would not have been selected. Id.; see also 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501(c)(1). As discussed above, the agency's rationale for finding that complainant was not qualified for the position lacks credibility. Additionally, the agency failed to provide a copy of complainant's graduate program transcript from the University of Michigan in the record to support its assertion that complainant was not qualified for the position. As a result, we find that the agency has not met its burden of establishing, by clear and convincing evidence, that absent the discrimination against complainant it still would not have selected complainant for the GS-13 Instructional Systems Specialist position. We concur with the AJ that retroactive placement in the GS-13 Instructional Systems Specialist position, or a substantially equivalent position, effective the date that the selectee entered into the position, is an appropriate remedy in this case.

McCarroll is clearly the better view on remedies since once discrimination is shown the burden of proof should shift to the agency to prove the complainant was not harmed by the discrimination. This is also consistent with the 1991 Civil Rights which requires that in cases where a violation is proven and the employer shows it would have taken the same action even absent discrimination, a limited remedy in the form of declaratory relief and attorney fees is still available to the employee.

This also appears to be the view now embraced by the Commission. In Eyslee v. Secretary of Treasury, 0720100050 (2011), the Commission held:

We agree with the AJ that the discriminatory evaluation caused Complainant not to be placed on the BQL. An employee has a right to be free from discrimination throughout the selection process. If the process is discriminatory at any phase, the employee must be awarded full relief, i.e., the position retroactively, unless the agency shows by "clear and convincing evidence" that even in the absence of discrimination, the applicant would not have been selected. See DeReyna, Ellis, and Gillespie v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal Nos. 01980077, 01980078, 01980079 (Jan. 19, 2001) (citations omitted). Placing such an onerous burden on the Agency is proper, inasmuch as the Agency's unlawful acts caused the difficulty in determining what would have resulted had there been no discrimination. See id. (citing Pryor v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05980405 (August 6, 1999)).
