1. Statutory Basis

The authority for treating drug and alcohol abuse as disabling conditions arises from five separate statutes. First, the Rehabilitation Act imposes its requirements on employers with regard to all disabling conditions. Second, the Drug Abuse Prevention, Treatment and Rehabilitation Act, 42 USC 290ee-1, as its name suggests, provides additional statutory guidance on dealing with federal employees who suffer from drug abuse. Third, the Comprehensive Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism Prevention, Treatment and Rehabilitation Act, 42 USC 290dd-1, provides similar guidance on federal employees suffering from alcohol abuse. The 1991 Civil Rights Act allows agencies to bar current illegal users of drugs from employment in most circumstances. Finally, the Americans with Disabilities Act, 42 USC 12114, substantially clarifies the existing law on dealing with the use and abuse of alcohol and illegal drugs by employees. The ADAAA did not change the ADA provisions on drug and alcohol abuse.

Under 42 USC 290dd-1 and 290ee-1, the Office of Personnel Management, in conjunction with the Department of Health and Human Services, was responsible for developing and maintaining programs of prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation for federal employees with drug or alcohol problems. Through a 1986 amendment to the provision, the responsibility now rests solely with the Secretary of Health and Human Services. But OPM still publishes its regulatory guidance requiring agencies to establish such programs and report annually on their programs. The basic OPM guidance on alcohol and drug use prevention, treatment and rehabilitation is set forth at 5 CFR Part 792. Specifically, 5 CFR 792.102 provides:

It is the policy of the Federal Government to offer appropriate prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation programs and services for Federal civilian employees with alcohol and/or drug problems. Short-term counseling and/or referral, or offers thereof, shall constitute the appropriate prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation programs and services for alcohol abuse, alcoholism, and/or drug abuse required under 42 USC 290dd-a(a) and 290ee-1(a). Federal departments and agencies must establish programs to assist employees with these problems in accordance with the legislation....

Subpart 105 requires that agencies establish programs through which employees with alcohol or drug problems can be provided with or referred to either short-term counseling by qualified practitioners or to long-term counseling or treatment. The consequences of refusing to accept counseling or treatment or failing at such counseling or treatment are severe, as explained at 5 CFR 792.105(c):

Whenever a manager/supervisor becomes aware that a Federal employee's use of alcohol and/or drugs may be contributing to a performance or conduct deficiency, the manager/supervisor shall recommend counseling and refer the employee to the agency counseling program. If an employee fails to participate in any rehabilitative program or, having participated, the employee fails to bring conduct or performance up to satisfactory level, the agency shall evaluate the employee accordingly and initiate an appropriate performance-based or adverse action.

Subsection (b) of 42 USC 290ee-1 provides that "[n]o person may be denied Federal civilian employment...solely on the ground of prior drug use." There are exemptions for employees from the CIA, the FBI, and other national security agencies. Subsection (d) goes on to state that "[t]his section shall not be construed to prohibit the dismissal from employment of a Federal civilian employee who cannot properly function in his employment." Section dd-1 contains identical provisions with respect to alcohol abuse.

The statutes and regulations make clear the tension between the mandate to provide preventative and rehabilitative services to federal employees and the authority of federal agencies to discipline employees who cannot conform their performance or conduct to accepted standards as a result of drug or alcohol abuse.

In addition to the protection provided through 42 USC 290dd-1, alcoholism is considered to be a disabling condition within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act and agencies are required to provide reasonable accommodation. See Jones v. USPS, 01841595 (1986); see also Brill v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01910547 (1991) ("It is well established that alcoholism constitutes a handicap within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act."). In certain circumstances, drug abuse can be a disabling condition under the ADA, but only if the there is no current illegal use of drugs.

Although it is well established that alcoholism and substance abuse can be disabilities, another problem in this area arises because not all persons who use, or even abuse, alcohol and drugs meet the medical or legal definition of a person with a disability. The employee who gets drunk once a year at the office Christmas party and proceeds to insult his supervisor is not an alcoholic. As a result, the question arises as to whether the employee who is an alcoholic and engages in the same behavior should be subjected to the same disciplinary measures or whether reasonable accommodation requires a lesser sanction or even no sanction at all. Over time, the answer to this question has varied to the point that, in some instances, it seemed an employee could find a safe harbor from some discipline in alcoholism or substance abuse. Now, though, the answer to the question is clear and consistent; nothing in the Rehabilitation Act or the ADA exempts an employee who suffers from alcoholism or substance abuse from being subject to the same discipline as any other employee for misconduct or poor performance.
