5. Position in Question

In a case that reaffirmed Ignacio, the Commission determined that the phrase "position in question," not only refers to the position held at the time but to any position the employee could have held as a result of accommodation. Corcoran v. National Gallery of Art, 03840058 (1986). If the employee is considered competitively for a reassignment position and does not receive it, the selection is analyzed under the McDonnell Douglas formula.

When an agency accommodates an employee by creating new job duties, those new duties become the "position in question" for the purpose of analyzing whether the employee is a qualified individual with a disability. As the Commission explained in a footnote in Meziere v. Postmaster General, 01942001 (1995):

Although the Commission agrees that appellant failed to prove that he was discriminatorily denied overtime, it disagrees with the AJ's finding that appellant is not a qualified person with a disability. The record indicates that following appellant's injury, the agency accommodated him by creating new job duties (e.g., answering telephones, taking reports, making copies). The "position in question," therefore, is appellant's new position, not his former letter carrier position. Accordingly, since there is no evidence that appellant was unable to perform the essential functions of his modified position, the Commission finds that he is a qualified person with a disability....

In its ADA regulations, the Commission has dropped the "position in question" language and refers to the position the individual "holds or desires." See 29 CFR 1630.2(n)(1).

One court has held that a position is not vacant if the agency has the position on hold while awaiting further organizational developments. In Ozlowski v. Henderson, 237 F.3d 837 (7th Cir. 2001), the court considered the issue of whether an agency must consider accommodating a complainant by placing him in a vacant position, for which he was qualified, but on which the agency had placed an informal hold due to its knowledge that the essential functions of the position might be changed in the near future. The court in Ozlowski, 237 F.3d at 841-42, found:

While we think it would have been prudent for Wawrzyniec to have communicated more effectively with Ozlowski, there is an independent reason that we decline to find that the Postal Service failed to provide a reasonable accommodation. Wawrzyniec stated that, while the mail-flow controller position was vacant, there was an informal hold on filling that position pending the installation of a new computer system nationwide which could change or even reduce job requirements. We do not believe that a reasonable accommodation means that an employer is required to fill a position which, based on a reason wholly independent of the employee's disability, it had chosen not to fill. Such a position is not "vacant." Cf. Equal Employment Opportunity Comm'n v. Humiston-Keeling, Inc., 227 F.3d 1024, 1028 (7th Cir. 2000) (ADA does not require employer to reassign a disabled employee to a vacant position where there is a better applicant, provided it is the employer's policy of giving vacant job to best applicant). Even if Wawrzyniec had the authority to fill the position regardless of the informal hold, the position regardless of the informal hold, the position would have been temporary until the arrival of a new computer system. The Postal Service was not obligated to place Ozlowski in such a position in order to accommodate him. See McCreary, 132 F.3d at 1165 ("Occasional opportunities to work in another department are not equivalent to a vacancy for a permanent position.").
