1. "Prima Facie" Case

One of the problems in dealing with the law as it relates to hostile environment harassment is sorting out the good case law from the bad case law. Not only has the theory developed rapidly, there has often been confusion and disagreement among the Commission and courts about the precise requirements and contours of the theory. Some of the problem also stems from the fact that hostile environment was not necessarily always recognized as a distinct theory of discrimination. The Commission and the courts often tried to treat it as a subcategory of disparate treatment. There are cases holding that the appellant, in order to establish a prima facie case of hostile environment harassment, must show by a preponderance of the evidence: (1) that the submission to sexual advances was a term or condition of employment; (2) that she was substantially affected by her failure to acquiesce to the sexual advances; and (3) that employees of the opposite sex were not affected in a similar manner. Sexton v. U.S. Marine Corps., 01821475 (1983). While it is most often true that members of the opposite sex are not similarly affected by the harassment, there is no real requirement that a hostile environment complainant demonstrate disparate treatment. The requirement is that the complainant establish the offending conduct is based on a prohibited category.

A separate problem is the use of the term "prima facie" in addressing the hostile environment analysis. As discussed in Chapter 10, a prima facie case is any set of facts that gives rise to an inference of discrimination. That inference creates a rebuttable presumption of discrimination. If the employer remains silent, the presumption is not rebutted and the employee prevails. Of course, all the employer need do to rebut the presumption is articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions. But that analysis makes no real sense in terms of sexual harassment or indeed any other form of prohibited harassment. After all, what possible legitimate reason can there be for the sexual or racial harassment of an employee? It is, quite frankly, nonsensical to talk about a prima facie case of harassment. What we're really talking about when referring to a prima facie case of harassment are those elements the complainant must prove to prevail in his or her case. If the complainant establishes those elements by a preponderance of the evidence, the complainant has succeeded in proving a hostile environment exists and the only remaining question is whether the employer should be liable for that environment.

So, we embark on our discussion of the prima facie case with the understanding that we are talking about something distinctly different than the prima facie case under McDonnell Douglas.

A far more accepted statement of the requirements of a prima facie case of hostile environment harassment was set forth in Trammel v. Postmaster General, 01871154 (1988). In Trammel, the Commission stated that to demonstrate a prima facie case of a hostile work environment the employee must show: "(1) she belongs to protected group; (2) she was subject to unwelcome sexual harassment; (3) the harassment complained of was based upon sex; (4) the harassment affected a term, condition, or privilege of employment (e.g., an intangible or non-economic benefit); and (5) the employer knew or should have known of the harassment." Id.

Note that the first four elements of a prima facie case relate to whether a hostile environment actually existed. The fifth element--showing the employer knew or should have known of the harassment--addresses the issue of whether the employer should be held liable for the existence of a hostile environment. The answer to that question depends, as discussed in detail below, upon whether the harassment was by a supervisor or coworkers and what action, if any, the employer took upon learning of the harassment. Trammel still correctly sets forth the rule of employer liability for cases involving harassment by a coworker. For example, in a hostile environment case involving a coworker, the employee must show as part of her prima facie case that the agency failed to take prompt remedial and appropriate action although it was aware of the harassment. Lutticken v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05900386 (1990). Where the agency issues a letter of reprimand to the offender within four days of the alleged harassment, the employee fails to establish a prima facie case. The Commission noted that what constitutes appropriate action varies with the circumstances. In Lutticken, the Commission found a reprimand was appropriate where there was no prior history of harassment. In a case involving a supervisor, the complainant is not required to make any showing with respect to liability as part of a prima facie case but is subject to the Burlington Industries affirmative defense.

One of the requirements for a complainant to make a prima facie case of hostile environment harassment is to show the conduct that allegedly created the hostile environment occurred. As the Commission held in Wilson v. Postmaster General, 01990803 (2001), this cannot be based solely on unsubstantiated rumors, no matter how pervasive the rumors may be:

Based upon review of the entire record, the Commission finds that complainant has not proven, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the Postmaster was engaged in making sexual advances towards or requesting sexual favors from his subordinate employees. In reaching this conclusion, we note that thirty-three statements were taken from a combination of both male and female craft and management employees at six stations. Twenty-three of these employees attested to the perception that the Postmaster was romantically involved with subordinate female employees. These employees admitted that the perception was based on "rumors," "gossip," and "hearsay," and that neither they nor those who passed on such information had directly witnessed the romantic involvement. Only one employee attested to witnessing an incident wherein he walked into an office and found the Postmaster engaged in an "intimate embrace" with a subordinate employee. This incident was never referenced by the twenty three employees whose perception was based on rumor and hearsay. The Postmaster, who denied that he had ever had a romantic relationship with any of the Aurora district employees, described this incident as a "hug." The record does not contain an affidavit from the subordinate employee. There is insufficient, credible evidence of record to permit the Commission to determine whether this was an "intimate embrace" or a "hug," thus leaving the nature of the Postmaster's conduct in equipoise. See LaMacchia v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01976971 (December 12, 1999).

Furthermore, the Commission declines to credit statements based on what one employee heard from another employee when there is no evidence that any of the rumored conduct actually occurred.

The employee, as part of a prima facie case, must show that he or she was "aggrieved" in the terms, conditions or privileges of employment by the conduct at issue. "In order to establish injury within the context of sexual harassment, the appellant must show that the offensive behavior complained of was sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of [her] employment and create an abusive working atmosphere." Cudjoe v. Secretary of Navy, 01880743 (1988). In that case, the Commission found the employee was not aggrieved by a coworker who wore short skirts. The fact that the complainant found the short skirts offensive did not establish that the "other female employee had the intent or effect of unreasonably interfering with appellant's work performance or creat[ing] an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment...." Id.

As noted above, the real question is whether the term "prima facie case" has any applicability at all in hostile environment cases. If the complainant establishes the five elements of Trammel, then he or she is entitled to more than the McDonnell Douglas rebuttable presumption that a hostile environment existed. He or she has proved a claim of hostile environment harassment and the simple fact remains that there can be no legitimate reason for engaging in sexual harassment. To the extent that the term "prima facie case" is useful at all in addressing hostile environment claims, its use is in describing what must be alleged at the complaint stage and not what must be proved when the merits of the allegations are considered.

A complainant's account of the events must be "sufficiently detailed and internally consistent so as to be plausible," as the Commission noted in Blanca B. v. Secretary of State, 0120151376 (2016).
