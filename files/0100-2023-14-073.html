3. Bankruptcy

With the immediate disclaimer that this is a treatise on discrimination law and not bankruptcy law, there can be questions of whether any judgment or settlement involving an EEO complaint is an asset of the bankruptcy estate. Of course, as a general matter, such questions are within the jurisdiction of the U.S. Bankruptcy Court and not the EEOC. But the question did arise in Sellman v. Postmaster General, 01A31350 (2004), where the complainant filed for bankruptcy and the agency appealed the decision of an administrative judge finding discrimination and awarding damages to the complainant. While the complainant's bankruptcy case was pending, the agency entered a settlement agreement with the bankruptcy trustee and withdrew its appeal. The complainant filed his own appeal after being made aware of the settlement. The Commission in Sellman found that no valid settlement agreement ever existed:

The record indicates that complainant filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy on July 9, 2002, and a Bankruptcy Trustee (BT) was appointed by the U.S. Bankruptcy Court to represent his estate. In a letter from the agency dated December 12, 2002, the agency's counsel stated that it had reached a settlement of the case with the BT. As such, the agency stated that the Office of Federal Operations need not decide the appeal it filed. The agency's counsel stated that when complainant filed for bankruptcy, his EEOC claim became an asset of the bankruptcy estate and as such, the BT takes the place of the complainant for purposes of settling or pursuing the appeal. The agency contended that as the BT chose to settle the appeal, the agency sought to withdraw its appeal of the AJ's decision. The Commission issued a letter dated December 18, 2002, granting the agency's request to withdraw its appeal. Sellman v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 07A30026 (December 18, 2002).

By letter to the Commission dated December 18, 2002, complainant alleged that he had only become aware of the agency's action when he spoke to the agency's counsel. Complainant alleges that the amount agreed to be paid by the agency pursuant to his discrimination complaint is property exempt from bankruptcy proceedings, and that the agency and the BT erred in entering into a SA without his knowledge. Complainant's counsel alleged that complainant has scheduled both full and partial exemptions of his claim against the agency under Debtor and Creditor law in the state of New York. Counsel also alleged that complainant does not accept the SA, nor does he accept the BT appointed by the Bankruptcy Court to represent his estate and seeks to have the SA declared invalid. The agency has not responded to complainant's statements challenging the SA between the agency and the BT.

However, the documentation of record does not indicate that the SA between the BT and the agency was approved by the Bankruptcy Court. In order for a settlement between a BT and a third party to be considered valid, it must be reviewed and approved by the Bankruptcy Court. See In re Del Grosso, 106 B.R. 165 (Bkrtcy. N.D. Ill., 1989). As such, the Commission cannot invalidate a SA which was never approved by the Bankruptcy Court. Furthermore, the records of the Bankruptcy Court indicate that on March 13, 2003, complainant converted his Chapter 7 bankruptcy filing to a Chapter 13 filing, meaning that there would be no Trustee appointed by the Bankruptcy Court to represent complainant's estate. At that point, complainant himself represented his estate and any SA entered into between himself and the agency would be the product of complainant's negotiations, with no third-party involvement. The Commission has no knowledge that complainant received any portion of the compensatory damages awarded by the AJ, or that the other, non-financial remedies ordered by the AJ were ever implemented. The records of the Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York indicate that complainant's bankruptcy filing remains pending with no settlement reached between himself and the agency. The Bankruptcy Court issued an order on December 18, 2003, confirming complainant's Chapter 13 plan, stating that complainant had 60 months to pay his creditors through a newly appointed trustee. As the agency sought withdrawal of its appeal in December 2002 based on its representation of a SA between itself and the BT, and that SA was never approved by the Bankruptcy Court, the Commission will consider the appellate contentions of both the complainant and the agency in the instant appeal.

It is not anticipated that the situation in Sellman will arise with any frequency, but the case is included to alert representatives to the hazards involved in any third-party settlements.
