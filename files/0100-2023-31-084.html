4. Fees as Sanctions

The fact that the underlying complaint is based on age discrimination does not preclude an administrative judge from awarding fees as sanction. In Adkins v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 0720080052 (2012), the Commission held:

In his interim decision, the AJ awarded as relief attorney's fees and costs because Complainant had prevailed on his age discrimination claim for claim E, under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501. But in his final decision, the AJ acknowledged that a prevailing complainant is not entitled to attorney's fees in an administrative proceeding under the ADEA, and instead ordered the Agency to pay attorney's fees as a sanction against the Agency for failing to timely complete the investigation.

Upon review, we find that the AJ did not abuse his discretion in ordering the Agency to pay attorney's fees as a sanction against the Agency in a proceeding alleging, in part, a Title VII violation.

Generally, there are two distinct provisions under which an AJ can order an Agency to pay attorney's fees and costs. The first is 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501(e), in which an AJ can award attorney's fees and costs to a complainant, who prevails on the merits in a case brought under Title VII or the Rehabilitation Act. Fitch v. Delft of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01A03071 (Aug. 15, 2002); Korchnak v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05910483 (Dec. 19, 1996). But this provision does not provide for the award of attorney's fees and costs at the administrative level to a prevailing complainant, who brings a federal sector complaint under the ADEA. See, e.g., Kinnear v. Dep't of Commerce, EEOC Request No. 05A30086 (Jan. 7, 2003) (citing Anderson v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01960716 (Aug. 27, 1998); Seymour v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05900257 (July 20, 1990)).

The second provision is 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3), which gives an AJ the authority to issue sanctions, including payment of attorney's fees and costs. This provision does not require a complainant to prevail on the merits in order for an AJ to issue sanctions.

For example, in Fitch v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01A03071 (Aug. 15, 2002), an AJ found that the complainant was not discriminated against under Title VII or the ADEA. But the AJ ordered the agency to pay reasonable attorney's fees associated with the filing of complainant's motion for sanctions for the agency's failure to timely respond to complainant's discovery requests. The Commission found that the AJ appropriately exercised her authority in ordering attorney's fees as a sanction for the agency's failure to timely comply with complainant's discovery request in a proceeding alleging a Title VII violation.

More recently, in Waller v. Dep't of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 0720030069 (May 25, 2007), the Commission upheld an AJ's order for an agency to pay attorney's fees and costs incurred in preparation of complainant's motion for sanctions, where the agency failed to provide responses to the complainant's interrogatories. The Commission upheld the AJ's finding of no discrimination under Title VII and the Equal Pay Act, but the Commission determined that the sanction of attorney's fees was appropriate because such monetary sanctions are necessary and appropriate to carrying out the responsibilities of the Commission.

We find the present case to be similar to Fitch, in that the AJ properly exercised his authority to issue sanctions, under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3), by ordering the Agency to pay attorney's fees in a proceeding alleging Title VII and ADEA violations. Accordingly, the Agency shall pay reasonable attorney's fees associated with preparing Complainant's motion for sanctions. The Commission notes that Complainant is also entitled to reasonable attorney's fees incurred in connection with this appeal, but only to the extent that such fees pertain to the scope of attorney's fees awarded as a sanction. See Waller, EEOC Appeal No. 0720030069.
