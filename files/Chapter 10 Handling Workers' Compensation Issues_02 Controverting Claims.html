CONTROVERTING CLAIMS

Sometimes an employee claims that they were injured at work, or that they sustained a work-related injury, and the agency has a reasonable basis to doubt the validity of the claim. For example, an employee may come in on Monday morning and tell a coworker that they hurt their back working in their yard over the weekend. An hour later, the employee tells their manager that they just hurt their back at work. In these cases, the agency may wish to dispute the validity of the employee's claim for workers' compensation benefits.

As a manager, you can exercise the agency's right to dispute a claim's validity by controverting it. Disputing questionable claims is important because you do not want the Department of Labor to approve a claim that, if the facts were fully known, would not have been approved. Keep in mind that your agency must reimburse the DOL for workers' compensation benefits paid to your employees. Just one workers' comp claim can financially burden an agency for many years.

However, managers and agencies should not dispute a claim based on hearsay or rumors. The agency's position should be supported by specific factual evidence. You will build your agency's credibility by sticking to the facts.

Note: You should only controvert a claim once you and your agency's workers' compensation team have supporting information and documentation from an investigation or medical report. The workers' comp specialist will provide you with important information and guidance on the controversion process.

When to controvert

Note: You should consult with a workers' compensation specialist before challenging an employee's claim.

If you decide to controvert an employee's claim, you must complete the relevant portion of Form CA-1 and submit detailed supporting information to the OWCP. In addition, you must notify the employee of a decision to controvert or to terminate continuation of pay, known as COP.

If the claim for COP is not immediately controverted by the agency and evidence supporting controversion comes to light after COP has begun, the agency may terminate and controvert COP.

Even if you decide to controvert the claim, the agency must continue the employee's regular pay unless at least one of the following conditions applies:

  • • The disability is a result of an occupational disease or illness.
  • • The employee is excluded by law.
  • • The employee is neither a citizen nor a resident of the United States, Canada, or the territory under the administration of the Panama Canal Commission (i.e., a foreign national employed outside these areas).
  • • The injury occurred off the employing agency's premises and the employee was not engaged in official "off-premises" duties.
  • • The employee caused the injury by their own willful misconduct or intended to bring about their injury or death or that of another person, or the employee's intoxication was the proximate cause of the injury.
  • • The injury was not reported on a form approved by the OWCP within 30 days of the injury.
  • • Work stoppage first occurred more than 45 days after the injury.
  • • The employee first reported the injury after employment was terminated.
  • • The employee is enrolled in the Civil Air Patrol, Peace Corps, Job Corps, Youth Conservation Corps, work study program, or other group covered by special legislation.

You may not continue pay under any of the above circumstances. Therefore, the employee would need to use leave or be placed in a leave without pay status until the case has been decided by the OWCP.

After COP has begun

Continuation of pay may be terminated or interrupted if the employee is removed or suspended as the result of a disciplinary action, but only if preliminary notice of the action was issued before COP began, and the action becomes final or takes effect during the COP period.

An agency may also terminate COP after it has begun if at least one of these conditions is present:

  • • Medical evidence showing that the employee is disabled because of an employment injury is not submitted within 10 calendar days. However, the agency need not wait 10 days to request this evidence. Pay may be continued without this evidence if the manager is satisfied that the employee sustained a disabling traumatic injury. Where the medical evidence is later provided, however, COP must be reinstated retroactive to the date of termination.
  • • The employee is no longer disabled according to medical evidence from the treating physician.
  • • Medical evidence from the treating physician shows that the employee is not totally disabled, and the employee refuses a written offer of a suitable alternative position (often referred to as a "light-duty position"), which is approved by the attending physician. If the OWCP later determines that the position was not suitable, the OWCP will direct the employer to grant the employee COP retroactive to the termination date.
  • • The employee returns to work with no loss of pay. However, if the employee's return to work is still within the 45-day window, they can use COP for medical appointments connected to the work-related injury, within the scheduled work shift.
  • • The employee's period of employment expires or employment is otherwise terminated, as established prior to the date of injury.
  • • OWCP directs the employer to stop COP, or COP has been paid for 45 calendar days.

An agency decision to terminate COP before the 45-day period ends must be supported by a written controversion setting forth the agency's reasons and filed with the OWCP no later than the effective date of the termination. The agency may support its decision with any of the reasons listed above.

If a claim is denied by the OWCP after COP has begun, any COP that was given will have to be converted to the employee's sick or annual leave or leave without pay.

However, after the OWCP makes the decision to allow a claim and workers' comp benefits begin, the agency may challenge the continuation of benefits if the claimant's situation changes, such as when:

  • • The employee's medical condition improves or no longer appears to be related to the work injury.
  • • New information comes to light about the extent of an injury.
  • • The claimant refuses a valid job offer within his medical restrictions.

Challenging claims after the end of COP. Agencies may decide to challenge the merits of a claim at any time in the process leading up to an OWCP determination. Specific opportunities occur when OWCP forms CA-1, CA-2, CA-2a, and CA-7 are submitted for response to DOL.

If the information that was submitted to DOL is vague, DOL will send a developmental letter to the agency and employee for clarification. If the information remains unclear to DOL, it will deny the claim but provide the employee with appeal rights.

If the claimant elects to appeal and a hearing takes place, a hearing transcript will be forwarded to the agency for comments.

Although controversion refers only to traumatic injury claims, which call for COP, the agency may challenge any workers' comp claim, including those for occupational disease.

What makes a claim questionable?

Workers' comp claims cost agencies a great deal of money. They also cost managers lots of time and effort and may create efficiency and production issues. Although your employees are entitled to the prompt payment of COP and the correct and timely processing of their FECA claims -- you may violate the law if you obstruct the process -- you should not let questionable claims through without controverting or challenging them.

What makes a claim questionable?

  • • Differing versions. If the employee has given differing versions of the incident to different people or several witnesses give differing accounts of what they saw.
  • • Previous injury. If the employee reported to work on the date of the claimed injury with the appearance of a preexisting condition or injury.
  • • Time lags. Look at the gap between the time the injury allegedly occurred and the time the employee reported it. Were they able to perform their usual duties during that gap?
  • • Other employment. If an employee who has claimed an injury is reported to be working at another job, you should ask them about the requirements of the other employment.

You need to discreetly investigate all reports of work-related injuries. Ask the claimant and any witnesses the following questions:

  • • How did it happen?
  • • What was the claimant doing right before the incident occurred?
  • • When did it happen?
  • • Where did it happen?
  • • Who was present when the claimant was injured?

Claimants must establish a causal relationship between the injury and work factors and meet the following additional conditions:

  • • Time. Claims must be filed within 30 days for COP entitlement and within three years of the date of injury for most injuries.
  • • Civilian employee. The claimant must have been a civilian federal employee when injured.
  • • Fact of injury. The report of injury must establish that the injury happened in the same way the employee described and should identify any witnesses. It should also note whether the manager agrees with the employee's account of the injury.
  • • Condition. Medical evidence must be submitted, including a history and diagnosis of the employee's condition, to establish whether an employee had a preexisting condition. Note that if an injury aggravates a preexisting condition, the claim may be accepted.
  • • Performance of duty. Claims must establish that the injury occurred during the course of employment or while the claimant was carrying out official duties. Performance of duty is discussed in greater detail in the next section.

Remember: You should work with workers' compensation and HR specialists who deal with these matters regularly because they are well-versed in what the law and regulations require. They know what to look for and how to justify controverting COP or contesting FECA claims. Don't be afraid to rely on their expertise to help you through the process.

Performance of duty

Performance of duty questions in particular may raise red flags. To establish performance of duty, an employee must show that the injury occurred during the course of employment or while they were carrying out official duties. Injuries should be looked at with special care if they occurred:

  • • During travel to and from work. Generally, an injury that occurs during an employee's commute to and from work is not covered under workers' compensation, unless the injury occurred in an area that is owned by the federal government or the employee is somehow furthering the agency's business.
  • • During the employee's lunch hour. An employee's injury during their lunch break may be compensable if it occurred on the agency's premises. If the employee leaves the agency's premises, the injury is generally not compensable.
  • • While on work-related travel. An employee who is on travel status is in the performance of duty 24 hours per day with respect to any injury that results from activities essential or incidental to their special duties. Injuries arising out of the necessity of sleeping in hotels or eating in restaurants are usually compensable.
  • • In vehicular accidents. This issue can come up during an employee's travel to and from work, during a lunch break, and while on work-related travel.
  • • During recreational activities. An injury occurring during a recreational or social activity is compensable when it occurs on the agency's premises, the agency requires participation, or the agency derives a substantial benefit beyond an improvement in employee health and morale.

For an employee with fixed hours and a fixed work location, an injury occurring on the agency's premises while the employee is going to and from work or at lunch time is compensable, but an off-premises injury is not compensable. However, there are exceptions to this rule. One of these is the proximity exception, which extends the agency's premises to the hazardous conditions adjacent to the agency's premises. For example, a crosswalk between the agency's building and the employee parking area can be considered part of the agency's premises.

Another exception is the special errand. Ordinarily, cases falling within this exception involve travel where the employee agrees to perform a special task. An employee is covered under the FECA from the time they leave home, even though the journey may be similar to the employee's normal route when going to work. For example, an employee who travels off the agency's premises to attend a mandatory training or to go to the bank for the agency is performing a special errand.

Even when an injury occurs on agency premises and during duty hours, consider whether factors outside of work actually caused the injury, such as deliberate misconduct or intoxication caused by alcohol or drug use.

Union activities. Injuries to employees performing representational functions entitling them to official time are covered under the Federal Employees' Compensation Act, as are injuries to union employees working on union grievances. Injuries to employees engaged in the internal business of a labor organization, such as soliciting new members or collecting dues, are not covered.

Telework. Employees directly engaged in the performance of their duties during authorized telework are covered under the FECA. In these cases, it must be determined whether the employee was performing assigned duties, was engaged in an activity reasonably incidental to the assignment, or deviated from the assignment and was engaged in a personal activity.

An agency can control the work environment on its premises and can maintain safety to reduce the likelihood of workplace injury. However, the environment of an employee's home is not under the agency's control and is treated differently from an injury that occurs on the agency's premises. Therefore, the rules applying to employees injured on an agency's premises during a break aren't applicable to teleworking employees.

Here's an example of when the ECAB found a telework injury was in the performance of duty: A claims examiner filed a traumatic injury claim alleging she sprained her right arm when she caught her computer monitor as it slid off her worktable while teleworking. She explained she was moving her worktable because she had a meeting with her supervisor and wanted only her window curtains to be visible in the background. The ECAB found that the claimant was in the performance of duty, as she was engaged in an activity essential to her employment or reasonably incidental to the duties that she was hired to perform.

Mistakes to avoid

The controversion process can be daunting, but you can go a long way by avoiding some common mistakes. After a traumatic injury has occurred or is claimed to have occurred:

Do not:

  • • Authorize COP for an occupational disease. Sometimes it's not easy to determine whether an employee's condition or injury occurred during a single shift, or whether it developed over a period of time. Try to clarify this issue with medical providers. If the claim is not clearly for a traumatic injury, you are not permitted to authorize COP.
  • • Act independently. Be sure to work closely with your agency's workers' comp specialists as well as HR before making any decisions to controvert or question an employee's claim.
  • • Obstruct an employee's rights. Your employees are entitled to the prompt payment of COP and the correct and timely processing of their FECA claims. They are also entitled to appeal OWCP decisions to the ECAB. Obstructing that process may violate the law.
  • • Fail to take action when necessary. It is important to question a potential fraudulent or questionable claim for benefits. Being a "nice manager" and allowing a claim through when you should challenge it can be bad for workplace morale.
  • • Guess at what might have happened. Make sure you take the time to investigate the situation thoroughly. Don't draw any conclusions until you have conducted an investigation that includes talking to witnesses and other employees to find out exactly what happened.

Ask FMG

Q. What are some of the most common reasons agencies controvert claims?

A. Common reasons for controverting a claim for continuation of pay include:

  • • The employee did not file a compensation claim within 30 days of the date of injury.
  • • The employee's disability was not caused by a traumatic injury. COP is not authorized for occupational diseases.
  • • The injury did not occur in the performance of the employee's federal duties.

Q. I've completed my investigation. What should I include in the case file?

A. Upon completion of the investigation, include the following documentation in the case file:

  • • A properly completed CA-1 or CA-2 form.
  • • A diagram of the accident site, to identify its exact location and cause.
  • • A copy of any accident reports.
  • • A complete and up-to-date medical report, including all treating physicians' and practitioners' notes.
  • • Any light-duty job offer or duty status report.
  • • A leave audit for the previous 12 months.
  • • A memo indicating any pending disciplinary action.
  • • A position description.

Q. Is an injured employee who is scheduled to be separated entitled to COP?

A. An employee who reports a traumatic injury on or before the date of separation is entitled to COP up to the date of separation and to compensation thereafter.

Q. Are there different COP rules for employees of the U.S. Postal Service?

A. Postal Service employees who request COP as a result of a traumatic injury are not entitled to COP for the first three days of temporary disability. During this waiting period, an employee may choose to use annual leave, sick leave, or leave without pay. If disability eventually exceeds 14 days or is followed by permanent disability, COP will be paid for the three-day waiting period and the USPS will reinstate any sick or annual leave used by the employee during this period.

Q. Are temporary employees entitled to COP?

A. Yes. Temporary employees are entitled to COP on the same basis as permanent employees. If a termination date has been set for a temporary employee prior to the injury, however, COP need not be continued past the date of termination as long as the Form SF-52 (personnel action) showing the date of termination has been completed. In this instance, the OWCP will pay compensation to a disabled worker after employment has ceased. Like any other employee, a temporary worker who first reports a traumatic injury after employment ends is not entitled to COP.
