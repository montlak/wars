i. Effect of Agency Anti-Harassment Policies on Liability

In Green v. Postmaster General, 01955142 (1997), a case decided before Burlington Industries and Faragher, the Commission found that an agency policy against sexual harassment did not insulate it from liability for supervisory harassment where there was no showing that the policy was widely distributed and enforced. The Commission also noted there was no "reasonably available avenue" for employees to pursue complaints of harassment with someone of authority who could investigate and resolve the complaints. As explained in Green:

The Commission's March 1990 Policy Guidance on Sexual Harassment provides that an employer can be liable for a supervisor's actions under the theory of "apparent authority," Id.; see also Restatement (Second) of Agency, 7, 8, 219(2)(d) (1958). That is, "[a]n employer is...liable for a supervisor's actions if these actions represent the exercise of authority that third parties reasonably believe him to possess by virtue of his employer's conduct." The Policy Guidance explains that:

[I]n the absence of a strong, widely disseminated, and consistently enforced employer policy against sexual harassment, and an effective complaint procedure, employees could reasonably believe that a harassing supervisor's actions will be ignored, tolerated, or even condoned by upper management.... The Commission generally will find an employer liable for 'hostile environment' sexual harassment by a supervisor when the employer failed to establish an explicit policy against sexual harassment and did not have a reasonably available avenue by which victims of sexual harassment could complain to someone with authority to investigate and remedy the problem.

Id. at 25-26.

Although it appears that the agency has a policy on sexual harassment, the agency failed to present sufficient evidence to show that it is "a strong, widely disseminated, and consistently enforced employer policy...and [that there is] an effective complaint procedure...." In this regard, the only evidence regarding the agency's policy was two management officials' testimony to the effect that sexual harassment "would not be tolerated" and appellant's testimony that she had seen the policy posted on a bulletin board and had "glanced at it" prior to the incidents at issue. Consequently, although the agency took prompt action to end the harassment, the Commission finds that the 204 B Supervisor was acting under apparent authority of the agency and that liability would ensue if the 204 B Supervisor's behavior constituted sexual harassment. See Parker v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01943227 (June 8, 1995).
