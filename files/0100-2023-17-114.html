3. New and Material Evidence

The criteria for reconsideration changed in accordance with the 1999 amendments to Part 1614. The Commission has held that a party is no longer entitled to reconsideration upon submission of new and material evidence not previously available. See, e.g., Farley v. Secretary of Navy, 05A20754 (2002). The Commission has also held that one of the criteria for granting a request for reconsideration is new and material evidence not previously available. For example, in Jordan v. Postmaster General, 05A51226 (2006), the Commission held:

In order to merit the reconsideration of a prior Commission decision, the requesting party must submit written argument or evidence which tends to establish that at least one of the criteria of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.407(c) is met. The Commission's scope of review on a request for reconsideration is narrow. Lopez v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05890749 (September 28, 1989). A request to reconsider is not merely a form of a second appeal. Regensberg v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900850 (September 7, 1990). Instead, it is an opportunity to submit newly discovered evidence, not previously available; to establish substantive error in a previous decision; or to explain why the previous decision will have effects beyond the case at hand. Lyke v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900769 (September 27, 1990).

Curiously, the Commission's cite to Lyke invokes a case decided nine years before its regulatory change.

The Commission may, however, reconsider any decision on its own motion, and submission of new and material evidence may still lead to reconsideration as a matter of discretion. See Morano v. Secretary of Treasury, 05A01203 (2000). It is unclear how the Commission will respond to such requests under the 1999 amendments. However, it will not reopen cases based on the submission of new evidence that was available at the time of the initial investigation. For example, in Luna v. Secretary of Defense, 05980814 (2001), the Commission warned the agency about the consequences of not gathering sufficient evidence during the investigation:

The Commission advises the agency that filing a request to reopen is not the proper vehicle for submission of the documentation at issue. To the extent that any witnesses are now unavailable, the agency may note that fact in the record of the supplemental investigation. However, insofar as the agency failed to obtain adequate statements and to secure the proper documents and records at the time of the oriGINAl investigation, the agency may wish to consider Commission precedent regarding the drawing of an adverse inference in such cases.

In Ccihuatl v. International Boundary & Water Comm'n., 05A20461 (2002), the Commission summarily rejected the agency's submission of the collective bargaining agreement in support of its request for reconsideration, noting that the agency should have provided the agreement as part of the record submitted during the initial appeal. See also Phillips v. Secretary of Defense, 05A21113 (2002).

By contrast, the Commission will consider new evidence that the complainant's appeal was actually timely. For example, in Hayes v. Postmaster General, 05A20689 (2003), the Commission reversed its previous decision that the appeal was untimely filed upon submission of evidence by the complainant establishing that it was filed seven days before the deadline. A distinguishing feature in the Ccihuatl and Phillips cases, as well as the Hayes case, is that the agency had ample opportunity to submit the evidence prior to the request to reconsider, but there was no such opportunity in Hayes since the complainant did not know his appeal was considered untimely until it was dismissed as such.

Prior to the 1999 amendments, the Commission held that a request to reopen is "an opportunity for a party to submit newly discovered evidence, to establish substantive error, or to explain circumstances that warrant special consideration. . . . The party requesting reopening must do more than reargue the facts and, instead, should explain why the Commission's prior decision was incorrect." Aragon v. Secretary of Interior, 05930556 (1994).

The Commission explained its reasons for the change at 64 Fed. Reg. 37,654 (1999):

[T]he Commission proposed to revise the reconsideration process to approximate the process used by the MSPB, reallocate some resources to the improvement of the appellate process and discourage automatic requests for reconsideration whenever a party loses on appeal. Parties may still request reconsideration but it will only be granted, in the discretion of the Commission, if the requester has demonstrated that the appellate decision involved a clearly erroneous interpretation of material fact or law, or the appellate decision will have a substantial impact on the policies, practices or operations of the agency. The comments received were mixed. The unfavorable comments were mostly from agencies although many other agencies favored the change. The objectors raised the same objections discussed in the preamble to the proposed rule. After considering all comments, we have decided to adopt the proposed rule without change. The proposal makes the reconsideration procedure available for those cases where the requestor demonstrates that there are errors of fact or law that would affect the outcomes of the cases and for those cases that will have a substantial impact. By preserving the Commission's discretion, it also will allow the Commission to reallocate its resources to the improvement of the appellate process.

The elimination of the criteria that allowed for the submission of new and material evidence not previously available will not deter all from filing requests for reconsideration based on new evidence and its pre-1999 amendment case law likely remains instructive. For example, in one case decided prior to the 1999 amendments, the EEOC adopted the date of receipt of the appeal due to an illegible postmark and subsequently determined the appeal was untimely. The Commission reopened the case when the appellant submitted a postmarked receipt as evidence that the appeal was timely mailed. See Rivers v. USPS, 05850320 (1986); see also Weinberger v. USPS, 05850105 (1986). Arguably, such cases would fall under the clearly erroneous interpretation of a material fact criterion.

When, under the 1992 version of Part 1614, a party could obtain reconsideration by submission of new and material evidence not previously available, the due diligence requirement for submission of new evidence was relaxed where the complainant was pro se and likely did not understand the significance of presenting the evidence at the hearing. In Fay v. Postmaster General, 05950612 (1996), the Commission accepted new evidence in the form of testimony from three witnesses that rebutted the credibility findings of the administrative judge. The Commission specifically noted that the complainant may not "have been aware of the importance of their testimony prior to the AJ's credibility determination and the previous decision's affirmance of that determination." Id. Similarly, in Hunter v. Postmaster General, 05960762 (1998), the Commission relaxed the reconsideration standards for a pro se complainant where it found she likely did not understand the nature of the evidence required to show her previous appeal was timely.

Although the previous reconsideration regulations required that new and material evidence not be previously available, in some cases the Commission has waived that requirement. See, e.g., Saunders v. Secretary of Navy, 05950859 (1996) ("The Commission notes that while the documentation submitted by appellant is not new or previously unavailable, as it concerned matters which occurred in September 1994, it is material to the underlying issue herein.").

However, in Lisk v. Secretary of Army, 05950896 (1997), the Commission denied the agency's request for reconsideration because the evidence it presented, while material, was available when the previous decision was issued:

The agency's proffer of additional documents at this late date does not warrant reconsideration. Not one of those proffered satisfies the requirement that proffered evidence be new, material, and not readily available when the previous decision was issued. We acknowledge that most of the proffered documents are documents developed as part of the processing of appellant's complaint, and thus material. For example, submitted are the complaints dated December 30, 1992 and June 1, 1994, and the AJ's February 1994 remand letter. However, all of the documents predate the agency's FAD of June 8, 1994 and thus obviously were readily available at the appeal stage. The agency cannot withhold material documents from the Commission during the processing of an appeal, and later use their absence from the record to justify an RTR. The Commission also rejects the agency's wish for a second look at its determinations of untimeliness and mootness; the time to support its determinations was at the appeal stage.

In Williams v. Secretary of Navy, 05890241 (1989), the Commission had the following warning for agencies that do not comply with requests to provide a complete record on appeal and then seek to introduce the record in requests for reconsideration:

The agency is fully responsible for the fact that the complaint file was incomplete when our previous decision was issued. We will not allow this agency to avoid the consequences of its incomplete submission through the utilization of a request to reopen wherein it attempts to cure its prior incomplete submission. In this regard, the agency's late or incomplete submission of complaint files is the rule rather than the exception. . . .

The Commission rejected the agency's plea that submission of its complaint file, while late, was prior to issuance of an appeals decision noting it had repeatedly warned the agency about late submission of complaint files.

The Commission also appeared willing to reopen and reconsider a previous decision based on evidence that should have been submitted earlier where the evidence established a procedural flaw in the complaint. For example, in Gaines v. Secretary of Air Force, 05960851 (1998), the Commission reopened its previous decision where the agency belatedly submitted the complaint file with its request to reopen:

In its response to appellant's request for reconsideration, the agency acknowledged that, on appeal, it had failed to provide the Commission with evidence then available to support its dismissal of the reassignment allegation. The agency did not request reconsideration. . .but requested the Commission to reconsider the matter on its own motion.

In addition to its comments, the agency submitted a number of exhibits. Exhibit 10 is the complaint file for Agency No. 5X1L95002. The complaint file reveals that the reassignment raised in the instant complaint is the same reassignment raised in the complaint in Agency No. 5X1L95002 and has previously been before the Commission. Therefore, the reassignment allegation in the instant complaint must be dismissed.
