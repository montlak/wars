e. Content of Notice of Right to a Hearing

The notice of right to a hearing is required to state clearly that the employee has a right to a hearing conducted by an impartial examiner who is not an employee of the agency. For example, the Commission in Spires v. Department of Navy, 01830150 (1984), held that proposed a disposition with a notice of hearing rights, under former Part 1613, was defective because the complainant was not advised of the right to hearing before an impartial hearing officer. In Spires, it was error for the notice to state that the hearing would be conducted by the agency head or his designee and, under such circumstances, the employee would be given another opportunity to request a hearing.

The notice of hearing rights must be sufficiently clear so that a reasonable person would understand the notice. Ling v. Secretary of Navy, 05910295, 05910752 (1991). A notice which simply states that the complainant is entitled to a final decision with or without a hearing, and that a request for final decision must be submitted within a specified time period is legally insufficient. The notice must clearly inform the complainant that he/she is entitled to a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge prior to the final agency decision and that a request for such a hearing must be submitted within the applicable time period. The fact that the agency used standard language from its complaint processing manual was not relevant to the determination of whether a reasonable person would understand the notice. Id.

In Coleman v. Secretary of Navy, 01922117 (1993), the Commission further explained the required hearing notice and held that a notice is "fatally defective" when it does not clearly inform the complainant of his right to a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge, even when the request for a hearing is admittedly late:

Previous Commission decisions have stressed the necessity for the agency to clearly notify the complainant of his/her right to a hearing, and to clearly inform the complainant that any request for a hearing must be made within 15 days of receipt of the proposed disposition. Indeed, in at least two recent Commission decisions, the identical statement of rights attached to the proposed disposition in the instant case was deemed unclear and inadequate. See Ornellas v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05910882 (February 10, 1992); Ling v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05910295 (June 17, 1991), aff'd, EEOC Request No. 05910752 (December 16, 1991).

The notice of right to a hearing must inform a complainant that he or she is obligated to copy the agency on any request for a hearing. See 29 CFR 1614.108(h) ("The complainant shall send a copy of the request for a hearing to the agency EEO office."). Normally, a failure to copy the agency could render the request legally deficient and deprive the Commission of jurisdiction over the complaint for the purposes of conducting a hearing. See Fawn G. v. Secretary of Navy, 0120151450 (2017). But, the Commission had held that an agency's notice is "fundamentally defective" if it does not put the complainant on notice that she may forfeit her right to a hearing for a failure to copy the agency on the hearing request, as was the case in Fawn G. The Commission in Fawn G. upheld the complainant's hearing request despite her failure to put the agency on notice of her hearing request.

The notice in Barabara C. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 2021000638 (2022), for instance, read:

I understand that if I have not provided the agency with a copy of my request for a Hearing, this request will have no effect and an Administrative Judge will not be appointed, or if appointed, the Administrative Judge will place my request into an inactive docket until such time as I inform the Agency of my request for a Hearing.

Based on that language, the Commission in Barabara C. found the notice to be "fundamentally defective":

[T]he Commission previously found that this identical language was fundamentally insufficient to put a complainant on notice that she risked forfeiting her right to a hearing, and in fact, would lead her to believe that she risked far less dire consequences for this omission. Specifically, the notice advised Complainant that her failure to provide a copy of her request for a hearing could result in non-processing of the request or placement on an inactive docket, which could then be remedied by Complainant subsequently notifying the Agency of her hearing request. Pearsey v. Dep't of Veterans Affs., EEOC Appeal No. 0120103636 (Apr. 19, 2012).

On April 24, 2020, the Agency completed the investigation and transmitted the ROI to Complainant. The Agency again informed Complainant that she could request an EEOC hearing, and advised, "[p]lease note that if these offices are not served with a completed copy of the 'Request for an Administrative Hearing form,' the EEOC will not be able to process your request." The Agency attached the same hearing request form. ROI at Exhibit 5. We find that this second notice simply warned Complainant that the EEOC would be unable to process her hearing request if she failed to serve a copy to the Agency, and there is no caution that she risked forfeiting her right to a hearing.

In response to Complainant's appeal, the Agency argues that it put Complainant on notice that her failure to properly serve the Agency with her request for a hearing would result in "substantial delays." However, we find that experiencing "substantial delays" is not the same as a risk of forfeiting a right to a hearing. We also note that this case is distinguishable from Blanca B., supra, because the agency explicitly informed the complainant that if she failed to send a copy of her hearing request to the agency, it "will issue a final decision on [her] complaint, resulting in the loss of my right to a hearing." In this case, the Agency's notices failed to properly inform Complainant of any risk to forfeiting her right to a hearing if she did not send a copy of her hearing request to the Agency.

There is no other evidence to show that Complainant had notice that she risked forfeiting her right to a hearing by failing to provide the Agency notice of her request. Without such notice, we find that Complainant's April 25, 2020, hearing request effectively transferred jurisdiction of the complaint to the AJ, and that the Agency had no jurisdiction to issue a final decision on August 11, 2020.
