8. Family Death or Illness

A death in the family can constitute a basis for waiving the time limit for contacting a counselor. The Commission has set no precise parameters for when a family death will excuse untimely counselor contact. But, in Davis v. Postmaster General, 01972450 (1998), the Commission remanded a dismissed complaint for further investigation of the timeliness issue where the complainant maintained that during the relevant time period his mother had become seriously ill and ultimately died. In Harmon v. Postmaster General, 01A05548 (2001), the Commission upheld dismissal of a complaint for untimely counselor contact where the complainant's mother was terminally ill during the relevant time period.

In Roig v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A12284 (2001), the Commission rejected the complainant's explanation that he did not timely contact a counselor because there had been deaths in his family and he was caring for a disabled child. In rejecting the explanation, the Commission noted that the complainant had been attempting to resolve his complaint over a nonpromotion with the personnel office.

Even if death or other family tragedy excuses a complainant from immediately seeking precomplaint counseling, in order to waive the time limit the complainant must demonstrate that he or she promptly sought counseling once the tragedy passed. For example, in Clayton v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A13615 (2001), the Commission declined to waive the time limit for counselor contact where the complainant's best friend died the day after the alleged discriminatory action and then she learned that her sister had cancer and had to undergo surgery. The Commission noted that the complainant in Clayton took 13 days off for these tragedies, but that did not justify her five-month delay in contacting a counselor.

In Complainant v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120151569 (2015), the Commission upheld dismissal of a complaint for untimely counselor contact where her husband was diagnosed with congestive heart failure and she contended that she was caring for him and bringing him to medical appointments.

The Commission has been inconsistent on whether a death in the family will waive its time limits, particularly in cases involving the 15-day time frame for filing the formal complaint. See the section on "Death or Illness in Family" in Chapter 3.
