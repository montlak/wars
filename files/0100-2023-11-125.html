XI. ALTERNATIVE DISPUTE RESOLUTION

In a continuing attempt to encourage early resolution of complaints, the 1999 amendments to Part 1614 expanded upon the alternative dispute resolution requirements of the 1992 version of Part 1614. Under the previous version of Part 1614, the precomplaint counseling period could be extended to 90 days where the agency offered ADR under an established plan and the complainant accepted. 29 CFR 1614.105(f) (1992). The 1999 amendments to Part 1614, which were carried forward in the 2012 revisions, require all agencies to either set up an alternative dispute resolution program or have ready access to one.

Under 29 CFR 1614.102(b)(2), agencies are required to:

Establish or make available an alternative dispute resolution program. Such program must be available for both the pre-complaint process and the formal complaint process.

Although ADR can be offered at any stage of the complaint process, the emphasis is on ADR during the precomplaint stage. This makes sense for a number of reasons. The parties have expended fewer resources at the precomplaint stage, making resolution more feasible from a cost perspective. Moreover, the longer the parties are engaged in a dispute, the more polarized and firmly entrenched they often become in their views of the case.

During precomplaint processing, the agency may offer ADR in lieu of precomplaint EEO counseling. The agency has discretion as to whether it will offer ADR in any given case. In other words, the agency is required to have an ADR program, but it is not required to offer ADR in every case. An agency may not, however, preclude entire bases of discrimination from its ADR program. Chapter 3 of MD-110 provides extensive guidance on ADR programs.

In cases where an agency does offer ADR, the complainant's decision regarding whether to participate is entirely voluntary. EEO counselors must advise complainants of their right to elect participation in either ADR or traditional counseling. 29 CFR 1614.105(b)(2). Complainants may always elect to undergo traditional EEO counseling instead of ADR if they so choose. A complainant who chooses ADR, however, waives his right to undergo traditional precomplaint counseling. If the complainant elects ADR, the precomplaint processing period is extended to 90 days.

In relevant part, 29 CFR 1614.105 provides:

(b)(2) Counselors shall advise aggrieved persons that, where the agency agrees to offer ADR in the particular case, they may choose between participation in the alternative dispute resolution program and the counseling activities provided for in paragraph (c) of this section.

...

(d) Unless the aggrieved person agrees to a longer counseling period under paragraph (e) of this section, or the aggrieved person chooses an alternative dispute resolution procedure in accordance with paragraph (b)(2) of this section, the Counselor shall conduct the final interview with the aggrieved person within 30 days of the date the aggrieved person contacted the agency's EEO office to request counseling

...

(f) Where the aggrieved person chooses to participate in an alternative dispute resolution procedure in accordance with paragraph (b)(2) of this section, the pre-complaint processing period shall be 90 days. If the claim has not been resolved before the 90th day, the notice described in paragraph (d) of this section shall be issued.

Simply put, the reason for an emphasis on ADR at December 27, 2005 the precomplaint stage is to resolve complaints as quickly as possible. The Commission noted some of the advantages of ADR in MD-110 at 3-1:

Agencies and complainants have realized many advantages from utilizing ADR. ADR offers the parties the opportunity for an early, informal resolution of disputes in a mutually satisfactory fashion. ADR usually costs less and uses fewer resources than do traditional administrative or adjudicative processes, particularly processes that include a hearing or litigation. Early resolution of disputes through ADR can make agency resources available for mission-related programs and activities. The agency can avoid costs such as court reporters and expert witnesses. In addition, employee morale can be enhanced when agency management is viewed as open-minded and cooperative in seeking to resolve disputes through ADR.

The emphasis is on ADR during the precomplaint stage, however, agencies are required to have ADR available during all phases of complaint processing.
