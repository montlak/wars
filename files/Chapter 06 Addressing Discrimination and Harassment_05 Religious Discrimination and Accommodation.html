RELIGIOUS DISCRIMINATION AND ACCOMMODATION

Religion can be a touchy subject, especially in the workplace. As a federal manager, you could be called on to balance many competing interests that are related to religion. Although this may seem like a complicated area, the basics provided here can put you well on your way to handling religious issues confidently. But if you run into a tricky situation, you're not limited to the knowledge you gain here -- you can always contact your HR and EEO offices for guidance.

Keep in mind that the EEOC and the U.S. Supreme Court view religion in very broad terms. The EEOC defines religion as moral or ethical beliefs as to right and wrong that are "sincerely held with the strength of traditional religious views." The Supreme Court looks at the sincerity of belief, as well as whether the individual views his beliefs as religious.

Although you don't need to focus on legal definitions, you should have a general idea that religion can extend beyond traditionally set boundaries.

All federal employees who belong to a religion, or have no belief in God at all, are protected by discrimination law. Because religion is defined broadly, the name of an employee's religion isn't as important as you might think. Your role as manager is to:

  • • Appropriately address requests for the accommodation of religious practices.
  • • Properly handle religious discussion and other religious expression in the workplace.
  • • Make fair employment decisions that are not based on religion.
  • • Ensure there is no religious harassment in your unit.

Reasonable accommodation

You're probably familiar with the term reasonable accommodation. But it's more likely that you're familiar with the concept as it applies to disability. As in disability accommodation, you must make a good-faith effort to reasonably accommodate an employee's religious needs. Employees who request a religious accommodation do not need to use technical or specific language. They do, however, have to tie their accommodation request to their religion.

Some examples of common religious accommodations are:

  • • Changing the work schedule in order to grant an employee's request for leave to celebrate a religious holiday.
  • • Modifying an employee's schedule to allow them to attend religious services.
  • • Permitting an employee to wear religious clothing or insignia.
  • • Allowing an employee to accrue comp time in order to leave a few hours early in order to celebrate their Sabbath at sundown.
  • • Finding an empty office that can be designated for individual prayer.

Whatever the request for accommodation, you can best avoid EEO trouble by taking the request seriously and carefully trying to find a way to meet the employee's religious needs. If you have any questions about your responsibilities, contact your HR or EEO office.

Requests for accommodations that impact work schedules are fairly common and often present a stumbling block for managers. These requests are often made so an employee can observe his Sabbath or attend religious services on a regular basis. Here are some potential accommodations to consider:

  • • Voluntary schedule swaps.
  • • Lateral transfers.
  • • Changing job assignments.
  • • Flexible scheduling.

Examples of flexible scheduling include:

  • • Flexible arrival and departure times.
  • • Flexible work breaks.
  • • Use of lunchtime in exchange for early departure.
  • • Staggered work hours.

Clothing accommodations. Another category of accommodations that has created uncertainty for managers involves dress codes. The wearing of headscarves and yarmulkes is generally accepted in the federal workplace. But what if you're worried that a request would "cross the line"?

Generally, you need to determine if accommodating an employee's request to wear certain religious dress would be an undue hardship on your agency. Denying the request out of hand would be a mistake. Discussing the issue and exploring options would establish that you made a good-faith effort to find a suitable accommodation.

Uniforms are a particular problem. You may determine that some sort of religious headdress would not significantly impact the effect of the uniform. On the other hand, you might determine that the clothing the employee seeks to wear would obscure the uniform such that it would not serve its function, i.e., it would not be easily recognized by the public or it would not convey sufficient authority.

Although a careful and logical determination would probably be sufficient to show that allowing the deviation in dress would be an undue hardship on the mission of your agency, it might be better to consult your HR or EEO office before making a difficult decision on a clothing issue.

Accommodating religious displays. There may be a situation where religious articles in a public office space, such as a public reception area or lobby, could be interpreted as the agency's endorsement of a particular religion. However, prohibiting an employee from displaying even a fairly large number of religious articles in their own non-public workspace may constitute religious discrimination. Accordingly, you must act with care in this area. When dealing with religion, it's best not to dismiss any requests or make any knee-jerk decisions, even if the display makes you or other employees uncomfortable.

First, be careful that you don't base a decision on content. You can't prohibit items from a religion you don't like or understand, while allowing displays by employees of other religions.

Next, think about where the employee works:

  • • Is it a public area?
  • • A private space?

Allowing an employee to have a large display in a public area might be perceived as an endorsement of a particular religion, and you should be able to ask that employee to limit, though probably not eliminate, the display of religious items.

If you aren't sure about a specific religious display, it's best to consult with your HR or EEO office before taking action.

Undue hardship. As in the case of disability accommodation, an agency may deny a requested religious accommodation if it would create an undue hardship. However, the standard of undue hardship is quite different in the case of religious accommodation. In the accommodation of disabilities, undue hardship means "significant difficulty or expense." It takes a lot less to show undue hardship when it comes to religious accommodation. You are only required to provide accommodations that cost relatively little or do not impact the efficiency of the agency.

But even though it takes less to show undue hardship in connection with religious accommodation, you are expected to make a reasonable effort to provide accommodation. If you don't even try, or if you give up easily, you may open your agency up to a claim of religious discrimination. Your efforts are viewed on a case-by-case basis, so pay specific attention to each request for accommodation.

Showing undue hardship. If you deny a request for religious accommodation, you should be prepared to explain why it would be an undue hardship. Deciding whether a religious accommodation would be an undue hardship will depend on your agency's circumstances and resources.

For example, if an agency has a lot of workers doing the same jobs and a flexible scheduling process, it could be a fairly simple matter to allow an employee to take Saturday as their permanent day off. The effort needed to schedule this would not be an undue hardship, and other employees should be readily available to work that day.

On the other hand, it would probably be an undue hardship for an agency to allow an employee to take every Sunday off if it would leave the agency short-staffed, prevent other employees from ever having Sunday off, or require the agency to pay significant overtime costs on a regular basis.

Claims of undue hardship must be supported by specific evidence, not mere speculation. An agency is not expected to make significant sacrifices in order to provide accommodation, but a request is not an undue hardship simply because it requires some time to implement or because you worry that other employees will ask for the same accommodation.

Non-essential religious activities. Some activities, like group-study classes and choir practice may be offered by a religious group, but are not required aspects of the religion. An agency has no obligation to accommodate these non-essential types of religious activities.

However, when assessing whether an activity is non-essential, managers must be very careful not to generalize. For example, attending a religious conference may not be essential to some religions. However, in other religions, it may be crucial to maintaining certain religious credentials. Listen carefully to your employees before making decisions.

Seniority. Agencies do not have to make an exception to collective bargaining agreements to accommodate an employee's religious beliefs.

This generally comes into play when seniority is involved. For example, if the normal work rotation includes a day the employee wishes to take off for religious reasons (as a weekly Sabbath, for example, or as a yearly holiday), and employees with more seniority have a superior right to take off on that day, the agency need not bump those senior employees. On the other hand, the agency should show a good-faith effort toward reasonable accommodation by trying to arrange voluntary schedule swaps. If it works, everyone is happy. If it doesn't, the employee should be satisfied that the agency tried. If the employee persists in an EEO complaint, the agency is protected from a finding of discrimination.

Religious discussion in the workplace

Many of us have been told since we grew into adulthood that religion is one of those subjects to avoid in general conversation. But not all federal employees heed that advice. What is your role as a manager when you fear religious discussion could be making some employees uncomfortable?

Generally, you can't suppress employees' private religious speech in the workplace while not regulating other types of private employee speech. An example would be discussion of a controversial television show that has a comparable effect on the efficiency of the workplace. To stop employees from talking about religion but not the television show would be to engage in presumptively unlawful content or viewpoint discrimination.

Similarly, you should not limit talk about one type of religion, while allowing employees to discuss another religion.

But limits can be drawn. Employees should be protected from religious harassment. Managers and supervisors should carefully monitor employee exchanges concerning religious matters and step in if it seems passionate emotions are overtaking rational discussion.

Be prepared to take swift action against an employee who engages in frequent demeaning and intimidating religious discussion. These discussions, particularly if directed at specific employees, could create a hostile work environment. And of course, you must at all costs avoid participating in these discussions yourself. As a manager, your own actions model acceptable behavior for your employees. Any attempt by you to promote your own religious views or attack those of others invites an EEO complaint and exposes your agency to a possible claim of religious discrimination.

Although it may be difficult to achieve, an atmosphere that promotes the appreciation of diversity and cultural education can help defuse individuals who seek to divide the workforce by highlighting differences, religious or otherwise.

Warning: Employees who are attached to their religious beliefs often want to share them with others. But when an employee tries to "spread the word," however positive and nonhostile the intent, the effort may be interpreted by those who don't share the employee's beliefs as an attempt at conversion. You will have to walk a fine line to avoid stifling protected speech on the one hand and exposing unwilling participants to harassment on the other. It's best to seek advice from your agency's EEO and HR experts. It may be a matter of degree. Employees who don't want to listen to pervasive religious speech from colleagues shouldn't have to.

Although most religious expression takes place as discussions among coworkers, you may need to address religious discussion with the public. Employees generally have the right to express religious views in the workplace, but agencies are not required to allow religious conversations that would create an undue hardship. This means agencies can prohibit employees from initiating religious discussions with members of the public in connection with their official functions.

Unfair treatment

Treating an employee less favorably or unfairly because of their religion is called disparate treatment and is against the law.

Unfair treatment may be conscious on the part of a manager who feels hostile toward a certain religion, or it may be relatively unconscious as a manager favors an individual who belongs to the same religion and thus seems to have a more similar outlook.

The bottom line is that a manager who can't provide a reasonable, nondiscriminatory reason for a decision that appears to be based on religion risks inviting a successful claim of religious discrimination.

Although you can't control whether an employee names you in an EEO complaint, you can take actions to ensure that your decisions aren't influenced by religious considerations. If your decisions are viewed as transparent and fair, employees should be less likely to falsely assume you are motivated by discrimination.

To avoid claims of unfair treatment based on religion:

  • • Consider employee qualifications using the same assessment criteria and tools for all employees.
  • • Document the reasons for your decisions. Not only will this allow you to carefully consider the validity of your judgments, but it will also help you remember the reasons for your decisions if they are challenged.
  • • If possible, explain the reasons for your decisions to impacted employees. Misunderstandings can sometimes lead to EEO complaints.
  • • Maintain positive relationships with employees of all religions. You may feel more comfortable with employees of a culture similar to your own, but socializing exclusively with individuals of one religion at least gives the appearance of partiality.
  • • Ensure that your workplace is free of religious ridicule or insult.

Your efforts to build a workplace that values individuals of all religions and cultures will not only help you maintain the diverse workforce valued by the federal government, but it will also give your subordinates confidence in the fairness of your decisions.

Religious harassment

The line between permissible religious expression and impermissible religious harassment can sometimes be difficult to draw, leaving managers uncertain about when to stop discussions involving religion.

Although situations have to be considered on a case-by-case basis, you can safely allow employees to discuss religion unless it becomes unwelcome by any party to the discussion or somehow impedes the employees' ability to carry out the agency's mission.

For example, you don't need to be concerned about two employees engaging in a conversation about their individual religions during their break time. However, if one employee is persistently trying to promote his religion to a coworker who consistently expresses disinterest, you should step in to avoid the possible creation of a religiously hostile work environment for the employee who doesn't want to discuss religion

Similarly, you should keep an eye out for religious discussions that become heated or offensive. A few comments about a religion that are extremely offensive might be severe enough to create a hostile work environment for a targeted employee. Milder comments might not create a hostile work environment unless they are made so consistently they become pervasive.

Warning: If religious comments are becoming strong or are being made on a regular basis, it's time to step in and steer employees to other topics.

You can avoid claims of religious harassment by:

  • • Creating a work environment that encourages employees to raise harassment issues.
  • • Making sure employees understand that you won't tolerate religious jokes by employees.
  • • Monitoring discussion in the workplace.
  • • Modeling an open-minded attitude toward religious matters.

It's important to recognize that religion can often raise strong emotions. Although you shouldn't stop employees from talking about religion altogether, it's up to you to make sure they act in a respectful manner. If you have any concerns about how to address a possible case of religious harassment, immediately contact your HR or EEO office to get help. If you wait to see what happens, your reaction might be too late to prevent a finding of religious harassment.

The following examples are taken from actual cases that appeared before the EEOC:

  • • The complainant, a law enforcement officer, alleged that he heard one coworker say to another, "Heads up, Goldstein is in the building." He further alleged that he learned that another coworker had referred to him as "a stupid Jew" or a "motherf------ Jew" and was not disciplined. The EEOC found that the Department of Homeland Security erred in dismissing the complaint for failure to state a claim.
  • • The complainant was subjected to a hostile work environment on the basis of her religion (Unity Thought) when a coworker routinely quoted Scripture, made religious comments, initiated religious discussions, and loudly sang religious hymns in the complainant's presence. The coworker was told by U.S. Postal Service management to stop making unwelcome religious comments, but she continued to do so.
  • • The complainant was subjected to discrimination on the basis of his religion (Muslim) when he was placed off the clock for refusing to remove his kufi, a prayer cap used by Muslims. An administrative judge found that the kufi was optional, not a fundamental tenant of the Muslim religion, but the EEOC disagreed, finding that wearing a kufi was a bona fide religious belief for the complainant because it followed the ways of the prophet Muhammad.

Mistakes to avoid

Here are the most common mistakes supervisors and managers make in dealing with issues of religious discrimination.

Do not:

  • • Make judgments about the validity of an employee's religion. If you have doubts about the religious sincerity of an employee seeking accommodation, seek guidance from your HR or EEO office rather than simply denying the request.
  • • Place additional burdens on one religion. You may be unfamiliar with some religions, but this does not mean you can require more documentation or evidence of sincerity from an unfamiliar religion when making reasonable accommodation decisions.
  • • Tell religious jokes or allow subordinates to do so. Although a few jokes may seem harmless enough, this type of activity may create a hostile work environment for some employees.
  • • Make derogatory statements about a religion.
  • • Fail to take action. An employee who makes demeaning religious comments to others could create a hostile work environment.
  • • Deny someone's "free speech" right to discuss religion in the workplace. But remember that those free speech rights do not override the right of employees to be free from religious harassment or the need of the agency to properly conduct its business with the public.

Ask FMG

Q. How is religion defined?

A. It's difficult to precisely define religion. It generally involves sincerely held moral or ethical beliefs about life, purpose, and death. Religion includes observances and practices related to those beliefs. Individuals are protected from discrimination whether their religion is traditional and organized, or new, uncommon or informal.

Q. How far does an agency need to go in providing religious accommodation?

A. An agency is not required to undertake significant expense or disruption that would be an undue burden. However, agencies are required to make a sincere and diligent effort in trying to achieve religious accommodation.

Q. What should I do if I believe an employee is asking for a religious accommodation for their convenience, not their faith?

A. Managers should recognize that there are a wide variety of religious beliefs and generally assume that an employee's request for religious accommodation is based on a sincerely held religious belief. However, questions about sincerity can come up if an employee requests reasonable accommodation for their religion, but acts in a manner that is inconsistent with their professed beliefs, or if the employee's actions otherwise create questions about whether the request is actually for secular reasons such as convenience. If there is a legitimate reason to question an employee's sincerity, you would be justified in asking for additional information to support a request for religious accommodation. Discuss your concerns with your HR or EEO office.

Q. What about accommodations related to attire? What if an employee works for an agency that requires a uniform? Can the agency deny an attire-related accommodation?

A. You should work with an open mind and make every effort to allow employees to wear the clothing required by their religion. For example, you may have a legitimate need to have employees wear a specific uniform that helps identify the employees to the public. However, you could work with an employee to allow certain religious head coverings to be worn under the uniform's cap. Some individuals may simply require a head covering; in which case the cap that is part of the agency's uniform could be sufficient. If a mutual agreement can't be reached, you should consider conflicts between agency operational needs and an employee's religious needs fairly with regard to undue hardship. For example, if a particular piece of religious clothing creates a real safety hazard, it would be an undue hardship to allow the clothing to be worn. Check with HR or EEO office when these situations come up.

Q. Must I allow employees time to attend regular church services? What happens if more than one person makes that request?

A. You must make a good-faith effort to accommodate this request. However, this does not mean you are required to violate a collective bargaining agreement or significantly disadvantage other employees to do so. You can try voluntary substitutes and schedule swaps, flexible scheduling, lateral transfers, and changes in job assignments as ways of providing reasonable accommodation. You must consider each request as it comes. The possibility that other employees might request a similar arrangement is not a good enough reason for denying accommodation. If you ultimately determine that you cannot accommodate an individual in any way, you must be able to show you worked actively to seek a feasible and long-term religious accommodation.

Q. What if the request isn't necessarily for services? What if the employee seeks regular time off for a peripheral event -- like a church meeting or choir practice?

A. For the sake of positive employee relations, it's a good idea to try to accommodate a peripheral religious activity if it can be done easily through flexible scheduling. However, participation in optional activities such as religious study and choir practice is treated differently than a church member's belief in the tenets of religion. You have no obligation to accommodate non-essential religious activities.

Q. What if an accommodation would impact seniority?

A. A religious accommodation that conflicts with a valid collective bargaining agreement is an undue hardship. This most frequently comes up when seniority issues are raised. As always, a good-faith effort to accommodate is necessary. You won't be let off the hook unless you proactively try to accommodate an individual, for example, through an attempt to arrange voluntary schedule swaps. If other employees don't respond to accommodation efforts, you can validly state that violating the collective bargaining agreement would be an undue hardship and the employee cannot be accommodated.

Q. What if an employee practices a strange religion -- one that no one at my agency thinks is credible?

A. You should treat matters involving religion evenhandedly, regardless of a religion's name or content.
