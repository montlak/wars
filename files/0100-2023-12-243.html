5. Compensatory Damages

While a simple request for compensatory damages does not serve to save an otherwise moot complaint, the possibility of recovering such damages does. In Thomas v. Secretary of Air Force, 01954164 (1996), the Commission found it was improper for the agency to dismiss a complaint as moot where, should the appellant prevail, the possibility of a damage award existed.

Stated another way, a claim for compensatory damages may keep alive a case that is otherwise moot. See Kyriazi v. Secretary of Defense, DLA, 05930086 (1994). In that case, the Commission found that a claim for compensatory damages in a sexual harassment hostile environment case meant the effects of the alleged discrimination were not completely eradicated. In Huhn v. Secretary of Treasury, 01940355 (1994), the Commission found that when a claim that would otherwise be moot raises the potential of compensatory damages, the agency must solicit evidence of damages and of a causal connection prior to making a determination as to mootness:

However, a review of the EEO Counselor's report reveals that appellant indicated she was seeking compensatory damages for emotional pain and suffering, and medical treatment sought due to stress caused by the alleged discriminatory actions. Specifically, appellant stated that on the day she received the third 2810 form, her physician found that the stress had caused her blood pressure to rise putting her at risk for a stroke or heart attack. Appellant again raised the request for compensatory damages in her formal complaint, noting that she had been receiving treatment for depression and traumatic stress disorder as a result of work-related stress caused by actions complained of in a previous EEO action. The Commission has held that an agency must address the issue of compensatory damages when a complainant shows objective evidence that she has incurred compensatory damages, and that the damages are related to the alleged discrimination. Jackson v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 01923399 (November 12, 1992), request to reopen denied, EEOC Request No. 05930306 (February 1, 1993). Because appellant in this case made a claim for compensatory damages related to the alleged discriminatory conduct of the agency, the agency should have requested that appellant provide some objective proof of the alleged damages incurred, as well as objective evidence linking those damages to the adverse actions at issue. See Benton v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01932422 (December 10, 1993). Therefore, we find that appellant may still be aggrieved as a result of the actions complained of herein....

On reconsideration in Huhn, the Commission held that where a complainant makes a viable claim for compensatory damages, the subsequent approval of her retirement by OPM does not make her claim moot. In Huhn v. Secretary of Treasury, 05940630 (1995), the Commission also held that the complainant had sufficiently described how stress caused by the alleged harassment affected her and also demonstrated that she had suffered a pecuniary loss in having to pay for health insurance benefits while she awaited OPM's approval of her retirement application:

Although appellant's retirement has been approved by OPM and her health insurance continued, the record clearly indicates that appellant has consistently argued that she remains aggrieved, because she has been harmed by the stress resulting from the agency's harassment. In her complaint appellant claimed that the discriminatory incidents caused her to suffer emotional stress, and deteriorating health. In the record appellant described how the stress of receiving the last SF 2810 elevated her blood pressure and resulted in her seeking immediate medical attention. On appeal, appellant argued that she had "overwhelming and permanent mental and physical disabilities" as a result of the discrimination. Appellant also noted on appeal that she paid $6,266.43 in insurance premiums. Accordingly, we find that reconsideration is not justified under 29 CFR § 1614.407(c)(2), because the agency has failed to take into account compensatory damages on the issue of mootness.

See also Oliver v. Postmaster General, 01997123 (2001) (grievance settlement resolving a LWOP claim does not moot an EEO complaint where the complainant requested compensatory damages); Christopher v. Postmaster General, 01A12114 (2001) (grievance settlement reducing a seven-day suspension to a discussion does not moot a claim where the complainant requested compensatory damages).

Dismissal of a claim for mootness is proper if the complainant requests compensatory damages, but fails to adequately present evidence of the damages. See Loving v. Secretary of Treasury, 01962262 (1996). In that case, the complainant failed to respond to the agency's request for evidence of damages. But, in Bertley v. Secretary of Navy, 01960951 (1996), the Commission held that the agency improperly dismissed the complaint for mootness where the agency found that the complainant's evidence did not support his claim for compensatory damages. Specifically, the agency rejected an unsworn statement as evidence. The Commission disagreed, holding in Bertley:

Appellant submitted his wife's unsworn statement as proper objective evidence of his damages. See Carle, supra. The statement outlined appellant's changed behavior after receiving the LOR (sleeplessness, isolation, withdrawal from the family, lack of concentration), as compared with his prior behavior. We find this evidence sufficient to, at this juncture, maintain a claim for compensatory damages as appellant need only come forward with some objective evidence to defeat the agency's dismissal of his complaint as moot.

Similarly, in Ambrosio v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A14587 (2002), the Commission upheld the agency's dismissal of a complaint on the basis of mootness where the complainant submitted evidence of damages but failed to establish any causal connection between those damages and the alleged discrimination.

A complainant need not use precise language when requesting compensatory damages in order to keep a complaint alive. In Jackie P. v. Postmaster General, 0120162633 (2016), the Commission rejected the agency's argument that the complaint was moot because the complainant did not request compensatory damages. Rather, the agency argued, the complainant requested "a monetary penalty" and pointed out that punitive damages are not available in federal sector complaints. Noting that the complainant was not represented by an attorney in Jackie P., the Commission was "willing to give Complainant the benefit of the doubt, particularly in light of the fact he specifically stated that he was seeking compensation 'for the harm done' which is the essence of non-pecuniary compensatory damages, which are available in the Federal sector."

An agency is required to give the complainant only one notice of the obligation to submit evidence of damages. It is not required to remind the complainant to submit such evidence if he or she fails to respond to the initial request. White v. Secretary of Defense, 01996826 (2001).

Where the sole claim presented is under the ADEA, a claim for compensatory damages will not serve to salvage an otherwise moot claim because compensatory damages are not available under the ADEA. See, e.g., O'Leary v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01A14522 (2002).

When an agency improperly dismisses a claim as moot and the only outstanding issue is entitlement to compensatory damages, the agency has two options: (1) it may process the complaint through the investigation stage; or, (2) it may request the complainant to produce evidence of damages and causation. See Pontious v. Postmaster General, 01970349 (1998). If the complainant is unable to produce sufficient evidence of damages, the complaint may be dismissed as moot. If sufficient evidence of damages is presented, the agency should then process the complaint through the investigation stage.
