UNION PARTICIPATION RIGHTS

Federal labor relations are based on an interlocking set of rights and responsibilities spelled out in the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute.

Generally, one party's right under federal labor relations law creates a corresponding responsibility for another party. For example, a union's legal right to be present during certain kinds of discussions with employees creates a corresponding responsibility for managers to give the union the opportunity to be present.

To meet such obligations, of course, it is first necessary to be able to recognize the situations in which they come up, and then to take the appropriate action.

Employee rights. Most federal employees have the right to form, join, or assist a union -- or to not do so. The law also guarantees them the exercise of these rights free from coercion, restraint, or retaliation by the agency.

Practically speaking, this means they can't be punished or discriminated against for their lawful efforts to establish a union, or for joining it, or for serving in it as a representative or officer. For that reason, such legal union-related functions are often referred to as "protected activities."

Most often, these rights come into play when employees contend they have been harmed in some way as a result of participation in union-related activities. Examples of such harm would include receiving a lower performance rating, or not being selected for a promotion because of involvement in presenting grievances.

Note: Union officials and other union members may not use their work time or paid official time to conduct purely union business, e.g., to sign up new members, hold union meetings, or conduct internal union elections. Union business can be done during breaks, lunch hours, or before or after the day's tour of duty.

Union rights. When a union has been elected to represent a bargaining unit by a majority of the votes cast by eligible employees, it automatically wins several important rights.

  • • It has the right to function as the exclusive representative of all employees in the bargaining unit, regardless of whether they voted for it, voted against it, or didn't vote at all. Being the exclusive representative means that the union may not be bypassed through direct discussions with employees concerning conditions of employment or active grievances or appeals.
  • • It gains the right to be informed of any non-trivial change in conditions of employment of bargaining unit employees, to receive adequate time to present its views and recommendations, and to engage in various forms of negotiation with management.
  • • It earns the right to be present at certain discussions or meetings between agency managers and bargaining unit employees. Such meetings are usually called formal discussions and investigative (or Weingarten) meetings.

Management rights. The law also reserves an impressive list of rights for managers to use in accomplishing the agency's objectives. Included among the reserved management rights is the authority to determine the agency's:

  • • Mission.
  • • Budget.
  • • Organization.
  • • Number of employees.
  • • Internal security practices.

In addition, managers are provided the legal authority to take necessary personnel actions, including:

  • • Hiring.
  • •Assigning.
  • • Directing.
  • • Disciplining.
  • •Rewarding.
  • •Laying off.

These management actions must be carried out in compliance with applicable laws, regulations, and negotiated agreements. In other words, management may not exercise these functions in a way that would coerce or restrain employees' efforts to join or assist a union. If an employee says "I'm thinking about joining a union," and you say "That's a bad idea," you may have just invited an unfair labor practice charge.

Executive Orders

In 2018, President Trump signed Executive Order 13837, regarding official time, and Executive Order 13839, regarding employee accountability and actions for unacceptable performance. Those EOs have since been rescinded by Executive Order 14003, signed by President Biden in 2021.

Because there has been significant political action in this area, it's possible that each new administration will issue its own orders regarding official time and employee accountability.

Managers should also note that President Biden issued Executive Order 14025, "Worker Organizing and Empowerment," which declares that the administration's policy is to encourage worker organizing and collective bargaining. To that end, it established a Task Force on Worker Organizing and Empowerment that directs agencies to identify policies, practices, and programs that could be used to promote the administration's support for worker organizing and collective bargaining. Most of these actions will occur at higher levels of management, but line managers should be aware of the increased support for collective bargaining.

Additionally, OPM guidance regarding Executive Order 14025 encourages agencies to notify bargaining unit employees of their statutory rights, including their right to join a union, on a quarterly or biannual basis. The responsibility for making these notifications could fall on managers.

Union officials

Union representatives may be paid employees of the union itself, or federal employees who also serve as union representatives. In most cases, managers find themselves dealing with agency employees who are also representing the union.

When managers encounter employees acting on behalf of the union, they should remember that as union officials, these employees have additional legal protections. Behavior that could normally land an employee in hot water -- using disrespectful language, for example -- could be viewed as protected activity if the employee is conducting representational business. Be sure to check with your LR specialist if you run into a conflict or an escalating situation with a union official. Your specialist will be up to date on the latest guidance in this area.

Status of union officials. Union representatives are involved in a range of activities, including grievance handling and investigation, meeting and negotiating with agency managers over personnel policies and working conditions, and serving on committees, e.g., a safety committee.

When agency employees serve in a union representational role -- for example, while presenting a grievance -- they are legally viewed as equal in status to the agency officials they are dealing with, rather than as subordinates.

Consequently, while they are engaged in representational activity, union representatives are entitled to state their opinions less politely than one might expect of a subordinate. The use of profanity, insulting names, ridicule of management actions, and threats to embarrass agency managers are usually allowable activities.

Protected activity. When union representatives perform legitimate activities under the labor relations law, they are said to be engaging in "protected activity," and they cannot be penalized for these actions.

For example, they cannot be given lower performance ratings, denied promotion, or given undesirable assignments because of their involvement in protected activities such as filing grievances or unfair labor practice charges. In fact, even threatening such retaliatory measures has been ruled an unfair labor practice.

One of the relevant factors in determining whether a union official's behavior exceeded the bounds of protected activity is whether the outburst was provoked. For example, a union official may allege that a manager was the first to raise his voice in a dispute, requiring the union official to shout in order to be heard. For this reason, it's important that federal managers always "keep their cool" when dealing with union officials. Reacting to a union official's shouts with shouts of your own can escalate the matter and potentially reduce the union official's culpability for any outburst.

Be sure to check with your LR specialist if you are questioning whether a union official's actions are protected activity. The specialist will be familiar with the latest rules in this area.

What is not allowed. Union officials are entitled to engage in "robust debate," which can include strong language directed against management officials. However, serious incidents that amount to "flagrant misconduct," or a pattern of lesser incidents amounting to "opprobrious conduct," may lead to discipline. There are limits to what union representatives can do with impunity. Actions outside the umbrella of protected activity are:

  • • Threats of physical harm.
  • • Attempted or actual physical attacks.
  • • Refusal to comply with lawful orders.
  • • Failure to comply with laws or regulations.

Pre-decisional involvement

Executive Order 13522, issued by President Obama in 2009, stated that agencies would "allow employees and their union representatives to have pre-decisional involvement in all workplace matters to the fullest extent practicable."

Although EO 13522 was later rescinded, the concept of pre-decisional involvement still exists. The term pre-decisional involvement, or PDI, refers to the union being involved in the decision-making process before the agency makes a final decision on a particular matter. Traditionally, agencies make decisions, take action, and then bargain with the union over the action's impact and implementation, if necessary. Under PDI, the union is allowed to consult with the agency before the agency makes its decision, voicing union concerns and giving input that could potentially reduce the time and effort expended on bargaining afterward.

OPM has stated that agencies have discretion to use PDI, and it has encouraged its use for agencies that think such a tool will be productive.

Example: An agency is considering whether to change a parking policy. It approaches the union and asks for the union's position on the proposed change. This would let the union's voice be heard and would give the agency an indication of which aspects of the proposed policy the union would likely find contentious. Such an approach could reduce the friction in the decision-making process and ease negotiations later.

PDI is not mandatory by statute, but it may be required by a provision in your collective bargaining agreement. Check with your labor relations specialist for your agency's policy.

Warning: If your agency does practice some form of pre-decisional involvement, it does not absolve management of its obligation to bargain over changes in working conditions. Speaking to the union about a change beforehand does not substitute for the required bargaining. In other words, don't think that having the union give its opinion on a matter gives you free rein to take unilateral action without the required negotiations. "But we already heard the union's input" is not a defense to a charge of failing to bargain.

Mistakes to avoid

Many avoidable labor disputes start because individual supervisors and managers make mistakes in dealing with union officials and labor agreements. Avoid these common errors.

Do not:

  • • Penalize union officials for participating in legitimate union activities. Retaliation in the form of lowered performance ratings, reduced advancement, or some other action, whether obvious or subtle, violates the law.
  • • Bypass the union. Directly working out arrangements regarding conditions of employment with bargaining unit members is known as bypassing the exclusive representative, an unfair labor practice.
  • • Make coercive statements, even inadvertently. Suggesting that employees shouldn't join a union or that doing so would hurt their career will be viewed as coercion and violates the law. Be on guard against careless statements.

Ask FMG

Q. If some of my employees want to work out certain arrangements -- for example, flextime -- with me directly (i.e., without the union's intervention), is that allowable?

A. No. The union is the exclusive -- i.e., only -- legal representative of employees in the bargaining unit in working out conditions of employment. Dealing with employees directly would violate the law.

Q. Can I stop an employee from trying to sign up union members on work time?

A. Yes. Employees are not entitled to carry out purely union business activities, e.g., soliciting new members, during duty time. They may do so before or after work, at lunch, and during break times, however.

Q. Can I take action against a union official who is not performing well?

A. Yes, so long as you're not rating the official's performance lower as a result of participation in union activities.

Q. Can I discipline a union official for misconduct?

A. Yes, provided you're not defining misconduct as the official's participation in legally protected activity, such as the aggressive presentation of a grievance.

Q. How should I rate the performance of a union official who uses 50 percent of work time on representational functions?

A. Assuming that is within the agreed-upon limits, you would treat the 50 percent of the time spent on regular duties as the entirety of the employee's time and rate accordingly. Employees should only be rated on the performance of the duties assigned by the agency.

Q. Do I have to exempt union officials from performing certain duties or from travel assignments?

A. No. Union officials are not exempt from the performance of regular duties included in their positions.

Q. Am I required to authorize overtime for representational activity carried out after normal work hours?

A. If you've already assigned overtime work, the employee may engage in representational activity. The employee may not work overtime simply to engage in representational activity.
