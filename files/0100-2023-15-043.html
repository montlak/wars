2. Conferences With Settlement Judge

In many cases, the administrative judge assigned to a case will be the judge who conducts any settlement conferences between the parties. However, several years ago, the EEOC's Baltimore District Office, in an informal pilot project, acted on the notion that a neutral administrative judge may be better able to aid in the settlement of difficult cases. Other district offices have adopted similar programs. Even in districts where formal programs do not exist, a request for a neutral settlement judge is usually honored.

In certain cases that may be difficult to settle, the chief administrative judge may appoint a neutral settlement judge. The settlement judge will conduct a settlement conference with the parties jointly and also may engage in ex parte communications with the parties for settlement purposes. In this manner, the settlement judge can give the parties some impression on the relative merits of their case without raising the issue of bias on the part of the administrative judge who ultimately will hear the case if settlement is not achieved. Also, the parties may request that a settlement judge be appointed.

Certainly, this is an idea with a great deal of merit. Many district court judges refuse to become involved in settlement discussions. Rather, the judge will assign the case to a magistrate or settlement counsel if the parties desire. In this way, the judge can protect his or her impartiality while the parties can get needed assistance in settlement discussions.

The problem with this approach is that settlement takes time. The neutral settlement judge is invariably another EEOC administrative judge with his or her own caseload. As a result, the settlement judge may not be interested in spending a lot of time attempting to settle a case not on his or her docket. To a degree, this problem is partially overcome by the Commission's decision to formalize the settlement judge process. Presumably, this formalization will result in settlement judges being given credit in performance evaluations for their efforts to settle cases in which they are not the judge of record.

The Judges' Handbook at 3-2, provides that the settlement judge may engage in ex parte communications with the parties and, with only limited exceptions, may not disclose those communications to the hearing judge:

The "Administrative Judge of record" may require the parties to participate in a settlement conference to be conducted by a different Administrative Judge (the "Settlement Judge"). The Settlement Judge has discretion to determine the location of the settlement conference. The Settlement Judge may order the parties to appear at a specific location. Alternatively, the Settlement Judge may permit participation by telephone (e.g., when the parties or the Settlement Judge reside in different geographic locations).

During a settlement conference, the Settlement Judge may provide the parties with substantive and legal information about what the disposition of the case might be if it were to go to an administrative hearing or to court. He/she may provide the parties with possible settlement ranges for their consideration. EEO MD-110, 3-20. The Settlement Judge may hold joint or individual discussions with the parties in an effort to settle the case. Generally, the Settlement Judge must not disclose anything said or done during settlement conferences to the Administrative Judge of record. Exceptions include a recommendation for sanctions, a threat of imminent physical harm, or incidents of actual physical violence that occur during the settlement conference.

Although the settlement judge is authorized to communicate with the parties regarding his or her impression of the case, including possible outcomes if the case goes to hearing, the drawback is that the settlement judge knows less about the case than the hearing judge and, as result, those impressions may be less reliable.

The bottom line is that there are strengths and weaknesses to each type of settlement conference and those factors must be weighed in each case, along with the willingness and ability of the hearing judge to facilitate settlement discussions.
