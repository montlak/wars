7. Spin-Off Complaints

In order to be treated as a spin-off complaint, the new allegations must be made by the same employee who filed the oriGINAl complaint and must relate to the processing of the oriGINAl complaint. A complaint filed by an employee who alleges that the agency interfered with his representation of an EEO complainant is not a spin-off complaint and must be accepted as a new complaint. Alikakos v. USPS, 01841973 (1985).

A complainant may not wait until the oriGINAl complaint has been completely processed before raising allegations of improper processing. Further consideration of such allegations after the closing of the oriGINAl complaint would "frustrate the goals of judicial economy." Kleinman v. Postmaster General, 01930916 (1993).

A spin-off complaint must relate to the processing of a complaint and not to the actual decision on the merits. In Rousseau v. Secretary of Education, 05900899 (1992), the complainant tried the novel approach of arguing that the result reached by the agency on the merits of his claim was in retaliation for filing an earlier complaint. The Commission, holding that it would not permit such collateral attacks on decisions, explained in Rousseau:

Appellant's claim herein is that FAD 2, finding no discrimination, was issued in reprisal for appellant's EEO activity. If appellant were to prevail on this claim, the appropriate remedy would be to place him in the position he would have occupied but for the discriminatory FAD (FAD 2). Appellant is essentially arguing that FAD 1, in which the agency found discrimination (but no constructive discharge) and awarded appellant a retroactive promotion with backpay, is binding on the agency. However, appellant's allegations of discrimination and constructive discharge were appealed to the MSPB. The MSPB determined that appellant's resignation was voluntary and that it lacked jurisdiction over the appeal. Appellant thereafter filed a petition for review of the MSPB final decision with this Commission (EEOC Petition No. 03910146). Appellant's petition was administratively closed and redocketed on November 13, 1991, as EEOC Appeal No. 01920410. Appellant's allegations of discrimination and constructive discharge are addressed in the Commission's decision in Appeal No. 01920410 which is being issued simultaneously with the instant decision. Accordingly, we conclude that by pursuing his reprisal claim, appellant is seeking two concurrent routes to a finding of discrimination.

Appellant's reprisal complaint herein constitutes a collateral attack on the agency's FAD 2. This Commission has in the past found that such collateral attacks are not permissible. See Ronald J. Merck v. Anthony M. Frank, Postmaster General (USPS-WR), EEOC Appeal 82814 (September 8, 1988); Paul P. Sachko v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01862902 (November 10, 1987).

We recognize that appellant has framed the reprisal issue herein in such terms so as to distinguish it from his underlying claims of discrimination. Nevertheless, the issue of the underlying discrimination is linked to the reprisal allegation. If appellant establishes a prima facie case of reprisal, the agency must set forth a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action. See Hochstad v. Worcester Foundation for Experimental Biology, 425 F. Supp. 318 (D. Mass), aff'd 545 F.2d 222 (1st Cir. 1976). In this case, that reason would be that the agency's decision was justified. The issue of whether or not it was justified (i.e., whether or not there was discrimination), would then once more become the focus of the case.

A "spin-off" complaint is one alleging that a previously filed EEO complaint is being processed improperly or unfairly by the agency. Federal Sector Complaint Processing Manual, MD-107 (September 1987), p. 6-41. Here, appellant's quarrel is not with the issuance of a new FAD, but with the result in the new FAD. His complaint is with the finding of no discrimination, rather than with the processing of his claim. Thus, appellant's complaint is not properly termed a "spin-off" complaint.

Spin-off complaints must be raised with the agency EEO director or manager, who is required to respond to the complaint and document the response in the complaint file. The spin-off complaint can then be addressed by the EEOC administrative judge at the hearing or in an appeal by OFO. When there is a processing allegation the agency must respond to it and include that response in the complaint file. In Smith v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120111954 (2013), the Commission found it was improper to include the processing complaint in the analysis of pretext and not respond to the complainant's allegation that her identify was disclosed to management officials during counseling when she had not waived anonymity:

As an initial matter, we note that Complainant alleged in her affidavit that the EEO Counselor improperly disclosed her identity to management officials when she had not yet waived her anonymity. We find that Complainant has alleged dissatisfaction with the processing of the instant complaint. We emphasize that the Agency has a duty to address this matter. EEO MD-110, Chapter 5, § IV.D provides, in relevant part, the following: "The agency official responsible for the quality of complaints processing must add a record of the complainant's concerns and any actions the agency took to resolve the concerns, to the complaint file maintained on the underlying complaint. If no action was taken, the file must contain an explanation of the agency's reason(s) for not taking any action." We note that the Agency's final decision briefly mentioned Complainant's anonymity concerns as part of its pretext analysis of incident 5. The record, however, does not reflect what, if any, actions the Agency took to address Complainant's concerns. Consequently, to remedy this deficiency, and out of concern for the integrity of the Agency's EEO process, we order the Agency to provide Complainant with a report of any actions it took to resolve her concerns regarding the processing of her complaint, or an explanation of its reasons for not taking action, in accordance with the Order below.

When the agency dismisses the oriGINAl complaint for failure to prosecute, it is proper to dismiss a spin-off complaint for the same reason. See Hicks v. Secretary of Navy, 01893047 (1989). Likewise, the Commission has held that when an underlying complaint results in a finding of discrimination and granting of full relief to the complainant, a complaint with respect to improper processing of the complaint is moot. See Bjugstad v. Secretary of Navy, 05930412 (1994). "[T]he resolution of the underlying complaint completely eradicated any effects of the alleged discriminatory processing."

A complainant over denial of official time to engage in EEO activity is not a spin-off complaint. Such a complaint alleges a per se violation of the Commission's regulations. Bledsoe v. Postmaster General, 01A31552 (2003). See also Chapter 9.

An agency is required to respond in writing and in detail to a processing or spin-off complaint and to include its response in the complaint file for further review by the administrative judge, if a hearing is requested, or the Commission on appeal. As the Commission explained in Gryder v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120091598 (2010):

In claim (3), complainant alleged that agency officials were improperly involved with the processing of the instant complaint resulting in delays by the EEO Office. According to the Commission's Management Directive, a complainant dissatisfied with the processing of his complaint must bring his allegation regarding the processing of his complaint to the appropriate agency officials. See EEO Management Directive 110, (MD-110) p. 5-25 (November 9, 1999); see also 29 C.F.R. § 1614. 107(a)(8). Therefore it was appropriate for the agency to dismiss this claim. See Carter v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Appeal No. 0120071166 (June 18, 2007) (Commission affirmed dismissal of claim expressing dissatisfaction with the processing of current EEO complaint). However, the agency official responsible for the quality of complaints processing must also add a detailed record of the complainant's concerns and any actions the agency took to resolve the concerns, to the complaint file maintained on the instant complaint. EEO MD-110, at 5-26. If no action was taken, the file must contain an explanation of the agency's reason(s) for not taking any action. Id. In light of the facts presented in the instant case and our concern for the integrity of the agency's EEO process, we order the agency to provide complainant with a report of any actions it takes to resolve his concerns regarding the processing of his complaint, or an explanation of its reason for not taking action, in accordance with the order below. See Losinger v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Request No. 0520070870 (September 26, 2007).
