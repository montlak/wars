G. SCRUTINY OF WORK

An allegation that the agency is subjecting an employee's work to a higher level of scrutiny than that of his coworkers states a claim for relief. Miller v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 05950577 (1996).

Such claims are hard to prove, but not impossible, as demonstrated by the Commission's findings in Schuler v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01995293 (2002):

The agency responded that complainant never exhibited the potential to perform beyond the GS-7 level. Complainant must now show that this reason is a pretext designed to hide a discriminatory or retaliatory motivation on the part of the supervisor. Placing an employee under close scrutiny, as the supervisor did here, may reflect an unlawful motivation on the part of the person doing the scrutinizing, especially if that person has made statements that could be characterized as discriminatory. See Winston v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 01985752 (December 13, 2000) (supervisor's subjecting employee to extremely close scrutiny, documenting late arrivals down to the minute, and taking other draconian measures found to constitute discriminatory harassment in light of racial remark attributed to supervisor). In this case, the record clearly establishes the draconian scrutiny that the supervisor had complainant under after the settlement agreement was signed. The agency has not presented any documents or affidavits which contradict the testimony of the union president and her husband concerning the supervisor's stated preference for working with females and her problems in working with men. When considered in light of these statements, the extensiveness and the strident, nitpicking tone of the supervisor's evaluations of complainant's work reflect that the supervisor was never interested in helping complainant progress up the career ladder. Rather, she had been doing everything possible to ensure that complainant would never progress beyond the GS-7 level. The union president and her husband provided further evidence that the supervisor exhibited bias toward men in her dealings with complainant.

The union president stated that complainant was never given the appropriate training or mentoring that he was to receive under the agreement. In particular, she pointed out that complainant was not allowed to participate in on-site investigations. She also cited a situation in which the supervisor blamed complainant for losing documents, rather than the female EOS with whom he was working, even though complainant maintained that the missing documents were in the file when he turned it in. In yet another incident, the supervisor blamed complainant for problems in another case rather than on the GS-12 EOS who was in charge of the investigation. She maintained that male employees who fell out of favor were placed under the supervisor's authority, and that the supervisor "had a legal mind and the viciousness to go after whoever it was they wanted to get rid of."

The union president's husband also provided an affidavit. He stated that the supervisor continuously discriminated against him while he was under her. He stated that, even though his work was correct, the supervisor would make changes in his reports and then chastise him for errors that she made. He stated that she placed him on a performance improvement plan, but that he kept careful documentation and wrote memoranda showing that he had done the work correctly, which frustrated the supervisor. He also stated that he had seen complainant's work, and that he had good writing skills. In addition, he stated that the supervisor tried to have several female employees accuse him of sexual harassment, to the point where she had actually tried to change the language in the affidavits of several women who denied that he tried to sexually harass them. In an EEO hearing on a matter involving the union president, an AJ found prior testimony by the union president about the supervisor's attempt to pin sexual harassment charges on her husband credible. Finally, he identified two other white males who left the agency because of the way that they were treated by the supervisor.

The record indicates that the supervisor fired the union president's husband, after at least one unsuccessful attempt to do so. While this could raise questions about the credibility of the union representative and her husband, other factors support their credibility. First, their descriptions of the supervisor's conduct are detailed, specific, and, as previously noted, unrebutted. Second, they were both EOS's, with more than forty years of experience investigating EEO complaints between them.

We therefore conclude that complainant has met his burden of proof and established that, after the 1995 settlement agreement had been entered into, the supervisor obstructed his career-ladder progression because of a bias that she harbored toward men. Had the supervisor not discriminated against complainant, then in accordance with the settlement agreement, he would have been given progress reviews of at least fully successful for his performance between January and November 1995, and would have demonstrated his readiness to handle more difficult assignments that the GS-9 level. We therefore find that complainant would have been promoted to GS-9 on or shortly after December 7, 1995, the date of the third progress review.
