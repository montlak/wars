8. Legislative and Judicial Employees

The status of coverage under Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act and the ADEA for legislative and judicial employees has changed many times over the last several years. The Commission's regulations at 29 CFR 1614.103(b)(4) reflect the current state of the law. Legislative employees are not covered by the discrimination statutes. Judicial employees in the competitive service are covered, except for complaints involving disability under the Rehabilitation Act. Id. See also Palmer N. v. Administrative Officer of the U.S. Courts, 0120142956 (2017) (dismissing for lack of jurisdiction complaint by a federal District Court probation officers; "according to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.103(b)(4), in order for this Commission's regulations to apply to an employee in the judicial branch of the federal government, such an employee must hold a position in the competitive service.").

As drafted, Title VII and the ADEA applied only to competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches of government. The Rehabilitation Act contained no specific language that either included or excluded competitive service employees from the legislative or judicial branches. After initially finding that it did have jurisdiction over complaints by legislative and judicial competitive service employees under the Rehabilitation Act, the Commission reversed itself and found it did not have such jurisdiction. It then, however, supported legislative changes that would have created EEOC jurisdiction over such complaints.

Through the Congressional Accountability Act of 1995, Pub. L. 104-1, Congress has attempted to resolve the issue of coverage for legislative and judicial employees. Coverage for legislative employees under Title VII was eliminated by the Act. See 2 USC 1311(d). However, the Act also established Administrative and Judicial Conferences to make findings and recommendations on coverage for legislative and judicial employees, including employees of the Government Printing Office, the General Accounting Office and the Library of Congress. See 2 USC 1371, 1434. However, jurisdiction over alleged violations was placed in the Office of Compliance instead of the EEOC. See Chapter 13 for discussion of "Administrative and Judicial Procedures" and "Election of Remedies" under the "Congressional Accountability Act--Legislative Employees."

Prior to the latest statutory developments, Faucett v. Government Printing Office, 01873052 (1988), addressed the argument that GPO was not required to submit an affirmative action plan under 501(b) of the Rehabilitation Act because it is an agency of the legislative branch:

The rights of competitive service employees within the legislative and judicial branches to seek redress is provided for in Sec. 717(a) of the Title VII. Sec. 505(a)(1) of the Rehabilitation Act specifically states that the "rights set forth in Sec. 717...shall be made available with respect to any complaint under Sec. 501." Thus, the right of competitive service employees within the legislative and judicial branches to file administrative complaints under Title VII appears to have been extended to these same employees vis-a-vis claims arising under the Rehabilitation Act. In this regard, the wording of 505(a)(1) does not state that any part of 717 does not apply to any "complaint under Sec. 501." Thus, "remedies, procedures, and rights set forth in Sec. 717" implies all the remedies, procedures and rights of Sec. 717.

The Supreme Court, in discussing the issue of statutory construction, noted that, "[i]t is always appropriate to assume that our elected representatives, like other citizens, know the law." Traynor v. Turnage, ____ U.S. ___, slip op. at 10 (1988) (quoting Cannon v. University of Chicago, 411 U.S. 677, 696-697 (1979)). Consequently, at the time Congress amended the Rehabilitation Act in 1978, we must assume that it was aware of the rights, remedies and procedures contained in 717 of Title VII, which Congress was about to incorporate into 501 of the Rehabilitation Act by adding 505, were available to those employees encumbering competitive service positions in the legislative and judicial branches of the federal government. We must therefore conclude that, by adding 505 to the Rehabilitation Act, Congress was extending coverage of the Rehabilitation Act to these very same employees.

While somewhat sketchy, the legislative history of the Rehabilitation Act reveals that when Congress added Sec. 505(a)(1) to the Rehabilitation Act, it appears to have intended to expand the jurisdiction of complaints brought under Sec. 501 to include "units of the legislative and judicial branches of the federal government having positions in the competitive service."

A Senate committee report commenting on the amendments states:

The new Sec. 505, Remedies and Attorney's Fees, provides that the remedies, procedures and rights in the appropriate Sec. of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 shall be available with respect to complaints under Sec. 501 and 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. (Footnote omitted.)

The report notes that "Sec. 501 was intended to provide a focal point for the hiring, placement and advancement of handicapped individuals in the Federal Government" and that the amendments will aid in making the federal government "an equal opportunity employer" by "providing for individuals aggrieved on the bases of their handicap the same rights, procedures and remedies provided individuals aggrieved on the basis of race, creed, color, or national origin."

That this Commission considers the protection of the Rehabilitation Act to extend to some positions in the legislative branch is clear from our regulation, 29 CFR 1613.701(b), which reads:

(b) Applicability. (1) This subpart applies to executive agencies as defined in sec. 105 of Title 5 of the United States Code and to those positions in the legislative and judicial branches of the Federal Government and the government of the District of Columbia which are in the competitive service. (2) This subpart applies to the U.S. Postal Service and Postal Rate Commission. (3) This subpart applies only to applicants and employees who have a handicap as defined in 1613.702(a).

Although it appears that the courts have not yet addressed whether the inclusion of Sec. 717 of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 of competitive service positions in the legislative and judicial branches of the federal government also applies to the Rehabilitation Act under Sec. 505 of that Act, courts have held that the 1978 amendments to the Rehabilitation Act intended to include within that Act other provisions of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.

The Commission concluded that competitive service employees in the legislative and judicial branches were covered by the Rehabilitation Act.

However, on reconsideration, Faucett v. Government Printing Office, 05880886 (1989), the Commission reversed itself, relying on Judd v. Billington, 863 F.2d 103 (D.C. Cir. 1988), and held that legislative and judicial branch employees are not covered by the Rehabilitation Act. The Commission codified this decision in the 1992 version of Part 1614 by omitting the reference to the judicial and legislative branches that appeared in 29 CFR 1613.701(b). In its 1992 explanatory comments to Part 1614 the Commission explained:

In a change from § 1613.701(b), § 1614.103 eliminates coverage of units in the legislative and judicial branches and restricts the coverage of part 1614, for purposes of the Rehabilitation Act, to military departments as defined in 5 USC 102, executive agencies as defined in 5 USC 105, the U.S. Postal Service, the Postal Rate Commission and the Tennessee Valley Authority. This definition of EEOC's charge processing jurisdiction is based on the plain language of sec. 501 of the Rehabilitation Act, which limits coverage to departments, agencies and instrumentalities in the executive branch, and brings the regulation into conformity with a recent decision of a United States Court of Appeals. In Judd v. Billington, 863 F.2d 103 (D.C. Cir. 1988), the court held that sec. 791 of the Rehabilitation Act "applies only to employees in the executive branch. See 29 USC 791(b)." 863 F.2d at 105. A couple of commenters urged us not to abide by this decision. The Commission, however, already acknowledged and adopted the Judd decision in Faucette v. Kennickell, Request No. 05880886 (March 1, 1989). Because the Commission already decided this issue, we are not reversing that decision by regulation.

The former Civil Service Commission had authority to issue regulations covering competitive positions in legislative and judicial branch agencies, 5 USC 7153; however, that authority passed, not to EEOC, but to the Office of Personnel Management in 5 USC 7203. EEOC has requested that the Office of Personnel Management issue a regulation under sec. 7203 extending regulatory coverage of the Rehabilitation Act to competitive positions in the legislative and judicial branches. The Office of Personnel Management has responded by stating that it does not believe it has authority under the Rehabilitation Act to issue such a regulation. In addition, EEOC has asked the Interagency Committee on Handicapped Employees to recommend a legislative change to sec. 501 of the Rehabilitation Act to provide competitive employees of legislative and judicial branch agencies with a remedy under the Rehabilitation Act.

Prior to the 1995 Act, Title VII coverage only applied to positions in the judicial branch that were part of the competitive service. A position had to be specifically included by statute in order to be considered a competitive service position. As the Commission noted in Hall v. U.S. District Court, 05960880 (1997):

Based on the record herein, we note that the prior decision improperly found that appellant occupied a temporary law clerk position. Notwithstanding this error, we further find that the prior decision properly dismissed her appeal for lack of jurisdiction. Under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, as amended, 42 USC § 2000e et seq., which prohibits discrimination based on, inter alia, race and sex, the Commission has jurisdiction only as to "those units of the legislative and judicial branches of the Federal Government having positions in the competitive service." 42 USC § 2000e-16(a). As noted in Bryant v. O'Connor, 671 F. Supp. 1279, 1286 (D. Kan. 1987), aff'd on other grounds, 848 F.2d 1046 (10th Cir. 1988), "[c]ompetitive service positions within the judicial branch consist of 'civil service positions not in the executive branch which are specifically included in the competitive service by statute,'" citing 5 USC § 2102(a)(2). Nothing in the statute authorizing magistrates to hire law clerks expressly includes the position within the competitive service. See 28 USC § 634(c).

We further note that the Commission does not have jurisdiction to address allegations of disability discrimination in the judicial branch. Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act applies only to executive branch agencies. See 29 USC § 791 et seq.; 29 CFR § 1614.103(b)(4) (noting limited Commission jurisdiction over legislative and judicial branches of the government "except for complaints under the Rehabilitation Act").
