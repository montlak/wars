C. SEVERE OR PERVASIVE REQUIREMENT

In addition to establishing that the conduct is sexual or gender-based, the complainant must establish that the conduct is sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile, offensive, or abusive work environment. See Meritor Savings Bank, FSB v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57, 106 S. Ct. 2399, 91 L. Ed.2d 49 (1986). Whether the conduct is sufficiently severe or pervasive is determined by a two-pronged test with both an objective and subjective element. In the objective prong of the test, the complainant must establish that the conduct is sufficiently severe or pervasive that a reasonable person would view the work environment created as hostile, offensive or abusive. In the subjective prong of the test, the complainant must establish that she actually perceived the work environment in that way. See Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., 510 U.S. 17, 114 S. Ct. 367, 126 L. Ed.2d 295 (1993). The subjective prong of the test is really just another way of stating that the conduct must be unwelcome.

Although the test requires that the conduct be both sufficiently severe and sufficiently pervasive to create a hostile work environment, the test is applied on a sliding scale such that the more severe the conduct, the less pervasive it is required to be; and, conversely, the less severe the conduct, the more pervasive it is required to be. For example, if the offending conduct consists only of verbal comments, it will likely take a greater number of occurrences in order to create a hostile environment. Microaggressions, though subtle and low-level slights, can rise to the level of harassment. See Anne G. v. Veterans Affairs, 2022003036 (2022) (the severity of any one comment alone does not rise to the level of harassment, but the cumulative effect of these microaggressions might). If, on the other hand, the conduct involves physical touching, as little as one incident may be severe or pervasive enough to create a hostile environment.

The general rule is that a single incident does not create a hostile environment. As the Supreme Court noted in Harris, Title VII is not a general "civility code" and was not intended to remedy minor or insignificant slights. As noted above, conduct that involves physical touching can provide an exception to the general rule.

The Commission has held that the objective prong of Harris is a legal standard. As such, it is possible that a complainant can fail to meet the threshold for stating a complaint. As the Commission explained in Wimberly v. Secretary of Treasury, 01953849 (1996), the complainant failed, as a matter of law, to allege that the conduct was sufficiently severe or pervasive to constitute a hostile environment. In rejecting the claim in Wimberly, the Commission wrote:

A fair reading of appellant's complaint indicates that appellant alleged that she was harassed by Supervisor A. Appellant's allegation of harassment includes several incidents. Appellant stated that Supervisor A took an investigative report about appellant from a grievance file, laughed, gave the report to a Labor Relations Representative, smirked, and looked at appellant. Appellant stated that in December 1993, Supervisor A said that an inspection officer had visited him about appellant, asking him if he had ever seen appellant using drugs. Appellant stated that in March 1994, Supervisor A gave her a pen that was inscribed "Just Say No to Drugs." According to appellant, Supervisor A used the inspection report to which he had access to meddle, insult, breach appellant's confidentiality, and malign her character through the disclosure of personal information. Appellant stated that it appeared that Supervisor A was trying to destroy her and her career. Thus, we find that appellant's complaint is properly defined as raising an allegation of harassment, which comprises a number of separate incidents as described above.

Based on the applicable standards, we find that appellant has not established that she has been aggrieved by the actions taken by the agency. Although appellant argues that the agency harassed her, she has failed to show that the alleged harassment was "sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a discriminatorily hostile or abusive working environment." James v. Department of Health and Human Servs., EEOC Request No. 05940327 (Sept. 20, 1994) (citing Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 (1986)). This standard requires an objectively hostile or abusive environment, one that a reasonable person would find hostile or abusive, as well as the victim's subjective perception that the environment is abusive. Id. (citing Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., 114 S. Ct. 367, 371 (1993)). Moreover, we note that unless the conduct is very severe, a single incident or group of isolated incidents will not be regarded as discriminatory harassment. Id. (citing Walker v. Ford Motor Co., 684 F.2d 1355, 1358 (11th Cir. 1982)). Pursuant to these standards, appellant has not established that the agency's actions were sufficient to constitute injury in fact and make her an aggrieved individual.

Similarly, in Buis v. Postmaster General, 01955397 (1996), the Commission held that allegations that the complainant had been "'made fun of,' subjected to name calling and was 'yelled at and singled out' regarding a policy that had not been applied to all employees," did not state a claim for relief. The Commission found the three instances cited by appellant were isolated incidents and did not rise to the severe or pervasive level, and the allegations did not meet the legal threshold of Harris for stating a hostile environment claim.

In Jackson v. Secretary of Air Force, 01955890 (1996), the Commission found the threshold was not met:

[I]n the present case, appellant alleged: 1) that he was verbally admonished on three occasions, 2) that a co-worker was solicited to make a statement against him; 3) that his personal lock was destroyed and some of his personal property was taken, and 4) that a memo containing a derogatory notation about appellant was circulated. These allegations do not assert a pattern of conduct showing harassment and the incidents did not result in a direct or personal deprivation in his employment sufficient to render appellant aggrieved.

Likewise, in Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, 523 U.S. 75, 118 S. Ct. 998, 140 L. Ed.2d 207 (1996), discussed further below in section of "Same-Sex Harassment," the Supreme Court cautioned that Title VII was not meant to govern the standards of civility in the workplace. As Justice Scalia noted in Oncale, 118 S. Ct. at 1002-03:

And there is another requirement that prevents Title VII from expanding into a general civility code: As we emphasized in Meritor and Harris, the statute does not reach genuine but innocuous differences in the ways men and women routinely interact with members of the same sex and of the opposite sex. The prohibition of harassment on the basis of sex requires neither asexuality nor androgyny in the workplace; it forbids only behavior so objectively offensive as to alter the "conditions" of the victim's employment.

But see Fisher v. Secretary of Treasury, 01970706 (1997) (holding agency's dismissal of complaint for failure to state a claim based on determination that alleged conduct was not sufficiently severe or pervasive to constitute a hostile environment was in error). In Fisher, the Commission held that the agency had made an impermissible decision on the merits without an investigation. However, as explained more fully below, the Fisher case misstated the law and the Commission routinely upholds dismissal of harassment complaints for failure to state a claim based on a finding that the allegations, if even taken as true, do not rise to the level of being sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile environment.

"[R]outine supervision, managerial discipline, personality conflicts, and general workplace disputes and tribulations" do not rise to the level of severity and pervasiveness to sustain a hostile environment claim. Florentino S. v. Postmaster General, 0120140526 (2016); see also Clinton C. v.
