1. Time Limits

When a complaint is amended, the agency has 180 days from the date of the last amendment to investigate the complaint, or 360 days from the date the oriGINAl complaint was filed, whichever is shorter. 29 CFR 1614.106(e). A complainant may request a hearing before an administrative judge, however, any time after 180 days from the date the oriGINAl complaint was filed. Id.

In what is perhaps an oversight by the Commission, the amended Part 1614.106 includes no time limits for a complainant to seek an amendment of a complaint. However, the Commission has held that requests to amend a complaint filed more than 45 days after the new alleged discriminatory incident are untimely. Coley v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A22374 (2003). But, in Henry v. Postmaster General, 0120103161 (2011), the Commission expressly rejected an agency argument that an amendment of a complaint could be dismissed on the basis of timeliness where it was not filed within 45 days of the incident:

With regard to claim 2, we note that the regulation at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(2) says that an agency shall dismiss a complaint that fails to comply with the applicable time limits contained in § 105. That section states that an aggrieved person must contact an EEO Counselor within 45 days of the alleged discriminatory action. The Agency determined that claim 2 should be dismissed because the incident occurred on April 5, 2010, but Complainant did not "bring forward [the matter] until...June 1, 2010," FAD, p. 4, which is beyond the 45-day limit. We note, however, that the 45-day limit only applies to Counselor contact, it does not apply to subsequent attempts to amend a complaint. See generally § 1614. Pursuant to § 1614.106(d), a complainant may seek to amend a complaint at any time prior to the conclusion of the investigation. That section makes no mention of any time limit. Since Complainant raised claim 2 in an effort to amend her complaint and not in the initial meeting with the Counselor, the 45-day limit does not apply and the Agency's dismissal for untimely Counselor contact is without merit.

A complaint may not be amended subsequent to the hearing. If the complainant believes that post-hearing events are discriminatory or retaliatory, he or she must initiate contact with a counselor and begin the EEO process anew. Criss v. Secretary of Army, 05990221 (2001).

A complaint may, however, be amended up until the time that the agency mails the notice of hearing rights to the complainant. Cadag v. Postmaster General, 0520120189 (2013). The Commission found that the agency erred in dismissing a claim of constructive discharge that was added to a hostile environment claim based on untimely counselor contact noting that since the claim was like or related to the oriGINAl claim no counseling was necessary. The Commission also vacated the agency's final decision finding no discrimination on the hostile environment claim finding that the agency had improperly fragmented the claims.
