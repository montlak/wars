5. Reprisal

The prohibition against reprisal for participating in EEO complaints extends to reprisal for filing a complaint involving sexual harassment. See Kent v. National Guard Bureau, 01841086 (1986). In Kent, the Commission determined that it was pretext for the agency to terminate an employee for having an improper attitude when the problems were nonexistent until the employee filed her original complaint of sexual harassment. See also Ceralde v. USPS, 01852352 (1988) (finding agency's failure to respond to continued charges of sexual harassment by a coworker was in retaliation for a previous EEO complaint). Nothing in Burlington Industries or Faragher should change the Commission's position.

When a complainant alleges reprisal for filing a sexual harassment complaint, the allegation is analyzed the same way as other allegations involving reprisal. The complainant must show: "(1) that she engaged in prior protected EEO activity; (2) that the alleged discriminatory official knew of that activity; (3) that she was subjected to an adverse employment action; and (4) that the adverse action followed the protected activity in such a time or in such a manner as to permit an inference of retaliatory motive." Velez v. Postmaster General, 01902746 (1990). In Velez, the Commission found that Postal Inspectors engaged in reprisal when they interrogated the complainant about her allegations of sexual harassment while investigating an incident that occurred prior to the time she made her allegations. The interrogation by the Postal Inspectors occurred only days after the complainant first contacted an EEO counselor. The Commission found that the interrogation occurred in close proximity to the complaint, that the inspectors were aware of the complaint because they referenced it in their interrogation, and that the inspectors were able to advance no legitimate reason for their questions in the context of their investigation.

It is not necessary for the employee to file a formal complaint of sexual harassment in order to be protected from reprisal. As with other forms of discrimination under Title VII, it is sufficient if the employee complains of or opposes sexual harassment. In Manley v. Postmaster General, 01863028 (1988), the Commission found that the agency engaged in reprisal where the employee brought allegations of sexual harassment to the attention of her supervisor and, thereafter, was sent for a fitness-for-duty examination, declared unfit for duty, and prohibited from returning to work. Under the facts of the case, the Commission had little difficulty determining that the agency viewed the complainant's behavior as "bizarre" because of the fact that she alleged she was sexually harassed by coworkers.

In Crawford v. Metropolitan Government of Nashville and Davidson County, TN, 129 S. Ct. 846 (2009), the Supreme Court held that it was protected activity for an employee to participate in an investigation into allegations of sexual harassment even when the employee's participation was involuntary. Crawford is discussed more fully in Chapter 17.

A claim that a complainant's performance appraisal was lowered after he intervened in a supervisor's sexual harassment of a female coworker is properly analyzed as a retaliation claim and not a claim of sexual harassment. See Smith v. Secretary of Air Force, 01932276 (1994). "Appellant failed to show that the alleged offensive conduct was specific to him due to his gender; i.e., he has not shown he was subjected to unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature." Id. But a claim that an employee is subjected to harsh treatment after rejecting a supervisor's advances states a quid pro quo claim of harassment. See White v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01950342 (1997). Obviously, in line with Burlington Industries, such a claim would now be analyzed under the hostile environment theory of harassment.

Where an agency fails to properly investigate a claim of sexual harassment and, instead, investigates and disciplines the accuser for improper behavior, this constitutes retaliation as well as discrimination on the basis of sex. In Hendley v. Attorney General, 01974582 (1998), the Commission upheld the decision of the administrative judge that it was not relevant whether the complainant had actually been harassed. Rather, the issue was the agency's motivation for turning the investigation against the complainant. As the Commission explained in Hendley:

[T]he AJ went on to find that the weight of the evidence in this matter did establish that the agency discriminated against appellant on the basis of her sex and/or retaliated against for engaging in protected EEO activity. In reaching this finding, the AJ concluded that the evidence indicated that as a result of reporting sexual harassment, the agency "turned the tables and accused and condemned [appellant] of engaging in consensual sexual behavior inappropriate in the workplace." The AJ found that she did not have to determine whether or not appellant was actually harassed by her coworker. Rather, the AJ noted that the case turned "on the motives of the Agency in investigating the incident and concluding that disciplinary action was warranted." The AJ found that, from the outset, the investigation by the SIS was biased and targeted at appellant and not her allegations. The AJ noted serious flaws with the investigation, including the fact that no statement was taken from appellant, that no signed or sworn statements were taken from the other witnesses, and that appellant was never given the opportunity to challenge any of the allegations made against her. The AJ raised concerns about the "chilling effect upon the reporting of sexual harassment when, if the accuser cannot prove his or her allegations, that person can expect to be disciplined." The AJ concluded that the agency failed to provide appellant with a fair chance to prove her allegations in retaliation for having raised allegations of sexual harassment.
