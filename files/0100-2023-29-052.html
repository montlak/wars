F. REVIEW OF MSPB DECISIONS BY EEOC

An employee dissatisfied with the results obtained before the Board, whether in the first instance or subsequent to the agency processing of the case under EEO procedures, has the option of seeking review before the EEOC. The EEOC's jurisdiction is limited to conducting a review of the discrimination allegations only. Note, however, that the Commission does not adhere to the general principles of appellate review. Prior to the 1999 regulatory amendments, the EEOC conducted what it referred to as a "de novo" review of the record. This meant that, on occasion, the Commission would enter its own factual findings, which were at variance with those of the Board. The Commission's current regulations provide that the factual findings of administrative judges are reviewed based on the substantial evidence standard. See 29 CFR 1614.405(a). The Commission's regulations give no indication it will accord the same deference to the factual findings of the Board's administrative judges. There is, however, no reason to believe that it will not give the same deference to Board judges. See, e.g., Thomas v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 03A20027 (2002) (holding that the MSPB decision was "supported by the evidence in the record as a whole"); Sacco v. Attorney General, 03A10104 (2002) ("The Commission must determine whether the decision of the MSPB with respect to the allegation of discrimination constitutes a correct interpretation of any applicable laws, rules, regulations, or policy directives, and is supported by the evidence in the record as a whole."); see also Jackson v. Secretary of Navy, 03A20093 (2002).

In matters related to the Board's interpretation of civil service law or regulations, or collective bargaining provisions, the EEOC must defer to the Board's interpretation, unless they have been applied in a discriminatory fashion. See, e.g., LeMond v. USPS, 03860089 (1986). The criteria for Commission review of Board decisions was set forth in Williams v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 03900084 (1990):

The Commission must determine whether the decision of the Board, with respect to the issues of discrimination, constitutes an incorrect interpretation of any applicable law, rule, regulation or policy directive, or is not supported by the evidence in the record as a whole.

The MSPB cannot confer upon a complainant EEOC appeal rights where no such rights exist. In Harris v. Postmaster General, 03960005 (1996), the Board, in its final decision, gave the complainant a notice of appeal rights to the EEOC. However, on review, the Commission found that the complainant waived his discrimination claim before the Board by not pursuing it. As the Commission explained in Harris:

In his appeal to the full Board, petitioner asserted that the agency failed to establish his inability to perform his job or show how his actions adversely affected the efficiency of the service. The full Board affirmed the AJ's Initial Decision and advised petitioner that, inter alia, he could seek further review of his discrimination claims before this Commission.

EEOC regulations implementing the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 set forth the procedures applicable when a petition to review a decision of the MSPB on an issue of discrimination is filed with the Commission pursuant to 5 USC § 7702(b). See 29 CFR § 1613.401(b). In the instant case, the MSPB AJ affirmed the agency's actions based on petitioner's alleged criminal activity, finding that the agency had a reasonable belief that petitioner committed a crime for which he could be imprisoned, justifying its decision to place him on indefinite suspension. Although petitioner's representative questioned the lone witness at the hearing whether the Postal Inspector's decision to target petitioner for investigation was a part of a racial conspiracy or whether he knew of petitioner's substance abuse, the clear and principal focus of the hearing and of his appeal to the full Board was whether the agency's actions were ethical or consistent with agency regulations. He did not introduce any evidence at the hearing in support of his allegations of discrimination based on race or physical disability; nor did he allege that the agency's actions were taken in reprisal for prior EEO activity. For this reason, and notwithstanding the full Board's provision of appeal rights to the Commission, we find that petitioner abandoned his claims of discrimination and we deny consideration.

The basic requirements for a petition for review are set out at 29 CFR 1614.303:

(a) Who may file. Individuals who have received a final decision from the MSPB on a mixed case appeal or on the appeal of a final decision on a mixed case complaint under 5 CFR part 1201, subpart E and 5 USC 7702 may petition EEOC to consider that decision. The EEOC will not accept appeals from MSPB dismissals without prejudice.

Petitions for review should be filed by return receipt mail with the EEOC Office of Federal Operations. See 29 CFR 1614.303(b). The appeal may be filed either by the employee or his or her representative. The petition must be filed within 30 days of receipt of the Board's final decision or within 30 days of the date when the Board's initial decision becomes final. See 29 CFR 1614.303(c). The 30-day period begins to run from the time the employee receives the Board's decision and not from the time the employee's representative receives the Board's decision. See Schaffer v. Department of Interior, 03840224 (1986).
