P. MISREPRESENTATIONS IN THE SETTLEMENT PROCESS

An agency may be held accountable for misrepresentations made to a complainant during settlement negotiations. Smith v. Secretary of Treasury, 05880523 (1988). The Commission did not require an affirmative finding that the agency had acted in bad faith in providing the complainant with a statement to the effect that she could not receive a retroactive promotion if she successfully prosecuted her complaint.

Allegations that an agency made misrepresentations in the settlement process must be supported with proof. Where an agreement has been subject to extensive negotiations and several draft versions, this can rebut a charge of misrepresentation. For example, in Jablonski v. National Labor Relations Board, 01970583 (1997), the Commission rejected the complainant's allegations that the agency had misrepresented the benefits of the settlement agreement at issue. The Commission in Jablonski noted that the terms of the settlement were very thoroughly and explicitly set forth, and that had the complainant desired different terms, he should not have entered into the agreement:

On May 3, 1996, appellant and the agency entered into a settlement agreement to resolve appellant's informal EEO complaint. The settlement agreement provided that appellant would resign from the agency effective May 3, 1996, and the agency was required, inter alia, to provide appellant $50,000.00 in compensatory damages. Provision 14, which is at issue in the instant appeal, states: "The Agency agrees that in the event [appellant] applies for unemployment compensation after the effective date of his resignation, the Agency will not object to his claim."

The record indicates that by letter dated August 6, 1996, appellant alleged that the agency had breached provision 14 of the settlement agreement. Appellant noted that after he applied for unemployment compensation, he learned that he could only receive six months of consecutive unemployment compensation during a 12-month period. Appellant alleged that the agency had represented to him during settlement negotiations that he would be able to receive unemployment compensation for 12 months, and therefore, according to appellant, the settlement agreement should be read as requiring that appellant receive unemployment compensation for 12 months. As a remedy, appellant requested that the agency comply with the terms of the settlement agreement, i.e., that the agency give him a lump-sum payment in an amount equal to six months of unemployment compensation, and that if the agency refused to comply, his complaint be reinstated for further processing.

...

On appeal, in addition to contending that the agency breached the settlement agreement, appellant contends that the agency made material misrepresentations during the negotiation process and that the agency acted in bad faith.

...

Initially, we address appellant's contention that the agency made material misrepresentations and acted in bad faith. According to appellant, during the settlement negotiations, the agency rejected his claim for $65,000.00 in compensatory damages, but the agency offered to give him $50,000.00 in compensatory damages and to pay his unemployment compensation for 12 months following his resignation from the agency. Appellant stated that he and the agency calculated the unemployment compensation to be $18,668.00 for 12 months, and that he considered this amount in evaluating whether to enter into the settlement. According to appellant, he explained to agency officials during the negotiation process that he was concerned about being unemployed after entering into the settlement agreement, and that he believed that he would probably need 12 months to find an acceptable position. Appellant stated that he expressed misgivings about entering into the agreement on several occasions, but Agency official A and Agency official B emphasized the benefits that appellant would receive from the agreement, including unemployment compensation, and they stated that they would intervene on his behalf should the agency not meet its obligation to provide 12 months of unemployment compensation as required by the agreement.

Under the circumstances, we find that appellant has not shown that the agency misrepresented the terms of the settlement agreement or acted in bad faith. In a memorandum to the record dated July 18, 1996, Agency official A stated that the agency had agreed not to oppose appellant's claim for unemployment compensation, but the agency has no authority over unemployment claims, and she had informed appellant during the negotiation process that she was unsure of the time period for which he could receive unemployment benefits, and therefore, she suggested that he call the unemployment office to find out the amount of benefits he would receive and for what period of time he would receive them. In addition, we note that although appellant was not represented by an attorney, appellant, himself, is an attorney, and most of the provisions in the settlement agreement explicitly indicate, in detail, the agency's obligations under the settlement agreement.

We note that the subject settlement agreement is extensive, 13 pages long with 30 separate provisions. The record further indicates that the settlement agreement underwent numerous revisions, and that the language of provision 14 was included in several draft settlement agreements that appellant considered. Therefore, if the agency represented during the settlement negotiations that appellant would receive unemployment compensation for 12 months and this representation was significant to appellant, then it would seem that language to that effect would appear in the agreement. Even so, the settlement agreement was never revised to explicitly indicate that appellant would receive unemployment compensation for 12 months. Based on the foregoing, we find no evidence that the agency misrepresented the terms of the settlement agreement or that the agency acted in bad faith.

A situation where the complainant claims that agency misrepresentations during settlement negotiations resulted in acceptance of the terms of settlement must be distinguished from the situation where the complainant alleges that the agency's conduct during settlement negotiations itself causes harm. For example, in the Smith case, supra, the agency misrepresented the scope of relief that was available to her if she successfully pursued her complaint--i.e., it told her she could not receive a retroactive promotion as relief. When such a misrepresentation, whether deliberate or inadvertent, induces a complainant to accept the offered terms of settlement, the agreement can be set aside. However, a complainant does not state a claim by alleging misconduct during settlement negotiations where that conduct did not induce the complainant to settle. As the Commission explained in Klauzinski v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A20863 (2002):

The Commission has held that settlement negotiations, involving any statements, requests and proposal made therein, are to be treated as confidential and privileged in order to facilitate candid interchange to settle disputes informally. To allow complainant to base a new claim on a settlement offer that she determines was predicated upon purportedly inaccurate information would defeat this purpose.

Similarly, in Connors v. Postmaster General, 01A21585 (2002), the Commission held that an allegation that the complainant suffered discrimination because of inappropriate comments made during a mediation session did not state a claim for relief.

In Perry v. Postmaster General, 01A45685 (2005), the Commission reinstated a complaint where the complainant entered into the settlement of a grievance on an unrelated matter. Pursuant to that agreement, the complainant was required to withdraw her grievance. However, the agency sent the complainant a letter stating that she "must" withdraw her EEO complaint as a condition of settlement. Under the circumstances, the Commission found that withdrawal of the EEO complaint was not knowing and voluntary.
