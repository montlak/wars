broida eeo Chapter 12: Theories of Discrimination--Hostile Environment Chapter 12: Theories of Discrimination--Hostile Environment, Subchapter 05: Overview of Liability for Harassment, Section D: Hostile Environment Harassment--Supervisors and Coworkers, Subsection 01: Prompt and Effective Corrective Action, Part a: Notice
a. Notice

The Court in Burlington Industries held that where the harassment does not result in a tangible employment action being taken against the employee, the agency can make out an affirmative defense by demonstrating: (a) that it exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct promptly any harassing behavior; and (b) that Complainant unreasonably failed to take advantage of any preventive or corrective opportunities provided by the Agency or to avoid harm otherwise. Notice or agency knowledge of the harassment is tied to the complainant's duty to take advantage of any preventative or corrective opportunities. While there is no requirement that the complainant be the one to notify the agency of alleged harassment for liability purposes, the agency must somehow be made aware.

The agency may not, however, simply punt the question of notice with responses like "I didn't know" and "I was not made aware," as was the Commission's conclusion in Sallie M. v. Postmaster General, 0120172430 (2018):

Here, Complainant stated in her affidavit that she had told management about the harassment by the Supervisor. She specifically stated that she informed the Carrier Supervisor of the sexual harassment prior to the egregious events of August 5, 2016. However, Complainant indicated that the Carrier Supervisor took no action.

In addition, Complainant stated that she showed the Supervisor Customer Service her phone when the Supervisor tried to call her. She claimed that the Supervisor Customer Service warned her that the Supervisor was dangerous and that Complainant needed to be aware of her surroundings. However, the Supervisor Customer Service took no action.

When asked by the EEO Investigator, the Carrier Supervisor stated, "I was not aware of the situation." The Carrier Supervisor provided very little information to the EEO Investigator. We note that the Carrier Supervisor's most common responses to questions were "nothing," "I was not aware of the situation," and "I do not know." The Carrier Supervisor also stated in summary, "In light of the situation, I was not aware of these circumstances and allegations upon me I was not her immediate supervisor nor did she ever contact me via text, or phone or by mouth." The EEO Investigator provided the Carrier Supervisor a supplemental interrogatory asking him if he was made aware of the harassment prior to the August 5, 2016, event. The Carrier Supervisor stated, "no."

The Supervisor Customer Service similarly responded to questions in the interrogatory by stating, "No, I was not aware," "Nothing was communicated with me," "I do not know." When asked if Complainant provided her cell phone to him, the Supervisor Customer Service stated simply, "No, that is not true." The Supervisor Customer Service was also asked if he warned Complainant that the Supervisor was dangerous. To which he replied, "No I never said anything." The Supervisor Customer Service provide no additional information beyond his negative responses.

Many of the other management officials in the same manner provided limited responses including Postmaster 1 and Postmaster 2. Postmaster 1 denied knowledge of the events. Postmaster 2 stated in his affidavit dated February 3, 2017, that he was not involved. However, the record indicated that Postmaster 2 was the official who drafted and signed the Supervisor's proposed removal signed November 2, 2016. He also averred that the Supervisor was removed for not participating in the Agency's internal investigation. He stated in the affidavit that the Supervisor's removal was not related to his harassment of Complainant and the Coworker. We note that this is not consistent with the Notice of Removal issued to the Supervisor. The Removal action specifically found that the Supervisor subjected Complainant and the Coworker to harassment from April 2016 to August 2016.

Based on the lack of information and inconsistencies in the record, the Commission is skeptical of the responses provided by the Agency's management officials. As such, we find that the Agency has not demonstrated that Complainant failed to report the actions of the Supervisor's prior to the August 5, 2016 event. In addition, Complainant has asserted and the Agency has not shown otherwise that members of management failed to take any corrective action.

Where the complainant informs the employer of the harassment, but requests the employer not to intervene, this may insulate the employer from liability for not taking prompt and effective remedial action. In Torres v. Pisano, 116 F.3d 625 (2nd Cir. 1997), the court found the plaintiff's requests to the employer to keep her allegations confidential precluded a finding of liability. The court declined to fashion any bright-line rule, but said it would look at the circumstances of each case to determine whether a confidentiality request would exempt an employer from liability for not taking action. The court in Torres found that the emphasis was not on what the plaintiff understood her confidentiality request to mean, but on what the employer reasonably would have, and did, find the request to mean.

By contrast, in Mangano v. Attorney General, 01971055 (1999), the Commission found the agency was liable for sexual harassment where rumors of an affair between the complainant and a supervisor persisted even after the agency conducted its investigation into the allegations. The Commission's decision in Mangano also is interesting because it makes no reference to the Supreme Court's decisions in Burlington Industries and Faragher, even though it was decided after those cases. In Mangano, the Commission found:

The Commission disagrees with the agency, however, that, when taken as a whole, RO1's conduct and that of appellant's co-workers did not create an environment so hostile or offensive as to affect the terms and conditions of appellant's employment. While the agency characterized the rumors of an affair as being of short duration, the record shows that such rumors persisted for at least 14 months. In addition, testimony reveals that the rumors of an affair spread to other offices within the INS. Further, given that RO1 was appellant's second-level supervisor, we find the attention appellant received was sufficiently offensive to rise to a level prohibited by Title VII. Appellant repeatedly stated that she was intimidated by RO1. In addition, all but one of the co-workers who addressed the matter stated that male agents were reluctant to work with appellant because of the rumored relationship with RO1. Such a finding is supported by the fact that appellant was referred to as "Princess" by her male co-workers. Therefore, appellant established a prima facie case of hostile environment sexual harassment.

The agency argued that it is not liable for the sexual harassment, because immediate and appropriate corrective action was taken as soon as management was put on notice thereof. Specifically, the agency stated that, after appellant complained of the rumors in November 1989, RO1 and RO3 both questioned other agents and the rumors ceased. Nevertheless, appellant asserted that the rumors persisted in 1991 and 1992, and there is some evidence in the record showing that the rumors had spread to another unit in December 1989, that is, after RO1 and RO3 conducted their investigations.

Furthermore, it appears that no action was taken with regard to the attention appellant received from RO1. RO3 acknowledged having concerns regarding RO1's contact with appellant beginning in March 1989. In addition, RO3 stated that RO1's attention appeared to make appellant uncomfortable. Given the persistent rumors of an affair and the reluctance of male co-workers to work with appellant, it would have been reasonable for RO3 to have taken action at that time. It is also noted that appellant asserted that the unwelcome contact from RO1 continued while she was under his supervision, and that the rumors persisted in 1991 and 1992. Finally, while the agency found that RO1 had no involvement in the proposed discipline, the record supports a finding that the action, which was then used as a basis for appellant's OCORS rating, was in fact initiated by RO1.

Where the agency knows or has reason to know about the occurrence of acts of sexual harassment on the part of a supervisor or employee, the employer should investigate promptly and thoroughly, and if it fails to take prompt and appropriate action reasonably calculated to end the harassment, it will be held liable for a violation of Title VII. Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment, EEOC Notice No. N-915-050 at 29-30 (March 19, 1990); Owens v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Request No. 05940824 (September 5, 1996). What is appropriate action will necessarily depend on the particular facts of the case and the severity and persistence of the harassment, and the effectiveness of any initial remedial step. Owens, supra. The employer should make follow-up inquiries to make sure the harassment has not resumed and the victim has not suffered retaliation. EEOC Notice No. N-915-050 at 30 (March 19, 1990). As stated, management, including RO3, knew of RO1's behavior and the persistent rumors of an affair, but failed to take appropriate corrective action. Accordingly, the agency is liable for the harassment by RO1 against appellant.
