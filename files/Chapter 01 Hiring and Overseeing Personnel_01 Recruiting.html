RECRUITING

Your agency is competing with the private sector, state and local governments, and other federal agencies to find the best candidates for positions. As a hiring manager, you will have to become more involved in the recruiting process if you want to draw those prospects toward your agency. Your value in the recruiting process comes from your acute understanding of available positions as well as a sense of the people you'll need and the knowledge, skills, and abilities they must possess in order to successfully work in your unit.

Job postings through public notice and other online outlets are fine, but they are simply passive announcements of open positions. Recruiting on your part will need to be proactive and ongoing throughout the year. It may involve building and developing relationships with sources who have strong candidate pools or who can steer you toward other resources. Pass these resources on to HR and consult with your agency staffing experts to determine recruiting alternatives and how you can strategize to pull in viable candidates. Effective recruiting efforts should produce your strongest candidates.

Your role in the process

As a supervisor or manager, you are central to the recruiting and hiring process. Hiring reform efforts direct you to take on more responsibility for ensuring the right people are selected for the right jobs. You:

  • • Determine what positions need to be filled now and in the future.
  • • Communicate the need to fill one or more positions to HR staffing experts.
  • • Complete the necessary Form SF-52, which formally requests initiation of a recruiting and hiring action.
  • • Identify the essential duties and responsibilities of the position to be filled. This is one of the areas where hiring reform efforts will place more responsibility on you. When you have a vacancy, don't simply pass on the current position description to HR and ask for a candidate who possesses the same skills as the employee who is leaving. You need to look at the actual duties of the position, how it could change in the future, and what competencies are needed for success.
  • • Identify the qualifications necessary to perform the duties of the position successfully, i.e., the knowledge, skills, and abilities or competencies required. Initial KSAs have been phased out, so applicants can apply for federal positions with a standard resume and cover letter. But hiring managers still need to work with HR to identify key competencies necessary for success. And applicants can be asked yes or no questions as part of the application process to assess whether they possess these skills. OPM suggests that minimum qualifications be boiled down to five or six bullets in a plain-language job announcement.
  • • Interview position candidates. OPM strongly encourages structured interviews to help assess competencies of the top candidates.
  • • Select the best-qualified candidate. Government hiring reform efforts direct agencies to use category ratings to assess candidates instead of the rule of three. This will give you more choices to select from top ranked candidates.
  • • Adhere to your agency's established timelines. Your agency is required to report hiring timelines and targets to OPM, so ensure that you handle your role in the hiring process in a timely fashion.
  • • Evaluate the selected candidate's performance carefully during a probationary or trial period.

Skills-based hiring. The Office of Personnel Management has issued guidance to help agencies transition to a skills-based hiring approach as directed by Executive Order 13932: Modernizing and Reforming the Assessment and Hiring of Federal Job Candidates. The executive order calls for agencies to ensure that, for competitive service positions, candidates be assessed in a manner that does not rely solely on formal education completed to determine the extent to which candidates possess relevant knowledge, skills, competencies, and abilities. The guidance, which includes an updated General Schedule Operating Manual, frequently asked questions document and a new guide on better occupational questionnaires, is designed to help agencies move away from relying on education and candidate self-assessments and allow hiring managers to better assess candidates' skills regardless of how they were acquired. Consult your HR office for more information.

Accountability

Through your agency appraisal system, you will likely be held accountable for how well you recruit, hire, and onboard employees into federal service. Here's a list of tasks you can follow to make sure you meet your agency's expectations. The elements and standards will vary between agencies, but this should set you on the right path:

  • • Base your selections on organizational goals, budget considerations, and staffing needs.
  • • Be involved in future workforce planning and keep succession plans up to date.
  • • Maintain competency plans for all the positions you manage and ensure they contain the core skills needed to successfully perform the position.
  • • Promptly provide performance standards and provide ongoing feedback and coaching to new hires so that they can successfully transition into their positions.
  • • Use the probationary period to assess the new hire's ability to perform in the position.
  • • Be an active participant in all steps of the recruiting and hiring process.
  • • When possible, attend agency recruitment fairs.
  • • Contribute to recruiting efforts. Provide HR with contact information for locations or organizations that produce potential candidates.
  • • Cooperate with HR to ensure the accurate posting of the job vacancy, proper applicant screening, and appropriate categorization of applicants based on qualifications.
  • • Personally interview all best-qualified candidates.

Working with HR

Although it's important that you understand the variety of alternatives open to you in filling positions, you can't expect to be as good at using recruiting and hiring tools as the HR professionals employed by your agency. But there must still be collaboration between you and HR. You need to help determine the duties of the job, the competencies necessary for success, and the best-qualified candidate for the position. Here are the steps you should take in developing a close and effective working relationship with your agency's staffing experts:

  • • Meet the expert responsible for staffing in your organizational component. Spend some time talking. Explain the needs and problems you face and discuss ways in which the expert might help.
  • • Pinpoint specific staffing needs and work out an overall staffing strategy. Talk with HR about how the needs of your department fit in with the agency's overall hiring goals. Do you need to make improvements in diversity? Or do you foresee a shortage of employees with a particular skill six months or a year down the road? If so, your staffing expert can help you formulate a plan to deal with the situation.
  • • Advise your staffing expert of expected vacancies as far in advance as possible. Get in the habit of looking ahead to spot potential vacancies -- permanent or temporary -- and promptly advise HR. The more advance notice you provide, the more options you'll be given.
  • • Make sure position descriptions are up to date. Too many position descriptions in the federal government are outdated and don't reflect the actual duties performed in the particular job. This is why a job analysis is so important. When you have a vacancy, don't simply look for a person to replace the current employee. Take a look at the position, what duties actually need to be performed, how this could change in the future, and what competencies you need the new employee to bring to the table.
  • • Participate in recruitment efforts. See if you can represent the agency at any recruitment events, and work with HR to identify any potential untapped recruitment sources.
  • • Sign a recruitment contract with HR when filling a vacancy. The contract should outline each step in the hiring process, who is responsible for each step, and the amount of time each step should take.

Your staffing expert can help you best if you communicate candidly, frequently, and in advance of hiring and recruiting needs.

Candidate sources

If you are unaware of the many sources from which you may attract and find job candidates:

  • • It will likely take longer to fill vacant positions.
  • • You will probably overlook excellent candidates.

Laws and regulations provide sources from which you can recruit to fill vacant positions, both from within the agency (internal) and outside it (external). Note that some agency labor agreements have provisions that affect the use of temporary promotions and details.

Internal recruiting sources

Merit promotion. This is probably the source you are already most familiar with, and it's obviously a good one. It may not be the best one for every situation, however, because:

  • • It might require more time to post a vacancy and process the applications than you can afford.
  • • It might limit your selection and therefore your ability to meet diversity goals.

Reassignment. This, as the name indicates, simply involves reassigning a person who is already a federal employee at the same grade level. This can't be done, however, if the reassignment candidate is currently in a position that has a career ladder ending in a lower grade than that of the vacant position.

Change to lower grade. As with reassignments, a vacant position can be filled through this method without competition -- but only if the vacant position does not have a career ladder ending in a higher grade than the candidate's current position.

Detail. This is only a temporary solution, but it can provide a quick way to fill an essential position while simultaneously providing a way to try out potential candidates and give them valuable experience. Note: Involuntary details may be subject to labor agreements and at times have led to workplace disputes, including grievances and EEO complaints.

Temporary promotion. This is essentially the same solution represented by a regular detail, except that the person temporarily occupying the higher-graded position is paid at the higher rate of pay.

Noncompetitive re-promotion. This can be used in the case of employees who are qualified for the vacant position and have held it either at or above the current grade level. Often such candidates have been moved to a lower grade as the result of a reduction in force.

Priority placement. Individuals may be entitled to priority placement under a variety of programs, such as those in effect within the Department of Defense. Note that priority placement is not the same as priority consideration.

Upward mobility. Under this program, managers can restructure positions to provide entry and intermediate levels of responsibility below the full performance level.

Phased retirement. This option would continue an employee in federal service for a period of time on a part-time schedule while they engage in mentoring. Although it probably won't answer your long-term needs, phased retirement is designed to assist agencies with knowledge management and continuity of operations in the short term. It will also prepare the next generation of experts for success.

External recruiting sources

Transfers. Good candidates are to be found not only within the agency, but also in other federal agencies. Since employees in other agencies are already in the federal service, they can be transferred into a vacant position.

Reinstatement. Former federal employees who attained career status may be noncompetitively reappointed.

Reemployed annuitants. Retired federal employees may be brought back on either a temporary or permanent basis.

OPM certificates. The Office of Personnel Management maintains certificates and registers of qualified applicants for a variety of positions.

Certificate sharing. Agencies may share best-qualified candidates with other agencies filling competitive service positions at the same occupational series and grade level. The appointing authority must select an individual from the certificate of eligibles within 240 days after the issuance of the certificate.

Military spouse preference. Certain agencies -- generally within the Department of Defense -- permit or require preference to military spouses in filling vacant positions.

Special hiring programs. Managers may hire through certain special programs -- often more quickly or easily than through regular merit promotion. These include:

  1. 1. People with disabilities.
  2. 2. Disabled veterans.
  3. 3. Veterans Readjustment Act.
  4. 4. Worker trainee opportunity programs.

Private sector temporary employees. These individuals are not actually hired but may be brought in on a contract basis to help with a particular project or workload problem.

Job fairs. Conducting them can provide leads, but attending them with other agencies or companies adds another layer of potential recruits. In addition to all the attendees, you are also exposed to representatives of other agencies or private sector companies. Only competent employees are selected to represent their organizations at job fairs, and they may be another source of recruitment for your agency.

Also, make sure to reach out to colleges/universities and professional organizations and tap into resume banks and websites that may be frequented by desired applicants.

In sum, there are a variety of sources from which to find well-qualified candidates. Determining which ones to use in a particular situation should be based on a careful assessment of the situation and of your specific organizational needs.

Mistakes to avoid

An effective recruiting effort can make all the difference in the quality of the employees you hire. Don't make the following mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Dismiss the importance of recruiting. This the critical step that builds a competent and effective staff that will carry out the mission of your agency.
  • • Leave recruiting efforts exclusively to HR. Get involved! Work closely with your agency's staffing experts, and be prepared to play an important role.
  • • Recruit at the last minute because you failed to plan. Develop a recruiting strategy that fits organizational needs.
  • • Overlook laws and regulations. There are rules that govern your recruiting efforts to fill vacant positions both from within and outside your agency. Check with your HR office.
  • • Ignore your agency labor agreement. Some labor agreements have provisions that affect the use of temporary promotions and details. Be sure to check it or consult with your LR specialist.

Ask FMG

Q. What's the difference between recruitment and public notice?

A. Public notice is one part of the overall recruitment process. Public notice, or simply posting a job announcement, is not a substitute for the more focused recruiting you may need to undertake to fill positions with highly qualified applicants. As a result, it is not recommended that your agency rely solely on the job announcement as the cornerstone of your recruitment strategy. Rather, use all of the tools at your disposal to produce a high-quality, diverse applicant pool.

Q. What laws and regulations must be observed when recruiting?

A. For starters, you must notify OPM of job opportunities in the competitive service, and public notice is required whenever you are considering hiring applicants from outside the federal workforce for competitive service positions. There are also merit system principles and several prohibited personnel practices that speak to recruiting and hiring practices. Follow proper protocol when seeking job candidates or risk violating ethical and legal standards.

Q. What recruitment tools are available to me and HR?

A. There are a variety of recruiting tools available. They include:

  • • Paid advertising.
  • • Job fairs at schools and community events.
  • • Special internet-based recruiting websites.
  • • Contracted recruiting support from other federal agencies or commercial firms.

In addition, there are also special software applications that screen applicants, monetary incentives such as recruitment and referral bonuses, and student programs such as internships and student loan repayment.
