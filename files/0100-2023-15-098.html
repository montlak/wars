10. Disability/Reasonable Accommodation Cases

However inarticulate a claim, an appeal is an extension of the investigative process and AJs are obligated to analyze the facts of a case to determine whether summary judgment is appropriate. This is especially true where complainants are not represented by an attorney. In the context of disability/reasonable accommodation claims, AJs should both the "record of" and the "regarded as" having a disability prongs. In Doria D. v. Secretary of the Army, 2020005412 (2022), for instance, the complainant alleged disability discrimination when the agency rescinded two job offers and unreasonably delayed her background investigation. Under the facts of the case, the AJ granted summary judgment in favor of the agency concluding that the agency official who rescinded the job offers did not know about her specific disability. The Commission in Doria D. reversed because the AJ failed to analyze the facts of the case under the "regarded as" prong:

Regarding her disability, Complainant identified her current disability as acute panic attacks. She asserted she was first diagnosed with the condition in 2001. However, while perhaps inartfully expressed, Complainant, who is not represented by an attorney, asserted that Agency perceived her as having severe mental health problems.

Complainant testified during the investigation that the ACS Director offered her the Family Advocacy Specialist, GS-11 position in July 2018 subject to the results of a background check. However, the offer of the position was later rescinded in November 2018. The ACS Director testified that he only became aware of Complainant's panic attacks after she filed her EEO complaint during the investigative interview. The ACS Director stated that he did not instruct CPAC Director to rescind Complainant's offer. Instead, he said the CPAC Director informed him that derogatory information had been uncovered during Complainant's background check, and it was the CPAC Director who suggested the rescission of Complainant's offer. The ACS Director testified that he had no real role in the decision to rescind Complainant's offer, and that he did not see the derogatory information was in her background check. The record contains an email from the ACS Director to Human Resources (HR), dated October 15, 2018, stated that it was in the best interest of the government to withdraw the tentative job offer to Complainant.

The CPAC Director testified that her involvement in the rescission action began when her staff informed her of an issue in the pre-employment process involving Complainant. The CPAC Director stated that information was provided by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to the Agency's security office, and the security office notified the HR office. The CPAC Director further testified that the procedure for this action likely involved coordination between her office and ACS Director, with the decision coming from the HR supervisor.

The HR Specialist testified that she received an email from the Agency's security office, on September 25, 2018, that contained documentation from the pre-employment investigation that closed on July 20, 2018, and that the information was provided to HR Specialist's lead supervisor and the CPAC Director. Agency witnesses stated that, due to the nature of the Family Advocacy Program Specialist position, background investigators had to go through previous investigations. The record shows that a previous investigation, that closed on November 3, 2008, contained a determination that Complainant was mentally incompetent in 2008. The record further showed that Complainant was placed on permanent disability due to major depressive disorder requiring psychotropic medicine. There was also mention in Complainant's military record that she was not able to display competence/show any ability as a responsible or acceptable officer in June 2000.

Claim 3

HR Specialist testified that she was assigned to begin the onboarding process by offering the tentative job offer, on June 27, 2018. HR Specialist stated that she advised Complainant that fingerprints were needed, or security would close/terminate the investigation.

HR Specialist testified that she also informed Complainant that security needed fingerprints from Texas and California. HR Specialist denied requesting fingerprints from Complainant three times. HR Specialist testified that it was the security office that sent fingerprint requests, and not her. HR Specialist further testified that the routine requests for Complainant's fingerprints did not delay or interfere with the background check process.

In deciding the case by summary judgment, the AJ, based on the evidence gathered during the investigation, concluded that Complainant failed to establish a prima facie case of disability discrimination. The AJ determined that Complainant did not demonstrate that the ACS Director, the CPAP Director, or the HR Specialist knew about Complainant's acute panic attacks until after the disputed decisions were made and she had filed her EEO complaint. Additionally, the AJ found with regard to claim 3, it was the security office, and not the HR Specialist, who was responsible for the additional fingerprint record requests.

On appeal, Complainant argues that the Agency failed to show any other reasons why the offer was rescinded other than its perceptions of her mental health. Complainant explains that she successfully worked as a Family Advocacy Program Specialist from July 2008 through December 2015, and she states that she resigned from the position in October 2016. She asserts that she passed child background checks in 2005, 2015, and 2016. Complainant states that, at the time that she was offered the West Point Family Advocacy Program position, she had not worked in the Federal government for three years. Complainant argues that the ACS Director did, in fact, instruct the CPAC Director to rescind Complainant's offer. She also points to the record as evidence that the offer rescission was based on her mental health. Complainant states that she contacted the ACS Director about the rescinded offer, and that when she did, ACS Director was rude and nasty towards her and refused to discuss why the offer was rescinded. Complainant contends that the AJ ignored the evidence proving that the Agency representatives only considered its perceptions of Complainant's mental health when deciding to rescind her job offer.

The ACS Director testified that he was unaware of Complainant's disability until the EEO investigation, and that he was not involved in the decision to rescind Complainant's offer. However, record evidence suggests that the ACS Director was apprised of derogatory information gathered about Complainant's mental health during the background check, and that he emailed HR Specialist, on October 15, 2018, and stated that rescinding Complainant's offer was in the best interest of the Agency. The record also contains one page from a report titled "Medical/Psychiatric" which contains what is described as "medical record obtained by investigator." Report of Investigation at 20. The medical record stated that, on June 10, 2008, Complainant was permanently retired due to the diagnosis of major depressive disorder, requiring psychotropic medication and outpatient treatment. The medical record also goes on to discuss the associated symptoms.

The AJ largely bases the granting of summary judgment on the fact that there was no evidence that "acute panic attacks," Complainant's only claimed actual disability, was the basis for the decision to rescind the job offer.

However, the AJ failed to consider whether the Agency's perception of Complainant's mental health that resulted from reports gathered during the background check resulted in the rescission decision and whether or not this amounted to a violation of the Rehabilitation Act.

EEOC regulations implementing the Rehabilitation Act make it clear that an individual is entitled to the statute's protections against disability discrimination if they have: (1) a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of the major life activities of such individual; (2) a record of such an impairment; or (3) is regarded as having such an impairment. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g). Here, the evidence developed during the investigation strongly suggests that the Agency's decision to rescind the job offer was based on information gathered during the background check that indicated Complainant had been diagnosed with mental health conditions in the past. Therefore, at least for the purposes of establishing a prima facie case of disability discrimination, it appears that Complainant was covered by the Rehabilitation Act as under either the "record of" and/or the "regarded as" prongs. As such, the AJ's decision by summary judgment concluding Complainant could not establish a prima facie inference of disability discrimination was in error.

In sum, we conclude that Complainant has, in fact, established a prima facie inference of disability discrimination. This, of course, is not sufficient to prove the Agency's liability for a violation of the Rehabilitation Act. However, based on our finding that Complainant has established a prima facie case and our determination that this case may involve the resolution of significant credibility issues, we conclude that summary judgment was not appropriate and are remanding the matter for a hearing.
