ETIQUETTE IN THE WORKPLACE

Employees who refuse to respect the most elementary rules of workplace etiquette, have personal hygiene issues, don't keep their workplaces neat and clean, or can't meet reasonable standards of personal appearance are difficult to deal with primarily because most managers find it embarrassing to speak to them about the offensive issue. These employees can ruin morale, harm productivity, and move your best workers to look for employment elsewhere. They also give rise to more than their share of workplace disputes.

Being a good leader sometimes requires you to deal with uncomfortable situations within the workplace. You should always remember that your mission is to create and maintain a productive, respectful work environment. This is especially true when it comes to handling issues of dress and behavior amongst your employees.

Dress code

When it comes to personal appearance, do your employees represent your agency well, or would you just as soon hope they were never seen by anyone outside your unit? Get control over employee standards in dress and grooming. You have the authority to enforce personal appearance rules.

But just what are the rules? Check with HR to determine the details of your agency's personal appearance policy. If you cannot find one, perhaps it is time to move on the issue with HR.

The reality is personal appearance issues regularly attract unwelcome media attention. This can make your agency look foolish. This is especially true in warm weather when employees are wearing flip-flops, shorts, and tank tops. It is better to have a permanent policy in the employee handbook that spells everything out so specific memos, which may attract media attention, can be avoided.

Where do you begin? Well, a standard policy for workers in an office environment may include these points:

  • • Employees must dress in a manner appropriate to a business culture. They may not wear suggestive or revealing clothing. Specifically banned items, such as flip-flops, T-shirts, jeans, and tank tops, should be listed.
  • • Visible piercings (other than earrings) and tattoos are not permitted. (If they are permitted, it is difficult to distinguish between acceptable and unacceptable.)
  • • Hair and facial hair must be neat and clean.

Dress codes may also consider the difference between jobs where there is contact with the public and positions with no contact, as well as uniforms and safety requirements.

If there is no written dress code, you should not just allow unprofessional dress to continue. You should address the issue. You can:

  • • Have a private talk with the employee. Make him aware of the need for tidying up, particularly if the employee lacks social skills.
  • • Be sensitive to personal issues. Personal problems including financial difficulties are better addressed by an employee assistance program counselor.
  • • Understand the potential for medical issues. An employee may raise a medical issue, which will give an opening to request medical documentation.
  • • Be detailed and specific about the problem. If the problem persists after an oral discussion, an employee will respond to a counseling memorandum that describes the grooming deficiencies in clothing and the time you have spent responding to complaints by coworkers about the situation.

EEO issues. Always be careful to avoid gender discriminatory language. In particular, it is never possible to insist women wear skirts or dresses. However, social norms may permit long hair and earrings for women, but not for men.

Religious discrimination must also be avoided. For example, Sikh turbans or other religiously required headgear generally cannot be prohibited, and policies should always consider possible adverse impacts on groups protected against discrimination.

Managers may limit religious expression on the basis of undue hardship if such expression creates a disruption of the workplace and interferes with maintaining the efficiency of operations. But be warned, the undue hardship argument will not always succeed, especially when it comes to public image. An argument that Muslim head scarves for women distract from the agency's image or might offend members of the public will not work.

General prohibitions against T-shirts or other items with slogans, or against visible tattoos, will avoid liability for discrimination.

Be reasonable. When it comes to enforcement, the general standard should be "reasonableness" -- based on clear business reasons. Do not enforce personal standards of what is appropriate attire, which may be unduly controlled by personal taste or even by limited knowledge of current styles.

It may be difficult, even embarrassing, for you to talk to employees about personal appearance issues, especially if poor grooming or bad hygiene is also involved, but you are entitled to do it and you should.

Most importantly, your employees should follow your lead. If you want your employees to maintain a certain level of professionalism in their dress, you should leave the tattered jeans and old wrinkled shirts at home.

Before you rein in any of your employees' questionable clothing choices, make sure their right to wear Hawaiian shirts isn't part of the collective bargaining agreement. Carrying out major changes to the office dress code could result in an unfair labor practice complaint by the employee union.

To a certain extent, though, you can mandate what is acceptable in the office.

And any time an employee shows up wearing something inappropriate, you should send him home to change. Allowing it to slip by might encourage him to try again.

Personal hygiene

While good grooming and hygiene ordinarily are matters only for an individual employee, lack of them can seriously affect coworkers and the efficiency of the agency. Managers are often reluctant to take on these problems because of their personal nature.

So why should you confront personal and workplace appearance and grooming issues?

  • • They lower the professional reputation of your entire work unit.
  • • Coworkers have a hard time working with people whose behavior seems offensive, or simply ridiculous.
  • • Your failure to correct their behavior hurts your authority as a supervisor.
  • • In extreme cases, there may be health and safety issues.

When you talk to an employee about work issues, you can speak as one adult to another. But grown-ups are expected to be able to clean and dress themselves and tend to normal housekeeping issues without supervision.

When they can't, the situation can become embarrassing. The only way to make the embarrassment stop is to talk to the employee.

And what gives you the right to talk about these things to another adult? This is part of your inherent authority as a supervisor, as long as your standards are reasonable and appropriate to your unit's mission.

Stick to the facts and business purpose when informing the employee:

  • • Describe the offending behavior.
  • • Explain the negative consequences (e.g., the unit's professional reputation has been hurt or the employee's own reputation has been harmed).
  • • Order the employee to correct the behavior.
  • • Explain that failure to correct the behavior will lead to discipline.

The first conversation can be as formal or informal as you want, but:

  • • Don't send mixed messages like, "You're doing a great job, but…"
  • • Remain polite and professional. Don't accuse, describe.
  • • Invite the employee to respond, and listen attentively.
  • • Sympathize with any legitimate mitigating factors, but insist the behavior must change quickly.
  • • Document the conversation.

In the overwhelming majority of cases, your first conversation on the subject will be your last. Most people with personal behavior issues have simply gotten into bad habits. Some may not even be aware of the difficulty. Your conversation may be upsetting, but most people will make a strong effort to change their behavior. If they begin to backslide, a gentle reminder may be all it takes to get them back on the right path.

Chances are their situation in the workplace will improve, and that will reinforce their new behavior. They may even thank you.

But it may not work. The employee may not want or even be able to change. In that case, you may need to move forward with some form of progressive discipline. Get with HR.

Warning: It is possible that a physical or mental disability is behind the personal hygiene problem. This is a potential EEO issue. The moment your employee makes you aware of a potential disability, alert HR. You still have a right to expect acceptable behavior, but you need advice on whether a disability is involved or there is need for a reasonable accommodation.

Workstations

Your employee has mountains of obsolete work materials, newspapers from 2004, dusty books, and who-knows-what-else cluttering up his workspace. Coworkers routinely make jokes about his work area and the general impression of most people at the agency is that you have allowed this to go on for far too long.

You would think that adults would clean up after themselves, but when days are busy and distractions are many, it can be easy to let things slide.

Physical clutter can be a major source of workplace stress. A lot of people are particular about cleanliness and are used to a certain level of hygiene or orderliness around them. When these standards drop below a certain level, it can have a huge impact on workplace morale. It may be time for you and your fellow unit chiefs to remind employees about some basic office etiquette -- and to do so in a diplomatic fashion.

There are a number of approaches to dealing with an unsightly cubicle.

First, you have to decide if this is something that should be handled on a one-on-one basis or on a group level, if it is more widespread than you care to admit.

If it's a group issue, an upcoming staff meeting may be the perfect opportunity to remind everyone of the requirements for office maintenance.

Do customers, clients, or senior managers visit the area on occasion? If so, a workstation becomes even more of an eyesore, and everyone should be expected to maintain a neat professional appearance.

Another useful tactic might involve scheduling a general "clean up/spruce up" day throughout the branch if this is a bigger issue than an individual desk. This approach singles out no one. Ask everyone to take a hard look at the state of his work area.

Peer pressure can be another great motivator. Sometimes suggestions from a "nagging" supervisor may not be well-received. However, suggestions from a coworker that a neater, cleaner work area would benefit everyone's morale might just do the trick. Only in rare situations should a supervisor employ a surrogate -- someone to carry out a task on his behalf. But the clutter and congestion created by a cubicle slob is not exclusively a management problem. It involves personal habits that impact everyone, so there is nothing wrong with getting coworkers involved.

Warning: It is not recommended that you have a group meeting to discuss cleaning workstations if it only applies to one person in the group. That sort of "singling out" is not a good management tactic when everyone knows who you are talking about. Employees will see it as a waste of time and morale killer.

Office parties

Office parties often signal a welcome break from routine. They usually involve festive and enjoyable events. But these parties can have some awkward moments. In extreme cases, they can even generate instances of employee misconduct or ethics violations.

The prospect of socializing with coworkers is a mixed bag. We already spend more time with these people each week than we do with our own families. Nevertheless, our better nature usually prevails. We recognize that making an appearance at the office party is "the right thing to do."

Eat, drink, and be merry -- in moderation. Because of a long history of problems, many agencies no longer permit alcohol at office social functions, although it is more common to have a bar when the event is held away from the workplace. You and your employees can certainly enjoy yourselves, but it is best to take it easy with the alcohol. You all want to avoid those "I can't believe I said that" moments?

Also, remember to dress appropriately for the occasion. An office party is not the right time "to strut your stuff." Leave anything short, tight, or revealing in the closet. Loosen your tie or shed your jacket, but resist loosening your good judgment. You've worked hard to create a professional image. Don't let a clothing gaffe alter the perception of you as a competent professional.

The bottom line is pretty much the same for all of us: We want to relax and enjoy a bit of camaraderie. Enjoy, but be sure to observe a few commonsense guidelines. After all, you don't want video clips of your party antics to show up on YouTube.

There is always a possibility of sexual harassment and other conduct violations during office parties as well. What's more, if it is the supervisor who is doing the groping, the possibility of discipline and EEO liability is much stronger. On- or off-duty, as a manager you are held to a higher standard of conduct.

You need to realize that you may be responsible and liable whether an event is on- or off-site or on- or off-duty, to the extent that it flows from work. If you say, "Let's go out for drinks," or send an email, or if something is posted on a bulletin board that increases the link to work, you are setting yourself up for potential liability issues.

When it comes to discipline, on-duty misconduct creates an automatic nexus linking conduct and the efficiency of the service. With off-duty misconduct, there may be a nexus, depending on the degree of connection to the work. So if you are posting a flyer for happy hour on the agency bulletin board, you could be creating a nexus with that action.

This also affects workers' compensation claims. The claim will depend on the degree of sponsorship of the event by the agency. So be careful about creating that connection and use some common sense.

Interacting with people with disabilities

Ensuring that you and your employees understand proper etiquette when interacting with people with disabilities is important for creating an inclusive workplace. Good disability etiquette can help employees feel more comfortable when interacting with coworkers with disabilities and help prevent awkward situations. It can also help your agency serve its customers more effectively.

First, consider these general steps for ensuring appropriate disability etiquette:

  • • Use "people first" language, which emphasizes the person before the disability. For example, say "people with disabilities" instead of "the handicapped" or "the disabled"; "She has autism" rather than "She's autistic."
  • • Don't ask questions about a person's disability unless the individual brings it up.
  • • If you offer assistance, wait until the offer is accepted, then listen to or ask for instructions. Don't be afraid to ask questions if you're unsure what to do.
  • • Make sure common areas are accessible and navigable.
  • • Provide alternative formats for audio and visual materials.
  • • Include employees who have vision or hearing impairments in social events by making events accessible.

People with vision and hearing impairments. Proper etiquette regarding individuals with vision impairments includes:

  • • Verbally greeting and identifying yourself before extending your hand to greet a person who is blind. Use the same courtesy when entering or leaving a room or saying goodbye. Don't just walk away when talking with a person who is blind or visually impaired.
  • • Offering your arm rather than just taking the arm of a person who is blind or visually impaired. As you walk, tell the person where you are going, make note of steps and slopes, and point out obstacles, such as opening doors.
  • • Not petting, playing with, distracting, or feeding a guide dog without the person's permission. A guide dog is working and is not a pet, so it's vitally important to not distract the animal.
  • • Walking on the side opposite the animal when walking alongside someone who is using a guide dog.
  • • Offering to read written information, where appropriate, and providing work-related materials in an accessible format, such as large print, Braille, or a screen reader.
  • • For meetings, providing descriptive directions that don't require the person to rely on visual references.
  • • Offering new employees a guided tour of the workplace.
  • • Informing individuals with vision impairments when structural changes, hazards, new construction, or workplace modifications occur.

If an employee has a hearing impairment:

  • • Get the individual's attention by waving, tapping her shoulder, or another appropriate gesture.
  • • Pay attention to cues indicating whether the person uses sign language or is reading lips, writing, or gesturing.
  • • Don't put your hands in front of your face or food in your mouth when communication with someone who is reading lips. Also, do not turn your head or walk away while talking.
  • • When speaking with someone who is using a sign language interpreter, maintain eye contact with, and direct your communication to, the person who is deaf.
  • • Speak using a normal tone of voice unless asked to raise your voice. Rephrase, rather than repeat the same words, if you aren't understood.
  • • When in a meeting or group setting, speak to one person at a time so the person who is deaf or hard of hearing can read lips if they are able to.
  • • Don't be afraid to say you don't understand if you have difficulty understanding a person's speech.
  • • Ask the person how they would prefer to communicate for job training or other work-related situations and provide a qualified interpreter, CART service (translation of speech into text), or captioned training videos, as appropriate.

People with mobility issues. Here are some tips for situations involving employees with motor or mobility impairment:

  • • Don't make assumptions about the extent of a person's limitations based on the use of assistive devices -- or the lack thereof. Individuals may use a cane, walker, or wheelchair regularly or only as needed, and people who appear to be mobile may still need accommodations.
  • • Be aware that people who do not have a visible disability may nonetheless have needs related to their mobility. For example, a person with a respiratory or heart condition may have trouble walking long distances or walking quickly.
  • • Don't touch or lean on a wheelchair or move a person's walker or cane without being asked to. Also, don't pet a service animal without permission. A wheelchair, mobility aid, or service animal is part of an individual's personal space, an extension of that individual.
  • • Upon hiring a new employee with an obvious mobility impairment, offer to provide a tour so the individual can evaluate the work site's accessible and inaccessible elements.
  • • Ask whether a person needs assistance before helping. People who use canes or crutches need their arms to balance themselves, so never grab them. People who have limited mobility may lean on a door for support as they open it. Pushing the door open from behind or unexpectedly opening the door may cause them to fall. Don't be afraid to ask how you can help.
  • • Sit down when speaking for more than a few minutes with a person who uses a wheelchair so you are at eye level. If it's not possible to sit at eye level, stand at a slight distance so the person in a wheelchair is not straining their neck to make eye contact with you.
  • • Be careful about the language you use. For example, people are not "confined" or "bound" to a wheelchair. Rather, the wheelchair enables them to get where they need to go.

If you have questions about how to properly interact with individuals with disabilities in other situations, contact your HR or EEO office for advice.

Mistakes to avoid

Workplace etiquette issues create a certain level of discomfort for everyone involved. Be sure to avoid these common pitfalls.

Do not:

  • • Cross the dress code line to sex discrimination. Although having different dress codes for men and women to create a professional atmosphere generally is not unlawful, a direction that a male employee dress in a more masculine manner could be sex discrimination. An employee claimed that his area manager told him his green pants violated the dress code because they "were not a boy color," yet female employees were not subject to color rules. The EEOC found that he stated a claim of sex discrimination that went beyond the agency's authority to determine appropriate business attire. It reversed the agency's dismissal and remanded the case for further processing.
  • • Make a group issue out of an individual one. If one employee needs to be talked to about inappropriate behavior, grooming, hygiene, or messiness, do not incorporate the group into the discussion. These are personal matters that should be handled as such. Unless the situation involves almost everyone in your unit, it should be handled on an individual basis.
  • • Promote off-duty activities that can lead to liability. You want to go out with a few colleagues and get a drink? Do it informally and don't advertise it. That will only lead to liability issues and potential EEO or workers' compensation claims.
  • • Throw common sense out the window. While etiquette issues may be somewhat sensitive by nature, don't overthink them. For instance, if you are going to the office holiday party -- don't drink too much.
  • • Forget to contact your HR, EEO, and LR specialists. To avoid fights with the union over any dress code situation, or to avoid liability for an EEO complaint, meet with HR, EEO, or LR specialists at your agency and discuss the problem. You might head off a ULP by letting the unions know there's a problem with the way employees are dressing and asking them to work with you on a solution.

Ask FMG

Q. What should I do if my agency doesn't have a dress code policy?

A. If there is no dress code involved, you should not allow unprofessional dress to continue. You should address the issue. You can:

  • • Have a private talk with the employee. Make him aware of the need for tidying up, particularly if the employee lacks social skills.
  • • Be sensitive to personal issues. Personal problems including financial difficulties are better addressed by an employee assistance program counselor.
  • • Understand the potential for medical issues. An employee may raise a medical issue, which will give an opening to request medical documentation and make the employee's physician aware of the situation.

Q. Do I have the authority to discuss etiquette issues with an employee?

A. It may be difficult, even embarrassing, for you to talk to employees about personal appearance issues, especially if poor grooming or bad hygiene are also involved, but you are entitled to do it and you should. You can't let these etiquette issues become accepted. You must be reasonable, and you can always contact HR, EEO, or LR specialists prior to addressing the issue.

Q. What should I do if an employee claims he has a disability that causes him to act a certain way?

A. It is possible that a physical or mental disability is behind the behavior. This is a potential EEO issue. The moment your employee makes you aware of a potential disability, alert HR. You still have a right to expect acceptable behavior, but you need advice on whether a disability is involved or a reasonable accommodation is called for.

Q. Some of my fellow managers and coworkers are going out for a drink and want me to join them. Should I?

A. Be careful here. Make sure that you aren't creating an on-duty employment situation. Also, if you choose to go for camaraderie's sake, do it just for that. Use common sense and don't act in an unprofessional way.
