23. Severity and Frequency of Conduct--Sliding Scale

The requirement that the conduct be sufficiently severe or pervasive to rise to the level of creating a hostile, offensive or abusive work environment is, in essence, another way of stating that the unwelcome conduct must rise to the level of becoming a term or condition of employment. See 42 USC 2000e-2 prohibiting discrimination on the enumerated basis in, among other things, the terms and conditions of employment.

The Commission has articulated several factors it will consider in evaluating whether the conduct is severe or pervasive enough to create a hostile environment. "The Commission's Policy Guidance indicates that the following factors are pertinent to the inquiry: (1) whether the conduct was verbal or physical, or both; (2) how frequently the conduct was repeated; (3) whether the conduct was hostile and patently offensive; (4) whether the alleged harasser was a co-worker or a supervisor; (5) whether others joined in perpetrating the harassment; and (6) whether the harassment was directed at more than one individual. EEOC Policy Guidance, supra, p. 14." Steele v. General Services Administration, 01933171 (1994).

As noted at the outset of this section, the severe or pervasive requirement represents a sliding scale. The more severe the nature of the conduct, the less frequent it needs to be to become a term and condition of employment. The converse is equally true. The less severe the conduct, the more frequent it need to be to become a term and condition of employment. As the Commission explained in Rodriguez v. Office of Personnel Management, 01A52688 (2006):

The agency highlights a few of our cases to argue that complainant's claim is neither sufficiently severe nor pervasive to establish a prima facie case of harassment. The agency attempts to factually distinguish the instant case from cases in which we found a hostile work environment based on isolated incidents so as to show that complainant's claim does not rise to the same level as those other successful claims. The agency further points to our case law to suggest that complainant must establish both the severity and the pervasiveness of the alleged conduct. The agency is mistaken.

We have repeatedly held that the more severe the conduct in question is, the less frequent it need be, and that no one factor alone determines whether particular conduct violates Title VII. See EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment (Mar. 19, 1990) at 8. We analyze each sexual harassment claim on a case by case basis. Applying this standard, therefore, we find that the unwelcome conduct unreasonably interfered with complainant's working conditions and created a hostile work environment. A reasonable trier of fact would not disagree--the pornographic photographs to which S1 subjected complainant were sexual in nature, base and offensive. A reasonable trier of fact would also not disagree that management's conduct was reprehensible, particularly where complainant reported the offensive incidents and S1 was disciplined, yet S1 disregarded the reprimand and continued the inappropriate behavior by bringing to the office and maintaining in plain view an adult magazine which he purportedly shared with male employees. Moreover, we note that subjectively complainant perceived such conduct coming from a supervisor to be wholly offensive and an abuse of authority. Lastly, we note that the record indicates that S1's actions and S2's inaction negatively altered complainant's working conditions. Some of complainant's colleagues subsequently treated her differently, even labeled her a "troublemaker." See Ex. 5-3. Her interactions with supervisors also changed significantly as a result of the alleged conduct. See Affidavit at Ex. 10. Given these particular facts, therefore, we find complainant successfully established a sexual harassment claim.

The Commission explained in Jackson v. Secretary of Air Force, 0720110036 (2012), that the severe or pervasive requirement should be read as requiring conduct that is either severe or pervasive:

Although the Agency argued that the alleged conduct was insufficient, as a matter of law, to establish a claim of hostile work environment harassment, we find that S1's actions were sufficiently pervasive to create an abusive working environment. We emphasize that the relevant question is whether the harassing conduct was "sufficiently severe or pervasive" [emphasis added]. The record reflects that S1 exhibited "a pattern of offensive conduct" towards Complainant and the women in the office over a 19-month period. As described above, testimonial evidence reflects that S1 belittled, publicly humiliated, and scrutinized Complainant and the other women in the office on an "ongoing," "continuous," and an "almost...daily basis." In addition, testimonial evidence reflects that S1's treatment of Complainant and her subordinates interfered with Complainant's performance by undermining her authority as a supervisor. Accordingly, we agree with the AJ that, from a reasonable person's point of view, the harassment had the purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with the work environment and/or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment.
