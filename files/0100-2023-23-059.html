d. Return to Work Requirement

However, in order for the complainant to demonstrate that he or she is a qualified individual with a disability, in addition to showing a nexus between the disability and the absences, the complainant must also show that his or her return to work will be in the foreseeable future. Nieto v. Secretary of Treasury, 03A10032 (2002). In Nieto, the Commission found the complainant was not a qualified individual with a disability because his disability prevented him from working for two years and he "failed to show that at any time in the foreseeable future he could have performed the essential functions of his job, or any other job, with or without reasonable accommodation."

In Jessica E. v. Social Security Administration, 0120150082 (2017), the Commission credited the agency's argument that the complainant was not a qualified individual because she could not work at all. Though not strictly discussed as a "job attendance" issue, the Commission's discussion in Jessica E. is instructive:

[T]he record indicates that Complainant stopped working on December 19, 2011. Complainant admitted in her affidavit that after December 15 th, her doctors did not "release [her] to work at all." Complainant also noted that "[t]he accommodations I had requested were moot because of my inability to work." The record is clear that Complainant was not authorized to return to work during the time she alleged a failure to accommodate. On March 9, 2012, Complainant's requests were denied because she was unable to perform the essential functions of her job since she could not be at work.

She subsequently requested a transfer to one of the Agency's field offices in April 2012. This request was accompanied by a doctor's letter indicating that Complainant could not return to work until June 11, 2012. The Agency denied this request, again stating Complainant was not a qualified individual with a disability. Complainant decided to grieve the denial of her April 2012 reasonable accommodation request through the Agency's negotiated grievance procedure. Complainant returned to work on June 11, 2012, with certain restrictions. Complainant was not assigned any telephone calls, and instead worked with a job mentor doing modified training to accommodate her. Two days later, Complainant became seriously injured while on the job and again was unable to work.

...

Assuming, arguendo, that Complainant established a prima facie case of discrimination based on race, and sex, we concur with the AJ's finding that she is unable to establish the same with respect to her disability claim. Complainant was unable to demonstrate the she is a qualified individual with a disability because with or without an accommodation, she was not capable of performing her duties as a TSR in Auburn or as a Service Representative at the Lynwood or Everett Field Offices. The record reflects that Complainant stopped working on or around December 19, 2011, and was unable to return to work because of her doctor's restrictions until June 11, 2012. In a March 2012 letter denying Complainant's accommodation request, the stated reason was that "[she was] unable to perform the essential functions of [her] job if [she] cannot reliably or predictably arrive to work."

The record supports a finding that Complainant failed to demonstrate that at any time between December 15, 2011 and June 11, 2012, she could perform the essential functions of her position with or without an accommodation. We find, as did the AJ, that regarding her December 2011 accommodation request, Complainant was not a qualified individual with a disability and thus did not establish a prima facie case of discrimination under the Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended, 29 U.S.C. § 791 et seq. This finding coupled with the fact that the decision to deny Complainant's accommodation request was made without regard to her sex or race, precludes a finding that she was subjected to discrimination on either of these bases. The record clearly reflects that Complainant was unable to establish that the Agency's actions were motivated by any discriminatory animus.

That job attendance is not considered an essential function of the position by the Commission does not mean that an agency must tolerate chronic absenteeism, particularly when that absenteeism is without notice. In Wiley v. Postmaster General, 07A20105 (2003), the Commission held:

In the instant case, complainant contends that he requested reasonable accommodation when he verbally asked that his tour of duty start two hours later and when his physician suggested that his disorders require accommodation when an episode occurs. We agree. The record shows that complainant's episodes often caused him to fall into a sleep from which he could not be awakened. The record further shows that complainant was a Mail-handler who could have incapacitating episodes of exhaustion monthly or bi-monthly for periods of three to five days. As a result, complainant typically called in beyond the two hour period he sought as an accommodation and often did not report at all. S1 stated that complainant failed to call in or report to work on August 12, August 15, August 26, August 27, September 9, September 13 and September 14, 1999. Specifically, S1 stated that complainant failed to report for scheduled overtime on three occasions, complainant completely failed to call in on one occasion, and complainant failed to call in until two to seven hours after his tour began on three occasions. The Commission finds that a two hour schedule change would not have proved effective in assisting complainant in complying with agency policy regarding absences. The record suggests that the only accommodation would have been to allow complainant to call in or report to work whenever he felt able. However, such an accommodation is not reasonable on its face. Id. It is not "plausible" or "feasible" for an employer to excuse chronic erratic absenteeism and tardiness by an employee who cannot provide timely notice sufficient to enable the employer to ensure adequate staffing. See id. Thus, the Commission concludes that complainant did not establish that the agency failed to provide a reasonable accommodation for his disability.

The Commission, however, takes a different position on job attendance when the disability claimed is alcoholism. In Bumgardner v. Secretary of Defense, 03970047 (1997), the Commission found that the complainant's alcoholism did not bar the agency from enforcing its attendance standards, provided it did so in a nondiscriminatory manner:

The Commission further finds, however, that the agency properly dismissed petitioner from his position because he was AWOL. The record shows that at the time of his dismissal, petitioner had been absent from the work place for almost four months and that he was going to be absent for a considerable time into the future. Petitioner's status as an individual with the disability of alcoholism does not bar the agency from holding him to the same job-related work place standards that it applies to all other employees. One work place requirement is that an employee report to work as scheduled. Petitioner was unable to meet this requirement. Although petitioner attempted to prove that the agency was holding him to a different standard than it applied to other employees, we find nothing in this record to support his contention that the agency did not dismiss non-disabled employees who were absent without leave for an extended period.

The Commission's decision in Bumgardner is consistent with the ADA, which permits agencies to hold employees who engage in alcohol abuse to the same conduct and performance standards as nondisabled employees. See 42 USC 12114(c)(4).

In a decision short on rationale, the Commission held that attendance was job-related and consistent with business necessity in considering a promotion to senior customs inspector. In Penney v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120071249 (2009), the Commission upheld the findings of its administrative judge that the agency legitimately considered the attendance record of the complainant in failing to promote her.
