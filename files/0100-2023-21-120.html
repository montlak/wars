J. DISCIPLINE OF RMO

Despite the constant recitation of general language to the contrary, it is clear that, more and more, the Commission views complaints of sexual harassment to be different from other Title VII cases. Recently, this has included an increased scrutiny of discipline for the offending agency official.

Consistent with the policy that Title VII is meant to be remedial and not punitive, the Commission held that it will not order or recommend discipline for RMO's involved in sexual harassment. However, the Commission also held it will examine the agency's actions regarding relief to ensure that it has taken proper action to prevent future sexual harassment. See Barton-Hernandez v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01882749 (1989). Further, if disciplinary action is taken, the Commission stated that it would not comment on its adequacy, nor disclose the nature of such discipline to the complainants.

In Anders v. Postmaster General, 01970893 (2000), however, the Commission did fault the agency for giving the supervisor who committed the harassment only "limited discipline" after an internal administrative complaint of harassment.

In Barton-Hernandez, supra, the Commission also found that republication of an agency policy against sexual harassment was not an adequate corrective measure. The Commission ordered the posting of a notice of violation because "[i]t serves to 'dispel the chilling effect' sexual harassment has on the exercise of employees' rights, 'assures employees that the status quo has changed,' and reminds them of their right to seek relief for similar or other violations of statutes administered by the Commission." Id.

However, in Velez v. Postmaster General, 01902746 (1990), the Commission departed from the Barton-Hernandez case and examined the agency's failure to give "due consideration" of the recommendation of the administrative judge to terminate the employment of the offending employee, or at least to impose some form of discipline:

The EEOC Policy Guidance statement on sexual harassment indicates that in fulfilling the agency's duty to take immediate and appropriate corrective action: "Disciplinary action against the offending Supervisor or employee, ranging from reprimand to discharge, may be necessary" [emphasis in original]. Id. p. 29.

In addition, EEOC Regulations at 29 CFR 1613.221(c)(3), state that when an agency finds discrimination it should consider whether disciplinary action is appropriate. Moreover, the Commission's Remedies Policy, Appendix A to 29 CFR Part 1613--Statement on Remedies and Relief for Individual Cases of Unlawful Discrimination fn. 7, provides that corrective action may be accomplished through discipline of the offending official. See also James A. Long v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05860021 (December 19, 1986).

Thus, we find that the agency must, at the least, fully consider appropriate disciplinary action against the offending supervisor and indicate to appellant and to the Commission, its basis for deciding to take or not take such action....

The Commission did not indicate what, if any, action it would take if it found an agency's determination not to take disciplinary action was inappropriate.

Although a complainant is not entitled to request, and the Commission may not order, disciplinary action against an offending employee, a cause of action is stated under Title VII when the complainant alleges that no disciplinary action was taken against the offender. In Steele v. Postmaster General, 01922864 (1992), the complainant alleged she was aggrieved by the agency's failure to take action against two male coworkers who engaged in sexual harassment. The Commission agreed in Steele, writing:

Here, the agency maintains that appellant has not been harmed by the Postmaster's alleged discriminatory lack of remedial action against the two carriers. We disagree.

An agency's liability in situations of alleged coworker harassment arises once it has been put on notice. Whether the agency took appropriate actions to discipline the alleged wrongdoers once it had appropriate notice from appellant states a valid claim of discrimination, and the agency's liability in this case depends on whether it acted appropriately in handling the situation. Notably, appellant states on appeal that the alleged lack of disciplinary action has resulted in further harassment against her.

The Commission held that it has the authority to order the transfer of a sexual harasser as part of the remedy to a complainant. See DePaul v. Postmaster General, 01912729 (1992). In the alternative, the agency may have the option of offering the victim a transfer. However, the Commission has made it clear that such a transfer must be entirely voluntary on the part of the victim. See also Taylor v. Secretary of Air Force, 01924488 (1993) (it is inappropriate for an agency to involuntarily reassign a victim of alleged sexual harassment). In one case, as part of the remedy, the Commission ordered the agency to offer the complainant an optional transfer even if the offender had already been relieved of supervisory duties over the complainant. See Mangano v. Attorney General, 01971055 (1999).

In Jones v. Postmaster General, 01952445 (1997), the Commission muddied the waters when it observed that the harassment in that case was not severe enough to warrant the removal of the supervisor. In ordering the agency to give the complainant the option of transferring, the Commission in Jones wrote:

As relief, we find that the agency should afford appellant the option of moving into a position and/or onto a tour where she will not have any interaction with the RO. Although the relief requested by appellant was to have the RO terminated, we find that such relief is not appropriate in light of the facts of this case. Specifically, the Policy Guidance states that disciplinary action against the offending supervisor, including removal, may be appropriate, but depends on the severity of the conduct. In this case, although the conduct in question was sufficient to establish a violation of Title VII, we are not persuaded that it rose to such a level as to warrant the removal of the RO. See Waltman v. International Paper Co., 875 F.2d 468, 479 (5th Cir. 1989) (appropriateness of remedial action will depend on the severity and persistence of the harassment and the effectiveness of any initial remedial steps).

Presumably, the Commission in Jones did not mean to suggest that it could order the removal of the supervisor but this is unclear from the language in Jones.

Although the Commission may not directly order discipline, it can certainly order the agency to consider whether discipline is appropriate. In Ayers v. Secretary of Treasury, 01970359 (2000), supra, the Commission issued the following order to the agency:

The agency is to review the matter giving rise to this complaint, and designate a high-level official outside the division(s) where CW1, S1, S2, S3, S4 or TL are employed. This high-level official shall conduct an inquiry and determine if disciplinary action against CW1 is appropriate. The agency shall record the nature of any disciplinary action taken, the basis for the decision to take or not to take such actions, and report same to the Commission in the same manner as implementation of the rest of the Commission decision is reported.

The Commission in Turner v. Secretary of Interior, 01956390 (1998), found that the agency improperly failed to consider disciplining a harasser. The agency voiced concerns that disciplining the harasser, who was an alcoholic, might be a violation of the Rehabilitation Act. The Commission rejected this argument in Turner, and ordered the agency to provide training to the harasser and to consider disciplining him:

On this point, we find that appellant has correctly pointed out the fallacy in the agency's reliance on her harasser's alcoholism and his potential coverage under the Rehabilitation Act as a basis for refusing to consider disciplinary action against him for his conduct herein. An employer may hold an employee who is an alcoholic to the same standards of job performance and conduct as other employees, even if any unsatisfactory job performance or conduct is related to the employee's alcoholism. See Johnson v. Department of the Interior, EEOC Petition No. 03940100 (March 28, 1996). Thus, the agency cannot use its responsibilities under the Rehabilitation Act to provide reasonable accommodation to a qualified individual with a disability as a shield to avoid considering appropriate disciplinary action against the appellant's former supervisor for his harassing conduct. Therefore, the agency should consider taking disciplinary action against the supervisor. The AJ also correctly recommended that the agency require the harasser to attend focused training concerning his responsibilities under Title VII to avoid such conduct in the future. Agency managers at the facility where the harassment occurred shall also be trained regarding their responsibilities under Title VII to investigate and take prompt and effective remedial action when sexual harassment occurs. We also order the agency to set up a procedure to monitor and make sure that sexual harassment such as that encountered herein does not recur in the work environment where appellant's former supervisor is now employed.

When the Commission orders training as a remedial measure, it may tailor an order to ensure the training targets the desired audience. For example, in Salazar v. Attorney General, 05980287 (1998), the Commission altered its original order for the agency to provide sexual harassment training to a particular office. By the time of its order, many of the employees at that office had been transferred or reassigned to other agency offices. The Commission ordered training for the particular office at which the discrimination occurred, as well as training for those individuals who were at the office at the time of the discrimination but had since moved to other agency offices.

The Commission, apparently, has tired of agency excuses that it cannot control the behavior of its supervisors. In the Commission's view, it is the responsibility of an agency to ensure that its supervisors behave in accordance with Title VII. In Everhart v. Secretary of Commerce, 01960123 (1998), the Commission reminded the agency that it needed to fulfill its Title VII duties, despite a finding of no discrimination in the case:

Although we affirm the FAD, we are concerned about the agency's indication of helplessness in dealing with the admittedly improper supervisory conduct. The FAD notes that B.H. is a "very coarse individual" who uses profane language, tells crude jokes, and habitually addresses women employees in an offensive manner. Even though the evidence does not support a finding of racial harassment in the appellant's case, the agency states that it does not condone B.H.'s improper conduct because "[c]rude and unprofessional remarks cannot be tolerated in the workplace." The FAD acknowledges that "Title VII imposes upon an employer, the affirmative obligation to maintain a working environment free from racial harassment and intimidation and that often prompt, yet reasonable, action must be taken by the agency in order to prevent such an atmosphere from occurring." The FAD "strongly advises the agency to arrange appropriate EEO training for [B.H.]." However, the FAD then notes that the evidence suggests that B.H. was "not responsive to EEO training in which he was enrolled as the result' of a 1993 management inquiry."

We remind the agency that our regulations at 29 CFR 1614.102 require an agency to, inter alia, "conduct a continuing campaign to eradicate every form of prejudice or discrimination from the agency's personnel policies, practices and working conditions" and to "review, evaluate, and control managerial and supervisory performance in such a manner as to insure...their understanding and implementation of the equal employment opportunity policy and program." In addition to "orientation, training, and advice," our regulation also requires the agency to "take appropriate disciplinary action against employees who engage in discriminatory practices." 29 CFR 1614.102(a) (6). Although an agency is not required to take a disciplinary action against a responsible management official in order to make whole a victim of discrimination, we remind the agency that it is not without power or responsibility to deal with improper supervisory conduct by taking appropriate disciplinary action, especially where, as here, the management official has admittedly failed to respond to previous EEO training.

In one case, the EEOC noted that it was troubled by the fact that the recommending official considered an allegation that the complainant had engaged in sexual harassment where no complaint was filed and the complainant was never given an opportunity to rebut the allegation. Yet the Commission gave "some weight to the recommending official's testimony that the colleague that confided to him about appellant sexually harassing her was a woman of integrity." Cole v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 01922553 (1993).
