CONSTRUCTIVE PERSONNEL ACTIONS

"Constructive personnel action" is a term used for an adverse action taken without the due process required by law and regulation. It may result from an agency's mistake in not providing due process when it is required. But, more commonly, an employee will claim that an agency's coercive actions have forced him to elect a purportedly voluntary action.

Constructive personnel actions can include removals, demotions, and suspensions, and can occasionally involve involuntary resignations and retirements.

So what does this mean to you? It means you need to think about the repercussions of your decisions to reassign and otherwise make decisions on behalf of your staff member's employment status. Remedies for such action taken against employees include back pay or reinstatement, and for you, possible administrative action, up to and including discharge.

This is a very complex area of the law. Consult with your HR and EEO specialists to get a better understanding of these concepts.

Constructive demotions

The Merit Systems Protection Board hears employee appeals of certain personnel actions. The MSPB developed the concept of "constructive demotion" to deal with a specific situation arising from an employee's reassignment.

Normally, the MSPB does not hear cases involving employees who are reassigned without a loss in grade or pay. However, there is a group of cases that is a bit trickier than that. Let's say you reassign an employee without changing his grade or pay and his former position is reclassified to a higher grade. You have effectively taken away the promotion he would have received. The MSPB considers that to be a "constructive demotion."

The MSPB developed a standard for evaluating an agency's actions in these types of cases. In order to establish a constructive demotion claim, the employee must demonstrate that:

  1. 1. He was reassigned from a position which, due to the issuance of a new classification standard or correction of classification error, is entitled to a higher grade; and
  2. 2. He meets the legal and qualification requirements for promotion to the higher grade.

Note: If the employee is reassigned and then management adds duties and responsibilities to his old position, making it essentially a new position, the employee will not have a claim. In other words, if the duties of the new position include the duties of the former position, augmented with additional duties, the employee has not demonstrated a constructive adverse action.

Also, the reassignment must be involuntary to support a claim of constructive demotion. If the employee volunteers to be reassigned to a new position, and his former position is changed, he will not have a claim.

Remedy. If the employee prevails in a constructive demotion appeal, the remedy includes back pay running from the date of the constructive demotion as well as the cancellation of the reassignment, i.e., the employee is placed back in the position held prior to the improper reassignment.

Constructive suspensions

The MSPB has recognized that constructive suspension claims may arise in two situations:

  1. 1. When an agency places an employee on enforced leave pending an inquiry into his ability to perform; or
  2. 2. When an employee who is absent from work for medical reasons asks to return to work with altered duties, and the agency denies the request.

To establish a constructive suspension, the employee would need to show that she contacted an agency official with decision-making authority to communicate unequivocally her availability and her desire to return to duty.

Note: If a placement on enforced leave lasts for more than 14 days, the MSPB will analyze the action like any other suspension of more than 14 days and not as a constructive suspension.

This also comes up in the workers' compensation realm when employees seek altered duties when returning to work after an injury. Again, this is a very complex area. Contact your HR, EEO, and workers' compensation staff before making any determinations on your employee's status.

Like other constructive actions, a constructive suspension has an involuntary aspect. However, it does not involve a loss of promotion opportunities like the constructive demotion claim.

Generally speaking, an employee cannot appeal a voluntary absence from work to the Merit Systems Protection Board.

Remedy. Similar to a constructive demotion, if the employee prevails in a constructive suspension claim, the remedy includes back pay running from the date of the constructive suspension.

Constructive removal or discharge

A constructive removal or discharge occurs when an employer, through its unlawful discriminatory behavior, makes an employee's working conditions so difficult that any reasonable person in the employee's position would feel compelled to resign or accept an early retirement.

Forcing an employee to quit in this manner is not the proper way to deal with a personnel problem. This can be very costly for you and your agency.

There are essentially three elements that are necessary to establish a constructive discharge or removal claim:

  1. 1. A reasonable person in the employee's position would find the working conditions intolerable.
  2. 2. The agency's conduct amounted to a personnel violation.
  3. 3. The employee's resignation was caused by the intolerable working conditions.

If the employee alleges that the constructive removal is a result of discrimination, harassment, or retaliation, he must initiate contact with an EEO counselor within 45 days of the matter. The U.S. Supreme Court has held that the 45-day period for a constructive discharge claim begins running after the employee gives notice of his resignation, not on the effective date of that resignation.

Remedy. Based on MSPB rulings, the most common and preferred remedy for a constructive removal is reinstatement. The reinstated employee likely would be placed in a different position with a different manager. That is not only costly to the agency, but the manager may be investigated by the Office of Special Counsel with actions up to and including removal for violating an employee's due process rights.

Involuntary resignations and retirements

An employee's voluntary action, such as a resignation, is not generally appealable. Resignations and retirements are presumed to be voluntary, and the employee bears the burden of proving otherwise. However, an involuntary resignation or retirement is equivalent to a forced or constructive removal and is a matter within the MSPB's jurisdiction.

To overcome the presumption that his resignation was voluntary, the employee must show that it was the result of the agency's misinformation or deception or he was coerced by the agency. That means the decision was made by the employee "with blinders on" based on misinformation provided by the manager or agency.

There is no requirement that the employee be intentionally deceived, provided that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency's statements. That is, the agency could have provided the misleading information negligently or even innocently; if the employee materially relied on the misinformation to her detriment, her retirement is considered involuntary.

What can you do to avoid these situations? You must provide information that is not only correct in nature but adequate in scope to allow an employee to make an informed decision regarding his retirement. This includes an obligation to correct any erroneous information that you believe an employee is relying on.

To establish involuntariness on the basis of coercion, an employee must show that:

  1. 1. The agency effectively imposed the terms of the employee's resignation or retirement.
  2. 2. The employee had no realistic alternative but to resign or retire; and
  3. 3. The employee's resignation or retirement was the result of improper acts by the agency.

The three-part coercion test is objective, and a court must consider the totality of the circumstances to establish that a reasonable employee confronted with the same circumstances would feel coerced into resigning. The employee must show she was effectively deprived of free choice in the matter and that there was no other option but to involuntarily resign.

A court will also consider any evidence of discrimination or retaliation together with all other evidence bearing on the voluntariness of the resignation, but it does not need to determine whether discrimination or retaliation has been established under the standards of Title VII or the Whistleblower Protection Act, unless and until it is established that the appellant was constructively discharged.

Notice of appeal rights. An employee who applies for retirement is generally not entitled to a notice of appeal rights from the action. This is because a retirement is an employee-initiated action that is presumed to be voluntary and not appealable to the MSPB.

However, the employee would be entitled to notice of their appeal rights if they have placed the agency on notice that, contrary to the presumption, they considered their decision to retire to have been involuntary, or the circumstances show that the agency was, or reasonably should have been, aware of facts indicating that the retirement was involuntary.

Example: For instance, if an employee tells their manager or the HR office that they are retiring because they were being forced to take a COVID-19 vaccine, the MSPB will almost certainly find that the agency was put on notice that the employee considered their retirement to be involuntary. This notice from the employee would trigger a duty on the agency's part to provide the employee with MSPB appeal rights. If the agency fails to inform the employee of their appeal rights, and the employee files an untimely appeal with the MSPB alleging that their retirement was involuntary, the filing deadline would likely be waived and the employee would be able to proceed with their appeal.

Remedy. If an employee shows she was coerced or forced into an involuntary resignation or retirement situation, the MSPB will consider it an adverse action and can offer up several forms of relief for the employee, including pay, damages, and reinstatement.

Mistakes to avoid

Constructive personnel actions are a very complex area of the law and require you to work with your HR staff to prevent a costly mistake. With that in mind, here are some don'ts you should consider:

Do not:

  • • Let things get to a bad point. If you are having issues with an employee, don't seek to make his work life miserable. Be an effective manager and deal with situations as they arise.
  • • Reassign blindly. You may think you are solving problems by reassigning employees to other positions, but you might be setting yourself up for a disastrous situation.
  • • Lie or misinform your employees. In order for an employee to show he was "forced" to do something he didn't want to do, he can show you provided him with misinformation with the intent to deceive him. Keep it honest with your employees.
  • • Assume HR will clean up your mess. It's on you as manager to do the right thing. Don't consult with HR only after you've found yourself in a tight spot. Be proactive and take care of your end of the bargain before things get away from you.

Q. What must an employee show to prove a constructive demotion claim?

A. In order to establish a constructive demotion claim, the employee must demonstrate that:

  1. 1. The employee was reassigned from a position which, due to the issuance of a new classification standard or correction of classification error, is entitled to a higher grade; and
  2. 2. The employee meets the legal and qualification requirements for promotion to the higher grade.

Q. What remedy is available for an employee who was constructively demoted from his position?

A. Remedies for employees subjected to constructive demotions include back pay running from the date of the constructive demotion and cancellation of the reassignment. This can prove quite costly for an agency.

Q. What is a constructive suspension?

A. A constructive suspension involves a situation where an employee appears to use leave voluntarily, but the use was actually involuntary. Constructive suspension claims typically arise in two situations: 1) when an agency places an employee on enforced leave pending an inquiry into his ability to perform; or 2) when an employee who is absent from work for medical reasons asks to return to work with altered duties, and the agency denies the request. This action is appealable to the Merit Systems Protection Board.

A constructive removal or discharge occurs when an employer, through its unlawful discriminatory behavior, makes an employee's working conditions so difficult that any reasonable person in the employee's position would feel compelled to resign or accept an early retirement. This is completely avoidable and completely inappropriate.
