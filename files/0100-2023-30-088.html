18. Systemic Relief

Even where the administrative judge issues a finding of no discrimination, he or she has the authority to order an agency to take actions to prevent future violations of the Commission's regulations. The precise contours of this authority are not certain.

In Toombs v. Attorney General, 07A20064 (2003), the Commission upheld an order by the administrative judge requiring the agency to develop a policy regarding the release of oriGINAl audio tapes where the EEO investigator inadvertently threw away tapes obtained during his investigation. In upholding the order in Toombs, the Commission found:

Following a hearing, the AJ found that complainant established a prima facie case of age discrimination. The AJ also found that the agency articulated a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions by stating that a career board panel recommended the individual whom it believed was most qualified based on an analysis of the applicant's interview and analysis of the Knowledge, Skills and Abilities (KSA's) for the position. The AJ concluded that complainant failed to establish that more likely than not, the reasons provided by the agency were a pretext for discrimination. In reaching this conclusion, the AJ found that complainant's only argument consisted of his belief that the agency failed to follow correct selection process procedures, that he was more qualified than the selectee, and that the selectee was preselected when he was detailed into the position.

The AJ found, however, little evidence that would support complainant's assertions. Rather, the evidence revealed that the selectee performed better than complainant did during the interview. Assuming that the agency did violate merit promotion procedures, the AJ found insufficient evidence that this was done because of an anti-age animus. Indeed, the AJ found that the agency's witnesses were credible, in that nothing in their demeanor suggested that they were evading questions, or otherwise appeared excessively nervous. Finally, the AJ found that complainant was not discriminated against, as alleged.

However, the AJ did note that on September 12, 2001, he issued a Notice to Show Cause why sanctions should not be imposed after learning that audio tapes of the interviews and panel deliberations were purportedly discarded by the agency's investigator. In response, the agency informed the AJ that sanctions were not appropriate in this instance, since there was no prejudice to complainant, and the preponderance of the evidence revealed that the tapes were disposed of by accident. In response, complainant argued that the agency's response was not credible. Specifically, complainant stated that the agency's practice was to provide a copy of the tape to the investigator, and for the investigator to make a copy, and return the oriGINAl copy back to the agency. However, this practice was not followed in this case.

The AJ in the case stated that the agency's investigator testified at the hearing regarding the missing tapes. The investigator testified that he saw no reason why he would not have followed his normal practice as it pertained to the tapes, but that he was not entirely sure because of his large case load at the time.

After examining the investigator's testimony, the AJ determined that there was no evidence that the tapes were lost due to anything other than careless error. In that regard, the AJ noted that the agency's acting EEO Officer made a declaration wherein she commented that she suggested that the agency adopt a policy that prohibited the release of oriGINAl audio tapes. To that end, the AJ ordered that the agency establish a policy concerning the release of oriGINAl audio tapes in order to ensure that no future incidents such as this recur. The AJ relied on 29 C.F.R. 1614.109(f)(3)(v) as support for the order.

The agency's final order rejected the AJ's decision. On appeal, the agency argues that the AJ erred by issuing the order because there was no evidence that the loss of audio tapes was anything other than a one-time occurrence, as opposed to a systemic problem. Furthermore, the agency maintains that the Investigator retired from the agency, suggesting that other incidents such as this would not reoccur. Forcing the agency to adopt an agency-wide policy was "extreme" and "too demanding" under the circumstances, according to the agency.

Pursuant to 29 C.F.R. 1614.405(a), all post-hearing factual findings by an AJ will be upheld if supported by substantial evidence in the record. Substantial evidence is defined as "such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion." Universal Camera Corp. v. National Labor Relations Board, 340 U.S. 474, 477 (1951) (citation omitted). A finding regarding whether or not discriminatory intent existed is a factual finding. See Pullman-Standard Co. v. Swint, 456 U.S. 273, 293 (1982). An AJ's conclusions of law are subject to a de novo standard of review, whether or not a hearing was held.

After a careful review of the record, we discern no basis to disturb the AJ's finding of no discrimination. The findings of fact are supported by substantial evidence, and the AJ correctly applied the appropriate regulations, policies, and laws.

As for the AJ's order, we find it to be appropriate under the circumstances presented in this case. In this regard, despite the absence of bad faith, the record reveals confusion as to what actually happened to the audio tape in question, and it is doubtful that the agency's normal business practice was followed, since the tape was lost. Indeed, a policy restricting the release of oriGINAl audio tapes should ensure that similar incidents such as that described herein will not reoccur. EEOC regulations require that any personnel or employment record made or kept by an employer be preserved by the employer for a period of one year from the date of the making of the record or the personnel action involved, whichever occurs later. 29 C.F.R. 1602.14. Where a charge of discrimination has been filed, the agency is required to preserve all personnel records relevant to the charge until final disposition of the charge. Id. The AJ's order ensures compliance with this regulation as it pertains to audio tapes, and is not unduly harsh so as to result in an inequity.

In Toombs v. Attorney General, 05A40064 (2003), the Commission denied the agency's request for reconsideration.
