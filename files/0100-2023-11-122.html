IX. EEO COUNSELOR'S REPORT

The EEO counselor is required to keep a record of his or her counseling activities and file a report concerning precomplaint counseling within 15 days of being advised that a formal complaint has been filed. 29 CFR 1614.105(c). The report must reflect the issues discussed and the actions taken during counseling. The failure to file such a report is not harmful error when, on the basis of a hearing, the record has been adequately established. Thompson v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01831428 (1983).

In the first instance when a formal complaint is filed, the agency should refer to the counselor's report to determine what claims are presented by the complaint and whether the claims require further clarification. See In re Gilmore, 05870505 (1988). In that case, the Commission held that it was error for the agency to cancel the complaint for failure to prosecute where no counselor's report was filed and the agency insisted that the complainant clarify the allegations in the complaint.

It is not necessary that the complainant bring "each and every reason" he believes he is aggrieved to the attention of the counselor in order to comply with the time limit for initiating counseling, "as long as the event itself was timely submitted." Martin v. Department of Transportation, 01831709 (1983) (the appellant initiated precomplaint counseling over a termination, but did not allege that similarly situated black females had not been terminated until the formal complaint stage).

Since the complaint process is designed for use by laypersons without the assistance of an attorney, the agency should refer to the counselor's report, if necessary, in construing the allegations in the formal complaint. Drayton v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 05910980 (1991). In that case, the Commission refused to find that the failure of the complainant to mention the issue of reasonable accommodation in his formal complaint was dispositive of the scope of issues raised by his complaint where the counselor's report clearly demonstrated his concerns about lack of accommodation and listed his disabled status as a basis of discrimination on the complaint form.

When there is a discrepancy between the EEO counselor's report and the formal complaint filed by the employee, the EEOC will examine the surrounding facts to determine the precise nature of the complaint. In Spears v. USPS, 01831865 (1983), the counselor's report determined that the employee's allegation of nonselection was untimely since it had occurred some five months before contact with the counselor. A review of the complaint revealed that the appellant had been kept on a selection certificate for several months following his initial nonselection and it was the agency's failure to interview him for a subsequent vacancy that was the issue of the complaint. The EEOC remanded the case to the counselor for processing of the matters timely submitted.

In the past, there were no clear requirements placed on EEO counselors for the manner in which they conducted precomplaint counseling. The counselor was required to make a "minimally adequate inquiry" into the basis of the alleged discrimination. Where the employee complained that he was not given a light duty assignment while an employee with a surname indicating a different nationality was granted such an assignment, the counselor failed to provide minimum assistance by not counseling the employee on discrimination on the basis of national origin. Olszewski v. USPS, 01812103, (1982). The Commission attempted to cure the lack of guidance to EEO counselors through MD-110.

The requirements of the EEO counseling report are explained in MD-110, at 2-19 to 2-20:

When advised that an aggrieved person has filed a formal complaint, the EEO Counselor will submit a written report pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.105(c). The report will contain relevant information about the aggrieved person, jurisdiction, claims, bases, requested remedy, and the EEO Counselor's checklist, as specified in the sample EEO Counselor's Report. See Appendix H of this Management Directive. If the aggrieved person attempted to resolve the dispute via counseling or EEO ADR, the report should state that the aggrieved person chose either traditional EEO counseling or the EEO ADR program and that the dispute was not resolved through either procedure. However, the report should not provide a summary of the resolution attempts, nor any opinion as to whether discrimination occurred.

Employees are within their rights to keep their own records on precomplaint counseling, In re Veterans Administration, 05870246 (1987), and are well advised to do so. In the absence of adequate recordkeeping by the counselor, the Commission will look to records kept by the complainant to determine what issues were brought to the counselor's attention.
