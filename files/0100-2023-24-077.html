G. CAREER DEVELOPMENT

Agencies are obligated to provide reasonable accommodation to qualifying individuals in the job application process, to aid in the "performance of the essential functions of the job" and to "enjoy equal benefits and privileges of employment as are enjoyed by [ ] other similarly situated employees without disabilities." 29 CFR 1630.2(o)(1). Agency efforts beyond those requirements are not legally required under the ADA.

In Arthur J. v. Secretary of the Air Force, 2021002720 (2022), for instance, the complainant was diagnosed with encephalomalacia, which can cause certain cognitive issues such as slowed thinking and poor memory. The complainant requested accommodation in the form of "enrichment training, technical and interpersonal coaching, mentoring, career counseling, as well as assistance with references and networking." The Commission in Arthur J. found that career development is "aspirational" and while "highly encouraged," not a reasonable accommodation:

As a teenager, Complainant was diagnosed with chronic encephalomalacia, very mild right hemiparesis and sensory deficits, decreased visual acuity, and learning disabilities. Since the 2001 medical diagnoses, the record contains a Neuropsychological Evaluation done in June 2012. Consistent with the earlier report, the 2012 evaluation determined that Complainant has a medical history for left temporal and inferior left occipital lobe infarct that occurred in utero. The evaluation also stated that Complainant reports doing well from a cognitive perspective. He acknowledged slowed thinking and poor memory for names, and stated it takes him longer to learn new tasks. The record shows, and Complainant stated, that he has successfully performed his job with the Agency for approximately 10 years and was able to perform all the functions of his position.

It appears, however, that Complainant was interested in career development and alleges that the Agency refused to acknowledge receipt of his reasonable accommodation forms and denied his requests for reasonable accommodation in the form of enrichment training, technical and interpersonal coaching, mentoring, career counseling, as well as assistance with references and networking.

The AJ concluded the Agency was not obligated under the Rehabilitation Act to provide Complainant with the requested career enrichment and development assistance because these accommodations were not related to his ability to successfully perform the essential functions of his current job. On the other hand, Complainant's supervisor explained a number of other accommodations Complainant was being provided that assisted him in the performance of his job, including the creation of special calendar and providing him with careful instructions. Both the supervisor and Complainant seem in agreement that he has been successfully performing the essential functions of his position for years.

The Commission's regulations at 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(1) provide that, under the Rehabilitation Act, "reasonable accommodation" is defined as:

- Modifications or adjustments to a job application process that enable a qualified applicant with a disability to be considered for the position such qualified applicant desires.

- Modifications or adjustments to the work environment, or to the manner or circumstances under which the position held or desired is customarily performed, that enable an individual with a disability, who is qualified, to perform the essential functions of that position.

- Modifications or adjustments that enable a covered entity's employee with a disability to enjoy equal benefits and privileges of employment as are enjoyed by its other similarly situated employees without disabilities.

Here, we agree with the AJ's conclusion that Complainant's accommodation requests at issue do not related to any of these three definitional circumstances. First, while Complainant is interested in career development, he has not asked for an accommodation that directly relates to an actual job application process. Second, the accommodations requested are not related to Complainant's performance of the essential functions of his current job or any other specific job he is seeking. Finally, Complainant has not produced any evidence that the "accommodations" he is requesting are being provided to any other similarly situated employees without disabilities.

As determined by the AJ, Complainant appears to conflate what is required as a reasonable accommodation under the Rehabilitation Act with the aspirational goals of federal affirmative action for individuals with disabilities. As already noted, Complainant is entitled to reasonable accommodation in the job application process, to perform the essential functions of his job, and to secure benefits and privileges of employment equal to those enjoyed by employees without disabilities. Efforts that go beyond these three areas and target career development may be highly encouraged, but they are not legally enforceable under the EEOC's federal sector regulations if comparable measures are not provided to non-disabled employees. Accordingly, we affirm the AJ's conclusion that the Agency did not deny Complainant reasonable accommodation in violation of the Rehabilitation Act.
