1. Prima Facie Case

Because the class action complaint, due to its very nature, alleges a concerted effort on the part of the agency to discriminate systematically against a certain group of persons, the factual allegations for a prima facie case of disparate treatment in class actions are significantly greater than those required in an individual complaint. In Dennis v. Department of Navy, 01840275 (1986), the Commission noted:

In order to establish a prima facie case of class disparate treatment discrimination the class must show that the employer regularly and purposefully treated protected class members less favorably than the majority group members. Teamsters v. U.S., 431 U.S. 324 (1977). The ultimate factual issues are "whether the differences were 'racially premised.'" Teamsters v. U.S., 431 U.S. at 335 citing, McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 805 n.18 (1973). Plaintiff must prove more than isolated, accidental or sporadic acts of discrimination....

Bare assertions of a difference in the treatment of classes will not suffice to establish a prima facie case. The class must produce "anecdotal evidence" tending to show a pattern of disparate treatment. In Dennis, the complainants were unable to produce any "anecdotes" of disparate treatment. The Commission did acknowledge that, in the alternative, the class can produce statistical data of disparate treatment and such evidence may, in some cases, be sufficient to demonstrate a prima facie case. The Commission found that many of the comparative data groups in Dennis were too small in size to be of statistical significance and, in any event, tended to disprove disparate treatment.
