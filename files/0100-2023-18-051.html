D. TECHNICAL REPRESENTATIVES

The Commission's official time regulations apply only to complainants, their representatives, witnesses and other persons whom the administrative judge specifically requests attend the hearing. Although the complainant is entitled to a technical representative if he or she so chooses, the technical representative is not entitled to official time. As the Commission explained in Mork v. Postmaster General, 01954909 (1997):

The AJ found that appellant was not entitled to administrative leave because, as a technical advisor, he was not in the capacity of a complainant or representative as contemplated by the cited provisions. The agency adopted the AJ's decision as the agency's final agency decision (FAD). Appellant appealed this decision to the Commission.

The appellate decision in Lee D. Mork v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01941045 (June 1, 1994) remanded the complaint to the agency for a supplemental investigation. Specifically, the decision found that it was appropriate to apply the Part 1613 regulations and Management Directive (MD)-107 to the subject situation since that was the authority in force at the time the agency refused to grant appellant's request for administrative leave. The Commission further found that the matter was best analyzed by determining, pursuant to the aforementioned authority, whether appellant had the right to official time for his service as a "technical advisor" in another employee's EEO hearing. The decision noted that 29 CFR § 1613.218(f) of the Commission's regulations provided:

The Administrative Judge shall request any agency subject to this subpart to make available as a witness at the hearing an employee requested by the complainant when the Administrative Judge determines that the appearance of an employee is necessary. The Administrative Judge may also request the appearance of an employee of any federal agency whose testimony he determines is necessary to furnish information pertinent to the complaint under consideration. . . . An employee of an agency shall be in a duty status during the time he/she is made available as a witness.

The decision also noted that EEOC Management Directive 107, Chapter 6, Section 5.3(g) (Sept. 1, 1987) stated:

"Witnesses. . .must be on official time, regardless of their normal tour of duty, during the period of time they are made available to testify."

The appellate decision found that it was unclear from the record whether appellant's status as a "technical advisor" in the present case was the same as a "witness" or whether the AJ had requested appellant's appearance at the hearing pursuant to 29 CFR § 1613.218(f). It noted that the Commission had held in interpreting Part 1613 regulations, that EEOC Regulations did not provide for the granting of official time for an advisor to a representative, citing Givens v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01822106 (September 7, 1983). Thus, the Commission remanded the case to the agency to determine whether appellant was a "witness" at the coworker's hearing and whether appellant's appearance was requested by the AJ at that prior hearing.

The agency conducted a supplemental investigation and subsequently referred the matter again to the same EEOC AJ for a hearing. The AJ again issued a decision pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.109(e)(3) which found that appellant had not been subjected to reprisal discrimination. The AJ once again found that the agency had articulated a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for the denial of the request that appellant had not proven to be pretextual; that the agency would not approve administrative leave for appellant's attendance at a coworker's hearing because he was not present in the capacity of a complainant, a witness or a representative at the hearing. The AJ noted that, as adduced during the two investigations and through an affidavit submitted to the complainant by the AJ, the coworker had requested appellant's presence as a technical advisor only at his EEO hearing without prior notice only after the agency representative arrived at the hearing with such an advisor. The AJ noted that the Commission's regulations do not provide for official time for such technical advisors. Thus, the AJ found no reprisal discrimination. Thereafter, the agency issued a second FAD adopting the AJ's decision. It is this decision from which appellant now appeals. Appellant raises no specific contentions on appeal.

After a careful review of the record, the Commission finds that the AJ's recommended decision correctly summarized the relevant facts and referenced the appropriate regulations, policies, and laws. Therefore, because the agency adopted the AJ's recommended decision, we find no basis for reversing its finding of no discrimination. We do again advise, however, that this matter is best analyzed as a request for official time rather than as an allegation of reprisal discrimination. However, regardless of the analysis used, we agree that under the circumstances of this case, and under any of the regulatory authority previously cited, this appellant was not entitled to the official time he requested as he was not functioning in the capacity of a witness, complainant or representative, was not called by the AJ to furnish relevant information, and did not present his request for official time to the agency in an appropriate timely fashion.
