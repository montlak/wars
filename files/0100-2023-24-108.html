O. JOB RESTRUCTURING

Part of the agency's burden of reasonable accommodation is to explore the possibility of job restructuring for a qualified individual with a disability. See 29 CFR 1630.2(o)(2)(ii). The precise agency efforts to restructure a particular position will depend on the duties involved and the nature of the disability, as well as a number of other factors. Each case must be assessed on an individual basis. It is clear error when the agency fails to consider job restructuring or to place a permanently disabled employee in a light duty status for a short period of time prior to terminating him without consideration of job restructuring. See Sanders v. USPS, 01811035 (1984).

According to the EEOC's Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Types of Reasonable Accommodations Related to Job Performance, Job Restructuring":

Job restructuring includes modifications such as:

• reallocating or redistributing marGINAl job functions that an employee is unable to perform because of a disability; and

• altering when and/or how a function, essential or marGINAl, is performed.

An employer never has to reallocate essential functions as a reasonable accommodation, but can do so if it wishes.

In order to accomplish job restructuring, an employer may switch the marGINAl job functions of two employees. Id. Of course, the job restructuring must be effective to qualify as a reasonable accommodation. See Bryant F. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120171192 (2019) (finding that the agency failed to reasonably accommodate the complainant where it provided him with light duty office work and by exempted him from conducting subject and witness interviews, but his supervisors repeatedly assigned him work outside of his medical restrictions).

Sometimes job restructuring can be rather simple. For example, in French v. Veterans Administration, 03850184 (1987), the Commission found that it was a reasonable accommodation to provide a tape recorder for an employee who was required to collect data through personal interviews. A stroke had effected the employee's memory, which made it difficult for him to recall the results of interviews. The Commission rejected the agency's assertion that use of the recorder would double the amount of time required for interviews on the basis that the agency presented no evidence to support its contention.

In restructuring a position, "[a]n agency is under no duty to accommodate a handicapped employee by elimination of the essential functions of the position in question." Stots v. USPS, 03830030 (1987) (citing Jasany v. USPS, 755 F.2d 1244 (6th Cir. 1985)).

Job restructuring would not suffice as a reasonable accommodation where a complainant is unable to perform at least 50% of the essential functions of his position. Dudley v. Postmaster General, 05920572 (1993). The agency must consider reassignment of the employee. For instance, the Commission upheld the agency's denial of a Legal Assistant/Case Technician's request for accommodation in the form of a permanent reduction in her caseload duties in Rosita R. v. Social Security Administration, 0120151444 (2017). The agency required that Case Technicians process ten to twelve cases per week. The complainant requested that she be allowed to process six cases per week. The agency denied the request, noting that processing cases is an essential function of her position, and the Commission agreed in Rosita R.:

The Agency has established that processing cases is an essential function of Complainant's position. According to the record, 50%-75% of an LASCT's time is spent performing this duty. Complainant was seeking a permanent reduction in her case processing duties. Complainant was not requesting an accommodation in order to help her perform the essential functions of her position, but rather was requesting a reduction in her workload so that she would not have to fully perform the essential functions of her position. Under the facts of this case, we do not find that the Agency was required to permanently reduce Complainant's case processing duties as a reasonable accommodation.

Not being able to accommodate Complainant in the position, we note that she was offered a receptionist position at a lower grade level, but she declined. The Commission has long held that reassignment is the reasonable accommodation of last resort and is required only after it has been determined that there are no effective accommodations that will enable a complainant to perform the essential functions of her current position or all other reasonable accommodations would impose an undue hardship. Zachary K. v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120130795 (November 19, 2015) citing EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship under the Americans with Disabilities Act, EEOC Notice No. 915.002 (October 17, 2002). While Complainant is entitled to an effective reasonable accommodation, she is not entitled to the accommodation of her choice. Lynette B. v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0720140010 (Dec. 3, 2015).
