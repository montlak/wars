Q. SPIN-OFF COMPLAINTS

See the discussion in Chapter 3 under the heading "Spin-off Complaints." In summary, a spin-off complaint relates to the processing of an EEO complaint. The general rule is that spin-off complaints should not be treated as new complaints but should be incorporated into the pending complaint for disposition.

Under the former regulations, the administrative judge had discretion to hear spin-off complaints. 29 CFR 1613.262(b). Requests to consolidate the spin-off complaint with the oriGINAl complaint for hearing had to be made within 30 days of the act that formed the basis of the allegation. The administrative judge also had discretion to determine not to hear the spin-off complaint and require that it be processed as a new complaint under 29 CFR 1613.262(a). See Pappas v. Postmaster General, 01881460 (1988).

It was not clear that administrative judges retained this authority under the 1992 version of Part 1614. In fact, it would appear they did not. Title 29 CFR 1614.109(a) (1992) provided that the administrative judge "shall" remand any "issues like or related to those raised in the complaint" for precomplaint counseling. The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 did away with remanding spin-off complaints. Instead, the new guidance instructs complainants to raise spin-off complaints with the agency official responsible for complaint processing. See MD-110 at 5-28. If the underlying complaint has been assigned to an administrative judge, the complainant may also raise the spin-off complaint with the administrative judge. If the administrative judge finds that improper processing had a "material effect on the processing of the complaint," the administrative judge will issue an order addressing the deficiencies. MD-110 at 5-29 to 5-30.

In most cases, spin-off complaints can be dealt with prior to the hearing. For example, a complainant might allege that the agency conducted an inadequate investigation by failing to get affidavits from key witnesses. If the administrative judge agrees, he or she will order the agency to produce the required evidence. In cases where the administrative judge is unable to determine whether the agency's alleged improper processing had a material effect on the processing of the underlying complaint, evidence may be taken during the hearing on the underlying complaint.
