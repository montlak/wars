II. MIXED CASES BEFORE THE MERIT SYSTEMS PROTECTION BOARD

The passage of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 created sweeping changes in federal personnel law. One of the Act's creations, the Merit Systems Protection Board, has jurisdiction over certain personnel actions taken against specific employees. In the context of those actions, the MSPB can exercise jurisdiction over allegations of discrimination raised as affirmative defenses to the underlying personnel actions. The Board's decisions on the discrimination aspects of cases can be reviewed by the EEOC. See 5 USC 7702.

What has developed in the last several years is a confusing overlap of MSPB and EEOC jurisdiction--an overlap that the Board, the Commission, and even the courts occasionally misunderstand. The overlap has been particularly confusing in cases filed with the Board where it subsequently determines that it lacks jurisdiction. The Commission made some changes to its "mixed case" regulations in an effort to reduce some of the confusion.

The time for revamping the mixed case appeals process and eliminating the confusion has long since passed. For years it has been clear that the process does not work and often leads to unreasonable delays while a case bounces back and forth between the Commission and the Board. In McAdams v. Reno, 858 F. Supp. 945 (D. Minn. 1994), the court, relying in part upon commentary in this book, noted that "courts and commentators appear to be unanimous in their denunciation of this unnecessarily elaborate and confusing system of intertwined administrative jurisdiction and judicial review."

Indeed, the situation is so bad that in Jenkins v. Secretary of Army, 05940721 (1996), the Commission declined to hold a practitioner experienced in federal EEO law to knowledge of the proper forum. As the Commission noted in Jenkins:

Based on the record herein, we find that appellant's appeal to the Commission should have been deemed timely. The record in this case is clear: the agency erred when it advised appellant that he could appeal its final decision only to the MSPB and not to this Commission. To date, the agency has never corrected its improper advice, notwithstanding requests from appellant for information as to the correct procedure.

The agency has argued and the Commission notes that appellant is represented by counsel experienced in Federal EEO matters. Nevertheless, the agency, which also is experienced in EEO matters, misled appellant by providing incorrect information concerning his appeal rights. For this reason, the agency either should have reissued its final decision with corrected advice or provided appellant with the corrected information by separate letter. We find that by choosing to do nothing, the agency cannot now assert that appellant's failure to seek Commission review of the final decision sooner than twenty-one months after the MSPB dismissal of his appeal renders this action untimely. Even assuming that the agency did not intentionally mislead appellant when it gave him incorrect advice, it is clear that the agency's advice was wrong; that appellant followed wrong advice when he filed his appeal with the MSPB; and that the agency did nothing after receiving the MSPB decision, which informed it that its prior advice was wrong. In these circumstances, we find that the limitations period for filing an appeal with the Commission was tolled as of the date appellant received the agency's final decision. See, e.g., Herrera v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05891351 (September 28, 1989); Ong v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05880290 (August 8, 1988); Martinez v. Orr, 738 F.2d 1107, 1110 (10th Cir. 1984) (tolling of time requirements permissible where agency lulls complainant into taking no action or actively misleads or prevents complainant from asserting rights). The appeal period, having been tolled by the agency's actions, never recommenced and appellant's appeal was timely.

When judges characterize a process as "unnecessarily elaborate and confusing" and even experienced practitioners cannot figure out which forum to proceed in, it is time to abandon the process.
