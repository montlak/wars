49. Testimony and Statements in Proceedings

In Bell v. Postmaster General, 01922671 (1992), the Commission found that the complainant was not aggrieved by a statement made by a management representative in a civil action alleging a breach of the union's duty of fair representation. "[T]he Commission has held that it is appropriate, and indeed mandatory, for an agency official to give an accurate and complete statement explaining the basis for an act of alleged discrimination and such an act does not aggrieve a complainant so as to give rise to a new complaint." Such statements do "not constitute a direct harm affecting a term, condition or privilege of appellant's employment." Id.

A similar result was obtained in Reed v. Postmaster General, 01922934 (1992). In that case, the Commission found the employee could not file a complaint over statements in a memorandum by an EEO complaints manager that cast doubts on her credibility. The memorandum, which the complainant considered derogatory, was drafted during attempts to resolve another EEO complaint. See also Jensen v. Secretary of Navy, 01A02404 (2000) (an agency official allegedly threatened the complainant with criminal investigation during settlement negotiations); Walker v. Social Security Administration, 01975252 (1998) (racist statements allegedly made by an agency representative during settlement negotiations were not actionable).

An EEO complaint filed as a result of an incident that occurred at a grievance hearing is beyond the scope of EEO procedures. Zurcher v. Postmaster General, 01972518 (1998) (statements made during a grievance procedure and in response to a disability retirement application were not actionable); see also Hillier v. USPS, 01831009 (1983).

In McBride v. Attorney General, 07A00010 (2001), the Commission held that there is an absolute litigation privilege that bars complaints over statements and testimony in administrative and judicial proceedings. The complainant in McBride alleged reprisal "when the agency's staff attempted to slander and defame him at a hearing related to a Competency to Stand Trial hearing." Id. The Commission upheld the finding of the administrative judge that the agency had retaliated against the complainant on other grounds in McBride, but not with regard to anything that was said to the complainant during the course of questioning while he was on the stand:

As an initial note, the Commission finds that complainant can not raise a claim of reprisal when the agency attempted to slander and defame complainant during a Competency to Stand Trial hearing. This claim against the agency is based upon a line of questioning by an Assistant U.S. Attorney. The Commission finds that complainant cannot raise such a claim based upon an absolute litigation privilege. An absolute privilege is provided for statements made as part of a judicial or administrative proceeding. See Cruey v. Gannett Co. 64 Cal. App. 4th 356, 368 (Cal. Ct. App. 1984); Hinds v. Magna Fabrics, Inc., 1997 WL 309378, *5 (S.D.N.Y. June 9, 1997). Based upon this absolute privilege, we find that complainant cannot raise such a claim based on statements made against him in a hearing.

Similarly, in Brown v. Secretary of Defense, 01A21914 (2002), the Commission held that the complainant could not be aggrieved by actions of an agency representative allegedly taken in proceedings before the Office of Special Counsel, the Department of Labor and during congressional inquiries. See also Kalil v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A62138 (2006) (complaint over statements made in MSPB brief constituted collateral attack on MSPB proceedings and was properly dismissed).

But, in Richardson v. Secretary of the Air Force, 01961068 (1996), the complainant alleged that she had been discriminated against when the agency released the testimony she provided during an EEOC hearing. She further alleged that "management's release of this information was designed to punish [her] for exercising [her] protected rights and testifying as a witness in an EEOC hearing." Id. This was sufficient to state a claim. Similarly, in Lombardo v. Postmaster General, 01A11257 (2002), the Commission addressed the merits of a claim that the agency violated the complainant's EEO right to confidentiality by submitting an email message to the hearing examiner in an OWCP hearing. In that case, the complainant at the OWCP hearing testified that he had challenged the validity of a warning letter and the matter was still under appeal. After the agency received a transcript of the hearing, it forwarded an email message to the hearing examiner explaining that an EEOC administrative judge issued a recommended decision with a finding of no discrimination which the agency adopted and the complainant appealed. The Commission in Lombardo found that the agency articulated a legitimate reason for forwarding the email to the hearing examiner and the complainant had failed to establish pretext.

In Gentry v. Secretary of Air Force, 01956913 (1997), the Commission held that placing an official charged with discrimination in a position of authority over the appellant's witnesses did not render the appellant aggrieved:

Appellant initiated contact with an EEO Counselor on June 5, 1995. On July 12, 1995, appellant filed a formal EEO complaint wherein she alleged that she was discriminated against on the basis of reprisal (previous EEO activity) when:

1. An official that she accused of sexual harassment in a previous complaint was reassigned as Director, Acquisition Security, and therefore he directed employees who would be called as witnesses in her EEOC hearing.

2. This official's presence in the office is intimidating to the potential witnesses because they are all in career fields in which this official can influence their futures.

3. This official has direct influence in the decision-making process of who will be promoted in the Directorate.

We note that the agency dismissed allegations 1-3 on the grounds of mootness and failure to cooperate. However, we find that the more appropriate grounds for dismissal is that appellant failed to state a claim. It is clear from these allegations that appellant lacks standing to pursue these issues. The reassignment of the relevant official and his authority and potential influence over appellant's prospective witnesses are matters that do not directly affect appellant: i.e., any actions taken against the witnesses would give rise to causes of action for the witnesses, not appellant. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss allegations 1-3 of appellant's complaint was proper for the reasons set forth herein and is AFFIRMED.

It would appear that the Commission's decision in Gentry is right for the wrong reasons. It is hard to imagine that interference with a complainant's witnesses would not aggrieve the complainant. The problem in Gentry is that the complainant did not allege that any such interference had actually occurred. The injury she complained of was speculative; namely, that the supervisor might interfere with the testimony of her witnesses. If the supervisor had attempted to interfere with the witnesses' testimony, a cause of action would accrue both to the witnesses and the complainant.

However, in Diamond v. Secretary of Army, 01A11728 (2001), the Commission reached a result similar to that in Gentry when it held that a complaint that a management official made inappropriate comments to a witness in the complainant's other discrimination complaint did not state a claim. Similarly, in Heins v. Postmaster General, 05990527 (2002), the Commission agreed with the agency that a claim that the responsible management official intimidated witnesses was a collateral attack on an EEO proceeding. In Heins, the management official allegedly made statements to witnesses to the effect that they would see who "had the power" in the case.

A claim that agency officials lied during the course of an EEO investigation does not state a new claim for relief. Such a complaint is "more properly raised within the context of the prior EEO complaint..." Reyes v. Postmaster General, 01A12095 (2001).
