b. Employee and Human Relations Specialists

In Darrell v. Secretary of Navy, 01934300 (1993), the Commission found an employee relations specialist had "so enmeshed" himself in the EEO process that he was "logically connected" to it. The specialist was on board a Navy ship to conduct an EEO investigation, discussed the complainant's allegations of sexual harassment with her and advised her as to what actions could be taken. Although the specialist advised the complainant to contact an EEO counselor, there was no evidence he had advised her of the proper time for doing so. Waiver of the time limit was appropriate. See also Hyman v. Secretary of Navy, 0210100060 (2011) (complainant contacted ship's first mate and no EEO counselor was on board).

Similarly, when the agency distributes a memo encouraging employees to report suspected discrimination to the human resources office and the agency inspector general, those offices are "logically connected" to the EEO process. As a result, the date of contact for counseling purposes is determined from the date the employee contacted either of those offices. Williams v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01974168 (1998). See also Thompson v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120080545 (2008) (contact with inspector generally deemed "logically connected" for timeliness purposes).

But, in Patterson v. Secretary of Navy, 01975770 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant's initial contact of an EEO counselor did not constitute EEO contact because the complainant had contacted the counselor in her role as director of human resources, rather than her role as a counselor:

On appeal, appellant asserts that allegation (a) was improperly dismissed for untimely EEO Counselor contact because she spoke to an EEO Counselor ("EC1") on March 29, 1995, and sent a letter ("contact letter") expressing her concerns a short time later. Accordingly, appellant contends that her initial contact was within the requisite time period.

In response, the agency concedes that appellant discussed the Cheating Accusation with EC1. However, the agency asserts that the contact was not made with the intent of initiating EEO counseling, but rather because EC1, in addition to her duties as an EEO Counselor, happened to be the Director of the Human Resources Center ("DHRC") for appellant's work location. Accordingly, the agency contends that appellant was speaking to EC1 in her capacity as DHRC, rather than as an EEO Counselor. In support of this contention, the agency points out that the contact letter contains no reference to allegations of discrimination. As a result of the foregoing, the agency contends that appellant's conversation with EC1 should not be considered a timely contact for the purposes of 29 CFR § 1614.

...

In the instant case, it is clear that unless appellant's March 29, 1995 discussion with EC1, or her follow-up contact letter, qualifies as initial EEO Counselor contact for the purposes of 29 CFR § 1614, appellant's subsequent EEO Counselor contact on December 4, 1996, is untimely. After a review of the record, we find that appellant's discussion and letter in March 1995, did not evidence the necessary intent to initiate EEO counseling. Instead, the contact letter and the agency's response identify the issue as whether the interview panel believed that appellant had seen the interview questions prior to her interview. EEO Counselor contact for purposes of tolling the 45-day time limit requires at a minimum that the complainant intend to pursue EEO counseling when she initiates EEO contact. See Snyder v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05901061 (November 1, 1990). We find that when appellant contacted EC1 in March 1995, she did not intend to initiate and pursue EEO counseling; appellant was instead making an inquiry of EC1 in her capacity as DHRC.

Accordingly, we find that appellant did not initiate contact with an EEO Counselor for purposes of pursuing EEO counseling until after the 45-day time limit from the March 1995 incident had expired and the agency's dismissal of allegation (a) as untimely was proper.
