2. Amended Complaints

In a recurring scenario, having filed a formal complaint, further events occur that the complainant believes are discriminatory or retaliatory. The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 address this situation and provide for different procedures, depending on whether or not the further events are "like or related" to the matters counseled. 29 CFR 1614.106(d), 1614.606. If the new allegations are like or related to claims raised in counseling, no additional counseling is required and the complaint can be amended. A claim is like or related to the initial complaint if it adds to or clarifies it, or could reasonably be expected to grow out of the initial complaint. See Scher v. Postmaster General, 05940702 (1995). If the new issues are not like or related to the matters that were raised in counseling, the complaints should be consolidated for processing under 29 CFR 1614.606. The employee will be required to undergo additional counseling on the new issues. See further discussion in Chapter 3, subheading "Amendment and Consolidation of Complaints."

Prior to the 1999 amendments, the Commission had no clear or consistent guidance on when such incidents could serve as the basis for amending a complaint and when they should be treated as separate complaints and, thus, be the subject of counseling. As noted below, in some instances, the Commission allowed an amendment of the existing complaint.

In Gentry v. Secretary of Air Force, 01960006 (1997), the Commission held that it would waive the time limit for EEO counselor contact where the employee believed that she would be permitted to introduce evidence of events subsequent to the oriGINAl counseling at the EEO hearing:

The record reveals that in August 1994, appellant was informed that she would be permanently assigned to the Civil Engineering Group. On April 28, 1995, appellant learned in an AJ conference concerning a previous complaint that she could not raise additional reprisal allegations during the hearing on that previous complaint. Appellant initiated contact with an EEO Counselor on June 5, 1995. Appellant stated that she planned to raise the issue of her assignment to the Civil Engineering Group as well as a statement made to her in August 1994 that "You can get a letter from the President and you still can't go out on stress leave," however, the meeting with the EEO Counselor was cut short and she did not have a chance to raise these allegations at that time. Appellant subsequently informed the EEO Counselor of these allegations on June 28, 1995.

The Commission found the complainant's belief that she would be able to raise the matters at the hearing was reasonable and that her contact with the counselor after learning this would not occur was timely.

However, if an initial contact with a counselor is untimely, the complainant may not amend a complaint in precomplaint counseling unless the amendment itself is timely, i.e., brought to the counselor's attention within 45 days. In McGautha v. Postmaster General, 01A12296 (2001), the complainant did not initiate counselor contact over his removal from a position until three months after the event. Several months later, the complainant attempted to amend his complaint. The Commission determined that the amendments were not timely because the initial contact was not timely and the events that constituted the amended complaint occurred more than 45 days before they were brought to the counselor's attention.
