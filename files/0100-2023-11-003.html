B. WAIVER OF PRECOMPLAINT COUNSELING

Precomplaint counseling is a requirement that cannot be waived, either by the employee or the agency. The only exception is that a complaint may be amended if a new claim is like or related to the existing claim, and precomplaint counseling is not required. See Chapter 3, "Amendment and Consolidation of Complaints."

In Carter v. Department of Navy, 01830362 (1984), the appellant filed a formal complaint without the benefit of precomplaint counseling after several attempts to initiate counseling failed due to the agency's inability to provide the appellant with a counselor. The agency then accepted some allegations for investigation while dismissing others. In Carter, the EEOC reinstated the entire complaint with an order to the agency requiring it to immediately provide the appellant with a counselor:

29 CFR 1613.213(a) states, in substance, that the agency shall require an aggrieved person who believes that he or she has been discriminated against to consult with an EEO counselor to try to resolve the matter. The section also provides, in substance, that if, within 21 days, the matter has not been resolved to the satisfaction of the aggrieved person, that the aggrieved person shall be immediately informed in writing of his right to file an EEO complaint. In the instant case, the agency not only did not require appellant to consult with an EEO Counselor; it appeared to be unable to provide him one. The intent of the Commission's regulations is that agencies shall take the necessary steps to insure that its EEO programs are implemented; 29 CFR 1613.204(c) specifically mentions that EEO Counselors shall be designated as necessary to assist the head of the agency to carry out its EEO functions. Thus, the agency had a duty, by regulation, to provide appellant with an EEO Counselor, and appellant could not make an "election" to proceed without one. What 29 CFR 1613.213(a) contemplates is that in the event that an appointed Counselor fails to complete the final interview within 21 days of his first contact with the aggrieved employee, then the aggrieved employee shall have the option of filing a formal complaint within 15 days of his receipt of the notification of his right to do so, or of awaiting the completion of counseling. Here, the appellant had no option, since, through the fault of the agency, he had no Counselor.

(Emphasis in original.)

Although Carter was decided under Part 1613, there is no reason to believe that precomplaint counseling may be waived under Part 1614. The complainant does, however, have the right to file a formal complaint any time after 30 days from the date counseling was requested. 29 CFR 1614.105(d). This right exists even if counseling has not been completed, or has not yet commenced, during the 30 days after counselor contact was made. In addition, as discussed more thoroughly later in this chapter under the subheading "Alternative Dispute Resolution," under some circumstances a complainant may opt for ADR in lieu of traditional EEO counseling.

When an employee files a formal complaint without first initiating precomplaint counseling, the agency should treat the complaint as a request for counseling, using the date of the complaint to determine whether the request is timely. Castaneda v. Postmaster General, 01913396 (1991); see also Baker v. Department of Navy, 01830104 (1984). An agency may not dismiss a complaint for failure to prosecute due to its own failure to provide an EEO counselor. Guyton v. USPS, 01831531 (1983).

An employee does not have the right to refuse counseling on the basis that he lacks confidence in the impartiality of the EEO office. Williams v. Secretary of Treasury, 01970942 (1998). Failure to obtain counseling constitutes a proper ground for dismissal under 29 CFR 1614.107.

The agency must be careful not to mislead, even inadvertently, a potential complainant over the requirement to participate in precomplaint counseling. In Geathers v. Postmaster General, 01922793 (1992), the complainant filled out an intake form for precomplaint counseling. The following appeared at the end of the form:

Note: Complete this Section only if you do not wish to receive counseling.

Please close the file from processing. I do not wish to receive counseling. I understand this complaint file will not be reopened once closed.

The complainant both requested counseling and signed the section closing out the complaint. The Commission held that the agency could not abdicate its responsibility to provide counseling due to an unintended error on the part of the complainant. It is worth noting that the Commission's decision did not depend upon any effort of the agency to mislead the complainant. Rather, it rested solely on the fact that the complainant had not intentionally waived her right to counseling.

Although the complainant cannot waive precomplaint counseling, she is not required to resolve her complaint as a result of counseling. For example, in Sinicki v. Secretary of Treasury, 01963971 (1997), the Commission determined that the complainant's discussions of her complaint with an EEO counselor satisfied the precomplaint counseling requirement. The Commission in Sinicki rejected the agency's argument that her complaint should be dismissed because she had no interest in informally resolving it.

The agency argues that appellant has thwarted the EEO complaint process by filing her complaint without an attempt at resolution. It further argues that because appellant expressed no intentions to try to informally resolve the matter with the Philadelphia EEO counselor, her complaint should be dismissed.

The Commission finds that appellant's discussion of her complaint with the Philadelphia EEO counselor and her subsequent contact with the Manhattan EEO officer, who failed to help her secure counseling by a local EEO counselor, is sufficient to satisfy the precomplaint counseling requirement. EEO MD-110, Chapter 2 provides that EEO counselors have a duty to seek resolution of a matter at the lowest possible level. However, there is no absolute obligation to resolve the complaint. Therefore, once the Philadelphia EEO counselor realized that resolution of this matter was futile he could have issued a right to file a complaint to appellant.

The Commission further notes that the Philadelphia counselor elected to refer appellant to a New York EEO office. When that contact was made, the New York office had an obligation to bring the contact to closure by resolving any perceived conflict: find a counselor, or issue a notice of right to file a formal complaint.
