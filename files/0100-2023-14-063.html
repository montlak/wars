N. SUPERVISION CLAUSES

Many employment disputes, whether or not they involve discrimination, also involve personality conflicts. The simple reality is that some people just don't get along. And, of course, some people just don't get along with others at all. In any event, sociology aside, such phenomena lead to settlements where it is agreed that a certain person will or will not supervise an employee. Such agreements are generally enforceable. However, they have their limits in that they seldom plan for a change in circumstances.

All of this becomes delightfully clear in Kulis-Robitaille v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01956429 (1997), where the complainant, pursuant to a settlement, was to report to a specific individual. The individual was mentioned in the agreement by both name and title. Shortly after execution of the settlement agreement, the named individual retired. The immediate question: To whom does the employee now report? The Commission in Kulis-Robitaille determined that the settlement agreement did not provide that the complainant directly report to the successor to the retiree's position:

Appellant seems to argue on appeal that the agency is in breach of provision 2(b) of the settlement agreement, because she is not reporting "directly to the chief of Service." The record shows that in March 1995, the chief, Pharmacy Service was Person D. Therefore, in March 1995 appellant was not reporting to Person B and was not directly reporting to the chief, Pharmacy Service. Appellant indicates that subsequent to when the settlement agreement was entered, Person B retired. Therefore, since the retirement of Person B, it was impossible for appellant to report to Person B.

The second sentence of provision 2(b) first states that appellant will report to Person B, by name, and then lists Person B's title. Because Person B is no longer with the agency, the Commission finds that the agency was no longer obligated under the settlement agreement to have appellant reporting to the new chief, Pharmacy Service. The title of "chief, Pharmacy Service" subsequent to Person B's name in provision 2(b) is not meant to indicate that appellant should always report to the chief, Pharmacy Service.

To interpret provision 2(b) otherwise would mean that appellant should always report to the chief, Pharmacy Service regardless of who held that position. Such an interpretation would raise a potential conflict with the first sentence in provision 2(b) if Person A were ever appointed to the position of chief, Pharmacy Service. The Commission finds that the plain interpretation of provision 2(b) is to require appellant to report to Person B, and that because Person B is longer employed by the agency, that requirement no longer exists.

The Commission also finds that there is no evidence showing that, subsequent to the March 1995 letter to appellant, Person A has any supervisory powers over appellant. Therefore, we find that appellant has failed to show that the agency breached the settlement agreement.

It is highly probable that Person B, who retired, got the best part of the bargain.
