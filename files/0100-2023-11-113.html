VI. PROTECTION FOR COUNSELORS

EEO counselors are protected from discrimination in employment actions just as any other employee. Of much greater concern is the prospect that counselors could be subject to reprisal for successfully carrying out their duties. In Part 1613, this prospect was specifically addressed. In pertinent part, 29 CFR 1613.261 provided:

It is unlawful to restrain, interfere, coerce, or discriminate against complainants, their representatives, witnesses, Directors of Equal Employment Opportunity, Equal Employment Opportunity Officers, Investigators, Counselors and other agency officials with responsibility for processing discrimination complaints because of involvement with a discrimination charge during any stage in the presentation of a complaint including the counseling stage under 1613.213....

Part 1614 does not separately address protection from reprisal for counselors. Rather, it contains a general provision at 29 CFR 1614.101(b) to the effect that:

No person shall be subject to retaliation for opposing any practice made unlawful by title VII of the Civil Rights Act (title VII) (42 USC 2000e et seq.), the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) (29 USC 621 et seq.), the Equal Pay Act (29 USC 206(d)) or the Rehabilitation Act (29 USC 791 et seq.) or for participating in any stage of administrative or judicial proceedings under those statutes.

Counselors are also free to file discrimination complaints on their own behalf. It does not constitute a conflict of interest, per se, for a counselor to file a complaint on her own behalf and continue to perform counseling duties for the agency. Lane v. National Archives, 01871482 (1988). The Commission found that a policy of suspending counselors from performance of counseling duties during the pendency of their own complaints was discriminatory. The Commission noted that an agency has authority to appoint and to deny appointment of its employees to counseling positions, but it cannot do so on the basis of participation in the EEO process.

The act of EEO counseling constitutes participation in the EEO process. Moreover, there is no requirement that an EEO counselor demonstrate an intent to restrain or interfere with his or her duties when an agency policy has that effect. In Simpson v. Secretary of Treasury, IRS, 05930570 (1994), the Commission found that an agency policy that prohibited EEO counselors from receiving assignments as acting supervisors constituted restraint, per se. Even though the agency contended there was a potential for a conflict of interest, the Commission found that the agency had produced no evidence of such conflicts.

Not every action taken with respect to an EEO counselor by management amounts to reprisal. In Valdez v. Secretary of Treasury, 01930969 (1993), the Commission found the agency was justified in temporarily replacing its counselors at the Denver mint with counterparts from IRS. The Commission found the agency had articulated a legitimate reason for the move where there had been several congressional inquiries and an Inspector General investigation had concluded that there was a severe communications breakdown between management and employees that had led to the filing of an excessive number of EEO complaints. The IG had further recommended modifications in the EEO process to ensure its effectiveness and objectivity.
