8. Official Time Requests and Denials

Of course, granting official time requires a request for official time. Complainants are not entitled to simply take official time without first asking for it and having it approved. The agency must establish a procedure for making such requests and inform the complainant of that procedure. In order to receive official time for participation in the EEO process, official time must be requested. Thornhill v. Postmaster General, 01880425 (1988). The agency cannot be deemed to have denied a request it never received.

MD-110 at 6-19 requires:

The agency must establish a process for deciding how much official time it will provide a complainant. Agencies further must inform complainants, their representatives, and others who may need official time, such as witnesses, of the process and how to claim or request official time.

It is not unreasonable for the agency to require some proof that the employee needs the official time to engage in EEO complaint activity. See Bagnell v. USPS, 01872779 (1987). In that case, the employee refused to provide his supervisor with any evidence that he had EEO forms to complete which required official time. The Commission found that refusal was tantamount to proceeding in bad faith. A complainant may be required to justify the amount of official preparation time requested where that request is for a substantial number of hours. See Brown v. Postmaster General, 04940004 (1995) (agency's determination that four hours of time was reasonable was upheld where employee failed to explain need for 24 hours of time requested). Similarly, in Bang v. Postmaster General, 01A15079 (2003), the Commission found the agency had not denied the complainant a reasonable amount of official time where the complainant submitted no evidence as to why he needed more official time than the agency granted.

A good example of the degree of information that can be required to provide an adequate justification for an official time request is provided by Shaw v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., 0120072091 (2007). In that case, the complainant requested eight hours of official time to prepare her formal complaint. After she refused to give specifics on why she needed eight hours, the agency granted her two hours. In finding that the agency was justified in Shaw, the Commission explained:

Complainant claims there was no meeting of the minds with her supervisor and that she did not receive notice of the grant of time until time had already "expired" on January 5, 2006. The email record, however, shows that a string of communications occurred; starting only hours after complainant sent the first email on December 23, 2005. The record further shows that complainant was unwilling to provide the information necessary to facilitate a grant of eight hours.

Complainant's supervisor, openly in communication with Employee Relations personnel, explicitly requested that complainant clarify why she needed the full eight hours. Complainant had an opportunity to justify the requested quantity of time but instead only replied that she would generally be working on preparing the formal complaint and would at some point in the process be conferring with someone. In an affidavit, complainant states her supervisor "did not know what [complainant] had to do to prepare [her] formal complaint" and that her supervisor thought all she had to do was "fill out a form." In this affidavit complainant states she had to read 30 to 40 pages in order to file her complaint and suggests that her supervisor should have had training in what was involved in the filing of a formal claim. Complainant purportedly withheld this information because her supervisor was the subject of the EEO claim which complainant was preparing. However, while this perceived conflict of interest justifies withholding some details, it does not seem to preclude divulging general details such as the fact that complainant had to read 30 to 40 pages. Complainant had an opportunity to illustrate why her particular circumstances required eight hours so she is responsible for the lack of information she perceives her supervisor employed in making the final official time determination.

The proper procedure for denying a request for official time is set forth in MD-110 at 6-19:

If the agency denies a request for official time, either in whole or in part, the agency must include a written statement in the complaint file noting the reasons for the denial. If the agency's denial of official time is made before the complaint is filed, the agency shall provide the complainant with a written explanation for the denial, which it will include in the complaint file if the complainant subsequently files a complaint.

Although an agency can deny a request for official time, or grant official time at a time other than that requested by the complainant, it must have a legitimate business reason for doing so. In Dillard v. Secretary of Defense, 0120113984 (2012), the Commission found that the agency had improperly denied official time to a meat cutter on the basis that the meat counter had to be filled. The Commission determined that the agency did not provide an explanation as to why another employee could not have filled the counter or why the complainant could not have been granted official time after the meat counter was filled or on another day.

By contrast, in Fulton v. Postmaster General, 0120111215 (2013), the Commission found that the agency was justified in denying a request for two hours of official time to prepare a formal complaint where the mail volume on Mondays was heavy. As an alternative, the agency granted the complainant two hours of official time at a later date that was beyond the 15-day period for filing the formal complaint. However, the complainant did not advise the agency of the impending deadline.
