J. TIMING OF AGENCY ACTION

The timing of the agency's action can provide strong evidence of pretext in an intentional discrimination case just as it can in a reprisal case. In Hilton v. Postmaster General, 01971239 (2000), the Commission paid particular attention to the timing of the agency's actions, and found that the agency's reason for terminating the complainant was pretext for disability discrimination for the following reasons:

First, we note that the agency's failure to take any disciplinary action against complainant during the fifteen-month period between her initial refusal to provide medical information related to her seizure disorder and her return from approved maternity leave without providing such information renders the agency's sudden decision that the failure to provide the information warranted removal inherently suspect. The agency has failed to explain why it was so concerned about the alleged safety risks her condition posed in the workplace yet permitted complainant to continue her duties during this lengthy time period with minimal restrictions. Instead of insisting that complainant produce the requested medical information at the time the alleged concern over safety arose, the agency waited until complainant requested extended sick leave for maternity purposes and used complainant's request for such leave as an opportunity to request medical information regarding a totally unrelated impairment.

Next, the Commission notes that there is no information in the record that suggests that employees without disabilities have ever been required by the agency to produce medical documentation unrelated to the reason for their extended absence as a precondition to returning to duty from such an absence. In fact, the agency's own ELRM provisions indicate that employees on extended sick leave can be required to submit to a fitness for duty examination when the reason for the employee's sick leave is of such nature as to raise "justifiable doubt" concerning the employee's ability to "satisfactorily and safely" perform his or her duties. There is no ELRM provision in the record that permits the agency to require an employee, such as complainant, who is absent on sick leave for one reason to present medical documentation regarding an unrelated medical condition in order to return to duty. Thus, the Commission finds that the agency's own policies undermine its rationale for complainant's removal.

The fact that the agency does not act immediately may be evidence of pretext, but the delay must be evaluated based on all the surrounding circumstances. For example, in Complainant v. Social Security Administration, 0120133416 (2015), the Commission found that the supervisor's failure to act immediately after the complainant referred to some coworkers as "bitches" and that said she "would take them out," did not undermine the credibility of the supervisor where he later learned she had purchased a handgun and had taken target practice:

We are not persuaded that the reason why S-1 did not make a report immediately was that she was lying. S-1 explained the reason for her delay in reporting the threats immediately was that she believed the incident was an "isolated" incident. However, she learned thereafter that that Complainant possessed a firearm. It is reasonable to conclude that tying the statements Complainant made on April 13, 2013 to having a firearm placed Complainant's statements into context for S-1. While S-1 may have initially misjudged the gravity of the statements, it is reasonable to believe that S-1 was influenced by her prior observation of Complainant's behavior which had never been a cause for alarm. It is also reasonable to believe that S-1 considered Complainant's statements on April 13, 2012 to be an anomaly. CW, Attorney Advisor-1 and Advisor-2 described Complainant generally as friendly, respectful, and professional. So did S-1.

Lending credence to S-1's statements that the threats did occur are statements by Attorney Advisor-1 that on April 13, 2012, Complainant had talked to her and she was visibly upset by events occurring in her personal life and at work. Attorney Advisor-2 also remembered that on April 13, 2012, Complainant appeared not to be her normal self. Further, although Complainant did complete her workday on April 13, 2012, she was hospitalized the very next day.

Complainant asserted in her affidavit that S-1 was aware of her disability when she came to the emergency room on April 14, 2012. In an April 30, 2012 e-mail to S-1, Complainant stated that it had been "confusing" for S-1 regarding her (Complainant) during the last three weeks. In another e-mail of the same date, Complainant wrote that she was under multiple doctors' care for physical and psychiatric issues. In a May 1, 2012 e-mail to S-1, Complainant stated that when she was discharged from the Florida hospital, she was discharged by psychiatry for having a nervous breakdown from stress. In a May 3, 2012 e-mail to S-1, she stated that she had been assessed with manic disorder recurrent episode in partial or unspecified remission. Complainant, however, did not submit a medical diagnosis until the May 17, 2012 medical note which diagnosed her condition as Bipolar I disorder. Complainant did not submit the medical note until after her termination. In addition, in a May 18, 2012 letter to the union, Complainant stated that when she was admitted to the emergency room on April 14, 2012, it was due to exhaustion and dehydration and that S-1 told her then to concentrate on getting better and to take the next week off.

The Commission notes that the timing of the termination and the termination process certainly overlapped and paralleled times when Complainant was hospitalized and during the time she provided information about her condition. However, Complainant has the ultimate burden of persuasion that the Agency's actions were motivated by discriminatory animus which Complainant has failed to do. Even if the Agency believed Complainant to be disabled, an Agency is not required to excuse misconduct or ignore conduct standards. An employer may discipline an individual with a disability for violating workplace conduct standards, provided that the workplace conduct standard is job-related and is consistent with business necessity. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities, No. 915.002, Question 30 (Mar. 25, 1997). For example, nothing in the Rehabilitation Act prevents an agency from maintaining a workplace free of violence or threats of violence. Id. Thus, an agency may discipline an employee with a disability for engaging in such misconduct if it would impose the same discipline on an employee without a disability.

We recognize that the Rehabilitation Act does not preclude the Agency from holding all employees to the same conduct standards and the Agency was under no obligation to excuse Complainant's misconduct. Moreover, Complainant has not shown that other employees not in Complainant's protected group were treated differently than she was for engaging in the same misconduct.
