H. CORRECTING DISCRIMINATION BY NONSUPERVISORY PERSONNEL

When nonsupervisory personnel engage in actions that violate Title VII the agency can take corrective action and avoid being found in violation. The corrective action must include steps that are "feasible and reasonable under the circumstances to correct discrimination by its nonsupervisory personnel." Craddock v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01880141 (1988) (citing Degrace v. Rumsfeld, 614 F.2d 796, 21 FEP 1444 (1st Cir. 1980)).

In Craddock, the employee applied for admission to a management training program and, along with other candidates, was screened by a three-member panel. One of the panel members made racially discriminatory remarks about the complainant and other black candidates. The agency subsequently appointed a new panel consisting of three different members and the candidates were reevaluated. The panel was told only that it was necessary to repeat the process because of "procedural reasons." On appeal, the employee in Craddock argued that the reevaluation was not adequate relief because the damage from discrimination already had been done:

Although appellant states that the reassessment could not constitute an adequate remedy because the damage had already been done, under Degrace, the agency is entitled to take reasonable steps to try to correct discrimination. The agency states that the investigative record neither confirms nor denies that a second assessment disadvantages candidates by causing mental stress and anxiety. The agency points out that the white male candidate stated that he believed his performance improved due to familiarity with the assessment procedures. The agency points out that both appellant and the other black female candidate improved their scores in some areas in the second assessment. We are, therefore, not persuaded that the second assessment was inherently unfair because it gave rise to mental stress and anxiety.

Appellant states that, rather than being subjected to reassessment, she should have been given a rating based upon the ratings given her by the white female and black male assessors of the original panel. The record indicates that both gave appellant a numerical score sufficient for recommendation.

The agency states that its decision to reassess the candidates rather than give them a rating based upon the ratings given them by the white female and black male assessors of the original panel was proper. The agency states that all other candidates were assessed by panels of three and that, owing to the variance in score determinations made by individual assessors, a rating based upon a panel of three was preferable and more equitable than a rating based upon a panel of two. We find that the agency has clearly set forth legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for reassessing appellant by an entirely new panel rather than giving her a rating based upon the ratings of two of the original assessors.

. . .

Accordingly, the agency was entitled to take reasonable steps to correct discrimination by its nonsupervisory personnel, the record indicates that it took such reasonable steps when it conducted the reassessment of appellant by a new panel of assessors, and the new panel of assessors did not recommend appellant for the agency's Management Intern Program. . . .[W]hile appellant established a prima facie case, the agency demonstrated that it took reasonable steps to correct the discrimination by nonsupervisory personnel. The Commission therefore concludes that appellant has failed to meet her ultimate burden of proving intentional discrimination.

Note the differences between correcting a violation by nonsupervisory personnel and the agency's burden in rebutting a finding of discrimination, as discussed below. The former situation rebuts a prima facie case of discrimination and there is no finding of a violation of Title VII. The latter situation applies after the complainant has proved discrimination by a preponderance of the evidence. In the former case, there is a determination that a violation of Title VII has occurred, but the employer is not liable for the violation. The distinction is important, especially under the 1991 Civil Rights Act, which provides limited remedies in the latter situation.
