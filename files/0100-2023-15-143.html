6. Relevancy

The Commission's regulations do not define relevant evidence. Perhaps the best, and most generally accepted, definition of relevant evidence is provided by Rule 401 of the Federal Rules of Evidence:

"Relevant evidence" means evidence having any tendency to make the existence of any fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action more probable or less probable than it would be without the evidence.

Relevancy should not be confused with the credibility of the evidence. Relevancy goes to whether a particular piece of evidence should be considered, whereas credibility goes to the weight the evidence should be given. It is entirely possible that evidence can be relevant, and yet its credibility be so suspect that it is entitled to no weight in the final decision.

Relevance has come to embody two separate, yet closely related concepts. The first concept is that of relevancy--i.e., whether the evidence tends to make the existence of a fact more or less probable. The second concept is that of materiality--i.e., whether the evidence pertains to a fact that is of consequence to the outcome of the proceeding. Over the years, these two concepts have been merged into the overall concept of relevancy, as reflected in the Federal Rules of Evidence definition cited above. Indeed most objections that are asserted on the basis of relevance go to whether the evidence is material. For example, in a case involving reasonable accommodation, the agency may object on the basis of relevancy to evidence offered by a complainant showing his supervisor has made racist statements in the past. Such evidence is in fact relevant--i.e., it would tend to establish that the supervisor has racist attitudes. However, it simply is not material in a reasonable accommodation case--i.e., the supervisor's attitudes are not material to any fact that affects the outcome of the case.

If there are questions about the relevance of a proffered piece of evidence, the party seeking its admission should be prepared to present arguments demonstrating why the evidence is relevant.

Background evidence often presents the most questions with regard to relevancy. Evidence related to claims dismissed as untimely by the agency for liability purposes may nonetheless be relevant as background evidence on the claims accepted as timely. Joslin v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01831086 (1983). For example, previous nonselections for promotion may be relevant background evidence on a particular nonselection, even though no complaint was filed over the previous nonselections and no remedy can be provided for those nonselections.

As noted above, the tendency of administrative judges is to err on the side of admission of evidence of questionable relevancy. The most obvious explanation of this tendency is that it avoids remand situations. If relevant evidence is excluded, the proper remedy is remand for the taking of the evidence and issuance of a new decision. If the evidence is admitted, but discounted by the judge, any error can usually be corrected by consideration of the evidence at the appellate stage.
