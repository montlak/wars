16. Seniority

The complainant is entitled to any seniority benefits that he or she would have accrued absent discrimination. Most often, seniority benefits come into play in cases of failure to hire or removal. In those cases, the employee is entitled to be treated as though he or she was hired or never removed.

However, when discrimination results in loss of seniority because of a forced transfer, make-whole relief also includes placing the complainant back into her position and retroactively restoring the complainant's seniority. In addition, collective bargaining agreement provisions on seniority cannot restrict make-whole relief. For example, in Wong v. Postmaster General, 01982144 (2001), the complainant sought a transfer to a different location because of the agency's failure to take action to stop sexual harassment. As a result of the transfer, the complainant lost approximately three years and 10 months of seniority. The administrative judge in Wong found that, as a matter of law, the complainant was entitled to restoration of the seniority, despite the agency's contention that restoration of seniority violated the collective bargaining agreement:

The AJ found that the "make whole" purpose of Title VII "[w]ould be frustrated if [complainant,] whom the agency found was a victim of sexual harassment, were penalized for taking the very action which ended the unlawful harassment which the agency, at that point, had failed to remedy." Accordingly, the AJ found, as a matter of law, that complainant was entitled to an award of remedial seniority of approximately three years and 10 months to fully compensate her for the seniority she lost when she was, "compelled to transfer." Since this determination was based on her previous finding that complainant had been discriminated against because of her sex, the AJ found that it was unnecessary to decide whether complainant was also discriminated against because of her race and previous EEO activity.

The agency, in its final decision, rejected the AJ's finding of discrimination regarding claim (2) because it disagreed with the remedial award of seniority. According to the agency, the award of remedial seniority violated the terms of the seniority provisions of its collective bargaining agreement and negatively affected the rights of incumbent employees. The agency, however, adopted the AJ's determination that claims (1) and (3) should be dismissed.

Like the AJ, we find that claim (2) flows directly from the earlier finding of discrimination concerning the prior complaint. A finding that was adopted by the agency. In a second affidavit, dated May 2, 1996, complainant stated, in pertinent part, that:

[I] was denied a reassignment without loss of seniority and eventually took a reassignment which reduced my seniority. This caused me to suffer a reduction in pay level and loss of an assignment and schedule due to my lowered seniority. This has continued to impose great burdens on me and my family. I was unable to retain a schedule and assignment. I would have otherwise have been able to hold. I have been in therapy continually for years as a result of the discriminatory action and the failure...and other officials to act on the clear reports of a hostile environment.

As noted by the AJ, complainant must be placed "[i]n the position she would have been in if she had not been forced to transfer but, instead, had been able to work in an environment free of harassment in Menlo Park Post Office."

With regard to the agency's assertion that an award of remedial seniority would conflict with the seniority provisions of its collective bargaining agreement, we note the Commission's ruling in Jo Anne Jones v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05970828 (September 24, 1998). In Jones, a case where a supervisor of the agency was found to have sexually harassed the complainant, the Commission held that:

[W]e reject the agency's contention that its CBA acted as a bar to the previous decision's ability to mandate the relief set forth in provision (2) of its order. Apparently, the agency has lost sight of the fact that prohibited discrimination was found to have occurred in this case. The Commission, as the entity authorized by Congress to enforce Title VII, has broad discretion to fashion remedies consistent with the specific employment discrimination involved. Thornhill v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05 900124 (June 26, 1990).

Therefore, we find, based on the specific facts, of this case, that an award of remedial seniority is more than appropriate. Management, by failing to take appropriate action over a period of years, forced complainant to transfer to another facility in order to escape from C-1. If the agency had, for example, removed C-1 from the work place, complainant would not have been placed in the position of either losing her seniority by transferring or being subjected to further harassment. Therefore, she must be placed in the position that she would have enjoyed had she not been compelled to transfer to San Francisco. See Albemarle Paper Company v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405(1975).
