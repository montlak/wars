XI. REQUESTS FOR CLARIFICATION

It is true that occasionally the Commission issues a decision that is unclear. Apparently, the Commission has anticipated this unlikely result. The pertinent portion of the regulation, 29 CFR 1614.503(c,) provides:

On behalf of the Commission, the Office of Federal Operations may, on its own motion or in response to a petition for enforcement or in connection with a timely request for reconsideration, issue a clarification of a prior decision. A clarification cannot change the result or diminish the relief ordered but may further explain the meaning or intent of the prior decision.

Because the right of a party to request clarification is linked with the procedures for a request to reopen, it must be submitted within 30 days of receipt of the Commission's prior decision. Mike v. Secretary of Army, 05880410, 05880411 (1988). Otherwise, the agency is required to implement the prior decision and has no right to delay implementation of any relief ordered. Id.

A request for clarification is proper where the Commission makes relief contingent upon a future event and it is not clear whether that event has occurred. For example, in Parvin v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04960027 (1997), the Commission found that the complainant would be entitled to relief on certain allegations in his complaint only if he was reinstated as a result of another pending EEO complaint. The employee was reinstated but the reinstatement came about as the result of an agency disciplinary procedure and not the EEO complaint. The Commission, in its reclarification decision, found the employee still was not entitled to relief on the allegations. Because his second complaint still was pending, there had been no finding of discrimination with regard to his removal and that finding was a prerequisite to any further relief.

However, if a party questions the scope of relief granted in a decision of OFO, the proper course is to timely file a request for reconsideration. A request for clarification "cannot change the result of a prior decision or enlarge or diminish the relief ordered." Adams v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04A10027 (2001). In Adams, the agency argued that OFO should not have remanded the complaint to the administrative judge for hearing addressing damages because the judge had not granted damages in her decision and a damage claim had not been previously raised by the complainant. The Commission found the agency was not seeking a clarification, but was in fact trying to change the final order in the case.

That was the case in Larraine D. v. Agency for International Development, 2022001084 (2022), where the Commission reclassified the complainants' request for clarification as a request for reconsideration. The complaints appealed the final agency decision maintaining, inter alia, that the decisions "appear to be the product of the Agency's Office of General Counsel rather than the Agency's EEO office." The Commission vacated the agency's final decisions and ordered the agency to issue new final decisions "by a fair, impartial, and objective official with no interference from the Agency's Office of General Counsel." Complainants filed a motion for clarification asking for sanctions. The Commission found that the request was more akin to one for reconsideration:

In consolidated Appeal Nos. 2020003744, 2020003745, and 2020003746, we vacated the Agency's final decisions and remanded the matter back to the Agency for further processing. Specifically, it was determined that that although the Agency's EEO Office had issued the final decisions, they did not appear to be from deliberation and work independent of the defensive function of the Agency's Office of General Counsel as required by regulation and EEOC guidance. On remand, the Agency was ordered to issue three new final decisions "by a fair, impartial, and objective official with no interference from the Agency's Office of General Counsel." In addition, the Agency was notified that the Agency's failure to maintain an independent EEO Office had been referred to EEOC's Federal Sector Programs, which provides oversight to federal agencies' EEO programs.

We note that while complainants' attorneys identify the matters raised before us as a "Request for Clarification," we conclude it is more appropriate to consider this matter as a request for reconsideration of our prior appellate decision under 29 C.F. R. § 1614.405(c).

Complainants' attorneys do not dispute the Commission's findings below. However, they argue that merely directing the Agency to issue new final decisions does not serve to deter the Agency from the actions taken in its prior final decisions.

They expressly seek attorney's fees as a sanction following the Agency issuance of new final decisions, and further request that the Commission hold in abeyance any ruling on the amount of fees to be awarded until they can review the new final decisions and, if appropriate, file new appeals and determine how much in attorney's fees the new final decisions and appeals cost complainants.

We have reviewed the instant request for reconsideration and determine that there is nothing that requires expansion from, or clarification of, the Commission's prior decision. Moreover, we find it unnecessary to hold in abeyance any request for added attorney's fees. Specifically, we note that this request can readily be raised if and when appeals are filed from the new Agency final decisions. A request for reconsideration is not a second appeal to the Commission. Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (EEO MD-110), Chap. 9 § VI.A (Aug. 5, 2015); see, e.g., Lopez v. Dep't of Agric., EEOC Request No. 0520070736 (Aug. 20, 2007). Rather, a reconsideration request is an opportunity to demonstrate that the appellate decision involved a clearly erroneous interpretation of material fact or law, or will have a substantial impact on the policies, practices, or operations of the Agency. Complainants have not done so here.

After reviewing the previous decision and the entire record, the Commission finds that the requests fail to meet the criteria of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.405(c), and it is the decision of the Commission to deny the requests. The consolidated decision in EEOC Appeal Nos. 2022001084 et al, remains the Commission's decision. There is no further right of administrative appeal on the decision of the Commission on this request.

Questions such as the retroactive date of an ordered promotion or the proper pay step for the employee are properly raised in a request for clarification. See, e.g., Munno v. Secretary of Agriculture, 04A10042 (2001). Such a request does not seek to enlarge or diminish the relief offered. Similarly, when the Commission orders an agency to place a complainant in a specific position but the complainant was removed during the pendency of the case, a request for clarification of the restoration order is appropriate. See, e.g., Bahaudin v. Secretary of Army, 04A50031 (2005) (removal rendered previous order moot and order modified to include appropriate back pay through date of removal).

Where the facility to which the Commission orders the complainant reinstated and orders the agency to post a notice of violation has been closed, a request for clarification on the relief ordered is proper. Hunter v. Secretary of Army, 04A10052 (2003). When the Commission order reinstatement of the complainant and there is a question about the position to which the agency is required to reinstate complainant, a request for clarification is proper. Brown v. Postmaster General, 0420070021 (2008). In Brown, the complainant was told to report for an interview for a mailhandler position and when he appeared, the agency terminated him from his casual clerk position. The Commission found clarification was proper to specify that the complainant should be reinstated to his clerk position since he had never been hired for the mailhandler position.

The Commission also granted a request for clarification in Cruz v. Secretary of Army, 0420070022 (2007), where the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the case on the basis that the complainant was an employee of a contractor. In remanding the case, the Commission ordered the agency to clarify the issues with the complainant and then process the complaint under Part 1614. The Commission granted the agency's clarification request where it stated it was unsure whether it should vacate the complaint and send the complainant back to counseling, which would require the filing of a new formal complaint, or to go through a process of clarifying the complainant issues and then make a new acceptance or dismissal interpretation. The Commission indicated that the agency's latter interpretation was correct and amended its previous remedial order to reflect that interpretation.

The Commission in Yvette H. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0420180017 (2018), granted a request for clarification where, in its previous decision, it had ordered the agency to provide the complaint file to the "appropriate EEOC Hearings Unit so that an AJ could issue a decision on Complainant's non-mixed claims." The previous decision also ordered that the complainant's constructive discharge claim be processed as a mixed case claim. The agency sought clarification, noting that the complainant, as a Title 38 employee, was not entitled to appeal her claim of constructive discharge to the MSPB. The agency asked in its request whether the complainant's constructive discharge claim was a mixed case claim and whether all of her claims should be remanded for a hearing. The Commission in Yvette H. clarified that the complainant's constructive discharge claim was a non-mixed claim, and that she was entitled to a hearing on that claim as well.
