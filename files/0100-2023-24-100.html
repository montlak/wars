3. Agency Good Faith

In Nutt v. Postmaster General, 01955714 (1997), the complainant alleged that the agency failed to accommodate her when it did not provide an interpreter for a meeting. The Commission found that there was no discrimination because, although the meeting concerned work procedures in the complainant's unit, the agency made a good faith effort to provide accommodation: the usual interpreter was not available, a substitute interpreter was not sufficiently skilled and the complainant was offered a synopsis of the meeting. Unfortunately, the Nutt decision is exceptionally brief and relies primarily on adopting the reasons set forth in the recommended decision of the administrative judge--reasoning which does not appear in the Commission's decision. It is unclear whether Nutt is suggesting that a good faith accommodation effort on the part of the agency is sufficient to avoid liability. Such a holding would be at odds with the plain language of the Statute which provides a good faith defense only to a damage claim. It is more likely that in Nutt the Commission was merely observing that the agency had proceeded in good faith and, independent of that fact, its efforts under the particular circumstances of the case were sufficient to constitute reasonable accommodation. However, the Commission used the same language in coming to a similar conclusion in Schrager v. Postmaster General, 01A41832 (2005), In Schrager, the Commission also found that the agency made a "good faith" effort to provide an interpreter for an emergency meeting and provided the information to the complainant in a subsequent meeting when it could not immediately find an interpreter. No doubt, both cases were correctly decided. However, in both cases it would have made more sense to focus on the undue hardship imposed by trying to secure an interpreter on short notice rather than suggesting that a good faith effort at reasonable accommodation entirely exonerates an agency from liability.

In Haggard v. Postmaster General, 01A41814 (2005), without resorting to the "good faith" language, the Commission found that the complainant had failed to show a failure to accommodate where the supervisor agreed to discuss the complainant's performance using notes and arranged for an interpreter to further discuss the complainant's work performance a few days later. In Haggard, the Commission held:

The record indicates that on March 26,2003, complainant approached the Manager to discuss his duty assignments. It is noted that complainant stopped working on his assigned machine in order to converse with the Manager which, the agency asserted, was a violation of office policy. Despite the violation, the Manager agreed to communicate with complainant through the use of written notes. The Manager indicated that an interpreter would be available on April 1, 2003, to discuss his job and performance issues. The Manager averred that complainant was argumentative. The Manager instructed complainant to return to his seat because she did not want to continue the argument and he was not permitted to leave his machine to talk with her. When the sign language interpreter was available on April 1,2003, complainant indicated that he did not want to meet with the Manager. Upon review, we find that the Manager provided complainant with a reasonable accommodation. The Manager communicated with complainant via the notes and would have also discussed the performance issues in the presence of a sign language interpreter. Further, we find that the breakdown in the discussion was due to complainant. The Manager ended the communication with complainant based on the argumentative nature of the conversation and on complainant's violation of the office policy regarding leaving machines. Therefore, we find that complainant has not shown a violation of the Rehabilitation Act as to the discussion on March 26, 2002.
