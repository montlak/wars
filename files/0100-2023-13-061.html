XI. OFFICIAL TIME

Generally, complainants and other employees of the agency required to cooperate in an EEO investigation should be granted official time for that purpose. See 29 CFR 1614.605. An agency is not required, however, to place a former employee on "official time" for the purposes of meeting with an EEO investigator. Telk v. USPS, 01820499 (1982).

A claim that an agency has denied the complainant a reasonable amount of time to prepare an EEO case alleges sufficient injury to state a claim for relief. Prior to the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, such allegations were processed as separate complaints of discrimination. As the Commission explained in Nicklin v. Postmaster General, 01954455 (1996):

The appellant filed a formal EEO complaint, dated February 16, 1995, wherein he alleged that the agency discriminated against him based on his disability (knee injury) and reprisal (prior EEO activity) when, on November 22, 1994, the agency initially approved and then disapproved his request for 3 hours of official EEO time to appeal a case to the EEOC, and, on November 25, 1994, the agency approved and then disapproved his request for 8 hours of official EEO time to prepare a response to the agency regarding 7 cases. On April 28, 1995, the agency issued a final agency decision which dismissed the appellant's complaint pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.107(a) for failure to state a claim. The final agency decision indicated that inherent in the Commission's regulations is the requirement that the complaining party alleges (s)he is an aggrieved person who has been injured because of an agency action or lack of action. The final agency decision represented that record evidence shows the agency gave the appellant a reasonable amount of time to respond to his EEO cases and concluded that the appellant had not alleged he had suffered any personal harm as the result of an agency action.

Neither party submitted any contentions on appeal.

ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS

An agency shall accept a complaint from any aggrieved employee who believes that he or she has been discriminated against by that agency because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age or disabling condition. 29 CFR 1614.103; 1614.106(a). The Commission has held that while the regulations do not define the term "aggrieved employee," the United States Supreme Court has interpreted it to mean an employee who suffers a present harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. Thus, to state a claim under our regulations an employee must allege and show a direct and personal deprivation at the hands of the employer, that is, the employee must allege and show a present and unresolved harm or loss affecting a term, condition or privilege of his/her employment. See Diaz v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (Apr. 21, 1994), citing Trafficante v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co., 409 U.S. 205 (1972).

As a complainant, the appellant has a right to a reasonable amount of official time to present his complaints and to respond to agency requests for information, if he otherwise is on duty. Equal Employment Management Directive (EEO-MD) 110, Chapter 5, Section VII.C. at 5-14. Although the final agency decision represents that evidence proves the agency gave the appellant a reasonable amount of time to respond to his EEO cases, there is no such evidence in the copy of the record submitted to the Commission. Moreover, the appellant's complaint alleges that the agency denied his two requests in total. Thus, the appellant has alleged that he has been harmed personally by two agency actions. Accordingly, the Commission reverses the agency's dismissal of the appellant's complaint.

Since the 1999 amendments, complaints regarding official time are viewed as processing complaints. During the investigation stage, such complaints should be brought, in writing, to the attention of the EEO manager or director responsible for the office investigating the complaint. At the hearing stage, complaints about official time will often be properly amended to the underlying complaint and should be raised with the administrative judge assigned to the case. Use of official time is covered in more detail in Chapter 9. See also Chapter 3 on "Spin-off and Dissatisfaction With Process Complaints."
