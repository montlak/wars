17. Inter-Office Mail Filing

In a bit of an interesting case, the Commission in Lilian C. v. Secretary of Interior, 0120161072 (2016), excused the complainant's untimely filing when she sent her formal complaint via inter-office mail. Evidence of the complainant's "diligence" seemed to save the day in Lilian C., as the Commission explained:

It is undisputed that Complainant received the notice of right to file a formal complaint on October 8, 2015. The Agency contends that the complaint was received by its Office of Civil Rights via inter-office mail on October 28, 2015, beyond the fifteen day limitation period.

On appeal, Complainant contends that she sent her formal complaint through inter-office mail on October 20, 2015.2 Complainant states she knew it was due October 23, 2015, and she thought she had plenty of time because the notice of final interview contained a statement that, in the absence of a legible postmark, the complaint would be deemed timely if received within five calendar days of the expiration of the filing period.

Based on the unique facts of this case, we find adequate basis to excuse the brief delay in the filing of Complainant's formal complaint. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c) (the time limitations provided for in the EEO complaint processing regulations are subject to waiver, estoppel and equitable tolling). We note that the formal complaint form was signed by Complainant on October 20, 2015, which is consistent with her representation that this was the date she placed the complaint in inter-office mail. There is also no indication that Complainant knew inter-office mail could take eight days for delivery. Finally, while we have previously held that the rule providing a five-day extension for lack of a legible postmark is in reference to documents sent through the public mail and not inter-office mail, we find Complainant's claim that she was confused over this issue to be credible. It is clear that this was a case where Complainant was diligently attempting to file her complaint in a timely manner albeit choosing the incorrect vehicle to accomplish that filing. In the future, Complainant is cautioned that she should file documents in the EEO process either in person or through public mail unless advised otherwise.

The Commission denied the agency's request for reconsideration in Horne a/k/a Lilian C. v. Secretary of Interior, 0520170077 (2017), and rejected its argument that the above ruling would substantially affect the agency's practices, policies or operations:

In its request to reconsider, the Agency contends that our prior decision should be reversed because Complainant knew or should have known that in order to ensure timely filing with supporting evidence, her complaint should have been filed in person or via public mail. The Agency argues that a finding in Complainant's favor substantially impacts its policies, practices, or operations. The Agency maintains that it would therefore be required to accept any EEO complaint filed through inter-office mail, timely or not. According to the Agency, it would be necessary to seek additional resources in its mailroom or in the alternative, prohibit the filing of EEO complaints via inter-office mail. The Agency cites previous Commission decisions that it argues support its position that an item of inter-office mail must be received before the expiration of the applicable filing period, just as any document delivered in person.

We do not find that the Agency has established that the previous decision involved a clearly erroneous interpretation of material fact or law, or that it will have a substantial impact on the policies, practices, or operations of the agency. The previous decision noted that the facts of this case are unique, such that there existed an adequate basis to excuse the brief delay in filing, in accordance with 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c). We find that the unique circumstances found here militate against this case being so broadly applicable such that the Agency will experience the adverse impact on its practices and operations which it posits in its request for reconsideration.
