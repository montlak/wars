2. Wrong Agency or Wrong Agency Component

Several of the government's larger departments are comprised, in part, of many smaller agencies. In such cases, a complainant is not required to file his complaint with any particular division or agency of the department. In Kennedy v. Secretary of Defense, 01961247 (1997), the Commission found it was improper for the Department of Defense to dismiss a complaint on the basis that some of the allegations involved the Defense Logistics Agency and the Department of the Air Force. As the Commission explained in Kennedy, if the department wants particular subagencies to process the complaint, it can assign the complaint to those agencies. However, the department may not dismiss the complaint and require the complainant to refile it with the desired agency. In Kennedy, the Commission held:

The agency dismissed allegations (a)-(d) for failing to state a claim. The agency found that allegations (a)-(d) were not filed with the agency that allegedly discriminated against appellant. The agency found that allegations (a)-(d) were "against the Defense Logistic[s] Agency, and the Department of the Air Force" and that the Defense Commissary Agency has no authority over those organizations.

The Commission finds that allegations (a) and (d) were properly filed by appellant with the Department of Defense. The agency that allegedly discriminated against appellant in allegations (a) and (d) is the Department of Defense. The Defense Logistics Agency and the Defense Commissary Agency are both part of the Department of Defense. Therefore, allegations (a) and (d) were filed with the proper agency. If the Department of Defense believes that a different portion of its agency (such as the Defense Logistics Agency rather than the Defense Commissary Agency) should process allegations (a) and (d), then the Department of Defense should transfer internal processing of those allegations. Appellant, however, is not required to refile his complaint merely because the agency wants a different portion of its agency to process the allegations.

Similarly, in Marguerite W. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120170318 (2017), the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal for untimely EEO contact where the complainant contacted the agency's local EEO office rather than the Office of Resolution Management as mandated by the agency's EEO regulations. The Commission in Marguerite W. explained:

It is well established that a complainant satisfies the requirement of counselor contact by contacting an agency official "logically connected" with the EEO process, even if that official is not an EEO Counselor, and by exhibiting an intent to begin the EEO process. See Cristantiello v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01992817 (Dec. 19, 2000), Cox v. HUD, EEOC Request No. 05980083 (July 30, 1998); Allen v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05950933 (Jul. 9, 1996); Jones v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05900435 (Sept. 7, 1990).

Here, Complainant satisfied the EEO Counselor contact requirement on May 6, 2015 even though she did not contact the Office of Resolution Management ("ORM") as dictated in the Agency's EEO regulations. We find that an EEO office within the facility, which Complainant reasonably considered her "Local EEO Office" to be logically connected to the EEO process. Complainant also exhibited an intent to begin the EEO process multiple times within the 45 day limitation period in phone calls and emails with the facility EEO office's EEO Assistant ("EA"). Complainant believed EA would file an EEO Complaint on her behalf, but on May 21, 2015, EA referred her to a union representative instead. Aware of the time limitation and that she could not pursue an EEO complaint through the Union, Complainant contacted EA again specifying that she wanted to file a complaint. Complainant alleges, and the record supports, that the only option EA provided to her was mediation between Complainant and S1, facilitated by EA and a union representative, which Complainant declined.

The Agency argues that Complainant's contact with the facility EEO office cannot be considered EEO Counselor contact for timeliness purposes because Complainant was aware that she needed to contact an EEO Counselor within ORM to initiate an EEO complaint. Where, as here, there is an issue of timeliness, "[a]n agency always bears the burden of obtaining sufficient information to support a reasoned determination as to timeliness." Guy v. Dep't of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05930703 (Jan. 4, 1994) quoting Williams v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920506 (Aug. 25, 1992). The Agency has not met this burden with respect to Complainant's knowledge that she had to contact ORM, The training documents in the record do not specify this information and although the Agency references an affidavit by the EEO Manager confirming Complainant was aware that EEO Complaints are to be brought to ORM, the affidavit is not in the record.

Notwithstanding the ORM requirement, we note that Complainant states that she went to the facility EEO office again on May 16, 2016 to retrieve her documents, which the Agency considers EEO contact. We find this inconsistency and the demonstrated lack of clarification from individuals in a position to direct Complainant to ORM sufficient to excuse Complainant from pursuing her initial complaint further prior to May 16, 2016. See Myers v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120120759 (May 1, 2012) (Complainant's delay should be excused when her EEO counselor first advised her to pursue her complaint with another office.)

In order to be "logically connected" to the agency's EEO process, the person contacted must be an employee of the agency. A complainant who was employed by the Department of the Air Force and contacted an Air Force EEO staffing specialist over his failure to be selected for a position at NASA did not contact an employee "logically connected" to the NASA EEO program. Rohar v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01920408 (1992). However, the complainant's representative had also contacted officials at NASA "logically connected" to the EEO process in a timely fashion and, therefore, the agency erred in dismissing his complaint.

In Gomes-Battle v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120073604 (2007), the Commission extended the 45-day time period where a victim of alleged sexual harassment reported the harassment to an agency "Accountability Board." The Commission noted the nature of the board was not described in the record. The record also contained no evidence of an agency anti-harassment policy, that EEO time limits and procedures were posted in the agency facility, or that the complainant was otherwise made aware of the time limits and procedures.

In Pierce v. Secretary of State, 0120110631 (2011), the Commission found that counselor contact was timely where the complainant, a Department of Homeland Security employee working as a liaison to the State Department, alleged she had been sexually assaulted by a State Department contractor. The complainant contacted a counselor at Homeland Security after attempting to report the assault to several DHS components and being referred to the agency's EEO office. The Commission, in Pierce, found:

The Agency does not dispute that the alleged assault occurred on May 10, 2009. Nor does it dispute that Complainant first sought counseling on June 1, 2009 with DHS. The Agency's finding that Complainant was untimely is premised on the apparent view that her DHS contact had no significance under subsection 105(a)(1). We conclude that it did. To rule otherwise would require the Commission to ignore the plain wording of the subsection, which provides only that aggrieved individuals contact "a" Counselor within the stated time. There is no requirement that the Counselor be from the agency that receives the complaint. In this case, Complainant logically initiated contact with a Counselor in the agency where she was employed.

It is self-evident that June 1, 2009 is within 45 days of May 10, 2009. We, therefore, find that Complainant's counseling contact was timely and reverse the Agency's dismissal on this ground.
