broida eeo Chapter 12: Theories of Discrimination--Hostile Environment Chapter 12: Theories of Discrimination--Hostile Environment, Subchapter 08: Social Media and Online Harassment, Section A: Claims of Harassment VIA Social Media

A. CLAIMS OF HARASSMENT VIA SOCIAL MEDIA

The Commission will consider evidence of social media activity to determine whether posted comments were severe or pervasive enough to constitute harassment and to assess whether the posts were motivated by discriminatory animus. See Corrina v. Postmaster General, 0120152365 (2017) (allegations that complainant's supervisor tracked her and her family on social media and, when she reported the harassing behavior, nothing was done, did not rise to the level of harassment); Complainant v. Attorney General, 0120132442 (2015) (finding that the complainant failed to prove that coworker comments made on the complainant's Facebook page like "best," "superior tribe" and "superior" were "racial code words" and racially motivated); Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120150714 (2015) (finding that alleged disparaging remarks made on social media by the complainant's peer were not sufficiently severe or pervasive to constitute harassment).

In Knowlton v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120121642 (2012), the complainant alleged that a coworker's Facebook post regarding his choice in restaurants was racist and created hostile work environment. In that case, after the complainant made a food run to Chick-fil-A for he and his coworkers, a coworker posted on Facebook that he "would make the next food run to a racist restaurant and see if his Black ass wants to complain. If he does, I will laugh in his face." The agency dismissed for failure to state a claim, but the Commission reversed and allowed the social media related claim, among others, to proceed.

Reversing the agency's dismissal of the complainant's harassment claim, the Commission in Complainant v. Secretary of Transportation, 0210151206 (2015), found that the following allegation "clearly states a justiciable claim for which there is a remedy":

On December 18, 2014, Complainant filed the instant formal complaint. Therein. Complainant claimed that the Agency subjected her to discrimination on the basis of sex (female) when:

a fellow controller posted vile and slanderous work-related comments about her on a social media website in which the NY Center controllers participated. He said Complainant traded sexual favors for preferential treatment at work. She alleged that the co-worker spread these rumors and lies about her to co-workers using electronic media, thereby creating a hostile work environment. Complainant alleged that she was publicly embarrassed and humiliated in her work place by his action.

That the harassing conduct occurred on a private social media account does not strip the agency of the authority and the obligation to address it. The Commission in Brenton W. v. Veterans Affairs, 2020002329 (2021), for instance, awarded $70,000 in compensatory damages where the complainant was made aware of a private Facebook conversation between two coworkers wherein one of the coworkers wrote, referencing the complainant, "Him and all his African home boys with their lazy nigga shit drives me insane; I know that's racial but I am sick of the home boy shit." Agency management refused to investigate or stop the harassment "as the statements were made on private Facebook accounts." Management pressured the complainant to tell them who showed him the private message, likening it to "breaking into" someone else's personal account. The agency in its final decision rejected management's defense that harassing comments posted on private accounts were not actionable. Management should have taken steps to stop the harassment. See also Harris K. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120180595 (2018) (upholding $40,000 award where the agency did not properly address a race-based hostile work environment created when coworkers made offensive comments about African Americans on a Facebook group page for agents in the office).

A single post on social media calling the complainant a "crybaby," however, did not rise to the level of actionable harassment in Frances A. v. Attorney General, 2019004187 (2020). A coworker, in that case, posted a comment that the complainant was a "crybaby" because he refused to carry pepper spray around the prison where he worked. The Commission in Frances A. concluded that the post was insufficient to establish a hostile work environment:

Complainant alleged that he was subjected to retaliatory harassment when he learned of harassing messages on social media, due to his request for an exemption to carry the OC spray. Complainant stated that there were "several negative comments" posted on social media, and he specified messages calling him a "crybaby," and statements that Complainant should find another job. However, we find that the complained of conduct was not sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile work environment. Complainant has not shown that these social media posts had the purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with his work performance, and we find that they were insufficient to create an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment.
