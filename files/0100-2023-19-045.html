11. Probationary Employees

To those familiar with MSPB practice, it is axiomatic that probationary employees and career civil servants are not similarly situated for purposes of consistency of discipline. However, the Board's primary concern is with defense of the merit system principles and civil service law under Title V. The Commission's concern is exclusively with whether discipline is the result of a motive prohibited under the anti-discrimination statutes.

For the most part, this difference in concerns results in the MSPB having a broader definition of similarly situated than the EEOC. That is not necessarily the case with probationary employees, however.

The Commission does acknowledge that "where the complainant is a probationary employee, she is subject to termination at the discretion of an agency so long as these decisions are not based on a discriminatory consideration." Dona C. v. Secretary of Navy, 0120133112 (2015). See also Complainant v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120132983 (2015)("[W]e note the Commission has long held that where a Complainant is a probationary employee, he or she is subject to retention, advancement, or termination at the discretion of an agency so long as these decisions are not based on a protected category.").

But, if the probationary employee is subject to the same conduct standards as a career civil servant, the Commission will find those employees similarly situated if they also share the same supervision. As the Commission explained in Idell M. v. Postmaster General, 0120132276 (2015):

First, with regard to the Agency's argument that the comparative employees were not PSEs, those employees still worked under the same supervisor and were disciplined by the same supervisors. Two of the employees shared the same first-level and second-level supervisor, and the remaining three shared the same second-level supervisor. (Complaint File, Ex. 10-14).

Second, the record indicates that the comparatives were subject to the same attendance standard. In its argument that other employees were also disciplined for absences, and in Complainant's termination letter, the Agency points to the all encompassing Employee Labor Relations Manual. In particular, sections 665.41, requiring regular attendance, and 665.42 outlining absences without permission, are implicated.

These sections are uniformly applied to all employees, regardless of their title or employment classification as a PSE or a regular full-time employee. Even if there is some theoretical basis for enforcing the rule differently based on job position, there is no evidence that the Agency took the roles into consideration when it disciplined the employees for attendance violations. See, e.g., Eaton v. Indiana Dept of Corrections, 657 F.3d 551, 559 (74. Cir. 2011) ("A characteristic that distinguishes two employees, regardless of its significance when objectively considered, does not render the employees non-comparable if the employer never considered that characteristic. . .[it] cannot provide any insight as to whether the employer's decision was motivated by discriminatory intent"); see also, Coleman, 667 F.3d at 848 (noting that in the context of a case of differential discipline, it is irrelevant to the comparison that one employee was a maintenance mechanic and the other was a maintenance support clerk).

Further, even though the Agency frames Complainant as a PSE, this is a characterization of her non-career employment status based on a Collective Bargaining Agreement. (Complaint File, p. 138). Complainant's title dictating her job function was Sales, Service, and Distribution Associate, and the Agency offered two comparative employees with this job title.

Third, the record reveals that comparators offered by the Agency were disciplined less severely for more culpable attendance issues. There may be explanations as to why these comparative employees were often disciplined less severely for more culpable conduct, but there is no indication in the record that it was because Complainant was a PSE and the others were not.

The Agency argues that Complainant was not similarly situated to comparators because she was a probationary employee. First, some comparators worked under the same first- and second-level supervisor, and all comparators worked under the same second-level supervisor. Second, there is no indication in the record that probationary employees were subject to a different attendance standard than non-probationary employees. The Agency based Complainant's termination on the same attendance sections in the Employee Labor Relations Manual as it did to discipline the comparative employees. (Complaint File, p. 231). Third, the comparators also were disciplined for attendance issues like the Complainant.
