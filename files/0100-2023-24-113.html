4. Family and Medical Leave Act

An employee who is disabled may have overlapping rights under the Rehabilitation Act and the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) when a medical condition requires the employee to take leave or seek a schedule modification.

The FMLA is codified at 5 USC chapter 63. Under 5 USC 6382, employees may use up to 12 weeks of leave per 12-month time period to care for a natural or adopted child, or a child placed with the employee for foster care; to care for a spouse, child, or parent with a serious health condition; or "[b]ecause of a serious health condition that makes the employee unable to perform the functions of the employee's position."

A disabled employee may be entitled to leave under both statutes. In such a case, the impact on the agency of the first 12 weeks of leave taken pursuant to the FMLA can be considered in determining whether granting additional time as a reasonable accommodation would be an undue hardship. The Commission's Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Types of Reasonable Accommodation Related to Job Performance, Leave," provides the following example:

Example A: An employee with an ADA disability needs 13 weeks of leave for treatment related to the disability. The employee is eligible under the FMLA for 12 weeks of leave (the maximum available), so this period of leave constitutes both FMLA leave and a reasonable accommodation. Under the FMLA, the employer could deny the employee the thirteenth week of leave. But, because the employee is also covered under the ADA, the employer cannot deny the request for the thirteenth week of leave unless it can show undue hardship. The employer may consider the impact on its operations caused by the initial 12-week absence, along with other undue hardship factors.

A request for leave under the FMLA may also constitute a request for reasonable accommodation. For example, in Thompkins v. Postmaster General, 01A24083 (2004), the Commission found the employee's leave request under the FMLA for a medical condition constituted a request for reasonable accommodation. However, the Commission also found the agency did not fail to provide reasonable accommodation in denying the leave because the employee did not show her condition substantially limited her ability to walk.

If the employee is unable to perform one or more of the essential functions of his or her position because of a medical condition, the employee may take intermittent leave or go to a reduced schedule. See 5 USC 6382(b)(1); 5 CFR 630.1204(b). If a disabled employee qualifies for a modified schedule under the FMLA, the agency cannot deny a request for modification by showing that the requested schedule would be an undue hardship. Another major consideration is that, under the FMLA, an employee utilizing a modified schedule still retains the benefits he or she received prior to the modification. If an employee moves to a part-time schedule as an accommodation under the Rehabilitation Act, however, the employee is entitled only to the benefits received by similarly situated part-time employees. The Commission's Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Types of Reasonable Accommodation Related to Job Performance" at Question 23, provides a comparison between the ADA and the FMLA regarding requests for schedule modification:

Under the ADA, an employee who needs a modified or part-time schedule because of his/her disability is entitled to such a schedule if there is no other effective accommodation and it will not cause undue hardship. If there is undue hardship, the employer must reassign the employee if there is a vacant position for which s/he is qualified and which would allow the employer to grant the modified or part-time schedule (absent undue hardship). An employee receiving a part-time schedule as a reasonable accommodation is entitled only to the benefits, including health insurance, that other part-time employees receive. Thus, if non-disabled part-time workers are not provided with health insurance, then the employer does not have to provide such coverage to an employee with a disability who is given a part-time schedule as a reasonable accommodation.

Under the FMLA, an eligible employee is entitled to take leave intermittently or on a part-time basis, when medically necessary, until s/he has used up the equivalent of 12 workweeks in a 12-month period. When such leave is foreseeable based on planned medical treatment, an employer may require the employee to temporarily transfer (for the duration of the leave) to an available alternative position, with equivalent pay and benefits, for which the employee is qualified and which better suits his/her reduced hours. An employer always must maintain the employee's existing level of coverage under a group health plan during the period of FMLA leave, provided the employee pays his/her share of the premium.

Example: An employee with an ADA disability requests that she be excused from work one day a week for the next six months because of her disability. If this employee is eligible for a modified schedule under the FMLA, the employer must provide the requested leave under that statute if it is medically necessary, even if the leave would be an undue hardship under the ADA.

The FMLA is not enforced by the Commission. It is enforced by the Department of Labor. However, the Commission does have jurisdiction to determine whether an employee was denied FMLA leave in a discriminatory manner. See Arreola v. Postmaster General, 01A60280 (2006); Tyndall v. Postmaster General, 01A11755 (2001). Similarly, the Commission has jurisdiction over a claim that, because of the agency's failure to provide reasonable accommodation, she was not allowed FMLA leave but required to take two days of sick leave. See Stewart v. Postmaster General, 01996570 (2001).

In Cyncar v. Postmaster General, 0720030111 (2007), the Commission approved a class complaint that alleged that the agency treated disabled employees less favorably than non-disabled employees in granting FMLA leave. The Commission, in Cyncar, rejected the agency's argument that it lacked jurisdiction over FMLA complaints:

We address first the agency's contention that the AJ's exercise of jurisdiction over a claim concerning application of the FMLA is ultra vires or outside the scope of the Commission's authority. The Commission does not have jurisdiction over the Family Medical Leave Act. However, as is the case here, the Commission does have jurisdiction in cases involving claims of discriminatory application of FMLA provisions based on an employee's membership in a protected class. The Commission has recognized a claim of discriminatory denial of leave under the FMLA. Urquidez v. United States Postal Service, 01A30793 (April 2, 2003) (reversing agency's dismissal of claim alleging discriminatory denial of leave under FMLA); See also Shannon v. United States Postal Service, EEOC 01952093, reconsid. den. Request No. 05970764 (July 6, 1999). Complainant/putative class agent has clearly alleged on behalf of the class that the agency discriminated against them in its administration of the FMLA because of their disabilities, which falls squarely within the Commission's jurisdiction under the Rehabilitation Act.

Moreover, Commission policy recognizes that leave, whether paid or unpaid, is a form of reasonable accommodation under the Americans with Disabilities Act, whether provided under FMLA or not. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under The Americans With Disabilities Act (Rev. 10/17/02) at questions 16-21, 28. Therefore, determinations surrounding the granting of leave under the FMLA to an individual with a disability may involve a determination whether to grant a reasonable accommodation under the Rehabilitation Act. Such a determination of reasonable accommodation as well as disparate treatment and disparate impact based on disability status is within the jurisdiction of the Commission.

The Commission has previously certified a class of individuals with disabilities as long as it meets the regulatory requirements. Glover v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A04428 (April 23, 2001), recons. den. Request No. 05A10711; see also Travis v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal 01992222, October 10, 2002. Therefore, we conclude that the complaint states a claim which may form the basis of a class action.
