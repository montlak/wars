16. Dismissal on Merits

A common problem with cases that raise issues relating to standing is that the agencies tend to make factual determinations that go to the merits of the case in finding the complainant is not aggrieved. For example, an allegation that an agency discriminatorily compelled a complainant to permit agency employees to enter his home and search his computer for classified information as a condition of continued employment states a claim within EEO purview. Such a claim raises an issue of "adverse treatment sufficient to warrant an investigation on the merits." Walls v. Secretary of Army, 01934659 (1993). The agency's justification for dismissing the complaint--i.e., that it was the complainant's own conduct of storing classified information in his computer that prompted the agency's actions--goes to the merits of the claim and cannot form the basis of dismissal without investigation. Similarly, in Tera B. v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120161024 (2016), the Commission reversed an agency's dismissal for failure to state a claim where the complainant alleged that the agency discriminated when it made an inappropriate medical inquiry. The agency's reported reason for the medical inquiry, that all air traffic control specialists must submit to a medical review, went "to the merits of Complainant's complaint, and is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether she has stated a viable claim under the Rehabilitation Act and the 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 regulations."

It is improper for the agency to dismiss a complaint prior to investigation due to lack of evidence. The employee has no burden of proof with respect to allegations made in the complaint, provided the allegations come within EEO purview. The employee's only burden prior to the investigation stage is to demonstrate that the EEO counselor was contacted within 45 days of the alleged discriminatory incident. See, e.g., Parsons v. USPS, 01842083 (1985) (decided under Part 1613 and applying 30-day time period).

An agency may not dismiss a complaint for failure to state a claim on the basis that the complainant was not qualified for the positions for which he applied. The respective qualifications of the candidates is an issue that goes to the merits of the complaint. In Chaklos v. Secretary of Army, 01941817 (1994), the Commission explained:

The agency argues on appeal that appellant is not aggrieved by his nonselections because he was not qualified for any of the positions for which he applied. We find that the agency has improperly addressed the merits of the complaint in a procedural decision. See Lee v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05930220 (Aug. 12, 1993) (citing Odoski v. Department of the Energy, EEOC Appeal No. 01901496 (Apr. 16, 1990)). Appellant need not prove his claim in order to state a claim under § 1614.103. We find that appellant's allegations concerning nonselection allege a direct harm of his employment--namely, that he was not selected. Therefore, we find that appellant is an aggrieved employee and that the agency's decision to dismiss the complaint for failure to state a claim under 29 CFR § 1614.107(a) was improper.

In Horacio M. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120162384 (2016), the complainant alleged religious discrimination when, in response to his refusal to provide a social security number because he believed that to do so would be a sin, the agency refused to add him to an administratively determined pay plan so that he could work as a firefighter and participate in firefighter training. The agency dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim, explaining that the complainant's social security number was necessary in order to process and pay him. The Commission in Horacio M. reversed the dismissal, reasoning that the agency's purported justification went to the merits of the claim:

In the instant case, we find that Complainant has stated a claim. Complainant in essence is claiming that the Agency has failed to provide him with a requested religious accommodation. Complainant has claimed a personal loss or harm regarding a term, condition or privilege of his employment. We note that the Agency asserts that Complainant's claim to be placed on an AD Pay Plan cannot be processed, because Complainant has not provided an SSN. However, the Agency has articulated a reason that goes to the merits of Complainant's complaint, and is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether he has stated a justiciable claim. See Ferrazzoli v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991).

An agency errs when it dismisses a complaint for failure to state a claim on the basis that the employee, who alleged failure to make reasonable accommodation, is not disabled. Such a determination is one that goes to the merits of the claim and not its legal sufficiency. "The only proper question under the purview issue is whether the complaint alleges employment discrimination on a basis covered by the EEO statutes (i.e., race, color, sex, national origin, age, handicap or reprisal for prior EEO activities). If the answer is yes then the agency must accept the complaint for processing regardless of what it may think of the merits." Fatigati v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 01941887 (1994); see also Jones v. Secretary of Navy, 01940216 (1994) (complainant has a right to develop evidence on the existence of disability at the hearing).

Whether or not the complainant previously engaged in EEO activity is a factual issue that goes to the merits of the complaint. An agency may not dismiss a complaint on the basis that the complainant has not previously engaged in EEO activity. If the complainant alleges that she suffered retaliation for previous EEO activity, the agency must investigate that complaint. Carney v. Secretary of Treasury, 01951773 (1996). But see section on "Proposed Action and Preliminary Steps."

The Commission reversed dismissal of a hostile environment claim in Diggs v. Small Business Administration, 01994937 (2000), where the agency contended that it followed its sexual harassment policy. The Commission "note[d] that the agency improperly addressed this matter on the 'merits' by arguing that it followed its harassment policy, rather than assessing whether complainant was aggrieved by this alleged action." Id. Similarly, in Malone v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A12125 (2001), the Commission reversed dismissal of a complaint alleging the agency took no action when a black female complained of sexual harassment, but immediately suspended the alleged perpetrator when three white females complained of harassment. The Commission found the agency could not consider the merits of the claim in determining whether to dismiss it.

Whether the abolition of a bid position is permitted by the collective bargaining agreement is an issue that goes to the merits of the complaint. In Lambert v. Postmaster General, 01A52474 (2005), the Commission found that the agency improperly dismissed a complaint that the employee's bid position was abolished due to discrimination on the basis that the complaint failed to state a claim. The agency dismissed the complaint finding that the abolition of a bid position was a matter that should be decided in the grievance process. The Commission held that this was an improper determination on the merits that went to the agency's reason for abolishing the position and not whether the complainant had stated a claim.

The Commission's rigid adherence to the doctrine that complaints can only be dismissed on strictly procedural grounds prior to investigation sometimes leads to ridiculous results. For example, in Beckman v. Postmaster General, 05910656 (1991), the Commission upheld its remand of a complaint filed by a 74-year-old applicant for employment who was challenging the agency's practice of not accepting applications for the position of postal inspector from persons over 35 years of age. The agency dismissed the complaint, relying on an opinion of the 11th Circuit that the age limitation was a bona fide occupational requirement. The Commission found the agency could not dismiss the complaint on that basis because it constituted a finding on the merits of the complaint. Precisely what was to be gained from investigating the complaint, the Commission did not say.
