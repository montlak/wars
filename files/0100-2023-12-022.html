8. Constructive Receipt

The Commission employs the doctrine of "constructive receipt." Under that doctrine, the notice of final interview is considered as received by the complainant when it is sent to the complainant's address and signed for by someone other than the complainant. See Pazinick v. Postmaster General, 05930337 (1993); Dimase v. Postmaster General, 0120130634 (2013).

Attempted deliveries will not support a constructive receipt claim. Where the report of investigation and notice of right to hearing were never delivered to the complainant's address, the agency could not establish constructive receipt in Emmett W. v. Postmaster General, 2019002184 (2020). To support its constructive receipt argument, the agency explained:

(1) the National EEO Investigative Services Office properly addressed the report of investigation and mailed it to Complainant's home of record; (2) delivery failed on September 13, 2018, and a postal form notice was left on Complainant's door; (3) the postal notice form apprised Complainant of the attempted delivery and contained several convenient options for Complainant to claim the report of investigation; (4) Complainant took no action to collect the report of investigation for 30 calendar days, despite having 10 days off during the period; and (5) after never receiving a hearing election from Complainant, a final decision was issued. The Agency maintains that Complainant received constructive notice of his right to request a hearing and his behavior does not establish good cause for his 65-day untimely hearing request.

Rejecting the agency's position, the Commission in Emmett W. wrote:

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(f) provides in relevant part that the agency shall provide the complainant with a copy of the investigative file and shall notify the complainant that, within 30 days of receipt of the investigative file, the complainant has the right to request a hearing and decision from an Administrative Judge or may request an immediate final decision pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.110 from the agency with which the complaint was filed.

Complainant, through counsel, maintains that he did not receive the report of investigation and notice of the right to a hearing prior to the issuance of the final agency decision. Complainant states that he was working at the time of the attempted delivery and did not learn that the report of investigation was available until after he received the final agency decision, which was sent without a required signature confirmation.

The Commission has vacated an agency's final decision when the agency failed to provide evidence that a complainant was in actual receipt of the investigative file and notice of right to request a hearing. Hill v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A60865 (May 12, 2006) (complainant denied receiving the report of investigation and the Agency did not assert that the complainant had been notified of the right to and/or the statutory time limit for requesting a hearing).

Here, the Agency does not dispute that Complainant was not in receipt of the report of investigation and notice of the right to request a hearing when it issued the final decision. Rather, the Agency asserts that Complainant should have availed himself of his hearing rights. We disagree with the Agency and find that the Agency failed to provide evidence that Complainant was in actual receipt of the report of investigation and notice of right to request a hearing prior to the issuance of the final decision.

On appeal, the Agency argues that Complainant "constructively received the report of investigation and adequate notice of his hearing rights on September 13, 2018 because he failed to exercise any diligence to claim his report for 30 calendar days." Citing Zemo v. Dep't of Commerce, EEOC Appeal No. 01912813 (September 16, 1991) and Woehr v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05960657 (July 3, 1997), the Agency asserts that complainants have an affirmative duty to exercise due diligence regarding the processing of their complaint.

In Zemo, supra, the agency sent a proposed disposition to the complainant's home by certified mail, which was returned unclaimed while the complainant was on vacation. The Agency also sent the proposed disposition via regular mail, which was delivered to the complainant's residence. In finding that the complainant received constructive notice of the proposed disposition, the Commission noted that the complainant's vacation was planned in advance and he could have notified the agency of his whereabouts, provided an alternate address, designated a representative to act on his behalf, or requested that the agency hold the processing of the claim in abeyance.

As for Woehr, supra, the Commission noted "[the complainant] failed to act with due diligence regarding his administrative rights. He filed his appeal with the Commission in October 1991 and did not contact the Commission with a communication mentioning the appeal until May 1996." In Woehr, the Commission had attempted to provide the complainant with a copy of an appellate decision by mailing it to the complainant twice, resulting in six attempts to deliver the decision over a two-month timespan, even though the decision was returned unclaimed. We find that the instant matter does not involve facts analogous to Zemo or Woehr.

The Agency further cites Otterstedt v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A05235 (March 19, 2002) for the proposition that a complainant is deemed to have constructively received documents mailed to his last known address when he fails to notify the agency of an address change, yet there is no dispute regarding Complainant's address in this matter. The Agency contends that an on-point appeal is Mazurek v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal Nos. 01A11033, 01995466 (January 23, 2002). We note, however, that in Mazurek, the Agency delivered the report of investigation to the complainant's post office box when certified-mail delivery failed, while delivery never occurred in this instance.

We find the Agency's argument that Complainant received constructive notice of his rights and failed to act with due diligence unpersuasive. It is undisputed here that Complainant did not receive a copy of the report of investigation and notice of the right to a hearing via certified mail or regular mail prior to the issuance of the Agency's final decision.

Moreover, Complainant immediately obtained legal counsel upon learning that procedural errors existed, and Complainant's counsel attempted to engage with the Agency regarding the errors. The Agency has not demonstrated that Complainant was in actual receipt or had constructive notice of the report of investigation and notice of the right to a hearing.

Further, to the extent the Agency asserts that Complainant filed an untimely hearing request, we note that the Commission has held that a complainant's actual receipt of the notice of the right to a hearing was necessary to commence the running of the time-limitation in which to request a hearing. See Kellus v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05920198 (Apr. 20, 1992) (hearing rights were sent certified mail and returned to the agency unclaimed and the agency failed to show that the complainant actually received copies sent by regular mail); Battle v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05910441 (June 13, 1991) (complainant alleged that she did not receive a proposed decision when certified mail was returned to the agency unclaimed, first-class mailing was never returned, and the agency failed to provide evidence of proof of service).

Here, actual receipt of the investigative file did not occur until December 13, 2018, when the Agency provided Complainant's counsel with the investigative file. There is no evidence in the record showing that Complainant received the report of investigation or notice of his right to a hearing prior to this time, nor any indication in the record that the Agency made any additional attempts to provide Complainant with the information after the certified mailing was returned. The Agency provided an affidavit from the postal carrier who stated she left notice of the package. However, Complainant stated that he received no such note. Contrary to the Agency's assertion that Complainant "at any time during the 30-calendar day period, could have requested redelivery or designated someone to claim the report of investigation on his behalf," the record does not establish that Complainant had actual knowledge of the redelivery notice left at his door or the contents of the undelivered package. Therefore, the Agency's insinuation that Complainant sat on his rights is purely speculative.

However, when an agency determines that it will rely on constructive receipt to dismiss a complaint as untimely, it must give the complainant a "full and fair" opportunity to rebut receipt. Gena C. v. Postmaster General, 0120152816 (2015), relying on Fontanella v. GSA, 05940131 (1995). Chistopher E. v. Secretary of Veteran Affairs, 0120170054 (2016), provides a good example. In that case, the complainant's son, who shared the same name with the complainant, received and signed for the notice of right to file a formal complaint but failed to give it to the complainant until days later. When the complainant inquired as to the status of his complaint, the EEO counselor advised that it had been closed for failure to timely file a formal complaint. The complainant filed a formal complaint, advising the agency as to why his complaint was late, and the agency dismissed the complaint for untimely filing. The Commission reversed the dismissal in Christopher E., noting:

We have held that receipt of a document at a complainant's correct address by a member of the complainant's family or household of suitable age and discretion creates a rebuttable presumption of constructive receipt of the document by complainant. Fontanella v. Gen. Svcs. Admin., EEOC Request No. 05940131 (Apr. 10, 1995) (citing Pazinick v. U.S. Postal Svc., EEOC Request No. 05930337 (Sept. 10, 1993) and Knowles v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920956 (Mar. 18, 1993). We have also held that equity demands that the Agency provide Complainant with adequate notice of its reliance on the constructive receipt presumption in dismissing for untimeliness so that Complainant has a full and fair opportunity to rebut it. See Fontanella, supra.

In the instant matter, the Agency did not explicitly notify Complainant that it had relied upon the presumption of constructive receipt in order to dismiss his complaint as untimely. In his June 28, 2016 email that accompanied his formal complaint, Complainant had explained to the Agency that his son with his same first and last name at the same address had been the one who actually received the Notice. According to the Complainant, his son did not review the Notice or provide it to the Complainant for days thereafter.

The record is devoid of evidence that the Agency followed up with Complainant to determine whether his son was of suitable age and discretion or that it provided Complainant with notice of its reliance on the constructive receipt presumption. As a result, the Agency improperly denied Complainant the opportunity to rebut the Agency's presumption that his son was of suitable age and discretion. When there is an issue of untimeliness, the Agency "always bears the burden of sufficient information to support a reasoned determination as to timeliness." See Guy v. Dep't of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05930703 (Jan. 4, 1994) (quoting Williams v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920506 (Aug. 25, 1992). In addition, in Ericson v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Request No, 05920623 (Jan. 14, 1993), the Commission stated that the Agency "has the burden of providing evidence and/or proof to support its final decision. See also Gens v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05910837 (Jan. 31, 1992). In the instant matter, we find that the Agency has not met its burden.
