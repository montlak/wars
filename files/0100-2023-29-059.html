1. Remand for Additional Evidence

There is a very basic distinction between EEO proceedings and proceedings before the MSPB. An EEO proceeding is basically a factfinding process. The Commission imposes, by regulation, information that must be included in complaint investigation reports. Because the hearing is a continuation of the investigative process, EEOC administrative judges have an obligation to ensure full development of the record. The Commission itself routinely remands cases to agencies for development of further information when the investigation report and hearing record are deficient. On the other hand, the MSPB conducts what can be characterized as adversarial proceedings. Each party is responsible for presentation of its own evidence. Generally speaking, the Board will not seek to correct evidentiary omissions, but will issue its decisions solely on the hearing record.

When the Commission feels that the record has not been fully developed with respect to allegations of discrimination, it will refer the case back to the Board for the taking of additional evidence. See, e.g., Ritter v. Postmaster General, 03880137 (1989). In Ritter, the complainant had Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)--an infection which itself leads to further infections that cause Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS). As a result, the Commission noted the complainant suffered from a life-threatening disease and requested the Board to complete its supplementation of the record and to resubmit the case to the EEOC within 60 days. The reader also is referred to the discussion above on "Failure of Board to Consider Allegations."

When the Commission does remand a case to the Board, it will give specific instructions on the additional evidence required. In Vyas v. Secretary of Army, 03A00002 (2001), the Commission remanded the case for the taking of additional evidence to determine if a reasonable accommodation was available to the complainant. The Commission in Vyas provided the following instructions:

1. The agency shall present evidence on the issue of whether the petitioner's job could have been restructured by temporarily reassigning marGINAl duties as a way of reducing the amount of stress associated with the position or by other means available.

2. The agency shall present evidence on the issue of whether the petitioner could have been reassigned during the time period in question.

After obtaining this additional evidence, the Board should refer the matter to the Commission. Thereafter, the Commission will review the additional evidence and then issue a decision on the merits of all of petitioner's allegations of discrimination, either concurring or differing with the MSPB's finding of no discrimination.

The remand for the taking of additional evidence should be regarded seriously by the agency. Failure to comply with the instructions on remand can lead to the Commission's application of the adverse inference rule. Williams v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 03900084 (1990).
