FLEXIBLE SPENDING ACCOUNTS

The Federal Flexible Spending Account Program (FSAFEDS) is a tax-qualified program based on the guidelines in Sections 105, 125, and 129 of the Internal Revenue Code. All employee contributions to FSAs are made from pretax earnings, lowering the employee's tax obligation. There are no government contributions to the program. However, all executive branch agencies, as well as other employing government organizations that provide the flexible spending account option are required to cover the administrative fees on behalf of their employees.

FSAFEDS is sponsored by OPM and is administered by HealthEquity, Inc. Up-to-date information about the program is available on the FSAFEDS website at www.fsafeds.com. You also can contact an FSAFEDS benefits counselor by telephone at 877-FSAFEDS (372-3337).

By law, retirees, federal and nonfederal, as well as active-duty military personnel are not eligible to maintain FSAs. However, employees in phased retirement status are considered employees for purposes of FSAFEDS and continue their eligibility to participate.

An FSA election is 100 percent voluntary. If you want to make an FSA election you must do so every year, during the Federal Benefits Open Season held every year at a time announced by OPM, generally from the Monday of the second full workweek in November through the Monday of the second full workweek in December.

There are three types of FSAs:

  1. 1. Health Care FSA. This program allows pretax reimbursement of eligible family medical costs not covered or reimbursed by FEHB or any other insurance. Examples include FEHB plan deductibles, copayments and coinsurance, dental and vision services not covered by FEHB plans, prescription drug costs, and certain over-the-counter costs. Insurance premiums, including life, TRICARE, Medicare, and Long-Term Care premiums, are not eligible expenses. Employees could elect up to the contribution limit per participant for a Health Care FSA. For "federal couples" where both spouses are covered by the FSAFEDS program, they each can contribute the full limit to the HCFSA. There is no household limit.
  2. 2. Limited Expense HCFSA. This option is designed for employees enrolled in a Federal Employees Health Benefits (FEHB) Program High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) with a Health Savings Account (HSA), or with spouses enrolled in a non-FEHB HDHP with an HSA. The LEX HCFSA is limited to eligible dental and vision expenses only. You may elect up to the contribution limit. For a "federal couple" where both spouses are covered under the FSAFEDS Program, each may contribute the full limit. There is no household limit for a LEX HCFSA, so you or your spouse may enroll through FSAFEDS or another plan. Under IRS rules, you are not eligible to contribute to an HSA and be enrolled in a FSAFEDS general-purpose HCFSA at the same time.
  3. 3. Dependent Care FSA. This program allows you to be reimbursed on a pretax basis for child care or adult dependent care expenses that are necessary to allow you or your spouse to work, look for work (with income during the year), or your spouse to attend school full time. You must be able to claim the dependent on your income tax return. The DCFSA qualifies for pretax reimbursement if the expenses for dependents meet the IRS definition of dependent for income tax purposes. Any adult may qualify as a dependent if the employee is providing more than half of that person's care for the year. The annual maximum allotment limits are set by law per household or if married and filing a separate return. If you are currently receiving a child care subsidy, you must ensure that the total amount you elect through the FSA combined with the total amount of the child care subsidy you receive does not exceed the limit. If you are married, the limit must also be observed by you and your spouse where both individuals have access to an FSA.

A qualifying child or dependent for a DCFSA is a:

  • • Dependent of the covered employee who is younger than 13; or
  • • Dependent or spouse of the covered employee who is mentally or physically incapable of caring for themself.

To claim the credit for child and dependent care expenses, you must meet the following conditions:

  1. 1. You must have incurred the expenses in order for you and your spouse to work or look for work unless one spouse was either a full-time student or was physically or mentally incapable of self-care.
  2. 2. The payments for care cannot be paid to someone you can claim as your dependent on your return or to your child who is younger than age 19.
  3. 3. Your filing status must be single, qualifying widow(er) with a dependent child, married filing jointly, or married filing separately.
  4. 4. The care must have been provided for one or more qualifying persons identified on the form you use to claim the credit.
  5. 5. You (and, if you are married, your spouse) must maintain a home that you live in for more than half of the year with your qualifying child or dependent.

Participant eligibility

HCFSA. Eligible participants for the Health Care FSA are those federal employees eligible to enroll in the Federal Employees Health Benefits program. All eligible federal employees of executive branch agencies and other federal employers who adopt the FedFlex plan may make an election immediately upon entry on duty with the exception that temporary federal employees are eligible upon completion of one year of continuous service in the job position.

LEX HCFSA. Employees who enroll in an FEHB High Deductible Health Plan with a Health Savings Account may not participate in a regular HCFSA under FSAFEDS but may still participate in a DCFSA if they are otherwise eligible. They may also participate in a LEX HCFSA. More information on these options can be found in the Health Insurance chapter in this Guide.

DCFSA. In addition to employees eligible for an HCFSA, eligible participants for the DCFSA include employees with temporary, seasonal and intermittent appointments who are expected to work at least six months, as well as all temporary and seasonal employees.

All eligible federal employees of executive branch agencies and adopting employers may participate in the DCFSA immediately upon their entrance on duty.

An employee who is enrolled in FSAFEDS may request reimbursement for eligible health care expenses incurred by a legally married same-sex spouse or related child. In addition, childcare for a child of an enrollee's same-sex spouse is eligible under a Dependent Care FSA.

Eligible expenses

FSAFEDS covers certain over-the-counter health products. OTC products that are medicines or drugs (e.g., acne treatments, allergy and cold medicines, antacids, etc.) are not eligible for reimbursement from a HCFSA -- unless the enrollee has a prescription for that item.

The only exception is insulin which does not require a prescription. Eligible OTC items that are not medicines or drugs, such as bandages and nasal strips, will not require a prescription to be eligible for reimbursement.

Vitamins and other dietary supplements that are merely beneficial to general health are ineligible for reimbursement unless your claim is supported by a letter of medical necessity. Gym memberships may be eligible if supported by an LMN, but cannot be paid in advance of the period covered, even if your gym requires payment in advance. Medicated shampoos and orthopedic shoes may also be eligible with an LMN. All eligible expenses must be incurred during your period of coverage and must not be reimbursable through another plan.

Mileage and parking costs for cars and motorcycles and actual fare for buses, taxis, subways, and trains for travel to and from health care providers, hospitals, pharmacies, and other places you receive medical care can be reimbursed through your HCFSA. The mileage rate is based on the current IRS rate for medical care. For a complete listing of eligible medical expenses, please refer to 26 USC 213(d) available in IRS Publication 502 for the tax year.

Note: While insurance premiums are included in Publication 502, they are not eligible expenses for FSA purposes. Insurance premiums, including those for Long-Term Care and Temporary Continuation of Coverage, are not eligible for reimbursement. Guidance is also available from FSAFEDS at www.fsafeds.com.

Eligible health care expenses:

  • • Cannot be taken as a deduction from your federal income tax return in any year even though they qualify as eligible expenses that could be deducted. You may not take both options -- you must either declare them on taxes or get reimbursed for those expenses through your FSA.
  • • Are not covered, paid, reimbursed, or reimbursable from any other source.
  • • Do not exceed the amount allotted for your HCFSA for the plan year.
  • • Do not include reimbursements for premiums for other health insurance.
  • • While not limited to the dollar amount in your HCFSA at the time a claim is reimbursed, are limited to the total amount you elected (minus any amounts you have been reimbursed for claims submitted earlier in the plan).

Eligible dependent care expenses are:

  • • Limited to amounts paid for services rendered in your home or amounts paid for services rendered outside of your home for the care of a qualified dependent. The services rendered must be necessary to allow you and your spouse to work, look for work, or attend school.
  • • Limited to the amount in your DCFSA at the time a claim is reimbursed.
  • •Cannot be taken as a federal income tax deduction even if otherwise eligible. You must either declare them on taxes or get reimbursed through a DCFSA.
  • •Not covered, paid, reimbursed, or reimbursable from any other source.

FSA program reimbursement

Claims may be submitted online, through the FSAFEDS App, or by fax or mail. Most claims are processed within one to two business days after they are received and verified. Payments are sent shortly thereafter.

You must have a receipt or an explanation of benefits from your insurance carrier for each health care claim you submit against your account. All receipts must include these five pieces of information:

  • • Patient's name. The name of the person who received the service or for whom the item was purchased. For retail store purchases, this information may be excluded.
  • • Provider's name. The provider that delivered the service or the merchant where the item was purchased.
  • • Date of service. The date when services were provided or the item was purchased.
  • • Type of service. A detailed description of the service provided or item purchased. A pharmacy prescription receipt is sufficient for prescriptions.
  • •Cost. The amount paid for the service or product and/or the portion that is not reimbursed through your insurance carrier.

For some expenses, a Letter of Medical Necessity Form (available on the FSAFEDS website) from a doctor may be required.

FSA automatic reimbursement

If you participate in paperless reimbursement, your FEHB plan or FEDVIP plan automatically forwards your claims to FSAFEDS each week. It may take up to 10 to 12 business days from the time your FEHB plan submits your claim until your funds are deposited into your account via electronic funds transfer – otherwise known as direct deposit.

Ask FMG

Q. Why should I use an FSA for health care expenses rather than deducting the expenses on my income tax return?

A. Only medical expenses that exceed 7.5 percent of your adjusted gross income can be deducted on your federal income tax form. However, FSAs are tax-free from the first dollar. You do not have to meet the 7.5 percent AGI minimum before receiving the deduction. Further, money set aside through an FSA is also exempt from FICA (Social Security and Medicare) taxes. This exemption is not available on your federal income tax return.

Q. My spouse has an FSA program at work too. Can I still contribute the full limit to the HCFSA even if my spouse is contributing at their workplace?

A. Yes, you can. There is no household limit on the amount that can be set aside for a Health Care FSA. The only limits are based on both specific plan limits; for instance, in FSAFEDS you can only elect up to the maximum limit for either account. However, the total that can be elected for Dependent Care FSA must not exceed the maximum dollar amount per household, in accordance with IRS rules.

Q. Must I have the full allotted amount in my HCFSA account before I can submit claims for expenses incurred?

A. No, regardless of how much you have contributed to the account, as long as you are an active participant in the program, you will be fully reimbursed for eligible claims incurred prior to the end of the plan year, not to exceed the amount elected up to the contribution limit. If you enter a period of LWOP, your account will be frozen for service incurred during your LWOP until you resume allotments. Upon your return, you need to catch up the remaining balance of your elected amount. Your pay period allotment will be doubled until your election is current. You may also prepay your election by choosing a number of allotments that will meet your obligation prior to your leave either during open season or by contacting FSAFEDS. If you choose to prepay, you will still be considered an active participant, and any claim you submit during your period of LWOP will be reimbursed.

Q. If I didn't use all the money allotted to my HCFSA or LEX HCFSA during the year, can I get the money refunded to me?

A. Under the IRS' "use it or lose it" rule, all money left in your FSA is forfeited after the benefit period ends (Dec. 31). If you don't use all of your FSA funds during the benefit period, you risk losing money. However, under current tax rules, you can carry over up to $610 in unused funds into the next benefit period if you reenroll in FSAFEDS. Any remaining unused funds over $610 will be forfeited. This is a major reason employees need to be conservative in their estimate of how much money to allocate to an FSA.

Q. How does a LEX HCFSA save me money?

A. Using pretax dollars gives you an immediate discount on your eligible out-of-pocket dental and vision care expenses. By using a LEX HCFSA, you can preserve the funds in your Health Savings Account to use or save for other purposes.

Q. Where can I find the "use it or lose it" rule?

A. The answer is derived from looking at several sections of the Code. Section 125 of the Internal Revenue Code (26 CFR 1.125) is the section of the IRS code that makes it possible for employers to offer their employees a choice between cash salary (your regular salary) and a variety of nontaxable benefits, called qualified benefits. A qualified benefit is a benefit that does not defer compensation and that is excludable from an employee's gross income under a specific provision of the Code. Employers may offer flexible spending accounts to employees under a cafeteria plan. Contributions to the FSA occur when an employer agrees to contribute that portion to an FSA for that employee. Since you never receive the money (called constructive receipt), you can't be taxed on it. If you were able to get unused amounts out of your FSA at the end of the year, you would be receiving deferred compensation, which is expressly prohibited by Section 125. Thus, the "use it or lose it" rule.

Q. Do I have a grace period for incurring health care expenses?

A. No, there is no longer a grace period for incurring health care expenses. This means all eligible expenses must be incurred between Jan. 1 (or your effective date in the plan) and Dec. 31.

Q. Do I have a grace period for incurring dependent care expenses?

A. Yes. After the plan year ends on Dec. 31, you have until March 15 of the following year to incur eligible expenses and use the DCFSA funds remaining in your account.

Q. Do all my claims have to be submitted before the end of the plan year?

A. No. You have until April 30 to submit claims for expenses incurred during the previous benefit period for the HCFSA, LEX HCFSA, and the DCFSA. This extra time to submit claims after the benefit period ends is called a "run-out period."
