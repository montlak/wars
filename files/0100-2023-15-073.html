2. Consolidation

In addition to granting amendments of existing complaints, administrative judges also have the authority to consolidate existing complaints. As noted above, consolidation may be of two or more complaints filed by the same individual when those complaints are not like or related, or may be of two or more complaints filed by different individuals when the subject matter of the complaints is related.

The administrative judge may issue an order consolidating complaints "at any time within a reasonable period prior to the hearing." Judges' Handbook at 1-4. All of the complaints are then subsumed under one EEOC docket number. Id. Although the administrative judge has discretion to consolidate complaints, he or she may not consolidate a case pending hearing with a case before the agency if the complainant opposes consolidation. Id. at n.1.

Further, the administrative judge has the discretion as to how to supplement the hearing record on the consolidated complaints. Supplementation can take place through discovery by the parties or through an expedited investigation. The Judges' Handbook at 1-4 provides:

The Administrative Judge has the discretion to direct the agency to conduct an expedited investigation of a complaint which the Administrative Judge has consolidated with the complaint at bar. Expedited investigations may be ordered at the request of either party or sua sponte. In order to determine the parameters of the expedited investigation and how long a period of time the agency is granted to conduct it, an Administrative Judge may require a party requesting the expedited investigation to clearly identify the matters proposed to be investigated; state why an expedited investigation is the appropriate method of obtaining the information; identify and explain the time required for the expedited investigation; and include an investigative plan, stating with specificity the documents to be gathered and the witnesses to be interviewed. Prior to submitting the request, the party proposing the expedited investigation should confer with the opposing party and indicate in the request whether it is opposed. The non-moving party should respond with specific reasons when it believes that the scope of the investigation should be widened or narrowed.

If the administrative judge believes that there are complaints pending appeal at OFO that should be consolidated with complaints pending a hearing, he or she should contact OFO to seek guidance on consolidation. Id.
