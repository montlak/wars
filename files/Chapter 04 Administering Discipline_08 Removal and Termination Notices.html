REMOVAL AND TERMINATION NOTICES

It isn't easy to fire someone. HR specialists can help you put together removal and termination cases against employees and ensure they meet the legal, regulatory, and contractual requirements for these actions. But the ultimate responsibility is yours.

The term "removal" is generally used whenever a non-probationary employee is separated via the adverse action procedures used by 5 USC Chapter 43 or 5 USC Chapter 75. Removals afford employees appeal rights to the Merit Systems Protection Board. They are used not only for performance and misconduct cases, but also for cases involving loss of some status required to do a job, such as a security clearance or professional license. Removals also apply to cases of medical inability and indefinite furlough, and to cases where an employee is unable to transfer or be reassigned when a job moves.

"Termination" is the term generally applied to the separation of employees with limited or no MSPB appeal rights, such as probationary or trial period employees, employees on temporary appointments, and employees removed because of negative suitability determinations.

From the employee's point of view, there is little practical difference beyond the question of due process and appeal rights. The employee is being fired.

We're going to discuss the issue of how to deliver the decision letter. It should be done in a professional, businesslike manner, respecting the employee's expectation of privacy. There's no need to make a big production out of what is essentially an administrative function unrelated to the merits of the case. Some agencies require, either in their regulations or a union contract, that a meeting be held to personally deliver the decision to the employee and his representative(s); others simply mail it. It's your choice -- if your agency has such requirements, you have to follow them; if not, there's no reason to do more than you have to in order to ensure proper delivery of the decision.

Prepare for an effective meeting

Assuming a personal meeting is required, you, as deciding official, will be conducting it. Don't try to wing it. The point of a personal meeting with the employee is to communicate management's decision in a dignified and respectful way and to facilitate the employee's orderly departure. The meeting is not a place to discuss the events that led to the employee's removal or termination, to question or debate the employee further about those events, or to explain or justify management's decision. It's important that you confine the meeting to administrative matters such as appeal rights, etc. It is not an opportunity to re-argue the case, nor a discussion about reducing the penalty. The primary purpose of the meeting is simply to deliver the decision letter.

At this point, you need to make sure you have been over the details with HR.

Be sure to review any agency regulations pertaining to the removal of employees, to ensure you follow any unique agency or local requirements. And if the employee to be separated is a member of a bargaining unit, review your local labor agreement.

You should have the following items on hand for the meeting:

  • • The original signed and dated Notice of Decision and at least one copy for the employee to receipt.
  • • A copy of the signed and dated Notice of Decision for the employee's representative, if any.
  • • A copy of the employee's grievance and appeal rights, if any.
  • • A copy of an outprocessing checklist or other instructions from the HR office to be followed when an employee is separating from service.
  • • A copy of an SF-8, Notice to Federal Employee About Unemployment Insurance (may be included in outprocessing instructions from HR).
  • • Information about your agency's Employee Assistance Program, including its telephone number, in the event it is required. It may also be helpful to include an EAP brochure in the information packet from HR if your agency's program covers former employees for some period after their separation.
  • • A box of tissues, just in case.
  • • A sufficient number of empty boxes for personal items from desk, locker, or workbench, if the employee is to be permitted the opportunity to pack.

Details to consider

Every situation is unique, and while planning for a separation meeting, you need to consider a few details in light of the employee affected and the reasons for the termination or removal.

The "best" day to deliver a notice is early in the week, like Monday or Tuesday, not Friday. This gives the employee the rest of the regular business week to visit the unemployment office, start looking for another job, consult with and hear back from an attorney, and actively do other constructive things about the situation instead of going home or a bar, sitting and brooding about the removal over a weekend before being able to do anything. Of course, if the circumstances of the employee's removal are such that separation is required as soon as possible, then by all means do it.

Unless there are good reasons to hustle the employee out of the work area immediately after delivering the notice, it is often better to issue the notice in the morning rather than at the end of the day. Doing so allows the employee time to gather and pack up personal belongings. You will need to assess the need for a supervisor to accompany the employee during this process. It is not usually a good idea to allow a "fired" employee to have free run of the agency.

If the employee is considered a sabotage or threat risk, niceties should go out the window, and you should take some or all of the following steps as appropriate:

  • • Set an earlier termination time and remove the employee immediately upon receipt of the notice, or place/continue them on administrative leave until the effective date.
  • • Have appropriate security or other personnel waiting to escort the employee from the workplace immediately after receipt of the notice.
  • • Have your information systems and physical security personnel disable the employee's access to the systems and areas that need protection while the manager is meeting with the employee to deliver the notice.

Witnesses. Many supervisors ask if they need a witness with them when they deliver a termination or removal notice. More often than not, this question comes from simple anxiety over facing the employee. While this is perfectly understandable, the delivery of a removal notice is not a spectator activity. The meeting should be held in private, with only the employee, the employee's representative (if any), and the supervisor present.

If, given the history of the employee, the situation has the potential to get out of control (emotional yelling and screaming, for instance), you may arrange for another manager, an EAP counselor, or a security guard to be stationed close by -- but out of sight -- and ready for immediate intervention if necessary. If there is a real concern about actual physical violence, forgo the personal delivery of the notice and simply send it to the employee via another method, such as by certified mail.

Prepare responses for questions

When a supervisor delivers a termination or removal notice to an employee, the employee may have a number of questions.

Here are some of the questions most frequently asked by employees, with the information you need to answer them:

  • • Will you change your mind about this? No. The decision is final. You must not become evasive or hedge with employees on this point. The decision is made. It's a done deal. If employees are entitled to grieve or appeal management's decision, the notice of decision will so inform them, and it will be up to them to take the appropriate steps if they wish. Only one out of every four or five federal employees who are fired ever file a challenge. The odds are with the supervisor that the employee will go quietly, and the removal process will be over. However, a supervisor who waffles in answering this question may give the employee the impression that some sort of last-minute deal can be had or that the agency isn't really confident in its case, and may therefore encourage a grievance or appeal.
  • • Who can I talk to in order to get this overturned? The answer is once again: The decision is final. Any rights the employee has to challenge the decision are in the notice letter. An employee who has questions about those rights needs to talk to the HR office. You don't need to be snippy about it, but you do need to project confidence and firmness on this point.
  • • When is my last day? The employee's last day is the effective date indicated in the decision notice. If the employee is to be removed earlier than the end of the regular tour of duty, make this clear. If the employee is to be placed or continued on administrative leave, you will need to address this.
  • • When do I have to leave? Depending on the circumstances, it will either be immediately or at the end of the duty day on the effective date. If employees want to leave earlier than the time set, it is permissible to let them (on an appropriate form of leave), after they have completed any necessary outprocessing.
  • • Can I tell everyone in the office good-bye? It's up to you, depending on the circumstances of the employee's removal. But the employee's departure should be prompt and efficient.
  • • What will you tell everybody about why I'm leaving? Assure the employee that management will treat the employee's separation as a confidential matter and not say anything to coworkers or others without an official need to know. At most, coworkers will be told that the employee is "no longer with the agency." Employees, of course, are free to tell coworkers what they wish.
  • • When can I get my personal belongings? If employees are leaving on the day of the notice, have moving boxes ready, if necessary, so they can pack and take their personal belongings with them. If you cannot allow employees to pack up themselves, tell them their personal belongings will be collected, inventoried, and sent to them by mail or through an arranged pickup.
  • • Will you give me a reference? What will you say if contacted for a reference? If your agency has a specific policy on references, follow it. If not, advise employees to make the HR office the point of contact for employment references. A supervisor personally contacted for a reference will be obliged to tell the truth. Be realistic -- it's not likely that you'll give a good reference to someone you just fired.
  • • Am I eligible for rehire? Can I get a job at another federal agency? This depends on the employee's type of appointment, the circumstances of the removal, and whether the employee has competitive status. It is a question best answered for the employee by the HR office. Most career and career-conditional employees, even those removed for misconduct, are eligible for rehire because of their career or career-conditional status. Don't try to go into detail. Refer the employee to HR. The bottom line is that being eligible isn't the same as getting re-hired.

Delivering the message

Hold the delivery meeting in a private place where other employees cannot overhear. The meeting should generally take no more than 10 minutes from beginning to end. Keep in mind these key objectives:

  • • Inform the employee of the decision verbally.
  • • Deliver the written notice of decision to the employee and obtain the employee's receipt.
  • • Provide instructions for the employee's orderly outprocessing and departure.

When the time of the meeting arrives, be calm, firm and direct. Watch your actions carefully.

Do not:

  • • Be apologetic.
  • • Try in any way to imply a distance from the decision.
  • • Attempt any form of humor to soften the impact.
  • • Gloat or suggest that the employee deserves what's happening.
  • • Use confusing or annoying euphemisms for being fired such as, "We're letting you go."
  • • Read the notice of decision to the employee.
  • • Argue the merits of the case with the employee or the employee's representative.

Any questions regarding the reasons for the decision should be answered by referring the questioner to the written notice -- which the employee may read after the meeting. The manager should be prepared, however, to answer several common questions that employees tend to ask when informed of their removal or termination.

When you present the original signed notice to the employee, there should be at least one copy for use as a receipt -- or a receipt form, if your agency uses such forms. The employee should keep the original notice and should be asked to sign and date the receipt copy or receipt form. The manager should return the receipt copy or form to HR for inclusion in the official agency case file.

Warning: Don't get hung up on the signature issue. Employees are never required to sign in receipt. If the employee declines to sign, simply annotate the receipt copy or form with a remark, such as: "[Employee Name] was given Notice of Decision on [date] and opted not to sign in receipt."

If the employee is represented in the meeting, ensure that a copy is provided to the representative as well.

Present the employee with any other documents that may be required at the time of separation from service as determined by HR, such as outprocessing forms and checklists, and arrange to turn in identification cards, keys, and equipment.

Follow up

Following the meeting with the employee, you should ensure that the receipt copy of the notice of decision or receipt form is returned to HR and that the employee complies with any outprocessing or other instructions.

If the meeting was eventful, e.g., if the employee wadded up the decision letter and threw it, or engaged in some other act of misconduct, the manager should prepare a memorandum for record describing what took place and send this to HR as well. This information could be useful in the event the employee files a grievance or appeal.

Warning: Once the decision notice has been issued to the employee, all management officials involved, including those who proposed or decided the removal or termination action, must refer any questions about their decision and any requests for information or documents related to the case to their servicing HR office or to the agency attorney's office. This is necessary in the event that a grievance, appeal, or other form of litigation is filed. Only the agency representative is permitted to respond to requests for information. No management official should ever respond directly to any questions without the presence of the agency representative. Any requests for documents should be fulfilled by HR using the official agency case file it is charged with maintaining.

Alternative delivery methods

Even if your agency requires personal delivery, in some circumstances it is not possible or advisable to personally deliver a termination or removal notice to an employee. Alternative methods of delivery, such as by mail or courier, may be more appropriate. These special circumstances include:

  • • The employee is in jail or prison.
  • • The employee is hospitalized or otherwise incapacitated for duty.
  • • The employee abandoned the position.
  • • The employee is on excused absence, enforced/administrative leave, or indefinite suspension.
  • • There would be a genuine risk to the safety of personnel, equipment, systems, or a disruption to the mission, if the employee were brought into the workplace to permit a personal delivery of the notice.

The delivery alternatives are:

  • • Delivery by mail. If you are going to deliver the notice to the employee by mail, try to ensure that the notice is mailed via regular, first-class mail at least five calendar days prior to the planned effective date of the employee's removal or termination. Certified Mail is not required, but provides third-party proof of mailing. For most litigation purposes, there is a rebuttable presumption that the individual received the notice within five calendar days of mailing. If you have fewer than five calendar days remaining before the planned effective date, you do not want to rely on the five-day rebuttable presumption of receipt, or you want to make sure that the employee receives the notice on a specific date, and then send the notice via some form of express mail or delivery or by a courier service.
  • • Delivery by courier. You can deliver your notice to the employee via a courier service. Be sure to allow sufficient time before the effective date for a courier to make the delivery. HR is available to assist you with courier service.
  • • Delivery by HR staff. An ER specialist or other HR staffer may be used to affect delivery.

Do not deliver the notice by email. There is no prohibition against it, but it may raise privacy issues.

Mistakes to avoid

Removal and termination actions are never easy, but a mistake on your part can make a difficult situation a good deal worse. Avoid these mistakes when delivering a removal notice.

Do not:

  • • Wing it. You need to be prepared and must not treat a meeting for the delivery of a separation notice as a minor matter or annoyance to be dealt with between other, more important, meetings or tasks. Poor supervisory preparation reflects badly on the agency, is insulting to the employee, and creates an impression that the agency does not have its act together.
  • • Be unsure and indirect. Preparation gives you the confidence to do what needs to be done in a calm, direct, and purposeful way, and can avoid arguments, awkward moments, or answers of "I'll have to get back to you" when the employee has a question that should have been anticipated.
  • • Allow the employee to seize control of the meeting. Some employees -- or their representatives -- will try to take control of the meeting in an attempt to either derail your agenda or to provoke you into doing or saying something stupid that would cast a doubt on an otherwise proper action. They may claim superior knowledge, use artificial legalisms, or insinuate that "proper procedures" aren't being followed.
  • • Debate or justify the notice of decision. The point of the separation meeting is to communicate management's decision to the employee and not to engage in further discussion or investigation about the matters involved in the action. Entering into a discussion simply increases the chances for an argument. It may even turn the meeting into a "formal discussion" or Weingarten investigative meeting. If that happens and the union's and employee's rights are not respected, you may be looking at an unfair labor practice charge.
  • • Deliver the notice by email unless absolutely necessary. There is no prohibition on delivering a removal or termination notice via email, but it's a really bad idea that raises privacy issues.

Ask FMG

Q. Just what is a removal?

A. The term "removal" is used whenever the employee is separated via the adverse action procedures used by 5 USC Chapter 43 or 5 USC Chapter 75. Removals afford employees appeal rights to the Merit Systems Protection Board. They are used not only for performance and misconduct cases, but also for cases involving loss of some status required to do a job, such as a security clearance or professional license. Removals also apply to cases of medical inability and indefinite furlough and to cases where an employee is unable to transfer or be reassigned when a job moves.

Q. What should be the first step when delivering a removal notice?

A. The first step should be to set an effective date and then proceed accordingly. If an employee is entitled to a specific advance notice period prior to being removed or terminated from federal service -- such as the 30-day advance notice periods established under 5 USC 4303 and 5 USC 7513 for performance and conduct-based adverse actions -- do not set an effective date any earlier than the end of the required advance notice period. Removal and termination actions take effect at midnight on their effective dates unless an earlier time for separation is clearly specified in the decision notice. Also check your agency regulations and the union contract, if applicable.

Q. What is a termination?

A. "Termination" is the term generally applied to the separation of employees with limited or no MSPB appeal rights, such as probationary or trial period employees, employees on temporary appointments, and employees removed because of negative suitability determinations.

Q. Do I have to deliver the separation notice in person?

A. No, there is no legal or regulatory requirement of personal delivery. But it may be required by your agency regulations or union contract. Your HR advisor on the case can advise you of your delivery options, which include the Postal Service, an HR staffer, or courier/messenger, in addition to a personal meeting.

Q. When is the best day to deliver a notice?

A. It may be counterintuitive, but the "best" day to deliver a notice is usually at the beginning of the week, like Monday or Tuesday -- not Friday. Doing so decreases the probability of the employee engaging in potentially destructive behavior. Of course, if the circumstances of the employee's removal are such that separation is required as soon as possible, then by all means do it.

Q. Does the employee have the right to a legal representative?

A. Yes, if the employee has already been represented by legal counsel during the removal or termination action. If the employee has not been represented, there is no right to such representation at the meeting to deliver the separation notice.

Q. Does the union have the right to be present?

A. No, unless such a right is provided in the contract. The delivery of a notice of removal or termination is neither an investigative (Weingarten) meeting nor a formal discussion.

Q. What if the employee refuses to sign in receipt of the notice?

A. Don't get hung up on the signature issue. Employees are never required to sign a receipt. If the employee declines to sign, simply annotate the receipt copy or form with a remark such as: "[Employee Name] was given Notice of Decision on [date] and opted not to sign in receipt."

Q. What are the alternative delivery methods if a meeting is not required or appropriate?

A. You may send the notice of removal or termination by first-class mail, allowing at least five days for delivery before the effective date of separation. Certified Mail is even better since it provides third-party proof of mailing. Return Receipt is not necessary unless this has been an issue with the employee in question. Or you may use a courier service.
