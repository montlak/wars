29. Mental Evaluations

Subjecting an employee to a mental evaluation, even when the evaluation does not require a fitness-for-duty examination, can sufficiently aggrieve an employee to state a claim. In Zuniga v. Postmaster General, 05950709 (1997), the Commission found that the complainant's allegation that the agency had a consulting firm perform behavioral analysis on his correspondence stated a claim. The Commission found the agency's conduct was actionable, particularly where there were indications that this action by the agency was aimed at discouraging EEO activity by the complainant. As the Commission explained in Zuniga:

In support of his request to reconsider, appellant has submitted evidence (obtained from the agency through a Freedom of Information Act request) which establishes that the agency took some of his correspondence and had it evaluated by a consulting firm (the Firm) which analyzes employee behavior. One such evaluation, dated August 19, 1994, refers to two letters appellant had written, the first of which involved a complaint he had made regarding the loss of wages. The evaluation states that the letter was rated Priority 3 insofar as "there was no direct threat of violence." In the second letter, appellant complained that he was being subjected to institutional racism and that he had been denied due process. The evaluation states that the letter merited a Priority 2 rating and that both letters suggested "potential deterioration."

Appellant argues that the decision to analyze his letters was unwarranted and that the evaluations "can now be readily used against [him] adversely and serve no other purpose than to further discipline [him]...." As relief, appellant requests that the evaluations be destroyed.

...

As an initial matter, we shall address the dismissal of Issue 2. As discussed, the agency's rationale for dismissing this issue was that appellant had not been ordered to undergo a psychological examination. We find, however, that the agency has misconstrued the nature of the allegation. Specifically, what appellant alleged in his complaint was that the agency had retaliated against him when it had the Firm analyze his correspondence. Furthermore, we find that this issue states a claim. Not only has the agency maintained the Firm's evaluations in its records, but these evaluations contain unflattering characterizations of appellant's mental state. For these reasons, we find that the existence of these evaluations is harmful to appellant, and, as such, sufficient to render him aggrieved.

Furthermore, although the reason(s) for the preparation of these evaluations has not been fully articulated, there is some indication that the agency was taking actions which were designed to discourage appellant from engaging in EEO activity. Not only was the agency targeting letters in which appellant was challenging alleged discriminatory practices, but, as was alleged in Issue 1, it had a postal inspector question appellant about his EEO complaints. The Commission finds that, to the extent both Issues 1 and 2 can be construed as involving attempts by the agency to discourage appellant from pursuing his EEO complaints, they state an actionable claim.

But, where the agency has already accepted a claim that the complainant was subjected to a psychiatric examination, a claim that the agency accessed his office and computer to obtain information used to order the exam does not state a claim. Smith v. Secretary of Army, 01974029 (1998). It is unclear from the Smith decision whether the Commission would have found that the allegations stated a claim in the absence of an actual examination. The Commission did note, however, that the "appellant has not shown how he has been harmed by the mere accessing of his office and computer." Id.

Ordering an employee to report for a fitness-for-duty examination is not a sufficient injury for purposes of stating a claim where the employee is not actually required to attend the examination. McDermott v. Postmaster General, 01A30389 (2004). The order itself is merely a preliminary step to a personnel action.

Merely referring an employee to an employee assistance program does not aggrieve the employee for complaint purposes. However, failure to pay the employee for the time spent with the counselor is a sufficient injury to state a claim. Thompson v. Postmaster General, 01971702 (1998).
