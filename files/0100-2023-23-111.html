D. HOSTILE WORK ENVIRONMENT

Complaints involving allegations of a hostile work environment based on disability are analyzed the same as complaints alleging a hostile environment on other bases. A typical analysis of such complaints is provided in Fetters v. Postmaster General, 0120131553 (2013):

It is well-settled that harassment based on an individual's disability is actionable. See Meritor Savings Bank FSB v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57 (1986). In order to establish a claim of harassment under those bases, the complainant must show that: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability covered under the Rehabilitation Act; (2) he was subjected to unwelcome conduct; (3) the harassment complained of was based on his disability; (4) the harassment had the purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with his work performance and/or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment; and (5) there is a basis for imputing liability to the employer. See Flowers v. Southern Reg'l Physician Serv. Inc., 247 F.3d 229 (5th Cir. 2001); see also Fox v. General Motors Corp., 247 F.3d 169 (4th Cir. 2001). The harasser's conduct should be evaluated from the objective viewpoint of a reasonable person in the victim's circumstances. Enforcement Guidance on Harris v. Forklift Systems Inc., EEOC Notice No. 915.002 (March 8, 1994).

Upon review of the record, we note that the Agency conceded that Complainant is an individual with a disability for purposes of the Rehabilitation Act and that he is qualified. As such, we determine that Complainant has met the first prong of his claim of harassment. Furthermore, Complainant has established prong (2) of his claim of harassment in that he indicated that he was subjected to unwelcomed conduct. As to prong (3), the events raised by Complainant involved his use of a hearing device and his hearing condition. Therefore, we find that Complainant has shown that the alleged harassment occurred because of his disability. Accordingly, we determine that Complainant has met the third prong.

Turning to prong (4), Complainant asserted that from the time he first wore the hearing amplifier in the workplace in March 2009 until he had Cochlear implant surgery in August 2011, he was harassed by his co-workers and management about the amplifier. Complainant averred that his co-workers began complaining about a whistling noise apparently resulting from feedback from the device. Complainant could not hear the noise himself. Complainant asserted that multiple times during the day his co-workers would him on the shoulder, stop him from working, and tell him about the whistling. Complainant stated that this would happen every day at work for a year. Complainant noted that the co-workers would do this almost every 15 minutes over the 2-3 hours he was in the office. The co-workers would become more belligerent about the whistling and raise their voice as they yelled at him about the noise. They would also say things like "here comes the whistler" or call him "whistler."

Complainant noted that it was not just co-workers, but that management would also engage in similar activity. Complainant averred that the Postmaster, the Supervisor, the Officer in Charge and the Acting Supervisor would similarly sneak up behind him and yell at him. Complainant also asserted that the Postmaster would sneak up on him to test if he was in fact deaf. Management would tap him on the shoulder, state he was whistling, and call him into the office to discuss it.

When management would discuss the issue, they told Complainant to work without his hearing device. It happened more than once despite Complainant reported that he was told by his physician that removal of the device was the cause of a panic attack. Complainant said he told management that he "had a right to hear," but the Acting Supervisor only rolled his eyes at the comment and walked away. The Supervisor took the issue to the Safety Coordinator. The Safety Coordinator emailed the Supervisor informing him that other hearing impaired employees at the Main post office were working without their hearing aids and that they did not pose a hazard. The Supervisor provided the email to Complainant.

Complainant also asked to move his case away from the complaining employees and closer to the clerks' station. A clerk (Clerk) provided an affidavit which Complainant submitted on appeal3 in which he stated that Complainant's hearing device did not whistle all the time. He averred that the co-workers would complain at the slightest noise. He also noted that he, himself, had also joined the office in joking about Complainant until he became aware of the situation. He said he stopped engaging in the joking.

Management asserted that its actions were to address the noise abatement issue which was caused by Complainant's hearing device. The Supervisor averred that he was only responding to the complaints. Upon review, we are not persuaded by management's assertions. The Clerk averred that the whistling did not occur all the time and that the co-workers, particularly the ones who filed the hazard report, would complain about the slightest noise and were overly sensitive. The Clerk also noted he initially also made jokes about the situation. Based on the totality of the circumstances, we find that Complainant was harassed on a daily basis about the use of his hearing device from March 2009 through May 2011. The actions not only involved name-calling and nagging, but bullying tactics to try to get Complainant to take off his hearing device. Therefore, we find that Complainant had met prong (4) of his prima facie case of harassment.

As to the issue of liability, where, as here, the harassment does not result in a tangible employment action being taken against the employee, the Agency can make out an affirmative defense by demonstrating: (a) that it exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct promptly any harassing behavior; and (b) that Complainant unreasonably failed to take advantage of any preventive or corrective opportunities provided by the Agency or to avoid harm otherwise. See Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 118 S. Ct. 2257, 2270 (1998); Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775, 118 S. Ct. 2275, 2292-93 (1998); Enforcement Guidance: Vicarious Liability for Unlawful Harassment by Supervisors, EEOC Notice No. 915.002 (Vicarious Liability Guidance) (June 18, 1999) at 12. Whether an employer can prove the first prong of that defense, i.e., that it exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct promptly any harassing behavior, depends on the circumstances of the particular situation. Vicarious Liability Guidance, at 15.

Based on our review of the record, the record establishes that Complainant clearly complained to management about the treatment he was receiving at the hands of the co-workers and his supervisors. Management witnesses, although denying these were "harassment" complaints, averred that they knew that co-workers complained about Complainant, called him names and filed hazard reports based on the whistling. A mail carrier who provided an affidavit in support of Complainant on appeal, also stated that she contacted the Supervisor regarding the harassment faced by Complainant. She noted that nothing occurred. A review of management's affidavits shows that they failed to recognize that Complainant had alleged harassment. Therefore, despite Complainant informing management of the harassment, Agency failed to take any prompt action. Accordingly, because the Agency has not proven its affirmative defense, the Commission finds that it is liable for the harassment of complainant that occurred and reverses the Agency's determination of no discrimination as to Complainant's claim of disability-based harassment.
