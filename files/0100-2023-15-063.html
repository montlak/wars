17. Test for Full Relief

The proper test to determine whether full relief has been ordered is: "if the case proceeded to final action on its merits with a finding of discrimination, would there be more relief to which the appellant is entitled than the agency has offered." Swenson v. USPS, 01872266 (1988) (removal of a letter of warning and written assurances of equal treatment constituted full relief in a reprisal complaint where a decision on the merits would not have resulted in back pay). For the purposes of determining full relief, the complainant's allegations must be accepted as true. See Meredith v. Postmaster General, 05890714 (1989). Full relief must "encompass[ ] both actions to eliminate the effects of discrimination upon the particular complainant and steps to ensure that similar discrimination does not occur in the future." Smith v. Secretary of Army, 01913188 (1991).

In Jackson v. Attorney General, 01A23736 (2003), the Commission substantially clarified its position on offers of full relief, holding that the case must be evaluated in terms of whether the relief granted in the agreement would render the case moot. In Jackson, the Commission held:

On November 9, 1999, revised regulations governing the EEOC's federal sector complaint process went into effect. These regulations apply to all federal sector EEO complaints pending at any stage in the administrative process. Under these revisions, the Commission eliminated the dismissal grounds of failure to accept a certified offer of full relief from the regulations. Therefore, agencies may no longer dismiss complaints due to a complainant's failure to accept a certified offer of full relief. Furthermore, in addressing the issue of non-compliance with a Poirrier offer of full relief, the Commission held that this regulatory change results in eliminating the elements of an offer of full relief from "any acceptable analytical framework." See Tripicchio v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01993307 (November 28, 2001); request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05A20432 (May 17, 2002).

In the instant case, we find that the "Offer of Full Relief" brokered by the AJ constitutes a Poirrier Offer of Full Relief pursuant to Poirrier v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01933308 (May 5, 1994). That is, we find that the record reflects that the July 3, 2001 Offer of Full Relief contained all of the relief specified by the AJ during the pre-hearing settlement conference, and that complainant, at that time, accepted it as such.

Therefore, because the new regulations have, in effect, eliminated the "offer of full relief," the issue of compliance with the agency's "Poirrier offer" of full relief becomes an issue of whether the captioned complaint was properly dismissed on the grounds of mootness pursuant to 29 C.F.R. 1614.107(a)(5). See Miller v. Department of Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01993839 (February 14, 2002); Nicholson v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A22195 (July 24, 2002).

The regulation set forth at 29 C.F.R. 1614.107(a)(5) provides for the dismissal of a complaint when the issues raised therein are moot. To determine whether the issues raised in complainant's complaint are moot, the fact finder must ascertain whether (1) it can be said with assurance that there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur; and (2) interim relief or events have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination. See County of Los Angeles v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 631 (1979); Kuo v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05970343 (July 10, 1998).

In this case, the agency agreed to undertake the following actions:

1. The Agency will cease and desist from requesting documentation from the complainant to support sick leave under circumstances where other employees are not required to do the same.

2. The Agency will post a reaffirmation of its support for EEO principles. The posting will not reference this case, admit to any liability of the Agency, or state that any discrimination action has occurred.

3. The Agency will provide diversity and sensitivity training to supervisors and employees.

4. The Agency agrees that it will not retaliate against the complainant for his protected activity under Title VII.

5. The Agency will ensure that the officials responsible for personnel decisions and terms and conditions of employment will abide by the requirements of all federal equal employment opportunity laws.

6. The agency will not in any manner restrain, interfere, coerce, or retaliate against any individual who exercises his or her right to oppose practices made unlawful by, or who participates in proceedings to, federal equal employment opportunity law.

7. The Agency will award three hundred dollars ($300.00) to the complainant in order to compensate him for his emotional pain and suffering.

We find that complainant does not claim that the agency failed to uphold any of the above provisions, nor does he present any evidence, beyond mere speculation, that the agency will repeat the purported violation set forth in the complaint. Therefore, we conclude that the remedial measures reflected above, when responsibly implemented by the agency, will have the effect of preventing a recurrence of the violation at issue, and will also serve to eradicate the effects of the alleged discrimination. Furthermore, we concur with the AJ that complainant presented no evidence to demonstrate that he incurred compensatory damages as a result of the alleged discrimination, not withstanding the agency's gratuitous payment of $300.00 to complainant for "emotional pain and suffering." See Jackson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01923399 (November 12, 1992); request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05930306 (February 1, 1993).

As previously noted, the remedial measures identified above, when responsibly implemented by the agency, would meet the two-pronged County of Los Angeles v. Davis test. However, the present record does not contain any evidence reflecting that the agency has expressly implemented these remedial measures. Accordingly, the agency's determination is VACATED, and this case is REMANDED to the agency with instructions to comply with the settlement actions set forth above, as specified in the ORDER below.

In determining what constitutes full relief, the focus is on the claims raised by the complaint and not on the relief sought by the complainant. In Elshamy v. Secretary of Navy, 01942237 (1994), the Commission explained that it is the scope of legal relief to which a complainant would be entitled and not the relief requested that controls:

The Commission finds that the agency's offer of full relief was less an offer of relief and more akin to a final decision dismissing appellant's complaint on the grounds that one claim was moot and the remainder failed to state a claim. In this regard, we note first that the agency's offer provided appellant with no relief. Rather, it merely informed appellant that his request for a private office was moot because that agency gave him one after he filed his complaint and that appellant was not entitled to the other forms of relief that he identified in his complaint--support when he disciplines subordinates and a certain level of staffing--because they were matters wholly within agency discretion.

Second, we reiterate that full relief must be evaluated in terms of a complainant's entitlement if s/he were to prevail on all of the allegations in his/her complaint and tailored to cure the discrimination identified therein. The agency's offer in this case focused not on the allegations appellant raised in his complaint, but rather the remedy he sought. The agency thus did not address appellant's allegation that the Executive Officer undermined his supervisory authority, addressed him in a demeaning and inappropriate manner, or encouraged a subordinate to be disrespectful. In the absence of relief carefully tailored to these allegations, the Commission finds that the remedy proffered by the agency did not constitute make-whole relief as described in Albemarle Paper Co., supra.

In Elshamy v. Secretary of Navy, 01941585 (1994), the Commission further expanded on the obligation to evaluate the claims raised instead of the relief sought, as well as upon the fact that the relief offered must provide some relief to the complainant:

In its settlement offer, the agency stated that management will continue to ensure a working environment free from discrimination; and all agency employees should work together to achieve a working environment free from discrimination. There is no provision in the settlement offer providing appellant with any corrective action to cure the alleged discrimination against appellant. Rather, the agency denied that management's actions taken against appellant were based on any discriminatory factors; and it also refused to support appellant's authority toward his subordinates. Upon review, we find that the agency's offer was not specifically tailored to cure or correct the particular source of the identified discrimination and minimize the chance of recurrence. See Alphonse Webb v. Department of Army, EEOC Request No. 05920831 (January 7, 1993). Specifically, the agency's offer should have provided appellant with assurances that he would not be subjected to harassment by his supervisory officials identified as responsible for the harassment. For example, the agency could have offered not to undermine appellant's supervisory authority concerning his subordinate's transfer and his subordinate's attending a meeting. The agency could also offer not to talk with appellant's subordinates behind his back, and not to single him out to verify his attendance and work conduct. After a review of the record, we find that the remedy proffered by the agency did not constitute make whole relief as described in Albemarle Paper Co., supra.

Although this may seem self-explanatory, something must actually be ordered as relief. A letter setting forth actions the agency already has taken and explaining that additional actions are not feasible because of the employee's resignation is not an offer of relief. Felton v. Secretary of Navy, 01944826 (1994). "An offer is essentially a promise to do or refrain from doing some specified act in the future in exchange for another promise or act." Id.
