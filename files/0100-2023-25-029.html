3. Bona Fide Occupational Qualification

As with other protected categories, the Commission construes the use of bona fide occupational qualifications as a narrow exception to the general rule barring discrimination on the basis of age. 29 CFR 1625.6(a). Any such qualifications must be job specific and "reasonably necessary to the normal operation of the particular business." Id.

As discussed above, Congress specifically provided that some federal jobs may use age per se as a bona fide occupational qualification. Age can present some special considerations, particularly in the area of public safety, as noted in 29 CFR 1625.6(b):

An employer asserting a BFOQ defense has the burden of proving that (1) the age limit is reasonably necessary to the essence of the business, and either (2) that all or substantially all individuals excluded from the job involved are in fact disqualified, or (3) that some of the individuals so excluded possess a disqualifying trait that cannot be ascertained except by reference to age. If the employer's objective in asserting a BFOQ is the goal of public safety, the employer must prove that the challenged practice does indeed effectuate that goal and that there is no acceptable alternative which would better advance it or equally advance it with less discriminatory impact.

Agencies will, thus, have somewhat more latitude when asserting a BFOQ based on age, but must still be able to make a demonstration that the qualification is reasonably related to the performance of the position and cannot be screened out by some other reliable means.

Although the establishment of a maximum age for the appointment of law enforcement officials is a bona fide occupational requirement, a question can arise as to the applicability of the requirement when a criminal investigator who is over age 35 applies for a law enforcement position with a different agency. In DiLuglio v. Secretary of Defense, Inspector General, 05930890 (1994), the Commission remanded such a case for further investigation and briefing on the issue. In the DiLuglio case, the Commission also held that a claim is stated when the employee alleges the agency grants waivers of the age limitation on a discriminatory basis:

The next issue is whether the agency correctly determined that 5 USC § 3307(d) is an exception to the ADEA's prohibition against using age as a factor in an employment decision. Section 3307(d) provides, in relevant part, as follows:

The head of any agency may determine and fix the minimum and maximum limits of age within which an oriGINAl appointment may be made to a position as a law enforcement officer...as defined by section 8331(20)...of this title.

Id. (Supp. 1993).

A law enforcement officer is defined as an employee--an officer appointed in the civil service, who investigates, apprehends, or detains individuals suspected or convicted of federal crimes. See 5 USC §§ 2105(a), 8331(20). The Office of Personnel Management has been designated by Executive Order as the entity to concur with a federal agency when it makes determinations fixing the age limits within which oriGINAl appointments may be made for law enforcement officers. See Exec. Order No. 11817 (November 5, 1974), 39 Fed. Reg. 39427 (1974); Exec. Order No. 12107 (December 28, 1978), 44 Fed. Reg. 1055 (1978). The legislative history of 5 USC § 3307(d) demonstrates that Congress sought to create a special age entry rule--an exception to the ordinary prohibition against age--based determinations under the ADEA--where a young work force was necessary due to the nature of the work, and where law enforcement officers would receive especially generous retirement benefits as an offset to a shorter work life.

Yet, an "oriGINAl appointment" is not defined explicitly in Title 5 of the U.S. Code. Nor is the legislative history of 5 USC § 3307(d) or related provisions absolutely conclusive. See S. Rep. No. 93-948, reprinted in 1974 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 3698, 3698-3707; S. Rep. 92-774, reprinted in 1972 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 2287, 2287-2295. Further, we observe that while the Federal Personnel Manual regularly uses the terms "oriGINAl appointment" and "initial appointment," it does not provide a definition of either term.

While some courts have addressed the "oriGINAl appointment" issue, there is no case directly on point, given the facts of the case before us. In Francke v. Department of the Treasury, 721 F. Supp. 47, 52 (S.D.N.Y. 1989), the complainant, an Import Specialist with the Department of Treasury, Customs Service, applied in 1986 for a position within the agency as a law enforcement officer. Agency regulations had set an age limit of thirty-five years for oriGINAl appointments to law enforcement positions. The Import Specialist, over thirty-five years of age in 1986, had previously worked as a law enforcement officer with the agency from 1970 to 1973, when he was under thirty-five. The Import Specialist argued that his application as a law enforcement officer in 1986 was therefore not an "oriGINAl appointment" under 5 USC § 3307(d), and the agency's age entry rule did not apply to him. The court disagreed, however, stating that such a reading of the law was not "sensible." Id.

According to the court, if this were a proper reading of 5 USC § 3307(d), then "simply by holding a law enforcement job while under age [thirty-five] an employee could guarantee himself the chance to apply for law enforcement positions throughout his career." Id.

In a similar case, Benford v. Frank, 943 F.2d 609 (6th Cir. 1991), an over thirty-five postal Police Officer applied for a position with the U.S. Postal Service as a Postal Inspector, within the Postal Inspection Service, the law enforcement division of the Postal Service. Although the court did not expressly address the "oriGINAl appointment" issue, it applied 5 USC § 3307(d), holding that the Postal Service's policy of refusing to appoint anyone over thirty-five years of age did not violate the ADEA.

In the case before us, appellant, a Criminal Investigator, GS-1811-13, with the Department of the Interior, applied for another position as a Criminal Investigator, GS-1811-14, at the Department of Defense. Appellant was fifty-one years old at the time. While we agree with the agency and the previous decision that 5 USC § 3307(d) is an exception to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, we find that there is a significant question as to whether there was an "oriGINAl appointment" in the case before us. The Commission concludes that due to the important nature of this legal issue on "oriGINAl appointment," because it is one of first impression, and because there are factual determinations yet to be made related to this issue, we will remand this case for a supplemental investigation and to give the parties an opportunity to explicitly address in detail whether appellant's application to the Department of Defense for a law enforcement position in the same job series, at a higher grade, was an "oriGINAl appointment" for purposes of the exception to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, found in 5 USC § 3307(d).

Age Waivers

As a final matter, we find that the previous decision properly reversed the agency's final decision which rejected the appellant's allegation of disparate treatment in the manner in which the agency granted age waivers to some individuals. Appellant has raised an allegation of discrimination in the application of the age waivers by the agency. Therefore, we find that appellant is aggrieved and has stated a claim pursuant to 29 CFR § 1613.212(a) that must be processed.
