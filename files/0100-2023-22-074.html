2. Foreign Languages/Bilingual Requirements

In some instances, fluency in a language other than English is either required, or highly desirable. When fluency in a foreign language is a bona fide position requirement, it is not necessarily discriminatory that a certain portion of the workload would fall on persons of a certain national origin. In Ortiz v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01852450 (1987), a Hispanic employee complained that an inordinate amount of the workload was placed on him and two fellow Hispanics because they were required to interview all Spanish-speaking claimants. The Commission found no discrimination where none of the non-Hispanic employees spoke Spanish and the evidence demonstrated that the non-Hispanic employees were required to relieve the Hispanic employees of some of their interviews with English-speaking claimants. See also Compliance Manual, 13-V, B2.

When it is relevant to the duties of the position, an agency may legitimately consider an applicant's knowledge and skill in communicating with a particular community. Davis v. Attorney General, 01931059 (1993). In that case, the particular community was Hispanic and the Commission noted it was not "unforeseeable" that a Hispanic would be selected. Id. However, there was no evidence that non-Hispanics with the skills of the selectee would not have been considered.

In Barnes v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120082532 (2011), the Commission found that a Hispanic meteorologist was not the victim of national origin discrimination when he was required to provide assistance in responding to inquiries from Spanish-speaking media during weather-related events. The complainant alleged that this required him to perform additional work not required of non-Spanish speaking, non-Hispanic employees. The Commission found that the agency's explanation--that it needed bilingual staff to provide a complete and thorough description of weather events to Spanish-speaking media and government agencies--was a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for giving the complainant the additional duties.

Illa S. v. EEOC, 0120172769 (2017), involved a proposed class of 142 bilingual investigators who were allegedly discriminated against when the agency engaged in a "pattern and practice of discrimination" by imposing additional duties and requiring higher expectations of performance without commensurate compensation. The Commission in Illa S. found that the claim failed the numerosity test because "bilingual" is not a protected class of persons:

Here, Complainant maintains that all 142 Bilingual Investigators employed nationwide by the Agency comprise the potential class. However, in order to constitute a viable EEO claim, Complainant must assert that discrimination against a protected class has occurred. In this case, Complainant ostensibly alleges discrimination based on national origin, but Bilingual Investigators can be of various national origins, including the United States. Further, a bilingual individual is not of necessity bilingual in a language associated with that individual's national origin. Complainant has not provided any information regarding the national origins of the Bilingual Investigators in the proposed class. To the extent that Complainant attempts on appeal to narrow the class to only Bilingual Investigators of Hispanic national origin, she still has not stated even an estimate of the number of Bilingual Investigators that fit into this category. As a result, we find that the total number of the class cannot be approximated. See Joel P. v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0120120181 (Oct. 13, 2017) (numerosity not established where Complainant asserted that thousands could be in proposed class but provided no evidence to support his assertion); Thompson v. Tennessee Valley Authority, EEOC Appeal No. 01A34535 (Sept. 23, 2004) (while class agent alleged 34 class members, no evidence showed that class members and class agent had similar bases and issues; putative class therefore could not be approximated and class agent failed to establish numerosity). Consequently, we find that Complainant has failed to establish the numerosity requirement.
