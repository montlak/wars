L. DRUG AND ALCOHOL TESTS

In Earl F. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2019004345 (2020), the complainant alleged disability discrimination when the agency terminated him for failure to complete a drug and alcohol test. He disclosed to the agency that he suffered from Generalized Anxiety Disorder prior to being hired. During the drug test, the complainant claimed to have experienced Paruresis ("shy bladder" syndrome) which he attributed to his anxiety disorder:

During the test, for over three hours, he drank 36 ounces of water and made three attempts to produce a urine sample without success. Complainant recounts asking the technicians collecting the samples if he could have a blood test instead, and they just laughed. After three hours, he was instructed to go back to work, and provided with the desk and cell phone numbers of a Human Resources Specialist with expertise on the drug testing procedures, as the on-site proctor was unsure what to do. Complainant "repeatedly" called and left voice mails at both numbers on July 18 and 19, 2016 with no response.

On July 19, 2016, Complainant was notified that because he was unable to produce urine for the random drug test, he must undergo an evaluation by a board-certified physician specializing in urology and/or nephrology within five days and submit the results to the Agency. Afterward, one of the Agency's contracted Medical Review Officers ("MROs") would assess the evaluation to determine whether Complainant had a valid medical explanation or condition to excuse his failure to produce a sample. Under Agency policy, "a medical condition includes an ascertainable physiological condition (e.g., a urinary system dysfunction) or a medically documented preexisting psychological disorder but does not include unsupported assertions of "situational anxiety" or "dehydration." If Complainant could not demonstrate a valid medical reason, he would be considered to have refused testing and face termination.

On July 28, 2016, Complainant was evaluated by a urologist. When completing the "Shy Bladder Evaluation" worksheet for Complainant, the urologist checked the option stating that "a medical condition has, or with a high degree of probability could have, precluded the employee from providing a sufficient amount of urine for a federally mandated drug test." As the basis for his conclusion, the urologist stated, "patient has baseline anxiety which in my opinion can bring out shy bladder syndrome when urine is needed on demand." The urologist also provided, under the "notes" portion of the evaluation documents, "[i]t is unreasonable to ask this individual with his situation to void on demand. He would need to prepare with hydration and be given at least 6 hours to void in a situation like this."

On August 11, 2016, an MRO reviewed the urologist's evaluation and determined that Complainant's Shy Bladder Evaluation was not supported by "adequate medical basis" and deemed Complainant's inability to produce a urine sample as "refusal to test."

Complainant attempted to explain his "shy bladder" condition to Human Resources and the Agency's Assistant Federal Security Director ("AFSD") (born 1960; testified he had previously engaged in mediation with Complainant over a prior EEO complaint) and provide additional medical documentation, including results from another drug and alcohol test. On August 19 and 23, 2016, the AFSD declined to discuss the matter with Complainant unless it concerned nonmedical mitigating factors, indicating all medical decisions were made by an MRO.

The agency terminated the complainant for "refusal to test." Before the Commission, the complainant alleged his termination amounted to disability discrimination. The agency moved for summary judgment, arguing that there was no disparate treatment--the agency terminates all employees who refuse to take mandatory drug tests. The decision notes that the complainant raised a reasonable accommodation argument in its response to the motion for summary judgment and that the AJ affirmatively declined to consider it in Earl F.:

In granting the Agency's motion for summary judgment, the AJ affirmatively denied consideration of the issue of reasonable accommodation raised in Complainant's response to the Agency's motion. Instead, the AJ analyzed the complaint solely as a claim of disparate treatment regarding the decision to terminate Complainant's employment, concluding there was no evidence that Complainant was treated any differently than any other TSO who had "refused" to take the random drug test. Complainant, however, has argued in opposition to summary judgment, and on appeal, that the Agency denied his reasonable accommodation requests in connection with taking the random drug test. He did not ask to be exempt from testing, but rather asserted that equally effective alternative testing methods could have been provided as accommodations to the symptoms of his known disability such as a blood test or extra time to take the urine test. Complainant points out that if he had been able to complete the test with one of these accommodations, the decision to terminate his employment for "refusing" to the test would never have occurred. In her decision, however, the AJ declined to address the accommodation issue, reasoning, without elaboration, "even if an agency's failure to engage in the interactive process stated a claim for relief, any alleged denial of a reasonable accommodation is not an accepted issue in this case." AJ Decision of April 10, 2019, footnote 3. After careful review of the record, we conclude that the AJ made a critical procedural error in making the decision not to address the reasonable accommodation issue.
