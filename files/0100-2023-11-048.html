1. Advice by Third Parties

Agency actions that mislead an individual concerning his EEO rights will toll the limitations period. However, where the allegedly misleading statements were made by fellow employees, the time limit will not be tolled. Santos v. Secretary of Navy, 01955477 (1996) (rejecting an argument that it was "common knowledge" among employees that the EEO office could not help).

An appellant may not normally rely on representations made by a third party in arguing that an agency has misled him into untimely counselor contact. However, the Commission held that the circumstances in Smith v. Secretary of Navy, 05960818 (1997), and its related cases, when considered as an aggregate, were sufficiently disturbing to warrant a remand to investigate whether, in fact, the agency had a policy or practice of providing misinformation to and/or discouraging individuals who received the job offers at issue from utilizing the EEO process. In Smith, nine employees who had been injured on the job alleged they were forced into lower level positions because of their disabilities. Each of the appellants alleged that a coworker had informed them that the EEO office would not accept their complaints because they raised workers' compensation issues. One of the appellants also asserted he had been told the same thing by an EEO employee. All of the complaints were dismissed as untimely only after the appellants had requested a hearing. Disturbed by the similarities in the allegations, the Commission vacated the dismissals and remanded the cases for further investigation in Smith noting:

We reach this holding based on several irregularities which we have observed herein: e.g., the fact that all of the present cases were procedurally rejected only after the appellants had requested a hearing by an EEOC AJ; the consistent assertions offered by all the appellants as to their impression, allegedly gleaned both from coworkers and career counselors, that they could not use the EEO process concerning the job offers; the assertions by appellant 2 and coworkers 1 and 2 regarding specific contacts they made with agency EEO staff in which they were allegedly either provided with misinformation concerning their EEO rights or were discouraged from using the EEO process; and the absence of specific testimony from agency EEO staff members or the career counselors cited by these individuals concerning their contacts with the appellants and coworkers 1 and 2.

Based on the foregoing ambiguities, and on our impression that it is unusual that such a large number of employees would so consistently claim to have been misled regarding their EEO rights, we will exercise our discretion pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.407(a) to VACATE the previous decisions and REMAND these complaints for a consolidated supplemental investigation. On remand, the agency shall obtain each of the specific items in our Order set forth below within sixty (60) calendar days of our decision and either reforward one or more of these cases to the appropriate EEOC District Office for a consolidated hearing on their merits or issue a final agency decision rejecting the matter(s) based upon the results of the supplemental investigation.

However, the advice of a union official does not waive the time limit for initiating counseling because it is not agency action. See Smith v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A01182 (2000).
