C. INTENTIONAL DISCRIMINATION CASES

The general analytic framework that applies to intentional discrimination cases applies to cases involving allegations of discrimination based on religion. As discussed fully in Chapter 10 under the, there are two primary methods of proving intentional discrimination: 1) circumstantial evidence; and 2) direct evidence. These are discussed fully in Chapter 10 under the subheadings "Circumstantial Evidence Cases" and "Direct Evidence or 'Mixed Motive' Cases."

Cases involving allegations of disparate treatment on the basis of religion are analyzed under the McDonnell Douglas formula. Castillo v. Department of Navy, 01853204 (1987). When the evidence of pretext in such a case involves disparaging comments made by the complainant's supervisor, the fact that the comments are not made in the employee's presence does not detract from the "seriousness or probative value" of the comments as evidence of discriminatory intent.

A complainant can make a prima facie case of disparate treatment by showing: "(1) that he is a member of a protected group...; (2) that he was qualified for the job he was performing; (3) that he was performing his job satisfactorily; and (4) he was suspended." Moore v. Secretary of Army, 01871600 (1988). Like other forms of discrimination in which the McDonnell Douglas analysis is used, the precise elements of the prima facie case will vary depending on the type of personnel action involved.

In the same case, the Commission found the complainant could make out a prima facie case of disparate treatment in the terms and conditions of employment by showing: "1) that he is a member of a protected class, 2) that was subjected to treatment adverse to his employment interests, and 3) that similarly situated employees were not subject to the same adverse treatment." The complainant alleged religious discrimination because he was not permitted to hold prayer sessions with his patients. The Commission noted a prima facie case could be demonstrated in two ways. First, the complainant could show that employees who were adherents of other faiths were allowed to incorporate prayer or a similar practice faith into patient therapy. In the alternative, the complainant could have shown that employees, "believers or non-believers, were allowed to incorporate into their patient care a mode of therapy which was not approved" by the agency. Id. In the absence of either showing, the complainant did not make out a prima facie case of disparate treatment.

In order to demonstrate that an employment action is based on religious discrimination, the employee must show that the agency officials responsible for implementing the action had knowledge of the employee's religion. See Sagalow v. Department of Interior, 01872220 (1988). The fact that the appellant was from New York--a state with a large Jewish population--did not establish that the RMO's must be aware of appellant's religion. While there was evidence that the appellant was rejected, in part, because his New York background was thought incompatible with the small town surroundings of the job in question, use of such a criterion did not amount to discrimination. See also Laber v. Secretary of Army, 01931497 (1993) (the Commission will not infer that a person is Jewish based on his last name).
