6. Physical Requirements

When a position has physical fitness requirements, these requirements can have a disparate impact on older persons. In Gallagher v. Attorney General, U.S. Marshals Service, 01941005 (1994), the Commission found the complainant failed to establish that the fitness guidelines had an adverse impact on him. However, the Gallagher case does set forth guidance on how an agency can account for age in physical fitness requirements:

Appellant argues that the use of some of the factors in the scoring system had a disparate impact on older persons. Appellant argues that he was penalized in the fitness portion of the scores because he could not perform as well as younger persons. Appellant argues that "[w]hen a twenty year [old] female is allowed to run less than I do and get the same score, that doesn't make any sense to me." The record shows that appellant completed the 1 mile walk in 14 minutes and 20 seconds while the Selectee completed the 1.5 mile run in 15 minutes and 34 seconds. Both times were rated as "good" in the fitness assessment summary. If appellant is arguing that the Selectee was allowed to run a shorter distance or at a slower pace than appellant, we do not find the record supports that assertion. The Selectee ran a further distance according to the record. Because appellant walked and the Selectee ran and because the distances were different, a direct comparison of the times is not possible.

Furthermore, the agency asserts, and the record shows, that age is factored into the fitness scores. The agency submitted a chart of the Men's Fitness Classifications showing that in all of the fitness categories a person in a higher age bracket was allowed to perform at a lesser level in order to obtain the same ranking as a younger person. For example, in order to earn a ranking of "superior" in the 1.5 mile run, a person between the ages of 30-39 would have to complete the run in under 10 minutes, while a person between the ages of 50-59 would have to complete the same run in under 11 minutes.

In McFadden v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01931593 (Sept. 9, 1993), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05940039 (Apr. 7, 1994), the Commission examined the fitness scoring system in another case involving the U.S. Marshal's Service. The Commission held in McFadden that appellant had "not established that he was held to the same fitness standards as younger workers or that the agency's contention that an employee's age is factored into the fitness score was untrue." McFadden, EEOC Appeal No. 01931593. The Commission finds the quoted statement from McFadden applicable to the instant case. The Commission finds that appellant has failed to show by a preponderance of the evidence that the use of the fitness scores had a disparate impact on older workers or that the agency's reliance on these scores was a pretext for discrimination.
