A. PRIMA FACIE CASE

The theory of adverse impact involves a showing that the facially neutral employment policies or practices of an agency have a disproportionate impact on a protected group. The appellant must show more than "isolated, accidental or sporadic discriminatory acts." Komp v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 01831271 (1983). Appellants need to demonstrate, by a preponderance of the evidence, that prohibited discrimination was "the [agency's] standard operating procedure--the regular rather than the unusual practice." Id. (citing International Brotherhood of Teamsters v. U.S., 431 U.S. 324, 97 S. Ct. 1843, 52 L. Ed.2d 396 (1977)). Cases involving adverse impact must be distinguished from cases involving disparate treatment. The difference is one of intent versus effect.

In cases of adverse impact, it is not necessary for the appellant to prove discriminatory intent. Rather, where the appellant establishes the adverse impact of an employment practice upon the protected class, a rebuttable presumption of discrimination is raised. The employer must then demonstrate that the practice is a business necessity. As the Commission found in Weinrauch v. Department of Treasury, 01790210 (1983):

Claims of disparate treatment may be distinguished from claims that stress "disparate impact." The latter involve employment practices that are facially neutral in their treatment of different groups but that in fact fall more harshly on one group than another and cannot be justified by business necessity.

Id. (quoting International Brotherhood of Teamsters v. U.S., supra).

In the Weinrauch case, the EEOC found the appellant demonstrated a prima facie case of age discrimination by showing the agency's practice of giving more credit for college education than actual work experience had a disproportionate impact on older workers who were less likely to possess a college education but had significantly more work experience. The agency failed to advance any business necessity as a reason for giving more weight to college education in relation to the particular positions at issue.

To establish a prima facie case of adverse impact, the employee must show that he is a member of a protected group and that he has been subjected to a neutral employment criterion or policy which disproportionately or adversely affects the protected class. See Mulrooney v. General Service Administration, 01842063 (1986). The employment practice or policy must be facially neutral for analysis under the adverse impact theory to be appropriate. See Pratt v. Attorney General, 01972502 (2000) (agency's analysis under the adverse impact theory was improper because the policy prohibiting detainees from being escorted by members of the opposite sex was discriminatory on its face).

Statistical evidence may be relied upon to establish a prima facie case of adverse impact. LaPalme v. USPS, 01840080 (1986). In LaPalme, the employee showed a prima facie case of adverse impact when it was demonstrated that 19 out of 20 employees reassigned from Los Angeles to New York were white males.

In Barela v. Secretary of State, 01965017 (1997), the Commission set forth the basic elements of a prima facie case of adverse impact discrimination:

To establish a prima facie case of race and national origin discrimination under a disparate impact theory, a complainant must show that the challenged practice or policy disproportionately impacts members of the protected class. Specifically, a complainant must: 1) identify the specific practice or practices challenged; 2) show statistical disparities; and, 3) show that the disparity is linked to the challenged practice or policy. Watson v. Fort Worth Bank and Trust, 487 U.S. 977 (1988).

In order to do a proper statistical evaluation for an adverse impact claim, an agency may have to consult the OPM Occupation Cross-Clarification Table to determine the applicable labor force statistics. The Table helps establish the proper external comparative group. As the Commission explained in Minton v. Attorney General, 05950616 (1996):

[T]he Commission finds that the agency failed to conduct a proper labor market or statistical analysis on the challenged employment practice. The proper analysis for determining the impact of the challenged practice should have included the specification of the appropriate data for the at-issue position in the General Schedule position and an analysis of the difference between the appropriate external and internal comparative groups.

Specifically, the agency should have examined the Census/OPM Occupation Cross-Clarification Table by OPM Occupational Code (1993) for data concerning paralegal specialists in the external comparative group. According to the table, the proper external comparative group for paralegal specialists is Census occupation 234, Legal Assistants, working in Houston. That classification includes all persons doing work similar to Federal paralegal specialists, both in the private and public sector. The appropriate internal comparative group consists of Paralegal Specialists, GS-950, employed by the agency in Houston. The data concerning the internal comparative group is available in the U.S. Office of Personnel Management, Central Personnel Data File. With such comparative data, the agency would then conduct a statistical analysis to determine whether the proportion of blacks in the external comparative pool was significantly different from the proportion of blacks in the internal GS-950 series pool.

In Minton, the Commission found, however, that the agency's error was harmless as even the proper comparison did not establish an adverse impact.

In Van De Car v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01971656 (2000), the complainants alleged they were discriminated against when the agency implemented the Nurses Pay Act of 1990 in a manner that resulted in their pay being less competitive than nurses under the age of 40. The Nurses Pay Act required all VA hospitals to survey the local geographic area and set pay for nurses at competitive rates. The Van De Car case consisted of the consolidated complaints of three Intermediate Enhanced Qualifications or Assignments (IEQA) nurses over the age of 40. They presented evidence that the agency implemented the Pay Act in a manner that resulted in smaller increments in pay increases for IEQA nurses than for entry level nurses, and presented evidence that 73% of the nurses classified as IEQA were over the age of 40. The Commission concurred with the AJ's findings that the complainants established a prima facie case in Van De Car:

The disparate impact occurred in the change to IEQA nurse pay schedule, which, she found, did not make them as competitive with nurses in the private sector as it did the entry level nurses. This affected mostly nurses over the age of 40, because, statistically, most nurses at the intermediate IEQA level of pay were over age 40. Under the agency's implementation of the Pay Act, the AJ found that maximum pay at the intermediate level increased by a smaller increment compared to the entry level. . . . Entry level nurses were competitive with their private sector counterparts at both the beginning and ending salary steps.

The complainants in the Van De Car case presented extensive statistical evidence demonstrating the differences in the salary increases between entry level and intermediate level nurses both before and after the Pay Act was implemented.

Conversely, the Commission found a requirement that applicants for a lawyer's position had to have graduated from an American Bar Association accredited law school did not have a disparate impact on black applicants where no statistical evidence of disparate impact was presented, the agency presented evidence that it hired black attorneys at a higher rate both locally and nationally, and the agency had a process for waiving the ABA requirement under certain circumstances. Lacy v. Secretary of Army, 0120073777 (2010).

As part of the complainant's prima facie case, he or she must demonstrate that any statistical disparity is the result of a specific employment practice. See 42 USC 2000-e(2)(k)(1)(A). Showing a mere statistical disparity is not sufficient. Nothing in the 1991 Civil Rights Act overruled Watson or Wards Cove in that regard.

This latter requirement is a difficult one for complainants to meet. In Brannon-Winters v. Secretary of Navy, 01A51549 (2006), the Commission found that the plaintiff failed to establish a prima facie case of adverse impact discrimination even though she demonstrated an underrepresentation of professional female engineers at the GS--12 and above level, and that the agency had a promotion practice that gave priority based on seniority. The complainant alleged that the policy gave an advantage to male engineers. The Commission found that the complainant had failed to present any evidence that the disparity was actually due to the agency's policy.
