A. EVIDENCE

What at first glance may seem like a new claim may simply be evidence relevant to the existing claim of discrimination. Evidence is information. It can come in the form of testimony or documents. When the information being provided relates directly to the claim or claims accepted by the agency for investigation, no problem is presented. No timelines change and no amendment or consolidation procedures are necessary. The problem occurs when the information relates to a past action of the agency or an action that has occurred after the claims for investigation have been defined.

Events that occurred before the complaint was filed or while the investigation is pending may constitute evidence relevant to the accepted claims. Particularly in cases involving claims of ongoing discrimination, such as failure to accommodate or hostile environment claims, new incidents may occur and be incorporated into the existing claim. Evidence is any event or incident that is part of the same pattern of discrimination alleged by the complainant and does not implicate any additional form of relief.

As discussed below in the section on "Amendments," the Commission has procedures to deal with new claims of discrimination that arise during the investigative stage. Briefly, the new claims should be referred to the EEO Director or Complaints Manager for a determination of how they should be processed. When new information constitutes nothing more than evidence relevant to the existing complaint, there is no need to refer the matter to the EEO Director or Complaints Manager. The information can and should be included in the investigation. The main question the investigator should ask is: If the complainant proves this new allegation, will he or she be entitled to any additional forms of relief? If there is any question as to whether or not new relief is implicated, the matter should be referred to the EEO Director or Complaints Manager.

Similarly, the complainant or others may bring forward information about events that previously occurred but were not mentioned in the counseling report or formal complaint, that relate to the underlying claim. Such information does not raise an additional claim for relief but should be included as evidence on the existing claim or claims. This type of evidence often is referred to as "background evidence." Background evidence includes incidents and events that occurred in the past and are not independent grounds for liability on the existing claim, but are nonetheless relevant to proving the existing claim.

A complete investigation includes relevant background evidence. See Ferguson v. Department of Justice, 05970792 (1999). The investigator should strive to be as inclusive as possible with regard to background evidence. Even if it is later decided that some of the evidence should not be considered during the adjudication phase, there is no harm in being inclusive. On the other hand, being under-inclusive can lead to inefficiency and increased expense for the agency because an administrative judge can either direct the agency to supplement the investigatory record, or require that the agency pay the complainant's discovery costs to supplement the record.

In Raney v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01962665 (2000), the Commission found that the administrative judge improperly excluded background evidence relevant to the complainant's hostile environment sexual harassment claim. Even though some of the alleged incidents of harassment were dismissed as untimely and not appealed by the complainant, they were still relevant as background evidence and should have been considered by the administrative judge. The Commission noted, however, that the agency could not be held responsible for any discriminatory incidents considered as background evidence because the complainant did not appeal their dismissal for being untimely. See also Sullivan v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., 01991021 (2000) ("While certain non-accepted claims involving S1's alleged conduct, such as oral abuse, denial of training, performance of menial tasks, and denial of a within grade increase, were properly dismissed for failure to timely contact an EEO counselor, they are still considered background evidence for the accepted issues.").

The bottom line is that the investigation must include all evidence relevant to the claim of discrimination. As noted in MD-110 at 5-8 to 5-9, this will often include background evidence:

If the claim itself is timely raised, the question remains as to how the agency is to treat those factual incidents that the complainant cited as evidence in support of his/her claim that occurred outside the 45-day time limit.

The answer is that an agency must consider, at least as background, all relevant evidence offered in support of a timely raised legal claim, even if the evidence involves incidents that occurred outside the 45-day time limit. This is true of supporting evidence that the complainant offered during EEO counseling as well as later in the investigative stage. During the investigation, the degree to which a certain piece of proffered evidence is relevant to the legal claim will determine what sort of investigation is necessary of that particular piece of evidence. For example, in a nonselection case, a selection decision made long before the one at issue, involving different agency officials, may have little relevance to the current claim. On the other hand, if the selecting official in the most recent nonselection also failed to select the complainant for a similar position six months before, that piece of evidence may be very relevant to the complainant's claim. Investigators should not simply disregard relevant information the complainant provided in support of his/her claim as untimely raised; nor should they send the complainant back to counseling as if the supporting evidence was a new claim to be processed as a separate complaint.

MD-110, at 5-11 also provides:

If the EEO Director or designee concludes that the new incident(s) provides additional evidence offered in support of the claim raised in the pending complaint, but does not raise a new claim in and of itself, then the EEO Director or designee should instruct the investigator to include the new incident in the investigation. A copy of this letter should be sent to the complainant unless they have provided notice that they have a representative. In such a case, the acknowledgment and all subsequent actions on the complaint should be mailed or delivered to complainant's representative with a copy to the complainant, unless the complainant has stated otherwise.

Example 4

During EEO counseling and in her formal complaint, an agency employee has alleged that her co-workers were harassing her because of her gender, and she cites five examples of harassment. During the investigation, she provides an initial affidavit detailing these incidents. Shortly thereafter, the employee contacts the investigator and tells him of several new incidents of gender-based harassment by these same co-workers. In this case, these new incidents are additional evidence offered by complainant in support of her pending claim of discriminatory harassment, and the investigator should be instructed to incorporate these new facts into his investigation of the pending claim. In this instance, the investigative period is not extended beyond 180 days, except with the consent of the complainant pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(e).

However, the Commission has held that where the dismissed claims are "distinct" and "independent" from those accepted, an investigation into those dismissed claims is "permissive" rather than "mandatory." Jimmie S. v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120141885 (2016).

As an initial matter, we note that Complainant contends that the investigation was incomplete because it did not investigate a dismissed claim as background evidence. The record reveals that in a letter dated April 27, 2012, the Agency dismissed Complainant's claim that he was denied detail opportunities and his detail was terminated in 2011. In that letter, the Agency notified the Complainant that the "information" about the dismissed claims would be treated as background information. Upon review, we note that even if discrete acts are arguably related to other discriminatory acts that occurred within the filing period, they are not actionable if untimely raised. Nat'l R.R. Passenger Corp. v. Morgan, 536 U.S. 101, 113 (2002). Nonetheless, the prior acts may be considered as background evidence to support a timely claim. Id.; see also EEOC Compliance Manual 915.003, Section 2: Threshold Issues, at 2-IV.C.1.a. (rev. July 21, 2005). However, the Supreme Court's language with regard to background evidence is permissive, not mandatory. Therefore, not all dismissed claims that could be considered background evidence must be investigated along with the accepted claim. In this case, we find that Complainant's accepted pay claim is distinct from and independent of his dismissed claims. Consequently, we do not find that the investigation is inadequate because it did not include an inquiry into the dismissed matters.
