12. Hearings

The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 brought about significant changes to the hearing and final agency decision process. The main changes are the following: (1) complainants now forward hearing requests directly to the appropriate EEOC district office instead of the agency; (2) administrative judges may no longer remand complaints or portions of complaints to the agency for supplemental investigation but can oversee supplemental investigations themselves; and (3) administrative judges no longer issue a recommended decision, but instead issue a decision requiring the agency to file an appeal with OFO if it decides not to fully implement the decision. These changes are later in this chapter under the subheading "Administrative Judge Decisions."

The hearing under Part 1614 remains essentially the same as it was under Part 1613. In the 1992 enactment of Part 1614, the Commission authorized the use of discovery prior to the hearing and set a 180-day limit from receipt of the request for a hearing for the administrative judge to issue a decision. 29 CFR 1614.109(b) and (g) (1992).

In its current state, 29 CFR 1614.108(h) provides:

Where the complainant has received the notice required in paragraph (f) of this section or at any time after 180 days have elapsed from the filing of the complaint, the complainant may request a hearing by submitting a written request for a hearing directly to the EEOC office indicated in the agency's acknowledgment letter. The complainant shall send a copy of the request for a hearing to the agency EEO office. Within 15 days of receipt of the request for a hearing, the agency shall provide a copy of the complaint file to EEOC and, if not previously provided, to the complainant.

The agency is required to advise the complainant in its acknowledgment of the formal complaint of the proper EEOC Office where the hearing request should be sent. See 29 CFR 1614.106(e).

Under Part 1614 as enacted in 1992, the employee was required to file a request for a hearing with the agency. The agency forwarded that request to the appropriate EEOC district office which then assigned an administrative judge to the case. No time period was imposed on the agency with respect to forwarding the request for a hearing to the EEOC. As a result, in many cases, there were significant delays due to an agency's failure to promptly forward the request for a hearing. Although many administrative judges would pressure agencies to forward requests when contacted by complainants, ultimately the judges were powerless to remedy the situation because, until the request was sent by the agency to the EEOC, the agency retained jurisdiction over the case. Under the 1999 amendments, the Commission has jurisdiction over the complaint at the time it receives the request for a hearing.

The complainant is required to serve a copy of the hearing request on the agency, 29 CFR 1614.108(h), and the notice of right to a hearing should include the name and address of the agency official upon whom service should be made. Complainants should be aware that the Commission requires, as part of the hearing request, a certification that service of the request has been made upon the agency. See MD-110 at App. M. In other words, serving the hearing request on the agency and certifying to the EEOC that service has been made are two separate requirements.

After the complainant requests a hearing and forwards a copy of the request to the agency, the agency must submit the complaint file to the EEOC within 15 days of receipt of the request. At this time, the agency must also forward a copy of the complaint file to the complainant if it has not already done so. See 29 CFR 1614.108(h). Under Part 1613, the agency was required to transmit the entire complaint file, including the investigation report, to the administrative judge. The administrative judge had the option of remanding the case to the agency for further investigation if he or she deemed the complaint file incomplete. See 29 CFR 1613.218(b). Under the oriGINAl Part 1614, the judge retained the authority to remand to the agency for further processing of any new claims raised by the complainant during the hearing process. 29 CFR 1614.109(a) (1992). Under the 1999 version of Part 1614, the administrative judge presides over any supplementation of the complaint file, including any amendments to the complaint. 29 CFR 1614.109(a). There is no longer any provision for remanding a complaint to the agency for supplemental investigation, and MD-110, at 7-7, expressly precludes such remands.

The administrative judge has the traditional powers of an administrative hearing officer and the hearings are conducted much like any other administrative hearing. There are, however, some differences. Hearings are considered an extension of the investigative stage and are closed to the public. See 29 CFR 1614.109(e). The purpose of the hearing is to supplement the investigatory record.

Each party has the right to call relevant witnesses and to cross-examine witnesses called by the opposing party. Documentary evidence may also be introduced. A verbatim transcript of the hearing is kept. See 29 CFR 1614.109(h). In some instances, the administrative judge may require prehearing or posthearing briefs.

Until the November 1999 amendments to Part 1614, after the closing of the record, the administrative judge issued a recommended decision to the agency head. This decision included findings of fact, analysis, conclusions of law and the recommended relief, if any. See 29 CFR 1614.109(g) (1992). This is the same process that was utilized under Part 1613. Despite repeated criticisms that the hearing process was rendered meaningless by the issuance of recommended decisions as opposed to binding decisions that are subject to appeal, the Commission, until 1999, persisted in giving agencies the final opportunity to reject the decisions of its administrative judges. Finally, with its 1998 proposed revisions to Part 1614, the Commission embraced the idea of giving its administrative judges final decisionmaking authority.

Of course, the idea had its opponents: namely, the agencies that were threatened with losing the unilateral right to determine that they had not engaged in discrimination. Agencies presented two arguments in favor of keeping the recommended decision. First, agencies argued that the decisionmaking quality of the Commission's administrative judges was poor and agency review of the decisions was needed to ensure their quality. Second, agencies focused on language in 42 USC 2000e-16 referring to final agency action on discrimination complaints to argue that the EEOC lacked the legal authority to make the decisions of its administrative judges binding on agencies. Agencies argued that only Congress could make such a change. The first argument had no merit. The issue of the quality of the decisions of administrative judges aside, allowing a party with a vested interest in the outcome of the proceeding to review the decisions did nothing to improve quality. The only thing such review accomplished was to leave complainants with a firm and understandable conviction that the process was inherently unfair. The second argument had considerably more force.

In the end, it was the second argument that moved the Commission to alter its 1998 proposal and adopt the current procedure, as discussed in the next section and reflected in the 1999 amendments to Part 1614.
