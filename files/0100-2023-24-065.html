A. AFFIRMATIVE ACTION

The agency's obligations under the Rehabilitation Act do not end with the hiring of an employee with a disability. The agency has a continuing obligation to provide opportunities for the employee with a disability to advance. In Stanton v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01811938 (1983), the Commission held:

The Rehabilitation Act of 1973 requires all government agencies of the executive branch to establish comprehensive affirmative action plans for the hiring, placement and advancement of handicapped individuals. By the 1978 amendments to the Act, Congress made it clear that non-discrimination against the handicapped was an "obligation" on the part of the federal government, not a "gratuity." Shirley v. Devine, 670 F.2d 1188, 27 FEP Cases 1148, 1154 (D.C. Cir. 1982). As the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia noted, "the rationale for any civil rights type affirmative action program is that simply preserving the status quo of past practices, even if they are not consciously discriminatory, can serve to perpetuate the effects of past discrimination, so that the effects cannot be eliminated without changing the practices." Shirley v. Devine, supra, 27 FEP Cases at 1160. The same circuit held in Ryan v. FDIC, 565 F.2d 762, 15 FEP Cases 1384 (D.C. Cir. 1977) that the Rehabilitation Act "imposes a duty upon federal agencies to structure their procedures and programs so as to ensure that handicapped individuals are afforded equal opportunity in both job assignments and promotions." In McNutt v. Hills, 426 F. Supp. 990, 15 FEP Cases 1370 (D.D.C. 1977), the court's finding of discrimination against a blind employee turned in large part on the agency's failure to take cognizance of his handicap and assist him in overcoming it. The agency's "failures to meet its obligations toward the physically handicapped on a department-wide level were an essential part of the discrimination that plaintiff suffered as an individual." McNutt v. Hills, supra, 15 FEP Cases at 1382. As the court pointed out, "discrimination against an individual is often nothing more than the manifestation of the employer's attitudes toward the class of which plaintiff is a member." Ibid.

Among the failures noted by the Commission in the Stanton case was the failure of the agency to allow the employee, who suffered from cerebral palsy, an additional seven minutes to complete a written promotional exam. The Commission also faulted the agency for failing to use a Schedule A appointment to assist the appellant, noting "[t]he goal of affirmative action is not merely the employment of a handicapped person but an appropriate placement for such an employee." Id.
