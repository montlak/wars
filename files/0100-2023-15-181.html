F. OBJECTIONS

Objections are a routine part of any hearing procedure. Since this is not a treatise on trial practice, there will be no attempt to cover the various bases on which objections may be made. However, a word about procedure is in order.

Generally, when an objection is made, unless the reason is obvious, the administrative judge will look for some explanation of the basis for the objection. The administrative judge also will permit a response to the objection before issuing a ruling. Representatives are right in forcefully arguing their positions on an objection. There are limits, however, and there comes a point in time where the proceeding must continue in accordance with the ruling, no matter how objectionable it may be. To preserve any objection for appeal, all one must do is state an exception to the ruling for the record.

The Judges' Handbook at 7-7 contains the following guidance on objections:

Objections may be made based on relevance, prejudice, or other grounds. The Administrative Judge may consult the Federal Rules of Evidence in ruling on objections. Generally, the Administrative Judge should sustain objections where the evidence is irrelevant, cumulative or unreliable. Prior to ruling on an objection, the Administrative Judge should permit the non-objecting party to respond to the objection. The Administrative Judge may take a recess or take the arguments under advisement and continue with the hearing. In ruling on objections, the Administrative Judge should remember that admission of evidence does not speak to the weight to be given the evidence once it is admitted.

There are also times when an objection must be asserted. When the complainant's representative fails to appear for the hearing, the complainant will not be heard to complain on appeal if no objection is lodged at the hearing and he proceeds with the hearing. Rawls v. Postmaster General, 01982793 (2001).

Similarly, when the administrative judge, at the outset of the hearing, asks if there are any objections to prehearing rulings and the complainant makes no objections, his later appeal of a witness ruling will prove futile. Wilson v. Postmaster General, 01A21911 (2003).

Refusing to participate in the remainder of a proceeding based on a ruling is a tactic that one engages in at great risk. It is not error for the administrative judge to cancel the remainder of the hearing and to remand the case to the agency for a decision on the record when the complainant refuses to continue in objection to a ruling. Henry v. Secretary of Labor, 01911925 (1991).
