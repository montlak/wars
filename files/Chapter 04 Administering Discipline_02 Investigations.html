INVESTIGATIONS

A key element in taking effective disciplinary actions is having your facts straight. That means a fact-gathering investigation must be conducted. The investigation could be as simple as cross-checking overtime hours with sign-in sheets. Or it could involve a criminal investigative division, undercover agents, and hidden cameras. In any case, it needs to be done right.

Managers are not generally called upon to conduct extensive investigations within most agencies. However, they often are required to develop information in connection with emerging workplace situations, even if only to determine whether further investigation is necessary.

For example, based on a complaint by a coworker, a manager may find it necessary to ask an employee whether he left work early without permission. Then it might be necessary to follow up by seeking additional information from other coworkers before deciding whether to counsel the employee or to propose disciplinary action.

Or, there may be an allegation of fraudulent travel vouchers. You as a manager might not conduct such an investigation as this is a job for the Office of Inspector General. But you would be called upon to produce evidence and arrange for employee interviews, among other things.

The key to an effective investigation is to remember that your sole purpose is to obtain information and identify relevant facts. It is not to confirm your opinions or suspicions or to accuse anyone of wrongdoing. Therefore, it's important to conduct investigations in a calm, professional, unemotional manner.

Effective investigation techniques

Managers generally conduct basic or preliminary investigations in which they ask employees direct questions to establish basic facts. If a preliminary investigation reveals a serious wrongdoing (e.g., theft) or a complicated issue (e.g., a complaint of sexual harassment), most agencies require the matter to be referred to the HR office or OIG for further investigation. The EEO office may also play a role.

You should begin your inquiry with the target of the investigation, unless the target is in a position to alter, destroy, or improperly influence information that might be available from other sources. For example, in response to a complaint of coworker harassment, it would not be appropriate to begin your inquiry with the alleged harasser. But it would be appropriate to begin with the target when investigating the possibility that an employee deliberately damaged a computer.

You should question sources or witnesses separately. It is never a good idea to interview witnesses in a group. The presence of others tends to influence individual impressions and can make witnesses unwilling to provide candid responses.

You also need to maintain witnesses' privacy and confidentiality. The information you obtain from a witness may inevitably become public knowledge, particularly if a disciplinary action results and is challenged in a hearing. However, you still must not disclose what anyone tells you in the course of an investigation.

It's important to focus on the facts. Although nothing prevents you from hearing opinions, guesses, gut feelings, and the like, you must recognize the difference between them and hard facts. You may take action on facts but not on feelings or suspicions.

Make sure to document everything you are told. Documentation doesn't have to be lengthy and formal. It also doesn't have to sound like a legal document or the report of a private investigator. But you should, at a minimum, jot down notes outlining what you learn.

It's also essential that you comply with your labor agreement during an investigation. Some labor agreements require managers to warn employees of their right to union representation before asking questions that could lead to discipline. If your agreement does, make sure you provide that warning before asking those questions. Check with your LR specialist.

It goes without saying that you must comply with the law. For example, you have to allow a bargaining unit employee to have a union representative present if the employee is being asked questions that could lead to discipline – or if the employee reasonably fears discipline – and requests assistance.

Failure to honor the employee's Weingarten rights can jeopardize your whole case. Bargaining unit employees have a statutory right to have a union representative present during an investigative interview if they reasonably believe that their answers may lead to disciplinary action, and they request such representation. Violating this right can lead to an unfair labor practice charge that, if upheld by the Federal Labor Relations Authority, could cause any resulting disciplinary action to be set aside.

Sometimes, the best way to conduct an investigation is to step back and let the experts, like your OIG and OGC, handle it.

Steps to conducting an investigation

Your job in conducting an investigation is to gather the facts from all angles so that a thoroughly justifiable conclusion can be reached. You must remain objective, unemotional, and unbiased during the investigation because even the appearance of partiality can impact employee faith in your integrity and that of your investigation. Your investigative materials may later be examined if appeal actions are taken, so it would benefit you greatly to approach each investigation with an open mind and reservation of judgment.

1. Understand the incident.

When an incident is brought to your attention, be sure you understand its entire scope. To gain a good understanding of the problem, consider:

  • • The rule that was violated. Research the rule, law, or procedure that was alleged to be violated. You want to ensure that it is clearly written, and the incident doesn't fall into a gray area.
  • • The magnitude of the violation. In some cases, you may have to involve investigative agencies such as the Office of Inspector General or the FBI. But in many instances, the issue may be a less serious violation of policy or failure to adhere to procedure in which case you can proceed on your own.
  • • The source of the information. If reported to you by another party, is the source reliable? What is the relationship of the person reporting the information to the alleged violator? Could there be an ulterior motive behind the reporting of the incident? Is it reported by someone who has or could have had firsthand knowledge? If the alleged violation is contained in documentation, is it an original or a copy? Is it signed? Did the source of information come from a reliable database?
  • • Other people. Who else was involved, had knowledge of the incident, heard or observed it?

2. Report it to your supervisor.

Whenever you receive information that may warrant an investigation and you have a good grasp on the issue, always report it to your supervisor and HR. You may receive further guidance on how to proceed if it's determined that investigative agencies need to be notified. At the very least, keeping your supervisor in the loop won't blindside her if the investigation reaches her desk or adverse action is needed.

3. Interview the players.

With the green light given to conduct the investigation, interview those involved. You should begin your inquiry with the target of the investigation, unless the target is in a position to alter, destroy, or improperly influence information that might be available from other sources.

You should question sources or witnesses separately. Interviewing them in groups is not recommended. The presence of others tends to influence individual impressions and can make witnesses unwilling to provide candid responses. During your interviews, you may come across information that may lead you to other interviews. Decide which ones are pertinent and follow up.

It is essential that you comply with your labor agreement and the law during an investigation. For example, you have to allow a bargaining unit employee to have a union representative present if the employee is being asked questions that could lead to discipline -- or if the employee reasonably fears discipline -- and requests assistance. Check with your LR specialist beforehand.

Note: Make sure to document everything you are told. Documentation doesn't have to be lengthy or sound like a legal document. But you should, at a minimum, jot down notes outlining what you learn.

4. Follow the trail.

In addition to following leads for people to interview, many violations will have some sort of paper or electronic trail to follow. Look to exchanges of letters, emails, intra-agency chats, agency financial statements or vouchers, system records, or even video footage. Do not delve into securing documentation until you have a complete understanding of your authority. Your access to some records may first need approval of your supervisor or higher. Gain the necessary approvals before going forward.

You may also need to document the rule or procedure that was not followed. So in addition to regulation or policy, emails and memos that were sent agencywide or to your work unit may be vital.

5. Analyze your findings.

Now that you've conducted the interviews and gathered all the documentation, put it all together. Constructing a chronological timeline of events will help to make your analysis clearer. It's important to consider days, dates, times, and places of the alleged violation. These are important to determine whether the accused had opportunity or accessibility to commit the act.

As you sift through the interviews, focus on the facts and recognize opinions, guesses, and gut feelings. You may take action on facts but not on feelings or suspicions. Corroborating versions and documentation usually provide the strongest evidence.

Once you've drawn conclusions, the final question to ask yourself should be: Will this conclusion stand up to an appeal if challenged? If not, then you've drawn the wrong or questionable conclusion. If so, decide the appropriate course of action.

Mistakes to avoid

Conducting investigations involving your employees can be stressful. Follow the tips below to ensure that the process goes as smoothly as possible.

Do not:

  • • Go it alone. Seek help when you need it. No one expects you to become the next Sherlock Holmes. If you are unsure of what to do next, ask for help from your HR office, OGC, or OIG, as appropriate. Don't just blunder on and hope that you won't mess anything up too badly.
  • • Rely on your memory. Document what you discover, both incriminating and favorable to the employee. There's a reason police detectives are inevitably shown writing notes on everything they hear and see in the course of an investigation. It's essential to getting the facts nailed down and keeping them straight.
  • • Assume you already know all the relevant facts. Many disciplinary actions have been aborted or later overturned when important facts came to light. The time to find those facts is before making a decision, not after.
  • • Ignore the collective bargaining agreement or the law. If you fail to check what the CBA or law requires or if you fail to comply, you have committed a mistake without an excuse. Similarly, failure to allow a union representative to be present when required by law is a mistake that can cause more delay and trouble than you can imagine. The bottom line is that you don't want to have an otherwise meritorious case overturned because of a procedural mistake.
  • • Violate privacy rights. For starters, this could make you liable for some very nasty -- and expensive -- penalties. Beyond that, it's just poor management technique to inappropriately disclose information that you uncover during an investigation.

Ask FMG

Q. Are federal employees required to answer questions during an investigation even if doing so might put them at risk of disciplinary action?

A. In most cases, yes. The only situation in which federal employees have the right to remain silent is when they reasonably believe that their statements may incriminate them in a criminal proceeding. Most administrative investigations, however, do not involve criminal issues, and employees have no protected right to remain silent. If an investigation you are conducting begins to lead into possible criminal conduct, you should stop and notify your Office of General Counsel or the OIG.

There is an exception to the self-incrimination rule. If the local assistant U.S. attorney formally waives prosecution, the employee must answer the questions. But this is a matter for your OGC to handle, not you.

Q. Is an employee required to answer my questions truthfully?

A. Yes. Federal employees may be subject to discipline for lying to investigators. This is in addition to, and may be more severe than, any disciplinary action based on the underlying misconduct.

Q. When is an employee entitled to have a union representative present during an investigative interview?

A. When the employee:

  • • Is a member of a bargaining unit.
  • • Is being questioned by a representative of agency management.
  • • Could reasonably fear disciplinary action might result.
  • • Asks for a representative.

The collective bargaining agreement may require the agency to inform employees of this right before the interview begins. This is not a direct union right. A union has no independent right to be present absent a request by the employee.

Q. When is an employee entitled to have an outside attorney present during questioning?

A. Only when the matter involves potential criminal charges. If the employee indicates that he is being represented or advised by an attorney, immediately notify your OGC.

Q. To what extent can a union representative participate in an investigative interview?

A. The union representative is entitled to take an "active" role. That generally means the representative can confer with the employee before and during questioning and can also ask questions that may help to elicit relevant facts and information. However, the representative is not entitled to disrupt the interview or to answer questions for the employee.

Q. Can I search an employee's desk or work area?

A. Usually – unless the employee has a "reasonable expectation of privacy" in the area to be searched. Most agencies have explicit policies informing employees that desks, lockers, and the like are subject to search at any time. Consequently, in most cases, the "expectation of privacy" does not exist. When in doubt, however, get advice from your HR or legal office before you search.

Q. What about an employee's car?

A. This is a little trickier -- consult with your OGC before conducting a vehicle search.
