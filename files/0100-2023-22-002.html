A. THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN RACE AND COLOR

Perhaps the least understood distinction in Title VII is the difference between discrimination based on race and discrimination based on color. This is only because many people equate race with color. It is also because different disciplines used different definitions of race, and race is often confused with ethnicity.

According to the Technical Assistance Manual, "Race & Color Discrimination," at p. 9:

A typical dictionary definition of race is "[a] local geographic or global human population distinguished as a more or less distinct group by genetically transmitted physical characteristics [or]...[a]ny group of people united or classified together on the basis of common history, nationality, or geographical distribution."

The U.S. Government currently recognizes five minimum race categories for legislative, programmatic and administrative requirements of the federal government: American Indian or Alaska Native; Asian; Black or African American; Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander; and White. Hispanic/Latino origin is characterized by the government as an ethnicity; because people of Hispanic/Latino origin can be of any race....

(Footnote omitted.)

The Commission itself has been partly responsible for blurring the distinction between race and color. In most instances, race and color are combined as the bases for a complaint. Race discrimination and discrimination based on color are not the same. As noted in Hilliard v. USPS, 01842267 (1986):

As enumerated in Title VII, "color" has been treated either as a basis indistinguishable from race or as a basis defining a subclass within a race subjected to disparate treatment. The appellant, who is dark complected, alleges that persons of the black race who are lighter complected than he received opportunities for overtime on their nonscheduled days which were denied to him.... Since the black employees who are part of the comparative group were lighter in complexion than appellant and received opportunities to work overtime on their nonscheduled days, the appellant has established a prima facie case of discrimination on the basis of color.

Confusing the picture further is the fact that many persons do not have a single racial heritage. In the final analysis, as the Commission notes in the Technical Assistance Manual, "Race & Color Discrimination," at p. 10:

Many Americans have a mixed racial heritage. In asserting a Title VII claim, however, an individual does not have to provide detailed evidence of that heritage, because the law protects individuals of all racial backgrounds from discrimination. Rarely is the racial make-up of the individual contested. The critical issue is whether the individual can prove that it was the individual's race that led to the adverse employment action.

There is no accepted definition of the term color. As used in Title VII, color has frequently been viewed as synonymous with race or as a basis for defining a subclass within a race subjected to disparate treatment.

Color discrimination can occur because of a White person's preference for, or aversion to, persons whose skin color differs from his own. It can also occur within the same racial group as members may have biases against those whose skin is lighter or darker than their own. For example, an African American may be biased against other African Americans whose skin is darker or lighter than his/her own. If race or color enters into an employment decision, Title VII is implicated, even if the discrimination involves a distinction among or by members of a particular racial group.

Resolving a largely academic argument, the Commission, in Scott v. Postmaster General, 01956000 (1998), held that a complainant could allege race discrimination based on his status as a Jew:

In the July 21, 1995 FAD the agency accepted for investigation the complaint as defined by appellant in the complaint with the exception that the basis of race would be considered under the basis of national origin (Eyawhudi (Jewish)). In the FAD the agency explained that although appellant alleged discrimination on the basis of race, "[i]ndividuals of Jewish heritage/origin are not classified as a separate race outside of the five (5) recognized race categories." On appeal, appellant argues that he is not alleging national origin discrimination, but that he is alleging "race (Jew)" discrimination.

Appellant has not alleged national origin discrimination in his complaint and has specifically stated on appeal that he does not want the basis of national origin discrimination included in his complaint. Therefore, the complaint shall be redefined to exclude the basis of national origin discrimination.

Appellant specifically stated in his complaint and realleges on appeal that he was discriminated against on the basis of his "race (Jew)." An agency shall accept a complaint from any aggrieved employee who believes that he or she has been discriminated against in violation of Title VII which includes discrimination on the basis of race. 29 CFR § 1614.103(a). The agency's FAD and statements on appeal effectively argue that the race of Jewish is not an acceptable basis for a complaint of discrimination because it is not one of the "five (5) recognized race categories."

The Commission need not decide whether the basis of discrimination set forth by appellant, Jewish, is a race, national origin, religion or some other basis. Because appellant is insisting that he was discriminated against because of his race, Jewish, and has so identified himself, we find that the agency should process the matter under the basis of race (Jewish).

In a footnote, the Commission acknowledged, without comment, that it previously accepted complaints alleging race discrimination based on the complainant's Jewish status. See Toney v. Secretary of Navy, 05930317 (1993); Castillo v. Secretary of Navy, 05870535 (1988).
