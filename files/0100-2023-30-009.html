G. ESCAPING LIABILITY FOR DISCRIMINATION

Due to some confusing language in a few federal court decisions, there was a debate over the circumstances in which an agency can escape liability once a complainant demonstrates, by a preponderance of the evidence, that discrimination was a factor in the agency's employment decision. Some believed that an agency could escape liability by demonstrating, through a preponderance of the evidence, that it would have taken the same employment action even absent discrimination. Others believed the proper burden of proof placed upon the agency was to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the same action even absent discrimination. Much of the confusion was engendered by a failure to properly distinguish between the proper burden of proof for the agency to avoid liability under the antidiscrimination statutes--i.e., to avoid a finding of a violation--and the proper burden of proof for the agency to avoid granting a remedy to a victim of discrimination even though it has violated the antidiscrimination statutes.

The Commission, in its 1992 adoption of Part 1614, firmly resolved this question and determined that the clear and convincing standard is the proper burden of proof to apply in the latter cases. See 29 CFR 1614.501(b); 1614.501(c). The confusion was oriGINAlly brought about by language in a Supreme Court decision involving a so-called mixed motive case. See Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989). In that case, the employer attempted to rebut direct evidence of discrimination by asserting that it actually acted for legitimate reasons as well as discriminatory reasons. The Supreme Court held that, unlike a case involving circumstantial evidence, in a direct evidence case the employer must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that it actually acted for legitimate reasons. As explained by the Commission in the prefatory comments in the 1992 notice of final rulemaking:

Currently, 29 CFR 1613.271 states that full relief should be provided to an individual when discrimination is found unless the record contains clear and convincing evidence that the individual would not have been selected in the absence of discrimination. During the public comment and interagency coordination of the 1987 amendments to part 1613, published at 52 FR 41919 (1987), commenters suggested that the burden of proof be changed from "clear and convincing evidence" to "a preponderance of the evidence" standard. Subsequently, the Supreme Court issued its decision in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 109 S. Ct. 1775 (1989), in which it held that an employer can avoid liability, and hence any relief, in a mixed motive case upon showing by a preponderance of the evidence that the same determination would have been made even absent discrimination. Proposed § 1614.501 contained the same clear and convincing evidentiary standard as § 1613.271 but EEOC invited comment on whether the Supreme Court's decision required any change to that standard.

Some agency commenters argued that Hopkins required a change to preponderance of the evidence. Other agency commenters and most non-agency commenters believe that no change was necessary because Hopkins concerned proof at the liability stage while the regulation concerns proof at the relief stage and because the Hopkins decision itself cited and distinguished our regulation on this basis. We agree that the Hopkins decision does not require a change in the regulation. Hopkins relates solely to liability in mixed motive cases; its does not affect the standards of proof applicable to liability or relief in single motive cases. We recognize that the regulation will be applied most often to determining whether class members are entitled to individual relief after a class finding of discrimination, but it is also applicable to individual cases where there has been a finding of discrimination.

We also believe that Hopkins only applies to contemporaneous mixed motive cases, i.e., those cases where both motives or reasons were actually known and actually affected the decision. If an agency proves by clear and convincing evidence that it had some legitimate reason for taking the action in question or not selecting the complainant but only discovered that reason after the actual decision was made, it would not escape liability. It may succeed, however, in limiting the amount of relief.

The Price Waterhouse decision and the distinction between the proper application of the preponderance of the evidence standard and the clear and convincing evidence standard also are discussed in Chapter 10 under the subheading "The Price Waterhouse Analysis," above.

As noted below, Section 107 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, codified at 42 USC 2000e-5(g), now provides that complainants can obtain injunctive relief and attorney fees in cases where they show that discrimination was a substantial factor in the agency's action, but the agency demonstrates, by a preponderance of the evidence, that it would have taken the same action even absent discrimination. However, the complainant cannot obtain such relief as reinstatement or back pay. The Act alters the Price Waterhouse holding by permitting a partial remedy in such situations. Where the employer prevails in a mixed motive case, compensatory damages are not available. See Parvin v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05950017 (1996).

Clear and convincing evidence is "'that proof which results in reasonable certainty of the truth of the ultimate fact in controversy.'" Beland v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01994590 (2002) (relying upon Black's Law Dictionary 251 (6th ed. 1990)). In Truell v. Secretary of Army, 01994532 (2002), the Commission explained the rationale for the clear and convincing standard:

As to remedy, the Commission notes that the AJ ordered the Position to be announced as vacant and provide complainant genuine consideration. In Day v. Matthews, 530 F.2d 1083, 1085 (D.C. Cir. 1976), the Court found that where there has been discrimination in the promotion process, an employer may avoid providing a remedy for the non-promotion by showing with clear and convincing evidence that complainant would not have been promoted absent the discrimination. The rationale for the clear and convincing evidence standard is that it is equitable that any resulting uncertainty be resolved against the party whose action gave rise to the problem. Hence, once discrimination is shown, relief should not be narrowly defined. Id. at 1086. See also Hong v. Department of the Interior, EEOC Request No. 05910297 (May 9, 1991) (finding that the rule in Day applied where an agency failed to refer complainant for promotion as one of six best qualified candidates for two positions). Upon review of the record, we find that there is sufficient evidence to determine if retroactive promotion to the Position would be appropriate. The AJ found that the Selectee was not qualified for the Position. Further, the Inspector General averred that complainant's qualifications were impressive and that he would be "a fine selectee for the [Position]." Based on the AJ's findings and the evidence within the record, the Commission concludes that complainant would have been promoted absent discrimination. Accordingly, the appropriate remedy would be retroactive promotion of complainant into the Position.

An appeal is not the place for an agency to submit evidence that it would not have selected a complainant even absent discrimination. The agency must present such evidence during the investigation or hearing stages of the complaint process. Lockhart v. Secretary of Treasury, 01911775 (1992). The proper remedy when the agency fails to introduce such evidence is to retroactively award the complainant the first vacancy for which he or she was eligible and not to permit the agency to reconstruct the selection process and determine if the complainant would have been selected in the absence of discrimination.

In Caldwell v. Postmaster General, 01954016 (1996), the Commission ordered a small back pay award even though it also determined that the agency demonstrated, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would not have allowed the complainant to transfer routes even absent discrimination. The Commission found that the agency discriminated against the complainant in denying her route transfer request, but that an employee with more seniority also expressed an interest in the position. The Commission ordered back pay from the time the complainant first requested the transfer until the date the position actually was given to the other employee. The rationale of the decision is hard to follow, given that the Statute clearly prohibits the award of back pay when the agency demonstrates it would have taken the same action even absent discrimination.

At least one court expressed skepticism that the clear and convincing evidence standard should be used to determine if an employer can escape liability for a violation of Title VII. In Doll v. Brown, 75 F.3d 1200, 1203 (7th Cir. 1996), the court noted it would not "conceal our skepticism" about the merits of applying the clear and convincing burden of evidence standard, as opposed to the preponderance of the evidence standard, to a case involving the federal government. Noting that common law tort cases do not impose such a heavy burden upon the defendant, the court observed that a remedy that compensates for a loss when there is none is punitive. The court in Doll did not specifically decide the evidentiary question because the issue was not contested by the government in the case.
