21. Review of Documents

In Proebstel v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A15270 (2003), the Commission rejected the notion that, because counsel reads documents as she receives them, the documents do not require further review. The Commission in Proebstel noted:

We are unwilling to agree to the application of a rule that would bar reimbursement for time spent reviewing legal documents more than once. Legal documents are frequently highly complex in nature and can require repeated, extensive, time-consuming review. In addition, documents which may not be of great importance when they are received may become more important as developments in the litigation unfold, necessitating additional study. We do not find that the attorney time claimed is unreasonably large.

The fact that the complainant suffers from a vision impairment that requires counsel to expend more time in the review of documents does not warrant a reduction in hours. In Kendall v. Office of Personnel Management, 07A20091 (2003), the Commission held:

Second, with regard to the overall fee petition, complainant's attorney's fee submission noted that the circumstances of this particular case necessitated some additional time to retrieve and interpret documents. As noted by the AJ in her decision, complainant is legally blind and could not read documents that were not in Braille without the help of an enlarger. Even with an enlarger, complainant could only read two or three letters at a time. Due to complainant's visual impairment, complainant's brother, with whom she lived before his death, was instrumental in collecting and organizing documents relating to complainant's EEO case. When complainant's brother died of an unexpected heart attack, the task of reviewing documents became much more difficult for complainant's attorney without complainant's brother to help locate and interpret the documents he had collected and maintained.

On appeal, the agency makes the same argument to complainant's fee petition that it did before the AJ. Specifically, that it is unreasonable for complainant to request attorney's fees for time spent recapturing information made unavailable by complainant's own actions. As stated by the AJ, "it is not clear from the agency's response what actions of complainant's the agency [is] referring to, since it would be ludicrous for the agency to characterize complainant's visual impairment or the death of her brother as being within her control." The AJ went on to find that it is eminently reasonable for an attorney to spend time gathering information and reviewing documents relating to complainant's case. The circumstances of complainant's visual impairment and her brother's death did not by themselves render the time sent on these tasks as unreasonable. And after reviewing the attorney's time records as a whole, the Commission agrees with the AJ that amount of time claimed from the filing of the formal complaint to the date of the hearing was reasonable.
