PROBATIONARY PERIOD

The probationary period -- or, as it's generally known in the excepted service, a trial period -- should be viewed as the final step in the hiring process. In other words, for covered positions, no appointment is final until the probationary period is successfully completed.

The probationary period is one year for competitive service appointees (two years for Department of Defense competitive service appointees hired before December 31, 2022), one year for excepted service preference eligibles, and up to two years for excepted service non-preference eligibles.

There are exceptions to these time periods, so rely upon your HR office to correctly determine the applicability and end date of any new appointee's probationary period, taking into consideration any prior federal employment that could be tacked on to the person's current service. This additional service could allow the completion of the probationary period in less time than the normally prescribed period for the position.

It's important to recognize that a new hire is not an "employee" until the probationary or trial period is completed.

While it is usually complicated to fire an employee, the rules on terminating probationers are almost totally in favor of the agency. The rules give you the opportunity to terminate probationers with little advance notice or procedural due process and appeal rights. As the manager of a probationer, you are responsible for providing an appropriate orientation to the work unit and job duties, observing/monitoring their performance, conduct, and general character traits, and taking notes. You also need to assist the new hire in developing full productivity and adjusting to the new work situation. You should provide the opportunity for appropriate training as well.

If necessary, it is your responsibility to recommend terminating the person prior to the end of the probationary period. At this point you need to consult your HR advisor. While it is never pleasant to terminate anyone, this is preferable to allowing an unacceptable individual to complete their probationary period and burdening the agency (and the taxpayer) with an undesirable employee.

Managers and their agencies should take advantage of probationary periods because:

  • • First-year performance is usually a good indication of how well an employee will do in the future.
  • • It's much easier to terminate new hires during the probationary period -- neither Title 5 Chapters 43 nor 75 apply.
  • • A probationer has limited rights to appeal a termination to the Merit Systems Protection Board.

Should you arbitrarily terminate any probationer who exhibits less than perfect performance? Of course not, but managers need to recognize the importance of the probationary period and how it fits into the appointment process.

In short, although it is relatively easy to terminate a probationer for any legitimate reason, including substandard performance or conduct, it is essential that you act promptly and coordinate closely with your HR advisors.

Mentoring and counseling

To help probationers succeed and determine whether they are worth keeping on, supervisors should provide mentoring and counseling. The purpose of mentoring and counseling is to help probationers improve their performance or conduct, place them on notice of your expectations, and provide guidance on how to improve performance or fix conduct issues.

Mentoring and counseling also help if you decide to terminate a probationer and they challenge the action. It will show you made legitimate efforts to coach and assist them. It also will establish a legitimate basis for the action if the probationer fails to heed warnings or to learn, act, or perform as expected.

However, you should not engage in counseling just to document performance or behavior issues. Rather, your goals should be to correct performance or conduct and to put the probationer on notice and help them improve.

Here are some best practices to keep in mind when mentoring and counseling probationary employees:

1. Follow agency-specific policies.

Managers must know and follow agency-specific policies on probationary employees. Not following an agency policy, such as on the timing of counseling, could lead to litigation, and the probationer could argue the supervisor had an ulterior motive. Work with HR, employee relations, and even the general counsel's office to make sure you're always in compliance with policy.

2. Weigh risks and benefits.

Managers should understand that counseling probationary employees could result in them feeling targeted, which can lead to complaints and litigation. This doesn't mean you shouldn't do it. Rather, ensure you can articulate a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for counseling. Even with a risk of litigation, counseling notifies probationers of expectations and gives them the opportunity to improve. If done the right way, counseling is designed to benefit the employee.

3. Be specific and decide on written versus oral counseling.

You should not engage in counseling, either orally or in writing, without being prepared. Your comments should be specific and focus on the conduct or performance, rather than the probationer's motivation. Describe the situation, the context of the individual's performance or conduct, the impact on the office, and discuss a plan for the future on how the probationer can improve. Don't be sloppy, and don't include things that are not necessary because describing things unrelated to the specific performance or conduct issues can lead to allegations of retaliation.

Counseling can be done orally, but it could become a problem if the person claims the counseling was in retaliation for an EEO complaint or whistleblower disclosure. When counseling is documented in writing, an administrative judge at the Merit Systems Protection Board will be able to see your efforts to help the probationer. At the same time, a written counseling could escalate the situation to an unnecessary level and a simple verbal conversation may be enough, depending on the circumstances. If you're not sure about the better route to take, ask HR for advice.

4. Be prepared to defend any actions.

No matter what you say or write, be prepared to see it again. Also be prepared to defend the action if the probationer initiates a grievance, files an EEO complaint, or appeals a termination to the MSPB. You must be able to explain and justify anything put in writing and should be careful with your words. You also may want to have recipients sign the counseling to show they were made aware of expectations.

Terminating probationers

Probationers are subject to "termination" rather than the formal removal actions set forth in Chapter 43 and Chapter 75. When terminating a probationer for performance or conduct reasons, the agency must provide written notice of the reasons for its action and the effective date of the termination. These are the only requirements of the governing regulations.

However, note that when an agency proposes to terminate a probationer for reasons based in whole or in part on conditions arising before the appointment -- such as falsifying a job application -- the employee is entitled to the following:

  • • Advanced written notice of proposed action.
  • • An opportunity to respond to the proposal notice.
  • • Written notice of the decision with a notice of appeal rights before the MSPB.

Check with HR for more assistance on terminating probationers for pre-appointment reasons.

You don't have to give probationers a formal opportunity to improve (PIP) since the entire probationary period is similar to an opportunity period. However, probationers should receive closer supervision, instruction, and training as needed during their first year of employment. The goal is that they successfully complete their probation.

Remember these key points when dealing with new hires:

  • • You can terminate a probationer for virtually any conduct or performance-related reason.
  • • You don't have to meet the "efficiency of the service" standard that applies in removing tenured employees when terminating a probationer.
  • • A probationer is entitled to a simple written notice of the reasons and effective date for the termination. Additional requirements apply to pre-appointment cases.
  • • Union contract procedures may apply but may not interfere with basic rights of the agency.

Calculating the probationary period

  • • The probationary period usually begins on the hiring date and ends on the last day before the new hire's one-year anniversary date. At the Department of Defense, the probationary period for competitive service positions is two years for appointees hired prior to Dec. 31, 2022. Appointees hired on or after Dec. 31, 2022, have a one-year probationary period.
  • • If a probationer is not terminated before the end of the scheduled tour of duty on the last day before their anniversary, they gain permanent status and full due process and appeal rights.
  • • If the anniversary date falls on a weekend or holiday, a probationer must be terminated no later than the day before the last regular workday.
  • • If a probationer is absent more than 22 workdays during probation, the agency can extend the probationary period by the number of days missed in excess of 22.

Reassignments. Agencies always have the right to reassign employees, including probationers. However, reassigning a probationer with performance or conduct problems is not normally recommended because it rarely solves the problem.

Make sure you work closely with the HR department (and your superiors) if you are considering reassigning a probationer.

Supervisory probationary period

There is another type of probationary period for supervisors and managers. When an employee is initially appointed to a supervisory or managerial position, she is required to serve a probationary period prescribed by the agency. Agencies are allowed to make exceptions to the probationary period for managers who have satisfactorily completed a probationary period as a supervisor if it's justified on the basis of performance and experience.

Regulations state that employees who are "reassigned, transferred, or promoted to another supervisory or managerial position" while in the process of completing a probationary period may count "service in the former position...toward completion of the probationary period in the new position." However, service in a prior supervisory role in an excepted service position cannot be tacked for purposes of completing a supervisory probationary period in the competitive service.

If a newly appointed supervisor or manager fails to satisfactorily complete the probationary period, the agency can return her to a position of no lower grade and pay than the position from which she was transferred, assigned, or promoted.

The employee has no right to appeal the demotion unless she alleges that the demotion was based on partisan political affiliation or marital status.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Wait until the last minute to take action. The probationary period lasts a specific period of time.
  • • Think that a person will improve to acceptable status after the first year. First-year performance is usually a good indication of how well the individual will do in the future.
  • • Provide a formal opportunity to improve by instituting a PIP. This is unnecessary since the entire probationary period is essentially an opportunity period. Probationers are specifically excluded from the procedures that require the opportunity to improve.
  • • Move your problems around. Reassigning a probationer with conduct or performance issues to another position passes the problem to another manager.
  • • Assume that every new hire is a probationer. Most career federal employees go through only one probationary period in their careers, although new supervisors are on probation as far as their supervisory status is concerned. So even though employees are new to your unit or your agency, they may not be on probation. There are some gray areas for employees who have had interruptions or irregularities in their career. It is best to clarify the status of your new hires with HR before they begin work.

Ask FMG

Q. Why are new appointees placed on a probationary period?

A. Probationary periods are used to give managers a full opportunity to determine whether new hires are capable of meeting performance and conduct requirements. It is also a legal requirement.

Q. How long is the probationary period?

A. In most cases it is one calendar year in length for the competitive service, one year for excepted service preference eligibles, and up to two years for excepted service non-preference eligibles. At the Department of Defense, the probationary period for competitive service positions is two years for appointees hired prior to Dec. 31, 2022, and is one year for appointees hired on Dec. 31, 2022, and later.

The probationary period usually begins on the effective date of hiring and ends on the last day before the employee's one-year or two-year (as applicable) anniversary date.

Q. Is the length of the probationary period different for part-time employees?

A. They also serve a one or two-year probationary period, as applicable, on the same basis as full-time employees.

Q. What if a new hire misses a substantial portion of the probationary period for some reason?

A. If a new appointee is absent more than 22 workdays during probation, the agency can extend the probationary period by the number of days missed in excess of 22.

Q. How long is the probationary period for new supervisors?

A. Employees appointed to their first supervisory position serve a one-year probationary period, during which they may be returned to a non-supervisory position for inadequate performance or conduct.

Q. Can I reassign a probationer instead of firing them?

A. Technically yes, but this is not recommended, since this may result in only moving the problem elsewhere, not solving it. Consult your HR department if you are considering reassigning a probationer.

Q. Do I have to give a probationer an opportunity to improve?

A. No. The law and regulations specifically exclude probationers from the procedures that require the use of an opportunity to improve since the entire probationary period is similar to an opportunity period. That said, new hires should receive closer supervision, instruction, and training as needed during the first year of their employment.

Q. Can I terminate a probationer who has not, strictly speaking, engaged in misconduct but just has a poor attitude and doesn't seem to fit in well?

A. Yes, so long as that description of the employee is not being used to mask discrimination based on race, sex, age, etc. You have a lot of discretion in deciding whether to accept an employee during their probationary period, and you should use it judiciously to terminate employees who are unlikely to work out well. Check with HR to make sure you are not getting into a situation that looks like you are terminating the employee for discriminatory reasons.

Q. Is it any easier to terminate a probationer than an employee who has completed the probationary period?

A. Yes. The agency does not have to meet the difficult "efficiency of the service" standard to justify the termination, and the person's appeal rights are extremely limited. The whole idea is to give managers a year (or two) in which they can easily terminate those who appear unlikely to be solid additions to the workforce.

Q. How much paperwork and effort is involved in terminating a probationer?

A. It depends on the agency, but usually very little. The law and the implementing regulations deliberately make it relatively easy for agencies to terminate probationers who do not meet performance or conduct requirements.

Q. Can I terminate a probationer at any time during the year, or do I have to wait until the end?

A. You can terminate a probationer at any time during the probationary period, usually by simply providing a written notice stating the overall reason for termination. Consult with HR as soon as you identify significant problems with a probationer's performance or conduct.

Q. Can I still terminate a probationer on the last day of the probationary period?

A. Yes, but this isn't recommended -- the termination must occur before the end of the probationer's scheduled tour of duty on the last day before the one-year anniversary (for the competitive service). If the employee's anniversary date falls on a weekend or holiday, the probationer must be terminated no later than the day before the last regular workday. Don't risk it -- contact HR early on.

Q. What about union contract provisions addressing probationary terminations?

A. If you have a union contract that contains provisions applicable to probationary terminations, you should try to follow them, but the courts have ruled that no contractual provision can provide probationers with substantive procedural rights beyond the limited rights contained in the governing regulations.

Q. What if I miss the deadline?

A. The individual gains "employee" status and full due process and appeal rights. Formal removal procedures would now apply to the employee, which are much more difficult and time-consuming than a probationary termination.

Q. Can a newly hired probationer start teleworking immediately?

A. There's no governmentwide rule regarding probationers and telework. However, those in a probationary status may not be eligible for telework under agency policies that have a tenure requirement for some or all positions. Contact your agency's telework coordinator.
