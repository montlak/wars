12. Failure to Provide Complaint Form

The Commission's regulations require that a formal complaint be filed in writing. 29 CFR 1614.106(c). They do not require that the complaint be filed on any particular form, but where the agency refers to a complaint form in its notice of final interview, does not enclose the form and fails to respond to the complainant's request to provide the form, the formal complaint was deemed timely where it was filed within six days of the counselor informing the complainant that a form was not required. Thompson v. Postmaster General, 0120110170 (2011).

But, where the agency can show good faith efforts to serve the complainant with a right to file a formal complaint form and the complainant refused delivery, the Commission will not waive the applicable time limits for filing. That was the case in Fernanda H. v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120170032 (2017), where the Commission upheld the agency's de facto dismissal of the complaint:

Here, there is no indication that the Agency issued a final decision dismissing Complainant's March 2015 complaint. The Agency argues that Complainant's informal complaint was properly closed, even if a formal decision was not issued. The Agency argues that since it made numerous, good faith efforts to deliver the notice of right to file a formal complaint, and one was never filed, the claim should be considered abandoned and Complainant's informal complaint be considered properly closed.

Under the unique facts of this case, and in order to clarify this matter for the parties and our administrative judge, we have decided to rule on the issue and construe the Agency's stated position as its final decision dismissing the March 2015 informal complaint.

With regard to that informal complaint, the record shows that on March 13, 2015, Complainant initiated EEO counselor contact.

On June 5, 2015, the EEO counselor held the final interview with Complainant and attempted to issue her a notice of right to file a formal EEO complaint. The EEO counselor informed Complainant that she had 15 days from that point to file a formal complaint. However, Complainant declined to accept notice of right to file by hand delivery and asked that the EEO counselor mail it to her.

On June 6, 2015, the EEO counselor mailed Complainant the notice by certified mail to her address of record. The mailing was returned unclaimed. The EEO counselor re-mailed by notice to Complainant, again by certified mail, but it was again returned as unclaimed. With each mailing, the EEO counselor stated she left a voicemail for Complainant indicating the notice was in the mail.

On February 26, 2016, Complainant contacted the EEO counselor to inquire about the status of her informal complaint. The EEO counselor was not available, and when she returned Complainant's call, later that day, she could not reach Complainant. On March 2, 2016, and April 1, 2016, the EEO counselor tried calling Complainant. She left a voicemail each time. On April 11, 2016, Complainant spoke with the EEO counselor, who informed her that her informal complaint was considered closed.

Based on this evidence, we find insufficient reason to waive the applicable time limits for filing a complaint set forth in 29 C.F.R. § 1614.106(b). Actual receipt of a document is generally necessary to commence the running of a regulatory time limit. However, in rare circumstances, a time limit may be deemed to run where due diligence was used to deliver an appropriate rights notice to a complainant, but the effort was unsuccessful. See Smith v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, EEOC Appeal. No. 0120090910 (March 24, 2009); Woehr v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05960657 (July 3, 1997). Here, while Complainant may not have received a written notice of right to file, she was offered the written notice in person by the EEO counselor, but refused to accept it at her final interview on June 5, 2015. The EEO counselor's detailed account of events, signed under perjury, indicated that at that meeting, she explained to Complainant her obligation to file a formal complaint within the fifteen-day time frame. She then mailed the notice to Complainant two times by certified mail, but Complainant did not accept delivery. Finally, she followed up with numerous telephone calls to Complainant. Under these unique circumstances, we conclude that Complainant was adequately notified of her right to file a formal EEO complaint and the timeframe for doing so, but failed to do so. As such, we determine that she abandoned the claim raised in her March 2015 informal complaint and cannot now revive it as an amendment to her February 2016 complaint.
