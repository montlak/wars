4. Discovery Conduct

In Laney v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120091518 (2010), the Commission vacated a grant of summary judgment that had been issued as a sanction against the complainant for dilatory discovery conduct. Although the Commission could have vacated Laney simply on the basis that summary judgment is not a proper sanction and can be granted only if there are no genuine disputes of material fact, the decision also analyzed the case in terms of the sanction being overly harsh:

After a careful review of the record in this case, we conclude that the AJ's sanction was too harsh. We agree with the AJ that Complainant and his counsel delayed the discovery process in a "highly unsatisfactory" manner. AJ Order on Agency's Motion to Dismiss/For Sanctions ("AJ Order"), at 2. While Complainant asserts that he did not understand his responsibility under the AJ's discovery order, the record shows that he spoke with the AJ about discovery deadlines. C's Appellate Brief, at 4; Id. at 1. In addition, expressions of surprise by Complainant's counsel that the Agency did not receive the discovery responses are disingenuous in light of the Agency's multiple re-filings of the Motion to Dismiss/For Sanctions. AJ Order, at 1-2. Even if we accept counsel's statement that the discovery responses got lost in the mail after he mailed them on November 10, 2008, the responses were already three days late. Id. at 2.

Despite the tardiness of Complainant and his attorney, there are extenuating circumstances at play here. We note, for example, that the AJ acknowledged that much of the information sought by the Agency was already contained in the record. Id. Therefore, the Agency was not unduly prejudiced by the delay since it could refer to the investigative file for the desired information. In addition, Complainant's attorney ultimately provided the discovery responses on November 25, 2008, well after the discovery deadline set by the AJ but well in advance of a hearing if one had been scheduled. Id. The AJ characterized counsel's responses as "canned" and "non-responsive." However, the responses do not appear in the record for us to assess. Id., at 2 n.2. Moreover, because the responses were duplicative of information in the record, the quality of the responses becomes less significant. In light of these mitigating circumstances, it was improper for the AJ to grant summary judgment to the Agency without notice to Complainant, without affording Complainant an opportunity to respond, and without including a comprehensive statement of the allegedly undisputed facts. Such a sanction does not reflect the nature and extent of the non-compliance at issue. Moreover, the record reflects that there are genuine issues of material fact with regard to claim 1, including a dispute as to whether Complainant was qualified for the desired position and could perform its essential functions. See ROI, at 8-9. Therefore, Complainant should receive a hearing on claim 1.

Interestingly, the Commission did not impose an alternative sanction in Laney, but simply remanded the case for a hearing.

In MacLin v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A14223 (2002), the Commission reversed dismissal of the complaint as a sanction and ordered the case remanded to the agency for a final decision on the record where the complainant failed to respond to discovery, failed to respond to an order compelling discovery and, ultimately, provided only partial responses. In finding the sanction of dismissal too harsh, the Commission in MacLin noted that the complainant's failures were due to his lack of understanding of the hearing process and not contumacious conduct. A similar result was reached in Caldwell v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A11201 (2002), where the complainant failed to respond to an order compelling discovery. See also Kammermeyer v. Postmaster General, 01A61330 (2006); Mitchell v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A52723 (2006). In King v. National Labor Relations Board, 05A60498 (2006), the Commission rejected the agency's request for reconsideration asking for reversal of a previous order remanding the case for a decision on the record. Once again, the Commission emphasized its strong policy in favor of deciding cases on the merits when there is a sufficient record even if the complainant's conduct warrants dismissal of the hearing request. Similarly, the Commission found that the administrative judge abused her discretion in Pantoja v. Attorney General, 0120100018 (2011), where she had ordered both parties to seek permission before filing any correspondence or motions requiring action by the other party and the complainant served discovery requests on the agency without permission. Again, the Commission found that cancelation of the hearing and remand for a decision on the record was the appropriate sanction.

Cancelation of the hearing and remand for a final agency decision was a proper sanction where the complainant improperly accessed personal identification information to use in her case. However, the agency erred in issuing a decision with a finding of no discrimination without an analysis of the existing factual record. Beavers v. Secretary of Interior, 0120110701 (2012).
