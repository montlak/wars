4. Failure to Disclose Criteria

On occasion, agencies fail to include selection criteria in the report of investigation contending that disclosure of the criteria would give future applicants an unfair advantage. The Commission does not have a bright-line rule that selection criteria can never be withheld, but it is disinclined to accept the unfair advantage rationale. In addition, even if the criteria can be legitimately withheld, the agency must otherwise include sufficient evidence in the investigatory record for the Commission to determine if the agency has advanced a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason. In Bakken v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120093529 (2011), the Commission found that agency's failure to include such information resulted in it not being able to sustain its burden of producing a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason:

The Agency offers as its nondiscriminatory explanation that the selection process was properly administered. The Agency noted in its final decision that a special crediting plan was used to grade and rank candidates, but did not produce the plan, contending it was confidential. Further, the Agency noted that Complainant received a lower rated score than most of the selectees, but did not provide any information about the bases, factors, or assessments motivating its decision. Such a non-descriptive and vague explanation does not satisfy the Agency's burden to produce a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions. While the Agency's burden of production is not onerous, it must nevertheless provide a specific, clear, and individualized explanation for the treatment accorded the affected employee. See Wilson v. Soc. Sec. Admin., EEOC Appeal No. 01A50140 (Mar. 10, 2006) (finding age discrimination based on the agency's failure to carry its burden and provide information about the bases, factors, or assessments motivating its decision to not select complainant); Eubanks v. Dep't of Housing and Urban Development, EEOC Request No. 01A40070 (Sep. 14, 2005) (finding age and sex discrimination based on the agency's reliance on a summary of the mechanics of its selection process and subsequent failure to meet its burden to produce an "individualized explanation" for why it did not select the complainant for the relevant position); Lorenzo v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05950931 (Nov. 6, 1997) (finding race discrimination based the agency's failure to carry its burden and "adequately explain" why complainant was not selected for the position). Therefore, we find that the Agency failed to carry its burden to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action.
