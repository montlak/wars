5. Case Law Prior to Hicks

Under Commission case law prior to Hicks, when the agency offered a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason or reasons for its actions, the burden shifted back to the appellant to establish "that a discriminatory reason more likely motivated the agency or that the agency's proffered reason. . .is unworthy of credence." Bowser v. USPS, 01830780 (1983). Essentially, "the Commission must decide which party's explanation of the agency's motivation it believes." Id. In cases under Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act, the employee must show that race, color, sex, religion, national origin, or reprisal was a motivating factor in the adverse employment action, even though other factors also may have motivated the employer. In cases under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, the employee must show that discrimination was a determinative factor "in the sense that 'but for' his age, he would not have been subjected to the action at issue." Williams v. Postmaster General, 01881756 (1988). See Chapter 13.

"Evidence that may be relevant to a showing of pretext includes: 1) the agency's past treatment of appellant; 2) the agency's general policy and practice in minority hiring, termination, and discipline including statistics reflecting a pattern of discrimination; 3) procedural irregularities in the termination or discipline process; 4) the agency's use of subjective criteria to evaluate employees who are not objectively equally situated or in the same job position; and 5) unequal application of agency policy." O'Neal v. Postmaster General, 01904090 (1991) (citing Mohammed v. Callaway, 698 F.2d 395 (10th Cir. 1983)).

As the Commission found in Stephenson v. Postmaster General, 01922643 (1992), there are many ways a complainant can establish pretext:

[A]ppellant can prove pretext by showing that the agency's proffered explanation is unworthy of credence. Appellant can prove pretext in this manner by showing that the agency failed to follow its own procedures in discharging appellant, advanced reasons for the decision only "after-the-fact," or discriminatorily created the conditions causing appellant's conduct. See Craig v. Y & Y Snacks, Inc., 721 F.2d 77 (3rd Cir. 1983); Foster v. Simon, 467 F. Supp. 533 (W.D. N.C. 1979); Weiss v. United States, 36 FEP Cases 1 (E.D. Va 1984). In addition, appellant can demonstrate pretext by showing that the testimony of agency officials is conflicting, that the agency's explanation for its actions is vague, or that the agency's explanation is uncorroborated. See Lineberry v. Dept. of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01873039 (June 22, 1988); Farrow v. Dept. of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01880309 (June 3, 1988); French v. Dept. of Agriculture, EEOC Request No. 05880170 (May 6, 1988).

A word to the wise: complainants often overlook the obvious in dealing with the issue of pretext. In Yen v. Department of Army, 01830137 (1983), the complainant alleged that his job had been reclassified as a GS-12 based on discriminatory motives. As proof, he demonstrated that his predecessor was classified as a GS-13 and further alleged that his supervisor rewrote the position description to achieve the downgrade to GS-12. The EEOC rejected the complainant's arguments, in part, because he made no attempt to prove that the position description did not accurately describe the job he was performing.

In assessing whether the employee demonstrated that the agency's proffered reasons are pretext, the EEOC will look at the totality of the circumstances. Lazurs v. Department of Navy, 01832761 (1985). The fact that the agency deviated from its selection procedure usually will not be a sufficient demonstration of pretext. However, a showing that the agency made numerous and substantial departures from its procedures can be sufficient.

Previously, in cases such as Jenkins v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01880452 (1988), the Commission held that the McDonnell Douglas analysis acknowledged that proof of the employer's actual motivation was nearly impossible:

The presumption in this approach is that when all legitimate reasons for an employment action have been eliminated, it is more likely than not that the employer, who we assume generally acts for some reason, based his decision on a discriminatory factor. Chipollini, 814 F.2d at 897 (citing Furnco v. Waters, 438 U.S. 567 (1978)).

As a result, "in addition to establishing a prima facie case by indirect proof, a plaintiff can prevail by means of indirect proof that the employer's reasons are pretextual without presenting evidence specifically relating to [the basis of discrimination alleged]." Chipollini, 814 F.2d at 898; See also La Montagne, 750 F.2d at 1409-10; Aikens v. United Postal Service Bd. of Governors, 460 U.S. 711, 717-18 (Blackmun, J., concurring) ("the McDonnell Douglas framework requires that a plaintiff prevail when at the third stage of a Title VII trial he demonstrates that the legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason given by the employer is in fact not the true reason for the employment decision.").

The complainant was not required, in addition to showing the employer's reason was pretextual, to show the actual reason for the employer's actions. The continued validity of this doctrine is now in question under Hicks. However, it is significant to note that Justice Scalia's opinion, at least in one part, seemed only to suggest that a showing of pretext did not compel judgment for the employee and not that judgment could not issue for the employee on that basis.

In some cases, the Commission has described the complainant's burden of proving pretext as the "burden of showing that this evidence demonstrates a 'substantial' or 'reasonable probability' that the agency's explanation is pretextual." Morris v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01880582 (1988), citing Fink v. Western Electric Co., 708 F.2d 909 (4th Cir. 1983).

Although the complainant bears the burden of establishing pretext, the Commission has sometimes expressed the proposition in the alternative; that is, an agency's action will be sustained if it is supported by substantial evidence. For example, in Emperado v. Secretary of Interior, 01930541 (1993), the Commission stated:

As with the establishment of a prima facie case, proof of pretext varies with each employment action at issue. See Furnco Construction Corp., 438 U.S. at 580; EEOC v. Gaddis, 733 F.2d 1373 (10th Cir. 1984); Craig v. Y&Y Snacks, Inc., 721 F.2d 77, 80 (3d Cir. 1983). Employers generally have broad discretion to carry out personnel decisions, and should not second-guessed by a reviewing authority absent evidence of unlawful motivation. Burdine, 450 U.S. at 259. Consequently, even if more than one conclusion may be drawn from the evidence, the agency's decision will be upheld if its conclusion is supported by substantial evidence. Steven v. Stubbs, 576 F. Supp. 1409, 1413 (N.D. Ga. 1983), citing Deutsch v. Atomic Energy Commission, 401 F.2d 404 (D.C. Cir. 1968). However, personnel decisions which are idiosyncratic or suspect will be subjected to heightened scrutiny. Loeb v. Textron, Inc., 600 F.2d 1003, 1012, n.6 (1st Cir. 1979).

In considering whether a complainant has established pretext, the Commission is not required to ignore evidence that was relied upon to establish a prima facie case. McDuffie v. Secretary of Navy, 05880134 (1988). Pretext is a determination that is made by assessing the record as a whole.

Pretext is a factual determination made on the basis of all available evidence. As with the complainant's initial burden of establishing a prima facie case and the agency's burden of producing a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions, extensive recounting of individual cases would do little to advance one's understanding of the legal concept of pretext. When dealing with specific personnel actions, the reader is referred to those sections of the Guide for more specific information.
