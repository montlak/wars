IV. AMENDMENT AND CONSOLIDATION OF COMPLAINTS

There are two situations that arise with respect to the amendment of complaints: (1) amendment to include a different alleged basis of discrimination; and (2) amendment to include a new personnel action or incident. The Commission, in the past, has not considered including another basis of discrimination as a substantive amendment to the complaint. Such an amendment merely attaches a different legal conclusion to the facts previously alleged. As a result, amending a complaint to add a basis of discrimination has always been permitted under Commission case law.

The Commission's position on a complaint to include a new action or incident of alleged discrimination has not always been clear. In some instances Commission case law indicated that the amendment should be referred back to EEO counseling and processed as a separate complaint. In other cases, the Commission allowed complaint amendments.

The 1999 amendments to Part 1614, for the first time, clearly allowed complainants to amend complaints to include like or related incidents of alleged discrimination that occur after the initial complaint has been filed. 29 CFR 1614.106(d). There is no requirement that the amendment be subject to counseling. If a new incident of discrimination is not like or related to the original complaint, the regulations provide that the complaints be consolidated. 29 CFR 1614.606. However, such a complaint is subject to the counseling requirements of 1614.105.

As drafted, 1614.106(d) does not specifically limit amendments of complaints to events that occur subsequent to the filing of the original complaint. However, as discussed more fully below, the Commission's guidance in MD-110 makes clear that the intent of the regulation was to cover subsequent events and not to cover amendments to complaints to include events that occurred prior to the original complaint. The Commission's policy on continuing violations conflicted with that guidance, arguably allowing a complainant to amend a complaint to include past acts by alleging those acts were interrelated with the act of alleged discrimination on which a timely complaint was filed. Any doubt about a complainant's ability to amend a complaint to include past personnel actions on which timely complaints were not filed was removed by the Supreme Court's decision in National Railroad Passenger Corp. v. Morgan, 122 S. Ct. 2061 (2002). The Court in Morgan held that the continuing violation theory could not be used to resurrect claims on past personnel actions where the statute of limitations--i.e., the 45-day period for contacting a counselor in the federal sector--had run. Morgan is discussed fully in Chapter 2, subheading "The Supreme Court's View--Morgan."
