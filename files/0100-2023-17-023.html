A. COMPLAINT FILE

Maintenance of a thorough complaint file assists in timely decisions on appeal and also reduces the possibility of remand to the agency for further processing. The former 29 CFR 1613.222 set out in detail what the agency was required to include in the complaint file transmitted to the EEOC. Though Part 1614 contains no similar provision, the Commission has, in MD-110, set out in detail the required contents and organization of the report of investigation. See Chapter 4, above.

When an agency fails to submit a properly tabbed and indexed copy of the complaint file, it is appropriate for the Commission to draw inferences in the complainant's favor. The Commission "should not be expected" to wade through a voluminous complaint file in search of evidence and exhibits referred to by the administrative judge and the agency in its decisions. This also includes complaint files where the tabs bear "no resemblance whatsoever to the page or exhibit designations used in the RD and the FAD." Draper v. Secretary of Army, 01923383 (1993). However, the Commission also has held that when an agency fails to submit a complete complaint file in accordance with MD-110 the Commission may remand the case with specific instructions for the agency to comply. Meeker v. National Transportation Safety Board, 01940718 (1994). The Commission has been less than clear on when it will remand a case with instructions for the agency to provide a complete case file and when it will draw an adverse inference as a result of the agency's failure to provide the file.

Haas v. Secretary of Commerce, 01954039 (1997), provides a good illustration of the overly patient attitude the Commission has had in the past for agencies that fail to cooperate by providing a copy of the complaint file:

Here, the agency issued a final decision dated April 26, 1995, finding no discrimination. Following appellant's appeal from that decision, the Commission requested that the agency submit a copy of the complaint file. However, we find that the record in this case does not contain a copy of the investigative report. In its final decision, the agency makes numerous references to exhibits contained in the investigative report, in support of its finding of no discrimination. Because the investigative report is absent from the record, we find that the record is not a complete factual record sufficient for a determination on the merits. Accordingly, the agency's finding of no discrimination is VACATED.

The agency was ordered to supply the file and issue a new final decision within 30 days.

The Commission has changed its position on the proper remedy when the agency fails to timely respond to an order to produce the complaint file in cases dismissed on procedural grounds. Instead of endlessly ordering the agency to produce the complaint file, the Commission has remanded some cases for processing. Although the decisions are less than entirely clear on the matter, it appears the Commission is deeming any procedural defense to be waived in those cases.

For example, in Patterson v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01972042 (1998), the agency issued a final decision dismissing the complaint for failure to make timely contact with an EEO counselor. On appeal, the agency failed to respond with either the complaint file or a brief. The Commission in Patterson found:

Following the receipt of the appeal and any brief in support of the appeal, the Director, Office of Federal Operations, will request the complaint file from the agency. The agency must submit the complaint file and any agency statement or brief in opposition of the appeal to the Director. . .[at the specified address] within 30 days of receipt of the Commission's request for the complaint file. . .29 CFR § 1614.403(d); see Burris v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01934327 (May 16, 1994); req. to recon. den., EEOC Request No. 05940845 (June 2, 1995). Because the agency has failed to comply with the Commission's request, and has not submitted any explanation for its failure to do so, the Commission finds that the agency is in violation of the Commission's regulations; specifically, section 1614.403(d). Accordingly, the FAD is VACATED and the non-selection allegation is remanded to the agency for processing in accordance with the Order of the Commission, below.

The order referred to in Patterson required the agency to acknowledge receipt of the complaint within 30 days and issue a report of investigation with the attendant hearing rights within 150 days. Such an order would seem to preclude the agency from issuing a new final decision dismissing the case for untimely counselor contact. See also Vasquez v. Postmaster General, 05971071 (1998) (agency's dismissal of a complaint on the basis that it was identical to a previous complaint was vacated for a failure to provide the complaint file, and the agency was ordered to investigate within 150 days); Jordy v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01971312 (1998).

In Shaw v. International Broadcasting Bureau, 01983995 (2002), the Commission imposed an adverse inference against the agency where it failed to provide the complaint file for three years and then could not locate the hearing transcript.

In Tsosie v. Secretary of Interior, 0120081612 (2012), the Commission imposed a series of adverse inferences, including that the production of the complaint file would have established that the agency's actions were based on sex and reprisal and that those actions created a hostile environment. Given that the harassment culminated in the complainant's job being improperly abolished, the agency was vicariously liable for the harassment. But see Chattopadhyay v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120091142 (2012) (sanction where administrative judge granted summary judgment to the agency was remand for a hearing and agency to pay for complainant's counsel).
