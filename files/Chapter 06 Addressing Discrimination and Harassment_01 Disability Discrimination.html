DISABILITY DISCRIMINATION

As a supervisor, you are responsible for making sure applicants and employees, including those with disabilities, are treated fairly and are not subjected to a hostile work environment.

One key factor in preventing discrimination is to make your agency more diverse by actively seeking to hire and retain employees with disabilities. Aside from putting to rest concerns of discrimination, employees with disabilities offer skills and perspectives that can add to your agency's pool of knowledge and advance its mission.

Federal policy. Federal agencies are charged with being the model employers of individuals with disabilities. Regulations, authorities, hiring initiatives, and executive orders have been aimed at preventing discrimination in the hiring and retention of employees with disabilities. Most recently, Executive Order 14035 imposed certain mandates on agencies to ensure accessibility for individuals with disabilities, although more guidance is expected in the future on specific requirements. For more on EO 14035, review the Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, and Accessibility section in the Demonstrating Professionalism chapter.

Federal law. The Rehabilitation Act of 1973 protects federal employees from disability discrimination. The goal of the law is to allow qualified applicants and employees who have disabilities with a fair opportunity to compete for and retain jobs in the absence of prejudice, stereotypes, or antiquated attitudes.

As part of creating fair opportunity, there are specific limits on the types of medical inquiries agencies can make, and agencies may be required to provide reasonable accommodations that will allow individuals with disabilities to perform the essential functions of the job. The law also protects individuals from being subjected to a hostile work environment based on their disabilities.

Although the Rehabilitation Act provides the protection for federal employees and applicants, the standards for determining whether a violation of the law has occurred are those set forth in the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 and more recently in the ADA Amendments Act. Through the ADAAA, Congress significantly strengthened the protections given to individuals with disabilities. Under the ADAAA, more individuals are protected against disability discrimination.

Rehabilitation Act coverage

The Rehabilitation Act protects individuals with disabilities who are qualified for the position in question with or without reasonable accommodation.

Determining if an impairment is a disability. A disability is a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity. This is sometimes called an "actual" disability for ease of understanding. A person can also fall within the definition of disability if they have a record of an impairment that substantially limits a major life activity or if they are regarded as having such an impairment.

Sometimes there is little question that a person has a disability, for example, if the person is blind or has a missing extremity. An individual who is hired through a special hiring authority for individuals with disabilities also safely could be assumed to fall within the protections of the Rehabilitation Act.

However, disabilities often are not obvious and usually aren't even relevant unless there's a request for reasonable accommodation or a claim of disability discrimination. If there is a reason to make a determination about whether someone is an individual with a disability, you generally won't have to make the final call. However, you may have to contribute to a determination, so it's important to have a good understanding of what's involved.

In brief, there are two steps to determining whether a condition is an "actual" disability.

  1. 1. Is there a physical or mental impairment? A physical impairment is any physiological disorder or condition, cosmetic disfigurement, or anatomical loss that affects a body system. A mental impairment is any mental or psychological disorder.
  2. 2. Does it substantially limit one or more of the employee's major life activities? Major life activities are things like caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, seeing, hearing, eating, sleeping, walking, standing, speaking, breathing, and working. Also included are the operation of major bodily functions such as the immune system, special sense organs and skin, normal cell growth, digestive, reproductive, bowel, bladder, neurological, brain, respiratory, circulatory, cardiovascular, endocrine, hemic (blood), lymphatic, and musculoskeletal functions.

The determination of whether any of these major life activities is substantially limited is best left to your HR or EEO office, but you should understand that the definition of disability is generous. Under this generous standard, an impairment can be a disability based on a medical diagnosis even if the individual is able to mitigate the effects of the impairment by, for example, taking medicine. Thus, the determination of whether a condition is a disability does not take into account measures the individual uses to mitigate or cope with the impairment.

A person has a record of a disability if they have a history of an impairment that substantially limited a major life activity. For example, a person who had cancer, but is considered cured, has a record of a disability.

An individual is regarded as disabled if they are subjected to an adverse employment action because of an actual or perceived physical or mental impairment, whether or not the impairment actually limits a major life activity. For example, a person who is denied a position because they are thought to be HIV-positive could bring a successful EEO claim even if they are not, in fact, HIV-positive.

It's useful to keep in mind that this is a generous standard. Although individuals who are regarded as disabled are not entitled to reasonable accommodation, they may raise claims about employment decisions and harassment.

Determining whether the employee is qualified. An individual with a disability has to be qualified for the job at issue when challenging an employment action under the Rehabilitation Act.

A person with a disability is qualified if they satisfy the requisite skill, experience, education, and other job-related requirements of the position at issue and can perform the essential functions of the position with or without reasonable accommodation.

A person is not qualified for a position if they pose a direct threat to safety in that position. A direct threat is a significant risk of substantial harm to the health or safety of the individual or others that cannot be eliminated or reduced by reasonable accommodation. Although you may raise safety issues you see or be asked for input about an individual's ability to safely perform their job, the ultimate decision about whether a person poses a direct threat to safety will usually be made by someone else. This decision cannot be made lightly and must be based on current medical information, and not on unfounded fears or assumptions.

Direct threat during pandemic

Whether a pandemic rises to the level of a direct threat depends on the severity of the illness. If the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention or state or local public health authorities determine that the illness is like seasonal influenza or the 2009 spring/summer H1N1 influenza, it would not pose a direct threat or justify disability-related inquiries and medical examinations.

By contrast, if the CDC or state or local health authorities determine that a pandemic is significantly more severe, such as COVID-19, it could pose a direct threat. The assessment by the CDC or public health authorities would provide the objective evidence needed for a disability-related inquiry or medical examination. During a pandemic, agencies will look to the latest CDC and state or local public health assessments. Managers should rely on agency guidance.

The EEOC has determined that the COVID-19 pandemic meets the direct threat standard. The circumstances of the coronavirus pandemic show that a significant risk of substantial harm would be posed by having someone with COVID-19, or symptoms of it, present in the workplace. This means that agencies may exclude employees from the workplace for having COVID-19, as well as make inquiries about related symptoms and exposure.

Disparate treatment

Disparate treatment is the term often used to describe unfavorable or unfair treatment that is based on a person's membership in a protected group -- in this case, the group of individuals with disabilities.

There certainly are managers who consciously exclude or otherwise unfairly treat individuals with disabilities. However, managers can also get into trouble by making decisions that are hasty or sloppy even if they didn't have a discriminatory motive.

Make documented decisions based on merit. You can avoid claims of disparate treatment by asking yourself two simple questions every time you make a decision about employees:

  1. 1. On what considerations do I base this decision?
  2. 2. Can I prove my reasons with documentation?

So long as you don't in fact discriminate and can show a legitimate justification for your actions, you have no reason for concern. As for documentation, sometimes it comes with the decision. For example, a good performance appraisal is sufficient documentation for a performance rating.

In other cases that may create disappointment, such as promotion or training selections, it is best to nail down the documentation at the time of the decision if only through notes for your own files. And make sure your documented justification is specific. Simply recording that the successful candidate was more qualified will not be helpful in jogging your memory or in providing a specific reason for your decision. Listing the selectee's training, education, interview skills, or other attributes, as compared to the other candidates, will provide you with much more useful documentation should your decision be challenged.

Warning: Making derogatory remarks or jokes about disabilities or allowing others to do so could lend credence to an argument that you took an action based on an employee's disability.

Special hiring authority. While you may get into hot water for basing an adverse action on an individual's disability, you are sometimes allowed to treat an individual with a disability more favorably when it comes to hiring. For example, Schedule A is an excepted service hiring authority that can allow you to quickly hire people with disabilities on a permanent, time-limited, or temporary basis, provided the candidate submits proof of disability.

According to the EEOC, Schedule A is intended to help people with disabilities obtain competitive status in the civil service. An agency may convert the employee to competitive service after the individual completes two years in a permanent or time-limited appointment.

Harassment

As a federal manager, it is your obligation to see to it that disability-based jokes or offensive comments are not tolerated. When incidents are severe enough or pervasive enough, they are considered harassment, and harassment is a form of discrimination.

Your primary strategy here is to promptly stop inappropriate behavior, to model appropriate behavior yourself, and to take every issue seriously from the moment it is raised or you witness it. Although a few isolated jokes or insensitive comments related to a disability are not necessarily enough to create a hostile work environment, harassment has a way of building over time.

Accordingly, it's best to act immediately if you witness or hear about conduct that could interfere with an employee's work performance or that creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment. You should seek advice from your agency HR and EEO specialists if you are unsure about how to handle possible harassment.

Here are a few cases where the employee was able to show that disability-based harassment occurred:

  • • An employee who used a pager to communicate due to his hearing impairment alleged that a manager yelled at him repeatedly for looking at the pager. He said the manager called him into his office five times to warn him about using the pager and followed him into the restroom three times to yell at him about the pager. Other employees were allowed to use their cellphones at work. The EEOC determined there was pervasive harassment that rose to the level of unreasonably interfering with the employee's work.
  • • Coworkers repeatedly referred to an employee with a disability as "Forrest Gump" and mimicked the way he groaned in pain when he walked. This caused the employee to tear up in front of them and plead with them to leave him alone. The EEOC found that the conduct was pervasive and that it was subjectively and objectively offensive, unwelcome, and abusive enough to create a hostile work environment.
  • • An employee complained that a manager told him he needed to get medical treatment and had poor hygiene because of his coughing, which was caused by cancer treatment the employee was receiving. The manager also told the employee, without a basis, that he needed to wash his hands after using the restroom. Additionally, the employee had a hearing impairment, and he requested a transmitter to help him communicate. Coworkers refused to use the device, and management discouraged it as well. In the EEOC's view, this interfered with the employee's work performance and created an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment.

Ableism

"Ableism" is a type of discrimination in which individuals without disabilities are viewed as "normal" and superior to those with a disability. The Center for Disability Rights describes ableism as "a set of beliefs or practices that devalue and discriminate against people with physical, intellectual, or psychiatric disabilities," adding that it often rests on the assumption that people with disabilities need to be "fixed" in one form or the other.

In a workplace environment, ableism may sometimes be reflected through people's best intentions, such as when a person without a disability attempts to solve a problem for a coworker without asking and without respecting the coworker's opinion. Therefore, it's important that you think about whether your interactions with employees with disabilities reflect ableist attitudes even if it was never your intention to do so. You also should observe interactions between employees in your work unit to see whether instances of ableism are occurring, and take action to correct the behaviors if they are. Seek advice from your HR and EEO offices if you are unsure how to handle the situation.

To help you recognize them, here are some examples of how ableism may occur in the workplace:

  • • Phrasing questions simply and using a raised tone to a person with a disability even though the person is not hard of hearing.
  • • Deciding not to assign a particular project to an employee who uses a wheelchair on the assumption that it would be difficult for that employee to get around.
  • • Providing assistance, such as a push, to a person using a wheelchair who has not requested help or exhibited distress.

Avoiding ableism. Here are some strategies for keeping your workplace free of ableism:

  • • Language. Many common workplace expressions are ableist in nature. For example, "our concerns fell on deaf ears" and "he was blind to the fact I was angry" are common -- but inherently ableist -- expressions. You can address this issue with your employees by initiating a discussion of ableist language and providing guidance for using inclusive language.
  • • Ableist assumptions. When interacting with an employee who has a disability, don't impose preconceived expectations or assumptions on what the employee's abilities, relationships, or interactions should be. As with all employees, each person with a disability has their own unique set of strengths and weaknesses, and supervisors should avoid viewing disabilities as inherently negative or challenging.
  • • Training. Seek out training on how to deal with ableism in the workplace so you can help dispel misconceptions and promote inclusion.
  • • Accommodations. When an employee's disability requires accommodation or affects his performance at work, you should avoid prescribing what you think the problem or solution is. For example, a manager should not unilaterally divert an assignment from an employee because of his disability or gloss over the presence of a disability when there is a clear or potential impact.

Associational discrimination

In addition to being protected from discrimination based on their own disabilities, applicants and employees who have relationships with individuals with disabilities are also protected from discrimination. This protection prohibits applicants and employees from being denied a position or otherwise being treated adversely because of unfounded stereotypes or assumptions about people with disabilities.

For example, it would be unlawful to deny an employee a promotion based on an assumption that the needs of the employee's daughter with a disability would prevent the employee from working in a more demanding position.

Although the relationship in question is often with a family member, the protection can extend to other associations, including those created through volunteering and friendships, if adverse agency actions are taken on the basis of those relationships.

It's important to note that you are not required to provide reasonable accommodation for applicants or employees based on their relationships with individuals with disabilities. However, you should make sure that employees are not treated less favorably than other employees in connection with leave or other requests connected to their association with an individual with a disability. So, for example, if you allow an employee of one race the flexibility of continuing to telework in order to protect a family member with a disability that makes them particularly susceptible to COVID-19, you should be careful not to deny a similarly situated employee of another race the same flexibility.

Medical information

Requesting medical information at the wrong time or storing it in the wrong place can create EEO problems. It's best to be well aware that specific rules apply to medical information, and you should always consult with HR if you find yourself in unfamiliar territory.

Medical exams. Generally, a medical exam may not be conducted and medical information cannot be requested prior to a conditional offer of employment. However, you may outline the essential functions of the job and ask applicants if they can perform the duties with or without reasonable accommodation.

You may also condition an offer of employment on the results of a medical examination conducted prior to the employee's entrance on duty, as long as all new prospects in the same job category are subject to the same examination.

After hire. After an employee has been hired, the agency's right to request medical information is limited. An employee may be asked for medical information if there is a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that they are unable to perform an essential function of their job or that they will pose a safety threat to themself or others because of a medical condition.

For example, an employee who has blacked out on the job may possibly pose a direct threat to safety if they work around machinery. Determining whether an employee is a safety threat requires an individualized assessment of current medical information. Because these decisions cannot be based on speculation or unfounded fears, you should not make hasty judgments or act without guidance.

In one case, an employee could not locate a parcel and the supervisor asked if she was dyslexic in front of a customer. The EEOC found that the supervisor's question about dyslexia was an improper inquiry because the evidence did not show the supervisor had an objective belief that the employee's performance was impaired by a condition. So even though the manager could have been making a rhetorical comment as opposed to an outright inappropriate request, such a comment can still violate the Rehabilitation Act.

An agency may also request medical information in connection with a request for reasonable accommodation when the disability or the need for accommodation is not obvious. Again, this is a decision you probably will not be making on your own, but it may require your input.

Additional documentation. A reasonable amount of medical documentation may be necessary to make disability-related decisions, but be aware that requests that go too far can violate the law. Always coordinate with HR when considering the need for additional medical documents.

There are five circumstances under which an agency can legitimately assert that medical information submitted by an employee is insufficient. Although you won't be expected to take the lead on retrieving medical information, as this will more likely be a function of HR or a reasonable accommodation committee, it can be useful for you to know why the agency needs more information. Additional medical information may be requested if:

  1. 1. The documentation does not specify the existence of a qualifying disability under the ADA.
  2. 2. The documentation does not explain the need for reasonable accommodation.
  3. 3. The health care professional who produced the documentation does not have the expertise to give an opinion about the employee's medical condition and the limitations imposed by it.
  4. 4. The information does not explain the employee's functional limitations.
  5. 5. Factors indicate that the information submitted is not credible.

Many times, an employee's disability and/or the need for accommodation may be obvious, in which case documentation may not be requested.

Storing information. Although medical information may be requested for certain lawful purposes, it's crucial to remember that it must be collected and maintained on separate forms, kept in separate medical files (not the official personnel folder), and be treated as confidential.

Medical information can be shared for limited purposes, including when a manager needs to be informed of medical restrictions or reasonable accommodations, or if there is a need for emergency treatment. However, it's important to know the disclosure rules or when to seek guidance, because the improper disclosure of medical information can be a violation of the law even if there was no intent to discriminate.

Confidentiality and need to know. Only those with a legitimate "need to know" related to business operations have a right to know an employee's medical information. In addition, you may not disclose employees' medical information except to those who are considered to have a legitimate need to know.

Under the Rehabilitation Act, those with a legitimate need to know include:

  1. 1. First aid and safety personnel if the disability or condition might require emergency treatment.
  2. 2. Government officials when the information is necessary to investigate the agency's compliance with the Rehabilitation Act and Americans with Disabilities Act.
  3. 3. Workers' compensation offices or insurance carriers in certain circumstances.
  4. 4. Agency EEO officials who need the information to maintain records and evaluate and report on the agency's performance in processing reasonable accommodation requests.
  5. 5. Those making the decision whether to grant a reasonable accommodation.

Even if you have a need to know, you are still only entitled to the information necessary to conduct business and manage the employee's duties.

Warning: Even if you think one of these exceptions apply, you must check with HR before disclosing medical information because you may be subject to additional restrictions or procedures. To be safe, always consult HR before taking any such action so that you do not risk revealing anything by mistake.

Remember that you are prohibited from revealing medical information to employees, coworkers, peers, or other individuals who do not qualify for an exception because they do not have a need to know. Here are some mistakes to avoid:

  • • Mailing confidential medical information about an employee's condition to colleagues.
  • • Discussing an EEO complaint and the employee's medical information on a public chat forum or social media website.
  • • Advising an employee -- in front of coworkers -- not to do something because it could exacerbate his medical condition.
  • • Emailing an employee's medical information to employees who are not his supervisors and not involved in the processing of his accommodation request.

Mistakes to avoid

While laws and policies serve to prevent disability discrimination, they do not have to be complicated. Follow these simple principles to help you succeed.

Do not:

  • • Assume that disability is the same as inability. The key consideration is whether an employee is able to perform the essential duties of a position to the required level of performance -- not whether the employee has a disability.
  • • Assume that an employee has a disability. Some disabilities are obvious, but many are not. It is not your job to determine whether an employee has a disability. Always consult your HR experts.
  • • Make assumptions about an employee's ability to safely perform their job. Any requests for medical information based on a safety concern must be based on objective evidence.
  • • Make adverse employment decisions based on an individual's relationship with an individual with a disability. This could be unlawful associational discrimination.
  • • Tolerate unacceptable performance or conduct. Employees with disabilities must maintain the same standards of conduct and performance as other employees.
  • • Hold an employee with a disability to a higher standard of conduct. The performance standards you apply must be fair and equal with regard to all employees. Whether conscious or unconscious, if you require a higher level of performance from an employee with a disability as compared with other employees, you are inviting a claim of disability discrimination.
  • • Ignore safety risks. Although it's probably not going to be up to you to determine whether an employee with a disability can safely perform a job, your observations and fair input are valuable to the process.
  • • Overlook jokes or remarks that are demeaning to individuals with disabilities. Letting inappropriate remarks or actions pass unchecked can signal your unwillingness to deal with the misconduct and lead to a hostile work environment.

Ask FMG

Q. What is my best defense against potential charges of disability discrimination?

A. Make sure you have a fair, specific, and documented reason for every personnel decision you make; comply with the confidentiality requirements concerning medical information; and stop any conduct that could be considered disability-based harassment.

Q. What if I don't intend to discriminate? Can I still get into trouble?

A. Yes, you can if your decision had a discriminatory effect and you can't provide a reasonable, nondiscriminatory reason for your decision. You can also get into trouble by mishandling medical information and by making discriminatory remarks even if they are meant in a joking manner or if you fail to stop subordinates from making such remarks.

Q. Does a disability entitle an employee to perform at a lower level or have excused conduct that otherwise would result in discipline?

A. No. Managers are required to make reasonable adjustments that enable qualified employees with disabilities to perform their jobs. However, an employee -- with or without disabilities -- is required to meet established performance and conduct standards.

Q. Do I have to hire or promote an individual with a disability who isn't qualified for the job?

A. No. However, using certain hiring authorities, including Schedule A, you are allowed to select an individual with a disability who is qualified, although not necessarily the most qualified for the job. Under this hiring method, you usually are able to hire an employee much faster and it benefits your agency's hiring statistics with regard to individuals with disabilities.

Q. Can I take disciplinary action against an employee with a disability who engages in misconduct?

A. Yes, an agency may discipline an employee for violating a workplace conduct standard even if the misconduct resulted from a disability, provided the conduct standard is job-related and consistent with business necessity. Disability law does not provide a shield for inappropriate conduct or unacceptable performance. However, discipline must be fair as compared to that received by other employees.

Q. Can I remove an employee if they are unable to do their job because of their disability?

A. Yes. You are not required to retain an employee who is not qualified to perform the essential functions of their position. However, in most cases, you will need to explore reasonable accommodation, including reassignment, before removing the employee.

Q. If an employee finds a joke or statement offensive, is that harassment?

A. A statement or joke is not necessarily unlawful harassment just because an employee is offended. Any determination about whether jokes or statements created a disability-based hostile work environment will depend on the specific circumstances of each situation. However, it's important to note that a discriminatory hostile work environment is often created over time. While an initial joke or two may not be a problem, failing to put an end to them could encourage more, which ultimately might lead to a hostile work environment.

Q. I don't engage in harassing conduct myself. Is that enough, or do I have to make sure my employees don't either?

A. As a federal manager, it's your responsibility to keep all discriminatory harassment out of the workplace. Although your commitment to appropriate behavior sets an excellent example for your employees, you still need to be watchful for harassing conduct by others and take the needed action to stop it.
