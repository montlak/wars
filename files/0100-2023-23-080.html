2. Updating Medical Information

Once an agency has determined that an employee is an individual with a disability entitled to accommodation, it may be entitled to periodic updates of medical information depending on the nature of the disability. If the disability is a permanent and stable condition, the agency may not request periodic updates of medical information. For example, common sense dictates that when an employee establishes that he or she is permanently blind or deaf, there is no need for the agency to seek periodic updates of the employee's condition.

When the condition is not permanent and stable, or when the employee does something that suggests his or her condition has changed, the agency is justified in requesting update medical information. For example, in Sabbers v. General Services Administration, 0120063209 (2008), the employee contended that the agency was not entitled to updated medical information to support her accommodation request of being allowed to work at home two days per week instead of the one day permitted under a revised agency telework policy. The complainant noted that she had previously provided documentation of her disability to the agency and a prior MSPB appeal had resulted in a decision that she was an individual with a disability. The Commission, in Sabbers, found that the agency was justified in requesting updated medical information and that a note from her physician that her condition was "unchanged" was not adequate where, in applying for a promotion, she had stated she could work "anywhere, anyplace, anytime":

First, we note that for purposes of this decision, we assume arguendo that complainant is disabled within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act. Second, we note that the agency states, and complainant does not dispute, that in the middle of 2004 the agency implemented a division-wide change in the work-at-home policy, reducing the maximum number of days from two to one per week. Management explained that the policy was prompted by a number of factors, including abuse and the need for closer communication. Complainant requested, as an exception to the new policy, to be permitted to continue to work two days a week from home as a form of reasonable accommodation. The agency asserts that complainant was requested to provide medical documentation in support of her request, but all that complainant provided was a prescription slip from her osteopath dated April 4, 2005, stating that her condition had remained "unchanged."

Complainant argues that she should not have had to provide any additional medical documentation since her disability, and the need for this particular accommodation, were already well-documented and well-known by the agency. Complainant further argues that in the past, the agency itself has acknowledged that she was being permitted to work from home two days in order to accommodate her disability. Complainant further notes that an MSPB decision dated February 24, 2004, specifically indicates that she is disabled, and that she required this particular reasonable accommodation. The agency disagrees.

Commission regulations state that when the disability and/or the need for accommodation is not obvious, the employer may ask the individual for reasonable documentation about his/her disability and functional limitations. EEOC's Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, Notice No. 915.002, Question 6 (as revised October 17, 2002).

In this case, we find that it was reasonable for management to seek an update of complainant's restrictions. First, the record shows that prior to the implementation of the work-at-home restriction, complainant had applied and was interviewed for the position of Customer Service Division, and that complainant stated during the interview that she was willing to work "anywhere, anyplace, anytime," and that "she realized that the position would allow for only 1 [work-at-home day] per week." Report of Investigation (ROI) at Table 10-A and B; see also ROI at Tab 7. Second, the record also indicates that the MSPB decision, which complainant relies upon, quotes a report from her osteopath, dated March 25, 2002, as follows:

No one can accurately predict the length of this medical condition [fibromyalgia]. I believe at some point my patient may not require the above accommodations but at this time she should adhere to my findings as previously outlined."

ROI at Tab 15, p.5.

Because complainant had implied to management in 2004, a year prior to the implementation of the work-at-home restriction, that she no longer needed to work from home two days a week and could work "anywhere, anyplace, anytime," and that in 2002, her doctor stated that "at some point" she may no longer require a medical accommodation for her condition, we find it reasonable for complainant's supervisor to request updated medical documentation. We disagree with complainant that it should have been obvious to the agency that in 2005 complainant would still require the same reasonable accommodation. Although we stress that a policy change such as the one here ordinarily would not be enough on its own to justify a request for medical documentation, we find that under these particular circumstances, it was reasonable to reevaluate complainant's accommodation needs anew.

See also Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120120559 (2015) (even where physician had previously noted "maximum medical recovery" request was justified where two years had passed and complainant had successfully bid on new route); Wood v. Postmaster General, 0120070486 (2008) (agency justified in requesting additional medical information even though it had previously accommodated complainant where there was conflicting medical evidence on her restrictions).

Absent some sort of disability-related triggering event, the agency cannot arbitrarily request updated medical information. In Velva B. v. Postmaster General, 0720160006 (2017), for instance, the agency initiated a program that, according to the agency, was meant to ensure that all injured-on-duty employees (IODs), who were either in limited-duty or rehabilitation status, were performing mission critical work for the agency. Implementing the program involved, among other things, gathering medical information on the IOD employees to make sure that their medical documentation was up to date. If it was not, the IOD employees were warned that if they did not submit updated medical information within two weeks of the request, their modified limited-duty and rehabilitation assignments might be withdrawn. The Commission found the agency's action to be unlawful disability-related medical inquiries:

The inquiries carried out in accordance with Phase 1 of the NRP were not merely "likely" to elicit information about IOD employees' disabilities; they were guaranteed to elicit that information.

...

It is not enough for a management official to merely suspect that an employee is unable to perform the essential functions of his or her job or poses a direct threat when ordering a medical inquiry. Such suspicions must be supported by enough documentation to establish that they are reasonable under the circumstances that the situation presents. Those responsible for implementing Phase 1 of the NRP based their inquiries solely on the status of the IOD employees as limited-duty or rehabilitation, without any evidence that those employees were not performing the essential functions of their positions or that they posed a direct threat to themselves or others by remaining in their positions.
