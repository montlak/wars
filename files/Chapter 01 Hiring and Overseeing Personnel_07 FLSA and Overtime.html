FLSA AND OVERTIME

The Fair Labor Standards Act requires agencies to follow certain guidelines when monitoring work, supervising schedules, counting hours worked, and paying overtime. You must understand the essential elements of FLSA compliance because violations can result in costly penalties. Work with HR to:

  • • Monitor the actual duties performed by your employees.
  • • Keep accurate lists of your employees' exemption status.
  • • Keep track of employee schedules and work performed.
  • • Count hours worked properly.
  • • Authorize and pay overtime properly.
  • • Ensure employees do not work beyond their regularly scheduled hours without approval.

Remember to document everything involving FLSA compliance. Not only does the National Archives and Records Administration require that all payroll records be kept for a minimum of six years, but having a strong written record will serve as your defense against potential FLSA claims.

Exempt vs. nonexempt

You have to follow different rules for exempt vs. nonexempt employees when counting hours and paying overtime. The Fair Labor Standards Act's requirements apply to nonexempt employees but do not apply to exempt employees.

Although your agency's HR office makes the exemption determination, you are responsible for providing them with the information necessary to properly classify your employees.

Ensure your employees are correctly classified by:

  • • Providing HR with the actual duties performed by your employees.
  • • Updating position descriptions on a consistent basis.
  • • Notifying HR of employees who temporarily perform different duties.

Exempt employees. It's helpful to have a general awareness of what types of employees are exempt from the FLSA, even though HR makes the actual determination.

To be classified as FLSA exempt, the employee's primary duties must satisfy the criteria for one of these types of exemptions:

  • • Executive employees. Individuals whose primary duties involve customarily and regularly directing the work of at least two employees, and who have authority to hire and fire or otherwise impact the employment status of other employees.
  • • Administrative employees. Individuals whose primary duties involve performing office or non-manual work directly related to managing or operating the business, and exercising discretion and independent judgment.
  • • Learned professionals. Individuals whose primary duties involve the use of advanced knowledge in a certain field, which was obtained by completing specialized academic training.
  • • Creative professionals. Individuals whose primary duties involve work in a recognized field of artistic or creative endeavor (such as music, writing, acting, and the graphic arts) that requires invention, imagination, originality, or talent.
  • • Computer professionals. Individuals whose primary duties involve high-level computer systems design, software, testing, analysis, or functional specifications.
  • • Foreign exemption. Individuals who are stationed in and spending all their work hours during the workweek in exempt areas. Foreign-stationed employees who work hours in a nonexempt area lose the foreign exemption for that workweek but may qualify for a special exemption.
  • • Employees under special rules. The following employees may also be exempt under special rules: 1) criminal investigators and U.S. Customs and Border Protection pilots receiving availability pay; 2) customs officers receiving overtime or premium pay; or 3) certain OPM-designated fire protection and law enforcement employees.

Nonexempt employees. Generally, employees who do not satisfy one of the tests for exemptions are nonexempt. You are required to count hours worked and overtime for nonexempt employees in accordance with the FLSA rules. However, you should not make this determination on your own because exceptions to the general rule may apply.

Hours worked

Make sure your employees only perform authorized work and keep accurate records of all their hours worked. For nonexempt employees, the Fair Labor Standards Act requires you to follow certain rules when counting hours of work.

These FLSA "hours of work" refer to any time an employee spends performing an activity for the benefit of an agency and under the control or direction of the agency. This includes any time that an employee is:

  • • Required to be on duty.
  • • Suffered or permitted to work.
  • • On standby, waiting or idle.

Counting hours of work. Generally, you must count any time an employee spends performing the job's principal activities as hours of work. However, you should pay extra attention and work with HR on certain types of hours that have special rules.

Often, counting time as FLSA hours of work depends on whether employees performed the activity within their regularly scheduled workweek as opposed to during unscheduled, irregular times.

Follow these rules for counting hours for the following activities:

  • • Preparatory or concluding activities. Any time above 10 minutes spent preparing for or finishing an activity that is critical to the employee's principal activities counts as hours of work. Time spent on noncore activities does not generally have to be included.
  • • Rest and meal periods. Any authorized rest period not exceeding 20 minutes generally counts as hours of work. Meal periods generally do not count unless an exception applies, such as for fire protection or law enforcement employees.
  • • Traveling time. Generally, travel that is required and scheduled during the regular workday counts as hours of work. Travel that requires driving a vehicle or performing other work, or being a passenger on a one-day or overnight assignment away from the official duty station counts as hours of work.
  • • Training, lectures, meetings, and conferences. These hours during a regularly scheduled workday count as hours of work. However, special rules apply to time spent at training, lectures, meetings, and conferences outside of the regular workday.
  • • Standby duty. This time counts as hours of work if the employee is restricted to a designated post for work-related reasons, must remain ready to perform work, and cannot use the time for personal reasons due to limits placed on their activities.
  • • On-call status. This time generally does not count as hours of work if the employee is allowed to: 1) leave a telephone number or carry an electronic device for the purpose of being contacted, even though the employee is required to remain within a reasonable call-back radius; or 2) make arrangements for another person to handle any work arising during the on-call period.
  • • Sleep time. Sleep generally does not count as hours of work if: 1) the shift is 24 hours or more; 2) adequate facilities are available, so an employee may sleep for an uninterrupted period; and 3) a five-hour sleep period is available. A maximum of eight sleep hours in a 24-hour period may be excluded from hours of work.
  • • Grievances or representational functions. This time counts as work hours when the grievance or appealable action with an agency requires the employee to be on the agency's premises, or the agency grants the employee "official time" status for performing representational functions.
  • • Medical attention. Receiving or waiting for medical attention counts as hours of work if the employee: 1) reported for duty and subsequently became ill or injured requiring medical attention on that workday; and 2) received medical attention on the agency's premises or at a medical facility off the agency's premises as directed by the agency during regular working hours. Time spent for an agency-required physical examination also counts.
  • • Charitable activities. This time counts when requested or directed by the agency, but excludes voluntary time outside the regular workday.

Counting nonwork hours. Certain paid time off also counts as FLSA hours of work, such as paid leave, holidays, compensatory time off, or excused absences. Generally, unpaid nonwork time is not counted as hours of work, including leave without pay, furlough, and absence without leave.

Suffered or permitted work

Suffered or permitted work is one of the most problematic areas for FLSA compliance. Failing to supervise an employee's hours can lead to agency liability under the FLSA's "suffer or permit" rule, which has become an increasing cause for concern with new technologies, such as smartphones.

Under the general rule for counting FLSA hours, agencies must pay nonexempt employees for all hours of work, which includes any time during which an employee is "suffered or permitted to work."

What is suffered or permitted work? Suffered or permitted work is any work that:

  • • Is performed for the benefit of the agency, whether requested or not; and
  • • The employee's supervisor knows or has reason to believe is being performed and could have prevented the work from being performed.

Suffered or permitted work may become an issue, for example, when:

  • • An employee is incorrectly classified as exempt and files a grievance for overtime pay or a change in classification, arguing they are owed overtime pay for the period they should have been classified as nonexempt.
  • • An employee who is classified as nonexempt claims they are due overtime pay because the agency permitted the additional hours even though they were not explicitly authorized.
  • • You inadvertently allow suffered or permitted work beyond the employee's regular work schedule. The hours count toward overtime. This includes any time you know the employee is performing work outside of authorized hours, but let the employee continue to work anyway. It is important to ensure employees know they must get approval to work outside of regularly scheduled hours.

Modern technology as evidence. Generally, when an employee asks for the records as part of an FLSA claim, the agency has to provide them. Employees can prove that the agency knew or should have known about the hours worked by showing records of their time from:

  • • Login/logout sheets.
  • • Computer logins and access.
  • • Emails.
  • • Smartphones.

In some cases, employees can count access from home and use the records to support their claim. Any time more than 15 minutes spent working -- even responding to or sending document drafts to the office -- will likely be considered as suffered or permitted work. However, if the employee checks email for himself, then that time should not qualify as suffered or permitted work.

This is a particularly tricky area because the law is unsettled with regard to whether usage of mobile devices over the weekend is considered suffered or permitted work. Nonetheless, an employee spending 20 to 30 minutes on their smartphone outside of scheduled hours could be considered doing suffered or permitted work.

Monitoring schedules and prevention. The best way to prevent suffer and permit problems is by monitoring employee hours. You should also:

Adjust an employee's schedule to meet the nature of the work, such as allowing the employee to set early/late hours or a variation of the eight-hour workday.

  • • Determine whether your agency permits flexible work schedules, and, if so, work with HR to begin implementing them.
  • • Make sure your employees are classified correctly by working with your HR office.
  • • Tell employees to stop working at the end of their scheduled workday and instruct them not to work from home after hours.
  • • Limit employee use of smartphones to their regular work schedule.
  • • Ensure that all telework agreements clearly contain a prohibition on working overtime without advanced supervisory approval.

Overtime

There are different rules for FLSA overtime versus Title 5 overtime. FLSA overtime applies to nonexempt employees, while the Title 5 overtime standard applies to exempt employees.

Under the FLSA overtime standard, you must pay nonexempt employees overtime for working more than eight hours per day or 40 hours per workweek at a rate of 1-1/2 hours of regular pay.

For regularly scheduled overtime, you must pay nonexempt employees for every minute worked. However, when crediting irregular or occasional overtime work, you may use fractional hours of work no larger than a quarter of an hour.

Exceptions and special rules. You should always check with your HR office about exceptions and special rules whenever you confront the following:

  • • Flexible or compressed work schedules and compensatory time off.
  • • Certain law enforcement and fire protection employees.
  • • Certain employees receiving premium pay for working night, standby, irregular, or hazardous duty differential hours.
  • • Certain employee hours spent on standby, on-call, eating, or sleeping.

Overtime pay rate. The FLSA overtime pay rate is 1-1/2 times the regular hourly rate, which must be calculated according to specific rules under the FLSA. If you have a question regarding how much it would cost you to pay an employee FLSA, contact your HR office.

Compensatory time off

Compensatory time off refers to overtime exchanged for paid leave from work. When administering compensatory time off, you must be extra careful because of the restrictions on offering comp time and the payout rules for unused comp time.

You should check with your HR department to determine whether your agency offers this option, and if any additional limitations on comp time apply.

Implement best practices. The agency's defense against FLSA claims involving comp time hinge on how well you have documented your efforts to comply with FLSA rules. Therefore, your best defense is to lay a heavily documented paper trail of evidence.

Work with your HR office to:

  • • Make sure you have the right forms for comp time approval.
  • • Have a system in place to ensure that you have enough funds to pay out any approved comp time in case the need arises.
  • • Keep a file of all the notices and memoranda you receive from HR about comp time policies.
  • • Contact HR when you have questions.
  • • Monitor and certify employee time and attendance vigorously.
  • • Challenge any incorrect or unauthorized comp time immediately in writing, such as through email.

Do not offer it first. FLSA nonexempt employees may request comp time in lieu of an equal amount of irregular or occasional overtime. However, managers may not:

  • • Initially offer comp time to employees. Employees must be the initiator when it comes to requesting comp time. The manager cannot ask the employee to take comp time.
  • • Require nonexempt employees to take compensatory time off instead of overtime.
  • • Directly or indirectly intimidate, threaten, or coerce employees to take comp time.

Pay out unused compensatory time. It's also important to remember that you must pay employees the value of any unused comp time if they:

  • • Have not used their comp time within 26 pay periods.
  • • Are separated from service.
  • • Leave the agency.
  • • Are placed in a leave without pay status because of duty in the uniformed services or an on-the-job injury.

If any of these events occur, you will have to pay the employee the equivalent of the FLSA overtime pay the employee would have otherwise received.

Travel time. You also should take extra care when employees are in travel status because additional rules apply. Check with your HR department before approving travel to determine which hours count for FLSA overtime purposes vs. comp time for travel.

Religious compensatory time off. Employees whose personal religious beliefs require absence from work may work overtime to earn religious compensatory time off. This special form of comp time can be used to make up for the time lost in meeting their personal religious requirements.

The personal religious requirements do not have to be officially mandated by the employee's religious organization and you are not charged with determining whether an employee's belief is the correct interpretation of a religious creed. It's sufficient that the employee's personal religious beliefs cause them to feel an obligation to be absent from work for a religious purpose.

When requesting the comp time off, the employee must provide the name and/or description of the religious observance, dates and times of absence, and dates and times the employee plans to earn religious comp time off. You must approve an employee's request to use religious compensatory time off unless you determine that approving the request would interfere with the agency's ability to efficiently carry out its mission.

The employee must perform the overtime work within 13 pay periods before or 13 pay periods after the pay period during which the religious compensatory time off is used.

It's important that you and your employees follow your agency's religious comp time policy. Failure to do so could result in misuse or abuse of this form of comp time and create an unplanned monetary liability for the agency. Here are some situations you need to avoid:

  • • An employee accumulates more religious compensatory time than needed.
  • • An employee submits and you approve a request to earn religious comp time after the employee earned the hours.
  • • You approve an employee's request to earn religious comp time without established dates, times, and purposes for the religious observance absences.
  • • An employee requested, you approved, and the employee earned religious comp time for a specific date, time, and named religious observance, but the employee then used annual leave instead of religious comp time for the requested absence.

Alternative work schedules and telework

As alternative work schedules and telework arrangements become more popular, you need to remember that FLSA rules still apply. You can prevent FLSA violations by instituting strict internal controls that satisfy the operational challenges associated with supervising nonexempt employees who telework or have alternative work schedules. You should make sure:

  • • HR has an accurate list of employees who telework or have alternative work schedules.
  • • The exemption status as either FLSA exempt or nonexempt for these employees has been reviewed and is correct.
  • • These employees do not perform work during unauthorized hours.

Alternative work schedules. Alternative work schedules generally come in two forms in the federal government -- flexible or compressed work schedules. Overall, the same general FLSA rules on counting hours and paying overtime still apply, but are just applied to the framework of these programs.

When comparing these programs, each has slightly different rules regarding the basic work requirement, credit hours, overtime hours, comp time, and Sunday or holiday hours. This means you have to take into account the different program structures when applying the FLSA rules. Work closely with HR to learn how to manage these programs in compliance with the FLSA.

Telework. Monitoring employees who telework is challenging because you cannot see the employee's arrival and departure as in the office setting. The key to FLSA compliance is still to make sure that these employees do not perform work during unauthorized hours. This can be done through internal controls that show a good-faith effort to comply with the FLSA. You should also:

  • • Instruct teleworkers not to work during unauthorized times.
  • • Follow proper recordkeeping procedures to track employee working hours.
  • • Regularly remind employees in writing that overtime is not permitted or authorized without a supervisor's prior advance approval.
  • • Set clear expectations for work progress updates, the daily schedule, and pre-approval for schedule changes.

Claims, good faith, and errors

Even if you do your best to comply with the FLSA, it's possible that employees may file a complaint challenging:

  • • Their FLSA exemption status.
  • • Any uncompensated overtime hours.
  • • Any alleged retaliation for making an FLSA complaint.

Work with HR on a regular basis to make sure you are gathering enough documentation to prove your good-faith efforts to comply with the FLSA, and do not retaliate, even inadvertently, against employees who file FLSA complaints.

Show good faith with documentation. You can minimize your agency's potential liability by consistently showing good faith in your efforts to comply with the FLSA. If the case is resolved in the employee's favor, the agency may owe up to two years of back pay for incorrectly administered overtime pay or up to three years of back pay when the agency is found guilty of willfully violating the FLSA.

By demonstrating your good-faith efforts, the agency can prove that it did not willfully violate the FLSA, and therefore reduce the cost to two years of back pay. You show good-faith efforts by providing documentation on how you monitored your employees' schedules and overtime hours. The more relevant documentation you have, the stronger your defense will be.

Do not retaliate. Remember that you may not retaliate against an employee who files an FLSA complaint, even if the complaint is only a verbal complaint. If an employee comes to you with an FLSA complaint, this may be sufficient to trigger your responsibility to refrain from any reprisal.

Comply with claim decisions. Upon receiving a claim decision, you should:

  • • Take all necessary steps to comply with the decision.
  • • Adhere to the compliance instructions and timeline specified in the decision.
  • • Identify any similarly situated employees so that they are treated in the same manner and inform them of their right to file an FLSA claim.

Fix any errors. Whenever you recognize an FLSA compliance error, work with HR to take remedial actions to correct the error immediately. If you allow it to continue, this will increase the likelihood of a willful violation of the FLSA and the agency may have to pay triple back pay in damages. You should:

  • • Work with HR to determine if the error will require you to make back pay payments.
  • • Remember to keep accurate records of how you fixed the problem.
  • • Implement new procedures to prevent the problem from happening again.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Assume your employees know when they must stop working. Make sure your nonexempt employees know that they are only permitted to work scheduled hours, unless a supervisor authorizes additional time.
  • • Neglect to monitor the actual hours worked by nonexempt employees. At times, the nature of the work will require an employee to work additional hours to complete an assignment, although the employee does not ask for or even record the hours as overtime. This can give rise to an FLSA claim for overtime pay because the work benefitted the agency and the supervisor permitted the work.
  • • Assume that an employee is exempt. Check with your HR office to determine whether a given employee is FLSA exempt. Provide HR with information on the employee's primary duties, focusing on the actual duties performed rather than only the job description.
  • • Try to determine FLSA work hours and overtime rights on your own. The FLSA imposes intricate requirements, many of which have numerous exceptions. Therefore, contact your HR department and work together to resolve issues on exemptions, work hours, and overtime.
  • • Ignore possible FLSA abuse. Provide information to your HR office and discuss how to resolve suspected abuse.

Ask FMG

Q. What's the difference between nonexempt and exempt employees?

A. Nonexempt employees are those who do not satisfy any of the exemption criteria and, therefore, are entitled to accrue Fair Labor Standards Act hours of work and overtime. Exempt employees are those who satisfy the test for one of the exemptions and, therefore, are not entitled to FLSA hours and overtime.

Q. How do I know which employees are FLSA exempt and nonexempt?

A. Your HR office makes FLSA exemption status determinations. Therefore, contact your HR office to make sure you have the most updated list of which employees are FLSA exempt and nonexempt. Also, the cover sheet to all position descriptions, OF-8, should indicate whether the position is exempt or nonexempt.

Q. When do I have to count on-call time as FLSA hours of work?

A. Time spent in an on-call status generally is not considered hours of work if: 1) the employee is allowed to leave a telephone number or to carry an electronic device for the purpose of being contacted, even though the employee is required to remain within a reasonable call-back radius; or 2) the employee is allowed to make arrangements such that any work that may arise during the on-call period will be performed by another person.

Q. How do I deal with employees using smartphones, emails, or laptops outside of regular hours?

A. Managers can prevent suffer and permit problems by instructing employees to limit their use of smartphones to their regular work schedule. Employees should also be instructed not to send emails or work at home after hours unless they have specific prior authorization. This needs to be periodically reinforced by instructions to employees to defend against a back pay claim.

Q. How do I stop employees from logging overtime without permission?

A. As a manager, you can prevent suffer and permit problems by monitoring employee hours, adjusting an employee's schedule to meet the nature of the work, implementing flexible work schedules where permitted, working with your HR office to make sure your employees are classified correctly, telling employees to stop working at the end of their scheduled workday, and instructing them not to work from home after hours.
