J. ELEMENT OF GOOD FAITH

There is an element of good faith that is inherent in every settlement agreement. The good faith element relates not only to settlement negotiations, but extends to actions taken to avoid the obligations of an agreement. As the Commission noted in Edmundson v. Secretary of Treasury, 01940972 (1994):

Moreover, a requirement of acting in good faith on the part of the agency and appellant can be presumed as a part of this agreement. See generally Stringfield v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01914018 (February 28, 1992); Buckley v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01913564 (September 17, 1991). Thus, if the agency contrived in some way so that no positions would become available for filling through a Merit Promotion Vacancy, the agency would have failed to satisfy the requirement of acting in good faith.

In this case, the agency has transferred in two persons at the GS-11 level, one person at the GS-12 level, and is under-filling a fourth position at the GS-9 level. The agency has therefore engaged in such actions to avoid fulfillment of the terms of the contract by evading the technical requirement of "Merit Promotion Vacancy." As such, the agency has failed to act in good faith and has breached the settlement agreement.

The element of good faith does not act so as to guarantee a position to an employee who is promised priority consideration. But, it does act to ensure that the agency cannot manipulate the selection system so as to prevent the referral of the employee for any positions.

Because an element of good faith is inherent in every settlement agreement, a party can breach an agreement by exhibiting bad faith in the execution of the terms. Although there is no single test the Commission has adopted for evaluating bad faith, a dubious argument over the interpretation of a settlement agreement combined with factual discrepancies in the agency's implementation of the agreement can lead to a finding of bad faith in failure to comply with the terms of a settlement. In Wong v. Postmaster General, 05931097, (1994), the agency and the complainant entered into a settlement agreement that provided that the complainant would be given a casual appointment "as soon as possible," but in any event "in this calendar year." The agency argued that the only requirement placed upon it by the agreement was to provide the complainant with a casual appointment before the end of the calendar year. After finding that the agency in Wong was also required to comply with the "as soon as possible" language of the agreement, the Commission noted:

Moreover, we suspect further bad faith in the agency's attempt to afford appellant a casual appointment in August 1993, when it denied appellant's appointment based upon his alleged inability to satisfy the physical examination. Specifically, we find several discrepancies on the appellant's Medical Examination and Assessment Form dated July 28, 1993, which cast doubt upon the agency's employment decision.

First, we note that, in the Medical History section, the Attending Medical Physician indicated that appellant suffered from "Monocular Vision." We further note that, in the Physical Findings section, the Attending Medical Physician checked the Limitations/Restrictions box and noted "No Job In An Area Where Excellent Vision Is Required." However, the Attending Medical Physician further indicated no significant findings in appellant's employment data which related to the appellant's ability to perform the essential functions of the position. In addition, the Attending Medical Physician checked the Moderate Risk Restriction box indicating that appellant would be medically qualified to perform the essential function of the position if appellant wore eyeglasses. The agency's apparent denial of the appellant's employment based upon his requirement to wear eyeglasses to perform the duties of the desired position is highly questionable, especially when one considers the fact that appellant had the same medical condition during his prior period of employment with the agency. In sum, it appears that, other then the appellant's medical history regarding his "monocular vision," the Medical Assessment Statement did not set forth any significant physical or medical findings which would preclude appellant from performing the essential functions of the desired position.

Second, we note that the appellant's Medical Examination and Assessment Form contained comments by two different authors, without an adequate explanation regarding the items crossed out or written above other comments. Third, we note that the appellant's Medical Examination and Assessment Form--Section F was not completed by the Appointing/Referring Official indicating whether or not appellant was medically suitable for employment. In the absence of an adequate explanation regarding the above-noted discrepancies, we find the agency's actions in this matter did constitute a breach of the terms of the settlement agreement.

An agency breaches a settlement agreement where the terms of the agreement allowed the complainant to resign instead of being terminated, but the termination was held against her when she reapplied for a position with the agency. Holley v. Postmaster General, 01960745 (1996). As the Commission in Holley noted:

It is clear from the record...that the agency failed to implement the agreement for over one year. Although the agency properly noted that nothing in the agreement guaranteed appellant a position, implicit in the agreement is that appellant would not be denied a position because the agency previously terminated her. We further find that the agency's April 1995 change to the PS Form 50 did not operate to cure its breach: appellant alleges without contradiction that she was injured as a result of the agency's breach.

The Commission found that the agency had breached a provision in a settlement agreement requiring it to give the complainant's request for reinstatement "serious, good faith consideration" when the agency rejected the application for reasons it was aware of at the time it entered into the agreement. Colmenero v. Postmaster General, 01956357 (1996). By examining the complainant's attendance record, the agency failed to give his reinstatement request such a consideration. As the complainant noted on appeal, the agency was well aware of his attendance record at the time the agreement was signed. Using that attendance record against him was evidence that the agency never intended to give any reinstatement request a "serious, good faith consideration." Id.

Although a complainant who agrees in settlement to accept the agency's best efforts to find her a position runs a substantial risk that those efforts will be less than vigorous, breach of the settlement agreement can be established by showing the agency made no effort to fulfill the promise. In Arriola v. Secretary of Energy, 01964876 (1996), the agreement called for specified agency officials and departments to make an "immediate good faith effort" to transfer appellant to a "suitable and acceptable position," ideally at the GS-15 level, at the agency's headquarters. The agreement also stated that appellant would be "fully involved" in this search and that he had the right to decline proposed positions for substantive reasons. In holding that the agency breached the agreement in Arriola, the Commission found:

There is no evidence in the record that the agency made any such efforts, except for the forwarding of two position announcements. The agency argued that it found appellant a GS-14 position in its Germantown, Maryland facility, which satisfies its obligations under the agreement. However, the Commission disagreed for several reasons.

First, the agency did not secure this position until almost a year had passed since the agreement was reached, and there is no evidence that appellant was involved in seeking this position.

Second, there is no evidence that the agency made any effort to search for a GS-15 position, as contemplated by the agreement.

Third, the agreement provides that appellant may decline a position for substantive reasons; here, appellant has stated that he declined the position because it involves a significant increase in his commute. For these reasons, we find that the offered position in Germantown, Maryland does not satisfy the agency's obligations under the agreement....

Similarly, in Quinn v. Secretary of Energy, 01A60880 (2006), the Commission found that the agency had breached a settlement provision calling for it to "fully cooperate and support" the complainant in finding a "mutually agreeable: Intergovernmental Personnel Act position or other inter/intra--agency assignment." The Commission found that the record contained no evidence of what reasons, if any, the agency had for denying the IPA request at issue.

But, when an agency need only "consider" a complainant for overtime, pursuant to a settlement agreement, the Commission will not require the agency to actually grant overtime absent a showing of bad faith. The Commission, in Nicklin v. Postmaster General, 01961155 (1997), explained:

Upon review of the record, the Commission finds that the plain language of the settlement agreement only requires the agency to "consider" appellant for any available overtime work in Presort Mail. The record indicates that appellant was provided with some opportunities for overtime, but later the agency decided it was more cost effective to use other employees for this work on straight time. There is no indication in the record that the agency was acting in bad faith in making this decision. Therefore, the Commission finds there was substantial compliance with the settlement agreement on the part of the agency. See, generally, Buckley v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01903564 (September 17, 1990), request to reopen denied, EEOC Request No. 05910082 (April 4, 1991).

The mere fact that a job obtained in settlement is abolished is not sufficient to show agency bad faith, where there is a reasonable period of time between the settlement and the job action. For example, in Nowlin v. Postmaster General, 01953605 (1996), the Commission held that the agency had not breached the settlement agreement where the promised position was abolished through an agency restructuring, eight months after the complainant settled. There was no specific evidence indicating the agency knew the position would be abolished when it entered the agreement.
