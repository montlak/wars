b. Disparate Treatment

Disparate treatment is just that--being treated differently from others that are similarly situated. This should be a straightforward analysis. That was the case in Lynwood R. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120171820 (2019), where the Commission wrote:

While we note that the AJ found that Complainant's lack of cell phone and GPS was not an adverse employment action, we find that Complainant has shown an injury or harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. See Diaz v. Dep't of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994). The Agency provided cell phones and/or GPS as a privilege of employment for their drivers, and CL denied a cell phone to Complainant. As such, Complainant has a viable discrimination claim that he was treated differently than other drivers.

The Commission is at times, however, inconsistent in dealing with the issue of standing. Part of the Commission's inconsistency has to do with the fact that sometimes the Commission views allegations from the perspective of whether the complaint alleges an injury-in-fact and sometimes it views allegations from the perspective of disparate treatment without regard to the issue of injury. For example, in Lee v. Postmaster General, 01963663 (1997), the Commission held that the appellant stated a claim of disparate treatment based on an incident with her supervisor:

The appellant alleges that an agency official treated her with disrespect and tapped her hard in the back on an occasion when she was working on a flat sorter machine. She alleges that the manager treated her in that manner because she is an Asian woman who does not speak fluent English. She alleges that the manager would never treat a white, Hispanic or African-American employee in the same manner. The appellant alleges that the alleged incident has adversely affected her physical and mental health.

In Lee, the Commission simply glossed over the issue of injury-in-fact by stating the complainant alleged she was affected physically and mentally by the supervisor's actions. Virtually every complainant alleges some such "injury." The simple fact of the matter is that, in Lee, the Commission focused exclusively on the complainant's allegation that she was treated differently than someone outside of her protected class. While disparate treatment is clearly a traditional element of a discrimination claim, the existence of disparate treatment by itself does not address the issue of whether the employee has suffered some tangible harm that meets the standing requirement. Until the Commission learns, on a consistent basis, to focus on the injury alleged, and fashions and applies a test that sets a threshold level of injury for standing purposes, it will continue to issue decisions that obscure, rather than clarify, what is actionable under the antidiscrimination statutes.

The Commission's decision in Douglas v. Secretary of Navy, 01963068 (1997), illustrates this point. In that case, the Commission held that the complainant failed to state a claim where he alleged that, because of his age, he was the only applicant required to take a driver's test. In Douglas, the Commission focused on whether the complainant had suffered some injury and found no claim had been stated, even though the complainant clearly alleged that he had been subjected to disparate treatment:

The record reflects that on November 28, 1995, appellant filed a formal EEO complaint alleging that the agency discriminated against him based on age (62) when he was the only one of seven applicants for two Driver positions who was requested to take a driving test. Appellant did not contest the outcome of the selection action. During counseling, appellant indicated that the selecting official continued to harass him in an unspecified manner, but the formal complaint did not include an allegation of harassment.

Here, there is no evidence that appellant suffered any loss or harm in connection with taking the driving test. The record reflects that appellant's supervisor requested him to take the driving test. The selecting official, however, stated that the supervisor requested appellant to take the driving test before the criteria for the Driver positions had been established. When the criteria were established, the driving test was not included. Therefore, the selecting official did not consider appellant's performance on the driving test (which was not recorded) in the selection action. Because there is no indication that appellant suffered a loss or harm on account of taking the driving test, the complaint fails to state a claim, and was properly dismissed.

Cf. McGill v. Secretary of Navy, 01962529 (1997) (a complainant who alleges he was not allowed in certain work area because of his race states a claim).

There simply is no reasoned basis for drawing a distinction between the allegations in Lee and those in Douglas. The only difference is in the Commission's approach; in one case, the Commission focused on that portion of the allegation dealing with disparate treatment, and in the other case, the Commission focused on whether some harm had occurred to the complainant. Cortez J. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120161134 (2016), is a simple, but instructive, example of a failure to allege an injury-in-fact:

On December 10, 2015, Complainant, a former Food Service/File Clerk the Agency's Hudson Valley Health Care System in Montrose, New York, filed the instant formal complaint. Therein, Complainant alleged that the Agency subjected him to discrimination on the basis of age when:

1. on November 6, 2015, he was issued an application as a "new applicant" for employment and not for a "reinstatement eligible" applicant, for the position vacancy of Food Service Worker, WG-2, Announcement Number VHA 620-2016-9-1535307-BU, and this action contributed to his non-selection; and

2. on November 20, 2015, he was informed that he was ineligible for consideration for the position vacancy of Food Service Worker, WG-2, Announcement Number VHA 6202016-9-1535307-BU.

On February 12, 2016, the Agency issued the instant final decision. The Agency dismissed the instant formal complaint for failure to state a claim, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(1). Specifically, the Agency stated that on September 17, 2013, Complainant signed a settlement agreement through the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) concerning his removal from Agency employment. The settlement agreement specified that Complainant agreed not to seek employment with the Agency or any of its affiliates in the future.

Further, the Agency determined that because Complainant voluntarily waived his rights to apply for employment with VA VISN 3 [Veterans Integrated Service Network 3] and its affiliates by the terms agreed in the settlement agreement, he does not have a standing to bring a claim of employment discrimination against the Agency.

The record contains a copy of the September 17, 2013 MSPB settlement agreement. Therein, Complainant agreed, in part, that "he will neither apply for nor accept employment with VISN 3 or any of its affiliates. If the appellant does obtain such employment, he further agrees that he may be removed pursuant to this agreement and waives all rights to contest the removal."

The instant appeal followed.

Analysis and Findings

The regulation set forth at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(1) provides, in relevant part, that an Agency shall dismiss a complaint that fails to state a claim. An Agency shall accept a complaint from any aggrieved employee or applicant for employment who believes that he or she has been discriminated against by that agency because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age or disabling condition. 29 C.F.R. §§ 1614.103, .106(a). The Commission's federal sector case precedent has long defined an "aggrieved employee" as one who suffers a present harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. Diaz v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994).

In the instant case, Complainant is not aggrieved because it is undisputed that he voluntarily waived his rights to seek employment with the Agency's VISN 3 or its affiliates pursuant to the MSPB settlement agreement he signed on September 17, 2013. A fair reading of his formal complaint and related EEO counseling materials reveals no claim beyond those directly related to his non-selection to positions covered by the MSPB settlement.

The Agency's final decision dismissing the formal complaint for failure to state a claim is AFFIRMED.
