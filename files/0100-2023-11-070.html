1. Identical Complaints

Issues relating to identical complaints are discussed fully in Chapter 3, subheading "Identical Complaints," because it is at the formal complaint stage that agencies dismiss complaints as being identical to previous complaints. However, the Commission has held that an agency may not assert that complainant's allegation is identical to one contained in a previous complaint, and, at the same time, claim that the new allegation may not be included in the previous complaint as part of a continuing violation. In Koch v. Securities & Exchange Commission, 01965642 (1997), the question arose when the complainant, in a second complaint, claimed the agency continued to deny his request for reasonable accommodation. The Commission, in Koch, held:

The agency dismissed the allegation on the ground that it "appears to be identical" to the allegation in the appellant's complaint previously filed in October 1994 under agency docket no. 58-94. Appellant concedes that the issue is identical but he expresses a concern that the agency might not address the claim in no. 58-94 as a claim of continuing violation. He asserts that the instant allegation is made to preserve his claim that the agency has continued to discriminate against him after the filing of the complaint in no. 58-94 by denying his requests for an accommodation. On appeal, the agency takes an internally inconsistent position. Although the agency maintains that the allegation is "identical" to the previous allegation, it disputes the appellant's position that the previous allegation states a claim of a continuing violation such as to include the appellant's post-October 1994 claim for an accommodation. Because the agency has clearly acknowledged its unwillingness to permit the appellant an opportunity to proceed with his post-October 1994 claim in no. 58-94 under a continuing violation theory, we find the agency erred in dismissing the instant allegation on the ground that it was "identical" to the previous allegation.

In view of the fact that the agency declines to view the previous allegation as a continuing violation that would include the incidents alleged in allegation 3, we find that the previous allegation merely states "the same type" of a claim as that alleged in allegation 3 but that it is not "identical" to allegation 3. Therefore, allegation 3 must be viewed as a separate claim of continuing violation that must be accepted for an investigation.

For a full discussion of "Identical Complaints," see Chapter 3.
