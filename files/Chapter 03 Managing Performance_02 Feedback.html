FEEDBACK

When used correctly, feedback is one of your most valuable tools in performance management. It not only promotes a workplace culture of open communication, but it also impacts a number of other important areas such as behavior, quality, productivity, and operations. The success of your work unit will depend, in large part, on your utilization of feedback.

Some managers avoid providing feedback because they feel any performance discussion raises the specter of negativity, accusations, and threats of negative consequences. While it is primarily designed to point out problems, feedback does not have to be contentious or critical. So, it is important to be attentive of the role feedback plays and the manner in which it is delivered, received, and used in the performance of your and your employees' duties.

Feedback guides the path. Not giving your employees feedback is like expecting them to find their way to Siberia without a map or GPS system. They can probably do it, but it will take luck, a lot of wrong turns, and a long time. You do not want the productivity of your work unit to be driven by luck and plagued by mistakes.

Be the GPS beacon for your unit. Guide them with the regular feedback they need to get directly to their goal. This will take the guesswork out of their daily tasks and allow them to focus on the mission. It also saves you from expending your precious time and energy on corrective actions when they travel too far down a wrong path. Feedback will keep your employees on the right road and ultimately make your job easier.

Don't wait to be asked. Certain managers may feel that if their employees want feedback, they'll ask for it -- not a wise approach. In general, employees don't want to give you the impression that they don't know the elements of their jobs. They'd rather seek advice from others than appear deficient in your eyes. Leaving it to others to steer your employees or letting them guess what direction to take can lead to disaster.

Your veteran employees, newbies, and everyone in between can benefit from feedback. Their performance and goal attainment depend on it. And because feedback is a two-way street, proactively seeking it can have a positive impact on operations and your own performance.

Giving feedback

Giving feedback can be a challenging aspect of your job. You may think that it's easier to just avoid it altogether. Unfortunately, you don't have that luxury. As a federal manager, it is essential for you to have an open line of communication with your work unit on a daily basis.

Don't wait until the performance appraisal to heap a year's worth of feedback on your employee. It needs to be an ongoing process in order to maintain performance and productivity levels, job satisfaction, and minimize conflict between you and your employees.

Positive feedback. This feedback method is your reaction to things that are done acceptably or well. Letting your employees know when they do something right is an excellent technique to reinforce the positive performance or behavior, which will encourage its repetition. When used properly, positive feedback is very effective.

Positive feedback may not be as common as you think. Most federal managers are more focused on fixing problems than recognizing good work, and therefore, giving positive feedback is more of an afterthought than a priority. You may even feel that if your employees perform their tasks correctly, positive feedback is unnecessary since they are doing the job for which they're being paid. In reality, positive feedback is critically important for raising performance and productivity levels, improving your workplace environment, and lowering staff turnover.

Positive feedback must be specific and given in a timely manner. Give the feedback immediately after the event to instill reinforcement. Your employees must also know what specific aspect of their work or behavior you are recognizing so that they can repeat it. Expressing praise is always appreciated by the recipient. It demonstrates to your staff that you're paying attention to their performance on a daily basis. This can serve to raise their performance and morale levels.

Constructive feedback. This feedback technique is used when specific performance or behavior issues occur. You begin by simply sharing your observations and considering your employee's perspective. Once all the facts are known, you give direction or you collaborate with the employee to reach an understanding of how best to move forward. It's constructive because its purpose is to alter behavior or build up performance, not tear it down.

Constructive feedback may be perceived by your employees as unpleasant criticism if you don't do it correctly. Preparation and timing are important. Gather all the necessary information and mentally prepare for the conversation. The feedback should occur contemporaneously with the event that triggered its need. Leave your emotions out of the equation. If you approach employees too aggressively, or they detect that you're upset, they will naturally get defensive and none of your feedback will be heard. Approach the situation with a positive tone. Be optimistic that your feedback will serve its purpose. Consider the following techniques:

  • • Start by asking the employee a question about the issue at hand. Break the ice with something as simple as "How do you think your presentation went?"
  • • Ask open-ended questions to get employees to discover their own shortcomings, such as, "How did you prepare for the presentation?"
  • • Listen to their responses. You won't be able to offer constructive feedback unless you listen to employees' perspectives.
  • • Ask permission to give feedback -- "Do you mind if I share my observations with you?" This sets a positive tone for the feedback and it gives your employee the feeling of being engaged and respected.
  • • Offer suggestions and collaborate on solutions that utilize the employee's strengths.
  • • Focus on specific situations and behavior. Generalizations will not accomplish the purpose of constructive feedback and will only serve to confuse or frustrate your employee.
  • • If dealing with a critical performance issue, be clear that change is necessary and discuss how the employee intends to improve.
  • • Seek agreement going forward and establish timelines for results.

Constructive feedback will require a lot of practice, but continued application, and rehearsal, will help you develop the skill.

Remember: At the conclusion of constructive feedback, it's a good idea to offer to help your employees in any way. This simple offering provides employees with a sense of security to know that a lifeline is available should they need it, and that you are vested in their success.

Receiving feedback

As a manager, you are the one who usually gives feedback, with your employees on the receiving end. However, you will also receive feedback from your boss, colleagues, customers and stakeholders, and your employees. It may make you feel like you're being judged or personally attacked. Banish the thought. Feedback is one of the most valuable gifts anyone can give you. Think of it as a learning opportunity motivated by the provider to improve your performance. Embrace constructive feedback and it will make you a better manager. Follow these tips to gain the most out of the experience:

  • • Incorporate active listening. Adopt alert and positive body language. Use good eye contact and facial expressions that show you are interested. Show you understand the speaker's point and respond in a way that moves communication forward.
  • • Listen carefully. Don't become defensive or interrupt. Don't think ahead to prepare a rebuttal. You may miss an important piece of information. Take notes to reference later.
  • • Ask for clarification. If you don't understand a point being made, ask for more information or specific examples.
  • • Compare the feedback. This may or may not be the first time you've heard this feedback, so compare it with others you've received.
  • • Discuss strategies. If your boss is providing the feedback, discuss the next steps to take and provide possible solutions.
  • • Show appreciation. Giving feedback is not easy for anyone. Express your appreciation to the provider whether or not you agree with it. This will invite feedback in the future.
  • • Follow up. Schedule a follow-up to your feedback session and share your progress with your boss.

If you establish an open communication office environment, employees will know you listen to their concerns and value and understand their work. They will be more inclined to let you know about problems that may stand in the way of their performance or operations. Receiving feedback from employees is valuable to any manager. Here are examples of the types of information they may feed you:

  • • Supply shortages.
  • • Understaffing.
  • • Workflow bottlenecks.
  • • Poor coworker communication.
  • • Unsuitable work assignment practices.
  • • Overloading of some employees.
  • • Underloading of others.
  • • Harassment or other discrimination practiced by coworkers or your subordinate supervisors.
  • • Distractions or failings in the work environment.

Requesting feedback

Requesting feedback is a way to obtain information that you deem important, but it also builds empathy. It helps you demonstrate that you place value on the perspective of those from whom you solicit feedback and that you are open to different positions. As a federal manager, requesting feedback is most valuable in two instances -- first, for operations, and second, for your performance.

Requesting feedback on operations. This is a prudent move for a federal manager. Feedback gives you guidance and direction from those who receive your work product, and feedback can be rich with information. Soliciting customers, stakeholders, vendors, and other agencies can give you valuable information on your work unit's strengths and weaknesses.

Requesting feedback can be as simple as a phone conversation, or you can solicit it through other means such as a feedback link on your agency website or a questionnaire placed in your lobby.

Requesting operational feedback from your own staff is probably your most valuable source of information. They can provide streamlining ideas or raise issues that impede operations. Be sure to give them an outlet, even anonymously, and remind them to use it.

Requesting feedback on your performance. Your boss should provide you feedback whether you ask for it or not. The performance feedback being referred to here is from your subordinates and staff. It's a scary proposition for most, but it gives you a better understanding of your strengths and developmental needs from their perspective.

Try a 360-degree feedback or assessment. They generally cover a broad range of workplace competencies. The process gives staff an opportunity to provide anonymous feedback to you which, understandably, would otherwise cause them discomfort. The feedback is intended for you to see how you're perceived by others and give you an opportunity to adjust behaviors and develop skills that will enable you to excel at your job. Contact your HR office for more information.

Using feedback

Now that you have it, what do you do with it? Feedback is a powerful tool that can be used to motivate staff, help with their development, uncover operational issues, solve problems, and shape your own professional development. Don't let it sit without giving it your full attention and consideration. You will do a disservice to your employees if you have feedback to give them, but just don't get around to it.

The same is true of feedback you receive. If you don't acknowledge or act on it in some manner, then those who provided the feedback will not likely continue to do so.

The benefits are immeasurable. Use feedback for the following reasons:

  • • To improve work unit situations. Feedback from your employees is especially useful to discover things that need attention. Use that information to address the issues within your control and send the others up the chain. How you use feedback tells the provider how much you value them. Keep your employees in the loop as you work on these challenges. This will keep the lines of communication open, which will keep the information flowing.
  • • To develop employees. Using feedback in this sense means that you effectively use the technique to convey information to your employees. The feedback you give them will develop and improve their performance and productivity. Using positive feedback serves to identify and endorse desired performance, while constructive feedback will point out areas that need attention. When used properly, feedback can make all the difference in the development of staff.
  • • To improve operations. Receiving feedback that your customers and stakeholders are dissatisfied with your service or product may sting a little, but it is good to know. The information they, and even your staff, provide serves to identify problems, thus enabling you to focus on solution development. Using feedback in this respect can have the greatest impact on the proficiency of your work unit and how you are perceived by the public. You can't fix what you don't know and using feedback to gain that knowledge is extremely beneficial on all agency levels.
  • • To develop your skills. Using feedback to sharpen your own skills and competencies is an important skill in itself. It will help you develop a more thorough understanding of your work and how it can be improved. Be aware that not all feedback is useful or accurate. Consider the source, examine feedback patterns, and use your best judgment. Using feedback from your boss or staff may cause you to adjust your developmental goals or even create a new plan.

Mistakes to avoid

Feedback is a communication skill that has real significance in all workplaces. Whether you're giving or receiving, it serves to improve individual performances and agency operations. The following guidance will help you avoid some mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Sit on feedback received without acting. Once you get feedback, do something with it, even if to acknowledge that you are considering it. If those who are feeding you information see that it is being ignored, they will discontinue providing it.
  • • Underestimate the value of staff feedback. They are the closest to operations and may have the most practical and useful information to provide you. Request their feedback often.
  • • Wait for the performance appraisal to give feedback. Feedback does have a usefulness expiration date and holding it, even for a semiannual performance appraisal, diminishes its effectiveness.
  • • Let your emotions influence the feedback you give. Being too aggressive or panicked will set off a counterproductive reaction and the purpose of the feedback will be lost.
  • • Discount the value of giving positive feedback. Sure, your employees are expected to do their work. But when you praise them for what they did correctly, you ensure that it will be repeated.
  • • Assume that your employees know what you want or expect. Nor can you assume that they will ask for your feedback. Take the initiative and proactively give feedback.
  • • Be averse to receiving feedback. Feedback given to you presents valuable learning opportunities. Embrace it to become a better manager.

Ask FMG

Q. I'm really busy and don't have time to provide detailed feedback to all of my employees. Where should this rank on my priority list?

A. Chances are you're spending a lot of time fixing mistakes made by your employees. This is a common consequence of not providing feedback. Giving detailed, timely feedback will actually save you time because it will result in future work being produced correctly. If you want to spend a little time now to save a lot of time later fixing mistakes, rank it as a high priority.

Q. I provide my staff feedback every year during their annual performance appraisal. Are you saying I should be giving feedback more often?

A. Yes -- definitely. Positive and constructive feedback needs to be continuous and frequent throughout the year. This will eliminate blindsides during the appraisal, which may lead to appeals.

Q. As I review work, I insert my comments on the document, copy it for my files, and send it back to the employee. There's not much need for verbal communication. As long as it's working, this method of feedback is acceptable, right?

A. While documenting the feedback you give employees is a good idea, don't underestimate the power of personal interaction. Try personally delivering your next work critique. Go over it with your employee and listen to their perspectives. You'll find that you pick up much more information about your employee and their response to your feedback than if you rely on email.

Q. Doesn't requesting feedback open me up to criticism? Why would I do that?

A. It all depends on how you approach it. Try looking at feedback as a fact-finding mission. Sure, people may be critical of you, but you'll learn valuable information about operations and gain a better understanding of your own strengths and developmental needs. The value gained will far outweigh any bruises to your self-esteem.

Q. Isn't positive and constructive feedback just another name for being "touchy feely"? Why not just tell it like it is?

A. You actually are being open and honest with your employees when you give positive and constructive feedback. But you're doing it in a respectful, productive manner. If done correctly, you will not only gain trust and develop your employees, but you will also inspire them to do better.
