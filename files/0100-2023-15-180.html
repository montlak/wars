E. ALLEGATIONS RAISED AT HEARING

The hearing judge is not precluded from accepting evidence at the hearing which tends to establish discrimination on a basis other than that alleged in the complaint. If such evidence is offered, the judge must make a finding on the additional basis. Godbois v. USPS, 01832382 (1984).

The same rule also applies to the theory of the case. It is error for the administrative judge to preclude evidence of disparate impact, even where the complainant alleged disparate treatment. Moss v. Secretary of Defense, 01902252 (1990).

Even though a complainant does not specifically raise the issue of retaliation, it is appropriate for the administrative judge to hear that issue when the investigation discloses evidence of retaliation. Dennis-Brown v. Postmaster General, 01930016 (1994).

In Reda v. Postmaster General, 01955889 (1997), the Commission specifically rejected the argument that it was legal error for the administrative judge to amend a complaint at the hearing to include disability discrimination. Noting that the testimony of agency officials contained direct evidence of such discrimination, the Commission in Reda held:

It was after this and other testimony by agency officials admitting their perception of the appellant's disability that the AJ properly ruled that disability was to be added as a basis for alleged discrimination. We reject the agency's contention that the AJ erred by adding disability as a basis for those claims. Following the court decision in Sanchez v. Standard Brands, Inc., 431 F. 2d 455 (5th Cir., 1970), the Commission has repeatedly held that a complainant may amend his or her complaint to add another statutory basis for an alleged discriminatory occurrence without changing the identity of the claim. See, for example, Zella R. Davis v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01965067 (April 21, 1997) (agency ordered to consider appellant's request on appeal to amend her complaint of race, color, age and reprisal discrimination to add the basis of disability discrimination); Sylvia E. Stack v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950125 (April 17, 1996) (appellant properly added reprisal as a basis to her age discrimination complaint); Dinah L. Vice v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01952380 (March 27, 1996) (agency erred by not permitting appellant to add disability as a basis of discrimination to her sex discrimination complaint); and, Paul Dragos v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940563 (January 19, 1995) (appellant properly attempted to add sex as a basis of discrimination during the hearing on his national origin discrimination complaint). Here, it was incumbent upon the AJ not to turn a blind eye to the responsible management officials' admissions that one of the reasons for denying appellant the coveted LSM position was their perception of his disability. The AJ, therefore, properly amended the complaint to add disability as a basis without changing the identity of the underlying claim.

When factual allegations are raised for the first time at the hearing, whether those facts are admissible depends on the purpose for which they are offered. If the allegations raised at the hearing relate to incidents of discrimination that have not been the subject of informal counseling and are offered for the purpose of obtaining relief, it is error for the hearing judge to consider the merits of those claims. Beverly v. USPS, 01830099 (1982). If the allegations are offered as background evidence relevant to the issue in the complaint, they should be allowed into the record.
