1. Sua Sponte Summary Judgment

The administrative judge may initiate the summary judgment process on his or her own initiative by sending the parties a notice of intent to issue a summary judgment decision. The notice of intent does not commit the administrative judge to issuing a summary judgment decision and, after receiving the responses of the parties, the judge may determine that there are material facts in dispute that require a hearing.

In the Judges' Handbook at 5-2, the Commission specifically describes the contents of the notice of intent:

The Administrative Judge's notice to the parties regarding his/her intent to issue a decision without a hearing must: (1) indicate in whose favor summary judgment is being proposed; (2) identify the applicable legal standards and burdens of proof with respect to each claim; (3) set forth the legal standards for issuance of a decision without a hearing; (4) identify the undisputed material facts which appear to be dispositive of the case; and (5) direct the parties to cite to specific evidence contained in the report of investigation which creates a factual dispute regarding a material issue and to include any relevant documentary evidence or witness statements, interrogatory answers, admissions, or other supporting materials from outside the report of investigation, with an explanation of the relevance of all materials submitted. The Administrative Judge need only identify those undisputed material facts which would be dispositive of the case. For example, in a retaliation case, the record may show that numerous facts are in dispute, including why a letter of warning was issued or why other comparative employees were not disciplined. However, where the record does not contain evidence of any prior protected activity, the Administrative Judge may identify only that fact as being material to the disposition of the case. A model notice is attached as Appendix E.

The Commission's position on the responsibilities of the administrative judge were reiterated in Petty v. Secretary of Defense, 01A24206 (2003):

[T]he administrative judge should not rule in favor of one party without holding a hearing unless he or she ensures that the party opposing the ruling is given (1) ample notice of the proposal to issue a decision without a hearing, (2) a comprehensive statement of the allegedly undisputed material facts, (3) the opportunity to respond to such a statement, and (4) the chance to engage in discovery before responding, if necessary. According to the Supreme Court, Rule 56 itself precludes summary judgment "where the [party opposing summary judgment] has not had the opportunity to discover information that is essential to his opposition." Anderson, 477 U.S. at 250. In the hearing context, this means that the administrative judge must enable the parties to engage in the amount of discovery necessary to properly respond to any motion for a decision without a hearing. Cf. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(g)(2) (suggesting that an administrative judge could order discovery, if necessary, after receiving an opposition to a motion for a decision without a hearing).

See also Barlow v. Postmaster General, 01A12500 (2002) (it is error for the administrative judge to issue a notice of intent to decide the case without a hearing without identifying the specific issues of material fact that he believes are not in dispute). But see Blanco v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01A52674 (2005) (finding the failure of the administrative judge to issue a proper notice was not harmful error because no material facts were in dispute).

The situation in Blanco may well be a case of the complainant not adequately demonstrating on appeal some likelihood that material facts were in dispute because the prevalent view expressed by the Commission is that its administrative judges must give notice of the specific facts they believe are not in dispute. For example, in Lacy v. Secretary of Army, 01A30650 (2006), the Commission reversed the summary judgment decision of the judge finding that the complainant may have been able to put facts in dispute if complainant knew why the judge believed there was no dispute:

As a preliminary matter, we find that the boiler-plate notice of summary judgment provided in the Scheduling Order was not sufficient notice pursuant to EEOC Management Directive 110 (MD-110) Chapt. 7 at 7-15 (November 9, 1999). The AJ issued a summary judgment decision sua sponte without advising the parties ahead of time of the specific issues of material fact which he thought were undisputed and did not need to be resolved through an evidentiary hearing. This did not provide complainant with a fair opportunity to address the AJ's concerns. See Kirsha v. United States Postal Service, EEOC No. 01A12500 (May 24,2002). Complainant was essentially left to guess the basis for a possible summary judgment decision. As complainant argued in his appeal brief, if he had sufficient notice to respond to the AJ's specific issues, he may have been able to provide additional affidavits from potential African American applicants, or resolve the AJ's questions in other ways.

Each party is given 15 days from receipt of the notice of intent to file a response to the notice. The parties are then given another five days after receipt of that response to file a reply. Id. The administrative judge may enlarge these time periods in the notice of intent.

It is error for the administrative judge to issue a decision prior to receipt of the complainant's response where the response period has not yet expired. As the Commission explained in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120142515 (2014):

In the instant case, we note that the record reflects that the AJ agreed to give Complainant additional time to submit documentation through April 11, 2012. Nonetheless, the AJ issued her decision on April 5, 2012, before Complainant had the opportunity to submit his April 11, 2012, response to the AJ's Notice of Intent to Issue a Decision without a Hearing. Therefore, we conclude that Complainant was not provided with an opportunity to file a response opposing the AJ's proposal to issue a decision without a hearing. As such, we determine that the AJ erred in issuing a decision without a hearing without affording Complainant the procedural safeguards required pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(g)(3). We find that the AJ's error was material and prejudicial to Complainant because Complainant's April 11, 2012, response, not considered by the AJ, clearly establishes that there are material facts in dispute, and that the credibility of witnesses is at issue in this case, as explained below.

It does appear, though, from the wording of the Complainant case, in addition to demonstrating the decision is issued before the response date the complainant must also establish the error was harmful--i.e., that there are material facts in dispute.

As noted in the section above on "Summary Judgment Standards," in Petty v. Secretary of Defense, the Commission set out the notice standards for sua sponte summary judgment--standards it reiterated in Lacy. However, when a party moves for summary judgment, the administrative judge is not required to follow the notice requirements of Petty. Gonzalez v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720090033 (2011) (rejecting agency argument that it did not have a sufficient opportunity to respond to judge's concerns where agency moved for summary judgment and judge granted it for complainant).
