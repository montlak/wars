8. Impeachment Evidence

Inconsistencies in the testimony of a witness that reflect on his credibility do not mean the entire testimony must be discounted. In McGowan v. USPS, 01832497 (1984), the selecting official testified that he consulted with other supervisors prior to making his promotion selection and found that the appellant was viewed as having an uncooperative attitude. Although all four supervisors testified that they were not consulted on the selection, they also testified that the appellant was uncooperative and the Commission found this substantiated the reason proffered by the agency for his nonselection.

Evidence impeaching the general credibility of an alleged discriminating official may not be sufficient to demonstrate pretext. This is particularly true where the impeachment evidence does not go directly to the employment action at issue. In Hinton v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05910968 (1992), the Commission found that evidence tending to impeach the selecting official's testimony that she had not received gifts from subordinates was not probative of pretext where other evidence in the record strongly corroborated her testimony regarding her assessment of the performance of both the complainant and the selectee.

A finding that an agency official is a credible witness does not insulate the agency from a finding of discrimination. It is possible that an agency official can be entirely credible in testifying as to his reasons for taking an action, but the testimony itself proves the action was discriminatory. For example, in Scully v. Secretary of Treasury, 05930638 (1994), the Commission found no contradiction in the credibility determination of the administrative judge that the recommending official had asked age-related questions as a means of exploring the employee's career goals and its own finding that such questions constituted illegal discrimination:

The record shows that prior to his January 9, 1991 meeting with appellant, the second-line supervisor gave to appellant a handwritten memorandum of specific topics to be discussed before he completed appellant's RMP. A review of the memorandum indicated that among the subjects to be addressed was "how much useful organizational life is there." In this regard, the memorandum further indicated that appellant's potential retirement age, his current age, and his years of service were considerations in making that determination, i.e., the amount of useful life appellant had left for the agency's purposes. The record further shows that the AJ found credible the supervisor's testimony that he scheduled a career counseling session with appellant prior to completing the RMP because he wanted to discuss appellant's career goals, and that he gave appellant a list of items--including the age-related statements at issue--to facilitate their discussion.

The agency argues that the previous decision failed to consider the AJ's credibility determination. We disagree. The previous decision did not address the AJ's credibility finding because it was unnecessary to do so. That is, the previous decision's finding that the supervisor's age-related questions were neither normal, routine, or proper, and that the supervisor improperly considered appellant's age in assessing his management potential, is not inconsistent with the AJ's finding that the supervisor's testimony about his reasons for presenting appellant with these questions was credible. Although it is clear that the supervisor raised these considerations in conjunction with an assessment of appellant's career aspirations, it is equally clear that the ADEA prohibits such age-related considerations. Consequently, we find that the agency failed to show that the previous decision erred when it found that the supervisor's age-related comments as they related to the RMP were evidence of age discrimination.

That a witness made inconsistent statements also is not dispositive of the issue of discrimination, particularly where the issue is the knowledge of the RMO at the time of taking the disputed action. In Richards v. Postmaster General, 01881821 (1988), the complainant's coworkers told his supervisor that he had taken a newspaper from a mail bundle. The supervisor then found the newspaper in the complainant's possession. Later, the coworkers told an EEO counselor that they had not actually seen the complainant take the newspaper. The complainant consistently maintained that he had not taken the paper. The Commission noted that "although these inconsistencies in the coworkers' testimony might tend to mitigate appellant's guilt in the matter if the offense could have been brought before a tribunal such as the Merit Systems Protection Board, they do little to assist appellant in proving discrimination when he has not been able to prove that the agency officials at the [time] were presented with this inconsistency." Id.

In the absence of inconsistent statements, it may be relevant to determine whether the witness made prior consistent statements. For example, in McEniry v. Postmaster General, 01891454 (1989), the Commission discounted the testimony of the complainant that his supervisor made discriminatory comments because complainant never mentioned the comments in a letter to the postmaster written one day after the incident, never mentioned the comments to the EEO counselor and never referenced the comments in his formal complaint. Given the significance of the alleged remarks to the issue of discriminatory animus, the Commission found it unlikely they would not have been disclosed prior to the hearing.
