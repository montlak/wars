broida eeo Chapter 17: Reprisal Chapter 17: Reprisal, Subchapter 04: Proof in Reprisal Cases, Section C: Adverse Treatment Requirement, Subsection 07: Chilling Effect and the Reasonably Likely to Deter Standard, Part q: Work Performance Critiques
q. Work Performance Critiques

As a general rule, the Commission has held that midyear performance reviews do not sufficiently aggrieve an employee for purposes of stating a claim. See Chapter 18 under the heading "Midyear Reviews." However, when an employee alleges that his midyear performance review was lowered in retaliation for complaint activity, the Commission held that this is adverse treatment for purposes of stating a reprisal claim. In Porter v. Secretary of Navy, 07A20080 (2003), the Commission found the midyear review created a chilling effect even where the final review was fully successful and the employee received the attendant pay increase. "In this matter, we note that the fact that the agency later upgraded this evaluation to fully successful does not preclude a finding that the original negative evaluation had the serious effect of likely deterring complainant or others from engaging in protected activity." Id.

Similarly, in Complainant v. Secretary of Navy, 0120142889 (2015), the Commission found that negative comments by a supervisor on a performance appraisal sufficiently aggrieved the complainant to state a claim:

Here, Complainant has alleged that in retaliation for filing an EEO complaint against a former supervisor, that supervisor added unfavorable comments to her performance appraisal that indicated that completion of medical histories and physicals on patients was less than satisfactory. Complainant further asserted that she bad never been counseled on this matter, and had always received favorable appraisals until she filed her EEO complaint. These allegations, viewed in the light most favorable to Complainant, are sufficient to state a viable claim of retaliation which requires further investigation and processing.

See also Lamar M. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120161555 (2017) ("being told that one cannot be trusted in an employee meeting in front of coworkers could serve to dissuade an individual from making or supporting a charge of discrimination and reasonably deter a person from engaging in protected activity."); Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120142658 (2015) (supervisor stood close behind complainant, altered timekeeping tasks and gave her additional tasks).

However, in Complainant v. Secretary of Interior, 0120123212 (2014), the Commission found that criticisms of a work report that did not specifically identify the complainant were not sufficient to aggrieve the complainant:

After careful review of the complaint and related EEO counseling materials, we concur with the Agency's determination that Complainant has failed to state a viable claim of retaliation. Here, the supervisor forwarded an email from Washington to her staff members containing criticism of a report produced by the office. The email basically focused on what needed to be done to prevent being such criticism in the future. Neither Complainant nor anyone else was identified by name as one of the staff members who worked on the project, although we will assume her assertion was correct that everyone knew she was involved. In sum, we find that under the circumstances as alleged, and viewed in the light most favorable to Complainant, this allegation amounted to no more than a relatively trivial harm that would not reasonably be likely to deter an employee from engaging in protected activity. Therefore, we agree that the matter as alleged, without more, fails to state a viable claim of unlawful retaliation that requires further investigation.

See also Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120152796 (2016) (one instance of denying complainant a 30-minute change in start time did not aggrieve complainant).
