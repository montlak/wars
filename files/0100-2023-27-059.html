L. RESCISSION OF VACANCY ANNOUNCEMENT

In some cases, agencies post vacancy announcements but never fill the positions. In the past, the Commission has treated such cases with a great deal of inconsistency. For example, in Byrd v. Department of Air Force, 01862028 (1986), the employee alleged that an agency purposely did not fill a vacancy for discriminatory reasons, and that if the vacancy had been filled he would have been selected as the best qualified candidate. The Commission found that the employee had not stated a claim for relief. The Commission stated that the agency was not required to fill the position, the employee had no entitlement to the position, and the employee's "assertion that he would be the selectee if the agency did fill the position is mere speculation." Id. The Commission seemed to suggest that the employee only could be aggrieved if the agency actually filled the position. There are other Commission cases with similar holdings. The general rule is that "[w]here no selection is made, claims of nonselection ordinarily fail to state a claim." Crenshaw v. Secretary of Interior, 01A11774 (2002).

However, in Pecker v. Heckler, 801 F.2d 709 (4th Cir. 1982), the court found that such a claim clearly stated a violation of Title VII. In that case, the plaintiff brought an action challenging an EEOC order to promote her to a GS-10 position. The Commission had previously found that the agency had, on a discriminatory basis, rescinded a vacancy announcement to prevent the plaintiff from being selected for the job. The plaintiff claimed that she was entitled to a position at the GS-11 level because the position she oriGINAlly applied for had been upgraded to that level after the agency withdrew the vacancy announcement. The Fourth Circuit agreed.

Without specific reference to Pecker v. Heckler, in other cases the Commission has analyzed the evidence to determine whether the complainant has demonstrated, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the agency rescinded the vacancy announcement for discriminatory reasons. See, e.g., Willingham v. Secretary of Navy, 01890760 (1989); Owens v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 01890084 (1989); see also Hillman v. Secretary of Defense, 01A14319 (2002) (allegation that the vacancy announcement was canceled on discriminatory basis states a claim for relief).

In Lapina v. Secretary of Navy, 01950656 (1996), the Commission specifically held that an allegation that a vacancy announcement was canceled to avoid selecting the complainant states a claim for relief:

The agency found that allegation (i) failed to state a claim because no selection was made in announcement number 92E-M67P. Appellant has alleged that the vacancy in allegation (i) was cancelled in order to avoid selecting appellant. The Commission finds that allegation (i) states a claim. Appellant has alleged a specific harm--she was not selected. We agree with the agency that cancelling a vacancy announcement does not automatically render a person aggrieved, but, under the circumstances of this case, where appellant is alleging that the agency is attempting to manipulate the selection process over a number of years to avoid selecting appellant, appellant's allegation concerning the cancellation of a vacancy announcement states a claim. Therefore, we find that the agency improperly dismissed allegation (i) for failing to state a claim.

See also Tien E. v. Postmaster General, 012018475 (2018) ("Complainant's allegation that the Agency cancelled and reposted the position in order to not select her due to her prior protected activity does not constitute a proposed action but an action that sets forth a current actionable claim.").

In Lucero v. Secretary of Air Force, 01961369 (1996), the Commission essentially overruled its previous holding in Byrd. Without exactly stating that it was overruling Byrd, the Commission rejected the agency's arguments based on Byrd and instead relied on more recent case law to hold that an alleged failure to fill a vacancy for discriminatory reasons states a claim. As the Commission explained in Lucero:

Arguments by appellant and the agency indicate that Person A resigned and that appellant alleged that he was discriminated against when the civilian position held by Person A was abolished and appellant was not given an opportunity to apply for the former civilian position held by Person A. Appellant and the agency indicate that the supervisory position formerly held by Person A was assigned to a military employee, TSgt A. Appellant alleged that these actions were taken because of appellant's race and age.

In support of its decision that appellant was not aggrieved, the agency cited the Commission's decision in Byrd v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01862028 (Sept. 24, 1986) for the proposition that a claimant did not state a claim of discrimination when a claimant alleged that an agency did not fill a vacancy for discriminatory reasons. The agency found in the instant matter that "there has been no position actually announced, advertised, or filled, pending revision and reclassification of the position description." The agency further argues:

The position to which [appellant] aspires does not exist. The retirement of [Person A] does not obligate the agency to hire or promote another as a WS-10 supervisor. [Appellant] has no entitlement to the position as a WS-10. Whether [appellant] would even have been selected for the position had it been announced and filled is too speculative. [Appellant] is not 'aggrieved.' His complaint is premature at best.

A more recent Commission decision discussed the holding in Byrd and whether the type of situation at issue in the instant complaint states a claim of discrimination:

[T]he agency's allegedly deliberate failure to announce higher level vacancies in order to prevent [B]lack candidates from attaining these positions has clearly harmed appellant in terms of wages, benefits, promotion potential and working conditions.

In support of its...argument that [the allegation regarding failure to announce vacancies] fails to state a claim, the agency cites a decision issued by the Commission in September 1986 [Byrd], prior to more recent decisions reached under the Commission's regulations. More recent decisions have found that arguments similar to appellant's allegation that the agency discriminatorily failed to fill vacancies have stated a claim within the meaning of the Commission's regulations. See Ellen E. Pearcy v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, EEOC Request No. 05890567 (October 12, 1989) (Agency's rescission of the vacancy announcement under which appellant applied for a position found to state a claim); Jeanne D. Walls v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01904267 (December 4, 1990) (Agency's alleged failure to fill vacancy in order to prevent appellant from applying for it found to state a claim); James E. Dixson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01900170 (December 15, 1989) (Allegation that an agency refused to fill a vacant position because of a discriminatory animus towards the applicant found to state claim).

Bradford v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920510 (Aug. 6, 1992).

The Commission finds that in accordance with the decision in Bradford, the instant complaint states a claim of discrimination under EEOC Regulations. Furthermore, we find that appellant is aggrieved now and that the agency's dismissal of the complaint pursuant to § 1614.107(e) was also improper.

See also Harp v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120071972 (2007) (cancelation of vacancy announcements because of alleged sex discrimination and reprisal stated a claim for relief); Odom v. Postmaster General, 01A60609 (2006) (complainant alleged agency canceled vacancy announcement in retaliation for EEO activity); Szymczak v. Postmaster General, 01A62551 (2006) (complainant alleged agency canceled vacancy announcement based on race); Opare-Addo v. Postmaster General, 01A51572 (2005) (complainant alleged that vacancy announcement was withdrawn and reposted to avoid selecting him); LeBlond v. Secretary of Treasury, 01985296 (1999) (Commission rejected the agency argument that the complainant was not aggrieved because the vacancy announcements were withdrawn). But see Raheem v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A05334 (2000) (cancelation of the announcement generally does not aggrieve employee); Taylor v. Secretary of Army, 01965038 (1997) (holding that there is no cause of action where an agency cancels a vacancy announcement and adds additional selection criteria before reposting).

An allegation that an agency changed a vacancy announcement for discriminatory purposes states a claim and is a separate and distinct allegation from one of nonselection for a position. The Commission in Thomerson v. Secretary of Navy, 01966583 (1997), noted the difference between the two types of allegations:

Appellant alleged that she was discriminated against when the agency reissued a vacancy announcement with an additional rating factor and an expanded area of consideration. Appellant claimed that this action was effected for the purpose of hiring a male candidate, who had previously been outside the area of consideration, and concomitantly, in order not to select her. A term, condition, and/or privilege of appellant's employment was affected, namely, the opportunity to compete for the Supervisory Contract Specialist position. Although the agency accepted for investigation appellant's allegation concerning not being selected from the second certificate, that allegation does not encompass allegation 1 and is distinct from allegation 1. The dismissed allegation is distinct because it involves the agency's decision to reannounce a position opening with a broader area of consideration than that specified on the oriGINAl vacancy announcement, not the ultimate selection decision, which is at issue in allegation 2. Therefore, we find that appellant has stated a claim. See Virginia Lapina v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01950656 (January 17, 1996).

A common allegation is that an agency manipulated a selection process to exclude the complainant. One way of doing that is by holding a position vacancy in abeyance until another qualified candidate emerges. In such a case, the proper focus is not on the respective qualifications of the selectee and the complainant, but the reasons advanced by the agency for not filing the vacancy in the first instance. See Gilbreath v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01A02754 (2002); Strain v. Postmaster General, 01921225 (1992). Likewise, in Ford v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01956161 (1997), the Commission found it was error for the administrative judge to issue a summary judgment decision in the agency's favor where the complainant claimed the agency discriminatorily rescinded a vacancy announcement and filled the position via a settlement agreement. The allegations raised a genuine issue of material fact that required resolution through hearing.

Likewise, an allegation that an agency created a permanent job for another employee but not for the complainant states a claim for relief. Tucker v. Veterans Administration, 05050813 (1996).

When a complainant has been discriminatorily deemed unqualified for a position and the vacancy announcement is subsequently canceled, the proper remedy is to provide the complainant with priority consideration for the same or a similar position. Morgan v. Administrator, General Services Administration, 01963534 (1997). But see McCarroll v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720070060 (2008), holding that the proper remedy when there is discrimination in the application process, the proper remedy is to retroactively award the complainant the position unless the agency can show that it would not have selected the complainant even absent discrimination.

In Williams v. Postmaster General, 0120073647 (2008), the Commission found that the complainant failed to state a claim for relief where the position was never posted or offered to anyone, applicants were never solicited, and no one was detailed into the position.
