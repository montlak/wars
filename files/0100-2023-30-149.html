i. $40,001-$50,000

In Bowden v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A00360 (2000), the Commission awarded $45,000 in compensatory damages even though there were several causes for the complainant's emotional distress. The Commission found the agency's retaliatory conduct severely aggravated her symptoms, requiring her to seek ongoing mental health treatment, including medication for depression.

Although compensatory damages are awarded to compensate the victim of discrimination for his or her suffering and not to punish the agency for its misconduct, it is reasonable to infer that the more egregious the discrimination, the more harm that is done. In awarding $45,000 in damages in Elliott v. Postmaster General, 07A20063 (2003), the Commission noted, "[t]he more inherently degrading or humiliating the defendant's action is, the more reasonable it is to infer that a person would suffer humiliation or distress from that action."

An agency award of $5,000 was increased by the Commission to $45,000 in Cottrell v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A30303 (2004). In Cottrell, the Commission found there were multiple causes for the emotional distress suffered by the complainant. One interesting thing about the Cottrell decision is the Commission's notation that:

The statements submitted by the complainant indicate she was highly traumatized by co-worker office gossip that followed discriminatory not being promoted. We note that multiple people apply for posted promotions, but only one can be selected. Vicious office gossip cannot fairly be said to be a natural outgrowth of not being the selectee for a promotion. The office gossip was a separate event, and there was not a finding of discrimination regarding it.

In Servold v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120053029 (2007), the Commission increased an award of $30,000 in damages to $45,000 where the complainant was subjected to sexual comments by a coworker and, on three occasions, entered his office only to find him undressed. In Servold, the Commission described the evidence of damages:

In her statement, dated September 24, 2004, complainant averred that she had difficulty understanding how CW, who had gained her "total and complete confidence and trust as a co-worker, a special agent, a fellow Navy comrade, and a fellow believer and brother in Christ could do such a thing." She explained that she became very emotional due to the incidents and experienced shock, disbelief, intense betrayal, fear, anger, and sadness, followed by sorrow, depression and frustration about the events. She further asserted that she had difficulty concentrating and cried most of time. In addition, she maintained that, when she heard that CW was involved in the sexual harassment of other women in the office, she felt sick to her stomach, and for the next ten days, could not sleep. She described herself as very edgy and nervous, and noted that she would shake and her heart would race when people walked up behind her, especially males. She asserted that, even up until the day of her affidavit, she had nightmares and required an over-the-counter sleep aid. She also discussed her need to take a Life Skills course in order to assist her in coping with the incidents.

Complainant's daughter stated that complainant has suffered a great deal of stress, misery, and torment, and that the strain has caused insomnia, which makes her mindless and exhausted. She further described her mother as high-strung and agitated. She noted that she too was affected, as she has had to assist her mother in dealing with recovery.

Complainant's father and brother described complainant as feeling nervousness and fear. Both stated that complainant would call them crying about the situation at work. She told them that she was fearful of losing her job, and both noted that she appeared to feel very helpless, was losing sleep, and was finding it hard to go to work and to concentrate. Her father stated that complainant was hurting emotionally and was getting sick. Her brother noted that the situation was causing her extreme stress and anxiety, and that the situation was becoming unbearable.

Two of complainant's friends also provided statements regarding complainant's emotional state. She also described how complainant was constantly in fear that another similar incident might occur. This friend provided childcare for complainant so that she could attend counseling services. The record further reflects that complainant attended 18 one-on-one counseling sessions from August 2002 to March 2003 due to the incidents. In numerous medical records, complainant's psychologist reported complainant's feelings of anxiety, sleep problems, agitation, fearfulness, and gastrointestinal problems. The psychologist noted that, during numerous counseling sessions, complainant described episodes of daily anxiety, increased stress, loss of sleep, depression, and feelings of being overwhelmed. Finally, the record reflects that complainant attended 24 group counseling courses at the Life Skills Learning Center to assist her in coping with the harassment.

Where the complainant required 77 therapy sessions to help manage her major depression, the Commission increased an award of $8,000 in damages by the administrative judge to $45,000 in McNeese-Ards v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720090027 (2010). The Commission also noted that the complainant presented the testimony of several witnesses that after her duties were changed following an EEO complaint that the complainant became worried, withdrawn, depressed and fretful and that her emotional state had considerably deteriorated.

After careful consideration of the evidence of record, including evidence not specifically mentioned herein, we find an award of $45,000 for non-pecuniary compensatory damages is appropriate. This amount takes into consideration the nature of the discriminatory acts, the severity of the physical and emotional harm suffered, the length of time Complainant suffered the harm, and is consistent with prior Commission precedent. See Servold v. The Commission in Greg M. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120160345 (2018), increased a $5,000 award to $50,000 where the complainant alleged that due the agency's discrimination "he suffered stress and emotional harm daily for two years in the workplace and after his retirement,...the discrimination caused his sleep to be disrupted with nightmares, made him not want to go to work, made him 'pissed off,' caused him to gain 100 pounds, caused stomach pain and panic attacks, and made him feel hopeless and suicidal,...the discrimination made him withdraw from his wife of 27 years, his children, and other people, [and] his home life 'sucks' now."

In Ward-Jenkins v. Secretary of Interior, 01961483 (1999), the Commission considered the request for $255,000 in nonpecuniary damages, but ultimately awarded only $50,000:

Appellant testified that subsequent to the reassignment, she became depressed and irritable, lost energy and interest, and abandoned her recreational and social activities. Appellant testified that her involvement in her daughter's school activities decreased on account of her loss of energy and interest. Appellant testified that she experienced anxiety and distress, withdrawal, loss of enjoyment in activities, difficulty focusing and concentrating, insomnia and nightmares, and weight gain. Appellant testified that in 1992 she contemplated committing suicide. Appellant acknowledged that she did not seek counseling until after the conclusion of the liability phase of these proceedings, but testified that it was because she had not realized earlier that she needed counseling....

Two lay witnesses who knew appellant during this period corroborated her testimony regarding the change in her personality following her reassignment. The witnesses also confirmed that appellant became unkempt in her appearance, and gained a substantial amount of weight (about 100 pounds) over two years....

Taking into account the evidence of non-pecuniary damages submitted by appellant, the Commission finds that appellant is entitled to non-pecuniary damages in the amount of $50,000. This amount takes into account the severity and duration of the harm done to appellant by the reassignment, and accounts for the fact that the harm done by the discrimination was an aggravation of a pre-existing condition. Further, this amount takes into account that, unlike cases where greater damages were awarded, appellant's injury did not render her totally incapacitated either for work or in her personal life....

Without any medical evidence and based on the testimony of the complainant, family members, and friends, the Commission upheld an agency award of $50,000 for emotional distress in Meyer v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01984033 (1999). The Commission further noted that the duration of the emotional distress spanned a two-year period of time while the complainant was under the offending supervisor and that her condition improved when she was placed under new supervision. The Commission in Meyer explained:

With regard to nonpecuniary damages, the agency indicated that appellant produced substantial evidence bearing on injury and causation, which supported a finding of a causal connection between the agency's actions and the resulting deterioration of her emotional condition. After a review of statements from appellant, her friends, and her daughter, and considering the duration of her stress, pain, humiliation, and suffering of approximately 2 years, the agency concluded that appellant was entitled to $50,000.00. The agency noted that in arriving at this amount, it considered the length of time appellant was assigned to the responsible supervisor, the severity of his discriminatory work place behavior towards appellant, and the resulting emotional and physical stress suffered by appellant. The agency further noted that it also considered the statements from appellant's daughter and friends that since being removed from the responsible supervisor's surveillance and control, she began to improve and although "not yet normal...was moving in that direction."

From this, the Commission in Meyer concluded:

After a careful consideration of the facts in this case, we find that the agency arrived at a fair determination when it awarded appellant nonpecuniary damages in the amount of $50,000.00, pursuant to the settlement agreement. In reaching this decision, we considered a number of factors including: the nature, severity, and duration of the discrimination, and the nature, severity, and duration of appellant's emotional stress and depression and related symptoms, including the post polio syndrome. Specifically, we have carefully examined appellant's explanation of her emotional distress from the incidents complained of based on her statement and supporting information from her friends and daughter. We also considered the fact that appellant did not provide any medical documents pertaining to her physical or mental conditions, including her post polio syndrome. Based on these considerations, we find that $50,000.00 is a proper award.

In Yeh v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01973721 (2000), the Commission awarded $50,000 in compensatory damages where the complainant was required to seek long-term psychiatric and physical care, including taking antidepressants, and the harm was still ongoing at the time of the decision.

Social Security Administration, 01976921 (2000), where the period of harassment lasted three years and the complainant continued to suffer from depression for at least four years after the harassment ended. The Commission noted that the complainant received treatment for clinical depression, including medication, and the complainant's physician attributed that depression to the harassment. The evaluation of the complainant's physician was corroborated by several witnesses who testified that "she has lost the zeal she formerly held for her job and continues to suffer from depression and sadness when she thinks about the harassment she endured." Id. In addition to depression, the complainant's symptoms included insomnia and other anxiety-related conditions.

In Williams v. The Commission in Williams found:

In reaching the above amount, the Commission has considered many factors. Thus, our total award of $50,000.00 reflects Doctor J's statement that complainant would, for an indefinite period of time, have recurring nightmares, insomnia, flashbacks, nervousness, impaired concentration, periods of sadness, feelings of emptiness, anxiety, and a sensation of being unfairly persecuted.

The Commission upheld an award of $50,000 in compensatory damages in Dukart v. Postmaster General, 01986798 (2001), where the complainant presented both medical and testimonial evidence that she suffered an extended period of depression and anxiety. The Commission in Dukart found:

In a February 13, 1998 affidavit complainant averred that, because of the PM's harassment she felt hopeless, confused, fearful, angry, and was visibly shaking. She stated that she suffered an inability to concentrate and could not function enough to concentrate on leisurely activities such as card playing. She stated that she had an inability to urinate due to tenseness, and had paranoid and suicidal thoughts, sleeplessness, extreme fatigue, loss of appetite, weight loss, hair loss including the hair on her eye brows, and was divorced from her husband in 1995 as a result of her anxieties. She stated that she would start to shake violently when she talked to others. Complainant averred that, although she made attempts to work, she was unable to hold gainful employment since she retired from the agency based on her physician's advice. She stated that prior to the harassment, she was a productive, ambitious, active, self-confident woman, but is now shaky, emotionally distraught, and mentally confused. She averred that since November 1997, she had been working with her physician to reduce the amount of medication she takes. She stated that she is still in the process of recovering.

Complainant's husband averred that prior to working with the PM at the agency, complainant was an energetic, enthusiastic, self-confident person. He averred she was happy when she was hired by the agency but that there were drastic changes in his wife's behavior during and following the period of harassment. He stated that she came home from work crying and emotionally upset, because the PM raised his voice and stood very close to her while belittling her work ability. He stated that she began having trouble eating and sleeping, and was very nervous. He continued that at the time when complainant's physician recommended that she retire, she would cry for no apparent reason, eat very little, shake continuously, and maintain an inconsistent sleep pattern. She began to think the phone was bugged and that people were following her. He said that following her resignation from the agency, he thought that complainant's condition would improve but it did not. He stated that complainant moved in and out of their home for no apparent reason. He stated that she is still unable to hold a job and will still get a lost look on her face.

In the case at hand, the Commission finds that complainant's statements, the statement of her husband and her doctor all support her contention that her depression and anxiety were caused by the PM's harassment and discrimination. The Commission found the harassment to be on-going and pervasive in its prior decision. Given the severity and duration of complainant's emotional and physical damage including weight loss, hair loss, sleeplessness, fatigue, an inability to urinate and an inability to concentrate which has affected her ability to find other meaningful work, we sustain complainant's claim for non-pecuniary damages of $50,000 as reasonable and comparable to awards in similar cases.

In Terrell, the complainant sought compensatory damages in connection with the depression he experienced after not being selected for a particular position. We find that complainant is entitled to a non-pecuniary award of $50,000. While the agency is not responsible for complainant's pre-existing condition which began in the early 1980s, it is responsible for the actions it took which aggravated that condition. The record shows that after 1991, complainant's pre-existing seizure disorder, brain disorder and psychiatric disorder substantially worsened. Complainant's seizures increased in frequency and severity after 1991. Specifically, the record shows that complainant suffered frequent seizures at home and work. The seizure episodes included a moderate to severe pain in complainant's left side, a sleep disorder, loss of dexterity, tremors, facial spasms, disorientation, and impaired balance. Complainant was treated with anti-convulsant medication. In addition, the record shows that complainant was depressed, had homicidal/suicidal ideations, and suffered from crying spells. Complainant was treated for major depression with group and individual therapy and anti-depressant medication. Complainant had been hospitalized from January to March 1994 after a suicide attempt. After complainant's release from the hospital he was evaluated by a psychiatrist who found complainant chronically depressed. Complainant's psychiatric condition was described as moderate to severe. During this period the Veteran's Administration increased complainant's rating for his seizure disorder from 10% to 20% and increased his rating for his organic brain disorder from 10% to 30%.

The agency concluded that complainant's more severe symptoms lasted to about July 1997. While complainant argues that he continues to suffer and will continue to suffer permanently, a review of the record indicates that complainant's symptoms substantially decreased after July 1997. Although we agree that complainant was not symptom-free after July, 1997, we find the post July 1997 symptoms more akin to the pre-1991 symptoms. Accordingly, we find that the harm caused by the agency did not exist after July 1997. We note that there is no medical evidence in the record which supports the finding that the harm caused by the agency continued after July 1997. Accordingly, we find that complainant suffered harm for approximately 6 and 1/2 years (February 1991 through July 1997) as a result of the agency's failure to reasonably accommodate complainant. However, as indicated above, complainant can only be compensated for approximately 5 years and 7 months (from November 1991 through July 1997).

We find the non-pecuniary award of $25,000 issued by the agency in its FAD improper because we find the harm that complainant suffered as a result of the agency's aggravation of his pre-existing condition to be of an extended duration, more akin to the facts in Wallis than those in Smith, Terrell or Hogeland.

An award of $50,000 in compensatory damages was considered appropriate in Force v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01995221 (2002), where the complainant suffered significant emotional distress for some three and a half years that was expected to continue for approximately two more years as the result of sexual harassment and a discriminatory transfer. The complainant suffered from a mood disorder prior to the sexual harassment and also experienced personal and domestic problems, including a divorce, that were unrelated to the discrimination. In adjusting the administrative judge's award from $10,000 to $50,000 in nonpecuniary damages, the Commission in Force explained:

Complainant's psychologist's records serves as the basis for most of the evidence regarding her emotional distress, as complainant did not substantially testify at the hearing. Complainant's psychologist diagnosed complainant as suffering from PTSD, which he found was directly related to sexual harassment incurred on the job.

The record also reveals complainant was treated by her psychologist between June 1994 and March 1998 and by a psychiatrist between August 1994 and January 1998. In a May 1996 report from complainant's psychologist, he reported: "[I]n my opinion, occupational issues have severely impacted upon, and exacerbated the remission of any symptoms which might have been preexisting."

Complainant's psychologist testified that he did not begin to treat complainant until her work issues arose. He did acknowledge, however, that for eight to twelve months prior to this time, he saw complainant in order to get help with the adjustment of her children to the divorce, and her son's Attention Deficit Disorder. Throughout his testimony, the psychologist took the position that the overwhelming bulk of the stressors in complainant's life during this time was the sexual harassment. He maintained that prior to the sexual harassment, complainant was able to cope with her children's problems, her marital problems, and issues with her mother. According to the psychologist, any financial, parenting, social and family issues were intensified due to the sexual harassment.

After a careful review of the record, including the psychologist's reports and notes, we agree with the AJ and the agency that complainant suffered other stressors in her life at the time she was subjected to the sexual harassment. We do not find, however, that complainant's parenting or family stressors served as the basis for the majority of complainant's emotional distress during this time. In this regard, we note that we have previously applied to nonpecuniary damages, the theory that a tortfeasor takes his victim as he finds him. Terrell v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, EEOC Appeal No. 01961030 (October 25, 1996), request for reconsideration denied EEOC Request No. 05970336 (November 20, 1997). Specifically, we have found that when a party has a preexisting emotional condition which deteriorates as a result of the discriminatory conduct, the additional harm may be attributed to the respondent. Wallis v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950510 (November 13, 1995) (citing Williamson v. Handy Button Machine Co., 817 F.2d 1290, 1294 (7th Cir. 1987). We have also taken the position that, where a complaining party's emotional harm is due in part to personal difficulties which were not caused or exacerbated by the discriminatory conduct, the employer is liable only for the harm resulting from that conduct. Compensatory and Punitive Damages Available Under Section 102 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, EEOC Notice No. 915.002 at 12 (July 14, 1992) (citing Vance v. Southern Bell Telephone and Telegraph Company, 863 F.2d 1503 (11th Cir. 1989)).

In determining the amount by which complainant's emotional condition was exacerbated by the sexual harassment, we note that the record reveals complainant was prescribed 10 mg of Prozac in early August 1994, approximately one month prior to the first instance of sexual harassment. However, complainant's dosage increased four times to 40 mg around the onset of the sexual harassment. In determining the award, we also find that complainant's transfer to Durham was a direct result of the discrimination. There is no indication in the record that complainant would have relocated her family to another city absent the discrimination. As will be discussed below, the record reveals that the move placed a tremendous amount of stress on complainant as her entire support structure was in Fayetteville.

We do note, however, that the record supports the notion that complainant suffered some emotional distress unrelated to the discrimination at the time she was in treatment. In that regard, we have reduced complainant's award to reflect that finding.

Calculation of Damages Payable

In his reports, complainant's psychologist states that complainant suffered from crying, anger, helplessness, and nightmares. Complainant reported that she was unable to sleep, yet also felt fatigued. She reported flashbacks and nightmares, and was fearful of retribution by the Director when she rejected his advances. Her psychologist reported that complainant appeared, at times, to suffer from the symptoms of depression. Complainant told him that she was confused, and had to focus on what she was doing in order to function on a daily basis. Complainant reported that she gained 30 pounds in six months. In her statement on appeal, complainant stated that she felt suicidal at times.

Subsequent to her move to Durham, complainant reported that her children suffered from the adjustment due to the move. Complainant herself suffered from feelings of isolation and victimization in the sense that the agency did not help her in the transition. Once she moved to Durham, complainant also reported financial problems, which necessitated the exhaustion of her savings account.

Throughout complainant's treatment, however, complainant discussed other stressors in her life, including conflicts with her children, which her psychologist termed "normal adolescent issues." Complainant also mentioned sadness related to the anniversary of her father's death and conflict with her mother, who she described as "controlling." In addition, complainant discussed with her psychologist the effects of Hurricane Fran, which destroyed her house in the Durham area and necessitated her living in a motel for several weeks.

Complainant's psychologist's reports from late in 1996 indicate that complainant was still suffering the effects of the sexual harassment, now two years after the onset of the sexual harassment. For instance, in October 1996, complainant reported that she still thought about the Director and continued having problems sleeping. She later reported feelings of anxiety anytime she went near the VA. Complainant's physician testified at the hearing in June 1998 that complainant will always have flashbacks, and that she required approximately two more years of therapy.

In his decision, the AJ found that $10,000 was the appropriate amount in which to compensate complainant for the emotional distress she suffered due to the sexual harassment. In support of this award, the agency cited to the AJ, Bever v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01953949 (October 31, 1996) ($15,000 in non-pecuniary damages) and Sinnot v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01952872 (September 19, 1996)($20,000 awarded for non-pecuniary damages).

It is a Commission goal to make damage awards for emotional harm consistent with awards in similar cases. In McCann v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal NO. 01971851 (October 23, 1998), the Commission awarded $75,000 in non-pecuniary damages for compensation for the agency's retaliatory removal when she suffered pain and suffering over a four year period. This included anger, insomnia, flashbacks, nightmares, fear, fatigue, less confidence on the job, and intrusive bad thoughts. In Finlay v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01942985 (April 29, 1997), we awarded $100,000 in non-pecuniary damages in order to compensate the complainant for severe psychological injuries over four years. The complainant's condition, which was expected to continue for an indeterminate period of time, included ongoing depression, frequent crying, concern for physical safety, loss of charm, lethargy, social withdrawal, concern for physical safety, recurring nightmares and memories of harassment, a damaged marriage, stomach distress, and headaches. In Santiago v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01955684 (October 14, 1998, the Commission awarded $125,000 in non-pecuniary damages for emotional distress, i.e., depression, anxiety, paranoia, confusion, moodiness, insomnia, social withdrawal, tearfulness, fatigue, loss of libido, loss of self-esteem, chest and stomach pains, digestive problems, and incidents of shortness of breath. The complainant in that case repeatedly sought medical treatment for her problems, including an invasive diagnostic procedure, after she was removed in 1993. The duration of the complainant's emotional and psychological distress and physical illness was found to be from November 21, 1991 to the date appellant was removed, on February 12, 1993.

Given the severity and the duration of the psychological and physical injuries experienced by complainant in this case, as well as the length of her treatment, the awards the Commission has made in similar cases and the amount provided by the agency, we find that appellant is entitled to non-pecuniary damages in the amount of $50,000.

Likewise, in Ardjoman-Kermani v. Postmaster General, 01996058 (2002), the Commission increased a $10,000 award by the administrative judge to $50,000 where the complainant was denied reasonable accommodation and subjected to a hostile environment:

The Commission finds that the AJ properly determined that complainant established a nexus between the alleged harm and discrimination. Therefore, we must review whether or not the AJ's award of $10,000 was appropriate. Complainant has requested compensatory damages without providing a specified amount. Complainant testified at the hearing along with three co-workers as to the effect the discrimination had on her. In particular, the witnesses stated that they saw complainant become stressed and that she cried. Furthermore, they testified that her personality changed from being happy to being down. Complainant indicated that she sought medical treatment by the Psychologist and provided medical evidence reflecting her treatment. Further, the AJ noted that complainant received psychological counseling for eight months; the discrimination lasted for eighteen months; and the damage to her foot which was caused by the agency's failure to provide an effective accommodation was permanent. The AJ, however, failed to take into account the issues of disability-based harassment and of complainant's "diminished ability to work and her eventual disability retirement" in awarding compensatory damages.

The AJ determined that complainant was entitled to $10,000 based on the agency's failure to provide her with a reasonable accommodation. Based on our finding that complainant was subjected to unlawful harassment, we determine that complainant suffered addition injury. Taking into account the severity and duration of the harm suffered, we find that complainant is entitled to $50,000 in non-pecuniary damages. See, e.g., Feris v. Environmental Prot. Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 01983167 (September 18, 1998) (awarding $35,000 for agency's failure to accommodate complainant's disability over a period of time which resulted in tension, damage to self-esteem, anxiety, anger and sleeplessness); Carpenter, EEOC Appeal No. 01945652 ($75,000 in a failure to accommodate case which resulted in emotional harm to complainant which damaged his relationships with family and friends and reduced his quality of life, as well as resulting in some physical manifestations such as a digestive disorder); Bernard v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01966861 (July 17, 1998) (awarding $80,000 to an employee who was denied a reassignment and not reasonably accommodated where he presented evidence that, over five years, he became depressed, withdrawn, hopeless about his ability to work independently, cynical, angry, and, during part of the time, experienced ringing in his ears, headaches, teeth grinding, and insomnia). Therefore, we conclude that the AJ properly determined that complainant is entitled to non-pecuniary damages, however, we find that an award of $50,000 is more consistent with our case precedent.

The agency awarded the complainant, in Blanca B., $5,000 finding itself liable only as to the sexual harassment claim:

Regarding non-pecuniary damages, the Agency found that it liable only for the finding that S1 sexually harassed Complainant, not the cultural reaction of her peers, family, her husband's family, and other members of the local Middle Eastern or Arabic speaking community. In support of this, the Agency cited Complainant v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120130174 (Sep. 5, 2014) (the complainant was discriminated against when after learning of an impending reduction-in-force, he was not selected for one of three slots of a supervisory position for which he applied. He requested $300,000 in nonpecuniary damages, averring that after he contacted an EEO counselor regarding his non-selection his anxiety and depression grew worse because his coworkers, including the three who were hired for the supervisory position and found out about his EEO activity, began to treat him negatively by refusing to talk to or eat in the break room with him. The Commission disagreed as to the breadth of the agency's liability in Blanca B.:

As an initial matter, we find that the damages caused by the ridicule and shunning by her peers, family and community arising from S1's sexual harassment is compensable. We agree with Complainant that this case is more like Lemons v. Federal Bureau of Prisons, EEOC Appeal No. 0120102516, than like Complainant v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120130174.

We agree with the Agency that Complainant has not shown that any injury arising from litigating her EEO complaint in 2015, is compensable. She did not state this injury arose from the appellate litigation causing her to relive the harassment.

We find it more likely than not that S1's sexual harassment was not the only factor that caused Complainant's depression and anxiety. These other factors include the execution of her brother, which she repeatedly verbalized made her sad. They also include the reaction to Complainant wearing modern clothing and being from Southern Iraq. Specifically, she wrote that in reaction to this, she believed some female FSI colleagues questioned her reputation because of the way she dressed, she was labeled as a southern peasant, and colleagues taunted and laughed at her. Finally, there was a situation at the Agency involving an altercation and resulting inquiry concerning another romantic relationship Complainant was in following the ending of her marriage (see footnote 3 of this decision).

Still, we find that the sexual harassment by S1 was a significant reason for Complainant being ridiculed by her community both inside and outside work. More importantly, it was a significant reason for Complainant being shunned by this community, her family and her husband's family. She was blamed for S1's actions, hurting her reputation within her community. The sexual harassment, and to a far larger degree the long term shunning caused, at least in part, by it, has and continues to take an immense toll on Complainant--causing a near severing of relations with her family and husband's family, loss of her friends, avoidance of going outside her apartment, ruminating most of the time about the harassment, poor self-esteem, bad depression, poor concentration, irritability, constant anger, poor sleep and exhaustion, crying nightly, gaining 40 pounds, thoughts of suicide, not creating a close connection with her young daughter, lost interest in sex, and being difficult to be around.

On the other hand, Complainant's contention of severe emotional disturbance starting in 2008, as outlined in the psychologist's evaluation report, is belied by some of her actions, which shows she was functional and doing fairly well. S1's harassment ended by around May 2009. By December 2009, Complainant was able to form a romantic relationship with an FSI male instructor that lasted for a year, continued to work at FSI for more than two years after S1's sexual harassment ceased and wanted to continue working there, then in 2012 or 2013 met a man who she dated for a year before marrying him in late in 2013 or early 2014.

Taking all these factors into account, we find that Complainant has proven that she is entitled to $50,000 in nonpecuniary damages for pain and suffering and damage to her reputation. This is consistent with amounts awarded in similar cases. See Santos v. Environmental Protection Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 0120091233 (July 12, 2012) (the complainant was awarded $50,000 in nonpecuniary damages for harassment that caused him anger, fright, resentment, frustration, loss of self-esteem, irritability, humiliation, embarrassment, anger, betrayal, family strain, anxiety, stress, decreased energy, decreased motivation, depression, and diarrhea), Harris K. v. Department of Homeland Security (Apr. 24, 2018) (the complainant was awarded $40,000 in nonpecuniary damages for harassment that caused him depression, anxiety, difficulty sleeping, fatigue, changes in appetite, and to often lock himself in his room at home and to lose his trust in his colleagues). In arriving at the $40,000 figure, we considered that while the sexual harassment by S1 was a factor in causing harm to Complainant, it is more likely than not that other factors also caused Complainant's sadness, damage to her reputation, her being taunted, and shunned long term, the latter of which is the cause of most of her emotional distress as time went on.

Rejecting an agency award of $9,000 based on lack of medical evidence, the Commission increased the award to $50,000 in Holliday v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A03047 (2002), where the complainant corroborated her own testimony with that of family and coworkers. The Commission in Holliday found:

Complainant submitted evidence, through her own affidavit as well as affidavits from relatives and former coworkers, of the emotional distress caused by the agency's action. Complainant stated that prior to the retaliation, she was "an upbeat, optimistic and happy person...[who] approached [her] work with a good attitude and was confident in [her] abilities," but that after the retaliation, "[her] whole world was turned upside down." Complainant stated that she found it difficult to report to work each day, began to question her own abilities, lost enjoyment in life, isolated herself from loved ones, and gained weight. Complainant added that she suffered financial difficulties due to the retaliation because she had to retire two years earlier than she planned and had difficulty obtaining employment. Complainant stated that she eventually obtained employment as a part-time bookkeeper earning $6.00 per hour, which is only a small portion of what she earned as a full-time GS-11 agency employee, so as a result, she had to sacrifice her own household and move in with her elderly mother. In addition, complainant stated that her professional reputation, which she worked twenty five years to build, deteriorated along with her self-esteem. Finally, complainant stated that she experienced stress, depression, distrustfulness, humiliation, fear, sleeplessness, exhaustion, headaches, higher blood pressure, and exacerbation of a physical impairment due to the agency's retaliation. Complainant indicated that the effects of the retaliation remain.

Complainant's children and former coworkers corroborated her statements. Complainant's daughter added that complainant complained about the retaliation daily and cried consistently. Complainant's son stated that complainant remains cynical and much less cheerful than prior to the retaliation. Complainant's former coworkers stated that complainant consistently called them to ask questions regarding matters about which she was very knowledgeable, was obviously stressed, and was placed on medication for high blood pressure.

As previously noted, the agency considered the absence of medical evidence in the record in awarding complainant $9,000 in non-pecuniary damages. The Commission has previously held that neither evidence from a medical provider nor expert testimony in general is a mandatory prerequisite for recovery of compensatory damages for mental and emotional distress, and that evidence may include statements from the complainant. Mayo v. Department of Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05990302 (July 21, 2000).

Based on the record, the Commission finds the agency's award insufficient to remedy the severe and persistent harm the agency's actions caused complainant. We find $50,000 an appropriate amount as complainant presented sufficient objective evidence to establish that the agency's actions caused her emotional distress on, minimally, a daily basis for fourteen months. Similar cases with somewhat similar evidence support this award. See, e.g., Bowden v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01A00360 (June 22, 2000) ($45,000 award for non-pecuniary damages where the agency subjected complainant to harassment, which resulted in exacerbation of depression, injury to professional standing, character, reputation, and credit rating, humiliation, physical manifestations, loss of self-esteem, and marital and family problems); Turner v. Dep't of Interior, EEOC Appeal No. 01956390 (April 27, 1998) ($40,000 award for non-pecuniary damages where the agency subjected complainant to sexual harassment and retaliation, which resulted in depression, anger, anxiety, frustration, sleeplessness, crying spells, loss of self esteem and strained relationships); Wallis v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950510 (November 13, 1995) ($50,000 award for non-pecuniary damages where the agency retaliated against complainant, which resulted in exacerbation of a preexisting mental condition, loss of reputation, injury to credit standing, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and loss of health).

In Lazard v. Postmaster General, 01A02426 (2002), the Commission awarded $50,000 where it determined that some of the acts that caused the complainant emotional distress were not the result of discrimination. The Commission noted it was reducing the award by 50% after comparing it to other cases in which it had awarded $100,000 in damages.

An award of $50,000 in damages was upheld in Germain v. Environmental Protection Agency, 07A10048 (2002), where the complainant experienced stress, worry, frustration, and anxiety for a period of six years because of the agency's failure to accommodate him.

Appeals of damage awards by complainants are not always successful. For example, in Savage v. Postmaster General, 01991774 (2002), the appeal resulted in the Commission reducing a $100,000 damage award to $50,000.

Where an agency did not provide the complainant with interpreter services over a four-year period, the Commission found $50,000 was an appropriate damages award. In Ortiz v. Postmaster General, 01A15376 (2002), the Commission found the complainant suffered both physically and emotionally because of the discrimination and exhibited insomnia, paranoia, vomiting, depression, and stomach ulcers.

Where depressive symptoms were exacerbated by a retaliatory reassignment and continued for some two years, the Commission awarded $50,000 in damages. In Ferrall v. Secretary of Navy, 07A30054 (2003), the Commission found:

The evidence presented by complainant regarding the nature, severity, and duration of the nonpecuniary harm she suffered as a result of the unlawful retaliation included her own testimony as well as statements from her therapist, former coworkers, friends, and family members. Complainant testified that subsequent to the retaliatory reassignment, she felt humiliated; that she suffered from depression and was prescribed anti-depressant mediation which she took for the next two years and which muted her emotional reaction to other events in her life; that she began to experience numerous physical and psychological difficulties; that these difficulties led to diminished physical activity and consequent related health problems, including weight gain, increased cholesterol level, high blood pressure (requiring medication), and increased knee joint problems. Complainant also stated that she has experienced diminished social activity. The statements provided by complainant's relatives, former coworkers, and friends corroborated her testimony.

Complainant's therapist stated that, while he had been treating complainant for anxiety prior to her retaliatory reassignment, after the reassignment she began "exhibiting more symptoms of depression than ever before." The therapist stated that he diagnosed complainant as suffering from "Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood," a condition which "involves one or more periods of depressive symptoms that are identical to Major Depressive Episodes in duration, but with fewer symptoms and less impairment." He added that an "episode" is defined as "either a sad or 'depressed' mood or loss of interest or pleasure in nearly all activities," and that "[i]n addition, at least two but less than five additional symptoms must be present nearly every day." The therapist identified the symptoms suffered by complainant as including "muscle tension, restlessness, shakiness (physical), sweatiness and clamminess, dizziness and light-headed[ness], diarrhea, nausea, flushes, shortness of breath (a feeling of being smothered), keyed-up (always on edge), exaggerated startle response, difficulty concentrating, insomnia, increased irritability, depression, [and] crying." The therapist also stated that his prognosis for complainant's future mental and emotional condition was "at best moderate," that complainant "will never be able to recover 100%," and that "it has become a way of life for [complainant] to be anxious." The therapist further provided that "while [complainant's] condition began as situational in nature (i.e. job related stress) it has now developed into a personality trait," that complainant "now permanently operates at a more anxious level than the average person, even in a relaxed state," and that "[a]t this point in time it is a struggle for [complainant] to function on a day to day basis as she reports consistent feelings of being burnt-out and unable to continue fighting." The therapist concluded that, even assuming she would not in the future be subjected to high levels of job-related stress, complainant would need to remain in therapy for several years and that she would need to resume taking anti-depressant medication.

In George v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A13775 (2003), the Commission awarded $50,000 where:

Complainant stated that he has been humiliated and traumatized and has developed hypertension, depression, Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), and sexual dysfunction. In addition, complainant stated that his professional career has been affected in that he suffers flashbacks of the agency's defamation, discrimination and retaliation against him so he can not work as a veterinarian full time. Finally, complainant stated that he experienced family problems. Complainant indicated that the effects of the retaliation remain.

Complainant's first physician (P1) stated that complainant sought treatment on January 27, 1993 and had hypertension, psychological distress, PTSD, sleeplessness, problems concentrating, withdrawal, flashbacks and crying. P1 stated that complainant's impairments were related to his work environment and employment career problems. His first psychiatrist (P2) stated that he treated complainant on February 1 and March 2, 1993 for Adjustment Disorder and Depressed Type rule out PTSD. P2 stated that complainant's stress seemed to be triggered by two terminations from Federal employment. In a letter dated October 29, 1999, complainant's second physician (P3) stated that complainant was currently under his care for pain, hypertension and sexual dysfunction. P3 attributed complainant's health concerns to employment and career problems. Finally, in a letter dated November 5, 1999, complainant's second psychiatrist stated that complainant was currently under his care for Major Depression and PTSD.

Where the complainant suffered from stress, anger, and frustration for a period of two years and provided medical documentation to corroborate his testimony, the Commission awarded $50,000 in damages. See Gilvin v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A23823 (2003). In Gilvin, the complainant also testified that his relations with his family were strained and that he became suicidal. Moreover, he testified he was devastated by not being able to provide for his family and that his removal was a factor that led to his separation from his common law wife.

There is no reward to an agency because it does not retaliate against an employee who files an EEO complaint. As the Commission noted in Arizola v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 07A30132 (2004), it expects agencies to treat employees who file complaints with "dignity and respect" and such treatment does not mitigate any harm done by discrimination. In upholding the award of an administrative judge of $50,000 to the complainant in Arizola, the Commission noted:

The AJ awarded complainant $50,000.00 in non-pecuniary compensatory damages. The AJ emphasized that complainant's physicians reported that complainant suffered from stress-related diseases, including chronic depression and anxiety. She also suffered from headaches, fatigue and uncontrolled tearfulness. Her doctors further reported that her symptoms are job-related, and that they have been ongoing for approximately three years. The AJ also took into account the fact that complainant had been given awards for her work since her demotion and that she had not been disparaged by her co-workers since the discriminating incident. The AJ cites Commission precedent to illustrate that prior decisions support her award of $50,000.00.

On appeal, the agency argues that the Commission should mitigate the non-pecuniary compensatory damages award in this case because following complainant's discriminatory demotion, she was treated with "dignity and respect" and the employees responsible for her demotion left the Houston District. Moreover, after complainant was demoted she received exemplary ratings and performance-based awards. The agency contends that these factors support a reduction in the non-pecuniary compensatory damage award from $50,000.00 to $15,000.00.

Treating an employee with dignity and respect, while it does not always occur, is expected. We cannot reduce the AJ's award of non-pecuniary damages because after retaliating against complainant by stripping her of her supervisory responsibilities and demoting her, the agency thereafter gave her favorable performance ratings and awards. If the agency had given her unfavorable performance ratings and not given complainant awards that she deserved based on her work performance, then complainant may have had an additional claim of retaliation against the agency. We cannot reward the agency for its failure to retaliate; we can only attempt to remedy the discrimination that has occurred.

The letters from complainant's family practitioner and psychologist persuade the Commission that complainant has experienced emotional distress sufficient to justify an award of $50,000.00. The record shows that complainant suffers from stress caused by her work situation. Complainant currently suffers from stress-related illnesses of hypothyroidism, hypercholesterolemia, and chronic depression. The stress experienced by complainant has manifested as headaches, fatigue, uncontrolled tearfulness, anxiety, fearfulness, inability to concentrate, profound dysphoria, and it has exacerbated her depression. A sworn and notarized statement from complainant's psychologist, dated January 31, 2003, states that while complainant's condition has improved, he expects that it will be necessary for complainant to continue in treatment for a minimum of two sessions per month for a period of three to four years following the resolution of her case. As a result of the agency's actions complainant feels disgraced among her colleagues, she has lost respect from her peers, subordinates, and superiors, and has suffered irreparable injury to her professional standing.

We find that an award of $50,000.00 in non-pecuniary compensatory damages is appropriate in this case. We note that this amount meets the goals of not being motivated by passion or prejudice, not being "monstrously excessive" standing alone, and being consistent with the amounts awarded in similar cases. The Commission finds that the weight of our prior decisions supports an award of $50,000.00 in the instant case. See Holliday v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01A03047 (June 12, 2002) (Commission awarded $50,000.00 in non-pecuniary damages where due to a retaliatory work environment complainant suffered emotional distress daily for at least 14 months, experiencing loss of enjoyment of life, loss of professional standing and forced early retirement); see Bowden v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01A00360 (June 22, 2000) (Commission awarded $45,000.00 in non-pecuniary damages where the agency subjected complainant to harassment which resulted in exacerbation of depression, injury to professional standing, character, reputation, and credit rating, humiliation, physical manifestations, loss of self-esteem, and marital and family problems); see Wallis v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950510 (November 13, 1995) (Commission awarded $50,000.00 in non-pecuniary damages where agency retaliated against complainant, which resulted in exacerbation of a preexisting mental condition, loss of reputation, injury to credit standing, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and loss of health).

In Leos v. Postmaster General, 07A40017 (2004), the Commission awarded $50,000 in damages based on the complainant's testimony that he:

[E]xperienced emotional distress sufficient to justify an award $50,000.00. The record shows that complainant suffers from long-term and significant anxiety and depression associated with his treatment in the workplace. He describes profound feelings of low self-esteem and extreme frustration associated with his work environment, where he endured on-going degrading acts by supervisors and co-workers because of his disability, to include being shot at with rubber bands. As a result of this demeaning treatment, complainant is "angry inside" and "hurting deep down," and feels isolated and ashamed because of his deafness. Additionally, complainant testifies that he has problems with sleep because of his workplace treatment.

The Commission awarded $50,000 in damages in Brown v. Postmaster General, 07A30050 (2004), where the complainant suffered from major depression as a result of discrimination and was unable to work for several months. The Commission in Brown held:

With regard to non-pecuniary damages, complainant stated that he has experienced, and will continue to experience, "emotional pain and suffering." Complainant further stated:

I have been inconvenienced, suffered mental anguish, and endured the loss of enjoyment of life. In Complainant v. Attorney General, 0120123467 (2015), the Commission upheld an award of $50,000 by the administrative judge where:

In this case, the AJ found that Complainant credibly and emotionally testified with the support of one friend/coworker (Coworker 4) that she suffered from extreme stress, shock, and humiliation, much of which she attributed to perceived harassment partly perpetrated by her coworkers' misbehavior or mistreatment of her. The AJ noted Complainant testified the stress manifested itself in frequent absences from work, headaches, rashes, weight fluctuation, high blood pressure, diagnosed depression and anxiety, insomnia, nightmares, excessively heightened emotions shown by crying spells and panic attacks, and suicidal ideations (showing a re-emergence of a pre-existing psychiatric problem). The AJ noted Complainant also testified that she felt as if her discriminatory superiors (the AO and the Supervisory Fiscal Specialist) "followed her" to her details and that she lost her confidence or esteem and withdrew from her family and friends.

The AJ noted that Complainant stated she did not obtain some relief for her sleep problems until after her recent hospitalization. The AJ noted that Complainant sought but had to stop her EAP counseling sessions, which she also testified provided her some relief. Additionally, the AJ noted that Complainant's testimony showed that in her new job (at time of hearing) she had been successfully accommodated, had job satisfaction, and was performing satisfactorily. The AJ determined that although Complainant clearly suffered severe and numerous mental and physical effects during the relevant time period, as was corroborated by extensive medical and counseling records, she did not prove that all of these effects were attributable to the Agency's illegal discrimination and its reprisal against her commencing in late October/November of 2010.

Additionally, the AJ found that the medical disclosure violation that Complainant showed does not support any compensatory damage award because the Agency hired Complainant despite that disclosure. The AJ determined Complainant failed to prove that the disclosure contributed to or otherwise caused the Rehabilitation Act violations that were supported by a preponderance of the evidence.

Thus, the AJ determined that after careful consideration of Complainant's and her co-worker's credible testimony, particularly Complainant's tearful hearing testimony showing a serious re-emergence of her suicidal ideations, as well as her supporting medical and counseling records, Complainant was not entitled to all damages sought on her behalf because she did not prove that all of her claimed damages were proximately caused by the illegal discrimination and reprisal. Moreover, the AJ noted that Complainant did not prove by a preponderance of the evidence that she would continue to suffer in the future, mental and physical effects proximately caused by such discrimination and reprisal.

The AJ concluded Complainant proved by a preponderance of the evidence that she suffered approximately 12 months of the described compensatory damages, i.e., for the period of November 20, 2010, until approximately 3 months after her promotion was effective in September 2011, with some intermittent psychological relief from her details commencing in late November 2010. The AJ found Complainant proved by a preponderance of the evidence that approximately 50% of those damages were directly or proximately caused by the illegal discrimination and reprisal. Thus, the AJ found Complainant proved an award of $50,000.00 in compensatory damages for the mental and physical suffering proximately caused by the Agency's discrimination.

An award of $30,000 by the agency was increased to $50,000 on appeal where the complainant's representation of a fellow employee in an EEO complaint resulted in daily harassment spanning two years. In Janet B. v. Attorney General, 0120132683 (2015), the Commission found:

Complainant was subjected to daily harassment by management for close to two years from April 2009 to February 2011, a prolonged duration. S1's refusal to speak with Complainant without a witness present, and her overall reduction of communication with her was isolating. As part of the harassment, S1 repeatedly did things which made Complainant not only fear the loss of her job, but her law license--her livelihood. Further, Complainant's sudden reassignment from the high profile Organized Crime Drug Enforcement Task Force to lower level cases damaged her reputation within the law enforcement and legal communities, something she greatly valued. We agree with the Agency that as a result of the harassment, Complainant suffered tremendous stress, anxiety, and exacerbation of her pre-existing medical conditions. Complainant disputed that her mother's death in November 2009 was a source of anxiety or depression. She explained that her mother was terminally ill for around four years, and she grieved her mother's condition and anticipated death in 2007. Nothing in the record contradicts this.

We disagree with the Agency that $30,000 is sufficient compensation for Complainant's non-pecuniary damages. The medical evidence shows that there were severe physical manifestations of the stress caused by the harassment it was a contributing factor to Complainant getting shingles and her hypertension. She also got nausea. Further, witnesses described an overall decline in Complainant's health, with at least one indicating it was visible. Complainant feared she would get a stroke from the hypertension, and talked about death frequently, something she did not do prior. On an ongoing bases Complainant dreaded coming to work and was made to feel helpless and unsafe. She had trouble sleeping and would wake up in a panic. Complainant was diagnosed with depression, and required medication for anxiety. She was given cases she was unprepared to prosecute, which caused her panic. She went from vivacious women to someone who was broken and tired who was unable to attend social events. Based on the duration and severity of Complainant's emotional pain and suffering, and also the damage to her reputation, we find Complainant suffered non-pecuniary damages in the amount of $50,000. See McCoy v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01A43628 (Sept. 22, 2005) (Complainant awarded $50,000 as a result of the Agency's failure to provide a reasonable accommodation which she stated resulted in her experiencing increased fatigue and exhaustion due to stress. She was unable to sleep, and experienced frequent crying, feelings of hopelessness and fear, and frequent headaches. Statements from family and friends corroborated her claim. Noted pre-existing depression and multiple sclerosis also contributed to her symptoms. De Los Santos v. Environmental Protection Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 0120091233 (July 12, 2012) (Complainant subjected to harassment for two years and eight months awarded $50,000 for mental anguish, anger, fright, resentment, frustration, loss of self esteem, irritability, humiliation, embarrassment, anger, betrayal, anxiety, stress, decreased energy, depression, headaches, upset stomach, numbness in limbs, and damage to his character and reputation). Complainant is awarded $50,000 in non-pecuniary damages for her pain and suffering and loss to reputation.

An award of $12,000 by the agency was increased to $50,000 by the Commission in Complainant v. Attorney General, 0120122924 (2015), where the complainant developed PTSD after being sexually assaulted by an inmate and the agency refused her request to be assigned to an area with no contact with male inmates as an accommodation:

As already noted, Complainant was diagnosed with PTSD in February 2009, following the assault in January 2009. By the end of September 2009, almost ten months after the assault, Complainant's mental health provider was still recommending continued treatment. Complainant requested $200,000 in damages for pain and suffering as a result of the Agency's failure to accommodate her PTSD. The Agency determined that an award of $12,000 in compensatory damages was appropriate for Complainant's emotional pain and suffering resulting from the Agency's actions.

Regarding her request for compensatory damages, Complainant wrote that she suffered from anxiety, hyper-vigilance, fear of crowds, and insomnia. She further stated that the assault was particularly painful because she had been molested as a young child, and continued to have flashbacks to this childhood victimization.

Complainant also provided written documentation from her mental health provider in the form of a July 6, 2009 progress note. The mental health provider noted, "a significant component of [Complainant's] situation is 'second injury,' that is the response of the environment to her assault and recovery. While this cannot be repaired, it is possible that removal from this situation might be of significant assistance to her." The progress note went on to state that Complainant was experiencing anger and frustration with the response to her assault from management and her coworkers. Specifically, Complainant "[r]eported dissatisfaction with the culture and support that she received in her current position and provided specific examples of conflict with supervisors and perceived lack of support by coworkers. She reported that she remains very angry about these situations and the assault...she reported that after the incident she urgently requested assistance but none was forthcoming...." In addition, the mental health provider wrote that Complainant "believe[s] that she will be able to function as a correctional officer if she feels support by supervisors and coworkers," and that she was "interested in pursuing a transfer to another facility and is able to travel anywhere for such a position."

On appeal, Complainant also submitted a statement from a Licensed Professional Counselor. The counselor states that Complainant is experiencing anxiety, inability to sleep and feeling like she was going to explode. The counselor stated that Complainant reported that she was particularly upset by the fact that when she was being assaulted by the inmate her coworkers refused to step in and help her.

Damages in this case are difficult to assess because, while it has been established that Complainant experiences ongoing symptoms and treatment for PTSD, it is unclear from the evidence the extent to which Complainant's emotional distress was caused by the initial January 2009 sexual assault versus the Agency's subsequent denial of her request for reasonable accommodation. See Grimes v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01A10328 (August 27, 2002) (where the complainant has pre-existing conditions, the agency is only liable for additional harm caused by the discrimination). We do, however, find that the evidence shows that Agency's actions in refusing to consider or denying her accommodation requests did cause Complainant significant emotional stress and exacerbated her PTSD. For example, we note Complainant's mental health provider stated that, "a significant component of [Complainant's] situation is 'second injury,' that is the response of the environment to her assault and recovery."

Again, while recognizing the difficulty in quantifying the extent of this "second injury," we conclude that the $12,000 awarded by the Agency in non-pecuniary compensatory damages is inadequate to compensate Complainant for the emotional harm she suffered as a result of the Agency's failure to accommodate her disability. Instead, we find that an award of $50,000 is more in keeping with the amounts awarded in similar cases. See Sartini v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120112305 (September 19, 2012) (EEOC awarded $50,000 in non-pecuniary compensatory damages where the agency the failed to provide the complainant with a reasonable accommodation which resulted in the aggravation of a preexisting back injury and severe aggravation and exacerbation of Complainant's relatively "stable" mental condition in the form of emotional distress, depression, substantial weight gain, anxiety, paranoia, marital trouble, humiliation, and embarrassment); Williams v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01990520 (February 14, 2002) (EEOC awarded $50,000 in non-pecuniary compensatory damages to a complainant who was denied a reasonable accommodation for his disability and who experienced an exacerbation of a pre-existing depression); Ortiz v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A15376 (September 25, 2002), req. for recons. den., EEOC Request No. 0520030082 (January 7, 2003) (EEOC awarded $50,000 in non-pecuniary, compensatory damages to a complainant, who was denied an accommodation for his disability, and who experienced emotional distress, sleeplessness, insomnia, depression, and ulcers).
