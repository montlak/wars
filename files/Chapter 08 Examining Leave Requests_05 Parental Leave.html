PARENTAL LEAVE

Pregnancy, birth, and adoption present employees with serious challenges. Although the major responsibility falls on the families themselves, as a federal manager you are expected to help your employees maintain the proper work/life balance.

The blanket term "parental leave" covers sick leave, annual leave, paid parental leave, and leave without pay -- often under the terms of the Family and Medical Leave Act -- taken by federal employees in connection with pregnancy and with the birth or adoption of a child.

Pregnancy, birth, adoption, and your employees' new parental responsibilities will require you to plan in advance, stay flexible, and make sure the work gets done while your employees are properly supported. In order to plan effectively, you need to know your employees' leave entitlements and your role as a manager in approving leave requests.

You also need to understand your staffing options, in case leave entitlements create a temporary staffing shortage in your unit. HR can help you and your employees to understand leave entitlements and to plan to fill any staffing gaps.

Federal rules can complicate the most common of events, including birth and adoption. But the rules are there for a reason, to protect you and your employees. Seek HR's guidance to make sure you and your agency are in compliance with regulations and to avoid employee complaints. Together with HR, you should inform your employees of the leave options available to handle an impending family event, and then work out a tentative parental leave schedule.

Warning: No matter how dedicated your employees, there are times when family comes first. Do not take it personally that your female employee "got pregnant and left you in a bind." Do not sneer at your male employee who wants some time off to be with his new child, because that doesn't suit your view of what men ought to do. Men are entitled to FMLA-based parental leave just as much as women are. A negative attitude toward parental leave requests is a good way to kill morale -- and to set yourself up for an EEO complaint.

Contact while away. In today's connected workplace, the lines between home and work have become blurred. Employees on leave for any purpose, including parental leave, may want to keep in touch by email, text, teleconference, or any other suitable way. Employees leaving for an absence of several months may be particularly anxious not to lose touch, so they can hit the ground running on their return.

Let the employee determine the terms of the contact. After all, approved leave is leave, and the employee won't be on duty. And the employee won't get paid for any contact with the workplace. Any but the most casual contact could create regulatory problems, depending on the employee's position (generally speaking, the more senior the position, the less problem there is). Consult HR to determine the limits on contact.

Pregnancy

Pregnant employees may present special challenges in the workplace long before they depart on parental leave.

Avoid assumptions. Do not simply assume there are some things a pregnant woman should not do, such as travel for training or business, or certain physical duties.

Pregnancy is not a disability. One way to approach managing a pregnant employee is to handle the situation as you would when dealing with employees with temporary impairments. If the employee's job has some physical aspects to it, such as heavy lifting or prolonged standing, they may be limited for a time from doing certain tasks. But avoid assumptions about what a pregnant employee should or should not do.

Even though pregnancy is not a disability, pregnancy-related impairments may be considered disabilities. Managers should be aware that if a pregnant employee mentions difficulty performing work due to something related to their pregnancy, the ADA's reasonable accommodations protections may be implicated.

For example, the EEOC has indicated that the following conditions could require accommodations -- including leave -- for pregnant employees:

  • • Pregnancy-related anemia.
  • • Pregnancy-related sciatica.
  • • Pregnancy-related carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • • Gestational diabetes.
  • • Abnormal heart rhythms.
  • • Swelling.
  • • Depression.

This list is not exhaustive, and managers should consult with HR if they have questions about whether a pregnant employee's impairment constitutes an ADA disability.

The issue of restrictions should be raised by the employee or their physician. If the pregnant employee's doctor says they need to have some of their duties temporarily modified or they need to be moved to a light-duty position, then do it. But avoid jumping the gun.

Pregnant employees also may need some time off from work that should be covered by their available leave, often under the Family and Medical Leave Act.

You do have some say in the situation. You can require your employee to provide you with medical documentation to help determine if duty changes are needed. You and your employee also need to figure out when they will need time off and how their duties will be handled in their absence.

Pregnant employees should be treated like any other employees. That means they should receive the same training and promotion opportunities as other employees. While pregnancy, birth, and parenthood might keep your employee out of the office for several weeks or months, when the employee returns, they have a right to continue on with their career without any additional obstacles placed in their way by any temporary pregnancy restrictions or by their time away from work.

As soon as you know an employee is expecting a child, start planning for their absence -- and for their return. Pregnancy should not be treated as the beginning of the end of an employee's time with you. Too often, managers assume that the employee will not return after leave, or will return with changed priorities, less engagement, and more scheduling problems, and eventually leave. But if issues of pregnancy and infant care are handled properly, you will probably retain the employee, and any initial problems on return are likely to be temporary.

Leave entitlements

Planning for parental leave is a routine part of your job. But for how much leave should you plan, and what kinds are involved?

Leave rules are complex, especially when the Family and Medical Leave Act is involved. You should consult with HR on each individual case.

The following official leave categories are available for periods of parental leave:

  • • Sick leave. Available as accrued to pregnant employees to cover incapacities of pregnancy and childbirth, and to both parents for the adoption of a child. To the extent permitted by law, your agency can also provide up to 240 hours of advanced sick leave at the request of an employee in connection with the birth or adoption of a child.
  • • Annual leave. Available as accrued to any employee for any purpose.
  • • FMLA leave. Up to 12 weeks a year of leave without pay, available to any employee for purposes related to the birth or adoption of a child. Up to 12 weeks of paid parental leave may be substituted for the LWOP provided by the FMLA. Paid parental leave is discussed in detail in the FMLA section.
  • • Leave without pay. Unpaid leave of any length, approved in the agency's discretion.

Unless they have already used part of their annual entitlement, employees may take up to 12 weeks of FMLA leave in connection with the birth of a child. Employees may substitute annual leave, sick leave, or paid parental leave, as appropriate, for LWOP to avoid losing pay. They may substitute as much annual leave as they have available. Sick leave may be substituted if the given situation is an authorized use of sick leave. Paid parental leave may be substituted if the requirements of the PPL regulations are met. FMLA leave is an entitlement. This means you must approve the leave request.

If new parents wish to stay out longer than their FMLA entitlement, you and your agency may approve additional LWOP at your discretion.

If an employee is experiencing a pregnancy-related impairment that meets the ADA definition of disability, leave may be a form of reasonable accommodation.

Employees who do not return after their approved leave may be considered absent without leave. Consult with HR as soon as any problems arise.

Return to work

Remember to plan not only for the employee's absence but for the employee's return. Consider both specific work assignments and reintegration into your unit.

It's unreasonable to expect returning employees to immediately re-engage full speed. Parenting, especially new parenting, takes a lot out of people, and it is distracting. But as long as they get the work done and quality holds up, you should be flexible and allow for a reasonable adjustment period.

Time issues, including overtime, may become more acute after the birth of a child. Scheduling difficulties may occur, particularly if the employee already has children at home. Use any flexibilities available to help out new parents.

Breastfeeding. Many employees choose to continue breastfeeding after they return to work, and agencies are required to make workplace accommodations for them. Agencies should provide a reasonable break time for an employee to express breast milk for their nursing child for one year after the child's birth, each time the employee has a need to express milk. Agencies also should provide a place, other than a bathroom, that is shielded from view and free from intrusion from coworkers and the public, for employees to express breast milk.

Contact your HR office with any concerns and questions you have about new parents who are returning to work.

Warning: Even though you may have the best intentions, just as with pregnancy, if you jump the gun by changing your employee's duties and making assumptions about what a new parent can and cannot or should and should not do, you will only create more problems in the long run.

Parental bereavement leave

The National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2022 introduced parental bereavement leave for the death of an employee's son or daughter. This type of leave is not the same as sick leave that may be used for bereavement. It is a stand-alone leave entitlement.

The parental bereavement leave provisions entitle employees to a total of two administrative workweeks of paid leave during any 12-month period because of the death of their son or daughter. The two weeks of leave must be used within 12 months of the death of the child.

This leave may not be taken by an employee intermittently or on a reduced leave schedule unless the employee and the agency agree otherwise. If intermittent use is authorized, it may be scheduled to allow the employee to work partial workdays (i.e., a reduced work schedule) or to schedule other types of paid leave or other paid time off on the same workday (for instance, sick leave for some hours and bereavement leave for other hours).

Notice. If the necessity for parental bereavement leave is foreseeable, the employee must provide the agency with reasonable and practicable notice. You may require the employee to provide a written self-certification or other documentation (for example, a death certificate) to verify that the requested parental bereavement leave is being used for bereavement purposes.

When parental bereavement leave may not be used. An employee must use parental bereavement leave during scheduled hours within their tour of duty when they would otherwise be working or using other paid leave. Therefore:

  • • A seasonal employee may not use parental bereavement leave during off-season periods.
  • • An employee may not use parental bereavement leave during furlough periods.
  • • An employee may not use parental bereavement leave during a period of suspension.
  • • An employee who is on leave without pay for service in the uniformed services may not use parental bereavement leave during periods of service in the uniformed services.
  • • An employee may not use parental bereavement leave during a period of separation from federal service (i.e., the employee must be currently employed).

In addition, parental bereavement leave may not be used on a holiday.

Also note that an employee is not entitled to bereavement leave if they are found by proper authority to have deliberately caused the death of the child whose death gave rise to the potential entitlement. Any bereavement leave that was provided to the employee prior to the finding of culpability must be retroactively canceled.

Mistakes to avoid

Pregnancy, childbirth, and adoption are routine human events, but they can create challenges in the workplace. Keep these simple tips in mind.

Do not:

  • • Make assumptions about pregnant employees. Don't place a pregnant employee in a light-duty assignment if they haven't requested it. Let the employee come to you. They and their doctor will know what work the employee can and cannot do and when they will need time off.
  • • Disrespect the rights of new fathers. Being a parent and needing time off to care for a newborn is just as important to new fathers. Be very careful to not discriminate against your male employees simply because of gender stereotypes.
  • • Take it personally. There are times when even the most dedicated employee's family comes first. Do not take it personally that your female employee "got pregnant and left your unit in a bind," or that your male employee wants some time to be with his new child. These are FMLA entitlements. A bad attitude on your part will only hurt morale and may set you up for an EEO complaint.
  • • Cut out HR. HR is there to help you come up with the best solutions to your staffing gaps and to advise you and your employees on leave entitlements.

Ask FMG

Q. Do I have to accommodate a pregnant employee as though they are disabled?

A. No. Pregnancy is not a disability. It is reasonable to handle the situation as you would when dealing with employees with temporary impairments. If the employee's job has some physical aspects to it, such as heavy lifting or prolonged standing, they may be limited for a time from doing certain tasks. But don't make assumptions about what a pregnant employee should or should not do. The issue of restrictions should be raised by the employee or their physician. Pregnant employees also may need some time off from work that should be covered by their available leave, possibly under the Family and Medical Leave Act. Consult HR.

Q. Can I require a pregnant employee to show medical evidence that they require light duty?

A. Yes. You can require your employee to provide you with medical documentation to help determine if any duty changes are needed. If the employee's doctor says they need to have some of their duties temporarily modified or to be moved to a light-duty position, then do so.

Q. If I think that the employee will not come back to work after having a baby, do I have to send them to a scheduled training session before they leave?

A. Yes, if you would have sent the employee to the training if they had not become pregnant. Any written or unwritten employment policy or practice that excludes an individual from employment, training, or promotion based on pregnancy, childbirth, or a related medical condition is illegal discrimination.

Q. Can I require my employee to keep in touch with me during the course of their parental leave?

A. No. Approved leave is approved leave, and your employee will not be on duty. Your employee may want to stay in contact, and you may discuss this with them and consider the best ways to keep connected. But the employee will not be paid for this contact and there may be regulatory difficulties. Consult HR on any limitations.

Q. Should I provide my employee with extra accommodations because they just returned from parental leave?

A. Let your employee ask for any accommodations, and consult HR if you have any concerns or questions. Don't make assumptions. However good your intentions may be, changing your employee's duties or schedule and making assumptions about what they can and cannot do will only cause problems.

Q. Does a man really have a right to "parental leave" to be with his new child?

A. Yes, under the FMLA. Work/life balance in the federal workforce is also a matter of public policy.
