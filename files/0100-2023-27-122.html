Z. WORKERS' COMPENSATION

Until recently, the Commission held that workers' compensation claims were within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs, Department of Labor. As a result, it tended to dismiss any complaint that an agency had attempted to interfere with an OWCP claim. See, e.g., Andel v. Postmaster General, 01975337 (1998). However, the Commission recently held that it is retaliation for an agency to make disparaging remarks about an employee's EEO activity in responding to an OWCP claim. See Mares v. Secretary of Army, 01960499 (1998). In such cases, a claim lies against both the employing agency and OWCP, if the claim is denied.

The Commission analyzes claims that involve an agency's actions in response to an OWCP claim to determine if the complaint constitutes a "collateral attack" on the OWCP process. If it does, the complaint does not state a claim within EEO purview. "[T]he proper forum for complainant to have raised her challenge to actions which occurred related to her worker's compensation claim was within the claim process itself. It is inappropriate to attempt to use the EEO process to collaterally attack actions which occurred related to the worker's compensation claims process." See Harvey v. Secretary of Defense, 01991945 (2001). Similarly, in Corcoran v. Postmaster General, 01A20013 (2002), the Commission held that an allegation that a supervisor lied in response to a OWCP claim is a "collateral attack" on the OWCP process and does not state a claim. See also Bonham v. Postmaster General, 01991149 (2002) (holding that an allegation that the agency provided false or misleading information to OWCP does not state a claim and, similarly, an allegation that the agency has contested the complainant's entitlement to continuation of pay does not state a claim within the EEO purview). However, a complainant alleging that her supervisor has failed to provide necessary information to OWCP because of her race, sex and disability states a claim for relief. See Brizenzo v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A14841 (2002); Muhammad v. Postmaster General, 01A03678 (2001); see also Gustafson v. Postmaster General, 01A00698 (2001) (holding that the "general rule" is agency opposition to an OWCP claim does not aggrieve an employee; to be aggrieved complainant must identify a specific omission by the agency that influenced the OWCP decision). The failure of the agency to submit required paperwork to OWCP as part of the claims process does aggrieve an employee. See Williams v. Postmaster General, 01A10999 (2001). Similarly, an allegation that an agency has not assigned the complainant work within her accepted OWCP medical restrictions states a claim. See Busscher v. Postmaster General, 01A33538 (2003).

In an unusual case, the Commission held that an allegation that the complainant should have been brought back to work earlier stated a claim for relief. The complainant filed the complaint after being denied a loan while on workers' compensation because of his uncertain employment status. The agency dismissed the complaint finding that the decision not to grant the complainant a loan was not an agency action. The Commission reversed the agency finding that the loan was the impetus for the complaint, but not the subject of the complaint. The claim, according to the Commission, was that the failure of the agency to return complainant to work earlier was discriminatory. See Sheets v. Secretary of Navy, 01994213 (2001). Similarly, in Daniels v. Postmaster General, 01A21500 (2002), the Commission found an allegation that the agency discriminatorily declined to reimburse the complainant for health insurance premiums paid to OWCP stated a claim for relief.

Thousands of workers' compensation claims are filed every year by federal employees. The workers' compensation process, archaic in its own rite, is beyond the province of this book. For those interested in a detailed description of the worker's compensation process, see Eleanor J. Laws, Federal Sector Workers' Compensation (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2022). Complaints over OWCP claims also are discussed in Chapter 3 under the subheading "Office of Workers Compensation Programs," and in the sections on "Standing: Failure to State a Claim" and "Standing: Failure to State a Claim--Agency Jurisdiction" and in Chapter 15.
