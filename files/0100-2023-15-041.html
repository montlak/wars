M. SETTLEMENT

In accordance with the EEOC policy that encourages settlement, it is neither unusual nor inappropriate for a judge to become involved in prehearing settlement negotiations. The degree of involvement varies depending on the judge, and can range from merely encouraging the parties to discuss settlement to giving some preliminary indications of how he or she may rule on certain issues. A judge does not have the authority unilaterally to order a settlement. Pearson v. USPS, 01842276 (1985).

Settlement is covered in detail in Chapter 5. Discussed below is the specific role of settlement and alternative dispute resolution in the hearing process.

Continuing its policy that settlement of EEO complaints should be strongly pursued as a method of voluntary compliance with the antidiscrimination statutes it enforces, the EEOC, in its Judges' Handbook at 3-1, provides:

The Commission encourages parties to seek informal resolution of their complaints. These informal methods may include:

• settlement conferences or mediation conducted by an Administrative Judge or other District Office staff;

• voluntary alternative dispute resolution programs as agreed to by the parties; and

• mediation programs conducted by volunteer mediators.

Recognizing the success of mandatory settlement processes, the Administrative Judge may order the parties to meet for the purpose of exploring settlement in good faith through mediation or other alternative dispute resolution methods. Informal resolution methods involve time and resources, and the parties must attend such efforts in good faith with the requisite authorization to resolve complaints. The Administrative Judge may not order the parties to settle. The Administrative Judge has discretion to terminate informal resolution processes (e.g., upon notice from a party of a hardship, earlier unsuccessful attempts at resolution, or other reasons as deemed appropriate by the Administrative Judge). When an Administrative Judge terminates the informal resolution process, the complaint(s) shall be returned to the formal hearing process, except under circumstances where sanctions (e.g., due to contumacious conduct during a settlement conference) lead the Administrative Judge to consider other alternatives. Cases that are referred for informal resolution attempts may be held in abeyance in the case tracking system if it is anticipated that such attempts will take at least thirty days. The Administrative Judge should notify the Supervisory Administrative Judge or appropriate Commission staff if the case is to be held in abeyance, for appropriate coding in the case tracking system.

The Judges' Handbook further provides that, as part of the administrative judge's authority to regulate the conduct of the hearing, he or she may impose sanctions against parties under 29 CFR 1614.109(f)(3) who disregard orders related to settlement. Id.

The primary methods at the disposal of the administrative judge to facilitate settlement are settlement conferences, conducted by the hearing judge or a settlement judge, and referral of the case to alternative dispute resolution.
