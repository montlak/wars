2. Use of Anecdotal Evidence

Also in Osolinik, the Commission addressed the issue of the amount of anecdotal evidence sufficient to support a finding of class-wide discrimination. The Commission in Osolinik found that the anecdotal evidence must be such that, when combined with the statistical or other evidence, it establishes that discrimination was the agency's "standard operating procedure":

Assuming the eight individuals who testified at the hearing established eight individual instances of discrimination, the agency avers that a finding of eight or fewer instances of discrimination in at least 394 promotion actions in the Solicitor's Office from 1970 through 1980 is insufficient to justify an inference of class-wide discrimination. In its request to reopen the agency cites a number of court cases where the individual instances of discrimination were found to be insufficient to support a determination of class-wide discrimination. The agency claims that these court cases are similar to the present case. The class maintains, in response, that the case law supports a finding of discrimination where over 13 percent of the class members were discriminated against. The Commission is mindful, however, that the case law is of limited precedential value, inasmuch as the definition of a pattern or practice is not capable of precise mathematical formulation. See Ste. Marie v. Eastern R.R. Ass'n, 650 F.2d 395 (D.C. Cir. 1981). Nevertheless, the Commission is convinced that the anecdotal evidence presented in this case must be such that it establishes that sex discrimination was the agency's "standard operating procedure." International Brotherhood of Teamsters v. United States, 431 U.S. 324 (1977).

The Commission finds that the anecdotal evidence does not establish that sex discrimination was the agency's standard operating procedure in promotion decisions. Even if the computations proposed by the class are used, the ratio of promotions allegedly involving discrimination, to those promotions allegedly not involving discrimination, is too small to establish class discrimination, considering the number of years involved, the size of the class, and the number of pertinent promotion actions. In addition, although the class states that the court in the case of EEOC v. Ball Corp., 661 F.2d 531, 537 (6th Cir. 1981), found that anecdotal evidence from just three female employees enabled the plaintiff to carry its burden of establishing a prima facie case, the Commission notes that the appellate court held the prima facie case was established by statistical evidence, buttressed by specific acts of discrimination. The court did not hold that the prima facie case was established by anecdotal evidence alone. Moreover, the court remanded the case to give the defendant the opportunity to demonstrate that its promotion and transfer policies constituted a bona fide seniority system. Id. at 530. Similarly, in Perryman v. Johnson Products Co., 698 F.2d 1138 (11th Cir. 1983), cited by the class, the appellate court remanded the case on the ground that the individual acts of discrimination were based upon an erroneous application of the McDonnell Douglas analysis. Id. at 1144. Accordingly, the court focused not on the quantum of anecdotal evidence needed to establish a prima facie case, but on the lower court's error in dismissing the defendant's legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its action. In addition, unlike the above-cited cases, the present case involves a combination of unrefined statistics and few instances of individual discrimination....

What constitutes sufficient anecdotal evidence is analyzed on a case-by-case basis, taking into account statistical and other evidence of class-wide discrimination.

The use of anecdotal evidence does not necessarily limit the findings of discrimination solely to the specific aspect of programs highlighted by that evidence. See Mosley v. Attorney General, 05870529 (1988). In that case, the class agents focused their anecdotal evidence on handgun training for females at the FBI. The Commission found that this did not prevent the administrative judge from finding that rifle and shotgun training were also discriminatory where 85% of all firearms training related to handguns and all firearms training was interrelated.
