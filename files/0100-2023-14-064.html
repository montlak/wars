O. GENERAL RELEASES

In the private sector, it is routine for employees to sign a "General Release" in conjunction with any settlement agreement. The release usually spells out in depth all of the claims and potential claims that the settlement agreement resolves and constitutes the employee's agreement to hold the employer harmless for any legal wrongs, real or imagined, known or unknown to the employee at the time of settlement. General releases are used only occasionally in federal sector cases. In part, general releases are of less value in federal sector cases because the doctrine of sovereign immunity exempts the federal government from potential liability for the many common law claims that can be brought against private employers. There is, however, nothing that prevents a federal agency from seeking a general release as part of a settlement agreement.

When a general release is signed subsequent to the settlement agreement, the Commission will interpret the release in light of the prior settlement. In Matos v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01953419 (1996), the agency entered into a settlement agreement that provided, as part of the relief granted to the complainant, that the agency would pay an amount of compensatory damages not to exceed $300,000 and to be determined through the submission of evidence on the damage issue. The complainant and the agency subsequently agreed to a damage amount. In conjunction with that agreement, the agency had the complainant execute a general release. The agency then argued that the general release negated the rest of the settlement agreement and that it was not required to perform its other obligations. The Commission in Matos rejected the agency's arguments as well as the complainant's argument that she was entitled to more in the way of compensatory damages, finding:

The agency argues that the release form, signed by appellant on October 13, 1994, abrogated the oriGINAl settlement agreement. In essence, the agency argues that the release form constitutes a second settlement agreement which supersedes the agreement of February 25, 1994. However, the Commission finds, based both on the language of the release and on equitable considerations, that the release supersedes only that portion of the settlement agreement which provides for the payment of compensatory damages.

The settlement agreement provided that appellant would be paid compensatory damages of up to $300,000 upon her provision of objective evidence showing that the damages were incurred and were caused by the discrimination alleged. However, this was not the only provision in the settlement agreement: as noted above, the settlement agreement also provided for changed proficiency ratings, the convening of a special board, reinstatement of appellant's previous responsibilities and shift, removal of specified records from appellant's personnel file, approval of up to 90 days of leave, and attorney's fees. In light of the length and specificity of the agreement, we cannot conclude that the subsequent release setting the amount of compensatory damages to be paid pursuant to paragraph 6 of the settlement has abrogated the agreement in its entirety.

Furthermore, the language of both the settlement and the release does not support the agency's contention that the release was intended to supersede the entire settlement agreement. It is clear from the language of paragraph 6 that the appellant and the agency left open the provision of compensatory damages for a later determination. The release applies to compensatory damages only: the language cited above refers to claims for damages caused by "personal injuries, death and property damage" growing out of the complaints filed by appellant. The release also waives agency liability for claims of "damages, costs, loss of services, expenses or compensation" growing out of the named complaints. The release does not mention any non-monetary remedies, nor does the release specifically refer to the remedies provided in the settlement agreement. In addition, the agency's representative referred to the release as an offer to pay compensatory damages, not a second settlement agreement or an abrogation of the earlier agreement. Thus, based on the plain meaning of the language in the release, the Commission finds that the parties intended it to apply only to the claims for monetary damages, and did not intend thereby to supersede the entire settlement agreement, but to supplement it. They are both valid and must be considered together. See Klein, supra; Eggleston, supra.

This determination is unchanged by the language in the release providing that "there is absolutely no agreement on the part of the parties hereto to make any payment or to do any act or thing other than is herein expressly stated and clearly agreed to." This clause does not specifically mention the prior settlement agreement. The release does not state that the prior settlement agreement is rendered invalid or superseded thereby. Because the rest of the language in the release clearly refers only to compensatory damages, this isolated provision does not expand the scope of the release to encompass non-monetary claims arising out of the settlement agreement.

Based on these determinations, the Commission finds that the release applies only to that portion of the settlement agreement providing for the payment of compensatory damages. In so holding, we note that the agency already bound itself, in the initial settlement agreement, to pay an unspecified sum of compensatory damages. As a matter of policy, the Commission will not allow the agency to require appellant to bargain away her other rights under the settlement agreement simply to get the agency to put a dollar amount on the damages to which she is already entitled. Cf. Alvidrez v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950633 (June 15, 1995) (agency's promise to treat appellant with dignity and respect provides no consideration for appellant's promise to withdraw her EEO complaint, because appellant is already entitled to such treatment).

Accordingly, the agency improperly determined that the release superseded the entire settlement agreement. Because the Commission has determined that the release abrogates that portion of the settlement agreement providing for compensatory damages, appellant's allegation that she should have received a larger compensatory damages award was properly dismissed. However, appellant's remaining breach allegations have not been addressed. Furthermore, there is insufficient evidence in the record for us to determine whether the settlement agreement has been breached as alleged by appellant. Therefore, we must REMAND these allegations for a supplemental investigation.

When an employee settles a claim in another forum, such as the MSPB, and the settlement provides for resolution of all pending claims, including EEO claims, that settlement deprives the EEOC of any jurisdiction over subsequent EEO complaints involving those claims. Spahos v. Secretary of Navy, 01A22260 (2003).

However, general language in a settlement agreement to the effect that the complainant is resolving all outstanding claims and complaints does not serve as a general release when all other provisions of the agreement are complaint-specific. In Black v. Postmaster General, 0420070002 (2007), the complainant appealed a final agency decision finding that he was not denied reasonable accommodation. While that appeal was pending, the complainant entered into a settlement agreement with the agency on another complaint in which he alleged both disability discrimination and retaliation. The agreement had one provision that contained general release language, but all other provisions related to the specific complaint settled. The Commission in Black held:

We recognize that the settlement agreement contains some generalized language that suggests that the agreement discharges and releases the agency from all claims, demands, obligations, etc., which complainant has brought against the agency. We find however, based in part on the wording of the Sixth Section of the agreement, that this encompasses only claims, demands and obligations that are related to the issues in Agency No. 4K-200-0038-03. We find that the issue in Agency No. 4K-200-0067-02 is distinct from the subject matter in Agency NO.4K-200-0038-03.

The Commission in Black also noted that the agency's actions after settlement were inconsistent with the interpretation it urged since it had never notified the Commission that the complaint subject to the appeal had been settled and took that position only after the complainant sought enforcement of the appellate decision.

The agency must, of course, timely raise a motion to vacate that is based on a complainant's prior release. For instance, the Commission in Randolph A. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0420170020 (2017), granted the complainant's petition for enforcement where the agency refused to implement the administrative judge's 2015 order. The agency explained that its refusal was grounded in a settlement agreement signed by the complainant in 2010, which allegedly contained a release agreeing to withdraw the EEO complaint that was the subject of the 2015 order. The Commission never had jurisdiction over the claim to begin with, the agency argued. The Commission in Randolph A. disagreed, noting that the agency's argument was untimely raised. The proper time to present the release to the Commission was during the initial hearing, not on a petition for enforcement.
