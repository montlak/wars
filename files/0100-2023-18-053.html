broida eeo Chapter 09: Representatives and Official Time Chapter 09: Representatives and Official Time, Subchapter 06: Use of Official Time, Section E: Compensation and Benefit Issues, Subsection 01: Overtime

1. Overtime

Initially, the Commission held that EEO regulations did not require that an agency pay overtime for participation in EEO activities. Thornhill v. Postmaster General, 01880425 (1988). Whether overtime was paid was a matter of agency policy.

However, MD-110 at 6-17 specifically provides that if a complainant or his or her representative has already worked a full week, the agency may be required to pay overtime for required attendance at EEO hearings and meetings. The statement must be read in conjunction with 29 CFR 1614.605(f), requiring that complainants, representatives and witnesses who are federal employees must be in duty status when their attendance is required or authorized by a Commission or agency official in connection with an EEO complaint. The duty status required is further explained at MD-110 at 6-19 to 6-20:

For purposes of these regulations, "duty status" means the complainant's or representative's normal hours of work.

It is expected that the agency will, to the extent practical, schedule meetings during the complainant's normal working hours and that agency officials shall provide official time for complainants and representatives to attend such meetings and hearings.

If meetings, conferences, and hearings are scheduled outside of the complainant's or the representative's normal work hours, agencies should adjust or rearrange the complainant's or representative's work schedule to coincide with such meetings or hearings, or grant compensatory time or official time to allow an approximately equivalent time off during normal hours of work. The selection of the appropriate method for making the complainant or representative available in any individual circumstance shall be within the discretion of the agency.

Any reasons for an agency's denial of official time should be fully documented and made a part of the complaint file.

Witnesses who are federal employees, regardless of their tour of duty and whether they are employed by the respondent agency or another federal agency, must be in a duty status when their presence is authorized or required by Commission or agency officials in connection with a complaint.

The seeming resolution of the regulations and guidance is that overtime can be avoided through the use of compensatory time. However, if compensatory time is not granted, the agency must pay overtime.
