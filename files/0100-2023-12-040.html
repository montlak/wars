26. Representative Mistake

As a general matter, an ordinary run-of-the-mill mistake by counsel will not provide good cause for setting aside the time period for filing a formal complaint. For example, in Abril v. Postmaster General, 01A32379 (2003), the Commission upheld the decision of the agency dismissing the complaint as untimely where the complainant's attorney attempted to justify a five-day delay in filing the complaint by explaining that an inexperienced secretary had made a mistake in docketing the due date. The Commission found the explanation was not excusable neglect.

Similarly, in Mazur v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120102632 (2010), the Commission rejected an argument that delay in getting the notice of final interview from the office receptionist to the attorney constituted good cause for setting aside the 15-day time limit. In Mazur, the Commission contrasted its finding with that in an earlier case setting aside the deadline:

The record in this case indicates that the Notice was sent and received at the address of record for Complainant's attorney on March 8, 2010. As noted above, Complainant's attorney argues that the business center receptionist signed for the Notice but did not give it to her at that time and therefore the fifteen-day time limit should not be based upon the March 8, 2010 date. In support of its arguments, Complainant's attorney cites prior Commission cases where the time limit was extended due to errors with internal mail systems. The Commission, however, is not persuaded and finds that the facts in the instant case are distinguishable.

For example in McWay v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 0120080106 (January 29, 2008), the complainant's attorney asserted that although his receptionist signed for receipt of the Notice, his staff did not receive it until five days later. However, in McWay, the Commission was unable to determine when precisely the Notice was received by the receptionist. The envelope did not bear any notation regarding the date, the receptionist did not date stamp it, and the Postal Service online tracking information was not available at that time. In contrast, the record in the instant case shows that the Notice was signed for on March 8, 2010. Further, Complainant's attorney herself admits that she was expecting the Notice, and contacted the agency on March 9, 2010 to inquire about it. A reasonable inquiry would have also included contact with the receptionist who receives her mail. The Agency's response should have also triggered the attorney to try and locate the Notice at her office. The Agency official observed that the Notice was sent in the mail on March 3, 2010, and suspected that perhaps the delay was due to the return receipt form.

Complainant's attorney also argues that when she received the agency's March 9, 2010 e-mail, she reasonably believed that the "clock started over." The Commission disagrees. The agency counsel clearly stated that "if" it had overlooked that the attorney represented Complainant, which was nor the case because the Notice was sent to the attorney's address of record, then she would see "if it [was] possible to reissue the [Notice]...." When the Notice was sent on March 10, 2010, attached to a short e-mail message, agency counsel specifically noted that it was sent on March 3, 2010. So reference was made to reissuing or restarting the time limitation. The Commission does not find that it was reasonable for complainant to assume that the time limit had been tolled. Complainant has failed to present adequate justification, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c) for extending the filing period.
