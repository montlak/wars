III. THE CIVIL RIGHTS ACT OF 1991

In 1991, Congress explicitly reversed the holding of the Supreme Court in Wards Cove with respect to burdens of proof and reinstated the adverse impact analysis originally set forth in Griggs. See Pub. L. No. 102-166, 105 Stat. 1071, 3(2). It further set down its own guidance. Section k of 42 USC 2000e-2 provides:

(1)(A) An unlawful employment practice based on disparate impact is established under this title only if--

(i) a complaining party demonstrates that a respondent uses a particular employment practice that causes a disparate impact on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin and the respondent fails to demonstrate that the challenged practice is job related for the position in question and consistent with business necessity; or

(ii) the complaining party makes the demonstration described in subparagraph (C) with respect to an alternative employment practice and the respondent refuses to adopt such alternative employment practice.

(B)(i) With respect to demonstrating that a particular employment practice causes a disparate impact as described in subparagraph (A)(i), the complaining party shall demonstrate that each particular challenged employment practice causes a disparate impact, except that if the complaining party can demonstrate to the court that the elements of a respondent's decisionmaking process are not capable of separation for analysis, the decisionmaking process may be analyzed as one employment practice.

(ii) If the respondent demonstrates that a specific employment practice does not cause the disparate impact, the respondent shall not be required to demonstrate that such practice is required by business necessity.

(C) The demonstration referred to by subparagraph (A)(ii) shall be in accordance with the law as it existed on June 4, 1989, with respect to the concept of 'alternative employment practice.'

(2) A demonstration that an employment practice is required by business necessity may not be used as a defense against a claim of intentional discrimination under this title.

(3) Notwithstanding any other provision of this title, a rule barring the employment of an individual who currently and knowingly uses or possesses a controlled substance, as defined in schedules I and II of section 102(6) of the Controlled Substances Act (21 USC 802(6)), other than the use or possession of a drug taken under the supervision of a licensed health care professional, or any other use or possession authorized by the Controlled Substances Act or any other provision of Federal law, shall be considered an unlawful employment practice under this title only if such rule is adopted or applied with an intent to discriminate because of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.

Section 105 of Pub. L. No. 102-166 added in a provision not codified to 20003-2(k):

(b) No statements other than the interpretive memorandum appearing at Vol. 137 Congressional Record S 15276 (daily ed. Oct. 25, 1991) shall be considered legislative history of, or relied upon in any way as legislative history in construing or applying, any provision of this Act that relates to Wards Cove--Business necessity/cumulation/alternative business practice.

Note that under the new legislation, the term "demonstrate" means "meets the burdens of production and persuasion." 42 USC 2000e(m). Under the 1991 Act, the plaintiffs have the burden of persuasion in establishing a prima facie case of adverse impact by showing that a particular employment practice causes a disparate impact on a protected group. The focus must be on particular employment practices, unless the employer's decisionmaking process is such that the effect of individual practices cannot be isolated.

If the plaintiffs succeed in making a prima facie case, then the employer bears the burden of production and persuasion to show that the challenged practice is job-related and supported by a business necessity. Finally, plaintiffs can rebut such a showing by the employer by demonstrating that there is an alternative method of screening employees which the employer refuses to adopt.

In Knight v. Secretary of Navy, 03930029 (1993), the Commission explained how the 1991 Civil Rights Act changed the Wards Cove analysis of disparate impact cases. Although the outcome was not changed, the Knight case is instructive in contrasting the new burden of proof placed on agencies under the 1991 Civil Rights Act with the previous burden imposed under Wards Cove:

Petitioner's allegation of race discrimination, rather than disparate treatment, was based on a claim of disparate impact. Hence, the intent of the employer is not the dispositive issue. See Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U.S. 424 (1971). Rather, the Commission's focus is on the effect or impact of a facially race-neutral practice or policy, i.e., the RIF, on petitioner's protected class. In order to establish a prima facie case of race discrimination under a disparate impact analysis, the petitioner must show that the challenged practice or policy disproportionately impacted members of her protected class. Specifically, petitioner must: (1) identify the specific practice or policy challenged; (2) show a statistical disparity; and (3) show that the disparity is linked to the challenged policy or practice. Watson v. Fort Worth Bank and Trust, 487 U.S. 977 (1988).

The Commission finds that petitioner established a prima facie case of disparate impact race discrimination. Throughout the agency as a whole, the RIF was twice as likely to affect a black employee as a white employee. Indeed, in the report generated for Congress, discussed in our earlier decision, the agency all but conceded that the RIF would have a disparate impact on black employees. For purposes of this decision, the Commission is primarily concerned with the effects of the RIF in petitioner's permanent duty location. Within Shop 72, 56 percent of the employees were black.

The General Foreman of Shop 72, asked to cut 11 non-management positions, eliminated these positions strictly from the ranks of the Rigger Helpers. The practical effect of this decision was that all 11 non-management employees separated from Shop 72 were black. Hence, the General Foreman's decision to confine the RIF to Rigger Helpers disparately impacted black employees. Accordingly, the Commission finds that petitioner established a prima facie case of race discrimination.

Once the employee establishes a prima facie case of disparate impact, under Wards Cove, supra, the burden shifts to the employer to provide a business justification for the challenged action. The employer's burden under Wards Cove was modified by Section 501 of the Civil Right Acts of 1991, Pub. L. No. 102-166, 105 Stat. 1071 (Nov. 21, 1991). Under the Civil Rights Act of 1991, once the complaining party establishes a prima facie case of disparate impact, the employer has the burden of proving that the challenged practice is job-related for the position in question and consistent with business necessity. If the employer satisfies this burden, petitioner may nevertheless prevail if she provides an alternative employment practice that would accomplish the same goal with a less adverse impact on her protected class.

The MSPB AJ analyzed the case under Wards Cove. Petitioner contends that her case should have been analyzed under the 1991 Civil Rights Act. The Commission finds that, because it has met the more stringent standard set forth in the 1991 Civil Rights Act, the agency satisfied its burden. Accordingly, we need not specifically address the question concerning the appropriate standard of proof.

Record evidence reveals that the RIF was necessitated by a reduction in scheduled workload caused by longterm budget reductions beginning in fiscal year 1991. The General Foreman testified that, as acting superintendent of Shop 72, he was charged with eliminating 11 non-management positions. The General Foreman testified that it was his decision to eliminate WG-5 Rigger Helper positions rather than higher level Rigger positions. He testified that Riggers could do the work of Rigger Helpers but Rigger Helpers lacked the skill and experience to do Rigger work. He testified that the Shop would function more efficiently if a greater number of higher skilled employees was retained. In sum, he testified that the Shop could most afford to lose the least skilled workers and that Rigger Helpers, therefore, were most expendable.

Whether the agency must simply furnish a business justification for the RIF or demonstrate that the RIF was job-related for the position in question and consistent with business necessity, the Commission finds that the agency satisfied its obligation under the law. Evidence in the record shows that the Shipyard was faced with the unpleasant reality of longterm budget cuts necessitating significant reductions in the number of employees. Agency officials credibly explained that any later temporary hiring was done because of a fleeting surge in activity caused by the return of ships from Operation Desert Shield/Storm. The General Foreman explained that the decision to eliminate Rigger Helpers was made with the goal of maintaining, as near as practicable, the continued productivity of the Shop. The General Foreman explained that Rigger Helpers, the least skilled workers in the Shop, were most expendable. He stated that Rigger Helpers could not perform all the functions of Riggers but that Riggers could assume the Rigger Helpers' tasks.

The Commission also finds that petitioner has failed to show that the termination by RIF of Rigger Helpers failed to advance the agency's articulated goals or that an alternative practice, serving the same purpose with less of an adverse impact on petitioner's protected class, was available. Each shop at the Shipyard was directed to reduce manpower in order to meet mandated agency-wide budget cuts. The majority of the employees in Shop 72 were black. Rigger Helpers were nearly all black. The impact on black workers was particularly pronounced because of the decision to eliminate only Rigger Helpers within Shop 72. The General Foreman explained that the continued efficiency of the operation was best served by the elimination of the least skilled workers. Petitioner has not shown that eliminating Rigger Helpers has not contributed to the agency's effort to reduce its budget while maintaining productivity. Petitioner has not brought forth evidence of a less onerous method for meeting budget constraints or reducing the workforce while maintaining the operation's efficiency. Accordingly, petitioner has not established race discrimination.
