e. "Swapping" Shifts

The Commission elaborates on voluntary shift swaps as an accommodation in its Compliance Manual, at Section 12-IV, C, 2:

The reasonable accommodation requirement can often be satisfied without undue hardship where a volunteer with substantially similar qualifications is available and willing to switch shifts, either for a single absence or multiple absences, including absences occurring over an extended period of time. "[T]he obligation to accommodate requires that employers and labor organizations facilitate the securing of a voluntary substitute with substantially similar qualifications. Some means of doing this which [covered entities] should consider are: to publicize policies regarding accommodation and voluntary substitution; to promote an atmosphere in which such substitutions are favorably regarded; to provide a central file, [physical or electronic] bulletin board or other means for matching voluntary substitutes with positions for which substitutes are needed." The employer's obligation is to make a good faith effort to allow voluntary substitutions and shift-swaps to accommodate a religious conflict. This does not require the employer itself to arrange a substitute or swap, but where it is difficult for employees to arrange shift substitutes or swaps on their own, the employer may have an obligation to do more to facilitate the search for volunteers. Likewise, if the employer is on notice that the employee's religious beliefs preclude him not only from working on his Sabbath but also from inducing others to do so, reasonable accommodation requires more than merely permitting the employee to swap. An employer does not have to permit a substitute or swap if it would pose an undue hardship. As noted above, under the de minimis cost standard, if a swap or substitution would result in the employer having to pay premium wages (such as overtime pay), the frequency of the arrangement will be relevant to determining if it poses an undue hardship; "the Commission will presume that the infrequent payment of premium wages for a substitute or the payment of premium wages while a more permanent accommodation is being sought are costs which an employer can be required to bear as a means of providing a reasonable accommodation."

If it does not pose an undue hardship, an employer must make an exception to its policy of requiring all employees, regardless of seniority, to work an "equal number of weekend, holiday, and night shifts," and instead permit voluntary shift swaps between qualified coworkers in order to accommodate a particular employee's sincerely held religious belief that he should not work on his or her Sabbath. Of course, if allowing a swap or other accommodation would not provide the coverage the employer needs for its business operations or otherwise pose an undue hardship, the accommodation does not have to be granted.

The Commission denied the agency's request for reconsideration in Marmuiszteyn v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0520120607 (2013), finding:

With respect to the Agency's claim that if offered Complainant the option of swapping shifts with coworkers who agreed to do so, we note that the Commission has found several alternatives acceptable for accommodating conflicts between work schedules and religious practices, including voluntary substitutes and swaps, flexible scheduling or lateral transfer and change of job assignments. See 29 C.F.R. § 1605.2(d). With regard to voluntary substitutions or swaps, the Commission maintains that the obligation to accommodate requires employers to facilitate the securing of a voluntary substitute. Some ways of doing this are publicizing policies regarding accommodation and voluntary substitution, promoting an atmosphere in which substitutions are favorably regarded, or providing a central file, bulletin board, or other means for making voluntary substitutes available. See Hoffman v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A01092 (June 29, 2001); request to reconsider denied, EEOC Request No. 05A10911 (Nov. 16, 2001). In this case, there is no evidence in the record that the Agency attempted to obtain voluntary substitutes or swaps for the Saturdays that Complainant was made to work. See White v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 0120080191 (May 7, 2010). In particular, the Agency failed to show that it even inquired into which colleagues might be interested in swapping shifts with Complainant. As such, we cannot find that the Agency provided Complainant with a reasonable accommodation.

See also Samuelson v. Postmaster General, 0120112777 (2013) (agency failed to facilitate shift swaps).

Where a permanent shift change would be an undue hardship on the agency, it does not deny accommodation by offering the complainant the opportunity to swap shifts with other workers. In Sanchez v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120072134 (2008), the Commission held:

The EEOC Administrative Judge (AJ) concluded that complainant failed to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that discrimination existed. The AJ found that the record established that the agency had accommodated complainant's religious needs by permitting him to swap shifts with other employees and placing him in a non-uniformed position which did not require such unpredictable scheduling demands. Further, the agency established that it would be an undue hardship for the agency to permanently change complainant's schedule. Specifically, complainant's position as a Customs and Border Patrol Officer required that he be on call at all times around the clock, and must be available at all times for unpredictable involuntary overtime and weekend assignments. Permanently changing complainant's schedule would have diminished the efficiency in other jobs, infringed on other employees' job rights, impaired workplace safety, and caused co-workers to carry the complainant's share of potentially hazardous and unpredictable work. Further the AJ found that complainant did not establish that the agency's articulated reasons for undue hardship were a pretext intended to hide discriminatory intent.

After a review of the record in its entirety, including consideration of all statements submitted on appeal, it is the decision of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to affirm the agency's final order, because the Administrative Judge's issuance of a decision without a hearing was appropriate and a preponderance of the record evidence does not establish that discrimination occurred.

The agency failed to accommodate the religious beliefs of the complainant where it allowed him to ask others to work on Sundays for him where his religious beliefs did not permit him to ask others to work on the Sabbath. In Hyde v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720110003 (2012), the Commission found:

The record is undisputed that in October 2007, the Agency informed Complainant that he could secure his own swaps of qualified individuals as a religious accommodation. Complainant then informed the Agency of his belief that to ask others to work on the Sabbath was against his faith. During discovery, Complainant declared that he could not ask another person to work on Sundays, and the agency did not present evidence that disputes this. Moreover, the evidence submitted by Complainant during discovery supports his contention. The AJ found that requiring Complainant to seek his own swaps after he indicated it was against his faith was not a reasonable accommodation, and after a review of the record, we find the AJ was correct. See E.E.O.C. v. Texas Hydraulics, Inc., 583 F. Supp.2d 904 (E.D. Tenn. 2008) (employer's offer that Complainant secure his own volunteers was not a reasonable accommodation because employee stated it was a sin for anyone to work on the Sabbath).
