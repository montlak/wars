10. Relief

When the administrative judge issues a decision with a finding of discrimination, he or she also must determine the appropriate relief. It is not always possible for the judge to be precise about the appropriate relief. For example, in Davis v. Secretary of Treasury, 01891868 (1989), the complainant alleged that she was denied an appointment as a special agent as the result of making earlier allegations of sexual harassment. It was unclear from the record exactly when such an appointment would have occurred.

In structuring relief, the administrative judge recommended the agency offer the complainant employment as a special agent, or in a substantially similar position and award back pay from the date she would have been hired until she was employed. In Davis, which was decided under the old Part 1613, the Commission found that in such situations, the agency must issue a final decision on its specific remedies in accordance with the requirements of 29 CFR 1613.221:

While the AJ's general recommendations on make-whole remedies are binding on the agency, the agency must still issue a FAD implementing the AJ's make-whole remedies, specifying, among other things, a determination of when appellant's starting date should have been, a determination of the particular grade level in which appellant would be placed and the amount of backpay to be awarded. We find that the subsequent letters of the agency to appellant detailing the grade level in which she would be placed, and the amount of backpay to be awarded, could not be considered final decisions because they failed to meet the technical requirements of 29 CFR 1613.221(b)(1) in that they were not sent by certified mail, and 29 CFR 1613.221(e) in that a statement of appellant's right to appeal to the Commission was not included. We therefore find that the agency has not issued a proper Final Agency Decision as it was required to do. See 29 CFR 1613.233(a).

The obligation of any agency, where discrimination is found, is not only to provide make-whole relief, but to provide prompt relief. See Ford Motor Company v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 458 U.S. 220, 221 (1982). To this end, the Commission's regulations provide that when the Commission finds discrimination, an agency must complete the corrective action ordered not later than 60 calendar days after the Commission's decision becomes final. 29 CFR 1613.237(c). We find that in a case where the AJ had recommended discrimination and this recommendation has become binding through failure of the agency to reject or modify the decision in a timely manner, the agency shall, no later than 60 days after receipt, provide, to the fullest extent possible, make-whole relief and issue its decision on remedies in the form of a FAD on all issues with specificity. Thus, if the AJ's decision provides for general make-whole relief remedies, but the agency and the appellant cannot agree on what the appropriate specific remedies should be, the agency must decide and provide the make-whole relief remedies with specificity and issue its decision as a FAD, no later than 60 days after receipt of the Recommended Decision. See Om Parkash Kalra v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01890987 (February 14, 1989).

When an administrative judge enters a finding of no discrimination, she does not have the authority to order the agency to take any particular action. See Campbell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01981997 (2001). In Campbell, the administrative judge expressed concern about desk audits that had taken place, and ordered the agency to conduct a new independent desk audit. The Commission noted that because the administrative judge found that there was no discrimination, she had "no authority to order the agency to conduct a third desk audit or to take any other action."
