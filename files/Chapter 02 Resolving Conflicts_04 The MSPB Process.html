THE MSPB PROCESS

You issued a notice of proposed removal of an employee who allegedly engaged in misconduct. The deciding official found that the charges were proven and that removal was warranted. What happens next?

One thing the removed employee can do is file a petition for appeal with the Merit Systems Protection Board.

The MSPB is an independent, quasi-judicial agency in the executive branch. Its mission is to protect federal merit systems and the rights of individuals within those systems. The board's primary functions are deciding individual employee appeals and conducting merit systems studies. In addition, the MSPB reviews some Office of Personnel Management actions.

The MSPB is composed of three members who are appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate. They serve overlapping, non-renewable seven-year terms. The board is bipartisan. No more than two of its three members may be from the same political party.

The MSPB can review appeals from the following actions, among others:

  • • Reductions in grade or removals for unacceptable performance under Chapter 43.
  • • Removals, reductions in grade or pay, suspensions for more than 14 days, or furloughs for 30 days or less under Chapter 75.
  • • Removals or suspensions for more than 14 days of a career appointee in the Senior Executive Service.
  • • Reduction-in-force actions affecting career appointees in the SES.
  • • Disqualifications of an employee or applicant because of a suitability determination.
  • • Termination of employment during probation when: 1) the employee alleges discrimination because of partisan political reasons or marital status; or 2) the termination was based on conditions arising before appointment and the employee alleges that the action is procedurally improper.
  • • Termination of a manager or supervisor during their probationary period when the manager alleges discrimination because of partisan political affiliation or marital status.
  • • Separations, demotions, or furloughs for more than 30 days that result from a RIF.
  • • Failure to restore, improper restoration of, or failure to return an employee following partial or full recovery from a compensable injury.

Once an appeal is filed

An appeal of an action you were involved in has been filed with the MSPB. Now what?

Mediation. One option is the MSPB's Mediation Appeals Program. MAP allows the employee and the agency to try mediation as an alternative to the formal MSPB appeal process. Mediators help the parties identify issues and barriers to coming to an agreement that will resolve their disputes. Mediation can help settle an appeal quickly and economically, benefiting all concerned.

Both the employee and the agency must agree to mediation before the appeal will be accepted for the MAP process, and both must agree on its resolution before any settlement is concluded. Unlike the traditional appeal process, the parties control the result of the case under the skilled guidance of the mediator. If a settlement cannot be reached, the mediator will not decide the appeal.

Hearings. If mediation is not chosen by the parties, the administrative judge who has been assigned to the case will issue an acknowledgment order to the employee, the employee's representative (if there is one), and the agency. The acknowledgement order directs the agency to explain its reason for taking the personnel action or decision being challenged, and to turn over all documents contained in the agency record of the action.

The AJ may dismiss the appeal if the MSPB does not have the authority to review the matter or if the appeal was filed late and the employee doesn't have a good reason for the delay. The AJ usually will conduct conferences to narrow and clarify the issues in the appeal. The agency and the employee can still voluntarily settle the case while this goes on.

Next, the AJ may hold a formal hearing to allow the parties to make arguments and present evidence, including witness testimony. If a subpoena is issued ordering you to appear as a witness at a hearing, you must appear or face potential penalties. The agency attorney should explain the hearing procedure to you and prepare you for testifying.

At the hearing, the employee will probably testify as to their version of the events. Other employees from your unit may be called to testify as witnesses, either in support of the agency or the employee. Remember to act in a calm and professional manner with employees who show up at the hearing.

Following a hearing, the AJ will issue an initial decision. In appeals that are decided on the merits (not merely dismissed or settled), the AJ may affirm the agency's action, reverse the action, or, in certain cases, mitigate the penalty imposed by the agency.

Petitions for review. When employees or agencies are dissatisfied with an AJ's initial decision, they may file a petition for review with the MSPB. Petitions are considered by the three board members, who issue a final decision. Dissatisfied employees may alternatively elect to file an appeal with the U.S. Court of Appeals, Federal Circuit.

The MSPB may dismiss a petition if it is not within the board's jurisdiction or if the petition was not filed within the required time limit. The MSPB may deny a petition if it does not meet the criteria for review.

If the board grants a petition, its final decision may affirm or reverse the initial decision, in whole or in part. The MSPB also may modify the initial decision, vacate it, or remand the case to the AJ for further processing.

Judicial review. Any employee or applicant for employment adversely affected by an MSPB final decision can file an appeal with the Federal Circuit or in cases involving allegations of discrimination with the EEOC or to a U.S. District Court. Cases involving whistleblower reprisal claims may be appealed to the Federal Circuit or any other court of appeals with jurisdiction over the case.

Your role in the process

Once an action is appealed to the MSPB, a manager's role is usually limited to helping the HR office and the agency attorney prepare a defense to the employee's claims.

You may be asked to assist in the discovery phase of the appeal. Discovery is the process that allows a party to obtain information from another party, generally in the form of documents and testimony. The requesting party uses the information obtained through discovery to plan and develop its case.

Depositions. You may need to appear for a deposition in which you will have to respond to questions while under oath or affirmation. A transcript of your testimony will be prepared, and you may be required to review it and make necessary changes. The agency attorney will help you prepare for a deposition.

You won't necessarily be deposed at your workplace. For example, your deposition may be held at the employee's counsel's office. As federal employees, managers provide their testimony on agency time. The agency also must pay your travel and per diem expenses associated with appearing as a witness.

The employee's counsel will have an opportunity to cross-examine you about your version of the events involving the employee. Stay calm, answer questions to the best of your ability, and follow the directions of your agency attorney.

The agency attorney also may need you to help produce documents, answer interrogatories, and respond to requests for admissions. The employee may ask for your emails, notes, memos, and other written documents regarding their employment.

Hearings. You may also have to participate in conferences and hearings as a witness for the agency. Your agency counsel and HR office will let you know if you've been scheduled to testify at a hearing. They will help you prepare for testimony and may show you documents in the case to help you remember the events of the action.

If you are asked to testify, answer the questions honestly and to the best of your ability. If you have any concerns about your role in this part of the process, consult with the agency attorney or your HR office.

It's important to understand that a manager is considered a witness, not a defendant. As a witness, a manager's obligation is to cooperate with the officials who process MSPB appeals and to tell the truth.

Mistakes to avoid

The MSPB appeal process can be confusing if you've never been through it before. Here are some tips to make the proceedings go more smoothly.

Do not:

  • • Retaliate. An employee who has filed an appeal but is still working under your supervision should never be subjected to retaliation.
  • • Destroy documentation concerning the adverse action. This is unlawful and will deprive you of the opportunity to refresh your memory.
  • • Go it alone. Contact the HR office or the agency attorney handling the appeal if you have any questions about the MSPB process.
  • • Forget you are a manager. Act in a professional, business-like manner throughout the MSPB process. Not only will this help the process run more smoothly, it will also help you avoid making statements that reflect poorly on your decisions.

Ask FMG

Q. One of my former employees appealed their removal. Am I a defendant in the case?

A. No, a manager or supervisor is considered a witness, not a defendant. As a witness, your obligation is to cooperate with the officials who process MSPB appeals and to tell the truth.

Q. I just found out that I have to go to a deposition. I've never done this before. How do I get ready for this?

A. You may need to appear for a deposition in which you will have to respond to oral or written questions while under oath or affirmation. The agency attorney will assist you in preparing for the deposition. The agency attorney will ask you questions about the circumstances which led to the adverse action taken against the employee, and the employee's counsel will also be given an opportunity to cross-examine you. Answer the questions honestly and to the best of your ability.

Q. The deposition is being held at the former employee's counsel's office, which is an hour's drive from work. Do I have to do this on my own time?

A. No, managers provide their testimony on agency time. The agency also must pay your travel and per diem expenses associated with appearing as a witness.

Q. The agency attorney asked me to look for documents requested by the employee's counsel. Some of my notes might make it look like I was being harder on them than on other employees. Do I have to give the notes to the agency attorney?

A. Yes, you must give the notes to the attorney if they meet the requirements for documents requested by the employee's counsel. You cannot hide or destroy evidence relating to the matter. This includes emails, notes, and other written documents.

Q. The administrative judge upheld the removal of a former employee. Can I put all this behind me now?

A. No. The employee can now file a petition for review with the MSPB in Washington, D.C. The petition will be considered by the three MSPB board members, who will issue a final decision. Employees have the alternative of filing an appeal with the U.S. Court of Appeals, Federal Circuit. A final order or decision of the MSPB may be appealed to the Federal Circuit or in cases involving allegations of discrimination to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission or to a U.S. District Court. Cases involving whistleblower reprisal claims may be appealed to the Federal Circuit or any other court of appeals with jurisdiction over the case.
