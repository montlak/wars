broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 05: Compliance With Settlement Agreements, Section H: Subsequent Acts

H. SUBSEQUENT ACTS

Settlement of an EEO complaint does not insulate an employee from any future actions that the agency may take. However, because there is an element of good faith in every settlement agreement, an agency may not negate a settlement agreement through subsequent actions that do not have an independent basis. In Pyles v. Postmaster General, 05920044 (1992), the Commission set aside the agency's subsequent action of demoting the complainant after reinstatement:

[A] settlement agreement which the parties knowingly and voluntarily agree to enter shall be binding on both parties.

In its previous decision, the Commission concluded that the agency had complied with the settlement agreement--despite the later demotion--because the agreement called for the agency to reinstate appellant to a PS-3 Custodian position and the agency had done so.

A complainant who enters into a settlement agreement such as this one has the right to believe that, with certain exceptions, his appointment to that position will be a permanent one. (Footnote omitted.) If the Commission were to hold otherwise, appellant, pursuant to the settlement agreement, would agree to withdraw his complaint in exchange for certain actions by the agency, and then the agency, once it met the terms of the agreement, however briefly, could reverse its action with impunity. Consequently, the Commission's previous decision erred in finding that the agency's action complied with the terms of the agreement.

Allegations that subsequent acts of discrimination violate the terms of a settlement agreement are properly processed as separate complaints. See Allen v. Postmaster General, 01956930 (1997); see also Batra v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01934736 (1993); 29 CFR 1614.504(c). But, it is not always so easy to determine if an allegation involves breach of a settlement agreement or a subsequent act of discrimination. As a general rule, when the allegation involves a personnel action that happens sometime after the agency's original compliance, it should be treated as a new complaint. In Royster v. Postmaster General, 05950843 (1997), the agency changed the employee's date of seniority six months after transferring him pursuant to a settlement agreement. In remanding the complaint for further investigation, the Commission in Royster held:

Furthermore, it is not entirely apparent whether this issue was properly addressed as a breach allegation, and such a determination is contingent upon when appellant's seniority date was changed from February 15, 1986, to December 29, 1990. In the event this change occurred at the time of appellant's transfer, then we would agree that the issue was properly addressed as a breach allegation. However, the Seniority Roster dated May 17, 1991, which was generated nearly six months after appellant's transfer, lists his date of seniority as February 15, 1986. Although it is possible that this document is inaccurate, it clearly suggests that appellant's seniority date was not changed until some time after he had transferred to the San Bernardino facility. In that event, we find that the allegation should have been processed as a subsequent act of discrimination.

Based on the foregoing, we find that the agency should conduct a supplemental investigation which addresses, at the outset, when appellant's seniority date was officially changed. In the event it was changed at the time of appellant's transfer, then the agency shall process the allegation as a breach allegation in accordance with the provisions of 29 CFR § 1614.504. However, if the agency determines that appellant's seniority was changed at some point subsequent to appellant's transfer, then it shall process the allegation as a subsequent act of discrimination. Furthermore, regardless of the manner in which the allegation is processed, the agency shall obtain sufficient evidence to determine its policy with regard to the retention of seniority in situations where an employee transfers within craft to a different facility.
