6. Findings Based on Evidence of Record

Credibility determinations based on the evidence of record, as opposed to the demeanor or conduct of a witness, are not entitled to deference. See Trina C. v. Postmaster General, 0120142617 (2016) (an administrative judge's finding that the accused management official's record testimony was credible, where the management official did not appear at the hearing, to establish that the complainant failed to prove her allegations was a "clear-cut abuse of discretion."); Burkhardt v. Postmaster General, 05880631 (1988); see also Van Gundy v. Secretary of Treasury, 01950862 (1997). In such cases, the Commission will independently review the evidence of record. The same is true where the administrative judge bases a credibility finding on whether it was credible that events occurred as the witness testified, instead of the demeanor or conduct of the witness. See DiPietro v. Postmaster General, 01883348 (1988).

When a credibility determination is based on whether the proffered testimony is reasonable in light of the contrary testimony of several witnesses, this is not a determination based on demeanor or conduct. See Rierson v. Secretary of Commerce, 01911027 (1991). The Commission will not defer to the administrative judge and will give such testimony whatever weight it deems appropriate. There is no need for the Commission to defer to the credibility determination of its administrative judge when that determination is based on the judge's interpretation of the plausibility of reasons offered by the supervisor for his actions, since the plausibility of a person's actions can be determined by review of the record. See Koritz v. Postmaster General, 01913183 (1991).

When an administrative judge bases a credibility determination on comparisons of the testimony of a witness with other evidence instead of on demeanor, the comparison must be appropriate for the Commission to defer to it. In Kilpatrick v. Secretary of Treasury, 01890453 (1989), the Commission overturned the credibility finding of its administrative judge based on comparing the complainant's application and work history with the performance of other applicants. "The more appropriate course of considering the [selecting official's] deliberations would be a consistent comparison of appellant's application and work history with the same for each individual referred." Id.

The Commission is not above using common sense in reviewing the credibility determinations of its administrative judges. In Fortson v. Department of Navy, 01873248 (1988), the Commission used a relentless logic that would have impressed Sherlock Holmes in finding the credibility determination of its judge on the time of closing of bids for a detail was correct. The RMO in Fortson testified that a noon deadline had been set and the complainant testified that the deadline was the end of the day:

The record contains the testimony of the other three employees at the January 7, 1986 meeting. The Asian, who immediately expressed no interest, and may have left the meeting early, either did not recall a time frame or believed a 3:00 P.M. deadline had been set. The white, who immediately expressed an interest, stated that the selecting official told them to respond "that day." The other black testified that the selecting official had set the noon deadline. We agree with the Administrative Judge that, although there is variation in the testimony, because the Asian immediately expressed no interest and the white immediately expressed interest, neither one of them had any reason to pay attention to the deadline set by the selecting official. The other black, on the other hand, said she would let the selecting official know later in the day and she therefore had more reason to note the deadline. Although the same is true of appellant, the other black has no stake in the outcome of these proceedings. Therefore, of the four employees, the testimony of the other black, that the selecting official had set a noon deadline, is the most persuasive.

Where the administrative judge enters no findings on the record with respect to the credibility of witnesses, the Commission will review such factors as the specificity of the testimony, and whether the testimony is corroborated by other evidence and testimony, in making its own credibility determinations. See, e.g., Butler v. Postmaster General, 01882521 (1988).

The fact that a witness does not testify at a hearing is not a sufficient basis for discrediting the affidavit testimony of that witness where there is other evidence in the record that corroborates that testimony, including the testimony of other witnesses at the hearing. Hawkins v. Social Security Administration, 01980629 (2001).
