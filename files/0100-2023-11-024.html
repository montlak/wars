2. Agency's Burden

Once a complainant alleges lack of notice of EEO procedures, the burden shifts entirely to the agency to rebut that allegation. The complainant is not required to make specific allegations about lack of notice. Moreover, once a lack of notice is established, the complainant need not further establish that she would have timely filed a complaint but for the lack of notice. In McGah v. Attorney General, 01955717 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant's assertion that she was not aware of the time limits triggered the agency's burden to show constructive knowledge:

The agency argues on appeal that because appellant never asserted that the agency failed to post notices explaining the EEO process, the agency has never had an opportunity to dispute such an assertion. The Commission disagrees with the agency's interpretation of appellant's statements. Appellant clearly stated in paragraph 6 of her supplemental affidavit dated July 27, 1987 that she was unaware of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor. The Commission finds that this statement was sufficient to put the agency on notice that appellant claimed she had no notice of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor.

If the agency wishes to show that appellant had constructive notice of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor, then it is the agency's burden to show that appellant had constructive notice via posted notices or by other means. In order to raise the question of whether appellant had constructive notice of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor, appellant need only claim that she was unaware of the EEO time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor; appellant need not provide a list of all possible methods (e.g., posters, handbooks, and newsletters) in which she was not informed of the time limits. Furthermore, because the agency addressed this issue on appeal, we find that the agency was aware of appellant's claim that she was unaware of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor. The agency had an opportunity to submit evidence showing that appellant had actual or constructive notice of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor, but failed to do so. Furthermore, the agency has not even claimed that appellant had constructive notice of the time limits via an EEO poster or by other means.

The agency argued in the FAD that appellant's claim was untimely raised because the time limit should not be extended when an employee suspected she was discriminated against and failed to diligently protect her rights. The Commission finds that the agency is arguing that appellant has not shown sufficient equitable reasons to extend the time deadline for contacting an EEO Counselor because she did not diligently protect her rights. The Commission finds that § 1613.214(a)(4) uses the term "shall" and mandates when the time deadlines are to be extended. This does not mean that equitable principles do not apply to the determination of timeliness, but we do find that appellant need not show that equity is on her side in order to have the time limit extended under § 1613.214(a)(4).

The Commission in McGah further found that the doctrine of laches did not apply to the complainant, based in part on her belief that she was a member of a class action suit:

To the extent that the doctrine of laches is applicable, we find that appellant did not fail to pursue her rights for such a period of time so as to preclude her from pursuing the instant matter. Our reason for not applying the doctrine of laches against appellant is twofold: first, there is no evidence that appellant knew of the time limits prior to her bringing the matter to the agency's attention; and second, appellant may have reasonably believed that she was part of the Hansen class (or that the agency was aware of the matter) when the agency issued a notice on March 13, 1978 stating that the Assistant Attorney General for the agency had accepted a class complaint in which it was alleged that the agency had engaged in personnel policies and practices that discriminated against women on the basis of sex. See Loos v. Department of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05920160 (Jan. 14, 1993). Even if the agency had not issued the March 13, 1978 notice, we still find that appellant sufficiently pursued her claim to ward off implementation of the doctrine of laches.

On appeal, the agency argues that in an affidavit (dated October 10, 1979), appellant indicated that she did not avail herself of the EEO complaint and counseling procedures because of the futility of the process. The agency also argues that the class counsel admitted in a Proposed Finding of Fact dated August 29, 1980 that appellant "indicated an awareness of the EEO procedures." The agency argues that "[e]leven years after her resignation, Ms. McGah sought to modify her [October 10, 1979] statement, asserting that she had not intended to imply that she was aware of the EEO procedures." The agency argues that:

It strains credulity that appellant, an attorney, and her retained counsel were unable to adequately articulate their thoughts.... However, even assuming that Ms. McGah's later statement is the more accurate, she still fails to assert that she would have filed a claim.... Ms. McGah has failed to provide evidence that she would have filed a claim but for her lack of specific knowledge of the procedure or time limits.

The Commission finds that appellant need not show that she would have timely contacted an EEO Counselor but for her lack of knowledge of the time deadlines for contacting an EEO Counselor. Once an appellant claims that she was unaware of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor, then the burden is on the agency to show that appellant had actual or constructive notice of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor.

In an affidavit dated October 10, 1979, appellant stated:

7. I did not use the EEO complaint and counseling procedures at the Bureau because I did not think that doing so would do any good. Furthermore, since I wanted to keep my job I was afraid of getting a reputation as a troublemaker; it was made clear to me that it would not be in my best interests to protest about treatment too vigorously. As a result, I generally tried to keep quiet.

In an affidavit dated July 27, 1987 (entitled Supplemental Affidavit), appellant stated:

6. I was never informed of the existence of EEO procedures for counseling or filing a complaint of discrimination while I was in New Agent Training or at any other time. Specifically, I was completely unaware of any time limits for contacting an EEO counselor or filing a written administrative complaint of discrimination. In fact, to my knowledge, there was no EEO counselor at the FBI Academy when I was in training there.

The Commission finds that appellant's October 10, 1979 affidavit does not constitute an admission that appellant had actual or constructive notice of the time limits for contacting an EEO Counselor and does not show that appellant did not diligently pursue her claim. Our interpretation of the October 10, 1979 affidavit would not differ in the absence of the July 27, 1987 Supplemental Affidavit.

Therefore, we find that, consistent with the Commission's ruling in Pride, EEOC Request No. 05930134, appellant timely raised her claim of discrimination with the agency pursuant to § 1613.214(a)(4).

When the complainant asserts that he or she was unaware of the time limits, the determination of whether the complainant actually was aware of the agency's EEO process and the time limit for initiating counseling are credibility determinations. McDonald v. USPS, 01831261 (1983).

Where an employee has previously engaged in the EEO process, she cannot later assert that she did not know of the EEO time limits because her employer failed to prove that it posted this information. Briley v. Archivist of the United States, 05950778 (1996). See also Cecile S. v. Postmaster General, 0120161104 (2016) (claims raised more than 45 days after the last alleged discriminatory event were untimely where complainant had prior experience with the EEO complaint process). If the agency relies on a complainant's previous EEO activity in asserting she had knowledge of the applicable time limits, then the agency must support that contention with evidence. The Commission remanded Carpenter v. Postmaster General, 01971355 (1998), for further investigation because the agency included no evidence in the record to support its contention that the complainant had filed two prior complaints. But, the mere fact that an employee has had contact with an EEO official for purposes other than filing a complaint does not suffice to show constructive notice. See Camberis v. Secretary of Treasury, 01961169 (1997) (the record established that, as Hispanic Program Manager, the complainant had previous contact with EEO officials).

An agency is not required to demonstrate that it posted EEO notices on its premises when the memorandum informing the employee of a personnel action specifically instructs the employee on his right to contact a counselor within 45 days if he believes the action is discriminatory. Spurlock v. Secretary of Army, 05960373 (1997). In Spurlock, the Commission held, however, that the notice was only dispositive for the reduction-in-force referred to in the memorandum and not for other issues raised by the complainant.

In Beckmeyer, et al. v. Secretary of Navy, 0120073167 (2007), the Commission found that the 20 consolidated complaints over 30-day suspensions were timely where the Filipino complainants with a limited ability to understand English did not initiate counseling until six months after the suspensions. The Commission noted that, while the agency asserted that EEO notices were posted in several places in the facility, there was no evidence in the record to that effect. Since the agency had the burden of proof on notice, the Commission deemed the dismissal for untimely contact to be improper.
