broida eeo Chapter 09: Representatives and Official Time Chapter 09: Representatives and Official Time, Subchapter 06: Use of Official Time, Section B: Definition of Reasonable Time, Subsection 02: EEO Counseling

2. EEO Counseling

The use of official time extends to EEO counseling and, where the agency requires the complainant to fill out a request for counseling form, it extends to the time necessary to fill out the form and consult with a representative, if necessary. In Shepherd v. Postmaster General, 05960547 (1997), the Commission held that the agency erred by denying the complainant official time to fill out her request for counseling form:

We do take exception to the agency's denial of official time to fill out a request for EEO counseling form. As a preliminary matter, the Commission points out an error in the way this allegation was processed. When a complainant alleges an improper denial of official time, the complainant is alleging a violation of our regulations, specifically 29 CFR § 1614.605, not a complaint of discrimination. Therefore, the agency shall make a determination as to whether the decision to deny official time was properly made, and remedy any violation which has occurred.

With respect to the propriety of the denial, we note that according to the responsible officials, when appellant asked for one to two hours to meet with her representative to fill out the Request for Counseling form, they denied her request, relying on the response of an agency investigator who allegedly told them that the form had to be filled out on her own time, though she could meet with her representative on the clock. When appellant refused a substitute representative, the agency denied her the time.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.605(b) requires an agency to provide official time to a complainant "to prepare the complaint and to respond to agency and EEOC requests for information." In this case, the record indicates that the agency requires complainants to fill out PS Form 2564-A, EEO Request for Counseling, in order to initiate the counseling process.

Given the agency's requirement that appellant promptly fill out the Request for Counseling form, and the fact that the form requires a response to an agency request for information, we find that appellant was entitled to official time to fill out the form, regardless of whether she was meeting with a representative to do so. Accordingly, we will order the agency to credit appellant for the time she spent off the clock, which appellant indicated was 1.5 hours, in order to prepare the form. The agency shall refrain from denying complainants reasonable official time to fill out such forms in the future.

Reasonable time spent preparing for the EEO counseling and performing other tasks ancillary to counseling must be calculated on a case-by-case basis. Lewis v. Postmaster General, 01A00484 (2000). The Commission reiterated this concept in Maddox v. Postmaster General, 0120081760 (2011), where the complainant's supervisor initially granted two and a half hours for the complainant to meet with representative and fill out a counseling request form and a higher level manager reduced the time granted to 15 minutes each for the complainant and her representative. The Commission, in Maddox, also found there was reason to doubt the manager's statement that he was advised by the agency EEO office that 15 minutes was reasonable:

The evidentiary record shows that Complainant requested by faxed letter and hand-delivered document two and half hours of official time to prepare her pre-complaint form and meet with her EEO Representative on April 23 and 24, 2006, respectively. It also shows that Complainant's supervisor (Supervisor) received and granted Complainant's request, and scheduled 12:30-3:00 P.M. on April 26, 2006, as the period Complainant was to meet with her EEO representative and receive assistance in working on her EEO complaint.

According to the Agency's final decision, the Manager indicated that she discussed Complainant's request with the EEO Office and was informed that anywhere between 15 to 20 minutes is a reasonable amount of time to meet with an EEO counselor because the Complainant would be interviewed and have the opportunity to be more specific at a later time. See Agency's January 29, 2008, Final Decision at 1. The final decision also indicates that once receiving this information from the EEO Office, the Manager granted Complainant and her representative 15 minutes each to complete a request for counseling form because, based on the Agency's operational needs that day, that is what she was able to grant. Finally, the final decision indicates that the Manager stated that all employees making such requests have been treated the same way and her decision was in no way meant to deny Complainant any of her rights.

We note that the Manager's statements were not a part of the evidentiary record. In light of this fact, and the final decision's assertion that it was Agency policy to inform all field managers that 15 to 30 minutes is a reasonable amount of official time, we find the Agency's justification for allowing Complainant only 15 minutes of reasonable time to be suspect. First, there is no explanation why the Agency chose to allow Complainant 15 minutes of official time after she had already been approved and scheduled for 2 and one-half hours. Second, a telephone call from the Manager to the EEO office to determine what was reasonable is not consistent with the Manager's statement that all employees making such requests were treated the same or the Agency's assertion that there exists some policy which advises managers that 15 to 30 minutes is reasonable. We therefore find that Complainant was not allowed an official amount of reasonable time.
