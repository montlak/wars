H. DEMONSTRATION OF QUALIFIED

Prior to passage of the ADAAA, to meet the requirement of a prima facie case that the complainant was a member of the protected group, he must have been able to show that he was a "qualified individual with a disability" within the meaning of 29 CFR 1630.2(m). See also 42 USC 12111(8); Reid v. Veterans Administration, 01830834 (1983).

A qualified individual with a disability was defined in the Commission's federal sector regulations at 29 CFR 1630.2(m) as follows:

Qualified individual with a disability means an individual with a disability who satisfies the requisite skill, experience, education and other job-related requirements of the employment position such individual holds or desires, and who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of such position.

The ADAAA did make a subtle, though important, change in the language to some provisions of the ADA. For example, 42 USC 12112(a) no longer employs the "qualified individual with a disability" language in prohibiting discrimination, but prohibits discrimination against a "qualified individual on the basis of disability." Other provisions, such as 42 USC 12112(b)(5)(A), which requires reasonable accommodation retains the "qualified individual with a disability" language. The Commission's new regulations no longer define a qualified individual with a disability. Instead, 29 CFR 1630.2(m) provides:

The term "qualified," with respect to an individual with a disability, means that the individual satisfies the requisite skill, experience, education and other job-related requirements of the employment position such individual holds or desires and, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of such position. See § 1630.3 for exception to this definition.

Section 1630.3 simply repeats the statutory exception for individuals currently involved in illegal drug usage and other exceptions, such as transvestitism, homosexuality, etc.

The change in language does not represent a change in the applicable law. An individual with a disability must establish that he or she meets the minimum skill, education, experience and other job-related requirements. This is akin to the requirement that a Title VII complainant establish that he or she was qualified for the position as part of a prima facie case of discrimination. It is also often referred to as a requirement that an individual with a disability establish that he or she is "otherwise qualified." If the individual does meet the minimum qualifications for the position, a complainant must also establish that he or she can perform the essential functions of the position, with or without reasonable accommodation. The ADAAA does not change these requirements.

An individual who does not meet the basic requirements of the position is not a qualified individual with a disability. For example, in Hovell v. Secretary of Navy, 0120060091 (2007), the Commission found that the agency had not failed to accommodate the complainant by allowing him leave for treatment of depression. The Commission determined that the complainant, an attorney who had been disbarred, was not qualified for the position of a JAG attorney and, therefore, was not entitled to accommodation.

In some instances, showing the existence of a disabling condition that substantially limits a major life activity is relatively easy. (In the Reid case, the appellant had a prosthesis in place of his right eye.) Showing that the employee can perform the essential duties of the position, with or without accommodation, can be much more difficult. For example, an employee who takes the position that he is totally disabled and will be absent from his duties indefinitely fails to meet the requirement. See Sandoval v. Department of Air Force, 03820068 (1983). An employee who is a 30% disabled veteran will not necessarily meet the prima facie requirements of demonstrating that he is a qualified individual with a disability where the record contains no evidence of the nature of the disability or whether it substantially impairs major life activities. See Schmidt v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01832032 (1984).

An employee is not a qualified individual with a disability when his physical limitation prevents him from performing the essential duties of the position safely and efficiently. See Cazenave v. Postmaster General, 03880050 (1988). In that case, the record established that an employee must be able to use both hands to perform the essential duties of a distribution clerk. Although the complainant's physician testified at the hearing that the complainant could perform the duties, the physician did not explain the discrepancy between his testimony and an earlier medical report indicating the condition was permanent. Likewise, in Malpass v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05920527 (1992), the Commission determined that the complainant was not a qualified individual with a disability where his doctor testified that he could not deal with stress, was unable to work, and should seek disability retirement or risk not recovering from his psychiatric illness.

The definition of a qualified individual with a disability is "not synonymous with the definition of an invalid." Rhodes v. USPS, 01841544 (1986). That the individual can undergo a strenuous physical exercise routine does not mean he cannot be a qualified individual with a disability within the meaning of the regulations.

The employee is not required to establish that she is a qualified employee with a disability within the meaning of the Act until the investigation stage of the complaint. An agency errs in refusing to investigate a complaint on the basis that the employee has not shown she is a qualified person with a disability. Thompson v. Department of Treasury, 01861847 (1986). Dismissing a complaint of disability discrimination on this basis prior to investigation is tantamount to a decision on the merits--a decision that can be made only after investigation.
