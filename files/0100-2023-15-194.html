A. DECISION OF ADMINISTRATIVE JUDGE

Under 29 CFR 1614.109(i), the administrative judge will issue a decision on the complaint within 180 days from receipt of the complaint file from the agency. If good cause exists, the 180-day time period may be extended. Id. The failure of the administrative judge to issue a decision within the 180-day time frame constitutes neither a basis for a judgment in favor of the complainant nor does it warrant any other adverse action against the agency on the merits of the complaint. See Graham v. Attorney General, 01A12649 (2002).

Under the 1992 version of Part 1614, at 29 CFR 1614.109(g) (1992), the administrative judge made only a recommended decision which the agency could either adopt, reject or modify:

Findings and conclusions. Unless the administrative judge makes a written determination that good cause exists for extending the time for issuing findings of fact and conclusions of law, within 180 days of a request for a hearing being received by EEOC, an administrative judge shall issue findings of fact and conclusions of law on the merits of the complaint, and shall order appropriate relief where discrimination is found with regard to the matter that gave rise to the complaint. The administrative judge shall send copies of the entire record, including the transcript, and the findings and conclusions to the parties by certified mail, return receipt requested. Within 60 days of receipt of the findings and conclusions, the agency may reject or modify the findings and conclusions or the relief ordered by the administrative judge and issue a final decision in accordance with § 1614.110. If an agency does not, within 60 days of receipt, reject or modify the findings and conclusions of the administrative judge, then the findings and conclusions of the administrative judge and the relief ordered shall become the final decision of the agency and the agency shall notify the complainant of the final decision in accordance with § 1614.110.

In a notice of proposed rulemaking, 63 Fed. Reg. 8557 (1998), the Commission unveiled its plans to eliminate the recommended decision and give its administrative judges the authority to make final decisions, subject to appeal to OFO by either party. The change was long overdue. Particularly with the creation of the right to a jury trial under the 1991 Civil Rights Act, more and more complainants had simply abandoned the EEO administrative process, with its recommended decisions, in favor of federal district court. Those that remained in the process ended up feeling it to be a mockery when an agency simply rejected any recommended decision with a finding of discrimination. Finally, the Commission acknowledged this "unavoidable conflict of interest" in allowing agencies to accept, reject, or modify the decisions of its administrative judges.

The proposed changes to Part 1614, relating to the authority of administrative judges, generated much agency opposition. From a practical perspective, there was no good argument against taking final decisionmaking authority away from the agencies involved. However, from a legal perspective, agencies were able to make a reasonable argument that the Commission lacked the authority to make the change.

As a result, the Commission ultimately resolved the problem by allowing agencies to retain final action authority but requiring an agency that rejects the decision of an administrative judge to simultaneously file an appeal with OFO. In effect, the Commission has shifted the burden of proof to agencies to justify rejection of an administrative judge's decision.

In order to ensure the parties are aware when a decision triggers appeals rights, the Judges' Handbook at 8-1 provides:

Decisions which trigger an agency's obligation to take final action and permit parties to file an appeal are decisions concluding all phases of the hearing process, issued pursuant to the following regulations: 29 C.F.R. §§ 1614.109(b); 109(g); 109(i) [for complaints from individual employees or applicants for employment] and 29 C.F.R. §§ 1614.204(d)(2); 204(d)(7) [for class complaints]. Except for three special situations in class complaint cases discussed below, it is only the decision concluding all phases of the hearing process that triggers the agency's obligation to take final action and permits parties to file an appeal.

In order to ensure that the agency is aware that it is required to take final action as well as to advise the parties of their appeal rights following an Administrative Judge decision in the above situations, it is imperative for the Administrative Judge to clearly state whether and when his/her decision triggers appeal rights and to attach a Notice To The Parties to that decision. See Appendix H.

In the absence of evidence of the agency's receipt of the judge's decision, the EEOC will adopt a presumption that the decision was received five days following its issuance. A final disposition issued after the time limit is a nullity and the agency will be bound by the decision of the administrative judge. Rich v. USPS, 01830560 (1985). Although Rich was decided under the previous version of Part 1614, its holding has been incorporated into the Commission's regulations at 29 Part 1614.
