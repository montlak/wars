broida eeo Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal, Subchapter 03: Bases for Dismissal of Complaints, Section E: Failure to State a Claim: Investigations and Other Proceedings--Collateral Attacks, Subsection 01: Arbitration Awards and the Grievance Process

1. Arbitration Awards and the Grievance Process

Generally speaking, the Commission will not accept jurisdiction over claims that arise out of grievance or arbitration proceedings. For example, in Hutchins v. Postmaster General, 01A53070 (2005), the Commission found an allegation that the outcome of grievance was discriminatory was a collateral attack on the grievance procedure. However, when a complainant alleges his reassignment is discriminatory and the agency contends the reassignment was the result of a grievance settlement not involving the complainant this is not a collateral attack on the grievance procedure. In Ware v. Postmaster General, 01A53452 (2005), the Commission noted that the complainant was not a party to the grievance that resulted in settlement, nor was there any specific language in the agreement that required the complainant's reassignment. The agency's reliance on the settlement agreement related to the merits of the claim and not whether a claim had been stated.

The Commission has held that a complaint to enforce an arbitration award is a collateral attack on the grievance process and should be dismissed for failure to state a claim. See Webster v. Postmaster General, 01A11827 (2001). A complaint that a labor relations specialist altered a document relating to an EEO complaint is a collateral attack on the EEO investigation and should be addressed at the hearing before the administrative judge or on appeal before the Commission. Council v. Postmaster General, 01996156 (2001). However, because Postal Service employees are not precluded by statute from filing an EEO complaint and a grievance over the same action, an EEO complaint over a removal is not a collateral attack on a grievance proceeding even when the grievance proceeding has upheld the removal. See Applewhite v. Postmaster General, 01A50965 (2005). Similarly, a claim that the complainant is discriminatorily denied a position is not a collateral attack on the grievance process when the position is given to another employee as a grievance settlement. The complainant was not a party to the underlying grievance proceeding. Blunk v. Postmaster General, 0120064368 (2006).

However, in Archibald v. Postmaster General, 0120131678 (2013), the Commission found that the agency erred in dismissing a complaint that the reinstatement of an employee through the grievance procedure was a collateral attack on the proceeding where the employee had been terminated for having racist materials in her workspace. The complainant alleged that the return of the employee created a hostile work environment. See also Brewer v. Postmaster General, 0120131786 (2013).
