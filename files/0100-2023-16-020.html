V. ENFORCEMENT OF FINAL AGENCY DECISIONS

Agencies are required to comply with their own final decisions on discrimination complaints when no appeal is filed. The Commission's former regulations, while providing for enforcement of its own decisions, did not specifically address the enforcement of final agency decisions.

Under Part 1613, when an agency failed to comply with its own decision on a complaint of discrimination, the proper course was a direct appeal to the Commission. The Commission reviewed such appeals on a case-by-case basis to determine if they were timely. Dolmage v. Postmaster General, 05910897 (1992). In Dolmage, the Commission noted:

In the case before us, appellant sought to compel the agency to comply with the remedial action granted in the final agency decision finding handicap discrimination. According to recent Commission precedent, where a finding of discrimination has been reached by a federal agency, the EEOC retains jurisdiction over any remedial issues arising from such a finding, including the matter of the agency's compliance with the remedy granted in its final decision. Jelks v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910608 (September 11, 1991). Therefore, where a complainant concludes that the agency has not complied with the remedy ordered in a final agency decision, the appropriate course of action lies in a direct appeal to the EEOC, Office of Federal Operations, pursuant to 29 CFR 1613.231(a)(3). Id.

The timeliness of such an appeal is determined on a case-by-case basis, according to the specific factual circumstances before the Commission. Jelks v. United States Postal Service, supra (citing Maczko v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05890376 (January 4, 1990)). The proper inquiry on appeal is, following a final agency determination as to the relief owed a complainant, whether the individual has "acted in a manner which demonstrates a prudent regard for his or her rights." Id. at 4. Put another way, the issue of timeliness will center on whether the individual seeking to enforce a remedy granted in a final agency decision carefully pursued the desired remedy within a reasonable time period.

In this case, the Commission's previous decision properly found that there was no existing order that could be the subject of a petition for enforcement. The previous decision treated appellant's May 13, 1991 letter as an appeal, but found it untimely. In doing so, the previous decision recognized that there may be times when an appellant may challenge the specifics of corrective action acknowledged by a federal agency following the issuance of a final agency decision with a finding of discrimination and a general statement of relief. At the time the previous decision was written, however, the Commission's decision in Jelks had not yet been issued.

Applying Jelks to the facts of this case, we find that the appeal filed by appellant was untimely. After some negotiations between the parties between June and November 1988, the agency sent appellant a November 2, 1988 letter which concluded that appellant was not entitled to out-of-schedule premium pay and that the agency would not provide this requested relief. It is at this point when the time period for an appeal of the premium pay issue began running. Appellant, through her attorney, and the agency continued to discuss the issue, however, through a series of letters sent between November 1988 and November 1989. Because appellant continued to pursue her rights and, more important, because the agency did not notify appellant that she could appeal to the EEOC the agency's refusal to award the requested relief, the time period between November 1988 and November 1989 was tolled.

In a November 17, 1989 letter issued to appellant's attorney, the agency again notified appellant that it would not award her out-of-schedule premium pay. Appellant took no further action until November 20, 1990 when she sent a petition for enforcement to the Los Angeles District Office of the EEOC. We observe that at all relevant times, appellant was represented by counsel. We therefore determine that appellant did not act in a manner demonstrating a prudent regard for her right to challenge the premium pay issue following receipt of the November 17, 1989 letter. Under the circumstances, the delay of one year was not a reasonable time in which to appeal the agency's refusal to grant appellant the relief she desired. No explanation for the delay is provided by appellant. For this reason, then, the action taken by appellant in November 1990 came too late and must be regarded as an untimely appeal.

Under Part 1613, when an agency failed to comply with its own decision, the Commission considered the situation as analogous to an agency's failure to comply with terms of a settlement agreement. As such, the Commission required use of the procedure for enforcement that was set forth in 29 CFR 1613.217(b). Ricks v. Department of Justice, 01880039 (1988). The complainant was required to notify the agency EEO director in writing, by certified mail, return receipt requested. If the agency did not respond in 30 days or gave a response that was unsatisfactory to the complainant, an appeal could be filed with OFO.

The result was that complainants were left guessing as to the proper procedures and time limits for enforcement of final actions. This has been remedied in the new regulations. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.504:

(a) Any settlement agreement knowingly and voluntarily agreed to by the parties, reached at any stage of the complaint process, shall be binding on both parties. Final action that has not been the subject of an appeal or civil action shall be binding on the agency. If the complainant believes that the agency has failed to comply with the terms of a settlement agreement or final decision, the complainant shall notify the EEO Director, in writing, of the alleged noncompliance within 30 days of when the complainant knew or should have known of the alleged noncompliance. The complainant may request that the terms of settlement agreement be specifically implemented or, alternatively, that the complaint be reinstated for further processing from the point processing ceased.

(b) The agency shall resolve the matter and respond to the complainant, in writing. If the agency has not responded to the complainant, in writing, or if the complainant is not satisfied with the agency's attempt to resolve the matter, the complainant may appeal to the Commission for a determination as to whether the agency has complied with the terms of the settlement agreement or final decision. The complainant may file such an appeal 35 days after he or she has served the agency with the allegations of noncompliance, but must file an appeal within 30 days of his or her receipt of an agency's determination. The complainant must serve a copy of the appeal on the agency and the agency may submit a response to the Commission within 30 days of receiving notice of the appeal.

(c) Prior to rendering its determination, the Commission may request that parties submit whatever additional information or documentation it deems necessary or may direct that an investigation or hearing on the matter be conducted. If the Commission determines that the agency is not in compliance and the noncompliance is not attributable to acts or conduct of the complainant, it may order such compliance or it may order that the complaint be reinstated for further processing from the point processing ceased. Allegations that subsequent acts of discrimination violate a settlement agreement shall be processed as separate complaints under § 1614.106 or § 1614.204, as appropriate, rather than under this section.

Although Part 1614 does not significantly alter the enforcement process, it does represent an improvement in that it clarified the process for seeking enforcement of a final agency decision.

Generally, an agency is required to document the actions it has taken to comply with a Commission decision. However, if the record in an enforcement action does not permit the Commission to determine if compliance has been achieved, remand for development of the record is appropriate. Clark v. Postmaster General, 01A15142 (2002).
