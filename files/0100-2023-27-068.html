1. Midyear Reviews

Many agencies give employees midyear reviews in addition to annual performance appraisals. The reviews, though not official performance evaluations, are intended to give employees an idea of their performance to date. Often, such reviews are used in making the final annual performance appraisal of the employee.

In Monagle v. USPS, 01881232 (1988), the Commission reversed an agency decision dismissing a complaint over a midyear review on the basis that the employee was not "aggrieved" within the meaning of Title VII. The agency argued that the "review was merely a progress report, no adjective rating was given and at best the review could only be considered a proposal." Id. The Commission found the employee was "aggrieved" because she had alleged that her subsequent annual appraisal and denial of a step increase in pay was based in part upon the midyear review. The Commission made no comment on whether an employee could be aggrieved by the midyear review alone, absent allegations of subsequent formal action by the agency.

Subsequently, the Commission has held that a midyear performance review used for any purpose other than discussion with the employee can sufficiently aggrieve a complainant to serve as the basis for a complaint. See Armitage v. Secretary of the Navy, 01910879 (1991). If the performance review is communicated to a third party, then there is sufficient harm to the employee to give rise to a complaint.

The Commission's prohibition against processing complaints over proposed actions, see Chapter 3, subheading "Proposed Actions," has confused its position over midyear performance, or progress, reviews. The general rule is that such reviews do not sufficiently aggrieve the employee to confer standing for a complainant. However, Armitage is still good law because the harm consisted of communicating the results of a review--an act that was independent of the review itself.

Moreover, there is an exception to the general rule when the employee alleges the proposed action is taken for the purpose of illegal harassment. In Spencer v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01941059 (1994), the Commission explained:

Appellant's complaint shows that appellant is alleging in allegation 6 that he was subject to a harassing progress review on February 26, 1993. Therefore, the claim can not be dismissed under § 1614.107(e). Furthermore, we find that such an allegation of harassment renders appellant aggrieved and states a claim under § 1614.103. The agency's reliance on Mosley v. General Services Admin., EEOC Request No. 05900103 (Mar. 29, 1990), is misplaced. Although the Commission found in Mosley that an appellant was not aggrieved by a semi-annual review, there was no indication, as there is in this case, that the appellant in Mosley had alleged that the progress review was done for the purpose of harassment. Therefore, we find that the agency improperly dismissed allegation 6 for failing to state a claim.

See also McSween v. Secretary of Navy, 01983107 (1999). Similarly, a midyear review is a sufficient injury when the complainant alleges that retaliation is the basis of discrimination because of the potential chilling effect of such reviews. See Talley v. Nuclear Regulatory Commission, 0120110737 (2012); Sylvain v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A14546 (2003). This is true even when the final evaluation is upgraded to fully successful and the employee is given the attendant step increase. See Porter v. Secretary of Navy, 07A20080 (2003).

But, the fact that the employee receives the same year-end rating does not bring the midyear review to the level of causing injury. In Ogert v. Secretary of Navy, 01955447 (1996), the Commission found:

[A]ppellant's complaint was properly dismissed as a proposal to take a personnel action. The record, the informal complaint and the EEO Counselor's report and Notice of Final Interview, reveal only the allegation of a mid-year review and a recommendation for future actions. Although appellant received the same rating at the year's end, no additional evidence of comparatives or effects was presented and appellant's present complaint does not challenge the final rating. This failure to demonstrate any permanence to the review and any significance to it, results in a failure to allege a direct and personal harm or aggrievement. Consequently, this complaint must be dismissed. See generally Diaz v. Dept. of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994).

However, when the midyear review becomes part of the final annual evaluation, it does aggrieve the employee. See Complainant v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120151860 (2015).

For a more detailed explanation of the Commission's position on proposed actions, see Chapter 3, section on "Standing: Failure to State a Claim" under the subheading "Proposed Actions."
