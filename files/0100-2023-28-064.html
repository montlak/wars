D. REPRISAL

The basis of reprisal is not included in the Commission's regulations regarding class complaints. 29 CFR 1614.204(a). Therefore, a plain reading of the regulations would indicate that a class complaint could not be based on reprisal. The regulations only apply to class complaints alleging discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, or disability.

In the past, the Commission issued conflicting decisions on the issue. In Levitoff v. Secretary of Agriculture, 05920601 (1992), the Commission held that reprisal could form the basis of a class complaint. But, the Commission also held that an agency properly rejects a class complaint alleging reprisal as beyond EEO purview. See Bumpus v. Postmaster General, 01930049 (1993).

The issue now seems resolved. In Alston v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01971752 (1999), the Commission found that a reprisal claim can form the basis of a class complaint. Though, it also highlighted a problem in such complaints; namely, demonstrating the typicality of claims. In Alston, the Commission held:

Here, the RD mistakenly states that generally, a claim of reprisal is not within the purview of a class complaint because reprisal is, by definition, individualized. As noted by the parties, there is case law holding that retaliation claims may be the subject of class actions where the plaintiffs establish a general practice of retaliation against employees who oppose discriminatory practices or exercise rights protected under Title VII. See Holsey v. Armour & Co., 743 F.2d 199, 216-17 (4th Cir. 1984), cert. denied, 470 U.S. 1028 (1985). The Commission also has held that reprisal is an appropriate basis for a class complaint when there is a showing that specific reprisal actions were taken against a group of people for challenging agency policies, or where reprisal was routinely visited on class members. Levitoff v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01913685 (March 17, 1992), request to reopen denied, EEOC Request No. 05920601 (September 10, 1992); George, supra. See also Howard v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Appeal No. 01956455 (June 4, 1997); request to reopen denied, EEOC Request No. 05970855 October 24, 1997) (remanding a class complaint alleging racial and reprisal discrimination in the areas of training, promotions, performance awards, performance evaluations and work assignments/environment for a determination of whether it meets the standards under an "across-the-board" theory).

In the present case, the Class Agents alleged that because they opposed the agency's discriminatory practices, the agency has retaliated against them by: denying promotions; subjecting them to unwarranted discipline; denying them the benefits of settlement agreements entered into in District Court; and ensuring their EEO complaints are dismissed by misrepresenting matters before EEOC Administrative Judges. The record does not reflect that any particular official was involved in more than one of the challenged actions and the issues raised by the Class Agents are so diverse that we are not persuaded that they constitute a showing of a specific agency policy or practice affecting the class. Rather, the Class Agents merely assert, in a conclusory manner, that the Hospital's "upper level" management colludes to retaliate against employees at or below the first-level supervisory level who engage in protected EEO activity.

After carefully evaluating all the evidence in the record, we find that the Class Agent has failed to meet the class complaint prerequisites of commonality and typicality. We therefore find that the AJ properly declined to certify the proposed class. Accordingly, it is the decision of the Commission to AFFIRM the FAD rejecting the proposed class certification.

See also Grigsby v. Postmaster General, 01A01516 (2002) ("The Commission has held that reprisal is an appropriate basis for a class complaint when there is a showing that specific reprisal actions were taken against a group of people for challenging agency policies, or where reprisal was routinely visited on class members."); Neufeld v. Postmaster General, 01A11916 (2001) (Administrative judge erred by finding that a reprisal cannot form the basis of a class complaint); Powell v. Secretary of Navy, 01974349 (2000) (same).
