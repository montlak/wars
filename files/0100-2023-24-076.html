F. BUILDING AND WORKSPACE ACCOMMODATIONS

One form of accommodation is modifications to a building or workspace. Because it is not always possible to accurately predict if a building or workspace modification will be successful in permitting an individual with a disability to perform the essential functions of a position, the test is whether it is "plausible" that the modification would allow the individual to perform the essential job functions. As the Commission explained in Yessenia H. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720070027 (2015):

One category of reasonable accommodation is a modification or adjustment to the work environment that enables a qualified individual with a disability to perform the essential functions of that position. Enforcement Guidance at 2. A modification or adjustment is "reasonable" if it "seems reasonable on its face, i.e., ordinarily or in the run of cases"; this means it is "reasonable" if it appears to be "feasible" or "plausible." Id. at 4. An accommodation also must be "effective" in meeting the needs of the individual: the accommodation must remove a workplace barrier, thereby providing the individual with an equal opportunity to apply for a position, to perform the essential functions of a position, or to gain equal access to a benefit or privilege of employment. Id. at 4-5, 18.

An agency may not refuse to hire or to promote an employee with a disability because of the inaccessibility of the agency facility. See 29 CFR 1630.2(o)(2)(i). Buildings are deemed accessible if they are in full compliance with the Architectural Barriers Act of 1968 and the Americans with Disabilities Act.

Not only must the building facilities where the complainant works be accessible, but facilities to which the complainant is sent for work, such as training facilities, must also be accessible. See Burchfield v. Secretary of Treasury, 01970152 (2000) (agency failed to reasonably accommodate complainant where it failed to inform him that training facilities did not have accessible bathrooms).

Allowing a complainant the flexibility to work at home because the agency's restroom facilities are inaccessible is not a reasonable accommodation. The bottom line, according to the Commission, was that the complainant was not accommodated on those occasions when he did work at the agency facility. Conner v. Secretary of Navy, 01996212 (2002).

Not all building accommodation cases involve alterations to a building. A common building accommodation is the provision of a parking space. Providing a qualified individual with a disability with a parking space close to a facility is a form of reasonable accommodation. See, e.g., Wheeler v. Secretary of Army, 01A30318 (2004).

There is a distinction between the Architectural Barriers Act and reasonable accommodation of an employee. Although the Architectural Barriers Act prescribes the number and location of handicapped parking spaces for the public, the provision of such spaces for employees with a mobility disability is a requirement of the Rehabilitation Act. In Thurman v. Postmaster General, 0120112902 (2012), the Commission found that the agency erred in dismissing a complaint because it was in compliance with the Architectural Barriers Act:

In its decision, the Agency reasoned that Complainant failed to state a claim because he was not put off the clock and suffered no other harm. Additionally, the Agency stated "the number and placement of handicapped parking spaces at one of our postal facilities is not an issue that can be accepted, since the number and placement of handicapped parking spaces is governed by the Architectural Barriers Act, not employment discrimination statutes (Title VII and/or Rehabilitation Act)."

The Commission disagrees. In the instant case, Complainant is alleging that the Agency is failing to provide him with reasonable accommodation in the form of a parking space designated for individuals with disabilities. Complainant clearly states that he wants to "park his car in any handicap parking space that is available when reporting to work." In doing so, Complainant is alleging a viable claim under the Rehabilitation Act, which places different requirements on the Agency than the Architectural Barriers Act. Moreover, by asserting that it is acting in compliance with the Architectural Barriers Act, the Agency is addressing the merits of Complainant's complaint without a proper investigation as required by the regulations. This is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether he has stated a justiciable claim under the Rehabilitation Act. See Osborne v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05960111 (July 19, 1996); Lee v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930220 (August 12, 1993); Ferrazzoli v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991). Complainant has alleged an injury or harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy.
