F. RESCINDING FINAL AGENCY DECISION

The archaic EEO process that, at times, leaves processing of the complaint to the employing agency and, at other times, leaves processing of the complaint to the EEOC, leaves itself open to jurisdictional questions. One of the jurisdictional questions relates to whether an agency can rescind a final decision after an appeal is filed and issue a new final agency decision. When the original final agency decision contains a finding of discrimination, it cannot be rescinded. See, e.g., Handy v. Secretary of Transportation, 01933298 (1993).

When the final agency decision does not contain a finding of discrimination, it is within the discretion of the EEOC to permit an agency to rescind a final decision for the purposes of supplemental investigation. In Peace-Jackson v. Secretary of Labor, 05950754 (1997), the employee filed an appeal with the Commission complaining of several defects in the agency's investigation. Perhaps sensing that the Commission would remand the case for further investigation, the agency purported to rescind its decision. The Commission, after initially upholding the right of the agency to rescind the decision and on reconsideration in Peace-Jackson held:

Appellant filed a complaint alleging race, sex, and reprisal discrimination in her receipt of a rating of "acceptable" on her 1990-91 performance appraisal and a continuing pattern of discriminatory treatment. After conducting an investigation, after which appellant failed to request a hearing, the agency issued a final agency decision (FAD) finding no discrimination. Appellant appealed this decision to the Commission, alleging numerous deficiencies in the agency's investigation, including, inter alia, that the agency investigator had failed to interview those supervisors who had supervisory oversight over 29 of the 31 assignments she worked on during the rating period.

While the appeal was pending, the agency rescinded its FAD based upon appellant's concerns and requested a supplemental investigation of appellant's complaint. The agency did not fully elaborate upon the specific deficiencies in the record that it would be correcting during the supplemental investigation. The agency indicated that after the supplemental investigation was completed, appellant would be provided with a copy of the investigative report and be given a final agency decision with appeal rights to the Commission. The previous decision dismissed appellant's appeal as premature, finding that there was no evidence that any rights of appellant's had been extinguished by the agency's action. The decision noted that there was nothing in the Commission's regulations which prohibited the agency's rescission of its final agency decision and that the agency stated in its rescission letter that it would conduct a supplemental investigation focusing on matters of primary concern to appellant. In support of the dismissal, the decision cited the Commission's ruling in Martin v. Dept. of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05930284 (August 12, 1993).

. . .

Appellant asserts in her request that the previous decision cites insufficient authority for its ruling and affords agencies unlimited power to rescind a final decision and restart the complaint process, even when there is no legitimate basis for doing so. Upon careful review of appellant's arguments, the Commission finds that the previous decision erroneously failed to clarify that once an appeal to the Commission has been taken, the Commission's jurisdiction over that appeal affords it discretion over whether to permit the rescission of an agency's final decision. The agency does not, as appellant apparently fears and the agency appears to argue, have the power to abrogate at will, the Commission's jurisdiction over appeals pending before it. Cf Warren R. Johnson v. Environmental Protection Agency, EEOC Request No. 05950140 (March 26, 1996) (Commission advised agency that it could not properly include allegations pending under the appellate jurisdiction of the Commission in an FAD dismissing the remainder of the appellant's complaint).

While the Commission has previously permitted the rescission of FADS in cases where such rescission is an appropriate action beneficial to both parties, appellant is advised that the Commission clearly reviews such rescissions on a case by case basis to avoid inequitable or prejudicial results. For example, the Commission does not permit such rescission in cases where the agency has issued a finding of discrimination on the merits or otherwise admitted discrimination. The Commission clearly has not and will not permit the type of bad faith actions which appellant claims she fears will occur. Moreover, the Commission has fully reviewed the circumstances involved herein and finds that while some delay has already occurred in processing, there is insufficient evidence to demonstrate that bad faith motivated the agency's rescission of its FAD.

The Commission in Peace-Jackson remanded the case for a supplemental investigation with specific instructions to the agency:

Further, we have reviewed the record herein and find that it contains numerous deficiencies which can best be remedied through a remand for the supplemental investigation previously requested by appellant. We note, however, that this remand is necessitated by the agency's failure to properly investigate this matter at the outset. Therefore, any further delays caused by agency omissions will not be looked upon favorably by this Commission. Our review of the record herein indicates that it contains several specific deficiencies. First, we observe that the agency never investigated or specifically rejected appellant's repeated allegation that the performance appraisal was the latest event in a pattern of discriminatory and retaliatory treatment. In her investigative affidavit, appellant specifically asserted that she was subjected to sex, race and reprisal discrimination from February 1988 until her receipt of the 1990-91 performance appraisal when agency officials: assigned her an inordinately heavy workload; demeaned and discredited her job performance; held her to subjective, strict and variable work standards; and denied her recognition through awards.

In view of its prolonged delay thus far in specifically addressing this allegation in any manner, the agency is ordered to include the allegation in its investigation on remand. Next, with regard to the performance appraisal issue, we note that the record does not contain affidavits from the attorneys who assigned work to appellant and provided the input into appellant's evaluation which supported the designated rating. Next, we note that the evaluations of the other attorneys, who were rated by the same individuals, are not included in the investigation. Appellant also correctly notes that neither one of the supervisors who provided affidavits during the investigation were asked whether they were aware of appellant's prior EEO complaint at the time appellant's rating was assigned. In view of these glaring deficiencies in the record pointed out by appellant on appeal, we agree with the previous decision's assessment that remand for a supplemental investigation in this case is appropriate. However, the agency is advised that, in view of the processing delays and investigative deficiencies it has already acknowledged, it is vital that the supplemental investigation be completed in a prompt and complete manner. To that end, we will issue an Order which specifically sets forth the investigative deficiencies to be rectified on remand and imposes a strict 90-day time period in which to conduct the supplemental investigation and issue the subject FAD.

Of course, nothing prevents an agency from amending a final decision prior to the time an appeal is filed. The Commission does not have jurisdiction over the case unless and until an appeal is filed. When the agency does amend a final decision, it restarts the time period for the filing of an appeal if the amendment is substantive. In Van Wersch v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05980219 (1998), the Commission found the appeal was timely filed where the agency amended its final decision changing the allegations that would be accepted for investigation and those dismissed. The Commission found that such an amendment was substantive and "reset" the time period for filing an appeal.

In Patrick v. Social Security Administration, 01954500 (1998), the Commission reaffirmed the Handy doctrine that once an agency reaches a finding of discrimination, the complainant is entitled to a remedy as a matter of law. The Commission applied the doctrine to a finding of breach of a settlement agreement. In Patrick, the Social Security Administration attempted to rescind a final agency decision issued by the Department of Health and Human Services just days before SSA became an independent agency. The Commission, in Patrick, held:

This case is similar to the Handy case in that there is no indication in the record that the appellant has filed an appeal from the Department's March 29, 1995 decision or that she has filed a civil action on this matter. Thus, pursuant to the express language of 29 CFR § 1614.504(a), as interpreted and applied in the Handy decision, the Department's March 29, 1995 final agency decision was binding on the Department.

As discussed above, the agency in this case was the successor in interest to the Department and, thereby, assumed the Department's rights and liabilities. Therefore, the Commission finds that the agency was bound by the Department's final agency decision to the extent to which the Department would have been bound by the decision if it had retained authority over its component agency. For the reasons set forth below, the Commission finds that the agency is bound by the Department's March 29, 1995 breach of settlement agreement finding pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.504(a).

The Commission does not find an express provision in 29 CFR Part 1614 which authorizes an agency to reverse, or to modify significantly, a prior agency finding of liability on the merits of a complainant's breach allegations. See 29 CFR § 1614.504; see also, Rousseau v. Department of Education, EEOC Appeal No. 01920410 (January 21, 1992) (no express authorization in 29 CFR Part 1613 to reverse or modify significantly a final agency decision that had found discrimination).

The Commission also does not find any public policy reason to read into 29 CFR § 1614.504(a) an implied authorization for an agency to issue a final agency decision which reverses a prior agency determination of liability on the merits of a complainant's settlement agreement breach allegations. on the contrary, the Commission observed in the Handy decision that its finding, that the final agency decision was binding on the agency under 29 CFR § 1614.504(a), was consistent with its decisions in prior cases that once an agency makes an admission of discrimination, or a finding of discrimination, a complainant becomes entitled to a remedy as a matter of law. The Commission then concluded in Handy that because the complainant became entitled to a remedy as a matter of law once the agency had made a finding of discrimination, the agency had no authority to "vacate" the final agency decision and to "rescind" its finding of race discrimination. Similarly, the appellant in the instant case became entitled to a remedy for the settlement agreement breach as a matter of law upon the determination of breach by the Department, and the agency had no authority to implicitly or explicitly rescind the breach finding.
