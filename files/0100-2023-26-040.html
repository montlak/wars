C. THREATS TO FILE COMPLAINTS

When a complainant "threatens" to file an EEO complaint, whether it is protected activity may depend on the employee's intent. In Dziak v. USPS, 01842494 (1986), the Commission found:

[T]he Commission does not find that mere threats are equivalent to engaging in a protected activity. See 29 CFR 1613.261. However, the Commission has previously found that the protections of Section 704(a) of Title VII are not limited to those who have participated in EEO proceedings. In Burke v. EEOC, Docket No. 01821756, April 9, 1984, the Commission noted that "nothing in 29 CFR 1613.261 or 29 CFR Part 1613 prohibits the acceptance and investigation of complaints alleging retaliation because of opposition to practices, made unlawful by Title VII." (Emphasis in oriGINAl) The question then becomes whether appellant's threats to file an EEO complaint were "equal to opposition to practices made unlawful by Title VII."

In Dziak, the Commission found the employee's threat to file an EEO complaint was not protected activity because her intent in making the threat was to express resentment at having to take part in remedial training and not to oppose any unlawful employment practice of the agency.

However, in Sellard v. Postmaster General, 01882492 (1988), the Commission found, without reference to Dziak or its intent analysis, that threatening to file an EEO complaint is a protected activity. See Westfall v. Postmaster General, 01922687 (1993) (threat to file complaint is protected activity).

In EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Retaliation and Related Issues, No. 915.004 at II.A.2.e. (2016), the Commission has affirmed its position that threatening to file a complaint is protected opposition. No reference is made to the intent of the employee. Aside from the difficulty in determining the intent of the employee, from a legal perspective, that intent should make no difference as to protected status. The focus in retaliation cases is the intent of the employer. If the employer acts because of an employee's threat to file a complaint, the intent of the employee in making the threat should make no difference, provided there is a good faith basis that an unlawful employment practice has occurred.

When threats to file lawsuits are disruptive to meeting and work flow, the agency does not engage in retaliation when it gives the complainant a warning about such activities and instructs her to stop. In Erickson v. Environmental Protection Agency, 0120102288 (2012), the complainant protested the incompatibility of new software with Dragon software by sending out mass emails and disrupting team meetings with threats of class action lawsuits under the Rehabilitation Act. The Commission, in Erickson, found that the complainant had not engaged in activity that was protected because of the disruptive nature of her activity:

Complainant alleged that she was retaliated against when her supervisor issued her a warning letter and told her to stop sending out mass emails and disrupting team meetings with threats of a class action lawsuit for violations of the Rehabilitation Act. We note that the manner in which an individual protests perceived employment discrimination must be reasonable in order for the anti-retaliation provisions to apply. EEOC Compliance Manual, 8-II(B)(3)(a). Federal courts have found that the requirements of the job and the tolerable limits of conduct in a particular setting must be explored when determining whether an employee's opposition went beyond the scope of protected activity because it interfered with the Agency's legitimate interest in seeing that its employees perform their work well. See Hughes-Moore v. Dep't of Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01862235 (Sep. 16, 1987); Hochstadt v. Worcester Foundation for Experimental Biology, 545 F.2d 222, 233 (1st Cir. 1976) citing McDonnell Douglas, supra. In this context, therefore, the question becomes whether Complainant went 'too far' in her particular employment setting. Hochstadt, 545 F.2d at 231; Hughes-Moore, supra.

Here, when Complainant was not satisfied with management's numerous responses about the new software's compatibility with Dragon, she caused a disruption to the work flow when she repeatedly threatened the Agency with class action lawsuits in numerous emails to a large number of Agency employees and during large team meetings and conference calls. The record reveals that Complainant's frequent threats of lawsuits prevented the team from addressing other issues that needed to be addressed in order for them to get their work done, it inhibited meetings to progress because the team always spent all of their time on this issue, it caused the team's time on this project to be ineffective, and it threatened to delay the entire software project. Complainant's disruptive behavior was not directed at just supervisory officials, but at employees who were not involved in or responsible for the alleged discrimination. In light of the specific facts in this case, Complainant's disruptive behavior cannot be considered reasonable protected activity. Sullivan v. Dep't of Health and Human Services, EEOC Petition No. 03890042 (March 1, 1990) (Because Complainant conducted himself in a manner which adversely impacted the accomplishment of work in his office, the manner of his opposition was unreasonable).

It is also important to note that in the warning letter the supervisor directed Complainant to instead use the EEO process or other official channels to raise her concerns about the software's compliance with the Rehabilitation Act. This indicates that the supervisor did not act with a retaliatory animus and was not trying to dissuade Complainant from pursuing the EEO process.
