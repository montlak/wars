d. Constructive and Imputed Notice of Right to Request a Hearing

The EEOC adopted the policies of constructive and imputed notice, but it does not apply either policy to the notice of hearing. The employee must receive personal notice of his right to a hearing. The agency does not satisfy this requirement by showing that a member of the employee's family signed a certified receipt for the letter containing the notice of hearing. See Rivers v. USPS, 01840259 (1985). Under Part 1614, the agency is required to serve the notice of right to a hearing on the complainant's counsel, if the complainant is represented by an attorney. 29 CFR 1614.605(d). Receipt by the attorney, and not the complainant, is the event that starts the running of the time limit for requesting a hearing. Id.

The Commission previously held that it would presume that the appellant received a notice of his hearing rights when the agency produced a certified mail receipt signed by a person at the appellant's address with the same surname. Taylor v. USPS, 01831849 (1984). The burden was on the employee to rebut the presumption of receipt, which the Commission suggested would require an affidavit from the person signing the mail receipt. However, in Battle v. Postmaster General, 05910441 (1991), the Commission retreated from its previous case law and held that the time period for requesting a hearing did not begin to run until the complainant was in "actual receipt" of the proposed disposition. Presumably, this rule now will apply to the notice of right to request a hearing since proposed dispositions have been eliminated under Part 1614. For example, in Ayers v. Postmaster General, 01980605 (2001), the agency produced evidence that it delivered the complaint file and notice of hearing rights to the complainant's mailbox on August 14th, but the complainant did not file her request for a hearing until September 17th. The request was accepted by the Commission as timely when the complainant produced hotel receipts and other evidence establishing that she had been out of town on union business and did not return home until August 17th.

However, the complainant may not actively avoid the notice of right to a hearing. If the agency demonstrates diligent attempts to deliver the notice, it properly issues a final agency decision when the 30-day period for requesting a hearing expires and the complainant has not responded. See Kaiser v. Postmaster General, 01971995 (2000), request for reconsideration denied, 05980414 (2000). In that case, as proof of service, the agency submitted a certified mail receipt that was returned unclaimed and a private courier receipt that showed actual delivery to the complainant's address. The Commission found the 30-day period began to run as of the date of delivery by the courier.

The agency must keep an accurate record of its mailing of the hearing notice, as well as the complainant's receipt of the notice. In the absence of clear-cut evidence of mailing and receipt, any benefit of the doubt will go to the complainant. Where there is no indication that the complainant was sent a notice of hearing rights, her request for such a notice was deemed a request for a hearing. Church v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01996766 (2002).

The Commission in Williams v. Postmaster General, 01960720 (1997), found that given the circumstances of the case, the complainant's request for a hearing should be deemed timely:

The Commission finds that the agency erred in its conclusion that appellant received his notice of right to request a hearing on July 22, 1995, because the agency's own certified mail receipt indicates that it was not mailed until July 26, 1995. Therefore, it would have been impossible for appellant to have received the notice on July 22. The evidence does establish, however, that appellant's request for a hearing was postmarked on August 29, 1995, thirty four days after the agency mailed its notice on July 26. The Commission finds that, in light of the absence of reliable evidence of appellant's receipt of the notice and the fact that his request for a hearing was postmarked only thirty-four days after the notice was mailed to him, his request for a hearing should be deemed timely filed.

The implementing regulations for Title VII are to be construed liberally in favor of layperson complainants and an employee who files an "appeal" requesting a hearing cannot be denied her "day in court" due to a technical error. Patterson v. USPS, 01832859 (1984).
