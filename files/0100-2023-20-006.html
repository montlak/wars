B. AGENCY'S BURDEN--BUSINESS NECESSITY

Once an employee makes out a prima facie case based on adverse impact, the burden shifts to the agency to show "that any requirement [has]. . .a manifest relation to the employment in question." Mulrooney v. General Service Administration, 01842063 (1986) (citing Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U.S. 424, 91 S. Ct. 849, 28 L. Ed.2d 158 (1971)). To make such a showing, the agency must demonstrate that the criteria is a "business necessity" which is required for the safe and efficient operation of the agency. "The practice must be essential, the purpose compelling." LaPalme, supra (quoting Williams v. Colorado Springs School District, 641 F.2d 835, 842 (10th Cir. 1981)).

In LaPalme, the agency sustained its burden by demonstrating that special problems in the New York region required the addition of experienced postal inspectors. Prior to issuing the directed reassignments, the agency attempted to alleviate the problems by recruiting inspectors for voluntary reassignment. It was not until that effort failed that the agency resorted to involuntary reassignments. The agency then compiled a list of experienced inspectors and had their supervisors rate them. The top 20 were taken from the list, which included only the last name and first initials of the inspectors with no information regarding race and sex.

In Van De Car, supra, the Commission examined whether the agency had a business necessity defense to the complainants' claim that the agency's implementation of the Nurses Pay Act adversely impacted nurses over the age of 40 who were classified as Intermediate Enhanced Qualifications or Assignments (IEQA) nurses. The agency argued that it was not feasible for it to extend the steps of the pay scale for intermediate nurses, because the Pay Act required that it have actual evidence of pay-related problems at the higher end of the scale. However, the Commission found in Van De Car that:

The Secretary of the agency reported to Congress in December 1991 that pay-related reasons were still a significant factor for nurses at all levels leaving the VA and that inadequate pay was the greatest reason for specialized nurses such as nurse anesthetists leaving the VA. The Secretary acknowledged that "senior employees" expressed dissatisfaction with the reduced potential for future earnings, and stated that the agency would study the problem. He also indicated a need for further study of rate range extensions. Therefore, the evidence in the record supported the AJ and established that retention of nurses at the Intermediate level was a problem which provided justification for adjustments to the pay schedule.

An agency's assertion that it conducted a RIF in order to reduce the number of employees in higher age brackets is not a business necessity. It is, in fact, overt evidence that the policy was motivated by discriminatory reasons. Christman v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01790345 (1980).

The 1991 Civil Rights Act, at 42 USC 2000e-2(k)(1)(A)(i), requires the employer to demonstrate that "the challenged practice is job related for the position in question and consistent with business necessity." Under the Act, this demonstration requires the employer to bear the burden of persuasion on that issue.
