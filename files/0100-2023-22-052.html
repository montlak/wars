c. Location of Work

Location of the work performed and supervisory chains will also help determine whether the work is substantially similar. In Stefan C. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120132211 (2017), the Commission considered those factors as well as the complexity of the work performed:

We concur with the AJ's reasoning in the decision finding that Complainant is unable to establish a prima facie case of discrimination under the EPA. A review of the record evidence establishes that OST cannot be seen as a single establishment; therefore, we find that the evidence shows that Complainant and E2 work in two different programs in two different locations, supervised under different management chains. Additionally, the EBSP technology is much more complex than the PSP technology; therefore Complainant and E2 are essentially performing substantially different job duties. The EBSP program and deployment requirements are substantially greater than the PSP branch. The EBSP equipment is integrated into a "networked environment" and is very technical in nature. PSP has more types of technology that do not network together, but that stand alone as a separate screening component. EBSP Explosive Detection System technology is typically configured with multiple computer processors, large rotating gantries, and has multiplexed remote viewing stations with banks of operators. Additionally, the EBSP testing supports a significantly larger program budget, receiving approximately seventy percent of the one billion dollar package given to the Agency, for an overall budget of approximately one billion dollars.

The Commission reached a similar conclusion in Herman F. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120150832 (2017), where it rejected the complainant's evidence of pay disparities between him (based in Dallas) and counterpoints in Laguna Nigel, California and Washington, DC.:

Upon review, we find that the difference in pay was justified by a factor other than sex. Specifically, we note that the Director explained that when the Agency was created in 2003, over 2,000 contracting jobs which had not previously existed suddenly needed to be filled.5 He noted that in the metro Washington, D.C. area, the Agency would have had to compete with every other contracting Agency in the area to fill those positions. The Director explained this meant competing with other Agency components, other civilian federal agencies, the Department of Defense, and private contracting agencies for a small pool of contracting talent. The Director noted this was during a period when budgets and appropriations were tight, which meant that the only way to attract talent was to fill the positions at higher grades. The Director stated this type of competition did not exist in Dallas; therefore, it would not have been necessary to elevate the grade structure to entice individuals into the Dallas positions. Additionally, the Director noted that the Laguna DAD position operates independently without an onsite Assistant Director (a GS-15 position) unlike the Dallas office which would justify the reason the Laguna DAD position was graded at the GS-15 level. We note Complainant does not dispute the Director's statement regarding the need to hire DADs at a higher level when the Agency was created. In addition, we find there is no evidence to suggest that the Agency's initial determination to hire employees in Washington, D.C. at a higher-grade level, was based on anything other than appropriate classification standards. Thus, as a DAD in Dallas, Complainant was paid at the GS-14 level while DADs in Washington, D.C. and Laguna Nigel, California were paid at the GS-15 level.

In Candace C. v. GSA, 0120151046 (2016), the complainant, a student intern, pointed to several coworkers, all in different positions, to establish a claim under the EPA. The Commission, in Candace C., rejected the coworkers as proper comparators because they were not GS-05 student trainees:

Additionally, assuming arguendo Complainant has established a prima facie case of a violation under the EPA, we find that the Agency has shown the difference in pay was justified based on a factor other than sex. The record shows that as an intern Complainant was not assigned one specific set of job duties, but she was moved around to learn the organization's different functions. We find the record shows that any pay difference is justified under a differential based on a factor other than sex--it was based on participation in the Student Intern Program."
