2. Complaints by Alleged Harasser

An alleged harasser does not state a claim within EEO purview by charging that the agency failed to advise him that an EEO complaint had been filed naming him as the offender. Nor does an additional allegation that the complaint amounts to a "character assassination" bring the claim within the EEO purview. In Smith v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01951545 (1995), the Commission held that neither claim established the agency had taken any action against the complainant:

The Commission finds that the agency properly dismissed appellant's sexual harassment and character assassination allegations on the grounds that they failed to state a claim. Appellant challenges the agency's processing of a sexual harassment allegation filed by another employee. We find that appellant's allegations constitute a collateral attack on another EEO matter and that to allow the processing of a complaint regarding the participation of an employee in another EEO complaint would have a chilling effect on the processing of EEO complaints. See Calloway v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01943406 (July 15, 1994). Additionally, we find that there is no remedial action for appellant's complaint, as the agency has no authority to restrain an employee from raising EEO violations through the EEO complaint process. See Sherwood v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01941846 (June 3, 1994).

The Commission further finds that there is no evidence in the record that appellant was harmed by any agency action. The Commission has held that a remark or comment, unaccompanied by concrete action, is not a direct and personal deprivation sufficient to render an individual aggrieved. See Simon v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900866 (October 3, 1990) (a threat to check an employee's personnel files and have her fired was not accompanied by any action to do so, and the Commission found that the employee was not aggrieved. We note that any comments concerning appellant's alleged conduct were not accompanied by any concrete agency action.

The case does not hold that an alleged harasser has no right to file a complaint. Rather, the case stands for the proposition that any such complaint must involve an action taken by the agency. The alleged harasser is not aggrieved for Title VII purposes by the action of the alleged victim in filing a complaint. See also Downey v. Social Security Administration, 05990546 (2001); Joseph v. Secretary of Transportation, 01981447 (1998).

By contrast, in Currin v. Secretary of Treasury, 05960188 (1997), the Commission held that the allegations did state a claim where the complainant maintained that the agency took actions to humiliate and degrade him after a complaint of sexual harassment was filed against him:

The record indicates that allegations 5-8 concern agency actions taken against appellant after another employee filed a sexual harassment complaint against him. In the prior decision, the Commission, citing Rogers v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05940157 (February 24, 1995), noted that allegations arising from an agency's investigation of a complaint of sexual harassment fail to state a claim, since an agency is legally obligated to investigate a complaint of sexual harassment. In his request for reconsideration, appellant argues that the agency treated him in a demeaning and humiliating manner and differently than other employees in similar circumstances.

The Commission finds that the prior decision erred as a matter of law when it dismissed allegations 5-8 on the grounds that they failed to state a claim. The actions that appellant challenges are not a part of the agency's investigation of the other employee's sexual harassment allegation, but likely were taken in an effort to respond promptly to the charge of harassment. Notwithstanding the agency's motivation, these actions were adverse to appellant. In Cerullo v. Department of Defense, EEOC Review No. 05930948 (July 15, 1994), the Commission held that an agency improperly dismissed an appellant's allegation that it had discriminated against him when it transferred him to another position as a result of a sexual harassment complaint filed against him. The Commission noted that the appellant alleged that the agency took a "direct, personal harm which resulted from the co-worker's conduct, as well as agency actions" and that he was required to do no more than that to state a claim. The Cerullo decision stated that the agency's reasons for dismissing the allegation, because it was based on the agency's response to the co-worker's sexual harassment action, "constituted a premature merits determination on the claims presented." As in Cerullo, the prior decision erred when it dismissed allegations 5-8 for reasons relating to the merits of appellant's claim.

The Commission further finds, however, that the agency properly dismissed allegations 5-8 on timeliness grounds. Appellant's complaint indicates that he learned of the agency's restrictions on his right to return to his office or otherwise go onto the 17th floor on March 16, 1994. Appellant's complaint also states that this restriction affected his ability to tutor on government time as he had in the past. The record indicates that appellant initiated EEO Counselor contact on June 3, 1994, seventy-nine days after March 16, 1994. We find appellant's EEO Counselor contact took place far in excess of the forty-five-day requirement set forth in Commission regulations. See 29 CFR § 1614.105(a)(1).

In Metzler v. Postmaster General, 01A34926 (2004), the Commission also found that the inclusion of an allegation that the complainant was falsely accused of sexual harassment, presented as part of a larger hostile environment claim, was valid. The Commission in Metzler found:

A review of the record indicates that complainant has alleged a pattern of harassment. In her formal complaint, complainant states, "a year ago [her] husband called [a named agency official] and told him that [the management employee in question] was harassing [her]." The record also contains a letter from complainant dated February 21, 2003. On appeal, complainant states, through her representative, that she provided this letter to the agency as part of her formal complaint. The February 21, 2003 letter sets forth the following claims: that the employee in question would kick complainant's chair when he walked by her cubicle; on an unspecified date, the employee in question blocked complainant's path to the printer with his body; on an unspecified date, the employee in question, while temporarily serving as complainant's supervisor, told her, "that he was in a position to delete [complainant's] job and that it would be in her best interest to do what he said; "and on an unspecified date, complainant called in sick and the employee in question called complainant three times at home and left a message the last time stating, "I know that you are not sick and you better have a doctor's excuse when you come back to work on Monday." According to complainant, after the agency official engaged in this alleged harassing conduct, the official attempted to caste her in an unfavorable way by accusing her of sexually harassing him. In addition, in the letter from complainant to the Postal Inspectors dated August 18, 2003, after the agency issued its final decision dated August 11, 2003, complainant indicates that the alleged harassment is still continuing. Specifically, complainant states that on August 18, 2003, she "was walking to the break room and [the employee in question] walked past [her] and was staring at [her]." Complainant further states that when she told him to stop staring, he flipped her "the bird."

We find that complainant's claim of being falsely accused of sexual harassment in conjunction with complainant's other claims as stated in her February 21, 2003 and August 18, 2003 letters are sufficient to allege a claim of harassment. By alleging a pattern of harassment, complainant has stated a cognizable claim under the EEOC regulations. See Cervantes v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930303 (November 12, 1993).
