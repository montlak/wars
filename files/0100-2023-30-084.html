15. Resignation or Retirement, Effect of

An employee's resignation during the pendency of a complaint does not per se deprive the Commission of jurisdiction over the complaint. It will have the effect of depriving the Commission of jurisdiction if the resignation deprives the EEOC of the power to order a remedy. In Taylor v. Department of Treasury, 01842878 (1986), the employee alleged that her supervisor engaged in harassment because of a previous EEO complaint. The employee subsequently resigned to accept other employment. Because the complaint did not involve a constructive discharge and the only remedy "would be one which in some way affects [the] relationship" between the employee and the supervisor, the Commission found it had no power to fashion an appropriate remedy.

Relief in the form of a retroactive promotion is not appropriate where the employee retired in the interim between the incident of discrimination and the entrance of a finding of discrimination. The employee is entitled, however, to retroactive compensation from the denial of the promotion to retirement, as well as an adjustment of retirement benefits to reflect any increases the employee would have earned if promoted. Sarter v. USPS, 01841575 (1985). However, in more recent cases, the Commission has held that the retirement of a complainant while litigation is pending is not an automatic bar to reinstatement. For example in Garcia v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0420060026 (2007), the Commission ordered the reinstatement of a complainant who had retired. In that case, involving an enforcement petition, the Commission noted the agency's own final action had ordered reinstatement and it had, therefore, waived any argument that the complainant's retirement precluded reinstatement.

Because the antidiscrimination statutes require make-whole relief, and the Commission has the authority to order that an agency take measures to prevent further discrimination, other remedies also can be granted in cases where an employee has retired. As the Commission further explained in Sarter:

The Commission must proceed to fashion "make whole" relief for the discrimination found herein. A make-whole remedy is defined as all that appellant would have been entitled to had discrimination not occurred. See Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405 (1975).

The record shows that the corrective action to which appellant would normally be entitled based on the instant complaint was substantially affected by subsequent occurrences. In particular, since the time of her complaint, medical information provided by appellant's psychologist shows that appellant, in September 1991, was not able to return to employment with the agency. Appellant's backpay would necessarily be limited to those periods during which she was ready, willing, and able to work at the agency. Additionally, appellant's backpay is cut off either by her subsequent disability retirement, effective February 4, 1992, or her second removal, if effectuated prior to the date of her disability retirement. With regard to reinstatement, we note that appellant (who apparently remains on disability retirement at the time of this appeal) implies in her appeal statement that she no longer desires reinstatement to employment with the agency. As a result of her disability retirement, the remedy ordered herein does not include reinstatement.

When the employee has retired between the discriminatory personnel action and the decision granting a remedy, the agency has an obligation to notify OPM of the remedial actions taken so that OPM can determine the effect, if any, on retirement benefits. For example, in Walker v. Secretary of Army, 04970009 (1998), the Commission ordered the agency to provide relevant information to OPM regarding the petitioner's seniority, retirement, and disability pay:

Petitioner asserts that his seniority, benefits, grade steps, and retirement and disability pay should have been adjusted as well. A review of the documentation submitted by the agency reflects that the agency has awarded appellant grade step increases. Without additional information from petitioner, we are satisfied with the agency's actions in this regard. In regard to petitioner's claims that his seniority and retirement and disability pay have not been properly adjusted, the agency should have submitted all the documentation concerning petitioner's promotion to the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) for a determination of the benefits to which petitioner was entitled. If the documentation has been properly submitted, petitioner's complaint is with OPM, not the agency. Accordingly, the agency must provide certification that it has, in fact, submitted the appropriate documentation to OPM.

See also Complainant v. Social Security Administration, 0420140005 (2014) ("The Agency, however, must provide accurate information to OPM to enable OPM to recalculate Petitioner's service credit and retirement benefits.").
