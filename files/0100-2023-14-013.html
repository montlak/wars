E. EFFECT OF SETTLEMENT

Once a settlement has been achieved, reduced to writing and signed by the parties, all that remains is for the parties to abide by its terms. At that point, the agency should close the case. It is improper for the agency to issue a proposed disposition and final decision finding no discrimination once it has entered into a settlement agreement. See Pardue v. USPS, 01842339 (1986). Such decisions are null and void. See also Pulido v. Secretary of Air Force, 01883584 (1988) (in the absence of a breach of the settlement agreement, the subsequent final agency decision on the merits of the complaint is null and void as a matter of law).

Both agencies and complainants should be diligent in determining that the settlement reflects all the terms actually agreed to and that the settlement does in fact resolve the complaint. In Storms v. USPS, 01873094 (1987), the agency reduced a proposed removal for fighting to a 14-day suspension. The agency subsequently rejected the employee's formal EEO complaint on the ground that he was not an aggrieved employee within the meaning of Title VII. The Commission reinstated the complaint because the settlement agreement did not provide for its withdrawal, and even the reduced penalty was sufficient to constitute a tangible injury to the employee.

Once an agency enters into a settlement agreement, it may not unilaterally impose additional terms on the complainant. In Pham v. Postmaster General, 01960807 (1996), the agency and the appellant signed a settlement agreement which provided:

1. It is agreed that the counselee will provide adequate medical documentation to establish her physical disability.

2. The agency, upon receipt will make a MI-307 evaluation and review for reasonable accommodation.

3. The agency agrees that the counselee's rights under Sec. 1614 of 29 CFR will be honored.

The agency then sought to unilaterally incorporate into the settlement agreement requirements beyond those it to which it had agreed. The Commission found that in doing so, the agency breached the settlement agreement.

Occasionally, it is true that one hand of the agency does not know what the other hand is doing. Such was the case in Lau v. Secretary of Army, 05880740 (1989). In that case, one branch of the agency was negotiating a settlement of an EEO complaint, while another was issuing a final decision. The complainant, while continuing to negotiate with the agency, filed an appeal. The agency's decision was affirmed on appeal. On reconsideration, the Commission reversed its previous decision based on the fact that the agency was still negotiating with the complainant. The complaint was remanded to the agency with instructions to straighten the matter out.

Once the parties sign a settlement agreement the litigation comes to an end. If the settlement is reached at the hearing stage and the parties neglect to inform the administrative judge, the signed agreement still controls. See Escobar v. Postmaster General, 01A05141 (2002) (where the Commission vacated a final agency action implementing the judge's finding of no discrimination where the parties had entered a settlement agreement prior to the judge's decision but neglected to inform the judge).

An agency is bound by its settlement agreement with a complainant, regardless of the facility involved. The fact that the complainant was at a different facility when she settled her complaint does not relieve another facility of the burden of compliance. Roberts v. Secretary of Treasury, 01890666 (1989).

A settlement agreement with an agency binds all divisions of that agency. In Contursi v. Postmaster General, 01955430 (1996), the Commission rejected an argument that the Postal Service had not breached an agreement when the Postal Inspection Service allegedly disclosed that the complainant was previously removed. In remanding Contursi for a determination of whether the Postal Inspection Service actually had made the alleged disclosure, the Commission observed:

In this case, appellant has alleged that the subject agency breached the terms of the settlement agreement by "misinforming" the DSS that he was removed for threatening behavior towards other agency employees. Under the terms of the settlement agreement, appellant agreed to resign from the agency, and the agency agreed that that agreement would serve as its reason for denial if appellant sought reemployment with the agency. In finding that the settlement agreement had not been breached, the agency noted that the Human Resources Department had not informed anyone that appellant had been terminated from the agency and that appellant's personnel folder indicated only that appellant resigned. The agency stated that the Postal Inspection Service was not a party to the settlement agreement and, therefore, was not bound by the terms of the agreement.

We find that the agency's determination that the Postal Inspection Service is not bound by the terms of the settlement agreement was improper. The subject settlement agreement was entered into between appellant and the agency, the United States Postal Service (USPS). Because the Postal Inspection Service is a component of the USPS, it is bound by the terms of the settlement agreement. Appellant has alleged that the agency, i.e., the Postal Inspection Service, breached the settlement agreement by "misinforming" the DSS that he was terminated from the agency. Although the agency determined that the Human Resources Department did not breach the settlement agreement, it did not determine whether the Postal Inspection Service breached the settlement agreement. Therefore, on remand, the agency must determine whether the Postal Inspection Service breached the settlement agreement.

A standard clause in many settlement agreements is that the agreement does not constitute precedent to be used in future cases. Although such agreements are not precedential, that does not bar their use in subsequent proceedings. For example, in DiMiceli v. Postmaster General, 05900709 (1990), the complainant used a previous settlement agreement involving another employee to demonstrate that the agency granted accommodations similar to those sought by the complainant. The Commission found the employee was not barred from using the agreement because the complainant, who was not a party to the agreement, was not bound by it. Also, the Commission found it was not being used for its "precedential" value, but only to establish the viability of the requested accommodations.

The fact that the agency entered into a settlement agreement with another employee over the same selection is not an admission of discrimination against the complainant. Such evidence is not material to the merits of the complainant's case. Brewer v. Secretary of Agriculture, 05920958 (1993).
