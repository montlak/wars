W. REASSIGNMENT, PROMOTIONS, AND DEMOTIONS

To qualify for a reassignment, a complainant must have, at some point, been qualified for the position for which he or she was hired. See Bruce P. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120182117 (2020) ("[B]ecause Complainant has not demonstrated that he could perform the essential functions of the position for which he was hired, we find that he has not established that he is a qualified individual with a disability entitled to a reassignment."); Shelley v. Postmaster General, 0720070076 (2012) (finding that the complainant, a probationary employee, could not establish that he was qualified or entitled to a reassignment because he could not show that he could perform the essential functions of his position for which he was hired). Once that is established, however, a complainant's position of record is no longer determinative as to whether an employee is "qualified." "The term 'qualified,' with respect to an individual with a disability, means that the individual satisfies the requisite skill, experience, education and other job-related requirements of the employment position such individual holds or desires and, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of such position." 29 CFR § 1630.2(m). The term "position" includes positions that the employee could have held as a result of reassignment. In determining whether an employee is "qualified," an agency must look beyond the position which the employee presently encumbers. EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (Enforcement Guidance) No. 915.002 (rev. Oct, 17, 2002); Alana v. Social Security Administration, 0120180037 (2019).

Reassignment is, however, the reasonable accommodation of last choice; that is, an agency is required to exhaust all other reasonable accommodation possibilities prior to considering reassignment. If reassignment is to be considered the burden is on the agency to conduct the job search. Joi J. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120150921 (2017) (rejecting the agency's explanation that the complainant failed to provide evidence that a job search would prove fruitful for its failure to consider the complainant's request for a reassignment). And this is how it should be given that only the agency has access to information regarding vacant positions and those that are likely to soon become vacant. An agency's search obligation with respect to reassignments "is not limited to vacancies within a particular department, facility, or geographical area." Victor S. v. Postmaster General, 0120160739 (2017). Rather, the extent of the agency's search for a vacant position is a question of undue hardship. Id. Absent a showing of undue hardship, an agency is obligated to conduct an agency-wide search for vacant, funded positions that the employee can perform with or without accommodation. Id.; see also Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation at Q. 27.

The complainant has an evidentiary burden in such reassignment cases to establish that it is more likely than not that there were vacancies during the relevant time-period into which he could have been reassigned. This can be established by producing evidence of specific vacancies. See Hampton v. Postmaster General, 01986308 (2002). In the alternative, the complainant need only show that: (1) he/she was qualified to perform a job or jobs which existed at the agency, and (2) there were trends or patterns of turnover in the relevant jobs that made a vacancy likely during the time-period. Id. (complainant can establish that vacant, funded positions existed by producing evidence of particular vacancies or by showing that s/he was qualified to perform a job or jobs that existed at the agency and there were trends or patterns of turnover in the relevant jobs so as to make a vacancy likely during the time period); compare with Cole M. v. Postmaster General, 0120182273 (2020) (finding the investigative record inadequately developed and remanding for evidence related to the agency's efforts to search for a reassignment); Bill A. v. Secretary of the Army, 0120131989 (2016) (during investigative stage of federal administrative process, agency has obligation to develop adequate investigative record and obtain information about availability of vacant, funded positions).

Reassignments are still the reasonable accommodation that has generated the most confusion over the years.

Only employees are entitled to reassignment as a reasonable accommodation. Although an agency is required to provide reasonable accommodation to applicants for employment, reassignment is not a reasonable accommodation for an applicant. See Kane v. Postmaster General, 01972048 (2001).

The issue of an agency's obligation to consider reassignment as an accommodation for a qualified employee with a disability provided the first major battleground between the MSPB and the EEOC, with their overlapping jurisdiction on allegations of discrimination. The issue ultimately was resolved by the Special Panel discussed in Chapter 20 under the subheading "Referral to Special Panel." In the past, the EEOC also found itself at odds with most federal courts on the issue of reassignment. In Ignacio v. USPS, 03840005 (1984), the Commission found that an agency must consider the possibility of reassignment of an employee as part of its reasonable accommodation considerations under the Rehabilitation Act. Ignacio is discussed in detail below.

Just prior to the adoption of Part 1614, the Commission again held that an agency should consider reassignment as a possible accommodation. See, e.g., Day v. Secretary of Army, 03910078 (1991). But an agency was not required to find a position for every employee with a disability who could not be accommodated in his or her current position.

In enacting 29 CFR Part 1614 in 1992, the Commission set forth specific regulations regarding when an agency must consider reassignment of a qualified employee with a disability. The regulations, which reinforced and strengthened the Commission's position on the reassignment of employees with disabilities, shifted emphasis away from reassignment as a form of reasonable accommodation. As explained more fully below, the Commission, in Part 1614, no longer viewed reassignment as a form of reasonable accommodation. Instead, the Commission invoked the affirmative action requirements of 501 of the Rehabilitation Act to require reassignment as a means of meeting the special needs of qualified employees with disabilities.

Things became slightly more confused with the passage of the Rehabilitation Act amendments which made the nonaffirmative action sections of the ADA applicable to federal employees. The ADA specifically requires reassignment as a reasonable accommodation where appropriate. As a result, reassignment has returned from the affirmative action requirements of 501 and is considered under the Act's reasonable accommodation requirements. See Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Types of Reasonable Accommodation Related to Job Performance, Reassignment."

Under its notice of proposed rulemaking issued in March 2000, the Commission suggested changing 29 CFR 1614.203(b)(3) to state, in part, that:

Reasonable accommodation may include reassignment to a vacant position. Reassignment is the reasonable accommodation of last resort and is required only after it is determined either that:

(i) There are no effective accommodations that will enable the employee to perform the essential functions of his/her current position; or

(ii) All other accommodations would impose an under hardship.

In its notice of final rulemaking, the Commission dropped the amendment from Part 1614 and instead relies entirely upon its guidance under the ADA and 29 CFR Part 1630.
