F. RECORD OF DISABILITIES

An employee may fall within the definition of a disabled person if he or she has "a record of such impairment." 42 USC 12102(1)(B). This means that persons who in the past have suffered from a physical or mental disability that did, but does not currently, substantially limit a major life activity are covered under the Rehabilitation Act. The Commission's new regulations also make clear that protection extends not only to those who have a record or history of a disability but also to those who have been misclassified as having a disability. 29 CFR 1630.2(k)(1). To a large degree, coverage for those who have a record of a disability is superfluous to the extent that those same individuals will be considered to have an actual disability under the ADAAA by virtue of the fact that substantially limiting impairments that are in remission are included in the first prong of coverage. See 42 USC 12102(4)(D); 29 CFR 1630.2(j)(1)(vii). Of particular note is the requirement that individuals with a record of a disability are entitled to reasonable accommodation to receive preventive treatment that may be required to keep a past impairment from becoming presently substantially limiting. The Commission's new regulations are 29 CFR 1630.2(k) provide:

In general. An individual has a record of a disability if the individual has a history of, or has been misclassified as having, a mental or physical impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities.

(2) Broad construction. Whether an individual has a record of an impairment that substantially limited a major life activity shall be construed broadly to the maximum extent permitted by the ADA and should not demand extensive analysis. An individual will be considered to have a record of a disability if the individual has a history of an impairment that substantially limited one or more major life activities when compared to most people in the general population, or was misclassified as having had such an impairment. In determining whether an impairment substantially limited a major life activity, the principles articulated in paragraph (j) of this section apply.

(3) Reasonable accommodation. An individual with a record of a substantially limiting impairment may be entitled, absent undue hardship, to a reasonable accommodation if needed and related to the past disability. For example, an employee with an impairment that previously limited, but no longer substantially limits, a major life activity may need leave or a schedule change to permit him or her to attend follow-up or "monitoring" appointments with a health care provider.

In addition to explaining that an individual covered under the record of provision may also have coverage as an individual with an actual disability, the corresponding appendix section to Part 1630 also notes that coverage is provided under the second prong even if the employer is unaware of the past impairment but the complainant would still need to prove that the employer acted on that basis:

The second prong of the definition of "disability" provides that an individual with a record of an impairment that substantially limits or limited a major life activity is an individual with a disability. The intent of this provision, in part, is to ensure that people are not discriminated against because of a history of disability. For example, the "record of" provision would protect an individual who was treated for cancer ten years ago but who is now deemed by a doctor to be free of cancer, from discrimination based on that prior medical history. This provision also ensures that individuals are not discriminated against because they have been misclassified as disabled. For example, individuals misclassified as having learning disabilities or intellectual disabilities (formerly termed "mental retardation") are protected from discrimination on the basis of that erroneous classification. Senate Report at 23; House Labor Report at 52-53; House Judiciary Report at 29; 2008 House Judiciary Report at 7-8 & n.14. Similarly, an employee who in the past was misdiagnosed with bipolar disorder and hospitalized as the result of a temporary reaction to medication she was taking has a record of a substantially limiting impairment, even though she did not actually have bipolar disorder.

This part of the definition is satisfied where evidence establishes that an individual has had a substantially limiting impairment. The impairment indicated in the record must be an impairment that would substantially limit one or more of the individual's major life activities. There are many types of records that could potentially contain this information, including but not limited to, education, medical, or employment records.

Such evidence that an individual has a past history of an impairment that substantially limited a major life activity is all that is necessary to establish coverage under the second prong. An individual may have a "record of" a substantially limiting impairment--and thus be protected under the "record of" prong of the statute--even if a covered entity does not specifically know about the relevant record. Of course, for the covered entity to be liable for discrimination under title I of the ADA, the individual with a "record of" a substantially limiting impairment must prove that the covered entity discriminated on the basis of the record of the disability.

The terms "substantially limits" and "major life activity" under the second prong of the definition of "disability" are to be construed in accordance with the same principles applicable under the "actual disability" prong, as set forth in § 1630.2(j).

Individuals who are covered under the "record of" prong will often be covered under the first prong of the definition of disability as well. This is a consequence of the rule of construction in the ADAAA and the regulations providing that an individual with an impairment that is episodic or in remission can be protected under the first prong if the impairment would be substantially limiting when active. See 42 U.S.C. 12102(4)(D); § 1630.2(j)(1)(vii). Thus, an individual who has cancer that is currently in remission is an individual with a disability under the "actual disability" prong because he has an impairment that would substantially limit normal cell growth when active. He is also covered by the "record of" prong based on his history of having had an impairment that substantially limited normal cell growth.

Finally, this section of the EEOC's regulations makes it clear that an individual with a record of a disability is entitled to a reasonable accommodation currently needed for limitations resulting from or relating to the past substantially limiting impairment. This conclusion, which has been the Commission's long-standing position, is confirmed by language in the ADA Amendments Act stating that individuals covered only under the "regarded as" prong of the definition of disability are not entitled to reasonable accommodation. See 42 U.S.C. 12201(h). By implication, this means that individuals covered under the first or second prongs are otherwise eligible for reasonable accommodations. See 2008 House Judiciary Committee Report at 22 ("This makes clear that the duty to accommodate...arises only when an individual establishes coverage under the first or second prong of the definition."). Thus, as the regulations explain, an employee with an impairment that previously substantially limited but no longer substantially limits, a major life activity may need leave or a schedule change to permit him or her to attend follow-up or "monitoring" appointments from a health care provider.

As the Commission has previously noted, "[p]rotection against employment discrimination is provided under the Rehabilitation Act for individuals with such a record because of the stigma associated with being a psychiatric patient, which does not disappear when the treatment ends." Jones v. USPS, 01862261 (1987) (classification as schizophrenic for purposes of disability retirement establishes record of mental disability).

The fact that an agency is in possession of a document that includes a diagnosis does not mean the complainant has established that he is a person with a record of a disability. See Black v. Postmaster General, 05930748 (1994). "A person has a record of a disability...if he or she has a hospital or other record documenting a substantially limiting impairment or misclassifying such an impairment. See School Board of Nassau County, Florida v. Arline, 480 U.S. 273 (1980); Mahoney v. Ortiz, 645 F. Supp. 22, 23-24 (S.D.N.Y. 1986)." The emphasis is on the fact that the record must document a condition that substantially limits a major life activity and not merely the existence of a condition. While the ADAAA did not specifically make changes to this category of coverage, still it must be viewed in light of the other changes brought about by the amendments to major life activities and substantial limitations.

In order to establish that he has a record of a disabling condition, a complainant must do more than present evidence of a diagnosis. In Zeigler v. Postmaster General, 01930854 (1994), the Commission further clarified the requirements:

With regard to the second criteria, the Commission is precluded from finding, on the present record, that appellant had a record of disability. A person has a record of a disability pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(4) if he has a hospital or other record documenting a substantially limiting impairment or a misclassification of such an impairment. See School Board of Nassau County, Florida v. Arline, 480 U.S. 273 (1980); Mahoney v. Ortiz, 645 F. Supp. 22, 23-24 (S.D.N.Y. 1986). Accordingly, the focus is not merely on whether the individual has a physiological disorder or condition, but rather on whether he has been classified (correctly or incorrectly) as having an impairment that substantially limits one or more of his major life activities. Thus, evidence that a person simply has been diagnosed as having an impairment does not establish that the person has a record of a disability.

However, it may be possible for a complainant who does not have a record of a disability under Zeigler to establish that he or she is regarded as having a disability. See discussion of "Regarded as Disabilities and Working," below.
