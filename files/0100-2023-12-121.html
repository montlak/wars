7. Office of Inspector General

In the agencies that have offices of Inspectors General (IG) by statute, the IG is separate from the agency. For that reason, the Commission has held that a complaint about an IG investigation does not state a claim for relief. As the Commission explained in Johnson v. Secretary of Navy, 05960699 (1998):

Notwithstanding the agency's arguments on RTR, we will reconsider the previous decision on our own motion. In Mattocks v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05950549 (August 29, 1996), a decision that had not been issued when the previous decision was decided, the Commission held that:

The Inspector General's (IG) Office is an independent unit whose head is appointed by the office of the President. (citations omitted). The purpose of the IG's Office is to conduct and supervise audits and investigations relating to the agency's programs and operations; to provide leadership and coordination and recommend policies for activities designed to promote economy, efficiency, and effectiveness in the administration of, and to prevent and detect fraud and abuse in, such programs and operations; and, to keep the agency head and Congress advised of any problems and deficiencies related to the administration of such programs and operations and the necessity for and progress of corrective action. Because the Commission's regulations cover complaints regarding an employer's, i.e., an agency's, acts of prohibited discrimination and because the IG's Office is separate from the agency, the Commission finds that appellant's allegations [that he was subjected to harassment and discrimination when he was the subject of an IG investigation] fail to state a claim under the regulations.

In the present case, appellant's complaint pertains to an IG investigation by the agency; therefore, we find that the complaint fails to state a claim pursuant to EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.107(a). Consequently, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint is AFFIRMED.

See also Jordan v. General Services Administration, 01A53432 (2005); Brown v. Environmental Protection Agency, 02A15260 (2002); Tucker v. Postmaster General, 01A15292 (2002); Guertin v. Secretary of Navy, 01976532 (1998).

The Commission similarly explained in Sorrell v. Secretary of Navy, 01A14314 (2002):

The Commission has held that the Inspector General's Office is an independent unit, and that the purpose of the Inspector General's office is to conduct and supervise audits and investigations relating to the agency's programs and operations; to provide leadership and coordination and recommend policies for activities designed to promote economy, efficiency, and effectiveness in the administration of, and to prevent and detect fraud and abuse in, such programs and operations; and to keep the agency head and Congress advised of any problems and deficiencies related to the administration of such programs and operation and the necessity for and progress of corrective action. Here, the Commission finds that complainant has failed to identify a harm or loss to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. See Mattocks v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05950549 (August 29, 1996); accord, Johnson v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05960699 (April 16, 1998). Therefore, we find that the complaint is properly dismissed for failure to state a claim pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(1). Accordingly, the agency's decision is AFFIRMED.

However, the Commission in Erick N. v. Attorney General, 0120160054 (2017), reversed the administrative judge's dismissal for failure to state a claim where the complainant alleged that agency officials were involved in the resumption of a previously suspended OIG investigation against the complainant. The Commission distinguished the complainant's case from Mattocks v. Department of the Navy, 05950549 (1996), explaining:

The AJ also reasoned that the resumption of the OIG investigation failed to render Complainant aggrieved, citing to Mattocks v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05950549 (August 29, 1996), which held that the actions of an OIG fail to state a claim because it is an entity legally distinct from the employing agency--in other words, the alleged discrimination is being committed by someone other than the employer.

In the instant case, however, we find that Mattocks does not apply. Most significantly, Complainant has explicitly alleged that Agency officials were involved in the OIG's investigation, including "initiating" the resumption of the investigation. The record does not currently contain definitive evidence about who was responsible for what and provides an inadequate explanation for why the OIG became involved in the matter in the first place, why the investigation was resumed, or the nature of the Agency's involvement with the OIG. These are issues which could have been fleshed out at a hearing, had the matter not been dismissed. Since the nature of the Agency's involvement in the OIG investigation has not been developed in the record as it stands and is material to Complainant's claims, it was inappropriate for the AJ to dismiss the timely raised portion of the complaint for failure to state a claim.

However, being subjected to an IG investigation may be considered sufficient injury for purposes of stating a claim when the underlying basis asserted is reprisal. See, e.g., Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120132115 (2014); Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120141889 (2014); Muschler v. Environmental Protection Agency, 0220123476 (2013). See also, Chapter 17, section on "Chilling Effect and the Terms and Conditions of Employment."

It is not a collateral attack on an OIG investigation when the investigation results in removal and complainant files a complaint over that action. In addition, in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120142821 (2015), the Commission found the complaint was still viable even where the removal had been rescinded because the underlying basis of the complaint was reprisal.

The Commission reached a similar result in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120142813 (2015), where the complainant alleged, in part, that she was subjected to an OIG investigation when subordinates complained of harassment but a white male supervisor was not:

A fair reading of the EEO complaint in this matter. as well as the related EEO counseling report, shows that Complainant is claiming she was treated more harshly than white managers when subordinate employees alleged they were subjected to a hostile work environment. Complainant claimed that when her employees apparently complained about her, Agency management placed her off work and referred the matter to the OIG for investigation. By contrast, Complainant alleges that similar allegations against a white manager were promptly and informally resolved 'by a round table meeting." She further alleges that the white manager was promoted a few months later.

The Agency dismissed Complainant's allegations of race discrimination, reasoning that this was a collateral attack on the OIG process. However, we do not find that Complainant is alleging discrimination within another adjudicatory process. Instead, she is asserting that Agency management treated her more harshly than similarly situated white managers when subordinate employees asserted a hostile work environment. She alleges that unlike a similarly situated white manager, Agency management put Complainant off work and referred the matter for an OIG investigation, while informally settling the matter with the white manager. This states a clear claim of disparate treatment and should not have been dismissed.

In Strickland v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A60493 (2006), the Commission reversed dismissal of a complaint for failure to state a claim where the complainant alleged that he was arrested and handcuffed while at work in connection with an OIG investigation. The Commission found that the complainant had alleged an injury and further alleged that he was treated differently from other employees accused of time and attendance infractions. But, the Commission has also held that simply being subjected to an OIG investigation does not aggrieve an employee. Ford v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A46109 (2006).
