A. PREFERENTIAL TREATMENT

The Act places an affirmative duty on federal agencies to provide opportunities for the hiring, placement, and advancement of qualified individuals with disabilities. In some ways, it may appear that the Act requires preferential treatment of persons with disabilities. Properly viewed, the Act does not require special treatment though it does, in some instances, permit it. Instead, the Act acknowledges that agencies are required to take into account the special needs of persons with disabilities to provide them with an equal opportunity to compete for jobs in the federal workplace. The law requires that an agency provide reasonable accommodation to a qualified individual with a disability if the accommodation would not place an undue burden on the agency. However, the law does not require the agency to give the employee with a disability preferential treatment by hiring or assigning him to a position for which he or she is not qualified. See Pepin v. Department of Transportation, 01831118 (1983) (citing Prewitt v. USPS, 662 F.2d 292 (5th Cir. 1981)).

The agency's obligation to a qualified employee with a disability does not end upon hiring. The agency has a continuing obligation to provide appropriate placement and advancement opportunities, as the Commission noted in McGee v. USPS, 01821538 (1987):

The Commission notes that the Rehabilitation Act "impose[s] a duty upon federal agencies to structure their procedures and programs so as to ensure that handicapped individuals are afforded equal opportunity in both job assignment and placement." Ryan v. FDIC, 565 F.2d 762, 15 F.E.P. Cases 1384, 1385 (D.C. Cir. 1977). In another case, the same court found that the federal employer failed to make adequate affirmative reassignment efforts for a handicapped employee. Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265 (D.C. Cir. 1977). The goal of affirmative action is not merely the employment of a handicapped person but an appropriate placement of such an employee. OPM has stated that agencies have a responsibility to make every effort for the continued utilization of disabled employees, recognizing that "[o]ne of the major problems facing persons with disabilities is underutilization of their skills, knowledge and abilities." Handbook of Selective Placement, (OPM Doc. 125-11-1, March, 1979). See also Handbook of Reasonable Accommodation (OPM Doc. 720-A. March 1980)....

An agency does not fulfill its obligation for appropriate placement of qualified employees with disabilities by creating a special unit where all employees with physical disabilities are assigned. See Gutzler v. Postmaster General, 01880954 (1988). In that case, the agency had a special section of permanent light duty assignments with less desirable hours and work assignments. The Commission noted that the complainant had been satisfactorily performing the essential functions of the position she was in prior to her assignment to the special unit. The Commission found the creation of such a work unit was not a legitimate or nondiscriminatory reason for assignment of workers with disabilities to the section.

Although agencies are required to develop programs designed to promote the hiring, advancement, and placement of employees with disabilities, it may not fulfill all of its obligations under the Rehabilitation Act by considering reasonable accommodation only for those persons with disabilities who seek employment through programs specifically designated for such employees. Baitey v. Postmaster General, 01914005 (1992). In Baitey, the complainant mounted a systemic challenge to the agency's practice of employing employees with disabilities exclusively through a special program:

Federal agencies must give full consideration to the hiring, placement and advancement of qualified handicapped persons and may not discriminate against them. EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.703. The Commission's regulations provide that a handicapped person is one who (1) has a physical or mental impairment which substantially limits one or more major life activities (2) has a record of such an impairment, or (3) is regarded as having such an impairment. 29 CFR 1613.702(a). A qualified handicapped person is one who can safely perform the essential functions of the position in question with or without reasonable accommodation. 29 CFR 1613.702(f). In providing protection under the Rehabilitation Act not only for those who are in fact handicapped, but also for those who are regarded as handicapped, Congress acknowledged that society's accumulated myths and fears about disability and disease are as handicapping as are the physical limitations that flow from actual impairment.... The Act is carefully structured to replace irrational reflexive reactions to actual or perceived handicaps with actions based on reasoned and medically sound judgments. School Board of Nassau County v. Arline, 480 U.S. 273, 284-285 (1987) (footnotes omitted).

An agency, accordingly, must do an individualized assessment of each person's alleged handicap and qualifications and of possible accommodations before taking the sort of significant adverse action alleged herein, i.e., denial of employment. Moreover, to show that its refusal to hire a person on the basis of medical unsuitability is not discriminatory, the agency must do more than show that s/he stands an elevated risk of injury. Mantolete v. Bolger, 767 F.2d 1416, 1422 (9th Cir. 1985). To lawfully exclude someone from employment, the agency must show that there is a "reasonable probability of substantial harm" in his or her performance of the duties of the job in question. Id. This determination cannot be based merely on an employer's subjective evaluation or, except in cases of a most apparent nature, merely on medical reports. The question is whether, in light of the individual's work history and medical history, employment of that individual would pose a reasonable probability of substantial harm. Id.

The Commission's regulations prohibit the use of a selection criteria that "screens out or tends to screen out qualified handicapped persons or any class of handicapped persons unless..." the criterion is job-related and an alternative job-related criterion which does not screen out or tend to screen out as many handicapped persons is not shown as available. 29 CFR 1613.705(a). Our regulations require that an "agency shall make reasonable accommodation to the known physical or mental limitations of a qualified handicapped applicant...unless the agency can demonstrate that the accommodation would impose an undue hardship on the operation of its program." 29 CFR 1613.704(a).

Appellant has alleged that the hiring practices of the agency's Omaha division operate to exclude from casual appointments handicapped applicants who are outside the agency's handicapped program. Specifically, appellant submits that an individual who has been offered a casual appointment pending a pre-employment physical, is, in essence, automatically rejected from casual employment if the individual is assessed as being at medical risk. According to appellant, once a medical risk has been assigned to an individual, no further inquiry is made, nor is a determination made by agency officials as to whether reasonable accommodation is necessary or possible. Appellant alleges that this practice operates to screen out from casual employment those individuals who have substantially limiting impairments and those who are perceived as having such impairments.

Based on the evidence of record, the Commission determines that appellant's allegation of systemic handicap discrimination in the hiring of casual employees is fully supported by the testimony of agency officials. According to the testimony, determinations as to reasonable accommodation are made for the most part only with regard to applicants referred through the agency's handicapped program. No such determination is made for individuals outside the handicapped program who are considered for casual employment. Rather, upon the assignment of a medical risk, such individuals are rejected for the casual position at issue. The record reveals that no individualized assessment of any sort is made, nor is there even a careful consideration of the pre-employment examination report. (footnote omitted). Rather, testimony shows that the rejection from casual employment is made based on a generalization about the likelihood of future injury. Moreover, the agency's determination as to whether an applicant is part of the handicapped program operates to screen out or tend to screen out qualified handicapped persons who are outside that handicapped program. While the Commission does not intend to denigrate in any way the agency's efforts at hiring severely handicapped persons through its handicapped program, such a program can not serve, as it does here, to screen out other classes of handicapped persons.

Agency officials testified that when hiring casual employees there is insufficient time to make a determination as to whether accommodation or job modification is possible. While the Commission recognizes that the agency legitimately may need to employ casual employees on an immediate basis, the agency failed to show that such a consideration justified its rejection of appellant. Likewise, the agency has failed to put forth sufficient evidence that in every situation when casual employees are hired, it is unable to make a determination as to reasonable accommodation. As evidenced by the testimony, even when the agency has sufficient time to look further into the possibility of accommodation (as when a casual applicant is initially rejected based on medical risk and subsequently comes up again for consideration for another casual position) there is no apparent mechanism in place to make such a determination. The evidence, therefore, shows that the agency presumptively rejects individuals from consideration for casual employment based on the finding of medical risk.

Note that the Commission did leave open the prospect that, in some instances, the agency's need for casual employees could be so immediate that further medical screening of applicants could impose an undue burden on the agency. Such an exception would very likely be narrowly interpreted and subject to the Commission's warning that a procedure for screening such applicants be in place prior to the next time casual employees are required.
