F. CONTACTS OUTSIDE EEO PROCESS

An employee is not required to invoke the agency EEO process to be protected from reprisal. A letter to a U.S. Senator requesting an investigation into a reduction in work, where the employee alleges the reduction is based on discriminatory motives, is protected activity. Mosby v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01911866 (1991).

Not all contact outside of the EEO process is protected. The fact that the employee engaged in prior EEO activity does not act as a blanket prohibition against discipline, even when the discipline is tangentially related to his complaint. It is not improper for an agency to instruct a complainant to pursue his EEO concerns through proper channels, unless the complainant can establish that others not engaged in protected activity are allowed to pursue concerns outside the proper channels. As the Commission held in Bresnan v. Secretary of Treasury, 01931622 (1993):

In order for appellant to establish that the agency's articulated reasons are pretext for discrimination, appellant must show that the agency was actually motivated by reprisal, or that its reasons are not credible. Appellant argues that the agency is unable to support its reasons for the suspension since the restrictions on appellant's access to communication with agency officials is a violation of free speech and the EEOC Regulations that prohibit interference with representation. However, based upon a review of the record, the Commission finds that the agency did not act improperly in disciplining appellant for contacting agency officials outside of his chain of command in violation of its instructions. Despite appellant's argument that the instructions were invalid because he was denied free speech, appellant failed to establish that the instructions were discriminatorily applied to him. Appellant did not establish that other employees without prior EEO activity were allowed to freely make contact with any agency official. Thus, appellant failed to show that he was denied access to outside officials due to his protected EEO involvement. Appellant did not demonstrate that there was a specific provision in the EEOC Regulations that would entitle appellant to make such contacts outside of an investigation. And, because appellant's contacts were not made within the framework of the EEO process (i.e. the contacts were not made with an EEO Counselor, a witness, or an investigator) the Commission finds no indication that the restrictions interfered with appellant's right to pursue his complaints. If appellant believed that the agency was improperly processing his transfer pursuant to the settlement agreement, he could have invoked the EEO process and sought the assistance of a Counselor regarding a breach of settlement agreement.

Appellant also argues that the agency forced him into noncompliance with the restriction since it was not responsive to his concerns about the discrepancies in the reporting dates. However, the record does not indicate that agency officials were holding appellant responsible for determining when the reporting date would be. Appellant contends that the agency's assertion that he did not seek help within his chain of command was not credible. Appellant notes that in its final decision, the agency relies upon the July 16, 1990 memorandum by the General Manager which stated that appellant did not approach her for assistance with his reporting date. In her affidavit however, the General Manager stated that twice appellant asked for her assistance. Although these accounts contradict one another, they do not indicate that the agency's reasons for suspending appellant are not credible. Appellant is unable to refute the evidence that he failed to follow the instructions given in the October 13, 1989 memorandum. Appellant has not established that it was necessary for him to ignore the rules about the chain of command, or that there was no other resource for him because the agency was failing to comply with the settlement agreement. Accordingly, the Commission finds that appellant failed to establish discrimination on this issue.

Without clarifying whether it was activity protected under the participation or opposition clause, in Michael v. National Security Agency, 01971283 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant's allegation of reprisal, based on his conversations with the agency inspector general, stated a claim:

Based on a thorough review of the record, the Commission finds that appellant's allegation containing reprisal as a basis was improperly dismissed on the grounds of failure to state a claim. The Commission finds that the appellant in the present case has alleged reprisal as a basis because, the appellant states "...some of the subject matter discussed with the IG is, in my opinion, covered by the EEO." Appellant alleges that as a result of this activity, he received adverse treatment. The Commission finds that appellant has alleged facts sufficient to raise the basis of reprisal under Title VII.

It is unclear from the decision in Michael whether the employee was seeking any remedial action from the inspector general or simply expressing opposition to alleged discriminatory practices.

It is not protected activity for an employee to state, in a private conversation with a coworker, that white males encounter difficulty in promotions at the agency. Leno v. Postmaster General, 03960034 (1996). "[P]etitioner thus neither alleged that the agency engaged in an unlawful practice nor presented this issue within the context of a protest against the practice directed towards agency management." Id.
