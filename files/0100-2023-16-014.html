III. REQUIREMENTS OF FINAL AGENCY ACTION

Whether the agency takes final action through an order or decision, there are certain requirements for the final agency action.

Although it may seem elementary, an agency may not issue a final agency decision on a complaint of discrimination against another agency. However, it is quite possible that more than one agency can be the proper defendant in a case. In Ronso v. Secretary of Army, 01960957 (1996), the complainant, an Army employee, was stationed at an Air Force facility. Separate complaints were filed with the Army and Air Force because the Air Force had screened applicants for the position even though the actual selection was made by the Army. In addition, the Army had no EEO presence at the facility where the complainant was located. Both agencies ultimately issued decisions finding discrimination. The Air Force then attempted to modify its final decision to blame the Army for the discrimination and ordering the Army to pay attorney fees.

In reversing Ronso, the Commission found that one agency could not enter findings of discrimination against another agency. As the Commission in Ronso explained:

As a preliminary matter, the Commission must address the effect of the FAD issued by the Air Force finding discrimination and awarding attorney fees against the Army. Section 1614.106(a) of the Commission's regulations provides, "A complaint must be filed with the agency that allegedly discriminated against the complainant." 29 CFR § 1614.106(a). These regulations further provide, "The agency shall dismiss a complaint or a portion of a complaint. . .[t]hat fails to state a claim. . .under § 1614.106(a). . . ." 29 CFR § 1614.107(a). Finally, the regulations provide, "The investigation of complaints shall be conducted by the agency against which the complaint has been filed." 29 CFR § 1614.108.

Under the Commission's regulations, it was improper for the Air Force to accept, investigate, and decide an EEO complaint alleging discriminatory conduct against an Army official, pertaining to a non-selection for an Army position. In fact, according to the Commission's regulations, the Air Force should have dismissed appellant's complaint. The record reflects that there was not an appropriate Army official available to accept appellant's complaint at the facility where the discrimination was alleged to have occurred; the Air Force should have directed appellant to appropriate Army personnel at another facility or, at the very least, sought explicit authorization from the Army to process appellant's complaint. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the Army is not bound by the terms of the Air Force's FAD.

However, on reconsideration, Ronso v. Secretaries of Air Force and Army, 05960418 (1999), the Commission found that, although one agency could not enter findings of discrimination against another agency, each agency was bound by its oriGINAl final decision:

As an initial matter, the Commission must decide whether the prior decision properly concluded that the Air Force did not have jurisdiction over appellant's complaint. In finding that it did not, the decision cited 29 CFR § 1614.106(a), which states that a complaint must be filed "with the agency that allegedly discriminated against the complainant." Although it is true that the SO, an Army official, is the individual who was found to have discriminated against appellant, we do not agree that the Army acted alone in the selection process. Specifically, officials in the Air Force's HRO, in addition to collecting the applications for the Position, categorized the applications in conjunction with the Air Force Personnel Action Directive. To the extent appellant's complaint was premised, in large part, on the manner in which her application was categorized, and because Air Force officials made that determination in accordance with an Air Force directive, we find that the Air Force was a participant in the selection process and that it had jurisdiction to process her complaint. Accordingly, because both the Army and Air Force played an active role in the selection process, the Commission finds that they are properly treated as joint defendants.

It is well-settled that, once an agency has issued a final decision finding discrimination, it is bound by that finding and may not relitigate the issue when the case is on appeal. Davis v. Department of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05931205 (January 9, 1994), citing Rousseau v. Department of Education, EEOC Appeal No. 01920410 (January 21, 1992). Both the Air Force and Army have issued final decisions finding discrimination. In accordance with the foregoing Commission precedent, we must reject the Air Force's subsequent attempt to modify Air Force-FAD 1 to conclude that the Army was responsible for that discrimination, and not the Air Force. We also reject the Air Force's attempt to modify Air Force FAD 2 by asking the Army to pay the attorney's fees and costs the Air Force had awarded appellant. In issuing Air Force-FAD 2, the Air Force was agreeing that it, not the Army, was going to pay appellant $15,419.25 in attorney's fees and $91.37 in costs. In this regard, we note that there is a memo from an Air Force official attached to Air Force-FAD 2 which directs one of the Air Force's offices to issue appellant a check in the amount of $15,510.62 (the total amount of the awarded fees and costs). We also find, under the aforementioned Commission precedent, that the Army is bound by its finding of discrimination in Army-FAD 1. Id.
