1. Personnel Actions

As a general rule, events which have a definite conclusion, such as promotions, performance appraisals, denial of quality step increases and denial of training opportunities are, in the EEOC's view, clearly isolated incidents not qualifying under the continuing violation theory, even if such events repeatedly occur. Robinson v. Department of Commerce, 01831212 (1983). However, there are personnel actions that the Commission has found do not have the requisite degree of permanency to put the complainant on notice that the time line for filing a complaint has started to run. The Commission has repeatedly mislabeled these cases as continuing violations, so they are discussed here. "Continuing Violations" is discussed earlier in this chapter.

The Commission has held that a performance appraisal has a "degree of permanence" that generally will preclude it from constituting a continuing violation. Bonner v. Secretary of Air Force, 01943631 (1994) (citing Berry v. Board of Supervisors, 715 F.2d 971 (5th Cir. 1983), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 868 (1986)). However, the employee states a claim for a continuing violation when he alleges that a quality step increase was withheld with the promise that it would be granted if his performance improved. Since the increase was still being withheld at the time the employee contacted the EEO counselor, this constituted a past policy that was still in effect. Murphy v. USPS, 01831221 (1983). However, the fact that allegations of past step increase denials are untimely does not mean such denials are not relevant as background in determining whether a timely complaint of a step denial is discriminatory. Lyles v. Department of Commerce, 01831224 (1983).

Denial of promotion is generally deemed to be a discrete event, but a cause of action under the continuing violation theory is stated where the action at issue is a career-ladder promotion. Dean v. Secretary of Treasury, 05920202 (1992). A new violation occurs for each and every day the agency fails to promote the complainant after his initial eligibility date. Technically, such a violation is not a continuing violation, but a recurring violation. See Sibold v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A14354 (2002) (the denial of a career-ladder promotion occurs each and every day after the date of eligibility that the promotion is not granted); Tate v. Secretary of Army, 01964045 (1997) (same).

An allegation that the agency failed repeatedly, over the course of seven years, to promote appellant, but continually promoted younger, non-Jewish employees was deemed to state a claim of continuing violation where one nonselection occurred within the 30 days prior to the onset of precomplaint counseling. Mandelberg v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01882883 (1988). "Caselaw suggests the interrelatedness can be even more apparent when, as in appellant's case, the promotions involved are 'career-ladder' promotions, rather than competitive promotions." Williamson v. Secretary of Commerce, 01913339 (1991). But see Ward v. Secretary of Air Force, 01913375 (1991) (holding that no continuing violation is established where the complainant failed for five consecutive years to attain her target grade but did not file a complaint).

Although a complainant may not be able to make a case for a continuing violation when challenging five separate nonselections over a two-year period, it is possible that a single complaint of discrimination regarding all selections could be timely. In Barone v. Defense Contract Audit Agency, 05890672 (1989)--a case decided under Part 1613--the Commission rejected the notion that the five selections were part of a discriminatory practice based, in part, upon the fact that two different selecting officials were involved. However, the Commission accepted the contentions of the complainant that she did not become aware that the four other nonselections were discriminatory until she was given the reason for her fifth nonselection--a reason which she deemed to be clearly pretextual. Upon further inquiry, she concluded she was the best qualified candidate for all five positions. The Commission found all five complaints were timely based on the fact that counseling had been initiated on all five within 30 days of the complainant becoming aware of possible discrimination. But see Darlene F. v. Social Security Administration, 0120140306 (2016) (a series of three nonselections over the course of less than three years for the same position does not constitute a continuing violation).

In Mateo v. Postmaster General, 01962884 (1997), the Commission reached back almost 20 years and found that nonselections from 1976 forward were timely under the continuing violation theory. Amply demonstrating its confusion in this area, the Commission, in Mateo, reached the conclusion that the complainant alleged a continuing violation, without any reference to when the complainant knew or should have known of the alleged violations, and also found that each nonselection constituted a new violation:

Appellant has alleged that she was subject to a continuing pattern of discrimination, the agency's ongoing failure to hire her since 1976, because of her sex. Therefore, consistent with Lee, supra, we find that appellant has stated a claim under EEOC Regulations. In addition, we note that appellant's complaint does not pertain to a discrete act that is a one-time occurrence but rather to a continuing pattern or practice of discrimination resulting in appellant's nonconsideration for employment and the agency's failure to so notify her. In this regard, we find that the agency committed a new violation each time it hired off the register but did not tell appellant to come in for an interview or inform her that she was not selected for a particular position. [T]he agency, by its own admission in the FAD, states that appellant's "name/score was not reached during hiring which took place between November 1993 and February 1994" [emphasis added]. As November 1993 fell within the 45-day charge-filing period, we therefore find that appellant timely contacted the EEO counselor in regard to her charge that the agency had not considered hiring her since 1976, pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.105(a)(1). See Verkennes v. Dept. of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05900700 (Sept. 21, 1990).

Since the discriminatory acts complained of were of the same identical nature, i.e., the agency's continuing nonconsideration of appellant for employment from 1976 onwards because of her low test scores, we further find that the acts complained of were therefore also sufficiently interrelated to constitute a continuing violation from the date appellant first applied for employment with the agency. See Woljan v. Environmental Protection Agency, EEOC Request No. 05950361 (Oct. 5, 1995).

Combining timely allegations of discrimination with untimely allegations does not constitute a complaint of a continuing violation when the allegations concern entirely different matters. Kalia v. Department of Army, 01831850 (1984).
