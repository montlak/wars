OFFICIAL TIME

If your agency or facility has a union and you supervise any of the union's designated representatives, you will find yourself dealing with the concept of "official time."

The term "official time" refers, in the labor relations context, to duty time that bargaining unit employees may use, without loss of pay or charge to leave, to perform various functions in their capacity as union representatives. Official time is a statutory provision of the Civil Service Reform Act as well as a binding contractual provision of nearly all collective bargaining agreements.

Functions for which official time may be used include:

  • • Negotiating agreements.
  • • Processing grievances.
  • • Presenting cases in arbitration.
  • • Representing employees at meetings with management.

Depending upon the union contract, employees who are not union representatives may also use official time to speak with union reps, and to perform other labor relations activities such as participation in grievance meetings and serving as witnesses in arbitration.

Note: A different type of official time may be used by employees to handle their EEO disputes. That type of official time is not covered here.

Federal law provides that union reps may use official time for the specific purpose of negotiating a labor agreement. For other purposes, however, the law leaves it up to the agency and the union to hammer out the details through negotiations.

Official time is not available for the conduct of purely union business, known as "internal union business." Examples include administering internal union elections, membership drives, and filing disclosure forms with the Department of Labor.

The manager's role. After the agency and the union agree on the rules governing official time use in an organization, the agency relies heavily upon individual managers to apply the rules as intended. In order to do so, you must be aware of the rules, and apply them fairly and consistently.

When you get a request for official time, check the agreement to ensure that it meets the CBA's requirements regarding the purpose, the individual making the request, the amount of time to be used, and that the procedures in making the request were followed (frequently, agreements require a form to be filled out). As always, check with your LR office if you have any concerns about approving the request.

Also, it's important that you actually enforce the rules. Managers who repeatedly fail to enforce rules limiting the use of official time may end up waiving them. Sometimes the problem isn't the official time allowed by the contract but rather managers' failure to enforce their limits.

Executive Orders. In 2018, President Trump signed Executive Order 13837, which, among other things, placed a cap on official-time usage and ordered agencies to implement procedures governing the authorization of official time. That EO has since been rescinded by Executive Order 14003, signed by President Biden in 2021.

Because official time has become a political issue, it's possible that each new administration will issue its own orders regarding the limits on, and usage of, official time.

Labor agreement provisions

Virtually every labor contract contains a provision that addresses the use of official time. Some are more detailed and specific than others, but most address the same issues. They are:

  • • The purposes for which official time may be used.
  • • The number and identity of officials who may use official time.
  • • The amount of time that may be used for a particular function, or overall.
  • • The procedures necessary to obtain and use official time.

Traditionally, many bargaining agreements allowed certain union representatives to spend up to 100 percent of their duty time on official time. In May 2018, President Trump issued an executive order capping the maximum official time usage for any federal employee at 25 percent of work time. That executive order was rescinded by President Biden in 2021, so it's possible that some union representatives will again be on 100 percent official time.

Some agencies incorporated the 25 percent cap into their bargaining agreements, and those provisions will survive the Biden executive order. Check with your labor relations specialist about the latest developments, and to determine the status of your CBA.

Some agreements list the particular functions for which official time will be granted, e.g., grievance investigation, while others use a more general term like "representational activities." In most cases, stating a specific number of hours and an itemization of authorized activities is more in management's favor. Ultimately, this is a tactical decision made by your negotiators during collective bargaining.

In addition to the reason for official time allowed by the agreement, there are also statutory entitlements to official time for certain purposes or there may be a local memorandum of understanding about specific rules governing the use of official time. In other words, there may be situations where official time should be allowed for a purpose not listed in the agreement. Check with your LR professional if you have any concerns before you act.

Manager control. Almost all agreements place the immediate manager of a union official in control of when official time is used. That is, most contracts require that union officials obtain supervisory permission before leaving the work site to participate in an activity on official time. Hopefully, your contract doesn't permit union reps to decide for themselves if their job responsibilities outweigh the need to meet with an employee about a grievance.

In most cases, the labor agreement permits the supervisor to deny permission to leave if work circumstances make it necessary for all employees to remain at their posts. The Federal Labor Relations Authority and labor arbitrators have made it clear that employees, including union officials, are required to obey orders first and grieve later.

In other words, disobeying a supervisor's order to remain on duty almost always provides a basis for discipline. However, your decision to deny an official time request must be reasonable and supportable. Arbitrary decisions to deny official time will not withstand legal scrutiny.

Forms. Many agreements require that a form be submitted to support an official time request and have procedures for how that form will be handled. Some supervisors, in an effort to stay friendly with the union rep, develop practices that don't include the agreed-upon form. Others may view the form as a rubber-stamping procedure. When examining your agreement, find out if there is a form, how it's submitted, what it must contain, and how it's maintained afterward.

The proper use of an official time request form is very important when it comes to recordkeeping. When your contract reopens for negotiations, it is essential that your bargaining team has a firm grasp of how much official time was used during the contract's term, and for what. If the union asks for 1,000 hours and your records show that they only used 300, management has a persuasive case to limit the time allowed in the next contract.

Handling official time requests

Your bargaining agreement will define your role in monitoring the usage of official time and making sure that official time requests conform to the agreed-upon standards and procedures. Your job is to keep track of employee's use of official time and to fairly and consistently enforce the rules. This will prevent the excessive use of official time, and will help you steer clear of an allegation that you have waived the negotiated rules.

When handling official time requests, be objective and keep your personal feelings out of it. Official time should not be disapproved merely because you dislike the concept or the employee. Only disapprove the use of official time when there is good reason to do so. Follow the contract and get advice from an LR practitioner.

Get it right. It's important to note that wrongly denying official time can result in an award of back pay, including interest and attorney's fees, among other possible remedies (such as restoration of leave for workers who, when wrongly denied official time, took leave to perform representational duties). On the other hand, granting unjustified official time requests can be just as much of a problem as denying valid ones. Management's continuous approval of illegitimate requests could, over time, create a "past practice" right for the union. That's why it's so important to "get it right."

Don't penalize union officials for the use of official time through lowered performance ratings, reduced advancement, or some other form of retaliation. Whether it's obvious or subtle, retaliating against an employee for the legitimate use of official time violates the law.

Lastly, as the "official time umpire," you must not change the official time rules in midstream. Suddenly imposing a sign-out requirement, for example, will undoubtedly cause a dispute -- one which you're likely to lose. If you think you have an official time abuse problem, contact your LR staff before making matters worse.

Factors involved. Whether you approve an official time request will depend on several factors, including:

  • • The types of activity for which official time is requested.
  • • The amount of time requested.
  • • The relevant release procedures.

Amount of official time

If you believe the purpose for which an official time request was made is valid, your next step is to consider whether the amount of time requested seems reasonable for the relevant activity. There will be some wiggle room here -- if you think the activity should take five to seven hours, a request for eight hours probably isn't unreasonable. It's best to err on the side of caution and approve the request.

Be aware that the agreement may specify an exact amount of time allowed, such as a 500-hour "bank" for each contract year, or a percentage of time a representative may use. That number may vary depending on the role the representative plays in the union -- president, chief steward, steward, etc. In cases where a bank of hours is allotted, you'll probably need to check with the LR office to ascertain the current balance.

Example: A union representative requests two hours of official time to fill out a first-step grievance form. Preparing a grievance is one of the activities for which official time is allowed, and you believe that the amount of time is reasonable under the circumstances. This is a situation where the official time request should be granted.

Example: A union representative requests 40 hours of official time to review a proposed new parking policy. Reviewing management proposals concerning changes in policies is one of the listed activities for which official time is allowed. However, you believe that the amount of time requested is excessive because in the past, the union has only requested eight to 12 hours to review similar policy changes. You informally contact the union rep about the amount of time requested, but he won't budge or give any more information about the need for so much time. You consult with the LR expert, who says that this amount of time is "clearly excessive." This is a situation where you should consider denying the official time request, at least in part.

Release procedures

Agreements that provide for official time almost always include "release procedures" for how the union representative will give notice of the need for official time, how to be contacted while away, and other related matters. Few contracts permit union reps to simply walk off the job.

A typical release procedures provision might read as follows:

The union representative and supervisor will communicate regarding:

  • • The type of representation matter involved and the approximate length of time needed.
  • • Where the union representative will perform the activities for which official time is needed.
  • • When the union representative must perform the activity.
  • • How management will contact a union representative who is away from the normal duty station.
  • • How and when the union rep will report back to duty.

The union representative will request release as far in advance as practical. Normally, ordinary workload will not preclude release. If the union representative cannot be released at the requested time due to work requirements, the union representative will be released when the workload requirements have been met or other arrangements have been made. If the union representative cannot be released the day of the request, the supervisor will execute a written denial that will include the reason for the delay and a statement of when the union representative will be released (normally within 24 hours).

Phrases like "as far in advance as practical" leave a lot of leeway, so if your agreement has similar wording, denying a union rep's release on grounds that the union gave insufficient notice of the need for official time is a bad idea. If the union rep reasonably fulfills the agreement's requirements -- here, advance notice, stating the location where official time will be used, and leaving a contact number -- you should approve the release.

In rare cases where workload will preclude a release, follow the agreement's procedures for such a scenario. Under the hypothetical provision above, you would give the union rep a written denial that would include the reason for the delay, and a statement of when the union representative would be released. Again, when in doubt, check with the LR office.

Tracking time. Keep track of official time used by the employees you supervise. Labor agreements often spell out a specific number of hours per year that union representatives can use for various duties, so it's important that the amount of time used be recorded. If you believe that the amount of time requested is approaching or has exceeded the agreed-upon amount, contact your LR expert.

Mistakes to avoid

Pointers to keep in mind when handling requests for LR official time:

Do not:

  • • Enforce the official time rules inconsistently. Be objective and keep your personal feelings out of the matter. Accept the concept of official time as a statutory or contractual right.
  • • Cut off an employee's use of official time or initiate discipline if you suspect the employee of using official time in excessive amounts or for invalid purposes. In such cases, you should contact your LR expert for help.
  • • Grant official time for "internal union business," such as the solicitation of union membership or elections of union officials. Official time is not available for such purposes, and you're on solid ground in denying it.

Ask FMG

Q. How much official time can a union representative use?

A. It depends on the contract. Some agreements allow for a specific bank of hours to be used, while many agreements simply allow for an amount that is "reasonable" for the relevant purpose. Check out your labor agreement or talk with your agency's labor relations experts to find the specific answer to this question.

Q. How should I rate the performance of a union official who uses 25 percent of work time on representational functions?

A. Assuming that is within the agreed-upon limits, you would treat the 75 percent of the time spent on regular duties as the entirety of the employee's time and rate accordingly. You may not penalize an employee's productivity rating because of the use of authorized official time.

Q. Am I required to authorize overtime for representational activity carried out after normal work hours?

A. If you've already assigned overtime work, such as a regular night shift, the employee may engage in representational activity. The employee may not work overtime simply to engage in representational activity.

Q. Am I expected to approve official time requests?

A. Most bargaining agreements call on managers to handle official time requests and set out the rules that must be followed in reviewing those requests. Generally, workload conditions and other relevant factors spelled out by the agreement will need to be taken into account when handling such requests.

Q. How much time is "reasonable"?

A. The answer depends on several factors, including the activity involved, the complexity of the work being performed, and how much time has been necessary to do the function in the past. And there's always common sense. In short, it's a judgment call. Using a "reasonable time" standard, as opposed to specific banks of time, can create problems -- you may think 10 hours is reasonable to meet with a grievant; the union may think 40 hours is more reasonable.

Q. What types of activities qualify for official time?

A. Grievance investigation and presentation, hearing attendance, meetings with management, bargaining, LR training, and, except in the Department of Defense, even lobbying members of Congress. However, this is not a complete list.

Q. What if an employee disobeys an order to remain on the job after a request for official time is denied?

A. Although every case must be judged based on the full set of facts involved, generally the employee is liable to a valid charge of insubordination or disobeying a direct order. It may ultimately be decided by an arbitrator.

Q. What can I do if an employee appears to be abusing official time?

A. If you suspect an employee of not using official time for valid purposes, or of using an unjustified or excessive amount of time, the first thing you should do is get your facts straight. Next, contact your LR expert for help. Don't just cut off the employee's use of official time or initiate discipline.
