2. Individual Claims for Relief

The final agency decision is binding on all class members and the agency must notify class members of the final decision within 10 calendar days of transmitting the decision to the class agent. See 29 CFR 1614.204(k). Under the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the administrative judge retains jurisdiction over the class complaint to resolve any disputes over individual relief. See 29 CFR 1614.204(l)(3). The amended version of Part 1614 further states that there is a presumption of individual entitlement that the agency can only rebut upon a showing of clear and convincing evidence that the individual is not entitled to the class relief. Under 29 CFR 1614.204(l), the notice of the final agency decision should advise all class members of the procedure for seeking individual relief:

(1) When discrimination is found, an agency must eliminate or modify the employment policy or practice out of which the complaint arose and provide individual relief, including an award of attorney's fees and costs, to the agent in accordance with 1614.501.

(2) When class-wide discrimination is not found, but it is found that the class agent is a victim of discrimination, 1614.501 shall apply. The agency shall also, within 60 days of the issuance of the final decision finding no class-wide discrimination, issue the acknowledgment of receipt of an individual complaint as required by 1614.106(d) and process in accordance with the provisions of subpart A of this part, each individual complaint that was subsumed into the class complaint.

(3) When discrimination is found in the final decision and a class member believes that he or she is entitled to individual relief, the class member may file a written claim with the head of the agency or its EEO Director within 30 days of receipt of notification by the agency of its final decision. Administrative judges shall retain jurisdiction over the complaint in order to resolve any disputed claims by class members. The claim must include a specific detailed showing that the claimant is a class member who was affected by the discriminatory policy or practice, and that this discriminatory action took place within the period of time for which class-wide discrimination was found in the final order. Where a finding of discrimination against a class has been made, there shall be a presumption of discrimination as to each member of the class. The agency must show by clear and convincing evidence that any class member is not entitled to relief. The administrative judge may hold a hearing or otherwise supplement the record on a claim filed by a class member. The agency or the Commission may find class-wide discrimination and order remedial action for any policy or practice in existence within 45 days of the agent's initial contact with the Counselor. Relief otherwise consistent with this Part may be ordered for the time the policy or practice was in effect. The agency shall issue a final decision on each such claim within 90 days of filing. Such decision must include a notice of the right to file an appeal or a civil action in accordance with subpart D of this part and the applicable time limits.

A class member, including a class representative, is not precluded from filing an individual complaint of discrimination over the same actions contested in the class action when the individual complaint alleges bases of discrimination not included in the class complaint. An award of relief in the individual complaint does not, per se, disqualify the class representative. As the Commission explained in Tarrats v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., 01960433 (1998):

With regard to the AJ's dismissal of Appellant Rivera as class agent, the Commission notes that Appellant Rivera has not raised his sex and age claims in the class complaint. Rather, on June 24, 1995, he filed an individual complaint alleging national origin, sex and age discrimination with regard to some of the same incidents as those raised in the class complaint. The Commission concludes that the mere existence of this individual complaint, without more, does not establish that Appellant Rivera's interests are antagonistic to those of the class. Rather, his individual complaint can be bifurcated, with his national origin claim being subsumed within the class complaint, and his age and sex claims processed as an individual complaint. The Commission notes that it has previously adopted the holding of several courts that an individual award of relief to a class representative, prior to the disposition of a class complaint, does not disqualify the class representative as long as his/her interest does not become antagonistic to the interest of the other class members. See Washington v. United States Department of Agriculture, EEOC Request No. 05890052 (May 12, 1989). See also Chisholm v. United States Postal Service, 665 F.2d 482, 492-93 (4th Cir. 1981) (the court noted that an employee who was awarded a promotion and front pay did not become disqualified from representing the class where there was no evidence to show that his interest became antagonistic to the interest of other class members); Jenkins v. United Gas Co., 400 F.2d 28 (5th Cir. 1968) (acceptance by class representative of promotion subsequent to the filing of a class complaint does not render the suit moot as to the representative individually or to the class he represents); Walker v. Jim Dandy Co., 638 F.2d 1330 (5th Cir. 1981) (it is inappropriate for the Court to dismiss a class claim simply because an individual claim by the class representative was previously adjudicated). Moreover, the fact that Appellant Tarrats is also serving as co-agent should further safeguard the interests of the class.
