2. Written Work Criticisms

Although a work discussion does not aggrieve an employee sufficiently to file an EEO complaint, a written letter of warning does. In Wilson v. Secretary of Navy, 01952753 (1996), the Commission found that a written warning advising the complainant he was a half-hour late and would either have to make up the time or charge it to leave was sufficient to render the complainant aggrieved. The Commission noted the agency failed to demonstrate that the letter was not being kept in agency records and would not be relied upon for future discipline.

In the same case, the Commission also determined that whether a notice of unacceptable performance aggrieves an employee depends on whether the document is placed into agency records that are available to anyone other than the officials issuing the notice. The Commission further observed in Wilson:

In the FAD the agency explained that the August 18, 1994 Notification of Unacceptable Performance at issue in allegation 1 was "not a final rating or rating of record, but an interim step in the process of determining an employee's correct rating." On appeal the agency argues that the Notification "served as a preliminary step to clarify appellant's performance requirements prior to conducting a final assessment of his performance." The agency has essentially described the August 18, 1994 Notification as a type of progress review. On appeal the agency argues that allegation 1 should be dismissed in accordance with § 1614.107(e) in addition to § 1614.107(a) (failure to state a claim).

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.107(e) provides that an agency shall dismiss a portion of a complaint that alleges that a proposal to take a personnel action, or other preliminary step to taking a personnel action, is discriminatory. The Section by Section Analysis that accompanied the issuance of 29 CFR Part 1614 states:

We intend [1614.107(e)] to require dismissal of complaints that allege discrimination in any preliminary steps that do not, without further action, affect the person; for example, progress reviews or improvement periods that are not a part of any official file on the employee.

57 Fed. Reg. 12643 (Apr. 10, 1992).

The Commission has explained that when determining whether a record is in "any official file on the employee," the concern is "with official records which could be available to personnel other than the personnel responsible for the challenged act." Jackson v. Central Intelligence Agency, EEOC Request No. 05931177 (June 23, 1994) (citation omitted). The August 18, 1994 Notification is included in the record but it does not indicate whether a copy of the Notification is part of any official file on appellant. After reviewing the record, the Commission finds that the record does not indicate whether the August 18, 1994 Notification is recorded in appellant's official file which is available to personnel other than the personnel responsible for issuing the Notification. Therefore, the Commission can not determine based on the record whether appellant is aggrieved under § 1614.107(a) or whether dismissal of allegation 1 is appropriate under § 1614.107(e). The Commission shall remand allegation 1 so that the agency can supplement the record with evidence (such as an affidavit from the person who issued the Notification) showing whether the August 18, 1994 Notification is recorded in appellant's official file which is available to personnel other than the personnel responsible for issuing the Notification.

See also Morano v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A02726 (2000).

Even though a letter of warning was rescinded and does not give rise to a claim against the agency, the letter can be used as background evidence for other viable discrimination claims. Ward v. Postmaster General, 01962443 (1996).

Although the Commission has held that a letter of counseling that does not become part of an employee's official personnel file generally will not give rise to a claim, such a letter can be the basis for a claim when it may be a consideration in future employment actions. In Norris v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01965766 (1997), the Commission found:

In this case, appellant alleged that he was subjected to discrimination when he received a letter of counseling dated March 26, 1996. According to the agency, appellant did not establish that he was harmed with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of his employment with the agency. We note, however, that the letter of counseling indicated that any further occurrences of unacceptable behavior on appellant's part might result in a letter of admonishment. In addition, although the record indicates that the letter of counseling was not placed in appellant's official personnel file, the letter of counseling was placed in Supervisor A's own files. Finally, according to appellant, his performance rating was lowered because of the counseling letter. Based on the foregoing, it appears that the letter of counseling was a consideration in future employment actions and could be a factor in future disciplinary actions. Accordingly, we find that appellant has established that the issuance of the letter of counseling of March 26, 1996, constituted harm to a term, condition, or privilege of his employment with the agency. Therefore, we find that the agency improperly dismissed appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim.

See also Bolton v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A05912 (2001) (written job expectation memo to the effect that the employee was expected to be honest and forthright in the future sufficiently aggrieved the employee for purposes of stating a claim); Daniels v. Secretary of Treasury, 01974323 (1998) ("We find that the written memorandum, containing derogatory comments regarding appellant's work performance affects a term, condition or privilege of appellant's employment. Therefore, the complaint states a claim."); Bowling v. Secretary of Navy, 01975123 (1998) (a claim is stated where the letter of counseling was retained by the supervisor and could be used in future discipline). But see Drummond v. Secretary of Army, 10961110 (1997) ("When determining whether a record is in 'any official file on the employee,' the concern is 'with official records which could be available to personnel other than the personnel responsible for the challenged act.'") Based on Daniels and Bowling, it is not clear that Drummond remains good law.

In a case of some interest because of the use of modern technology, the Commission found an appellant's allegation that email messages were created in an attempt to support future disciplinary action against him stated a claim. In Patterson v. Secretary of Navy, 01965255 (1997), the Commission held:

In this case, appellant alleged that he was subjected to discrimination when Person A wrote e-mail messages accusing appellant of refusing assignments and unprofessional behavior. According to appellant, Person A and Person B, appellant's second-line supervisor, were trying to develop a record to support future disciplinary actions against appellant. The record indicates that the e-mail messages were forwarded to the Civilian Personnel Office. Under the circumstances, we find that the alleged harassment affected a term, condition, or privilege of appellant's employment with the agency. Therefore, the agency's dismissal was improper.

Although the Commission did not discuss its finding to any degree, it could well be that it was persuaded that the complainant suffered harm because the email messages were transmitted to the personnel officer. In a footnote, the Commission observed that the record was unclear on whether the messages made their way into the complainant's official personnel file.
