C. HOURS REASONABLY EXPENDED

"[I]t does not always follow that the amount of time actually expended is the amount of time reasonably expended...'billing judgment' is an important component in fee setting, and hours that would not be properly billed to a private client are also not properly billed to an agency pursuant to a successful EEO claim." Velva B. v. Postmaster General, 2020002159 (2022). The prevailing party's attorney must review the time spent and make a good faith effort to exclude hours that were "excessive, redundant or otherwise unnecessary." Id.; In re Reitan, 01832610 (1985); see also Toliver v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01890107 (1989).

The starting point here, of course, is the number of hours actually expended in representation. As a practical matter, time sheets should be kept on a daily basis. A minimal entry should include the date the service was performed, a brief description of the service performed, and the amount of time it took to perform the service. Billing practices vary with some lawyers using tenths of an hour and others using quarters of an hour.

Once it is determined how many hours the lawyer actually spent in representation, a determination will be made, by the agency or the Commission, as to the number of hours that it was reasonable to expend in the representation. In the ideal case, the number of hours spent and the number of hours reasonably expended will coincide. However, hours can be excluded for a number of reasons, including the fact that the hours were spent pursuing a claim on which the complainant was not successful, or the hours exceed those deemed reasonable by the agency or the Commission to perform the particular service. In the first instance, the burden is clearly upon the complainant's counsel to give a detailed accounting of the hours spent and the tasks involved. A "raw total of hours spent does not complete the inquiry. It does not follow that the amount of time actually expended is the amount of time reasonably expended." Atienza v. Secretary of Navy, 01872727 (1988), quoting Copeland v. Marshall, 641 F.2d 880, 891 (D.C. Cir. 1980).

The Commission will consider standard billing practices in private practice to determine whether the hours would have been billed to the client if he or she had not been the prevailing party. See also Knott v. Postmaster General, 0720100049 (2011), rejecting agency's contention that hours were excessive noting that counsel had exercised "billing judgment" that did not charge for some time spent in the case.

When a fee is reduced because of excessive hours, it is not necessary for the Commission to "perform a detailed analysis to determined [sic] precisely the number of hours or types of work for which no compensation is allowed; rather, it is appropriate to reduce the hours claimed by an across-the-board reduction." Finch v. Postmaster General, 05880051 (1988) (citing Brown v. Gillette Co., 34 FEP 1828, 1833 (D. Mass. 1982)); Copeland v. Marshall, 641 F.2d 880, 903 (D.C. Cir. 1980); Bradford v. Blum, 507 F. Supp. 526, 534 (S.D.N.Y. 1981).

When an agency excludes payment for hours on the grounds that those hours were not necessary and were duplicative of other work in the complaint, the agency must justify its denial with specific reference to instances of duplication or waste. See Berry v. Department of Army, 01841966 (1986). In this regard, the Commission noted that establishing the critical element of pretext in a discrimination case "may well require more than normal preparation and execution." Id; see also McClendon v. Postmaster General, 01883431 (1989) ("Arbitrary reductions of hours claimed in pursuit of EEO complaints conflict with the agency's obligations under the regulations."). In cases where an agency has disallowed hours as unreasonable without providing an explanation, the Commission has remanded the case to give the agency an opportunity to explain the reduction. See Tate v. Secretary of Air Force, 01931143 (1993). Hours denied by agencies are more likely to be upheld on appeal where the agency gives a detailed explanation of its reasons for reducing the hours. See Webb v. Secretary of Army, 01963056 (1998). But, in the first instance, the complainant bears the burden of submitting sufficiently detailed information to justify the hours expended. See Davis v. Secretary of Navy, 01961461 (1998) (time sheets failed to sufficiently detail how hours were expended due to the vague nature of entries).

Although the agency must support any reduction in the hours with specific instances of unnecessary or duplicative work, it is not necessary for the agency to go through the fee statement line by line and make reductions in the hours. Just as the Commission can engage in an across-the-board percentage reduction in the number of hours, so, too, can agencies. See Abbate v. Secretary of Navy, 01971418 (2000). In that case, the Commission criticized the agency's item-by-item approach to reducing the claimed hours, but still supported a 40% reduction in those hours, citing specific examples of excessive charges.

Every lawyer knows that advance preparation is essential to providing good representation. Of course, as in all else, there are extremes. In Martin v. Department of Education, 01850737 (1987), the Commission found that the agency properly disallowed hours expended in the preparation of an expert witness. Counsel was overeager in preparing the expert while the case was still under investigation by the agency and before a proposed disposition had been issued.

On the other hand, a skilled advocate will not be penalized for zealously representing a client. In Thomas v. Secretary of Navy, 01985094 (1999), the Commission rejected an argument that the number of hours expended should be reduced because not all of the hours were spent on tasks required by order of the administrative judge or by Commission regulation. The Commission soundly rejected the agency's contention that "mere adequacy" would have been sufficient in the case. In explaining its decision in Thomas, the Commission wrote:

A review of the record in this case reveals that counsel provided appellant with zealous representation which achieved favorable results. While the agency contends that counsel failed to exercise proper billing judgment and the fee requested should be reduced accordingly, a careful review of the particulars of this matter demonstrates that the agency's contentions lack merit. Specifically, the Commission finds unpersuasive the contention that all hours expended on a trial brief should be struck merely because such a brief was not mandated by Regulation or by Order of the AJ. In addition, a review of the record leads the Commission to believe that counsel's ardent protection of his client's interests in the reselection matter was largely responsible for appellant's placement in a comparable position with full back pay and interest. In this regard, the Commission notes the agency's truculent attitude persists, as demonstrated by its assertion that appellant prevailed only because the agency neglected to issue its initial FAD in a timely manner. Counsel's thoroughness is apparent in the comprehensiveness of the briefs filed on appeal, each of which was well-researched and appended copies of all relevant background documents. These briefs and the other work performed by counsel may perhaps be overly meticulous in some instances. However, a reduction in the hours expended is never compelled simply because mere adequacy may have been sufficient. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the agency erred in striking 47 hours from the fee request.

In a footnote in Thomas, the Commission further observed:

As an example of its truculence, the Commission notes that in its FAD, the agency stated that the check in payment of the $25,800 in fees which the agency determined was due "will be issued only after the appeal periods involved have run, and no appeal has been filed by you," although the agency also offered as an "alternative...to issue a check for the undisputed portion of your requested fee and withhold the remainder pending any appeal by you." The Commission advises the agency that it views this language as an attempt to chill appellant from exercising his right to appeal the FAD. As a matter of public policy, an agency should issue a check for the amount of fees and costs which it has determined to be due contemporaneously with issuance of the FAD, and not attempt to tie issuance of the check to the failure to file an appeal.

Similarly, in Swanson v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720100026 (2011), the Commission upheld a fee award of over $227,000 noting:

[W]e note that the AJ put great emphasis on what she observed in overseeing the litigation between these two parties. She personally found the litigation to be extremely complex and described the level of research and writing required "extraordinary in all respects." Specifically the AJ found that Complainant faced not only the Agency's "overwhelming power, force [and] resources," but also its "obstructionism and failure to cooperate."

Those who practice regularly in the field of federal employment law know that a great deal of preparation time is spent understanding the work done by the agency and the particular persons involved in an individual dispute. At times, this means acquiring specialized or technical knowledge in order to fully understand the nature of the dispute. At other times, it simply means decoding the sometimes arcane and byzantine ways of the federal government. Time spent in such a manner is compensable, assuming it is reasonable in the context of a given case. See, e.g., Gatewood v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01976511 (1999) (awarding fees for time spent by counsel developing a detailed understanding of dental procedures and technology).

The hours expended must be in the course of a Title VII "proceeding." Patney v. Department of Air Force, 01861636 (1987). Contacts with a congressional representative are not part of the proceeding even when Title VII issues are raised.

Time records submitted in support of a fee petition must be sufficiently specific to permit an evaluation of the time spent on individual tasks. The burden is on the complainant to submit such information in the first instance and not on the agency to request clarification if the initial submission is insufficient. Childs v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01994951 (2002).

Discrimination cases present unique circumstances and, while categories of cases may have similar characteristics, each case must be approached on an individual basis. In Byrd v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A20706 (2003), the Commission rejected an agency argument that a fee reduction was warranted because the hours expended by counsel were excessive compared to other cases with similar fact patterns. "The agency's determination ignores the individual circumstances and requirements of each case, fails to explain what specific work was 'excessive,' or give recognition for other factors, such as counsel's high level of success." Id.
