B. CIVIL RIGHTS ACT OF 1991

As set forth in Price Waterhouse, an employer's proof that it would have taken the same personnel action even absent discrimination was a total defense. If the employer prevailed in such a defense, it was absolved of any liability to the employee. In passing the 1991 Civil Rights Act, Congress altered the Price Waterhouse decision by permitting a partial remedy in applicable cases. See 42 USC 2000e-5(g)(2)(B). Under the Act, a complainant may receive injunctive relief and attorney fees. However, reinstatement and back pay may not be part of the remedy.

In Hannon v. Chater, 887 F. Supp. 1303 (N.D. Cal. 1995), the court further explained how the 1991 Civil Rights Act impacted upon the Price Waterhouse decision. In that case, the agency admitted that one of the reasons it chose a female selectee over the male plaintiff was because of her gender, although the female selectee also was well qualified for the position. The Hannon court, 887 F. Supp. at 1314, explained the amendment to 42 USC 2000e-5(g)(2)(B):

The 1991 amendment was designed to reverse in part the holding in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989), wherein the Supreme Court ruled that in a mixed motive case a "defendant may avoid liability. . .by proving by a preponderance of the evidence that it would have made the same decision even if it had not taken the plaintiff's gender into account." 490 U.S. at 258, 109 S. Ct. at 1795. The superseding legislation precludes an employer from escaping liability altogether in a mixed motive case. However, pursuant to § 107(b) of the 1991 Civil Rights At, 105 Stat. at 1075-76, the available relief in a mixed motive case may be more limited:

"On a claim in which an individual proves a violation under section 2000e-2(m) of this title and a respondent demonstrates the respondent would have taken the same action in the absence of the impermissible motivating factor, the court--(i) may grant declaratory relief (except as provided in clause (ii)), and attorney's fees and costs demonstrated to be directly attributable only to the pursuit of a claim under section 2000e-2(m) of this title; and (ii) shall not award damages or issue an order requiring any admission, reinstatement, hiring, promotion, or payment. . ."

42 USC § 2000e-5(g)(2)(B); e.g., Riess v. Dalton, 845 F. Supp. 742, 744 (S.D. Cal. 1993) ("Section 107(b). . .provides limited equitable relief, including attorney's fees and costs, for a Plaintiff who proves a Section 107(a) violation.") see also 42 USC § 2000e-16(d) (applying same rule to federal employers).

The term "motivating factor" used in the 1991 legislation is the same term used, interchangeably with "motivating part," by the Supreme Court in Price Waterhouse, supra, 490 U.S. at 249-50, 109 S. Ct. at 1790. Its derivation can be traced to the Court's analogous constitutional jurisprudence. See id., at 248, 109 S. Ct. at 1789-90. ("We have reached a similar conclusion in other contexts where the law announces that a certain characteristic is irrelevant to the allocation for benefits and burdens."), citing Mt. Healthy City Bd. of Ed. v. Doyle, 429 U.S. 274, 287, 97 S. Ct. 568, 576, 50 L. Ed.2d 471 (1977) (equating "motivating" with "substantial" factor). . . .

See also Sheppard v. Riverview Nursing Ctr. Inc., 88 F.3d 1332 (4th Cir. 1996) (holding that the 1991 Civil Rights Act alters Price Waterhouse mixed-motive analysis for liability purposes).

The Commission has also held that the 1991 Civil Rights Act modified Price Waterhouse so that, in a mixed motive case, Title VII liability is established once the complainant demonstrates that discriminatory remarks were made and there is a connection between those statements and the challenged employment action. Even where the employer then shows it was also motivated by legitimate reasons, the complainant is entitled to some relief. However, the scope of that relief is limited to declaratory relief and attorney fees. In Parvin v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05950017 (1996), the Commission found that the 1991 Civil Rights Act reversed, in part, the Supreme Court's holding in Price Waterhouse:

For the appropriate analysis of the evidence in this complaint, appellant cites to, inter alia, Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228 (1989). The Civil Rights Act of 1991 (CRA), which became effective November 21, 1991, reversed the Price Waterhouse approach to liability. Under the CRA, an agency is liable for discrimination once a prohibited basis is found to be a motivating factor in an employment decision. Because the COS made the discriminatory statement, and the statement is linked directly and closely with the actions he took against appellant, we find that appellant's national origin was a motivating factor in the actions. Pursuant to the CRA, liability is established once the trier of fact has accepted direct evidence. EEOC Policy Guidance on Recent Developments in Disparate Treatment Theory, No. 915.002, July 14, 1992, Section III.

The Commission has taken the position that once an appellant proves that discrimination was a motivating factor, the burden of proof shifts to the employer to establish that it would have taken the same action absent the discrimination. See 1992 EEOC Policy Guidance, supra. There is proof in this case of legitimate motives in addition to the unlawful motive. We find most persuasive the reports from two site visits conducted during the relevant period, as they were conducted by individuals from outside the facility. They establish the presence of problems in appellant's Service that the COS averred he was trying to correct. They also corroborate the COS' view that appellant was responsible for the problems. While appellant claims that the problems in his Service were caused not by him but because of the lack of staff, funding, and time, the report of the first visit, in March 1992, did not fully agree. It was accepted that the Service needed more clerical help, but the physician conducting the March review opined that the technical workload of the unit was not too great. He also reported low morale in the Service that appeared to him to be due to a perceived lack of leadership and guidance, and a lack of recognition and positive feedback. His report also specifically corroborates the COS' estimation of the Service, in stating that the COS' "concern about the administrative skills or interests of the radiologists is echoed universally by the other departmental staff members and the referring clinicians. The reviewer conducting the July 1992 report was more emphatic: "[T]he Chief of Radiology has abdicated any responsibility for and performs no substantive activity related to the actual operation of the service other than reading radiographic studies."

Appellant dismisses these reports in his RTR as fabrications directed by the COS, terming the second one "the proverbial smoke and mirrors." The reports do show that the COS requested these reviews of the Radiology Service, but nothing in the record supports appellant's implication that the outside reviewers slanted their reports because of the COS' influence. Appellant focuses on the statements of several of the deponents in this case. He points out that several believe that foreign-born employees are not given the same considerations as Anglo- or American-born individuals. As the previous decision noted, four physicians testified to the conflict between the COS and appellant, each making favorable comments about appellant's past performance and criticizing the COS' management style. Several of the deponents also stated that they too were being treated badly by the COS, although only two attributed the treatment to their national origin. The Chief of Surgery who, like appellant, was a Chief of Service, stated in his deposition his opinion that the COS treated Asian doctors more harshly, and gave an example of what we agree would, if true, be an entirely inappropriate comment regarding the surgeon's age. In that regard, however, it does not appear that any adverse employment actions were taken against the Chief of Surgery. While these depositions make clear that the staff disliked the COS' management style, they do not detract from the evidence showing problems with appellant's performance. Based on the record evidence, we find that the COS' performance-based actions against appellant, i.e., allegations 2 and 5, were motivated by more than discrimination; his performance was also a motivating factor. The Commission finds that the same actions regarding appellant's performance would have been taken absent the discrimination.

Under these circumstances, the relief permitted pursuant to the CRA is limited. For example an employer making this showing is not required under the CRA to pay compensatory damages, or to be subject to any order requiring backpay or reinstatement. It is, however, required to pay appropriate attorney's fees. See 1992 EEOC Policy Guidance, supra. Here, appellant's requested relief included revision of his proficiency rating, but since we find that the rating would have been the same even absent discrimination, such relief would not be appropriate under the CRA. However, the Commission does find that the declaratory relief of a posted notice is appropriate in this case, and the award of attorney's fees may be appropriate. We so order below.
