RETURN TO WORK

It may take a while for an employee to make a full recovery from a workplace injury, and some may never return to gainful employment. You might even be tempted to urge the employee back to work as quickly as possible. But it's important that you remain flexible and not force the employee to come back before they are fully recovered from their injury.

Offering the returning employee a modified or limited-duty job has benefits for your agency and the employee. The employee returns to the workplace, which often accelerates the healing process, while the agency reduces the workers' compensation benefits it would otherwise have to pay out.

Most medical restrictions limit the number of hours an employee can work each day or the work functions they can perform. Ideally, if the employee cannot return to full duty, you should look at modifying their position to bring it within their medical restrictions. They can work in a temporary, alternate work assignment until they are able to return to their full duties.

You should let injured employees know upfront that they will be returning to work and that they have a valuable contribution to make. By being proactive, you can avoid having to replace injured workers and train new ones, saving administrative and supervisory time and cost to your agency.

PEER Initiative

The White House announced the Protecting Employees, Empowering Reemployment Initiative on Jan. 9, 2020. One of the goals of the initiative is to increase the rate of return to work for federal employees. Agencies must coordinate with the Department of Labor's Occupational Safety and Health Administration and the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs to develop strategies aimed at achieving this goal.

Some ways you can help your employees successfully return to work after an injury include:

  • • When an employee is injured, take immediate action to start the workers' compensation process.
  • • Communicate with the employee, the workers' comp specialist, HR, and the physicians involved.
  • • Coordinate with disability and safety staff to ensure that reasonable accommodations, if needed, address the employee's injury.
  • • Modify the employee's position to meet their medical restrictions even if it is temporary.

Communication

Arguably, the most important aspect of returning an employee to work involves the communication between you, the workers' comp specialist, HR, the employee, and the physicians involved.

Make the employee -- and their doctor -- aware from the first day of injury that your goal is to return them to work.

Contact the employee regularly to let them know that they are missed at work. Offer your assistance with any job or claim concerns. Employees are frequently anxious about what is happening with their jobs and futures.

Bringing an employee back to a positive environment will help your employee continue on the road to recuperation and help in the adjustment to job rehabilitation.

Remember: Following up with an employee is a responsibility and not harassment. However, managers need to be sensitive to employee concerns. Let the employee know what will happen and when it will happen. This way the employee will understand your motives.

Medical information

Along with the agency's workers' comp specialist, you are entitled to obtain detailed medical documentation of the employee's current condition for the purpose of returning the employee to work. For example, you may monitor the employee's medical progress and duty status by obtaining periodic medical reports. To aid in returning an injured employee to suitable employment, you can also contact the employee's physician, in writing, concerning the employee's work limitations and possible job assignments. You must be careful because mishandling your employees' medical documentation can lead to liability under the Rehabilitation Act and Americans with Disabilities Act, regardless of whether the employee actually has a disability.

The law requires agencies and managers to:

  • • Maintain confidentiality, subject to limited exceptions.
  • • Keep medical documentation in a file separate from the personnel file.

Extent of confidentiality

As a general rule, supervisors and managers have the right to know about necessary work restrictions or accommodations. You do not have a right to more medical information than is necessary and may not disclose it except under the following circumstances:

  1. 1. First aid and safety personnel may be told if the disability might require emergency treatment.
  2. 2. Government officials may be given information necessary to investigate the agency's compliance with the Rehabilitation Act.
  3. 3. The information may, in certain circumstances, be disclosed to workers' compensation offices or insurance carriers.
  4. 4. Agency EEO officials may be given the information to maintain records and evaluate and report on the agency's performance in processing reasonable accommodation requests.
  5. 5. The information may be disclosed to those making the decision whether to grant a reasonable accommodation.

Even if you think one of these exceptions apply, you must check with HR before disclosing medical information because you may be subject to additional restrictions or procedures.

Remember: You are prohibited from revealing medical information to employees, coworkers, peers, or other individuals who do not qualify for an exception because they do not have a need to know. For example, the disclosure of an employee's medical diagnosis and symptoms to managers who do not need to know constitutes a prohibited medical disclosure. However, a general disclosure indicating that an employee is medically disqualified for a position does not constitute a disclosure of confidential medical information.

Safeguarding and storing medical information

It is also critical that you collect medical information on separate forms and maintain them in separate medical files that are treated as confidential. Accidentally revealing an employee's medical information because of a failure to maintain separate, confidential files will not protect you from liability.

Returning to work after a recovery period

Federal regulations require that agencies restore current and former employees who have fully or partially recovered from work-related injuries. This is based on medical reports provided by the physician.

Light-duty work. A temporary light-duty assignment can be provided to the employee during a period of recovery while his work restrictions are temporary in nature. For example, an employee with temporary lifting restrictions can perform duties that do not involve lifting until he is medically cleared to return to full duty. A temporary assignment may also be provided to the employee when he has permanent restrictions and the agency is actively pursuing permanent employment opportunities to accommodate those restrictions. Generally, the length of a period of light duty is determined by the medical restrictions provided by the employee's health care provider.

If the employee held a temporary position at the time of injury, a temporary offer of at least 90 days can be found suitable.

Fully recovered within one year. A current or former employee who fully recovers within a year from the date of eligibility for compensation is entitled to be restored immediately and unconditionally to their former position or an equivalent one in their local commuting area.

Fully recovered after one year. A current or former employee, whose full recovery takes longer than one year from the date of eligibility for compensation, is entitled to priority consideration for restoration to their former position or an equivalent one, as long as their application is made within 30 days of DOL notification.

Partially recovered. Managers must make every effort to restore, in the local commuting area, an individual who has partially recovered and is able to return to work in some capacity. This could mean reengineering their former position, if feasible, or placing them in another position that is within their work capabilities.

Disqualified for former position. An employee who is physically disqualified from performing the duties of their former position or an equivalent position because of a work injury is entitled to be placed in another position that will provide them the same status and pay, or the nearest approximation thereof, consistent with the circumstances in each case. The DOL will determine if additional benefits will be paid for wage loss.

If the residual effects of the injury prevent the employee from returning to full duty in the position they held at the time of injury, a formal written job offer must be issued for a different position. Contact your workers' compensation specialist for assistance in this area.

The rights of returning employees. Once the employee returns to work, the entire time during which they were receiving compensation is credited to them for the purposes of within-grade increases, retention purposes, and other rights and benefits based upon length of service.

An employee who returns to the agency in their former position or an equivalent position within one year from the date compensation began will resume all attendant rights they would have had, or acquired, had they not been injured or disabled. This includes the rights to tenure, promotion, and safeguards in reductions in force.

Note: If the work-related disability extends beyond one year, the agency may remove the employee based on their physical inability to perform the duties of the position. However, the employee will continue to receive compensation from the OWCP as long as the work-related disability exists and is supported by medical documentation. Also, the employee has restoration rights when fully recovered.

Mistakes to avoid

Managers are a critical component in returning an injured employee to work. Follow these tips for return-to-work success.

Do not:

  • • Bring an employee back to work before they are ready. Recovery periods vary, so it's important that you remain flexible and bring the employee back to work only when they are ready. Skipping steps or unreasonably accelerating the process can do more harm than good.
  • • Push an employee beyond their physical limitations or allow them to perform tasks outside their medical restrictions. Doing so can cause reinjury or delay recovery.
  • • Keep an employee's medical information in the same file as their personnel information. You must keep all medical information confidential and stored separately.
  • • Forget that the returning employee has rights. Any employee who returns to the agency in their former position within one year of the date compensation began will resume all attendant rights they would have had, or acquired, had they not been injured or disabled. If a position cannot be located in the employee's former commuting area, their restoration rights are agencywide.
  • • Go overboard to accommodate employees. Accommodation requests must be reasonable under the circumstances and must not cause undue hardship on the agency.
  • • Bring back an employee who simply cannot perform the essential tasks of an available position. You need to keep in mind the effect bringing back an employee will have on all the other workers.
  • • Work alone to bring back the employee. Make sure that you are in constant contact with a workers' comp specialist and your HR office. This is a team effort and you should not make decisions on your own.

Ask FMG

Q. What type of medical documentation do I need to return an employee to work?

A. You are entitled to obtain detailed medical documentation of the employee's current condition so that you can make an informed decision about returning them to work. However, you may only contact the employee's physician in writing. If your agency has a medical officer, you may want to contact that person for assistance. Check with your workers' compensation specialist. The medical documentation should include:

  • • A firm diagnosis of the injury.
  • • A current course of treatment (physical or occupational rehabilitation, medications, number of office visits, etc.).
  • • A prognosis of recovery (when the person is expected to recover).
  • • Current work capabilities.

Q. What kind of temporary work can I assign?

A. You can assign almost any work that needs to be done in your unit that is within the employee's work restrictions and abilities.

Q. Can constantly following up with an employee be considered harassment?

A. Simply following up with an employee is a responsibility and not harassment. However, you should be sensitive to employee concerns. Let the employee know what will happen and when it will happen. This way, the employee understands your motives.

Q. What rights will the employee have when they return to work?

A. An employee who returns to the agency in their former position or an equivalent position within one year from the date compensation began will resume all attendant rights they would have had, or acquired, had they not been injured or disabled. This includes the rights to tenure, promotion, and safeguards in reductions in force.

Q. What if an employee never fully recovers from their injury?

A. Managers must make every effort to restore, in the local commuting area, an individual who has partially recovered and is able to return to work in some capacity. This could mean reengineering their former position, if feasible, or placing them in another position that is within their work capabilities.

Q. Are returning employees covered by disability discrimination laws?

A. Yes, all federal employees are entitled to the protections of the Rehabilitation Act. The Rehabilitation Act protects qualified individuals who have substantial limitations of their major life activities. This may include employees who are returning to work after a work-related injury or illness. Employees who are qualified under the Rehabilitation Act may be entitled to reasonable accommodations that will enable them to perform the essential functions of their jobs, such as task modifications, scheduling changes, and rest periods. This area of the law is complicated. Be sure to contact your HR and EEO office for advice.

Q. Does the term "disability" have different meaning for workers' compensation and EEO purposes?

A. Yes. For workers' compensation purposes, disability refers to an employee's inability, because of an employment injury, to earn the wages they were receiving at the time of the injury. For disability discrimination and reasonable accommodation purposes, an individual with a disability is a person who: 1) has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of the person's major life activities; 2) has a record of such an impairment; or 3) is regarded as having such an impairment. If you aren't sure about these differences, consult your workers' compensation specialist or your EEO specialist.
