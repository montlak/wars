32. Telefax Filing

The Commission has approved the filing of documents, such as complaints, by telefax transmission. For example, in DeBerry v. Secretary of Navy, 01966379 (1997), the Commission found that the complaint was timely filed, even though it was received by the agency via facsimile on the final date after normal business hours:

Alternatively, even if the appellant, or his attorney if the appellant was represented by an attorney at the time, received the Notice of Final Interview on March 27, 1995, the record demonstrates that the appellant's attorney submitted the appellant's formal complaint to the agency by facsimile transmission on April 11, 1995, the 15th calendar day following March 27, 1995. See 29 CFR § 1614.605(a). The Commission repeatedly has treated the date of a facsimile transmittal as the date a document is filed with the Commission even though the Commission's regulations do not expressly provide for filing by facsimile transmission. See, e.g., Brophy v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05960376 (December 19, 1996) (appeal); Bobo v. Department of the Interior, EEOC Request No. 05950383 (December 14, 1995) (request for reconsideration); Stein v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05940410 (November 18, 1994) (appeal). Consistent with these decisions, the Commission finds that the date of the appellant's facsimile transmittal was the date of filing. The Commission observes that under this application of the Commission's regulation to a technological advance, a complainant must still submit a complaint to the agency within the 15 day period as required by regulation. The agency, far from being disadvantaged by this application of the regulation, benefits from the instant receipt of a facsimile transmittal, rather than being required to wait several days for mail delivery of the formal complaint.

The agency contends that the appellant's attorney transmitted the complaint to the agency after the normal duty hours of the EEO Office and that, therefore, the complaint was not received and processed until April 12, 1995, one day beyond the 15 day period for filing a complaint. As set forth above, 29 CFR § 1614.106(a) requires that a formal complaint be filed with the "agency" that allegedly discriminated against the complainant. The Commission repeatedly has held in cases involving the initiation of a filing period, that it is the date of the agency's receipt of a document, not the date of the receipt of the document by a particular office or official within the agency, that triggers the filing period. Klippness v. Department of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05920517 (August 6, 1992), and cases cited therein. The Commission has applied this principle to a case involving the expiration of a filing period, finding that any delay caused by the agency's internal mail procedures, whether it occurs at the beginning or at the end of a filing period, is insufficient reason to justify waiver or equitable tolling of a filing deadline. Gray v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05950168 (February 8, 1996) (request for reconsideration). Consistent with these decisions, the Commission finds that the agency received the appellant's formal complaint on April 11, 1995, the date that the agency's facsimile machine received the facsimile transmission, whether or not the agency's EEO Office was open at the time.

See also Phillips v. Secretary of Navy, 01974075 (1998).

Of course, modern electronics are not infallible, and it was only a matter of time before the issue of the timeliness of a complaint surfaced where the facsimile transmission failed. In order to establish that the complaint is timely, the complainant must produce evidence of the failed transmission. As the Commission explained in Carr v. Postmaster General, 01A22447 (2002):

While the agency FAD indicates that the Notice was received on or before December 9, 2001, the certificate of delivery clearly indicates the complainant's attorney received it on December 11, 2001. The Notice itself is dated December 10, 2001, and therefore could not have been received by complainant on December 9, 2001. Although the Notice indicated that complainant had to file a formal complaint within fifteen (15) calendar days of its receipt, complainant did not file her formal complaint until January 11, 2002, which is beyond the limitation period regardless of which date the complainant received the Notice. While the FAD indicates that the complainant's attorney unsuccessfully attempted to facsimile the complainant's formal complaint on December 24, 2001, there is no record of the attempt or record of a successful attempt on a later date. In Gaines v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05970321 (June 12, 1997), the Commission indicates that a facsimile that was received two days late was acceptable because the attempt failed on the proper date and the following day was a holiday. The present case necessitates the opposite holding, however, because in the present case there is no evidence of a successful transmission of the facsimile containing the formal complaint. On appeal, complainant has not offered adequate justification to warrant an extension of the time limit for filing the complaint, and has not offered any proof of her attempt to file the formal complaint within the 15 day time period.

In Kelley v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120111169 (2011), the use of an electronic fax service caused some confusion because the service used Greenwich Mean Time for all faxes. The Commission found the complaint was timely filed where the fax service indicated it was filed one day late because of the use of GMT, but the complaint was filed with the agency's EEO office on Central Standard Time where it was several hours earlier and still the previous day.
