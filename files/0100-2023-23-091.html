1. Investigations

The burden is on the complainant to demonstrate that he or she is a qualified individual with a disability. However, the agency has its own corresponding obligation to conduct a thorough and impartial investigation. Specifically, the investigator must gather detailed information on whether the complainant has an impairment, whether the impairment affects a major life activity, and whether any effect is a substantial limitation of a major life activity.

As the Commission explained in Serna v. Postmaster General, 0120053789 (2007):

The investigator is required to conduct a thorough investigation-identifying and obtaining all relevant evidence from all sources regardless of how it may affect the outcome. EEOC Management Directive (MD)-110, p. 6-8 (Nov. 9, 1999). Therefore, an investigator must exhaust those sources of information likely to support the positions of complainant and the agency. Id.

In particular, in investigating a claim of disability discrimination, the agency must ensure that the investigator "asks the right questions" of complainant, ones designed to elicit pertinent evidence on the threshold issues of whether complainant has an impairment; whether it affects a major life activity; and whether it substantially limits a major life activity. See Can v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A43665 (May 18, 2006).

While the agency asserts complainant was not substantially limited in a major life activity, the record does not establish that the major life activity of working was considered. The major life activity of working should be considered when the evidence does not support a finding that an individual is substantially limited with respect to any other major life activity. See Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans with Disabilities Act, Appendix to 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j). The inability to perform a single, particular job does not constitute a substantial limitation in the major life activity of working. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(3)(i). To be substantially limited in the major life activity or working, one must be precluded from more than one type of job, a specialized job or a particular job of choice. Sutton v. United Airlines, Inc., 527 U.S. 471 (1999). Complainant must prove that he was substantially limited in working in a class of jobs or a broad range of jobs in various classes. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(3)(i).

In the instant matter, complainant claims in his affidavit that he was unable to perform work outdoors. In addition, the Postmaster at the Richmond, Texas facility asserted in his affidavit that complainant's restriction was that he could not work outdoors at all. The Commission has stated that the exclusionary criterion of being able to do work outside is not unique to the position of letter carrier, and could disqualify a complainant from a broad range of outdoor positions. See Cordero v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01963946 (March 13, 1997).

However, the instant record does not contain sufficient evidence to determine whether complainant was substantially limited in the major life activity of working. The medical documentation referenced above provides, in pertinent part, that complainant had a "low tolerance to heat exposure" and a "low tolerance to activity (outdoors)." However, the medical documentation lacks specificity. We note, for example, that the medical documentation does not indicate whether or not complainant can perform any work outside, or can only work outside in certain temperatures, or can only work outdoors for a certain amount of time per day. Based on these circumstances, we remand this matter to the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation in accordance with the Order below.

See also Bartuseck v. Postmaster General, 01A45204 (2006).
