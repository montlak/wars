8. Job Assignments

Details, temporary assignments, and special job assignments are usually of such short duration that they have expired long before an EEO complaint can wind its way through the process. Agencies generally take the position that once the detail or assignment has ended, the complaint is moot. That is not a view always shared by the EEOC. The Commission will review such assignments to determine if there are any lingering effects that could continue to impact a complainant's career development.

In Hill v. General Services Administration, 05890383 (1989), relying on County of Los Angeles v. Davis, supra, the Commission found an allegation that a detail was discriminatory was moot where the Commission concluded that the appellant lost no pay or benefits as a result of the detail and the persons supervising the appellant at the time of the detail were no longer his supervisors. Although the Commission did not specifically indicate, presumably the latter fact satisfied the requirement that there was no reasonable expectation the alleged violation would recur. But, when an agency takes an action that allegedly impairs career advancement, a complaint is not rendered moot simply because the action only lasts for a finite period of time. For example, in Shelborne v. Postmaster General, 05941025 (1995), the complainant alleged that nonselection for a task force impaired her career development. The agency dismissed the case as moot because the task force had already completed its mission and disbanded. The Commission reversed finding that the nonselection would continue to have an impact on the complainant's career advancement.

In Oliver v. Postmaster General, 01997123 (2001), the Commission found that a return of the complainant to the oriGINAl position she held prior to reassignment did not moot her EEO complaint where she alleged the reassignment was not as desirable and that she lost her carpool arrangements because of the shift change.

Caitlyn H. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120160654 (2018), involved a defense Information systems specialist who alleged discrimination based on sex and age when she was paid less than her colleagues for performing the same work. Specifically, the complainant in Caitlyn H. relied on the following evidence:

Complainant alleged that there was no clear delineation in the work performed by GS-11 and GS-12 IT Specialists between 2011 and 2014. Complainant averred that all Service Desk employees answered calls for assistance at random and that there was only one GS-12 IT Specialist who performed duties that she did not perform. According to S3, he was Complainant's second-level supervisor until April 2012. S3 averred that while he supervised the Service Desk GS-12 employees were expected to provide a faster response and resolution of trouble tickets than GS-11 employees. S2 stated that he became Complainant's supervisor in April 2012. According to S2, in October 2012 he gave GS-12 IT Specialists additional responsibilities, and in October 2013 he implemented higher performance standards for GS-12 IT Specialists. Complainant stated that in late 2014 S2 attempted to create more of a division in the work performed by the different grade levels by designating GS-12 IT Specialists as subject matter experts (SMEs) in various areas.

The record contains the GS-11 and GS-12 IT Specialist position descriptions, and the duties and supervisory control levels are different for the positions. Complainant averred that sometimes on weekend shifts she works without a GS-12 IT Specialist present and that she is therefore responsible for additional responsibilities that would ordinarily be handled by a GS-12 employee. According to S2, Complainant received a performance award in 2014 for taking on more responsibility such as when she worked without a GS-12 IT Specialist present and prepared the daily brief at the end of her shift.

Before the Commission, the agency in Caitlyn H. argued that the complainant's claim was moot because her supervisory chain had changed and because the agency had instituted changes to differentiate GS-11 job duties from the GS-12 job duties, but the Commission disagreed:

As a preliminary matter, we note that Complainant's complaint is not moot merely because S3 is no longer in her supervisory chain and because S2 implemented changes to differentiate the work of the GS-11 and GS-12 IT Specialists. To determine whether the issues raised in complainant's complaint are moot, the factfinder must ascertain whether (1) it can be said with assurance that there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur; and (2) interim relief or events have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination. See County of Los Angeles v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 631 (1979); Kuo v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05970343 (July 10, 1998). When such circumstances exist, no relief is available and no need for a determination of the rights of the parties is presented.

Here, Complainant alleged that she was paid less than her coworkers for equal work for a period of three years. Although the changes to Complainant's supervisory chain and to differentiate workloads make it possible that there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur, there is no indication that interim relief or events have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination. If Complainant establishes that she was subjected to discrimination as alleged in her complaint, she might be entitled to compensatory damages. Therefore, Complainant's complaint is not moot, and we will address the merits.
