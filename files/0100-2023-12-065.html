5. Deceased Employees

If a pending complaint presents a claim for monetary relief, and there is no other obstacle to further processing of the claim, it will survive the death of the employee. Amongst the obstacles that can prevent further processing of the claim are the clarity of the allegations and the stage of the processing of the complaint. In Smith v. Secretary of Transportation, 05920530 (1993), the Commission explained:

As stated previously, subsequent to the agency's request to reopen and submission of its supporting arguments, the Commission received from the agency a "Suggestion of Death Upon the Record," indicating that appellant had died during the pendency of the request to reopen. The question thus becomes whether appellant's death results in the inability to further process her complaint.

At the outset, we note that appellant's claim is not extinguished merely because of her death. Generally, a federal employee's discrimination complaint that has the potential for monetary relief will survive the death of the complainant. See, e.g., Phillips v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05910930 (January 31, 1992); Hanley v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01890296 (April 11, 1989); Clig v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05850281 (June 23, 1986). Here, however, our review of the particular circumstances of appellant's complaint leads us to conclude that it would be futile to further process this complaint.

We emphasize that our decision is based on the specific facts of appellant's case. In addition to the early point at which processing of appellant's case ceased, we note that the record contains absolutely no identification of specific promotions that were allegedly denied appellant. Rather, appellant merely made the general allegation that she had been denied promotional opportunities over a period of several years. Although there were two individuals interviewed by the EEO counselor who appeared to have some knowledge related to appellant's allegations, neither of these persons (at least one of whom is no longer an employee) specified particular promotions that appellant had been denied. Therefore, given appellant's death and the vagueness of her complaint allegations, we find that it would be difficult, if not impossible, for the agency to further process her complaint. Accordingly, we reopen the prior decision to vacate its order remanding the case.

Cf. Trujillo-Lucero v. Secretary of Air Force, 01990392 (1999) (case remanded for further processing where allegations sufficiently clear to raise hostile environment claim). According to the Commission in Wilbert W. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120160204 (2016), the complainant's death prior to resolution of the case does not necessarily render a complaint moot even where the complainant did not specifically request compensatory damages. The Commission in Wilbert W. reversed an agency's final decision that dismissed the deceased complainant's case as moot because the complainant failed to request compensatory damages, and, as such, the complainant's death "had completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination." On appeal, the complainant's attorney noted several instances in the complaint alleging that the agency's actions caused anxiety and stress and reiterated that a complaint need not use "magic words" or contain an expressed request for compensatory damages to establish a claim for same. The Commission agreed that the complainant's description of alleged harm could be construed as a request for compensatory damages, and the complainant's death did not moot his claim. Note also that the complainant's attorney formally requested to amend the complaint to include a claim for compensatory damages, which the Commission allowed.

The survivor of a deceased federal employee has no standing to file an EEO complaint on behalf of that former employee. See Rosita R. v. Secretary of Defense, 2019002358 (2019) ("[an estate] has no standing to file an EEO complaint when the deceased did not initiate the complaint"). Although a complaint initiated by a federal employee may survive his or her death, the estate of that employee has no right to file a complaint. See Barnes v. Postmaster General, 01923654 (1992). No regulation provides for the filing of complaints by a former employee's estate. In Estate of Nason v. Postmaster General, 01972839 (1998) and Estate of Hu v. Postmaster General, 01961473 (1996), the issues involved in the complaints were not raised before the employees' death and could not be pursued after their death.

However, if the employee dies during precomplaint counseling and the agency subsequently issues a notice of final interview to the deceased employee, his claim survives. See, e.g., Estate of Ginter v. Postmaster General, 01997239 (2001). See also Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120131950 (2014). Moreover, the time limit for filing a formal complaint can be tolled in such circumstances. Id.

When an agency errantly processes a complaint filed on behalf of a deceased employee and subsequently enters into a settlement agreement with the complainant's spouse, the agreement is unenforceable. In Werner v. Postmaster General, 01A23611 (2003), the Commission noted that contact with an EEO counselor was not made by the employee prior to his death, but was initiated by his surviving spouse. "[W]e find that his surviving spouse lacked standing to enter into a settlement agreement on behalf of her deceased husband pursuant to EEO Regulations." Id.

It is improper for an administrative judge to dismiss a deceased employee's claim on the basis that no legal representative has been appointed for the employee's estate. Estate of Vaisberg v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A04522 (2002). However, the complaint was dismissed on the grounds that it was moot. But, in Noah S. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120141200 (2015), the Commission upheld dismissal of the complaint where the agency requested details on the appointment of an executor and received no response.

In earlier cases involving whether the death of the employee moots the underlying complaint of discrimination, the Commission looked to state law. For example, in Malone v. Department of Army, 03860042 (1986), the Commission permitted the spouse of the late employee to pursue his appeal. The Commission noted that under Missouri law, where the complaint arose, the general rule is that personal injuries to both a person's body and rights survive the death of either party. The Eighth Circuit had applied this rule to cases under 42 USC 1983 and, the EEOC concluded, adopting the same rule was a "logical extension." In the later cases noted above, the Commission did not rely on state law and looked exclusively to whether it was possible, from a practical perspective, to adjudicate the claim.
