EFFECTIVE MEETINGS

Are you making the most of your meeting time or wasting it?

When conducted correctly, meetings are an invaluable way to get things done. When conducted incorrectly, they are a terrible drain on time and energy. Meetings that drag on endlessly can destroy the energy and enthusiasm of the participants, and the initial focus often ends up muddled or lost.

Most managers look at meetings like they look at the weather. Everybody complains about meetings, but nobody does anything about them. Well, you may not be able to do much about the weather, but there is a great deal you can do to harness meetings to your purpose and to avoid wasting time.

The key to a successful and efficient meeting is the preparation you do beforehand. This includes:

  • • Setting the agenda.
  • • Preparing visual aids.
  • • Letting participants know what's expected.
  • • Having a time frame in mind.

Once a meeting begins you need to keep everyone, including yourself, on track. You need to allow for active participation and present the agenda items in a thoughtful and engaging way.

The final aspect of a well-run meeting is the follow-through. Will you provide minutes? Are there tasks assigned for people to complete? Is a follow-up meeting necessary? When should it be scheduled? Thinking through "what comes next" will maximize the effectiveness of your meeting.

Preparing for an effective meeting

If you want your meeting to go as smoothly as possible, you are going to have to put some planning into it.

First, you need to establish a purpose for having a meeting. It is amazing how often this obvious step is overlooked. Determine what you expect or hope for from the meeting. If you can't find a clear purpose, you should not have the meeting.

Once it is determined you need a meeting, you need to decide who should attend. Limit your list to those whose expertise or authority is needed to get things done. Generally, those responsible for carrying out or supervising required actions need to be included. Expert consultants may be added.

Involve participants in the planning by asking them to submit agenda subjects ahead of time. Think through the technology you will use and troubleshoot connectivity issues (Internet and teleconference access, for example) before the day of the meeting.

The agenda. Preparing for the meeting starts with setting an agenda. Prioritize items and send the agenda to participants well before the meeting. You may want to invite feedback and consider changes to increase buy-in. Make a realistic estimate of the time required for each agenda item.

Notify participants of the time, place, length, and plans for the meeting. If this is a regularly scheduled meeting, consider varying the meeting place and time to accommodate the needs of the various participants. If a meeting is scheduled but not necessary, cancel it. Small items of business can probably be handled by phone or email.

Even when the participants know about the meeting, you should always expect the unexpected. Make preparations to accommodate more or fewer people than expected. Allow for necessary last-minute changes in agenda. Don't be caught unprepared.

Warning: If your meeting includes a discussion of the conditions of employment of bargaining unit employees, and includes at least one bargaining unit member, it may be considered a "formal discussion" under federal labor law. This would require that the union be given advance notice and afforded the right to attend. Consult your labor relations specialist for guidance.

Accessibility. In all your meetings with employees, you are responsible for accessibility. That is, you must assure that your message can be understood, and the employee can attend and respond despite any disability, cultural, or language barriers. You may need to provide for sign-language or foreign-language interpreters. You should also know if physical barriers will be a problem for an employee with a mobility impairment. Consult with HR prior to the meeting to work through these issues.

Running the meeting

The most obvious way to start an efficient meeting is to start it on time. If you want a prompt on-time arrival to meetings, you may choose to start by serving coffee and whatever light refreshments are appropriate to the time of day 15 minutes before the meeting -- and then pack the goodies away just prior to start time. This practice:

  • • Sends a clear message to latecomers. But you don't have to resort to the usual threats and whining about tardiness.
  • • Encourages early arrival. The food acts as an incentive for everyone to arrive at least a few minutes early to get settled. And those few minutes are important because they give participants time to shift their focus from outside to inside the meeting.

Keep to the schedule.

  • • Limit the number of agenda items. Discuss those items that are most important first, pushing back the less important ones to the end (or to the next meeting). Items that cannot be resolved in the time allotted should also be tabled for further discussion, either at the next meeting or among the participants most directly concerned.
  • • Use visual aids to move things along. Charts and wall boards can be used to list ideas and chart the progress of the meeting. This may avoid misunderstandings and keep participants from circling around issues indefinitely.
  • • Don't get stuck on "round robin" reports. Show-and-tell sessions about what everyone is working on don't add value, unless you are in the middle of a project in which each person has a part or there is some specific reason why everyone needs to know what the others are doing.
  • • Avoid distractions. Consider asking participants to turn off all personal devices such as mobile phones and tablets. Those who need to be on call must be reachable, of course, but try to limit outside interruptions to those with real operational necessity.
  • • Meet in the morning. People tend to be more productive and less distracted during the earlier part of their workday. It is the best time to have an efficient meeting. A good time is generally 10 a.m., when participants have had a chance to check their email and get their day organized, but before they are fully engaged or rushing to get projects out the door before close of business.

Resolve disagreements. You must have standard procedures for resolving disagreements, or you risk wasting time and alienating the group. Participants will disagree, and there is no reason for you to be caught by surprise. Develop procedures for handling disagreements that everyone is familiar with. Deal with the disagreement, and then move on.

Different procedures may be adopted for different types of issues. Common options for handling disagreement include:

  • • Vote by all participants.
  • • Decision by the meeting's leader.
  • • Decision by consensus among key members.
  • • Reference to outside authority for decision.
  • • Tabling for further discussion at another time.

Maintain control. It isn't always easy to maintain control of a meeting, even if you are the formal leader. Here are tips to help you keep or regain control without being too obvious about it. Remember, not all are appropriate for every setting.

  • • Lead the meetings by walking around. When you stand up and move around during an in-person meeting, all eyes follow you, and participants may concentrate more on what you are saying. This is a good idea with larger groups. It not only gives you more control, it keeps the group focused.
  • • Arrange roundtable seating. This works best with smaller groups. If each participant is facing all other participants, fruitful interaction is encouraged and hostility and tuning out are discouraged.
  • • Direct questions to specific participants. Refer to them by name. Use first names unless a very high degree of formality is required. Get the right people involved at the right time.
  • • Increase the volume of your voice. Participants are apt to tune out when they can't hear you easily. But learn to project your voice. Don't shout.
  • • Facilitate interaction. Ask for the views of specific participants. Get them talking to each other, rather than only to you. Avoid unnecessary lecturing. If the issues are appropriate, you should be more of a facilitator than a leader. This can be a more effective way of maintaining control than simply dominating the discussion, and it is certainly a more effective way to produce valuable results.

Warning: Above all, you need to avoid obvious annoyance at any failure of participants to follow your direction. Complaining and whining will only make you appear weak. Don't be afraid to be firm and take charge. If participants can see you are trying to run an effective meeting, most of them will support you. Nobody wants to waste time.

Wrapping up. Capitalize on a successful meeting and carry over its success to the next. If the meeting has been less than fully successful, that also should be addressed. You should set aside some time at the end of the meeting for your team to understand what they have just accomplished and where they still need to go.

Keep these tips in mind when you wrap up your next meeting:

• Consider shortfalls. What went wrong? Invite discussion of possible solutions.

• Consider achievements. What went right? What must be done to keep on moving in the most productive direction?

• Summarize the action points. You may get participants to help by asking them to go over what they will do after the meeting to move things forward.

• Note the next meeting. Give location, time and a general idea of the agenda.

• Keep the conclusion brief. You've had your meeting. Wrap it up and let everyone get back to their desks.

Finally, you should follow up each meeting by quickly distributing a written record of what was covered, whether as formal minutes or in an email with bulleted action points. Do it while participants' memories of the meeting are fresh. Be sure to include the plans for the next meeting.

A note on minutes: Without a written record, it will be difficult to build on the results of even the most effective meeting. And there is a leadership aspect to the minutes. Even if you don't prepare them, it may be appropriate for you to edit and approve them before they are sent out. The written record is your chance to highlight the most important aspects of the meeting's work and to influence the direction of the next meeting.

Virtual meetings

As employees transitioned to more remote work due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the need to plan, host, and attend virtual presentations and meetings increased dramatically. Now it appears that telework and remote work will continue to be widely used even after employees return to physical workplaces.

Therefore, managers should think about issues that specifically relate to meetings and calls held on videoconference platforms like Zoom, Teams, and Skype. Awareness of these issues can help you avoid problems that could detract from the effectiveness of virtual meetings in your unit.

Listening carefully. Even the most well-intentioned listeners can be guilty of finishing sentences for colleagues or skipping ahead in their thoughts. Presumptions and failing to pay attention can damage the useful flow of information and can also have a negative impact on working relationships as employees who feel unheard on a consistent basis may become reluctant to communicate.

Make an effort to hear what's being said presently and wait for others to finish their thoughts. This is especially important in virtual meetings, where some participants may be prevented from speaking or are talked over in the absence of careful management. You can also encourage the use of hand raising features on videoconference platforms to cut down on employees speaking over each other during virtual meetings.

Accessibility. With working from home becoming the "new normal," agencies must ensure that all employees have equal access in the virtual workplace. A vital part of this is making sure virtual meetings and presentations are accessible to everyone, including employees with disabilities. Here are some strategies you can use prior to meetings to ensure virtual presentations are accessible:

  • • Prior to the meeting, identify all the accessibility features of the online platform you intend to use. These features include closed captioning, automatic transcripts, keyboard accessibility, and screen reader support.
  • • When inviting people to join the virtual meeting, encourage participants to share their requirements and accommodation needs prior to the event. You can use language like this: "We strive to host an inclusive and accessible presentation. If you have questions about the accessibility of our presentation, or want to request accommodations, please reach out to [name] at [email]."
  • • Arrange captioning for the presentation in advance, such as through the Federal Relay Service for government employees or another service provider. If requested, secure sign language interpreters in advance.
  • • Also ahead of the meeting or presentation, create and share accessible slide decks and other presentation materials with the audience. Sending materials ahead of time ensures that participants have access to electronic versions in case they encounter accessibility issues during the live event.
  • • Make sure speakers understand how to use key features of the online platform and convey content in a manner that promotes accessibility.

During a meeting, ensure that:

  • • All attendees are participating from a quiet location with minimal background noise.
  • • Presenters identify themselves and speak slowly and clearly.
  • • All participants enable video and provide a clear view of their face.
  • • Only one person speaks at a time.
  • • If a screen is shared, the visuals are described.
  • • Comments that come through a chat function are read aloud.

'Zoom fatigue.' Employees with mental health conditions such as general or social anxiety may have difficulties with being required to attend multiple online meetings. Video platforms that require them to be seen for extended periods of time can be stressful, as can having to speak with everyone watching and maintaining eye contact. To alleviate this problem, you could allow some meetings to take place by conference call or make appearing on video optional as ways to help employees stay calmer and more focused during virtual meetings.

Hatch Act concerns. Employees engaging in prohibited political activities during a virtual meeting may also be detrimental to its effectiveness, since -- in addition to being prohibited -- it may cause tensions between employees. The Office of Special Counsel has issued guidance regarding how the Hatch Act applies to teleworkers. OSC made clear that political activity is prohibited during work videoconferences and other virtual communications. Managers should advise teleworkers that during work videoconference calls they should not:

  • • Wear a campaign T-shirt or hat.
  • • Have any partisan materials that are visible to others on the call.
  • • Add a profile picture to advocate for or against a partisan political party or group or a candidate for partisan political office. This includes posting candidate images, campaign slogans, or political party symbols as their profile picture.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Over-organize. All meetings need some sort of procedure, but most don't require full parliamentary procedure. Too much formality can stifle spontaneous discussion and creative thinking. Too little formality leads to time-wasting and aimlessness. Use common sense, be guided by the successful meetings you have attended, and learn from your mistakes.
  • • Fail to state your purpose. Begin your meeting with a statement of why you and the others are there and what you intend to accomplish. This intention should also be in the agenda. This lets participants know what is expected of them and serves as a reference point in case of digression.
  • • Monopolize the discussion. You are not there solely to give a lecture or to answer questions. The most effective meetings are interactive, with participants talking at least as much with each other as they do with the leader.
  • • Lose control of tone. Keep things calm and professional. Never answer anger with anger. Stop interruptions and other lapses of etiquette. Model the behavior you expect of participants.
  • • Fail to follow up. Any productive meeting can lose its effectiveness if the decisions made aren't followed up with appropriate action. Make sure a written record is distributed promptly to all participants and other interested parties. Follow up on all action points in individual interactions and at the next meeting.

Ask FMG

Q. Is it ever a good idea to cancel scheduled meetings?

A. Yes. The best way to avoid wasting time at meetings is to cancel scheduled meetings that don't need to be held. Reasons for cancellation include:

Lack of important business. Minor items can be dealt with by email or individual contact.

Absence of key participants.

More pressing priorities.

Q. How do I keep control of meetings when participants become angry or upset?

A. Stay calm and professional. Model the behavior you want to see in the other participants. Never answer anger with anger. Do not permit personal attacks or inappropriate language. Use your authority as meeting leader to deal with disagreements and move on. At the same time, uncover legitimate complaints or problems and try to get the group to deal with them effectively and come up with solutions. Most participants will support your efforts to retain control.

Q. How can I get people to really participate in my meetings?

A. Don't monopolize the discussion. Try to act less like a lecturer and more like a facilitator. The most important thing is to get participants talking with each other, rather than solely with you as the meeting leader. Try to direct questions to specific participants and then encourage others to respond to the points raised, rather than responding yourself. Just make sure you keep to the agenda and stay on schedule, and you can retain control.

Q. What can I do to resolve disagreements among meeting participants?

A. Different procedures may be adopted for different types of issues. Participants should understand how each type of disagreement will be resolved. Common options for handling disagreement include:

  • • Vote by all participants.
  • • Decision by the meeting's leader.
  • • Decision by consensus among key members.
  • • Reference to outside authority for decision.
  • • Tabling for further discussion at another time.

It is important to deal with the disagreement and move on to the other items on the agenda, rather than let it sabotage what might otherwise be a productive meeting.
