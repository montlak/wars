H. EEO COUNSELING

As noted above, the protection offered by the participation clause extends to all parts of the complaint process, including informal counseling. The clause is interpreted broadly enough to encompass the activities of the EEO counselor. An appellant who alleges that an EEO counselor discouraged him from filing complaints because of his past EEO activity stated a complaint within EEO purview. Danzig v. USPS, 01832241 (1984). Because contact with an EEO counselor is clearly protected activity that can form the basis of a reprisal complaint, most significant cases in this area involve the circumstances under which the Commission has allowed an agency to take action based on activity related to EEO counseling.

In Hashimoto v. Dalton, 118 F.3d 671, 679-80 (9th Cir. 1997), the court found that contact with an EEO counselor was a protected activity even though the plaintiff did not allege discrimination during that contact:

On May 21, 1985, Hashimoto contacted an EEO counselor about filing a complaint. According to the EEO counselor, Hashimoto believed that she was being evaluated for work which was no longer her responsibility to perform. Upon being advised by the EEO counselor of her right to file a discriminatory complaint, Hashimoto stated that she "did not really believe that this is a discriminatory complaint." Nevertheless, the EEO counselor sent a memorandum to the personnel office entitled "POTENTIAL EEO COMPLAINT" detailing the interview with Hashimoto.

...

The district court concluded that Hashimoto's contact with the EEO counselor was not a "protected activity" in this instance because, at the time of her meeting with the EEO counselor, she did not "reasonably believe that the employer had engaged in an unlawful employment practice" under Title VII. Rather, in the district court's view, she "merely sought to have her personal problem [of an outdated job description] remedied." The court analogized Hashimoto's retaliation claim to the claim at issue in Jurado v. Eleven-Fifty Corp., 813 F.2d 1406 (9th Cir. 1987). There, an Hispanic disc jockey complained to his program director about an on-air English-only policy instituted at the radio station where he was employed. He was subsequently fired. The court upheld the district court's grant of summary judgment on his retaliation claim, reasoning that his complaint to the program director evidenced concern over his personal success at the station rather than a concern about discrimination. Thus, it was not a "protected activity" for purposes of establishing a prima facie retaliation claim. Jurado, 813 F.2d at 1411-12.

Hashimoto asserts that her meeting with the EEO counselor was "protected activity" because, at the time she set-up the meeting, she felt that the failure to update her job description was motivated by race and sex discrimination. In Hashimoto's view, that she no longer felt this action was discriminatory at the end of the meeting is of no moment, and the district court erred in focusing on this fact. See Gifford v. Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Ry. Co., 685 F.2d 1149, 1156-57 (9th Cir. 1982) (employee need not be aware that practice is unlawful under Title VII at time of complaint in order for complaint to be protected). Hashimoto's argument is compelling, but we conclude that the district court erred at a more fundamental level.

Adopting the approach taken in Jurado, the district court here "looked behind" Hashimoto's meeting with the EEO counselor and concluded that it was not protected activity because she did not at that time allege discrimination. Unlike in Jurado, however, Hashimoto did more here than merely complain to a superior. Rather, she contacted an EEO counselor about her concerns. Even assuming her concerns were "personal" in nature, we conclude that this contact with the EEO counselor was itself "protected activity."

An employer can violate the anti-retaliation provisions of Title VII in either of two ways: "(1) if the [adverse employment action] occurs because of the employee's opposition to conduct made an unlawful employment practice by the subchapter, or (2) if it is in retaliation for the employee's participation in the machinery set up by Title VII to enforce its provisions." Silver v. KCA, Inc., 586 F.2d 138, 141 (9th Cir. 1981) (interpreting 42 USC § 2000e-3(a) (S 704(a))). "The considerations controlling the interpretation of the opposition clause are not entirely the same as those applying to the participation clause. The purpose of the latter is to protect the employee who utilizes the tools provided by Congress to protect his rights." Sias v. City Demonstration Agency, 588 F.2d 692, 695 (9th Cir. 1978). The district court appears to have examined Hashimoto's retaliation claim under the opposition clause only. Under the participation clause, however, there can be little doubt that Hashimoto's visit with the EEO counselor constituted participation "in the machinery set up by Title VII." As such, it was protected activity. See, e.g., Eastland v. Tennessee Valley Auth., 704 F.2d 613, 627 (11th Cir. 1983) (contacting an EEO officer is protected activity); Gonzalez v. Bolger, 486 F. Supp. 595, 601 (D.D.C. 1980) ("Once plaintiff...initiates pre-complaint contact with an EEO counselor...he is participating in a Title VII proceeding." (citations omitted)), affd, 656 F.2d 899 (D.C. Cir. 1981). Thus, we conclude that the district court erred in determining that Hashimoto failed to establish a prima facie case of retaliation.

In a surprising case, the Commission determined that contact with an EEO director to protest a letter of warning was not protected activity for the purposes of establishing a prima facie case of reprisal. Gillilan v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01842596 (1986). The contact with the EEO director was made on the suggestion of an EEO counselor. The director then referred the employee back to the counselor. The case gives a somewhat lengthy and confused version of the facts; one suspects the Commission was unconvinced of the overall merit of the case and, that given the opportunity, it would not hold again that such contact could not be used to establish a prima facie case.

It is not reprisal for an agency to consider the substance of allegations made in the EEO process, where the agency establishes that it has a legitimate concern about the welfare of other employees. In Walker v. Postmaster General, 01922439 (1993), the agency ordered the employee to report for a fitness for duty examination after she initiated EEO counseling on a sexual harassment allegation. Although the Commission found the employee in Walker established a prima facie case of reprisal, it found that the agency advanced a legitimate reason for its action where it maintained its concern was over a portion of the allegation that the complainant was being "spied on":

The Station Manager and the Area Manager each provided explanations for the decision to refer appellant for the FFD. The Station Manager testified that he believed the FFD was necessary because appellant persisted in her belief that she was being spied upon in spite of his repeated attempts to reassure her that she was not being watched. He testified that prior to appellant's submission of the May 14, 1990, letter, he had several conversations with her during which he repeatedly attempted to reassure her that she was not being watched, but that appellant continued to maintain that agency employees were spying on her. The Station Manager further testified that his concerns about appellant's behavior were due, in part, to his involvement with another employee several years earlier who, like appellant, believed he was being watched and could not be convinced otherwise. The employee eventually became threatening, attacked two other employees, and placed himself in front of the wheels of the car of an acting supervisor.

The Area Manager testified he was provided with a copy of appellant's May 14, 1990, letter and felt that some of the statements in the letter were illogical. He testified that after meeting with other agency officials, a decision was made that appellant's referral for a FFD would be in appellant's best interests and the best interests of the agency. The Commission agrees with the AJ that, through this testimony, the agency has articulated legitimate nondiscriminatory reasons for referring appellant for the FFD.

As was noted above, although the AJ ultimately affirmed the agency's action, she did find that the agency had improperly considered the allegation of sexual harassment initially raised by appellant in her May 14, 1990 letter in making its decision to refer appellant for the FFD. The Commission, however, disagrees. The Station Manager indicated in his request for the FFD and in his testimony at the hearing that he made the decision to request the FFD because of appellant's repeated statements that she was being singled out and spied on by agency employees. He stated that although he had repeatedly attempted to reassure appellant, the statements and accusations made by appellant in her May 14, 1990 letter clearly indicated that she persisted in her suspicions. The Commission finds that the incident that resulted in appellant's allegation of sexual harassment, i.e. the clerk supervisor's alleged observation of her in the restroom, was only considered as one of a series of allegations of agency surveillance described by appellant. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the Station Manager's decision was based on his concern that appellant's persistent suspicions indicated that she might pose a danger to herself or others, rather than on the fact that she raised an allegation of sexual harassment.

It is not protected activity when an employee leaves his workstation, in defiance of his supervisor's order, to look for an EEO counselor. See Butler v. USPS, 01872877 (1988). The Commission noted it did not condone "leaving one's work shift in defiance of a supervisory instruction under the guise of EEO activity." Id. However, the Commission did find that an emergency suspension and removal reduced to a two-week suspension was an overly harsh penalty, given the offense. Based upon prior EEO activity, the employee established a prima facie case of reprisal and further demonstrated pretext.

Prior to Hashimoto v. Dalton, supra, the Commission held that an employee was not protected under the participation clause when he contacted an EEO counselor to discuss criticism he was receiving from his supervisors but stated that he did not wish to file a complaint. However, the Commission held that such contact may be protected under the opposition clause. See Manning v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01944218 (1994). Given that the Commission has cited Hashimoto v. Dalton with approval in its Enforcement Guidance on Retaliation and Related Issues, No. 915.004 (2016), it is doubtful that Manning remains good law with respect to the participation clause.
