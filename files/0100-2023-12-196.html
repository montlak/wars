broida eeo Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal, Subchapter 03: Bases for Dismissal of Complaints, Section H: Standing: Failure to State a Claim--Reprisal Complaints, Subsection 02: Actions Related to the EEO Process

2. Actions Related to the EEO Process

In Lavern B. v. Postmaster General, 0120171308 (2017), the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the complainant's claim of discrimination and/or retaliation where he alleged that the agency denied his right of full access to the EEO complaint process when it refused to respond to, or accept and process, the complainant's EEO claims.

The Commission, similarly, reversed the agency's final decision dismissing the complainant's reprisal complaint in Cami C. v. Secretary of Defense, 2020000507 (2020). The allegation in Cami C. was that the agency subjected her to retaliation when it denied her transfer request after she refused to sign a Negotiated Settlement Agreement. The complainant sought the transfer to "escape" a hostile work environment. According to the complainant, she and the agency agreed to the transfer, but when she refused to sign the settlement agreement waiving her right to file an EEO complaint, the transfer was denied. The complainant filed a reprisal complaint. The agency dismissed for failure to state a claim, reasoning that the complainant could point to no previous protected activity. The Commission disagreed in Cami C.:

Under the regulations set forth at 29 C.F.R. Part 1614, an agency shall accept a complaint from an aggrieved employee or applicant for employment who believes that he or she has been discriminated against by that agency because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, disability, genetic information, or retaliation. See 29 C.F.R. 1614.103(a) and 1614.106(a).

Here, a fair reading of Complainant's claim is that the Agency retaliated against her for refusing to sign the referenced negotiated settlement agreement by denying her request for a transfer to the Night Stalker Clinic. The Agency's dismissal decision is premised on its argument that Complainant had no prior protected activity to support the retaliation claim.

We disagree. An examination of the proposed Negotiated Settlement Agreement clearly indicates that it was designed to settle Complainant's claims, communicated to Human Resources, that she was being subjected to a hostile work environment. It is further clear that the drafters of the proposed agreement believed that Complainant was, at least in part, raising employment discrimination claims and the purpose of the agreement was to settle those claims in exchange for allowing her to transfer to the Night Stalker Clinic. Significantly, one of the provisions of the proposed agreement expressly required Complainant to waive her right to brings any claims arising from her employment under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, the Rehabilitation Act, the Equal Pay Act, or "any other federal, state, or local laws or regulations prohibiting employment discrimination." In style and content, the proposed agreement looked like any other agreement with the Agency settling an EEO matter. As such, we conclude that Complainant engaged in protected activity within the meaning of the anti-discrimination statutes and the 20 C.F.R. Part 1614 EEO complaint process when she refused to enter into the proposed agreement with the Agency. Subsequent to her refusal, she alleges she was denied her request to transfer to the Night Stalker Clinic. This states a valid claim which the Agency erred in dismissing.

In Krinke v. Postmaster General, 01985696 (1999), the Commission found that a complaint that one postmaster had shown another postmaster the complainant's EEO file stated a claim where there was no reason for the second postmaster to review the file. In a similar case, the EEOC found an allegation that the agency had disclosed confidential personnel information about him in retaliation for testifying in an investigation and hearing stated a claim for relief. See Stamper v. Secretary of Defense, 01A01509 (2002).

It's not uncommon for an agency employee to be reticent about participating in another employee's complaint. That was the case in Joann F. v. Secretary of Army, 0120170177 (2017), and the Commission found that the complainant stated claims of reprisal and hostile work environment where she alleged that an agency attorney's correspondence with her regarding another employee's MSPB complaint became abusive and harassing. The Commission reviewed the complainant's alleged factual scenario in Joann F:

In the formal complaint, Complainant claimed that, from April 22, 2016 through August 8, 2016, on several occasions Complainant was harassed, subjected to a hostile work environment, bullied, and targeted by an Army Materiel Command Attorney-Advisor for personnel matters (AMC lawyer). AMC lawyer sent Complainant several emails that Complainant felt were hostile, scathing, badgering, unwarranted, abusive, harassing, insulting, and with malice intent to defame Complainant's character in front of DCMA's legal staff, her supervisor, and her leadership.

The record contains the following alleged events that precipitated the pursuit of the instant formal complaint. Complainant had once been detailed to same office with AMC lawyer. The Agency acknowledged that AMC lawyer had a role in discontinuing Complainant's detail. Later, AMC lawyer represented the Agency in a contested January 2016 removal action of a former Agency employee. Complainant had previously supervised the former employee and submitted documents in support of that former employee. In early April of 2016, AMC lawyer notified Complainant that she was to appear as a witness concerning the former employee's appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB).

The Agency record includes the following email correspondence between AMC lawyer and Complainant.

On April 5, 2016, AMC lawyer emailed Complainant: "...you are a witness in an upcoming MSPB hearing. Within the next couple weeks I will need to interview you. Please provide me the contact information for your attorney if you are represented by one.... If you do not respond to this email, 1 must notify the [MSPB] Judge and a subpoena will be issued for your appearance. Please reply indicating whether you plan to be present 16-17 May 16."

On April 7, 2016, AMC lawyer warned Complainant: "If I do not hear from you by (close of business) COB today I will elevate this to your supervisor, and if needed file a subpoena with the [MSPB] judge to ensure your appearance."

On April 25, 2016, Complainant responded to AMC lawyer: "I am in the process of obtaining legal representation. Your email has been forwarded for their review and guidance. I will abide by my duty, as a US citizen and a government employee, to participate in the hearing...your continued contact with my current employer's (DCMA) legal team is unwarranted, excessive, harassing and a malice intent to defame my character. Please, discontinue the communication with my employer's legal staff and my Supervisory Chain of Command."

That same day, AMC lawyer answered Complainant and courtesy copied Complainant's immediate supervisor, her deputy director and a DCMA attorney:

You [sic] behavior has been very unprofessional to say the least. Furthermore, you are not at liberty to direct me to stop communicating with your legal or your supervisory chain of command. You have persistently ignored my official requests over the past month to provide a basic answer and response to the witness request regarding your attendance at [the former employee's] MSPB hearing.

Though federal employees are obligated to cooperate in investigations, the Commission in Joann F. found that the agency attorney's behavior was reasonably likely to deter the complainant from serving as a witness:

Upon review, we find that the Agency improperly dismissed the formal complaint pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(1). First, we find that the matter as articulated by Complainant in her formal complaint addresses a purported pattern of harassment by the AMC lawyer during the litigation of the MSPB complaint of a third party. By alleging a pattern of harassment, Complainant has stated a cognizable claim under the EEOC regulations. See Cervantes v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05930303 (November 12, 1993).

Second, we consider reprisal claims with a broad view of coverage. See Carroll v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05970939 (Apr. 4, 2000). Regarding the basis of reprisal, the Commission has stated that adverse actions need not qualify as "ultimate employment actions" or materially affect the terms and conditions of employment to constitute retaliation. Lindsey v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05980410 (Nov. 4, 1999) (citing EEOC Compliance Manual, No. 915.003 (May 20, 1998)). Instead, the statutory retaliation clauses prohibit any adverse treatment that is based upon a retaliatory motive and is reasonably likely to deter the charging party or others from engaging in protected activity. Id. Here, AMC lawyer purportedly sent Complainant emails motivated by her frustration with Complainant's reluctance to serve as a witness for the Agency and Complainant's equivocation about providing information regarding her own representation. Such activity was reasonably likely to deter Complainant or others from serving as a witness for the former employee.

The Commission in Shanta S. v. Secretary of Transportation, 2020003787 (2021), reversed dismissal of the complainant's reprisal complaint where the agency disclosed the complainant's EEO activity to the complainant's coworkers and managers in the context of processing a FOIA request. Exercising jurisdiction over the claim, the Commission in Shanta S. wrote:

The AJ dismissed allegation (3), Complainant's claim regarding the mishandling of her FOIA request, for failure to state a claim. The Commission has held that it does not have jurisdiction over the processing of FOIA requests. Instead, persons having a dispute regarding such requests should bring any appeals about the processing of their FOIA requests under the appropriate FOIA regulations. Gaines v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05970386 (June 13, 1997).

Similarly, the Privacy Act, 5 U.S.C. § 552(g)(1), provides an exclusive statutory framework governing the disclosure of identifiable information contained in federal systems of records and jurisdiction rests exclusively in the United States District Courts for matters brought under the provisions of the Privacy Act. Bucci v. Dep't of Educ., EEOC Request Nos. 05890289, 05890290, 05890291 (Apr. 12, 1989). To the extent that Complainant alleges that the Agency violated the Privacy Act or mishandled the processing of her FOIA request, we agree that such claims are beyond the Commission's jurisdiction and fail to state a claim.

However, we find that a fair reading of allegation (3) reflects that Complainant is alleging she was subjected to reprisal when G1 disclosed her EEO activity to her coworkers and supervisors, who did not have a need to know about Complainant's EEO activity in order to respond to the FOIA request. The Commission has a policy of considering reprisal claims with a broad view of coverage. See Carroll v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05970939 (Apr. 4, 2000). Under Commission policy, claims of retaliatory actions are not restricted to those which affect a term or condition of employment. Rather, a complainant is protected from any discrimination that is reasonably likely to deter a reasonable employee from engaging in protected activity. Burlington N. and Santa Fe Ry. Co. v. White, 548 U.S. 53 (2006); see also, EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Retaliation and Related Issues, No. 915.004 at II.B (Aug. 25, 2016).

Given the importance of maintaining "unfettered access to [the] statutory remedial mechanisms" in the anti-retaliation provisions of Title VII, our cases have found that a broad range of actions can fall into this category. Burlington N. and Santa Fe Ry. Co. v. White, 548 U.S. 53, 64 (2006) quoting Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 519 U.S. 337, 346 (1997). For example, we have held that a supervisor threatening an employee by saying "What goes around, comes around" when discussing an EEO complaint constitutes an adverse action. Vincent v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120072908 (Aug. 3, 2009), request for recon. denied, EEOC Request No. 0520090654 (Dec. 16, 2010). We have also found that a supervisor attempting to counsel an employee against pursuing an EEO complaint "as a friend," even if intended innocently, is an adverse action. Woolf v. Dep't of Energy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120083727 (June 4, 2009).

Similarly, the Commission has held that disclosure of EEO activity by a supervisor to coworkers constitutes reprisal. Candi R. v. Envtl. Prot. Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 0120171394 (Sep. 14, 2018) (finding reprisal where a Regional Counsel for the Agency sent to all regional office attorneys two emails which contained personally identifiable and confidential information about Complainant's prior EEO complaint); Complainant v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120132430 (July 9, 2015) (reprisal found where a supervisor broadcast complainant's EEO activity in the presence of coworkers and management); Complainant v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0720120032 (May 1, 2014) (complainant subjected to retaliation when HR employee and coworker inadvertently left message on complainant's work voicemail berating her and using strong language while discussing settlement of complainant's prior EEO complaint).

The fact that the disclosure of Complainant's EEO activity occurred within the context of processing a FOIA request does not remove the claim from the Commission's jurisdiction.

Analogously, the Commission has repeatedly held that, although the Commission does not have jurisdiction over the substance of a security clearance context, the Commission is not precluded from determining whether the grant, denial, or evocation of a security clearance is conducted in a discriminatory manner. See, e.g., Henry S. v. Dep't of Def., EEOC Appeal No. 0720170020 (March 28, 2018); Schroeder v. Dep't of Def., EEOC Request No. 05930248 (Apr. 14, 1994); Lyons v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05890839 (Mar. 22, 1990). Similarly, the Commission reversed the dismissal of a claim, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(8) for alleging dissatisfaction with the processing of a prior complaint and being a "spin off" complaint, where a complainant alleged that his supervisor showed his coworkers documents from his EEO complaint in an effort to affect the outcome of an EEO investigation. Maynard G. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 2019002518 (Sep. 17, 2019) (agency improperly dismissed claim as a "spin off" complaint because allegation that supervisor showed coworkers complainant's EEO paperwork would constitute reprisal if substantiated).

Therefore, we find that allegation (3) states a justiciable claim of reprisal and reverse the AJ's dismissal of this claim. We further find that the record is sufficiently developed to address this claim and there are no genuine issues of material fact or issues of credibility that warrant a hearing. Consequently, we will consider the merits of allegation (3) herein.
