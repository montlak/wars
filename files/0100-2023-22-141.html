3. Bona Fide Occupational Requirement

Though the exception is narrow, especially as applied to federal government employment, it is possible for an agency to have a bona fide occupational requirement that permits it to base employment actions on religion. In Norton v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 03A30013 (2003), the Commission upheld a decision by the MSPB that the complainant's separation by reduction-in-force did not violate Title VII because it was based on a BFOQ.

In Norton, the complainant, a Catholic chaplain, alleged that his separation was discriminatory because a Protestant chaplain with less seniority was retained. In rejecting the complainant's arguments in Norton, the Commission explained:

On November 13, 2002, petitioner filed a timely petition with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission asking for review of a decision issued by the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) concerning his claim of discrimination in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (Title VII), as amended, 42 U.S.C. 2000e et seq. Petitioner, a Chaplain at the agency's Sheridan, Wyoming facility, alleged that he was discriminated against on the basis of religion (Catholic) when he was separated from employment as part of a Reduction-in-Force (RIF) in November 1999.

Petitioner filed a mixed case appeal with the MSPB. After a hearing, the Administrative Judge found that petitioner failed to establish that the agency abolished his position for an impermissible reason personal to him. The AJ further found that the agency established that religious affiliation was a bona fide occupational qualification (BFOQ) in this case and that the agency was justified in classifying chaplains based on denomination and separating complainant rather than the Protestant Chaplain (PC) who had less seniority. The AJ noted that PC and complainant, while having many duties that were similar due to the ecumenical nature of those duties, also had significant tasks that were denomination-specific due to their "sacramental" nature. The AJ concluded that this difference established that religious affiliation was a BFOQ justifying the difference in treatment between PC and complainant during the RIF. Petitioner did not petition the MSPB for review and the MSPB AJ's decision became final on October 31, 2002. Petitioner filed a petition for review with the EEOC on November 13, 2002.

EEOC Regulations provide that the Commission has jurisdiction over mixed case appeals on which the MSPB has issued a decision that makes determinations on allegations of discrimination. 29 C.F.R. 1614.303 et seq. The Commission must determine whether the decision of the MSPB with respect to the allegation of discrimination constitutes an incorrect interpretation of any applicable law, rule, regulation or policy directive, or is not supported by the evidence in the record as a whole. 29 C.F.R. 1614.305(c). The issue at hand concerns the agency's claim that religious affiliation is a BFOQ for the Chaplain position in question.

Section 703(e) of Title VII only permits classifications based on religion "where...religion...is a bona fide occupational qualification reasonably necessary to the normal operation of that particular business or enterprise." 42 U.S.C. 2000e-(2)(e)(1). This exception was "meant to be an extremely narrow exception to the general prohibition of discrimination" enunciated in Title VII. See Dothard v. Rawlinson, 433 U.S. 321, 334 (1977) (considering case of alleged sex discrimination). In order to come under the protection of this exception, the agency must establish that "the essence of its business operation would be undermined" by not considering denominational/religious affiliation when classifying the chaplain positions and conducting the RIF. Diaz v. Pan American World Airways, 442 F.2d 385, 388 (5th Cir. 1971) (emphasis in original), cert. denied. 404 U.S. 950 (1971); Dothard, 433 U.S. at 333 (quoting Diaz with approval).

In this case, the record establishes that due to budgetary concerns that required a RIF, the agency could only retain one full-time chaplain. It is undisputed that if petitioner and PC had been considered to be in the same job classification, i.e. Chaplain, petitioner would have been retained instead of PC because he had more seniority.

Furthermore, it is undisputed that the essence of the agency's operations is to provide the highest quality health care to veterans and that such care includes meeting their medical, psychological and spiritual needs. The parties agreed that a chaplain's role in this mission is a dual one: (1) to provide pastoral/ecumenical services to patients of all faiths, including, among other things, assessing spiritual needs, providing support and respect, and leading counseling sessions; and (2) to provide denomination-specific sacramental rites to patients of the appropriate denomination. The agency argued that because of the sacramental duties of a chaplain, the denomination of the chaplain had to be considered when filling positions or, in this case, conducting a RIF. In other words, in this situation being Protestant was a BFOQ for the chaplain position because hiring a non-Protestant would have undermined the essence of its business. In support of this argument, the agency noted that Catholics made up, at most, 25-30% of the patient population, while Protestant denominations made up the majority of the remaining 70-75%. Because a Catholic chaplain could not meet the denomination-specific needs of a Protestant patient (and vice versa), retaining only a full-time Catholic chaplain in a facility where the majority of the patient's were Protestant would undermine the agency's ability to provide the highest-quality care to its patients. The agency further noted that it provided for the faith-specific needs of its Catholic patients, as well as patients of other non-Protestant denominations, with part-time/contract chaplains.

Petitioner argued that the agency's evidence did not establish that religion/denomination was a BFOQ for the chaplain position. He contended that the agency only began classifying chaplains at the facility by denomination recently when it altered the relevant position descriptions in July 1999. He acknowledged, however, that this change in position description did not correspond to a change in duties. Moreover, testimony establishes that the agency-wide policy had long been to consider the denomination of chaplains when making hiring decisions. This, the agency noted, was necessary to ensure that the chaplains hired could meet the needs of the patient population in each facility. In the case at hand, the majority of patients were Protestant and because the agency's budget only permitted one full-time chaplain, it was necessary to have a Protestant chaplain. In response, petitioner argues that this merely establishes that the agency has discriminated based on religion for a long time. We note, however, that the issue is not whether the agency discriminated against petitioner based on his religion, but whether it was permitted to do so because religion was a BFOQ for the position in question.

Petitioner also contended that previous cases have determined that customer preference is not a BFOQ and that the agency's preference for a Protestant in the chaplain position rather than a Catholic carries no more weight than the "customer preference defense." This argument is without merit. The essence of the agency's business is to provide quality health care to patients. This includes meeting the spiritual needs of those patients, including sacramental needs. Because the faith-specific needs of the majority of patients can only be met by a Protestant chaplain, hiring a Catholic would undermine the essence of the business. A patient's desire to obtain sacramental rites from a individual permitted to conduct those rites cannot be compared to an airline passenger's desire to be served by a female rather than a male. See Diaz v. Pan American World Airways, 442 F.2d. 385 (5th Cir. 1971 (finding that hiring females exclusively as stewardesses could not properly fit into the BFOQ exception, as the primary function of the airline business was the safe transportation of customers).

We find, therefore, that the agency sustained its burden of demonstrating the necessity of a denominational hiring policy for chaplains. Although petitioner is correct that a significant portion of chaplain duties are ecumenical in nature and therefore could be conducted by a chaplain of any religion and/or denomination, the record establishes that there are also significant tasks which require a chaplain of a particular denomination. The instant case is factually distinguishable from a case on which petitioner relies, Rasul v. District of Columbia, 680 F. Supp 436 (D.D.C. 1988), in which the court determined that an applicant's religious affiliation was, at best, of secondary importance. In the case at hand, petitioner agreed that the chaplain's role was a dual one and that the sacramental duties necessary to the position were not interchangeable. Furthermore, he did not dispute the agency's claim that PC could meet the faith-specific needs of the majority of the patients, whereas petitioner could not. We find that, in these circumstances, placing a non-Protestant into the chaplain position at the facility would undermine the essence of the agency's business, providing the highest-quality health care to its patients.

Based upon a thorough review of the record and for the foregoing reasons, it is the decision of the Commission to concur with the final decision of the MSPB finding no discrimination. The Commission finds that the MSPB's decision constitutes a correct interpretation of the laws, rules, regulations, and policies governing this matter and is supported by the evidence in the record as a whole.
