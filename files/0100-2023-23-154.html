1. Disclosure in EEO Investigations

An agency does not violate the confidentiality provisions of the ADA when an EEO investigator in the course of investigating a complaint discloses the complainant's disability in order to collect evidence relevant to the complaint. In Davis v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120102597 (2012), the EEO investigator included the complainant's disability--recovering alcoholic, addict--in interrogatories to managers and coworkers. The Commission in Davis found no violation explaining:

The Commission's regulations implementing the Rehabilitation Act provide for the confidentiality of medical records. Specifically, "information obtained...regarding the medical condition or history of any employee shall...be treated as a confidential medical record, except that: (i) [s]upervisors and managers may be informed regarding necessary restrictions on the work or duties of the employee and necessary accommodations." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.14(c)(1). This requirement applies to confidential medical information obtained from "any employee," and is not limited to individuals with disabilities. See Hampton v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A00132 (Apr. 13, 2000). Although not all medically related information falls within this provision, documentation or information concerning an individual's diagnosis is without question medical information that must be treated as confidential except in those circumstances described in 29 C.F.R. Pan 1630. See Hampton, supra; ADA Enforcement Guidance: Preemployment Disability-Related Questions and Medical Examinations, at 22: (Oct. 10, 1995); EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities, at question 15 (Mar. 25, 1997); EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Disability Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations of Employees under the Americans with Disabilities Act, at p. 4 (July 27, 2000).

Complainant alleges that the Agency committed a per se violation of the Rehabilitation Act when its EEO Investigators included confidential medical information on the interrogatories distributed to potential witnesses, including Complainant's supervisors, managers, and coworkers, during the investigation of Complaint 3. Specifically, Complainant alleges that the questions prompting the witnesses to address discrimination on the basis of "disability (recovering alcoholic, addict)" improperly revealed the confidential, specific nature of his disability.

On review of the record, we find that the Agency acted appropriately when the EEO investigators sent interrogatories to Complainant's supervisors, managers, and co-workers. MD-110 directs EEO Investigators to gather information from witnesses that is "material to the complaint, relevant to the issue(s) raised in the complaint, and as reliable as possible." Further:

Agencies may disclose information and documents to a witness who is a federal employee where the investigator determines that the disclosure of the information or documents is necessary to obtain information from the witness, e.g., to explain the claims in a complaint or to explain a manager's articulated reason for an action in order to develop evidence bearing on that reason.

Id., 6-14.

The Commission has articulated limited exceptions to the Rehabilitation Act's confidentiality requirements, including an exception for government officials investigating compliance with the Act who must be given relevant information. EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities, at question 15 (March 25, 1997). Agency officials may share confidential medical information about an employee with other Agency officials on a "need-to-know" basis, if doing so is necessary to ensure compliance with the Rehabilitation Act. Compare Skarica v. Dep't of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 0120073399 (Mar. 5, 2010) (finding that the Agency did not violate the confidentiality provisions of the Rehabilitation Act when Complainant's Supervisor consulted with a personnel official and agency physician to ascertain how to accommodate complainant's medical condition, because these officials had a legitimate "need to know" Complainant's medical information) with Griffin v. Dep't of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 120073832 (May 15, 2009) (finding that Agency management's disclosure of Complainant's EEO medical information in an Agency public chat form constituted a per se violation of the Rehabilitation Act).

Upon review of the record, we find that the Agency did not violate the Rehabilitation Act because the EEO Investigators acted within their duty to fully investigate Complainant's claims of discrimination. The record reveals that EEO Investigators submitted interrogatories that contained the words "recovering alcoholic, addict" describing the specific nature of Complainant's disability. The record contains no evidence, however, that the EEO Investigators made any other disclosures of Complainant's medical information. Further, we find that the EEO Investigators disclosed only the information that was necessary to gather specific information about the Complainant's claims. All of the Complainant's supervisors, managers, and co-workers were federal employees whom the EEO Investigators determined as potential witnesses to the discrimination Complainant allegedly experienced. Thus, all of the employees who received the interrogatories had a legitimate "need to know" the limited information provided about the bases of Complainant's claims, in order to give complete and reliable testimony. We therefore find that the EEO Investigators disclosed the Complainant's medical information only as a necessary part of the Agency's process for ensuring compliance with the Rehabilitation Act.

Accordingly, we agree with the Agency that the Agency did not reveal the Complainant's confidential medical information in violation of the Rehabilitation Act.

In Moore v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120112882 (2012), the Commission found a violation where a supervisor allowed coworkers to overhear a telephone conversation with the complainant about her medical condition and also told her coworkers she had scabies and this came from living in filth.
