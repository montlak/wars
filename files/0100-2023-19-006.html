4. Discretionary Acts

There are many personnel actions that are ultimately within the discretion of the agency to take, for example, extensions of casual or temporary appointments and the granting or withholding of within grade increases. The mere fact that the employee becomes eligible for such things does not equate with an entitlement to them. In these instances, an employee must submit some evidence of a causal connection between the denial of the benefit and the alleged basis of discrimination. Denial of the benefit itself does not establish a prima facie case. In Deans v. Department of Labor, 01842864 (1986), the Commission stated:

To establish a prima facie case of discrimination in the context of the agency's decision not to extend appellant's [casual] appointment. . ., the appellant has the burden of establishing that (1) she is a member of a protected class; (2) she was subject to an adverse employment action; and (3) there is a causal relationship between appellant's membership in the protected class and the adverse employment action. . . .Where a work force is being reduced, an appellant need not show that she was replaced by a person not of her protected group. . . .(Citations omitted.)

In the Deans case, the Commission assumed, without specifically deciding, that a white appellant's testimony that she had received disapproving looks from the director of personnel for having lunch with a black supervisor was a sufficient demonstration of a causal relationship for prima facie case purposes.

Another variation on the theme occurs when the agency formally abolishes a position and has another employee assume substantially all of the duties of the former position. The employee can establish a prima facie case by showing that her duties have been assumed by another employee outside her protected class, even though her own position no longer exists and, therefore, was not filled after her removal. Samuels v. USPS, 01842585 (1986). It is not necessary that the employee demonstrate that all of her former duties have been assumed; it is sufficient to demonstrate that the essential functions of the former position have been assumed.
