M. RECORDS OF DISCIPLINARY ACTION

It is not unusual that cases involving disciplinary action result in settlement. Often, such settlements provide that the disciplinary action will be rescinded. However, there is a distinction between rescinding the disciplinary action and expunging records involving the disciplinary action from agency files. If the latter is intended by the parties, then it should be expressly stated in the agreement.

The days of expunging records and "clean paper" agreements were effectively ended with Executive Order 13839. This is true at least as it relates to sharing those records with other federal agencies. On May 25, 2018, President Trump signed Executive Order 13839, "Executive Order Promoting Accountability and Streamlining Removal Procedures Consistent with Merit System Principles." The Order reads:

By the authority vested in me as President by the Constitution and the laws of the United States of America, including sections 1104(a)(1), 3301, and 7301 of title 5, United States Code, and section 301 of title 3, United States Code, and to ensure the effective functioning of the executive branch, it is hereby ordered as follows:

Sec. 5. Ensuring Integrity of Personnel Files. Agencies shall not agree to erase, remove, alter, or withhold from another agency any information about a civilian employee's performance or conduct in that employee's official personnel records, including an employee's Official Personnel Folder and Employee Performance File, as part of, or as a condition to, resolving a formal or informal complaint by the employee or settling an administrative challenge to an adverse personnel action.

The Office of Personnel Management issued "Interpretative Guidance on Section 5 Ensuring Integrity of Personnel Files Contained in Executive Order 13839." The Guidance reads:

Section 5 of EO 13839 is intended to promote the highest standards of integrity and accountability in the federal workforce by requiring agencies to maintain accurate personnel records and to not alter the information contained in those records in connection with a formal or informal complaint or adverse personnel action. It is further intended to ensure that those records are preserved so that agencies can make appropriate and informed decisions regarding an employee's qualification, fitness, and suitability as applicable to future employment. It is for this reason that agencies are directed not to remove or otherwise modify information contained in a personnel file as a means of resolving an employee-initiated action.

An agency may still rescind an adverse action should it discover that the adverse action was taken illegally or in error. OPM's Interpretative Guidance provides additional clarity:

Corrective Action Based on Discovery of Agency Error

Section 5 requirements should not be construed to prevent agencies from taking corrective action should it come to light, including during or after the issuance of an adverse personnel action, that the information contained in a personnel record is not accurate or records an action taken by the agency illegally or in error. In such cases, an agency would have the authority, unilaterally or by agreement, to modify an employee's personnel file to remove inaccurate information or the record of an erroneous or illegal action. An agency may take such action even if an appeal/complaint has been filed relating to the information that the agency determines to be inaccurate or to reflect an action taken illegally or in error. In all events, however, the agency must ensure that it removes only information that the agency itself has determined to be inaccurate or to reflect an action taken illegally or in error. And an agency should report any agreements relating to the removal of such information as part of its annual report to the OPM Director required by Section 6 of EO 13839. Documents subject to withdrawal or modification could include, for example, an SF-50 issuing a disciplinary or performance-based action, a decision memorandum accompanying such action, or an employee performance appraisal.

OPM Guidance also allows agencies to cancel or vacate proposed actions:

When persuasive evidence comes to light prior to the issuance of a final agency decision on an adverse personnel action casting doubt on the validity of the action or the ability of the agency to sustain the action in litigation, an agency may decide to cancel or vacate the proposed action. Additional information may come to light at any stage of the process prior to final agency decision including during an employee response period. To the extent an employee's personnel file or other agency records contain a proposed action that is subsequently cancelled, an agency would have the authority to remove that action from the employee's personnel file or other agency files. Section 5's requirements would, however, continue to apply to any accurate information about the employee's conduct leading up to that proposed action or separation from federal service.

The Commission has held that an agreement to rescind a disciplinary action only requires the agency to remove records of that action from the complainant's official personnel file. The agency may continue to maintain other records of the disciplinary action. However, it may not cite the discipline in subsequent proposals to discipline. As the Commission explained in Frankel v. Postmaster General, 01912274 (1991):

While appellant argues that the letter of warning has to be expunged from his records, the settlement agreement stated only that the agency agreed to rescind the letter of warning. Thus, the Commission finds the agreement to rescind the letter of warning obligated the agency only to remove all records of this action from appellant's Official Personnel Folder.

The Commission agrees that the letter of warning should not have been cited in the Notice of Suspension. Paraphrased, the oriGINAl agreement reads that management agrees to annul or void the letter of warning and place appellant in the same status he would have been in had the letter of warning not been issued. The act the Commission envisions under the agreement is a legal cancellation of the notice. Thus, appellant should be protected from reference to this record and/or prior personal knowledge by a present or future supervisor of the existence of this record.

The Commission believes that the agency would not violate the settlement agreement, as written and agreed to, if its records were only collaterally used, however, any use of such records for disciplinary purposes would violate the settlement agreement.

The Commission did not explain exactly what "collateral" use the agency could make of the records within the confines of the agreement.

However, in Kelleher v. Chairman, Merit Systems Protection Board, 01924644 (1993), the Commission did expand on its view of collateral uses. In that case, the agency agreed to expunge "all records adversely reflecting upon (appellant's) management abilities or EEO performance." Thereafter, one of appellant's supervisors used a memorandum critical of his performance in another complaint to attack the credibility of the appellant as a witness. The appellant in Kelleher alleged breach of the agreement. The agency responded by asserting that it had not used the document to take any action against the appellant and, in any event, the document was not taken from agency files but from the supervisor's personal file:

[T]his provision of the agreement further required the agency to expunge its files of all records adversely reflecting upon appellant's management abilities or EEO performance, without specifically mentioning the memorandum in question or any other particular documents. From the record it can be determined that the memorandum in question does concern appellant's EEO performance and, therefore, execution of managerial duties. As such, the memorandum falls within that group of documents to be expunged from "agency files."

Moreover, since the expunction of the "agency files" is with respect to all records adversely reflecting upon appellant's management abilities or EEO performance, a fair reading of this provision would limit the agency's obligation to expunge only the records in any agency file which could have an adverse impact upon performance ratings, promotion potential and assignments. This would necessarily include appellant's Official Personnel File, Employee Performance File and the files of the Performance Review Board.

In addition, the expunction would include files held by management officials, including first and second level supervisors and their successors who might consider such memorandum in future decisions about appellant. See Reynolds v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910404 (August 28, 1991) (management retention of expunged document in file outside of personnel file for non-litigation purposes violated settlement agreement provision for expunction of that document). However, this would not include the copy of the memorandum retained with litigation files. See generally Reynolds, supra. Moreover, the Commission has found that where such provision to expunge a document is included in the agreement, collateral use of the document does not violate the settlement agreement. Frankel v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01912274 (August 13, 1991).

Applying these principles to the present matter we do not find that the agency's Executive Director's collateral use of the documents in connection with her testimony in another matter constitutes a breach of the agreement, because such matter does not involve an assessment by management of appellant's capabilities regarding EEO performance and managerial skills.
