21. Interest and Penalties

The general rule is that the complainant is entitled to interest incurred on loans and penalties related to early withdrawal from accounts when the amounts withdrawn are because of the agency's discrimination. For example, in Barrington v. Postmaster General, 0120101822 (2011), the Commission allowed as damages a $1,000 tax penalty on $10,000 that she withdrew from her thrift savings plan account shortly after her termination. However, the Commission did not award withdrawal penalties for funds withdrawn from CD accounts just before her reinstatement finding "[t]he time frame of these withdrawals and the large amounts concerned suggest that Complainant needed the funds to cover expenses that would have occurred even absent the discrimination."

The Commission noted, without approving or disapproving, an agency award to the complainant for the amount of withdrawals from her thrift savings account as damages in Padilla v. Postmaster General, 0120090062 (2010). In that case, the Commission noted that neither the complainant nor the agency had challenged the award and, therefore, it would not review it. However, it is worth noting that the Commission's recitation of the facts in the case included that the complainant slept in his car and frequently did not have food--facts that would certainly tend to establish that the complainant was required to make the withdrawals in order to meet basic living expenses.

In Brown-Fleming v. Attorney General, 0120082667 (2010), the Commission awarded $27,659.59 in past pecuniary damages where the complainant liquidated her retirement account in order to pay off a car loan after she was terminated by the agency. At the time of the liquidation, the account was valued at $33,074.65. The Commission, in Brown-Fleming, explained that the complainant was not entitled to the amount of the car loan because she would have incurred that expense regardless of the termination, but was entitled to fees, penalties and lost interest:

The record reflects that Complainant liquidated her retirement account in order to pay off her car note after she was terminated by the Agency. Complainant submits documentation establishing the amount she withdrew, as well as the broker's fees, tax penalties, and lost interest. As previously found by the Commission, a complainant is not entitled to reimbursement of expenses that would have been incurred whether they were discriminated against or not. See Lee v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 01995204 (July 11, 2001). Complainant is, however, entitled to reimbursement for foregone interest or penalties on the withdrawals, as complainant would not have incurred these "but for" the agency's discrimination. Mature v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 07A20065 (March 17, 2003). Here, Complainant has submitted sufficient evidence of foregone interest and penalties incurred as a result of the withdrawal of her TSP account. The evidence reflects that the account value at the time of liquidation was $33,074.65 and that she incurred a brokerage fee of $2,727.00; a penalty of $3,004.00; and a premature tax liability of $5,731.00, for a total of 11,462.00. Complainant also provided sufficient evidence to support her claim that the foregone interest lost as a result of having to liquidate her account totals $27,659.59. Supplemental Investigation, Exhibit F. Accordingly, we find that Complainant is entitled to $39,121.59 in pecuniary damages.

The interest and penalties on loans taken out and necessitated by the agency's discrimination are also compensable. See Lisa C. v. Postmaster General, 2021002674 (2022) (upholding award of $576.57 for interest on expenses placed on the complainant's credit card that were necessitated by the agency's discrimination).

In Arreola v. Attorney General, 01A03342 (2002), the complainant presented sufficient evidence to demonstrate that his discriminatory termination required him to borrow money to continue to meet basic family needs. The Commission did apportion the award of reimbursement for the loan interest because it found some of the purchases covered did not relate to those needs. The Commission in Arreola found:

Complainant requested $4,748.00 in credit card and loan interest, which he stated would not have accrued but for his termination. Complainant stated that he had zero balances on his two credit card accounts prior to his termination, but accrued $22,000.00 of debt following his termination to pay his family's monthly expenses. Complainant submitted an agreement with a debt consolidation program dated July 18, 1998. The agreement stated that complainant would borrow $22,000.00, which he would repay including interest and fees at $595.00 per month for 42 months for a total of $24,990.00. Complainant, therefore, has shown that he will incur finance charges of $2,990.00 over the four year term of his consolidation loan. The Commission notes that complainant had an obligation to mitigate losses but some of the accrued finance charges were for the purchase of furniture, jewelry, a television and video cassette recorder, and a camera. The Commission finds that 84% of the finance charges complainant will incur on his consolidation loan should be reimbursed. Complainant is awarded $2,511.60 for credit card and loan interest.

See also Campos v. Attorney General, 01A03368 (2002) (awarding 90% of the complainant's accrued interest charges on loans noting that some of the items purchased were not necessities); Wan v. Postmaster General, 01995204 (2001) (awarding excess interest costs and penalties on a home mortgage and credit card balance).

In Tyler v. Postmaster General, 01A31207 (2004), the Commission awarded over $2,700 in interest payments to a complainant who had to take out a mortgage on his home to meet daily living expenses. The Commission denied the complainant's request for reimbursement of some $27,000 in principal because it found that the complainant would have incurred the living expenses even in the absence of discrimination.

In Hughes v. Postmaster General, 01A33597 (2004), the Commission awarded $2,630.27 in oriGINAtion fees and interest for an educational loan taken out by the complainant when the agency failed to provide reasonable accommodation. In making the award, the Commission noted that prior to the discrimination the complainant had not taken out loans to meet educational expenses. However, the Commission denied the complainant interest on educational loans in Forde v. Postmaster General, 01A50188 (2006), where it found no causal connection between the complainant's failure to obtain a bid position and attendance at school some two years later.

When a complainant makes a viable claim that he or she has accrued additional interest charges as a result of discrimination but does not present specific evidence of the amount of interest, the Commission may assign a percentage of the total interest accrued as past pecuniary damages. For example, in Stephens v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01A53933 (2006), the complainant, who suffered from severe asthma, retired after she was denied leave for treatment and transferred to a facility where her asthma would be exacerbated. The complainant presented evidence that her income was reduced by nearly $20,000 due to the difference between her salary and retirement benefits. She also presented evidence that she incurred approximately $65,000 in credit card debt and, as a result, paid over $22,000 in interest. However, the Commission found that the complainant had presented no evidence of what she purchased or what the interest was on those purchases. As a result, the Commission awarded 10% of the total finance charges as damages.

In Drennon-Gala v. Attorney General, 0420060025 (2006), the Commission determined that the agency may be liable for interest that the complainant had to pay on a military retirement buy--back when he alleged his discriminatory termination caused him to miss payments and accrue interest. The Commission remanded the case for the complainant to submit evidence on the alleged loss.
