C. SELECTION CRITERIA

In any promotion or appointment case, the criteria used to make the selection are of primary importance. Generally, applicants for a position are sought through a vacancy announcement. The vacancy announcement will delineate the basic requirements for the position. If applied uniformly to all candidates without discriminatory intent, a selection factor that is deemed to be relevant to the position at issue may be used in the selection process although it is not explicitly described in the vacancy announcement. See Yancey v. Secretary of Defense, 01985517 (2001); Espinoza v. Secretary of Navy, 01955446 (1997). Of course, as discussed in more detail below, use of a criteria not listed in the vacancy announcement can be an indication of discriminatory motivation.

The fact that a selection procedure can be used as a means of discriminating against certain individuals does not mean the procedure itself is illegally discriminatory. As the Commission explained in Pachecano v. Postmaster General, 05990053 (2002):

Although the Commission encouraged the agency to abolish the CEB system in its prior decision, it did not address this issue in the body of the prior decision. In effect, the Commission was merely upholding an item of relief ordered by the AJ in her recommended decision. In reaching her decision that the agency's CEB system was inherently suspect and created a risk for discrimination, the AJ argued that "it is conceivable that they [panel members] can, in a discriminatory manner, write evaluations which will generate high scores for favored applicants while writing evaluations that will not generate high scores for disfavored applicants." The AJ also noted that the CEB guidelines covered five of the rated categories and the panel members developed standards for four additional categories. The AJ also found that the panel members articulated inconsistent reasons as to how much weight they gave to the supervisor's evaluation. This fact added to the AJ's concern that scores could be manipulated through use of supervisory appraisals, "further exemplifies the risk of discrimination inherent in this 'secret' system used by the agency to place employees in first-time supervisory, upwardly mobile positions."

Based on the AJ's analysis, it is obvious that the examples of "risk of discrimination" mentioned in her decision are truly examples of how the process could be applied rather than any internal discriminatory features of the selection process. Nowhere in her analysis does the AJ show where the guidelines were not a reliable measure of the skills that the agency has identified to be critical skills for a successful manager. Moreover, the AJ does not question the validity of the guidelines, nor does she claim that the guidelines have a discriminatory impact on any segment of the agency's workforce. Rather, it is evident that the AJ's discomfort with the CEB selection system was because the agency refused to release its rating guidelines to the complainant. The appropriate penalty for the agency's refusal to release its rating guidelines to complainant is for the AJ to draw an adverse inference. (See Joseph Sellman v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05890993 (December 14, 1989) (Commission held that the agency's refusal to disclose certain critical information regarding its rating guidelines deprived complainant of the opportunity to prove pretext). Therefore, we find that the previous decision lacked sufficient information to determine that the agency's CEB system was internally discriminatory.
