broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 05: Compliance With Settlement Agreements, Section F: Reprisal

F. REPRISAL

Many settlement agreements provide that the agency will refrain from any reprisal against the complainant for EEO activities. Such provisions have no real effect. Reprisal for EEO activities is prohibited exclusive of any such provisions in settlement agreements. As a matter of law, a promise to refrain from reprisal cannot constitute consideration in a settlement agreement. Because the law already prohibits reprisal, the complainant gains nothing through such a provision. If it is the only promise made by the agency in an agreement, there is no settlement because the complainant has received nothing of value for withdrawing his or her complaint.

Assuming the complainant received something else of value as consideration and there is a legally binding agreement, two distinct problems arise involving an alleged breach of a reprisal provision. In the first situation, the complainant alleges the agency has breached the settlement agreement by engaging in reprisal. In the second situation, the complainant alleges that the agency has engaged in reprisal as a result of the settlement agreement. Neither situation presents a true allegation of breach of a settlement agreement. Both situations present new complaint issues.

An allegation that an agency has engaged in reprisal by taking adverse personnel actions subsequent to settlement of an EEO complaint is not a basis for reinstating the original complaint. See Wilburn v. USPS, 01831478 (1984). The allegation is appropriately referred to an EEO counselor as raising a new matter. As the Commission noted in Moore v. Postmaster General, 05900252 (1990), "[a]s the last sentence of Section 1613.217(b) makes clear, further acts of discrimination, even if they violate a settlement agreement, and irrespective of a provision in the settlement agreement stating that a complainant can reinstate the settled complaint, must be processed as a separate individual complaint and do not give rise to the reinstatement of the previously settled complaint." See also Algya v. Postmaster General, 01A14689 (2002); Lee v. Secretary of Army, 01994727 (2001).

Similarly, an allegation that an agency refused to reinstate a complaint based on reprisal or continuing discrimination is a new complaint. LeDuc v. General Services Administration, 05880506 (1988).

Complaints alleging that reprisal or further discrimination violates the terms of a settlement agreement must be processed as separate complaints and not as enforcement petitions. See Dogans v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01930189 (1993). In that case, the agency reached a settlement agreement with the complainant that provided she would be placed in a position with promotion potential to GS-11. The complainant alleged that the agency delayed her promotion to the GS-9 level and denied her promotion to the GS-11 level in retaliation for her EEO activity. The agency treated the complaint as a breach allegation and issued a decision finding there was no breach of the agreement. The Commission reversed and ordered the allegations be processed as a new complaint.

The Commission held that an element of intent is required when an employee alleges retaliation as the result of a settlement agreement. In Hughes v. USPS, 01853312 (1987), the agency considered past discipline that had been expunged in a previous settlement in considering the employee for entry into a supervisory training program. The Commission found the agency official had no knowledge of the settlement and was acting in good faith in accordance with the agency's regulations.

An interesting situation arises where the complainant alleges that the agency breached a settlement agreement in reprisal for previous EEO activity. In Wagner v. Postmaster General, 01912484 (1991), the complainant alleged the failure of the agency to grant him the next available officer-in-charge position in conformity with a settlement agreement was reprisal. The agency treated the allegation as a new complaint and subsequently offered, as full relief, the next OIC position. It also promised to notify those responsible for selecting OICs to ensure compliance. The Commission found the agency was correct in processing the allegation as a new complaint instead of a breach allegation. The Commission also found in Wagner that, under the circumstances, it was proper for the agency to make an offer of full relief:

The agency's offer did not state that the agency would enforce compliance with the settlement agreement, but rather that appellant would receive the next O.I.C. assignment...and that the agency notified the officials responsible for assigning O.I.C.'s in this area to ensure compliance. Inasmuch of the original agreement, which the agency essentially concedes it breached, provided, in part, that appellant would receive the next assignment, we find that compliance with the agreement will not be obtained until appellant receives an assignment and also receives backpay for the period of time in which the agency's breach of the settlement agreement prevented him from obtaining his assignment. This is due to the fact that if the agency complied with the agreement, appellant would currently hold the "next" assignment as provided for in the agreement. The agency should not be able to delay the fulfillment of the settlement agreement by repeatedly promising that appellant will receive the "next" assignment. We subsequently find that the agency's offer did not constitute make whole relief.

The issue thus becomes what relief is appropriate in this case. Ordinarily, when we find that an agency's offer is not full relief, we order the agency to resume processing the case from the point processing ceased. In this case, the agency would simply continue processing the reprisal complaint, whose sole issue is whether agency breached the settlement agreement to retaliate against appellant. Inasmuch as the agency readily admits the breach, it would seem more appropriate to require either specific performance of the settlement agreement or reinstatement of the underlying complaint that resulted in the agreement.

The Commission ordered enforcement of the agreement by requiring the agency to give the complainant the next OIC job as well as back pay for the missed assignment.

In Allen v. Postmaster General, 01956930 (1997), the Commission observed that no reprisal clauses in agreements, if enforced, could lead to the endless reopening of complaints:

In the instant case, we find that appellant's allegation concerning being assigned additional duties and being reassigned to the Airport Mail Center is an allegation that a subsequent act of discrimination violated the May 1993 settlement agreement. With regard to the agency's alleged breach of the "no reprisal" clause, as a general matter, the language of such provisions (and of the provision in the instant matter) is so broad that it would, in essence, permit an appellant to reopen his complaint endlessly. Thus, if a party to a settlement agreement is not required to file a new complaint with respect to reprisal allegations, a "no reprisal" clause would destroy the finality of all settlement agreements even though the agency had, in fact, complied with all substantive provisions of the settlement agreement. Therefore, the Commission finds that appellant's allegations of breach of the no reprisal clause were properly processed as a separate complaint.

Given the Commission's views in Allen and other cases, it would make far more sense for the EEOC simply to declare no reprisal clauses as being void and unenforceable. See also Reynolds v. Social Security Administration, 01970005 (1997).

Still, on occasion, the Commission has found a breach of a no retaliation clause. For example, in Holloway v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A55422 (2006), the agency promised to support the issuance of a handicapped parking sticker to the complainant. However, the agency's physician then sent a letter to the granting authority urging it to reevaluate a state disability determination. The Commission determined that the agency had breached the no reprisal clause by "violating the spirit, if not the letter of the instant agreement."

When a complainant alleges that reprisal or further discrimination violates the terms of a settlement agreement, those allegations should be processed as a separate individual complaint rather than a complaint of noncompliance. However, it is understandable that a complainant would view further alleged incidents of reprisal or discrimination to constitute breach of a settlement agreement. Eby v. Secretary of Agriculture, 05910875 (1992). In Eby, the Commission permitted the complainant to seek EEO counseling after denying his request to reopen. Although the agency had already instructed the complainant to seek counseling instead of pursuing an allegation of breach, the Commission found that it would be unfair to require the complainant to comply with the agency's instructions. To do so would "be contrary to the interests of its administrative efficiency and economy as well as unfair to appellant." Id.

Even though proposed disciplinary actions are no longer subject to challenge through the EEO process, 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(5), an allegation that the agency has proposed to suspend a complainant to induce settlement of pending complaints is within the EEO purview. Roberts v. Secretary of Treasury, 05900097 (1990). In that case, the agency physically attached a notice of a proposed 14-day suspension to a proposed EEO settlement agreement. The complainant maintained the agency was using the threat of proposed discipline to force her to accept the terms of the agreement, which she found undesirable. The Commission also rejected an agency argument that the complainant violated the basic tenet that statements made during settlement discussions can not be used to establish the liability of either party. "[T]he fact that the alleged coercion occurred at the settlement negotiation phase does not insulate the agency from liability." Id.

Because settlement agreements are intended to be voluntary, an agency may not take actions against a complainant for failure to accept a settlement offer. To do so is a form of retaliation. In Lonnie v. Secretary of Interior, 01A31700 (2005), the Commission found that the agency engaged in retaliation when it revoked her turn at rotational details when she failed to accept a 120-day detail in settlement of a complaint. The Commission in Lonnie found:

We note that, in concluding no discrimination occurred, the AJ credited as legitimate the agency's rationale for taking complainant out of the rotation for the promotional detail--that complainant was offered the full 120-day detail and that she rejected it. The AJ, however, appears not to have recognized that this "offer" was made during settlement discussions between complainant and agency regarding a prior EEO complaint. See Report of Counseling, Complaint FNP-00-006. In exchange for withdrawing an EEO complaint, the agency offered to provide complainant with the whole 120-day detail. Complainant refused the offer in order to pursue her EEO complaint. The evidence is clear that she was then removed from her turn at the rotational details, that she had already been granted, because of her refusal to accept the settlement offer. EEOC Management Directive (MD)-IJO, Chapter 12, is clear that settlements of EEO disputes must be voluntary in nature, with the complainant free to choose not to settle and instead pursue processing of his or her EEO complaint. Moreover, "[s]ettlement negotiations, including any statements or proposals, are to be treated as confidential and privileged to facilitate a candid interchange to settle disputes informally." Harris v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05941002 (March 23, 1995). For these reasons, we find that the agency's decision to pull complainant out of the rotation for the promotional details was unlawful retaliation for complainant's decision to exercise her protected right in the EEO complaint process to pursue her previously filed EEO complaints rather than settling with the agency.
