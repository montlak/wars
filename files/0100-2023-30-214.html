2. Arbitration

In McGuire v. Secretary of Agriculture, 02960010 (1997), the Commission declined to specifically address whether an arbitrator had the authority to award compensatory damages. Instead, the Commission remanded the case to the agency for a supplemental investigation on compensatory damages. In essence, the Commission in McGuire found that the question of whether an arbitrator may award compensatory damages is academic. In reviewing the discrimination findings of an arbitration award, the Commission may order such relief. As explained in McGuire:

Notwithstanding his finding that appellant had been subjected to sexual harassment for which the agency was liable in violation of Title VII, the Arbitrator found that he lacked authority to award such damages within the confines of the grievance process. We are reluctant to examine the Arbitrator's ruling concerning his own authority to award relief within the confines of the grievance process. Although we decline to address this issue, the Arbitrator's ruling does not end our inquiry, since compensatory damages are clearly available to this Commission to award as an aspect of "make whole" relief for a Title VII violation such as the one involved herein. Moreover, we have reviewed the Arbitrator's decision and agree with his ruling that appellant was subjected to sexual harassment of which the agency was on notice and for which it failed to provide prompt or effective remedial action. Thus, the question of whether appellant is entitled to an award of compensatory damages is squarely before us. We also recognize that the Arbitrator commented in his decision on the inadequacy of the evidence in the record to prove either that the sexual harassment was the cause of appellant's emotional distress or that the emotional distress she allegedly underwent was severe enough to warrant a damage award. However, our review of the record herein persuades us that appellant was not adequately placed on notice by either the Arbitrator or the agency of her obligation to present sufficient evidence during the arbitration process to prove that her emotional condition was caused or aggravated by the sexual harassment and to support her request for such damages with adequate documentation.

...

Accordingly, the Commission will remand the case to the agency for supplemental investigation. See Lesley S. Hostettler v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 02940019 (June 3, 1994). Upon remand, the agency will afford appellant a period of time, as specified below, to produce evidence in support of her claim for nonpecuniary compensatory damages and in support of her claim for out of pocket medical expenses. Thereafter, the agency will issue a FAD addressing whether, and to what extent, appellant has established her entitlement to compensatory damages.
