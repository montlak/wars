2. Waiver as a Defense

A signed waiver of rights, however, is not a complete defense. Such a defense must be timely raised. For instance, in Randolph A. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 2020000861 (2020), for instance, after litigating the case through a hearing and two appeals, the agency moved to vacate citing to a global settlement signed by complainant six years earlier.

The Agency argues that Complainant is not entitled to compensatory damages because he signed the November 15, 2010, global settlement agreement. The Agency also contends that, because of the settlement agreement, the Commission does not have the authority to award compensatory damages.

In a number of decisions, the Commission has found that the dismissal of claims was appropriate where there was an applicable settlement agreement. See, e.g., Lyle S. v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 2019002466 (June 13, 2019) (dismissing settled claims pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(5) as moot); Green v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120101695 (Aug. 9, 2010) (settlement agreement rendered claims moot); Johnson v. Tennessee Valley Authority; EEOC Appeal No. 0120092209 (Sep. 15, 2009) (settlement agreement rendered complaint subject to dismissal under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(1) for failure to state a claim); Tittle v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01A44708 (April 13, 2005) (after signing settlement agreement, complainant lacked standing, and his claim failed to state a claim); Hedman v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A32743 (Sep. 23, 2003) (settled claim failed to state a claim).

However, even assuming for the purposes of this decision that the November 15, 2010, global settlement agreement applied to the EEO complaint at issue in the instant request for reconsideration, we decline to find that the complaint should be dismissed as moot or for failure to state a claim. In the cases cited above, the existence of a settlement agreement was raised prior to a finding of liability by the Commission. In the instant case, the Agency did not disclose the existence of the global settlement agreement until June 23, 2016, well after the Commission had found the Agency liable for discrimination and denied the Agency's first request for reconsideration. The Agency also subsequently issued its February 21, 2018, decision awarding Complainant $96,976.81 in compensatory damages and its August 10, 2018, decision awarding Complainant reasonable attorney's fees and costs. Because the Agency did not raise the 2010 settlement agreement until after the Commission found that the agency had subjected Complainant to discrimination, we find that equitable considerations weigh against dismissing the underlying EEO complaint and that Complainant is still entitled to compensatory damages, attorney's fees, and other relief.

Although generally a valid, binding settlement agreement would be grounds for dismissing the EEO complaint, under the specific circumstances of this case, the equitable doctrine of laches precludes dismissing the complaint and vacating the Commission's prior decisions. Under laches, if a party neglects to assert rights against an adverse party over a period of time, the delaying party can be precluded from asserting the neglected rights. Laches generally requires proof of two elements: (1) lack of diligence by a party; and (2) proof of prejudice to the adverse party. See Costello v. United States, 365 U.S. 265, 282 (1961). Proof of prejudice includes consideration of whether the adverse party changed their position or detrimentally relied upon the party that delayed asserting its rights. See Fulps v. City of Springfield, Tenn., 715 F.2d 1088, 1096 (6th Cir. 1983). The Commission has consistently held that a complainant must act with due diligence in the pursuit of his claim or the doctrine of laches may apply. See O'Dell v. Dep't of Health and Human Serv., EEOC Request No. 05901130 (Dec. 27, 1990); see also Elayne C. v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 2020001137 (Feb. 14, 2020); Fonda M. v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 2019005651 (Feb. 6, 2020). The Commission has also found that laches can apply where an agency neglects to timely assert a defense. See Bornemann v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05890989 (Dec. 21, 1989).

Here, we find that laches applies and that equitable considerations weigh against vacating our decisions finding that Complainant was subjected to discrimination and ordering relief. In the instant case, there was a nearly six-year delay in asserting that the complaint was settled, and the Agency did so only after the Commission had found the Agency liable for discrimination and ordered the Agency to award compensatory damages and other relief. The Agency contends that it was not aware of the settlement agreement because the Agency does not maintain a central repository for settlement agreements.

We find that the lack of a recordkeeping system to facilitate discovery of relevant settlement agreements involving EEO matters shows that the Agency did not act with due diligence when it failed to raise the settlement agreement until this matter had been litigated for years.

We further find that Complainant was prejudiced by the Agency's delay. According to the record, Complainant was pro se when he filed his EEO complaint on September 22, 2010, during the investigation, during the hearing process, and when he filed EEOC Appeal No. 0120120184 on October 3, 2011.3 Complainant did not hire an attorney until after the Commission found that he had been subjected to discrimination by the Agency and ordered relief. Even after the Agency produced the settlement agreement, Complainant continued to incur litigation costs based on his detrimental reliance on the Agency's actions, and the Agency recognized Complainant as a prevailing party by awarding him compensatory damages in its February 22, 2018, decision and by awarding him attorney's fees and costs in its August 10, 2018, decision. We therefore find that equitable considerations weigh in favor of Complainant, and we decline to vacate our prior decisions finding discrimination and ordering relief, including compensatory damages, for Complainant.

After reviewing the previous decision and the entire record, the Commission finds that the Agency's request fails to meet the criteria of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.405(c), and it is the decision of the Commission to DENY the request. The decision in EEOC Appeal No. 0120181473 remains the Commission's decision. There is no further right of administrative appeal on the decision of the Commission on this request. The Agency shall comply with the ORDER as set forth below.
