3. Documentary Evidence

There is no requirement that either party submit original documents as evidentiary exhibits. Photostatic copies will suffice. Copies should be legible. Documents that are not legible, or have portions that are not legible, may be rejected. Handwritten notes sometimes present copying problems, especially if, on the original, the handwriting is not particularly legible. One solution is for the parties to attach a typewritten transcription of the notes, provided both parties agree the text of the transcription is accurate.

Always bring an adequate number of copies of documentary evidence to the hearing. An excess of paper can be a problem in federal personnel cases, but it is better to have too many copies of a document than too few. Usually, four copies will be adequate. This permits submission of a copy to the administrative judge, submission of a copy to the opposing party, a copy for the witness and a copy for yourself. It can be extremely difficult to question a witness about a document if the document is not in front of you.

The judge may require the parties to submit any proposed hearing exhibits in advance. This will eliminate some of the copies required for the hearing. This also allows the parties to examine documents prior to the hearing and decide if there will be objections. Finally, it can avoid unnecessary duplication where both parties seek to introduce the same documents. In any event, the regulations provide that the administrative judge shall furnish to the opposing party a copy of any document accepted into the hearing record. 29 CFR 1614.109(h). The documents in evidence are typically attached to the transcript of the hearing by the court reporter.

Because the rules of evidence are not applied strictly, introduction of documentary evidence into the record is easier in administrative proceedings. The formal process is to mark the exhibit for identification. Many judges require one party to use numbers for identification and require the other party to use letters. Once the exhibit has been marked for identification, it should be shown to the witness and the witness should be asked if he or she can identify the document. If the witness identifies the document, the party offering it should move for its admission into evidence. In most cases, the relevancy of a document will be apparent on its face. If not, this is the time to object to the admission of the document. This also is the time to be prepared to make a proffer as to why the document is relevant. In some instances, it may be necessary to ask the witness further questions about the document in order to establish its relevancy. Once the document is admitted, the party then can proceed to question the witness about the document. If the document is used with another witness, it should be referred to by the exhibit number or letter assigned to it at the time of admission.

The process of having a witness identify a document is generally referred to as authentication. That a witness cannot identify a document does not per se mean the document will not be admitted. Some documents, such as agency regulations and records kept in the normal course of government business, are self-authenticating. Other documents, such as personal notes, memoranda, letters, etc., require identification. There are several options when a witness cannot identify a document. One option is to withdraw the document and seek its admission through a subsequent witness. Another option is to request permission to question the witness about the document on the representation that it will be admitted into evidence through another witness. This method can be useful particularly when witnesses are called out of order to accommodate the other party. If no other witness can identify the document and it is rejected, the judge should be requested to establish a rejected exhibits file and place the document into the file. The rejected exhibits file then becomes part of the hearing record. In this manner, the rejected evidence can be considered on appeal, if it is found the judge erred in not admitting it.

Much of the above guidance on admission of documents has now been incorporated in to the Judges' Handbook. With respect to admission and rejection of documents, the Judges' Handbook at 7-7 to 7-8 provides:

The parties are permitted to introduce documents as exhibits that are relevant and material to their case. The Administrative Judge must assure that the record accurately reflects when an exhibit is offered, the marking of the exhibit, any objections to its admission, and the Administrative Judge's ruling on the objections. The report of investigation is already part of the record and need not be introduced as an exhibit. The Administrative Judge should instruct the parties to make specific references to the report of investigation as necessary.

Exhibits should be marked with unique identifiers. The Administrative Judge may require the parties to exchange exhibits and submit a list of exhibits to the Administrative Judge prior to the hearing. Each proposed exhibit should be identified by number. Complainant's exhibits should be marked as hearing exhibit C-1, C-2, etc. The agency's exhibits should be marked as hearing exhibit A-1, A-2, etc. The Administrative Judge may also order that certain documents be entered into the record as the Administrative Judge's exhibits. Such exhibits should be marked as AJ-1, AJ-2, etc.

The Administrative Judge should instruct the parties as to the number of copies of exhibits required for the hearing. The Administrative Judge should instruct the court reporter to assemble and keep all hearings exhibits offered at the hearing and provide them with the bound transcript of the hearing.

a. Offering and Admitting Exhibits

The party offering the exhibit must formally offer the exhibit into evidence. If not already marked, the Administrative Judge or court reporter will mark the exhibit, and the Administrative Judge will describe the exhibit for the record. The exhibit should be shown to the opposing party who must be given an opportunity to question whether the witness has some knowledge of the exhibit (voir dire) and to object to the exhibit. The party offering the exhibit should have a competent witness identify and authenticate the exhibit. If the parties cannot stipulate as to the authenticity of a document, and the complainant is not represented by counsel, the Administrative Judge may make a reasonable attempt to establish the authenticity by making the necessary inquiries at the hearing or by providing specific instructions to the complainant. Documents found by the Administrative Judge not to be authentic should be excluded. All exhibits should be referred to by number during the course of the hearing to ensure that the hearing record and transcript are clear.

b. Non-admitted Exhibits

Any exhibits that are not admitted into the hearing record should be retained by the Administrative Judge and clearly identified as not admitted. These documents must be transmitted with the hearing record. This is important to preserve the evidence if an appeal of the case involves examination of whether the decision to exclude the evidence was correct. If a party withdraws an exhibit, the Administrative Judge should acknowledge the withdrawal on the record and return the exhibit to the party withdrawing it.

In many cases, the offering and admission of exhibits is much less formal. It is not unusual for administrative judges to deal with admission of exhibits at the prehearing conference.

The agency is not required to submit official documents in order to carry its burden of production as to legitimate reasons. Drafts of selection documents, accompanied by testimony from agency officials, provided a sufficient basis for the hearing examiner to conclude that the appellant had not been the victim of discrimination in Terry v. USPS, 01831852 (1984). However, an EEO counselor's report which purports to summarize the statements of the RMO as to his legitimate reasons, standing alone, is not direct evidence sufficient to carry the agency's burden of production. See Butler v. Postmaster General, 01882521 (1988). But the fact that an agency document is "unsworn testimony" does not mean, by itself, that the document is less credible than sworn testimony. Albert v. Postmaster General, 01912534 (1991).
