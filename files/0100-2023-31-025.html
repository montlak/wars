A. REASONABLE HOURLY RATE

A reasonable hour rate is determined by the "prevailing market rates in the relevant community." Blum v. Stenson, 465 US 886 (1984). Typically, "the best evidence of a reasonable hourly rate for the community in general is the rate customarily charged by the attorney or law firm for fee-paying clients."). Washington v. Postmaster General, 01991703 (2002).

A number of factors determine what is a reasonable hourly rate for legal services in connection with EEO complaints, including the experience of the attorney, the difficulty of the case, the rate charged by other attorneys in the geographical area for similar services, often referred to as the "prevailing market rate," and past hourly rates that have been awarded. It is incumbent upon counsel to establish the reasonableness of the requested hourly rate and to present evidence on that point. See Torrens v. Department of Air Force, 01830801 (1983). Documentary evidence may include an affidavit by the complainant's attorney establishing his/her normal billing rate, the attorney's resume, a list of the cases litigated by the attorney, or a list of comparable cases where the asking rate was accepted. See Goldie S. v. Postmaster General, 2019000575 (2020), citing EEOC Management Directive 110, Chapter 11, VII.A. (1999). An affidavit by another attorney who is also familiar with the "prevailing market rates in the relevant community" is also competent supporting evidence. Id.

When approval of an hourly rate is premised on past fee awards, the attorney must substantiate that he, in fact, was the attorney in that case. Morgan v. Secretary of Army, 01911752 (1991). Demonstrating that the Commission has approved a certain hourly rate in a previous case does not make the rate reasonable without a demonstration that the award was to the same attorney.

The Commission has rejected the notion that administrative litigation requires less skill than court litigation and, therefore, should be compensated at a lesser rate. Acknowledging that the rules of evidence are more strictly enforced in court proceedings, the Commission "disagrees with the agency's conclusion that an attorney in an administrative proceeding does not need comparable litigation skills in order to satisfactorily represent his client." Lowe v. Department of Air Force, 01851668 (1987).

There is also no correlation between the quality of the agency's representation and the reasonable hourly rate of the appellant's attorney. The fact that the agency was not represented by an attorney is not a rationale for reducing the reasonable hourly rate. McClendon v. Postmaster General, 01883431 (1989).

However, an agency may take into account the past conduct of the attorney seeking fees. As the Commission noted in Monroe v. Secretary of Navy, 01913953 (1992):

With regard to the hourly rate requested by [Attorney] #1, the Commission finds that the agency properly set the reasonable hourly rate at $100.00 based on the charges of professional misconduct pending when he was retained by appellant. We further find that the agency's consideration of the legitimate questions raised by competent legal authorities regarding [Attorney] #1's ability to handle Title VII litigation in an effective manner which were addressed in Young v. Secretary of the Navy, Appeal No. 88-2947 (July 13, 1989), Slip Opinion at pp 3-4 was properly factored into its determination of a reasonable hourly rate. Appellant has not provided any substantive evidence which would demonstrate that, in light of the agency's legitimate concerns, [Attorney] #1's request for an hourly rate at 225.00 or $150.00 was reasonable. Therefore, we find the agency's decision to modify the hourly rate requested by [Attorney] #1, based on the factual circumstances presented herein, was proper and affirmed.

An agency may not set an arbitrary hourly rate as a matter of policy and then apply that rate to all fee awards. See Qazei v. Secretary of Interior, 01860376 (1987). The hourly rate must be determined on a case-by-case basis by reviewing the factors listed at 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(2)(ii)(B).

There are many factors that go into establishing an attorney's hourly rate. Not all of those factors are directly related to the attorney's skill level and experience. Notably, the attorney must factor into his or her hourly rate the cost of doing business. Rent and staff must be paid. Many services are rendered by attorneys that are not billed separately but are included in the hourly rate. For example, attorneys do not routinely bill for such things as secretarial time spent typing correspondence. Those are subsumed in the attorney's hourly rate. Word processing time is not separately compensable because it is a normal component of the attorney's hourly rate. Ciesla v. Secretary of the Army, 01911648 (1991).

Those who work for the federal government often find the rates charged by private sector counsel to be excessive, if not obscene. But there is no correlation between the hourly salary rate of government counsel and the hourly rate of private sector counsel. See Tilden v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01982768 (1999) (rejecting an agency argument that $60 per hour was excessive for a paralegal because the agency counsel only received about $40 in salary). Simple common sense dictates that there is no basis for comparing the hourly rates of private attorneys and the salary rates of government attorneys. The latter are not required to pay for such items as rent, supplies, equipment and staff salaries from their bi-weekly paychecks.

"The goal of the fee award should be to place the attorney in the same economic position as if the matter has been billed and paid like any regular component of the attorney's practice for a paying client rather than having to wait for payment at the end of litigation." Burns v. General Services Administration, 01932480 (1993) (citing Pennsylvania v. Delaware Valley Citizens Council for Clean Air, 483 U.S. 711 (1987)). An adjustment in the hourly rate for the delay in payment is an appropriate factor for adjustment of the rate.

Given the amount of time that EEO cases take, it is not unusual that counsel's rate has changed between the time of the oriGINAl retainer agreement and the time of submission of the fee petition. The appropriate hourly rate is the one in effect at the time of submission of the petition. See, e.g., Coard v. Attorney General, 01A02439 (2002).
