1. Priority Referrals

A situation closely akin to the cancelation of a vacancy announcement occurs when an agency fills a position from a priority referral list without posting any vacancy announcement. The most accurate statement of the Commission's current position on such a situation is that if there is any conceivable way the complainant could have received consideration for the position, a claim for relief is stated. Whether the complainant should have received any consideration goes to the merits of the claim. As a result, the claim must be investigated even if there is virtually no likelihood the complainant could succeed on the merits.

The Commission fully explained this position in Lambright v. Secretary of Army, 05A01053 (2001), relying on its previous decision in Pena v. Secretary of Army, 01A02980 (2000). The Commission in Lambright held:

In Lambright, complainant alleged discrimination based on age (57) when the agency filled the position of Production Controller (Metals Products), GS-11, based on Special Consideration Referral (Retained Pay). Agency regulations required that candidates on a special referral list be considered for any vacancy prior to the posting of a general vacancy announcement. Here, the selecting official exercised his discretion and made a selection from the referral list; thus, no vacancy announcement was posted for general application. The agency determined that complainant was not harmed by the selection, since he was not on the special referral list and never considered for the vacancy. The previous decision agreed with the agency that complainant failed to state a claim. Complainant requests reconsideration of the previous decision, relying on the decision in Pena v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01A02980 (June 26, 2000). In Pena, which presented an identical factual situation, we reversed the agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to state a claim and remanded the complaint to the agency for continued processing.

In Pena, complainant filed a formal complaint alleging discrimination on the basis of race (Hispanic) when he was not selected for the same position. In its final decision, the agency dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim, concluding that complainant's claim did not set out a personal harm or loss. Complainant appealed, and the previous decision reversed the agency and remanded the complaint for processing. The agency requests reconsideration of the previous decision. In its comments, the agency argued that, because it was required to initially consider persons on the special referral list, complainant was never in competition for the position, and thus his complaint was properly dismissed for failure to state a claim.

The Commission's regulations require an agency to accept a complaint from an aggrieved employee (or applicant for employment) who believes that s/he is the victim of discrimination based on a purview covered by the EEO statutes. 29 CFR 1614.103; 1614.106(a). An employee is "aggrieved" if s/he has suffered direct and personal deprivation with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. Diaz v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994). To state a justiciable claim, therefore, the complainant need only set out a basis of discrimination within the purview of the EEO statutes with regard to some term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. See Cobb v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05970077 (March 13, 1997); Ferrazzoli v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991).

Where a complaint does not allege a legally sufficient claim, the Commission's regulations provide that an agency dismiss the complaint because it fails to state a claim. 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(2); See also Fed.R.Civ.P. 12(b)(6). In deciding to dismiss a complaint, like motions to dismiss filed in the federal court, an agency must construe the complainant's allegations as true and in the light most favorable to the complainant. See Cobb, supra; Wright and Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure, Civil 2d 1357. In addition, dismissal for failure to state a claim necessarily includes a finding that no relief could be granted under any set of facts that could be proved consistent with the allegations. Hishon v. King & Spalding, 467 U.S. 69, 73 (1984); see Rosa v. Park West Bank & Trust Co., 214 F.3d 213 (2000).

In dismissing a claim, however, an agency may not consider the ultimate merits of a complainant's claims. The Commission has repeatedly reversed agencies' dismissals for failure to state a claim where the agency based the dismissal on its view of the ultimate merit of the complaint allegations. Cobb v. Department of the Treasury, supra, fn. 3. A complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the complainant can prove no set of facts in support of the claim which would entitle the complainant to relief. Hishon v. King & Spalding, supra; see Rosa v. Park West Bank & Trust Co., supra.

Here, both complainants alleged that the agency improperly filled the position at issue. In dismissing the complaint for failure to state a claim, the agency determined that, because the selectee was chosen from a special referral list, complainants were not aggrieved. We find, however, that the agency's explanation is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether complainants have stated a justiciable claim, and it improperly addresses the merits of complainants' complaint. See Ferrazzoli v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991). This violates the principles stated, above, that the alleged facts must be construed in the light most favorable to the complainant and that an agency may not dismiss a complaint for failure to state a claim where relief may be available to complainant. For example, in the instant matter, an investigation may allow complainant to show that the agency's use of the special referral list was in fact a pretext to prevent complainant and members of his protected group from applying for the position or that the selectee was not properly on the Special Consideration Referral list.

In support of its argument that it properly dismissed the complaint in Lambright, the agency cites Simpson v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01992899 (February 8, 2000), a non-precedential decision. In Simpson, unlike the situation herein, the complainant was confronted with an absolute bar to participation in the program which would have allowed her to receive the reassignment she sought. Complainant alleged discrimination on the bases of race (black), sex, and age (DOB 12/5/52) when she was not considered for a reassignment. The reassignments were non-competitive positions, limited to employees who received a reduction-in-force (RIF) notice and were eligible for participation in the agency's VSIP (Voluntary Separation Incentive Pay) program. In her complaint, the complainant did not allege that she had received a RIF notice or that she participated in the VSIP program.

Unlike the situation in Simpson where complainant was not eligible for reassignment under any reasonable view of the facts alleged, the agency does not contend that its regulations required selection from among those on the special referral list so that all others could not have applied under any circumstances. Moreover, we note that the selecting official acknowledged that it was within his discretion to choose a selectee from the special referral list and that he could have broadened his selection base, presumably by posting a vacancy notice open to all, including the complainants.

The allegations herein are sufficient to render both complainants aggrieved, i.e., their claims of discrimination based on age and race raise allegations within the purview of the EEOC regulations for which relief is available. For this reason, the agency's decisions to dismiss complainants' complaints for failure to state a claim was improper. The previous decision in Lambright is REVERSED, the previous decision in Pena is AFFIRMED, and the complaints are REMANDED to the agency for further processing in accordance with this decision and applicable regulations.
