B. DISMISSAL OF COMPLAINTS

EEO counselors have no right to dismiss complaints, regardless of their beliefs on the merits of the complaint. In Cardova v. Secretary of Army, 05880702 (1988), the Commission found:

In this regard, the Commission first notes that, pursuant to the provisions of FPM Letter 713-21, if the allegations in a complaint are vague, the agency must first reference the EEO Counselor's Report in order to sufficiently identify and specify the unclear allegation. If such reference does not succeed in identifying the allegation with sufficient specificity, then the complainant should be given the opportunity to furnish more specific information. Only if the complainant is unable or refuses to do so, may the agency--in a final agency decision--cancel the complaint for failure to prosecute. The EEO Counselor's refusal to even take appellant's proffered complaint clearly failed to comply with the standards set forth in FPM Letter 713-21. Moreover, appellant was not only denied proper processing of his complaint, but also, as a result, was not issued a final agency decision.

The Commission next notes that, even absent the "two sentence statement" which constituted appellant's complaint, the Commission can clearly identify at least one allegation from the EEO Counselor's Report. Moreover, appellant's allegation can be stated in one sentence, to wit; He alleges he was subjected to discrimination based upon his Hispanic origin when he was nonselected for the position of Heavy Mobile Equipment Foreman, WS-07. There would be no adequate basis for rejecting such an allegation on the grounds of "vagueness" and the EEO Counselor's rejections are completely without merit, as the "how" is appellant's nonselection, the "when" is the effective date of the selection, the persons "who discriminated against him" would be the persons involved in the selection decision and the person "who was treated differently" was the selectee. Finally, the appellant was under no obligation to transmit his formal EEO complaint on any agency form. While the use of such a form may be desirable and provide guidance to complainants in structuring their complaints, the failure to use such a form provides no basis for rejection or cancellation of the complaint.

EEO counselors must also be careful not to discourage complainants from pursuing claims through the EEO process because they believe they are better addressed through labor-management dispute resolution processes. "Matters of controversy may appear at times as labor management issues and the same issue can also involve EEO matters and the Counselors should not attempt to discourage employees seeking counseling from further processing their legitimate concerns." Loeza v. Postmaster General, 01903230 (1990) (concerning an allegation that an employee's supervisor wrote a defamatory letter to the San Francisco Labor Board Commissioner).

A refrain heard all too often from complainants is that they have been discouraged by the EEO counselor from filing a complaint. Much of this may occur because the counselor does not believe the employee has a valid complaint. It is not, however, the role of the counselor to determine the validity of the complaint or the likelihood of success. The Commission will waive the time limit for filing a complaint where there is clear evidence that the counselor's actions served to discourage the complainant from proceeding. McGrath v. Veterans Administration, 01850530 (1987) (counselor advised a complainant not to "rock the boat"). As noted above, this situation has been addressed specifically in the new regulations at 29 CFR 1614.105(g).

If the complainant alleges a nexus between some agency action and his employment, the agency must provide EEO counseling even if the complainant is not an employee of that agency. It ultimately may be that the complainant does not have standing to pursue the complaint, but that is a determination that must be made after counseling has been provided and a formal complaint of discrimination has been filed. In Jordan v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 05930454 (1994), the Commission found it was improper for the agency to deny EEO counseling to the employee of a contractor where the complainant alleged it was the agency's action in denying his security clearance that resulted in loss of employment with the contractor.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.105 provides that aggrieved persons who believe that they have been discriminated against on the bases of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, or disability must consult an EEO Counselor prior to filing a complaint in an effort to try to informally resolve the matter. Our regulations further provide that the EEO Counselor shall conduct a final interview with the aggrieved individual within 30 days of the date the aggrieved person brought the matter to the EEO Counselor's attention. However, our regulations also provide that, if the matter has not been resolved within this time period, the aggrieved individual shall be informed in writing by the EEO Counselor, not later than the thirtieth day after contacting the EEO Counselor, of his/her right to file a discrimination complaint. But, most importantly, our regulations provide that an EEO Counselor shall not in any manner attempt to restrain the aggrieved individual from filing a complaint.

Appellant contended that he was hired by SWEC as an Electrical Field Engineer (permanent position for 3-5 years) and was subsequently assigned to the agency's Brown's Ferry Nuclear Plant facility on August 17, 1992. Appellant further contended that, based upon the agency's internal policies, he was required to apply for unescorted access clearance to actually work within the agency's facility. Appellant indicated that his application was not denied by SWEC, but by the agency's managerial officials. Appellant also indicated that, as a direct result of the agency's actions, SWEC terminated his employment in September 1992. Appellant appears to contend that the agency used intrusive practices to control the means and manner of the employment of contract employees, which ultimately led to his termination. We find that, despite the lack of a conclusive finding on the dispositive issue as to whether appellant was an employee of the agency, appellant has alleged a nexus between his employment and the agency to necessitate that the agency afford him EEO counseling. In reaching this conclusion, the Commission advises the parties that this decision does not constitute a decision on the merits of the appellant's arguments. Rather, it is the determination of the Commission that appellant has proffered sufficient facts as to provide a basis for EEO counseling. As previously discussed, the agency can thereafter make a determination, once a formal complaint has been filed, as to whether appellant is an "aggrieved person" under EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.105. Based on the foregoing, we find the previous decision properly found that the agency erred when it denied appellant pre-complaint counseling on the allegations raised by appellant. Notwithstanding our concurrence with the previous decision's procedural determination, the ORDER set forth in the prior decision is MODIFIED as noted herein.

The Commission notes that, on remand, the agency shall provide appellant EEO counseling and afford appellant an opportunity to clarify his allegations of discrimination. Thereafter, the agency shall issue appellant a notice of his right to file a formal complaint in this matter. Subsequent to filing the complaint, the agency shall make a proper determination as to whether the allegations raised in the appellant's formal complaint involve claims of disparate treatment or merely question the substance of the agency's security clearance decision. If the agency determines that this matter is limited to a merits determination regarding its decision to deny appellant's security clearance, then the agency may procedurally dismiss appellant's complaint for failure to state a cognizable claim under EEOC Regulations. However, if the agency makes a proper determination that this matter is not purely related to a security clearance issue, and finds that appellant's complaint also involves claims of disparate treatment, the agency must make a proper determination as to whether appellant is an "employee" of the agency pursuant to the Commission's application of factors set forth in Spirides v. Reinhardt, 613 F.2d 826, 831-32 (D.C. Cir. 1979), a case examining whether the plaintiff was an independent contractor or an employee of the agency.

It is improper for a counselor to refuse to interview the complainant in the presence of counsel. See In re Taylor, 05850072 (1987). However, there is no error where the agency takes prompt action and assigns another counselor to the case.

An agency may not dismiss a complaint because of a failure to timely initiate counseling if timely contact with a counselor is made, but that counselor refuses to process the complaint. See Harris v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01973674 (1998).

The Commission has now codified its previous case law in 29 CFR 1614.105(g), which provides that the "[c]ounselor shall not attempt in any way to restrain the aggrieved person from filing a complaint."
