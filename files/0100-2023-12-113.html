12. Unions

As discussed in Chapter 1, the Commission has held that an employee cannot file a complaint with his employing agency alleging that his union engaged in discrimination. Reardon v. USPS, 01860152 (1986). That does not preclude an employee from filing a complaint alleging discrimination against a union directly with the EEOC. However, the mere involvement of union officials in the alleged discriminatory actions does not remove the complaint from the ambit of the agency EEO process. As the Commission explained in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120141058 (2014):

Here, Complainant alleged in his complaint that on June 4, 2013, a vote was taken at a union meeting to remove him from his position as a union steward. The Agency correctly determined that the EEOC lacks jurisdiction to adjudicate the matter as it under the jurisdiction of a separate forum relating to administrative proceedings of union elections. See Wills v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05970596 (July 30, 1998). Complainant should raise his concerns within the grievance process itself, or some related process, and not through the EEO complaint process.

Even though a complaint involves the actions of union officials, it should not be dismissed where there is some evidence that the officials are using the shelter of the union to engage in activities prohibited by Title VII. In Bray v. Secretary of Treasury, 01941865 (1994), the complainant alleged, inter alia, that union officials reported her to the Inspection Service, threatened to file an unfair labor practice against her, and accused her of falsifying government documents. The agency dismissed the complaint. The Commission reversed and remanded the complaint for further investigation, noting:

The agency asserted that the individuals in question were acting in their official capacity as Union representatives, and, therefore, it had no authority to implement the relief requested. The Commission recognizes that actions of Union officials in the course of legitimate labor-management disputes are usually not actionable under the EEOC Regulations. However, the Commission is concerned that, in certain cases, officials may be merely acting under the guise of their Union status in order to engage in conduct prohibited by Title VII. In the case at hand, it is unclear from the record whether the actions complained of herein occurred during the course of an ongoing labor-management dispute. In addition, there is no evidence showing that the alleged responsible officials were employees of the agency. Without such evidence, we cannot determine whether appellant's allegations do in fact state a claim within the meaning of 29 CFR § 1614.103. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.107(a) is REVERSED, and the complaint is REMANDED to the agency for a supplemental investigation in accordance with the following Order.

The distinction is that in Bray, the complaint raised the prospect that the union officials may be acting on behalf of the agency. If that was found to be so, the complaint would not properly be against the union, as an entity, but the agency.

On reconsideration, the Commission modified the Bray decision to change the focus from whether the actions occurred in the context of an ongoing labor-management dispute to whether the actions were taken by union officials "acting in their representational capacity concerning protected matters." In Bray v. Secretary of Treasury, 05940748 (1995), the Commission wrote:

In its request to reconsider the previous decision, the agency argues that the decision involves an erroneous interpretation of federal labor management law in that the decision ordered the agency to determine whether the actions complained of by appellant occurred within the context of an ongoing labor-management dispute. The agency asserts that this statement reflects the previous decision's erroneous determination that the actions complained of needed to be related to an "on-going" dispute in order to be protected under the Federal Labor Management Statute (FLMS). Specifically, the agency asserts that the Federal Labor Relations Authority determines whether the conduct of Union officials is subject to agency control by addressing 1) whether the disputed conduct or speech occurred while the Union official was engaged in "protected activity" and 2) whether the conduct or speech was so outrageous as to be removed from the protection of the FLMS. The agency indicates that in order to be protected, the official must be acting in a "representational" rather than in his personal capacity, concerning "protected matters." In this regard, the agency asserts that in the present case, the information contained in the EEO Counselor's report on the subject complaint demonstrates that the complained of matters occurred while the named individuals were acting in their representational capacity concerning protected matters. The agency asserts that allegation no. 6 involved a complaint typed on official Union stationary concerning situations which could have constituted, respectively, an unnegotiated change in working conditions and an ethics violation, both matters appropriate for Union attention. The agency noted that it responded to these complaints by letter directed to the Union President in his official capacity at the Union's address. The agency noted that the January 28, 1993 letter described by allegation no. 2, which warned appellant of a possible ULP charge, was written by the Union President in his official role and concerned appellant's alleged interference with the Union's organizational integrity by encouraging a Union steward to resign her stewardship.

The agency also asserts that it is presenting new and material evidence warranting reconsideration, in the form of a copy of a March 30, 1994 ULP complaint filed by the Union alleging that the agency violated the FLMS when appellant, in her capacity as an agency representative, frivolously and maliciously filed an EEO complaint against the Union President. The agency points out that the ULP complaint alleges that appellant stated before the EEO counselor that her complaint was based on the Union President's union activities. The agency asserts that the filing of the ULP complaint by the Union only points up that the complaint actually concerns the relationship between agency management and local Union officials rather than any alleged failure by the agency to correct race or gender harassment.

...

We reach this determination based upon our view that the record indicates that several of appellant's allegations involve actions taken by the subject Union officials in their representational capacity. Specifically, we find that there is sufficient evidence in the record to indicate that allegation nos. 2, 3, 5a, 6, and 7 were actions taken by the Union President and/or Union Steward in their official representational capacities, e.g. the letters at issue were on official Union stationary and involved matters of potential concern to the Union. Therefore, the previous decision's remand for a supplemental investigation on these issues was erroneous.

However, we find that the previous decision correctly determined that there was insufficient evidence in the record to decide this question with regard to allegation nos. 1, 4, and 5, since it is unclear from the record what capacity the subject officials were acting in when they complained to the agency's District Director and Inspection Service concerning appellant and whether the matters complained of involved Union issues. However, we will modify the Order of the previous decision to reflect that the more precise inquiry to be conducted on remand is whether the actions complained of occurred while the subject officials were "acting in their representational capacity concerning protected matters" rather than whether the actions complained of occurred "within the context of an ongoing labor-management dispute." Finally, we note that the agency's reliance on the ULP complaint recently filed by the Union concerning appellant's EEO complaint as new and material evidence warranting reconsideration of the previous decision is misplaced, in that it constitutes hearsay on the part of the Union concerning appellant's view of the subject actions.

Damage to an employee's reputation as a union steward does not state a claim of discrimination. See Sortino v. Postmaster General, 05960067 (1998) (citing Bray, supra). But, the mere fact that the allegation involves union business does not remove it from the ambit of Title VII. As the Commission found in Morelli v. Postmaster General, 01985141 (1999):

With regard to claim 1, we find that complainant does state a claim. Complainant alleged that the OIC failed to notify him of three calls concerning union business. Clearly, notification of telephone calls could be a condition or privilege of employment. Since complainant alleged that this failure was due to his national origin and in reprisal for his prior EEO activity, he has stated a claim within the purview of the EEO regulations. The agency's apparent contention that complainant was properly not notified of the subject three calls because they involved union business, goes to the merits of the claim and is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether he has stated a justiciable claim under Title VII. See Ferrazzoli v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991). Accordingly, the agency's dismissal of claim 1 is hereby REVERSED.

The Commission's decision in Bray does not insulate an agency from liability for sexual harassment by union officials. See Carpenter v. Postmaster General, 01971355 (1998). In Carpenter, the Commission reversed an agency dismissal of the complaint where the complainant alleged that a local union president circulated a letter accusing her of sexual promiscuity. In rejecting an argument that it was a matter between the complainant and her union, the Commission noted the agency would be liable for harassment by coworkers where the harassment was reported and the agency failed to take prompt and effective remedial action. A similar result was obtained in McKee v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01973460 (1998), where the complainant alleged that the union president had distributed a letter, at an agency meeting, accusing her of having an affair with her supervisor. The Commission held that the complaint "does not exclusively (if at all) concern union matters." Id. By contrast, in Collier v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A12194 (2001), the Commission found that an allegation that a union official and coworker removed the complainant from his union steward position did not state a claim because it related to representational duties taken in an official union capacity.

Although Title VII does not normally protect conduct relating to union activity, a complaint is within the purview of Title VII if there is evidence that any interference with union activity was based on an animus related to a protected group under Title VII. In Porter v. Postmaster General, 01954125 (1996), the complainant alleged that management disclosed a letter which revealed his services to the NAACP to intentionally provoke racist sentiments in his white coworkers in order to defeat his election as shop steward in November 1994. See also Mitchell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05960656 (1998) (an employee stated a claim where she alleged that she was treated more harshly than male union representatives because of her sex and prior EEO activity).
