5. Hearsay Evidence

Mastering the rules of evidence with respect to so-called "hearsay" evidence or testimony has proved the undoing of many a law student. Fortunately, the Commission, like most other administrative bodies, does not strictly follow the Federal Rules of Evidence. The good news is that, with limited exceptions, hearsay is not a legitimate objection to documentary evidence or testimony and so it is not necessary to have a detailed understanding of the hearsay rule and its many exclusions and exceptions. The not-so-good news is that a basic understanding of hearsay evidence is important because the probative value and credibility given to a particular piece of evidence can be affected by whether or not it is hearsay.

First, some definitions. Rule 801 of the Federal Rules of Evidence provides:

(a) Statement. A "statement" is (1) an oral or written assertion or (2) nonverbal conduct of a person, if it is intended by the person as an assertion.

(b) Declarant. A "declarant" is a person who makes a statement.

(c) Hearsay. "Hearsay" is a statement, other than one made by the declarant while testifying at the trial or hearing, offered in evidence to prove the truth of the matter asserted.

Evidence can be hearsay whether it is written or oral and even if it is merely conduct, provided that the conduct is intended to convey a message. However, not every statement made out of the hearing context is hearsay. In order to properly be considered hearsay, the evidence must be offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted by the statement or conduct. For example, if Witness A testifies that Witness B told him it was raining in Washington, D.C. on a certain date, that statement may or may not be hearsay, depending on the reason it is offered. If it is offered to prove that it was in fact raining in Washington on that particular day--the truth of the matter asserted--then it is hearsay. If, however, it is offered merely to show that Witness B was not in Washington on that day because weather reports show it was sunny, it is not hearsay; that is, the statement is not being offered to prove what it asserts.

Since hearsay evidence is admissible in EEO hearings, the recognition of hearsay is not important in terms of objecting to the evidence itself. It is important in making arguments about how the administrative judge should view that evidence. The primary reasons for the exclusion of hearsay in court proceedings is that the person to whom the statement is attributed was not under oath at the time of making the statement and, thus, had no particular obligation to tell the truth; and that the person to whom the statement is attributed may not be available to testify and, therefore, his or her credibility cannot be directly challenged through cross-examination. For the same reasons, hearsay admitted into EEO hearings should generally not be given the same weight as direct testimony under oath.

Not all hearsay evidence, however, is considered unreliable. The Federal Rules of Evidence provide for several exclusions and exceptions to the hearsay rule. Certain types of evidence are specifically excluded from the definition of hearsay even though they otherwise would fall within the general definition of hearsay. For example, an admission by a party-opponent is excluded from the definition of hearsay. See Fed. R. Evid. 801(d)(2). Other types of evidence are considered exceptions to the hearsay rule. For example, an excited utterance--a "statement relating to a startling event or condition made while the declarant was under the stress of excitement caused by the event or condition"--is admissible even though it is hearsay. See Fed. R. Evid. 803(2).

As a general rule, administrative judges will not exclude evidence because it is hearsay. Instead, the evidence will be admitted and the administrative judge will use the fact that the evidence is hearsay as one criterion in evaluating the weight to be given that evidence. An exception to the general rule is made by some judges where the hearsay is simply too attenuated to have any credibility. This exception is applied where a witness is asked to testify about something he or she heard second or third-hand.

In Anderson v. Department of Commerce, 01871383 (1988), the Commission found that it was error for the administrative judge to rule that an unsworn affidavit was "unreliable hearsay." The error made by the judge was not in discounting the affidavit but in making a ruling on the record that made it unclear whether he was admitting or rejecting the evidence. The Commission noted that such a ruling left the respective parties unclear as to whether or not they needed to introduce further evidence corroborating or rebutting the affidavit.

Hearsay evidence not only is admissible in administrative proceedings, but where the hearsay constitutes direct evidence of discrimination it can be sufficient to warrant a finding in the complainant's favor. In Thomas v. Secretary of State, 01932717 (1994), the complainant presented hearsay testimony of a statement by an unidentified personnel official to the effect that the complainant would receive a low promotion rating based on her involvement in an "administrative proceeding." Since the only administrative proceeding the complainant in Thomas was involved in was her EEO complaint, the Commission determined the comment was sufficient indicia of retaliation:

Appellant persuasively argues that the fact that the comment is hearsay does not detract from its evidentiary value. She cites to Hoska v. United States Dept. of the Army, 677 F.2d 131 (D.C. Cir. 1982) in support of the admissibility of hearsay in administrative proceedings. The Court in Hoska states that under certain circumstances, hearsay can constitute substantial evidence, 677 F.2d at 138, and cites to several cases for the standards determining its acceptability. For example, in Calhoun v. Bailar, 626 F.2d 145, 149 (9th Cir. 1980), the tests are whether the hearsay is probative and bears indicia of reliability. We agree with appellant that the comment is relevant and material. We also agree that the comment bears sufficient indicia of reliability as suggested by Calhoun: Because the CDO reporting the comment was removed from the withholding action by both time and office, and any bias she may have would more likely be in favor of the agency than of appellant, she may be viewed as a disinterested witness; the CDO provided a sworn affidavit; and her testimony is not contradicted by any other evidence. In that regard, we note that the agency in its investigation could have, but did not, canvas employees then in PER/PE to verify or rebut the truth of the reported comment. We conclude therefore that the reported comment is substantial and probative evidence, and find that the FAD was in error on this point. The Commission finds that appellant has shown that the agency retaliated against her for her participation in EEO activity.
