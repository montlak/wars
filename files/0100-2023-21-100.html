4. Past Harassment by Perpetrator

One constant battleground in sexual harassment cases is the issue of whether a complainant can introduce evidence that the alleged perpetrator previously sexually harassed other victims. The argument against the introduction of such evidence is based on Fed. R. Evid. 404(b) which provides:

Evidence of other crimes, wrongs, or acts is not admissible to prove the character of a person in order to show action in conformity therewith. It may, however, be admissible for other purposes, such as proof of motive, opportunity, intent, preparation, plan, knowledge, identity, or absence of mistake or accident.

In other words, a complainant may not introduce evidence that an alleged perpetrator previously engaged in sexual harassment for the purpose of corroborating the allegations of harassment at issue in a given complaint. But such evidence is admissible to demonstrate that the employer had knowledge of past sexual harassment complaints and/or failed to take prompt and effective corrective action.

When the case is being tried to an administrative judge, the rule poses little problem. Judges are presumed to be able to separate the various purposes for which evidence can be offered and to apply that evidence accordingly. In jury trials, the evidence can pose more of a problem; namely, that the jury will be confused by such evidence and use it impermissibly. Fed. R. Evid. 403 provides:

Although relevant, evidence may be excluded if its probative value is substantially outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues, or misleading the jury, or by considerations of undue delay, waste of time, or needless presentation of cumulative evidence.

Prior to excluding such evidence, a judge will consider giving a jury a so-called "limiting instruction." Essentially, the judge will instruct the jury that evidence of past harassment may be used to determine the employer's knowledge and the effectiveness of any remedial actions but may not be considered in determining whether a particular plaintiff's allegations of sexual harassment are true. In most instances, such evidence will be admissible with a limiting instruction. See, e.g., Jonasson v. Lutheran Child and Family Serv., 115 F.3d 436 (7th Cir. 1997).

In Frick v. Postmaster General, 05920272 (1992), the Commission, in the absence of corroborating testimony, nonetheless found sufficient evidence in the fact that the victim made contemporaneous complaints and also that evidence existed showing that the supervisor engaged in similar conduct with other employees.
