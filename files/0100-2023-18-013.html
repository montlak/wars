A. SCOPE OF REPRESENTATION

From every perspective, it is best for a complainant and his or her representative to clearly define the scope of representation. Although there may be less potential for confusion with an attorney, the potential is still there. This potential is even greater when dealing with lay representatives who may not be accustomed to making distinctions between legal determinations, which properly are made by a qualified representative, and other determinations on which a client should be consulted and, in many instances, should make the ultimate determination with the advice of the representative. If more than one representative is involved in a complainant's case, the scope of each representative should be clearly defined.

For the complainant who is considering retention of a representative, a discussion of the scope of representation should take place prior to the decision to retain. If there are conditions or limitations to be placed on the representative, he or she should know that in advance. In some instances, an attorney may believe such limitations or conditions would limit or otherwise interfere with the ability to adequately represent the client. In other instances, representational efforts can be seriously undermined in situations where the representative, exercising discretion within the traditional scope of representation, is forced to renege on commitments made to the opposing party or administrative judge.

Having selected a representative, the employee is then responsible for the actions or inactions of that representative. See Abatecola v. Veterans Administration, 01861810 (1986) (citing Johnson v. Department of Treasury, 721 F.2d 361 (Fed. Cir. 1983)). The fact that the employee's attorney had not followed his client's instructions in filing a timely appeal did not waive the filing period for the employee. See also Donaldson v. Postmaster General, 01954571 (1997) (that representative was injured did not excuse an untimely request for a hearing since the complainant remains responsible for processing the complaint). The complainant's responsibility for proceeding with the complaint, even when represented by an attorney, is now codified at 29 CFR 1614.605(e).

Conversely, the agency may not rely on any representations or contact with the complainant's attorney when the agency has not been notified that the attorney represents the employee in the discrimination case at issue. See Medina v. USPS, 01862587 (1987) (agency erred in dismissing a complaint after contacting complainant's criminal attorney about an investigative interview).

The Commission's regulations do not speak to the issue of the scope of a designated representative's authority. Jones v. Secretary of Army, 01881619 (1988). Agencies should pay particularly close attention to the Commission's findings in the Jones case, wherein the complainant's representative signed a memorandum of understanding which the agency believed settled the case. The document was not signed by the complainant. The complainant subsequently requested to have his complaint reinstated on the basis that he was unaware of the agreement and had neither consented to it, nor given his representative the authority to consent to it. To determine whether the agreement was binding, the Commission, in Jones, went beyond the four corners of the agreement:

Because there is no written document expressly stating the scope of the appellant's. . .representative's authority in our record, the scope of the representative's authority must be gleaned from the record. Under pertinent law, an agency's scope of authority can be defined by the way the principle (appellant), the representative, and the third party (agency) interact with each other. We can examine these relationships and make a determination on the. . .representative's scope of authority at the time he executed the settlement on behalf of appellant.

Under the facts of the case, including that appellant was not present at the settlement meeting and subsequently retained another representative, the Commission found the original representative exceeded the scope of his authority, vacated the settlement agreement and remanded the complaint for processing.

The Commission has since abandoned this approach, holding that a settlement agreement must be signed by the complainant. "Unless an attorney (representative) has express authority from an appellant to settle a case on his or her behalf, the representative has no authority to do so, and, as such, [any] agreement is not binding on the appellant." Finch v. Postmaster General, 05880030 (1988). This is clearly the better view, since it not only establishes a "bright-line" rule in such situations but also recognizes the proper roles of a representative and client. See also 29 CFR 1614.603.

The fact that an employee filed an EEO complaint does not entitle her to representation in all dealings with agency management. The employee only is entitled to a representative in those matters that relate to the EEO complaint. Matuszeski v. Secretary of Treasury, 01956479 (1997).
