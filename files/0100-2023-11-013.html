D. ESTABLISHING DATE OF COUNSELOR CONTACT

The critical date for determining whether precomplaint counseling is timely initiated is the date that counseling was first requested and not the date counseling actually started. For example, the date for determining when counseling was initiated is not the date of the contact with the individual who provides counseling, where the initial contact is made with the EEO office. See, e.g., Widjaja v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A53673 (2005) (date of contact with EEO manager was timely even though contact with counselor occurred outside 45-day period); see also McDonald v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A03321 (2002).

It is the agency's responsibility to maintain records on the date of counselor contact. Further, it is the agency's burden to demonstrate the date of contact when it dismisses a complaint on the basis of timeliness. Baker v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01903288 (1990). When a complainant alleges that he contacted several officials in an attempt to timely obtain a referral to an EEO counselor, the agency may not dismiss such a complaint as untimely without securing affidavits from those officials. Ganino v. Postmaster General, 01920962 (1992).

In the event that the record is unclear as to when precomplaint counseling was initiated, the EEOC will look to the available evidence concerning agency contacts to determine whether a counselor likely was contacted on a timely basis. For example, the Commission in Myron S. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 202000024 (2019), considered who the complainant contacted and the content of those discussions to find that the complainant had initiated timely contact:

The alleged discriminatory event occurred on January 23, 2019, and the Agency justifies its dismissal by concluding Complainant did not initiate contact with an EEO Counselor about the matter until April 2, 2019, which is beyond the 45-day limitation period. The Agency acknowledges that Complainant spoke with the facility's EEO Manager on February 6, 2019, two weeks after his employment ended, and was provided with a brochure explaining the EEO complaint process. However, the Agency argues, in its brief submitted on appeal, that this contact was not related to the claim raised in his formal complaint and, therefore, did not fulfill his obligation to initiate timely EEO counselor contact.

A fair reading of Complainant's claim shows that he is alleging discrimination when his intermittent appointment as a food service worker expired on January 23, 2019, and it was not renewed. With regard to this contact, the EEO counseling report states that:

On or about February 6, 2019, the [Complainant] asked [named individual], the facility EEO Manager, about his employment and on March 27, 2019, he saw [the EEO Manager] again and told her that he submitted all the paperwork that the Human Resources Specialist told him about regarding Schedule A, VA disability letter, DD214. [The EEO Manager] then told him to contact ORM [Agency EEO complaint office].

The record also contains an August 16, 2019 letter from Complainant's representative to the Agency's EEO program. In that letter, Complainant's attorney represented that when Complainant spoke with the EEO Program Manager on February 6, 2019, "he understood it to mean that he met his obligation to come forward within 45-calendar days."

Despite the Agency's representations to the contrary, we conclude that Complainant's contact with the facility EEO Manager on February 6 was sufficient to constitute initial contact within the 45-day limitation period. It is clear that the topic of the contact was Complainant's concern that his employment with the Agency had been terminated. It appears that the EEO Manager, a person reasonably connected with the EEO complaint process, understood that he was intending to file a complaint because she provided him with a brochure concerning the complaint process as well as contact information for the complaint processing office. The fact that she also suggested that he explore being rehired through the Schedule A hiring authority for individuals with disabilities does not change the conclusion that Complainant evinced sufficient intent to file a complaint concerning his loss of employment. As such, we conclude that the Agency erred in dismissing the complaint for untimely EEO counseling contact.

When an employee receives errant advice from an EEO counselor on complaint rights, the date of contact will be the first date on which the counselor actually was contacted. See Krueger v. Department of Defense Dependent Schools, 01863102 (1987). In that case, the counselor told the employee that she could not initiate an EEO complaint because she had requested the union to initiate a grievance, but the employee was not covered by the negotiated grievance procedure.

When the complainant raises allegations in the formal complaint that were not raised with the EEO counselor, the agency should refer these allegations back to the counseling stage unless they are like or related to the allegations raised in counseling, in which case they constitute an amendment to the complaint. The Commission's policy is to use the date of the filing of the formal complaint as the date for establishing the timeliness of counselor contact. However, the Commission will deviate from this policy in appropriate circumstances. See, e.g., Fagarasan v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05910664 (1991). The complainant in Fagarasan presented unrebutted testimony that she had in fact raised the allegations with the counselor and further demonstrated that she was not fluent in conversational English and the counselor may not have fully understood her allegations. The Commission found the date of contact was the date of original contact with the counselor.

Most agencies have precomplaint counseling forms for the complainant to complete. There is nothing wrong with such forms. However, for timeliness purposes, the appropriate date is the date on which the complainant requests counseling and not the date on which the complainant submits the precomplaint forms. For example, in Nunez v. Postmaster General, 01A41365 (2004), the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of a complaint on timeliness where the complainant contacted the EEO office two weeks after his termination but did not submit the precomplaint forms until after the expiration of the 45-day period.

Referral back to precomplaint counseling is not required when the new allegations raised at the formal complaint stage are "like or related to" the allegations brought to the attention of the counselor. Like or related allegations constitute amendments to the complaint and do not require precomplaint counseling. For further discussion of complaint amendments, see the section on "New Claims Raised after Counseling," above and Chapter 3.

Where the complainant does not exhibit an intent to initiate the EEO process, the date of initial contact with an EEO counselor is not the date on which contact occurs for timeliness purposes. For example, in Miller v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A60846 (2006), the Commission held that conversations that the complainant had with the counselor did not count for purposes of timeliness where, during those conversations, the complainant specifically communicated a reluctance to initiate the EEO process. The date of contact was instead when the complainant's attorney sent a letter to the counselor indicating her intent to proceed.
