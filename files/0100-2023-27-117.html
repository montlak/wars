1. Collective Bargaining Agreements

The EEO process and its relationship to collective bargaining creates much confusion. The use of collective bargaining through negotiated grievance procedures to resolve discrimination issues is addressed in Chapter 20 under the heading "Negotiated Grievance Procedures." Collective bargaining agreements play a large part in establishing the terms and conditions of employment. The fact that a term or condition of employment is set through such an agreement does not remove it from the ambit of the discrimination statutes. But the Commission also must be on guard that it is not used as a mechanism to subvert the agreement.

Rogers v. USPS, 01881366 (1988), gave some insight as to how the Commission resolves the tension between collective bargaining rights of labor and management and its own role in the field of employment discrimination. In the Rogers case, the agency dismissed an allegation that the complainant was not given equitable treatment in the assignment of overtime hours on the basis that assignment of overtime was not a term, condition, or privilege of employment. The agency maintained that the assignment of overtime hours was covered by the applicable collective bargaining agreement and that the complaint, therefore, concerned the interpretation of that agreement. The agency noted previous Commission decisions where there was evidence of collusion by union employees in filing numerous EEO complaints regarding overtime assignments. The Commission rejected the agency's findings, noting there was no evidence of collusion in the Rogers case:

In the previous cases noted by the agency, the case records contained evidence of collusion, whereby numerous EEO complaints were filed during the same time period, alleging discrimination with respect to overtime distribution under virtually every basis provided by Title VII. The evidence revealed that discrimination was not the motivating factor in these complaints. Instead, the employees were using the EEO process to modify the Collective Bargaining Agreement on the issue of overtime distribution. In the present case, there is no evidence in the record establishing that appellant is abusing the EEO process by filing her complaint of discrimination. First, appellant's complaint is based on sex and reprisal only. Second, it was not filed within the same time period as the previously mentioned complaints. Finally, the agency has provided no evidence, other than its mere assertions, that discrimination is not the motivating factor in appellant's complaint....

The Commission does have the authority to determine if a collective bargaining agreement provision is being applied in a discriminatory fashion. See also Encomienda v. Postmaster General, 05920834 (1994).

A common area where contract rights come into conflict with individual rights under the discrimination statutes is reasonable accommodation under the Rehabilitation Act. For the Commission's treatment of an agency's obligation to set aside contractual provisions to accomplish reasonable accommodation, see Chapter 15 under the heading "Collective Bargaining Agreements."
