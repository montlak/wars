1. Actions Taken Against Persons Other Than the Complainant

In Thompson v. North American Stainless, 562 U.S. 170, 131 S. Ct. 863 (2011), the Supreme Court held that protection extended to the fiancé of an employee who filed a sexual harassment complaint when the fiancé was later fired. Thompson is discussed more thoroughly in Chapter 17 under "Complaints by Others--Association Complaints."

In Burak v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01971107 (1998), the Commission held that an allegation that an agency caused a former employee to be terminated from private sector employment as retaliation stated a claim.

A complainant who alleges that an investigation was initiated against her fiancé to keep him from testifying on her behalf in a sexual harassment complaint stated a claim for relief. Garcia v. Secretary of Treasury, 01932967 (1993). The Commission, in Garcia, rejected the agency's argument that the complainant had not alleged any personal harm:

The agency argues that this allegation fails to state a claim because appellant has not shown that she was the aggrieved party. The Commission disagrees. While it is true that appellant's fiance has suffered harm from the investigation, a close reading of appellant's allegation also sets forth harm personal to appellant. Appellant states that the DADLE ordered a biased investigation against appellant's fiance in an effort to deter him from testifying as a witness in her case. She claims that the action taken against her fiance could have a chilling effect on the processing of her case. She argues that it is possible that the DADLE, given his position of authority within the agency, will take such action against all of appellant's potential witnesses. Thus, we disagree with the agency that this allegation fails to state a claim. See Watlington v. Defense Logistics Agency, EEOC Request No. 05920080 (March 6, 1992).

But in Munson v. Secretary of Navy, 05930571 (1994), the Commission appeared to limit the scope of a complaint based on actions against third parties that are alleged to have a chilling effect on the complainant. In Munson, the Commission dismissed a complaint based on the allegation that the complainant's husband had been fired in an effort to discourage her from pursuing her complaint:

Appellant argues that she should be able to file a complaint concerning her husband's termination since she is suffering personal financial injury as a result of the termination. This argument, however, is not persuasive. The previous decision correctly held that appellant's husband is the injured party in this regard, not appellant. (Footnote omitted.)

We also find unavailing appellant's contention that the agency's termination of her husband was intended to have a chilling effect on her complaint. The Commission has found that such a third-party allegation may state a claim where it involves a witness in the complainant's complaint. See Shirley A. Garcia v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01932967 (August 27, 1993). However, no such assertion--i.e., that the agency was trying to prevent appellant's husband from testifying in her complaint and thus undermine the EEO process--is made here. Therefore, we find that the previous decision correctly held that appellant's Complaint #2 failed to state a claim.

The Commission did not explain why coverage should not be extended to a situation where the complainant alleges that an agency is attempting to restrain her from pursuing a complaint by taking action against her husband. It would seem that such an action would undermine the EEO process, regardless of whether the husband had any testimony relevant to his wife's complaint. It could well be that the Commission, as noted in its decision, found "unavailing" the contention of the complainant and was making more of a factual pronouncement than setting down a rule of law. In Lizarraga v. Secretary of Defense, 01983025 (2001), the Commission found prohibited retaliation where the agency moved two of the complainant's coworkers to another work area to separate the three individuals so they would not continue to file EEO complaints. Although the action was taken specifically against the coworkers, the Commission found there was an injury to the complainant because the agency's intent was to separate her from her coworkers.
