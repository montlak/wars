3. Breaks

Allowing a disabled employee to take additional work breaks can be a form of reasonable accommodation. Although it is somewhat less than clear from the Commission's case law, it appears that an agency may also be justified in reducing an employee's pay by the time not worked during the breaks. That would seem to be the implied holding of Mar v. Postmaster General, 01995071 (2002), where the Commission held that the agency had engaged in discrimination when it deducted the break time even when the complainant did not take the breaks. Using a McDonnell Douglas analysis, the Commission in Mar held:

The FAD erred in its analysis of the pay reductions which began once complainant notified the agency of her need to take as many as six five minute breaks per day. The agency ignored the foremost issue relevant to the pay reductions; namely, whether these pay reductions occurred because of complainant's request for an accommodation. We remind the agency that the Rehabilitation Act prohibits discrimination performed in reprisal for requesting for a reasonable accommodation. 29 C.F.R. 1630.12(b); EEOC Compliance Manual, Vol. II, Sec. 8-II(B)(2) (May 20, 1998). For the reasons we discuss below, the agency unlawfully retaliated against complainant for requesting an accommodation when it reduced her pay for time she actually worked, from May 23, 1994, until January 30, 1995.

Initially, we note that complainant has established a prima facie case of reprisal discrimination. Complainant delivered a request for reasonable accommodation to the agency on March 2, 1994, in the form of medical restrictions which provided that complainant should be allowed sit for five minutes for each hour she climbs, stands, and walks. These medical restrictions informed the agency that complainant needs an adjustment or change at work for a reason related to a medical condition and as such, constitute a request for reasonable accommodation. EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, EEOC No. 915.002, (March 1, 1999) at 25-26; see also, McNeil v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 05960436 (July 28, 1998). The agency does not dispute that it reduced complainant's pay by 30 minutes each day, following her request for the accommodation, even when she did not take the breaks. As a result, complainant, was not paid for all of the time she actually worked. We find that this denial of pay for hours worked constitutes an adverse action. The Commission has previously stated that denial of a job benefit is an obvious type of retaliation. EEOC Compliance Manual, Vol. II, Sec. 8-II(D)(1) (May 20, 1998). We further find that complainant has established a nexus between her request for an accommodation and the reduction in her pay. Specifically, the agency does not deny that it reduced complainant's pay to compensate for time she actually worked, from May 23, 1994, until January 30, 1995. Accordingly, we conclude that complainant has established a prima facie case of retaliation.

Since complainant has established a prima facie case of reprisal discrimination, the burden of production shifts, and the agency must provide a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions. Burdine, 450 U.S. at 253 (1981). We have considered the agency's rationale for reducing complainant's pay by 30 minutes per day. Apparently, the agency was attempting to recoup the losses it incurred when complainant took her doctor-recommended breaks. We find that this articulation is not responsive to complainant's claim that the agency reduced her pay even when she did not need to take the breaks recommended by her doctor.

We therefore find that complainant was discriminated against in reprisal for requesting the accommodation of six five-minute breaks from climbing, standing, and walking each day. We find that the agency unlawfully retaliated against complainant by reducing her pay even when she did not take the doctor-recommended breaks. It appears that the Department of Labor halted the pay reductions and that the agency returned pay improperly withheld. However, to make certain, we order the agency to pay complainant for the hours she worked, but was not paid, following her request for an accommodation....

In Dybvik v. Postmaster General, 0720070028 (2008), the Commission found that the agency failed to accommodate a diabetic complainant where it reprimanded him for being away from his machine to obtain insulin, gave him a negative performance evaluation and failed to renew his temporary appointment.

The Commission found that an employee with a mobility impairment was denied reasonable accommodation where the agency failed to consider his request for additional break time and modified arrival and departure times in Harland B. v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120130672 (2015), explaining:

Complainant requested accommodation for additional time during his breaks, apparently ranging from 5-15 minutes, in order to go on break and return to his work station. ROI, p. 107. Complainant also requested a modification to his arrival and departure work times. Id. Complainant had limited ambulation, therefore, it took him longer to get places. The record reveals that Complainant requested this, but was told he could not attain an accommodation. The declaration of the Department Manager confirms this: Once before we had.... RA [reasonable accommodation]/EE0 come over to our building to have a discussion with [Complainant] about RA was to help employee[s] do their job and not change their TOD [time on duty]. [Complainant] was under the impression that because he had difficulty walking, he could file an RA [reasonable accommodation] to let him come to work five minutes late each day, have an additional five minutes added to breaks and lunch and then leave five minutes early each day." Declaration of Dept. Manager, ROI, p. 107. The declaration of the Supervisory Contract Representative, Complainant's supervisor, also indicated that Complainant was denied his request for accommodation: "EEO explained that even though he had a disability he could not get additional time because other disabled employees are not granted additional time." ROI, p. 86.

Complainant identified a number of available potential reasonable accommodations. First, the Agency could have allowed Complainant extra time during breaks and to adjust his arrival and departure times, thereby eliminating the constant threats of placing him in AWOL. See, for example, Holland v. Social Security Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 01A01372 (Oct. 2, 2003) (finding that allowing for intermittent breaks away from the telephone at a teleservice representative job could have been a reasonable accommodation); EEOC v. Convergys Customer Management Group, 491 F.3d 790 (8th Cir. 2007) (finding an extra 15 minute break was a reasonable accommodation that would allow the plaintiff to comply with the employer's punctuality requirement; noting that employer did not put forth any evidence showing that extending the plaintiff's lunch break by an 15 minutes would eliminate its punctuality requirement); see also, EEOC Enforcement Guidance On Reasonable Accommodation, No. 915.002 (Oct. 2002) ("An employer [must] allow an employee with a disability to work a modified or part-time schedule as a reasonable accommodation, absent undue hardship." "A modified schedule may involve adjusting arrival or departure times..." (emphasis added. Complainant also indicated that there were "paper cases" available for employees to work at rather than being on the telephone. See ROI, p. 78. Finally, the Agency could have also considered reassigning Complainant to another position. EEOC Enforcement Guidance On Reasonable Accommodation, No. 915.002 (Oct. 2002).

Since Complainant identified possible accommodations, and the Agency did not effectively respond to any of them, much less demonstrate that any of them would have posed an undue hardship, Complainant demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that he was denied reasonable accommodation.

However, the employee must establish that there is a connection between the symptoms of the disability and the need for breaks. In Rasmussen v. Secretary of Navy, 0120080945 (2011), the Commission found that the agency had not failed to accommodate the complainant's diabetes where it allowed him to snack throughout the day but he insisted he needed to eat full meals in the break room. The complainant's supervisor testified that he requested documentation of the complainant's need to eat full meals in the break room and that he would have accommodated the complainant if such documentation had been provided. The Commission, in Rasmussen, noted:

The Agency is not required to provide an accommodation which Complainant fails to establish is necessary to enable him to perform the essential functions of his position, or to gain equal access to a benefit or privilege of employment. Frische v. Dep't of Def., EEOC Appeal No. 0120071609 (Jul. 1, 2009) (citing McGuffin v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A14173 (Dec. 3, 2002)). We note that the record is devoid of evidence indicating that Complainant's condition required him to sit down and eat full meals in order to regulate his glucose. Based on a review of the evidence, we find that the Agency did not deny Complainant a reasonable accommodation because Complainant failed to establish a link between his condition and the requested accommodation. See Frische, Appeal No. 0120071609 (citing Smith v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120055839 (Oct. 18, 2007); Brown v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A42650 (Sept. 2, 2004).
