broida eeo Chapter 10: Theories of Discrimination--Intentional Discrimination Chapter 10: Theories of Discrimination--Intentional Discrimination, Subchapter 02: Agency Burden of Production--Circumstantial Evidence, Section A: Disparate Treatment

A. DISPARATE TREATMENT

Once the appellant establishes a prima facie case of discrimination in a circumstantial evidence case, the agency bears the burden of production of evidence to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action. See Miller v. USPS, 01822272 (1983) (citing Burdine, supra. In the alternative, the agency can refute the existence of facts sufficient to support a prima facie case. See Rodriquez v. USPS, 01842037 (1986). In either instance, the burden of the agency is one of production of evidence, not the burden of persuasion. See Keval v. Commodity Futures Trading Commission, 01832127 (1984). The distinction is that the agency is not required to prove that its actions were legitimate. The burden of proof is always on the complainant to show the agency's actions were based on prohibited discrimination. When the agency offers no explanation for the discrepancy in treatment between the complainant and a similarly situated employee it does not carry its burden of production and the complainant prevails on the presumption established by the prima facie case. See Frady v. Postmaster General, 01A05317 (2003).

The purpose of having the employer articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action is to frame, with specificity, the inquiry into pretext. Quite simply, the complainant cannot be given a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate that the employer's reasons are pretextual, if those reasons are not articulated with sufficient clarity and specificity. For example, merely contending that the selectee was better qualified than the complainant in a selection or promotion case does not satisfy the employer's burden of production. Without an articulation of the specific ways in which the employer deemed the selectee to be better qualified, the complainant cannot reasonably be expected to demonstrate pretext.

If the agency offers a legitimate reason for its actions, the explanation offered by the agency must be legally sufficient to justify judgment for the agency. In Roseboro v. USPS, 01831308 (1983), the Commission explained:

The function of placing the burden of production on the agency to articulate a legitimate reason for its nonselection of appellant or its selection of the selectee is to frame the factual issue with sufficient clarity so that the appellant will have a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate pretext. Burdine, supra. Blanket denials of having engaged in any discriminatory conduct, without more, does not meet the agency's burden of production.

The agency's assertion that the appellant could not have been selected because he was not recommended as one of the best qualified candidates was rejected by the Commission as insufficient evidence to establish a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason where the selection panel relied on the recommendations of the appellant's supervisor and none of the panel members had firsthand knowledge of appellant's work performance. The Commission's decision was based to a large degree on the fact that the agency failed to establish, on the record, the criteria used by the selection panel and how those criteria were used to rank the various candidates. The agency's mere conclusory assertion that the other candidates were better qualified did not sufficiently frame the issue to allow the appellant to demonstrate pretext.

Sometimes, the agency's burden is stated in terms of substantial evidence on the record as a whole test. For example, in Dorofeeff v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 02900043 (1990), the Commission held:

[E]mployers generally have broad discretion to set policies and carry out personnel decisions, and should not be second-guessed by the reviewing authority absent evidence of unlawful motivation. Burdine, 450 U.S. at 259. Consequently, even if more than one conclusion can be drawn from the evidence, the agency's decision is to be upheld if its conclusion is supported by substantial evidence. Stevens v. Stubbs, 576 F. Supp. 1409, 1413 (N.D. Ga. 1983) citing Deutsch v. Atomic Energy Commission, 401 F.2d 404 (D.C. Cir. 1968). However, personnel decisions which are idiosyncratic or suspect will be subjected to heightened scrutiny. Loebb v. Textron Corp., 600 F.2d 1003, 1012, n.6 (1st Cir. 1979).

Stated another way, an agency meets its burden of production if it introduces evidence "sufficient 'to allow the trier of fact rationally to conclude' that the agency's action was not based on unlawful discrimination." Johnson v. Postmaster General, 01931010 (1993), quoting Burdine, 450 U.S. at 257.

In Wienecke v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 01941659 (1995), the Commission suggested that the agency's burden is not only to articulate a legitimate reason, but to provide at least some evidence in support of that reason. In the absence of any testimony of specific examples to support the articulated reason, the Commission in Wienecke held that the agency failed to fulfill its burden of production:

In the present case, the SO stated the reason appellant was not selected was that, although he was an excellent technician, "he was somewhat lacking in people skills," which he stated were very important because Position 1 is a "Lead" position. However, the SO offered no examples in support of this conclusion, nor did the agency submit sufficient independent evidence to support its explanation. Although the agency states in the FAD that the record contains a "plethora" of evidence regarding recommendations and selections, the SO was the individual responsible for the selections. Therefore, we fail to see, in light of the SO's vague and inexplicit affidavit, how the agency could expect to meet its burden of production absent the testimony of the SO. Accordingly, we find that the agency has not given appellant a "full and fair opportunity" to establish pretext, and find, for this reason, that the agency has failed to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for appellant's non-selection.

In Prevo v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 01972832 (2000), the Commission reaffirmed this principle and held the agency had not met its burden of production where the record contained no independent evidence to support the selecting official's contention that the complainant was less qualified than other applicants. The Commission in Prevo found:

While this is only evidence of the general experience complainant had, with nothing in the record to indicate the requirements of the positions she applied for, the evidence of this experience casts doubt on D's general statement that complainant lacked experience in banking, savings and loan, real estate and financial instrument matters. As for D's statement that complainant gained a reputation for being confrontational with supervisors, we note that complainant's performance appraisal from 12/31/90-12/31/91 gave her high marks for her ability to maintain a cooperative working relationship with others, as well as for accepting responsibility and making an extra effort. The reputation she had allegedly developed, as described by D, must therefore not have been made until the early months of 1992 at the earliest. Interestingly, these months were the very time during which complainant first complained about harassment at the hands of S1.

We are cognizant that the agency's burden to articulate a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for its actions is not an onerous one. In the case at hand, however, the record is without any independent evidence that demonstrates complainant was less qualified for the positions in question. Moreover, the evidence that does exist in the record casts doubt on D's explanation of complainant's qualifications and "confrontational" reputation. Consequently, the Commission finds that the agency has failed to set forth, with sufficient clarity, reasons for complainant's nonselection for a position in the Tampa office such that she has been given a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate that those reasons are pretext. See Parker v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900110 (April 30, 1990): Lorenzo v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05950931 (November 6, 1997). The agency has therefore failed to provide an articulation of its reasons for not selecting complainant for a position in the Tampa office sufficient to overcome complainant's prima facie case of reprisal.

We further find that even assuming the agency satisfied its initial burden, complainant established that the agency's articulation is pretext. Commission precedent holds that when a party fails to produce relevant evidence within its control, the failure to produce such evidence raises an inference that the evidence, if produced, would prove unfavorable to that party. See 29 CFR § 1614.108(c)(3)(i); see also, Holm v. Department of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05940916 (December 14, 1995), citing Medinav v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940468 (December 9, 1994). Here, the agency failed to provide any documentation on the positions at issue or the applicants and/or selectees for these positions. This failing on the agency's part makes it impossible to determine the accuracy of D's contention that complainant was not qualified. Moreover, the evidence that was provided casts doubt on the explanation offered by D as to why complainant was not selected. We find this to be an especially appropriate case in which to draw an adverse inference against the agency because, as noted above, the agency completed the investigation three years before it issued a FAD, indicating that it had no shortage of time in which to ensure that the record was complete. We therefore find that if the appropriate documentation had been provided, it would have established that complainant was not selected due to retaliation.[11]

[11] In writing this decision, the Office of Federal Operations attempted to contact someone with the agency who could establish whether it was still possible to obtain documents relating to 1992 RTC promotions. Despite repeated attempts, the OFO staff was not able to find any agency personnel who could address this possibility.

See also Gabriel v. Attorney General, 01A10946 (2002) (no explanation why the complainant, a Haitian female, was the only individual disciplined for the incident); Smithson v. Social Security Administration, 01A03598 (2000) (a vague explanation was insufficient to sustain agency's burden of production); Harding v. Postmaster General, 01983654 (2000) (asserting that managers have the authority to decide which employees to develop was not a sufficient explanation of selection for detail).

Failure of the agency to carry its burden of producing a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason will result in a failure to rebut the presumption of the complainant's prima facie case and warrants a finding of discrimination. See, e.g., Capers v. Postmaster General, 01A21933 (2003).

In Eisenhauer v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A01502 (2002), the Commission held that the administrative judge did not err in imposing an adverse inference against the agency where it failed to respond to her request to produce documents related to the veracity of the proffered reason. As a result, the judge was correct in determining that the agency had not produced a legitimate reason worthy of belief and, consequently, finding in favor of the complainant.

The evidence introduced by the agency must support a legitimate reason it has articulated prior to the hearing. For example, in Redondo v. Attorney General, 01995675 (2002), the Commission found that the agency did not sustain its burden when it asserted that the complainant was not granted a spousal transfer because he was not actually married at the time he requested it. The Commission rejected the evidence as insufficient because the agency did not assert prior to the hearing that it applied this criterion to the complainant.

Agencies must be careful of presenting only indirect evidence of their legitimate reasons, particularly where direct evidence is readily available. See Butler v. Postmaster General, 01882521 (1988) (complainant's supervisor did not testify but was available to do so; no affidavit was submitted and no one with direct personal knowledge of the legitimate reasons testified on behalf of the agency).

The legitimate reason offered by the agency must be reviewed to determine if it satisfies both the requirement of legal sufficiency for judgment and framing the issues with sufficient particularity. See Davis v. USPS, 01831721 (1983) (agency's explanation that it terminated appellant rather than impose progressive discipline satisfied both burdens where it demonstrated the particular offense never occurred at the installation and the agency did not wish to establish a precedent of progressive discipline in cases involving conversion of funds). Where the agency denies discrimination, but offers no alternative explanation for the conduct at issue, it has not satisfied its burden of production. See Houston v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01976054 (1999). In that case, the agency denied that a baseless theft charge brought by the supervisor was discriminatory, but offered no other explanation as to why the charge was made.

The agency must support its reason with "clear and specific" admissible evidence. See Burnside v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01831501 (1985). Subjective determinations, particularly, require that the agency produce evidence to substantiate its claim. Failure to do so exposes the agency to the risk that the appellant will take advantage of the agency's lack of proof in order to establish pretext. See Weaver v. USPS, 01860291 (1987).

The agency's evidence regarding its burden of production must go to the reasons why the agency took the disputed employment action. In Sloan v. Secretary of Defense, Defense Logistics Agency, 01933894 (1994), the Commission found that evidence and argument about the de minimis nature of the impact on the complainant does not constitute a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason:

The agency argued that appellant was not harmed because he retained his salary and his seniority when he was detailed to the lower grade. However, there is no evidence that the uncertainty appellant experienced about his place in the organization and the loss of face by appellant was unavoidable. There is no evidence that the agency could not have successfully accomplished its mission by detailing someone other than appellant to the lower grade, nonsupervisory position. The Commission finds that the agency has not articulated a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its employment decision. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the agency discriminated against appellant on the basis of his age as alleged.

When subjective determinations make up the agency's legitimate reason, the agency must present more evidence than just the conclusory testimony of the selecting official. Parker v. Postmaster General, 05900110 (1990). In that case, the selecting official testified that the successful candidate explained why he was assigning tasks to subordinate employees but the complainant did not give such explanations, and the successful candidate was more aggressive than the complainant in seeking out additional tasks. However, the selecting official could not cite a single example to illustrate his conclusions. The Commission found the agency failed to sustain its burden of production because the issue of pretext was not sufficiently framed by such conclusory testimony.

Similarly, in Swift v. Postmaster General, 01975658 (1999), the Commission found that the agency failed to carry its burden of production because its reason was not sufficiently specific to give the complainant an opportunity to demonstrate pretext. Simply stating that the complainant performed poorly in the interview did not give the complainant a fair opportunity to dispute that assertion. As the Commission explained in Swift:

Here the agency has failed to meet this burden. The explanation for its actions is neither specific, clear, nor individualized. The only explanation the agency offers for its actions is the assertion in the FAD that appellant was "not recommended to the selecting official because of [his] presentation during the interview." This statement is based on an affidavit submitted by one of the agency's interviewers. The affidavit is no more specific than the FAD. The only portion of the affidavit that addresses the reasons for appellant's nonselection states:

All applicants were interviewed however, based on the complainant's presentation during the interview he was not recommended for the job.

This evidence is insufficient to satisfy the agency's burden of articulating a legitimate reason for its action.

This is particularly so because of the highly subjective nature of the criterion the agency employed as the basis for its nonselection of appellant. The agency relied on a (presumably poor) "presentation" by appellant at his interview as the sole basis for his nonselection. The record contains no information other than the subjective opinion of two of the three agency interviewers by which the correctness of this assessment may be evaluated. There is no information whatever in the record that would permit a comparative analysis of the interview performance of the selectees with that of appellant.

A vague assertion that the complainant did not receive a preferred reassignment because of the "needs of the service" is not sufficient to sustain the agency's burden of production. As the Commission noted in Brooks v. Postmaster General, 05930625 (1994):

In short, the agency did not explain why similarly situated employees outside appellant 1's protected classes received preferred reassignments while appellant 1 did not and thus did not meet its obligation to afford appellant a fair opportunity to show pretext. Accordingly, the agency failed to rebut the prima facie inference of discrimination by articulating a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions. Therefore, we find that appellant 1 was subjected to discrimination based on race and sex when she was reassigned to the clerk craft.

Likewise, merely citing the "operational needs" of the agency does not fulfill the burden of production. As the Commission found in Cooper v. Postmaster General, 01943785 (1996):

Furthermore, we find that the agency failed to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for appellant's reassignment. The U.S. Supreme Court, in Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 253 (1981), explained that the employer's explanation for its actions must be legally sufficient to justify a judgment for the employer. The Commission has previously interpreted the term "legally sufficient" to mean that the explanation is set forth with sufficient clarity as to allow the employee a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate pretext. Parker v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05900110 (April 30, 1990). In this case, the agency asserted only that appellant's reassignment back to the GMF was based upon operational needs. The agency offered no further explanation or substantive evidence as to what specific operational needs required appellant, who was admittedly performing at the "good" level at the CRC, to be reassigned. Accordingly, we find appellant has established that the agency discriminated against her based on her sex with regard to this issue.

An agency does not fulfill its burden of production merely by asserting the existence of a policy that explains its actions. The agency must make some showing that the policy actually exists, that it was in operation at the time of the alleged discrimination, and that the agency was adhering to the policy. Brown v. Postmaster General, 01930077 (1993). In that case, the agency asserted that it had not reinstated the complainant because of an agency policy to downsize as the result of increased automation. The Commission remanded the case for further investigation because the mere assertion the policy existed was insufficient to make a well-reasoned decision on the merits.

Along similar lines, speculation that the agency was following a policy in making an employment decision is not sufficient to satisfy the agency's burden of production. As the Commission explained in Mayfield v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A21083 (2003):

In order to rebut complainant's prima facie case of race discrimination, management relied upon MCD's statements that generally, agency policy provided that complainant should have been realigned. MCD speculated that the reason the responsible person may have decided that complainant needed to apply was because he believed that the facility was operating under a 1997 agreement with the union that provided the opportunity for employees to compete for position instead of being realigned. We note, however, that MCD was not the official responsible for determining complainant's employment future at the Medical Center. COS was the management official responsible for the decision. COS did not explain his actions, and the record is silent as to how or why this decision was made.

We are cognizant that the agency's burden to articulate a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for its actions is not an onerous one. In the case at hand, however, the record is without any independent evidence that explains why complainant was required to apply for the position instead of being realigned. Consequently, the Commission finds that the agency has failed to set forth, with sufficient clarity, reasons for complainant's need to apply such that she has been given a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate that those reasons are pretext. See Parker v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900110 (April 30, 1990); Lorenzo v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05950931 (November 6, 1997). The agency has therefore failed to provide an articulation of its reasons for its action sufficient to overcome complainant's prima facie case of race discrimination. See Prevo v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, EEOC Appeal No. 01972832 (March 10, 2000).

The focus of the Commission is not in determining the legitimacy of the agency's actions, but whether the actions were based on discriminatory motives. As the Commission explained in criticizing the decision of one of its administrative judges in Williams v. Postmaster General, 01922318 (1993):

The AJ. . .found that there was no "just cause" for the agency's action, because the alleged crime did not involve an on-the-job or job-related action, and because it "did not have an adverse effect on employee or public relations, nor did it pose a threat to postal operations, property or personnel. In truth, it had no effect on the agency."

The AJ then cited Phillips v. Bergland, 586 F.2d 1007 (4th Cir. 1978), a case which did not involve a Title VII question, but addressed the discharge of a federal employee. The court stated, inter alia, that when a federal employee is discharged for misconduct entirely unrelated to the employee's work, the agency has a greater burden to justify its actions, and the agency must provide a rational basis that the discipline will promote the efficiency of the service. Relying on this case, the AJ stated that the agency "failed to provide independent evidence and failed to cite specific examples to support its reasons and, therefore, (appellant) was denied a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate that the (a)'s reasons are a pretext for discrimination." For this reason, the AJ determined that the agency had discriminated against appellant.

Upon careful review of the recommended decision, the Commission finds that the AJ's analysis is inappropriate to the extent that it goes beyond the bounds of a proper Title VII analysis. The issue, in this case, is not whether the suspension of appellant was appropriate, or whether there was "just cause" for it, but whether it was motivated by discriminatory reasons. Absent evidence of unlawful motivation, it is not the policy of the Commission to second-guess an agency's personnel decisions. Burdine, supra. The Burdine decision merely requires an agency to articulate a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for its action, at which point the burden shifts to appellant to show pretext. In this case the AJ, relying on Phillips, erroneously required the agency to establish that its articulated reason "promoted the efficiency of the service." Additionally, the AJ, contrary to the holding in Burdine, second-guessed the agency by finding that retaining appellant during the period in question would have posed no threat to personnel, and would have had no adverse effects. Therefore, the Commission finds appellant has not established that the reason articulated by the agency was a pretext for discrimination, and finds appellant has not established that the agency discriminated against her based on either race or sex.

Evidence that tends to disprove the validity of the agency's proffered reasons should be evaluated as evidence of pretext and in light of whether or not the complainant proved his or her case by a preponderance of the evidence. Evaluating the agency's proffered reasons in the absence of any evidence of pretext results in an impermissible shifting of the burden of persuasion to the agency.

The fact that the agency's articulated reason may be contrary to agency policies, collective bargaining agreement provisions or even statutes not enforced by the EEOC do not necessarily demonstrate that the responding management official had a discriminatory motive. For example, in Kimble v. Postmaster General, 0120093286 (2011), in response to the complainant's arguments that her request for medical treatment for an on-the-job injury was delayed contrary workers' compensation laws and a suspension for failure to report the accident violated the collective bargaining agreement, the Commission noted:

Complainant argues that the supervisor's articulated reasons are pretextual because they are inconsistent and contradictory to labor management agreements and workers compensation statutes. We find that Complainant's arguments are not sufficient to affirmatively demonstrate that the supervisor's real motivation was discriminatory animus based on Complainant's disability, race, national origin, sex, or age. An employer's business decision cannot be found discriminatory simply because it appears that the employer acted unwisely, or that the employer's decision was in error or a misjudgment. EEOC Compliance Manual, No. 915.003, at 15-17 (Apr. 19, 2006). Therefore, we find that Complainant failed to show that the Agency's explanations were pretexts for discrimination.

Just as the elements of a prima facie case vary depending on the circumstances so, too, does the evidence needed to support the agency's burden of production. The agency must tailor its rebuttal of a complainant's prima facie case to the individual circumstances of that case. See Osolinik v. Secretary of Interior, 05870101 (1988).

When the agency advances a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action, the Commission is not required to address the issue of whether the complainant has established a prima facie case. Kornis v. International Trade Commission, 02840001 (1987). "The reason for this holding is that 'now there is enough evidence available to make a decision on the merits of the complaint.'" Jiminez v. USPS, 01870265 (1988), citing United States Postal Service Board of Governors v. Aikens, 460 U.S. 711, 716, 103 S. Ct. 1478, 75 L. Ed.2d 403 (1983).
