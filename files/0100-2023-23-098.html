2. Forced to Work in Excess of Medical Restrictions

An employer is not required to lower production standards for a disabled person as long as those standards are uniformly applied to employees with and without disabilities. Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "General Principles, Reasonable Accommodation." In Belia S. v. Postmaster General, 2019002594 (2020), for instance, the complainant claimed that the agency forced her to work outside of her medical restrictions when it assigned her more work than she could complete. The Commission disagreed:

S2 stated as follows. Complainant told her a few times that she was given more work than she could complete, and on those days a portion of what was assigned was given to another employee by her. Likewise, Complainant was advised to let management know if she could not complete an assignment, and on several occasions, she returned with undelivered mail because of her restrictions. Complainant was not forced to work beyond her restrictions. On November 10, 2017, she explicitly told Complainant that she needs to let her know if she is assigned more walking than she can handle (a reference to mail delivery). We note this was shortly after the OWCP informed S2 by email that Complainant said she was working outside her restrictions (referring to delivering mail).

Complainant stated as follows. S2 was the person who forced her to work beyond her medical limitations, and this happened daily except when S2 did not do the daily assignments. S2's assignments to deliver mail were based on DOIS time, not the time it took her to deliver. When she got her assignments from S2, she advised she would need help to not exceed her restrictions, but was always informed that the assignment was within her restrictions based on DOIS time. Because this was a daily occurrence, she did not have evidence of the specific dates this occurred, except for a text exchange on October 24, 2017, which showed that after she was told she would get help that day, she was advised none was coming.

Complainant's first line supervisor (S1, a 204 B acting supervisor4) stated as follows. When she assigned Complainant routes or portions thereof, she always tried to give her mounted work (not required to exit the vehicle to deliver mail except when a package does not fit in the customer's mailbox), and S2 assigned routes that required more walking.

Complainant often said that because she could not keep up with the DOIS time delivery expectation, she often went over her walking limitation. Anytime S1 was working and Complainant went over her walking limitation, she told Complainant that if it was too much she needed to come in. When Complainant's doctor wrote a note that her walking restriction was based on what she could do, not DOIS time, S2 instructed S1 to bring Complainant back whenever she was going over her walking restriction. We find that the reference to the doctor's note likely regards the November 9, 2017, restriction that Complainant be allowed to work at a slower pace.

The preponderance of the evidence shows that by using DOIS to determine the amount of time it took to complete deliveries for Complainant, she was often assigned work that exceeded her walking limitation. S1 stated that from May-November 2017, the office was incredibly understaffed, detailed the shortage, and that this caused all carriers, including regulars, to frequently work past the office dispatch time of 6 P.M. S1 stated district management pressed hard for the work to get done, saying there were no excuses and to push carriers harder.

While the way delivery work was assigned to Complainant undoubtedly made her feel pressured to exceed her walking limitation to complete her deliveries, we find that she was not forced to do exceed her limitations. S1 stated that anytime she was working and Complainant went over her walking limitation, she told Complainant that if it was too much she needed to return. S2 stated that Complainant was advised to let management know if she could not complete an assignment, and on several occasions she returned with undelivered mail because of her restrictions. On November 10, 2017, S2 explicitly told Complainant that she needs to let her know if she is assigned more walking than she can handle. While the record does not reflect when Complainant was told to let management know when she could not complete an assignment, we find it significant that she does not contend she was disciplined for not timely completing an assignment. And while Complainant stated that she was forced to deliver mail even after informing management she already went beyond her restrictions, she did not contend that she was directed to continue delivering mail after informing management, nor contest that she was told if the delivery work was too much, to return to the office.

To prevail under the disparate treatment theory of discrimination, Complainant must satisfy the three-part evidentiary scheme fashioned by the Supreme Court in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973).

She must generally establish a prima facie case by demonstrating that she was subjected to an adverse employment action under circumstances that would support an inference of discrimination. Furnco Constr. Co. v. Waters, 438 U.S. 567, 576 (1978). The prima facie inquiry may be dispensed with for this claim, however, since the Agency articulated legitimate and nondiscriminatory reasons for its conduct. See U.S. Postal Serv. Bd. of Governors v. Aikens, 460 U.S. 711, 713-17 (1983). To ultimately prevail, Complainant must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the Agency's explanation was a pretext for discrimination. Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products, Inc., 530 U.S. 133 (2000); St. Mary's Honor Ctr. v. Hicks, 509 U.S. 502, 519 (1993).

Complainant contended that she was disparately treated from Coworker 1 (supervised by S1 and S2, Regular City Letter Carrier), because he was only assigned work within his restrictions, while she was assigned work using DOIS standards. All carriers were assigned work using DOIS standards. The Agency has a right to hold all employees to the same standard of production, even if they have a disability. Complainant was not forced to exceed her medical restrictions. Complainant has not shown she was disparately treated.

Complainant contended that being assigned to work beyond her restrictions constituted harassment. For the same reasons that we find Complainant was not denied reasonable accommodation or disparately treated, we find she was not harassed.
