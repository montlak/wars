1. Notice of Election Rights

An agency is required to fully inform an employee of his or her election of forum rights in mixed cases. The EEOC provides recommended language for such a notice in its Complaint Processing Manual, MD-110, Chapter 2. The notice must unambiguously inform the employee of the right to pursue his or her complaint through the agency EEO process or before the MSPB and also must inform the employee that the election of forum is irrevocable. See Archulet v. Department of Agriculture, 01851427 (1986).

There are actually two separate junctures at which the agency must advise a complainant of his or her MSPB appeal rights in a mixed case. The agency must advise the complainant of MSPB appeal rights at the time it advises the complainant it has accepted the mixed case complaint, and it must again advise the complainant of those rights upon completion of the investigation. Brevard v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01A11449 (2002).

The bottom line, however, is that the Commission has retreated from earlier case law and now adheres to the rule that an election of forum need not be informed. As a result, even if a complainant is erroneously not provided with appeal rights to the Commission, an election to proceed before the MSPB is binding. In Casias v. Postmaster General, 01972311 (1998), the Commission held:

The Commission agrees with the appellant that the agency erred when it did not provide him with appeal rights to the Commission. However, the Commission finds that the agency properly dismissed the appellant's complaint based on his prior MSPB appeal. The appellant had the right to challenge his demotion in the EEO process or before the MSPB, but not both. He elected to challenge his demotion before the MSPB, rather than in the EEO process, by filing first in the MSPB forum. See EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.107.302(b). If the appellant thought the MSPB Administrative Judge erred when it purportedly rejected his discrimination claim, he could have petitioned the full Board to correct the error.

In Archulet, supra, the agency informed the employee that he had the right to pursue his complaint of discrimination relative to his removal through the agency EEO process or to "combine" it with any appeal filed with the Board. The appellant in Archulet initially raised his allegations of discrimination in his Board appeal but never pursued them in the hearing before the Board:

The Commission finds that the language used in the agency's notice of removal to appellant concerning his "appeal rights" falls short of the standard required by 29 CFR 1613.403. The essence of that language is that appellant could continue with his EEO complaint, or "combine it" with an appeal to the MSPB. But the Commission finds that language fails to impart to a reasonable lay person that he must make an irrevocable election concerning which forum to use at the outset. In the absence of written evidence that appellant and/or his attorney were notified of the urgency of the election requirement, the Commission is not persuaded by the agency's claim that they were "so informed on numerous occasions orally and in writing in advance of the MSPB hearing of the election requirement...." If the agency's use of the expression "in writing" in this context is a reference to the agency's claim that it sent appellant's attorney a copy of the pertinent MSPB regulations, the Commission finds that the mere sending of such regulations to appellant's attorney is not sufficient to comply with the notice requirements....

The Commission ordered the agency to reinstate the EEO complaint for further processing.

Similarly, the Commission held that when an EEO counselor failed to advise a complainant of his MSPB appeal rights and the necessity of electing a forum, it would not serve as a bar to a subsequent EEO complaint that the employee had filed an MSPB appeal. Zuniga v. Postmaster General, 05920857 (1993). In that case, the agency did not assign a counselor to the complainant for 10 months and there was no indication that the complainant raised the issue of national origin discrimination before the MSPB.

However, the agency's failure to inform a complainant of election rights did not necessarily result in a reversal of any election. For example, in King v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05950154 (1995), the complainant filed an appeal with the MSPB. However, she later withdrew her allegation of discrimination in the Board proceeding and subsequently filed an EEO complaint. Whether the complainant in King was entitled to proceed with her complaint, the Commission explained, depended upon whether she contacted an EEO counselor prior to filing her Board appeal:

In this case, we do not know whether the agency ever informed appellant of the election requirement. If appellant sought EEO counseling regarding her removal before she filed her MSPB appeal, the agency counselor should have advised her at that time of the election requirement. It follows that if the agency failed in this duty, the agency's failure can be deemed to have caused appellant to mistakenly withdraw her race discrimination allegation from her MSPB appeal. Under those circumstances, appellant could not be said to have made a valid election, and therefore the agency would be estopped from relying on the MSPB appeal as a basis for dismissing appellant's EEO complaint. See Zuniga, supra.

If on the other hand, appellant filed her MSPB appeal before she sought EEO counseling on the removal, it was too late for the agency to provide information on the election requirement, and the agency's duty did not arise. Lance Lufkin v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05920187 (April 9, 1992). Under those circumstances, the only basis for faulting the agency for appellant's action in withdrawing her discrimination allegation would be if the agency affirmatively misled her or tricked her into the withdrawal. We note that neither appellant's appeal nor her RTR makes such a claim and indeed, appellant's attorney gave no reason for withdrawing the allegation other than to state that the discrimination claim was being preserved for an EEO complaint. Again, however, because of the scanty record submitted by the agency, we do not know whether misinformation is an issue here. Since the record as it now stands does not permit any findings on the question of whether appellant made a valid election, the record must be supplemented with sufficient information to support a determination on that question. The complaint is remanded for the agency to reach such a determination and, if it is determined that appellant did not make a valid election, the agency is further directed to continue processing appellant's complaint.

The net result of decisions like Casias, Archulet, Zuniga and King is that there are very specific requirements for what constitutes adequate notice of election rights, but there is no real remedy for failure to give adequate notice. Because of the brevity of the Casias decision, it also is unclear how that case effects earlier case law discussed below. But, the clear trend of the Commission is toward a position that an informed election is not necessary and the only questions to be resolved are whether an election of forum has been made and, if so, which forum has been elected. This is truly the simpler approach because it eliminates determinations regarding the employee's knowledge in making an election. Of course, simpler is not always better.

When the agency gives the complainant incorrect or confusing information that leads the complainant to forego his or her right to a hearing, the Commission will likely reverse any final agency decision on the matter and order issuance of a new decision with a proper election notice. For example, in McRoberts v. Archivist of the United States, 01993987 (2002), the agency errantly informed the complainant that she could appeal to the MSPB or request a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge. The complainant initially requested a hearing, but changed her mind and withdrew that request, asking instead for a final decision. Because the Commission believed the agency's errant notice had confused the complainant into foregoing an MSPB hearing, the Commission remanded the case to the agency with instructions to issue a new final agency decision correctly informing the complainant of her right to a hearing before the MSPB.

Though merely sending the regulations is not sufficient notice of a complainant's election rights, it is acceptable to give written notice of those rights provided the notice informs the complainant that he or she must make an informed election between the EEO forum and the MSPB forum. Faine v. Secretary of Air Force, 01911236 (1991).

When an agency improperly continues to process an EEO complaint after it has knowledge that a Board appeal on the same matter was previously filed, it may not later dismiss that complaint based on the mixed case regulations. See Johnson v. Attorney General, U.S. Marshals Service, 01920885 (1992). In that case, the Commission found that the complainant failed to raise his allegations of discrimination in the Board appeal because the agency accepted his EEO complaint and proceeded with an investigation. Because the Board already completed its processing of the complainant's case, the Commission ordered the agency to continue its processing of the complaint as a "non-mixed" case.
