9. Reinstatement

An agreement that provides for reinstatement does not necessarily provide for all benefits that go with reinstatement. In the absence of a specific clause addressing the issue of benefits, the Commission will assume that benefits are not included in the settlement.

For example, a settlement agreement that provides for reinstatement does not automatically restore leave and other benefits. In Hayward v. Postmaster General, 01966783 (1997), the Commission found that because the settlement agreement was silent regarding prior leave earned, the agency was not required to reinstate the complainant's leave:

The record shows that appellant was reinstated as per the settlement agreement. Appellant is alleging that the agency breached the settlement agreement when the agency failed to credit him with four more hours of annual leave upon his reinstatement with the agency. Appellant may also be arguing that the agency breached the agreement by failing to credit him with four hours of sick leave upon his reinstatement with the agency. Appellant argues that he should have been reinstated as if he had never been separated.

The Commission finds that the settlement agreement does not provide that appellant would be reinstated as if he had never been separated. Instead the settlement agreement provides that appellant's "enter on duty date, leave comp date, retirement comp date, and seniority date" will be June 11, 1994 upon reinstatement. The settlement agreement is silent on the issue of crediting prior leave earned. Therefore, we find that the agency was not required under the settlement agreement to provide any reinstatement of leave. If appellant wanted such consideration, then he should have had such a term included in the agreement.

Similarly, the Commission stressed the need to be explicit in the wording of a settlement agreement in Pearson v. Secretary of Transportation, 01983854 (1999):

Appellant's attorney contends, in a letter dated April 8, 1998, that the settlement agreement presupposed that the resignation letter would be taken out of appellant's employment files. To support his contention, he points to Paragraphs 7 and 10 of the agreement. Paragraph 7 provides that "[f]ollowing completion of a fully executed agreement and processing of the necessary forms and documents, the FAA [Federal Aviation Administration] will retroactively promote [appellant] to the position of aviation safety inspector (GS-1825-14). Paragraph 10 states that "(t)he FAA will amend any and all records and files, of [appellant's], including [appellant's] Official Personnel File, to reflect the retroactive promotion dating from November 1994." According to him, both provisions were included to address the issue that there would be no break in time reflected in the records arising from the previous (and inappropriate) processing of the letter of resignation. If that was the meaning that appellant's attorney hoped would be applied to provisions 7 and 10, then the agreement should have been crafted to explicitly, rather than implicitly, reflect such intentions. Of the provisions contained within the settlement agreement, none of them expressly mentions the removal of the resignation letter. Therefore, consistent with the way we generally interpret contracts, i.e., pursuant to the plain meaning rule, we find that there was no breach.

Where an agreement provides for the reinstatement of certain duties, the parties should take special care to expressly list those duties and the expectations that accompany them. For instance, in Darlena H. v. Postmaster General, 0120173018 (2018), the parties agreed that the complainant would "resume her FMO [Field Maintenance] Duties." The parties interpreted that provision differently, and the complainant alleged that the agency was in breach. The Commission found that provision "void for vagueness" and reinstated the complainant's EEO complaint, explaining:

Further, as to provision (2), Complainant asserted that she has not been provided with her FMO duties. As to the specific FMO duties, Complainant was primarily concerned with the fact that she had the keys of a staff car removed from her which she used to conduct her field visits and EWP reviews in the field with Postmasters. In addition, Complainant indicated that she had conducted EWP assessments and provided training to all new custodians in the field office in the Honolulu District. As such, she claimed that the Manager failed to return her to these duties following her EWP assignment. In support of her claim, Complainant provided a copy of the position description which provides overall information regarding the position but does not specify which duties Complainant performed or the duties to which she should have resumed. In response, the Agency pointed to information provided by the Manager who indicated that the car is not part of Complainant's duties and EWP assessments and training were Complainant's collateral duties which Complainant had performed. However, the Manager has since been assigned these duties to other employees. Again, we note that the Agency failed to provide a copy of a statement.

In reviewing the settlement agreement, we determine that this provision is too vague and general to have allowed for a meeting of the minds between the parties. Specifically, we find that the settlement language that Complainant "will resume her FMO Duties" is too ambiguous. In this regard, we note the divergent interpretations by the parties with respect to what these terms mean. Complainant believed it meant that she was to return to the duties she had been performing. The Agency, on the other hand, stated that the Manager believed that these duties were not within Complainant's position description. Consequently, we find that there was no contemporaneous meeting of the minds between the parties and that the settlement agreement is void for vagueness. See Mullen v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05890349 (May 18, 1989). When voiding an agreement, the parties are returned to the status quo at the time the agreement was entered.
