6. Past Experience

Obviously, an applicant's past work experience is a legitimate promotional consideration. However, length of experience does not necessarily equate with better qualifications. See Complainant v. Secretary of Navy, 0120131000 (2015); Macready v. Attorney General, 01991433 (2002); Waters v. Postmaster General, 01A11554 (2002). This is particularly true where the selectee has specialized experience within the agency that directly relates to the vacancy in question. See Walsh v. Secretary of Air Force, 01986453 (2001); York v. USPS, 01840072 (1985). Where the employee's prior work experience is similar to the requirements of the vacancy announcement, an agency is justified in giving that experience significant weight in the selection process. See Hallman v. USPS, 01851711 (1987) (prior unsatisfactory work performance can be a determinative selection factor).

In Moore v. Secretary of Army, 0120070573 (2010), the Commission found type of experience was a legitimate, nondiscriminatory selection criterion even where the complainant had a greater length of experience. In that case, the complainant was not selected for a Transportation Planning Specialist position at the Military Traffic Command at Fort Eustis, VA. The selecting official testified that the selectees were chosen because of "on the ground experience" that included work with war fighters, planners and operations staff, units support and contracts. By contrast, the complainant's experience was largely administrative.

Another example of how length of experience does not always equate with being more qualified for a position is Jeffrey G. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120132988 (2015). In that case, the complainant had more years of EEO experience but the selectee had more specialized experience in MD-715. The Commission, in Jeffrey G., noted:

The SO noted that it was the panel's unanimous decision initially to select the highest ranked candidate but that candidate declined; thus, the position was offered and was accepted by the second highest ranked selectee. The SO indicated that she ultimately selected the selectee because he had extensive complaint processing experience and experience with MD-715 (Affirmative Action); he trained EEO practitioners; he had over eight years' leadership experience as an Equal Employment Manager, GS-13; and he performed excellently in the interview, providing examples of strategic projects and their outcomes and showing technical and leadership skills.

The SO noted that Complainant had over one year experience as a Senior EEO Specialist, final decision writer, GS-14; one year and nine months experience with the Agency as a Complaints Manager, GS-13; and 13 years varied experience in the EEO field. The SO stated that she did not select Complainant because he had no experience with MD-715; he did not interview well; he provided a poor response concerning affirmative employment programs and reasonable accommodation issues; and he did not have extensive leadership experience as a supervisor. We find that Complainant failed to show that his qualifications for the position were plainly superior to the selectee's qualifications or that the Agency's action was motivated by discrimination. See Wasser v. Department of Labor, EEOC Request No. 05940058 (November 2, 1995).

Certainly, varied experience can be an advantage for a candidate. However, selection cases are evaluated on a case-by-case basis and, as is demonstrated by Virgilio M. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120142901 (2016), varied experience can legitimately lead to concerns about job stability:

SO1 acknowledged that Complainant performed well in many varied jobs, but pointed out that having an extensive resume does not necessarily make one the best school secretary. IR 274, 276. SO1 admitted to having concerns about the fact that Complainant had a multitude of jobs as an indicator of his employment stability. As to her alleged failure to check Complainant's references, SO1 testified that it was their normal protocol not to contact a candidate's references until they had made the decision that they were going to select that candidate, and that they already made the decision not to select Complainant. IR 274. Finally, SO1 averred that she did ask Complainant how it felt to be in a position that was traditionally held by a woman, but stated that it was in response to Complainant's comments that in his then-current job, he was the only male in an office with so many females, and that he often had to run the office by himself. IR 272.

The statutes the Commission enforces cannot prevent employers from making hiring decisions that applicants disagree with unless those decisions are rooted in an expressly prohibited consideration such as sex. And on this crucial issue, Complainant did not provide evidence of any of the above-referenced indicators of pretext beyond his own assertions. He has not submitted any sworn statements from other witnesses or documents that contradict the explanations provided by SO1 or SO2, or which call their veracity into question, particularly with regard to SO1's question to Complainant about how he felt about working in a job normally held by a woman. The fact that Complainant subsequently received an offer of a similar job in Europe does not establish that his qualifications were plainly superior to those of the selectees. Rather, it tends to confirm the SOs' assessments that all of the candidates they interviewed, including Complainant, were highly qualified for those jobs, and that any one of them could have been chosen. We therefore agree with the Agency that Complainant failed to establish the existence of unlawful considerations of his gender on the part of SO1 or SO2 with respect to their decisions not to select him for the PES or MES secretarial positions.

An agency may give legitimate preference to "inside" candidates over "outside" candidates for a position. It is true that placing a higher value on experience inside the particular unit will disadvantage outside candidates, but without more, the mere disadvantage does not violate Title VII. See Lockhart v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01850057 (1986). Regardless of unfairness, it is likewise not discriminatory that the selecting official has personal knowledge of the performance of inside candidates and gives greater credit to that experience. See O'Malley v. USPS, 01852836 (1987).

The Commission has held that it is "patently false, illogical and irrational" for an agency to assert that a candidate only can become qualified for a position by previously working in that position. See Wallen v. USPS, 01872864 (1988). The Commission noted that under such a rationale no one would ever be qualified for the position.

Lack of recent experience may be a valid criterion to use in position selection and is not necessarily indicative of discrimination on the basis of age. In Davis v. Secretary of Air Force, 01913882 (1992), the Commission noted that focusing on the recency of experience is different from focusing on the recency of education, which can be used to discriminate against older workers. Specifically, the Commission distinguished the Davis case, where the selecting official gave greater credit to the younger candidate who had more recent court reporting experience, from Coates v. National Cash Register Company, 433 F. Supp. 655, 662 (W.D. Va. 1977), where the employer relied on training and there was a disparity between the training the employer provided for older and younger employees, and Hodgson v. Approved Personnel Services, 529 F.2d 760 (4th Cir. 1975), where the court found that employment ads seeking "recent school graduates" could be violative of the ADEA.
