ATTENDANCE

An important part of your job as a federal supervisor is to make sure your employees are at work when they are expected to be and all absences are accounted for -- and properly reflected in your employees' personnel records and pay. But your job is not merely to administer absences -- you are expected to help your employees find the motivation to show up at work when they are expected, to observe agreed upon schedules for breaks, and not to leave before they are supposed to leave.

Should a modern boss be a 'clock watcher'? Federal managers have generally gone beyond the old-fashioned idea that civil servants are paid primarily to be at their workstations during duty time (which is why telework and flexible work schedules are possible and often popular). In today's government employees are paid to perform productively, and managers are paid to help them to do that. But your employees cannot perform at all when they are not working. Nor are they likely to be motivated to give their jobs their best shot, if they can't even be bothered to show up on time.

Even your best, most motivated employees are apt to start slacking off if they never know when coworkers are going to show up. They may begin to wonder why they are working when "no one else is." When you take care of attendance problems you show your employees you care about and value them and their work.

If you slack off on these responsibilities, the human desire to be somewhere other than at work is apt to take over. You may find yourself with too few employees on duty to do your unit's work, declining unit performance, and embarrassing questions from HR and your bosses.

Pay and attendance. For almost all positions in the federal government, pay is calculated based on the time employees are present for duty.

As a manager, you have the authority to approve or reject absences from the workplace, within the limits set by law and regulation. Employees who are absent without your approval from their assigned duty stations during their scheduled shifts may be considered absent without leave. Employees in AWOL status will lose pay and may be disciplined up to the point of removal from the federal service.

But there are limitations to your authority, as well as flexibilities you may use to approve excusable unscheduled absences after the fact. Be sure to consult with HR on any chronic attendance problem.

Respond to challenges. Not every unit has significant attendance issues. Does yours? It is easy enough to find out. Just take a good look at your current attendance system, however it is constructed. In most cases, a quick glance at your attendance records will tell you what you need to know. If there is no problem, there is no need to make one. Just monitor from time to time.

If you don't have accessible attendance records, or don't know how to monitor them, it's time to have a chat with HR.

How much of a problem is really a problem, in that it requires your action? That may be a judgment call. Minor attendance lapses that have no real effect on performance or morale are inevitable, and everyone is better off if you let them go. Again, there is no need to manufacture a problem when none exists.

That said, you should be able to:

  • • Handle tardiness.
  • • Use excused absence appropriately.
  • • Deal with unplanned absences.
  • • Encourage good attendance.

Handle tardiness

Are you annoyed when employees come in late for work, or return late from lunch and other authorized breaks? You are not alone. Tardiness can range from an occasional event justified by circumstances to a chronic problem. If it becomes widespread within your unit, it can significantly lower its productivity.

Tardiness may be one of the most common attendance issues, but it need not be a difficult one. Although many supervisors dislike having to deal with tardiness, it is usually a minor problem. It simply requires your constant attention and good judgment to prevent a major problem.

Remember these basic points:

  • • Excused absence (administrative excusal) is usually appropriate for occasional instances of late arrival, particularly if the employee provides a plausible reason and the period of the absence is short. You may also allow the employee to make up lost work time, within the limits of your agency's policies.
  • • Annual leave may be authorized to cover tardiness that extends beyond a short period if the employee presents a plausible reason and is not frequently tardy. Leave without pay is also possible when the employee has no available annual leave or does not want to use it. Sick leave is limited by law and regulation to specific situations. It normally does not apply in tardiness cases, but if the reason for the tardiness justifies sick leave, it may be used.
  • • Supervisors may charge tardiness to AWOL when, in their judgment, tardiness occurs too frequently or without a sufficiently good reason. Disciplinary action may be taken in serious or chronic cases.
  • • It is nearly impossible to change approved leave to AWOL.

Here are some of the most common challenges posed by tardiness, with appropriate responses:

  • • Employee is chronically late. Advise the employee that this is not acceptable behavior -- particularly if it reduces time available for agency customers or interferes with team or work group operation. Document the discussion. If the problem occurs again, charge the time to unauthorized absence or AWOL and initiate discipline.
  • • Employee has numerous reasons and excuses. Point out that it is the employee's responsibility to take whatever steps are necessary to arrive at work on time -- and this includes solving problems in getting to work. If, for example, the employee claims chronic car problems, it's up to the employee to find an alternative mode of transportation or face discipline for chronic tardiness. Document the discussion and take action as appropriate.
  • • Employee contends discipline is not possible because others' tardiness is occasionally excused. Point out that your policy is to excuse occasional late arrivals for good cause. It is not to excuse chronic tardiness without explanation or with questionable excuses. Be sure to consistently apply your agency's and unit's tardiness policies to avoid fairness and consistency challenges.
  • • Employee claims entitlement to extension of the workday to cover late arrivals. Point out that this is true only if the employee is on a flexible work schedule and if the time of actual arrival and the extended workday would fit within the established time band. When this is not the case, you may have the right in some circumstances to allow the employee to make up missed time, but you do not have the obligation to do so, and your flexibility should not be used to permit chronic tardiness.
  • • Employee claims entitlement to annual leave or leave without pay to cover lateness. Point out that this is incorrect. Annual leave and LWOP are subject to approval in advance, or to retroactive approval only for good cause. They are not available on demand to subsidize tardiness, and discipline may be taken for failing to follow established leave procedures.
  • • Employee claims right to use sick leave to cover lateness. Point out that sick leave is subject to specific request procedures and may only be used to cover purposes permitted by regulation. If you suspect an employee is abusing sick leave, you may want to demand written proof of illness. Consult with HR and your collective bargaining agreement where applicable.

Do it right. You have a lot of flexibility in your responses to tardiness challenges, but you can't have it both ways. You can't approve leave or use administrative excusal to cover tardiness, and then discipline an employee for the same instances of tardiness.

When you see a chronic problem developing, the correct procedure -- in consultation with HR -- is to:

  • • Warn the employee that further tardiness will not be tolerated.
  • • Document the discussion.
  • • At the next instance of tardiness, determine that the employee is AWOL.
  • • Initiate disciplinary action.
  • • Use progressive discipline to the point of removal if tardiness continues. The Merit Systems Protection Board will uphold removals for tardiness in serious cases, if progressive discipline is properly applied.

Warning: There are limited circumstances where you may have to approve leave, even if it is requested after the employee's absence, as in certain instances of sick leave or leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act. Some of these circumstances may be covered in labor agreements. Consult with HR.

Use excused absence appropriately

Agencies may determine situations where employees may be excused from duty without leave being charged. Excused absence, or administrative excusal, is often called administrative leave or admin leave, although it is not true leave and is covered by the regulations dealing with hours of work. Excused absences may be for extreme or hazardous weather conditions or other emergencies, but may also be for routine matters such as voting or blood donation.

When a determination has been made that early dismissal or closure of an organization is warranted due to hazardous weather conditions or other emergencies, employees (other than those who perform essential services) may be excused without charge to leave for the specified period of time prior to the activity's normal closing time or for the entire workday. Similarly, supervisors and managers can authorize employees to report to work late without leave being charged.

Since many employees are required to telework on closure days, OPM does not advise agencies to cancel an employee's pre-approved paid leave and automatically grant the employee excused absence on a day when federal offices are closed. This also applies to employees outside the Washington, D.C. area. When such a closure occurs during working hours, those employees on leave will continue to be charged leave.

Excused absence and tardiness. Generally, excused absence is the best way to cover employees who arrive late to their shifts, provided you are satisfied they have a reasonable excuse and the period to be covered is short. You may also use excused absence to cover a brief absence from work for some good reason during a shift. Check with your agency's HR office if you have any doubts about your agency's policy on excused absence.

Blood donation. Blood donors may be excused from work by the agency without charge to leave for the time necessary to donate blood and to recuperate after donating blood.

Excused absence for returning activated military employees. In recognition of the sacrifices civilian employees activated for military duty have made, President Bush directed that all agencies grant five workdays of excused absence, without charge to leave, to these employees upon notification to their employing agencies of their intent to return to federal civilian employment. This directive is still in effect and covers all employees who were activated for military service in connection with Operation Noble Eagle, Operation Enduring Freedom, Operation Iraqi Freedom, or any other military operations subsequently established under Executive Order 13223.

Agencies may grant this period of excused absence prior to the employee's resumption of duties, or at a time mutually agreeable if the employee has already returned to duty.

Deal with unplanned absences

All forms of unplanned absences from work may be considered attendance problems, but some you can't do much about:

  • • Sick leave because of unexpected illness or injury.
  • • LWOP or annual leave in emergencies covered by the FMLA.
  • • Excused absences because of weather emergencies.
  • • Lateness or sudden absence because of genuine personal emergencies.
  • • Absences required as reasonable accommodations for employees with disabilities.

Since you cannot avoid all forms of unplanned absences, you owe it to yourself and your unit to prevent those unplanned absences that are preventable.

Fortunately, you have administrative tools that allow you to limit the abuse of sick leave, to insist that proper request procedures for all forms of leave are followed, and to discipline employees who are found to be AWOL. HR will guide you on these matters.

Availability for work. You have a right to expect that, within well-established limits, your employees will show up for their shifts when they are supposed to and stay at their workstations in accordance with policy and regulation. Even when leave is granted in specific instances, including extended LWOP, sooner or later the employee has to show up.

Employees with very bad attendance records may be charged as not available for work and may be removed for that reason, even if their absence might be otherwise excusable (e.g., as due to lengthy illness or disability). This is especially true if there is no end in sight to their very poor attendance or prolonged absence. Consult with HR.

Warning: Employees absent for prolonged periods because of workplace injuries or occupational illnesses compensable under workers' compensation are subject to very different rules. Consult with HR. Special rules also apply to employees absent because of extended periods of active military duty.

Encourage good attendance

Enforcement of regulations and rules is only part of the solution to unplanned absences, the negative part. As with most management issues, you are better off if you can also use positive incentives that make people want to come to work and lead them to avoid abuse of leave regulations and of the flexibilities you may grant them.

Here are some positive actions you can take to encourage good attendance:

  • • Value the contributions of employees. If employees feel the value of their work is not recognized, they are more likely to slack off, and that includes pushing the envelope on attendance. Encouraging good attendance should be part of your personal performance enhancement strategy.
  • • Encourage teamwork and explain the mission. Show employees how their work affects the jobs of their coworkers and fits into your unit's mission, so they will understand the negative consequences of unplanned absences and try to avoid them.
  • • Organize jobs optimally. Make sure your employees are not being frustrated by unclear duties, confusing chains of command, and other organizational problems that hurt job motivation and lead to increased absences.
  • • Try to obtain the resources your employees need. Do your best to alleviate real problems and let your employees know that you see their difficulties and are trying to obtain relief. When you can't provide more resources, or don't believe they are needed, work with your employees to determine how they can do the best job possible with the resources at hand.
  • • Consider "demotivators" such as harassment or discrimination. If an employee has a poor attendance record -- especially an employee who has previously had a good one, ask the employee if there is a problem at work you should know about. The employee may feel harassed or discriminated against. Your timely action may not only improve the employee's attendance, it may spare you a grievance or EEO complaint.
  • • Be flexible and generous in granting leave requests and excusing unavoidable absences. Your employees must be able to balance their personal and professional lives. Grant legitimate leave requests whenever possible, and be flexible when employees ask for leave at the last minute. The more your employees see you do your best to accommodate their personal needs, the less likely they are to abuse your flexibility and your agency's leave procedures.
  • • Above all, set a good example. Supervisors who are quick to criticize the attendance records of their employees may be slow to examine their own. If you abuse sick leave, come to work late, take three-hour lunch breaks, or are absent for long periods during your shift for no apparent reason, your employees will notice and model their behavior on yours. Of course, your boss is also apt to notice.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Ignore chronic tardiness. It takes more effort to confront this problem than to ignore it, but ignoring it inevitably leads to more problems.
  • • Take a rigid approach to tardiness. The answer is not to require employees to use annual leave -- or to charge them AWOL -- for every late arrival. You're being paid to exercise your judgment; so don't be afraid to use it.
  • • Approve tardiness, then try to take action. If you don't think the employee should have been late again, don't approve leave or use excused absence. Log it as AWOL, counsel the employee, and then take stiffer steps if the problem continues. But approval followed by discipline won't fly.
  • • Overuse excused absence. Excused absence is a useful tool to cover a variety of absences from work, but it should not be overused to the point of excusing chronic attendance problems.
  • • Accept very poor attendance records. Just because employees have good reasons for staying away from work, such as serious illness or disability, doesn't mean they are relieved of any responsibility for showing up. Employees who are not available for work for extended periods may be removed, especially if there is no end in sight to their absence.

Ask FMG

Q. Can I disapprove a request for annual leave?

A. Yes, but leave should only be rejected for good reasons; for example, workload or staffing requirements. Dislike of the employee's reason for taking annual leave is not a valid reason.

Q. Can I actually discipline an employee for tardiness? Isn't this a trivial offense?

A. Yes, you can discipline for it, and no, it's not trivial. Left unchecked, it can become anything but trivial. Just keep a sense of proportion -- discipline is only needed in chronic, serious cases. The Merit Systems Protection Board will uphold even removals for tardiness in serious cases, if progressive discipline is properly applied. While discipline usually cannot be taken for approved leave, an employee may be disciplined for failing to follow established leave procedures found in the agency's personnel manual or its collective bargaining agreement.

Q. Do I have to grant annual leave or LWOP to cover an employee's late arrival?

A. No, there is no automatic right to annual leave or LWOP on a retroactive basis. It's up to you. If, in your judgment, leave is appropriate, grant it. If not, don't. You also have the option of a simple excused absence (administrative excusal). This is best when the period of lateness is relatively short and there is a good excuse.

Q. Am I required to allow an employee to work late to make up for a late arrival?

A. Only if the employee is on a flexible hours arrangement and both the late arrival and the made-up time would fall within the established boundaries of the workday. This is not a solution to chronic tardiness problems.

Q. Can I simply chalk up lateness to administrative leave?

A. Yes, under some circumstances. Administrative or admin leave is the common if somewhat inaccurate name for excused absence or administrative excusal. It is often the best way to cover occasional lateness, provided you are satisfied the employee has a reasonable excuse and the period to be covered is short. You may also use it to cover a brief absence from work for some good reason during a shift. Be careful here because the use of this discretionary rule can establish a "past practice" in a unionized environment. A past practice could hinder your ability to take disciplinary action when warranted. Consult your HR office.

Q. Do I have to treat all employees the same?

A. In the sense that you have to treat them all fairly, yes. But you can differentiate between those who are rarely late or otherwise absent without prior excuse -- and then for good reason -- and those who have chronic attendance problems. You can and should treat different cases differently. If employees ask why the other person gets excused absence and they get AWOL, explain your policy.

Q. Are there some instances of unexcused absence where I have to approve leave, even when requested after the fact?

A. Yes. There are some circumstances where you may have to approve leave, even if it is requested after the employee's absence, as in certain instances of sick leave or leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act. Some of these circumstances may be covered in labor agreements. Consult with HR.

Q. Do I have to grant employees excused absence to vote?

A. If employees are eligible to vote, they may be granted a limited amount of excused absence for the express purpose of voting. This applies where polls are not open at least three hours either before or after regular hours of work. The excused absence should allow them to report for work three hours after the polls open or to leave work three hours prior to closing of the polls, whichever requires the least amount of time away from work. However, excused absence should rarely be needed because polling places are usually open for extended periods.

Q. Do I have to accept that some employees can stay out indefinitely because of illness or disability?

A. No. Even when leave is granted in specific instances, including extended leave without pay, sooner or later the employee has to show up. Employees with very bad attendance records may be charged as not available for work and may be removed for that reason, even if their absence may be otherwise excusable (e.g., as due to lengthy illness or disability). This is especially true if there is no end in sight to their very poor attendance or prolonged absence. Note that workers with injuries or illnesses covered under workers' compensation are subject to separate rules, as are those absent on extended military service. Consult with HR.

Q. Are there things I can do to encourage good attendance besides threatening discipline?

A. Yes. If your employees feel undervalued or are frustrated by organizational problems or lack of resources -- or even by harassment or discrimination -- they are less likely to want to come to work. Take care of those problems as far as you can, and help your employees to perform within constraints that can't be changed. On the positive side, if you reward and recognize your employees for good work, and if you show them how important their work is to the agency mission and to you, they are more likely to want to come to work. You have an obligation to help your employees to motivate themselves. You can start by setting a good example in your own attendance record and work attitude.
