10. Light Duty

Certainly, it is appropriate for an agency to consider requests for light duty assignments from pregnant workers to the same extent it would consider those requests from other workers with similar medical restrictions. However, it is discriminatory for an agency to solicit light duty assignments for all pregnant employees. "Such across the board special treatment of all 'female' employees with similar conditions appears discriminatory on its face." Minsen v. Postmaster General, 01901941 (1990).

In evaluating a request for a duty restriction because of pregnancy, an agency cannot rely on a conclusory risk assessment when there is detailed medical evidence to the contrary. For example, in Stephens v. Postmaster General, 01973385 (1999), the Commission found pretext where the supervisor testified that she considered both the opinion of the complainant's private physician and the agency physician. The agency physician, after examination, reported the complainant to be normal, but evaluated her as a high risk for a letter carrier position. In finding pregnancy discrimination in Stephens, the Commission noted:

In considering whether the agency's proffered reason was a pretext for pregnancy discrimination, we note that the findings on the agency's own examination report show appellant to be "normal," and appellant's private physician, a medical expert in pregnancy, specifically states that her pregnancy was normal and that she could perform the functions of a city letter carrier with a (temporary) 30 pound lifting restriction during the pregnancy and six week postpartum period. However, inexplicably, in the "Risk Assessment" portion of the agency's medical examination report form, "High Risk/Restriction" is checked, notwithstanding these clear medical findings and expert opinion to the contrary. In affidavit testimony, SO indicates that she considered both the private physician's opinion and the agency's medical report in finding that appellant was medically unsuitable for the Position. In other words, she did not rely on the "Risk Assessment" alone, but considered all of the medical evidence in making her decision not to hire appellant. Therefore, because the medical evidence does not support the "High Risk" designation, and because SO testified that she based her decision on all of the medical evidence, we find that SO's proffered reason for not hiring appellant is not credible.

Moreover, SO does not state why the agency cannot accommodate appellant's temporary 30 pound weight lifting restriction, or why accommodating this restriction for less than a three month period (the remainder of the pregnancy plus a six week postpartum period) would create an undue burden on the agency.

Therefore, we conclude that appellant has shown by a preponderance of evidence, that the agency engaged in pretext by characterizing her pregnancy related 30 pound lifting restriction as a permanent disability which could not be accommodated, and that her nonselection for this reason was a violation of the PDA.

The PDA Enforcement Guidance, at Sec. IC1 provides:

If there is evidence that pregnancy-related animus motivated an employer's decision to deny a pregnant employee light duty, it is not necessary for the employee to show that another employee was treated more favorably than she was.

EXAMPLE 9--Evidence of Pregnancy-Related Animus Motivating Denial of Light Duty

An employee requests light duty because of her pregnancy. The employee's supervisor is aware that the employee is pregnant and knows that there are light duty positions available that the pregnant employee could perform. Nevertheless, the supervisor denies the request, telling the employee that having a pregnant worker in the workplace is just too much of a liability for the company. It is not necessary in this instance that the pregnant worker produce evidence of a non-pregnant worker similar in his or her ability or inability to work who was given a light duty position.

b. Disparate Treatment: Evidence of Other Workers Similar in Their Ability or Inability to Work Who Are Given Light Duty

In the absence of pregnancy-related statements evidencing animus, a pregnant worker may still establish a violation of the PDA by showing that she was denied light duty or other accommodations that were granted to other employees who are similar in their ability or inability to work. The PDA provides that "women affected by pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions shall be treated the same for all employment-related purposes, including receipt of benefits under fringe benefit programs, as other persons not so affected but similar in their ability or inability to work." Accordingly, an employer's failure to treat pregnant employees the same as non-pregnant employees similar in their ability or inability to work is a violation of the PDA.
