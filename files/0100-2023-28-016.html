1. Lack of Specificity and Detail

Class action complaints require far greater detail and specificity than individual complaints. Unless the issue or issues in the complaint are set forth with sufficient specificity, it is impossible to determine if there are issues common to the proposed class and claims that are typical of the proposed class. This is particularly significant because it is not uncommon for class complaints to contain more than one claim.

If the administrative judge finds the complaint lacks clarity or specificity, he or she must notify the class agent and give him or her 15 calendar days to provide more detailed information. The judge must notify the agent, in writing, that failure to provide the requested information or explanation within the 15-day period may result in dismissal of the complaint. 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(4). If the additional information includes allegations outside the scope of the oriGINAl complaint, the administrative judge must advise the agent on how to properly proceed with either an individual complaint or class complaint on the new issues.

The failure of an administrative judge to give a complainant 15 days to clarify the complaint is error under 1614.204(d)(4). See Jackson v. Secretary of Air Force, 05940324 (1994). Whether to give a class agent any additional time beyond the required 15 days, however, is purely subject to the administrative judge's discretion. See Reid v. Secretary of Army, 01951458 (1995). In Reid, the agency had apparently agreed to an extension of time for the attorney representing the class to provide more specific and detailed information. The Commission noted that it is the administrative judge, and not the agency, who may grant an extension of time. The Commission in Reid found that dismissal was proper, noting other deficiencies exhibited by the class agent and attorney:

Appellant requested an extension of time because he and his attorney both had vacations scheduled which conflicted with the due date. The attorney argued that he believed an extension of time had been granted. Upon review of the record, the Commission finds that this belief, while not irrational, was nonetheless unreasonable. The regulation cited above plainly states that the AJ, not the agency, is responsible for the granting of time in which to provide additional information. Therefore, it is wholly irrelevant whether the agency was willing to agree to an extension of time. In addition, although the AJ may have been silent while appellant and the agency representative negotiated a possible extension date, the AJ did not rescind his request for the parties to put their positions in writing for his consideration. The attorney did not do so, in reliance upon the agency's letter stating that it would agree to an extension even though it did not believe that appellant had cited good cause for an extension; on this latter point the AJ and the agency were apparently in agreement. The Commission agrees that previously scheduled vacations do not constitute "good cause shown" for failure to comply with a deadline.

The Commission also notes, as did the agency, that the information requested by the AJ was information which appellant should already have possessed or have engaged in some effort toward acquiring and assembling, given that appellant first contacted an EEO counselor regarding a class complaint some three years before the formal complaint in this case was actually filed. The Commission further notes that appellant did not file a notice of representation, which would have caused a copy of the Request to be sent directly to the attorney; delayed in contacting the attorney after he received the Request; and neither argued nor showed that any efforts were made to secure the information during the period August 15 through August 23, 1994, lest the AJ deny the requested extension. Based upon all of the foregoing, the Commission finds that the AJ did not abuse his discretion when he recommended that the class complaint be dismissed; the agency properly adopted this recommendation.

Note that under the current regulations, the administrative judge's decision to dismiss is a final decision, rather than a recommended decision. See 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(4).

If the administrative judge dismisses the complaint for a reason other than lack of clarity or specificity under 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(4), the class agent will not be successful in arguing that he was not given 15 days to cure defects in the complaint. In Garza v. Secretary of Treasury, 01965214 (1998), the Commission noted that the administrative judge recommended dismissal because the complaint did not meet the prerequisites for a class complaint, and not because the complaint lacked specificity or detail. The Commission found, therefore, that dismissal was proper, even though the class agency was not given 15 days to cure the defect in his complaint by providing more specific information.
