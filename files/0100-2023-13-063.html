B. TRAVEL EXPENSES

In the McGurran case, the Commission also set down a similar rule for travel expenses for complainants, representatives, and witnesses who are agency employees with one notable exception:

Travel costs for employees are to be governed by an agency's normal travel regulations. The primary consideration to be kept in mind is that such meetings and hearings are official business of the agency. In most instances, meetings and hearings are held at the agency facility where the complaint arose and all parties entitled to be present work at that facility. In some instances, however, the parties entitled to be present work elsewhere. When those instances arise, an agency is normally obligated to pay travel expenses for those individuals who are entitled to be present, or whose presence is required (e.g., witnesses at a hearing), insofar as those individuals are currently employed by the respondent agency. The only exception would be with respect to an employee of the agency acting as the complainant's representative, where that representative lives or works outside the normal commuting area of the facility where the complaint arose. A complainant may select a representative from anywhere in the world, if (s)he chooses; an agency, however, is not obligated to pay travel expenses for a representative from outside the normal commuting area of the agency facility where the complaint arose. Although we do not define "normal commuting area," most agency travel regulations define it as up to 50 or, in some cases, 75 miles from an employee's normal duty station.

In so holding, the Commission noted that 29 CFR 1613.214(b)(2) provided that "[t]he agency is not obliged to change work schedules, incur overtime wages, or pay travel expenses to facilitate the choice of a specific representative." Id. The Commission also found that nothing in its regulations would lead to a conclusion that an agency is required to pay a per diem to complainants and their representatives.

However, an agency is required to pay per diem travel expenses to an employee in order to attend a hearing held at a location other than the employee's work location. See Rose v. Postmaster General, 01912275 (1991).

An agency is not required to pay in advance the travel expenses of a former employee to attend a hearing. Ray v. Postmaster General, 01922008 (1992). The agency is not required to pay such expenses unless and until the complainant prevails.

The Commission has addressed the situation where a witness is a federal employee, but is not employed by the responding agency in MD-110, at 6-15:

Section 1614.605(f) requires that a witness be in an official duty status when his/her presence is required or authorized by agency or Commission officials in connection with a complaint. A witness is entitled to travel expenses. If a witness is employed at an agency other than the one against which the complaint is brought and must travel to provide the attestation or testimony, the witness is entitled to reimbursement for travel expenses. The current employing agency of a federal employee must initially authorize and pay the employee's travel expenses and is entitled to reimbursement from the responding agency, which is ultimately responsible for the cost of the employee's travel. Decision of the Comptroller General, Matter of John Booth--Travel Expenses of Witness--Agency Responsible, File: B-235845, 69 Comp. Gen. 310 (1990). An agency would not be responsible for paying the travel expenses of non-federal witnesses.
