7. Threats of Tangible Employment Actions

The precise nature of the Commission's view on whether the threat of an employment action for rejection of an advance constituted quid pro quo harassment was a bit muddled. To say that courts were not entirely in agreement on the issue either was an understatement. In Jansen v. Packaging Corp. of America, 123 F.3d 490 (7th Cir. 1997), a case that was consolidated with Ellerth v. Burlington Industries, Inc., 102 F.3d 848 (7th Cir. 1996), the Seventh Circuit, sitting en banc, wrote seven separate opinions, each concurring in part and dissenting in part. Although the Jansen case involved several issues that helped fragment the court, there was substantial disagreement within the panel as to whether a threat of an adverse action could constitute quid pro quo harassment. The Supreme Court granted certiorari in the case and in Burlington Indus., Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 118 S. Ct. 2257, 141 L. Ed.2d 633 (1998), resolved the issue by essentially rephrasing the question. Under Burlington Industries, threats of adverse action for rejecting sexual advances must be analyzed under the hostile environment theory. A threat does not constitute a tangible employment action.

In Burlington Industries, the Supreme Court determined that whether a threatened personnel action constituted quid pro quo harassment was really the wrong question to be asking. The Court noted that the terms quid pro quo and hostile environment harassment do not appear in the text of Title VII but "appeared first in the academic literature, see C. MacKinnon, Sexual Harassment of Working Women, (1979); found their way into decisions of the Courts of Appeals, see, e.g., Henson v. City of Dundee, 682 F.2d 897, 909 (C.A. 11 1982); and were mentioned in this Court's decision in Meritor Savings Bank, FSB v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57, 106 S. Ct. 2399, 91 L. Ed.2d 49 (1986)." Burlington Indus., 118 S. Ct. at 2264. The terms became accepted, the Court noted, and the rules of employer liability came to depend on what type of harassment was involved. The real usefulness of the terms quid pro quo and "hostile environment" is to distinguish between those claims where a definitive personnel action is involved and those claims where no definitive personnel action has been taken, according to the Court. The Court in Burlington Industries, 118 S. Ct. at 2265, went on to observe:

[W]hen a plaintiff proves that a tangible employment action resulted from a refusal to submit to a supervisor's sexual demands, he or she establishes that the employment decision itself constitutes a change in the terms and conditions of employment that is actionable under Title VII. For any sexual harassment preceding the employment decision to be actionable, however, the conduct must be severe or pervasive....

In other words, the Court found that in the typical quid pro quo case, the employee was able to establish that a term or condition of employment had been affected--i.e., the employee was aggrieved--by virtue of the fact that an employment action had clearly been taken. In all other cases, the employee only demonstrates a term or condition of employment is affected by demonstrating that the conduct rises to the level of being severe or pervasive--the standard used in evaluating whether a hostile environment exists.

Prior to Burlington Industries, it appeared that the threat of an adverse employment action for failure to submit to sexual advances did not, in the Commission's view, constitute quid pro quo harassment. In Lewis v. Postmaster General, 01922440 (1994), the Commission found that the complainant failed to establish that the management official who made the sexual advances actually affected any of the employment decisions involving her. The Commission found the complainant was required to demonstrate that the official actually made or "substantially affected" the employment decisions. Minimal involvement in those decisions, which included actions taken at the direction of higher level management, was "too attenuated and minimal to support a finding" that the accused official substantially affected the employment decisions. Id. While the lack of actual involvement in employment decisions was a bar to a finding of quid pro quo harassment, the Commission did consider the advances in determining that a hostile environment existed.

The treatment of threats of adverse employment actions in sexual harassment claims remains somewhat problematic despite the Supreme Court's pronouncement in Burlington Industries. The problem often is cured by the fact that even though the threats do not constitute tangible employment action harassment, they are sufficient to establish a hostile environment. In such cases, the problem, under Burlington Industries, is more theoretical in terms of the proper label to be placed on the harassment. The problem is more distinct in cases where the threats, perhaps because of their infrequency, do not rise to the level of creating a hostile environment. In those cases, Burlington Industries bars recovery. A far better approach than focusing on whether the harasser substantially affected an employment decision would be to focus on whether the victim had a reasonable belief that the harasser could so affect a decision. If the victim has such a belief, then the harassment is not diminished by the failure of the harasser to carry through on the threat.

The Commission used that approach in Complainant v. Secretary of Defense, 0120132274 (2013), finding that the threats were not tangible employment actions but could be considered as more severe because a tangible employment action was threatened:

Complainant cites two comments made by his supervisor regarding transfer to a different location and pay: (1) supervisor told Complainant that he would seek to transfer Complainant if he could not perform his job; and (2) supervisor told Complainant that he would not pay Complainant if he did not have leave planned when a mandatory meeting was scheduled.

First, these comments could be construed as threats to deny the Complainant tangible job benefits on the basis of his disability, and is exacerbated by the fact that they were made by Complainant's immediate supervisor. A reasonable person in Complainant's situation could perceive the conduct as hostile or abusive and Complainant attests that he did perceive it as such. See e.g., Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Servs., Inc., 523 U.S. 75, 82 (1998) (Actionable conduct is "conduct which a reasonable person in the plaintiff's position would find severely hostile or abusive"); Burlington Indus., Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 754 (1998) (Expressing no opinion as to whether a single unfulfilled threat is sufficient to constitute discrimination in the terms or conditions of employment). Construing all facts in favor of the Complainant, the Agency was incorrect in holding that Complainant failed to state a claim of hostile work environment harassment based on his disability. Complainant is entitled to fact specific inquiry into his allegations.
