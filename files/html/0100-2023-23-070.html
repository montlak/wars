b. Reversal of Criminal Conviction

If the appellant is removed on the basis of a criminal conviction, but not for the underlying misconduct, reversal of the conviction in the courts entitles the employee to restoration and back pay, Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 270, 273-74 (1984):

[T]he agency's first contention is that back pay is not proper under 5 USC § 5596(b) because that section only authorizes the award of backpay when an agency's action is "unjustified or unwarranted." It asserts that it waited until appellant was convicted to take the removal action to avoid interfering with the criminal process; and more importantly, that at the time it took the removal action, it was substantively warranted by the then standing criminal conviction. Thus, it concludes that a back pay award is not justified regardless of the outcome of the criminal charge against appellant.

In support of this contention, the agency cites Jankowitz v. United States, 533 F.2d 538, 544, 209 Ct. Cl. 489 (1976), in which the court found that where an indefinite suspension was properly based on an indictment and the appellant was subsequently acquitted of the underlying criminal charges, back pay for the suspension period was not awardable because the suspension was a justified personnel action when taken. Further, the agency cites United States v. Abbett, 381 F.2d 609, 612 (5th Cir. 1967), wherein the court found that because the Civil Service Commission (CSC) originally granted an involuntarily filed disability claim and later in the appeals process denied the disability claim based on new medical evidence, the CSC's prior grant was not "unjustified or unwarranted," and appellant was precluded from receiving back pay benefits for the leave she was forced to us[e] during the CSC appeals process.

We find that Jankowitz and Abbett are distinguishable from the instant case. Each represents a unique situation. In Jankowitz, the indefinite suspension action was based on the fact of indictment. See Martin v. Department of the Treasury, 10 MSPB 568 (1982), holding that an indefinite suspension, by its nature, is not based on provable misconduct. In Abbett, the CSC's initial grant of disability was based on the medical evidence then available and the reconsidered denial was based on new medical evidence. Juxtaposed to these cases, the present case represents the normal situation where a removal action is taken based on a charge of misconduct which the agency must prove in order to prevail. The overturned conviction represented the sole proof offered by the agency to establish such misconduct. Thus, the Board's finding that the agency did not prove its case by preponderant evidence rendered the removal an unwarranted and unjustified personnel action, entitling appellant to an award of backpay and benefits under 5 USC § 5596(b) and 5 CFR § 550.805. See Ciambelli v. United States, 203 Ct. Cl. 680, 686-88 (1974) (a Federal employee separated on grounds that are subsequently judicially reversed is entitled to an award of back pay).

Additionally, although our holding is based on the above analysis, we note that in both Jankowitz and Abbett, the courts relied heavily on the fact that the agencies involved followed all procedural requirements mandated by the applicable regulations and the suggested practices listed in the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM). See Jankowitz, supra at 544; see also Summers v. United States, 648 F.2d 1324, 227 Ct. Cl. 353 (1981). In contrast, although the agency afforded appellant all the applicable regulatory safeguards in the instant case, it admits that relying solely on the fact of conviction, with no independent proof of the underlying conduct is not the suggested practice under FPM Chapter 752, Subchapter 3-2.

Further, we find that the agency's alternative position that, if not precluded, then at least an award of back pay is premature since the conviction could be reinstated, see n.1, supra, represents only its understandable desire to cure its error of not submitting evidence other than the certificate of conviction to establish the misconduct.[6]

[6] This is not to say that its justifiable goal of avoiding interference with the criminal process, see Brown v. Department of Justice, 715 F.2d 662, 668 (D.C. Cir. 1983), could not have been satisfied. Here, the agency merely failed to supplement the evidence of conviction with the underlying facts relied on in the criminal proceeding. Such additional evidence may have been sufficient to sustain the charge even when the conviction was reversed. See Messersmith v. General Services Administration, 8 MSPB 496, 500 (1981).

Payne v. USPS, 69 MSPR 503, 508-09 (1996), also explained that when a removal and related Board decision depend upon the appellant's criminal conviction, subsequent judicial reversal of that conviction should entitle the appellant to back pay, once such entitlement is sought through a petition to reopen the Board appeal:

Finally, we address the issue of the appellant's entitlement to back pay. The appellant argues that he is entitled to back pay from the effective date of his first removal, May 17, 1991. The agency argues that any entitlement to back pay commences on the date that the Board vacates the two decisions at issue.

An appellant who is affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action is entitled to receive back pay for the period in which the personnel action was in effect. 5 USC § 5596(b)(1)(A). For purposes of the Back Pay Act, an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action is one found to be without basis by an authorized tribunal such as the Board. See Kelley v. Department of the Air Force, 50 MSPR 635, 639 (1991).

As previously noted, the administrative judge reversed the appellant's May 17, 1991 removal, and his initial decision became final when the agency did not file a petition for review. Thus, the May 17, 1991 removal was found to be an unjustified and unwarranted personnel action. Likewise, the judicial reversal of the conviction that formed the basis for the second removal action renders that removal an unjustified and unwarranted personnel action for purposes of the Back Pay Act. See Robinson v. Department of the Army, 21 MSPR 270, 273-74 (1984) (where a mail fraud conviction formed the basis for removal and the conviction was reversed on appeal, the removal action was unjustified and unwarranted for purposes of entitlement to back pay).[*] Thus, we find that the appellant is entitled to back pay from May 17, 1991, until the present.

[*] We note that the appellant expressed concern that the Board's decisions in cases involving indefinite suspensions based on convictions that were later reversed, see, e.g., Newbold v. Department of the Treasury, 58 MSPR 532, 536 (1993), would mandate denial of back pay for the period of the second removal. Indefinite suspension appeals, however, differ from other adverse action appeals in that indefinite suspensions are not based on proof of misconduct but rather upon the examination into that misconduct. Thus, the basis for an indefinite suspension related to an indictment or conviction may remain despite a subsequent acquittal or reversal. See Martin v. Department of the Treasury, 10 MSPB 568, 12 MSPR 12, 20 (1982). Accordingly, a validly initiated indefinite suspension is not an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action prior to dismissal of the indictment or acquittal and thus does not generate entitlement to back pay. Cir. 1988) (Table); see also Richardson v. U.S. Customs Service, 47 F.3d 415, 421 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (an agency is "neither required to nor precluded from" awarding back pay retroactive to the date of the suspension with respect to an employee reinstated from a summary suspension following an acquittal; that decision is a matter left to the agency's discretion).
