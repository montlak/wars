1. Collateral Estoppel; Res Judicata

That the Board sustained action against an appellant in an earlier appeal does not collaterally estop the appellant from litigating his suitability for a job later sought. In Hurlbut v. OPM, 36 MSPR 250, 254-58 (1988), an engineering technician was removed for failure to comply with a written directive and careless workmanship. The removal was sustained by the Board. That determination did not, however, collaterally estop the appellant from contesting a negative suitability determination pertaining to his application for a construction representative job. The Board reviewed some determinants, including rehabilitation or efforts toward rehabilitation, of suitability appeals:

The Board's authority to invoke the doctrine of collateral estoppel is well recognized. See, e.g., Graybill v. USPS, 782 F.2d 1567, 1571 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, [479 U.S. 963], 107 S. Ct. 462, 93 L. Ed.2d 407 (1986). The term "collateral estoppel" has generally been used to refer to the binding and conclusive effect given a prior judgment in a subsequent action. See Williams v. USPS, 35 MSPR 581, 584 (1987). Collateral estoppel, however, only applies to issues actually litigated in a prior proceeding. See id., citing Chisolm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 656 F.2d 42, 47-48 n.10 (3d Cir. 1981). Under this doctrine, a judgment on the merits in one lawsuit precludes the relitigation of the same issues in a second suit, regardless of whether the first and second suits were based on the same cause of action. See Thomas v. GSA, 27 MSPR 401, 406 (1985), aff'd, 794 F.2d 661 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

The Board may apply the doctrine of collateral estoppel to any issue previously adjudicated where three prerequisites exist: (1) The issue in the second action must be identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue must have actually been litigated in the prior action; and (3) the determination in the prior action must have been necessary to the resulting judgment. See Williams, 35 MSPR at 584; Thomas, 27 MSPR at 406.

In this case, the agency argued that the Board was precluded from examining the appellant's removal based on the doctrine of collateral estoppel. The agency also argued, at the hearing, that the Board's decision which upheld the appellant's removal from his prior position precluded it from rating the appellant eligible for a position similar to that from which he was terminated. Finally, the agency objected to the testimony of Mr. Howard Fleeger, the appellant's former supervisor, concerning the circumstances surrounding the appellant's past misconduct. The basis for the agency's objection to Mr. Fleeger's testimony was that the appellant was precluded from relitigating the merits of the past removal action.

The record in this appeal discloses that all of the prerequisites for the application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel have not been met. The issues involved in this action simply are not identical to the issues involved in the prior action. The issues involved in the removal action were: (1) Whether the appellant committed the acts of misconduct for which he was removed; (2) whether disciplinary action for those charges would promote the efficiency of the service; and (3) whether the penalty of removal for the sustained misconduct charges was within the tolerable bounds of reasonableness. The issues involved in this appeal are framed by the agency's own regulations. Section 731.202(a) of Title 5 of the Code of Federal Regulations provides that the agency shall make its determination that an individual is unsuitable for appointment on the basis of: (1) Whether the conduct of the individual may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent the effective performance in the position applied for; or (2) whether the conduct of the individual may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent effective performance by the employing agency of its duties and responsibilities.

Contrary to the agency's contention in its petition for review, the issues involved in this action were not litigated in the removal action. The Board's decision sustaining the appellant's removal from Federal employment made no findings concerning whether the appellant's conduct may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent performance in a position he subsequently applied for. Similarly, the removal decision made no findings concerning whether the appellant's conduct may reasonably be expected to interfere with, or prevent the effective performance by an employing agency of its duties or responsibilities. The Board's decision sustaining the appellant's removal only concerned whether he should have been removed from the position of Engineering Technician and did not consider his suitability for other positions.

Since the removal decision did not consider the issues or factors involved in a suitability determination, we agree with the administrative judge's determination that the appellant's removal from a prior position did not preclude her from finding that the appellant was eligible for future Federal employment....

We also find that, since the doctrine of collateral estoppel was not applicable, the administrative judge properly admitted and considered the testimony of Mr. Fleeger. In this regard, when the agency determines that an individual is unsuitable for Federal employment based on misconduct in prior employment, it is required, by its own regulations, to consider, among other factors: (1) The circumstances surrounding the conduct; and (2) the absence or presence of rehabilitation or efforts towards rehabilitation. See 5 CFR 731.202(c). See also Morris v. OPM, 33 MSPR 619, 621 (1987) (if the Office of Personnel Management's determination that a person is unsuitable for Federal employment is based on misconduct in prior employment, it must take into consideration the recency and circumstances surrounding the misconduct, and the presence or absence of efforts toward rehabilitation).

The agency also asserts in its petition for review that it "rated the appellant unsuitable solely upon a charge of misconduct in prior employment and a determination that the appellant failed to show any rehabilitation." According to the agency, it is only required to consider the factors set forth at 5 CFR 731.202(c) "to the extent these factors are deemed pertinent [by OPM] to the individual case," and that it considered the circumstances surrounding the appellant's misconduct and "deemed the circumstances irrelevant." We agree. Since the Board has been granted de novo review authority under 5 USC 7701, however, the fact that the agency asserts that it considered the circumstances surrounding the appellant's misconduct and deemed them irrelevant cannot serve as a basis for excluding testimony offered at the hearing in connection with those circumstances. See Kirkland v. OPM, 27 MSPR 159, 162 (1985).

The agency next argues that the administrative judge erroneously shifted the burden of proof to the agency on the issue of rehabilitation of the appellant. This argument is without merit, and misapprehends the burdens of proof in an action taken by the agency under 5 CFR Part 731. We specifically addressed the issue of who has the burden of proof in a suitability determination appeal in Kirkland, 27 MSPR at 161. See also 5 USC 7701(c)(1); 5 CFR 1201.56(a)(1)(ii) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1)(ii)] .

As we stated in Kirkland, 27 MSPR at 161, the agency is required to support its negative suitability determination by a preponderance of the evidence. In proving its case, the agency must show that its determination was for such cause as would promote the efficiency of the service. See id. The criterion used by the agency in determining whether a negative suitability determination promotes the efficiency of the service is whether the conduct on which the determination is based may reasonably be expected to interfere with, or prevent effective performance of, the agency's mission or the job for which the appellant is employed or has applied. See id. Among the factors the agency itself has stated shall be considered are the recency of the misconduct, circumstances surrounding the misconduct, and whether there has been rehabilitation or efforts towards rehabilitation. See 5 CFR 731.202(c). See also Morris v. OPM, 33 MSPR at 621 (1987); O'Dell v. OPM, 23 MSPR 297, 299 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 176 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (if OPM's negative suitability determination is based on incidents of misconduct in prior employment, OPM must consider the recency and circumstances surrounding the misconduct, as well as the absence or presence of efforts towards rehabilitation).

The administrative judge, in determining that the agency failed to support its negative suitability determination by preponderant evidence, merely found that the agency did not give much consideration to the facts surrounding the appellant's prior removal or his subsequent efforts towards rehabilitation. She then considered such circumstances, as is appropriate under Kirkland, 27 MSPR at 162, and found that the circumstances surrounding the appellant's misconduct--the troubles the appellant had in his former position--were unique to that particular situation. In this regard, she further noted that the appellant's former supervisor testified at the appellant's suitability hearing that the appellant could not demonstrate his ability to perform the duties of his prior position because he had an unenforceable contract to administer. She also found that, subsequent to his removal, the appellant was employed by the Pittsburgh Testing Laboratory for some three years, and that his performance in that position was good. Based upon this evidence, the administrative judge found that the agency failed to show by preponderant evidence that the appellant's past misconduct could reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent the effective performance in the position of Construction Representative or prevent effective performance by an employing agency of its duties or responsibilities.

The agency's argument that the administrative judge erroneously shifted the burden of proof with regard to efforts towards rehabilitation to the agency misstates the nature of the administrative judge's findings. She simply found that the agency failed to support its burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the appellant was unsuitable for appointment because of conduct which could reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent the effective performance in the position of Construction Representative or prevent effective performance by an employing agency of its duties or responsibilities. We agree with her findings.
