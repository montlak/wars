a. Legal Error by OPM

Earlier discussion concerned OPM's failure to respond to evidence. The "clearly without merit" standard also applies to retirement appeals involving a failure of OPM to take the existing evidence and apply to it a proper formulation of the law. Legal error may provide a basis for an award of fees under the standard. Shirley v. OPM, 36 MSPR 179, 181-82 (1988), explained that an error of statutory interpretation by OPM may also supply the basis for a fee award:

In Obremski at 556, the Board found that fees should be awarded in a retirement case if the agency based its case on incredible or unspecific evidence or reasoning fully countered by the record.

In its earlier decision in the appellant's case, the Board found that the agency had interpreted 5 USC § 8344(a), the law under which it found the appellant restored to earning capacity, in an inconsistent manner and pointed out that, although the agency asserted that it followed long-standing policy in deciding the appellant's case, it failed to present persuasive evidence of such policy. Further, in Blunk v. Office of Personnel Management, 34 MSPR 267, 270 (1987), the Board held that the agency's interpretation of 5 USC § 8344(a) was not entitled to deference. Thus, OPM relied on reasoning that was not specific, not credible, and fully countered by the record as a whole.

The administrative judge correctly relied on Obremski. Obremski found that only Allen category two was applicable in a case...where no facts were in dispute, the dispositive issue was legal, and the agency was unable, during the appellate process, to persuade the adjudicator that its interpretation of statute was correct, despite the deference accorded the agency's interpretation of the statute it administers. Subsequently, in Kent v. Office of Personnel Management, 33 MSPR 361 (1987), the Board gave guidance for determining when fees would be awarded under Allen categories two and five when the facts presented by the appellant were the key to the disposition of the appeal. Although the disposition of by far the majority of retirement cases rests on facts to be established by the appellant and disposition of the attorney fee applications in those cases would thus be subject to a Kent analysis, nonetheless, Obremski remains applicable to the limited number of retirement attorney fee cases where the disposition of the case on the merits turns on OPM's ability to be persuasive about its statutory interpretation.

Obremski was followed in Babakitis v. OPM, 60 MSPR 35, 37 (1993), noting that "[t]he Federal Circuit found that OPM's decision, that double crediting had occurred, was capricious and contrary to [c]ongressional intent." Babakitis summarized Obremski: "In a similar case that turned on OPM's failure to be persuasive about its statutory interpretation, the Board relied upon the findings of the Federal Circuit in finding entitlement to fees...." Davis v. OPM, 64 MSPR 6, 11 (1994), awarded fees when OPM misconstrued retirement law and regulation:

We note further that, in concluding that a fee award was not warranted, the administrative judge relied in part on a finding that "OPM's final basis relied on a good faith interpretation of the applicable statutes, its own regulations, and prior Board case precedent," and in part on a finding that "no harm accrued to the appellant simply because [OPM] abandoned its original reason, or 'theory,' for denial." These findings do not support a conclusion that a fee award is not warranted. Although evidence that the agency acted in good faith in interpreting and applying applicable statutes and regulations would preclude a finding that an attorney fee award was warranted under the third Allen category, it is not relevant to the second Allen category. Furthermore, the administrative judge's statement that the appellant was not harmed by OPM's reliance on a new "theory" in its reconsideration decision is not persuasive. That reliance required the appellant to oppose, in his appeal, an incorrect and unsupported theory he had no opportunity to oppose before OPM.

If OPM's decision depends on application of precedent to a particular type of claim, e.g., entitlement to law enforcement retirement credit, it cannot be said that OPM's decision was wholly unfounded or lacking a reasonable basis when the Board departs from that precedent. See Peek v. OPM, 63 MSPR 430, 432-33 (1994) (law enforcement retirement appeal). A fee was denied in Gammill v. OPM, 35 MSPR 279, 283-84 (1987), after the Board directed that a retirement application be granted on the basis of an interpretation of a regulation concerning the effect of a mobility agreement on the right to a discontinued service annuity. The outcome of the appeal turned on the impact of a settlement agreement between the agency and the employee. The agreement was vitiated by the agency's good faith but incorrect representations concerning the impact of the mobility agreement. The Board appeared to conclude that OPM was not aware that the settlement agreement was being directly challenged; additionally, the Board concluded that OPM's construction of its regulation was reasonable at the time it was made. The Board determined that OPM's action was not clearly without merit and that it could not be said that OPM knew or should have known that its action would not be sustained.
