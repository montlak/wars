a. Deviation From Assigned Route; Minor Personal Use; Public Service Exception

A statutory violation is not made out if the employee, in the course of official trips, stops off at an establishment, e.g., his dentist's office, while on route. The primary use of the GOV was business. No evidence demonstrated that the appellant manipulated government work to carry out his personal business. Lynch v. DOJ, 32 MSPR 33, 39 (1986) (citing D'Elia v. Dept. of Treasury, 14 MSPR 54, 56, 12 MSPB 242 (1982) (no vehicle misuse when on the way back from dinner, the appellant stopped off with his GOV at the disco and then proceeded to the motel; the primary use of the GOV was for government business even though it may have involved some minor personal use); Fischer v. Dept. of Treasury, 69 MSPR 614, 617 (1996) (only minor personal use of the vehicle occurred when the appellant allowed a friend to ride along with him on a brief trip concerning a matter of agency business); Ferguson v. Dept. of Army, 8 MSPR 615, 618 (1981) (securing a GOV in a place other than instructed did not establish personal use in violation of the statute (distinguishing Clark v. United States, 162 Ct. Cl. 477 (1963) (involving a GOV left at home with occasional use by wife)); cf. Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 353-54 (1987) (visiting home in a government vehicle when the visit was not for an official purpose supported adverse action).

The "minor personal use" exception is unavailable to an appellant charged with using a vehicle for purely personal use, i.e., a side venture. Whether the appellant was aware that the agency was lax in enforcing the prohibition against misuse of its vehicles was not relevant to willfulness; the only inquiry was "whether the appellant voluntarily and consciously used the vehicle with knowledge or reckless disregard of whether the use was for other than official purposes." Madrid v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 418, 422-23 (1988) (transportation of an individual to facilitate a personal business transaction); see Utnage v. Dept. of Army, 119 Fed. Appx. 269, 272 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (sustaining penalty for using GOV to drive home for meal breaks; parking a block away from the house did not avoid the misuse charge: "[w]ere the Board to have found that parking a block from Utnage's residence was permissible while parking at his residence was not, it would be subordinating substance to form and make a mockery of the law. ").

Improper use of a vehicle should be distinguished from poor judgment. If an employee uses a vehicle for official purposes, but takes an improper or inefficient route, the employee would not be properly charged with misuse of a government vehicle, particularly if the employee is allowed some latitude in the routes he uses. Cf. Honeyman v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 136, 140 (1990) ("There is no doubt that the appellant used poor judgment in leaving his zone under the circumstances, and that his trip was an inefficient use of his time. Because his use of the vehicle was for official agency business, however, we find that the agency did not prove by preponderant evidence the charge of misuse of a government vehicle."; sustaining removal for offenses that included negligent use of vehicle involved in property damage and injury).

Agency regulations or practice may classify certain uses of vehicles as permitted under the statute. The Board must then interpret the agency regulation or practice. Schumacher v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3473), vacated and remanded a decision to the Board on willful misuse of a government vehicle under 31 USC 1349(b). The court was concerned that there was a public service exception to § 1349(b) that the Board failed to address:

Presumably, if an exception exists and if petitioner reasonably believed his activity qualified for the exception, he could not be found to have violated the statute.... On remand, if the board determines that a public service exception exists, it will be important for it to consider the activities, if any, for which the agency has granted exceptions in the past.

Much is also made of Schumacher's failure to secure permission to use the car...The failure to obtain approval, however, may only be a factor in the determination of whether petitioner willfully used a government vehicle for a nonofficial purpose. On the other hand, it may be of little or no significance to this determination. It is conceivable that one could use a government car for an official purpose without obtaining the required permission. Failure to obtain permission may only indicate that he did not abide by the procedures for requisitioning a car, an offense not charged here, and not necessarily that he willfully used a government car for a nonofficial purpose.

[W]e must be given more than this to conduct a meaningful review, particularly where violation of the statute carries nondiscretionary minimum penalties.

Cf. Sinclair v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-16-0376-I-1 (NP 7/22/2022) (considering appellant's personal circumstances in using the GOV and applying an agency regulation that included negligent use of a GOV, but finding no negligence and only a minor personal use of the vehicle: "his deviation from the base in the GOV was short in distance and duration, and expedited Government business"; "appellant's actions are not outside the degree of care expected of a person of ordinary prudence in the same situation with equal experience").
