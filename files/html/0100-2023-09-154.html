f. Agency-Initiated Request; Prior Practice

Addressing practice before the Board's 2012 regulation allowing either party to request a DWOP, Thomas v. VA, 51 MSPR 218, 220-22 (1991), considered whether an agency could secure a DWOP of the appellant's appeal. VA sought the delay because its principal witness was scheduled for surgery at the time set for the hearing. The Board decided that an agency request could not be granted:

Congress, in 5 USC 7701(i), has directed the Board to expeditiously process appeals consistent with fairness and other Board priorities, and the Board has maintained a policy of holding hearings promptly unless extraordinary circumstances have been shown.... This policy was implemented, in accordance with the intent of Congress, because delays adversely affect employees who might be unemployed while their appeals are pending, or who, at the least, are subject to great uncertainty about the future course of their careers....

Moreover, the hearing is the employee's only opportunity to probe anew the factual portion of his case before an impartial tribunal, and, thus, will not be cavalierly or lightly disregarded.... Significantly, the agency has no statutory right to a hearing, and the Board no longer provides for consideration of an agency's request for a hearing....

The Board has not previously directly addressed the issue of whether an administrative judge can grant an agency's motion to dismiss an appeal without prejudice despite the appellant's objection. In keeping with the statutory language and the legislative intent, we hold that, as a matter of policy, an agency's request for a dismissal without prejudice should not be granted. When the delay is for the benefit of the agency, the appellant should not face the additional burden of refiling or otherwise reinstating his appeal. Rather, when the circumstances of a case warrant, a continuance should be granted and the agency should be required to set a date certain by which it agrees to proceed with the appeal.

Whether good cause exists for postponement or continuance of a hearing is generally determined under the unique circumstances of each case and rests with the sound discretion of the administrative judge.... In determining whether to grant an agency's request for a continuance, the administrative judge should consider the specific reasons for the agency's request. These include why the agency considers the witness' testimony essential and how long the agency was aware that the witness would be unavailable before the administrative judge and the appellant were informed of the need for a delay.... The administrative judge should also consider whether the agency could have anticipated the delay and preserved the testimony through a deposition or affidavit.[2] The availability to the agency of alternative sources of proof should also be considered, as should the length of the delay sought. Finally, the extent of the financial burden placed on the appellant should be considered.

[2] In Ceja v. DLA, 27 MSPR 531, 534-535 (1985), the Board held that the presiding official did not err in denying a continuance when the agency failed to preserve, through a deposition or affidavit, the testimony of a witness it knew would be unavailable for the hearing.

The Board reiterated its caution against agency-initiated DWOPs in Ballard v. VA, 73 MSPR 598, 599-602 (1997), involving an appeal from a performance-related separation that the agency suggested would be mooted by possible revocation of the appellant's nursing license by a state licensing board for the same problems that led to the appellant's removal:

There are no Board regulations specifically governing the dismissal of an appeal without prejudice. Rather, dismissal without prejudice is a procedural option that is committed to the sound discretion of the administrative judge. See, e.g., Lewis v. Department of the Air Force, 69 MSPR 40, 43 (1995). However, the Board has held that, as a matter of policy, an administrative judge should not grant an agency's motion to dismiss an appeal without prejudice over an appellant's objection. Thomas v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 51 MSPR 218, 221 (1991). In Thomas, the Board stated that the appellant should not face the additional burden of refiling or otherwise reinstating the appeal when the delay is for the agency's benefit. Id. Rather, where the circumstances warrant, the administrative judge should grant a continuance and require the agency to set a date certain by which it agrees to proceed with the appeal. Id.; accord Walton v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 MSPR 294, 296 (1994); Pidock v. Department of the Treasury, 54 MSPR 28, 30-31 (1992); Draper v. Department of the Army, 51 MSPR 518, 520-21 (1991).

Pidock v. Dept. of Treasury, 54 MSPR 28, 30 (1992), reaffirmed the doctrine of Thomas, noting that "where the circumstances of a case warrant, the AJ should grant a continuance and require the agency to set a date certain by which it agrees to proceed with the appeal." Thomas was applied in Walton v. OPM, 62 MSPR 294, 296 (1994), to set aside a DWOP requested by the agency and approved by the AJ but not consented to by the appellant, who apparently requested only a continuance. See Rittgers v. Dept. of Army, 117 MSPR 182, 187-88 ¶ 12, 2011 MSPB 101 (2011) ("Given the Congressional directive that the Board expeditiously process appeals because delays adversely affect employees who might be unemployed while their appeals are pending, as a matter of policy an agency's request for a dismissal without prejudice generally should not be granted over an appellant's objection."). [Refer to Chapter 3, "Continuance Procedure."]

In Black v. USPS, 89 MSPR 204, 206 ¶ 6 (2001), disregarding (or at least not mentioning) earlier law, an agency-initiated DWOP request was acceptable, although the agency apparently initiated the DWOP through a request to postpone or continue the hearing, as to an appeal involving past discipline and another pending appeal requiring interpretation of the Board's rules on reliance on past discipline. See Gingery v. Dept. of Treasury, 111 MSPR 134, 138 ¶ 9, 2009 MSPB 59 (2009) (reciting that an AJ may order a DWOP at the request of one or both parties, or to avoid a lengthy or indefinite continuance); Goode v. Dept. of Air Force, 91 MSPR 198, 200 ¶ 5 (2002) (stating that a DWOP may be granted "at the request of one or both of the parties or in order to avoid a lengthy or indefinite continuance"); cf. Keefer v. USDA, 92 MSPR 476, 480 ¶ 8 (2002) (same as Goode).

Although, at least through Black, the AJ was not to grant an agency motion for dismissal without prejudice, nothing prevented an agency from suggesting to the AJ that the Board DWOP the appeal on the AJ's initiative. Nothing barred a stipulation, subject to approval at the sound discretion of the AJ, dismissing a case without prejudice on terms agreed to by the parties. See Jones v. DHHS, 52 MSPR 669, 672 (1992). These decisions are of historical interest: under 5 CFR 1201.29 (2022), the agency may now initiate a DWOP request.
