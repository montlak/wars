a. Supporting Evidence

The Board provides somewhat inconsistent explanations of what type of evidence should be supplied to its AJs to warrant excusing a belated appeal for medical reasons. Lacy v. Dept. of Navy, 78 MSPR 434, 437 n.* (1998), attempted to set a straight course by requiring AJs to evaluate all probative evidence, whatever its source, of a claim that medical incapacitation delayed an appeal:

[*] In Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 54 MSPR 254, 256 (1992), aff'd, 991 F.2d 808 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table), the Board stated that "[t]o establish good cause for the untimely filing, the appellant must establish how her illness prevented her from meeting the Board's filing requirements, and she must submit corroborating medical evidence." Id. at 256 (emphasis added). In subsequent restatements of the test, the Board sometimes referred simply to "corroborating evidence" without limiting the evidence to "medical" evidence, see, e.g., Andrews v. Department of the Army, 76 MSPR 147, 151 (1997), while at other times the Board required "medical evidence," see, e.g., Edwards v. Department of the Army, 63 MSPR 680, 682 (1994). Further, some cases have added a requirement that the appellant establish a "mental or physical incapacity." See, e.g., Gilley v. Department of the Army, 65 MSPR 277, 280 (1994).

Although medical evidence is preferable, we see no reason to limit the supporting ("corroborating") evidence only to "medical evidence" if the appellant explains why medical evidence is not available. Nor do we see a reason to impose a general "incapacitating" requirement. Rather, as set forth above, the appellant is only required to explain why the alleged illness impaired his ability to meet the Board's filing limits or seek an extension of time. To the extent that previous cases impose a requirement that the appellant be "incapacitated," or that medical evidence be required in every case, they are overruled.

See Burton v. VA, 83 MSPR 174, 177-78 ¶ 7 (1999) (summarizing Lacy and other decisions).

The critical factor is whether the appellant suffered from an illness during the filing period that affected or impaired his ability to file a timely appeal. The appellant is not required to show that he was incapacitated. Gatlin v. OPM, 91 MSPR 272, 274-76 ¶¶ 7-13 (2002) (analyzing evidence from a psychiatrist, family members, the appellant, and others who observed the appellant's ability to recall matters, deal with paper work, and understand what people wanted). Evidence supporting claims of medical inability to pursue Board processes governed by deadlines is not restricted to medical evidence, although that evidence may be preferred by the Board, Jerusalem v. Dept. of Air Force, 107 MSPR 660, 664-65 ¶¶ 7-8, 2008 MSPB 16 (2008) (aff'd, Jerusalem v. MSPB, 280 Fed. Appx. 973 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)):

The medical evidence indicates that the appellant suffers from hypertension and paranoid schizophrenia; however, other than a 3-month hospitalization, he was treated for his conditions as an outpatient. The medical notes of his discharge from that hospitalization in April 2002 conclude that "[h]e is competent and responsible at this time." There is no medical evidence to support his assertions that he was hospitalized between 2002 and 2006 and that he was declared incompetent in 1987. Although medical evidence is preferable in supporting a claim of good cause for the delay, supporting evidence is not limited to medical evidence alone if the appellant explains why medical evidence is unavailable. Lacy, 78 M.S.P.R. at 437 n.*. The appellant has not explained why evidence of these two assertions is not available and has not been provided. Nor has he explained why he has not provided more comprehensive medical records covering the entire 19-year period of his delay, rather than just the period between 1997 and 2002. Nor has he explained why he was still impaired when he was adjudged by his doctors to be "competent and responsible" in April 2002.

While the appellant is not required to demonstrate that he was hospitalized or generally incapacitated to establish good cause, he is required to explain why his alleged illness impaired his ability to meet the deadline or to seek an extension of time. Lacy, 78 M.S.P.R. at 437 n.*. Beyond the assertion in his affidavit that he was not sufficiently competent to pursue this appeal, the appellant has provided no evidence as to how his medical condition impaired him from pursuing this Board appeal for more than 19 years. Given the absence of any medical evidence for all but 5 of those 19 years, the appellant has failed to prove that he was impaired from filing his PFR for the entire period of his delay. See Perrot, 84 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶¶ 8-9 (appellant failed to explain why he could not have filed his petition during the period following his improvement); Sing, 51 M.S.P.R. at 253-54 (medical documentary evidence did not address the entire period of the delay). The appellant therefore has failed to establish good cause for his delay and so we dismiss the PFR as untimely filed.

The Board's willingness to accept anecdotal as well as medical evidence of sickness or incapacitation was reaffirmed, Fedon v. USPS, 78 MSPR 657, 661 (1998):

The Board will find good cause for waiver of its filing time limits where a party demonstrates that he or she suffered from an illness that affected his or her ability to file on time. See generally Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 54 MSPR 254, 256 (1992), aff'd, 991 F.2d 808 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). In our recent decision in Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 MSPR 434, 436-437 (1998), we held, based on a synthesis of precedent, that to establish that an untimely filing was the result of an illness the filing party must: (1) Identify the time period during which he or she suffered from the illness; (2) submit medical or other evidence showing that he or she suffered from illness during that time period; and (3) explain how the illness prevented him or her from making a timely filing or requesting an extension of time. With regard to part (2) of the test, it is preferable that the appellant submit medical evidence, but an appellant can establish good cause without submitting medical evidence if he or she provides a satisfactory explanation for why such evidence is not available. Id. at 437 n.*. We also held in Lacy that when a party claims that he or she was prevented from meeting a filing deadline because of illness, the party must be specifically informed of the legal standard for establishing good cause and be given a fair opportunity to submit argument and evidence relevant to the standard. Id. at 437-438.

A general allegation of a medical condition does not establish good cause for the untimely filing of an appeal. See Walker v. USPS, 89 MSPR 210, 214 ¶ 9 (2001) (insufficient to offer a generalized claim of "medical hindrance" due to anxiety); Horton v. USPS, 52 MSPR 401, 404 (1992) ("The Board has long held that the mere allegation of a medical condition does not establish good cause for an untimely filing."). If there is an allegation that a medical condition caused the delay in filing the appeal, it is best to offer the Board some substantiating medical evidence. See Coates v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 382, 383-84, 6 MSPB 325 (1981) ("She has averred, however, that her alleged nervous condition prevented her from contacting either her attorney or her agency. This alleged condition could constitute a circumstance beyond appellant's control which affected her ability to comply with the time limits or at least attempt to do so; but she has presented no evidence to establish such incapacity beyond her own statement."). The submission of a doctor's certificate indicating that an appellant was distraught and taking medication that caused some confusion was held sufficient to justify a four-week extension of time for filing an appeal in Darrah v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 375, 376, 3 MSPB 455 (1980); see Parker v. USPS, 3 MSPR 385, 387, 3 MSPB 465 (1980) (excusing a filing delay for an appellant who provided evidence of mental disorder). If an extension of time is requested for submitting medical evidence justifying a late appeal, the AJ should respond to and allow a reasonable request. See Lodree v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 253, 255 (1992) ("The administrative judge abused his discretion, however, in not allowing the appellant, appearing pro se, the extension of time he requested to file a physician's statement regarding his medical condition. The administrative judge received the request on March 27, 1991, two days prior to the issuance of the initial decision, but he did not respond to, or even acknowledge, the request.").

Medical evidence explaining a delay in the filing of an appeal should be provided to the AJ and not for the first time to the Board with a PFR. See Mathews v. USPS, 34 MSPR 645, 648 (1987). In Evans v. USPS, 29 MSPR 144, 145 n.* (1985), the Board affirmed the rejection of an appeal as untimely. The appellant submitted with his PFR a statement from a psychiatric social worker that the appellant had been under psychiatric treatment and was incapable of filing an appeal during the time limit. A letter was also submitted from the appellant's representative stating that the appellant's failure to timely file was caused by his confusion regarding appeal procedures. The Board concluded that the appellant failed to adequately explain why he had not submitted these materials to the AJ. The Board also questioned how the appellant's mental condition affected his ability to timely file. The Board noted that during the removal process, the appellant sought assistance from his congressional representative and that the appellant was informed by the agency of the time limit for appealing to the Board. See Lifschitz v. DLA, 48 MSPR 487 (1991) (Board declined to reach or develop the issue of the mental condition of an appellant who was, during the appeal, represented by counsel: "Under these circumstances, it does not appear that the appellant was incapable of representing himself below.").
