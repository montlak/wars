broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section D: Review of New Evidence, Subsection 02: What Evidence Is New, Part a: Previously Available
a. Previously Available

Evidence is not new if, although recently located or developed, it was previously available, Brenneman v. OPM, 439 F.3d 1325, 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2006):

Both this court and the Board have held that a party submitting new evidence in connection with a petition for review must satisfy the burden of showing that the evidence is material and that it could not have been obtained earlier with the exercise of due diligence. See Azarkhish v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 915 F.2d 675, 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990); Comer v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 78 M.S.P.R. 633, 636 (1998); Davis v. Veterans Admin., 31 M.S.P.R. 368, 369 n.* (1986); Landon v. Dep't of the Navy, 27 M.S.P.R. 374, 378 (1985); Abbey v. Dep't of Transp., 15 M.S.P.R. 650, 653 (1983). When documentary materials are asserted to be new and material evidence, "the information contained in the documents, not just the documents themselves, must have been unavailable despite due diligence when the record closed." Comer, 78 M.S.P.R. at 636. In the absence of an explanation for the delay in obtaining the evidence in question, the appellant "has not shown that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence to obtain the evidence." Id., see Azarkhish, 915 F.2d at 679.

An agency may not rely in a PFR on new documentary evidence if the documents were available to the agency before and during the hearing and the agency had knowledge of the documents. "To permit the agency to correct its judgmental error after the fact would result in a state of infinite litigation." Powell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 513, 517, 3 MSPB 35 (1980); see Williams v. OPM, 25 MSPR 92, 94 (1984) (medical report that OPM may have inadvertently omitted from its response to the appellant's appeal was not "new"). The agency does not present new evidence if it belatedly recognizes the importance of documents in existence prior to the hearing, as long as the agency was on notice of the claim involving the documents. See Keely v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 157, 159 (1984).

Documents were "previously available" if they could have been procured before the record closed, even though the documents were not actually in existence before the close of the record. If counsel is aware that the appellant has a medical condition that could constitute a defense to an action and counsel waits until after the record closes to document the condition with a medical report, the evidence is not new. It could have been created and presented earlier. See Dyer v. OPM, 41 MSPR 244, 247 (1989); Abatecola v. VA, 29 MSPR 601, 609 (1986) ("Because we find that the appellant has presented no evidence that his alleged psychiatric disability adversely affected his ability to raise the issue of his psychiatric impairment, and because we find that the appellant's counsel failed to exercise due diligence in discovering evidence relating to this issue, Dr. Garman's report cannot be considered new evidence that was not available, despite due diligence, when the record closed."). If information in documents prepared after the hearing could have been obtained before the hearing through some other means, the evidence is not new. Thomas v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 579, 581 (1987); see Stoglin v. MSPB, 603 Fed. Appx. 952, 956 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) ("Although Mr. Stoglin argues the evidence was unavailable prior to the closing of the record, he has not provided any evidence that he attempted to obtain the evidence prior to the close of the record. See Phillips v. U.S. Postal Serv., 695 F.2d 1389, 1391 (Fed. Cir. 1982) (stating delay is excusable where, under the circumstances, a petitioner exercises diligence or ordinary prudence)."); Alexander v. VA, 90 MSPR 591, 594 ¶ 8 (2002) (reciting, applying the rule); Marsh v. USPS, 32 MSPR 68, 69 (1987) (the appellant should have been on constructive notice of agency regulations or demonstrated to the Board that they were not matters of common knowledge); cf. Bournes v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-16-0103-I-1 (NP 8/15/2022) ("Regarding his burden to show that this evidence previously was not available despite his due diligence, the appellant stated in his petition for review that he 'was unable to obtain a statement from this individual before the record closed.' Such a bare allegation is insufficient to establish that the appellant could not have obtained an affidavit from the witness prior to the record closing had he exercised due diligence. See Terry v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 111 M.S.P.R. 258, ¶ 8 (2009).").

The rule is that the information contained in the documents, not just the documents themselves, must have been unavailable despite due diligence when the record closed. Conaway v. USPS, 93 MSPR 6, 14 n.6 (2002); see Agbenyeke v. DOJ, 111 MSPR 140, 146 ¶ 13, 2009 MSPB 60 (2009) (stating the rule); Parkin v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 411, 413-14 ¶¶ 5-7 (2002) (appellant knew of the agency contention of the scope of a waiver in settlement well before that contention surfaced during a deposition that led to a proceeding seeking invalidation of settlement for a mutual mistake); Salaz v. OPM, 91 MSPR 300, 302 ¶ 7 (2002) (stating rule); Murphy v. Dept. of Treasury, 91 MSPR 239, 245 ¶ 12 (2002) (stating rule); Ernst v. Dept. of Treasury, 69 MSPR 133, 137 n.2 (1995) (rejecting the contention that evidence was "new"; "This evidence was not submitted below, claims the appellant, because he recently discovered it buried under 200 pounds of documents."); Wilson v. DOJ, 58 MSPR 96, 99-110 (1993) (although the appellant discovered, after an initial decision tape recording of a conversation having relevance to the case, the content of that conversation was known prior to the initial decision; the tape did not qualify as new evidence); Dougherty v. FDIC, 52 MSPR 311, 316 (1992) (stating rule); Grassell v. Dept. of Transp., 40 MSPR 554, 563 (1989) (restating rules defining new evidence).

Transcripts of testimony from an EEOC hearing were not new evidence when testimony of the witnesses could have been taken during the Board hearing. But the decision of the EEOC following the close of the Board record was new evidence, and because it related to sexual harassment--one of the allegations made in support of the appellant's claim of an involuntary resignation--the evidence was sufficient to warrant a remand to the Board for consideration of the EEOC decision. Davis v. DHS, 239 Fed. Appx. 586 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), following remand, Davis v. DHS, 305 Fed. Appx. 659 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (affirming dismissal by Board for lack of jurisdiction over constructive adverse action appeal). An allegation that an appellant was coerced into signing a settlement did not serve as new evidence when the appellant made no showing of why he could not have presented the allegation within the time for filing a PFR from the initial decision adopting a settlement. Harris v. USDA, 54 MSPR 211, 214 (1992).

The Board examines the appellant's efforts during the period preceding the issuance of the initial decision to determine if the appellant could have earlier secured a medical report on his condition. See Webb v. USPS, 38 MSPR 35, 38 (1988); Frailey v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 608, 610-11 (1982) ("Although the report was unavailable before the record was closed, the presiding official did not render his initial decision until two weeks after this EEO report was issued"; "Although the Board may grant the petition upon a showing that the new evidence was unavailable when the record was closed, the Board is not required to do so where the appellant had the opportunity to submit the same new evidence to the presiding official."). Results of a polygraph examination taken after hearing do not constitute new evidence without an explanation of why the examination could not have been administered and admitted at an earlier date. See Tate v. Dept. of Interior, 4 MSPR 106, 107 (1980). Evidence is new if it could not have been secured through due diligence prior to the closing of the record before the AJ. Evidence was new when it was provided by a treating physician who could not offer a final report on the appellant's medical condition at the time the record closed. Jolly v. OPM, 7 MSPR 507, 509, 7 MSPB 363 (1981); see Davis v. Dept. of Navy, DA-0752-14-0285-I-1 (NP 7/5/2016) ("However, as the agency notes, the appellant was aware of the existence of the recordings, which were mentioned in LtCol M.J.'s report of the command investigation. His remedy for the agency's omission was to file a motion to compel under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.73(c), which he failed to do. Hence, he failed to preserve his right to discovery of these recordings. See Brown v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 45 M.S.P.R. 144, 145 (1990), aff'd, 928 F.2d 412 (Fed. Cir. 1991). In any case, the appellant was not prejudiced by the agency's failure to provide the audio recordings because he had ample opportunity below to obtain additional statements from those same witnesses. Hence, assuming arguendo that the transcripts of the appellant's conversations with LtCol M.J. and Col K.S. are authentic and legally obtained, they do not warrant a different result."); Carroll v. OPM, 50 MSPR 152, 154-55 (1991) (evidence of the appellant's death during the pendency of a PFR was new and material, by reason of the spouse's changed financial circumstances, to OPM's refusal to waive the overpayment of an annuity); Harrison v. OPM, 38 MSPR 218, 220-21 (1988) (new and material evidence that the annuitant's husband died after the record was closed but two weeks before the initial decision in an annuity overpayment case). But see O'Brien v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-14-0640-I-1 (NP 2/10/2015) (without discussion of whether the information could have been obtained prior to the close of the record when the case was before the AJ, the Board remanded after it credited the appellant's assertion that he first learned of information material to his involuntary resignation appeal during the Board hearing, promptly investigated the information, and presented through PFR an affidavit describing the material information he then developed). The value of new medical evidence may be diminished by the amount of time that lapses from the condition at issue in the appeal and the date of the medical examination. See Bibby v. Dept. of Transp., 33 MSPR 88, 90 (1987). [Refer later in this chapter to "Due Diligence."]

One exception to the rule excluding new evidence involved parties engaged in a jurisdictional dispute who were led by the AJ to believe that it would be appropriate to submit evidence on the jurisdictional issue with the PFR. See Wilkins v. USPS, 31 MSPR 579, 583 (1991). On occasion the Board considers information on a nonjurisdictional issue that could, and should, have been presented before the record closed. See Lanza v. Dept. of Army, 71 MSPR 6, 8 (1996) (letter from a witness to the Board was previously unknown to representatives of the agency and the appellant: the letter was evaluated because of conversations it described). A rule of necessity may be implied. See Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 8 MSPR 541, 544 (1981) ("exceptional circumstances" concerning a last-minute emergency necessitating the continuance of a hearing). At times the Board performs a sleight of hand to avoid the new evidence limitation. A position description not introduced as an exhibit during the hearing was properly relied on because it was incorporated by reference into the respondent's answer to an OSC complaint, making it part of the pleadings filed and relied upon and, therefore, part of the record in the adjudication. Special Counsel v. Starrett, 28 MSPR 60, 69 n.6 (1985). It is doubtful that incorporation by reference will often be used by the Board to avoid submission of the genuine article during the case before the AJ.

The Board does not permit parties to conduct discovery during a PFR to develop new evidence. See Forston v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 154, 158 (1993). The Board will not grant a belated PFR for the purpose of permitting development through discovery of factual information that was available, and could have been obtained through due diligence, before the record closed when the appeal was pending with an AJ. See Flaminio v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 520, 523-25 ¶¶ 3, 7-9 (2004).
