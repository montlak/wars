b. Dismissal Without Prejudice

Although the procedure is not often invoked, the Board can dismiss a PFR without prejudice to its refiling. Woofter v. VA, DE-0752-11-0333-I-1 (NP 7/11/2014), described circumstances warranting DWOP of a PFR:

Dismissal without prejudice of a petition for review is a procedural option that is available at the Board's discretion. Brigham v. Office of Personnel Management, 110 M.S.P.R. 108, ¶ 8 (2008). The Board has dismissed petitions for review without prejudice to avoid lengthy or indefinite delays in processing that could occur while allowing matters where the outcome is material to the petition before the Board to be resolved in other fora. In Brigham, for example, the Board dismissed a petition for review without prejudice to avoid a lengthy continuance while awaiting the appointment of an administrator for the appellant's estate. Id., ¶ 9; see Rivera v. Department of the Air Force, 110 M.S.P.R. 119, ¶ 8 (2008) (petition for review dismissed without prejudice to allow the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit to review a Board decision in a different appeal where pertaining to same legal issue); Rockefeller v. Department of Energy, 80 M.S.P.R. 252, 253 (1998) (petition for review dismissed without prejudice where the appellant asked to proceed in another forum and it was possible that those proceedings would result in the resolution of the matters to which his petition for review pertained).

Rockefeller v. Dept. of Energy, 80 MSPR 252, 254 ¶¶ 5-6 (1998), dismissed a PFR in an IRA appeal without prejudice to permit the appellant to pursue whistleblower claims with the Labor Department:

Dismissal without prejudice of a petition for review is a procedural option that is available at the Board's discretion. See Benson v. Department of Agriculture, 80 MSPR 463 (1998); Palomo v. Department of Justice, 73 MSPR 306, 307, aff'd, 132 F.3d 51 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table); Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 63 MSPR 291 (1994). Although petitions for review should not be dismissed without prejudice over an appellant's objection, see Thomas v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 51 MSPR 218, 221 (1991), we discern no reason why a petition for review should not be dismissed based on an appellant's request, even where the agency objects, see Kravitz V. Office of Personnel Management, 75 MSPR 44, 46-47 (1997).

Thus, while we note the agency's objections to dismissing the appellant's petitions for review without prejudice, the agency has not articulated a reason why doing so would be improper or how it would be prejudiced by such a dismissal. The appellant based his requests on a desire to proceed in another forum and it is possible that those proceedings may result in the resolution of these matters such that the appellant does not refile his petitions before the Board.

Rockefeller was distinguished in McQuade v. Dept. of Energy, 83 MSPR 125, 127 ¶ 5 (1999), denying a motion to stay or dismiss the PFR without prejudice pending pursuit of Labor Department whistleblower proceedings:

The facts in this case are distinguishable in several respects from those in Rockefeller. First, here, in his Board appeal the appellant raised affirmative defenses of discrimination and retaliation for filing the DOL complaint. Since these claims cannot be resolved before the DOL, unlike in Rockefeller it is unlikely that a decision on the appellant's DOL whistleblower complaint will render further proceedings before the Board unnecessary. Second, as stated above, the agency has asserted that the appellant requested a continuance of the June 7, 1999 hearing that had been scheduled in the DOL matter, and the appellant has not rebutted that assertion. Under these circumstances, the delay in concluding the Board case could be lengthy, and has the potential to prejudice the agency. Finally, here, the appellant is represented by counsel, while the appellant in Rockefeller was proceeding pro se. 80 MSPR at 252. Thus, should it become necessary for the instant appellant to proceed in both forums simultaneously as a result of the denial of his request to dismiss his petition for review without prejudice, he is in a better position to ensure that his rights are protected.

See Rivera v. Dept. of Air Force, 110 MSPR 119, 121 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 223 (2008) (on its own initiative the Board dismissed a PFR without prejudice pending remand from the Federal Circuit of a case involving a related issue and requiring reconsideration and a new decision from the Board); Brigham v. OPM, 110 MSPR 108, 110 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 222 (2008) (Board used a DWOP rather than a continuance to allow the entry into the retirement overpayment dispute of a representative of the late appellant's estate after an administrator was appointed); Taylor v. DOD, 101 MSPR 438, 441 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 73 (2006) (dismissing an appeal without prejudice at the PFR stage, although without a remand, "so that the appellant can have additional time in which to obtain the evidence he seeks in support of his appeal"; left unstated was how that evidence was to be developed without formal discovery procedures available to an administrative judge upon a remand); McQuade v. Dept. of Energy, 85 MSPR 451, 452 ¶ 4 (2000) (DWOP was granted for a PFR while the appellant exhausted a DOL whistleblowing remedy); cf. Ryan v. DHS, 112 MSPR 43, 45 ¶ 4, 2009 MSPB 136 (2009) (using DWOP for an arbitration review proceeding before the Board), upon refiling, Ryan v. DHS, 113 MSPR 234, 2010 MSPB 25 (2010) (PFR was on DWOP while the parties awaited a decision by OPM on the appellant's disability retirement application; the agency notified the Board that OPM approved the application and the PFR was dismissed with prejudice).
