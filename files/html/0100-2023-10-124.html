A. WITNESS REQUEST

Assuming that a hearing was requested and is in order, the AJ will approve witnesses from among those offered by the appellant and the agency. The request for witnesses is generally made in the parties' prehearing submissions. By approving witnesses listed by the parties, the AJ implicitly or explicitly directs the agency to produce at the hearing the approved witnesses who are employed by or otherwise within the control of the agency. The AJ may also direct nonparty agencies to supply employees as witnesses. 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(8), (10) (2022). Subpoenas may be required for individuals who are not federal employees. If the appellant desires to testify, he must list himself as a witness. The appellant may be called as a witness by the agency if he is placed on the agency's witness list.

Some AJs seem to believe their job is to decide the case on the basis of the least evidence and the fewest witnesses. This is the minimum due process theory of adjudication, the principal benefit being reduction of AJs' workloads. The agency frequently submits a monstrous response to the appeal that seems quite adequate to an AJ who has not yet reviewed the appellant's case. That is why the prehearing submission is so important: properly structured and carefully articulated, it puts the appellant's case before the AJ, assisting the AJ to understand the importance of granting the appellant's witness requests.

The attitude of some AJs concerning witnesses may be the result of occasional abuses of the system by the parties. Some appellants request witnesses of marginal importance from all over the nation. Agency representatives then argue that the hearing process is becoming too expensive and an administrative burden. The same result may occur if an insecure agency representative summons every witness who knows anything about the case, even though they add nothing to what is offered through other witnesses or the information they have is gossip. If those witnesses make it to the witness stand, they will be excused when the nature of their testimony becomes clear. Another factor figuring in the drive to cut down on witnesses is the interest in avoiding duplication of what is already in the record. Unlike a jury, the AJ is educated about the appeal before the hearing. The appellant filed his appeal, and the agency filed a response replete with exhibits and statements, examples of work product, investigation reports, performance evaluations, as well as a summary or transcript of the appellant's reply to the proposed action. In some cases, there is not much more that can be said about the case unless there are credibility issues. Discovery may supplement the record, but that too is documented. The Board hearing could as well be conducted by mail or e-filing in many cases, and it is not astonishing that AJs listening to hundreds of cases believe that testimony often does not often add much to the documentary record that forms the basis of their initial decisions.

Whatever the cause, the result of preclusion of witnesses by the AJ is that some witnesses who really are needed are rejected. It will not do to sit back and allow this to happen, hoping that any error will be cured by the Board through a PFR or by a reviewing court. If the AJ disapproves witnesses during the prehearing conference, tell the AJ that you object to the AJ's refusal to approve the witness, take exception to the AJ's ruling, and request the AJ to reconsider that ruling. Additionally, counsel may write to the AJ and request reconsideration of the ruling on a particular witness, giving an offer of proof of what the witness would say and explaining, again, the importance of the testimony. Even during the hearing, reconsideration may be requested of a rejected witness, and some AJs will state during the prehearing conference that rulings will be reserved until the hearing as to some witnesses whose relevance may be questionable before the hearing. That places a burden on counsel to remember during the hearing to request reconsideration of the earlier ruling denying the witnesses--a matter to note in the hearing notebook that will be assembled and present at counsel's side during the hearing.
