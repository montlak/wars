f. OSC Closeout Letter

Assuming that OSC does not initiate a corrective action of its own or work with the parties to settle an IRA complaint, the current process of the office is to send out a preliminary letter advising the complainant that she has not made out a reprisal case to the OSC, with an explanation of OSC's analysis and an invitation to respond, the "13-day letter"). Then, if the complainant responds, or if the complainant does not respond, OSC generally then sends out two more letters (in the same mailing). The first letter explains, again, and with reference to any prior response by the complainant, why OSC does not think the case rises to the level of reprisal. That is called a closeout letter for purposes of this discussion. The second letter advises the complainant that she may appeal to the MSPB, and the time limit is stated for the initiation of the appeal. That letter is the notice of appeal rights. The complainant then attaches the notice of appeal rights to the MSPB IRA appeal, and the complainant becomes an appellant. Agencies are not served by OSC with a copy of the closeout letter. Agencies may desire that closeout letter, as may the Board AJ assigned to the case, to see how OSC analyzed the case. The appellant may be reluctant to provide that closeout letter to the agency or to the Board, although the appellant may do so if she wishes, and there may be reasons, such as the jurisdictional need to demonstrate to MSPB the scope of the complaint to OSC if it is not apparent from the content of the OSC-14 form [formerly OSC-11] or supplemental submissions to OSC filed with the appeal.

Congress stated in the Whistleblower Protection Act that an OSC determination that a prohibited personnel practice has not occurred (a statement that may be found in a closeout letter) "shall not be cited or referred to in any...administrative or judicial proceeding for any purpose, without the consent of the person submitting the allegation of a prohibited personnel practice." 5 USC 1214(b)(2)(E); see Smith v. USDA, 64 MSPR 46, 55 (1994) (OSC closure letter filed with the AJ is not to be relied upon to make findings of fact based on statements in the letter), followed in Singleton v. Ohio Nat'l Guard, 77 MSPR 583, 586-87 (1998), and Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 639 ¶ 97 (2001); cf. Sato v. Dept. of Army, SF-3443-16-0122-I-1 (NP 7/19/2016) ("While OSC's opinions and conclusions are not binding on the Board, see Smith v. Department of Agriculture, 64 M.S.P.R. 46, 55 (1994), considering the content of the appellant's purported OSC complaint in OSC File No. MA‑15‑4735, we agree with OSC's conclusion that the appellant alleged that the agency committed prohibited personnel practices, but did not allege reprisal for whistleblowing."); Thorne v. DHHS, 79 MSPR 583, 588 ¶¶ 9, 11 (1998) (recitations in an OSC closure letter concerning the appellant's complaints did not preclude the appellant from demonstrating a broader scope of complaints to OSC); Pashun v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 374, 380 (1997) (IRA complainant has the right to a de novo review of OSC findings, including those affecting Board jurisdiction; the fact that an OSC closure letter opined that the Board would not likely view the appellant's disclosures as protected did not establish that the appellant could not have reasonably believed his disclosures evidenced violations of law or regulation or that his allegations supporting Board jurisdiction were frivolous).

If offered by the appellant, the closeout letter from OSC may be relied on by the Board to demonstrate the scope of complaints made by the appellant to OSC. See McVay v. Arkansas Nat'l Guard, 80 MSPR 120, 124 (1998) (appellant demonstrated through his correspondence to OSC, rather than OSC's correspondence to him, that nonpromotion was brought to OSC's attention); O'Brien v. Office of Indep. Counsel, 79 MSPR 406, 411-12 (1998) (although the appellant's OSC complaint was submitted before issuance of the termination letter, he demonstrated that he subsequently complained to OSC of the termination, because the OSC closeout letter referenced termination); Costin v. DHHS, 64 MSPR 517, 531 (1994), post; Gonzales v. DHUD, 64 MSPR 314, 319 (1994) (relying on the closeout letter to show what claims the appellant did and did not raise with OSC); Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 325, 333 n.7 (1993) (one means of showing what was alleged to OSC is through placement into the record of the appellant's written OSC complaint and the OSC closure letter); cf. Thompson v. Dept. of State, DC-1221-14-0827-W-1 (NP 5/6/2016) (if the closeout letter does not provide information demonstrating what disclosures were made to OSC, that information must be provided through other means or the Board may dismiss the IRA appeal as jurisdictionally deficient); cf. Taylor v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-1221-17-0088-W-1 (NP 8/12/2022) ("On review, the appellant also argues that OSC improperly closed its investigation into her OSC complaint without completing a proper investigation. However, the alleged inadequacy of OSC's investigation has no bearing on our jurisdictional analysis. Salerno, 123 M.S.P.R. 230, ¶ 15.")

When closeout letters contain information bearing on the jurisdiction of the Board, it is immaterial whether the letters were furnished to the Board by the appellant or by the agency, Costin v. DHHS, 64 MSPR 517, 531 (1994):

We note that the OSC termination letter discussed above was introduced into evidence by the agency. Under 5 USC §§ 1214(a)(2)(B) & 1221(f)(2), the Board is generally precluded from considering an OSC termination letter absent the appellant's consent. The purpose of this evidentiary rule, though, is to ensure that a whistleblower is not "penalized" or "prejudiced" in any way by OSC's decision not to pursue a case. See S. Rep. No. 413, 100th Cong., 2d Sess. 18, 33 (1988). We conclude that Congress would not have intended for us to ignore an OSC termination letter, the contents of which indicate that an appellant failed to exhaust his OSC remedy, given that Congress itself imposed the exhaustion requirement as a jurisdictional prerequisite to an IRA appeal.

Because the OSC closeout letter is deemed without evidentiary value by statute, it becomes what amounts to a privileged document that should not be required to be produced by an AJ, Bloom v. Dept. of Army, 101 MSPR 79, 84 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 16 (2006):

The AJ also erred in ordering the appellant to produce a copy of the OSC decision discussing the allegations raised in her complaint to the OSC. Pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(2)(B), OSC's written statement containing its summary of relevant facts, including the facts that support, and those that do not support, the appellant's allegations, "may not be admissible as evidence in any judicial or administrative proceeding, without consent of the person who received such statement," namely, the appellant. This section of the statute would be rendered meaningless if the AJ could order the production of the appellant's OSC complaint over the appellant's objection. Although the AJ has a legitimate need to know, and the appellant must prove, what disclosures and personnel actions were raised and therefore exhausted before OSC, see Yunus, 242 F.3d at 1371, this issue may usually be resolved by the appellant's submission of the complaint submitted to OSC. Further, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 1221(f)(2), OSC's decision to terminate its investigation may not be considered in an IRA action. "The purpose of this evidentiary rule...is to ensure that a whistleblower is not 'penalized' or 'prejudiced' in any way by OSC's decision not to pursue a case." Costin v. Department of Health & Human Services, 64 M.S.P.R. 517, 531 (1994). Finally, an IRA appeal is a de novo action, and the Board must therefore rely on its independent analysis of the parties' evidence, and not on OSC's characterizations of the appellant's allegations, which are not binding on the Board. Wheeler v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 88 M.S.P.R. 236, ¶ 22 (2001). An AJ should not ordinarily order a party to submit a copy of the OSC letter referred to in 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(2)(B), and if such an order is issued, the AJ must explain, on the record, why the letter is necessary and explicitly advise the appellant that the letter is inadmissible without the appellant's consent.
