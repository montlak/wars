d. Office Equipment, Technology, Related Problems

That familiar problem--loss of a word processing file--garnered no sympathy. The appellant did not notify the Board of the problem or seek an additional filing extension. De La Garza v. USPS, 45 MSPR 357, 358-89 (1990); see Heath v. USDA, 109 MSPR 684, 686 ¶ 6, 2008 MSPB 204 (2008) ("The agency's representative, having already successfully filed an extension request, concedes that she was aware of the April 4 deadline, that she realized on the evening of April 4, that she could not meet it due to the loss of a thumb drive, but that she chose not to take the simple step of requesting a further extension based upon her unusual circumstances. While her predicament may have been unfortunate, her failure to follow the Board's instructions constitutes failure to exercise due diligence and ordinary prudence."); Davis v. Dept. of Treasury, 95 MSPR 207, 209 ¶ 6 (2003) (delay not excused because "my typewriter broke down"); Gunn v. USPS, 63 MSPR 513, 516 n.1 (1994) (PFR response late by one day was not excused because of a glitch in docketing software); Labelle v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 465, 468 (1993) (allegations concerning the loss of personal papers did not explain how the appellant was prevented from timely filing a PFR or seeking an extension for its filing); Branch v. DOD, 51 MSPR 537, 539 (1991) (declining to accept a PFR late by a day because the attorney's "unanticipated office [equipment] problems" prevented the timely printing of a PFR); Fitzgerald v. VA, 45 MSPR 222, 224-25 (1990) (problems getting a PFR typed were no reason for a delay; no requirement in Board regulations calls for a typewritten submission); King v. Dept. of Transp., 14 MSPR 652 (1983) (malfunction of a word processor did not provide cause for a delayed PFR). But see Laus v. OPM, 91 MSPR 369, 371 ¶ 5 (2002) (excusing a one-day delay in a PFR caused by computer or software problems preventing the appellant from printing the PFR on the day he completed it). The invariable placement of some significant email in a spam folder did not serve to excuse a belated PFR. Simmons v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-18-0225-I-1 (NP 2/9/2023) ("Regarding due diligence, the appellant asks that the Board excuse his untimely petition because the email containing the initial decision was sent to his spam folder. Because the appellant registered as an e-fi ler, he was responsible for ensuring that emails from @mspb.gov were not blocked by fi lters and for monitoring his case at the Repository at e-Appeal Online to ensure that he received all case-related documents. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.14(j)(2)-(3). Although the appellant indicates that, prior to the initial decision, he never had problems with emailed pleadings being blocked by fi lters, it is clear that he failed to monitor his case at the Repository, which demonstrates a lack of due diligence."). Although not concerning the timeliness Although not concerning the timeliness of a PFR, the Board noted as to one issue concerning timely submission of evidence that a computer glitch causing Internet or email access loss was not excused when "businesses and libraries provide Internet access to the public for little or no charge, and the appellant failed to assert that she attempted to retrieve her email from any such location." Bynum v. USPS, 112 MSPR 403, 411 ¶¶ 21, 2009 MSPB 194 (2009) (aff'd on other grounds, Bynum v. USPS, 382 Fed. Appx. 934 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Failure to Receive Initial Decision; E-Filer."]

Fax problems have mixed results. See Huntington v. OPM, DE-844E-14-0314-B-1 (NP 2/27/2023) ("We recognize that the appellant is pro se and it appears that she tried to fax her petition to the Board's phone (not fax) number on November 3, 2016, before the filing deadline. Ordinarily, such a mistake would result in an error message, indicating that the fax transmission was unsuccessful. However, there is no indication on the 'Transaction Report' that the fax transmission was unsuccessful. Accordingly, under these unique circumstances, we find good cause to excuse the untimeliness of the appellant's petition for review."). But see Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 63 MSPR 28, 30-31 (1994) (unsuccessful attempt to transmit a PFR by fax on the last day of filing was not excused).
