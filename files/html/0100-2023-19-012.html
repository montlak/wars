e. What Constitutes a Complaint

The deferral process depends on the filing of a proper complaint. Not every protest to an agency constitutes an EEO complaint sufficient to defeat or defer Board jurisdiction over an adverse action appeal. Chamberlain v. VA, 1 MSPR 560, 561, 1 MSPB 540 (1980), decided that a letter concerning the effect of a reassignment, advising an agency of a medical condition--a letter that did not initiate formal EEO proceedings with the agency--was not a "complaint" sufficient to divest the Board of jurisdiction over a demotion appeal. Under EEOC regulations, a complaint must be preceded by "counseling." If no counseling has occurred, but an EEO complaint is filed before the Board appeal, the appeal will not be stayed because the EEO complaint is not valid (lacking exhaustion of the counseling requirement) and does not operate to stay the Board's jurisdiction. Ritchards v. DHHS, 24 MSPR 335, 337 (1984). EEO counseling or some other precomplaint step alone does not divest the Board of jurisdiction. Economou v. Caldera, 286 F.3d 144, 149 (2d Cir. 2002) (citing 29 CFR 1614.301); see McNair v. VA, PH-0752-13-0183-I-1 (NP 4/18/2014) ("Contacting an EEO counselor does not waive the time limit to file a Board appeal. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(a)."); Dowell v. USPS, 113 MSPR 250, 253 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 30 (2010) (remand to determine, relative to the timeliness of the Board appeal, if the appellant had filed an EEO complaint or had pursued only a preliminary EEO step); Gonzales v. USPS, 11 MSPR 574, 576 (1982) (letter stating an interest in filing a complaint was not the same as a complaint); Freeman v. USPS, 5 MSPR 543, 545, 6 MSPB 6 (1981) (informal EEO complaint did not prevent immediate Board jurisdiction in an appeal); Kelly v. USPS, 3 MSPR 381, 382, 3 MSPB 461 (1980) (an informal complaint that initiated counseling did not constitute the formal complaint necessary to prevent the Board from assuming immediate jurisdiction). In Christo v. USPS, 3 MSPR 22, 29, 3 MSPB 145 (1980), and Cardenas v. USPS, 1 MSPR 578, 579, 1 MSPB 560 (1980), the Board immediately accepted jurisdiction of an appeal even though EEO complaints were filed soon after the initiation of Board appeals, meaning that counseling must have been initiated prior to the Board appeals. See Kelly v. USPS, 3 MSPR 381, 382, 3 MSPB 461 (1980).

If there is some confusion as to whether the appellant filed a formal complaint or only an informal charge with a counselor, the Board examines the circumstances of the filing and the contacts with agency EEO personnel, as well as the treatment of the EEO case by the agency, to determine whether the appellant filed a formal complaint for purposes of determining the filing time for a Board appeal. See Moody v. USPS, 91 MSPR 319, 321-22 ¶ 9 (2002); McBeen v. Dept. of Interior, 22 MSPR 46, 48 (1984) (construing the appellant's intent relative to the filing of an EEO complaint based on the treatment of the matter by the agency's EEO office).

If EEO precomplaint procedures are pursued but the appellant fails to follow through with the filing of a formal complaint because, he alleges, the agency did not provide proper notice of his right to file a complaint, the appellant must act promptly either to pursue the complaint with the agency or to appeal his case to the Board. Baxter v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 663, 667 (1990) (declining to excuse a filing delay of 21 months that was not attributed to a delay involving an EEO complaint). If the appellant's EEO complaint is rejected or returned by the agency for lack of proper formalities, e.g., signature, it does not bar the Board appeal. See Ishimatsu v. Dept. of Interior, 20 MSPR 569, 570 (1984). If the appellant asserts that he filed an EEO complaint and the agency disagrees, the AJ must resolve the issue. That resolution may not depend upon the agency representative's assertions, Felton v. Dept. of Air Force, 106 MSPR 198, 200-01 ¶¶ 6-7, 2007 MSPB 170 (2007):

The administrative judge dismissed the appeal as untimely based on the 30-day time limit under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b), without discussing the alternative time limit under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(b). The appellant's appeal form contains the following statement from his representative, "I certify that all of the statements made in this form and all attached forms are true, complete, and correct to the best of my knowledge and belief. The form also contains the statement, "This is a mixed case, he originally filed with EEO--the 120-day period has lapsed." In response to the question, "Have you filed a formal discrimination complaint with your agency concerning the action you are appealing?," he checked the "Yes" box, provided the address of the place where he filed the complaint, and indicated that he filed it on August 29, 2006.

In its motion to dismiss the appeal, the agency contended that the appellant had begun the equal employment opportunity (EEO) process, but it stopped the pre-complaint process when he failed to respond to the agency's notice of final interview. According to the agency, he did not file a formal complaint. The agency thus disputes the appellant's statements, but it has presented no evidence to support its statements. The statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence. Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995). Although the agency included in its response file a statement that the appellant did not file a formal complaint, this submission is not sworn and bears no indication that the author, the agency representative in this appeal, has any personal knowledge of the appellant's EEO activity. See, e.g., Singletary v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 76, 79 (1998) (agency's statement was not in affidavit form and therefore does not constitute evidence).

Assuming a timely complaint, the complaint may be amended through an allegation that is like or related to the allegation leading to the original complaint. If the amendment adds a matter otherwise within the Board's jurisdiction, then the appellant need not reinitiate EEO counseling and may proceed, based on the amended complaint, to request a Board hearing on the mixed case matter. See Cano v. USPS, 107 MSPR 284, 291 ¶¶ 15-16, 2007 MSPB 273 (2007) (constructive adverse retirement appeal).
