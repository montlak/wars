b. Prior Resort to Agency-Employer for Disciplinary Action

In a disciplinary case, OSC need not seek to secure action by the agency-employer against the respondent before OSC initiates action through a disciplinary complaint with the Board, Special Counsel v. Filiberti, 27 MSPR 498, 506-08 (1984):

[T]he agency contends that 5 USC § 1206(c)(1)(A) requires that the Special Counsel, as a precondition to filing a disciplinary action against an employee for committing a prohibited personnel practice, must first provide the employing agency with an opportunity to investigate the matter and take whatever corrective action is appropriate. The Special Counsel concedes that the requirements of 5 USC § 1206(c)(1)(A) are a prerequisite to its filing of a corrective action under that subsection against the employing agency, but contends that the requirements do not apply to disciplinary actions against employees which are authorized by 5 USC § 1206(g) and brought under 5 USC § 1207. We agree with the Special Counsel's interpretation of the law.

The legislative history of section 1206(c)(1)(A) does not reveal a Congressional desire to establish, by implication, an interconnection between the pre-filing limitations governing the Special Counsel's actions against agencies under subsection (c) and the Special Counsel's ability to file actions against employees under subsection (g). As originally introduced in S. 2640 and H.R. 11280, section 1206 did not allow the Special Counsel to proceed against agencies at all. S. 2640 and H.R. 11280, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. § 202 (1978), reprinted in House Comm. on Post Office and Civil Service, 96th Cong., 1st Sess., Legislative History of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 at Vol. II at 1320, Vol. I at 11 (Comm. Print No. 96-2 (1979)) [hereinafter cited as Comm. Print]. The then relevant section, section 1206(d), permitted the Special Counsel only to file a report containing recommendations regarding corrective actions with the employing agency and the Office of Personnel Management. The final decision on what corrective action should be taken, if any, rested with "the agency involved, subject to guidance and instruction from the Office of Personnel Management." S. 2640, § 202, Comm. Print, supra at Vol. II at 1320. The House Committee on Post Office and Civil Service amended H.R. 11280 to allow the Special Counsel to proceed against agencies. Under its revised version of section 1206(d)(1), the Special Counsel filed a report directly with the Board, and the Board, rather than the agency, decided on the appropriate corrective action. H.R. 11280, § 202, Comm. Print, supra at Vol. I at 520. The Senate version of that provision, while also amended, was not comparably revised. Therefore, when the two versions were considered in conference, under the Senate's version "the final decision on corrective action would be made by the agency involved" and, under the House's version, "the Merit Systems Protection Board, rather than the agency involved, would make the final decision concerning what corrective action is to be taken." H.R. Conf. Rep. No. 1717, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 134, reprinted in part in [1978] U.S. Code Cong. & Ad. News 2723, 2860, 2867.

In order to limit the number of adjudicatory confrontations between the Special Counsel and employing agencies and in the expectation "that the Special Counsel and the agencies involved will, whenever practicable, resolve questions concerning proper corrective action without resorting to the Merit Systems Protection Board," the Conference Committee substituted the provision now codified at 5 USC § 1206(c)(1)(A) for the House and Senate versions. Id. It continued to provide for the report to be sent to the agency, but denied the agency final decisionmaking authority by providing that, after a reasonable period of time, the Special Counsel could "request the Board to consider the matter" and the Board would "make the final decision...." Id. This compromise, which was intended to allow for, but to limit, adjudicatory disputes between the Special Counsel and employing agencies, does not provide support for the proposition that Congress, in enacting it, intended to place pre-filing limitations on the Special Counsel's ability to bring disciplinary actions against employees under subsection (g).

In fact, the Conference Committee, while providing for this pre-filing limitation on actions against agencies under subsection (c), expanded the authority of the Special Counsel contained in S. 2640 to bring actions against employees under subsection (g). That Senate bill gave the Special Counsel only limited authority to seek to discipline employees for conduct that did not constitute a prohibited personnel practice. S. 2640, § 202, Comm. Print, supra at Vol. II at 1748. The comparable provisions of H.R. 11280 (§§ 1206(e) and (g)), which were adopted by the Conference Committee, allowed the Special Counsel to more broadly investigate "activities prohibited by any civil service rule or regulation" and authorized him to bring a disciplinary action "after any investigation under this section." H.R. 11280, § 202, Comm. Print, supra at Vol. I at 520-521. We, therefore, do not discern a Congressional intent to place pre-filing limitations upon the Special Counsel's authority, under subsection (g), to bring disciplinary actions against employees from the fact that the Conference decided also to allow the Special Counsel to bring corrective actions against agencies under subsection (c).

Special Counsel v. Zimmerman, 36 MSPR 274, 283 (1988), also rejected the argument that OSC was required to seek that the employing agency take corrective action before OSC could initiate a disciplinary action with the Board.

OSC is not required to provide the agency with an opportunity to investigate a case before starting a disciplinary action, but prior notification is helpful. If the agency takes disciplinary action on its own, OSC need not request disciplinary action. Special Counsel v. Starrett, 28 MSPR 60, 72 (1985). Alternatively, OSC may defer to the employing agency to take disciplinary action against an employee. Should this occur, the agency sets the penalty, and the penalty is then subject to Board mitigation analysis through an adverse action appeal, even if OSC first agreed with the disciplinary action taken by the agency. See Frederick v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 517, 536 (1994), rev'd on other grounds, Frederick v. DOJ, 73 F.3d 349 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Should OSC seek disciplinary action, the Board considers mitigating factors if it assesses a penalty. See Special Counsel v. Costello, 75 MSPR 562, 611, 616 (1997), rev'd on other grounds, Costello v. MSPB, 182 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999).
