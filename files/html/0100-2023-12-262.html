5. Standard of Review

The Board is indirect in defining how it reviews arbitration awards, Wright v. Dept. of Commerce, 110 MSPR 349, 352 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 251 (2008):

The standard of the Board's review of an arbitrator's award is narrow; such awards are entitled to a greater degree of deference than initial decisions issued by the Board's administrative judges. De Bow v. Department of the Air Force, 97 M.S.P.R. 5, ¶ 5 (2004); see Robinson v. Department of Health & Human Services, 30 M.S.P.R. 389, 392 ("[T]he relevant statutory provisions do not provide for de novo review of arbitration decisions appealable under § 7121(d) in light of the statutory language, legislative history and the policy considerations of the Civil Service Reform Act"), recons. denied, 31 M.S.P.R. 479 (1986). The Board will modify or set aside an arbitration decision only where the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation. De Bow, 97 M.S.P.R. 5, ¶ 5. Absent legal error, the Board cannot substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator, even if it would disagree with the arbitrator's decision. Id.; see Devine v. National Treasury Employees Union, 737 F.2d 1031, 1033 (Fed. Cir. 1984) ("Whether the MSPB might have reached a contrary conclusion is simply not relevant to our review of the arbitrator's award, which was rendered pursuant to authority given him by agreement of the parties to the arbitration and pursuant to law.") (quoting Devine v. Sutermeister, 724 F.2d 1558, 1566 (Fed. Cir. 1983), superceded by rule on other grounds as stated in Bloomer v. Department of Health & Human Services, 966 F.2d 1436 (Fed. Cir. 1992)); Jones v. Department of the Treasury, 93 M.S.P.R. 494, ¶ 8 (2003) (noting that an arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis by, for example, misallocating the burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework).

See Jones v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-15-0011-R-1 (NP 2/16/2016) ("We construe such claims as challenges by the appellant to the arbitrator's credibility determinations, factual findings, and legal conclusions. However, the appellant's mere disagreement with the arbitrator does not show legal error. See Cirella v. Department of the Treasury, 108 M.S.P.R. 474, ¶¶ 15-16, aff'd, 296 F. App'x 63 (Fed. Cir. 2008)."); Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 (NP 5/2/2013) ("Even if the Board disagrees with an arbitrator's decision, absent legal error, the Board cannot substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator. Thus, an arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, for instance, by misallocating the burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework. Kirkland v. Department of Homeland Security, 119 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 12 (2013).").

From Moore v. Dept. of Commerce, 55 MSPR 451, 457 (1992): "The arbitrator's finding that the appellant did not prove his disability discrimination claim is a factual determination entitled to deference, unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, e.g., by misallocating burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework." Means v. Dept. of Labor, 63 MSPR 180, 182 (1994), added:

The scope of the Board's review of arbitration decisions is limited. Arbitration decisions are entitled to deference, and will be modified or set aside only where the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rules, or regulation. Moore, 55 MSPR at 457. The arbitrator's finding that the appellant did not prove his disability discrimination claim is a factual determination entitled to deference, unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, e.g., by misallocating burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework. Id.

More informative is Horne v. Dept. of Air Force, CB-7121-10-0002-V-1 (NP 11/18/2010):

The standard for Board review of an arbitrator's decision is limited, and arbitrators' decisions are entitled to greater deference than initial decisions issued by the Board's administrative judges. Vena v. Department of Labor, 111 M.S.P.R. 165, ¶ 5 (2009). The Board will modify or set aside an arbitrator's decision only where the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation. Id. Even if the Board disagrees with an arbitrator's decision, absent legal error, the Board cannot substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator. Adams v. Department of Labor, 112 M.S.P.R. 288, ¶ 7 (2009). Thus, the arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, for example, by using the wrong analytical framework. Id.

Adams v. Dept. of Labor, 112 MSPR 288, 291-92 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 185 (2009), cited in Horne, stated:

[T]he standard of the Board's review of an arbitrator's award is limited. See FitzGerald v. Department of Homeland Security, 107 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 9 (2008). The Board will modify or set aside such an award only when the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation. Id. Even if the Board disagrees with an arbitrator's decision, absent legal error, the Board cannot substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator. Id. Thus, the arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in her legal analysis, for example, by misallocating the burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework. Berry v. Department of Commerce, 105 M.S.P.R. 596, ¶ 5 (2007).

If the arbitrator fails to make proper factual findings, the Board may do so, Brookens v. Dept. of Labor, 120 MSPR 678, 685 ¶ 15, 2014 MSPB 27 (2014):

[T]he Board can only defer to the arbitrator's findings and conclusions if the arbitrator makes specific findings on the issues in question. Hollingsworth, 115 M.S.P.R. 636, ¶ 8. As the appellant asserts, the arbitrator failed to provide a legal or factual analysis to support his findings that the agency did not retaliate against the appellant for his union activities and did not discriminate against him because of his age and race. The Board may make its own findings when the arbitrator failed to cite any legal standard or employ any analytical framework for his evaluation of the evidence. Id. Here, the arbitrator did not set forth any analytical framework for his determinations on the appellant's claims of discrimination or retaliation for union activity. Accordingly, the Board has no basis upon which to defer to the arbitrator. Therefore, we are vacating the arbitration decision as to the findings of no discrimination and no retaliation. Pursuant to the Board's authority in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.155(e), we forward the matter to the Board's Washington Regional Office for assignment to an administrative judge to make recommended findings on the appellant's discrimination and retaliation claims under the appropriate legal standards. See Sadiq v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 450, 456 (2013).

See Luther v. Dept. of Commerce, 107 MSPR 616, 620 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 7 (2008) (reiterating standard of review); Berry v. Dept. of Commerce, 105 MSPR 596, 600 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 129 (2007) ("An arbitrator's finding that an appellant did not prove his discrimination and EEO retaliation claims is a factual determination entitled to deference, unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, e.g., by misallocating burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework.").

The Board restated its review standard, Kendricks v. VA, 79 MSPR 510, 514 ¶ 6 (1998):

The scope of the Board's review of an arbitrator's decision is limited and the arbitrator's findings are given greater deference than an administrative judge's initial decision. The Board will modify or set aside an arbitrator's decision only when the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation. Absent legal error, the Board will not substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator, even if it would disagree with the arbitrator's decision. Colon, 73 MSPR at 663.

As to issues of law, Weng v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-13-0180-V-1 (NP 4/17/2014), explained that the arbitrator is held to the same standards that apply to Board decisions:

Where the employee invokes binding arbitration, see 5 U.S.C. § 7121(b)(1)(C)(iii), the arbitrator is to apply the same substantive standards that the Board would apply if the matter had been appealed. Marshall v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 5, ¶ 13 (2008). The purpose behind this rule is to "promote consistency in resolution..., and to avoid forum shopping." Cornelius v. Nutt, 472 U.S. 648, 661 & n.16 (1985) (citations omitted).

See Agbaniyaka v. Dept. of Treasury, 115 MSPR 130, 2010 MSPB 211 (2010) (reviewing award for alleged civil service law errors as well and considering whether the appellant established discrimination or reprisal for protected EEO activity); Gabriel v. Dept. of Labor, 108 MSPR 186, 189 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 39 (2008) (arbitrator should have considered as an adverse action a claim by the grievant that her separation by RIF was the result of reasons personal to her); Pinegar v. FEC, 105 MSPR 677, 688 ¶ 21, 2007 MSPB 140 (2007) ("Thus, the arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, for example, by misallocating the burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework."); Pleasant v. DHUD, 98 MSPR 602 (2005) ("The scope of the Board's review of an arbitrator's award is limited; such awards are entitled to a greater degree of deference than initial decisions issued by the Board's administrative judges. De Bow, 97 M.S.P.R. 5 ¶ 5; Weaver, 94 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶ 8. Thus, the Board will modify or set aside an arbitration decision only where the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation. Id. Absent legal error, the Board cannot substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator, even if it would disagree with the arbitrator's decision. Id."); Holly v. DHHS, 92 MSPR 601, 604 ¶ 5 (2002) (restating deference rule); Cloutterbuck v. Dept. of Labor, 85 MSPR 684, 686-87 ¶ 5 (2000) (restating deference rule); cf. Slavich v. SSA, 102 MSPR 171, 175 ¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 145 (2006) (the Board deferred to the arbitrator's application of a harmful error argument made in a performance case relative to the agency's compliance with a negotiated agreement during the development of performance standards). But see FitzGerald v. DHS, 107 MSPR 666, 674 ¶ 16, 2008 MSPB 17 (2008) (Board, Chairman McPhie dissenting, declined to follow factual findings of an arbitrator when the Board concluded that the analysis constituted a misinterpretation of civil service law). The Board is deferential to the arbitrator's contract construction, Pinegar v. FEC, 105 MSPR 677, 697 ¶¶ 47-48, 2007 MSPB 140 (2007):

The Board generally defers to arbitrators on issues involving the interpretation of a collective bargaining agreement. Slavich v. Social Security Administration, 102 M.S.P.R. 171, ¶ 8 (2006); Hayes v. Department of Labor, 65 M.S.P.R. 214, 218 (1994). It is well established that an arbitrator is uniquely qualified to interpret a collective bargaining agreement and that it is the source of his authority and his task to effectuate the intent of the parties through the interpretation and application of that agreement. Pleasant, 98 M.S.P.R. 602, ¶ 17; Luna, 85 M.S.P.R. 301, ¶ 14. The harmful error standard applies to an alleged violation of a collective bargaining agreement. Slavich, 102 M.S.P.R. 171, ¶ 8; Pleasant, 98 M.S.P.R. 602, ¶ 8. Reversal of an action for harmful error is warranted where the procedural error likely had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency. Rawls v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 23 (2003); Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 681, 685 (1991). In order to show harmful error, the appellant must prove that any procedural error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Rawls, 94 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 23; Stephen, 47 M.S.P.R. at 685.

Here, the arbitrator correctly held the appellant to a harmful error standard by requiring him to show that he was prejudiced by the agency's purported violations of the collective bargaining agreement and, as noted above, he determined that the appellant failed to show how he was harmed by the agency's alleged procedural errors. In his request for review, the appellant disputes this finding, asserting that the agency's delay had a negative impact on his professional development and personal well-being. These assertions, even if true, do not show that the agency would likely have reached a different result had it made its decision sooner. The appellant further asserts that one allegedly key witness resigned or retired shortly before the arbitration hearing and was not available to testify. It appears from the appellant's pleadings, however, that this potential witness did not leave the agency's employ until after the appellant was removed. Moreover, from the appellant's description of the witness's minimal involvement in his removal, any connection that the witness may have had to this case is tenuous at best. We see no basis on this record to conclude that the arbitrator's finding that the appellant failed to prove harmful error is incorrect as a matter of civil service law, rule, or regulation.

Again stating its deference to arbitrators' constructions of labor contracts, Huynh v. SSA, CB-7121-14-0023-V-1 (NP 1/22/2015), observed:

...An arbitrator is uniquely qualified to interpret a CBA, which is the source of his authority. Fanelli v. Department of Agriculture, 109 M.S.P.R. 115, ¶ 6 (2008). An arbitration award that "draws its essence" from the CBA is entitled to deference, and the Board will vacate it only when it manifests an infidelity to this obligation. Id., ¶ 10. An arbitration award is deficient as to failing to draw its essence from the CBA if the award: (1) cannot in any rational way be derived from the agreement; (2) is so unfounded in reason and fact and so unconnected with the wording and purposes of the CBA as to manifest an infidelity to the obligation of the arbitrator; (3) does not represent a plausible interpretation of the agreement; or (4) evidences a manifest disregard of the agreement. Id., ¶ 11.

Interestingly, the Federal Circuit seems to give little if any deference to an arbitrator's construction of a collective bargaining agreement. The court views the agreement as a contract that the court can construe by itself. See Miskill v. SSA, 863 F.3d 1379, 1383 (Fed. Cir. 2017) ("'Interpretation of a collective-bargaining agreement is a question of law we review de novo.' Garcia v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., 780 F.3d 1145, 1147 (Fed. Cir. 2015). The Court 'begin[s] with the plain language of the agreement' and then 'give[s] the words in the agreement their ordinary meaning unless the parties mutually intended and agreed to an alternative meaning.' Id. (citing Harris v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 142 F.3d 1463, 1467 (Fed. Cir. 1998))."). Some might say the Federal Circuit takes the wrong approach. A better approach was stated in United Steelworkers v. Enterprise Wheel & Car. Corp, 363 U.S. 593, 597, 80 S. Ct. 1358 (1960):

When an arbitrator is commissioned to interpret and apply the collective bargaining agreement, he is to bring his informed judgment to bear in order to reach a fair solution of a problem. This is especially true when it comes to formulating remedies. There, the need is for flexibility in meeting a wide variety of situations. The draftsmen may never have thought of what specific remedy should be awarded to meet a particular contingency. Nevertheless, an arbitrator is confined to interpretation and application of the collective bargaining agreement; he does not sit to dispense his own brand of industrial justice. He may, of course, look for guidance from many sources, yet his award is legitimate only so long as it draws its essence from the collective bargaining agreement. When the arbitrator's words manifest an infidelity to this obligation, courts have no choice but to refuse enforcement of the award.

[Refer to Chapter 17, "Interpretation of Collective Bargaining Contract."]

Review of arbitration awards proceeds as if the Board were reviewing the decision of its AJ. Provisions of a collective bargaining contract will be treated as though they were agency regulations. Appling v. SSA, 30 MSPR 375, 381 (1986). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Collective Bargaining Contract Violation."] Of the deference due an arbitrator's interpretation of a negotiated agreement provision, the Board explained in a case involving a contract provision that arguably limited the agency to the use of Chapter 43 procedures for performance issues (not the view drawn by the arbitrator), Sadiq v. VA, 119 MSPR 450, 454 ¶ 7, 2013 MSPB 36 (2013) (following remand to consider allegations of discrimination, Sadiq v. VA, CB-7121-12-0004-V-1 (NP 3/13/2015)):

As set forth above, the Board's standard of review of an arbitrator's award is narrow; such awards are entitled to a greater degree of deference than initial decisions issued by the Board's administrative judges. Fanelli v. Department of Agriculture, 109 M.S.P.R. 115, ¶ 6 (2008). The Board will modify or set aside an arbitration decision only when the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation, and absent such legal error, the Board cannot substitute its conclusions for those of the arbitrator, even if it would disagree with the arbitrator's decision. Id. An arbitrator is uniquely qualified to interpret a CBA, which is the source of the arbitrator's authority. Id. Thus, an arbitration award that "draws its essence" from the CBA is entitled to deference, and should only be vacated when it manifests an infidelity to this obligation. Id., ¶ 10. In making this determination, any doubts concerning the merits of the arbitrator's decision must be resolved in favor of the decision. Id. An arbitration award is deficient as failing to draw its essence from the CBA when the award: (1) Cannot in any rational way be derived from the CBA; (2) is so unfounded in reason and fact and so unconnected with the wording and purposes of the CBA as to manifest an infidelity to the obligation of the arbitrator; (3) does not represent a plausible interpretation of the agreement; or (4) evidences a manifest disregard of the agreement. Id., ¶¶ 10-11.

A arbitrator's procedural ruling imposing an evidentiary sanction does not rise to the level of an interpretation of a civil service law, rule, or regulation, Karlik v. SSA, CB-7121-11-0008-V-1 (NP 10/20/2011):

The appellant asserts in his request for review that the arbitrator abused his discretion when he excluded the testimony of Cathy Young, the appellant's former supervisor, as a sanction against the appellant's former attorney for her ex parte contact with Ms. Young. As the Board's review of an arbitrator's award is limited, the appellant has failed to show that the arbitrator erred as matter of law in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation in his decision to exclude Ms. Young's testimony as a sanction for his former attorney's contact with Ms. Young. See FitzGerald v. Department of Homeland Security, 107 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 9 (2008) (the Board will modify or set aside such an award only when the arbitrator has erred as matter of law in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation). Further, given that the arbitrator clearly credited the testimony of Cynthia Romania, Jill Behrens, and Fred Bourjaily regarding various supervisors' observations of the appellant's mistakes and errors, it is not clear that Ms. Young's anticipated testimony regarding the appellant's work ethic, his interactions with his coworkers, and the assistive techniques she provided to him would have changed the outcome of the arbitrator's decision.

The arbitrator conducts a de novo hearing in a case otherwise within the Board's jurisdiction and may receive any evidence obtained up to the time of the hearing. See Brook v. Corrado, 999 F.2d 523 (Fed. Cir. 1993). The arbitrator is expected to apply law developed by the Board under the Reform Act in adverse action cases. See Cornelius v. Nutt, 105 S. Ct. 2882 (1985). Board decisions involving analysis of charges may support analysis by an arbitrator. See Oates v. Dept. of Labor, 105 MSPR 10, 15 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 46 (2007) ("even if the arbitrator's rationale for sustaining some specifications and not others was inconsistent, the arbitrator did not violate civil service law. Where more than one event or factual specification supports a single charge, proof of one or more, but not all, of the supporting specifications is sufficient to sustain the charge"). But see FitzGerald v. DHS, 107 MSPR 666, 674 ¶¶ 16, 2008 MSPB 17 (2008) (when the arbitrator's award was arguably at odds with a Board decision interpreting the requirements of an application form requesting information on academic accomplishments, the award was set aside as contrary to civil service law; also reexamining issue of EEO reprisal, and finding reprisal, when the arbitrator failed to properly articulate the legal analytical standards and the facts warranted a reprisal finding) (Chairman McPhie dissented, arguing that the Board should not employ a "legal error" analysis to set aside what were factual findings of an arbitrator).

The Board may rule on any challenges to the award that properly raise issues of discrimination. Marenus v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 498, 504 (1989) (issue of disability discrimination). If a discrimination allegation is disputed, it appears sufficient that the arbitrator discuss the evidence concerning the disabling condition and arrive at conclusions consistent with the evidence. The arbitrator is not required to refer specifically to a disability discrimination allegation, that is, it appears that the arbitrator may treat the evidence without specifically identifying defenses or claims. See Fryc v. VA, 38 MSPR 360, 362 n.2 (1988). Under Hairston v. Dept. of Labor, 45 MSPR 415, 418 (1990), the Board applies traditional EEO burdens of proof to determine whether an arbitrator erred in his analysis of discrimination allegations. Although an arbitrator does not cite applicable cases, his decision will receive deference from the Board if it properly applies civil service law and contains the appropriate "implicit findings" on EEO issues presented by the appellant. Bardill v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 599, 602 (1989). If the appellant demonstrates that the arbitrator erred in his or her analysis, the appellant should demonstrate that the error prejudiced her substantive rights. See Weaver v. SSA, 94 MSPR 447, 453-54 ¶ 17 (2003).

Cockrell v. Dept. of Air Force, 58 MSPR 211, 216 (1993), rejected a challenge to an award but explained the Board's mode of analysis:

...First, the arbitrator's failure to mention all of the evidence of record does not necessarily mean that he did not consider it in reaching his decision....

Second, where, as here, the agency has established a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the appellant's removal by meeting its evidentiary burden on the merits, the issue of discrimination may be resolved by determining whether the appellant has shown that the agency's asserted reason for the action was merely a pretext for discrimination.... The employee may meet this burden by showing that the discriminatory reason, more likely than not, motivated the employer, or that the employer's explanation was unworthy of credence.

In Cockrell, supra the Board noted that because an arbitrator must apply the same standards as the Board, the arbitrator had no authority to mitigate a removal action taken under 5 USC Chapter 43 for unacceptable performance. Sands v. Dept. of Labor, 88 MSPR 281, 286 ¶ 8 (2001), reviewed an award, ultimately conducting a legal analysis of the facts and sustaining the result reached by the arbitrator, although faulting the arbitrator for using an improper standard of proof:

Here, the arbitrator applied the substantial evidence standard of proof in analyzing the agency's charges. This was legal error because the proper standard of proof in an action brought under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75 is the preponderant evidence standard, a higher standard of proof than the substantial evidence standard. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.56(a),(C) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b), 1201.4(p)-(s)]; Bennett, 79 MSPR at 295. We therefore will not defer to the arbitrator's findings and conclusions with regard to the merits of the charges in this case. See Bennett, 79 MSPR at 295. Because the record is fully developed and credibility determinations are not necessary for us to make factual findings and conclusions, we will examine the record evidence and submissions on review to determine whether applying the preponderance of the evidence standard to the merits of the charges renders the award contrary to civil service law, rule, or regulation. See Appling v. Social Security Administration, 30 MSPR 375, 379 (1986) (when the Board is unable to give deference to an arbitrator's findings and conclusions on certain issues, resolution of those issues by the Board may be preferable to remanding the case to the arbitrator).

An example of application of Board substantive law to arbitrators' awards is Hayes v. Dept. of Labor, 65 MSPR 214, 217-19 (1994), correcting an error in civil service law (that the agency could charge an employee with making a false statement in defending against a charge of misconduct, a legal position that was subsequently overruled), but sustaining an award challenged on other grounds:

The Board's scope of review of an arbitration decision is limited. The Board will not provide de novo review of arbitration awards. The arbitration decision is entitled to deference, and will be modified or set aside only if the appellant establishes error in the arbitrator's interpretation of law, rule, or regulation. See Hilton-Boy v. Department of Health and Human Services, 56 MSPR 176, 178 (1992). The arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis, e.g., by misallocating burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework. See Lisboa v. Dept. of the Air Force, 46 MSPR 6, 8 (1990).

...

The arbitrator is uniquely qualified to interpret the collective bargaining agreement. His source of authority is the collective bargaining agreement and his task is to effectuate the intent of the parties through the interpretation and application of that agreement. In this case, there was a dispute between the parties over the intent of the contract provision providing for the Step 2 official. The arbitrator decided this dispute after hearing all of the evidence. The Board defers to the decision of the arbitrator on this issue. See generally Cornelius v. Nutt, 472 U.S. 648, 663, 105 S. Ct. 2882, 2891 (1985); Robinson v. Department of Health and Human Services, 30 MSPR 389, reconsideration denied, 31 MSPR 479 (1986).

The appellant's assertion that the arbitrator failed to address instances in which management witnesses were impeached is a challenge to the arbitrator's factual findings underlying his credibility determinations. The arbitrator's failure to mention all of the evidence of record does not mean that he did not consider it in reaching his decision. See Marques v. Department of Health and Human Services, 22 MSPR 129, 132 (1984), aff'd, 776 F. 2d 1062 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table), cert. denied, 476 U.S. 1141, 106 S. Ct. 2247 (1986). The Board therefore defers to the arbitrator's factual findings regarding the credibility of the agency's witnesses.

An arbitrator's finding that an appellant did not prove his discrimination claim is also a factual determination entitled to deference unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis. Moore v. Dept. of Commerce, 55 MSPR 451 (1992). The appellant alleged that an Assistant Secretary of Labor held a personal vendetta against him because he is a black male. The arbitrator rejected the appellant's assertion that an Assistant Secretary "poisoned" the grievance process and implemented his own racist preference against the appellant because he found that the Assistant Secretary was not involved in the decision to remove the appellant. The arbitrator found that it was the undisputed testimony of the deciding official, a subordinate of the Assistant Secretary of Labor, that she made the decision without advice or instruction from her superior. Thus, the appellant's contention that the arbitrator erred with regard to his finding of no discrimination is at best mere disagreement with the arbitrator's credibility determinations and other findings of fact. These findings are entitled to Board deference.

Benson v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 548, 554-55 (1994), explained the limitations the Board imposes on itself when it reviews the form and substance of arbitrators' factfinding:

Arbitration is a "bargained-for 'part of...self-government created by and confined to the parties.'" Devine v. White, 697 F.2d 421, 432 (D.C. Cir. 1983) [footnote omitted]. It plays a "unique role in labor-management relations." Robinson, 30 MSPR at 395. Accordingly, arbitration awards are entitled to a greater degree of deference than initial decisions issued by the Board's administrative judges. See Bean v. EEOC, 55 MSPR 609, 612 (1992). The Board will modify or set aside such a decision only where the arbitrator has erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rules, or regulation. Id. Even if the Board would disagree with the arbitrator after reviewing the facts of record, it lacks the authority to second-guess the arbitrator and to substitute its conclusions for the specific conclusions in the arbitrators award absent legal error.

Here, with regard to Ponce, the arbitrator specifically found that he was inciting a riot when he attempted to assist his friend, Benson, who was being confronted by a Master at Arms about a possible parking violation. This finding directly addresses the charge that Ponce incited a riot, and it does not conflict with a law, rule, or regulation. It is entitled to our deference.

...

Although the arbitrator did not specifically address the other charges against the appellants, this omission does not mean that his decision is contrary to law, rule, or regulation. An arbitrator need not make explicit findings, or give his reasons for an award. See Girani v. Federal Aviation Administration, 924 F.2d 237, 242 (Fed. Cir. 1991). Moreover, any ambiguity in his award is not necessarily reason to set it aside. Id. In any event, the arbitrator did explicitly find that the agency's actions were supported by a preponderance of the evidence, and that the appellants' removals were reasonable under the circumstances. We defer to these findings.

See Lee v. Dept. of Labor, 110 MSPR 355, 360 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 252 (2008) ("These are specific findings, not in conflict with the Board's substantive law, and are therefore entitled to our deference. See Benson v. Department of the Navy, 65 M.S.P.R. 548, 554-55 (1994) (deferring to arbitrator's specific findings of fact).")

An example of an unsuccessful attempt to secure what amounted to reversal of an arbitrator's analysis of a point of law is Dobruck v. VA, 102 MSPR 578, 585 ¶ 13-15, 2006 MSPB 213 (2006):

The appellant claims that he was denied due process because the deciding official, in reaching the decision on his proposed removal, improperly considered allegations that the appellant had inflated his production numbers by making erroneous entries in a computer data system used to monitor production. The appellant asserts that there were improper ex parte communications between agency managers and the deciding official concerning these allegations, to which he was unable to respond. Id. He also asserts that the agency rescinded the proposed admonishment based on these allegations.

The arbitrator found, however, that the deciding official relied solely on the data regarding the appellant's performance in making the decision to remove him. The appellant has failed to show that the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in reaching this conclusion. See Weaver, 94 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶ 13 ("The arbitrator's factual determinations are entitled to deference unless he erred in his legal analysis, e.g., by misallocating burdens of proof or employing the wrong analytical framework."); Holly v. Department of Health & Human Services, 92 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 10 (2002) ("An appellant seeking review of an arbitration decision has the burden of providing the Board with materials necessary to support matters raised on review, including a transcript or tape recording of the arbitration hearing."), dismissed, 69 Fed. Appx. 467 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Higgs v. Social Security Administration, 71 M.S.P.R. 48, 51-52 (1996) (employee's unsupported assertions that arbitrator erred in her fact findings were insufficient to show that the arbitrator's findings were in conflict with Board substantive law, and thus did not provide a basis for setting aside or modifying the arbitrator's award where the record did not contain the hearing transcript or exhibits).

Because the arbitrator found that the deciding official's decision to remove the appellant was based solely on his performance record, we cannot conclude that the alleged ex parte communications were "so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that no employee can fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances." See Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Gerges v. Department of the Navy, 89 M.S.P.R. 669, ¶¶ 23, 26-27 (2001), aff'd, 52 Fed. Appx. 513 (Fed. Cir. 2002).

General challenges to findings by an arbitrator will not likely secure alteration in the result, Cirella v. Dept. of Treasury, 108 MSPR 474, 480-81 ¶¶ 15-16, 2008 MSPB 79 (2008):

Before the Board will undertake a complete review of the record, the petitioning party must explain why the challenged factual determination is incorrect and identify the specific evidence in the record that demonstrates the error. Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 M.S.P.R. 129, 133 (1980), review denied, 669 F.2d 613 (9th Cir. 1982) (per curiam). A petition must contain sufficient specificity to enable the Board to ascertain whether there is a serious evidentiary challenge justifying a complete review of the record. Tines v. Department of the Air Force, 56 M.S.P.R. 90, 92 (1992). Mere disagreement with the arbitrator's factual findings and credibility determinations does not warrant full review of the record by the Board. See, e.g., Weaver, 2 M.S.P.R. at 133-34.

Here, the appellant has simply made vague allegations, unsupported by specific references to the record. Moreover, in sustaining the specifications, the arbitrator found that Jarnagin's, Wright's, and Abreu's testimony was more credible than that of the appellant and explained his reasons for his findings. The appellant's mere disagreement with the arbitrator's explained factual findings and legal conclusions does not show legal error. See, e.g., Dobruck, 102 M.S.P.R. 578, ¶ 14; Holly v. Department of Health & Human 7 Services, 92 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 11 (2002), dismissed, 69 Fed. Appx. 467 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Weaver, 2 M.S.P.R. at 133-34.

See Jones v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-15-0011-V-1 (NP 4/16/2015) ("The appellant also generally alleges that the arbitrator improperly gave more weight to the agency's opinions and speculations and 'ruled only on opinion.' We construe such claims as challenges by the appellant to the arbitrator's credibility determinations, factual findings, and legal conclusions. However, the appellant's mere disagreement with the arbitrator does not show legal error. See Cirella v. Department of the Treasury, 108 M.S.P.R. 474, ¶¶ 15-16, aff'd, 296 F. App'x 63 (Fed. Cir. 2008)."); Young v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 326, 330 ¶ 5 (2003) ("the Board will grant deference to the arbitrator's finding of intent unless the arbitrator erred in his legal analysis"); Luna v. SSA, 85 MSPR 301, 307 ¶ 14 (2000) (same as Armstrong and Hayes), followed, Cloutterbuck v. Dept. of Labor, 85 MSPR 684, 688 ¶ 9 (2000); Kendricks v. VA, 79 MSPR 510, 518 ¶ 19 (1998) (arbitrator's "failure to mention all of the evidence alone will not afford a reason to disturb the decision below"); Hunter v. Dept. of Air Force, 77 MSPR 589, 594 (1998) (arbitrator need not make explicit findings in an arbitration decision); Armstrong v. ITC, 74 MSPR 349, 355 (1997) ("[T]he arbitrator is uniquely qualified to interpret the collective bargaining agreement."); Colon v. VA, 73 MSPR 659, 664 (1997) ("An arbitrator's decision is not contrary to law, rule, or regulation...simply because he does not discuss all of the evidence."); cf. Cirella v. Dept. of Treasury, 108 MSPR 474, 480 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 79 (2008) ("The appellant's statements do not provide a basis for Board review because they do not identify specific errors in the arbitrator's decision.").

The Board defers to the credibility determinations of the arbitrator, "who stands in the best position to make credibility determinations after being present to hear the testimony and judge the demeanor of the witnesses." De Bow v. Dept. of Air Force, 97 MSPR 5, 9 ¶ 6 (2004). Benson, supra, suggests that arbitrators' awards may include little by way of findings and still pass muster. The exact format of the arbitrator's decision has not been dictated by the Board, but a decision was found sufficient in Armijo v. VA, 40 MSPR 694, 698 n.2 (1989), despite absence of references in the award to a hearing transcript. The Board noted that the decision identified all material issues of fact and law, summarized the evidence, resolved credibility issues, and included the arbitrator's conclusions of law, legal reasoning and the authorities supporting that reasoning.
