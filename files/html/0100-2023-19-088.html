b. Postal Service Collective Bargaining Contracts

The Postal Service labor contracts include provisions relating to light and limited duty, so that a disability discrimination case involving the USPS will often include discussion of both the law developed under the Rehabilitation Act and the requirements of the negotiated agreement. Addressing conflicts between the contract and the statute, Thibeault v. MSPB, 611 Fed. Appx. 975, 979 (Fed. Cir. 2015), observed:

While there were part-time positions available during this time period, the AJ relied upon unrebutted evidence to find that it would violate the CBA to assign Thibeault to such a position. Although an agency can override a CBA if special circumstances exist, it does not appear Thibeault made such a showing. See U.S. Airways, Inc. v. Barnett, 535 U.S. 391, 405-06 (2002) ("[t]he plaintiff must bear the burden of showing special circumstances that make an exception from the seniority system reasonable in the particular case.")....

The structure of the Postal Service contract (or one of them) as it stood in 1981 was described, Harrison v. USPS, 8 MSPR 359, 361 (1981):

[Appellant] further contends that the Pasadena post office is in violation of the National Agreement, between the U.S. Postal Service and the recognized unions representing that agency's employees,[1] by refusing to make available light-duty positions to ill and injured workers. The Board notes that Article XIII, Section C of the National Agreement provides that permanent and temporary light-duty assignments will be established within each craft through local negotiations with the agency. There is no evidence in the record that any such permanent assignments had been negotiated at the local level in this case and agency witnesses and documents attested that no permanent light-duty assignments existed at the Pasadena post office. Further, while the National Agreement provides that the agency give each request for light duty careful attention and make every effort to reassign an employee within his or her present craft or occupational group, we do not believe that the provisions mandate that the agency reassign or create a permanent light-duty position for appellant in his craft where the agency has demonstrated that it has insufficient work available to warrant the establishment of such a permanent light-duty assignment....

[1] Agreement between United States Postal Service and American Postal Workers Union, AFL-CIO; National Association of Letter Carriers, AFL-CIO; National Post Office Mail Handlers, Watchmen, Messengers and Group Leaders Division of the Laborers' International Union of North America, AFL-CIO; July 21, 1978--July 20, 1981.

Schoener v. USPS, 9 MSPR 239, 242 (1981), added:

...Inasmuch as the National Agreement reserves the issue of light-duty positions to local negotiation depending upon the requirements of each individual post office, evidence of the procedures of one facility is clearly irrelevant to those of another. Further, while the National Agreement provides that the agency give each request for light-duty careful attention and make every effort to reassign an employee within his or her present craft or occupational group, we do not believe that the provisions mandate that the agency reassign or create a permanent light-duty position for appellant in his craft where the agency has demonstrated that it has insufficient work available to warrant the establishment of such a permanent light-duty assignment. See Stalkfleet v. USPS, 6 MSPB 536, 6 MSPR 637 (1981).

Johnson v. USPS, 27 MSPR 426, 428 (1985), distinguished light duty and limited duty under the then-extant Postal Service national agreement:

With respect to appellant's allegation of handicap discrimination, the presiding official found that appellant sustained his injuries at work and, therefore, was entitled to consideration for limited duty under the provisions of the National Agreement. The National Agreement requires the agency to show the "greatest consideration" for employees entitled to such duty. Appellant's tour superintendent testified that there are many employees on limited or light duty and employees entitled to limited duty are reassigned in almost every case. The agency, however, has failed to adequately explain why such assignment was not made available to appellant.[2] Further, the agency has essentially conceded that it could have made such an assignment available since it in fact did so after OWCP accepted his claim for workers compensation benefits. Although the agency allowed appellant to return to work the case was not rendered moot since the agency did not provide appellant back pay and benefits. We, therefore, find that the agency failed to reassign appellant as required under the National Agreement.

[2] At the hearing, the agency distinguished between light duty and limited duty. Employees injured at work were entitled to limited duty while employees injured off the job were entitled to light duty. Employees entitled to limited duty had priority in reassignments. Appellant was assigned limited duty pending a claim determination by the Office of Workers Compensation Program (OWCP). When OWCP denied his claim the agency converted appellant from limited to light duty and instituted the present action. OWCP, however, changed its decision and the presiding official found that appellant's injury occurred at work. Thus, it appears that the agency failed to afford appellant priority for reassignment because he was incorrectly considered in the wrong category of injured employees.

See Anthony v. OPM, 58 F.3d 620 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (discussing National Agreement's provisions concerning light duty assignments); Perry v. USPS, 46 MSPR 456, 458 n.1 (1990) (distinguishing "light duty" under the contract for employees whose medical condition is not compensable and "limited duty" for employees following return from a compensable injury); Zackery v. USPS, 15 MSPR 265, 267-68 (1983) ("Because the National Agreement does not require a doctor's statement of recovery, expiration of light-duty status for failure to comply with agency recertification procedures is not an inconsistent provision."); Marsh v. USPS, 9 MSPR 548, 551 (1982) (remanding for consideration of the contract requirement of consideration of a light duty assignment); cf. Lunkin v. USPS, 20 MSPR 220, 223 (1984) (discussing a provision of a negotiated agreement requiring agency consideration of reassignment with appropriate medical justification; denial of reassignment consideration constituted harmful error requiring reversal of removal of a city carrier for not being physically fit for duty).

A request for light duty by a Postal Service employee suffering a physical problem constitutes articulation of an accommodation, for light duty is a term well recognized within the Postal Service. Nordquist v. USPS, 37 MSPR 244, 246 (1988). In Stalkfleet v. USPS, 6 MSPR 637, 645, 6 MSPB 536 (1981), the appellant was removed for failure to meet the physical requirements of his position. He alleged that the physical problems resulted from an injury received during military service. The appellant's argument that his national collective bargaining contract required a permanent light duty assignment was rejected. The case was remanded for development of additional information concerning the appellant's ability to perform his position as a city carrier with restructuring of portions of the job. See Underwood v. USPS, 9 MSPR 210, 213 (1981) (disability discrimination against a mailhandler who was limited in the length of time he could remain standing; other mailhandlers had been assigned to repair damaged mail, a function that allowed them to sit while working).

Gallagher v. USPS, 31 MSPR 53, 56-57 (1986), involved a part-time letter carrier who had a 50% disability of his arm. The appellant could perform his job with several limitations: he should not lift more than 50 pounds; he should not pull tubs from a truck without assistance; he should case mail with his right hand. The agency's medical officer determined that these restrictions would not significantly impact appellant's performance. The Board made two important points concerning job restructuring. First, although restructuring may cause the appellant to have a job structure different from that available to nondisabled employees, that difference does not equate to undue hardship; rather, it is within the spirit and provisions of the Rehabilitation Act. Second, in Gallagher the Board made it clear that the Rehabilitation Act requires appropriate job restructuring even if a collective bargaining contract would preclude the restructuring. The Act takes precedence over the contract. That light duty may be the subject of a contract does not lessen the agency's obligation under the Rehabilitation Act. Vaughn v. USPS, 33 MSPR 496, 501 (1987). But see Konieczko v. USPS, 47 MSPR 509, 514-15 (1991) (modifying earlier law and resolving that contracts may preclude as well as require some accommodation involving reassignment). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Light and Limited Duty; Medical Restriction," for light duty requirements under negotiated agreements covering postal employees.]
