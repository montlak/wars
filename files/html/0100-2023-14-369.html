broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 12: Details, Temporary Duty, Transfer and Reassignment, Section A: Selection Procedures, Subsection 01: Seniority; Volunteers

1. Seniority; Volunteers

In AFGE Local 695 and Dept. of Treasury, U.S. Mint, Denver, 3 FLRA 43, 45-47 (1980), a proposal was nonnegotiable that required management to select personnel for details on the basis of greatest seniority from volunteers and, lacking volunteers, to select employees for detail on the basis of lowest seniority. The proposal required rotation regardless of the status of jobs in progress. By disregarding management's needs to have particular work completed pursuant to schedules, the proposal violated management's right to assign work. Another proposal was nonnegotiable that set seniority as the sole criterion for reassignment, notwithstanding variations in employees' abilities to perform particular assignments. Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE and Dept. of Labor, 3 FLRA 290, 292-93 (1980); see AFGE Local 987 and Dept. of Air Force, Warner Robins Air Force Logistics Ctr., Robins AFB, 35 FLRA 265, 266-67, 272 (1990) (rejecting volunteer/seniority scheme; not reaching appropriate arrangement issue for lack of adequate record); AFGE Local 738 and Dept. of Army, Combined Arms Ctr., 33 FLRA 380, 381 (1988) (rejecting, for lack of adequate record, contention that proposal was arrangement). A proposal interfered with the right to assign work that required the agency to accept volunteers for details irrespective of whether they were qualified to perform the particular work required. AFGE Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals and SSA, Office of Field Operations, Baltimore, 17 FLRA 11, 12 (1985); see NAGE Local R1-109 and VAMC Newington, 53 FLRA 403, 409-11 (1997) (Proposals 1-3: reaching same result as to requirements that seniority control selection for detail when qualifications were at issue); Int'l Plate Printers Local 2 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 25 FLRA 113, 122 (1987) (concerning details of senior qualified employees to new equipment).

A union may negotiate that as to equally qualified employees available for a detail, management will select the more senior for higher-level duties and the least senior for details to lower-level duties, Laborers Int'l Union and VA, Nat'l Cemetery Office, 9 FLRA 703, 706-07 (1982):

The right to assign employees which is reserved to management...encompasses management's discretion to establish the qualifications necessary to perform the duties generally assigned to the position and to determine whether an employee meets those qualifications....

Similarly, management's right to assign work...encompasses discretion to establish the particular qualifications and skills needed to perform the work to be done, and to exercise judgment in determining whether a particular employee meets those qualifications. Thus, when management determines that only one employee possesses the requisite qualifications to do certain work, section 7106(a)(2)(B) reserves to management the right to assign the work to that particular employee. Wright-Patterson, 2 FLRA at 631. Where, however, in management's judgment, two or more employees are equally qualified and capable of performing the work, the selection of any one of those employees to perform the work would be consistent with management's exercise of discretion. Under such circumstances, the procedure by which employees previously judged by management to be equally qualified will be selected to perform the work is negotiable under 7106(b)(2)....

A negotiable proposal required the employer to normally request volunteers for formal details and required assignment of details on an equitable basis, NFFE Local 797 and Dept. of Navy, 29 FLRA 333, 336-38 (1987):

...Provision 3 is similar to Provision 1 found negotiable in National Federation of Federal Employees Local 1622 and Department of the Army, Headquarters, Vint Hill Farms Station, Warrenton, Virginia, 16 FLRA 578 (1982). Provision 1 in that case required that details to positions affording qualifying experience or to those of a higher grade "be rotated among qualified employees to the fullest extent feasible." The Authority noted that the provision did "not in any way limit management's discretion to establish the particular qualifications and skills needed to perform the work...or limit management's judgment in determining whether a particular employee meets those qualifications[.]" Rather, the provision "established a general, nonquantitative standard by which the Agency's exercise of its reserved authority to assign employees could be evaluated in a subsequent grievance." 16 FLRA at 579....

Similarly, in the present case, Provision 3 establishes a procedure by which the Agency would detail employees. The record indicates that the provision refers only to employees who are qualified to perform the duties and responsibilities of the detail and only applies in situations in which management determines that more than one employee possesses the requisite qualifications. Moreover, as the Union acknowledges, nothing in the provision restricts management's discretion to determine: (1) the qualifications necessary to perform the duties of the detail; (2) whether the volunteer has the necessary qualifications; and (3) the appropriate areas from which volunteers could be requested....

...

In holding Provision 3 to be within the duty to bargain we find the Agency's reliance on American Federation of Government Employees, AFL-CIO, Mint Council 157 and Department of the Treasury, Bureau of the Mint, 19 FLRA 640 (1985), and National Federation of Federal Employees Local 29 and Department of the Army, U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, 16 FLRA 75 (1984), to be misplaced. The provision found nonnegotiable in Bureau of the Mint required that noncompetitive temporary promotions were to be distributed or rotated in 30-day increments, to the extent practicable, among eligible employees in the particular work unit. The Authority held that because the provision required management to discontinue or reassign the work involved at the end of the 30-day period, regardless of whether it had been completed, it violated management's right to assign work.... The proposal found nonnegotiable in Army Corps of Engineers, Kansas City District required that selection for details was to be based on the employees' retention standing. The Authority held that the proposal's requirement that selections for reassignments were to be based on seniority divested management of its discretion to designate which particular employee would be reassigned....

In contrast, Provision 3 does not restrict management's discretion to determine the particular employee to be detailed nor does it limit the Agency to selecting one of the volunteers for the detail. Rather, Provision 3 is intended to establish the normal procedure for detailing employees and...if the volunteer procedure for assigning employees to details does not meet the needs of the Agency, the Agency is free to assign whomever it wishes to the detail.

A proposal was negotiable that an agency not impose grade limits for employees volunteering for details. The agency was not limited in its ability to restrict selection to particular grade levels or to decline to select any employee. IFPTE Local 4 and Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, 14 FLRA 52, 53 (1984). But a proposal that restricted the agency from detailing employees who held certain grade levels or positions or prior details was nonnegotiable. It precluded the agency from assigning work through determinations of duties to be assigned and persons to whom work would be assigned. NTEU Chap. 22 and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 29 FLRA 348, 349-50 (1987) (Proposal 1).

A negotiable proposal required that considerations of grade, date applied for transfer, length of service in a field office, and length of service with the agency be guidelines for lateral transfers. AFGE Int'l Council of Marshals Serv. Locals and DOJ, Marshals Serv., 4 FLRA 384, 389 (1980). The proposal did not violate the right of the agency to assign employees; rather, it was a negotiable procedure under § 7106(b)(2) that management officials would observe in exercising their authority to assign employees. The agency was not required to fill a vacancy by lateral transfer (as opposed to detail of a lower-grade employee, promotion, or new hire). The guidelines would only be used if the agency decided to fill a position through a voluntary lateral transfer. Even then the use of the guidelines would not require the agency to select a particular employee or any employee at all. Not negotiable was a proposal requiring an agency to honor an employee's request for voluntary transfer as long as that request did not conflict with the instructional requirements of the school system. It prevented the agency from deciding not to fill the position at all or to fill the position with an outside applicant. Ft. Knox Teachers Ass'n and Ft. Knox Dependent Sch., 19 FLRA 878, 881 (1985). Seniority was a permissible factor in selection of employees for loans from one position to another involving no change of duties, just a change of location. The proposal did not concern the assignment of employees or of work. Wright-Patterson was relied upon by the Authority. AFGE Local 916 and Tinker AFB, 7 FLRA 292, 298 (1981).

Temporary duty assignments are those requiring temporary performance by an employee of the duties of his position at a different duty station. FLRA determined to be negotiable a proposal that temporary duty be offered to qualified and available employees with requisite skills on the basis of seniority; if there were no volunteers, the proposal required temporary duty assignments to be rotated among qualified and available employees with requisite skills in inverse order of seniority. The proposal did not interfere with the right of management to assign work. The only issue was where the work would be performed, there being no change in the regular duties of the position. The proposal did not limit the agency decision as to when the duties would be performed, nor did it require that all employees be rotated through all the duties in their position descriptions. The proposal did not involve an assignment of employees to positions within the agency. Nonnegotiable proposals involved a change of position for the employee rather than a change in location of where the work would be performed. AFGE and Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB, 5 FLRA 83, 83-84 (1981).

Determined nonnegotiable in AFGE Council 119 and DHS C&IS, 72 FLRA 63, 67-68 (2021) (Member Abbott dissenting in part), was Proposal 3b, "Details for USCIS employees outside of USCIS must be negotiated with the Union prior to opening the detail for volunteers or assignment":

...The plain wording of Proposal 3b imposes a general bargaining obligation that requires the Agency to substantively bargain with the Union prior to announcing a detail that is outside the Agency. Also, the Union concedes that Proposal 3b solely concerns details that are outside the Agency. The Authority has previously held that agencies do not have a duty to bargain over proposals that concern conditions of employment outside the bargaining unit.[77] Therefore, because Proposal 3b requires the Agency to bargain over the conditions of employment for BUEs while they are outside the bargaining-unit, we find that Proposal 3b is outside the duty to bargain.[78]

[77] AFGE, Local 1827, 58 FLRA 344, 349 (2003) (Member Armendariz dissenting) (finding proposals outside the duty to bargain because "there is nothing in the proposals having a significant and material effect on the working conditions of unit employees while they are unit employees") (emphasis added).

[78] Chairman DuBester notes that the Union's response did not rebut the Agency's assertion that the plain wording of Proposal 3b would create a duty to bargain over the working conditions outside the bargaining unit. Therefore, he concurs that the Union has not demonstrated that Proposal 3b is negotiable.
