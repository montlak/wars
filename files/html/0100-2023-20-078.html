broida mspb Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices, Subchapter 03: Individual Right of Action, Section A: Statutory and Regulatory Requirements and Limitations, Subsection 02: Time for Initiating IRA Appeal With OSC and MSPB, Part d: Premature Appeal Following Resort to OSC
d. Premature Appeal Following Resort to OSC

If the IRA appellant first seeks redress with OSC, even as to an otherwise appealable action, the appellant must allow limited exhaustion of OSC remedies before filing an appeal directly with the Board, Hornby v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 246, 247-48 (1994):

Effective September 24, 1993, based on a charge of "Repeated acts of misconduct," the agency removed the appellant from his position of Freight Rate Specialist. On September 29, 1993, the appellant filed his appeal with the Board's Boston Regional Office. In his appeal form, the appellant indicated that, also on September 29, 1993, he had filed an appeal with the Office of Special Counsel (OSC). In her acknowledgment order, the administrative judge notified the appellant that the Board might dismiss his appeal if he had sought corrective action from the OSC and it had not terminated its investigation or if 120 days had not passed since he sought corrective action; accordingly, she ordered him to file evidence and argument to prove that the action was within the Board's jurisdiction. The appellant did not respond to the order, however.

In her initial decision, the administrative judge noted that, pursuant to 5 CFR § 1209.2(b)(2), an appellant who has been affected by an otherwise appealable action may seek corrective action with the OSC before appealing to the Board, and, if he does so and then chooses to appeal to the Board, he must file an appeal no later than 65 days after issuance of the OSC's notice terminating its investigation or, in the absence of such notification, at any time after the expiration of 120 days of filing with the OSC. 5 CFR §§ 1209.5(a) and (b). Since the OSC had not issued a letter terminating the investigation and 120 days had not elapsed, the administrative judge dismissed the appeal without prejudice to a subsequent refiling.

The appellant alleges that he mistakenly indicated that he had filed a complaint with the OSC. The agency essentially states that it has no objection to adjudication on the merits of the case, if the appellant shows that he has no complaint pending before the OSC.

We find that the administrative judge correctly dismissed the appeal on the basis of the appellant's claim that he had filed with the OSC. See 5 CFR § 1209.5(b). The record contains no evidence which would have shown that the appellant was mistaken, and it was appropriate, therefore, for the administrative judge to enable the appellant to pursue his remedies before the OSC. See generally Massimino v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 58 MSPR 318, 322-323 (1993).

We note that, even if the appellant filed an OSC complaint on September 29, 1993, and has not received a letter from the OSC terminating its investigation, the appellant could now pursue his appeal before the Board, since 120 days would have passed. 5 CFR § 1209.5(b). See, e.g., Weber v. Department of the Army, 45 MSPR 406, 410 (1990). In the interest of administrative efficiency and convenience, we therefore find that it is appropriate to remand this appeal.

The same result was reached in Hartfield v. DOD, 70 MSPR 20, 23 (1996):

We further find that the administrative judge correctly dismissed the appeal as premature. When an employee raises a claim of reprisal based on whistleblowing and the personnel action giving rise to the claim is an otherwise appealable action, she may elect to seek corrective action from the Special Counsel before appealing to the Board or file the appeal directly with the Board. See 5 CFR § 1209.5(b); Hornby v. Department of the Navy, 61 MSPR 246, 248 (1994); Douglass v. Department of Transportation, 60 MSPR 1, 3-4 (1993); Massimino v. VA, 58 MSPR 318, 322-23 (1993).

Here, the appellant's 30-day suspension is an otherwise appealable action. See 5 USC §§ 7512 and 7513(d). Thus, the appellant had the option of seeking corrective action from the Special Counsel regarding her suspension or appealing directly to the Board. See 5 CFR § 1209.5(b); Hornby, 61 MSPR at 247-48; Douglass, 60 MSPR at 4; Massimino, 58 MSPR at 322-23. Because, however, the appellant sought corrective action from the Special Counsel and failed to demonstrate below that she had exhausted Special Counsel proceedings, the administrative judge properly dismissed her appeal on this basis. See Hornby, 61 MSPR at 247-48; Douglass, 60 MSPR at 4.

Hornby, supra, reached a result that is followed in mixed case appeals involving affirmative defenses of EEO violations first raised in the agency EEO process. The usual rule in an appeal first pursued through the EEO process is that an appeal to the Board is premature if it is filed prior to either the issuance of the agency final decision on the EEO complaint or the passage of 120 days without a final agency decision following the filing of the EEO complaint. However, if the appeal in a mixed case is premature when filed, the Board will require its adjudication if, during the litigation over its prematurity, sufficient time passes that the claim matures. With an IRA case, if the Board appeal is filed without an OSC closeout and before the lapse of 120 days, the Board will adjudicate the appeal if the 120th day passes while the initially premature appeal is still before the Board. See Lopez v. Dept. of Interior, 94 MSPR 393, 396 ¶ 10 (2003); Schoenrogge v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 216, 219 (1997) (120 days passed from the appellant's requests to OSC for corrective action by the time the AJ issued the initial decision dismissing the appeal as premature); Ratliff v. GSA, 66 MSPR 394, 397 (1995) ("Because, however, 120 days have now elapsed since June 20, 1994, when the appellant indicated that he sought corrective action from the Special Counsel, we find that his IRA appeal is currently ripe and that remand is appropriate for a new jurisdictional determination on the basis of reprisal for whistleblowing."); Hartfield v. DOD, 70 MSPR 20, 23 (1996) ("We note that 120 days have now elapsed since...the appellant indicated that she sought corrective action from Office of Special Counsel. Therefore, we find that this appeal is currently ripe and that remand is appropriate for adjudication of the appeal."); Weber v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 605, 607-08 (1993) (remanding because of the passage of the 120th day while the case was on PFR; declining to consider, however, as a more direct means to jurisdiction, evidence that OSC issued a notice terminating its investigation--information that should have been presented initially to the regional office when the evidence was available before the date that the region dismissed the appeal). [Refer to Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases"; refer to Chapter 2, "Premature Appeals."]

In an unusual appeal, King v. Dept. of Air Force, 116 MSPR 423, 2011 MSPB 56 (2011), the appellant first filed a Board adverse action appeal (shortly before the action was effective) and then filed a whistleblower complaint with OSC, but the AJ dismissed the adverse action appeal because the information presented to the AJ mistakenly suggested that the OSC complaint was filed before the Board appeal. When the appellant initiated the IRA appeal, it was filed just after the permissible deadline and the AJ dismissed it as late with no possibility of waiving the brief delay. The Board straightened the facts out through further inquiries to the parties during the PFR process and remanded for adjudication of the first-filed adverse action appeal, while noting that the subsequently-filed IRA appeal was barred by the earlier election of the adverse action appeal.
