17. Agency Abuse of Discretion

The agency must establish that the appellant was AWOL and that it properly denied any request for leave that the appellant made. Ferguson v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 143, 144 (1990); see Chance v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 583, 588 (1983) ("It is not clear what more appellant could have done to gain approval of his sick leave request."); Mullen v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 34, 36 (1983) (agency that denies leave must establish the propriety of the denial); Hockman v. American Battle Monuments Comm'n, 12 MSPR 642, 645 (1982) (agency's burden of proof under 5 USC 7701(c)(1)(B) never shifts: since the appellant had submitted a valid reason, i.e., a family emergency, for the failure to obtain advance leave permission, it was incumbent on the agency to show that the reason offered by the appellant was not bona fide). If an AWOL case is determined by the reasonableness of an agency refusal to excuse an absence, the agency's burden of proof must be met by a preponderance of the evidence. McDonough v. USPS, 4 MSPR 382, 384, 4 MSPB 441 (1980); see In re Van Sciver, 1 MSPR 99, 99, 1 MSPB 94 (1979) (preponderance of evidence standard applies to the agency's burden of demonstrating that it acted reasonably in denying a leave request); Henderson v. USPS, 36 MSPR 11, 15 (1987) ("When a disciplinary action is based on a charge of AWOL because of denial of leave, the circumstances of the denial will be reviewed to determine if the denial of leave is reasonable."); cf. Willis v. DOD, 105 MSPR 466, 2007 MSPB 108 (2007) (remand for consideration of whether the agency improperly charged the appellant with a leave infraction, leading to a violation of a last chance agreement, or whether the agency should have granted the employee leave had it followed its policies under the circumstances applicable to the employee's early departure from the worksite); Amat v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 204, 206-07 (1982) (agency failed to abide by its regulations requiring that notice of illness should be considered a request for approved leave, that the employee should be carried on sick leave pending the submission of substantiating evidence, and that annual leave or LWOP is to be used if sick leave is not available).

An AWOL defense that an agency failed to properly apply leave procedures is not an affirmative defense in the nature of an allegation of harmful error in arriving at a decision; proper administration of leave procedures goes to the agency's responsibility to prove the elements of its case by a preponderance of the evidence. See Daywalt v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 478, 479 n.1 (1981) (AJ erred in treating as an issue of harmful error whether the agency erred by refusing to grant the appellant's leave request; because approval of the leave was properly addressed as part of the merits of the case, the harmful error rule did not apply). But see Elsayed v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-21-0114-I-2 (NP 9/29/2022) ("The appellant has not filed a copy of an agency policy requiring the Director to approve or disapprove an employee's request for LWOP. Even assuming the agency had such a policy, and to the extent the appellant claims that the agency committed harmful procedural error, he has not shown that the Director likely would have approved the appellant's request. See Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 681, 685 (1991) (explaining that the Board will reverse an action for harmful error if the appellant shows that the procedural error likely would have caused the agency to reach a different conclusion in the absence or cure of the error); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(r).").

Although an employee is required to submit a written application for sick leave within the time limits set by the agency, an agency must grant sick leave to an employee who has provided it with administratively acceptable evidence that she was incapacitated for the performance of her duties due to illness or injury, regardless of whether the employee has complied with applicable sick leave procedures. Riley v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 683, 689 (1992), followed in Allen v. Dept. of Army, 76 MSPR 564, 568 (1997) (AWOL charge cannot be sustained if the appellant submitted administratively acceptable evidence of incapacitation due to illness or injury; the Board accepted evidence from a clinical psychologist in evaluating the appellant's psychological condition). The Board evaluates the evidence submitted by the appellant to determine whether it should have been considered acceptable by the agency. See Amat v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 204, 207 (1982). An agency may not summarily deny a request for sick leave. The agency cannot remove an appellant if it improperly denied the individual sick leave while he was incapacitated for duty. Abblett v. Dept. of Transp., 23 MSPR 375, 379 (1984); see Billingsley v. USPS, 18 MSPR 666, 669 (1984) (when the agency's local instructions called for sick leave to be used when an employee is unable to perform his assigned duties, the agency acted unreasonably by imposing a requirement that the employee provide evidence of an inability to perform any duties in the Postal Service).

An agency may not deny sick leave to an employee and then remove him for AWOL if he qualifies for leave under 5 CFR 630.401 (2022) because he has received medical examination or treatment or otherwise qualifies for leave under the regulation. A reviewing court concluded that the Board disregarded a controlling section of the leave regulation. Wade v. Dept. of Navy, 829 F.2d 1106 (Fed. Cir. 1987). If an employee, fired for AWOL, presents proper documentary evidence of illness and provides an acceptable excuse for failure to provide notice of the illness in the allotted time, the agency action is tantamount to improper denial of sick leave. The AWOL charge is not supported by a preponderance of the evidence. Jackson v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 451, 454, 5 MSPB 456 (1981). The agency may not disregard medical evidence of impairment because of an irrational refusal to accept the diagnosis and prognosis of an attending physician. See Loots v. Dept. of Air Force, 42 MSPR 571, 576-77 (1989).

The agency may not refuse to grant an employee sick leave when the leave is justified and then charge the appellant with both insubordination (for refusing to stay at work) and AWOL (for departing from work) and expect to carry its case before the Board, Castellanos v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 315, 322-24 (1994):

Dr. Penick stated in his hearing testimony that it was his general practice to recommend that an employee return to work or request annual leave when there was no "medical emergency." He stated that a medical emergency, in his mind, occurred when someone needed medical treatment immediately, "not tomorrow or the next day or three hours from now." By his own testimony, Dr. Penick has shown that he does not understand when an employee might be incapacitated for duty due to illness and entitled to a reasonable grant of leave. Nothing in law, rule, or regulation suggests that a federal employee must remain at work unless in immediate need of medical intervention. If that were the case, then every employee who comes down with the flu, who needs to go home because of a bout of diarrhea, or who has a recurrent migraine headache and needs to lie down, would have to stay at his assigned duties until his medical condition deteriorated. This is not a reasonable policy. We therefore find that Ms. Dove did not act reasonably in ordering the appellant to stay at work despite his gastrointestinal complaints and that the agency's charge of insubordination cannot be sustained. See Harmon, 10 MSPR at 526.

Regarding the related charge of AWOL on May 24, 1990, when an agency's charge of AWOL is based on its denial of leave, the Board will review the circumstances to determine if the denial of leave was reasonable. See Haine v. Department of the Navy, 41 MSPR 462, 470 (1989); Murray v. Department of the Navy, 41 MSPR 260, 263 (1989).

This appeal does not involve a situation where an employee has been placed on sick leave restriction requiring formal justification for each instance of absence from work. It involves a situation where an employee tells a supervisor that he is too sick to work, has the leave to cover his absence, and then is thwarted by a local policy that is apparently designed to curb the use of sick leave, or even emergency annual leave in lieu of sick leave, and is supported by an agency physician who believes employees should return to work unless they require immediate medical treatment. As discussed above, we find that this policy and the resultant denial of leave on May 24, 1990, was unreasonable. We therefore conclude that the charge of AWOL on May 24, 1990, cannot be sustained. See Willis v. Department of the Army, 52 MSPR 9, 11-12 (1991).

It appears from the record that May 29, 1990, was the appellant's next scheduled work day after May 24, 1990, when he left work due to gastrointestinal problems. It is undisputed that the appellant timely telephoned Ms. Dove at the beginning of his shift on May 29, 1990, and informed her that he was going to be hospitalized. Further, it is undisputed that, on May 30, 1990, the appellant was hospitalized at a San Antonio VA hospital, a 3-hour drive away from the appellant's work site, for a psychiatric condition.

As the agency argues on petition for review, the appellant did not submit any medical records regarding this hospitalization or otherwise establish that he was incapacitated for work on May 29, 1990, the day before his hospitalization. Nevertheless, the appellant's psychiatric condition was apparently so serious that it required a 2-week hospital stay. Moreover, there is no record evidence that the appellant was on a leave restriction status that required him to document every unscheduled leave request. Under these circumstances, we find that Ms. Dove's denial of the appellant's request for leave on May 29, 1990, was unreasonable.

Annual leave is an entitlement, but the agency has the right to determine when it can be taken. The Board reviews an agency's denial of a request for annual leave. Dobert v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 148, 150 (1997). Sigler v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 103, 108 (1994), considered absence charged against an employee who requested emergency annual leave when, after receiving supervisory denial of the request, the employee used some additional time to secure higher-level review of the initial leave denial. The appellant was then operating under a settlement agreement stipulating that "[d]isputes between the employee and the first level supervisor as meeting the conditions of an emergency will be reviewed by a higher level supervisor in the chain of command for final determination." The Board declined to sustain the charge:

To some extent, the agreement is ambiguous, inasmuch as it does not specifically provide that the appellant's right of review by a higher level supervisor would be contemporaneous with any leave request. However, because of the unplanned nature of emergency leave, this appears to be the most reasonable construction, and we find that the agency's submissions do not show that a contrary meaning was intended. Consequently, we find that the agency was precluded from basing an adverse action upon the appellant's lateness, when that lateness was caused by her reasonable exercise of the review right granted her by the agreement. Therefore, this charge cannot be sustained.
