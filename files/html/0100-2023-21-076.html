17. Refusal to Undergo Diagnostic or Other Procedure

Konopka v. OPM, 36 MSPR 314, 320 (1988), explained that a disability retirement application will not be denied solely on the basis that the appellant reasonably declines to undergo risky diagnostic procedures. But the appellant's assessment must be reasonable, Bernard v. OPM, 37 MSPR 486, 490 (1988):

The appellant next argues that the administrative judge erred in finding that the use of orthotic devices or surgery would have helped the appellant's condition and that the appellant's failure to accept such treatment was a relevant factor for consideration. As the administrative judge found, when pain and discomfort can be relieved through medical treatment, such pain and discomfort is not necessarily disabling within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. § 8337(a). See Williams v. Office of Personnel Management, 10 MSPR 1, 2 (1982). An employee's failure to follow his physician's recommendations for treatment may be significant. See Baker v. Office of Personnel Management, 782 F.2d 993, 994 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Letter 831-78, Attachment 1, S10-3h (Documentation of a Medical Condition) (June 23, 1983) ("Disability retirement will not be approved when persistence of the medical condition that has caused the employee's service deficiency results from his/her failure or refusal to accept treatment that he/she would ordinarily be expected to accept in support of activities of daily life, unless acceptance of treatment conflicts with the employee's religious beliefs"). Here, the evidence supports the administrative judge's finding that the use of corrective orthotic devices would relieve or correct the appellant's condition. It is not unreasonable, as the appellant claims, to expect an employee to accept such therapeutic help. Consequently, it was not error for the administrative judge to consider the appellant's voluntary failure or refusal to take the available corrective or ameliorative noninvasive treatment, orthotic devices prescribed by his physician, as a factor in evaluating his disability retirement application. See Baker, 782 F.2d at 994.

Hillis v. OPM, 40 MSPR 592, 598 (1989), noted:

With regard to the appellant's refusal to undergo invasive surgery to attempt to correct her varicose veins, we note that we have recently stated in Konopka v. Office of Personnel Management, 36 MSPR 314, 320-21 (1988), that a refusal to undergo invasive diagnostic testing is not a basis for denial of disability benefits unless the refusal to undergo such procedures is unreasonable. We further stated that refusal to undergo such treatment was not unreasonable when the treatment conflicted with an applicant's religious beliefs or when the proposed treatment was dangerous and the prospects of success somewhat speculative. Id. Here, the appellant and her primary physician have both stated that her refusal to undergo the surgery was based upon her religious beliefs. Furthermore, Dr. Toth stated that the "surgery would be extensive and with no guarantee of success." Id. Under these circumstances, we find that the appellant's refusal to undergo surgery was not unreasonable and does not provide a basis upon which her application for a disability retirement annuity can properly be denied. See Konopka [supra] .

If the appellant declines to follow prudent medical measures, retirement may be denied, Baker v. OPM, 782 F.2d 993, 994-95 (Fed. Cir. 1986):

We do have authority to consider the legal question of whether OPM and MSPB correctly held that an applicant cannot be granted disability retirement if the applicant's condition continues because of her own refusal or failure to follow reasonable medical advice for curing or alleviating that condition. See Lindahl, supra, 105 S. Ct. at 1628, 776 F.2d at 277. In this instance, the OPM determined that Ms. Baker's obesity contributed significantly to her back problems, "noting that various doctors had suggested that her compliance with an exercise and weight reduction program could produce clinical improvement." After considering the record, the MSPB concluded that "there is general agreement that [petitioner's] back pain is exacerbated by her obesity and poor muscle tone. Despite having been repeatedly so advised, there is little indication that [petitioner] is complying with the suggested measures of weight reduction and regular exercise."

In taking account of this continued failure by Ms. Baker to help herself, OPM and MSPB did not misinterpret the retirement legislation or commit any legal error. That very interpretation has been formally adopted by OPM. Its Federal Personnel Manual Letter 831-78, (on Disability Retirement), attachment 1, S10-3h (Documentation of a Medical Condition) (June 23, 1983), expressly provides:

Disability retirement will not be approved when persistence of the medical condition that has caused the employee's service deficiency results from his/her failure or refusal to accept treatment that he/she would ordinarily be expected to accept in support of activities of daily life, unless acceptance of treatment conflicts with the employee's religious beliefs.

This construction is a reasonable and appropriate implementation of 5 U.S.C. Sec. 8337 ("Disability retirement") which declares that an employee "shall be considered to be disabled only if the employee is found by the Office of Personnel Management to be unable, because of disease or injury, to render useful and efficient service in the employee's position..." (emphasis added). Where, as here, an employee is unable to render useful and efficient service because that employee fails or refuses to follow or accept normal treatment, it is wholly proper to say that the employee's disability flows, not from the disease or injury itself (as the statute requires), but from the employee's voluntary failure or refusal to take the available corrective or ameliorative action. Congress evidently wanted the "disease or injury" to be the fount of the findings of disability, not the applicant's unreasonable refusal to accept proffered and reasonable medical or therapeutic help.

That type of reasonable regulation by the administering agency, entirely consistent with the text and purpose of the statute, is entitled to great deference and acceptance. See, e.g., Consumer Prod. Div., SCM Corp. v. Silver Reed America, 753 F.2d 1033, 1039 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Burlington Northern R.R. v. United States, 752 F.2d 627, 629 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Accordingly, we accept the Manual's rule on this point.
