a. Mootness

Events can overtake enforcement litigation; the dispute is settled by the passage of time alone, and the enforcement petition is dismissed as moot. Bowen v. USPS, 51 MSPR 620 (1991) (Table); see Dalton v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 429, 434 (1995) (by the time the compliance issue reached the Board, the appellant's "home leave status" had ended, the appellant lost no pay or benefits, and the issue of whether the status quo had been restored was moot); Osler v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 539, 542 (1994) ("Because the agency, albeit belatedly, demonstrated compliance with the settlement agreement, the appellant's petition for enforcement should have been dismissed as moot."); Reed v. Dept. of Army, 24 MSPR 88, 89 (1984) (enforcement petition was moot when agency complied by the time the Board adjudicated PFR); cf. Suggs v. VA, SF-0752-09-0734-C-2 (NP 2/11/2015) ("While we understand the appellant's frustration with the agency's delay in complying with the Board's final decision, once compliance is obtained, the compliance matter is moot. See Henry v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 108 M.S.P.R. 458, ¶ 25 (2008); Woodson v. Department of Agriculture, 94 M.S.P.R. 289, ¶ 8 (2003). We also note that the Board's regulations do not provide for out-of-pocket costs in a petition for enforcement."). If the Board issues an enforcement order and, on the petition of OPM the Federal Circuit reverses the Board's decision on the merits, the enforcement petition is mooted by the court's action. See Azdell v. OPM, 94 MSPR 40, 42-43 ¶ 5 (2003). If the appellant asserts the agency failed to comply with a provision in a settlement agreement that was designed to facilitate a disability retirement, the enforcement issue is moot should the appellant be granted the retirement during the pendency of the enforcement litigation. See Harlan v. USPS, 111 MSPR 645, 2009 MSPB 139 (2009).

When the Board is satisfied that the parties are satisfied that compliance is finally proceeding apace, the AJ issues an initial decision dismissing the enforcement petition as moot with PFR rights and a notice to the appellant that he may refile if additional problems develop. Burch v. USPS, 52 MSPR 26, 28 (1991). If an appellant protests about noncompliance and the Board then remands the case for further proceedings, the enforcement petition will be dismissed if, after further adjudication, the agency certifies compliance and the appellant does not contest the agency's certification. Borowski v. USDA, 53 MSPR 296 (1991) (Table). The enforcement proceeding is moot if, by the time the compliance issue reaches the PFR stage, the appellant has received all the relief he could have obtained in a successful compliance proceeding. Mascarenas v. DOD, 60 MSPR 320, 323-24 (1993). If enforcement is a moot issue--for example, if the appellant was removed subsequent to a compliance dispute over whether the status quo was restored after an earlier removal rescinded by the agency--the Board may decline to adjudicate the compliance issue should it find that it cannot grant effective relief. See White v. Int'l Boundary Water Comm'n, 59 MSPR 62, 65 (1993). An enforcement petition may be dismissed as moot if the agency shows good faith in resolving compliance issues and the appellant fails to respond to or challenge the agency's submissions. See Prioleau v. VA, 109 MSPR 509, 2008 MSPB 181 (2008). But see Miller v. Dept. of Navy, AT-3443-11-0083-X-1 n.3 (NP 8/15/2014) ("We reject the agency's argument that this case is now moot due to the appellant's retirement. The Board has held that an appeal may be dismissed as moot only when the employee has received all of the relief he could have received if the matter had been adjudicated and he had prevailed. Haskins v. Department of the Navy, 106 M.S.P.R. 616, 624 (2007). Therefore, the mere fact of the appellant's retirement does not cause this case to be moot."); cf. ABS Global, Inc. v. Cytonome/ST, LLC, 984 F.3d 1017 (Fed. Cir. 2021) (patent infringement: "This case presents an issue of mootness based on voluntary cessation. Our resolution of this issue is guided by the Supreme Court's framework in Already, LLC v. Nike, Inc., 568 U.S. 85 (2013)....On appeal, the Supreme Court explained that as a threshold matter, a case becomes moot 'when the issues presented are no longer "live" or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome.' Id. at 91 (quoting Murphy v. Hunt, 455 U.S. 478, 481 (1982) (per curiam)). But 'a defendant cannot automatically moot a case simply by ending its unlawful conduct once sued.' Id. (citing City of Mesquite v. Aladdin's Castle, Inc., 455 U.S. 283, 289 (1982)). Instead, 'a defendant claiming that its voluntary compliance moots a case bears the formidable burden of showing that it is absolutely clear the allegedly wrongful behavior could not reasonably be expected to recur.' Id. (quoting Friends of the Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Env't Servs. (TOC), Inc., 528 U.S. 167, 190 (2000)).").
