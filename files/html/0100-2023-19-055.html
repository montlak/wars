II. REHABILITATION ACT: DISABLING CONDITIONS

Many Board decisions analyze allegations of disability discrimination. Central to the discussion of disability cases are medical evidence of an incapacitating condition, linkage between the condition and the inability to perform job requirements, and means reasonably available to the employer and employee to accommodate the disability and permit the employee to perform essential requirements of the position.

Accommodation for disabled federal employees has long been required. See Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 281 n.32 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (noting the 1969 provisions of the Federal Personnel Manual: "Emphasis is on ability rather than disability and on rehabilitation efforts and present job readiness."). HIV infection was recognized as a disability in Bragdon v. Abbott, 118 S. Ct. 2196 (1998). But the Board's curative efforts in a disability case appear to be somewhat limited. In Rodriguez v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 279 (1994), the Board made an interesting observation: when it reverses an action for lack of appropriate accommodation, the status quo is restored by directing placement of the appellant to a position of the same grade, pay, status, and tenure. Left unexplained was why the Board failed to implement its plenary authority by directing restoration of Rodriguez with appropriate accommodation. [Refer to Chapter 16, "Reinstatement with Accommodation for Disability," concerning enforcement.]

Many disability cases arise in cases involving constructive adverse actions: employees appeal when they believe that a resignation, retirement, or downgrading was forced on them because agencies would not provide proper accommodation. [Refer to constructive adverse actions in Chapter 6; refer to Chapter 8 concerning employees who appeal leave-related adverse actions because agencies failed to accommodate disabilities.]

At the onset we offer that the terms "handicap" and "disability" discrimination are used interchangeably. The Board explained the evolution of the terminology, Wilson v. VA, 63 MSPR 63, 65-66 (1994):

At the outset, we wish to clarify our use of terminology herein. The Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as originally enacted, prohibited discrimination in employment against individuals with "handicaps" by, inter alia, federal agencies. See 29 USC 791 (1988 ed.). The protections afforded by the Rehabilitation Act are expressly incorporated into the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (CSRA), under which this appeal arises. 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(D); see also 5 USC 2301(b)(2). Hitherto, Board decisions that discussed claims of discrimination prohibited under 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(D) have used the term "handicap" discrimination. See, e.g., Mazzarella v. USPS, 56 MSPR 47, 50 (1992).

In 1992, Congress amended the Rehabilitation Act, replacing all references to "handicap" and similar terms with "disability" and similar terms. Pub. L. No. 102-569, 106 Stat. 4344. The committee that reported on the bill containing the amendments noted that, in the eyes of many advocates for the disabled, the term "handicap" has a "pejorative" connotation. See S. Rep. No. 357, 102d Cong., 2d Sess. 13-14, reprinted in 1992 USCCAN 3712, 3724-25. Congress intended to "update" the statutory language in amending the Rehabilitation Act, but there is no indication that it intended any definitional changes or changes in the Act's coverage by the amendments described above. Id.

Accordingly, we refer to the appellant's claim that the agency discriminated against him in violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(D) as a claim of disability discrimination. We note that the current version of the CSRA employs variations of the term "handicap." See, e.g., 5 USCA 2301(b)(2), 2302(b)(1)(D), 2302(d)(4) (West Supp. 1994). Also, the current regulations that implement the Rehabilitation Act continue to use variations of the term "handicap." 29 CFR 1614.203 (1993). Nevertheless, we shall employ the language for which Congress expressed its preference when it amended the Rehabilitation Act in 1992. By using terms such as "disability" in lieu of terms such as "handicap," we do not intend any change in meaning. Compare 29 CFR 1614.203(a)(1) (1993) with 29 CFR 1630.2(g) (1993) ("disability" under the regulations implementing the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, 42 USC 12101-12213 (Supp. III 1991), prohibiting private employers from discriminating against the disabled, and "handicap" under the regulations implementing the Rehabilitation Act, are defined in substantively identical terms).

More succinctly, under Groshans v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 629, 632 n.1 (1995):

In 1992, Congress amended the Rehabilitation Act, replacing all references to "handicap" and similar terms with "disability" and similar terms. Congress did not intend any definitional changes or changes in the Act's coverage in so doing. Thus, the Board in employing the term "disability" in lieu of the term "handicap" intends no changes in meaning. See discussion of the statutory changes and their purpose as set forth in the legislative history of the 1992 amendments to the Rehabilitation Act in Wilson v. VA, 63 MSPR 63, 66 (1994).

Disability discrimination is a prohibited personnel practice and a violation of EEOC regulations and several federal statutes, depending on the nature of the discrimination. If the personnel action appealed to the Board results from disability discrimination, the action cannot be sustained. 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(B); 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(D). The allegation of disability discrimination is an affirmative defense. To establish the defense, the appellant will need to make out a prima facie case by showing: that he is disabled; that he is a "qualified" disabled employee in cases involving drug or alcohol abuse and actual or potential serious misconduct or workplace risks; that the disability is the cause of the action; that the agency knows or should know of the disability; that his disability can be accommodated; that the accommodation will allow the employee to perform position responsibilities in the position or through reassignment; and that the accommodation is reasonable, i.e., that the agency can provide the accommodation without incurring an undue hardship (also referred to as undue burden). Disability discrimination may also be asserted as a basis for a constructive adverse action appeal in the nature of a coerced resignation or retirement. The Board distinguishes its treatment of cases involving physical disabilities and cases involving drug or alcohol dependency. Mental disability cases fall somewhere in between. The basis for the distinction in treatment is the element of misconduct. Physical disabilities normally involve employees who desire to continue working but cannot because of a medical or physical impairment. The question becomes whether the impairment is sufficiently disabling as to constitute a disability and, if it is, whether the agency can accommodate the impairment or disability without incurring expense or administrative problems sufficient to constitute an undue hardship. Some mental disability cases are treated exactly as in the usual physical disability case: the employee wants to work; the agency would like to have the employee work, but the question is whether the accommodation required constitutes an undue hardship. The substance and alcohol dependency cases pose a different problem, and into that group of cases go some mental disability cases presenting employees with psychiatric impairments that may result or have resulted in misconduct. The substance abuser has broken the law by improper possession or use of a controlled substance. The alcoholic employee may have been charged with AWOL or insubordination, poor work, or some other misconduct. The employee may have a disability, but the employer desires to remove or otherwise discipline the employee for the misconduct resulting from or associated with the disabling condition.

The Board generally follows the law established by EEOC in determining disability law issues, as Alvara v. DHS, 116 MSPR 627, 631 ¶ 8, 2011 MSPB 75 (2011) (on EEOC review, Petitioner v.
