d. Privity of Agencies

Issue preclusion applies to agencies as well as to appellants. The identity of parties rule does not necessarily require the same formal parties when the government is a party through one or another of its agencies. If the United States is a party to a criminal action and its agency, Department of the Army, is party to the appeal before the Board, collateral estoppel may be applied following the result in the criminal case. Raymond v. Dept. of Army, 34 MSPR 476, 482 (1987). More unusual is the case involving a finding against one agency that a second agency tries to challenge when the appellant seeks a benefit from the second agency on the basis of that finding. In Taylor v. OPM, 82 MSPR 237, 241-44 (1999), the Postal Service unsuccessfully defended a disability discrimination case brought in court that had the effect of requiring retroactive appointment of Mr. Taylor. It appears that Mr. Taylor never went to work for the Postal Service, but he did receive back pay and retirement contributions. OPM denied Taylor's retirement application on the basis that Taylor never actually worked for the Postal Service. The Board ruled, however, that OPM was collaterally estopped from challenging the existence of Taylor's employment because estoppel was created through the court case defended by the Postal Service. OPM and the Postal Service were in privity and treated as the same entity for purposes of collateral estoppel:

[W]e do not agree with OPM that the issue here is whether OPM should be bound by a settlement agreement between the appellant and the USPS to which it was not a party, one that it characterizes as an attempt to give the appellant a fictitious employment status. The agreement between the Postal Service and the appellant, to the extent that it provided for the appellant's retroactive employment by the USPS, merely implemented the decision of the court of appeals in the appellant's employment discrimination suit. The issue here is not the effect of a settlement agreement, but whether OPM is bound by the Sixth Circuit's decision holding that the Postal Service discriminated against the appellant by refusing to hire him and remanding for the district court to provide him the relief that he sought.

...The question is...whether issue preclusion or collateral estoppel requires that the court's ruling concerning the appellant's entitlement to employment be given effect by OPM and the Board. The Board set out the elements required for collateral estoppel to apply in Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 341 (1995): 1) the issue previously adjudicated must be identical to the issue currently presented now; 2) the issue must have actually been litigated in the prior case; 3) the prior determination of that issue must have been necessary to the resulting judgment; and 4) the party precluded must have been fully represented in the prior case. OPM argues that it is not collaterally estopped by the Sixth Circuit's decision in Taylor II because the issue there was not the same as the issue in this disability retirement proceeding and because it was not a party to that case.

OPM is right, of course, that the ultimate issue in the appellant's employment discrimination case is not identical to the ultimate issue in this case as to whether he is now entitled to disability retirement. However, the application of collateral estoppel is not limited to the ultimate issues in the cases involved. The issue that was resolved by the Sixth Circuit, and that was necessary to its judgment, was whether the appellant was entitled to have been employed by the Postal Service at the time of his application in 1987. That issue is identical to an issue in this case, whether the appellant should be deemed to have been employed by the Postal Service, and we find that this element required for the application of collateral estoppel is present here.

OPM also argues that it is not collaterally estopped by the Sixth Circuit's decision of that issue because it was not a party to the case. In other words, OPM contends that it is entitled to relitigate an employment discrimination issue that has already been litigated unsuccessfully by the employing or non-hiring agency. We find that OPM's position is contrary to the general rule that there is privity between agencies or officers of the same government so that a judgment on an issue in litigation between a party and one federal agency is generally binding in relitigation of the same issue between that party and another federal agency. Sunshine Anthracite Coal Co. v. Adkins, 310 U.S. 381, 402-03 (1940). Thus, for example, in United States v. Rogers, 960 F.2d 1501, 1509 (10th Cir.), cert. denied, 113 S. Ct. 817 (1992), the court found that issue preclusion bound the U.S. Attorney in a criminal prosecution for fraud that followed dismissal on the merits of an action by the SEC to enjoin continuation of the same claimed fraudulent acts because the two federal agencies were in privity. Cf. Briggs v. State, 732, P.2d 1078, 1082-83 (Alaska 1987) (state agency was collaterally estopped from relitigating in driver's license revocation proceeding issue that had been resolved in motorist's favor in prior criminal proceeding because the agency's interests in litigating the issue were adequately represented by the state prosecutor at the suppression hearing).

It is true, that privity between agencies of the same government is not always found, but the exceptions to the rule do not apply in this case. Preclusion will not be applied where important differences in the functions of different agencies are such that the agency that has litigated the issue did not have authority to represent the agency that is sought to be bound on that issue. See Wright & Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure, Vol. 18, § 4458, p. 507 (1981). "The crucial point is whether or not in the earlier litigation the representative of the United States had authority to represent its interests in a final adjudication of the issue in controversy." Sunshine Anthracite Coal Co. v. Adkins, 310 U.S. at 403. Here there is no question that the Postal Service had authority to represent the government's interests in the appellant's employment discrimination action, which was brought under a statute specifying that the head of the agency involved will be the named defendant. See 42 USC § 2000e-16(c). As the non-hiring agency, the Postal Service was the agency with the primary interest in whether the appellant should have been hired when he applied for a position in 1987 and in defending its decision not to hire him as a non-discriminatory one. For that reason, we believe that OPM is bound by the finding of the Sixth Circuit, in a case clearly within the court's jurisdiction, that the appellant was entitled to the relief that he requested, to be retroactively employed in the position for which he applied.

The remedy of retroactive employment that the appellant sought and that the court awarded was a remedy authorized by Title VII in 42 USC § 2000e-5(g). See, e.g., Brown v. Secretary of the Army, 918 F.2d 214 (D.C. Cir. 1990) (affirming award of retroactive promotion with back pay under Title VII, while denying interest because Back Pay Act did not apply), cert. denied, 112 S.C. 57 (1991); Sanchez v. Philip Morris Inc., 774 F. Supp 626, 629 (W.D. Okla. 1991) (victim of Title VII violation is presumptively entitled to retroactive employment, including promotions, raises and benefits). Furthermore, the AJ's interpretation of that relief was consistent with the regulations issued by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, the agency with responsibility for implementation of the Rehabilitation Act and other discrimination laws. 42 USC § 2000e-16(b); 29 USC § 794a(a)(1). The applicable regulation provides that, in the case of discriminatory failure to hire, the appropriate remedy is retroactive appointment on the date that the individual would have been hired absent the discrimination, and it adds that "[T]he individual shall be deemed to have performed service for the agency during [the back pay] period for all purposes except for meeting service requirements for completion of a required probationary or trial period." 29 CFR § 1614.501(b)(1)(ii) (emphasis added). In view of the relief ordered by the court, we find that the appellant must be deemed to have entered on duty and performed a federal function for purposes of eligibility for retirement and that OPM's decision to the contrary cannot be affirmed.
