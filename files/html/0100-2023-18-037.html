c. Consecutive Adverse Actions

An agency may take successive adverse actions against an employee--even if the agency could have consolidated its charges in an earlier action. The agency is not required to act but once on all known misconduct, Silva v. USPS, 59 MSPR 268, 272-73 (1993):

The appellant argued below and reiterates in his response to the petition for review that the agency should be precluded from bringing a charge of failure to abide by the terms of the settlement agreement requiring regular attendance at EAP meetings because of the application of the doctrine of res judicata. The appellant's argument is misplaced. Res judicata bars relitigation of the same cause of action. Although the appellant was removed twice for alleged breach of the "last chance" settlement agreement, the removals are separate claims because each was brought on different charges. See Wexler v. Department of the Interior, 48 MSPR 513, 517-18 (1991), aff'd, 954 F.2d 733 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). Therefore, the res judicata bar does not apply.

The appellant correctly states that the agency could have based the October 1, 1991 removal on his failure to attend the EAP meetings. We know of no requirement, however, that an agency charge an appellant with every instance of misconduct. Likewise, we know of no statute, regulation, or precedent that bars successive disciplinary actions. See, e.g., Crespo v. United States Postal Service, 53 MSPR 125, 129 (1992) (an agency could undertake an indefinite suspension that was not based on the same charge in a prior removal action that was reversed); Parker v. United States Postal Service, 46 MSPR 214, 219 (1990) (agencies are not precluded from taking legitimate personnel actions pending Board disposition of an appeal, or after Board reversal of other appealable actions, so long as a valid reason exists for the later action); Rose v. Department of the Navy, 31 MSPR 232, 235 (1986) (an agency may reinstate a rescinded removal based on the same charges); cf. Marshall v. Government Printing Office, 39 MSPR 272, 275-76 (1988) (an agency may not take a retroactive removal action to avoid complying with a Board back pay award).

Finally, the appellant has argued that allowing an agency to remove him under these circumstances will encourage agencies that do not prevail in a Board appeal to institute additional disciplinary actions rather than challenge an initial decision by filing a petition for review. While we acknowledge the possibility, we believe that agencies have little incentive to do so. If it fails to file a petition for review, an agency must comply with the administrative judge's award of back pay and may be liable for attorneys fees. Furthermore, agencies are presumed to carry out their duties in good faith....

Cargile v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-14-0056-I-2 (NP 10/3/2022), considered the existence of charges in the second adverse action that were independent of those in a prior adverse action:

It is a well-established principle of civil service law that an agency may not discipline an employee twice for the same misconduct that formed the basis of a prior disciplinary or adverse action when imposing a subsequent disciplinary or adverse action. Frederick v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 401, ¶ 6 (2015). In this case, however, the 15-day suspension action was based entirely on incidents that occurred before the appellant's detail. Furthermore, while the unserved portion of the suspension was reinstated based on the agency's determination that her performance on the detail was less than satisfactory, that determination was not itself a charge underlying the suspension. See Girani, 924 F.2d at 241-42; Walton, 24 M.S.P.R. at 569. Thus, the prohibition against double punishment does not apply to specifications (2)(b)(1) and (3).

As to the remaining specifications, the administrative judge found below that the agency proved the specifications listed under paragraph (2)(a) and 2(c) of the proposal notice, and the appellant has not challenged that finding on review. These specifications alone are sufficient to sustain the charge. See Burroughs v. Department of the Army, 918 F.2d 170, 172 (Fed. Cir. 1990). Nonetheless, it is possible that the agency's success or failure in proving specifications (2)(b)(1) through (3) may be relevant to the penalty analysis. See Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 646, 651 (1996) (holding that an agency's failure to sustain all of its supporting specifications may require, or contribute to, a finding that the agency's penalty is not reasonable). Accordingly, on remand, the administrative judge should make a new finding on the merits of specifications (2)(b)(1) through (3), without reference to the prohibition against double punishment.

An indefinite suspension does not bar a removal based on criminal conduct, Agnew v. USPS, NY-0353-14-0337-I-1 (NP 12/22/2016):

The appellant next asserts that the agency's removal action was improper because he already had served an indefinite suspension for the same misconduct. We disagree. It is well established that an agency may indefinitely suspend an employee based upon reasonable cause to believe he has committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment could be imposed and thereafter remove him based upon either his subsequent criminal conviction or his underlying misconduct. Frederick v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 401, ¶ 14 (2015). Accordingly, the agency's (now canceled) indefinite suspension did not preclude the subsequent removal action.
