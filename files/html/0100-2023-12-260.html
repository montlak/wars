c. Allegation of Bias, Unfair Hearing

In a case otherwise properly before it, the Board considers an allegation of bias against an arbitrator, but the chances of the Board finding bias are probably about the same as the Board finding one of its own AJs biased--slim or less. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Treatment of Bias Allegations."] To warrant consideration by the Board, the appellant must explain to the Board the basis of his bias allegations. See De Bow v. Dept. of Air Force, 97 MSPR 5, 10-11 ¶ 9 (2004) (presumption of honesty and integrity of arbitrator); Gustave-Schmidt v. Dept. of Labor, 87 MSPR 667, 679 ¶ 20 (2001) ("Allegations of bias based on an arbitrator's findings and rulings do not warrant further review by the Board because they are insufficient to overcome the presumption of honesty and integrity accorded to arbitrators."); Colon v. VA, 73 MSPR 659, 664-65 (1997) ("The appellant's bare allegation of bias by the arbitrator (apparently based on her mere disagreement with his findings) similarly does not require further review because such disagreement is not enough to overcome the presumption of honesty and integrity accorded to arbitrators."). The Board is not inclined to make general reviews of the adequacy of the arbitration system. See Cirella v. Dept. of Treasury, 108 MSPR 474, 480 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 79 (2008) (declining to consider the complaint that arbitration is an "antiquated" system).

Cosio v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 691, 697-98 (1994), described the governing standard of review for bias allegations involving arbitrators:

The appellant also alleges that the arbitrator exhibited bias against her by calling her representative by an incorrect name, by interrupting the agency's witnesses' testimony and telling them not to answer or answering for them, and by being lenient with the agency's witnesses on cross-examination. The appellant also alleges that the agency "[took] care" of a parking ticket that the arbitrator received during the hearing. The appellant further alleges that the arbitrator brought pressure to bear on the appellant's representative to encourage the appellant to settle the grievance, telling her representative that she should accept the settlement offer because he had spoken to agency managers who told him that the appellant was "a bad person" and made other negative comments.

As stated above, an arbitrator's decision is entitled to deference and will only be disturbed upon a showing that the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service laws, rules, or regulation. Means, 63 MSPR at 182. We find that, in keeping with that standard of deference, arbitrators are entitled to the same presumption of honesty and integrity that administrative judges enjoy. See, e.g., Oliver v. Dept. of Transp., 1 MSPB 368, 1 MSPR 382, 386 (1980) (in making a claim of bias or prejudice against an administrative judge, a party must overcome the presumption of honesty and integrity that accompanies administrative adjudicators). The appellant's allegations, unsworn and uncorroborated, do not overcome that presumption. The record does not contain a transcript or tape recording of the hearing. The appellant, in her closing brief to the arbitrator, summarized the testimony of many of the witnesses but did not raise any concerns with the manner in which they testified. As for the allegation that the arbitrator exerted undue pressure on the appellant to settle her grievance, we note that any alleged pressure was unsuccessful because the appellant did not enter into a settlement agreement.

Finally, the appellant's allegation that the arbitrator spoke ex parte with the agency's representative between the time of the hearing and the issuance of his final decision does not warrant disturbing the arbitrator's decision. The appellant has not shown how these alleged ex parte contacts prejudiced her substantive rights. See Muzzipapa v. VA, 53 MSPR 53, 59 (1992).

Gomez v. SSA, 70 MSPR 257, 266 (1996), added as to a bias claim:

Finally, we note the appellant's contentions that the arbitrator, inter alia, denied him a fair hearing, and was biased against him in his rulings on motions and in his failure to draw an adverse inference with respect to allegedly lost or destroyed agency evidence that the appellant claims would have exonerated him.

To prove an allegation of bias, a party must make a substantial showing of personal bias to overcome the presumption of honesty and integrity that accompanies administrative adjudicators. Sirgo v. Dept. of Justice, 66 MSPR 261, 268 (1995). Arbitrators are entitled to the same presumption of honesty and integrity that administrative judges enjoy. Cosio v. Dept. of Justice, 65 MSPR 691, 697-98 (1994). Here, the appellant's uncorroborated, unsworn allegations do not establish bias by the arbitrator. Rather, they indicate that the appellant disagrees with the arbitrator's rulings. Those rulings, even if erroneous, are insufficient to establish bias. See, e.g., Wildberger v. Small Business Administration, 65 MSPR 673, 678 (1994); Garrett v. Dept. of Defense, 62 MSPR 666, 670 n.5 (1994). Thus, we find that the appellant has not established bias or prejudicial conduct by the arbitrator.

See Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 (NP 5/2/2013) ("The appellant argues that the arbitrator was biased against him. He cites a factually similar case in which the same arbitrator reached a different result, and he asserts that the arbitrator's actions were an attempt to avoid being struck from the panel. The appellant also takes exception to the arbitrator characterizing his absences as 'outrageous,' and he argues that this constitutes additional evidence of bias. We find that the appellant's allegation that the arbitrator's decision was based on his own career interests rather than on the facts of the case is mere conjecture. Neither this nor the arbitrator's use of the word 'outrageous' is sufficient to overcome the presumption of honesty and integrity that the arbitrator enjoys. See Costa v. Department of Defense, 88 M.S.P.R. 293, ¶ 15 (2001)."); Holly v. DHHS, 92 MSPR 601, 607 ¶ 11 (2002) (Board did not find that the appellant was denied a fair hearing based upon the appellant's assertion that the arbitrator limited the hearing to two hours); Costa v. DOD, 88 MSPR 293, 299 ¶ 14 (2001) (conversations between the arbitrator and agency officials were not shown to be ex parte, i.e., involving the merits of the case or violating rules requiring submissions to be in writing; the arbitrator's acceptance of rides from management officials and sharing lunch with management officials was not shown to prejudice the appellant's substantive rights or overcome a presumption of the arbitrator's honesty); Higgs v. SSA, 71 MSPR 48, 52 (1996) (appellant failed to show how the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation regarding the fairness of a hearing when the appellant protested conversations among witnesses at the hearing but did not contend that the witnesses were discussing their testimony); cf. Perkins v. Dept. of Interior, CB-7121-12-0020-V-1 (NP 10/24/2013) (if the arbitrator precluded evidence on an EEO issue, the error was cured by the appellant's ability to submit evidence on the issue to the Board when the appellant requested review of the award).
