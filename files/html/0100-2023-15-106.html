6. Acting as Agent or Attorney Against Government

Readers who are government attorneys, take note of O'Neill v. DHUD, 220 F.3d 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2000), construing 18 USC 205:

(a) Whoever, being an officer or employee of the United States in the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the Government or in any agency of the United States, other than in the proper discharge of his official duties--

...

(2) acts as agent or attorney for anyone before any department, agency, court, court-martial, officer, or civil, military, or naval commission in connection with any covered matter in which the United States is a party or has a direct and substantial interest;

shall be subject to the penalties set forth in section 216 of this title.

Ms. O'Neill, a typing clerk, was charged with acting as an agent for the Altarmont Program, a nonprofit group that was attempting to acquire military housing at an air base that was scheduled to be closed. The housing was to be used to provide shelter for homeless men. DOD and DHUD were responsible for converting military installations designated by Congress for closure. A court decided that the term "agent" in the statute should be construed in accordance with the common law, the court explained the common law of agency, and the court concluded that Ms. O'Neill had not violated 18 USC 205(a)(2). Id. at 1360-63. See Van Ee v. EPA, 202 F.3d 296 (D.C. Cir. 2000) (interpreting 18 USC 205, prohibiting federal employees from acting as the agent or attorney of a private group relative to investigations, contracts, or "other particular matters" of interest to the United States; the case involved an injunctive proceeding to prevent the EPA from disciplining an employee who was actively involved with environmental advocacy groups). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Right to Representation" and "Conflicts" for the ability of, and limitations upon, federally-employed lawyers seeking to represent federal employees in personnel cases; refer later in this chapter to "Employment in Violation of Agency Restriction; Outside Practice of Law."]
