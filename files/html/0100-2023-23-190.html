3. Agreement That Appellant First Notify Agency of Noncompliance Allegation

Some settlement agreements have procedures that require compliance issues to be raised first with the agency before they are initiated with the Board. If the appellant delays or obstructs the process of agency notification, the Board may, under the agreement, limit the monetary remedy the employee may obtain through enforcement proceedings to the period following effective notification to the agency of the alleged breach of the settlement. See Farrero v. NASA, 78 MSPR 382, 388 (1998) (appellant delayed providing the agency with the details concerning an alleged breach of a confidentiality clause; the Board limited appellant's back pay remedy following a determination of breach), rev'd, Farrero v. NASA (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3253) (appellant was only required to provide agency with sufficient information to support a plausible contention that agency breached the confidentiality terms; appellant's duty of good faith did not include an obligation to identify both individuals who disclosed, and those who were recipients of, confidential information).

Galloway v. USDA, 110 MSPR 311, 316-17 ¶¶ 6-8, 2008 MSPB 250 (2008) (aff'd, Galloway v. USDA, 398 Fed. Appx. 591 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)) [Galloway was overruled as to VEOA right to compete claim for merit promotion positions sought by federal employees, Oram v. Dept. of Navy, 2022 MSPB 30 (2022)], decided that a prenotification provision was not vital to the agreement and would not displace Board's enforcement authority when compliance was first raised with the Board rather than with the agency:

The AJ determined that the appellant materially breached the settlement agreement when he filed this petition for enforcement without first providing written notice of noncompliance to an agency employee designated in the settlement agreement. The pertinent sections of the settlement agreement provide as follows:

If a party believes the other has not fully complied with one or more terms of this Agreement, that party or his/her representative shall make a good faith effort to contact the other party to discuss and seek correction of any compliance or implementation issues before taking any formal non-compliance actions.

...

...If Appellant believes that the Agency has failed to comply with the terms of this Settlement Agreement, Appellant shall provide written notice of the alleged noncompliance to Yvonne Fair, Lead Employee Relations Specialist, Forest Service,...within thirty (30) days of the date on which Appellant knew or should have know [sic] of the alleged noncompliance.

The AJ concluded that the appellant's failure to give advance notice to the agency was a material breach of the settlement agreement that precludes the appellant from seeking enforcement before the Board. We disagree.

A breach of a settlement agreement is material when it relates to a matter of vital importance, or goes to the essence of the agreement. Lutz v. U.S. Postal Service, 485 F.3d 1377, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Thomas v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 124 F.3d 1439, 1442 (Fed. Cir. 1997). For instance, in Thomas, our reviewing court found that the agency's breach of a confidentiality provision in the parties' settlement agreement was material because it "was the major benefit that Thomas received in exchange for agreeing to resign from his position." Thomas, 124 F.3d at 1442. The court concluded that, because the breach was material, Thomas was discharged from his contractual duty to resign. Id.

Although the above-quoted portion of the settlement agreement demonstrates that the parties saw a benefit to resolving informally any problems with the settlement agreement prior to initiating formal enforcement proceedings, it was not a "major benefit" that the agency received through the agreement. Cf. Thomas, 124 F.3d at 1442. The major benefits the agency received under the settlement agreement were the appellant's withdrawal of his Board appeal with prejudice, his agreement not to file any further actions that could have been raised therein, and the waiver of any further claims the appellant had or may have had in relation to the appellant's Board appeal and related EEO complaints. Thus, the AJ's determination that the appellant materially breached the settlement agreement by failing to notify the agency before petitioning the Board for compliance is in error because the notice provision is not a matter of vital importance, which goes to the essence of the contract, and prior notice of an alleged breach was not the major benefit the agency received under the settlement agreement. See Lutz, 485 F.3d at 1381; see also Thomas, 124 F.3d at 1442. Therefore, the appellant's failure to provide advance notice to the agency of its alleged breach would not excuse the agency from complying with a material term of the agreement.[4]

[4] We note that the agency has never argued that the petition for enforcement was untimely because the appellant failed to notify the agency of the alleged noncompliance within 30 days of the date he knew or should have known of the alleged noncompliance. Further, we note that the agency did not argue below that the appellant had materially breached the settlement agreement thereby excusing the agency from complying with its terms. Rather, the agency merely argued that the petition for enforcement was premature, and asked the AJ to dismiss the petition so that the parties could first utilize the informal compliance procedures in the settlement agreement. Although it may have been appropriate at an earlier stage of the proceeding to follow the agency's suggestion and dismiss the petition as premature without prejudice to refiling, there is no benefit to doing so now, because the agency has learned of the appellant's allegations of noncompliance through this proceeding and the parties have not been able to resolve the matter.

In related litigation, the Federal Circuit noted in Galloway v. USDA, 398 Fed. Appx. 591, 593 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP):

We note that Mr. Galloway failed to report any alleged noncompliance to the Department of Agriculture as required by the settlement agreement. While we agree with the full Board that Mr. Galloway's failure to notify the agency of its default was not a material breach of his obligations under the agreement, it nonetheless likely contributed to the delay in processing his retirement application, because if he had advised the agency of its error, it would have been alerted to the error sooner and presumably could have acted to correct it at that time.
