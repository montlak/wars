broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section B: Interim Relief, Subsection 08: Factors Limiting or Warranting Denial of Interim Relief, Part a: OWCP Benefits
a. OWCP Benefits

As a matter of policy, interim relief should not be provided when the appeal involves an appellant who is receiving OWCP compensation at the time the initial decision was issued, Davis v. DOJ, 61 MSPR 92, 95-96 (1994):

We find that where, as here, an appellant is receiving OWCP compensation at the time the initial decision is issued, interim relief generally should not be ordered because doing so could result in the agency's payment of monies in contravention of 5 USC § 8116(a). Section 8116(a) provides, in relevant part, that "[w]hile an employee is receiving [OWCP] compensation...he may not receive salary, pay, or remuneration of any type from the United States, except...in return for service actually performed...." If, for instance, the relief provided in the initial decision is that an appellant be returned to work, and the agency decides not to return him to work under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii)(II), the agency is nevertheless required to pay him during the interim relief period under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(B). The appellant's receipt of pay under these circumstances would be contrary to 5 USC § 8116(a), which prohibits his receipt of pay except in return for service actually performed.

We recognize that there may be established administrative procedures that would allow the employing agency or the OWCP to avoid the prohibition in section 8116(a). Cf. 20 CFR § 10.301 (providing that the "employee has the right to elect whether to receive pay for leave or to receive compensation"); 20 CFR §§ 10.320-10.324 (pertaining to recovery of overpayments of compensation). We find, however, that for the employing agency and/or the OWCP to undertake such administrative steps would generally entail significant administrative costs and would be unduly burdensome when weighed against the fact that the appellant is receiving monetary compensation from the OWCP and is thus not without some income. See Steele v. OPM, 57 MSPR 458, 463-64 (1993) (the Board instructed administrative judges to exercise caution in granting interim relief in Office of Personnel Management retirement appeals for similar reasons).

Davis was followed in Walters v. USDA, 63 MSPR 348, 352 (1994), Evono v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 541, 544 (1996), and Brownlow v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 349, 351 ¶ 5 (2000) (because interim relief was incorrectly ordered, based on the appellant's receipt of OWCP benefits, the Board would not consider whether the agency fully complied with the interim relief order); see Holmes v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-11-0263-C-1 (NP 4/15/2022) ("If an appellant is receiving OWCP compensation when the initial decision is issued, interim relief generally should not be ordered because it could result in the agency's paying funds in violation of 5 U.S.C. § 8116(a). Davis v. Department of Justice, 61 M.S.P.R. 92, 95-96, aff'd, 43 F.3d 1485 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table)."); cf. Roja v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 618, 621 (1992) (employee whose removal was rescinded was not entitled to back pay, annual or sick leave as part of the restoration of the status quo when the employee was receiving OWCP benefits for the same period of time).

The Board's policy precluding an award of interim relief to an individual receiving OWCP benefits was reiterated, Paris v. Dept. of Treasury, 104 MSPR 331, 335-36 ¶¶ 7-9, 2006 MSPB 362 (2006):

Under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(b)(2)(A), a prevailing appellant is entitled to "the relief provided in the [initial] decision effective upon the making of the decision, and remaining in effect pending the outcome of any petition for review...," unless the administrative judge "determines that the granting of such [interim] relief is not appropriate" under the circumstances. Davis v. Department of Justice, 61 M.S.P.R. 92, 95, aff'd, 43 F.3d 1485 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table).

When an appellant is receiving OWCP compensation at the time that the initial decision is issued, interim relief generally should not be ordered because doing so could result in the agency's payment of monies in contravention of 5 U.S.C. § 8116(a). Section 8116(a) provides, in relevant part, that, "[w]hile an employee is receiving [OWCP] compensation..., he may not receive salary, pay, or remuneration of any type from the United States, except...in return for service actually performed...." If, for instance, the relief provided in the initial decision is that an appellant be returned to work, and the agency decides not to return him to work under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii)(II), the agency is nevertheless required to pay him during the interim relief period. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(b)(2)(B). The appellant's receipt of pay under these circumstances would be contrary to 5 U.S.C. § 8116(a). See Davis, 61 M.S.P.R. at 95.

On petition for review, the agency argues that the administrative judge should not have ordered interim relief because the appellant was receiving OWCP compensation at the time the initial decision was issued. Although the record was unclear as to whether the appellant was receiving benefits when the initial decision was issued, the agency has submitted evidence that the appellant has received OWCP compensation from the date of his removal to the present. Thus, the record now shows that the appellant is not entitled to interim relief because he is receiving OWCP compensation. Accordingly, the appellant's request that the agency's petition for review of the initial decision be dismissed for failure to comply with the administrative judge's interim relief order is DENIED.

The appellant states in her request that she "has been and continues to receive [OWCP] compensation...due to injuries sustained in the January 25, 2010 incident referenced in the proposed removal."
