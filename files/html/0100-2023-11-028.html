7. Evidence From Other MSPB Hearing; Other Judicial Proceeding

Although it is not improper for a AJ to direct or allow admission into the record of evidence from another Board hearing, the evidence is to be treated with concern for the due process rights of the parties. It is not the obligation of a party to locate and review documents that another party wishes to introduce from another case. The Board's regulations require that the parties be given copies of the documents, not only that they be given access to them. See 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2) (2022); Strauss v. OPM, 39 MSPR 132, 137 (1988) (allowing for a possible exception when the documents in question are easily accessible to the appellant); Meade v. Dept. of Treasury, 4 MSPR 211, 213 (1980) (AJ acted properly by insisting that evidentiary material associated with a prior initial decision be moved into evidence on remand); cf. Berry v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 95, 96 (1984) ("an initial decision issued in another case does not constitute new and material evidence" supporting a PFR; distinguishing outcome of similar cases with different results).

Findings contained in court orders may be considered along with other evidence concerning an issue. Fields v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 332, 334 n.2 (1983) (participation of employees in an air traffic controllers' strike). Of a dispute over the validity of a state court divorce decree in a survivor annuity determination, Higgins v. OPM, SF-0831-11-0572-I-1 (NP 1/27/2012), stated at n.2:

...Furthermore, even if the appellant could was invalid or inoperative, the Full Faith and Credit Clause set forth in Article IV, Section 1 of the U.S. Constitution ("Full Faith and Credit shall be given in each State to the public Acts, Records, and judicial Proceedings of every other State.") required California to give full faith and credit to the Texas divorce decree. See Johnson v. Muelberger, 340 U.S. 581 (1951) (where the party attempting to attack a divorce decree would not be permitted to make a collateral attack in the courts of the granting state, another state, by virtue of the Full Faith and Credit Clause, must give full faith and credit to an out-of-state divorce by barring either party to that divorce who has been personally served or who has entered a personal appearance from collaterally attacking the decree); Hagen v. Hagen, 282 S.W.3d 899, 902 (Tex. 2009) ("As with other final, unappealed judgments which are regular on their face, divorce decrees and judgments are not vulnerable to collateral attack."); (the Texas divorce decree indicated that the appellant appeared personally and by her attorney before the District Court of Bowie County, Texas).
