3. Challenge; Enforcement

Any person against whom a subpoena is directed may move to quash (or squash) or limit it. The motion should be reasoned and it should be filed with the AJ. 5 CFR 1201.82 (2022) provides:

Any person to whom a subpoena is directed, or any party, may file a motion to quash or limit the subpoena. The motion must be filed with the judge, and it must include the reasons why compliance with the subpoena should not be required or the reasons why the subpoena's scope should be limited.

See Finnegan v. USPS, 23 MSPR 94, 97 (1984) (example of subpoena quashing: the Probation Office could not release a presentence report without court approval).

The party under subpoena should not ignore the writ. If an agency decides unilaterally to ignore Board discovery orders, the AJ may properly invoke sanctions against the agency, including striking its evidence. Eaks v. DOJ, 18 MSPR 328, 333-34 n.5 (1983). In Eaks, the Board was presented with the unusual situation that the agency was in control of third-party witnesses who were inmates in a federal penitentiary. The agency argued that the subpoena issued by the AJ was unenforceable and that only a writ of habeas corpus could be used to obtain the presence of the inmates to testify. The Board rejected the argument, noting that a writ of habeas corpus is needed only if an inmate is removed from the prison. A subpoena would be sufficient in Eaks if the hearing could be organized to permit the prisoner to testify at a conference room within the correctional institution.

5 CFR 1201.85 (2022), establishes the procedure for subpoena enforcement:

(a) If a person who has been served with a Board subpoena fails or refuses to comply with its terms, the party seeking compliance may file a written motion for enforcement with the judge or make an oral motion for enforcement while on the record at a hearing. That party must present the document certifying that the subpoena was served and, except where the witness was required to appear before the judge, must submit an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746 (see appendix IV) describing the failure or refusal to obey the subpoena. The Board, in accordance with 5 U.S.C. 1204(c), may then ask the appropriate United States district court to enforce the subpoena. If the person who has failed or refused to comply with a Board subpoena is located in a foreign country, the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia will have jurisdiction to enforce compliance, to the extent that a U.S. court can assert jurisdiction over an individual in the foreign country.

(b) Upon application by the Special Counsel, the Board may seek court enforcement of a subpoena issued by the Special Counsel in the same manner in which it seeks enforcement of Board subpoenas, in accordance with 5 U.S.C. 1212(b)(3).

The Board elaborated on and further defined its subpoena enforcement procedure, Porter v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 301, 305-06, 6 MSPB 257 (1981):

Because subpoenas are issued for the benefit of the requesting party, when a subpoena is not honored the requesting party must indicate in a motion for enforcement that it considers the witness' testimony to be of sufficient importance to the case to warrant invoking the authority of a United States district court. It is incumbent on the party to inform the Board that it considers the testimony necessary to its case. It is logical that all information initially sought by a party will not necessarily prove important in the final analysis. Proffered testimony which initially appears important upon issuance of a subpoena may prove to be irrelevant or duplicative as other information is developed. The party who sought the subpoena is in a unique position to decide whether it would be productive to move for compliance. In the absence of such motion, the presiding official would reasonably conclude that the agency did not view the testimony of Mrs. Wadley of sufficient importance to warrant a Board request for enforcement. Therefore it was proper for the presiding official to proceed with the hearing in the witness's absence since the agency made no request for enforcement.

Porter observed that there may be exceptional circumstances justifying an AJ's decision to seek enforcement on his own, although it did not suggest what those circumstances might be.

The subpena enforcement process disrupts orderly case processing. First there is the motion filed with the AJ to enforce the subpoena, leading to a likely opposition and a ruling. Then if the ruling does not result in the desired discovery, the AJ refers the matter to the Board's General Counsel's office at headquarters. The attorney assigned the matter in the MSPB General Counsel's office then attempts to negotiate compliance with the subpoena by the resisting individual or organization. When that fails, the Board's General Counsel files a lawsuit in a federal court with jurisdiction over the person or entity whose evidence is sought, the complaint is served and answered, and a federal district judge then determines if the matter can be resolved through an agreed-upon order, or whether a formal decision, possibly involving a hearing, is required before an order enforcing the subpoena is issued. The process takes months.

Not many cases are brought in the courts to enforce Board subpoenas. In Puhar v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 204, 206 (1986), the Board ordered the Department of Justice to show cause why enforcement proceedings should not be commenced or sanctions imposed after the Marshals Service failed to produce documents subpoenaed at a hearing. It was stated that the Justice Department was "of course obligated to assure that its organizational components cooperate in the adjudicatory processes of the Board." The Board entered a later order in Puhar v. DOJ, 31 MSPR 71, 73 (1986), determining that DOJ substantially complied with the subpoena and remanding the case to the AJ for in camera review of the documentation to determine relevance and the propriety of any requested protective order. By way of examples of the occasional case brought by the Board to enforce subpoenas, two cases were initiated during 1990: MSPB v. Littlefield, No. MS-2-90-52 in the Southern District of Ohio, involving the need to produce a witness in an adverse action hearing; MSPB and Special Counsel v. Hartin and Craig, No. Civ-90-201-T in the Western District of Oklahoma, to require testimony of the respondents in an OSC investigation.

One case that reached an appeals court determined that a federal court, not a state court, is the proper forum for issues involving enforcement of MSPB subpoenas. The reviewing court's inquiry should be limited to whether the investigation furthered by the subpoena is for a proper purpose and whether the documents sought are relevant to the investigation. Gilbreath v. Guadalupe Hospital Found., 5 F.3d 785, 790 (5th Cir. 1993), declared as to an MSPB subpoena obtained by an agency to secure hospital records:

[T]he subpoenas challenged by the Gilbreaths were issued under the authority of a federal statute. See 5 USC 1204(b)(2)(A) (Supp. III 1991). The same statute expressly confers jurisdiction upon the district court to enforce the subpoenas. See Id. at (c). The Gilbreaths therefore could have, indeed should have, filed their action challenging the enforceability of the MSPB subpoenas in federal district court. Consequently, the action was properly removed to the district court....

...

Gilbreath...makes a somewhat unfocused argument that the district court erred in ordering the enforcement of the subpoenas. The only substantive argument she presents, however, is that the subpoenas should not be enforced because the MSPB is merely on a "fishing expedition in an attempt to justify the removal action taken [by the DLA against] Vance H. Gilbreath in March 1991." Needless to say, this argument fails to persuade us.

As the district correctly observed, when reviewing an administrative subpoena, a district court plays a "strictly limited" role. Sandsend Financial Consultants v. Federal Home Loan Bank Bd., 878 F.2d 875, 879 (5th Cir. 1989). The court's inquiry is limited to two questions: (1) whether the investigation is for a proper purpose and (2) whether the documents the agency seeks are relevant to the investigation. Id. Here, the district court found that the subpoenas in question met the enforcement criteria. Gilbreath has offered nothing to persuade us that the district court reached the wrong conclusion.

Cf. In re Subpoena in Collins, 524 F.3d 249 (D.C. Cir. 2008) (state-issued subpoena requiring a federal employee to testify about matters known through his work is subject to a sovereign immunity analysis, and the case may be removed to federal court for subpoena enforcement; a distinction is drawn in terms of sovereign immunity between information that is governmental and information of a private nature gained during the employment of the witness).
