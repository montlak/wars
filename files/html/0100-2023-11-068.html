b. Other Experts

Expert analysis included testimony of a handwriting expert whose testimony the Board relied on to demonstrate the source of anonymous writings. See Martinez and Gomez v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3001); Kane v. VA, 46 MSPR 203, 212 (1990) (handwriting expert's evidence concerning who affixed a signature to a grievance form). The Board does not require expert testimony on handwriting. The Board may draw inferences concerning by comparing handwriting styles to determine the likely authors of portions of documents. Vanderpool v. USPS, 56 MSPR 116, 118 (1992); see Ciccone v. Dept. of Air Force, 37 MSPR 594, 597 (1988) (same result). A supervisor may interpret agency audit reports even if he has not prepared them. See Harms v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 165, 166 (1986) (former supervisor was appropriate as expert witness to testify whether appellant's alleged performance failure constituted proper judgment under circumstances of agency charges); Armorer v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 103, 106 (1982) (evidence from supervisor evaluating audits performed by others of appellant's performance).

Expert testimony was allowed as to position classification in Mitchell v. DOD, 54 MSPR 641, 644 (1992), although the AJ gave the testimony little weight. The Supreme Court held permissible testimony from experts who can offer their conclusions pertaining to discriminatory employment practices. See Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775 (1989); cf. Holliman v. USPS, 75 MSPR 372, 374 (1997) (noting evidence from a psychologist who concluded that the appellant's management style created a potential for violence at the workplace). For a discussion of the factors used to evaluate expert scientific testimony, refer to Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 113 S. Ct. 2786 (1993). An AJ apparently permitted a criminal investigator to venture his opinion as to whether the appellant intended to distribute marijuana based on the investigator's long experience in investigating drug cases and his knowledge of the appellant's circumstances. See Williams v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 173, 176 & n.2 (1989).

A party might call an expert in the law, for example, an individual who wishes to give an opinion concerning regulatory or statutory interpretation. The witness could be an agency personnel specialist or an OPM official. In Hanks v. FEMA, 23 MSPR 567, 573 (1984), the agency enlisted the aid of an OPM official who testified about the meaning of an OPM regulation. The testimony was improper. It was the AJ's job to interpret law. Although OPM could provide advice to agencies, at a hearing the only proper function of an OPM official was to state whether OPM waived a particular regulation it had authority to enforce. If OPM wished to have its views considered, its proper course was to intervene and submit a memorandum of law. The Board determined that an AJ properly declined to allow a personnel specialist who was offered to testify about application of the agency's table of penalties. The Board hedged its ruling by noting that the agency failed to demonstrate how the result of the case would have changed had the personnel specialist's testimony been allowed. Basquez v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 215, 220 (1991). Another decision determined that an agency may offer testimony concerning the interpretation and application of an agency regulation; deference is usually given to the reasonable interpretation of the agency that administers the provision in question. Glencoe v. SBA, 29 MSPR 537, 539 (1985). A personnel specialist was relied upon to provide "expert" testimony as to an employee's competitive status, a jurisdictional issue. The AJ properly relied on affidavit evidence from personnel officials to straighten out confusing or conflicting notations on SF-50s in the employee's official personnel file. Lowe v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 223, 225-26 (1985). The expert in law can assist in a few situations: presentation of evidence concerning the reasonableness of a counsel fee request or explanation of foreign law.
