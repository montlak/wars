a. Proposed Action

Adverse actions come in two stages: the proposal and the decision. The appellant may initiate an IRA case with OSC concerning a proposal and later appeal to the Board from the ensuing decision. A proposal to take adverse action is a covered personnel action under § 2302(a)(2)(A). Bacas v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 464, 467 ¶ 5 (2005) (proposed removal). The resort to OSC on the proposal (treated as a threatened action) does not preclude direct appeal to the Board from the subsequent decision even though OSC has not closed out its case on the proposal, Douglass v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 1, 4-5 (1993):

...The Board does not have jurisdiction over a proposed action, other than as an individual right of action appeal. See Weber v. Department of the Army, 45 MSPR 406, 409 (1990). OSC, on the other hand, may investigate and order corrective action of a threatened personnel action. See 5 USC 1221(a); Gergick v. General Services Administration, 43 MSPR 651, 655-57 (1990), appeal after remand, 49 MSPR 384 (1991). Thus, the appellant had only one course of action on May 17, to seek corrective action with OSC. Although she stated in her request for corrective action that the personnel action at issue was her removal, she did not know for certain that she would be removed when she filed her request. The fact that the removal became effective four days later does not affect this analysis.

When the appellant filed her removal appeal with the Board, she appealed the effected removal, a different action, over which the Board has jurisdiction. Thus, the administrative judge should have adjudicated the appeal. Cf. Smith v. Department of Energy, 49 MSPR 110, 113-115 (1991) (an EEO complaint regarding different actions did not affect the jurisdiction of the Board to hear a demotion appeal); Coleman v. Department of Treasury, 22 MSPR 519, 520 (1984) (filing an EEO complaint before the effective date of the removal does not constitute filing a complaint on the removal absent a clear showing of the intent of the parties to act on the complaint as one concerning the on-going procedure including all steps of the removal action); but see Riggs v. Department of Defense, 48 MSPR 652, 656-57 (1991) (the language of the statute indicates that filing a grievance before the effective date of the removal, but after receipt of the removal notice, is sufficient to constitute an election between fora).

See 5 CFR 1209.2(b)(2) (Example 5) (2022); Westmoreland v. VA, 77 MSPR 315, 317-18 (1998) (resort to OSC concerning a proposal did not preclude a direct appeal to the MSPB concerning a removal decision); cf. Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 640 ¶ 98 (2001) (IRA complaint as to a proposal was denied when a removal decision was sustained through the agency's clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action without regard to whistleblowing activities); Krape v. DOD, 87 MSPR 126, 128-29 ¶¶ 6-8 (2000) (removal appeals were dismissed without prejudice pending the termination of an OSC investigation into the appellants' complaints of reprisal relative to proposed adverse actions).

It may seem desirable to take a proposal to OSC: a stay might be obtained; the matter might be settled. But if fortune does not favor the employee at OSC, care must be taken, when challenging a proposal by an IRA complaint to OSC, to avoid inadvertently merging the later removal decision into the OSC complaint, for in the ensuing appeal to the Board from the removal, important defenses may be lost. See 5 CFR 1209.2(b)(2) (Example 5) (2022). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Election and Preclusion of Remedies; Managers and Supervisors; Notice of Rights."]

The doctrine of mootness may affect the viability of an IRA claim based on a threatened action. The initiation of paperwork, apparently a SF-52, to terminate an appellant's position, was insufficient to sustain IRA jurisdiction when the agency decided not to process the requested personnel action, Shires v. DOD, 60 MSPR 13, 16-17 (1993):

In dismissing the portion of the IRA appeal pertaining to the agency's preparation of the form SF-50 as moot, the administrative judge relied upon the Board's decisions in Mulherin v. Dept. of Air Force, 45 MSPR 289 (1990), and Godfrey v. Dept. of Air Force, 45 MSPR 298 (1990). In these cases, the Board concluded that it would follow its holdings under pre-Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA) appellate jurisdiction cases, that an agency's rescission of an action divested the Board of jurisdiction over an appeal. Mulherin, at 291; Godfrey, at 302. In doing so, the Board rejected the appellants' assertions that rescission did not provide them with a complete remedy because they sought discipline for agency officials who retaliated against them. Mulherin, at 291-92; Godfrey, at 301-02.

Under these cases, the administrative judge correctly concluded that the agency's decision not to process the SF-52 rendered the IRA appeal of this matter moot. Further, the appellant's assertion that she has not received a complete remedy is unavailing for the same reasons discussed in Mulherin. There, the Board found that neither the language of the WPA, nor its legislative history, supported a finding that the cancelled actions, absent more, provided a sufficient basis for Board jurisdiction under the circumstances presented in those cases. Here, as in Mulherin, we find that there would be no further relief that we could grant the appellant even if we found her allegations of reprisal substantiated. We therefore conclude that the administrative judge correctly dismissed this portion of the IRA appeal as moot.

In reaching this conclusion, we note that both Mulherin and Godfrey involve personnel actions that were effected, rather than just threatened. Although threatened actions may, arguably, not be cancelled in the same sense that effected actions are cancelled, it is illogical for the Board to adjudicate an appeal of a threatened action after the threat has been withdrawn, even though it would not have adjudicated the appeal if the agency had effected the action before cancelling it.
