1. Nonappropriated Fund

Bowen v. Culotta, 294 F. Supp. 183 (E.D. Va. 1968), succinctly described the nature of a nonappropriated fund (NAF) employer:

A non-appropriated fund activity is one which the government has initially provided funds to permit it to begin operation. The governmental loan is repaid out of the profits earned by the activity. Thus, the activity is created by the government with governmental funds for governmental personnel, and is administered by governmental employees for the use and benefit of the United States.

Mann v. Haigh, 120 F.3d 34, 35 (4th Cir. 1997), added as to a NAFI (nonappropriated fund instrumentality):

...A NAFI is a branch of the federal government, and, as such, enjoys immunity from suit absent explicit waiver. AAFES v. Sheehan, 456 U.S. 728, 733-34, 102 S. Ct. 2118, 2121-22, 72 L. Ed. 2d 520 (1982). A nonappropriated fund instrumentality is usually one created by the government for use by government personnel. Military exchanges and similar entities are the major type of NAFIs. The instrumentality is generally one to which the government has provided funds, largely by loan, to initiate operations. The government loan is repaid out of the profits earned by the instrumentality's activity. Dupo v. OPM, 69 F.3d 1125, 1127 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 1995). NAFI employees are paid primarily from income generated by the activity itself. Hostetter v. United States, 739 F.2d 983, 986 n.2 (4th Cir. 1984).

With greater specificity, DeGrella v. Dept. of Air Force, 2022 MSPB 44, n.1 (2022):

A NAF employee is "a civilian employee who is paid from nonappropriated funds of Army and Air Force Exchange Service, Navy Exchange Service Command, Marine Corps exchanges, or any other instrumentality of the United States under the jurisdiction of the armed forces which is conducted for the comfort, pleasure, contentment, or physical or mental improvement of members of the armed forces." 10 U.S.C. § 1587(a)(1).

Taylor v. Dept. of Navy, 1 MSPR 591, 596, 2 MSPB 6 (1980), considered whether NAF employees are entitled to Board appellate procedures. 5 USC 7701(a) permits an employee to appeal to the Board from any action appealable to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation. 5 USC 2105(c) states that NAF employees are not deemed "employees" for the purpose of laws administered by OPM. Taylor argued that because appellate procedures under § 7701 are administered by the Board and not by OPM, the § 2105 exclusion was inapplicable to a NAF employee's appeal. He also argued that Board appellate jurisdiction should cover NAF employees because they are covered by the labor-relations provisions of the Reform Act. The Board disagreed, concluding that OPM is responsible for defining employee coverage. Because OPM did not extend coverage to NAF employees, the Board could not unilaterally extend that coverage and circumvent § 2105. NAF employees have no MSPB adverse action appeal right. See Lewis v. Dept. of Air Force, 31 MSPR 328, 331 (1986) ("[A]ppellant, as a nonappropriated fund employee, is not an employee for the purposes of 5 U.S.C. chapter 81, subchapter I, and therefore is not an employee subject to the restoration rights provided at 5 C.F.R. part 353. Appellant, therefore, does not have a right of appeal to the Board under 5 C.F.R. § 353.401 from the agency's denial of her request for restoration."). Taylor was extended to preclude Board jurisdiction over IRA cases brought by NAF employees in Clark v. AAFES, 57 MSPR 43, 45 (1993) [discussed in Chapter 13, "Status of Particular Agencies and Employees," concerning covered agencies in prohibited personnel practice cases] . Cf. Ayson v. OPM, 53 F.3d 1268, 1269 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (In the context of an annuity claim, with the exception of certain creditable service from 1952 through 1966, "Congress has excluded all NAFI employees from the civil service rules and regulations. See Act of June 19, 1952, Pub. L. No. 82-397, 66 Stat. 138 (1952)."); Suarez v. OPM, 58 MSPR 639, 643 (1993) (discussing retirement credit for NAF employees: "The legislative history of subsection 2105(c) states that civilian employees compensated from the nonappropriated funds of military exchanges and similar instrumentalities of the United States, 'shall not be held to be employees of the United States for purposes of laws administered by the Civil Service Commission.' 1952 USCCAN 1520, 1521-22.").

The place of NAF employees within the civil service scheme, and their exclusion from the protections of 5 USC Chapter 43, governing performance actions, was explained by the Federal Labor Relations Authority, deciding exceptions to an arbitration award, AFGE Local 2921 and DOD, AAFES, Dallas, 47 FLRA 446, 451 (1993):

The grievant in this case is employed by the Army and Air Force Exchange Service (AAFES). "The AAFES is a nonappropriated fund instrumentality [NAFI] of the United States, a term denoting an activity whose monies are not received by congressional appropriation, and whose employees are paid primarily from income generated by the activity itself." Perez v. AAFES, 680 F.2d 779, 780-81 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (footnotes omitted). Although it is undisputed that employees of AAFES, including the grievant in this case, are Federal employees, "it was never the intent of Congress that NAFI employees be entitled to the same levels of employment protection as other federal employees." McAuliffe v. Rice, 966 F.2d 979, 980 (5th Cir. 1992). In fact, Congress specifically provided in 5 USC 2105(c) that such NAFI employees are excluded from coverage of laws (other than subchapter IV of chapter 53 and sections 5550 and 7204 of Title 5 U.S. Code) administered by OPM. Where Congress has made NAFI employees subject to laws applicable to other Federal employees, it has done so "by expressly including [NAFI employees] within the coverage of specific laws." Perez v. AAFES, 680 F.2d at 787. See, for example, 5 USC 3326(b), 5531(2), 5342(a)(2)(B), 7103(a)(3) and 29 USC 203(e)(2)(iv).

In this case, the Union claims that the grievant, a NAFI employee, is covered by the performance appraisal system which Federal agencies are required to establish under 5 USC 4301-4305. As noted previously, agencies are obligated under this system to undertake various actions in order to provide employees with performance appraisals which will be used as a basis for training, rewarding, reassigning, promoting, reducing in rank and removing Federal employees. However, 5 USC 4301-4305 are administered by OPM. 5 USC 4305. As such, NAFI employees are excluded from coverage of 5 USC 4301-4305 by operation of 5 USC 2105(c) unless Congress otherwise expressly included them. In this respect, we note that the definition of employee set forth in 5 USC 4301(2) does not expressly include NAFI employees. Accordingly, we conclude that the grievant is not covered by 5 USC 4302 and we will deny the Union's exception that the award is inconsistent with this section.

See Johnston v. Dept. of Navy, DA-0432-16-0022-I-1 (NP 8/19/2016) (Board lacked jurisdiction over a performance-based appeal by a NAF employee).

The result is that employees of the Army and Air Force Exchange Service do not have Board appeal rights. Perez v. AAFES, 680 F.2d 779 (D.C. Cir. 1982); Ivy v. MSPB (5th Cir. 1981 NP No. 80-2337). Under Perez, it is immaterial that AAFES employees may have cases adjudicated under collective bargaining contracts under 5 USC 7121. That statute does not create independent MSPB jurisdiction over appeals of grievances involving prohibited personnel practices. The Board has jurisdiction under that statute only if it would otherwise have jurisdiction over both the action appealed and the appealing employee. See Vela v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3323) (demotion appeal of an employee of the Morale, Welfare, and Recreation Department, NAS Kingsville, Texas: "Employees of NAFIs are excluded from the definition of 'employee' under Title V of the United States Code."). In a successor appeal to the Vela demotion case, the Federal Circuit affirmed a Board decision concerning Vela's removal, Vela v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3101), noting as to the Board's jurisdiction over NAFI employees:

Employees of such a NAFI are expressly excluded from the definition of "employee" under the CSRA by 5 USC § 2105(c), which provides that, absent certain exceptions not applicable here, "an employee paid from non-appropriated funds [of the armed services for activities] conducted for the comfort, pleasure, contentment, and mental and physical improvement of personnel of the armed forces is deemed not an employee for the purpose of.... laws administered by the Office of Personnel Management." See McAuliffe v. Rice, 966 F.2d 979, 979-80 (5th Cir. 1992) ("As a NAFI employee, Ms. McAullife was statutorily excluded from the personnel scheme newly created by the CSRA.... [I]t was never the intent of Congress that NAFI employees be entitled to the same levels of employment protection as the other federal employees."); Perez v. Army, 680 F.2d 779, 785-87 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (holding that the removal of a NAFI employee is not appealable to the Board).
