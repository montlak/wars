c. Privity; Appellant as a Party to Prior Case

McNeil v. DOD, 100 MSPR 146, 152 ¶ 15 (2005), squarely embraced the concept of virtual representation:

...Specifically, collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when: (1) The issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action.

In oblique commentary on the necessity of privity of parties for collateral estoppel to operate against an appellant, Van Prichard v. DOD, 117 MSPR 88, 94-95 ¶¶ 11-13, 2011 MSPB 94 (2011) (aff'd, Prichard v. DOD, 484 Fed. Appx. 489 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP)), rejected the agency's argument concerning the validity of a particular performance standard litigated in another case involving a different appellant:

In its petition for review, the agency asserts that the issue of whether the agency's performance standards are valid is precluded by administrative collateral estoppel. It asserts that the issue of the validity of the agency's performance standards is identical in the instant case as in DeShields v. Department of Defense, MSPB Docket No. DA-0432-06-0559-I-1, Initial Decision (Nov. 28, 2006), aff'd, 105 M.S.P.R. 557 (2007) (Table), that the issue was actually litigated in DeShields, that the determination on the issue in DeShields was necessary to the resulting judgment, and that the appellant here has not "raised any performance standards legal issues that had not been finally decided by the Board in DeShields in 2007."

Collateral estoppel, or "issue preclusion," is appropriate when: (1) An issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action. Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Encarnado v. Office of Personnel Management, 116 M.S.P.R. 301, ¶ 13 (2011).

We have reviewed Critical Element #1 of the performance standard at issue in DeShields, which involved the removal of an Industrial Security Representative by the agency, as set forth in the Initial Decision in that matter. While the first paragraph of Critical Element #1 in DeShields appears to be identical to the Performance Plan at issue here, the performance plan in DeShields also included four sub elements to Critical Element #1 that do not appear in the Performance Plan submitted by the agency in the instant appeal. Thus, it is far from clear that the performance standards are identical in every respect. Further, there is nothing in DeShields that indicates whether the performance plan contained the same negative definition of the Marginal level of performance on which the administrative judge based her decision that the standards at issue here are invalid. Moreover, there is nothing in the initial decision in DeShields that suggests that Mr. DeShields challenged the validity of the performance standards applied to him, or, if he did so challenge the validity of the standards, the extent or the details of his challenge. Thus, even if the performance standards at issue were identical, the validity was actually litigated in DeShields, and the determination was necessary to the resulting judgment in DeShields, we cannot say that the appellant's interests were fully represented in that action with respect to challenging the validity of the performance standards. See Encarnado, 116 M.S.P.R. 301, ¶ 13. Accordingly, the agency's assertion that the appellant is barred from relitigating the validity of the performance standards at issue here by collateral estoppel is without merit.

Before McNeil, collateral estoppel occasionally required identity of parties from one case to another. If the appellant was to be bound by a prior proceeding, he must have been a party to that proceeding or his interests must have been protected. In Aulik v. USPS, 1 MSPR 501, 502, 1 MSPB 485 (1980), the appellant was demoted as a result of a grievance brought by another employee. In his appeal, the appellant challenged the grievance decision and claimed that his interests were not adequately represented during the grievance process. The AJ did not relitigate the issues of the grievance and framed the issue as whether the grievance decision constituted a proper basis for downgrading the appellant. The Board reversed and remanded, noting that the burden was on the agency to justify the downgrading. To the extent that meeting the burden required a decision on the issues involved in the prior grievance, the agency was required to reestablish those matters or otherwise establish that the appellant was estopped from challenging the grievance decision. For that estoppel to occur the appellant must have been a party or otherwise protected in the grievance case. Since Aulik was not in that position he was not estopped from relitigating the issues presented in the grievance. An appellant was not bound by findings in a sentencing proceeding in a criminal court when the appellant, whose conduct may arguably have been reviewed in court proceedings leading to the conviction of another individual, was not a party to the sentencing proceeding. Bledsoe v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 93, 100 ¶ 14 (2002). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Personal Fault" and "Merit Promotion Grievance."]

As to being "fully represented" in the prior action, under Seward v. VA, DA-3443-11-0638-I-1 (NP 4/27/2012), "The last element is satisfied even though an appellant represents herself. See Fisher v. Department of Defense, 64 M.S.P.R. 509, 515 (1994) (this requirement does not refer to whether the party was represented by an attorney, but rather whether their interests were fully represented in the action)."
