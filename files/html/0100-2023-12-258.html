a. Issue Preclusion

If an issue was not raised first with an arbitrator, the Board will not review the issue (excepting EEO issues under practice before 2012, when an EEO issue could first be raised with the Board). The Board declined to permit an appellant to raise issues not involving discrimination for the first time with the Board in Means v. Dept. of Labor, 60 MSPR 108, 115-16 (1993). The appellant contended that the agency disciplined him in retaliation for his grievances. The union did not assert this claim before the arbitrator. The union did not present any evidence on this issue, and the arbitrator made no finding on it. There was nothing to review:

It is a well-established principle, in private-sector labor relations, that a party who is dissatisfied with an arbitrator's decision may not seek to set aside or modify the decision on grounds not raised before the arbitrator. See, e.g., Automobile Mechanics Local 701 v. Joe Mitchell Buick, Inc., 930 F.2d 576, 578 (7th. Cir. 1991); International Chemical Workers Union v. Mobay Chemical Corp., 755 F.2d 1107, 1112 (4th Cir. 1985); United Steelworkers of America v. SmokeCraft, Inc., 652 F.2d 1356, 1360 (9th Cir. 1981), cert. denied, 455 U.S. 1021 (1982). Similarly, where a party to arbitration involving a federal agency files an exception to the arbitrator's award with the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA), the FLRA will not consider evidence or issues that were not presented before the arbitrator. See 5 USC 7122(a); 5 CFR 2429.5. We adopt the same approach, and hold that the appellant may not argue for the first time in this proceeding that the agency retaliated against him for filing grievances.

We acknowledge that an employee may raise for the first time, on review of an arbitrator's decision under 5 USC 7121(d), a claim that the agency's action was based on a prohibited form of discrimination. Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 898 F.2d 133 (Fed. Cir. 1990), cert. denied, 112 S. Ct. 1600 (1992). The Jones court based its decision on the plain language of 5 USC 7121(d), which explicitly provides for a right of review of an arbitrator's decision, "pursuant to [5 USC] 7702," where an employee who was subjected to an action that could have been appealed to the Board elected to pursue a grievance. 898 F.2d at 134-35. As the court observed, section 7702(a)(1) provides that where an employee who was subjected to an action appealable to the Board claims that the action was based on a prohibited form of discrimination, "the Board shall within 120 days of the filing of [an] appeal [by the employee], decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action..." The court thus inferred that sections 7121(d) and 7702, taken together, permit an employee to raise a discrimination claim for the first time before the Board in an arbitration review proceeding. 898 F.2d at 135.

By contrast, sections 7121(d) and 7702 make no reference to retaliation for protected union activities. Indeed, section 7121(d), by its terms, applies only to an employee who believes that he was "affected by a prohibited personnel practice under [5 USC] 2302(b)(1)." Section 2302(b)(1) relates exclusively to various forms of prohibited discrimination, whereas retaliation for filing grievances is prohibited under 5 USC 2302(b)(9)(A). Thus, there is no statutory basis for interpreting Jones as allowing matters other than discrimination to be raised for the first time in a review proceeding brought under 5 USC 7121(d).

Established policy also requires a restrictive reading of Jones. Review of an arbitrator's decision, whether it be before the FLRA or the Board, is highly deferential. See 5 USC 7122(a); Moore, 55 MSPR at 457. Such narrow review reflects a legislative judgment that, when an employee chooses to grieve an agency action, an arbitrator's decision on the grievance will be accorded finality, unless the arbitrator made an error in interpreting either public law or a collective bargaining agreement. An expansive reading of Jones would run counter to this policy favoring finality.

Finally, an expansive reading of Jones would do violence to the notion that an employee may challenge an agency action "under a statutory procedure or [a] negotiated grievance procedure, but not both." 5 USC 7121(d) (emphasis supplied). If Jones were not limited to allegations of discrimination, an employee could raise one set of arguments before an arbitrator, and if they proved unsuccessful, could then raise an entirely different set of arguments before the Board; the Board proceeding, with respect to the newly-raised arguments, would amount to de novo review of the underlying agency action. Further, such a scheme ostensibly would require reopening of the record, if the newly-raised arguments depended on evidence that had not been introduced before the arbitrator. There is no indication that Congress, in enacting 5 USC 7121(d), intended such a scheme.
