C. REASONABLENESS OF FEES

"Reasonableness is the overarching standard by which an attorney fee should be measured." Mansfield v. OPM, 73 MSPR 602, 608 (1997). "A party requesting an award of attorney fees and costs must show that the amount claimed--including the billing rates and the number of hours of work claimed--is reasonable." Santella v. Special Counsel, 86 MSPR 48, 66 ¶ 36 (2000), reconsideration denied, 90 MSPR 172 (2001). "Reasonableness of fees is determined, in part, by the quality and length of the work product produced rather than on the strength or weakness of the position defeated." Weaver v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 565, 568 (1985). Agency lawyers, particularly those who do not handle much litigation that results in counsel fees, decry the fees that are demanded in fee petitions. How, one wonders, can a lawyer be worth $300-400 an hour or more (the Laffey Rate for senior counsel is over $700 an hour)? The real, but unstated, problem in this business is that private counsel earn their living through fees and the level of their fees sets the level of their yearly earnings. If counsel decides he wants to bill 2,000 hours a year and earn $200,000, the fee that will probably be charged will exceed $200 per hour, given office overhead, uncollected bills, insurance costs, and retirement contributions. An attorney who wants to earn the same or more and work fewer hours will charge more. If the rate is challenged as unreasonable, the challenge really goes to the compensation that counsel feels is merited for his work and his standing in the community and at the bar. There are no easy solutions, unless Congress establishes by law a maximum and predictable fee level (as with the Equal Access to Justice Act) that can be awarded in proceedings involving the government.

Fee awards are increasing and some of the largest awards result from settlements. [Refer later in this chapter to "Examples of Fee Awards."] The representational structure in the civil service arena is much different than it was 25 years ago. Many lawyers are entering the field and some specialized small law firms devote their principal energies to this work. Unions, whose lawyers may secure market rates, are concentrating their energies on Board appeals and arbitration cases that have the potential for generating considerable recovery of counsel fees. The use of Laffey rates, subsequently discussed, makes the fee structure more attractive. To obtain fees, a merits decision is not required; a settlement agreement can produce a fee award. When there is a settlement agreement that provides for fees, or if there is a Board decision awarding fees, the government pays the bill. The same cannot always be said for clients. It can be expected that as more lawyers find that they have sufficient time and expertise to handle Board cases, more time will be devoted to administrative litigation and the fee demands will continue to increase. A by-product of the litigation over counsel fees is the development of an increasing body of case law. The area of counsel fees produces more substantive law from the Board in some years than nexus, mitigation, and hearing procedures combined. Examined here are some decisions concerning the reasonableness of fee demands that have been litigated before the Board.

The Board is responsible for determining the reasonableness of fees. It is not bound by the agreement of the parties. McLane v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 404, 407 (1987). "The number of hours reasonably expended on a case multiplied by a reasonable hourly rate is the lodestar used by the Board in fee calculations." Foley v. USPS, 59 MSPR 413, 423 (1993). Reductions in fee requests may be made based on the AJ's experience with the case and with other similar cases. See Wilson v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 158, 162 (1987). Under O'Donnell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 445, 455, 2 MSPB 604 (1980), a Reform Act fee award must not provide a windfall to counsel at the expense of the public fisc. From this proposition, the Board has developed a plethora of cases resulting in confusion over the calculation of the amount of fees for the few appellants who not only win their cases but demonstrate fee entitlement. The Board started by essentially adopting the fee actually charged to the client, as a measure of a reasonable fee, but then went on to complicate matters in decisions as cumbersome for appellants as they are for agency counsel and judges. Before addressing these convolutions, a few preliminary observations are offered.

If the rate is reasonable and the fees are justified under the statutory standard, the Board will not cut the fee award because the amount recovered by the appellant is small. In Koenig v. OPM, 13 MSPR 538, 542-43 (1982), the appellant prevailed in an appeal from the denial of a within-grade increase. Although the amount involved in the step increase was not large, probably amounting to less than $1,000, the Board granted a fee award of about $3,000, rejecting OPM's argument that the fee should be granted to the extent justified by the monetary recovery. The Board noted that more is at stake than the step increase denial, often the prelude to an adverse action or performance-based action. Woodall v. FERC, 33 MSPR 127, 131-32 (1987), reaffirmed that a small recovery does not limit the appropriate counsel fee. Nor does ordinary fee entitlement depend upon whether the case was difficult or a breeze. In Weaver v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 565, 568 (1985), the Board observed that reasonableness of fees is determined, in part, by the quality and length of the work product rather than on the strength or weakness of the position defeated. See Rhea v. OPM, 59 MSPR 254, 259 (1993) ("The evidence before OPM at the time it issued its reconsideration decision is relevant only to the appellant's entitlement to attorney fees, and not to the amount of fees to be awarded. The amount of the attorney fee award will be governed by the 'lodestar' figure[.] "). But see Stein v. USPS, 65 MSPR 685, 690 (1994) (when litigation is principally over money, e.g., fee entitlement, if fees are appropriate, they will be paid in the proportion that the results obtained compare to the amount demanded); cf. Farrar v. Hobby, 113 S. Ct. 566 (1992) (a possibility of a reduction of fees for diminished results).

Past practice of the Board was generally to award all or nothing (the award that reduces a request as a result of an unreasonably high rate or an unreasonably large investment of time was another matter). But the approach is changing. Thomson v. MSPB, 772 F.2d 879 (Fed. Cir. 1985), concluded that the Board incorrectly applied a per se rule rejecting fee awards in factually close cases. The appeal was remanded to the Board to "determine whether full fees, no fees, or partial fees should be allowed." Thomson was followed by VanFossen v. MSPB, 788 F.2d 748 (Fed. Cir. 1986), directing payment of fees for a gentleman whose removal was mitigated to a suspension, but noting that the Board could make a small deduction in the fees to compensate for the imposition of some penalty. Del Prete v. USPS, 104 MSPR 429, 431-37 ¶¶ 3-17, 2007 MSPB 13 (2007), although analytically confusing in its analysis, suggests reduction in fees when charges are sustained but a penalty is mitigated. For a case correlating the degree of success and the amount of a fee award, refer to Stein v. USPS, 65 MSPR 685, 690 (1994), post. The Federal Circuit, although not altogether deferential to the Board on issues of the reasonableness of fees, can affirm the Board without becoming engaged in extensive analysis of debated points. Representative of a circuit decision is Bullion v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3004):

In reviewing the board's decision of what a "reasonable" fee award is, we have likened the board to a district judge entertaining a request for statutory fees. Crumbaker v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 195 (Fed. Cir. 1986), modified, 827 F.2d 761 (Fed. Cir. 1987). The deciding official "has to make a judgment--considering the nature of the case and the details of the request," and "defending his judgment in a reasoned (though brief) opinion...." Id. (citations omitted). The evidence supports the AJ's conclusion that the increase in Bullion's attorney's billing rate was unwarranted and that the lower billing rate originally agreed upon was reasonable. In addition, the board's conclusion that Bullion failed to justify the presence of an additional attorney at the hearing, while debatable, was not reversible, and the resulting reduction in the fee award must stand.
