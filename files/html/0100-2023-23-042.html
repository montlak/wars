d. Lack of Understanding

The appellant cannot readily disclaim lack of knowledge or understanding of his settlement, Devera v. Smithsonian Inst., 217 Fed. Appx. 964, 966 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Before the Board, Mr. DeVera essentially argued that he was induced to settle based upon bad advice from his attorney and an alleged failure by the agency to explain the scope of the release contained in the settlement agreement. However, Mr. DeVera agreed that he had read the settlement agreement and understood its terms. Moreover, even if true, Mr. DeVera's allegations are insufficient to show that the settlement agreement was involuntary. Accordingly, Mr. DeVera is bound by the settlement agreement and not entitled to any relief.

Montoya v. USPS, 40 MSPR 314, 317-18 (1989), stated as to the terms of a last chance agreement allegedly unknown to the appellant:

[T]he appellant contends that he was denied due process, and reiterates his contention that he should not have been bound by the last chance agreement because it was entered into by his union representative without informing him of its specific terms and conditions....

...The appellant has presented no evidence to show that he was unaware of his representatives' efforts to reach an agreement with the agency or that he was personally uninvolved in the negotiations leading to the agreement. Nor has he presented any proof that his representatives were not authorized to secure an agreement on his behalf. Further, the appellant has not shown that the execution of the agreement was in any way the result of duress or bad faith on the agency's part....

...The inadequate exchange of information between the appellant and his representatives, without more, does not provide a basis for setting aside the agreement or for making its terms unenforceable.... The appellant was responsible for the actions and inactions of his designated representatives, and therefore must suffer the consequences of any deficiencies....

In Montoya, the Board at least implied that the appellant could be held to the terms of the last chance agreement even if he was unaware of them, 40 MSPR at 318:

Although the parties stipulated that the appellant was not aware of the specific provisions of the last chance agreement prior to his car trouble and consequent tardiness, the evidence does not support a finding that he was not aware of the consequences of any further misconduct on his part. The agreement states that the appellant was to incur "no instances of Absence Without Leave (AWOL)." This language is unambiguous and plainly was meant to cover the type of unexcused absence that occurred here. Moreover, the appellant, through his representatives, explicitly agreed to the terms of the agreement, and the agreement functioned exactly as the parties had intended, i.e., as a "last chance" agreement. See Gonzales, 38 MSPR at 166, 167, citing Tootsie Roll Industries v. Local Union No. 1, Bakery, Confectionery and Tobacco Workers' International Union, 832 F.2d 81 (7th Cir. 1987) (even though the term of the last chance agreement which states that the employee would be discharged if she was absent for any reason whatsoever is very harsh and perhaps may be viewed as unreasonable, the parties agreed to it and the contract is exactly what it purports to be--a last chance agreement).

See Williams v. MSPB, 350 Fed. Appx. 464, 467 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) ("Her active involvement in the discussions shows that she was aware of her options, weighed them, and voluntarily chose the benefits afforded by resignation (rather than removal) and expungement of her 'unacceptable performance' rating"); Griffin v. MSPB, 263 Fed. Appx. 867, 870-71 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (Board properly considered negotiating demands made by appellant during the course of settlement negotiations to determine what appellant knew concerning the intent and scope of operation of the agreement that was finally reached); Fried v. VA, DA-0752-13-4649-I-1 (NP 5/15/2014) ("The appellant in this case is arguing at most that she did not realize the magnitude of relief that she could have obtained had she prevailed on the merits or that she could have bargained for additional terms to the agreement. However, an appellant's lack of legal acumen does not provide a basis for setting aside a settlement agreement, see Wade v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 580, 583 (1994), nor does an appellant's change of heart or post-settlement remorse, Hinton, 119 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 4. Although the appellant is a pro se non-attorney, there is no indication that she lacks the legal capacity to enter into a contract. See Potter v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 374, ¶ 5 (2009) (a party to a settlement agreement is presumed to have full legal capacity to contract unless she is mentally disabled, and the mental disability is so severe that she cannot form the necessary intent)."); Pugh v. USPS, 91 MSPR 578, 582 ¶ 7 (2002) (party to a settlement was presumed to have the full legal capacity to contract, when there was no guardianship, infancy, mental illness, or intoxication; a diagnosis of paranoid schizophrenia was insufficient to set aside a settlement when the evidence did not show a lack of comprehension and control of the illness through medication); Thompson v. VA, 52 MSPR 233, 236 (1992) ("alleged inadequate exchange of information between the appellant and her representative does not provide a basis to set aside the settlement agreement").
