1. Loss or Destruction of Evidence

If an agency destroys or loses evidence that could have helped the appellant, for example, in demonstrating disparate treatment, the harmful error rule is applied. The adjudicator may consider whether the evidence might have been developed through secondary sources, e.g., through witnesses rather than documents, had the appellant chosen to pursue the defense. See Hanratty v. Dept. of Transp., 819 F.2d 286 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Sullivan v. Dept. of Navy, 13 MSPR 413, 417 (1982) (in determining whether to impose a sanction for loss of evidence, the Board considers the culpability of the agency, evidence of the presence or absence of bad faith, and the availability of testimony as to the destroyed evidence). If a sanctions motion is filed based on spoliation of evidence, the moving party should not wait to file the motion until right before the hearing if the motion could have been earlier filed. An AJ could deny the motion if its consideration would likely delay hearing. Herman v. DOJ, 119 MSPR 642, 647 ¶ 9, 2013 MSPB 60 (2013) ("Although the administrative judge does not appear to have explicitly set a deadline for filing such a motion, he would likely have had to delay the hearing if he had granted it to the extent that the adverse inferences would have affected the agency's litigation strategy.").

In Ivery v. Dept. of Transp., 96 MSPR 119, 127-28 ¶¶ 19-22 (2004), the agency failed to follow a drug testing split sample procedure. The Board reversed the appellant's removal, with an interesting variant on the harmful error rule, determining that "an agency's procedural error may constitute harmful error when it effectively destroys, or precludes an appellant from acquiring, the only available evidence by which he can show that the agency likely would have reached a different conclusion in the absence of its error":

The agency bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that the test on which it relied to remove the appellant was valid. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(1)(ii) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1)(ii)]; Boykin v. U.S. Postal Service, 51 M.S.P.R. 56, 58 (1991). Here, as previously noted, DOT Order 3910.1C provides that the split-specimen procedures set forth by 49 C.F.R. part 40 "must be used for all drug testing of DOT employees and applicants for the test to be valid." The agency's own rules and regulations dictate that its failure to afford the appellant the right to pursue split-specimen testing following his positive test result for adulteration rendered the agency's drug testing of the appellant's urine invalid. Thus, the sole charge underlying its removal action, which is based on the appellant's positive test for adulteration, cannot be sustained. See Boykin, 51 M.S.P.R. at 58.

Moreover, in reviewing an appellant's claim that an agency has violated procedures in administering a drug test, the Board has held that the harmful error standard is appropriate. See Storm v. Department of the Army, 64 M.S.P.R. 14, 22-24 (1994) (the appellant failed to show that the 23-day delay by the agency in reporting the results of his drug test, instead of reporting the results within 5 days under HHS guidelines, constituted harmful error); Vincent v. Department of Transportation, 47 M.S.P.R. 550, 555 (1991) (the Board applied the harmful error analysis to HHS guidelines). Harmful error under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(A) cannot be presumed; rather, the appellant must show that the agency committed an error in applying procedures that, in the absence or cure of the error, would have been likely to cause the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it reached. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(r)]; Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 681, 685 (1991); Vincent, 47 M.S.P.R. at 555.

In this case, the appellant has not shown that, had the agency performed a split-specimen test on Bottle B, as required by 49 C.F.R. part 40, it would have likely reached a conclusion different from the one it initially reached, or, more specifically, that Bottle B would not have also likely tested positive for chromium. However, an agency's procedural error may constitute harmful error when it effectively destroys, or precludes an appellant from acquiring, the only available evidence by which he can show that the agency likely would have reached a different conclusion in the absence of its error. See Storm, 64 M.S.P.R. at 23 (citing Banks v. Federal Aviation Administration, 687 F.2d 92, 96 (5th Cir. 1982) (a testing laboratory's disposal of urine samples required the suppression of the positive results of its tests in the appellants' removal appeals because the samples were not available to the appellants for their independent testing and inspection)). Here, it is clear that the agency's denial of the appellant's request for the required split-specimen testing procedures on Bottle B effectively precluded the appellant from acquiring the only available evidence by which he could prove that the agency likely would have reached a different conclusion in the absence of its error. Thus, we find that the agency's failure to honor the appellant's request for split-specimen testing constitutes harmful error. Cf. Storm, 64 M.S.P.R. at 23-24 (no harmful error shown where the urine sample was available for the appellant's independent testing and inspection and was retested and confirmed its original positive test result for marijuana).

Accordingly, we REVERSE the initial decision and DO NOT SUSTAIN the agency's removal action.

The action will not be reversed if files were destroyed as part of the record maintenance process without fraudulent or malicious conduct by the agency.
