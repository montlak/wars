CHAPTER 5

SUBJECTS OF BARGAINING:
SUBSTANTIVE LIMITATIONS ON NEGOTIABILITY DETERMINATIONS

I. INTRODUCTION

The determination of a negotiability appeal depends on the interplay of several statutory concepts, principally outlined at 5 USC 7117, 7103, and 7106, post. Speaking generally, "if a proposed matter relates to the conditions of employment of an appropriate unit of employees in an agency and is not inconsistent with law or regulation--i.e., is within the discretion of an agency--it is within the scope of bargaining which is required of that agency." VA Cent. Office, VAMC, Long Beach and California Nurses' Ass'n, 9 FLRA 325, 328 (1982). Not every case requires complex analysis, but implicit in each case is that the proposal at issue relates to a condition of employment. If that test is met or if there is no contest on the issue, the next question is whether the proposal is specifically covered by statute or barred, either by a government-wide regulation or by an agency regulation supported by a compelling need. Should the proposal survive those tests, the proposal is negotiable unless it infringes to any degree upon one of several enumerated management rights. If so, the analysis turns to whether the proposal is negotiable as a procedure or as an appropriate arrangement for the employees whose working conditions are affected by management's exercise of its reserved rights. Negotiations over procedures that management follows when it Implements its statutory management authority, and negotiations over arrangements for employees affected by the exercise of management rights, are collectively referred to as impact and implementation bargaining. Added to the matters that are negotiable as arrangements or procedures are what are described as permissive subjects of bargaining, that is, certain statutorily defined matters concerning agency organization and methodology of performing work that the agency may bargain if it so elects. If there are no negotiability objections, or if those objections are resolved in the union's favor by FLRA, assuming no other impediments to negotiations, the parties bargain on the proposal. Refer the FLRA General Counsel's September 10, 1998, Guidance in Determining Whether Union Bargaining Proposals are Within the Scope of Bargaining Under the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute, at FLRA's website, www.flra.gov. [Guidance from the General Counsel was rescinded in 2006.] Refer to the FLRA Guide to Negotiability, https://www.flra.gov/system/files/webfm/Authority/NG%20Forms,%20Guide,%20Other/Negotiability%20Guide%206-17-13.pdf. If the parties cannot agree on bargainable proposals, the Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service may assist in resolution, and, lacking resolution, the Federal Service Impasses Panel stands ready to break the deadlock. Specific terms and conditions of employment subject to negotiation are addressed in the next chapter.

DOD, Army Garrison, Yongsan v. FLRA, 685 F.2d 641, 664 (D.C. Cir. 1982), summarized some substantive and procedural aspects of federal sector collective bargaining:

[T]he duty...to bargain in good faith...is central.... Once an agency has recognized a union as the exclusive representative of the employees in a bargaining unit, the two sides must "meet and negotiate in good faith for the purpose of arriving at a collective bargaining agreement."... The duty to bargain includes any "condition of employment," § 7114(b)(2), which in turn is defined to include "personnel policies, practices, and matters, whether established by rule, regulation, or otherwise, affecting working conditions." Id. § 7103(a)(14).

The scope of the duty to bargain in the context of federal labor relations is subject to a number of important limitations.... First, the definition of a "condition of employment" excludes matters to the extent that they are "specifically provided for by Federal statute," id. § 7103(a)(14)(C); and the duty to bargain includes matters covered by federal rules or regulations, but only "to the extent not inconsistent with any Federal law or any Government-wide rule or regulation." Id. § 7117(a)(1). Second, the "management rights" section of the statute reserves to agencies the exclusive authority to determine their "internal security practices," id. § 7106(a)(1), although this reservation is hedged somewhat in that "[n]othing in this section shall preclude any agency and any labor organization from negotiating...procedures which management officials of the agency will observe in exercising any authority under this section." Id. § 7106(b)(2).

The Statute outlines the basic determinants of the bargaining equation. Under 5 USC 7114:

(b) The duty of an agency and an exclusive representative to negotiate in good faith under subsection (a) of this section shall include the obligation--

...

(2) to be represented at the negotiations by duly authorized representatives prepared to discuss and negotiate on any condition of employment[.]

As to conditions of employment, the framework for analysis is in the definitional section, 5 USC 7103(a)(14):

"[C]onditions of employment" means personnel policies, practices, and matters, whether established by rule, regulation, or otherwise, affecting working conditions, except that such term does not include policies, practices, and matters--

(A) relating to political activities prohibited under subchapter III of chapter 73 of this title;

(B) relating to the classification of any position; or

I     to the extent such matters are specifically provided for by Federal statute[.]

Next, concerning the effect of government regulations on the subject matter of bargaining, 5 USC 7117 provides:

(a)(2) The duty to bargain in good faith shall, to the extent not inconsistent with Federal law or any Government-wide rule or regulation, extend to matters which are the subject of any agency rule or regulation referred to in paragraph (3) of this subsection only if the Authority has determined under subsection (b) of this section that no compelling need (as determined under regulations prescribed by the Authority) exists for the rule or regulation.

(3) Paragraph (2) of the subsection applies to any rule or regulation issued by any agency or issued by any primary national subdivision of such agency, unless an exclusive representative represents an appropriate unit including not less than a majority of the employees in the issuing agency or primary national subdivision, as the case may be, to whom the rule or regulation is applicable.

(b)(1) In any case of collective bargaining in which an exclusive representative alleges that no compelling need exists for any rule or regulation referred to in subsection (a)(3) of this section which is then in effect and which governs any matter at issue in such collective bargaining, the Authority shall determine under paragraph (2) of this subsection, in accordance with regulations prescribed by the Authority, whether such a compelling need exists.

As to management rights, 5 USC 7106 establishes limits on bargaining:

(a) Subject to subsection (b) of this section, nothing in this chapter shall affect the authority of any management official of any agency--

(1) to determine the mission, budget, organization, number of employees, and internal security practices of the agency; and

(2) in accordance with applicable laws--

(A) to hire, assign, direct, layoff, and retain employees in the agency, or to suspend, remove, reduce in grade or pay, or take other disciplinary action against such employees;

(B) to assign work, to make determinations with respect to contracting out, and to determine the personnel by which agency operations shall be conducted;

I     with respect to filling positions, to make selections for appointments from--

(i) among properly ranked and certified candidates for promotion; or

(ii) any other appropriate source, and

(D) to take whatever actions may be necessary to carry out the agency mission during emergencies.

Having defined certain conditions of employment as negotiable and having defined the blocking effect of certain government regulations and nonnegotiable management rights, § 7106 of the Statute also establishes the scope of permissible bargaining and bargaining obligations when employment conditions are affected through the exercise of reserved management rights:

(b) Nothing in this section shall preclude any agency and any labor organization from negotiating--

(1) at the election of the agency, on the numbers, types, and grades of employees or positions assigned to any organizational subdivision, work project, or tour of duty, or on the technology, methods, and means of performing work;

(2) procedures which management officials of the agency will observe in exercising any authority under this section; or

(3) appropriate arrangements for employees adversely affected by the exercise of any authority under this section by such management officials.

Negotiation over procedures and arrangements, referred to as impact and implementation or arrangements bargaining, stands as an exception to the management rights established at 5 USC 7106(a).

The interaction of these statutory provisions is the subject of considerable, not altogether consistent, analysis and commentary by FLRA and the courts. In summary, management officials of the federal agencies must bargain with employee representatives over conditions of employment. But the duty to engage in collective bargaining is not absolute. Setting aside for the moment the impact of government regulations, § 7106 of the Reform Act enumerates certain reserved rights of management that cannot be lawful subjects of negotiation. It is permissible to bargain over the "procedures" governing the exercise of those rights but not over the substance of the rights themselves. Compare DOD, AAFES v. FLRA, 659 F.2d 1140, 1151 (D.C. Cir. 1981) (proposals concerning disciplinary procedures, temporary promotions, and details: "[t]he difficulty arises, of course, because the distinction between procedure and substance is not always crisp"), with NTEU v. FLRA, 691 F.2d 553, 564 (D.C. Cir. 1982) ("the agency construction of Section 7106(a)...does not preclude bargaining...on matters not directly related to the substantive components of performance standards or critical elements of the job"). Arrangements bargaining, it will be seen, permits negotiation to ameliorate the impact of the exercise of management rights. As to matters negotiable at the election of an agency under § 7106(b)(1), so-called permissive subjects of bargaining, agencies have absolute discretion as to matters covered by the section before final agreement. They may consider proposals, confer with the union about them, and then withdraw them from consideration prior to final agreement. AFGE, Nat'l INS Council and DOJ, INS, 8 FLRA 347, 382 (1982) (Union Proposal 15).

That a proposal is consistent with law, or even if it duplicates an agency regulation or policy, does not ensure its negotiability. To lock agency rules or policies into a contract divests the agency of the ability to change those policies. See, e.g., AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council and DOJ, INS, 40 FLRA 521, 527 (1991). [Refer later in this chapter to "Proposal Incorporating Statutory Requirements" and "Proposal to Enforce Agency Regulations and Policies."] That proposals affect significant public policy issues does not lessen their negotiability. The statutory guidelines, not the significance of the issues presented, govern negotiability. See DOJ, INS and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 55 FLRA 892, 897 (1999) (rejecting agency's claim that proposals pertaining to use of force by law enforcement agents were matters of important public policy exempt from negotiation). That a proposal directly implicates the rights of supervisory employees does not exclude it from the negotiability process. The union is still entitled to a decision on whether the agency must negotiate the proposal. See NATCA, Rochester Local and Dept. of Transp., FAA, 56 FLRA 288, 291 (2000). The bottom line is that there are very few bottom lines concerning negotiability. Each case is examined on its own, making the process of ascertaining negotiability relatively difficult for novel proposals.

Before reaching specific statutory guidelines and contract proposals, it may be helpful to examine some general pronouncements from FLRA and the courts concerning the scope of employment conditions, management rights, and negotiable procedures.
