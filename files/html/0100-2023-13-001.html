CHAPTER 6

ADVERSE ACTIONS

I. ADVERSE ACTIONS DEFINED

In this Guide we speak of adverse actions, and for our discussion those are disciplinary cases within the jurisdiction of the Board. 5 USC 7512 and 5 USC 7513(d) place within the Board's jurisdiction an employee's removal, suspension for more than 14 days, reduction in grade, reduction in pay, and a furlough of 30 days or less. According to the Board's FY 2019 Annual Report, adverse actions account for a little less than half of the Board's appeals. Although we refer to adverse actions as the disciplinary cases within the Board's ordinary appellate jurisdiction, Chapter 75 of Title 5 of the U.S. Code is entitled "Adverse Actions," and within Chapter 75 are: Subchapter I, defining procedures to be followed for suspensions of 14 days or less; Subchapter III, involving actions against administrative law judges; Chapter IV, concerning actions taken against employees for reasons of national security; and Subchapter V, covering actions involving members of the Senior Executive Service. Under OPM regulations, 5 CFR 752.201-03 (2022), covering suspensions of less than 14 days, refer to "actions covered," while the regulations describing the more severe disciplinary actions with the Board's jurisdiction, 5 CFR 752.401-06 (2022), refer to "adverse actions" covered. Nuance and linguistics aside, our references to adverse actions in the Guide will be to the several actions statutorily placed within the Board's jurisdiction.

The basic analysis in an adverse action appeal is supplied by Leija v. VA, 530 Fed. Appx. 945, 951 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP):

...A governmental agency, such as the VA, may discipline and remove an employee under Chapter 75 "for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service." 5 U.S.C. § 7513(a). To support taking such adverse action against an employee for committing misconduct, an agency must: (1) "establish by preponderant evidence that the charged conduct occurred"; (2) "show a nexus between that conduct and the efficiency of the service"; and (3) "demonstrate that the penalty imposed was reasonable in light of the relevant factors set forth in Douglas v. Veterans Admin., 5 MSPB 313, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 307-08 (1981)." Malloy v. U.S. Postal Serv., 578 F.3d 1351, 1356 (Fed. Cir. 2009); see King v. Frazier, 77 F.3d 1361, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 1996); Miller v. Dep't of the Interior, 119 M.S.P.R. 438, 441-42 (M.S.P.B. 2013)....

See Pope v. USPS, 114 F.3d 1144, 1147 (Fed. Cir. 1997) ("[A]n agency must establish three things to withstand challenge to an adverse action against an employee. First, it must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the charged conduct occurred. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B) (1994). Second, the agency must establish a nexus between that conduct and the efficiency of the service. 5 U.S.C. § 7513(a) (1994); Hayes v. Department of Navy, 727 F.2d 1535, 1539 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Third, it must demonstrate that the penalty imposed is reasonable. See Douglas v. Veterans Admin., 5 MSPB 313, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 306-07 (1981)."); Sarratt v. USPS 90 MSPR 405, 409 ¶ 6 (2001) (restating the rule, adding that once the agency learns that employee is fit for duty, the employee must be restored immediately to active duty status; citing Mercer v. DHHS, 772 F.2d 856, 858 (Fed. Cir. 1985)); Gregory v. Dept. of Educ., 16 MSPR 144, 146 (1983) (when the agency proves its charges and shows a nexus between the charges and service efficiency, the Board makes a separate determination concerning the penalty); Healy v. USPS, 9 MSPR 635, 636 (1982) (restating rule); Williams v. USPS, 5 MSPR 5, 7, 5 MSPB 88 (1981) ("In every appeal from an adverse action, this Board is mandated to determine both that the alleged employee misconduct has in fact occurred, and that the disciplinary action taken against the employee will promote the efficiency of the service."). [Specific types of misconduct are discussed in Chapter 8. Nexus and penalty determinations are the subject of Chapter 7.]

A decision to discipline a federal employee through an adverse action must have a rational basis. Kmiecz v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 673, 676 (1986). There must also be an actual adverse decision. On occasion an agency may take the steps to remove an employee, but then separate the employee through other means, e.g., a RIF. The Board may need to sort through the chronology of events and the attendant paperwork and circumstances to determine the basis, if any, of its jurisdiction over the employee's appeal. See Martin v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 21, 25 (1994) (removal for physical inability to perform work was superseded by a RIF).

Described here are decisions spawned from controversy over whether a particular set of facts constitutes an adverse action. These decisions define the outer parameters of the Board's adverse action appellate jurisdiction. Included are appeals involving matters that do not clearly meet the statutory definition of adverse action but that are treated by the Board as the equivalent of adverse actions--principally involuntary retirements, resignations, and demotions. We also discuss in this chapter some unusual forms of adverse actions and unusual adverse action procedures, principally indefinite suspensions for those believed to have been involved in criminal activity and for those whose security clearances are suspended pending investigation. There follows some consideration of two basic concepts in adverse action adjudication: the efficiency of the service standard and the concept of personal responsibility. Finally, we touch on two categories of employees whose adverse action procedures are governed by separate statutory requirements: employees in the Senior Executive Service and administrative law judges. [Refer to Chapter 1, "Jurisdictional Overview"; refer to Chapter 11, "Shortened Notice Period; Crime Provision."]

The phrase "adverse action" is one that describes a component of Board jurisdiction over an appeal by an individual who meets the statutory definition of "employee," discussed in Chapter 1. "Adverse action" is a term of law, not the subjective impression of an employee. Some actions ordinarily thought of as voluntary--resignations and retirements, or even use of leave--may become involuntary and by definition "adverse" if they result from mental impairment or coercion. Administrative actions correcting errors in the appointment process may seem adverse to an employee. We consider the most commonly encountered cases involving disputed adverse action status. [Refer to employment status of probationary employees in Chapter 1, "Probationary Classification Issues."]

Adverse actions subject to Board jurisdiction are enumerated at 5 USC 7512:

This subchapter applies to--

(1) a removal;

(2) a suspension for more than 14 days;

(3) a reduction in grade;

(4) a reduction in pay; and

(5) a furlough of 30 days or less;

but does not apply to--

(A) a suspension or removal under section 7532 of this title,

(B) a reduction-in-force action under section 3502 of this title,

(C) the reduction in grade of a supervisor or manager who has not completed the probationary period under section 3321(a)(2) of this title if such reduction is to the grade held immediately before becoming such a supervisor or manager,

(D) a reduction in grade or removal under section 4303 of this title, or

(E) an action initiated under section 1215 or 7521 of this title.

Nexus--the necessary impact of an employee's conduct on the effectiveness of the government as an employer as justification for discipline of its employee--is treated in Chapter 7, along with the determinants, or mitigation, of penalties. Chapter 8 treats substantive offenses. The concept of harmful error, and the affirmative defenses of discrimination and whistleblowing reprisal are treated, respectively, in Chapters 11, 12, and 13. Chapter 1 considers the sometimes complex definition of when an individual is an "employee" who may bring an adverse action appeal, and that chapter also touches on Board jurisdiction over pay corrections resulting in reductions in pay and problems with employment benefits, e.g., insurance.
