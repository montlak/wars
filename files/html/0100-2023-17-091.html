broida flra Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards, Subchapter 07: Procedures for Review of Arbitration Awards, Section B: Requirements of Exceptions; Regulations, Subsection 04: Timely Filing, Part e: 30-Day Period for Exceptions From Service of the Award
e. 30-Day Period for Exceptions From Service of the Award

Section 7122(b) of the Reform Act was amended by the Civil Service Miscellaneous Amendments Act of 1983 (Pub. L 98-224) (1984), to provide that the 30-day period for filing exceptions to an arbitrator's award begins on the date the award is served on the filing party. See Dept. of Army, Army Troop Support Agency and AFGE Local 1178, 15 FLRA 191, 191-92 (1984). The 1984 amendments changed prior practice. Previously, exceptions to an award were required to be filed "during the 30-day period beginning on the date of such award." 5 CFR 2425.1(b) (1983). The time limit for filing exceptions to an arbitration award was calculated as 30 days beginning on and including the date of the award. Dept. of Army, Watervliet Arsenal and NAGE, Local R2-98, 8 FLRA 387, 387 (1982).

The 2010 regulations, revising past regulatory practice, initiate the filing period with the day following the date of the award. Under 5 CFR 2425.2(b) (2022):

(b) Timeliness requirements--general. The time limit for filing an exception to an arbitration award is thirty (30) days after the date of service of the award. This thirty (30)-day time limit may not be extended or waived. In computing the thirty (30)-day period, the first day counted is the day after, not the day of, service of the arbitration award. Example: If an award is served on May 1, then May 2 is counted as day 1, and May 31 is day 30; an exception filed on May 31 would be timely, and an exception filed on June 1 would be untimely. In order to determine the date of service of the award, see the rules set forth in subsection (c) of this section, and for additional rules regarding computing the filing date, see 5 CFR 2429.21 and 2429.22.

As a result of the changes in the regulations over time, each case that follows should be considered with reference to the regulatory requirements then in effect.

A succinct statement of FLRA's pre-2010 rules concerning the filing date for exceptions is presented by DHUD, Washington and AFGE Local 476, 46 FLRA 878, 879 (1992):

[T]he time limit for filing exceptions to an arbitration award is 30 days beginning on the date the award is served on the filing party. 5 C.F.R. § 2425.1(b). The date of service is the date the arbitration award is deposited in the U.S. mail or is delivered in person. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.27(d)... If the award is served by mail, 5 days are added to the period for filing exceptions to the award. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.22. Further, the last day of the period so computed is to be included unless it is a Saturday, Sunday, or a Federal legal holiday, in which case the period shall run until the end of the next day which is neither a Saturday, Sunday, or a Federal legal holiday. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.21(a). The time limit may not be extended or waived by the Authority. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.23(d).

Further reconsideration of DHUD, 46 FLRA 878 (1992), was denied in DHUD, Washington and AFGE Local 476, 46 FLRA 1277, 1278 (1993), deciding that no basis existed for changing the result because of the Supreme Court decision in Irwin v. VA, 111 S. Ct. 453 (1990) (interpreting filing times for Title VII civil rights actions in court following notice of EEOC decisions). See AFGE Local 2054 and VA Central Arkansas VHS, 63 FLRA 169 n.3 (2009) (Member Beck opining that whether date the award is served, or day after, should start the clock for filing exceptions should be revised through rulemaking or when issue is properly raised by party filing exceptions); Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs and NFFE Bureau of Indian Affairs Council, 53 FLRA 984, 989-90 (1997) (same), reconsideration denied, 54 FLRA 9 (1998); DHHS, Pub. Health Serv. and LIUNA Local 1376, 52 FLRA 909, 912-13 (1997) (same); cf. AFGE Council 73, Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals and Dept. of Labor, Employment Standards Admin., 50 FLRA 79, 81-82 (1995) (time limit and extension (for mailed awards) are computed separately and, if either time period would expire on a Saturday, Sunday, or federal legal holiday, then the period is deemed not to expire until the end of the next workday).

Under regulations before 2010, the 30-day period for filing exceptions began on the date the award was served, not on the day after the award was served. Dept. of Army, Hq., XVIII Airborne Corps, Ft. Bragg and AFGE Local 1770, 37 FLRA 877, 878 n.2 (1990); see AFGE Local 2401 and VA Health Care Sys., Prescott, 58 FLRA 1, 1 (2002) ("[I]t is the date of service--not the date of receipt--that controls in determining the timeliness of exceptions."). Unless there was contrary evidence, there was a presumption that the award was mailed on the day it is dated. VAMC, Gainesville and AFGE Local 2779, 31 FLRA 1108, 1108 (1988); see VAMC, Richmond and AFGE Local 2145, 34 FLRA 524, 525 (1990) ("In the absence of evidence to the contrary, we conclude that the award was served on the parties by mail that same day" [as date of award, served by mail]). A delivery delay within a given city would not serve to rebut the effect of the date shown on the arbitration award. See Dept. of Labor and AFGE Local 12, 35 FLRA 920, 921 (1990). If the arbitrator sent his award to the American Arbitration Association, the time ran from the date the AAA sent the award to the parties. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Memphis Serv. Ctr. and NTEU, 34 FLRA 355, 356 (1990). FLRA ruled that an award, dated June 27, was not actually served until June 28, the date of the arbitrator's invoice accompanying the award. Dept. of Labor, OSHA, Region VI and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, Local 2139, AFGE, 32 FLRA 1233, 1233 (1988).

Service of an arbitration award is calculated from the date that service is made on the representative of record, not the grievant. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 16 FLRA 350, 351 (1984). The date of service is calculated from the date the award is delivered to the Postal Service with an address that allowed for delivery to be perfected. If an award is mailed and returned as undeliverable and then the award is properly addressed and remailed, the latter date controls for purposes of calculating the time for filing exceptions. See AFGE Local 2 and Dept. of Army, Military Traffic Mgmt. Command, Falls Church, 48 FLRA 1394, 1396 (1994).

With allowance for changes in the 2010 regulations, it is important to understand that it is the date of service of the award, not the date of receipt, that controls the filing date for exceptions. Overseas Fed'n of Teachers and DODDS, Mediterranean Region, 29 FLRA 230, 231 (1987), reconsideration denied, Overseas Fed'n of Teachers and DODDS, Mediterranean Region, 32 FLRA 366 (1988). Whenever the award is served by mail on a party, five days are added to the period for filing the exception. 5 CFR 2429.22 (2022). VAMC, Gainesville, supra; see DOJ, INS, San Diego and AFGE INS Council, 51 FLRA 1094, 1094 n.1 (1996) ("The time limit for filing exceptions to an arbitrator's award is 30 days beginning on the date the award is served on the filing party. 5 C.F.R. § 2425.1(b). Because the award was served on the parties by mail...5 days are added to the period for filing exceptions to the award. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.22."); INS and AFGE Local 1917, 33 FLRA 843, 843 (1988) (when an award is mailed, five days are added to the prescribed period for filing the exception).

Under § 2429.22, when exceptions are served on a party by mail only five days are added to the prescribed period, even if it takes more than five days from the date of service before the party actually receives the exceptions. DHHS, SSA and AFGE Gen. Comm., 50 FLRA 296, 298 (1995). If the 30-day period, with the five-day extension for awards served by mail, ends on a Saturday, Sunday or legal holiday, the time limit runs until the end of the next work day. Dept. of Commerce, PTO and POPA, 37 FLRA 1204, 1205 (1990); see VAMC, Hot Springs and AFGE Local 1539, 48 FLRA 804, 805 (1993) ("The 5 days are added only after the 30-day period is calculated, with due allowances for weekends and holidays as required by section 2429.21 of the Authority's Rules and Regulations."). The allowance of the additional five days was considered and affirmed as consistent with the statutory intent in AFGE Local 1960 and Dept. of Navy, Dev. Ctr., 29 FLRA 680, 682-83 (1987), and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council and DOJ, Border Patrol, 54 FLRA 905, 908-09 (1998). For application and history of the current rules governing the timeliness of exceptions, see ACT and Pennsylvania NG, 27 FLRA 96, 97-98 (1987).

Under 5 CFR 2429.21(b) (2022), mailed exceptions are filed with FLRA when they are mailed, not when they are received by FLRA. When the submission is received by FLRA, there are normally only two ways to determine its mailing date: the postmark on the mailing; lacking a postmark, by the date of receipt minus five days. See Dept. of Navy, Naval Air Station, Corpus Christi and NFFE Local 797, 35 FLRA 1161, 1162 (1990); Dept. of Army, Hq., XVIII Airborne Corps, Ft. Bragg and AFGE Local 1770, 37 FLRA 877, 878-79 (1990) (reciting, applying five-day rule). For purposes of determining the date of service by mail, a "presumed" mailing date (one calculated by the date of receipt less five days) is used only in the absence of a postmark. DOJ, INS, Border Patrol, El Paso and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 37 FLRA 1310, 1312-13 (1990) (on timing of filing the opposition to exceptions). There are instances when exceptions are filed by mail but then lost in the mail. When FLRA learns that exceptions are filed but lost in the mail, it will secure information concerning the circumstances of the mailing from both parties and then use that information to arrive at a conclusion concerning the likely mailing date. Dept. of Labor, Mine Safety and Health Admin. and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE, 32 FLRA 302, 304 (1988).

FLRA described the interaction of the five-day allowance and the allowance for holidays, USDA, Forest Serv., N. Region and NFFE Local 1818, 49 FLRA 1063, 1065-66 (1994):

The time limit for filing exceptions to an arbitration award is 30 days beginning on the date the award is served on the filing party. 5 C.F.R. § 2425.1(b). The date of service is the date the arbitration award is deposited in the U.S. mail or is delivered in person. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.27(d). See U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development, Washington, D.C. and American Federation of Government Employees, Local 476, 46 FLRA 878, 879 (1992) (HUD). If the award is served by mail, 5 days are added to the period for filing exceptions to the award. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.22. Further, the last day of the period so computed is to be included, unless it is a Saturday, Sunday, or a Federal legal holiday, in which case the period shall run until the end of the next day that is neither a Saturday, Sunday, or a Federal legal holiday. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.21(a). The time limit may not be extended or waived by the Authority. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.23(d).

In this case, the 30 day time for filing exceptions to the Arbitrator's award began December 11, 1993, and expired on Sunday, January 9, 1994. Because the award was served by mail, 5 additional days were added to the filing period. However, because the last day of the 30-day period was a Sunday, the time period was advanced until the next workday, Monday, January 10, 1994, prior to adding the additional 5 days. HUD, 46 FLRA at 880. With the additional 5 days, the time period was advanced to Saturday, January 15, 1994. Because this too was a nonworkday, the time limit for filing exceptions was again advanced to the end of the next workday. As Monday, January 17, 1994, was a Federal legal holiday, the next workday was Tuesday, January 18, 1994, the date on which the Union filed its exceptions. Accordingly, we find that the Union's exceptions were filed timely. See id.

Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Oklahoma City Dist. and NTEU Chap. 45, 37 FLRA 775, 779 (1990), explained that a request to an arbitrator for clarification of his award will not necessarily extend the time for filing exceptions:

...The time period for filing an exception begins with the service of an arbitrator's original award. A request for clarification of an award does not operate to extend the time limit for filing exceptions. U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Social Security Administration and American Federation of Government Employees, AFL-CIO, 23 FLRA 157, 158 (1986). Only when an arbitrator modifies an award and the modifications give rise to the deficiencies alleged in the exceptions has the Authority held that the filing period began with the arbitrator's response to the request for clarification....

But a ruling by an arbitrator on a counsel fee request starts the period for exceptions as to that request, as opposed to the earlier, underlying merits award. See AFGE Local 44 and USDA, FSIS, 67 FLRA 721, 721 (2014) (Member Pizzella dissenting).
