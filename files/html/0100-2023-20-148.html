10. Probationary Termination

Termination of a probationary employee is a personnel action for purposes of the WPA. Thorne v. DHHS, 79 MSPR 583, 589 n.2 (1998) ("There is no question that the appellant's termination during probation was a covered 'personnel action' under the WPA. See, e.g., Lewis v. Department of the Army, 63 M.S.P.R. 119, 122 (1994), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1238 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table)."); Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 325, 331 n.4 (1993) ("Among other things, the matter raised to the Special Counsel must constitute a 'personnel action.' See 5 U.S.C. §§ 1214(a)(3) and 1221(a); 5 C.F.R. § 1209.2(b)(1). Termination of a probationary employee is a 'personnel action' for purposes of the WPA. See Wardleigh v. Department of the Air Force, 50 M.S.P.R. 622, 624 (1991)."); see Lucas v. Dept. of Navy, 63 MSPR 455, 459 n.3 (1994) (concerning an IRA appeal by a terminated probationer: jurisdiction was limited to whistleblower allegations, not age discrimination or constitutional violations); Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 397, 401 (1994) (terminated probationer may raise through the IRA process whistleblowing allegations but cannot tack on to those allegations claims of harmful procedural error or equitable estoppel; however, the Board has ordinary appellate jurisdiction over allegations that the termination discriminated against the appellant based on partisan political reasons or marital status). An evaluation of an employee as an unsatisfactory probationer not suitable for permanent appointment constitutes a covered personnel action, Frederick v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 517, 527-28 (1994), rev'd on other grounds, Frederick v. DOJ, 73 F.3d 349 (Fed. Cir. 1996):

Mr. Womack was a probationary employee undergoing field training in order to obtain an appointment as a Border Patrol Agent. As part of this process, the appellant was required to evaluate Mr. Womack's conduct and efficiency as a Border Patrol Agent trainee and to recommend whether Mr. Womack should be retained by the agency. The appellant completed a conduct and efficiency report in which he recommended that Mr. Womack not be retained, but Mr. Womack resigned before a committee that reviewed such recommendations had made its own determination; the recommendation could, however, have resulted in Mr. Womack's termination from the agency.

The appellant and NBPC contend that his completion of a conduct and efficiency report did not involve a personnel action which fell within the jurisdiction of the WPA. The report evaluated Mr. Womack's performance as a trainee and analyzed his ability to perform the duties of a Border Patrol Agent, but it did not do so under a performance appraisal system that measured his performance regarding each critical element of a position. Instead, it appears to have served as a tool for enabling the agency to decide whether to retain him. The report therefore may not fall within the jurisdiction of the WPA as a performance evaluation under chapter 43. In any event, however, the appellant's recommendation that Mr. Womack not be retained does appear to fall within the WPA's jurisdiction as a recommendation for "an action under chapter 75 of this title or other disciplinary or corrective action"; as discussed in more detail below, the appellant recommended that Mr. Womack not be retained because the latter's disclosures had undermined the appellant's confidence in his mental and emotional fitness to be a Border Patrol Agent. See 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(iii). The recommendation that Mr. Womack not be retained necessarily was also a recommendation that he not be appointed to the position of Border Patrol Agent, an action which also falls within the WPA's jurisdiction. See 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(i).

Kewley v. DHHS, 153 F.3d 1357, 1365-66 (Fed. Cir. 1998), considered a probationer's discharge in an IRA case. The "clear and convincing" standard of proof was applied following establishment of a prima facie case through a presumptive timing test; the court determined that the Board correctly decided that the agency's proof that the employee would have been terminated without whistleblowing reprisal was clear and convincing. The court did not make clear the distinction in the application of the "clear and convincing" standard to cases involving probationary, as opposed to tenured, employees:

One additional argument raised by the petitioner is that her status as a probationary employee should not have affected her right as a whistleblower to challenge her removal to the Board as retaliatory. Ms. Kewley argues that the AJ's statement that he had a limited scope of review in addressing her removal was used to lower the agency's burden of proving that it would have fired her, even in the absence of the protected disclosure. We disagree.

As a probationary employee Ms. Kewley's right to obtain Board review of the agency's removal decision is restricted by statute and regulation. See 5 USC § 7511 (1994); 5 CFR § 315.806(b) (1998). In fact, the only type of Board review a probationary employee is entitled to is an IRA arising under 5 USC § 1221, which is based upon a prohibited personnel practice as described in 5 USC § 2302(b)(8) (1994), here retaliation. Beyond the allegations brought under such an IRA, however, the Board may not address the merits of the agency's removal action against a probationary employee other than on the narrow grounds, not applicable here, of 5 CFR § 315.806 (1998) (appeal permitted when adverse action was based on marital status, partisan political reasons, or certain pre-employment conditions). Because of these statutory and regulatory restrictions, the AJ is required to narrow the scope of his review to address only the IRA allegations. That is all the cited statement did and hence it could not be error.

Even though Ms. Kewley was a probationary employee and the AJ had a limited scope of review, the agency was still required by statute to meet the burden of proof of clear and convincing evidence, set forth in 5 USC § 1221(e)(2), and was required to show that it would have discharged Ms. Kewley, even in the absence of her protected disclosure. Because the AJ properly limited his scope of review to only the whistleblower allegations, reasonably considered all the evidence of record, and found on the basis of substantial evidence that the agency demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that Ms. Kewley resisted becoming involved in child abuse investigations, we hold that he acted correctly.
