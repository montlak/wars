d. Conviction, Misconduct Distinguished

If adverse action is based on a conviction alone, it is improper for the AJ to take evidence from the agency concerning the underlying conduct; it is also improper to take evidence from the appellant concerning his defense to the conduct leading to the conviction, e.g., self-defense. See Palomo v. DOJ, 73 MSPR 306, 308 (1997) ("The appellant appears to recognize that his arguments concerning the merits of his removal could be considered only upon the reversal of his conviction."); O'Connor v. VA, 59 MSPR 653, 657 (1993) (when removal was proposed on the basis of a conviction, the agency erred in relying on the underlying misconduct in the removal decision); Brown v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 132, 135 n.1 (1987) ("[W]e stress that the Board will not delve into the circumstances behind the fact that the appellant was convicted by a jury of intentionally killing her boyfriend"). The entry of a nolo plea, like the conviction for a crime, may furnish cause in itself for disciplinary action, depending on the circumstances. See Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 354 (1987); cf. Taylor v. Macy, 252 F. Supp. 1021, 1023 (S.D. Cal. 1966) ("[w]hile the State of California has forgiven the petitioner to the extent of releasing certain penalties and disabilities, the acts continue to exist and it would be absurd to find that the executive authorities were arbitrary and capricious in considering these expunged convictions in assessing the character of the petitioner in proceedings for the removal of a civil service employee."). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Arrest," for the nexus between off-duty criminal activities and disciplinary action.]

State law may need to be examined to determine the effect of various pleas made in state criminal proceedings. Some states have a process that allows a judge to assess criminal responsibility without entering a conviction. If state law establishes that a conviction does not result from a plea of guilty resulting in probation without entry of judgment of guilt, involving the suspended imposition of sentence, the Board will not sustain a removal based on a conviction. See O'Connor v. VA, 59 MSPR 653, 659 (1993).
