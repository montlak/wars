broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section H: Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals, Subsection 04: Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ("DWOP"), Part e: Effect of PFR
e. Effect of PFR

Once the appeal is dismissed without prejudice, the appellant reopens the appeal by refiling it or taking whatever other action the AJ directs to reopen the appeal, although the appellant need do nothing if the AJ provided for automatic refiling on a set date. If the appeal is dismissed without prejudice by an initial decision and the appellant then files a PFR from that initial decision, the Board treats the PFR as a refiled appeal and forwards it to the regional office for adjudication and decision on timeliness if that is an issue. Under Gardner v. Dept. of Treasury, 68 MSPR 258, 262 (1995), if the refiling deadline falls during the Board's consideration of a PFR challenging the DWOP, the Board may set the refiling period to run from the date of its decision on the PFR: "Because the administrative judge's stated time limit for refiling the appeal expired while the appellant's petition for review was pending, the appellant may refile his appeal within 35 days of the date of issuance of this decision. See Zell, 57 MSPR at 88 n.2." See Sadler v. OPM, 93 MSPR 48, 50 ¶ 5 (2002) ("appellant's petition for review of the initial decision dismissing the appeal without prejudice constitutes a timely refiled petition for appeal"). Or the Board may treat the appeal as refiled and send it back for regional adjudication. See Soto v. VA, 88 MSPR 626, 629 ¶ 6 (2001) (appellant's response to an agency PFR constitutes notice that he is refiling his appeal); Milberger v. DHHS, 82 MSPR 72, 74 ¶ 4 (1999) (deeming the appellant's PFR constituted notice of the refiling of an appealed that had been DWOP'd); Hart v. VA, 80 MSPR 279, 282 ¶ 7 (1998) ("[A]ppellant's petition for review constitutes notice that he is refiling his appeal, and we deem the appeal to be timely refiled."); Ayers v. NASA, 80 MSPR 550, 554 ¶ 9 (1999) ("We note that the time limit for refiling of the appellant's appeal expired while her petition for review of the initial decision was pending before the Board. Therefore, the appeal is now properly before the Board and ready for adjudication on the jurisdictional issue."); Baskin v. Dept. of Treasury, 78 MSPR 156, 158 (1998) (the appellant's cross-PFR was treated as a refiling of the appeal and dismissed without prejudice); Gidwani v. VA, 74 MSPR 509, 511 (1997) (same result as to a DWOP involving a counsel fee petition); Starling v. VA, 74 MSPR 254, 256-57 (1997) (appeal was deemed refiled through a PFR following dismissal of the appeal without prejudice when the AJ's refiling instructions were not clear and the appellant had no contact with his designated representative since filing his appeal); Deaton v. USDA, 71 MSPR 544, 546 (1996) (treating the appellant's PFR from the dismissal of his appeal without prejudice as a timely refiled appeal); Cloonan v. USPS, 70 MSPR 226, 229 (1996) (same result as Jones, post); Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 70 MSPR 221, 224 (1996) (remanding appeal following PFR from dismissal without prejudice: "[T]he appellant's petition for review was timely filed and therefore a refiled petition for appeal would have been only 4 days late. He has not abandoned his claim, and, in fact, has acted at each juncture to pursue his appeal."); Ben Espinoza v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 679, 684 (1996) (adjudicating the appellant's PFR objecting to the dismissal of an enforcement proceeding without prejudice, finding the dismissal appropriate, and then remanding the case to the region for continued adjudication); Lewis v. Dept. of Air Force, 69 MSPR 40, 44 (1995) ("We note that the time limit for the refiling of the appellant's appeal expired while her petition for review of the initial decision was pending before the Board. Therefore, we find that the appeal is now properly before the Board and ready for adjudication on the merits."); Gardner v. Dept. of Treasury, 68 MSPR 258, 260 (1995) (same result as Deaton, supra); cf. Wheeler v. DOD, 113 MSPR 519, 2010 MSPB 71 (2010) (VEOA/USERRA cases) (Board may remand a case when there is a dispute raised by PFR over the legitimacy of the AJ's DWOP order and the Board remanded a related case following a separate PFR); Soto v. DOJ, 84 MSPR 623 (1999) (Table) (appellant filed a PFR from an initial decision dismissing the case without prejudice; the Board honored an uncontested motion to withdraw the PFR; the case was forwarded to the regional office for adjudication as timely refiled appeal); Braswell v. USPS, 58 MSPR 353, 353 (1993) (DWOP because the appellant was the subject of a criminal proceeding and a related Board appeal); Jackson v. Dept. of Army, 7 MSPR 506, 507 (1981) (although a PFR challenging the initial decision dismissing the case was untimely, the case was remanded for adjudication because the PFR was filed within the period for refiling set by the initial decision). Under Stewart v. DHHS, DC-1221-20-0666-W-2 (NP 7/26/2022), a PFR from a DWOP is moot if the appeal is automatically refiled while the PFR is pending.

If a DWOP'd case is brought to the Board on PFR and remanded, the Board does not adjudicate merits arguments raised in the PFR. See Kramer v. DHS, DC-0752-10-0634-I-1 (NP 6/15/2011) (PFR during period of DWOP led to remand: "the appellant's arguments on review are essentially an attempt to reach the merits of the appeal, which are irrelevant to the issue here of whether the administrative judge abused her discretion in dismissing the appeal without prejudice to refiling. See Gingery v. Department of the Treasury, 111 M.S.P.R. 134, ¶ 11 (2009). When an appeal is dismissed without prejudice to refiling, the Board will not consider arguments raised on review concerning matters that should be considered by the administrative judge once the appeal has been refiled. See, e.g., Lewis v. Department of the Air Force, 69 M.S.P.R. 40, 44 (1995)."); Ratliff v. OPM, 74 MSPR 317, 319 (1997) ("Where, as here, an appeal has been dismissed without prejudice to refiling...and the appellant then files a petition for review of that decision, the Board will not consider arguments raised on review concerning matters that should be considered by the administrative judge once the appeal has been refiled."). If the appellant files a PFR from the initial decision dismissing his appeal without prejudice, the issue before the Board (or, upon further review, before the Federal Circuit) is the propriety of the dismissal without prejudice, not the merits of or Board jurisdiction over the underlying appeal. See Green v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3203). Although the agency may file a PFR from a DWOP in an attempt to secure Board consideration of procedural or jurisdictional complexities that the AJ should have earlier addressed, see Quiet v. Dept. of Transp., 104 MSPR 292, 295-96 ¶¶ 5-9, 2006 MSPB 366 (2006) (Board determined that the AJ improperly failed to analyze the jurisdictional implications of a constructive adverse action appeal), if the agency files a PFR challenging only the propriety of a DWOP, ordinarily the Board does not consider arguments concerning the merits of the case or discovery disputes that can be considered when and if the appeal is refiled. See Lewis v. Dept. of Air Force, 69 MSPR 40, 44 (1995) ("If an appeal has been dismissed without prejudice in an initial decision and the appellant then files a petition for review of that decision,...the Board will not consider arguments raised on review concerning discovery disputes, the imposition of sanctions, or other matters that should be considered by the administrative judge once the appeal has been refiled."); Padilla v. Dept. of Air Force, 58 MSPR 561, 564 (1993) ("We find that, in granting additional discovery, the administrative judge would have been unduly broadening the scope of the issue before him: whether to grant the appellant's notion to dismiss the appeal without prejudice."). If the Board decides on the appellant's PFR that the AJ properly entered a DWOP because of related criminal proceedings, the Board may establish new refiling dates if the criminal proceeding is still underway when the PFR is adjudicated. See Dale v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-10-0300-I-2 (NP 6/22/2012).
