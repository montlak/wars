4. Past Discipline; Performance Record; Aggravating Factors

If an agency seeks to rely on aggravating factors, including past discipline, it should place the appellant on notice of those factors in the proposal letter so that the appellant can respond to them during the reply to the agency deciding official. The question that resulted in different approaches by the Board over the years has been the impact on the validity of the action of consideration by the deciding official of aggravating factors not referenced in the proposal.

Although the harmful error analysis was in place before 2011, and retains some vitality, under Ward v. USPS, 634 F.3d 1274 (Fed. Cir. 2011), a constitutional due process analysis generally is applied to a decision based on aggravating factors not noticed in the proposal. The distinction between harmful error, with a burden of proof on the appellant to demonstrate harm, and a constitutional due process analysis, setting aside an action based on the error if the Ward test is met, may determine the outcome of an appeal. [Lopes, post, is discussed in Chapter 7, "Proposal to List Aggravating Factors; Treatment of Warnings; Due Process Considerations"; Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274 (Fed. Cir. 2011), is discussed in this chapter, "Information Pertaining to Aggravation or Mitigation of Penalty; Ward."]

Lopes v. Dept. of Navy, 116 MSPR 470, 472-77 ¶¶ 2-13, 2011 MSPB 63 (2011), decided a few months after Ward, supra, announced an approach to the issue that emphasized constitutional due process considerations that, if not followed, could result in reversal of an action because the deciding official considered aggravating factors, such as past discipline, not noticed in the proposal:

When an agency intends to rely on aggravating factors, such as prior discipline, as the basis for the imposition of a penalty, such factors should be included in the advance notice of adverse action so that the employee will have a fair opportunity to respond to those factors before the agency's deciding official. Vena v. Department of Labor, 111 M.S.P.R. 165, ¶ 9 (2009); Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 304 (1981). Similarly, it is improper for a deciding official to rely on an employee's alleged negative past work record in determining the penalty when the employee was not disciplined for the purported misconduct and where the incidents are mentioned as an aggravating factor for the first time in a Board proceeding. Westmoreland v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 83 M.S.P.R. 625, ¶¶ 7-9 (1999) (citing Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1999)), aff'd, 19 Fed. Appx. 868 (Fed. Cir. 2001).

Our reviewing court has recently held that, if an employee has not been given "notice of any aggravating factors supporting an enhanced penalty[,] " an ex parte communication with the deciding official regarding such factors may constitute a constitutional due process violation. Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274, 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2011). When such circumstances are present, the court directed the Board to analyze whether the additional aggravating factors supporting an enhanced penalty constituted new and material information under the factors set forth in Stone. See Ward, 634 F.3d at 1280. The court in Ward made clear that, if a constitutional violation has occurred, it cannot be considered a harmless error and the agency action must be reversed. Id.
