A. PFR PROCESS

A PFR is a procedural device--a pleading--to obtain review of the initial decision by Board headquarters staff and Board members. Essentially, the PFR is no more than a set of objections to the initial decision. If the PFR is timely filed, the Board must adjudicate the PFR through the point of the issuance of a final decision, excepting cases resulting in remands. Even if the PFR is untimely, the Board closes the PFR out with a final decision, often on timeliness, excepting cases resulting in remands. The significance of the PFR is apparent when the nature of administrative litigation is considered. The Board is an administrative agency. The goal of Board litigation is a final administrative decision on the action appealed. That final decision is usually denominated an "Opinion and Order" for a precedential decision or "Final Order" for a nonprecedential decision. From the final decision one can celebrate (or mourn), go to EEOC or to court, or seek further remedial action through counsel fee petitions, compliance initiatives, or claims for damages. A final decision is res judicata, meaning that the issues raised in the appeal may not be relitigated before the Board. See Fuentes v. OPM, 40 MSPR 276, 279 (1989). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Previous Determinations of Disputed Facts, Issues, Claims: Res Judicata, Collateral Estoppel, and Law of the Case--The Doctrines"; refer to descriptions of aspects of the appeal and petition for review process, on the Board's website at https://www.mspb.gov/appeals/appeals.htm#petition, and refer to the Board's website entry, "How to File an Appeal," http://www.mspb.gov/appeals/appeals.htm.]

Under the MSPB regulatory scheme, a PFR is a request filed with the three-member Board in Washington, D.C. for review of an initial decision of one of the Board's judges. 5 CFR 1201.4(g) (2022). The PFR (and cross-PFR, subsequently discussed) should be filed with the Clerk of the MSPB, Washington, D.C. 20419. 5 CFR 1201.114(d) (2022); e-filing is also an option through the Board's website. A request for reopening or reconsideration, and a request for review of an arbitration award, is also filed with the Board's Clerk. The effect of a PFR is that instead of an initial decision becoming final as a result of the passage of time, a separate final decision is written by the attorneys and Board members working in the Board's central office in Washington. Although the Board may remand the case to the region for some reason requiring additional adjudication, usually the final decision from MSPB Headquarters ends the Board's involvement in the appeal unless there is an issue of compliance. Further review must then be sought through the courts or EEOC. The Board considers that its decision is final after the PFR process, or if the PFR process is not pursued and an initial decision becomes "final" through lapse of time, even if court review is undertaken; absent a stay judicial review does not suspend the finality of the Board's final decision. See Sledge v. DOJ, 44 MSPR 455, 458 n.2 (1990). [Refer in this chapter to "Stay Requests."]

Once an initial decision becomes final, review is sought through the MSPB by a late PFR (with a request to excuse or extend the time limit for filing a PFR) or by a petition to reopen (with an explanation of the grounds for reopening); alternatively, review, if timely, may be pursued with EEOC or in the courts. [Refer to Chapter 12 for review of EEO allegations; Chapter 17 should be consulted for judicial review.] An initial decision will not become final if it is challenged through a timely PFR filed with the Board headquarters office in Washington. If a PFR is filed, the other party may simply oppose the PFR or it may file its own set of objections to the initial decision through a cross-PFR, as well as a responsive opposition to the underlying PFR. A response to a PFR is discretionary with the party that has the opportunity to respond. Jordan v. OPM, 108 MSPR 119, 122 n.1, 2008 MSPB 31 (2008). A party may reply to the response (opposition) to a PFR. The Board will adjudicate a PFR even without a filed opposition, and there are occasional PFRs from appellants that agencies elect not to challenge--perhaps on the assumption that the appellant's case is not strong enough to require a response. On occasion a remand is required but the agency did not file a PFR from an initial decision awarding some relief to the appellant. If the remand does not affect the relief awarded, the Board may direct the agency to implement the relief as it issues a remand order for disposition of the other component of the case. See Shingles v. USPS, 90 MSPR 245, 247-48 ¶ 8 (2001) (directing the agency to implement the AJ's order to cancel a suspension while remanding the case for disposition of an EEO claim).

The discussion that follows applies to initial decisions issued by AJs in regional offices involving the usual appeals of adverse and performance actions, probationers' appeals, retirement and Individual Right of Action appeals, VEOA and USERRA cases, collateral counsel fee, damages, and enforcement decisions; and to the less frequent OSC Hatch Act, corrective, and disciplinary action cases, and to cases by agencies against administrative law judges. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Administrative Law Judges," for actions against administrative law judges; Chapter 13, "Cases Brought by OSC"; Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases," for mixed case administrative processing and judicial review, and Chapter 17 for judicial review rights.]

Board decisions on PFRs are drafted by the legal staff at Board headquarters employed in the Office of Appeals Counsel (OAC). The draft decisions and case files are reviewed by the Board members, aided by their own staff attorneys. Board members may modify the OAC drafts or OAC may be directed to rewrite the drafts. The final product is a final decision, usually issued for the Board by and under the signature of the Clerk of the Board, and sometimes signed off on by the Board members. [Refer to Chapter 1, "Office of Appeals Counsel."]

An internal Board document, "Guidelines for Processing PFRs," provides the Board's perspective on the PFR process from the perspective of the drafting attorneys in OAC:

1. Every case goes forward to the Board members with a memorandum and a recommended decision.

a. Precedential Opinion and Order (O&O). Used when making a change to the existing body of law, clarifying current case law, providing extensive analysis that may be helpful to individuals beyond the parties, or providing legal analysis that has not been discussed in the Board's recent history.

b. Non-precedential Final Order (NPFO). Unless there needs to be a correction or supplement to the analysis in the initial decision, NPFOs generally contain only a brief discussions of the issues raised on review.

c. Non-precedential Order (NPO). Used when remanding or when a case is not final for some other reason and it is unnecessary to issue an 0&0.

2. Memorandum. The cover memorandum is intended to assist the Board members' review of the case. The memorandum typically contains three main sections: Recommendation, Background, and Analysis. As recommended O&Os typically contain all of the information necessary for the Board to decide a case, cover memoranda with O&Os are typically very brief and often simply cross-reference the background and analysis contained in the 0&0. Cover memoranda with NPFOs or NPOs, however, are usually much more detailed and comprehensive than the recommended decisions themselves.

a. Recommendation Section. This section normally consists of only one or two sentences that summarize OAC's recommendation. Ideally, the Recommendation Section provides enough basic descriptive detail such that the reader will get a sense of the type of case and the important issues. For instance: "Uphold the initial decision that dismissed this individual right of action (IRA) appeal for lack of jurisdiction because the appellant failed to make a nonfrivolous allegation that he engaged in protected whistleblowing activity."

b. Background Section. In most cases, the memorandum only needs to contain a very condensed version of the factual and procedural background of a case. As a rule of thumb, this section is often a few paragraphs to a couple of pages in length depending on the complexity of the case. This section will briefly explain what the case is about, condense the critical information in the parties' submissions, and focus the Board members on the key issues and information. Avoid cluttering the memorandum with extraneous procedural or factual detail that is immaterial to resolving the issues that must be addressed on review. Accurate, specific citations to the case file are expected and help to expedite the Board members' review process.

c. Analysis Section. The primary focus is on the issues raised by the parties on review, but the drafting attorney should also note significant issues discovered in reviewing the case (e.g., the AJ made an incorrect jurisdictional determination, the initial decision applied incorrect legal standards, or due process/Burgess notice-type issues). Attorneys may find it helpful to consult with the appropriate Team Leader if they spot a potentially significant issue that was not raised by the parties.

3. Recommended decisions. The Board's decision is generally not a mere repetition of the initial decision, but is typically more focused on the particular issues that must be resolved on review. Here are some drafting pointers.

a. O&Os are comprehensive decisions, which include a full discussion of the background and analysis. Because these cases will be circulated to the public, the audience will not know all of the relevant facts or be familiar with the ID.

b. NPFOs are written for the parties and a potential reviewing court. Assuming that there are no errors in the ID or additional issues that need to be addressed, the main purpose of the NPFO is to give the parties a brief explanation as to why the PFR is being denied. NPFOs are less detailed than O&Os or even internal OAC case memos. Background sections and lengthy recitations of general legal principles are generally unnecessary if the initial decision has sufficiently covered these issues. The parties know the facts and the reviewing court will also have the benefit of the ID. Generally, we do not need to repeat a discussion of issues that the AJ properly adjudicated and that the parties have not raised on review. The order can state as briefly as possible why the arguments on review are not availing. Often the crux of the analysis from the case memorandum can form the basis of the NPFO. As a rule of thumb, these orders can generally be one or two paragraphs to a couple pages of analysis in length.

c. An NPO typically follows the NPFO model in many respects. However, it will often require more in-depth discussion of the legal principle at issue, and what the Board expects for processing as the case goes forward.

4. OAC Team Leaders are always available to discuss issues regarding both the general process as well as your specific case. The team from which each case was assigned is indicated on the screening sheets provided with the case files.

Board decisions routinely address the points raised by and addressed by parties through PFRs or cross-PFRs. The Board may raises issues on its own, even if the parties did not. Haas v. DHS, 2022 MSPB 36, ¶ 10 (2022) ("Though not raised by either party, we modify the initial decision to the extent that the administrative judge relied on 5 C.F.R. § 339.206 to analyze the agency's charge. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(e) (providing that although the Board normally will consider only issues raised by the parties on review, it reserves the authority to consider any issue in an appeal before it).").
