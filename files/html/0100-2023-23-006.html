a. Hatch Act Case

A settlement provision that is contrary to a statutory constraint will not be enforced. Special Counsel v. Reckard, 69 MSPR 130, 132 (1995) (rejecting a Hatch Act settlement for nonconformance with the statutory penalty scheme; remanding the case for further adjudication), following remand, 77 MSPR 651, 653 (1998) (dispute over the propriety of settlement terms was mooted by the passage of time and the respondent's resignation); Special Counsel v. Baker, 69 MSPR 36, 39 (1995) (rejecting a proposed Hatch Act settlement that could not be sustained under law dictating removal in the absence of necessary mitigating factors); Special Counsel v. Bradford, 62 MSPR 239, 240-41 (1994) (declining to accept a Hatch Act settlement: "To permit respondent to retain both her federal government job and her elected, partisan seat would essentially place the Board in the position of endorsing a continuing violation of the Hatch Act."); Special Counsel v. Doyle, 45 MSPR 43, 49 (1990). But see Special Counsel v. Cowan, 2022 MSPB 2 (2022) (deciding on interlocutory appeal that the Hatch Act Modernization Act of 2012, 126 Stat. 1616 (2012), allowing MSPB greater discretion in setting penalties for Hatch Act violations, permitted a settlement requiring suspension of the respondent but allowing him to retain elective office; no separate mitigation analysis required for acceptance of agreement); Special Counsel v. DeWitt, 113 MSPR 458, 2010 MSPB 59 (2010) (considering mitigating factors, including a long unblemished service record, resignation from elective office upon knowledge by the employee of the OSC investigation; accepting a settlement of a Hatch Act case imposing a 30-day suspension rather than removal). [Refer to Chapter 13, "Settlement"; refer in this chapter to "Provision Unlawful or Contrary to Public Policy."]
