c. OPM

Retirement appeals add a wrinkle to Board jurisdiction. OPM often processes retirement applications through an initial determination and a reconsideration decision. The appellant ordinarily appeals to MSPB from the reconsideration decision. Under 5 CFR 1201.22(b) (2022), the appellant must appeal a reconsideration decision denying retirement benefits to the Board within the regulatory time from the date of the issuance of the OPM decision. The time limit, as in other appeals, is subject to waiver for good cause. Alo v. OPM, 45 MSPR 571, 574 (1990). The reconsideration process and its relation to MSPB appellate jurisdiction is also described, with some criticism of the ambiguous nature of OPM's written advice to claimants concerning the process, in Toquero v. MSPB, 982 F.2d 520 (Fed. Cir. 1993). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Appeal Misdirected Within MSPB, to OPM, or to OSC," "Negative OPM Reconsideration Decision Appeal" and "Failure to Seek Timely Reconsideration; Requests to Extend Time to Request Reconsideration"; the process is considered at length in Chapter 14 on retirement cases.]

The regulatory time limit for appeals to MSPB from OPM reconsideration decisions has changed over time. Through 1994, 25 days were allowed to file with the Board, clocked from the date of issuance of the reconsideration decision. The time was lengthened in 1995 to 35 days from the issuance of the reconsideration decision. In 1997 the Board again modified its regulatory guidance governing the time limits for appeals. See 62 Fed. Reg. 59991-92 (11/6/97). Current practice, under 5 CFR 1201.22(b)(1) (2022), calculates the time for filing from "no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of the appellant's receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later."

If OPM does not advise the appellant of the right to request a reconsideration decision, but instead issues a decision denying the claim for a benefit, that decision is an OPM final decision appealable to the Board. Perske v. OPM, 48 MSPR 678, 680 (1991) (MSPB had jurisdiction to review an OPM ruling denying the appellant's request for an enhancement of his annuity). Gosse v. OPM, 86 MSPR 566, 569-70 ¶ 7 (2000), examined the appellant's intent to pursue a Board appeal and avoided defaulting him when he directed his arguments to OPM despite having been advised by OPM to appeal to the Board:

Here, the appellant, in his July 14, 1999 letter, sent to OPM well within the time limit for appealing the final decision to the Board, sought review of OPM's finding that he did not serve in a position subject to the Civil Service Retirement Act. Since OPM's June 21 final decision did not provide for requesting reconsideration of its decision, we will consider that letter as the appellant's intent to appeal OPM's final decision to the Board and consider it a timely but misdirected appeal. Thus, we find that the administrative judge erred in dismissing the appeal as untimely with no good cause shown.

Assuming the issuance and timely receipt of a reconsideration decision on the merits, the appellant must then file a timely appeal with the Board. However, if the appellant fails to follow instructions in OPM's reconsideration decision and requests further consideration from OPM within the deadline for filing an appeal, and if OPM in turn fails to forward the otherwise timely appeal to the Board, then good cause exists for an untimely appeal. House v. OPM, 44 MSPR 161, 165 (1990); see Patterson v. OPM, 51 MSPR 408, 411 (1991) (excusing a late appeal despite the attempt by counsel to obtain additional consideration from OPM rather than filing an immediate MSPB appeal); cf. Frederick v. OPM, 13 MSPR 141 (1982) (copy of a letter received by the Board regional office, apparently addressed to OPM, requesting appeal of an OPM reconsideration decision, should have alerted the Board to treat the letter as an appeal). [Refer to this chapter, "Failure of OPM to Act on Reconsideration Request; Failure of OPM to Advise of Reconsideration Right."]

When the reconsideration request is undated and there is substantial confusion as to the actual date of issuance of that decision, reasonable delay in filing the appeal will be excused. Pinder v. OPM, 45 MSPR 133, 136 (1990). A filing delay may also be excused if the OPM reconsideration decision fails to explain to the appellant that the filing date for a Board appeal is calculated from the date of the OPM decision, rather than from the date of receipt of that decision. See Boulware v. OPM, 70 MSPR 136, 139 (1996) (OPM letter and outdated copy of Board regulations sent by OPM to the appellant may have caused her confusion over the proper date for filing her appeal). If there are conflicting or unclear dates on the reconsideration decision, the date of issuance of the decision should be resolved by the AJ if that date is determinative of the timeliness of an appeal. May v. OPM, 39 MSPR 576, 578 (1989); see Ampania v. OPM, 71 MSPR 427, 429 (1996) (delayed appeal was excused: OPM "Handiletter" provided misleading information concerning the time available for an appeal; the situation was further complicated when OPM provided an incorrect fax number to the appellant that the appellant then attempted to use to file the appeal).

Tress v. OPM, 109 MSPR 126, 128 ¶ 5, 2008 MSPB 125 (2008), summarized the approach of the Board toward appeals misdirected to OPM by appellants whose appeals are later but belatedly lodged with MSPB:

...The Board generally holds that an appellant's failure to follow explicit filing instructions does not constitute good cause for any ensuing delay. White v. Department of the Navy, 55 M.S.P.R. 376, 379 (1992). However, the Board has recognized an exception to this rule in cases where appellants have timely but mistakenly sent appeals of OPM final decisions to OPM rather than to the Board. In such cases, the Board has found good cause for the untimely filing when the following conditions have been met: (1) The delay was caused in part by the appellant's failure to follow the directions set forth in OPM's decision and in part by OPM's failure to redirect an otherwise timely appeal to the Board; (2) the appellant clearly intended to seek further review of OPM's decision; (3) the appellant was pro se; and (4) there was no showing of prejudice to the agency. See, e.g., House v. Office of Personnel Management, 44 M.S.P.R. 161, 165 (1990); DeAstrero v. Office of Personnel Management, 39 M.S.P.R. 431, 433-34 (1989), aff'd, 907 F.2d 157 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table); Anderson v. Office of Personnel Management, 24 M.S.P.R. 267, 269 (1984).

See Custodio v. OPM, 113 MSPR 639, 2010 MSPB 95 (2010) (long series of somewhat inconsistent exchanges with OPM concerning the status of the appellant's reconsideration request, coupled with OPM's failure to refer appellant's inquiries to the Board as an appeal, justified waiver of his deadline for filing a retirement benefits appeal).

Karker v. OPM, 80 MSPR 235, 240 ¶ 9 (1998), emphasized the statutory preference for adjudicating claims to vested retirement benefits. This preference was applied in resolving doubt concerning the timeliness of correspondence that OPM should have redirected to the Board as an appeal:

...Even if the letter arrived one or two days late, we would find good cause to waive the filing deadline because the Board has placed a high priority on resolving retirement benefit cases on the merits. Hemphill, 77 MSPR at 512. As we explained in Edney v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 MSPR 60, 61-63 (1998), the whole purpose of the retirement application and appeal process is to determine whether the applicant is entitled to a retirement benefit. Unlike discipline or adverse action cases where an expedient response to an appeal enables the agency to move forward with the management of its programs, there is no such need for finality to compete with the right of an appellant to a decision on the merits. Id. Therefore, any doubt as to whether the Board should waive the filing deadline for an adjudication on the merits should be resolved in favor of the appellant, even more so than in a disciplinary appeal. Id. Lastly, the agency has not established that any prejudice would result from a waiver.

See Brink-Meissner v. OPM, PH-844E-14-0077-I-1 (NP 8/4/2014) (excusing an appeal untimely by six days when an attorney's office staff stamped the OPM reconsideration decision with an improper receipt date but the appellant diligently checked with her counsel on the progress of the filing of the appeal and the appellant was unintentionally misled by her attorney: "the appellant has shown good cause for the 6-day delay in filing her appeal. See Dunbar, 43 M.S.P.R. at 645. In so finding, we acknowledge that the Board has placed a high priority on resolving retirement benefits cases on the merits, see Karker v. Office of Personnel Management, 80 M.S.P.R. 235, ¶ 9 (1998), on the basis that such cases are not adversarial proceedings, and thus 'any doubt as to whether the Board should waive the filing deadline for an adjudication on the merits should be resolved in favor of the appellant....' Id. (citing Edney v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 60, 61-63 (1998)).").

Should OPM issue a reconsideration decision with notice of Board appeal rights, and if the appellant then requests a second reconsideration decision from OPM, delay in a subsequent appeal to the Board will be excused if that delay is at least partially caused by OPM's failure to redirect the second reconsideration request to the Board as an appeal. Villarico v. OPM, 56 MSPR 491, 494-95 (1993); Myers v. OPM, 43 MSPR 323, 327 (1990); DeAstrero v. OPM, 39 MSPR 431, 433 (1989) (citing Anderson v. OPM, 24 MSPR 267, 268-69 (1984)); cf. Yongao v. OPM, 80 MSPR 389, 393 ¶ 10 (1998) (misdirection of an appeal to OPM does not defeat the timeliness of the appeal); Hughes v. OPM, 9 MSPR 395, 397 (1982) (same). Hemphill v. OPM, 77 MSPR 508, 511 (1998), summed up the law:

[I]n cases where the delay in filing was caused by both a pro se appellant's failure to follow OPM's instructions in its reconsideration decision for appealing to the Board and OPM's failure to forward an appellant's otherwise timely appeal to the Board, the Board has found good cause for waiver of its filing time limit. Villarico v. Office of Personnel Management, 56 MSPR 491, 494-95 (1993); DeAstrero v. Office of Personnel Management, 39 MSPR 431, 433-34 (1989), aff'd, 907 F.2d 157 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table). In so holding, the Board has considered as further grounds for waiver, the appellant's clear intention to seek review of the reconsideration decision within the Board's filing time limit and the lack of evidence indicating that OPM would be prejudiced by granting the waiver. Villarico, 56 MSPR at 495; DeAstrero, 39 MSPR at 434.

All of the above-mentioned factors are present here.... Instead of directing his appeal to the Board, the appellant sent two letters to OPM following its issuance of the reconsideration decision, both challenging that decision. Further, despite the appellant's clear expression in the first letter of disagreement with the reconsideration decision, OPM's reply of January 6, 1997 was not responsive and OPM did not forward the first letter to the Board. Id. Not until the appellant's second letter, dated January 10, 1997, did OPM reply responsively, reiterating the procedures for filing a Board appeal. Id. Promptly thereafter, the appellant filed this Board appeal.

Maggard v. OPM, 102 MSPR 75, 78 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 128 (2006), also summarized the considerations governing Board acceptance of an appeal in a retirement case when the appeal is misdirected to OPM:

OPM's reconsideration decision instructed the appellant that she should file her petition for appeal with the Board's Central Regional Office, and the Board generally holds that an appellant's failure to follow explicit filing instructions does not constitute good cause for any ensuing delay. White v. Department of the Navy, 55 M.S.P.R. 376, 379 (1992). However, the Board has recognized an exception to this rule in cases where appellants have timely but mistakenly sent appeals of OPM reconsideration decisions to OPM rather than to the Board. In such cases, the Board has found good cause for the untimely filing when the following conditions have been met: (1) The delay was caused in part by the appellant's failure to follow the directions set forth in the reconsideration decision and in part by OPM's failure to direct an otherwise timely appeal to the Board; (2) the appellant clearly intended to seek further review of the reconsideration decision; (3) the appellant was pro se; and (4) there was no showing of prejudice to the agency. See, e.g., House v. Office of Personnel Management, 44 M.S.P.R. 161, 165 (1990); DeAstrero v. Office of Personnel Management, 39 M.S.P.R. 431, 433-34 (1989), aff'd, 907 F.2d 157 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table); Anderson v. Office of Personnel Management, 24 M.S.P.R. 267, 269 (1984).

See Killian v. OPM, 100 MSPR 583, 585 ¶ 6 (2005) (misdirected and procedurally incomplete appeal from the denial of a disability retirement application: "a pro se appellant should not be denied an opportunity to have her case heard on the merits where the intention to file an appeal within the Board's time limit was clear and there was a lack of evidence indicating that OPM would be prejudiced by granting the waiver"); Nichols v. OPM, 96 MSPR 34, 37 ¶ 8 (2004) (restating rationale of Hemphill); cf. Camilo v. OPM, SF-0831-05-0449-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010) (question of when the appeal was actually sent to OPM: "the first page of the appellant's submission July 6, 2004. The Board has accepted OPM's date stamp as the date of receipt and extrapolated the mailing date by presuming that the petition was mailed five days prior to receipt. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(1); see, e.g., Karker v. Office of Personnel Management, 80 M.S.P.R. 235, ¶ 9 (1998).").

Under Sanchez v. OPM, 50 MSPR 96, 98 (1991), although a pro se appellant might be excused for an appeal misdirected to OPM, the excuse did not avail the represented appellant. If the reconsideration decision clearly states appeal rights and OPM repeatedly instructs the appellant concerning the proper place to lodge an appeal, the Board may decide that the appellant consciously elected not to pursue his appeal rights with the Board. A subsequent appeal will then be untimely. Roy v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3228) (MSPB properly failed to excuse an appeal from OPM that was five years late; the appellant "continued to seek review by OPM despite OPM's further notices that the board was the proper forum for review."); Maniago v. OPM, 45 MSPR 553, 557 (1990) ("We find that the appellant's persistence in continuing to write OPM, thereby further delaying his appeal to the Board, in spite of clear notice that his appeal rights lay with the Board, does not demonstrate either prudence or diligence under the circumstances"; the appellant's efforts were characterized as a specific rejection of the OPM's instructions to file an appeal with the Board; distinguishing the cases of appellants who "merely fail[ed] to follow OPM's instructions" and filed the appeal initially with OPM), followed Chavez v. OPM, 46 MSPR 390, 393-94 (1990) (repeated protests to OPM; failure to follow instructions to appeal to MSPB).

The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's rejection of a late retirement appeal despite some earlier correspondence to OPM, Hapgood v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3336):

[S]he contends that her November 16, 1986, letter to OPM tolled the filing deadline and constituted timely filing. We disagree. The regulations are specific: petitions for appeal must be filed in the appropriate regional office. 5 CFR 1201.22(a). The fact that other tribunals may operate differently is irrelevant. Likewise, the fact that Hapgood was unaware of Board procedure and may not have been sent the instructions on how to file an appeal does not change the fact that the attempted "petition for appeal" was not correctly filed. At best, such evidence is relevant in determining whether good cause existed for waiving the filing deadline....

OPM may object to the timeliness of a Board appeal if the appellant was required to use the reconsideration process but did not timely invoke it.
