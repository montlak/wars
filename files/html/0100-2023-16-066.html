d. Necessity for Specific Warning

The opportunity to improve depends on notice of deficiencies. An employee must be informed that his performance falls below minimum standards for a critical element. Informal discussions about performance problems do not equate to a specific warning. Lacking proper notice, the first notice to the employee cannot be the removal proposal under Chapter 43. Grant v. Dept. of Transp., 28 MSPR 106, 109 (1985) ("[t]he Board has found that counseling sessions which fail to warn the employee of the possibility of an impending or contemplated action do not satisfy an agency's obligation to afford the employee an opportunity to improve. Zang v. Defense Investigative Service, 26 MSPR 155, 157 (1985)."); Grant v. Coast Guard, 24 MSPR 663, 667 (1984) ("All of the specifications contained in the notice involve incidents of unacceptable performance which occurred after the date of the performance appraisal. At no time following that appraisal was appellant notified that his performance was, in fact, unacceptable, nor was he given an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance.").

5 CFR 430.204(b) (2022) explains that performance appraisal systems are used to provide information to employees on their performance and on how it may be improved. In a challenge to a performance action based upon an allegation that the agency failed to advise the employee how to improve performance, consideration was given to whether notice of the errors alone informed the employee of what is needed for improvement. Dietrich v. Dept. of Energy (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-1958). Early decisions suggested that sufficiency of notice is determined by what the employee knew, as evidenced by meetings with him to discuss deficiencies. Coleman v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 305, 310 (1985). One decision was unclear as to whether the notice must be written or whether the notice could be delivered through counseling or an appraisal discussion. Stubblefield v. Dept. of Commerce, 28 MSPR 572 (1985). The Board implied that notice in the form of counseling sessions is sufficient, without a showing of harmful error, even if an agency does not follow its own regulation requiring a specific written notice of poor performance. Ketchum v. FAA, 28 MSPR 268, 271 (1985); see Heller v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 35, 39-40 (1985) (seemingly concluding that through his appraisal, the appellant was placed on notice of deficiencies and the need to improve: "The presiding official, however, found that appellant was given a reasonable opportunity to improve because he was advised by performance appraisals for periods ending in June, 1981, and February, 1982, that his performance was unacceptable. Appellant asserts that the presiding official erred because the counselings appellant received did not identify the specific critical elements he failed to meet and the February, 1982, appraisal was not a formal evaluation. However, the February 1982, appraisal specifically stated that appellant performed unacceptably in critical element 4-4. That this appraisal was not formal is irrelevant. Col. Wrigley testified that appellant was made aware of the appraisal. Indeed, as previously noted, appellant himself submitted the appraisal as an exhibit. Therefore, we find appellant was notified that he was performing unacceptably and was given a reasonable opportunity to improve."); cf. Coleman, supra.

Agencies now generally use written notices identifying unacceptable performance and defining PIP requirements. The beauty of a well-written warning notice lies in its clarity and specificity. The employee must be told specifically that his work is in the unsatisfactory range for at least one critical element, and given this information without equivocation. Counseling can be vague and misunderstood. The counseling of an employee concerning work product and errors is not considered under Sandland to be the same as informing the employee that his work is unacceptable and offering him an opportunity to improve. Beasley v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 213, 214 (1984). It is notice of deficiencies that is required, not notice of a future penalty if deficiencies are not corrected. In an unacceptable performance action, the law does not require the agency to warn the employee that failure to improve will result in removal. The agency is required to give an employee a reasonable opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance after at least being counseled as to unacceptable performance. Nothing requires the agency to specify, before the notice of proposed action, what action it will take. Egleberry v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 217, 218 (1985).

A written notice of deficiencies, or of an improvement period, need not be particularly elaborate, Bare v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 684, 686 (1986):

[M]ore than four months after the change to lower grade was effected, the supervisor formally evaluated the appellant's performance as unacceptable with respect to the critical element of his position (which concerned the performance of "technical services assigned by supervisor").

Two months after the appellant received his performance appraisal, his second-level supervisor issued a memorandum in which he assigned three tasks to the appellant. In that memorandum, he stated that the appellant was to complete all the assignments within 17 days; that he could request assistance on the first two tasks from his immediate supervisor; that he could request "advice, explanations, etc." on the third task from another agency official; that his performance would be discussed with him after the second-level supervisor had reviewed the completed assignments; and that the appellant's failure to show substantial improvement in his performance could result in his removal.
