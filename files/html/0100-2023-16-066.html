broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section D: Unilateral Changes in Employment Conditions and Past Practice, Subsection 02: Past Practice, Part a: Employment Condition
a. Employment Condition

For there to be a past practice, a condition of employment must be involved. A matter that is not a condition of employment does not become a condition of employment either through practice or agreement. Dept. of Labor and AFGE Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, Local 1748, 38 FLRA 899, 908 (1990); see Customs Serv. and NTEU, 29 FLRA 307, 308 n.2 (1987) ("A matter does not become a condition of employment through past practice. Rather, an independent analysis of whether a matter is a condition of employment at the time a dispute arises is necessary.").

To sustain a charge concerning a unilateral change in past practice involving a condition of employment, the past practice must relate to working conditions affecting unit employees. There was no requirement for the agency to negotiate on the composition of a committee concerned with patient care, having nothing to do with working conditions. That management occasionally appointed unit physicians to the committee did not mean that management had abdicated its right to determine unilaterally the membership of the committee. Membership on the committee had no effect on working conditions of the doctors appointed to it, other than possible prestige--insufficient to trigger a requirement for impact bargaining. VAMC, Bath and AFGE Local 491, 4 FLRA 563, 573 (1980) (ALJ Decision). Use of exchange facilities did not establish a past practice when their use did not rise to the level of a working condition. DOD, Ft. Buchanan, San Juan and AFGE Local 2614, 24 FLRA 971, 974-75 (1986).

The characterization of benefits does not depend on whether the benefits are necessities or luxuries. In Dept. of Labor, Employment Standards Admin. and AFGE, Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, Local 948, 37 FLRA 25, 34-36 (1990), the battle was over whether management unilaterally and unlawfully discontinued its support and payment for water coolers in the office. FLRA concluded that use of coolers became a condition of employment that was improperly discontinued without bargaining. FLRA rejected the argument that an old ruling of the Comptroller General, 6 Comp. Gen. 619, precluded expenditures for water coolers because they did not constitute a "necessary expense." The necessity, the Authority stated, could be determined through negotiations. FLRA directed restoration of the status quo. For another water cooler case, refer to Dept. of Labor and AFGE Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, Local 1748, 38 FLRA 899 (1990). Conditions of employment, and related past practices, may concern benefits to union officials. An agency improperly declined to allow a union official to have the same equipment at another worksite that he used for union business at his original work location. DHHS, SSA, Field Operations, Region II and AFGE, 38 FLRA 193, 198 (1990) (self-correcting typewriter, office partitions). Ordinarily, a unilateral change in official time practices impacts working conditions. A unilateral change in the amount of official time permitted a union officer for contract negotiations and preparation was a ULP. SSA and AFGE, 13 FLRA 112 (1983); see VAMC Muskogee and AFGE Local 2250, 53 FLRA 1228, 1241-42 (1998) (ALJ Decision) (ULP unilaterally to change official time allocations for union official representing employees in EEO hearings).

AFGE Local 2761 v. FLRA, 866 F.2d 1443, 1448 (D.C. Cir. 1989), involving the use of exchange facilities, implied that the existence of a past practice may suggest the formation of an employment condition:

If a matter is unquestionably within or outside of the duty to bargain--because, for example, the effect on working conditions is clear and direct, or, conversely, because the matter does not affect bargaining unit employees or is within management's exclusive authority--the existence or lack of past practice is irrelevant to the question whether a matter is a condition of employment.... In close cases, however, past practice can be determinative. See e.g., Newport Naval Base, 3 F.L.R.A. at 414 (union president's use of official time for representational purposes found to be a condition of employment where the agency had allowed the practice over an extended period of time); Mid-America, 9 F.L.R.A. at 240 (government could not unilaterally change established practice regarding retirement receptions, employee activities during breaks, and administrative time for luncheon preparation and cleanup).
