b. Security Arrangement

There will be the occasional case presenting exceptional concerns about physical security or safety. Without any statement explaining why the AJ did so, the Board noted in Wyse v. Dept. of Transp., 39 MSPR 85, 89 (1988), the AJ's approval of the agency's motion for an armed guard at a hearing. When the appellant declined to attend the hearing in the presence of the guard, the Board concluded that he was not prejudiced, for his representative continued with the hearing in his absence. Groshans v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 629, 640-41 (1995), explained that the AJ may call for the presence of guards at the request of an agency (perhaps at the request of an appellant as well) without much by way of justification to the opposing party. The theory is that no harm is done by treating a Board hearing like a potential insurrection. Because the AJ does not have to provide a cogent explanation for his actions, because the "justification" can be provided ex parte (the justification not going to the merits of the case), and because prejudice will be difficult to prove, the AJ's discretion may become unarticulated and unreviewable, a poor position for the Board to place itself in. Yet Groshans considered the process unobjectionable:

The agency's representative contacted the administrative judge the day before the scheduled hearing and stated that an agency witness had requested a security presence at the hearing. The administrative judge convened a teleconference with the parties and advised the appellant's attorney of this fact and that he had decided to grant the request. The appellant and her attorney objected to any security presence, in the absence of more information as to who had requested it and why.

At the hearing, the appellant submitted a document titled "Appellant's motion for full disclosure; motion for recusal of the administrative judge; motion for hearing; motion for sanctions and motion for continuance." The appellant's attorney explained the appellant's position and extensively discussed the matter with the administrative judge at the hearing. However, the administrative judge denied the motions without disclosing the basis for the agency's request, and he declined to permit the appellant's attorney to cross examine agency witnesses to determine who had requested the security presence and why such request had been made.

Thus, two armed Federal Protective Service Officers were stationed outside the witness room. They visually inspected witnesses' brief cases and purses, and conducted searches with wands similar to those used at airports. The officers were there only for the first day of the hearing. We note that all of the appellant's witnesses, as well as the appellant herself, testified on the second day of the hearing.

In her petition for review, the appellant renews the objections raised in the motion presented at the hearing and in the discussion with the administrative judge. She contends that the administrative judge erred in accepting and acting upon ex parte communications from the agency; namely, that the agency should have filed a written motion under 5 CFR § 1201.55(d) for an order under 5 USC § 1204(e)(1)(B) to protect a witness or other individual from harassment, and that such motion should have been filed as early as practicable in the proceedings. She requests that the Board take appropriate action against the agency, the agency's counsel, and the administrative judge. The appellant also objects to the administrative judge's ruling that she would not be allowed to cross examine witnesses in order to show prejudice to her case, through possible intimidation of witnesses due to the presence of the officers.

We find, however, that an administrative judge's decision regarding a security presence at the hearing is a decision encompassing many concerns. It is a procedural matter related to the administrative judge's broad discretion in conducting hearings and not a matter that must be argued, justified and explained. Moreover, while an administrative judge may entertain either informal requests from the parties or a written motion, the decision to require a security presence at the hearing may properly be based upon the administrative judge's own initiative, considering the unique circumstances of each case, including the nature of the hearing site.

We do not find that the administrative judge violated the prohibition against ex parte communications under 5 CFR § 1201.102, because his conversation with the agency representative concerned a procedural security matter, which need not be raised in writing, and not the merits of the case. Nor is a decision to require a security presence necessarily a request for a protective order under 5 USC § 1204(e)(1)(B), which speaks to protecting witnesses from "harassment."

While a party may rightly complain about the manner in which a security measure was carried out, it would be a rare instance in which a party could show intimidation by the mere presence of security guards outside the witness room. Witnesses attending our hearings should no more be intimidated by our security measures than they are when they encounter security at airports, court houses, and many federal office buildings.

Accordingly, a decision whether to allow security measures, such as those employed here, is a matter left to the sound discretion of the administrative judge and such decision need not be based solely on good cause shown by a moving party. Thus, we find that the appellant has not shown prejudice to her substantive rights.

Chapter 10 of the Judges' Handbook provides some guidance concerning security arrangements at Board hearings:

8. SECURITY: The Board has held that an AJ's decision to have a security presence (e.g., two Federal Protective Service Officers) at a hearing "is a procedural matter related to the [AJ's] broad discretion in conducting hearings and not a matter that must be argued, justified, and explained." Groshans v. Department of the Navy, 67 M.S.P.R. 629, 641 (1995). The AJ may request such a presence on the motion of a party or on the AJ's own initiative, based on the exercise of sound discretion. A party's request to the AJ that security be present is not a prohibited ex parte contact since it does not address the merits of the appeal. Id. Moreover, the mere presence of security guards outside the hearing room would rarely constitute intimidation, although a party may rightly complain about the manner in which a security matter was carried out. Id. Regardless of whether the security presence is requested by a party or the AJ, to maintain security and the appearance of fairness, all parties, representatives, and witnesses should be subject to the same measures.

Along these lines, while there is no law on the matter, if a party, witness or observer wears a gun and the AJ is not comfortable with the presence of the weapon, it would seem to be within the AJ's discretion to require the person to store it in a safe location outside the hearing room, and if the individual does not comply, he may be ejected from the hearing.

Some agencies call for the presence of guards in an attempt to confirm in the AJ's mind the validity of a threat charge in an adverse action--the idea being that it is inconsistent to fire an employee for making a threat and then participate in a hearing in that employee's presence without protection. Appellants could follow the same tack when alleging that they were coerced into retiring or resigning by a bullying or bizarre supervisor. The AJ is in no position to resolve disputes over security, so the AJ is given what amounts to unreviewable discretion. AJs should be able to separate their conclusions about security arrangements from the decisions they make on the merits of appeals after hearing the evidence.
