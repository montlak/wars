3. Specific and Supported Objection

The litigation styled In re Chase, 1 MSPR 323, 1 MSPB 310 (1980), found the Board entering a summary order dismissing a PFR that cited no law alleged to have been misinterpreted. The same result was reached in Clay v. USPS, 1 MSPR 444, 445, 1 MSPB 429 (1980) ("The instant petition, while referring to all of the criteria for review, neither directly cites any criterion as a basis for the petition, nor presents argument or documentation tending to meet any criterion for review."). Unless the formalities are met, and either allegations are made of new and material evidence or arguments are made that the initial decision erroneously interprets a statute or regulation, the PFR will be dismissed for failure to meet the regulatory criteria for petitions at 5 CFR 1201.115 (2022). See Mahler v. Marine Corps, 1 MSPR 456, 1 MSPB 441 (1980). Argument should be explained. A PFR citing an erroneous interpretation of a statute or regulation must consist of more than a statement that the law cited in the AJ's discussion was misstated. Tibbits v. VA, 5 MSPR 61, 5 MSPB 136 (1981); see Bajuscak v. VA, DA-1221-15-0552-B-1 (NP 11/8/2022) ("Although the appellant asks the Board to review all of the documents that he submitted on appeal to take notice of 'the dates of actions against [him] and the timing of final actions taken,' we find that his petition for review is not sufficiently specific to enable the Board to ascertain whether there is a serious evidentiary challenge justifying a complete review of the record. See Tines, 56 M.S.P.R. at 92; Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 M.S.P.R. 129, 133 (1980) (determining that, before the Board will undertake a complete review of the record, the petitioning party must explain why the challenged factual determination is incorrect and identify the specific evidence in the record which demonstrates the error), review denied, 669 F.2d 613 (9th Cir. 1982) (per curium)."); Barnes v. Dept. of Navy, PH-315H-15-0143-I-1 (NP 8/4/2015) ("A petition for review must state a party's objections to the initial decision, including all of the party's legal and factual arguments, and must be supported by specific references to the record and any applicable laws or regulations. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(b). The appellant's bare statement that his case should be reopened due to "prejudice" fails to satisfy these requirements and does not establish any basis for review under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115. See Stoglin v. Department of the Air Force, 2015 MSPB 43, ¶ 6; Rumsey v. Department of Justice, 120 M.S.P.R. 259, ¶ 11 (2013)."); Dinkins v. OPM, 70 MSPR 427, 429 (1996) (PFR was insufficient that argued only that "the record should be reopened for further scrutiny" because "all of the facts relevant to the issues in question were not properly reviewed and taken into account in their entirety"); Schenarts v. Dept. of Treasury, 30 MSPR 415 (1986) (citing Jones v. VA, 3 MSPB 182 (1980)) (unelaborated allegation that the AJ misinterpreted the law is an insufficient basis for review). Frankfort v. USPS, SF-3443-10-0352-I-1 (NP 9/28/2010), rejected as insufficient to constitute a properly reasoned PFR a submission by the appellant characterized by the Board as a repetition of her response to the acknowledgment order and to an order to show cause.

Reiteration of the basis for appeal argued to the AJ is insufficient to trigger a complete review of the record on appeal. The Board will not undertake the burden of a complete review if the petitioning party does not explain in the petition why the challenged determination is incorrect and identify the specific evidence in the record demonstrating the error. Barber v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 520, 521, 3 MSPB 41 (1980); see Bell v. USPS, DC-3443-16-0170-I-1 (NP 7/28/2022) ("the Board will normally embark upon a complete review of the record only if the appellant identifies a specific error that the administrative judge made in evaluating the evidence or applying the law. See Baney v. Department of Justice, 109 M.S.P.R. 242, ¶ 7 (2008); Tines v. Department of the Air Force, 56 M.S.P.R. 90, 92 (1992); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115"). In a PFR, it is insufficient to incorporate by reference all the allegations raised before the AJ. The petitioner must set forth specific objections to the initial decision under 5 CFR 1201.114(b) (2022) ("A petition or cross petition for review states a party's objections to the initial decision, including all of the party's legal and factual arguments, and must be supported by references to applicable laws or regulations and by specific references to the record."). Hulett v. Dept. of Navy, 120 MSPR 54, 55-56 n.2, 2013 MSPB 48 (2013) (Vice Chairman Wagner concurring and dissenting) ("We disagree with the dissent that the appellant satisfied the Board's pleading standards by stating that his filings from below are "incorporated" by reference into his petition for review. Attempts to incorporate by reference briefs that were filed below are insufficient to meet the Board's standards. See Cole v. Department of Transportation, 18 M.S.P.R. 102, 105 n.3 (1983)."); see Washington v. USPS, SF-0752-16-0008-I-1 (NP 7/29/2016) ("Stating that pleadings filed in the regional office proceeding are incorporated by reference into a petition for review does not satisfy the Board's regulatory requirements; a petition for review must contain sufficient specificity for the Board to ascertain whether there is a serious evidentiary challenge justifying a complete review of the record, and the petition itself must identify any procedural or adjudicatory errors and explain how they affected the outcome of the initial decision. Hulett v. Department of the Navy, 120 M.S.P.R. 54, ¶ 5 n.2 (2013)."); Agoranos v. DOJ, CH-1221-11-0466-B-1 (NP 7/22/2014) ("By merely stating that he incorporates by reference his April 2, 2012 petition for review, the appellant has not made clear whether he is asserting a challenge to the Board's decision to affirm portions of the March 1, 2012 initial decision, which the administrative judge reincorporated into his September 23, 2013 remand initial decision, or alleging that the administrative judge made the same errors on remand that he made in adjudicating the appellant's IRA appeal in MSPB Docket No. CH-1221-11-0466-W-1, or raising other bases for the Board's review. Thus, we decline to consider arguments incorporated by reference from the appellant's April 2, 2012 petition for review. See Mulroy v. Office of Personnel Management, 92 M.S.P.R. 404, 410 (2002) (finding that a pleading, which merely incorporated arguments set forth before the administrative judge, failed to meet the criteria for review because it did not explain how or why the administrative judge erred)."); Blumenson v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 644, 646 n.1 (1986) (agency cannot state in its PFR that it is incorporating by reference all arguments presented to the AJ); Farris v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 299, 301 n.1 (1985); ("The petition for review requests that we incorporate by reference all of the allegations raised before the presiding official. This request is DENIED because it fails to meet the criteria for review established by 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115, which requires that the petition set forth specific objections to the initial decision."); Hagan v. USPS, 25 MSPR 64, 64 n.* (1984) ("the agency has simply resubmitted the argument in opposition to appellant's motion for attorney fees that it filed with the presiding official. This argument, by definition does not meet the criteria for review because it does not make specific objection to the presiding official's findings").
