f. Coextensive Issues; Scope of Complaint

Board processing of mixed cases may be initiated following the 120th day after the filing of an EEO complaint with the employing agency, assuming no earlier final agency resolution. But the blocking effect of an EEO complaint for that 120-day period presumes that the Board appeal and the EEO complaint are premised on the same or coextensive issues. "In analyzing an EEO discrimination complaint, the Board looks to the appellant's complaint, the agency's treatment and processing of the claim, and the surrounding circumstances." Kozak v. DHHS, 90 MSPR 398, 401 ¶ 6 (2001) (examining an EEO complaint to determine if it fairly encompassed the elements of a constructive demotion claim); Wegener v. Dept. of Interior, 89 MSPR 644, 648-49 ¶ 11 (2001) (noting the action by the EEOC judge, returning a complaint to the agency without hearing for the issuance of a final decision, was based on the conclusion that a constructive discharge was appealable to the Board; noting also the agency decision providing the appellant appeal rights to the Board as to that claim); Lott v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 666, 669 ¶ 8 (1999) (citing Mincey v. USPS, 79 MSPR 663, 666 (1998)), post.

In Allen v. USDA, 29 MSPR 115, 117 (1985), the appellant alleged that the agency directed his geographic reassignment to force his involuntary retirement. He contended that the action constituted a reduction in force without RIF procedures. He filed both an EEO complaint and a Board appeal. The Board lacked jurisdiction over the then-premature appeal, citing Lewis v. IRS, 2 MSPR 8, 9, 2 MSPB 181 (1980):

Here the appellant's Board appeal and EEO complaint are both premised on the same personnel action, a directed reassignment. The issues on the face of his EEO complaint and his Board appeal may not be deemed totally coextensive because the appellant's EEO complaint, which mentions his reassignment, does not mention RIF or involuntary retirement. Nonetheless, since the Board appeal and the prior-filed EEO complaint are premised on the same personnel action, and since resolution of all the allegations made to the Board depends on consideration of the same set of facts, we find that the Board lacked jurisdiction over all the allegations made in the petition for appeal at the time it was filed. Thus, the presiding official erred in considering the allegation that the Board had jurisdiction of the appellant's appeal as a constructive RIF while not reaching the allegation that the Board had jurisdiction over it as an involuntary retirement.

Allen was remanded because by the time the PFR was decided, 120 days elapsed from the date the appellant filed his EEO complaint; through lapse of time, the Board acquired jurisdiction.

In Lott v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 666 (1999), the appellant received a notice that he would be separated by RIF. Before the date of separation he filed an EEO complaint protesting the abolition of his position. A few weeks later, the agency issued the appellant a second, superseding RIF notice advising him that his position was abolished, but offering to him a position at a lower grade. A year or so later, an EEOC judge decided that the appellant's EEO complaint presented a mixed case and dismissed the matter with instructions to the agency to treat it as a mixed case complaint. Within 30 days from the EEOC dismissal, Mr. Lott appealed the RIF to the Board, whose AJ held the appeal was untimely. The AJ reasoned that the EEO complaint did not protest a demotion, meaning that the filing of the demotion appeal was calculated from its effective date rather than from a conclusion of the EEO proceedings. The Board recognized the confusion generated by the circumstances and noted that the appellant was not informed that he had to refile or amend his pending EEO complaint to assert that it covered the demotion. The EEOC judge and the appellant both considered the EEO complaint to have encompassed the demotion. The Board reversed the initial decision dismissing the appeal as untimely.

An example of a case involving dissimilar EEO claims, leading to a conclusion that an appeal from a removal was not barred by the earlier filing of an EEO complaint, is Bibby v. Dept. of Transp., 30 MSPR 507 (1986). The appellant was removed for AWOL. Her appeal form noted that she already filed an EEO complaint. The AJ dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction; the appeal seemed premature. On PFR, the Board vacated and remanded, observing that the EEO complaint was based on an allegation that the agency discriminated because it failed to pay the appellant a salary equivalent to that of two men who performed the same duties, an issue unrelated to the agency's removal action. The Board added in its opinion a cryptic reference to Burgess v. MSPB, 758 F.2d 641 (Fed. Cir. 1985), determining that before an AJ can dismiss an appeal for lack of jurisdiction, his order must be reasonably calculated to tell the appellant what is required to obtain a hearing. Perhaps the Board was suggesting that the appellant should have been told what the exact issue was--demonstrating that her earlier EEO complaint involved a claim or personnel action unrelated to her appeal--and placing a higher duty on the AJ, requiring the AJ to advise the appellant on how to respond to an agency's motion to dismiss, e.g., by providing factual evidence to counter the agency's representations.

To determine whether an EEO complaint encompassed an appealable action, e.g., a removal, with the result that a subsequent Board appeal of the adverse action was timely, the Board considered the totality of the circumstances: the actions described in the complaint (to be "liberally" interpreted), correspondence concerning the complaint, the interpretation of the complaint by an EEOC administrative judge and an agency representative before that judge, and the wording of the agency's final decision on the adverse action proposal. See Gray v. USPS, 93 MSPR 161, 165-66 ¶ 12 (2002); cf. Fenner v. DHHS, SF-0752-17-0214-I-1 (NP 12/16/2022) (agency's treatment of the EEO complaint did not demonstrate it encompassed the removal; remand to adjudicate timely-filed appeal that postdated the EEO complaint); White v. DHHS, AT-0752-16-0029-I-1 (NP 8/5/2016) ("The administrative judge found that the appellant failed to establish that her EEO complaint was a mixed-case complaint or that her removal was an accepted issue in her complaint. This finding is supported by the EEO documents in evidence, including the appellant's April 2008 formal complaint, a November 2011 remand order issued by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) Office of Federal Operations (OFO), and the September 30, 2015 FAD, none of which list removal as an accepted issue. Furthermore, by order dated March 3, 2014, the presiding EEOC administrative judge expressly rejected the appellant's motion to amend her complaint to include the issue of removal.").

The Board appeared to conclude that if the underlying personnel action is appealable to the Board, the scope of the EEO complaint does not bar the appellant from asserting additional bases of discrimination as a challenge to the action when it is taken to the Board. Williams v. USDA, 106 MSPR 677, 682-83 ¶ 12, 2007 MSPB 244 (2007), remanded an appeal based upon the AJ's failure to hold a hearing on the appellant's claim that he was forced to retire because of the agency's failure to accommodate his disability:

In this case, we find that the AJ should have considered, in his jurisdictional analysis, the appellant's claim of denial of a reasonable accommodation. The permissible scope of the Board's review of an appellant's discrimination claims on the question of coercion is not confined solely to the specific allegations accepted by the agency for investigation; rather, it may extend to any discrimination like or related to the substance of the allegations in the claim and which reasonably can be expected to grow out of the investigation triggered by the claim. See Pacheco v. Mineta, 448 F.3d 783, 789 (5th Cir. 2006); E.E.O.C. v. Delight Wholesale Co., 973 F.2d 664, 668 (8th Cir. 1992). Further, in determining the nature of the appellant's discrimination clams, the administrative complaint is to be construed liberally. Stuart v. General Motors Corp., 217 F.3d 621, 631 (8th Cir. 2000).
