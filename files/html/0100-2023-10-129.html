d. Agency Head

In a separate opinion attached to a pro forma affirmation of an initial decision, the Board's Vice Chairman, Maria Johnson, stated in Barber v. FDIC, 37 MSPR 620, 622-27 (1988):

The agency proposed to remove the appellant for placing personal long distance calls...and for purchasing and reselling two used agency mobile telephones in violation of agency regulations.

The three members of the agency's Board of Directors were the "deciding official" in this case, as in all removal actions....

...

The agency...refused to make the Board members available to testify at the hearing. The appellant moved to dismiss the charge, and the administrative judge denied that motion. Instead, as a sanction, the administrative judge precluded the agency from introducing any new evidence concerning the reasonableness of the penalty and the allegation concerning ex parte communications.

...

[T]his case presents an important policy question: Whether an agency head, who is also the deciding official in a chapter 75 removal case, may be ordered to appear as a witness in an MSPB proceeding where the appellant has questioned whether the relevant Douglas factors have been considered and whether prohibited ex parte communications have occurred in the decisionmaking process.

In support of its position that the agency need not make its Board members available to testify at the hearing, the agency has relied primarily on Bacon v. DHUD, 757 F.2d 265 (Fed. Cir. 1985), as well as on United States v. Morgan, 313 U.S. 409 (1941) (Morgan IV), and Simplex Time Recorder Co. v. Secretary of Labor, 766 F.2d 575 (D.C. Cir. 1985).

...Bacon does not support the agency's refusal to make the Board members available to testify in this case. In Bacon, the appellants were affected by a reduction in force..."[t]o resolve workload and skills imbalances."...On appeal, the court found that the administrative judge properly denied the request to call Secretary Pierce because neither the MSPB nor the court was empowered to look behind the stated basis for the RIF.

Citing Morgan IV, the court noted that the Supreme Court had unequivocally drawn the line against inquiry into the mental process of an agency head where his or her decision involved a matter committed to agency discretion.... It concluded that the MSPB had no authority to probe into the mental processes of the Secretary to ascertain whether a particular "political philosophy" influenced the RIF decision, that the reasons were formally stated, and that the only function of review was to determine whether the record supported the stated reasons.

Thus, the significance of Bacon lies in the fact that the testimony sought by the appellants was not subject to review by the MSPB or the court because the information sought related to a matter that was committed to the agency's discretion; the information was, therefore, irrelevant and unnecessary....

In the instant case, however, the Board members were the sole decisionmakers concerning the reasonableness of the penalty, as well as the individuals to whom the new ex parte information was communicated. In a chapter 75 removal action, the agency has the burden of proving that it considered the relevant Douglas factors and that the penalty was within the bounds of reasonableness. The Board members therefore had information that--unlike the information sought in Bacon--was both relevant and material, and they were, in fact, the sole source of that information.

Further, as the administrative judge noted...the reasons for the agency's action in Bacon were "formally stated," while the agency's decision letter in this case did not set forth the Board members' reasons for concluding that removal was a reasonable penalty. See Bacon, 757 F.2d at 269....

The court's decision in Simplex...also fails to support the agency's position.... The court found that the administrative law judge properly denied Simplex's request to call four officials of the Department of Labor to testify at the hearing because the information sought involved discretion decisions concerning enforcement of the Occupational Safety and Health Act.... This, it concluded, would run counter to the cautions set forth in Morgan IV. The court also noted that Simplex did not identify any information in those officials' possession that it could not obtain from published reports and available agency documents.

Contrary to the agency's assertions throughout this proceeding, the appellant did not wish to probe the mental processes of the Board members in an area that was forbidden.... In Morgan IV, the Supreme Court held that the Secretary of Agriculture should not have been required to testify concerning his decision to set maximum rates to be charged by market agencies for their services at the Kansas City Stockyards because it was not the function of the court to probe the mental processes of the Secretary. The court found that the proceeding before the Secretary had a "quality resembling that of a judicial proceeding."...It concluded that, just as a judge could not be subjected to such scrutiny, the administrative process should be equally respected....

[T]he disciplinary proceeding before the Board members was not similar to a judicial proceeding where the reasons for their decisionmaking were beyond scrutiny. Issues concerning the reasonableness of the penalty and harmful ex parte communications with the deciding official are, in fact, subject to review by the MSPB and the courts.

...

[T]he fact that the deciding officials were also agency heads cannot operate to relieve them of their responsibility to testify when they have relevant, first-hand knowledge of issues that are raised by the appellant and that are reviewable by the MSPB. The deciding officials were the sole persons who could testify about the penalty determination and about the effects of the alleged ex parte communications....

Oddly, in Barber, 37 MSPR at 626, supra, the Vice Chair found that the Board's chief administrative law judge was not in error in denying depositions of the FDIC officials; the relevant information sought "could be, or had already been, obtained through a less intrusive method than oral deposition, and he ruled that the Board members could 'be deposed by written interrogatories.'" [Refer to Chapter 2, "Deposition of Agency Head."]
