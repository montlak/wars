c. Measure of Substantive Review

Harmful error aside, there is no substantive review of the legitimacy of the preappointment conditions relied on by the agency. A commonplace basis for removing an employee for preappointment reasons is falsification of an employment application, e.g., an SF-171. See Hunter v. DOJ, 73 MSPR 290, 294 (1997) (noting that if a probationer is removed for falsifying an application for employment, he is removed for a preappointment reason); James v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 124, 126 (1992) (same). Morrison v. DOJ, 34 MSPR 281, 282 (1987), informs us that:

Under 5 C.F.R. § 315.806, a probationary employee terminated for pre-employment reasons...may appeal on the ground that his termination was not effected in accordance with the procedural requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 315.805. In such an appeal, the Board's review of the action is limited to a determination of whether the agency adhered to the regulatory procedural requirements.

Under early decisions, if in a preappointment case involving falsification of an SF-171, the employee attempted to justify or mitigate his conduct, the Board determined only whether there was discrimination based on marital status or partisan politics or if there was a departure from the procedural requirements of 5 CFR 315.805 (2022) (notice, opportunity to file a written answer, bona fide consideration of the answer, and notice of the decision and appeal rights). The Board would not review the agency action on its merits. Gaxiola v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 515, 520, 6 MSPB 432 (1981), supra; Perry v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 399, 403, 6 MSPB 339 (1981) ("appellant's petition for review is denied on the grounds that he is not entitled to a review of the agency's actions on the merits"). Later cases still demonstrate no merits review and, aside from consideration of alleged discrimination based on marital status or partisan political affiliation, analyze a procedurally defective termination for preappointment reasons for harmful error, for example, LeMaster v. VA, 123 MSPR 453, 459-60 ¶¶ 13-14, 2016 MSPB 25 (2016):

This is not to say that, after becoming aware of the terms of the appellant's probation agreement, the agency could not have terminated his employment during his probationary period. The agency was nonetheless required to afford the appellant the procedural protections set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 315.805. It is undisputed that the agency did not provide the appellant with these procedural rights, which include advance notice of the termination, an opportunity to respond, and consideration of the response. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.805. Therefore, the relevant inquiry is whether the agency's failure to follow these procedures was harmful error. Hope, 108 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 7.

It is well established that harmful error cannot be presumed; an agency error is harmful only when the record shows that it was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Id., ¶ 8; 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(r). The burden is on the appellant to show that the procedural error was harmful. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(r). Because the administrative judge found that the Board lacks jurisdiction over this appeal, the record is not developed on the issue of whether the appellant met his burden of establishing that the agency would not have terminated him had it provided him with advance notice of the termination, an opportunity to respond, and consideration of his response. On remand, the administrative judge shall render a new decision after affording the parties the opportunity to present evidence and argument on this issue.

See Jones v. DOJ, 524 Fed. Appx. 660, 664 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP) (implying that the Board applied a harmful error approach to a procedural error involved in a probationer's termination for a preappointment condition).
