d. Delay Excused; Inconsequential Delay by Representative

The Board's increased flexibility toward late filings when counsel demonstrates bona fide but insufficient efforts to ensure a timely filing is exemplified by Dow v. OPM, 66 MSPR 21, 24-25 (1994):

In Anderson v. Dept. of Justice, 999 F.2d 532 (Fed. Cir. 1993), the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit concluded that "[r]elevant to th[e good cause] determination is...the length of the delay." Id. at 534. Furthermore, the court held that, "[i]n view of the minimal [one-day] delay," among other considerations, a good cause waiver of the filing deadline was warranted. Id. at 535. Similarly, in this case, we find that the minimal nature of the appellant's filing delay is a consideration in her favor.

In this connection, we note that the appellant's attorney filed an affidavit below explaining that his legal assistant erroneously calculated the filing deadline to be October 13 instead of October 12; that the petition for appeal was actually completed on October 12, leaving what was thought to be a one-day cushion; that the legal assistant was to mail the petition for appeal when she left the office on October 12; but that she called the attorney the next morning and advised him that "due to her child being ill she hadn't mailed" the petition. The attorney stated that his office then faxed a copy of the petition to the regional office and mailed it as well. We therefore conclude that these circumstances establish a reasonable excuse for the one-day delay in filing especially since, as the appellant argued below, the appeal was actually received by the Board as quickly as would have been the case if it had been mailed before the deadline. See Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578, 1583-84 (Fed. Cir. 1994). That the appellant in this case was represented, unlike the appellant in Walls, does not require a different result because the Board has previously excused the delay occasioned by a representative's mistaken filing. See Sanford v. Dept. of Defense, 61 MSPR 207, 210 (1994).

Upon the appellant's showing of good cause for the untimely filing of her appeal, waiver of the filing deadline is appropriate, "absent a showing of substantial prejudice to the agency caused by the delay in filing." 5 CFR §§ 1201.12, 1201.22(c); Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 184. Because the appellant has shown good cause for her untimely filing and because the agency did not respond to the timeliness issue and did not show substantial prejudice due to the one-day filing delay, we find that waiver of the filing deadline is appropriate. See Walls, 29 F.3d at 1583-84.

Baumunk v. DHHS, 69 MSPR 622, 625-26 (1996), excused a short delay in filing an appeal by an attorney whose client was hospitalized and unable to monitor her counsel's progress in filing the appeal:

Recently, the Federal Circuit emphasized the efficacy of the Alonzo factors in good cause determinations and criticized truncated analyses of any applicable factor, but especially the length of delay factor. Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1994). The court also noted with approval the Board's statement in Alonzo that policy considerations counsel against dismissals for untimely filing and militate for providing hearings on the merits, and that broad equitable principles of justice and good conscience should be applied in good cause determinations. Additionally, the court expressed agreement with the Board's statement in Alonzo that, if the employee gives a reasonable excuse for a filing delay, such excuse should be accepted by the Board, absent a showing of substantial prejudice to the agency caused by the delay. Walls, 29 F.3d at 1581-82 (citing Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 183-84).

The Board has consistently used the Alonzo standard to determine whether good cause exists to waive the timeliness requirement for both original actions and refiled actions. See Ramos v. Dept. of the Navy, 63 MSPR 142, 144-45 (1994), affd., 47 F.3d 1183 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); Thompson v. Dept. of the Navy, 62 MSPR 145, dismissed, 36 F.3d 1114 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Caballero v. Dept. of the Army, 59 MSPR 298, 301-02 (1993); Bianco v. OPM, 48 MSPR 291, 293 (1991). Thus, the appellant's argument that her refiled appeal should be subject to a different standard is without merit.

A number of the Alonzo factors are relevant to the circumstances of the appellant's refiling. Clearly, the appellant was notified of the time limit for filing. On the other hand, the delay was minimal, one day. Nothing contradicts the appellant's statement that, prior to refiling, her attorney and the agency representative discussed her intent to refile and agreed that, immediately after the appeal was refiled, both parties would seek an additional dismissal without prejudice. Further, counsel's neglect of drafting the refiled petition for appeal until the last minute is excusable in light of the uncontradicted evidence of his extremely busy schedule immediately prior to the refiling deadline, and the fact that he gave his attention to the appellant's case on the day that he mistakenly believed the appeal had to be refiled. See Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 184 n.1. Counsel's inattention, causing him to misread the exact day that the appeal had to be refiled was, however, negligence. As explained below, however, the appellant's illness and inability to proceed with her appeal on the refiling deadline militate against holding her responsible for this negligence of her representative.

Generally, an appellant is responsible for the errors of his chosen representative. Sofio v. Internal Revenue Service, 7 MSPB 493, 7 MSPR 667, 670 (1981). In Cox v. U.S. International Trade Commission, 4 MSPB 272, 4 MSPR 191 (1980), a case in which the appellant was represented, the Board, however, weighed the circumstances limiting the appellant's ability to communicate with her attorney around the time that her petition for appeal should have been filed. The Board found that the appellant's absence to care for her ill mother was a factor in determining that there was good cause to waive the time limit for counsel's one-day late filing.

Here, it is undisputed that, on the last day for refiling the appeal, the appellant was still recovering from brain surgery, during which a shunt was implanted to allow the drainage of an accumulation of water on her brain. This unavoidable misfortune affected the appellant's ability to proceed with her appeal. Further, her illness rendered her unable to remind her attorney of the filing deadline and to assure timely filing. Under these circumstances, we find it inappropriate to hold the appellant fully responsible for her attorney's negligence.

Weighing the Alonzo factors, the Board finds that the minimal delay, counsel's diligent attempt to refile on the appellant's behalf on the date that he believed was the last day to do so, and the appellant's inability to assist with the timely refiling of her appeal or to proceed with her appeal at the time of refiling establish good cause for the delay in refiling. Because nothing indicates that the agency was prejudiced by the brief refiling delay, the Board waives the time limit for refiling in this appeal.

Young v. Dept. of Commerce, 737 F.2d 1029 (Fed. Cir. 1984), excused appeals delayed when a union representative had trouble locating some appellants in what amounted to a class-type appeal, resulting in appeals being originally submitted without the appellants' signatures, when the union could not obtain the signatures within the time permitted by the Board. Because the agency suffered no prejudice from the delay and the appellants' explanations were reasonable, the Board abused its discretion in not accepting the appeals.
