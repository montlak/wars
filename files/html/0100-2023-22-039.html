1. Failure to Adhere to Legal Precedent

If an agency takes an action ignoring legal precedent, e.g., refusing to reemploy an employee because of absence during a period of recovery from a work-related injury, the action is clearly without merit and fees are appropriate. Emelio v. USPS, 27 MSPR 233, 236 (1985). In Jarze v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 164, 168-69, 4 MSPB 247 (1980), an employee was removed who had shown a history of mental illness but who was not referred for involuntary retirement before the removal action was taken. The Air Force acted with concern for the employee, but the agency made a significant procedural error. Fees were warranted because the agency action was "totally unfounded" and "clearly without merit." Also "clearly without merit" was an indefinite suspension effected without providing constitutional due process (prior notice and an opportunity to respond). Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 602, 605 (1992); Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 542, 546 (1991) (constructive suspension imposed without due process warranted fees under the clearly without merit standard); Wilson v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 146, 154 (1991) (adverse action taken without constitutional due process could not have been sustained on appeal and warranted fees because the action was clearly without merit); see Mitchell v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 103, 115-16 (1991) (fees granted under the "clearly without merit" standard to a nonprobationary employee who was suspended without minimal constitutional due process).

If a Board decision breaks with precedent followed by an agency, fees will not be awarded because it cannot be said the agency's position was "wholly unfounded" or "lacking a reasonable basis." Peek v. OPM, 63 MSPR 430, 434 (1994) (law enforcement retirement appeal). If the precedent relied on by appellant is being challenged by OPM through Board and court litigation, it cannot be said that the agency position was clearly without merit when that position is ultimately vindicated during judicial litigation. See Uhlig v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 660, 668 ¶ 21 (2000) (successful agency challenge to the Board rule permitting an "exculpatory no" during administrative investigations).

An error of law that the agency should have recognized permits a fee award under the "clearly without merit" standard in a proceeding brought to enforce a settlement, Stewart v. Dept. of Army, 102 MSPR 656, 661-62 ¶¶ 13-15, 2006 MSPB 223 (2006):

In determining whether an attorney fees award is in the interest of justice the Board looks to five non-exclusive categories: (1) Whether the agency engaged in a prohibited personnel practice; (2) whether the agency action was clearly without merit or wholly unfounded, or the employee is substantially innocent of the charges; (3) whether the agency initiated the action in bad faith; (4) whether the agency committed a gross procedural error that prolonged the proceeding or severely prejudiced the employee; or (5) whether the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits when it brought the proceeding. Dunn v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 F.3d 1308, 1311-12 (Fed. Cir. 1996); Allen, 2 M.S.P.R. at 434-35.

Here, the appellant argues that an attorney fees award is warranted in the interest of justice because the agency's action was clearly without merit, and the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits when it began withholding salary from him. It is within the interest of justice to award attorney fees on the basis that the agency's action was clearly without merit when an agency's action cannot be sustained on appeal as a matter of law because the legal issue raised by the agency's action has been resolved and the agency presents no reasonable basis for relitigating the legal issue. See Keely v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 760 F.2d 246, 249 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Wilson v. Department of the Navy, 51 M.S.P.R. 146, 154 (1991). The Board has long held that it is a violation of 5 U.S.C. § 7501(2) to retroactively suspend an appellant who has worked during the period of the suspension. See Schoeffler v. Department of Agriculture, 50 M.S.P.R. 143, 146 (1991); Smith v. Department of the Air Force, 14 M.S.P.R. 112, 114 (1982). Additionally, with exceptions not applicable here, 31 U.S.C. § 1342 prohibits the government from accepting voluntary services. As the Federal Circuit noted in its decision in this case, by withholding monies from the appellant to recoup the salary it paid him for work he had already performed, the agency conceded that it would be accepting voluntary services from the appellant in violation of the law. Stewart, 104 Fed. Appx. at 720.

In light of these laws, the agency's action in withholding monies from the appellant to recoup his salary for days he had worked could not be sustained. Further, the Board has long resolved the principle that it is a violation of 5 U.S.C. § 7501(2) to retroactively suspend an appellant who has worked during the period of the suspension, and the agency presents no basis for relitigating this well-settled legal principle. See Keely, 760 F.2d at 249; Schoeffler, 50 M.S.P.R. at 146; Smith, 14 M.S.P.R. at 114. Thus, the agency's action was clearly without merit. See Keely, 760 F.2d at 249; Wilson, 51 M.S.P.R. at 154. Therefore, we find that it is in the interest of justice to award the appellant attorney fees. See Allen, 2 M.S.P.R. at 434-35.

Price v. SSA, 398 F.3d 1322, 1326-27 (Fed. Cir. 2005), determined that fees are not warranted when an agency makes a material error--in Price, a suspension without a due process hearing--when there is some support for the agency's action:

If accepted, Ms. Price's argument would mean that any denial of a due process hearing would give rise to an automatic right to an attorney fee award, which would be contrary to the highly discretionary and fact-specific nature of the Board's attorney fee determinations. Even if Ms. Price is right that a due process violation necessarily results in a prohibited personnel practice, we do not agree that the Board is deprived of its discretion over attorney fees in any case in which a due process hearing is improperly denied. That is particularly true in a case such as this one in which, as Administrative Law Judge Hermele acknowledged, the question whether a pre-suspension hearing was required was "novel" and the suspension "had some merit and was founded on some reasonable legal basis." We therefore do not agree with Ms. Price that the first factor requires an award of attorney fees in this case.

Ms. Price cites Wilson v. Department of Navy, 51 M.S.P.R. 146 (1991), for the proposition that SSA's failure to provide adverse action protections mandates an award of fees. Wilson is readily distinguished from Ms. Price's case. In Wilson the Board found that an award of fees was within the interest of justice under the "clearly without merit" rubric because the agency's failure to afford the petitioner with adverse action protections was so lacking in merit that the agency's position "could not have been sustained on appeal." Id. at 154. In contrast, Administrative Law Judge Hermele found that "the improper suspension had some merit and was founded on some reasonable legal basis." Addendum Decision, slip op. at 4. SSA's legal position had at least a chance of being sustained on appeal and was not completely without merit as was the agency's position in Wilson. Substantial evidence supports Administrative Law Judge Hermele's findings on this issue given the "novel issue and [SSA's] reliance on the OPM," which advised SSA that Ms. Price was no longer considered "ready, willing and able" to work. Id.
