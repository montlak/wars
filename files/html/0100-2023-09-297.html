B. FAILURE TO PROSECUTE APPEAL

The ultimate sanction against an appellant is dismissal of the appeal, just as the extreme sanction against an agency is striking agency submissions from the record, depriving it of an opportunity to meet its burden of proof. Dismissal for failure to prosecute is authorized as a sanction by 5 CFR 1201.43(b) (2022).

Williams v. USPS, 116 MSPR 377, 380-82 ¶¶ 7-12, 2011 MSPB 53 (2011), explained that dismissal for failure to prosecute an appeal may occur when a party fails to exercise basic due diligence in complying with Board orders or a party exhibits negligence or bad faith in its efforts to comply:

The sanction of dismissal with prejudice may be imposed if a party fails to prosecute or defend an appeal. Ahlberg v. Department of Health & Human Services, 804 F.2d 1238, 1242 (Fed. Cir. 1986); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(b). Although the regulation at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(b) does not set forth guidelines for applying this sanction, the Board has held that

The imposition of such a severe sanction...must be used only when necessary to serve the ends of justice...as when a party has failed to exercise basic due diligence in complying with an order, or has exhibited negligence or bad faith in his efforts to comply.... The severe sanction of dismissal with prejudice for failure to prosecute an appeal should not be imposed when a pro se appellant has made incomplete responses to the Board's orders but has not exhibited bad faith or evidenced any intent to abandon his appeal, and appears to be confused by Board procedures.... Further, failure to obey a single order does not ordinarily justify dismissal for failure to prosecute.

Chandler v. Department of the Navy, 87 M.S.P.R. 369, ¶ 6 (2000); see Wright v. Department of the Treasury, 53 M.S.P.R. 244, 249 (1992). Sanctions should only be imposed when: (1) a party has failed to exercise basic due diligence in complying with Board orders; or (2) a party has exhibited negligence or bad faith in its efforts to comply. Chandler, 87 M.S.P.R. 369, ¶ 6; Wright, 53 M.S.P.R. at 249. Nevertheless, absent a showing of abuse of discretion, the Board will not reverse an administrative judge's determination regarding sanctions. Holland v. Department of Labor, 108 M.S.P.R. 599, ¶ 9 (2008).

We note that in recent Board cases citing Chandler, the Board has omitted the language authorizing the imposition of the sanction of dismissal for failure to exercise basic due diligence and has stated that the sanction of dismissal may be imposed only when the appellant has exhibited bad faith or evidenced any intent to abandon the appeal. See Holland, 108 M.S.P.R. 599, ¶ 9; Gordon v. Department of the Air Force, 104 M.S.P.R. 358, ¶ 4 (2006); Robinson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 94 M.S.P.R. 509, ¶ 10 (2003). The shift in language occurred because the Board tailored the standard to the particular circumstances of the cases. For example, in Gordon, the appellant's non-responsiveness arose from the Regional Office's failure to serve several orders on his representative in the manner elected, not from his bad faith or intent to abandon the appeal. See Gordon, 104 M.S.P.R. 358, ¶¶ 4-5. Consequently, the Board omitted the due diligence language and emphasized the second prong of the standard. We make clear that the Board has neither overruled, nor abandoned, the two-prong standard set forth in Chandler and Wright and reaffirm that the sanction of dismissal may be imposed when: (1) a party has failed to exercise basic due diligence in complying with Board orders; or (2) a party has exhibited negligence or bad faith in its efforts to comply.

Where an appellant's repeated failure to respond to multiple Board orders reflects a failure to exercise basic due diligence, the imposition of the sanction of dismissal for failure to prosecute has been found appropriate. See Ahlberg, 804 F.2d at 1242-45; Heckman v. Department of the Interior, 106 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶ 16 (2007); Murdock v. Government Printing Office, 38 M.S.P.R. 297 (1988). For example, in Murdock, the sanction of dismissal was appropriate because the appellant completely failed to respond to or comply with any of the Board's orders. Murdock, 38 M.S.P.R. at 298-99. We analogize the facts in this appeal to those in cases like Murdock where the appellant failed to exercise basic due diligence.

Here, the undisputed record shows that the Atlanta Regional Office sent three orders to the appellant's e-mail account prior to her filing a Termination of E-Filer Status. The appellant made no attempt to respond to or comply with any of the Board's orders, despite explicit warning that noncompliance with the show cause order may result in a dismissal for failure to prosecute. After the administrative judge issued the May 7, 2010 show cause order, the appellant merely filed a withdrawal of her e-filer status and a form designating a representative. Neither the appellant, nor her representative, filed a response to the show cause order prior to the May 13, 2010 filing deadline or requested an extension of time to file a response. The appellant's lack of effort to comply with the Board's orders is made clearer by the fact that she capably filed evidentiary submissions in response to the Board's acknowledgment order in her alleged constructive suspension appeal, which she filed while the instant appeal was pending.

On review, the appellant merely asserts that she was unable to meet any of the deadlines for responding to the orders because of her inexperience seeking representation and wishes to proceed with her case "as initially intended" now that she has representation. However, the Board has held that an appellant's difficulty in obtaining a representative does not excuse her failure to prosecute her appeal by not complying with the Board's orders. Murdock, 38 M.S.P.R. at 299. A pro se appellant may not escape the consequences of inadequate representation. Id. Even if the appellant's difficulty in obtaining a representative presented a recognizable excuse, the record does not reflect that she sought extensions of time to respond to the three Board orders or otherwise apprised the administrative judge that she was unable to proceed with her appeal until she obtained a representative.

Based on the foregoing, we find no abuse of discretion in the administrative judge's decision to impose sanctions under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(b) based upon the appellant's failure to exercise basic due diligence.

The Board does not favor the use of a dismissal for failure to prosecute, as opposed to another sanction, but appeals are dismissed for failure to prosecute, as with a mixed case appeal, Wilburn v. USPS, 43 MSPR 609, 613-15 (Spec. Pan. 1990):

Both the Merit Systems Protection Board and the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission authorize the dismissal of a matter based upon the complaining party's failure to prosecute. See 5 CFR § 1201.43(b) (MSPB regulations authorizing a presiding official to dismiss an appeal with prejudice based upon an appellant's failure to prosecute); 29 CFR § 1613.215(a)(6) (EEOC regulations authorizing the agency to cancel a complaint based upon a complainant's failure to prosecute). Like the inherent power of federal courts to order dismissal sua sponte if the grounds for dismissal exist (Link v. Wabash R. Co., 370 U.S. 626, 629-630, 82 S. Ct. 1386, 1388-89, 8 L. Ed.2d 734 (1962)), the respective regulations of the MSPB and EEOC authorize dismissal of an action for failure to prosecute without requiring a motion by the defending party. For example, the regulations governing appeals to the MSPB authorize a presiding official to "impose sanctions upon the parties as necessary to serve the ends of justice," including the authority to dismiss an appeal if the appealing party fails to prosecute. 5 CFR § 1201.43(b). The EEOC regulations governing the processing of complaints of discrimination authorize the agency head or designee to cancel a complaint where the complainant has failed to prosecute, only after the agency "has provided the complainant with a written request that includes a notice of the proposed cancellation, to provide certain information or otherwise proceed with the complaint, and the complainant has failed to satisfy the request...." 29 CFR § 1613.215(a)(6).

As the federal courts have recognized, an involuntary dismissal with prejudice for failure of prosecution is a harsh sanction and one to be ordered only in extreme circumstances. See Schmidt v. Herrmann, 614 F.2d 1221 (9th Cir. 1980); McCargo v. Hedrick, 545 F.2d 393 (4th Cir. 1976); Pacheco v. Phelps Dodge Refining Corp., 531 F.2d 709 (5th Cir. 1976). Thus, for example, prior to dismissal for failure to prosecute, a federal trial court must explore less drastic alternatives. See Hornbuckle v. Arco Oil & Gas Co., 732 F.2d 1233 (5th Cir. 1984) (requiring trial court to fully consider any sanction less severe than dismissal with prejudice for failure of prosecution, including sanctions that might be imposed against counsel); Ramsey v. Signal Delivery Service, Inc., 631 F.2d 1210 (5th Cir. 1980); Tolbert v. Leighton, 623 F.2d 585 (9th Cir. 1980). Although courts need not examine every single alternative before ordering dismissal, it must reasonably consider those alternatives that are possible and meaningful. See Anderson v. Air West, Inc., 542 F.2d 522 (9th Cir. 1976).

The MSPB, while authorizing dismissal for failure of prosecution, also adheres to the principle that this sanction should be used only in extreme circumstances, when all possible alternatives have failed. In Harrison v. FAA, 15 MSPR 400 (1983), an employee was removed from her position as air traffic controller based upon a charge of participating in an illegal strike. The employee petitioned the MSPB for review of the removal decision. An order directed her to attend a pre-hearing conference and to submit a witness list and proposed exhibits. When the appellant failed to comply, she was ordered to show cause for her failure to appear and further ordered to file the previously requested materials. The appellant again failed to comply and she was then ordered to show cause why the appeal should not be dismissed for failure to prosecute. Appellant failed to respond by the stated time and the presiding official dismissed the action with prejudice, based upon the appellant's failure to respond to and comply with the MSPB's orders. In upholding the dismissal on appeal, even though the appellant argued that her subsequent out-of-time filing of materials made the dismissal moot, the Board noted that:

[It] has upheld such a dismissal for a continued failure to respond to a presiding official's orders, especially when the failure relates to the most basic, preliminary matters of the case. [citation omitted] .

We also recognize the harsh nature of an involuntary dismissal and agree that its use should be limited to extreme circumstances only after less drastic alternatives have been considered and implemented. Mindful of these considerations, and in view of appellant's lack of representation by counsel and his apparent failure to prosecute or show any interest in his appeal, we dismiss the matter before us.

Dismissal is ordinarily not appropriate for a single miscue. The extreme sanction of dismissal of an appeal for failure to prosecute should not be imposed for a single instance of failure to comply with a Board order. Smith v. DOJ, 103 MSPR 207, 212 ¶ 12, 2006 MSPB 260 (2006). Walker v. NRC, 103 MSPR 200, 205 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 258 (2006), reprised the "single order" rule:

We further note that our regulations do not permit dismissal with prejudice as a sanction for failure to comply with a single order. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a). Dismissal may be an appropriate sanction for failure to prosecute an appeal, provided that the sanction is necessary to serve the ends of justice. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(b). However, we have specifically held that the "extreme sanction of dismissal of an appeal for failure to prosecute should not be imposed for a single instance of failure to comply with a Board order." Burnett v. Department of the Navy, 71 M.S.P.R. 34, 38 (1996). As we discern no bad faith on the part of the appellant, or any evidence that he intended to abandon his appeal, we find that the sanction of dismissal was inappropriate. See id.
