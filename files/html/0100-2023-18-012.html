3. Adverse Action

FLRA is directly precluded by 5 USC 7121(f) from reviewing arbitration awards that involve adverse actions under 5 USC 7512 or performance-based actions under 5 USC 4303. Review lies with the Federal Circuit (or MSPB, EEOC, and federal district courts if the case is also litigated on the basis of an EEO affirmative defense; or in the Federal Circuit or other circuit court if the case involved whistleblowing reprisal). As to government requests for judicial review, FLRA explained in DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI, Ray Brook and AFGE Local 3882, 22 FLRA 939, 950-51 (1986):

[S]ection 7121(f) and Section 7703 do not provide for direct agency appeal of an arbitrator's decision in such cases. Only the Director of OPM may initiate an appeal. Section 7703(b)(1) of the Statute provides:

Except as provided in paragraph (2) of this subsection, a petition to review a final decision of the Board shall be filed in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. Notwithstanding any other provision of law, any petition for review must be filed within 30 days after the date the petitioner receives notice of the final order or decision of the Board.

The 30-day period referred to in Section 7703(b)(1) has been considered by the Federal Circuit, and it has been held that Section 7703(d) first conditions OPM's appeal right upon OPM's prior intervention in the arbitration proceeding. If OPM was not made a party to the arbitration, OPM must first intervene and request reconsideration of the arbitration award. Devine v. Nutt, 718 F.2d 1048 (1983), rev'd on other grounds sub nom. Cornelius v. Nutt, 105 S. Ct. 2882 (1985); Devine v. Sutermeister, 724 F.2d 1558 (1983); Devine v. Pastore, 732 F.2d 213 (1984); Devine v. Levin, 739 F.2d 1567 (1984).

Section 7703 does not provide a specific time for OPM to intervene and request reconsideration of an arbitration award covered by Section 7121(f) and Section 7703. However, the Federal Circuit has held that the time for filing a request for reconsideration "runs from the date OPM receives notice of the decision of the MSPB or arbitrator, as the case may be." Devine v. Sutermeister, supra, at 1562, note 4. This case makes it clear that the failure of OPM to take appropriate action within 30 days of OPM's receipt of notice operates to extinguish OPM's right to request intervention and reconsideration in the arbitration proceeding and the right to appeal to the Federal Circuit as well.

Should OPM be unable or unwilling to secure Federal Circuit review of an award involving an adverse action, the award is final. If FLRA must enforce the award through ULP litigation, then the resulting ULP decision, during enforcement proceedings in court, cannot be used to revisit the merits of the arbitration award. Only a statutory bar to the award might be reviewable through the judicial enforcement route. See DHHS, SSA v. FLRA, 976 F.2d 1409, 1414-15 (D.C. Cir. 1992) (referencing AFGE v. FLRA, 850 F.2d 782, 786-87 (D.C. Cir. 1988)) [discussed in Chapter 9, "Enforcement of Arbitration Awards"].

As to "mixed cases," i.e., those cases that go to arbitration concerning actions that involve defensive allegations of discrimination in the context of personnel actions that could have been initially taken to the MSPB, at least one court required that review be first sought with the MSPB before the case is taken into district court under 5 USC 7702. See AFGE Local 2052 v. Reno, 992 F.2d 331, 333-34 (D.C. Cir. 1993). Although not involving adverse actions, the D.C. Circuit determined that for "pure" (nonmixed) EEO cases (challenges to nonselections) that go to arbitration, district court review may be obtained only after the case is taken to EEOC (either from the final award if no exceptions are filed, or from the FLRA decision affirming an award). Johnson v. Peterson, 996 F.2d 397, 399-400 (D.C. Cir. 1993). The path for judicial review of mixed cases is complex and should be researched.
