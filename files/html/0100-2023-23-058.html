5. Unfiled Settlement Agreement; Preappeal Settlement

Under Wade v. VA, 61 MSPR 580, 582-84 (1994), "a party may challenge the validity of a settlement agreement resulting in the withdrawal of an appeal, even when that agreement is not entered into the Board's record for purposes of enforcement, if the party believes that the agreement is unlawful, was involuntary, or was the result of fraud or mutual mistake":

The Board held in Schultze v. Department of the Army, 57 MSPR 250, 255 (1993), that it lacked the authority to set aside a settlement agreement that had not been incorporated into the record of a Board appeal for purposes of enforcement of the agreement. The Board further held that coercion by an agency representative or an administrative judge might nonetheless invalidate the withdrawal of an appeal that results from such an agreement. The Board noted in Schultze that the administrative judge erred by informing the appellant that she could request Board review of the initial decision if she believed that the settlement agreement was unlawful, involuntary, or the result of fraud or mutual mistake. See id. at 254 n.6.

While the Board does not have the authority to enforce a settlement agreement not entered into the record for enforcement purposes, see Diamond v. US Postal Service, 51 MSPR 448, 450 (1991), aff'd, 972 F.2d 1353 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table), that principle does not preclude us from ensuring the validity and integrity of the appellate process. The essence of a settlement agreement is that it must be a voluntary action. An agreement that is reached by virtue of fraud or mutual mistake is, by definition, involuntary. Similarly, we do not countenance an agreement that is unlawful on its face or otherwise. Thus, we find that a party may challenge the validity of a settlement agreement resulting in the withdrawal of an appeal, even when that agreement is not entered into the Board's record for purposes of enforcement, if the party believes that the agreement is unlawful, was involuntary, or was the result of fraud or mutual mistake. See King v. US Postal Service, 52 MSPR 60, 64-65 (1991). We therefore overrule the holding in Schultze to the contrary.

The appellant does not explain how conducting the settlement negotiations over the telephone caused his acceptance of the agreement to be involuntary. His allegation that the settlement agreement should be set aside because the negotiations were conducted over the telephone lacks specific support and does not show that he did not willfully and knowingly enter into the agreement. See Clark v. Department of the Treasury, 48 MSPR 330, 333 (1991).

The appellant also claims that the settlement agreement should be set aside because he had only a high school education and was unrepresented during the telephonic settlement conference, while the agency's representative during the conference was an attorney. The appellant apparently believes that he was somehow disadvantaged during the proceedings before the Board because the agency's representative was an attorney. The Board's regulations, however, allow parties wide discretion in choosing their representatives, subject only to disqualification for conflict of interest or position. See 5 CFR § 1201.31; Walton v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 48 MSPR 462, 469 (1991). The appellant appears to have elected not to have his representative present during the telephonic conference, and must now accept any consequences of that election.

Moreover, parties to a settlement agreement are presumed to have full legal capacity to contract unless they are under guardianship, an infant, mentally ill or defective, or intoxicated. See Ray v. Department of Health and Human Services, 57 MSPR 16, 20 (1993). The appellant did not assert below, and he does not now assert, that he lacked full legal capacity to enter into the settlement agreement. He has submitted no evidence showing that he was unable to understand the nature of the settlement agreement or that his decision was not an informed one. See Riley v. Department of the Army, 55 MSPR 671, 677 (1992).

The appellant also does not explain why he neglected to request the presence of his representative during the settlement conference. Even assuming that the appellant's representative had been unable to participate in the settlement conference despite such a request by the appellant, the appellant is responsible for the actions or inactions of his chosen representative and cannot avoid the consequences of his choice. See Walton, 48 MSPR at 469.

The appellant has filed a petition for review of the initial decision issued on September 14, 2010, that dismissed her individual right of action appeal as withdrawn pursuant to settlement. For the reasons discussed below, we GRANT the appellant's petition for review, VACATE the initial decision, and REMAND the appeal to the Western Regional Office for further adjudication in accordance with this Order.

A party may challenge the validity of a settlement agreement resulting in the withdrawal of an appeal, even when the agreement is not entered into the Board's record for enforcement purposes, if the party believes that the agreement is unlawful, was involuntary, or was the result of fraud or mutual mistake. Wade v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 580, 583 (1994). The appellant submits a declaration sworn under the penalty of perjury asserting among other things that the agency misled her during July 22, 2010 settlement negotiations of the agreement that was executed on August 30, 2010, by telling her that there were no positions open in the Hindi Department. The appellant's attorney also submitted a declaration sworn under the penalty of perjury supporting the appellant's claim of agency misrepresentation and stating that he would not have counseled his client to enter into the August 30, 2010 agreement if he had known the truth about the availability of a position in the Hindi Department. The agency responded by submitting an unsworn declaration generally objecting to the appellant's sworn declaration. Although the sequence of events is not entirely clear, the appellant's new evidence indicates that there was an overlap in time between the date that the parties signed the settlement agreement on August 30, 2010, and an opening in the Hindi Department that was advertised on the USAJobs website with an open application period from August 25, 2010, through January 28, 2011.

We find that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of facts, which if proven, could justify setting aside the settlement agreement. Therefore, we remand the appeal to the regional office to afford the parties the opportunity to present evidence and argument on the appellant's new claim of agency misrepresentation. See Henson v. Department of the Treasury, 86 M.S.P.R. 221, ¶ 8 (2000). With regard to the appellant's assertions that the agency failed to comply with certain terms of the settlement agreement, we note that generally allegations of noncompliance with a settlement agreement are considered, in the first instance, by the regional office that accepted the agreement into the record for enforcement. Id., ¶ 15. Here, however, the administrative judge did not enter the agreement into the record for enforcement. The Board has no authority to enforce a settlement agreement that was not entered into the record for enforcement purposes. Thus, we find no reason to remand the noncompliance issue to the administrative judge for further consideration. Wise v. Department of the Navy, 73 M.S.P.R. 95, 97 (1997).

He stated that he was being treated for insomnia, depression, difficulty in concentration, stress, diabetes, cholesterol, and back pain. He stated that Ms. Good informed him that the agency's 1993 reorganization might have been the source of his stress, and he described his work-related problems leading up to the settlement agreement.

She stated that she diagnosed him as suffering from "Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood," and that she "did not observe any significant improvement or deterioration" in his condition. Additionally, as to his argument that the settlement agreement was procured under time pressure, the appellant stated in his affidavit that, on January 31, 1994, he received a call at home from the proposing official, Nearis Harvey, ordering him to report at 10:00 A.M. the following morning to pick up the decision letter on the removal proposal even though he had been granted an indefinite extension of time to find another position within the agency and was told that the agency would notify him when a written response to the removal proposal notice was required. We find that the facts alleged by the appellant, if true, would establish that the settlement agreement was invalid and that his resignation procured through that settlement agreement was involuntary. See Riley v. Department of the Army, 55 MSPR 671, 677 (1992). Therefore, we find that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal that entitles him to a hearing on the issue of jurisdiction. See Lewis v. United States Postal Service, 39 MSPR 236, 238 (1988).

We note that the agency has disputed the appellant's claim of involuntariness. Ferdon v. US Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994).

There is a limitation on the Board's authority under Wade to consider the validity of an unfiled settlement. The Board must have jurisdiction over the appeal to consider the challenge to the settlement, meaning that an AJ may have to conduct a jurisdictional hearing to address the settlement, Evans v. Selective Service System, 91 MSPR 376, 380 n.3 (2002):

A party may challenge the validity of a settlement agreement resulting in the withdrawal of an appeal, even when that agreement is not entered into the Board's record for enforcement, if the party believes that the agreement is unlawful, was involuntary, or was the result of fraud or mutual mistake. Wade v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 580, 582-83 (1994). The Wade principle applies if the issue of Board jurisdiction is unresolved or the Board has jurisdiction over the underlying appeal. See Roberts v. Department of the Navy, 81 M.S.P.R. 519 (1999); Boucher v. Department of the Treasury, 68 M.S.P.R. 40 (1995); Wade, 61 M.S.P.R. at 582-83. However, if, after conducting a jurisdictional hearing on remand, the administrative judge dismisses the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, the Board will lack authority to further address the validity of the settlement agreement. The Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). "Once the Board has determined that there is no agency action over which it can exercise jurisdiction, it has no authority to make any other determinations or grant any relief." Moscato v. Department of Education, 75 M.S.P.R. 261, 263 (1997).

To challenge dismissal of an appeal based on an unfiled settlement, the appellant must file a PFR within the time allowed by Board regulations, or the appellant must explain to the Board's satisfaction why that time limit should be waived, Chapman v. TVA, 67 MSPR 246, 249-50 (1995):

The Board lacks authority to enforce a settlement agreement not entered into the record for enforcement purposes. A party nonetheless may file a petition for review challenging the validity of such an agreement where it results in the withdrawal of an appeal and if the party believes that the agreement is unlawful, was involuntary, or was the result of fraud or mutual mistake. See Wade v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 MSPR 580, 582-83 (1994). A petition for review, however, must be filed within 35 days after the issuance of the initial decision. See 5 CFR § 1201.114(d). The Board will waive this time limit only upon a showing of good cause for the delay in filing. See 5 CFR §§ 1201.12, 1201.114(f). To establish good cause for an untimely filing, a party must show that he exercised diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. See Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980).

The initial decision dismissing the appeals as withdrawn based on the settlement agreement informed the appellant that he could file a petition for review of the initial decision no later than June 24, 1993. His challenge to the validity of that agreement, submitted to the regional office on June 11, 1994, is considered a petition for review filed with the Clerk of the Board on that date.... The petition for review therefore was filed almost one year late.

As a basis for waiving this untimeliness, the appellant's argument that he was unaware of the consequences of not entering the agreement into the record lacks merit. After the administrative judge advised him that the agreement would not be made part of the record for Board enforcement purposes, the appellant acknowledged those consequences by expressing his intent to pursue enforcement issues through the offices of elected officials. In any event, an appellant's claim that he was unaware that the settlement agreement did not serve his best interests does not show good cause for a delay in filing. See Malfitano v. Department of the Navy, 63 MSPR 260, 262 (1994), aff'd, No. 94-3488, 1995 WL 7447 (Fed. Cir. Jan. 9, 1995) (Table). A claim of financial difficulties and lack of representation similarly does not excuse such a delay. See Georgeoplous v. US Postal Service, 61 MSPR 411, 413 (1994). Further, the Board has held that an appellant's unfamiliarity with Board procedures does not show good cause for untimeliness. See Miller v. US Postal Service, 63 MSPR 646, 648 (1994); see also Simon v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 65 MSPR 176, 181 (1994) (an appellant's failure to heed notice of the actual deadline date for filing does not excuse his untimeliness). Finally, the appellant's discussion of the validity of the settlement agreement and related compliance issues does not pertain to the timeliness issue and does not establish good cause for the delay in filing. See Fritz v. Department of the Air Force, 59 MSPR 87, 90 (1993).
