2. Service of Pleadings

Once an appeal is filed with the Board, the Board's regulations require that the submissions from the parties that follow, generally referred to as pleadings, be filed with the Board and served on the other party to the appeal. It may be that an ex parte submission is filed with the Board with advance permission of the AJ, e.g, a settlement offer that is only directed to the AJ (for later discussion with the other party), but that type of submission is exceptional. There is a special rule concerning discovery requests and responses. They are exchanged between the parties. Discovery materials are not filed with the Board unless they are required in the course of a motion to compel discovery, later discussed, or when the fruits of discovery are used as evidence in the appeal, and then the parties ordinarily attach those materials as exhibits with their prehearing submissions, subsequently discussed.

The appeal itself is filed with the Board, and the Board then serves the other party to the case. The regulation calls for submission to the Board of the original of the appeal and a copy. The copy is used by the Board to serve the other party, generally known as the respondent, to the appeal. When the Board receives an appeal by fax or e-filing, it determines how to go about duplicating the appeal as part of the process of serving the respondent. E-filing eliminates the need for multiple copies of an appeal.

Pleadings are written submissions (other than the appeal itself) setting out claims, allegations, arguments, or evidence (e.g., briefs, petitions, attachments, and responses) 5 CFR 1201.4(b) (2022), and motions, defined as a request submitted to AJ asking that official to take an action, 5 CFR 1201.4(c) (2022). Paper size for submissions is 8 1/2 by 11 inches. 5 CFR 1201.26(c) (2022). Rules governing electronic submissions obviate the need for most paper filings--for those who decide to become e-filers, assuming that the other party joins in the e-filing process (if not, the other party must be served with paper copies of pleadings). Some submissions are not permitted through the e-filing system. The rules for e-filing, below, should be referenced.

Service on an opposing party or upon an intervenor must be distinguished from filing a document with the Board. Filing a document with the Board is the act of delivering a document to the Board. With the exception of an appeal, the parties have the responsibility for serving on opposing parties the pleadings that they file with the Board. An exception to the usual service requirement is the appeal itself. Appeals are filed with the Board but then served by the Board on the respondent. 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(1, 2) (2022). Some agencies issue decision letters calling for the appellant to serve a copy of the filed appeal on an agency representative. Service of the appeal on an agency representative is a convenience for the agency; the Board provides official service on the agency. The agency's request for service of the appeal on its representative is not binding; the failure to do so is not error. Board regulations govern filing and service of the appeal.

Service is defined as "[t]he process of furnishing a copy of any pleading to Board officials, other parties, or both, by mail, by facsimile, by commercial or personal delivery, or by e-filing in accordance with § 1201.14." 5 CFR 1201.4(i) (2022). Service also encompasses documents filed with pleadings. 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2) (2022). Appellants serve documents on agency representatives. The agency should serve documents and pleadings on both the appellant and his or her representative. 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2) (2022). Board regulations allow the parties, by their mutual consent to exchange discovery material by email. 5 CFR 1201.73(b) (2022). Some agencies still use mail (or even more expensive certified mail or a commercial service) to duplicate service of discovery materials by email.

The date of service is, under 5 CFR 1201.4 (2022):

(j) Date of service. "Date of service" has the same meaning as "date of filing" under paragraph (l) of this section.

The date of filing is defined in the section that follows.

When a document is served in paper form, it is to be accompanied by a certificate of service, defined as "[a] document certifying that a party has served copies of pleadings on the other parties or, in the case of paper documents associated with electronic filings under paragraph (h) of § 1201.14, on the MSPB." 5 CFR 1201.4(k) (2022). Certificates of service will be interpreted reasonably, without undue emphasis upon technicalities. See Blue v. USPS, 65 MSPR 370, 374 (1994) (applying presumption of proper mailing despite lack of statement in certificate of service regarding use of prepaid postage). It there are multiple parties to an appeal, service is on each party or representative of each party or parties. An intervenor is treated as a party and is entitled to service of submissions to the Board. In turn the intervenor must follow the rules of service as to the parties to the appeal. E-filing accomplishes electronic service of a pleading and associated e-filing attachments on everyone who is an e-filer. Paper copies must be served through another means of delivery on parties or counsel who are not e-filers.

The Board discourages the use of sanctions, including dismissal of an appeal, for the failure of a party properly to serve a document. Particularly as to pro se appellants who may have difficulty following MSPB rules, the better course may be for the AJ to serve the document rather than for the AJ to sanction the appellant for failing to serve the document. See Hamner v. DHUD, 93 MSPR 84, 89 ¶¶ 13-14 (2002) (noting the AJ's failure to inform the appellant that his appeal could be dismissed for failing to serve a submission on the other party; citing Herring v. VA, 72 MSPR 96, 99 (1996)); Fickie v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 525, 535-36 ¶¶ 24-25 (2000) (AJ should have informed the appellant of the problem concerning service of documents and explained to the appellant how the problem could be remedied: "nothing in the record indicates that the administrative judge previously expressed his disapproval of the service practices mentioned above, that he instructed the appellant or his representative to alter those practices, or that he instructed the appellant or his representative to cure the deficiencies noted above.").
