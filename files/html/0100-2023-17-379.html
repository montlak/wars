3. Uncertain, Ambiguous or Contradictory Award; Implementation Impossible; Involvement of Third Parties

An award is deficient when it is incomplete, ambiguous, or contradictory, making implementation of the award impossible. Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters Local 13 and Panama Canal Comm'n, Gen. Servs. Bureau, Balboa, 43 FLRA 1012, 1023 (1992). The principle was explained in AFGE Local 1843 and VAMC Northport, 51 FLRA 444, 448 (1995):

We construe the Agency's argument that the order is logically inconsistent as an assertion that the award is incomplete, ambiguous, or contradictory so as to make implementation impossible. In order for an award to be found deficient on this ground, the appealing party must show that implementation of the award is impossible because the meaning and effect of the award is too unclear or uncertain. E.g., Veterans Administration Hospital, Newington, Connecticut and National Association of Government Employees, Local R1-109, 5 FLRA 64, 66 (1981). The Agency has failed to show that implementation of the award is impossible. Moreover, the Arbitrator's order that the grievant be selected is clear and unambiguous. Accordingly, we deny this exception.

Of the impossible-to-implement exception, Dept. of Air Force, Grissom AFB and AFGE Local 3254, 67 FLRA 302, 304 (2014) (Member Pizzella concurring), declared:

The Authority will set aside an award that is "incomplete, ambiguous, or contradictory as to make implementation of the award impossible."[27] In order to prevail on this ground, "the appealing party must demonstrate that the award is impossible to implement because the meaning and effect of the award are too unclear or uncertain."[28]

[27] 5 C.F.R. § 2425.6(b)(2)(ii) (emphasis added).

[28] U.S. DOD, Def. Logistics Agency, 66 FLRA 49, 51 (2011) (citing NATCA, 55 FLRA 1025, 1027 (1999)).

Here, the Agency does not explain how the award is contradictory on its face. Instead, it simply argues that the award is "completely contradictory" and imposes "an utterly impossible requirement" because it "requires the Agency to 'afford' the Union an opportunity 'to attend' future training events" and "arguably creates an affirmative requirement or obligation of the Agency to 'approve' future requests notwithstanding the Agency's right to disapprove all such training requests after an evaluation and application of the factors agreed to by the parties under the provisions of [Article 19]." Thus, the Agency has not established that that the award itself is so contradictory as to make its implementation impossible.

Of the litigation over the underlying training dispute in Grissom AFB, Member Pizzella stated, concurring with the outcome, id. at 305:

I do not believe that any part of this case contributes to the "effective conduct of public business" or fosters "work practices [that] facilitate and improve...the efficient accomplishment of the operations of the Government." All the parties involved in this case--the Agency, the Union, and the Arbitrator--contribute to a commonly held belief that, the federal government is unable to effectively manage its multitude of agencies, bureaus, and over two million employees. This belief is shared by many taxpayers, including well-known, former government executives. Recently, Fareed Zakaria, Time Magazine Editor-at-Large, reminded us that former Federal Reserve Chairman, Paul Volcker, has warned repeatedly that it is a "profound problem" that most Americans believe that the "government can no longer act effectively [and competently]." Similar concerns have been echoed by Harvard University Kennedy School of Government professor, Linda Bilmes, and former White House Chief of Staff and Commerce Secretary, William Daley, who have warned that "the absence of good management...is undermining public trust in our government."

Under these circumstances, I am reluctant to lend my imprimatur to a decision that expends valuable Authority resources to resolve a claim that is clearly frivolous and that impedes the Authority's ability to address in timely fashion those good-faith disputes that will actually promote more effective labor-management relations when they are adjudicated. But the parties here have pressed this vacuous dispute to the Authority for resolution. I, therefore, join with my colleagues to deny the Agency's exceptions.

See NLRB Region 9 and NLRBU, 66 FLRA 456, 460 (2012) ("The Agency does not explain why it is impossible to implement the award's requirement that the Director personally interview affected employees to assess their individual circumstances and determine whether they should be charged with annual leave. As the Agency does not provide a basis for finding that the award is impossible to implement, we deny this exception."); Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs and UPTO, 63 FLRA 161, 163 (2009) (reciting rule); AFGE Local 3342 and SSA, 58 FLRA 448, 449 (2003) (reciting rule); USIA Voice of Am. and AFSCME Local 1418, 55 FLRA 197, 200 (1999) (reciting rule); EEOC, Baltimore Field Office and AFGE Nat'l Council of EEOC Locals, 59 FLRA 688, 692 (2004) (award was incomplete, and required clarification, that determined that five grievants involved in group grievance were entitled to overtime but it was unclear who the grievants were); cf. DHS, C&BP and AFGE Local 1929, 72 FLRA 253, 257 (2021) (Member Abbott dissenting in part) ("Because the relevant payroll policy is clear from the award as a whole, we find that this part of the awarded remedy is not ambiguous or contradictory."); DHS, C&BP and NTEU, 68 FLRA 157, 165 (2015) (Member Pizzella dissenting) ("Even if the Agency were correct in its contention that the Arbitrator completely incorporated another arbitration award into the award here, the Agency fails to explain how that incorporation renders the Arbitrator's award impossible to implement.") (recon. den., DHS, C&BP and NTEU, 68 FLRA 722, 724 (2015) (Member Pizzella dissenting)).

A mistaken reference in an award to a termination rather than suspension of the grievant did not establish a "nonfact" exception when the award itself correctly described the disciplinary suspension. SSA Huntington Park and AFGE Local 2452, 63 FLRA 391, 393 (2009). An award is not so uncertain as to preclude compliance that directs an agency to correct unsafe and unhealthy conditions reported by a safety and health committee. The award properly directed compliance with the committee report to the extent that the action directed was within the authority of the agency and would not violate any law, including management's reserved rights under § 7106(a). The arbitrator may not, however, direct the agency to take actions within the purview of other organizations, e.g., GSA as building manager, although the agency may be directed to request that the action be taken. An award may not require preliminary actions, e.g., inspections, that must be undertaken by other agencies. INS and AFGE Local 1917, 20 FLRA 391, 397 (1985); cf. Dept. of Army, Aviation Sys. Command, St. Louis and NFFE Local 405, 36 FLRA 418, 425 (1990) ("Although the Agency claims that the Arbitrator lacked authority to direct the Agency 'to stop payment on funds that have already been committed to another [F]ederal agency[,]' the Agency cites no authority for its claims and none is apparent to us.").

The arbitrator's award is not incomplete, ambiguous, or contradictory because an arbitrator retains jurisdiction to address questions or problems concerning payment of environmental differential pay ordered by the award. VAMC, Huntington and AFGE Local 2344, 46 FLRA 1160, 1167 (1993); see VA Puget Sound Health Care System and AFGE Local 3197, 72 FLRA 441, 445–46 (2021) (Chairman DuBester concurring) (EDP: "The Authority has held that such ambiguities are for clarification by the arbitrator and provide no basis for finding the award deficient. Here, the Arbitrator retained jurisdiction 'in the event the parties need [the] Arbitrator's assistance to implement th[e] decision and award.' Accordingly, the alleged ambiguities can be clarified by the Arbitrator and provide no basis for finding the award deficient."); VA Peters VAMC and AFGE Local 1168, 71 FLRA 1003, 1004 (2020) (Member Abbott dissenting) ("While the Agency claims that the award is ambiguous because the Arbitrator did not expressly state that former employees should receive environmental differential pay, the award clearly delineates the backpay period and specifies that any grievant who was in pay status during the relevant period is entitled to the remedy. Further, the Authority has specifically rejected alleged ambiguities as a basis for finding an award deficient on this ground when--as here--the arbitrator has retained jurisdiction to clarify the award"; "an award is not impossible to implement merely because its implementation may depend on bargaining between the parties"); VA Health Care Sys., Temple TX and AFGE Local 2109, 66 FLRA 71, 73 (2011) ("the Authority has specifically rejected alleged ambiguities as a basis for finding an award deficient on this ground when the arbitrator has retained jurisdiction to clarify the award. The Authority advised that such ambiguities are for clarification by the arbitrator and provide no basis for finding the award deficient. U.S. Dep't of Veterans Affairs Med. Ctr., Huntington, W. Va., 46 FLRA 1160, 1167 (1993)."); NATCA and Dept. of Transp., FAA, 55 FLRA 1025, 1027 (1999) (for an award to be set aside as contradictory, appealing party must show implementation of award is impossible because meaning and effect of award is too unclear or uncertain).

When an award is arguably unclear, e.g., on the issue of whether an arbitrator ordered a temporary or permanent promotion, FLRA may attempt to read between the lines, and FLRA may look outside the award--as when it examined how the same arbitrator approached a promotion issue in another award that was not pending before FLRA. AFGE Local 916 and Dept. of Air Force, Oklahoma City Air Logistics Ctr., Tinker AFB, 43 FLRA 1070, 1072 (1992); cf. DHS, C&BP and NTEU Chap. 149, 73 FLRA 293 (2022) (Member Kiko dissenting) (FLRA reconciles reasoning of arbitrator and result in a disciplinary case; award was not unclear, contradictory, or impossible to implement). Alternatively, if the award is unclear, FLRA may remand the case to the parties, who are then encouraged to resolve the dispute or, lacking resolution, resubmit the award to the arbitrator for clarification. Dept. of Navy, Navy Inventory Control Point and AFGE Local 1156, 59 FLRA 698, 699 (2004) (remanding award because it conditioned expungement of suspension on conditions that were unclear). Or, if the exceptions claim that the award was missing some significant language, FLRA may decline to grant the exception if the party failed to request a corrected award. See AFGE Local 953 and Dept. of Army, Pine Bluff Arsenal, 68 FLRA 644, 646 (2015); cf. Dept. of Air Force and AFGE Local 3854, 71 FLRA 906, 908 (2020) (Member DuBester concurring) ("Although the Agency may have liked a different award, merely arguing that the Arbitrator did not provide enough detail in reaching her conclusion that the Agency violated the CBA does not demonstrate how that conclusion is implausible or unconnected to or in manifest disregard of the parties' agreement.").
