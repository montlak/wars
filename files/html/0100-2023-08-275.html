D. EXCLUSIVITY OF VEOA OR USERRA APPEAL; JOINDER OF CLAIMS

A direct USERRA appeal is limited to a USERRA violation. See Lee v. DOD, DC-3443-10-0666-I-1 (NP 2/11/2011) ("Further, the administrative judge correctly found that the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appellant's race, age and national origin discrimination claims, as well as his sexual harassment claim. The Board has held that USERRA does not allow for a decision on the merits of the underlying matter except to the extent necessary to address the appellant's military or veterans' status claims, and thus does not include a review of other claims of prohibited discrimination. Metzenbaum v. Department of Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 285, ¶ 15 (2001)."). However, under Sears v. Department of the Navy, 88 MSPR 31, 34 ¶¶ 6-7 (2001), USERRA and VEOA claims may be pursued together, if need be by joinder, assuming jurisdictional requirements are met:

In the instant appeal, the appellant is only alleging a violation of a veterans' preference right in his VEOA appeal. His USERRA appeal does not involve any veterans' preference rights. It involves a claim of discrimination based on the appellant's veteran status. Thus, 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(e)(2) does not prevent the appellant, here, from pursuing both his VEOA and USERRA claims. To the extent the initial decision appears to hold that it does, we now correct it.

Because the outcome of the appellant's USERRA appeal could directly impact this VEOA claim, we believe that joinder of the two appeals is warranted. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(f); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.36(a)(2). Joining these two appeals would not adversely affect the interests of the parties, and, given the interdependence of these two appeals, it would expedite the processing of them both to have them decided simultaneously, instead of waiting for a final decision in the appellant's USERRA appeal before adjudicating the appellant's VEOA appeal.

The VEOA appeal excludes other remedies based on the same claim, although it does not bar joinder of a USERRA claim, Sears v. Dept. of Navy, 88 MSPR 31, 33-34 ¶¶ 5-7 (2001):

The administrative judge cited to 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(e)(2) as support for his decision to dismiss the appellant's appeal without prejudice. Section 3330a(e)(2) states, "A preference eligible may not pursue redress for an alleged violation described in subsection (a) under this section at the same time the preference eligible pursues redress for such violation under any other law, rule, or regulation." Subsection (a) describes violations of veterans' preference statutes or regulations. 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a). It is clear from reading these two subsections together that a preference eligible may not pursue a claim of a violation of a veterans' preference statute or regulation through the administrative process described in 5 U.S.C. § 3330a while also pursuing a claim for this same violation under any other law, rule, or regulation. For example, a preference eligible who is removed from federal service through a reduction in force may pursue a claim of a violation of his veterans' preference rights through the 5 U.S.C. § 3330a administrative process, but he can not also pursue this claim of a violation of his veterans' preference rights through a reduction-in-force appeal to the Board.

In the instant appeal, the appellant is only alleging a violation of a veterans' preference right in his VEOA appeal. His USERRA appeal does not involve any veterans' preference rights. It involves a claim of discrimination based on the appellant's veteran status. Thus, 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(e)(2) does not prevent the appellant, here, from pursuing both his VEOA and USERRA claims. To the extent the initial decision appears to hold that it does, we now correct it.

Because the outcome of the appellant's USERRA appeal could directly impact this VEOA claim, we believe that joinder of the two appeals is warranted. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(f); 5 CFR § 1201.36(a)(2). Joining these two appeals would not adversely affect the interests of the parties, and, given the interdependence of these two appeals, it would expedite the processing of them both to have them decided simultaneously, instead of waiting for a final decision in the appellant's USERRA appeal before adjudicating the appellant's VEOA appeal.

Leno v. VA, 90 MSPR 614, 617 ¶ 5 (2002), restated the exclusivity rule for VEOA appeals:

...The VEOA provides that an individual may not seek redress for a violation of veterans' preference rights under the VEOA and some other law simultaneously. 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(e)(2). However, a USERRA claim seeks redress for alleged discrimination on account of military service and not for violation of veterans' preference rules. 38 U.S.C. §§ 4311, 4324. Accordingly, the VEOA's prohibition against dual proceedings challenging the same alleged violation of veterans' preference rules does not apply. See Sears v. Department of the Navy, 86 M.S.P.R. 76, 11 2-7, 12 (2000).

Bennington v. USPS, CH-3330-10-0938-I-1 (NP 6/30/2011), noted as to a RIF appeal that the remedial exclusivity operations in reverse, so that an RIF appeal raising a VEOA claim bars a related VEOA appeal:

Finally, the agency claims that the appellants' RIF and adverse action appeals are barred by 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(e)(2). Under 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(e)(2), a preference eligible "may not pursue redress for an alleged violation described in subsection (a) under this section at the same time the preference eligible pursues redress for such violation under any other law, rule, or regulation." Thus, even assuming arguendo that the appellants' RIF and adverse action appeals overlap with their VEOA appeals, we find that the VEOA appeal would be barred, not the RIF and adverse action appeals.

As to USERRA, an assertion of discrimination based on military service may be raised as an affirmative defense in an adverse action appeal. See Holmes v. DOJ, 92 MSPR 377, 380 ¶ 8 (2002).

USERRA establishes primacy in the MSPB to adjudicate disputes involving federal employees or applicants for federal employment.
