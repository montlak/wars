b. Motivation of Whistleblower

The 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act amended 5 USC 2302 to make clear that the motives of whistleblowers need not be pure to secure protection for covered disclosures:

(f)(1) A disclosure shall not be excluded from subsection (b)(8) because--

...

(C)  of the employee's or applicant's motive for making the disclosure[.]

As to this portion of the WPEA, Day v. DHS, 119 MSPR 589, 2013 MSPB 49 (2013) (Member Robbins concurring in part and dissenting in part), determined that 5 USC 2302(f)(1) could be applied to cases pending on the effective date of WPEA. [Refer to Chapter 17, "Retroactivity," for Day.]

Most cases predating the WPEA did not strip a disclosure of protection because of the motivation of the person making the disclosure. With all the discussion of "reasonable" and "genuine" beliefs concerning allegations, a whistleblower remained a whistleblower even if his intent is to evade disciplinary action, Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 397, 402-03 (1994), aff'd, Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 66 F.3d 279 (Fed. Cir. 1995)) :

In finding that the appellant had failed to establish the reasonableness of his belief, the administrative judge relied upon Fiorillo v. Department of Justice, 795 F.2d 1544, 1550 (Fed. Cir. 1986), for the proposition that a disclosure is not protected where the employee's primary motivation is to protect himself from possible adverse action.

Finding that "given the motivation, timing and baseless substance of appellant's disclosures, they, as a whole, represent[ed] nothing more than thinly veiled attempts by the appellant to shift blame, create discord, and evade imminent disciplinary action," the administrative judge concluded that they were not protected under the act.

It should be noted that, in enacting the WPA, Congress expressly rejected the Fiorillo decision to the extent that it holds that an employee's disclosures are not protected where his "primary motivation" was not for the public good, but rather for his personal motives. See S. Rep. No. 413, 100th Cong., 2d Sess. 12-13 (1988). Accordingly, we find that the administrative judge erred to the extent that he relied upon the appellant's motivation as a primary factor in determining the reasonableness of his belief....

Horton echoed the sentiments in In re Frazier, 1 MSPR 163, 187, 1 MSPB 159 (1979), that the law permits an element of self-interest in whistleblowing activities protected by § 2302(b)(8). See Coons v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 631, 639 ¶ 14 (2000) ("The fact that the appellant may have disclosed information...to deflect blame from himself during the agency investigation did not render his disclosure unprotected"; "[A]n employee's motives should not be considered in determining whether a disclosure is protected."). Kinan v. DOD, 87 MSPR 561, 566 ¶ 13 (2001) (overruled on other grounds, Edwards v. Dept. of Labor, 2022 MSPB 9 (2022)), added as to a whistleblower's self-interest:

As the agency points out by its citation to Lachance v. White, 174 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 1999), the Board is expected to consider the appellant's self-interest and potential bias in making its determination on whether an appellant had a reasonable belief in the truth of his disclosures, and that others may have shared his belief is not sufficient in and of itself to find that the appellant possessed a reasonable belief.

See Delgado v. DOJ, 966 F.3d 556, 562 (7th Cir. 2020) (amended, without change to quoted portion, on petition for rehearing, Delgado v. DOJ, 977 F.3d 1295 (7th Cir. 2020)) ("In finding that Delgado's disclosure was not protected, the Administrative Judge also relied in part on the fact that there had been a history of friction between Delgado and Labno, and she wrote that the Whistleblower Protection Act was not intended as a vehicle for resolving such conflicts. That portion of the AJ's decision reflected another legal error. The Act provides specifically: 'A disclosure shall not be excluded from subsection (b)(8) because of the employee's or applicant's motive for making the disclosure.' 5 U.S.C. § 2302(f)(1)(C)."); Ayers v. Dept. of Army, 123 MSPR 11, 20 ¶ 20, 2015 MSPB 58 (2015) ("Further, while motive may be relevant to the determination of a reasonable belief, a disclosure is not excluded from protection based on an appellant's motive in making it. Carter v. Department of the Army, 62 M.S.P.R. 393, 402 (1994), aff'd, 45 F.3d 444 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); see WPEA, Pub. L. No. 112-199, § 101(b)(2)(C), 126 Stat. 1465, 1466 (codified at 5 U.S.C. § 2302(f)(1)) (codifying this rule in the WPEA)."); Swinford v. Dept. of Transp., 107 MSPR 433, 436 ¶ 5, 2007 MSPB 296 (2007) ("If the reasonable belief test has been met, the personal motives of the whistleblower are irrelevant."); cf. English v. SBA, DE-1221-15-0090-W-1 (NP 4/8/2015) ("Also, the Board's case law is clear that time and attendance abuse is a violation of law, rule, or regulation. DiGiorgio v. Department of the Navy, 84 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 14 (1999); Frederick v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 63 M.S.P.R. 563, 570 (1994). Further, the appellant's disclosure of a violation of law is protected even if he was attempting to impose his personal views of right and wrong on the workplace. Berkley v. Department of the Army, 71 M.S.P.R. 341, 352 (1996).").
