a. Need to Prepare

Personal inconvenience or the desire to further research Board law will not justify a continuance. Hersman v. Nat'l Science Found., 1 MSPR 724, 727-28, 2 MSPB 132 (1980) (appellant requested a continuance in the processing of the case so he could seek counsel in person rather than by telephone). If a continuance is requested due to lack of access to documents, the continuance is properly denied if the appellant failed to obtain those documents from alternate sources. Aguilar v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 263, 264 (1984); cf. Preny v. IRS, 24 MSPR 669, 672 (1984) (no error in the denial of a continuance because of the appellant's need to secure additional information; the appellant failed to use discovery procedures and received a hearing notice late due to failure to notify the Board properly of a new address). A continuance requested on the day of the hearing to obtain testimony by a psychologist was denied. No reversible adjudicatory error occurred when the appellant was given the opportunity but failed to obtain the testimony of the psychologist by deposition and submit it after the close of the hearing. Hinchman v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 770, 771-72 (1983). No reversible adjudicatory error occurred when an AJ denied a continuance necessary to obtain hearing testimony from an absent witness, but allowed the parties to depose the witness upon his return and place the transcript into the record, yet the appellant refused to participate in the remainder of the hearing or the deposition of the absent witness. Risher v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 430, 432-33 (1982); see Buniff v. USDA, 79 MSPR 118, 122 n.2 (1998) (noting the admission of a deposition in lieu of testimony; the appellant was notified of the time and date of the deposition but failed to appear to cross-examine the witness); cf. West v. DHUD, 20 MSPR 194, 195 (1984) (no error when the AJ failed to postpone the hearing after the appellant failed to follow proper procedures to take the depositions of witnesses); Varrone v. VA, 14 MSPR 246, 247-48 (1982) (no prejudicial error by the AJ in denying a continuance when the appellant, two days prior to the hearing, informed the AJ that he wanted to take depositions; the deponents were appearing as hearing witnesses). An appellant who complained that he had inadequate time to prepare for a Board hearing gained little sympathy on review when he failed to ask for a continuance below. See Tilley v. Frank, 728 F. Supp. 1293, 1296 (M.D. La. 1990).

Although it may be proper to stay the hearing pending the outcome of discovery or jurisdictional disputes, there is no good cause for delay of the hearing if jurisdiction is tied to the merits of the case and the requested discovery is not relevant. Brace v. DHUD, 13 MSPR 187, 191, 11 MSPB 451 (1982). The corollary to this, although not yet found in a Board opinion, is that if discovery is delayed through no fault of the requestor, the hearing should be continued, on motion, to avoid foreclosing that party from introducing evidence at the hearing. Board law does make it clear, however, that if a continuance is sought to permit discovery, there should be evidence presented of what discovery was in progress, the record should show pending motions to compel discovery, or there should at least be a showing of the evidence the appellant sought to obtain through discovery. Shamblen v. USPS, 45 MSPR 620, 625 (1990). Notwithstanding the decisions involving continuances, 5 CFR 1201.28(a)-(b) (2022), case suspension and DWOP procedures, earlier discussed, allow for a joint or unilateral request for additional time to pursue discovery.
