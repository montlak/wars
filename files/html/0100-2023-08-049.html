A. NATURE OF CIVIL SERVICE APPOINTMENTS

Under 5 USC 7701(a), an "employee" (or "applicant for employment") appeals to the Board from any action appealable to the Board under a law, rule, or regulation. That statute also establishes basic procedures for actions appealable to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation: the right to a hearing, a transcript, the right of representation. See Garza v. OPM, 83 MSPR 336, 343 ¶ 16 (1999). Because only statutorily defined employees (or certain classes of applicants) may bring cases to the Board under 5 USC 7701, it is an appellate jurisdictional prerequisite that the appellant meet the statutory definition of employee. See Powell v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 540, 541, 5 MSPB 66 (1981).

For discussion of federal appointment authorities, especially SES and excepted service, refer to the OPM website publication, "Primary Appointing Authorities for Career and Career-Conditional Appointments," at https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/hiring-information/competitive-hiring/#url=Types-of-Appointments. Refer as well to the OPM Presidential Transition Guide to Federal Human Resources Management (June 2020), https://presidentialtransition.org/publications/2020-presidential-transition-guide-to-federal-human-resources-management-matters/. An MSPB study, Federal Appointment Authorities: Cutting through the Confusion (June 2008), https://www.mspb.gov/studies/studies/Federal_Appointment_Authorities_Cutting_through_the_Confusion_350930.pdf, describes some principal appointment authorities: competitive examining (declining in use in recent years), the Veterans Employment Opportunity Act, the Veterans Reemployment Act, and the Federal Career Intern Program. These authorities account, according to the study, for about two-thirds of new hires. Interestingly, for FY 2005, competitive examining was used for only 28% of new hires. The 2008 report noted that "Confusion about the different authorities caused misunderstandings regarding the length of an individual hire's probationary or trial period, potentially hampering the ability of the supervisors to use that period to effectively evaluate that person," and as will be seen in our later discussion, supervisors are not the only ones who are confused. The use of various appointment authorities and the associated probationary and trial period requirements, and exceptions to those requirements, have confounded more than a few litigants, litigators, Board AJs and members. By definition, Federal Circuit judges are never confused by those appointment authorities.

Distinctions exist among the statutory appeal rights to the MSPB of preference eligibles in the excepted service, competitive service employees, and excepted service employees covered by the 1990 Due Process Amendments. The distribution of appeal rights based upon these statutory definitions seems arbitrary at times, but the benefits allowed civil servants are those provided by statute or regulation, not by fairness and equity. That some employees are in the excepted service with no appeal rights, while others are in the competitive service with appeal rights, does not create jurisdiction in the Board through an equal protection argument. Dunigan v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-631). Veterans preference benefits that confer appeal rights are constitutional; they do not improperly deny equal protection to those who do not qualify for those rights through military service. See, e.g., Frederick v. United States, 507 F.2d 1264 (Ct. Cl. 1974). An equitable claim to competitive service status does not confer Board jurisdiction. The Board lacked jurisdiction over the appeal of an excepted service disabled employee whose agency could have, but did not, recommend him for conversion to competitive status under Executive Order 12125. Duvall v. NIH, 3 MSPR 81, 82-83, 3 MSPB 196 (1980). Kaapana v. Dept. of Interior, 1 MSPR 556, 557, 1 MSPB 536 (1980), rejected the appeal of an employee in the excepted service who argued that the Board has inherent power to review violations of civil service regulations.

An agency should classify a position to the excepted or competitive service before an appointment letter. See Neighoff v. DHS, CH-315H-14-0365-B-1 (NP 6/6/2016) ("Further, the civil service rules contemplate that a position must be classified as competitive or excepted service at an earlier stage than an appointment letter. See Dean v. Office of Personnel Management, 115 M.S.P.R. 157, ¶ 23 (2010) (stating that such classifications are required even before a vacancy announcement is issued)."). An employee does not automatically retain competitive service status if his functions are transferred into an agency and he becomes employed by an agency that provides excepted service appointments. See Haley v. Dept. of Treasury, 977 F.2d 553 (Fed. Cir. 1992). [Refer later in this chapter to "Loss of Appeal Rights by Change in Position," and "Disabled Employee."]

Appointments aside, also complicated are the multitude of pay systems for appointees. Those complexities warranted mention in one decision, IAM v. Brown, 698 F.2d 536, 538-39 (D.C. Cir. 1983), involving a dispute over the methodology for setting compensation of federal civilian mariners:

Before plunging into the intricacies of the mariners' pay-setting statute, Sec. 5348, and of the pay cap contained in Sec. 5373, it is useful to make three basic observations about the universe of federal pay systems and where this case fits with it. First, there are many different federal civilian pay systems. They include: the General Schedule ("GS") (the basic pay system for federal white-collar employees); the Foreign Service schedules; the schedules for the Department of Medicine and Surgery at the Veterans' Administration; the U.S. Postal Service pay system (which includes a schedule determined through collective bargaining); the Executive Schedule (for key management and policymaking positions); the Federal Wage System (the basic pay system for blue-collar employees); and, depending on the degree of disaggregation, over forty other, separate pay systems. These pay systems vary considerably in numbers of employees covered and method of determining pay. See Staff Report of the President's Panel on Federal Compensation, 1-5, 159-62 (1976) ("Rockefeller Report ").[6]

[6] See generally There's More to Federal Pay than the GS Schedule, Wash.Post, Oct. 26, 1982, at A17, col. 1, which contains a "roll call" of some 50 federal pay systems.

Second, although some pay systems are "linked" to one another, Congress has not fastidiously integrated all the systems to achieve uniform federal compensation policies. Instead, Congress has frequently, and sometimes haphazardly, soldered different pay ladders together at various points to construct a semblance of structure. Third, at times the alloys linking pay systems break under the stress of shifting salary scales or conflicting pay policy objectives. This case involves one such crack in the amalgam.
