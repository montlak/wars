E. PROBATION OR TRIAL PERIOD

OPM states the purpose of probation at 5 CFR 315.803 (2022):

(a) The agency shall utilize the probationary period as fully as possible to determine the fitness of the employee and shall terminate his or her services during this period if the employee fails to demonstrate fully his or her qualifications for continued employment.

Section 315.803 was revised in 2022 to delete a regulatory requirement that agencies provide periodic notice to supervisors of the dates of expiration of employees' probationary periods. See 87 Fed. Reg. 67765 (Nov. 10, 2022).

Considerable discussion is earlier devoted to determining whether an individual is an "employee" under 5 USC 7511 with an appeal right. If an individual does not meet the convoluted definition of "employee," and if the employee is a competitive service probationer, the individual discharged during probation has but limited Board appeal rights, later discussed. That individual also has access to the EEO process; to a prohibited personnel practice complaint to the Office of Special Counsel and a possible Individual Right of Action appeal to the MSPB for whistleblower reprisal, discussed in Chapter 13; and a USERRA or VEOA complaint or appeal, discussed later in this chapter. An individual in the excepted service does not have a probationer's right to appeal under 5 CFR 315.806 (2022), and the Board lacks authority under that regulation to review allegations that the excepted service appellant, who did not meet the definition of an employee under § 7511, was terminated from an excepted service position based on marital status or partisan political discrimination. Ramirez-Evans v. VA, 113 MSPR 297, 301 ¶ 10, 2010 MSPB 37 (2010). An exception: the Veterans Benefits Improvement Act of 1984, Pub. L. 98-543, provided employees who receive Veterans Readjustment Appointments in the excepted service, that is, before conversion to the competitive service, with an appeal to the Board from a termination if it is alleged that the termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status discrimination, or was not effected in accordance with the procedural requirements of 5 CFR 315.805 (2022). [Refer later in this chapter to "Veterans Readjustment Act" concerning probationary-type appeal rights of individuals with VRA appointments.]

To determine whether the individual fired as a probationer is actually a probationer, or whether she has survived probation and is an "employee" under 5 USC 7511, there may be the question of how the probationary period was calculated and the effect of service prior to probation, that is, the matter of "tacking" prior service, to determine if probation is complete. Recall discussion earlier in this chapter, "Current Continuous Service." The probationary calculation is nuanced. A related issue is whether the appointee was required to serve a probationary period. Then there is math: whether the agency counted days correctly, that is, whether the employee served less than a year at the time of the action or whether the individual actually served a little longer than the required year but the agency made a miscalculation. There may be a concern about whether the agency is supposed to count time of the employee in nonduty status (e.g., leave without pay). Because the statute, 5 USC 7511 refers to but does not define probation, the analysis is complex, depending on OPM regulatory definitions and interpretive decisions. Given the number of ambiguous or obscure decisions from the Board and the Federal Circuit, in all but fortuitously simple cases, resolution of whether an ostensible probationer earned sufficient service credit for a Board appeal can be difficult.

We turn to an OPM regulation on probation. For competitive service employees, 5 CFR 315.801 (2022):

Probationary period; when required.

(a) The first year of service of an employee who is given a career or career-conditional appointment under this part is a probationary period when the employee:

(1) Was appointed from a competitive list of eligibles established under subpart C of this part;

(2) Was reinstated under subpart D of this part unless during any period of service which affords a current basis for reinstatement, the employee completed a probationary period or served with competitive status under an appointment which did not require a probationary period.

(b) A person who is:

(1) Transferred under § 315.501; or

(2) Promoted, demoted, or reassigned; before he completed probation is required to complete the probationary period in the new position.

(c) A person who is reinstated from the Reemployment Priority List to a position in the same agency and the same commuting area does not have to serve a new probationary period, but, if separated during probation, is required to complete the probationary period in the new position.

(d) Upon noncompetitive appointment to the competitive service under the Postal Reorganization Act (39 U.S.C. 101 et seq.), an employee of the Postal Career Service (including substitute and part-time flexible) who has not completed 1 year of Postal service, must serve the remainder of a 1-year probationary period in the new agency.

(e) A person who is appointed to the competitive service either by special appointing authority or by conversion under subparts F or G of this part serves a 1-year probationary period unless specifically exempt from probation by the authority itself.

In November 2015, 10 USC 1599e established a two-year probationary period for DOD competitive service employees appointed following enactment.
