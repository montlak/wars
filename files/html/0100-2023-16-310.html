i. New Evidence; Proof of Compliance

The ALJ presiding at the hearing has discretion to determine whether the record should be reopened for additional evidence or argument. That decision is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. DHUD, Region X, Seattle and AFGE Local 3294, 41 FLRA 363, 364 (1991), followed in Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp. and NTEU, 52 FLRA 1390, 1398, 1399 (1997) (noting that motions to reopen a record are disfavored; reopening is reserved for extraordinary circumstances; "Where a party seeks to have a record reopened for the particular purpose of receiving "newly discovered evidence," the party must show, inter alia, that the evidence was in existence at the time of the original trial. Decker, 569 F.2d at 363; NLRB v. Hanna Boys Ctr., 940 F.2d 1295, 1299 (9th Cir. 1991), cert. denied, 504 U.S. 985 (1992). To rule otherwise, and require the reopening of a record to receive evidence that had come into existence after the close of the hearing, would be to approve '"a procedure [that] could mean the perpetual continuation of all trials."' Decker, 569 F.2d at 364 (quoting Prostrollo v. University of South Dakota, 63 F.R.D. 9, 11 (D.S.D. 1974))."); see SBA and Wildberger, 54 FLRA 837, 848 (1998) (applying an "abuse of discretion" standard to the judge's ruling on a motion to reopen hearing). Documents submitted to FLRA after the conclusion of a ULP hearing will generally be rejected, without some showing that they were not previously available. INS and AFGE Local 1917, 16 FLRA 1007, 1007 n.1 (1984) (ALJ Decision); see Dept. of Treasury, IRS and IRS, Detroit Dist., 43 FLRA 1378, 1383 n.3 (1992) (ALJ Decision) (no good cause was shown why arbitration award could not have been offered during hearing or why it should be accepted after hearing; to receive document would "deprive the other parties of an opportunity to challenge, question or rebut the matters contained in the document or arguments related thereto").

FLRA rejects evidence offered for the first time with exceptions to the decision of an ALJ if that evidence could have been submitted to the ALJ after the close of a hearing with a motion to reopen the hearing. See Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. and NTEU Chap. 143, 56 FLRA 398, 402 (2000) ("The Authority will not consider evidence that was not presented in the proceedings before the Judge."); Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 40 FLRA 303, 308-09 (1991) ("The documents encompassed by Exhibits 1 and 2 came into existence within 2 months of the close of the hearing before the Judge. Accordingly, if the Respondents deemed these exhibits relevant to the issues in this case, the Respondents should have sought permission from the Judge to make them a part of the record in this case. As the Respondents do not justify their failure to do so, and as there is no basis in Authority precedent for concluding that we should otherwise take official notice of the exhibits, we will not consider them here."); 5 CFR 2423.31(b) (2022). A case qualified for remand because new evidence--an arbitration award--was presented that clarified whether the issues raised in the ULP and grievance cases were the same, a matter going to the jurisdiction of FLRA under 5 USC 7116(d). Dept. of Army, Hq., Presidio of San Francisco and AFGE Local 1457, 30 FLRA 50, 52 (1987). Submissions may not necessarily constitute evidence. An exhibit submitted for the first time with exceptions that does no more than graphically describe an argument is not evidence and will not be excluded. See Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, supra.

Dept. of Transp., FAA and NATCA, 55 FLRA 293, 298, n.6 (1999), considered whether the agency could properly present to the judge in a ULP proceeding information, not previously introduced, to demonstrate the invalidity of an unexcepted arbitration award after the agency was charged with failing to implement the award. The information was received because of its relevance to the agency's compliance efforts:

In its opposition, the General Counsel requests that the Authority strike the Respondent's references to the cost of complying with the Judge's order on the basis that the cost figures are unsupported by the record. We deny the General Counsel's request. The Authority has declined to consider evidence in support of a party's position where the information contained therein was available prior to an arbitration or unfair labor practice hearing or prior to the issuance of a decision by an arbitrator or judge. See, e.g., U.S. Agency for International Development and American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1534, 53 FLRA 187, 187-88 n.2 (1997); U.S. Department of Transportation, Washington, D.C., 47 FLRA 110, 119-20 (1993). On the other hand, the Authority has considered evidence addressing whether an award is deficient. See, e.g., U.S. Department of the Air Force, Sacramento Air Logistics Center, McClellan Air Force Base, California and American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1857, 50 FLRA 96, 99 (1995); U.S. Department of Justice, Immigration and Naturalization Service, El Paso, Texas and American Federation of Government Employees, National Border Patrol Council, Local 1929, 40 FLRA 43, 51-52 (1991).

Although the Respondent was aware that the offer made by Denver would require an expenditure of funds, the cost figures and related arguments are directed toward compliance with the Judge's remedy and to alleged deficiencies in that remedy. As such, the specific arguments could not have been presented in the proceedings before the Judge. Therefore, the General Counsel's reliance on Antilles, 36 FLRA 776, and APHIS, 26 FLRA 630, is misplaced. In those cases, the Authority appropriately declined to consider documents that were not raised in proceedings before the judge, as required by section 2429.5 of the Authority's Regulations. There was no contention or finding in those cases that the evidence could not have been presented to the judge.
