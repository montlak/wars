c. Bonus

At issue in Moore v. OPM, 8 MSPR 560, 561-66 (1981), was whether earnings included a bonus, and the question was resolved by reference to IRS treatment of bonuses:

Appellant, a former employee, filed a timely petition for appeal from a reconsideration decision of the Office of Personnel Management discontinuing his receipt of a disability retirement annuity. The basis for OPM's decision was appellant's alleged restoration to earning capacity because his income from wages or self-employment in each of the prior two years equaled at least eighty percent of the current rate of pay of the Engineer Equipment Mechanic position he last occupied immediately before retirement. See U.S.C. § 8337(d).

...

The record discloses that appellant was granted disability retirement in 1972 from his position as Engineer Equipment Mechanic, Department of the Army. He was subsequently employed by the Southeastern Maritime Company (SEMCO). In 1977 and 1978, appellant reported his annual income to the former Civil Service Commission (now OPM) as $12,581.00 and $15,001.00, respectively. The current salary for appellant's former position during those years was $14,747.00 and $16,099.00, respectively. OPM notified appellant that his disability annuity of $625 per month would be discontinued as of December 31, 1979, because he was considered restored to earning capacity under section 8337(d). In a reconsideration decision dated June 3, 1980, OPM affirmed its prior decision and informed appellant that he would receive a discontinued service retirement annuity of $358 per month effective January 1, 1980,[2] plus subsequent cost of living adjustments from the date at which the annuity commenced.[3] See Loube v. Office of Personnel Management, 5 MSPR 531 (1981).

[2] See 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d).

[3] See 5 U.S.C. § 8340(c).

Appellant asserted that he inadvertently overstated his income from his employment with SEMCO in 1977 and 1978 to include the $3,524.00 and $5,000.00 in quarterly bonuses he received from SEMCO. Appellant contended that the bonuses constituted gifts from his employer and were not "income...from wages or self-employment" within the ambit of section 8337(d) for purposes of determining restoration to earning capacity. Appellant also submitted letters from the Vice-President of SEMCO and the company's payroll department, along with copies of some stubs of the bonus checks, which stated that the monies appellant received were a gift in the form of a bonus. However, appellant's representative at the hearing stated that "a good part of...[appellant's] income is received as bonus payments from the company he works for because he is a salaried employee. All salaried employees receive these bonuses quarterly." Further, appellant's representative conceded that the bonuses are taxable but asserted they are not earned income.

OPM asserts that it construes the word "wages" for purposes of civil service retirement to have the same meaning provided for that word by the Internal Revenue Code. OPM argues that were appellant not employed by SEMCO he would not have received the bonuses and that since he was employed by the company at the time he received the bonuses they constitute "wages" under section 8337(d).

Under the Internal Revenue Code the definition of "wages," for purposes of employment taxes, is set forth in 26 U.S.C. § 3401(a):

(a) Wages.--For purposes of this chapter, [CHAPTER 24--COLLECTION OF INCOME TAX AT SOURCE ON WAGES] the term "wages" means all remuneration (other than fees paid to a public official) for services performed by an employee for his employer, including the cash value of all remuneration paid in any medium other than cash....

The Internal Revenue Service at 26 C.F.R. § 31.3401(a)-1(a) has construed the term "wages" as defined in section 3401(a) to include bonuses paid as compensation for services performed by the employee for his or her employer:[5]

(a) In general,

(1 The term "wages" means all remuneration for services performed by an employee for his employer unless specifically excepted under section 3401(a) or excepted under section 3402(e).

(2) The name by which the remuneration for services is designated is immaterial. Thus, salaries, fees, bonuses, commissions on sales or on insurance premiums, pensions, and retired pay are wages within the meaning of the statute if paid as compensation for services performed by the employee for his employer.

(3) The basis upon which the remuneration is paid is immaterial in determining whether the remuneration constitutes wages. Thus, it may be paid on the basis of piecework, or a percentage of profits; and may be paid hourly, daily, weekly, monthly, or annually.

[5] 26 C.F.R. § 31.3121(a)-1 also construes "wages" as defined in 26 U.S.C. § 3121(a) to include all remuneration for employment, including bonuses.

Neither OPM nor our review of the legislative history of section 8337(d) discloses any congressional intent construing the term "income...from wages or self-employment." However, although the Board is not bound by the practices and decisions of the former Civil Service Commission, the Commission held that bonuses received from an employer for work performed are remuneration for employment or "wages" for purposes of section 8337(d). See Glen D. Boland, No. RB083170102 (ARB March 2, 1977); George C. Diem, CSC Minute No. 2 (August 13, 1962).

OPM's interpretation of the language of section 8337(d), as the agency charged with the administration of the statute, is entitled to deference. See Griggs v. Duke Power Co., 401 U.S. 424, 433-34 (1971); Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC, 395 U.S. 367, 371 (1969); Zemel v. Rusk, 381 U.S. 1, 11 (1965); Udall v. Tallman, 380 U.S. 1, 16 (1965). Further, construction of the word "wages" as utilized in section 8337(d) to include bonuses received from an employer for services rendered is reasonable to avoid the possible unauthorized receipt of a disability retirement annuity by former employees whose income from wages is artificially adjusted so as to be insufficient to qualify for restoration to earning capacity under section 8337(d), but whose total remuneration for employment, including bonuses, meets the earnings criterion of the statute.

In the instant case, appellant received his quarterly bonuses from his employer, SEMCO. The stubs of the bonus checks of record reflect that, for federal and state income tax purposes, the company reported the bonuses as taxable income to appellant and not as tax exempt gifts. This clearly indicates that SEMCO acknowledges that its bonuses did not constitute gifts to appellant, at least for federal income tax purposes under 26 U.S.C. § 3401(a), but were remuneration for services performed.[7] Further, appellant's representative conceded that the bonuses were taxable as income and that all employees of SEMCO received the bonuses.

[7] Adoption of OPM's assertion that the meaning of "wages" under section 8337(d) may be determined by resort to the Internal Revenue Code logically leads to the conclusion that gifts and inheritances excluded from gross income under 26 U.S.C. § 102 would not constitute income from wages or self-employment for purposes of determining restoration to earning capacity under 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d).

The Board agrees with OPM that were appellant not employed by SEMCO, it is realistic to conclude that he would not have received the bonuses in issue. Based on the foregoing findings of fact and analysis, the Board finds that appellant's bonuses constituted remuneration for services he performed for SEMCO and are income from "wages" within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d). Therefore, the Board finds the preponderance of the evidence establishes that appellant's income from wages for 1977 and 1978 exceeded eighty percent of the current rate of pay of the Engineer Equipment Mechanic position he occupied immediately before retirement and that OPM properly determined that appellant was restored to earning capacity under section 8337(d).
