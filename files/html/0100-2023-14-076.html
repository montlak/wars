9. Disparate; Comparable Penalties

Thomas Jefferson spoke of "equal and exact justice to all men" an element of "the creed of our political faith, the text of civic instruction, the touchstone by which to try the services of those we trust." Even-handed application of discipline has traditionally been of singular importance in the administration of "just cause" provisions of labor contracts in the private sector. Disparate treatment is a mitigating factor that will be considered once the Board determines that the adverse action is otherwise proper. Tucker v. VA, 11 MSPR 131, 133 n.1 (1982); see Bencomo v. DHS, 115 MSPR 621, 625 ¶ 11, 2011 MSPB 22 (2011) ("An appellant's allegation that the agency treated him disparately to another employee, without a claim of prohibited discrimination, is an allegation of disparate penalties to be proven by the appellant and considered by the Board in determining the reasonableness of the penalty, but it is not an affirmative defense."); Jefferies v. Dept. of Navy, 78 MSPR 255, 261 (1998) (in absence of a claim of prohibited discrimination, an allegation of disparate treatment is a mitigating factor, not an affirmative defense); Gadsden v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 74, 75 (1985) (restating Tucker); Stromfeld v. DOJ, 21 MSPR 428, 430 n.1 (1984) (disparate treatment goes to mitigation, not to an element of the charges). [Compare discussion involving EEO affirmative defenses in Chapter 12, "Comparable Employees; Disparate Treatment"; and in Chapter 13 (discussing prohibited personnel practices) to "Comparator Employees," and in Chapter 9 (discussing performance cases) to "Comparison of Employees' Performance."] Exempted from comparability analysis is the adverse action based on loss of a required security clearance. See Munoz v. DHS, 121 MSPR 483, 489-90 ¶¶ 14-16, 2014 MSPB 66 (2014) (indefinite suspension based on suspension, then revocation, of a security clearance). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Alternatives to Suspension" for Munoz.]

In 2020, OPM issued regulations addressing comparator analysis. How much emphasis will be placed on the regulation, or past law, in the future remains to be seen. Under 5 CFR 752.202 (2022):

(d) Employees should be treated equitably. Conduct that justifies discipline of one employee at one time does not necessarily justify similar discipline of a different employee at a different time. An agency should consider appropriate comparators as the agency evaluates a potential disciplinary action. Appropriate comparators to be considered are primarily individuals in the same work unit, with the same supervisor, who engaged in the same or similar misconduct. Proposing and deciding officials are not bound by previous decisions in earlier similar cases, but should, as they deem appropriate, consider such decisions consonant with their own managerial authority and responsibilities and independent judgment. For example, a supervisor is not bound by his or her predecessor whenever there is similar conduct. A minor indiscretion for one supervisor based on a particular set of facts can amount to a more serious offense under a different supervisor. Nevertheless, they should be able to articulate why a more or less severe penalty is appropriate.

In 2022, OPM revised its regulations to delete Subsection (d). 87 Fed. Reg. 67765 (Nov. 10, 2022). [Refer to March 5, 2021, OPM Guidance for Implementation of Executive Order 14003-Protecting the Federal Workforce. https://www.chcoc.gov/content/guidance-implementation-executive-order-14003-protecting-federal-workforce as to continuing validity of regulations issued by OPM under the 2018 Executive Orders.]

To be credited by the Board, a claim of dissimilar treatment must be factually substantiated, Trujillo v. DHS, DA-0752-10-0436-I-2 (NP 6/29/2012):

The appellant alleges that he was treated less favorably than other employees who were charged with falsification, but he does not cite any evidence that the circumstances surrounding the behavior of other employees charged with falsification were substantially similar to those surrounding the appellant's behavior. Therefore, we find that the appellant has failed to establish a claim of disparate penalties.

McNab v. Dept. of Army, 121 MSPR 661, 2014 MSPB 79 (2014) (Vice Chairman Wagner concurring and dissenting in part), essentially imposed on the appellant the burden of developing through discovery (or at hearing) information demonstrating treatment of comparators sufficient to establish a disparity. Vice Chairman Wagner disagreed, suggesting that if there is some evidence of disparity, the burden is on the agency to produce the information, in sufficient detail, to provide a firm factual basis for distinguishing the treatment of the appellant and the comparators. See Morgan v. USPS, DA-0752-13-0239-I-1 (NP 8/21/2013) ("The appellant asserts on review that more senior employees have been given lesser discipline for more serious offenses. He does not indicate to which employees he is referring, what offenses these unidentified employees committed, or what penalties they received. Therefore, this argument does not establish a basis for review. See Hamilton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 115 M.S.P.R. 673, ¶ 36 (2011) (to raise a disparate penalty claim, the appellant must first point to specific employees that were similarly-situated and received a more lenient penalty); see also 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(b) (a petition for review must be supported by specific references to the record and must identify all legal and factual arguments)."); cf. Daymude v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-12-0531-I-1 (NP 9/6/2013) ("Moreover, the appellant admits on review that the comparator is still under investigation for her alleged misconduct. Because the agency has not yet made a decision on what discipline to levy against the comparator, the appellant cannot show that she was subjected to a disparate penalty.").

Mickens v. Dept. of Transp., CH-0752-14-0094-I-1 (NP 2/6/2015), pointed out that the comparability analysis involves charged conduct, not uncharged but alleged misconduct of other employees:

...Regardless of any discovered misconduct, however, a disparate penalty analysis focuses on charged conduct and a deciding official is not required to consider all conduct that could have been charged. See Chavez v. Small Business Administration, 121 M.S.P.R. 168, ¶ 19 (2014) (finding that when conducting a disparate penalty analysis, the deciding official is not required to consider the universe of conduct of other employees under investigation that was both charged and could have been charged). These employees were not charged with misconduct, and therefore they are not appropriate comparators.

Reonal v. USPS, SF-0752-12-0589-I-1 (NP 8/5/2013), added that the appellant must make at least a minimal showing of disparate treatment to shift the defensive burden to the agency:

Second, we concur with the administrative judge that the appellant failed to make his threshold showing of a claim of disparate penalties. Both before the administrative judge and on review, the appellant only makes general allegations that he feels he was treated differently than other employees; in making this generic claim, however, the appellant fails to provide the required specificity that would shift the burden to the agency to articulate a legitimate basis for the differing treatment, see Downey v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 302, ¶ 11 (2013). The Board's decisions, while not imposing an inflexible standard, require an appellant to make a de minimis showing of differing treatment before shifting the burden to the agency. Id., ¶ 12. Without supplying even basic information about his putative comparators, such as the charges involved, the circumstances surrounding the discipline, or the managerial officials involved, see Voss v. U.S. Postal Service, 119 M.S.P.R. 324, ¶ 7 (2013), the appellant has failed to shift the burden to the agency to proffer a legitimate reason for the allegedly different treatment. Moreover, as noted by the administrative judge, the agency submitted evidence of similar discipline imposed on other employees in the appellant's work unit for theft of mail, thus further eroding the appellant's disparate penalties claim.

In 2022, the Board decided that analysis of comparability needed a substantial rewrite, and that occurred in Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022), overruling some decisions (that follow). Among the revisions offered by Singh, comparability analysis is for the same types of offenses, not for different offenses of comparable severity; analysis is limited to those employees whose misconduct or other circumstances closely resemble those of the appellant; comparability analysis does not extend to nation-wide searches for comparators in agencies with employees throughout the country or elsewhere in the world; there must be a close connection between the misconduct or some other factor for an employee from another work unit or supervisory chain to be a proper comparator for disparate penalty purposes; and while one employee receives a more severe penalty than that imposed on a comparator who has committed the same or similar misconduct should be considered in favor of mitigating the penalty in a given case, mitigation is not always required. The precedential Singh decision explained, as it determined that the inquiry should consider whether whether the agency knowingly treated employees differently in a way not justified by the facts, and intentionally for reasons other than the efficiency of the service, id. at ¶¶ 9-20:

The appellant's disparate penalty claim does not provide a basis for reversing the initial decision.

The appellant argues that he was denied discovery regarding the agency's treatment of other employees who engaged in similar misconduct. He speculates that such discovery would have revealed that the agency treated similarly situated employees more leniently. Before we address the appellant's arguments relating to discovery, we take this opportunity to reinstate our former law governing the analysis of disparate penalty claims and thereby overrule Figueroa v. Department of Homeland Security, 119 M.S.P.R. 422 (2013); Villada v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 268 (2010); Woebcke v. Department of Homeland Security, 114 M.S.P.R. 100 (2010), abrogated in part on other grounds as recognized in Bowman v. Small Business Administration, 122 M.S.P.R. 217 (2015); Lewis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 657 (2010), and their progeny, except to the extent that the law may have been modified by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit's (Federal Circuit's) decision in Williams v. Social Security Administration, 586 F.3d 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2009), discussed infra.

It is well settled that among the factors an agency should consider in setting the penalty for misconduct is "consistency of the penalty with those imposed upon other employees for the same or similar offenses." Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305 (1981). For decades after Douglas was decided, for a disparate penalty claim to succeed, the Board required close similarity in offenses between the appellant and any comparator(s), and that the appellant and the comparator(s) worked in the same unit and for the same supervisors. E.g., Jackson v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 7 (2005); Fearon v. Department of Labor, 99 M.S.P.R. 428, ¶ 11 (2005); Rasmussen v. Department of Agriculture, 44 M.S.P.R. 185, 191-92 (1990); Archuleta v. Department of the Air Force, 16 M.S.P.R. 404, 407 (1983).

In a series of cases issued in 2010, however, the Board changed its approach to disparate penalty claims. Under the new precedent, broad similarity in misconduct between the appellant and the comparator(s) was sufficient to shift the burden to the agency to explain the difference in treatment, and the universe for potential comparators was seemingly limitless. See Figueroa, 119 M.S.P.R. 422, ¶¶ 3-4, 10-12; Villada, 115 M.S.P.R. 268, ¶¶ 10-12; Woebcke, 114 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶¶ 19-22; Lewis, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶¶ 5-15.

In announcing its "more flexible approach" to disparate penalties claims, the Board relied in large part on a 2009 decision from our reviewing court. In Williams, 586 F.3d 1365, the Federal Circuit remanded an appeal to the Board for further development of the record as to the agency's treatment of an alleged comparator. Id. at 1368-69. Previously in that case, the administrative judge had found the comparator evidence irrelevant because the comparator was not in the same chain of command as the appellant. Id. at 1368. The court found that "[w]hile the fact that two employees are supervised under different chains of command may sometimes justify different penalties," the record before it did not justify the alleged disparity in treatment, particularly because the appellant was a mere participant in a tax fraud scheme orchestrated by the comparator. Id. at 1368-69. Applying Williams, the Board in Lewis held that there must be enough similarity between both the nature of the misconduct and the other factors to lead a reasonable person to conclude that the agency treated similarly situated employees differently, but that the Board would not have "hard and fast rules regarding the 'outcome determinative' nature of these factors." Lewis, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶¶ 15, 21.

Under the binding precedent of Williams, a comparator need not always have to be in the same work unit or under the same supervisor.[3] Williams, 586 F.3d at 1368-69. Thus, the Board's pre-Williams statements to the contrary are no longer valid. Nevertheless, while no single factor is outcome determinative, the fact that two employees come from different work units and/or supervisory chains remains an important factor in determining whether it is appropriate to compare the penalties they are given. In most cases, employees from another work unit or supervisory chain will not be proper comparators. In Williams, the court emphasized the fact that the comparator had originated and organized a tax fraud scheme in which Mr. Williams was one of several participants. Id. at 1366-69. Given the unusually close connection between the two employees' misconduct in that case, the court found that the agency needed to justify the alleged disparity in the discipline they received.[4] The unique circumstances in Williams illustrate that there must be a close connection between the misconduct or some other factor for an employee from another work unit or supervisory chain to be a proper comparator for disparate penalty purposes. The universe of potential comparators will vary from case to case, but it should be limited to those employees whose misconduct and/or other circumstances closely resemble those of the appellant.

[3] A panel of the Federal Circuit recently held that "[a] comparator employee is an employee that 'was in the same work unit, with the same supervisor, and was subjected to the same standards governing discipline.'" Miskill v. Social Security Administration, 863 F.3d 1379, 1384 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (quoting Lewis). To the extent this statement can be read as an absolute requirement that comparator employees be in the same work unit with the same supervisor, it conflicts with the prior panel decision in Williams, in which the court held that "the fact that two employees are supervised under different chains of command may sometimes justify different penalties." Williams, 586 F.3d at 1368 (emphasis supplied). We remain bound by Williams, the earlier panel decision. See Deckers Corporation v. United States, 752 F.3d 949, 959, 966 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (explaining that only an en banc court opinion, intervening Supreme Court precedent, or Congressional change of an underlying statute may overrule prior precedential panel decisions).

[4] Mr. Williams alleged that the agency reemployed the comparator, an assertion the agency disputed. Williams, 586 F.3d at 1368-69. The court remanded the appeal for further development of the factual record. Id. at 1369.

Prior to Williams, a panel of the Federal Circuit held that when an employee raises an allegation that he received more severe discipline than another employee, the proper inquiry is whether the agency knowingly treated employees differently "in a way not justified by the facts, and intentionally for reasons other than the efficiency of the service." Facer v. Department of the Air Force, 836 F.2d 535, 539 (Fed. Cir. 1988). To the extent the panel in Williams intended to remove the knowledge portion of the disparate penalty analysis, as we similarly noted, supra, in footnote 3, we are bound to follow the court's previous analysis, as set forth in the court's earlier panel decision in Facer, which includes the knowledge portion. See Deckers Corporation v. United States, 752 F.3d 949, 959, 966 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (holding that, "[i]n this Circuit, a later panel is bound by the determinations of a prior panel, unless relieved of that obligation by an en banc order of the court or a decision of the Supreme Court[ ]"). Thus, we overrule Lewis and subsequent cases to the extent they have deviated from the standard set forth in Facer. In assessing an agency's penalty determination, the relevant inquiry is whether the agency knowingly and unjustifiably treated employees differently.

Since 2010, the Board has used its "flexible" approach to disparate penalty claims as a basis for mitigating agency-imposed penalties. For example, in Portner v. Department of Justice, 119 M.S.P.R. 365, ¶¶ 2-6, 9, 16-22 (2013), the Board mitigated the removal of a supervisor who operated his official Government vehicle (OGV) and made multiple false statements to agency employees and the police regarding his actions in an attempt to hide the fact that he parked the OGV in a parking lot adjacent to a Hooters restaurant, where he consumed alcohol and ate dinner. In reducing the penalty to a 45-day suspension, the Board relied in part on evidence that other employees who had been charged with misuse of an OGV and other misconduct had received lesser penalties, even though none of the comparators had been charged with both misuse of an OGV and making false statements, like the appellant. Id., ¶¶ 20-22. The Board found that, although the misconduct of the comparators was not the same as the appellant's, it "appear[ed] at least as serious as the appellant's wrongdoing" and that the agency failed to offer a sufficient explanation for the significantly harsher penalty imposed on the appellant. Id., ¶¶ 21-22.

In Boucher v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 640, ¶¶ 2-13, 20-29 (2012), the Board affirmed an administrative judge's decision mitigating the removal of a mail handler who was arrested near agency property while on duty and later convicted of a felony, unlawful possession of a controlled substance. In mitigating the removal to a 90-day suspension, the administrative judge cited another employee who worked at the same facility as the appellant and was not removed after being charged with drug possession. Id., ¶¶ 3, 11, 13. The Board recognized several differences between the appellant's circumstances and those of the comparator, including that the comparator was charged with possession of marijuana only, whereas the appellant was charged with possession of both cocaine and marijuana, and that the comparator was not arrested on or near agency property. Id., ¶¶ 21-22. Nevertheless, the Board determined that the comparison between the two penalties was appropriate because the comparator had engaged in "a more serious act" by attempting to smuggle marijuana into a prison facility. Id.

The Board's disparate penalty analysis in cases like Portner and Boucher represents a departure from the standard set forth in Douglas, which calls for comparison with penalties "imposed upon other employees for the same or similar offenses." Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305 (emphasis added). The Board in Portner and Boucher did not find that the comparators had engaged in the same or similar offenses as the appellants. Instead, the Board found comparisons of the penalties appropriate in those cases because the comparators' misconduct was "as serious as" or "more serious" than that of the appellants. Portner, 119 M.S.P.R. 365, ¶ 22; Boucher, 118 M.S.P.R. 640, ¶ 22. We overrule Portner and Boucher to the extent they held that the disparate penalty analysis should extend beyond the same or similar offenses. The Board should not attempt to weigh the relative seriousness of various offenses in order to determine whether two employees who committed different acts of misconduct were treated disparately.

Additionally, the consistency of the penalty with those imposed upon other employees for the same or similar offenses is simply one of a nonexhaustive list of 12 factors that are relevant for consideration in determining the appropriateness of a penalty. Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305-06. The Board has frequently stated that the nature and seriousness of the offense, and its relation to the employee's duties, position, and responsibility, is the most important factor in assessing the reasonableness of a penalty. E.g., Batara v. Department of the Navy, 123 M.S.P.R. 278, ¶ 8 (2016); Spencer v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 132, ¶ 7 (2009). Under the Board's post-Lewis standard, in some cases the consistency of the penalty has become not only more important than any of the other Douglas factors, it has become the sole outcome determinative factor. We hereby reiterate that the consistency of the penalty is just one of many relevant factors to be considered in determining an appropriate penalty. Therefore, while the fact that one employee receives a more severe penalty than that imposed on a comparator who has committed the same or similar misconduct should be considered in favor of mitigating the penalty in a given case, mitigation is by no means required in all such cases. There often will be a range of penalties that would fall within the tolerable limits of reasonableness in a given case. That an agency chooses to impose a penalty at the more lenient end of that range in one case should not mean that it cannot impose a penalty at the more severe end of that range in another case.[5]

[5] There is no guarantee that a prior agency penalty determination even fell within the tolerable limits of reasonableness. Thus, the consistency called for under Villada, Woebcke, and Lewis might be rooted in an earlier disciplinary decision that was unwise, meaning that a manager could be forced to go easy on an employee who committed serious misconduct because of the unwarranted leniency of some other manager in the past.

In light of our reinstatement of the former legal standard for analyzing disparate penalty claims, we now turn to the appellant's argument that he was improperly denied discovery regarding potential comparators. Specifically, the appellant sought information regarding the treatment of employees agency-wide who had engaged in similar misconduct. The agency objected to the appellant's request as overbroad and limited its response to employees at the Los Angeles P&DC. The appellant reiterated his request for agency-wide information. The agency reiterated its objections, but it did supplement its discovery response with information regarding EAS employees within the Pacific Area (i.e., California and Hawaii) who were charged with arguably similar misconduct over the prior 2 years. The appellant asked the administrative judge to compel the agency to produce information about potential comparators agency-wide. The administrative judge denied the appellant's motion to compel, finding the requests to be "overbroad and burdensome and not likely to lead to the discovery of relevant admissible evidence."

An administrative judge has broad discretion in ruling on discovery matters, and absent an abuse of discretion the Board will not find reversible error in such rulings. Kingsley v. U.S. Postal Service, 123 M.S.P.R. 365, ¶ 16 (2016). We find no abuse of discretion in this matter, particularly in light of the above reinstatement of our former legal standard for analyzing disparate penalty claims. The agency provided information regarding potential comparators within reasonable geographic and temporal limits. Information regarding the treatment of employees across the country is simply not likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence regarding whether the agency knowingly and unjustifiably treated employees differently.

Post Singh, as to comparators in different work units, if the agency cites a comparator in its proposal notice, the Board will consider it. See Williams v. DHHS, DC-0752-16-0558-I-1 (NP 2/24/2023) (Member Leavitt dissenting); cf. Childress v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-14-0190-B-1 (NP 3/27/2023) ("This comparator employee's disciplinary action, however, was not decided by the same deciding official in the appellant's case, and so the totality of the facts in that case was not readily known by her. Consequently, we find that the difference in treatment was not done knowingly and intentionally.").

In 2010, at the behest of the Federal Circuit, the Board shifted away from an analysis requiring near identity in circumstances of the appellant and another employee to serve as a comparator. The Board began to apply a broader approach examining many circumstances. Lewis v. VA, 113 MSPR 657, 663-64 ¶¶ 12-15, 2010 MSPB 98 (2010) (overruled, Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022), except to the extent the decision is consistent with Williams v. SSA, 586 F.3d 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2009), and Facer v. Dept. of Air Force, 836 F2d 535 (Fed. Cir. 1988)), discussed in a subsection that follows, stated the old law, and the new:

[F]or the consistency of the penalty factor to be considered in a penalty determination, there must be a great deal of similarity, not only between the offense(s) committed by the appellant and a proposed comparator, but as to other factors, such as whether the employees were in the same work unit, had the same supervisor and/or deciding official, and whether the events occurred relatively close in time. In the past, the Board has considered some of these factors, such as whether the appellant and the comparator were in the same work unit, as outcome determinative; if the "comparator" was not in the same work unit, there could be no determination that disparate penalties were imposed. See, e.g., Von Muller, 101 M.S.P.R. 91, ¶ 22 ("With the exception of Hoyle, who was also removed, no other target of the agency's investigation was in the appellant's work unit.... Thus, [deciding official] Norman had no obligation under Douglas to consider the consistency of the appellant's removal with other disciplinary actions arising from the investigation.").

Our reviewing court has recently indicated that a more flexible approach is warranted under the circumstances presented by this appeal. In Williams, 586 F.3d at 1366...

[T][he court stated that the fact that the supervisor that testified did not directly supervise Mr. Edwards did not make his testimony regarding disparate treatment irrelevant:

While the fact that two employees are supervised under different chains of command may sometimes justify different penalties, in this case the administrative judge has not explained, and the record does not reveal, why the different chains of command would justify the agency treating Edwards, the perpetrator of the tax fraud, more favorably than Williams, who merely had participated in it.

Id. at 1368-69....

Consistent with the court's rationale in Williams, we hold that there must be enough similarity between both the nature of the misconduct and the other factors to lead a reasonable person to conclude that the agency treated similarly situated employees differently, but we will not have hard and fast rules regarding the "outcome determinative" nature of these factors.[4] ...

[4] Similarly, consistent with the court's rationale in Williams, factors such as whether an agency treated similarly-situated employees differently, whether the difference in treatment was knowing and intentional, whether an agency began levying a more severe penalty for a certain offense without giving notice of a change in policy, and whether an imposed penalty is appropriate for the sustained charge(s) are all relevant considerations but not outcome determinative nor threshold requirements in disparate penalty analysis under Douglas. To the extent that Taylor v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 423 1111 (2009), Quander v. Department of Justice, 22 M.S.P.R. 419, 423 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 180 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table) and other cases are inconsistent with this analysis, they also are hereby modified.

Lewis was summarized, Ellis v. USPS, 121 MSPR 570, 577-78 ¶ 11, 2014 MSPB 73 (2014):

The Board will review an agency-imposed penalty only to determine if the agency considered all the relevant factors and exercised management discretion within tolerable limits of reasonableness. Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 306 (1981). One of the relevant factors is the consistency of the penalty with those imposed on other employees for the same or similar offenses. Id. at 305. If an appellant shows that there is enough similarity between both the nature of the misconduct and other factors to lead a reasonable person to conclude that the agency treated similarly-situated employees differently, then the agency must prove a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment by a preponderance of the evidence before the penalty can be upheld. Boucher v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 640, ¶ 20 (2012); Lewis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶ 15 (2010). If the agency fails to meet its burden, the Board will not automatically reduce the penalty to the same penalty imposed on the comparator employee(s). Lewis, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶¶ 17-18. If the circumstances cited by the agency justify a harsher penalty on the appellant than on the comparator(s), but not the penalty imposed by the agency on the appellant, the Board will mitigate the penalty to the maximum reasonable penalty. Id.

Although the analysis has changed as to what constitutes a comparator, now as before, when an employee raises an allegation of disparate penalties in comparison to specified employees, the agency must prove a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment by a preponderance of the evidence before the penalty can be upheld. Ellis v. DOD, 114 MSPR 407, 411 n.*, 2010 MSPB 141 (2010) (citing Lewis v. VA, 111 MSPR 388, 391-92 ¶ 8 (2009), post).

Although there are decisions post-dating Lewis, subsequently discussed, examining comparability, there are relatively few Board decisions mitigating penalties determined upon a disparity analysis. One pre-Lewis result favoring an appellant is Taylor v. VA, 112 MSPR 423, 428-30 ¶¶ 11-13, 2009 MSPB 197 (2009) (specifically modified as to the scope of the organizational measure of comparable discipline by Lewis v. VA, 113 MSPR 657, 660-66 ¶¶ 5-18 & n.4, 2010 MSPB 98 (2010) (overruled, Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022), except to the extent the decision is consistent with Williams v. SSA, 586 F.3d 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2009), and Facer v. Dept. of Air Force, 836 F2d 535 (Fed. Cir. 1988)), discussed later in this chapter, "Same Work Unit; Evolving Case Law: Earlier Decisions of Woebcke; Lewis"), involving an appeal by an employee who was fired for violating agency guidance by shredding 55 sets of materials and who defended, in part, by demonstrating that two other employees with the same job and in the same work unit received suspensions for conduct that was the same in kind but differed slightly in degree from that charged against the appellant:

The appellant's allegation that the agency treated him disparately to another employee, without a claim of prohibited discrimination, is an allegation of disparate penalties to be proven by the appellant and considered by the Board in determining the reasonableness of the penalty, but it is not an affirmative defense. See Vargas v. U.S. Postal Service, 83 M.S.P.R. 695, ¶ 9 (1999). The consistency of the penalty is only one of the factors to be considered under Douglas in determining the reasonableness of an agency-imposed penalty. See Thomas v. Department of Defense, 66 M.S.P.R. 546, 552, aff'd, 64 F.3d 677 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Moreover, where an imposed penalty is appropriate for the sustained charge(s), an allegation of disparate penalties is not a basis for reversal or mitigation of the penalty unless the agency knowingly and intentionally treated similarly-situated employees differently or if the agency decides to begin levying a more severe penalty for a certain offense without giving notice of the change in policy. See Whelan v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 474, ¶ 13 (2006), aff'd, 231 Fed. Appx. 965 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Jefferies v. Department of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 255, 261-62 (1998). To establish disparate penalties, the appellant must show that the charges and the circumstances surrounding the charged behavior are substantially similar. Archuleta v. Department of the Air Force, 16 M.S.P.R. 404, 407 (1983). We find that requirement satisfied under the circumstances of this case because the disciplinary actions taken against the appellant and the two comparative employees were the result of the agency's unified response to the OIG findings of veterans' benefits documents in shred bins in all four of the regional offices investigated.

The appellant's evidence regarding the two comparative employees, who were disciplined as a result of the agency's unified response to the OIG investigation, shows that the agency proposed to remove those two employees for having 28 veterans' benefits-related documents and 87 veterans' benefits-related documents in their shred bins respectively....

...

Both of the removal proposals were issued to the comparative employees on October 23, 2008, prior to the removal proposal issued to the appellant on November 7, 2008. The agency mitigated the removal proposal for the employee with 28 veterans' benefits-related documents in his shred bin to a 15-day suspension. The agency mitigated the removal proposal of the employee with 87 veterans' benefits-related documents in his shred bin to a 30-day suspension. The decision notices were issued to both of the comparative employees on November 21, 2008, prior to the removal decision notice issued to the appellant on November 29, 2008.

Where, as here, an employee raises an allegation of disparate penalties in comparison to specified employees, the agency must prove a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment by a preponderance of the evidence before the penalty can be upheld. See Lewis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 388, ¶ 8 (2009); Woody v. General Services Administration, 6 M.S.P.R. 486, 488 (1981). The record reflects that the agency failed to explain its disparate treatment of employees disciplined as a result of its unified action in response to the OIG investigation, despite the appellant's record evidence of that disparate treatment. We find that the agency failed to prove a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment by a preponderance of the evidence. See Lewis, 111 M.S.P.R. 388, ¶ 8. We also find that the agency improperly imposed a more severe penalty for the same misconduct without giving prior notice of a change in its policy. See Jefferies, 78 M.S.P.R. at 261-62. Thus, we find that a 30-day suspension is the maximum reasonable penalty given the penalties the agency imposed on the two comparative employees. See Tucker v. Veterans Administration, 11 M.S.P.R. 131, 133-34 (1982). Accordingly, we VACATE that portion of the initial decision affirming the agency's removal penalty and we MITIGATE the removal penalty to a 30-day suspension.

After the 2010 Lewis decision, Board law started to develop sporadically as to comparators, for the Board conducts the analysis on a case-by-case basis rather than through a multifactor analysis, as in Douglas, established by a lead decision. As decisions are reviewed that were issued before Lewis, it should be understood that the earlier decisions were developed through a comparability analysis limited to similar offenses by individuals within the same organizational unit with nearly identical past disciplinary records. Earlier decisions provide some guidance; post-Lewis decisions bear careful scrutiny. Complicating the analysis is the Board's position that if an allegation of disparity is tied to an assertion of discrimination under the civil rights laws, the ensuing disparity analysis follows EEOC decisions that impose essentially the rigid analysis that the Board followed before Lewis. The result is that the comparability analysis is more oriented to the employee under Lewis and its progeny if EEO considerations are not the sole basis of the disparity allegations. Some decisions will analyze disparities in penalties under both the more generous post-Lewis analysis and, as to EEO allegations, under the less generous EEOC analysis. The Board would do well--being the Board--to apply the same approach to all cases raising an allegation of disparity and save the EEOC analysis only for those cases where disparity is found, mitigation or reversal of a penalty is required, and the remaining issue is one of whether the disparate treatment rises to the level of discrimination or reprisal that could entail an award of compensatory damages or other relief under the civil rights laws.

The defense of disparity of penalties can be raised based on evidence that similarly-situated employees received no discipline after committing conduct similar to the appellant. Fearon v. Dept. of Labor, 99 MSPR 428, 434 ¶ 11 (2005). To successfully argue a disparity in penalties, there must be situations comparable to the appellant. See Powe v. USPS, 282 Fed. Appx. 808, 810-11 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) ("Mr. Powe, however, did not present any evidence of penalties imposed on other employees for similar misconduct. Without such evidence, the absence of which is unsurprising given the uniqueness of Mr. Powe's offense, neither the agency nor the Board was required to consider this factor.").

Restricted old law and evolving new law aside, "where an appellant raises an allegation of disparate treatment in comparison to specified employees, the agency must establish a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment before the penalty can be upheld." Wittenberg v. USPS, 12 MSPR 617, 619-20 (1981). "In the absence of a rational reason for distinguishing these two categories of employees, the agency is obligated to discipline members of both groups in a consistent manner." Washington v. TVA, 8 MSPR 27, 29, 7 MSPB 525 (1981). See Butler v. Dept. of Navy, 23 MSPR 99, 101 (1984) (false information on a timecard was sustained but the penalty was mitigated on the basis of evidence that other supervisors charged with falsification offenses graver than the appellant's were given less severe penalties; it was insufficient for the agency to assert that it had made an effort to publicize a tougher policy against the falsification of timecards; the demotion was mitigated to a suspension); Landreth v. TVA, 20 MSPR 359, 361 (1984) (removal for gambling, selling "slip tickets" on the job was not inconsistent with reprimands given to those who purchased the tickets); Allen v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 625, 630 (1982) (falsification of travel vouchers was compared with falsification of time and attendance cards; the conduct was not precisely similar, but the "gravamen of each set of charges is similar, namely falsification"); Sims v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 680, 682 (1981) (removal for the fraudulent use of a government purchase document was mitigated to a 10-day suspension based on evidence showing similar suspensions by the agency of other employees who were charged with falsifying government travel vouchers, the attempted theft of government property, and the actual theft of government property); Jenkins v. IRS, 8 MSPR 536, 540 (1981) ("[W]here an appellant raises an allegation of disparate treatment in comparison to specified employees, the agency must prove by a preponderance of the evidence a legitimate reason for the difference before the penalty can be upheld."); Simmins v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 352, 354, 4 MSPB 413 (1980) ("[L]esser penalties imposed in 1979 were not relevant to these appeals because the false statements made by the other employees in those earlier cases related only to their general character and did not bear directly on their qualifications for the positions which they sought[.] ").

A single charge may reasonably provide a range of penalties, each appropriate in a particular set of circumstances. Furlick v. EEOC, 20 MSPR 71, 74 (1984). In the best case, consistency of penalties is but one factor considered in the mitigation analysis. Baker v. DHHS, 41 MSPR 363, 368 (1989). In other than the hypothetical best case, however, the Board historically avoided intensive disparate penalty analysis. Nevertheless, the nonfrivolous allegation of disparity in penalties must be addressed by the AJ, although the Board may address the issue on PFR, should the AJ not provide the necessary analysis, if the record permits it to do so without a remand. See Lunny v. VA, 7 MSPR 654, 657-59 (1981). But the disparity defense must at least be raised with the AJ to warrant consideration by the Board. See Wilson v. DOJ, 58 MSPR 96, 101 n.4 (1993) ("Because the appellant failed to timely object to the omission of the disparate treatment issue from the administrative judge's prehearing conference summary, and has stated no reason for failing to do so, we find that he is precluded from now raising that issue with respect to the former employee."); Simmons v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 82, 83 (1982) ("Appellant, however, did not specifically raise the disparity issue before the presiding official even though he was provided a hearing and had the opportunity to call witnesses on this issue."). If several levels of employees are involved in an incident, or in failing to prevent the incident, the Board discounts arguments of loss of trust in the appellant if the agency imposes comparatively light discipline on the higher-level employees. See Watkins v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 146, 148 (1985). [Refer to Chapter 2 concerning discovery of information pertaining to disparate penalties; refer to Chapter 6, "Respondeat Superior."]

Assuming viability to a mitigation argument based on disparate penalties, discussion begins with the theory of disparate penalty analysis under Douglas. The burden is on the employer to come forward with details supporting a difference in treatment once the appellant identifies a disparity in result for the same offense. See Drummer v. GSA, 22 MSPR 432, 434 (1984). This burden applies to all types of adverse actions, including indefinite suspensions. See Beamer v. DOJ, 25 MSPR 483, 486 (1984). The proposed penalty is relevant to the disparity analysis. The ultimate disposition of the employee's case does not justify the agency's decision to propose disparate penalties. Wilson v. USPS, 21 MSPR 490, 492 (1984). If an appellant raises an allegation of disparate treatment in comparison to specified employees, the agency must prove by a preponderance of the evidence a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment before the penalty can be upheld. The comparison should be on the basis of the charges actually made against the appellant, not the charges that could have been levied. Hooks v. TVA, 18 MSPR 294, 296 (1983) (also noting that if the agency seeks to show similarity in the treatment of similar offenders, the agency should cite specific cases to support its assertions). If the agency cannot justify the disparity in penalties, the Board will likely reduce the penalty for the appellant to bring it in line with a penalty administered to a similarly situated employee. See Gage v. Dept. of Air Force, 11 MSPR 147, 149 (1982); Bivens v. TVA, 8 MSPR 458, 463 (1981) (penalty was reduced on charges of misuse of government equipment by managers; the agency failed to rebut or explain evidence showing that other managers received more lenient penalties for substantially similar offenses).

Differences in mitigating factors among employees may be significant, Camaj v. DHS, NY-0752-11-0048-I-1 (NP 12/21/2012) (aff'd, Camaj v. DHS, 542 Fed. Appx. 933 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP)):

The appellant also asserts that the agency used disparate penalties. To establish disparate penalties, the appellant must show that there is "enough similarity between both the nature of the misconduct and the other factors to lead a reasonable person to conclude that the agency treated similarly-situated employees differently...." Lewis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶ 15 (2010); Archuleta v. Department of the Air Force, 16 M.S.P.R. 404, 407 (1983). When an employee raises an allegation of disparate penalties in comparison to specified employees, the agency must prove a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment by a preponderance of the evidence before the penalty can be upheld. Lewis, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶ 6.

On petition for review, the appellant asserts that there were four other employees who were treated differently with respect to the penalty imposed. However, the deciding official consistently explained that the appellant was different from these employees, particularly because the appellant failed to recognize the magnitude of his actions.

Although the appellant need only allege with specificity the persons allegedly treated differently, Scarberry v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 246, 248 n.* (1984), the Board is unclear concerning the appellant's burden of demonstrating disparate treatment. The initial proof from the employee need not always be specific. If the employee establishes that under unknown circumstances another, unnamed employee received a lesser penalty for a similar offense, this is enough to require the agency to justify the disparity in penalties. Ramirez v. Dept. of Air Force, DA07528010067 (12/2/1981) (decision unreported in the MSPR). The Board retreated from that expansive interpretation, however, in Arnold v. Dept. of Energy, 36 MSPR 561, 566 (1988), apparently deciding that the appellant had not put the agency to its burden of proof when he identified an employee involved in arguably comparable conduct more than five years earlier. The appellant could not recall the name of the proposing official, and the deciding official no longer worked for the agency. In McDaid v. DHUD, 46 MSPR 416, 424 (1990), an Individual Right of Action appeal, the Board made clear its interest in having specific information placed before its AJs when a disparate treatment defense is raised:

In his petition for review, the appellant alleges that the administrative judge erred in finding that the penalty was reasonable. He contends that another employee was only given a three day suspension for offenses more serious than his. As the administrative judge found, however, the appellant failed to introduce into the record any evidence regarding this claim. He merely alleged at the hearing that he had obtained such evidence through discovery. In the absence of specific evidence concerning this allegation, the administrative judge did not err in concluding that the appellant was not differently treated from another employee....

Relatively early cases gave serious consideration to disparity defenses. When an agency offered no legitimate reason for disparate treatment of the appellant, the Board must consider the appropriateness of the agency-imposed penalty. In Filip v. VA, 15 MSPR 329 (1983), the appellant brought an unauthorized weapon onto VA property, where he was employed as a police officer. The agency was aware that appellant and other agency police officers (including supervisors) occasionally carried handguns when traveling to and from work but took no action against any of them. The Board decided that a reprimand, the minimum penalty in the agency table of penalties for a first offense, was the maximum reasonable penalty. The appellant and his fellow police officers were not on notice that the offense would result in a disciplinary action as severe as removal. Another case involved an IRS criminal investigator removed for failure to pay $1,100 in past due balances on his tax returns; he had received some past discipline. But another criminal investigator was given only a three-day suspension for the same type of offense. The appellant's prior suspensions on unrelated charges did not explain the disparity in treatment. The removal was mitigated to a 60-day suspension. Gibbs v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 646, 651 (1984) (citing Davis v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 317, 321, 8 MSPB 17 (1981)). A supervisor and employee were involved in a fight, provoked by the supervisor. The supervisor pulled a three-day suspension; the appellant was (what else?) removed. The Board mitigated the removal to a three-day suspension; the grossly disparate treatment of the individuals manifested bad faith justifying an award of counsel fees. Woodard v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 614, 617 (1984). Removal for AWOL was reversed and reduced to a 30-day suspension for an appellant who presented evidence that after the record closed in his case another employee in the same work unit was given a 30-day suspension for essentially the same offense during the same general time period. Mille v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 248, 251-52 (1985). Mitigation may be required when the appellant presented evidence during a PFR that the agency took lesser action against another employee similarly situated following the issuance of the initial decision in the appellant's case. Shuff v. Panama Canal Comm'n, 14 MSPR 295 (1983). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of New Evidence," concerning petitions for reopening based on new evidence.]

The Board's approach toward an uneven penalty defense is uneven. Although the basic principle of like penalties for like offenses should be followed in most instances, an agency may validly impose different sanctions if its decision is based on full management consideration of all relevant factors under Douglas. Gilmore v. Dept. of Army, 7 MSPR 253, 255, 7 MSPB 155 (1981). Parker v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 343, 352-55 (1991), provides an example of the analysis:

The record thus documents at least 10 disciplinary violations on Squires's part: Three instances of leaving his assigned duty station without permission, two instances of failing to carry out a supervisor's instructions,...one instance of sleeping on duty, one instance of insubordination, and one instance of clocking out early. By comparison, the agency documented four disciplinary violations in effecting the appellant's removal: Two instances of leaving his assigned duty station without permission, one instance of insubordination, and one instance of sleeping on the job. Squires's violations thus encompassed each of the types of charges sustained against the appellant, plus additional offenses. Accordingly, we find that the appellant satisfied his burden to show a substantial similarity between his offenses and those of Stephen Squires.

An agency may refute a charge of disparate treatment by establishing a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment, either by showing that the offenses in question were not really equivalent, or that mitigating or aggravating factors justified a difference in treatment.... The agency adduced no evidence tending to show that Squire's offenses were dissimilar to those of the appellant, or the existence of mitigating or aggravating factors justifying a difference in treatment. The deciding official conceded in his testimony that Squire's offenses appeared to be similar to those of the appellant, but stated that he was unfamiliar with the mitigating circumstances in Squire's case. It was the agency, however, not the appellant, that had the burden to demonstrate any significant difference in mitigating circumstances.... The agency did not meet that burden here.

The consistency of a penalty with those imposed on other employees for the same or similar offenses is only one factor to be considered in mitigation of an agency-imposed penalty, however.... We must therefore analyze the relevant Douglas factors in reviewing the reasonableness of the appellant's removal.

...

Leaving a work site without permission and sleeping while on duty are both serious offenses that reflect unfavorably on an employee's dependability and upon the efficiency of the service.... [T]he appellant had only four years of satisfactory service, a more serious record of prior offenses, no excuse for falling asleep while on duty, and was on notice that further misbehavior could lead to his removal.

...The administrative judge found the appellant's demeanor at the hearing indicative of good potential for rehabilitation. We find, however, that the deciding official properly relied on the appellant's leaving the Shipyard without permission after meeting with the preaction investigator as showing the appellant's lack of rehabilitative potential. We further find that his actions in this regard are entitled to more weight than his hearing demeanor.

In sum, we find that the agency's removal action to be within the bounds of reasonableness when considered without regard to the consistency of the appellant's penalty with that imposed on Stephen Squires. We must next consider whether the disparate penalty imposed on Squires requires a different result.

Where a penalty is otherwise appropriate, the existence of a disparate penalty imposed on another employee in the same work unit will not ordinarily require reversal or mitigation. See Yeager, 39 MSPR at 151; Quander v. Department of Justice, 22 MSPR 419, 420 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 180 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table). Two qualifications must be added to this general proposition, however. First, an agency may not knowingly and intentionally treat similarly-situated employees differently.... Second, where an agency has consistently imposed a certain range of penalties for a certain type of offense, the agency may not start enforcing a more stringent penalty without giving its employees fair notice of its change in policy....

Nothing in the record indicates that the agency consistently imposed lighter penalties on employees committing offenses substantially similar to those of the appellant. There is also no evidence that the agency consciously treated the appellant more harshly than it did Squires. The appellant's penalty was decided by a different official than was Squire's penalty. Captain Nickelsburg, the deciding official in this case, testified that he was unfamiliar with Squire's case when he decided the appellant's penalty, having reviewed only his own previous cases for consistency....

Accordingly, we find that removal was a reasonable penalty under all the circumstances.
