a. Effect of Settlement on Claims of Multiple Counsel

Because a Board appeal may settle after the case has been litigated in other forums (or before the Board) with prior or other counsel, care should be taken to delineate fee claims that are covered, excluded, and waived by the settlement. Dixon v. Dept. of Army, 80 MSPR 51 (1998), illustrated how a problem can arise involving the services of lawyers prior to the counsel who finally settles a case. In Dixon, originating with an appeal from a denial of restoration rights, an attorney, Mr. Gebhardt, represented the appellant through the onset of the appeal to the date, some months later, when he withdrew from the case. In his letter withdrawing from the case, Gebhardt indicated that if the appellant prevailed, he would claim fees of $35,000 for his work. Another attorney, Mr. Miller, then assumed representation and soon the parties entered into a conditional settlement that permitted the refiling of the appeal under stated conditions. The settlement specifically addressed only the fees of Miller, the successor attorney, and specifically disclaimed any resolution of Gebhardt's fees. The appellant refiled her appeal and another settlement was reached. The second settlement resolved the fees claimed by Miller, but stated that "the appellant would remain neutral in any other claim for attorney fees." The initial decision dismissing the case on the basis of the second settlement became final; Gebhardt then filed a fee motion for his work, and the AJ dismissed his fee motion because Gebhardt lacked standing to pursue a fee motion without evidence that the appellant authorized Gebhardt to seek fees.

Readily determining in Dixon that Mr. Gebhardt's fee motion was permissible, the Board remanded to the AJ for consideration of the motion, 80 MSPR at 55-57:

We reopen this appeal because we find that the administrative judge should not have dismissed this appeal for lack of standing. See, e.g., Juidice v. Vail, 430 U.S. 327, 331, 97 S. Ct. 1211, 51 L. Ed.2d 376 (1977) (the Court may be obligated to consider the issue of standing sua sponte). Rather, the administrative judge should have considered whether the Board has jurisdiction over the motion for fees in light of the parties' settlement agreement. See McAlear v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 806 F.2d 1016, 1017 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (the Board lacks jurisdiction over a motion for fees filed by a prevailing appellant's attorney where the prevailing appellant enters into a settlement agreement that plainly and explicitly withdraws any request for attorney fees).

The right to claim attorney fees vests in the prevailing party, and it is the appellant's burden to establish entitlement to an award of attorney fees. See Allen v. U.S. Postal Service, 2 MSPB 582, 2 MSPR 420, 427, 436 (1980). A fee award, however, must not provide a windfall to an appellant and thus must accrue to counsel. See O'Donnell v. Department of the Interior, 2 MSPB 604, 2 MSPR 445, 454 n.10 (1980), overruled on other grounds, Koch v. Department of Commerce, 19 MSPR 219 (1984). When counsel for an appellant files a motion for fees, he does so on behalf of the appellant. See Brown v. Department of Health & Human Services, 42 MSPR 291, 299 (1989).

Here, Gebhardt filed a motion for fees on behalf of the appellant. The appellant consented to his filing the fee motion before she entered into the May 1, 1996, settlement agreement involving Miller. In addition, the contingency retainer agreement between the appellant and Gebhardt stated that Gebhardt would provide the appellant with services in her "MSPB appeal," and that Gebhardt would receive one-third of any net monetary recovery and one-fourth of the appellant's first year's pay upon her reemployment. The retainer agreement also recognized the possibility that the Board might award attorney fees and provided that, "[i]f attorney fees are paid in whole or in part, [Gebhardt] will make an adjustment in [the appellant's] favor that is consistent with [the] contingency fee arrangement." Thus, through the retainer, the appellant authorized Gebhardt to represent her in her Board appeal, including filing a motion for fees.

Further, there is no evidence that when Gebhardt's representation of the appellant ceased, the appellant and Gebhardt terminated their attorney-client relationship with regard to attorney fees for services rendered in the past. Indeed, the evidence of record shows that their attorney-client relationship with regard to fees continued in force. The parties' September 21, 1995, settlement agreement shows that the appellant continued to consent to Gebhardt's filing a motion for fees on her behalf. Further, on February 22, 1996, the appellant executed a declaration, which was attached to the motion for fees, which stated that she "fully support[ed] Mr. Gebhardt's claim for attorney fees in [her] case."

A settlement agreement is a contract, and its interpretation is a question of law. See Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). In interpreting an agreement, the Board looks to the language used by the parties, and will not interpret that language in a way that unilaterally modifies the agreement's material terms. See Zamora v. Department of Defense, 58 MSPR 544, 546 (1993).

The May 1, 1996, settlement agreement does not retract the appellant's authorization for Gebhardt to represent the appellant in a motion for fees for his services. The agreement provides that the appellant will "remain neutral in any other claim for attorney fees." Nothing in the language of the settlement agreement requires that the appellant retract her retainer agreement, her September 21, 1996 settlement agreement, or her February 22, 1996 authorization of Gebhardt's motion for attorney fees. We therefore do not interpret the appellant's agreement to "remain neutral" as retracting her authorization of a motion for fees based on Gebhardt's services. Cf. McAlear, 806 F.2d at 1017 (when a prevailing appellant "plainly and explicitly" withdraws a request to the Board for attorney fees, there is no further cognizable request for such fees before the Board). Thus, the Board has jurisdiction over Gebhardt's motion for fees as it does other fee motions filed by counsel on behalf of prevailing appellants.
