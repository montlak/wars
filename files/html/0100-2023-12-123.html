3. Unnecessary Findings

If an appellant prevails and then files a PFR concerning procedural errors not adjudicated because the AJ reversed the action on the merits, no useful purpose is served by Board findings on those errors; the Board will not remand the case to the regional office for additional findings. McGahey v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 115, 117, 6 MSPB 108 (1981); see Katchmeric v. OPM, 33 MSPR 118, 121-22 (1987) (no error when the AJ failed to address a specific ground advanced by OPM for determining an appellant unsuitable for employment; the AJ sustained OPM on other grounds); cf. Herrera v. Dept. of Labor, 14 MSPR 460, 461 n.* (1983) (AJ's reference to matters raised in a companion case by another appellant in the same agency represented by the same counsel as the appellant was not harmful error).

As to multiple charges of misconduct, the Board may decide that the AJ correctly adjudicated one or more charges that sustain the penalty imposed by the agency and decline to consider other charges on PFR that, if sustained, are unnecessary to support the penalty. See Leach v. VA, 107 MSPR 229, 231-32 ¶ 10, 2007 MSPB 266 (2007) (if one sustained charge properly supports the penalty, it is not necessary to address the propriety of other charges on PFR); Alaniz v. USPS, 100 MSPR 105, 110 ¶ 10 (2005) ("Because these proven specifications are sufficient to sustain Charges 1, 3, and 5, and the sustained charges are sufficient to warrant the appellant's removal, we do not reach the merits of the other charges and specifications"); Lavette v. USPS, 96 MSPR 239, 244 n.1 (2004) (citing Luciano v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 335 ¶ 10 (2001), and New v. VA, 40 MSPR 212, 215-16 (1989)). The Board once determined that it need not decide prohibited personnel practice or EEO claims if an agency action was reversed on the basis of harmful error. Swanson v. DLA, 21 MSPR 589, 589 (1984). Wilkes v. VA, DA-0432-11-0466-I-3 (NP 8/21/2014), discerned no error when an AJ reversed a performance case for substantive deficiencies but did not make a determination on an alleged constitutional due process error:

[W]e discern no error in the administrative judge's failure to make findings regarding the appellant's due process allegations. Here, the administrative judge did not make findings on the due process allegations because he reversed the removal. Because it would not serve any purpose for the Board to make a finding on these due process allegations where the appellant has prevailed on the merits, there is no basis to review these allegations or to remand the case to the regional office for findings on these allegations. See Oulianova v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, 120 M.S.P.R. 22, ¶ 7 n.4 (2013); see also Jenkins, 118 M.S.P.R. 161, ¶¶ 12, 14 (a reversal based upon a due process violation merely entitles an appellant to a new, constitutionally correct removal proceeding; whereas a reversal on the merits of a removal precludes the agency from reinstituting the action); see also Wagner v. Environmental Protection Agency, 51 M.S.P.R. 337, 352 (1991) (there is no requirement for an administrative judge to cite every possible alternative basis for the disposition of an appeal when the basis that he does cite suffices as legal support for his decision), aff'd, 972 F.2d 1355 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

These results can cause some difficulties: for example, if there is a valid claim of discrimination or a prohibited personnel practice, counsel fees are awarded and compensatory damages are a possibility. Additionally, a finding of discrimination or of a prohibited personnel practice might encourage the employing agency or the Office of Special Counsel to take disciplinary action against a supervisor or manager. There is a salutary purpose for full and final resolution of discrimination and prohibited personnel practices that outweighs the Board's interest in deciding a case on the narrowest grounds. More recent cases changed the Board's decisional direction; now the AJ is expected to decide EEO issues presented, whether or not they are dispositive of the case. A due process error would make a strong case for a counsel fee award.
