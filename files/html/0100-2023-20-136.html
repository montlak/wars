b. Action Arising During Compliance Proceedings

Assuming an appeal that has not raised a WPA claim, the appellant may raise, and the Board must resolve, a timely allegation raised in the course of compliance proceedings that a personnel action (including a decision "concerning pay") is being or has been taken in reprisal for whistleblower activities. Reid v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 663, 666-69 (1991), rev'd on other grounds following Board reconsideration, King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215, 1217-19 (Fed. Cir. 1995), said of the appellant's allegation that the agency failed to properly implement a settlement because of his whistleblowing activities:

The agency removed the appellant from the position of Electronics Engineer, GS-12. On appeal to the Board, the parties entered into a settlement agreement. That agreement provided, in pertinent part, that the agency would rescind the removal action, pay the appellant $15,000 and the appellant's attorney $2,200.00 "for reinstatement and monetary relief pertaining to the subject matter of this agreement, including but not limited to claims for back pay, severance pay, contributions, benefits, attorneys fees, and costs or damages," and not "interfere with or delay any possible award determination judgment or payment directed by the [Department of Labor] related to any workers' compensation claims...."

The agreement provided further that the appellant would resign and accept the agency's action "as full satisfaction and settlement of any and all personal legal claims...." The parties entered the settlement agreement into the record, thereby making it enforceable by the Board, see, e.g., Shaw v. Department of the Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 594 (1989), and the administrative judge dismissed the appeal.

Subsequently, the appellant's application for Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP) benefits was approved. In making its determination of the benefits due the appellant, OWCP asked the agency to supply the amount of wages earned by the appellant. The agency notified OWCP that the appellant earned back pay from the date following the effective date of his removal, March 7, 1989, to July 24, 1989.

The appellant then filed a petition for enforcement, alleging that the agency representation that he had received back pay for the entire interval between March 7 and July 24 interfered with OWCP's determination on his claim. The appellant asserted that the agency, by informing OWCP that he had received back pay for the disputed time period rather than damages for the agency's cancelled adverse actions, committed unlawful disclosures regarding the terms of the agreement, fraud and deceit in the execution of the agreement's requirements, unilateral modifications of the agreement, mischaracterization of settlement terms, libel, criminal suppression and subordination of evidence, gross obstruction and interference with his OWCP benefits, and gross interference with the settlement agreement's lump-sum provisions.... In submissions following the filing of the petition for enforcement, the appellant asserted that the agency's communications to OWCP were also retaliation against him for his protected whistleblowing activities--revealing through his chain of command and in testimony to the Senate Subcommittee on Federal Services, Post Office, and Civil Service that the agency was going to award a contract to GTE for a submarine communications system for $392 million, while the agency could devise the system itself for $91 million.

...

The administrative judge...erred in failing to address the appellant's allegation of reprisal for whistleblowing activity. See Spithaler, 1 MSPR at 589. The Board has jurisdiction over whistleblower reprisal allegations raised in otherwise appealable actions by employees and former employees. 5 USC 2302(b)(8); 5 CFR 1209.2(a) and 1209.2(b)(2). Further, the Board retains jurisdiction in appealable actions to adjudicate petitions for enforcement filed with respect to a Board compliance order or with respect to a settlement agreement entered into the record. 5 CFR 1201.112. See generally Graff v. Department of the Air Force, 39 MSPR 639 (1989). Thus, we find that where a timely allegation that a personnel action is being or has been taken in reprisal for whistleblower activities is relevant to a compliance proceeding, the Board must resolve the issue.[3]

[3] The agency's action of allocating the settlement amount to back pay, which occurred after issuance of the initial decision on July 31, 1989, is a "personnel action" as that term is defined under the Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA), 5 USCA 1221 (1990). Under the WPA, a personnel action includes any decision "concerning pay" occurring on or after July 9, 1989. See 5 CFR 1209.4(a)(9) 5 CFR 1201.191(b)(2).
