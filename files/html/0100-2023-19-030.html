b. Mixed Motives; Proof That Action Would Have Been Taken Without Discriminatory Motive

The mixed motive analysis, governed by 42 USC 2000e-5(g)(2)(A)(2)(B)(ii), describes situations involving discrimination as one, but not the only, reason for a personnel action. An amendment to the 1964 Civil Rights Act was explained in Jones v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 398, 403-05 (1995), involving an indefinite suspension that the appellant believed resulted from discriminatory animus of the proposing official:

This case must be remanded with respect to the appellant's allegation of race discrimination and other affirmative defenses. On remand, it must be determined whether the legal standard to be applied to the appellant's claim of race discrimination is that for "mixed-motive" cases set forth in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989), or that for "pretext" cases set forth in Texas Department of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 101 S. Ct. 1089, 67 L. Ed.2d 207 (1981), and McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). See Davis v. Department of the Navy, 67 MSPR 590, 598 (1995); Beam v. Office of Personnel Management, 66 MSPR 469, 474-75 & n.1 (1995).

The Board has defined a "mixed-motive" case as one in which an appellant presents "direct," rather than "circumstantial," evidence that discrimination was a motivating factor in the agency's action. Johnson v. Defense Logistics Agency, 61 MSPR 601, 604 (1994). In Beam, 66 MSPR at 475 n.1, the Board noted that section 2000e-2(m) was added to Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, effective November 21, 1991, to replace the former "mixed-motive" framework for analyzing "direct" evidence discrimination claims set forth in Price Waterhouse. Under 42 USC 2000e-2(m), "an unlawful employment practice is established when the complaining party demonstrates that race, color, religion, sex, or national origin was a motivating factor for any employment practice, even though other factors also motivated the practice." The amendment at 42 USC 2000e-2(m) modified the rule of Price Waterhouse to hold the employer "liable once the plaintiff shows that a protected characteristic played a motivating part in the employment decision (the employer's proof that the same employment decision would have been made in the absence of the impermissible motive goes only to the issue of relief)." Lam v. University of Hawaii, 40 F.3d 1551, 1564 n.24 (9th Cir. 1994). In Beam, the Board stated that, under 42 USC 2000e-2(m), an appellant will prevail if he can show by "direct" evidence that discrimination was a motivating factor in the agency's action. See Beam, 66 MSPR at 475.

Direct evidence may consist of documentary or testimonial evidence. See, e.g., Price Waterhouse, 490 U.S. at 234-36, 109 S. Ct. at 1782-83 (the specific proof of sex discrimination upon which the Court focused was, primarily, documentary evidence of numerous negative gender remarks made about the employee by partners in the evaluation process and documentary evidence of the employer's reliance on those remarks in denying her admission as a partner in its accounting firm); Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Alton Packaging Corp., 901 F.2d 920, 922-23 (11th Cir. 1990) (a discriminatory motive in black employee's nonselection for promotion was established based on direct testimony by witnesses that the employer's general manager had stated that "if it was his company he wouldn't hire any black people" and the production manager yelled at a black employee, ""____ ____ it, you people can't do a ___ thing right"); Lee v. Russell County Board of Education, 684 F.2d 769, 772, 774 (11th Cir. 1982) (a discriminatory motive in the discharge of minority teachers was established based on direct testimony by witnesses that there was no administrative reason not to re-employ the minority teachers and that the school board had expressed concern about "getting a greater 'white presence'" and stopping "white flight" at the school, and had asked the school principal to "build files" on those minority teachers); Johnson, 61 MSPR at 609 (the Board's finding of "direct" evidence of a discriminatory motive in the appellant's removal was based on the proposing official's "admission" that he made racially derogatory comments about the appellant, including a comment that he was going to "burn [the appellant's] black ass"); see also National Labor Relations Board v. Transportation Management Corp., 462 U.S. 393, 395-96, 404-05, 103 S. Ct. 2469, 2470-72, 2475-76, 76 L. Ed.2d 667 (1983) (in an analogous unfair labor practice case, a supervisor's comments, after learning of the employee's union activities, that he would "get even with him" and that he would remember the employee's activities when the employee asked for a favor were sufficient to establish that the employee's discharge 3 days later for an offense for which no other transgressor had been disciplined was sufficient to establish dual motives); but see Price Waterhouse, 490 U.S. at 275-76, 109 S. Ct. at 1803-04 (O'Connor, J., concurring) ("stray remarks in the workplace," "statements by nondecision-makers," and "statements by decisionmakers unrelated to the decisional process itself" do not "justify requiring the employer to prove that its hiring or promotion decisions were based on legitimate criteria").

In the present case, the record contains documentary evidence, i.e., the agency's August 18, 1994 investigatory report, that could constitute direct evidence of a racially discriminatory motive by the proposing official in proposing the indefinite suspension. The investigatory report includes statements by several witnesses regarding the proposing official's allegedly discriminatory remarks about the appellant, including several statements that he was going "to fire that Nigger," that he "intend[ed] to fire" the appellant, and that he was going to "get rid of" the appellant. The record also contains affidavits attesting that the proposing official repeatedly made racial slurs and racial jokes about the appellant, posted racially derogatory posters implicating the appellant, posted a dictionary of ethnic-type words allegedly used by the appellant, harassed the appellant, was harsher in his dealings with the appellant than with other employees, and intimidated and retaliated against employees who associated with or assisted the appellant. The investigatory report also contains an affidavit by the proposing official denying that he had engaged in racially discriminatory behavior against the appellant and affidavits by three other witnesses stating that they were unaware of any such behavior by the proposing official.

The witnesses' sworn statements of the repeated racial slurs and threats against the appellant that are attributed to the proposing official in the agency's investigatory report are similar to the statements of the witnesses in Alton Packaging Corp. and Lee. These sworn statements constitute more than the mere "stray remarks" to which Justice O'Connor referred in her concurring opinion in Price Waterhouse. See Price Waterhouse, 490 U.S. at 275-76, 109 S. Ct. at 1803-04. Further, while the proposing official was not the decisionmaker in the indefinite suspension, he was part of the decisionmaking process. Thus, the racial remarks attributed to him, if believed by the "trier of fact," in light of the conflicting sworn statements, could constitute direct evidence of a discriminatory motive, particularly since the proposing official sought out imposition of an indefinite suspension after the deciding official had merely reassigned the appellant following his arrest. See Lee, 684 F.2d at 774. Thus, on remand, the administrative judge must make specific credibility findings as to these witnesses.

Following remand, the Board determined that the proposing official was biased and that he influenced the disciplinary process; however, no remedy was available because the agency would have acted against the appellant without discriminatory motive, Jones v. Dept. of Army, 75 MSPR 115, 120-24 (1997):

In addition to finding that the appellant's and Robinson's testimonies at the remand hearing corroborated the findings in the investigative report, the administrative judge also found that the proposing official "is a terrible supervisor, readily disposed to abusing his position and generally mistreating employees he disfavors," that "[h]e appears to be bigoted in a general sense," and that there "is no doubt that during the course of his employment, [the] appellant suffered abuse from his supervisor," the proposing official, that "includ[ed] some racially offensive behavior." The administrative judge further found that, although the appellant "established an abysmal work environment, the statements and actions of [the proposing official] are 'unrelated to the decisional process itself' that was utilized in effecting [the] appellant's suspension." In this regard, the administrative judge found that information regarding the appellant's arrest was transmitted to the agency's Management and Employee Relations (MER) personnel specialists in Fort Lewis, Washington, and that these personnel specialists determined that an indefinite suspension was appropriate. Further, the administrative judge noted that the deciding official testified that his reassignment of the appellant was only a temporary solution pending a decision on an appropriate action to take in the matter.

It is always difficult to assess the evidence of a person's motivation and to determine the actual motives for an act. Although the administrative judge found no discrimination, we conclude that the better view of the facts here is that the appellant has proven that the proposing official's actions were based on the mixed-motive of unlawful discrimination and legitimate management concern. The direct evidence of record, including the documentary and testimonial evidence, establishes that the proposing official made the racial slurs and threats against the appellant as alleged and that his comments directed at the appellant were not general in nature and were not merely "stray remarks in the workplace." See Price Waterhouse, 490 U.S. at 277, 109 S. Ct. at 1804 (O'Connor, J., concurring); see also Jones, 68 MSPR at 404. Rather, the proposing official's racial comments and harassment of the appellant were continual and unrelenting, and he ultimately proposed the appellant's indefinite suspension consistent with his threats that he would "fire" or "get rid of' the appellant. Further, irrespective of whether the MER first recommended the indefinite suspension or whether the proposing official influenced the deciding official into indefinitely suspending the appellant, the proposing official was still actively involved in the removal process by virtue of his submission of information regarding the appellant's arrest to the MER, his proposal of the indefinite suspension, and his advice to the deciding official that the appellant should be indefinitely suspended immediately rather than temporarily reassigned. See Jones, 68 MSPR at 405.

Therefore, in view of the proposing official's racially improper comments and actions towards the appellant, his involvement in the indefinite suspension tainted the process. Under these circumstances, we find that the appellant has proved by preponderant evidence his claim of race discrimination because he has established by direct evidence that race discrimination was a motivating factor in the agency's action and, therefore, as discussed below, his indefinite suspension was based on prohibited "mixed-motives." See 42 USC 2000e-2(m); EEOC v. Alton Packaging Corp., 901 F.2d 920, 922-23 (11th Cir. 1990); Lee v. Russell County Board of Education, 684 F.2d 769, 772, 774 (11th Cir. 1982); Johnson v. DLA, 61 MSPR 601, 604, 609 (1994).

We note that the appellant requested, both below and on review, compensatory damages relating to his discrimination and retaliation claims. An appellant who prevails in an appeal before the Board based on a finding of discrimination may, in certain circumstances, recover compensatory damages from the agency pursuant to the Civil Rights Act of 1991, Pub. L. No. 102-166, 105 Stat. 1071 (codified in pertinent parts at 42 USC 1981a, 1981(b), 2000e-5). See Totten v. USPS, 68 MSPR 255, 257 n.2 (1995); Hocker v. Dept. of Transp., 63 MSPR 497, 505 (1994), aff'd, 64 F.3d 676 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), cert. denied, __ U.S. __, 116 S. Ct. 918, 133 L. Ed.2d 848 (1996). Neither compensatory damages nor back pay can, however, be awarded in cases in which a finding of mixed-motive discrimination has been made and the agency has established that it would have taken the same action absent the discriminatory motive. See 42 USC 2000e-5(g)(2)(B)(ii).

Here, inasmuch as the appellant has established that race was a motivating factor in his indefinite suspension, the burden of production shifts to the agency to show that it would have taken the same action "in absence of the impermissible motivating factor[.] " 42 USC 2000e-5(g)(2)(B). Here, we find that the agency has met this burden. As we found in Jones, 68 MSPR at 401-02, the agency established by preponderant evidence that it had reasonable cause to believe that the appellant had committed a crime for which imprisonment could be imposed, i.e., "burglary and possession of stolen property." The alleged criminal conduct of "burglary and possession of stolen property," some of which appeared to be government property, is very serious. Thus, with or without a discriminatory motive, we find it more likely than not that the agency would have taken the indefinite suspension action against the appellant pending an investigation into the matter.

Therefore, because the agency has demonstrated that it would have taken the indefinite suspension action even "in the absence of the impermissible motivating factor"--42 USC 2000e-5(g)(2)(A)(2)(B)(ii)--the Board is precluded by statute from awarding the appellant damages or back pay, or cancelling the indefinite suspension. See id.; Tanca v. Nordberg, 98 F.3d 680, 682 (1st Cir. 1996), cert. denied, __ U.S. __, 117 S. Ct. 1253, 137 L. Ed.2d 333 (1997).

...

...The appellant's burden of proof in a retaliation case is greater than his burden of proof in a mixed-motive discrimination case, as to prove retaliation he must establish, after careful balancing of the intensity of the motive to retaliate against the gravity of the misconduct, a nexus between the motive and the subsequent action. See Adair, 66 MSPR at 165, Richard, 66 MSPR at 154; see also Tanca, 98 F.3d at 682-84 (the mixed-motives provisions of the Civil Rights Act of 1991 do not apply to retaliation claims). Thus, no nexus was established between the appellant's EEO activity and the indefinite suspension action. After carefully balancing the intensity of the proposing official's motive to retaliate against the gravity of the appellant's alleged criminal misconduct, we agree with the administrative judge's ultimate finding that the appellant failed to establish the requisite nexus because he failed to show that the proposing official's motive to retaliate outweighed the gravity of the alleged misconduct involved. See Adair, 66 MSPR at 165; Richard, 66 MSPR at 154; see also Tanca, 98 F.3d at 682-84.

The statutory amendment defining a "mixed motive" analysis was explained and compared and contrasted to the circumstantial proof analysis of McDonnell Douglas by George v. USPS, 74 MSPR 71, 79-86 (1997):

[T]he administrative judge employed the "mixed-motive" standard of analysis in reviewing the appellant's claim of discrimination and found that he had presented direct evidence of discrimination, that such discrimination was a motivating factor in the demotion action, and that, therefore, he was entitled to a finding that the agency had violated Title VII of the Civil Rights Act by taking gender and race into account in its placement procedures.

The Board has defined a "mixed-motive" case as one in which an appellant presents "direct," rather than "circumstantial," evidence that discrimination was a motivating factor in the agency's action. Johnson v. DLA, 61 MSPR 601, 604 (1994). In Beam v. OPM, 66 MSPR 469, 475 n.1 (1995), the Board noted that section 2000e-2(m) was added to Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, effective November 21, 1991, to replace the former "mixed-motive" framework for analyzing "direct" evidence discrimination claims set forth in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989). Under 42 USC 2000e-2(m), "an unlawful employment practice is established when the complaining party demonstrates that race, color, religion, sex, or national origin was a motivating factor for any employment practice, even though other factors also motivated the practice." The amendment at 42 USC 2000e-2(m) modified the rule of Price Waterhouse to hold the employer "liable once the plaintiff shows that a protected characteristic played a motivating part in the employment decision (the employer's proof that the same employment decision would have been made in the absence of the impermissible motive goes only to the issue of relief)." Lam v. University of Hawaii, 40 F.3d 1551, 1564 n.24 (9th Cir. 1994).

In Beam, the Board stated that, under 42 USC 2000e-2(m), an appellant will prevail if he can show by "direct" evidence that discrimination was a motivating factor in the agency's action. See Beam, 66 MSPR at 475. Direct evidence may consist of documentary or testimonial evidence. Jones v. Dept. of the Army, 68 MSPR 398, 403 (1995). It may be any written or verbal policy or statement made by an employer that "both reflect[s] directly the alleged discriminatory attitude and bear[s] directly on the contested employment decision." Fuller v. Phipps, 67 F.3d 1137, 1142 (4th Cir. 1995) (quoting Ostrowski v. Atlantic Mutual Insurance Companies, 968 F.2d 171, 182 (2d Cir. 1992)).

The administrative judge found, in the testimony of two members of the selection committee formed to fill positions in the reorganized Facilities Department, "direct and undeniable" evidence that the agency considered sex and race as factors in its placement process. One of the members of the committee was Louis Norris, at the time in question the Director, Office of Facilities, Program, Planning, and Management. He was the individual responsible for selecting positions to be filled in the Windsor Facilities Service Center, successor unit to the Boston Facilities Service Center at which the appellant was employed and which was abolished in the restructuring. The other member of the selection committee upon whose testimony the administrative judge relied was Philip Wilson, then the Director, Facilities Services Center in Windsor, Connecticut.

A brief explanation of the way in which the selection committee operated is in order, by way of background. In the restructuring, the Facilities Department, nationwide, underwent a complete reorganization, suffering a forty percent reduction, and going from twenty field offices to twelve, and from approximately 950 employees to approximately 450. Mr. Norris testified that a committee, composed of fifteen managers, met in a large room to make selections for a number of newly-created EAS positions, following a procedure rather like the "NFL draft." Positions were filled through a process of elimination. As each employee was "selected" for a position by an individual manager, the selection was placed on a bulletin board in full view of the committee members who then essentially ratified the selection. Each placement became the basis for an "offer" to the selectee, with final placement being subject to the selectee's concurrence. The committee had before it for its consideration PS Forms 991, Applicant Information, and a "Requirements by Applicant Matrix" which was a chart reflecting managers' ratings of candidates' demonstrated achievements in each KSA (Knowledge, Skill, and Ability) identified for each position for which he or she was being considered. The committee members were, of course, permitted to use any personal knowledge they had of the candidates. A special list of individuals designated as "high performers" was provided to the committee and these individuals were identified by a color-coded dot.

The testimony which the administrative judge found provided direct evidence that the agency considered race and sex as factors in the placement process was that other color-coded dots identified candidates as minorities and females. He found, based on this testimony, that the agency required the selection committee to consider whether a candidate was a woman and/or a minority in making its placement decisions, and that this represented conduct that undeniably revealed a discriminatory attitude that could not be reasonably construed as having a legitimate nondiscriminatory purpose.

In concluding that the committee members were required to consider the candidates' race and sex in making their selections, we find that the administrative judge misinterpreted the testimony, and that it did not, in fact, constitute direct evidence of discrimination. Mr. Norris testified that, after selections were made, colored dots were applied to the names of the selectees who were females and minorities as a means of identification so that no candidate was overlooked because of his or her race or sex and for purposes of general equal employment opportunity reporting requirements. He denied that the dots influenced his selections or that there was any preestablished quota for minority or female selections. He also testified that, after the selections were made, and the dots affixed, no changes were made based on the race or sex of the selectees. Mr. Norris further testified that, apart from the dots, he was aware of the race and sex of the candidates from his personal knowledge. The testimony of Mr. Wilson was in accord.

In view of the above, we conclude that the testimonial evidence upon which the administrative judge relied does not support his finding that the selection committee considered candidates' race and sex in making its placement decisions. Weaver v. Dept. of the Navy, 2 MSPB 297, 2 MSPR 129, 133 (1980) (in reviewing an initial decision, the Board is free to substitute its own determinations of fact for those of the administrative judge, giving his findings only as much weight as may be warranted by the record and by the strength of his reasoning), review denied, 669 F.2d 613 (9th Cir. 1982) (per curiam). We further find that the testimonial evidence, as described above, does not constitute direct evidence of discrimination but rather suggests the agency's awareness of its equal employment opportunity obligations. See, e.g., 29 CFR 1614.601; cf. Price Waterhouse, 490 U.S. at 234-36, 109 S. Ct. at 1782-83 (direct evidence of sex discrimination was found in documentary evidence of numerous negative gender remarks made about the employee by partners in the evaluation process and documentary evidence of the employer's reliance on those remarks in denying her admission as a partner in its accounting firm); EEOC v. Alton Packaging Corp., 901 F.2d 920, 922-23 (11th Cir. 1990) (direct evidence of racial discrimination was found in testimony of witnesses that the employer's general manager had stated that "if it was his company he wouldn't hire any black people" and that the production manager had yelled at a black employee, "___ ___ it, you people can't do a ___ thing right"); Lee v. Russell County Bd. of Educ., 684 F.2d 769, 772, 774 (11th Cir. 1982) (direct evidence of discrimination against minorities was found in testimony by witnesses that there was no administrative reason not to re-employ the minority teachers and that the school board had expressed concern about "getting a greater 'white presence'" and stopping "white flight" at the school, and had asked the school principal to "build files" on those minority teachers); Jones, 68 MSPR at 404-05 (statements by witnesses that the proposing official made discriminatory remarks, racial slurs, and racial jokes about the appellant, put up racially derogatory posters implicating the appellant, posted a dictionary of ethnic-type words allegedly used by the appellant, harassed him, was harsher in his dealings with the appellant than with other employees, and intimidated and retaliated against employees who associated with or assisted the appellant, if credible, could be direct evidence of racial discrimination); Johnson, 61 MSPR at 609 (direct evidence of racial discrimination was found in the proposing official's "admission" that he had made racially derogatory comments about the appellant, including a comment that he was going to "burn [the appellant's] black ass"). Accordingly, in the absence of direct evidence of discrimination, the administrative judge's analysis of the appellant's claim under the "mixed-motive" theory was improper. Id. at 604.

In the absence of direct evidence, we find that the appellant's evidence of discrimination is, at best, circumstantial, and that his claims must, therefore, be reviewed under the "pretext" standard of analysis. The Board has adopted the basic test for proving intentional discrimination as set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-04, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 1824-25, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973), and clarified in St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, 509 U.S. 502, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 125 L. Ed.2d 407 (1993). To establish a claim of prohibited employment discrimination through circumstantial evidence under that theory, the employee first must establish a prima facie case; the burden of going forward then shifts to the agency to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action; and, finally, the employee must show that the agency's stated reason is merely a pretext for prohibited discrimination. Beam, 66 MSPR at 475. To establish a prima facie case of discrimination, an appellant must show that he is a member of a protected group, that he was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of his protected group, and that he was treated more harshly than that individual. Coleman v. Dept. of the Air Force, 66 MSPR 498, 508 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1165 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

Although many positions were filled at the EAS-25 level in the RIF, the appellant argues only that there were two such positions for which he was not selected based on his race and/or sex. The two positions were an EAS-25 Real Estate Specialist position in Windsor, Connecticut, filled by Kathleen Walker, a white female, and an EAS-25 Facility Requirements Specialist in Washington, D.C., filled by Marianne Sinclair, also a white female. The appellant, as a white employee, is protected by Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, McDonald v. Santa Fe Trail Transp. Co., 427 U.S. 273, 280, 96 S. Ct. 2574, 2578, 49 L. Ed.2d 493 (1976), but because both selectees were also white, we find that he has not established a prima facie case of race discrimination. Because both selectees were female, however, we find that the appellant has established a prima facie case of sex discrimination.

An agency meets its burden to articulate a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason when it introduces evidence, "which taken as true, would permit the conclusion that there was a nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse action," regardless of whether the agency actually proves its charges. Hicks, 509 U.S. at 508, 113 S. Ct. at 2748 (emphasis in original); Carter v. SBA, 61 MSPR 656, 666 (1994). The Board has applied this analysis to RIF appeals. Di Pietro, 65 MSPR at 262; Decker v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 119, 132 (1989). Here, the agency produced evidence, in the form of testimony from the two individuals who selected Ms. Walker and Ms. Sinclair, that each was better qualified for the particular position in question than the appellant. Specifically, Mr. Norris testified that he selected Ms. Walker for the position of Real Estate Specialist because her entire work career, outside and inside the Postal Service, had revolved around real estate. In addition, she had a degree in real estate and was a certified property manager. She also was serving in the position of Real Estate Specialist, Sr., at the time of the selection. Mr. Norris further testified that the appellant's name was not among those listed on the "Placement Pool" for the Real Estate Specialist position, because he lacked the qualifications for it. Specifically, according to Mr. Norris, the only indication that the appellant had any real estate knowledge was that he had served a one-month detail, in 1989, as a manager of Realty Acquisition. Id. at 129.

Similarly, Mr. Wilson testified that he selected Ms. Sinclair for the position of Facility Requirements Specialist because she was an EAS-24 Program Manager, Facilities Requirements, and because, prior to that, she had been an EAS-23 Facilities Requirements Specialist since 1986. Mr. Wilson further testified that Ms. Sinclair had a considerable amount of operations experience which he felt was very important to the job. According to Mr. Wilson, he was also swayed by the fact that Ms. Sinclair's former supervisor gave her a very high recommendation. Mr. Wilson testified that he did consider appellant for this job, even though his name was not among those on the "Placement Pool" for the Facility Requirements Specialist position, but that his operations experience was not as extensive as Ms. Sinclair's, and she had attained a higher level in the requirements function than the appellant, who had not been in the job for approximately two years. We find that this testimonial evidence, as supported by documentary evidence, if taken as true, clearly permits the conclusion that there was a nondiscriminatory reason for these two selections.

The burden then shifts to the appellant to show that the agency's reason is a pretext for discrimination. In this regard, the appellant must actually show, by a preponderance of the evidence, both that the stated reason was false, or not the real reason for the action, "and that discrimination was the real reason" for the agency's action. Hicks, 509 U.S. at 515, 113 S. Ct. at 2752 (emphasis in the original). On review, we do not find that the appellant has met this dual burden. As noted, he has not established that the stated reason for the action was false because he has not shown that he was better qualified, or even as well qualified, for the two positions in question, as were the selectees.

In an attempt to show that prohibited discrimination was the real reason for the demotion, the appellant argues that the Postal Service intended, by its placement decisions for higher-level positions, to achieve a forty percent quota of minorities and women. In this regard, he provided testimony from three employees who stated that they had been so advised at various meetings conducted in connection with the reorganization. Specifically, Domenic Giarratani, Supervisor, Customer Services, testified that Mr. Norris spoke at a meeting he attended in November 1992, and stated that forty percent of the new Facilities organization would be made up of women and minorities. Joseph Kenneally, Elevator/Boiler Inspector, testified that he was present when Mr. Wilson told a group that he addressed in October 1992 that forty percent of the positions would go to minorities. And Diane Gay, Confidential Secretary to the Manager of Finance testified that, at a meeting she attended in October or November 1992, management said that women and minorities would be considered first for the newly-created positions. The appellant, himself, testified that he was privy to several conversations wherein management emphasized the forty percent quota. Mr. Norris, however, denied any mention of preferential treatment for women or minorities or that there were any quotas, as did Mr. Wilson.

The administrative judge did not make any credibility findings to resolve the conflict in the testimony. Spithaler v. OPM, 2 MSPB 2, 1 MSPR 587, 589 (1980) (an initial decision must identify all material issues of fact and law, summarize the evidence, resolve issues of credibility, and include the administrative judge's conclusions of law and his legal reasoning, as well as the authorities on which that reasoning rests). Although resolving conflicts in the evidence and deciding issues of credibility is normally the province of the administrative judge, as set forth below, we deem it unnecessary to remand this case. See, e.g., Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 557 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995), cert. denied, __ U.S. __, 116 S. Ct. 728, 133 L. Ed.2d 680 (1996). Assuming arguendo that we resolve the disputed evidence in a manner consistent with the appellant's position, that is, that the agency did intend, by its placement decisions, to achieve a forty percent quota of minorities and women, we would still be constrained to find that the appellant has not established that the agency's reason was a pretext for prohibited discrimination. This is so because of the appellant's failure to show that the agency's stated reason, i.e., selection of superior candidates, was false. Hicks, 509 U.S. at 515, 113 S. Ct. at 2752.

We conclude, therefore, that the appellant has not carried his burden of showing that his demotion was due to discrimination based upon either race or sex, and that, therefore, he is not entitled to relief on that basis....

The Board distinguished the McDonnell Douglas circumstantial evidence test and the Price Waterhouse mixed motive analysis, Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 590, 597-99 (1995):

[T]he appellant contends that Bland discriminated against him. We find that the appellant did not establish his affirmative defense of race discrimination. The administrative judge, however, applied the wrong legal standard to the appellant's discrimination claim. The administrative judge should not have applied the legal standard for "mixed motive" cases set forth in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989), to analyze the appellant's discrimination claim. Id. Instead, the administrative judge should have applied the legal standard set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973).

In her initial decision, the administrative judge relied on Johnson v. Defense Logistics Agency, 61 MSPR 601, 604 (1994), where the Board found that a case in which the agency responds to the employee's direct evidence of discrimination with a legitimate reason(s) for its adverse action is referred to as a "mixed motive" case. In such a case, the appellant must prove by direct evidence that membership in a protected category was a "substantial factor" in the employment decision at issue. Id. Since the appellant here presented solely circumstantial evidence to support his discrimination claim, the administrative judge should have applied the McDonnell Douglas framework test rather than the Price Waterhouse framework.[2]

[2] We further note that the Civil Rights Act of 1991 modified the framework for analyzing claims of discrimination based on direct evidence in Price Waterhouse. See 42 USC 2000e-2(m); Beam v. Office of Personnel Management, 66 MSPR 469, 475 n.1 (1995).

To establish a claim of prohibited employment discrimination through circumstantial evidence under the McDonnell Douglas framework, the employee must first establish by preponderant evidence a prima facie case by showing that: (1) He is a member of a protected group; (2) he was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of his protected group; and (3) he was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of his protected group. See Coleman v. Department of the Air Force, 66 MSPR 498, 508 (1995). The burden of going forward then shifts to the agency to articulate a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its action. See Carter v. Small Business Administration, 61 MSPR 656, 666 (1994) (citing St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 2748, 2752, 125 L. Ed.2d 407 (1993)). An agency meets this burden when it introduces evidence, which, taken as true, would permit the conclusion that there was a nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse action, regardless of whether the agency actually proves the charges. Id. Finally, the employee must show by preponderant evidence that the agency's stated reason is merely a pretext for prohibited discrimination. See, e.g., McDonnell Douglas Corp., 411 U.S. at 802-04, 93 S. Ct. at 1824-25; Carter, 61 MSPR at 666 (1994). Thus, the appellant must actually show that both the stated reason was false, or not the real reason for the action, and that discrimination was the real reason for the action. See Carter, 61 MSPR at 666 (citing Hicks, __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2742, 2752).

Here, although it is undisputed that the appellant is a member of a protected group (African-American), he has not established that he was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of his protected group, and that he was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of the protected group. In this regard, we note that the appellant's evidence of discrimination suggests only that the appellant and Bland had a poor working relationship, and that Bland spent more time observing the appellant than white employees under his supervision. The record does not indicate that any of these employees had engaged in any kind of misconduct, that they faced discipline based on charges of failing to follow instructions and careless workmanship, or that they had a prior disciplinary record similar to that of the appellant. Therefore, since the appellant failed to demonstrate that he was similarly situated with regard to these employees, he did not establish all of the required elements of a prima facie case of disparate treatment based on race. See Golden v. United States Postal Service, 60 MSPR 268, 274 (1994).

Even if the appellant had established a prima facie case, however, the agency provided a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for removing him, i.e., the agency's claims that the appellant failed to follow instructions, and that his careless workmanship endangered the safety of others. Therefore, since the appellant produced no evidence to show that the agency's action was pretextual, he has not established his affirmative defense of discrimination by the requisite evidence. Consequently, the administrative judge's error in applying the Price Waterhouse framework provides no basis for reversal of the initial decision. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984).

Garrison v. Dept. of Navy, 88 MSPR 389, 392-93 ¶¶ 6-10 (2001), presents another example of mixed motive analysis and a remand for further analysis of the agency's defense in response to direct evidence of racial discrimination as a factor in the appellant's removal:

...We...agree with the administrative judge that the statements described in Mr. Wagner's testimony constitute direct evidence that racial discrimination was a factor in the appellant's removal.

It is apparent, however, that racial discrimination and reprisal were not the only factors motivating the appellant's removal. As we have indicated above, the administrative judge sustained the charge of excessive absence, and the appellant has not challenged her findings regarding the merits of that charge. This case therefore is one involving "mixed motives," i.e., one in which the action was motivated both by unlawful discrimination and reprisal and by a legitimate management concern. In such a case, the extent of the remedy available to the appellant may be limited if the agency proves, by a preponderance of the evidence, that it would have taken the action in question in the absence of the unlawful discrimination and reprisal. See 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000e-2(m), 2000e-5(g)(2)(B)(ii); Caronia v. Department of Justice, 78 MSPR 201, 212 (1998); Jones v. Department of the Army, 75 MSPR 115, 121-22 (1997); 1 Lindemann & Grossman, Employment Discrimination Law at 40-44 (3d ed. 1996). If the agency meets this burden in a removal appeal, the appellant is not entitled to reinstatement or back pay. Jones, 75 MSPR at 121-22; see 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000e-2(m), 2000e-5(g)(2)(B)(ii).

The record includes some evidence that the agency may have removed the appellant in the absence of any racially discriminatory motive. It shows that the appellant had been absent from work for nearly 7 months by the time his removal was proposed, and that he was expected to be absent for at least an additional 6 months. Prolonged absences can provide just cause for removal. E.g., Allen v. Department of the Army, 76 MSPR 564, 570 (1997); see also Cresson v. Department of the Air Force, 33 MSPR 178, 184-85 (1987) (removal for lengthy absence within parameters of reasonableness); McKenzie v. United States Postal Service, 1 MSPB 480, 1 MSPR 496, 497 (1980) (prolonged absence with no foreseeable end constitutes just cause for removal). Moreover, in his position, the appellant performed important, safety related, responsibilities.

The existence of the evidence described above, however, does not necessarily establish that the agency would have considered the particular absence at issue in this case a sufficient basis for removal in the absence of any racially discriminatory motive. Moreover, the administrative judge did not specifically address the issue of whether improper discrimination caused the appellant's prolonged absence--an absence that is described in the record as related to stress and anxiety, and that is alleged to be work-related.... Such a causal relationship could evidence that the agency would not have removed the appellant in the absence of racial discrimination.

For the reasons stated above, we VACATE the initial decision insofar as it ordered cancellation of the appellant's removal. We also note that determining whether the removal would have been effected in the absence of improper discrimination is a factual matter that may require assessment of the credibility of witnesses. The administrative judge, who held the hearing in this case, who has had the opportunity to observe the demeanor of the witnesses at that hearing, and who would hold any additional hearing that may be required in this case, is in the best position to determine whether the agency has met its burden of proof regarding this matter. See Jackson, 768 F.2d at 1331-32. Accordingly, we find that remanding this case to the field office for the purpose of making that determination is appropriate.

Before Southerland, Brott v. GSA, 116 MSPR 410, 414-15 ¶¶ 12-14, 2011 MSPB 52 (2011) (overruled in part by Southerland v. DOD, 119 MSPR 566, 577 ¶ 24, 2013 MSPB 46 (2013) (Southerland overruled as to motivational analysis in Rehabilitation Act retaliation claims, Pridgen v. The U.S. Supreme Court has held that in Title VII cases, a plaintiff will prevail under a "mixed-motive" analysis if he shows that proscribed discrimination was a motivating factor in the adverse employment decision, but that an employer may avoid liability by showing it would have taken the same action absent the prohibited discrimination. Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 244-45 (1989), superseded by statute as recognized in Lewis v. American Foreign Service Association, 846 F. Supp. 77 (D.D.C. 1993). In Gross v. FBL Financial Services, Inc., 129 S. Ct. 2343, 2351-52 (2009), the Court found that lower courts had erroneously applied the mixed-motive analysis announced in Price Waterhouse to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA). In holding that the ADEA does not authorize mixed-motive age discrimination claims, the Court relied on the language of the ADEA requiring a showing that the employer took adverse action "because of" age and the fact that Congress had not amended the ADEA to allow for recovery in mixed-motive cases, as it had in Title VII cases. The Court, therefore, held that a plaintiff alleging that an employer took an adverse employment action proscribed by the ADEA must prove by preponderant evidence (either direct or circumstantial) that the employer took the action "because of" the plaintiff's age and that the burden of persuasion does not shift to the employer to show that it would have taken the action regardless of age, even when the plaintiff has produced some evidence that age was one motivating factor in the adverse employment decision. Gross, 129 S. Ct. at 2351-52.

Relying on Gross, the Seventh Circuit has concluded that lower courts, including the Seventh Circuit, had erroneously applied the mixed-motive analysis to disability discrimination cases arising under the pre-2009 Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).[*] Serwatka v. Rockwell Automation, Inc., 591 F.3d 957, 961-62 (7th Cir. 2010). The court reasoned that the ADA, like the ADEA, renders employers liable for decisions made "because of" a person's disability, and that this language in the ADA necessitated finding an employee alleging disability discrimination under the pre-2009 ADA must establish that his disability was the "but for" causation of the employer's adverse action. Id. Thus, the Seventh Circuit found that a plaintiff alleging that an employer took an adverse employment action proscribed by the ADA must prove by preponderant evidence that the employer took the action "because of" the plaintiff's disability and that the burden of persuasion does not shift to the employer to show that it would have taken the action regardless of disability, even when the plaintiff produced some evidence that disability was one motivating factor in the adverse employment action. See Serwatka, 591 F.3d at 961-962.

[*] We note that the ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA) removed the provision in the ADA that an employer may not discriminate "because of" an employee's disability, and amended the ADA to provide that an employer may not discriminate against an individual "on the basis of" disability. 42 U.S.C. § 12112(a) (2009). To resolve this case, the Board need not make a finding on whether the phrase "on the basis of" means something different from "because of" and whether this, or other revisions to the ADA, affects the viability of a mixed-motive claim under the ADA for a disability discrimination case arising after the effective date of the ADAAA. The Board overrules Caronia v. Department of Justice, 78 M.S.P.R. 201, 214-16 (1998), overruled in part on other grounds by Carter v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 641, ¶ 25 n.5 (2001), which held that, under the ADA, an appellant is entitled to some relief for discrimination on the basis of disability if he shows that the agency had "mixed-motives" for its action. However, the Board reserves for a future decision whether the reasoning employed in Caronia remains applicable to cases arising under the ADAAA.

We agree with the Seventh Circuit's analysis and find that, in disability discrimination cases arising before the January 1, 2009 amendments to the ADA, the appellant must prove by preponderant evidence that the agency took an adverse action "because of" his disability, and that the burden of persuasion does not shift to the agency to show that it would have taken the action regardless of disability, even when the appellant produced some evidence that disability was one motivating factor in the adverse employment action. Accordingly, we must analyze the disability discrimination claim in this matter without relying on a mixed-motive framework.

Caronia v. DOJ, 78 MSPR 201, 210-16 (1998) (overruled in part by Southerland v. DOD, 119 MSPR 566, 577 ¶ 24, 2013 MSPB 46 (2013)), explained that in a mixed motive case, when one charge is sustained against an employee but another charge is tainted by discrimination, the Board fashions a remedy appropriate to the sustained misconduct:

The remarks, referenced above, indicating what entered into the decision-making process regarding the appellant, show that the removal decision was made in reliance on the impermissible motive of the appellant's perceived disability. The evidence of discrimination therefore is tied directly to the alleged discriminatory animus, the perception of the appellant as disabled by his depression and treatment, and constitutes direct evidence of disability discrimination. See Fields v. New York State Office of Mental Retardation and Developmental Disabilities, 115 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 1997); Ostrowski v. Atlantic Mutual Insurance Cos., 968 F.2d 171, 181 (2d Cir. 1992). Where an appellant establishes direct evidence of discrimination on the basis of disability, to prevail, that is, to show that discrimination on the basis of disability was a motivating factor, he must also show that the agency's admittedly discriminatory action was prohibited, i.e., that he is a qualified disabled person against whom the agency may not discriminate. See Monette v. Electronic Data Systems Corp., 90 F.3d 1173, 1184 (6th Cir. 1996); Clark v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 MSPR 552, 561 (1997).

A "qualified" disabled person is a disabled person who can perform the essential functions of his position, with or without reasonable accommodation, without endangering the health and/or safety of himself and/or others. 29 CFR § 1614.203(a). The appellant showed that he was a qualified disabled person protected by the discrimination laws because, as discussed above with respect to the charges, the evidence showed that he was capable of performing his job duties without accommodation. Thus, in this case, the appellant established by direct evidence that his perceived disability was an impermissible "motivating factor" in the agency's removal action. The record shows, however, that another factor also motivated the agency's decision, specifically the sustained charge of bringing an unauthorized firearm onto the grounds of a correctional facility. Thus, the appellant's evidence of discrimination based on disability merely establishes that the agency had mixed-motives, both permissible and impermissible, for its decision. See Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 246 (1989).

Under the Rehabilitation Act, the remedies available to an employee who proves discrimination because of his disability are defined by Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. See 29 U.S.C. § 794a. Under Title VII, a federal employee is entitled to some relief if he proves that his disability was a "motivating factor" in the decision made, "even though other factors also motivated" the agency's decision. See 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000e-2(m), 2000e-16; Jones v. Department of the Army, 68 MSPR 398, 406 (1995). In other words, a federal employee is entitled to some relief for discrimination on the basis of disability if he shows that the agency had "mixed-motives" for its action.

The agency may limit the extent of the remedy available to the employee, however, if it proves that it would have made the same decision "in the absence of the impermissible motivating factor," i.e., discrimination on the basis of disability. 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(g)(2)(B). In that event, the appellant is not entitled to the remedies of reinstatement, back pay, or damages. Id.; Buchanan v. City of San Antonio, 85 F.3d 1967 200 (5th Cir. 1996); Pedigo v. P.A.M. Transport, Inc., 60 F.3d 1300 (8th Cir. 1995). See also Jones v. Department of the Army, 75 MSPR 115, 121-24 (1997) (applying the mixed-motive analysis in the context where a federal employee proved race discrimination was a motivating factor in the removal action for misconduct).

We must therefore decide whether the agency met its burden to prove by preponderant evidence that it would have taken the same action absent the impermissible motive. See 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(g)(2)(B); 1 Barbara Lindemann and Paul Grossman, Employment Discrimination Law 41 (3d ed. 1996). In other words, the Board must decide whether the agency met its burden of persuasion to prove that it would have taken the same action based on the sustained charge, which we find was not tainted by the impermissible motive.

The deciding official testified that he viewed the appellant's misconduct of bringing an unauthorized weapon onto the grounds of the facility as it "an extreme breach of institutional security." He testified that

[e]ven though the parking lot [where the appellant parked the car in which the agency found the weapon] is not within...the secure perimeter of the institution, we have minimum security camp inmates who would have access to the parking lot, and therefore it creates an unacceptable security hazard.

He testified further that "even if one goes to a...considerable degree to conceal the weapon, make it hard for anybody to find it, nonetheless it's a very serious violation, especially a knowing violation." The deciding official testified further that he did not find credible the appellant's statement that he forgot that the weapon and ammunition were in his car. He testified that he believed that the appellant intended the firearms violation because he believed that the appellant lied to him by telling him that the appellant did not construct a hiding place for the gun in his car. Thus, the agency argues that it would have taken the removal action absent the impermissible motive based on both the seriousness of the sustained offense and whether the appellant committed a "knowing violation."

As noted above, the appellant maintained both before the agency and before the Board that he brought the weapon and ammunition onto the grounds of the facility inadvertently. The appellant provided an explanation for forgetting that the weapon and the ammunition found by the agency were in his car. He testified that in June 1995, before he took stress leave beginning on June 22, 1995, he put the gun and ammunition, for security, into the family car before an outing. He testified further that he forgot that the gun and ammunition were in the car. He attributed his forgetfulness to his illnesses, depression, diverticulosis, and severe gastrointestinal reflux disease, which, shortly after the appellant placed the gun in the car, became so severe that he had to take stress leave while he was undergoing treatment. He indicated that, upon his return to work from stress leave, he and his wife usually drove her van to work, but they had begun driving the car where the gun was found a few days before the search because the car "had been sitting for so long and [the appellant] thought it would do some good to run [the car] and drive in to work."

The appellant also presented a plausible explanation for his statement that he did not construct a secret compartment as a hiding place for the gun that the agency found in his car. The appellant testified that he had constructed a compartment in which to hide valuables and a firearm in his van and that he had told his subordinate about this. He testified also, however, that he had not constructed a hiding place in the car. During the oral reply to the deciding official, he testified that he answered no to a question regarding whether he had constructed the hiding place for the gun that the agency found in his car. He testified that his answer had been accepted without follow-up questions, and thus he had not been able to explain the distinction between the constructed hiding place in his van and the existing hiding place where he placed the gun in his car.

Intent is a state of mind which generally is proven by circumstantial evidence. See Denney v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 191, 193 (1995). Where proof of intent must be derived from circumstantial evidence, all of the evidence regarding intent must be considered. See Johnson v. Department of the Army, 48 MSPR 54, 57 (1991). aff'd, 960 F.2d 156 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). Plausible explanations are to be considered in determining intent. See Deskin v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 MSPR 505, 510 (1997); Kuykendall v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 68 MSPR 314, 327 (1995).

As evidence of intent, the agency presented only the testimony of the deciding official that he did not believe the appellant's statement that he had forgotten that the gun and ammunition were in his car, and that the deciding official believed that the appellant lied when he stated that he did not construct the hiding place in his car where the agency found the gun. The appellant's plausible explanations for why he had forgotten the gun and ammunition, and why he answered no to the deciding official's question regarding whether the appellant had constructed a hiding place for a gun in his car, outweigh the agency's evidence. Thus, we find that the agency failed to prove that the appellant's bringing an unauthorized firearm and ammunition onto the grounds of a federal correctional facility were knowing violations. Additionally, the agency searched the appellant's car based on its reaction to his statements to coworkers as colored by its perception of him as disabled. Further, the deciding official linked the appellant's past ideation of harming his supervisor and the fact that the unauthorized weapon was found in his car in reaching the decision to remove him, and in his belief that the appellant knowingly had the gun and ammunition in his car. Thus, we find that the agency did not prove that it would have taken the same action, removal, based on the sustained charge in the absence of the impermissible motivating factor, discrimination on the basis of disability.

...Title VII provides that, in a dual motive discrimination case, if an appellant establishes that an employment practice was motivated by impermissible and other factors, and an agency shows that it would have taken the same action in the absence of the impermissible factors, the appellant may not be awarded damages, reinstatement, or back pay. See 42 USC §§ 2000e-2(m) and 2000e-5(g)(2)(B). Here, we have found that an employment practice, removal, was motivated by both an impermissible factor, disability discrimination, and another factor, the sustained misconduct of bringing an unauthorized firearm and ammunition onto the agency premises, but that the agency did not prove that it would have taken the same action, removal, in the absence of the impermissible factor. We find, however, that although a finding that the agency would have taken the same action in the absence of the impermissible factor would have disallowed an award of damages, reinstatement, or back pay, the contrary finding does not require the opposite result. Rather, as explained more fully below, the contrary finding merely allows the award of damages, reinstatement, and back pay, if those remedies are appropriate under all of the circumstances of the case.

Douglas, 5 MSPR at 308. See generally 2b Singer, Sutherland Statutory Construction, § 51.02 (5th ed.) (it is axiomatic that statutes should be construed to give effect to every provision, and must be construed to be in harmony, if possible). Further, such a reading of Title VII serves to assure that, where an appellant is guilty of misconduct, he will not avoid discipline because the agency has coupled a charge based on a permissible motive with one that may not be sustained because it has been tainted by an impermissible motive. Thus, we find that the appellant is not entitled to reinstatement and full back pay under the Rehabilitation Act, and we consider the appropriate penalty for the sustained offense under the CSRA.

We note that this rule is applicable only in cases of dual motivation, one permissible and the other impermissible. The rule is inapplicable in a case where an appellant proves that the agency's single motive for taking an adverse action was prohibited discrimination. See Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 79 (1997). Where an appellant proves that the agency's single motive for taking an adverse action was prohibited discrimination, that is, the action was based solely on an impermissible motive, the remedy for the discrimination is to make the appellant whole. See Sublette v. Department of the Army, 68 MSPR 82, 86 (1995). In a case where the Board finds that an impermissible motive, prohibited discrimination, was the sole motive for the agency's action, the Board will reverse the adverse action completely. See Ellshoff, 76 MSPR at 80.

When, as in this case, not all of the agency's charges are sustained, the Board independently and responsibly balances the Douglas factors to determine a reasonable penalty. White, 71 MSPR at 527. Here, the sustained charge is serious. Further, the appellant was on notice of the rule that he violated. As found above, however, the offense was not intentional. Additionally, the firearm found in the appellant's car was well concealed and was not within the secure perimeter of the prison. Further, the appellant had no prior disciplinary record, and his performance was above average. The agency's table of penalties prescribes a range of penalties for the sustained offense from official reprimand to removal for the first offense. There is no evidence of record to show that there was any notoriety associated with the appellant's offense. Finally, the appellant's misconduct resulted from inattention caused by his depression, for which he is undergoing a course of treatment, which had been so successful that he could return to work from stress leave. See Sublette, 68 MSPR at 87. Under these circumstances, we find that his potential for rehabilitation is good. Balancing the Douglas factors, we find that a reasonable penalty for the sustained misconduct is a 30-day suspension.

The Board has held that in order to establish a prima facie case of reprisal under section 2302(b)(9), an appellant must demonstrate that he engaged in an activity protected by that section; that he subsequently was treated in an adverse fashion by the employer; that the deciding official had actual or constructive knowledge that the employee was engaged in the protected activity; and that there is a causal connection between the protected activity and the adverse action.
