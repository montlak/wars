q. VA Disability Rating

VA ratings may be, or they may not be, probative of disability annuity entitlement, depending on the analysis contained in the rating linking the disability with illness or injuries that the appellant asserts prevents her from working, Hunt v. OPM, 105 MSPR 264, 276-77 ¶¶ 36-37, 2007 MSPB 77 (2007):

Finally, the appellant correctly notes that the administrative judge erred by not considering a DVA rating decision that found him to be entitled to "individual unemployability" effective July 1, 2004. The appellant submitted a copy of the rating decision below, and the administrative judge was required to consider it, see Sachs v. Office of Personnel Management, 99 M.S.P.R. 521, ¶¶ 10-12 (2005). Contrary to the appellant's suggestion on review, however, the rating decision is not dispositive of the issues in this case.

In finding that the appellant is entitled to "individual unemployability," the rating decision listed the documentary evidence on which the decision was based, enumerated the appellant's physical impairments, noted that he resigned from his position "due to health reasons," and recited Dr. McLeod's conclusion that the appellant's ability to interact with others and his ability to maintain employment was "considerably impaired." The decision concluded that the appellant is "unable to secure or follow a substantially gainful occupation as a result of service-connected disabilities." However, the decision did not relate any of the appellant's medical conditions to his specific job duties or explain why he was unable to perform his duties. Cf. Johnson, 87 M.S.P.R. 192, ¶ 16 (a physician's conclusion that an employee is disabled is persuasive only if the physician explains how the medical condition affects the employee's specific work requirements). We find that the conclusion of the DVA that the appellant is disabled is outweighed by the evidence of record discussed above. See Sachs, 99 M.S.P.R. 521, ¶ 11 (while the Board must consider an award of benefits by DVA based on the same medical conditions as the appellant's disability retirement application, it may find that this evidence is outweighed by other evidence); cf. Lynum v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 426, ¶ 9 (2006) (where the appellant submitted a SSA decision awarding her disability benefits and that decision relied on a specific medical report that was not in the record, a remand was necessary to afford the appellant the opportunity to submit a copy of that medical report in support of her claim for disability retirement under FERS). Thus, while the administrative judge erred by not considering the DVA rating decision, this error did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights and does not warrant a different outcome in this case. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981).

See Simpkins v. OPM, DC-844E-11-0968-I-1 (NP 8/22/2012) ("Although the Board will consider a DVA disability rating along with all other relevant evidence in disability retirement appeals, DVA disability ratings are based on different criteria than are Federal Employees' Retirement System disability retirement claims, and they are not binding on the Board in disability retirement matters. See Hunt v. Office of Personnel Management, 105 M.S.P.R. 264, ¶ 37 (2007)."); Dunbar v. OPM, 107 MSPR 32, 36 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 230 (2007) (considering an award of benefits by the DVA, but it is not dispositive of entitlement to disability retirement benefits).

VA disability ratings are entitled to weight under the Trevan analysis applied to SSA determinations, Sachs v. OPM, 99 MSPR 521, 524-25, 527 ¶¶ 5, 11-12 (2005):

[T]he appellant submits a letter, dated April 15, 2004, from the Department of Veterans Affairs (DVA) stating that he was entitled to service-connected compensation benefits based on PTSD and major depressive disorder, which the DVA found were 50 percent disabling effective May 27, 2003....

...

In Trevan v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 F.3d 520 (Fed. Cir. 1995), our reviewing court held that "OPM and the Board must consider an award of Social Security disability benefits, and any underlying medical data provided to OPM by the Social Security Administration or the employee, along with any other evidence of disability, in determining entitlement to FERS benefits." Id. at 526 (emphasis omitted). Although the holding in Trevan was based on the court's finding of an express Congressional intent to coordinate FERS and Social Security benefits, id., the Board has determined that an award or a termination of Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP) benefits, and any underlying medical data provided to OPM by OWCP or the employee, are "other evidence of disability," as contemplated by Trevan, that must be considered by OPM and the Board in determining entitlement to FERS disability retirement benefits. See Suter v. Office of Personnel Management, 88 M.S.P.R. 80, ¶ 12 (2001); see also Harpole v. Office of Personnel Management, 98 M.S.P.R. 232, ¶ 14 & n.6 (2005) (recognizing that, in disability retirement cases, the Board has "frequently accepted as new evidence medical reports that were developed in concurrent proceedings conducted in other fora and unavailable to the appellant when the record closed"). We find here that, in determining entitlement to FERS disability retirement benefits, OPM and the Board must similarly consider an award or termination of benefits by DVA based on the same medical conditions as the appellant's disability retirement application, but may find that this evidence is outweighed by other evidence. See Trevan, 69 F.3d at 526; Suter, 88 M.S.P.R. 80, ¶ 12.

In this case, the February 15, 2005 DVA Rating Decision postdates the close of the record on review and thus was not readily available before the record closed. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(i). As neither OPM nor the Board has considered the DVA decision, remand to the administrative judge for reconsideration is appropriate. See McCurdy v. Office of Personnel Management, 96 M.S.P.R. 90, ¶ 9 (2004); Gardner v. Office of Personnel Management, 91 M.S.P.R. 391, ¶ 7 (2002).

Although VA disability ratings are entitled to weight, they are not determinative of FERS disability retirement entitlement, Simpkins v. OPM, 513 Fed. Appx. 950, 954 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP):

First, as the Board noted, different statutes and regulations govern service-connected disability determinations by the DVA. The DVA bases disability ratings on criteria different from those at issue in determining eligibility for retirement benefits under FERS. While we must consider an applicant's established eligibility for other disability benefits, including underlying medical data, such eligibility is not determinative of disability benefits under FERS. Dunbar v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 280 Fed. Appx. 984, 985 (Fed. Cir. 2008) ("Thus, while petitioner here had received...VA disability ratings, these ratings did not bind OPM to find him disabled under FERS."); cf. Trevan v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 69 F.3d 520, 526 (Fed. Cir. 1995) ("There is no indication in either the statute or the legislative history that...determinations made by the Social Security Administration regarding award and continuation of Social Security disability benefits govern OPM's administration of FERS benefits."); Furlong v. United States, 153 Ct. Cl. 557 (1961) ("So far as the action of the Veterans Administration is concerned, we have many times held that the basis for its action is different from the basis upon which a retiring board determines whether or not an officer is entitled to retirement for physical disability, and that a finding by the Veterans Administration, that a plaintiff is disabled, does not show that the finding of the Retiring Board, that he was not entitled to retirement for physical disability, is arbitrary or capricious or even erroneous. After all, jurisdiction to determine a person's entitlement to retirement is vested by law in the Retiring Board, and not in the Veterans Administration." (internal citations omitted)).
