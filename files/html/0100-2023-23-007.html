3. Filing, Acceptance of Settlement for Enforcement

The settlement must be filed with and accepted by the Board if it is to be enforced. Without a settlement agreement entered into the record, the Board may not order compliance in a matter not decided on the merits. See Richardson v. EPA, 5 MSPR 248, 250, 5 MSPB 289 (1981); cf. Estate of Becker v. OPM, 45 MSPR 349, 351 (1990) (Board could not enforce an OPM decision to rescind a reconsideration decision and refund survivor benefits when the merits of the right to an annuity payment were not adjudicated because the appeal was dismissed as moot; the remedy was to reopen the appeal and remand to the region for adjudication: "Absent a settlement agreement that has been entered into the record, the Board may not order compliance in a matter that has not been heard on the merits. Yuni v. Small Business Administration, 38 M.S.P.R. 574, 576 (1988)."). During the pendency of an appeal, the Board does not accept a settlement that evolved from some other proceeding not involving the Board. See Guthrie v. USPS, 41 MSPR 102, 105 (1989) (rejecting an agreement concerning the appellant's status during the notice period and not involving the removal or the merits of the charges against the appellant).

With the caveat that Delorme v. Dept. of Interior, 124 MSPR 123, ¶¶ 9-21, 2017 MSPB 2 (2017), likely obviates the need for a jurisdictional determination, when a PFR is settled, the Board examines the agreement to ensure that the usual criteria are met, i.e., that there is an agreement, that the parties understand its terms, that the agreement is lawful on its face, freely entered into by the parties, and that the subject matter of the appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction. Camon v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 74 (1993) (Table) (IRA appeal); McGann v. DHUD, 56 MSPR 17, 18 (1992); see Payton v. DHS, 300 Fed. Appx. 890, 891 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (settlement of an appeal before the EEOC did not involve an action appealable to the Board; Board did not have jurisdiction over an alleged breach of that settlement). The Board reopened one case to permit the acceptance of an addendum to a settlement that the parties sought to file for purposes of enforcement. See Cruz v. VA, 99 MSPR 450 (2005).[Refer later in this chapter to "Jurisdiction to Accept Settlement: Delorme"; and to PFRs in Chapter 5 under "Settlement; Vacatur of Initial Decision."]

"Before dismissing a matter as settled, the Board must determine whether the parties have entered into a settlement agreement, understand its terms, and intend to have the agreement entered into the record for enforcement by the Board." Cox v. USPS, 79 MSPR 434, 435-36 (1998); Rios v. DHS, AT-0752-16-0619-I-1 (NP 1/3/2017) (when it became clear that the parties did not agree that a settlement would be entered into the Board's record for enforcement, the appeal should have been dismissed as withdrawn rather than dismissed as settled); Linares-Rosado v. USPS, 112 MSPR 599, 605 ¶ 12, 2009 MSPB 219 (2009) (AJ erred by accepting the agency's representation that the settlement resolved the appeal without confirming that the case actually settled).

The Board does not give effect to an attempted rescission of a settlement agreement after the settlement is filed and accepted but before the issuance of the initial decision approving the settlement. Hoyos v. VA, 63 MSPR 532, 534 (1994) (oral settlement during prehearing conference: "[T]he appellant's attorney expressly stated during the prehearing conference that she had authority to settle the case for the appellant. The appellant's attorney further stated that the appellant understood the terms of the settlement agreement, entered into it voluntarily, and wished it to be entered into the record. Moreover, as previously stated, the appellant's representative subsequently sought to withdraw her 'consent' to the agreement, revealing again her authority to act on behalf of the appellant."). But see Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 132, 136 (1988) (Board may effectuate a rescission communicated to the AJ before the initial decision issues accepting the settlement). If settlement is reached after the initial decision was issued and while one or more PFRs are pending, the settlement agreement should be filed with the Board rather than with the regional office. See Littlejohn v. DHUD, 47 MSPR 331, 332 (1991). If an appellant withdraws his appeal on the basis of representations from the AJ, those representations should be stated in the record in the withdrawal. The Board seems disinclined to consider nonrecord evidence offered about what the AJ said. Hazen v. OPM, 20 MSPR 98, 99 (1984). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Misleading or Incomplete Information From Judge," for withdrawal of appeals.]

Once a settlement is accepted into the record for enforcement, the AJ issues an initial decision stating that the agreement was submitted in an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction and is accepted for enforcement. The initial decision then dismisses the appeal "with prejudice," or "as settled"--there are no magic words--and the initial decision informs the parties of their right to file a PFR with the Board or secure judicial review of the initial decision. As a practical matter, the settlement merges into the initial decision and takes the place of a merits disposition, as was indirectly explained, Lachiewicz v. DHS, PH-0752-10-0490-C-1 (NP 8/28/2014):

On review, the appellant also challenges the merits of the rescinded removal action and asks the Board to consider her allegations of discrimination, nepotism, and harassment. However, once a settlement agreement is entered into the record, the original appealable action is withdrawn or dismissed. See King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215, 1218 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The Board retains jurisdiction over a settlement agreement made part of the record pursuant to its power under 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2) to enter and enforce its own orders. King, 59 F.3d at 1218. The Board lacks jurisdiction to hear the appellant's claims regarding the merits of the rescinded removal and her related allegations of discrimination, nepotism, and harassment, in connection with a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement. See generally Walker-King v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 414, ¶ 15 (2013) (the Board lacks jurisdiction to hear pendent discrimination claims in connection with a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement) (citing King, 59 F.3d at 1219; Diehl v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 11 (1999)).

See Horn-Cruder v. DHHS, DC-0752-14-0795-C-3 (NP 12/2/2016) ("Once a settlement agreement is entered into the Board's record, the original appealable action is withdrawn or dismissed. King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215, 1218 (Fed. Cir. 1995). Accordingly, the Board lacks jurisdiction to hear the appellant's claims regarding the merits of her removal, as well as her allegations of discrimination in connection with a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement. See id.; Walker-King v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 414, ¶ 15 (2013) (stating that the Board lacks jurisdiction to hear pendent discrimination claims in connection with a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement).").
