a. Time, Attendance, Travel Record

Failing to return to work after travel for a day, and fabrication to avoid admitting to the conduct, was insufficient to support removal of an employee with 16 years of service, even for an employee in a position of trust. The removal was reduced to a 30-day suspension. Deyoung v. USPS, 7 MSPR 609, 611, 7 MSPB 446 (1981). Removal was reduced to a 60-day suspension for alteration of timecards by an employee to show overtime worked but not authorized: there was no fraudulent intent by the employee, who had a good record. Jensen v. USDA, 18 MSPR 555, 556 (1984); see Fidelibus v. DLA, 24 MSPR 198, 201 (1984) (removal for a conspiracy with other workers to falsify overtime time records); cf. Colbert v. USPS, 93 MSPR 467, 473 ¶ 15 (2003) (setting aside removal based on an allegation that a supervisor deprived employees of earned overtime; the evidence did not support the agency's charges). Removal was mitigated to a 30-day suspension and demotion to a nonsupervisory position for an appellant who falsely claimed $9.50 in parking expenses and improperly claimed about 25 hours of working time. The employee had nine years of good service and lacked sophistication as to matters of travel and time accounting. Drowns v. OPM, 24 MSPR 395, 397 (1984); see Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 186, 202-03 (1994) (mitigating employees' demotions and suspensions to reprimands for false claims on time and attendance reports: the time involved was but two hours; there was no personal gain; the time was claimed with the knowledge and consent of the supervisor; there was no past discipline and relatively long prior service).

An employee's removal was mitigated to a 90-day suspension for falsification of time records for a day. The employee had five years of service, did not occupy a supervisory position, did not have to work independent of supervision, was well-regarded by his coworkers, admitted his misconduct and showed remorse. Perez v. USPS, 48 MSPR 354, 357-58 (1991). Removal was mitigated to 30 days off for a Postal Service worker with 20 years of service and no prior disciplinary record who clocked in at 3:30 A.M., but did not begin working until 4:45 A.M. The interval was spent resting because he was ill. The appellant was remorseful and showed potential for rehabilitation; not all of the agency charges were sustained. Hart v. USPS, 53 MSPR 228, 232 (1992). Christopher v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 92-3382), remanded for consideration of a penalty less than removal of an employee who falsified a time and attendance report by stating he was on jury duty for five and a quarter hours when he was not. The court likened the case to Miguel v. Dept. of Army, 727 F.2d 1081 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (involving petty theft), determining that the agency abused its discretion in selecting the penalty even though the penalty was within the range permitted for the offense.

Removal was mitigated to a 60-day suspension for a food inspector who falsified time records after being warned about being away from the job without permission; a charge of failing to follow orders (also supporting the removal) was not sustained. The appellant was under a much stress. He took unduly long lunch breaks that led to the improper records because he was attempting to sort out a serious domestic problem. The Board remarked that the conduct was serious because the employee could have used annual leave to accomplish his personal business rather than falsifying time and attendance records. Affsa v. USDA, 7 MSPR 446, 454, 7 MSPB 314 (1981). Although the record contained evidence that the Postal Service did not use progressive discipline in fraud cases, removal was mitigated to a 60-day suspension when there was potential for rehabilitation and a long, satisfactory service record before the appellant was determined to have deviated from his assigned route, falsely stated he needed overtime, and misrepresented the time worked. Anderson v. USPS, 29 MSPR 374, 377 (1985).

A supervisor's removal for falsification of time records was mitigated to demotion to a nonsupervisory position based on 12 years of otherwise good performance, the absence of financial gain, good prospects for rehabilitation, and the failure of the agency to use progressive discipline. in McAllister v. USPS, 42 MSPR 658, 663 (1989). Demotion was mitigated to a suspension in similar circumstances. Schoeffler v. USDA, 47 MSPR 80, 90 (1991) (misstatement of the allocation of time spent in travel status on a travel document; the agency did not show that the offense affected the appellant's ability to perform and the agency did not show that the offense caused any specific harm to the agency mission or notoriety) (Schoeffler was modified by Boo v. DHS, 122 MSPR 100, 2014 MSPB 86 (2014), to require an element of material gain in a falsification or misstatement case); see Jackson v. USPS, 48 MSPR 472, 476-77 (1991) (supervisor of mails was demoted to a nonsupervisory job for erroneously clocking in another employee; the appellant had a long and unblemished service record, gained nothing from her actions, and had an excellent performance record). Removal of a supervisor was mitigated to a 30-day suspension when the amount of time improperly claimed was brief (less than an hour); the misconduct occurred but once; the employee was salaried and did not financially benefit from the time entry; his time entry was not directly related to his supervisory duties; another specification was not sustained; the appellant had a long service record; and the deciding official (who was also the investigator of the misconduct) may have been predisposed against the appellant. House v. USPS, 80 MSPR 138, 144 ¶ 15 (1998) (AJ mitigated penalty to a demotion).

Prior good service alone will not necessarily mitigate a penalty for serious timekeeping infractions. Flanagan v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 378, 382-83 (1990). Removal was sustained based on false claims for official travel and AWOL: the misconduct was serious, particularly in light of the high degree of responsibility the appellant held, the considerable independence required by his position, and the national security interest of the agency. Marable v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 622, 632-33 (1992); see Stewart-Maxwell v. USPS, 56 MSPR 265, 275 (1993) (declining to mitigate when specifications of falsification extended over a period of about nine months and an argument was not credited that there was no element of financial gain when a family member benefited from the timekeeping infractions).

Evaluation of de minimis but improper claims must include the nature of the appellant's position. Considering mitigation on remand from the Federal Circuit, the Board said of a small sum improperly claimed for a travel voucher, Gleason v. FDIC, 29 MSPR 330, 331 (1985):

...Appellant's conduct, knowingly submitting a false travel claim--albeit for only a small monetary amount--must be considered a serious breach of the employer-employee relationship. This is particularly true due to the nature of his employment as bank auditor. Such conduct cannot be condoned. A significant penalty is appropriate to both impress upon appellant the seriousness of his conduct and to deter other individuals from making false travel claims. As the court found, however, appellant's twenty-two years of exemplary service with the agency makes the penalty of removal excessive and disproportionate to the offense committed. Under the circumstances, we find that the maximum reasonable penalty for the one sustained charge is a suspension of sixty days. See Harkess v. Bureau of Indian Affairs, 7 MSPB 40, 7 MSPR 114 (1981).
