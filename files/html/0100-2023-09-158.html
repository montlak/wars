b. Imperfect Attempt to Refile; Confusion Caused by Judge

If the appellant's actions demonstrate timely efforts to continue with the appeal, the Board may deem the appeal refiled even if the appellant does not properly label his pleadings or precisely state his intent. The AJ should to recognize the reality of the transaction. Hoehing v. USPS, 83 MSPR 314, 316 ¶ 7 (1999), deemed as a refiling a motion that the DWOP be continued:

It is undisputed that the appellant's representative did not file a document entitled "a refiled appeal" by the September 30, 1996 deadline for doing so and that the appellant's case was not docketed as a refiled appeal until October 27, 1998. However, it is also undisputed that 5 days before the September 30, 1996 filing deadline, the appellant's representative filed a motion entitled "Motion for Continuation of Voluntary Dismissal Without Prejudice" in which she requested that the dismissal without prejudice continue for an additional 180 days. In that motion, the appellant clearly indicated her intent to pursue her appeal. Further, the appellant has shown no intention of abandoning her appeal during the 2-year period between her filing of that motion and the October 27, 1998 redocketing of her appeal. Indeed, according to numerous letters from the appellant's representative to the AJ, as well as the agency's representative's December 1996 letter to the AJ, the AJ continued to assist the parties in their settlement efforts for approximately 2 years after the refiling deadline, i.e., between September 30, 1996, and October 27, 1998. Under these circumstances and contrary to the AJ's finding, we deem the appeal to have been timely refiled as of September 25, 1996, the date the appellant filed her motion for a continuation of the voluntary dismissal without prejudice. Cf. Baskin v. Department of the Treasury, 78 MSPR 156, 158 (1998) (where the appellant filed a cross petition for review within the 90-day period for refiling the appeal set forth in the initial decision and she plainly intended to pursue her appeal, the Board deemed the appeal to have been timely refiled).

A PFR was treated as a refiling following a DWOP when the appellant's intent appeared to be to submit information to the AJ and to request a hearing. The refiling and PFR deadlines were the same. The appeal was remanded to the AJ. Burton v. Dept. of Army, 96 MSPR 350 (2004); cf. Fuller v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-12-0499-I-1 (NP 9/11/2013) (treating pro se appellant's PFR as a refiling following DWOP of an indefinite suspension appeal).

If the AJ contributes to appellant's confusion concerning the process of continuing an appeal dismissed without prejudice, reasonable delay will likely be excused by the Board, as it explained in Gilmore v. USPS, 85 MSPR 536, 539-40 ¶¶ 10, 12 (2000), involving a constructive suspension that was reversed with an AJ's restoration order, followed closely by a removal appeal that was DWOP'd pending a Board decision on the constructive suspension:

[T]he administrative judge in the constructive suspension appeal erroneously ordered on August 21, 1998, that the appellant be restored to duty, effective August 6, 1997, although she knew at the time of her order that the appellant had been removed, effective April 13, 1998. We agree, therefore, with the appellant's reassertion on review that it was reasonable for him and his representative to believe that a removal following closely on the heels of a related suspension must automatically fall upon a reversal of the suspension, when the Board final decision reversing the suspension expressly ordered "restor[ation]." Thus, we find that the administrative judge's error was the cause of the appellant's 31-day delay from October 20, 1998, until November 19, 1998, before refiling the removal appeal. See DeVitto, 64 MSPR at 356.

In sum, for all of these reasons-the appellant's initial filing of a timely removal appeal; his reasonable belief, based on the initial decision in the constructive suspension appeal, that he would be reinstated and did not need to refile the removal appeal following the dismissal without prejudice; his timely filing of a petition for enforcement seeking reinstatement; and his refiling of his removal appeal just two days after the administrative judge clarified that the removal and constructive suspension were two separate actions--there simply is no indication that the appellant intended to abandon his challenge to the removal. Cf. Toombs v. Department of the Army, 69 MSPR 78, 81-82 (1995). Thus, we find that the appellant has shown good cause for his untimely refiling. Further, we find that that agency has not shown that it would be prejudiced by a waiver of the time limit. See Alonzo, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR at 184. Therefore, we waive the time limit for refiling this appeal. See Shiflett v. U.S. Postal Service, 839 F.2d 669, 670-74 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (the Board may grant or deny the waiver of a time limit for filing an appeal, in the interest of justice, after considering all the facts and circumstances of a particular case).

Typographical errors in refiling orders that, read literally, lead to unrealistic conditions on refiling have been disregarded, particularly when there is no evidence of reliance on the error. See Shenwick v. Dept. of State, 90 MSPR 192, 195 n.1 (2001). The Board may decide that the conditions describing the time for refiling an appeal are sufficiently confusing that the appeal should be processed without determining that the AJ erred in setting the conditions or the appellant erred in meeting them. In Whitfield v. Dept. of Air Force, 79 MSPR 651 (1998), the appeal was dismissed pending the appellant's receipt of an OPM decision on a disability retirement application. The AJ assumed the appellant would file the application for retirement. She did not, but she did refile the appeal after she thought the time for filing a disability retirement application expired. Although the Board decided that the AJ acted reasonably by setting a refiling date based on the date that the appellant would have exhausted her OPM administrative remedies, and although the appellant should have sought clarification from the AJ concerning her filing deadline, the appellant's refiling was technically timely because it was not later than the deadline provided: 30 days following denial of her reconsideration request to OPM. The Board noted that the AJ could have established an alternative refiling date if the appellant did not file a retirement application or if OPM did not issue a reconsideration decision within a reasonable time. Whitfield also noted the agency did not allege prejudice through delay resulting from the appellant's failure to file a retirement application with OPM. See Moore v. DOD, 84 MSPR 341, 342 (1999) (excusing a refiling delay of six days when the AJ issued a confusing set of orders both dismissing the appeal and setting the hearing date).

In Hall v. Dept. of Interior, 90 MSPR 32, 35 ¶¶ 6-7 (2001), the AJ dismissed an appeal without prejudice to its refiling within 35 days, and the AJ also ruled that she would refile the appeal, sua sponte, no later than the finality date of the initial DWOP decision. The AJ failed to refile the appeal on time, but she essentially waived the refiling date, held a hearing on the case, and issued an initial decision on the merits. The Board declared that the AJ erred by waiving a refiling deadline that passed, reopened the case, and affirmed the initial decision:

The circumstances in this appeal present a unique situation. The administrative judge dismissed the appellant's initial appeal without prejudice to its refiling, but in the same order, she stated that she would refile the appeal. Unfortunately, the administrative judge failed to refile the appeal before the deadline she had established, and the initial decision became final on July 26, 1999. Although an administrative judge may waive any deadline that is not statutorily imposed, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.12, 1201.41, an administrative judge cannot reopen an appeal in which there is a final Board decision, 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.112, 1201.118; Moriarty v. Rhode Island Air National Guard, 56 M.S.P.R. 144, 147 (1992). Thus, the administrative judge erred by refiling the appeal after the initial decision had become final.

The Board has the statutory and regulatory authority to reopen, on its own motion, an appeal in which a final decision has been rendered. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(e)(1)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.118. The Board exercises its discretionary authority to reopen an appeal only in extraordinary circumstances. Moriarty, 56 MSPR at 148. We believe that such extraordinary circumstances exist in the present appeal. The administrative judge bears complete responsibility for failing to timely refile the appeal. Moreover, because the deadline for refiling the appeal was also the finality date of the initial decision, once it was clear to the appellant that the administrative judge had not refilled the appeal, it was too late for him to refile the appeal himself or to file a petition for review. It would be against the interests of justice to deny the appellant his right to appeal his removal to the Board because of an error made by the administrative judge; therefore, we would have granted a request to reopen this appeal and remanded the appeal for a hearing. Since the administrative judge conducted a hearing after refiling the appeal, neither party's substantive rights were harmed by the administrative judge's error.

The challenge to an initial decision dismissing a refiled appeal as untimely is through petition for review to the Board. "Administrative judges lack the authority to reopen or reinstate appeals in which there has been a final Board decision; that authority is reserved to the Board." Pradier v. USPS, 113 MSPR 495, 498 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 65 (2010).
