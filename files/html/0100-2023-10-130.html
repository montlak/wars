2. Obligation to Request Witness

The party seeking to have a witness (including the appellant) testify at the Board hearing must request approval for that witness in that party's prehearing submission. If the appellant wants to testify, the appellant must list himself as a witness. Although an AJ is authorized to rule on offers of proof and receive relevant evidence, the AJ need not advise an appellant on the necessity to secure witnesses or to call on his own the witnesses needed to present the appellant's case. Di Biase v. Dept. of Energy, 15 MSPR 180, 182 (1983). To challenge rejection of a witness by an AJ, the witness must be requested. McKenzie v. District of Columbia Gov't, 5 MSPR 457, 458 n.2, 5 MSPB 460 (1981). If the AJ wants a witness list--generally required by a prehearing order from the AJ--and the employee fails to provide one, the appellant will not be heard on PFR that his adjudication was prejudiced for lack of a witness. See Taylor v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 471, 474 (1990); cf. Mitchell v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 24, 26 (1987) (appellant was responsible for the failure of her representative properly to file a witness list; other objections were waived because at the hearing, without the list, the only witness requested for the appellant was the appellant, who was permitted to testify; rejected witnesses were not shown to have relevant information).

The appellant has no complaint for lack of witnesses if he does not follow the Board's procedures to secure them at a hearing. It is not the job of the AJ to do this for him. Yanopoulos v. Dept. of Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 470-71 (Fed. Cir. 1986); see Hauges v. USPS, 40 MSPR 407, 409 (1989) ("[A]lthough appellant had ample opportunity to request assistance securing witnesses, he failed to do so."); Hernandez v. USPS, 12 MSPR 275, 276-77 (1982) (by failing to move for a subpoena to compel the attendance of a witness at the hearing, and by failing to request that the record be kept open after the hearing to receive testimony of the witness, the appellant waived the right to submit the evidence); Pellum v. VA, 12 MSPR 208, 210-11 (1982) ("Since appellant did not request the assistance of the presiding official in securing witnesses, she cannot now be heard to complain that witnesses were absent through the fault of another."); Armorer v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 103, 105 (1982) (the appellant did not request the AJ's assistance in securing witnesses when he directed his request to the agency with a copy to the AJ; a formal request for assistance should have been made to the AJ).

Board rules, 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022), contemplate that witnesses who are employees of federal agencies other than the respondent will appear based on notice of approval of the witness request and without a subpoena. The appellant must attempt through subpoena or other means to secure the presence of witnesses he desires to examine at the hearing. Hanley v. GSA, 829 F.2d 23, 26 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Gildersleeve v. USPS, 4 MSPR 409, 411 (1980) (appellant was informed of the procedure for subpoenaing a witness). There is no error if the appellant fails to seek a subpoena for a desired witness who is not a federal employee at the time of the hearing. See May v. ICC, 20 MSPR 557, 560 (1984).

There is no adjudicative error if the appellant fails to call a witness because the agency advises him he has no right to do so; the Board's hearing notices gave the appellant sufficient and correct information about his right to call witnesses. Reed v. USPS, 33 MSPR 9, 12 (1987). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Duty of Judge to Inform an Appellant of Governing Law; Pro Se Appellant; Liberal Construction of Pleadings."]

If an agency does not call witnesses but assumes the appellant will call those witnesses in his case and he does not, the agency cannot complain of prejudice if those witnesses do not testify. That includes the appellant, who is sitting in the hearing room. If the appellant listed himself as a witness and then decides not to testify, the AJ may decline to permit the agency to call the appellant as a witness if the agency did not list the appellant as a witness. The agency should ensure that the witnesses are available in its own case rather than relying upon the appellant. Hansen v. DLA, 10 MSPR 444, 447 (1982). The appellant must protect his right to call witnesses as well, Ingram v. VA, DA-0752-12-0386-I-2 (NP 9/26/2013):

Although the appellant asserts on review that the administrative judge would not allow him to call his supervisor as a witness, we note that the appellant did not list this individual as a witness in his prehearing submission. The agency listed her in its prehearing submission, and it asserted that she would testify as a rebuttal witness and about "other relevant matters." Nevertheless, the agency did not call this witness during the hearing, and our review of the hearing record, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.53, indicates that, after the appellant rested his case, the administrative judge specifically commented on the agency's failure to do so. The appellant's representative did not lodge an objection at that time, nor did he attempt to call the witness himself, even though the appellant had reserved the right to call any of the agency's witnesses to which he did not object. Thus, the record does not support the appellant's assertion that the administrative judge denied him this witness, and, even if the administrative judge had done so, the appellant's failure to timely object to rulings on witnesses precludes him from doing so on petition for review. See Tarpley v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 579, 581 (1988).

If a witness called by the appellant does not appear, the agency should at least object at the hearing and request another means to secure the testimony if it expected to elicit information from that witness. Sanders v. EEOC, 38 MSPR 309, 312-13 (1988). (It is not stated in Sanders why the agency did not forfeit the right to call the witness by not listing him in the agency's prehearing submission.) The same for the appellant: the agency is not obliged to make employees available at the hearing until their presence is directed by the AJ. Cf. SSA v. Glover, 23 MSPR 57, 66 (1984) (agency properly informed employees that they need not appear for depositions that were not noticed in accordance with the Board's discovery regulations); Oberman v. USPS, 13 MSPR 218, 220 (1982) (same result as Glover). There is no statutory or regulatory requirement that an AJ order the appearance of a witness who has been approved to testify if the party calling her changes its mind and the opposing party does not specifically move to compel her appearance. Dodson v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 386, 390 (1987). Note that the appellant must list himself as a witness in the prehearing submission if the appellant desires to testify.

Assuming a request for a witness, the request must not be waived, Walton v. TVA, 48 MSPR 462, 466-67 (1991):

...The record reflects that the administrative judge approved Mr. Sutherland as a witness for the appellant during...[a] conference call. During a subsequent conference call...the agency advised that Mr. Sutherland would be unable to testify at the September 5, 1990, hearing due to a previously scheduled vacation. The administrative judge indicated during the conference call that, at the conclusion of the hearing, the appellant could renew his request for Mr. Sutherland's testimony, that the substance of it could conceivably be agreed to by means of a stipulation at that time, and that, if not, the testimony might be secured through another means, such as deposition.

At the close of the hearing, after the appellant and his witnesses had testified, the administrative judge asked if the appellant had any further witnesses to present, and he replied "no additional witnesses." Having failed to preserve the opportunity which the administrative judge afforded him to secure the testimony of Mr. Sutherland through other available means, the appellant cannot now be heard to complain about the lack of such testimony.... Moreover, the appellant has not indicated the nature of Mr. Sutherland's expected testimony and thus has not shown that the administrative judge's alleged error denigrated his substantive rights.

See Curry v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 301, 303-04 (1987) (appellant waived requests for witnesses who were released, without objection, following their testimony during an agency case, by failing to state, in response to a question from the AJ, that witnesses other than the appellant were desired during the appellant's case); Nestro v. USPS, 34 MSPR 242, 246 (1987) ("Having waived the appearance of the deciding official below, appellant cannot raise the issue in his petition for review."); Duncan v. USPS, 9 MSPR 318, 320 (1981) ("[A]ppellant, at the hearing, declined to request continuance of the hearing to allow the other witnesses to testify, even though the presiding official specifically asked him if he wished to do so at that time.").

Witnesses can become unavailable between the time they are approved as witnesses and the date of the hearing. A party should request, or the AJ may offer, an alternative means of securing their testimony, e.g., a recorded conference call. Should that procedure be adopted and the party seeking the witness later withdraws the request for his testimony, the ability to secure that witness is waived. If the AJ finds the testimony of a witness irrelevant, but offers the appellant the opportunity to submit a written statement from that individual, the AJ's ruling cannot be challenged on PFR if the party does not introduce the statement of the witness. See Flanagan v. Dept. of Army, 48 MSPR 430, 433 (1991).
