1. Yorkshire

The doctrinal confusion was in part eliminated by an appellate decision in Yorkshire v. MSPB, 746 F.2d 1454 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The appellant, a nursing assistant at a VA medical center, was discharged for three counts of verbal and physical abuse of one patient. An investigation by a VA employee turned up a witness, a coworker, who said she saw appellant slap and verbally demean the patient; a second witness, another occupant of the hospital room used by the patient, said he also saw the appellant strike the patient. During the hearing, the co-occupant of the room recanted his earlier statement and admitted that he did not see the patient struck, but heard a slapping sound that he presumed to be the appellant striking the patient. The other witness, the coworker, presented testimony that the AJ found to be self-contradictory, often incoherent, and incredible. The coworker was determined to be prone to fabricating outlandish tales and was, at the time of the hearing, under a psychiatrist's care. The AJ concluded that the slapping sound heard by the co-occupant of the room resulted from appellant's legitimate efforts to restrain the patient, who was undergoing psychiatric treatment. The appellant was reinstated with back pay. VA did not appeal the initial decision.

Yorkshire applied for counsel fees, claiming substantial innocence. VA did not oppose the motion. Between the time the initial decision was issued and the counsel fee petition was adjudicated, the AJ who heard the removal case left the Board's employment. Another AJ was assigned to decide the fee motion. The newly-appointed AJ declared in his decision that Yorkshire was not substantially innocent of the charges--apparently basing his opinion on the fact that the initial VA investigation developed enough information to warrant charges against Yorkshire--and denied the fee petition. On PFR the Board ignored the legal issue presented and dismissed the petition as unworthy with a standard, though meaningless, recitation of the Board's regulatory review standard.

Matters advanced to the Federal Circuit. Initially, the court decided that the second AJ had no basis for finding that the appellant was not substantially innocent--a finding at odds with the controlling disposition of the case by the AJ who reinstated Yorkshire. The second AJ improperly made this determination by examining evidence accumulated by the agency prior to the hearing. The court determined that the decision on fees is not the Board's opportunity to reexamine and restructure the case against the appellant. The fee decision is to be based on the underlying decision on the merits and nothing else. Parenthetically, it should be noted that the Board has since applied the rule explained in Yorkshire: that it is the "first" initial decision that contains the factual analysis controlling disposition of the fee request. The fee proceeding is not an opportunity to reconstruct the factual basis of the case. Mitchum v. TVA, 29 MSPR 682, 685 (1986) (citing Yow v. Dept. of Treasury, 28 MSPR 411, 413 (1985)). The court then moved on to the pivotal issue: the meaning of the substantial innocence test.

Yorkshire determined that it was not the evidence gathered by the agency prior to the hearing that determined substantial innocence; rather, it was the holding after the hearing that was determinative of the innocence of the employee--a result consistent with the purpose of a hearing and the right to confront adverse witnesses. Commenting on the Board's Allen analysis and focusing particularly on the second entitlement standard ("clearly without merit," "wholly unfounded," and "substantially innocent") and on the fifth standard (the agency "knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits" when it brought the action), the court determined, in an opinion written for the court by the late Judge Oscar Davis, 746 F.2d at 1456-58:

The parties' briefs on the standards contained in these categories reveal two points: first, there is confusion as to the difference between category 2 and category 5; second, a concern exists (particularly on the Board's part) that the substantially innocent standard is merely a restatement of the prerequisite that the employee prevail. We turn to these matters.

Category 5 requires a sensitive evaluation of the agency's original action, i.e., whether the agency "knew or should have known" that it would not prevail on the appeal. In analyzing a request for fees under category 5, therefore, the appropriate procedure is to appraise the agency's decision to carry through the action against the employee. If the agency never possessed trustworthy, admissible evidence, or if the agency was negligent in its conduct of the investigation, then the "agency knew or should have known" not to take the action. Attorney's fees are then warranted in the interest of justice.

Category 2, on the other hand, refers to the result of the case in the Board, not to the evidence and information available prior to the hearing.... It is plain that petitioner prevailed on all the charges against him, and that the original presiding official considered (on the basis of the hearing record) that the VA's charges were "wholly unfounded"--based on incredible or unspecific evidence fully countered by appellant.

The Government's arguments and the MSPB's fee decision muddy the waters by seeking to excuse the VA, as if the only fee criteria were contained in category 5. For instance, it is argued that Mr. Hadley's testimony that he had not actually seen petitioner strike Mr. Young caught the VA by surprise.... But all that is irrelevant under the category 2 standard of "substantial innocence." The end result here was that Mr. Hadley and Nurse Davis failed to corroborate the VA's charges, according to the final decision of the presiding official who heard their testimony.

The Government, in sum, seeks to defend the Board and the VA by pointing out that further agency investigation would not have revealed in advance the surprises in store at the hearing. Petitioner enters this debate with charges that the VA mishandled the investigation. This entire discussion, however, rests of a faulty premise, at least insofar as category 2 is concerned. That aspect of the attorney's fee provision in the Reform Act is not punitive; rather, it is an effort to minimize the burden an unsubstantiated accusation places upon innocent employees.... When dealing with the "substantially innocent" standard for award of attorney's fees, the question of an agency's original fault need never arise.

We are told that this definition of the "substantially innocent" standard will lead to an award of attorney's fees for every prevailing employee. As Allen makes emphatically clear, this is not the law. 2 MSPB at 587. For one thing, the employee must prevail on substantially all the charges to be found "substantially innocent." In Sterner, for example, we affirmed the Board's denial of fees to an employee who confessed to two of the five charges against him and successfully defended the other three--on the ground that he was not "substantially innocent" despite our recognition that the employee prevailed before the Board on every contested charge, 711 F.2d at 1567-69. As we stated in Sterner, "[T]he extent of a party's victory is clearly relevant generally to the justice of his cause." Id. at 1567.[9]

[9] See also, Allen, supra, at 593 n.35: "In making such [an award] determination, the presiding official should examine the degree of fault on the employee's part and the existence of any reasonable basis for the agency's action."

Yorkshire clarified the meaning of the "substantial innocence" standard, an adjudicatory factor significant to employees vindicated of serious misconduct charges. The Federal Circuit since reaffirmed the principle that the counsel fee provisions of the Reform Act are not penalties, but rather means for providing the compensation necessary to make the wronged employee whole. See Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 845 F.2d 976 (Fed. Cir. 1988).

There are other aspects of the Yorkshire case worth noting: the fees involved in the Board litigation (through the point of Yorkshire's reinstatement) were about $3,600; after the case was argued to the court, a supplementary fee petition was submitted for about the same amount. This means that the VA roughly doubled its expense by failing to pay or settle the attorney fee claim. Much Board litigation is more costly than in Yorkshire, and the consequences of failing to reach reasonable fee settlements can be costly to agencies. Another point should be made. VA originally failed to oppose the fee motion, apparently trusting the AJ to deny the petition. VA did oppose the administrative PFR. The Justice Department opposed the case in court. During the oral argument of the case, Judge Davis asked government counsel whether he thought it was significant that the VA had not opposed the initial fee motion. Government counsel said that it was irrelevant whether the agency opposed the fee motion. Judge Davis made an interesting point in reply to government counsel. He said that although the failure to oppose the fee petition was not controlling, it was certainly relevant. This is a point agencies should take to heart: if they will not bother to oppose a petition, they must recognize that the value of a subsequent litigating position may be undermined. In most agencies and in most courts, an uncontested motion is deemed conceded and is normally granted. The Board does not operate this way with respect to fee motions--a result that makes it appear less impartial than it might otherwise desire--but other adjudicators take note of how agencies react to issues before the Board. Positions are best maintained that are early presented and consistently maintained.

Although Yorkshire reoriented fee analysis to results rather than motivation, it is significant that the court's opinion was graced with footnote 9, drawing attention to the component of Allen requiring a balancing of employee fault and any reasonable basis for the agency action. A year later, the Federal Circuit issued its decision in Wise v. MSPB, 780 F.2d 997 (Fed. Cir. 1985), involving further elucidation of the elusive "substantial innocence" test. Judge Davis was on the Wise panel. The decision in that case included footnote 4, repeating the reference to the Allen test balancing employee fault and any reasonable basis for the agency action.
