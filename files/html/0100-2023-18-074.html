1. Procedure to Invoke Shortened Notice

An agency is not required to provide a hearing to an employee before invoking the crime exception, unless there is a labor contract or regulatory requirement. Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 310, 312 (1983). But the adverse action notice invoking the crime provision must be specific. If an agency does not both specifically invoke the crime exception in suspending an appellant during the 30-day notice period for his removal and refer to the charge against the appellant as a crime allowing a sentence of imprisonment, the suspension violates 5 USC 7513 and constitutes harmful error under § 7701(c)(2)(A). Henscheid v. USPS, 14 MSPR 422, 426 (1983); Ware v. Dept. of Education, 12 MSPR 666, 668 n.5 (1982) ("The notice of the proposed suspension did not charge appellant with battery, nor did it refer in any way to a 'crime.'"); cf. Stewart v. USPS, 14 MSPR 102, 105-06 (1982) (sustaining removal but setting aside the suspension during the notice period: "In this case, the agency did not invoke the crime provisions of § 7513(b)(1). Instead, it based the suspension on the grounds that appellant's continuation in active duty status might 'result in damages to government property, loss of mail or funds, or be detrimental to the interests of the government or injurious to [appellant], [his] fellow workers or the general public.' Therefore, we find that appellant's emergency suspension was effected in violation of § 7513(b)(1) and therefore constituted harmful error under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(r)(2)(A). See Romero v. Department of the Army, 9 MSPB 330, 331 (1982).").

The constitutionality of the shortened notice provisions of § 7513(b)(1) was considered in Mack v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 288, 290 (1985):

Appellant also contends that the "crime provision" of 5 USC 7513(b)(1) violates due process. In support of this contention, appellant argues that the Supreme Court's recent decision in Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 105 S. Ct. 1487 (1985), requires that public employees be given a pre-termination hearing in all cases. He further argues that the failure of the agency to hold a pre-termination hearing was prejudicial per se, as it was a violation of his right to due process.

In Loudermill, the Court held, "the tenured public employee is entitled to oral or written notice of the charges against him, an explanation of the employer's evidence, and an opportunity to present his side of the story."... The Court noted that a full evidentiary hearing was not required. 5 USC 7513 provides all of the procedural protections set forth in the Court's opinion in Loudermill. Appellant was afforded advance written notice of the charges against him, an opportunity to review the agency's evidence and an opportunity to respond both orally and in writing. Thus, appellant's argument that he was denied due process is without merit.

Hawkins v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 501, 503-04 (1991), observed:

...Although the agency's use of the crime provision to shorten the appellant's notice period where it did not charge him with any crime was improper, the record indicates that he was provided advance notice of the charge against him and an explanation of the agency's evidence, and that he submitted oral and written replies. He failed to show that the shortening of the notice period affected his ability to present his case....

Without a showing of harmful error negating the appellant's reply rights, the remedy under Hawkins for an improperly shortened notice period was that "the agency shall amend its records so as to retroactively afford the appellant the full 30-day advance notice period with pay and benefits." Id. at 504. See Kenny v. United States, 145 F. Supp. 898 (Ct. Cl. 1956) (if a shortened notice period was improperly imposed, the employee is entitled to recover his salary for the time improperly deducted from the statutory notice period). As a procedural condition to its validity, the agency's notice to the employee should state that the paid period of advance notice for the proposed adverse action is being precluded through reliance upon the agency's belief that the appellant engaged in activity that could support criminal sanctions, Cox-Vaughn v. USPS, 100 MSPR 246, 254-256 ¶¶ 18-22 (2005):

We have indicated above that the appellant was suspended from her position without pay from the time she received notice that the agency was considering removing her up through the time when she was actually removed. In effect, therefore, she was suspended during the entire notice period of her removal. The Board has held, however, that the statutory provisions governing adverse actions do not envision a suspension of the employee during the notice period of an adverse action. Clark v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 M.S.P.R. 634, 639 (1992); Anderson v. U.S. Postal Service, 24 M.S.P.R. 488, 491 (1984), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1060 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table). It also has indicated that the law does not permit an agency to place an employee on an emergency suspension during the notice period of a removal and then effect a removal for the same act of misconduct. See Clark, 52 M.S.P.R. at 639. The relevant statutory provisions do permit a shortened notice period in certain kinds of situations in which an employee must be separated from the premises quickly. See 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(1); Clark, 52 M.S.P.R. at 639. An agency may rely on this authority, however, only when there is reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime for which a period of imprisonment may be imposed. 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(1); Clark, 52 M.S.P.R. at 639.

In this case, the administrative judge found that the agency's investigation of the appellant's alleged misconduct had led to evidence--including the appellant's own admission that she had been gambling during the time in question--that "gave rise to reasonable cause for the agency to believe the appellant had defrauded the government of her pay for at least four hours of time...." He accordingly concluded that the agency had properly invoked 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(1). The appellant challenges this finding in her petition for review, asserting that no crime was committed.

The December 20 notice of the appellant's suspension does not indicate that the reason the agency had provided the appellant with less than the required 30 days' notice of the action was that there was probable cause to believe that she had committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment could be imposed. Instead, the notice indicates that the agency was relying on an entirely different reason. That is, it indicates that the agency suspended the appellant because a collective bargaining agreement it believed was applicable provided that it could do so when the allegation of misconduct against the employee "involve[d] intoxication ([u]se of drugs or alcohol), pilferage, or failure to observe safety rules and regulations, or in cases where retaining the employee on duty [might] result in loss of mail or funds." Statutory requirements, however, take precedence over the terms of a collective bargaining agreement. White v. U.S. Postal Service, 46 M.S.P.R. 48, 53 (1990). Because the appellant was a preference eligible to whom chapter 75 applies, the only legal basis on which the agency could have suspended the appellant during the notice period of her removal was the crime provision of 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(1).

As indicated above, the agency did not invoke 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(1) in the December 20 notice. In fact, there is no indication in the record that it even referred to that provision at any time prior to the hearing in this case; and, even at the hearing, the agency failed to prove that it had held a reasonable belief, at the time of the suspension, that the appellant had committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment could be imposed. The inadequate nature of the agency's presentation on that subject is reflected in the nonspecific nature of the administrative judge's findings. That is, the administrative judge did not specifically identify any criminal provision the appellant might have violated; he provided no explanation of his basis for concluding that a sentence of imprisonment could have been imposed for such a violation; and he did not indicate when the agency allegedly concluded that the appellant had committed the violation.

For the reasons stated above, we find that the appellant's suspension was not effected in accordance with the crime exception. The agency's placement of the appellant in a nonduty, nonpay status during the notice period of her removal therefore is not in accordance with law and cannot be sustained. See Clark, 52 M.S.P.R. at 639; see also 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(C) (an agency action may not be sustained if the appellant shows that it was not in accordance with law); Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 683-84 (1991) (an appealable action should be reversed as being "not in accordance with law" under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(C) if the agency's action is unlawful in its entirety, i.e., if there is no legal authority for the action).
