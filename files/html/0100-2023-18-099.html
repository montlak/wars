a. Information From Witness

A deciding official may receive, consider, and weigh evidence from ex parte sources, subject to constitutional due process requirements of fair notice to the appellant of the information obtained and an opportunity to the appellant to respond to that information. In Amar v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 505, 510-11, 513-15 ¶¶ 12-15, 20-25 (2001), the oral reply was taken by an official, Greenstein, who assured the employee that the decision would be made on the basis of information then available to the employee. After the reply, Greenstein conducted some interviews and she included information from these interviews in her report to the deciding official, Tapley, who relied on the additional information obtained through the interviews. The employee was not on notice of the additional information considered by the deciding official. The agency's failure to provide to the employee the new and material information obtained during the post-reply interviews was cause sufficient to set aside the ensuing adverse action for lack of due process when the new information was significant as to the appellant's credibility and when the appellant's credibility was critical to resolution of the underlying charge. Relying on Stone v. FDIC, 179 F.3d 1368 (Fed. Cir. 1999), Amar determined:

...In this case...the agency's designated official, Greenstein, conducted a further investigation after hearing the appellant's oral reply, and the agency did not afford the appellant the opportunity to respond to the additional evidence Greenstein uncovered during her investigation.

In Stone, 179 F.3d at 1376, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit found that "[T]he introduction of new and material information by means of ex parte communications to the deciding official undermines the [tenured] public employee's constitutional due process guarantee of notice (both of the charges and of the employer's evidence) and the opportunity to respond." The court set forth the test for deciding whether new and material information that has been received by the deciding official through ex parte communications has occasioned a due process violation. The court specifically identified three factors "[a]mong the factors" that the Board should consider: "whether the ex parte communication merely introduces 'cumulative' information or new information; whether the employee knew of the error and had a chance to respond to it; and whether the ex parte communications were of the type likely to result in undue pressure upon the deciding official to rule in a particular manner." Stone, 179 F.3d at 1377. The court determined that "[u]timately, the inquiry of the Board is whether the ex parte communication is so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that no employee can fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances." Id.

In this case, the ex parte September 16, 1999 Greenstein memorandum introduced new information into the decision-making process. Arguably, the statements that Orozco and Meyer made to Greenstein were "cumulative" because Greenstein described these statements as merely confirming their earlier accounts. Evans's statement, however, was not cumulative. Although Greenstein described his statement as corroborating statements made by Orozco and Meyer, the agency never notified the appellant that it intended to rely on evidence provided by Evans. See Stone, 179 F.3d at 1376 (the constitutional due process guarantee of notice applies to both the charges and the employer's evidence). In addition, as more fully described below, the fact that Greenstein indicated that the appellant's own representative confirmed the statements of the agency managers proved to be an important factor in Tapley's assessment of the appellant's credibility. Therefore, although the factual information that Evans purportedly provided to Greenstein may have been cumulative, we conclude that, at a minimum, Greenstein's statements concerning the fact that Evans corroborated the statements of Orozco and Meyer was new information under Stone.

With regard to the second Stone factor, i.e., whether the employee knew of the error and had a chance to respond to it, the transcript of the appellant's oral reply makes it clear that the appellant knew that Greenstein would make a recommendation to Tapley based on the record evidence. However, no evidence suggests the appellant knew that Greenstein intended to investigate the matter further following the appellant's reply. In addition, no evidence suggests that the agency afforded the appellant an opportunity to respond to the additional evidence Greenstein uncovered during her investigation....

...

With regard to the third Stone factor, Greenstein's ex parte communication was of a type likely to result in undue pressure upon Tapley to rule in a particular manner. First, the fact that the ex parte communication came from a trusted source likely resulted in undue influence upon Tapley. Greenstein, the source of the ex parte communication, was the Acting Assistant Chief of the Examination Division and the agency's designated official for hearing the appellant's oral reply. Tapley clearly trusted Greenstein's judgment. She testified on both direct and cross-examination that she relied chiefly on Greenstein's memorandum to determine the credibility of Orozco, Meyer, and the appellant, and she spoke approvingly of Greenstein for taking the "extra step" of interviewing the appellant's union steward.

Second, the contents of Greenstein's communication were of a type likely to unduly influence the decision-maker. Although one could argue that the statement Greenstein attributed to Evans did nothing more than corroborate the statements of Orozco and Meyer, Tapley may also have given undue weight to Evans's purported statement....

Furthermore, under the agency's table of penalties, the charge that the appellant failed to properly file his 1993 income tax return carried a maximum penalty of removal only if the appellant's unsubstantiated or overstated deductions were attributed to more than negligence or carelessness.... The appellant consistently claimed that the errors on his tax return were unintentional and resulted either from his misunderstanding of the law or mistakes made by the tax preparer upon whose professional judgment he relied. Tapley's assessment of the credibility of the appellant was therefore critical to her decision whether he engaged in reckless or intentional misconduct and, if so, whether to remove him for it.

Likewise, the charge of making false statements in matters of official interest carried a maximum penalty of removal under the table of penalties only if the appellant made intentionally false or misleading statements.... Tapley's assessment of the credibility of Orozco, Meyer, and the appellant was therefore critical to her determination of whether he made intentionally false statements and, if so, whether to remove him for it.

Finally, the charge of failing to obtain proper approval for outside employment warranted discipline under the table of penalties only if the appellant did not request approval. The appellant claimed in a supplementary written explanation to his oral reply that he did turn in an outside employment request form to his former manager. Therefore, Tapley's assessment of the appellant's credibility was critical to her decision whether to sustain that charge. Thus, to the extent that Tapley's assessment of the appellant's credibility relied on the fact that Greenstein reported that Evans corroborated the statements of Orozco and Meyer, Greenstein's ex parte communication may have affected Tapley's penalty determination as well as her decision to sustain the charges. See Stone, 179 F.3d at 1376 ("the employee's response is essential not only to the issue of whether the allegations are true, but also with regard to whether the level of penalty to be imposed is appropriate").

Under these circumstances, the agency's failure to give the appellant notice of and the opportunity to respond to Greenstein's ex parte September 16, 1999 communication with the deciding official prior to the decision constituted a due process violation. Greenstein's memorandum to Tapley was an ex parte communication containing new and material information that was so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that the appellant cannot fairly be deprived of his property interest in continued employment with the IRS. See Stone, 179 F.3d at 1377. Therefore, the removal action cannot be sustained, and the appellant "is entitled to a new constitutionally correct removal procedure." Id.

See Carter v. DOJ, CH-0752-10-0224-I-1 (NP 8/23/2011) ("Although some of the factual information in Bowling's affidavit may be cumulative to other information known to and addressed by the appellant to the deciding official, the Board has held that an affidavit such as Bowling's may still be 'new' and not cumulative under Stone if it corroborates contested material evidence and is an important factor regarding credibility. See Amar, 89 M.S.P.R. 505, ¶ 14.").

In Hull v. Dept. of Air Force, 374 Fed. Appx. 981, 982-93 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), an interview by the deciding official of a significant witness did not prove a constitutional barrier to removal when the information conveyed was not a reason for the deciding official's termination decision:

The only issue Hull raises on appeal is whether the deciding official violated Hull's Fifth Amendment due process rights when the deciding official conducted an ex parte communication of which Hull was not informed. This court has held that "[T]he introduction of new and material information by means of ex parte communications to the deciding official undermines the public employee's constitutional due process guarantee of notice (both of the charges and of the employer's evidence) and the opportunity to respond." Stone v. Fed. Deposit Ins. Corp., 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1999). We emphasized, however, that not every ex parte communication violates due process; rather only ex parte communications providing "new and material information" violate the due process guarantee of notice. Id. at 1376-77.

As the arbitrator correctly found, the deciding official's ex parte communications provided no "new and material information." Hull points to statements Wolf made during the ex parte communication that he "would not work in a boiler plant with Mr. Hull unless escorted" as the only new, non-cumulative information. However, the Arbitrator properly found that these statements were not material because the Notice of Proposed Removal and the Decision to Remove cite only Hull's disruption in the work place, disregard for directives, and conduct unbecoming a federal employee. There is nothing in either notice that suggests the deciding official's decision was based on Wolf's unwillingness to work with Hull.
