3. Emergency Suspension for Reason Other Than Reasonable Belief of Crime

The authority for shortened adverse action notice periods is not broader than the statute describes. At one time an OPM regulation, 5 CFR 752.404(d)(3) (1983), provided:

[T]he 30 days' advance written notice is not required for a suspension during the notice period of a removal or of an indefinite suspension when the circumstances are such that retention of the employee in an active duty status during the notice period may be injurious to the employee, his or her follow workers, or the general public; may result in damage to Government property; or because of the nature of the employee's offense may reflect unfavorably on the public perception of the Federal service.

The conflict between the limited statutory notice (crime) exception and the broader language of the OPM regulation was resolved in Cuellar v. USPS, 8 MSPR 624, 632 (1981) (involving "emergency" suspensions of various individuals for a variety of reasons, e.g., the use of intoxicating beverages on federal property, a physical altercation at the work site, and insubordination). Suspensions under (the former) 5 CFR 752.404(d)(3) were invalid because emergency suspensions may be effected only under the crime provision at 5 USC 7513(b)(1). Cuellar considered the validity of the regulation in light of the "crime provision," 5 USC 7513(b)(1), comprising a congressional determination that the statutory basis for removing an employee during the notice period was the only situation that allowed an agency's need to protect its employees, property, or reputation to outweigh the right of an employee to 30 days' notice. The Board searched the legislative history of the Reform Act and prior legislation and found nothing authorizing OPM to add further exceptions to the 30-day notice requirement beyond the one exception there provided. The Board reversed the suspensions of the appellants who were deprived of notice provisions in situations not within the crime exception. Cuellar was followed in Eaks v. DOJ, 18 MSPR 328, 331 (1983), and Wink v. USPS, 9 MSPR 431, 434 (1982). Blow v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 102, 105 n.2 (1982), added:

In passing the Reform Act, Congress maintained the "crime exception" now contained in 5 USC § 7513(b)(1) as the only instance in which an agency's need to protect its employees, property, and/or reputation could outweigh the employee's right to a 30-day notice prior to disciplinary actions. See also, S. Rep. No. 969, 95th Cong., 2nd Sess. 50 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1978, p. 2723, stating that "the 30-day period may be reduced only when there is reasonable cause to believe the employee is guilty of a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment can be imposed." (emphasis supplied)

Cuellar was expanded upon in Anderson v. USPS, 24 MSPR 488, 491 (1984), setting aside an "emergency suspension" imposed for an alleged assault:

In our view, and as indicated in Cuellar, 5 USC 7513 does not envision a suspension action during the notice period of a removal action. The "crime exception" of 7513(b)(1) adequately provides for those situations in which an employee must be separated from the agency premises quickly by permitting a shortened notice period in any adverse action where "there is reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment may be imposed." Since an agency may institute a removal action on shortened notice where it has such "reasonable cause to believe," the emergency suspension is extraneous. The Board concludes that an agency may not effect both a suspension during the notice period preceding a removal action and a removal action where an agency has "reasonable cause to believe." Such action is not envisioned in 7513, and once disciplinary or adverse action is imposed because of an employee's misconduct an agency is barred from subsequently taking another adverse action for the same reason.... We re-emphasize, however, that an agency may shorten the notice period of the ultimate adverse action itself under the "crime exception" of 7513(b)(1), and place the employee in a nonduty status with pay for up to ten days while processing that action. See 5 CFR 752.404(d)(1); Martel v. Department of Transportation, 15 MSPR 141, 146-47 n.3 (1983), aff'd, 735 F.2d 504 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

See Littlejohn v. USPS, 25 MSPR 478, 481 (1984) (same analysis as Anderson).

To impose an emergency suspension, the agency must specifically invoke the crime exception to the statutory advance notice requirement. See Romero v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 56, 57-58 (1982). An emergency suspension imposed for nonstatutory reasons, i.e., not in accordance with the crime provision, was not sustained for misconduct when it was effected without constitutional minimal due process, i.e., notice and an opportunity to respond. Clark v. USPS, 52 MSPR 634, 643 (1992) (majority opinion was joined by Chairman Levinson, who offered a concurring opinion noting that the Board should decide that the Postal Service National Agreement could not furnish a basis of independent "legal authority" for an emergency suspension); see Fiori v. USPS, 104 MSPR 278, 282 ¶ 20, 2006 MSPB 355 (2006) ("The Board has held that the statutory provisions governing adverse actions do not envision a suspension of the employee during the notice period of an adverse action, and that the law does not permit an agency to place an employee on an emergency suspension during the notice period of a removal and then effect a removal for the same act of misconduct. Cox-Vaughn v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 246, ¶ 18 (2005). This is true even where, as here, there may be a collective bargaining agreement provision to the contrary. Id. at ¶ 20.").

A Postal Service national agreement that permitted what amounted to a brief emergency suspension was indirectly approved in Bradley v. USPS, 96 MSPR 539, 543-44 ¶¶ 13, 17 (2004), involving the National Postal Mail Handlers Union contract, containing Article 16.7, providing that "[a]n employee may be immediately placed on an off duty status (without pay) by the Employer, but remains on the rolls where...the employee may be injurious to self or others." The appellant was notified that he would be off the rolls pending the outcome of an investigation into his conduct, and that status continued for 12 days. He was then suspended for 14 days for unacceptable conduct involving the incident that was investigated. The Board lacked jurisdiction over the emergency suspension because the placement on off-duty status and the subsequent suspension arose out of separate events and circumstances and did not constitute a single suspension for the purpose of determining Board jurisdiction. The OPM regulations that led to litigation over emergency suspensions were modified to allow agencies flexibility while avoiding loss to employees of the financial benefit of the statutory notice provision, 5 CFR 752.404(b)(3) (2022):

(3) Under ordinary circumstances, an employee whose removal or suspension, including indefinite suspension, has been proposed will remain in a duty status in his or her regular position during the advance notice period. In those rare circumstances where the agency determines that the employee's continued presence in the workplace during the notice period may pose a threat to the employee or others, result in the loss of or damage to Government property, or otherwise jeopardize legitimate Government interests, the agency may elect one or a combination of the following alternatives:

(i) Assigning the employee to duties where he or she is no longer a threat to safety, the agency mission, or to Government property;

(ii) Allowing the employee to take leave, or carrying him or her in an appropriate leave status (annual, sick, leave without pay, or absence without leave) if the employee has absented himself or herself from the worksite without requesting leave;

(iii) Curtailing the notice period when the agency can invoke the provisions of paragraph (d)(1) of this section; or

(iv) Placing the employee in a paid, nonduty status for such time as is necessary to effect the action. After publication of regulations for 5 U.S.C. 6329b, and the subsequent agency implementation period in accordance with 5 U.S.C. 6329b, an agency may place the employee in a notice leave status when applicable.

If an agency desires to suspend an employee but cannot do so under the "crime provision," it should follow adverse action procedures. Alternatively, placing an employee on administrative leave pending the adverse action reply process is not unusual. Under Oliver v. USPS, 696 F.2d 1129 (5th Cir. 1983), regulations do not prohibit an agency from placing an employee on paid leave during the 30-day advance notice period of an adverse action. A right to remain on duty during the notice period is not the kind of protection (or tenure) that the CSRA was intended to grant.
