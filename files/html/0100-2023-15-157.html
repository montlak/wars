15. Defenses

To determine whether an employee's conduct was insubordinate, any justification for the employee's action should be examined. An employee was directed to correct some work papers; when he refused, he was discharged. He claimed it was essential that he not alter the work papers because they were the subject of a grievance proceeding that he initiated. The Board remanded the case to the AJ to determine whether the agency maintained copies of the original work papers so that the employee's grievance could be prosecuted, and to determine whether the employee was informed of the agency's efforts to safeguard the essential evidence. Jones v. Farm Credit Admin., 3 MSPR 471, 473-74, 3 MSPB 540 (1980); see Jones v. Farm Credit Admin., 11 MSPR 159, 162 (1982) (following remand, the Board sustained the adverse action: the appellant could have complied with the supervisor's instructions without negating relevant evidence). A reviewing court sustained the Board, Jones v. Farm Credit Admin., 702 F.2d 160, 162 (8th Cir. 1983):

Jones first argues the FCA violated his due process rights by commencing the removal proceeding before the pending grievance was resolved. We disagree. The removal proceeding was based upon additional instances of misconduct. Moreover, in its final decision, the MSPB found Jones could have corrected his reports as instructed without jeopardizing his grievance. The MSPB further found Jones' actual motive in refusing to correct the papers was not to protect his due process rights, but to vindicate his belief that he was right and his supervisors were wrong. His refusal to complete tasks unrelated to his grievance bears out this conclusion. Jones' grievance did not suspend his duty as an employee to follow the directions of his supervisors regardless of his personal views....
