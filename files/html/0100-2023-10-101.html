2. Regional Authority After Initial Decision

Once the initial decision is issued by an AJ, that AJ loses control of the appeal, with several defined exceptions. Errors of substance and procedure are then addressed through the petition for review process described in Chapter 5. Under 5 CFR 1201.112 (2022), the AJ has limited jurisdiction after issuance of the initial decision:

(a) After issuing the initial decision, the judge will retain jurisdiction over a case only to the extent necessary to:

(1) Correct the transcript; when one is obtained;

(2) Rule on a request by the appellant for attorney fees, consequential damages, or compensatory damages under subpart H of this part;

(3) Process any petition for enforcement filed under subpart F of this part;

(4) Vacate an initial decision to accept into the record a settlement agreement that is filed prior to the deadline for filing a petition for review, even if the settlement agreement is not received until after the date when the initial decision becomes final under § 1201.113 of this part.

(b) Nothing is this section affects the time limits prescribed in § 1201.113 regarding the finality of an initial decision or the time allowed for filing a petition for review.

After the initial decision becomes final through passage of time, or through a Board decision on a PFR, there are other processes that can place the case back into the jurisdiction of the AJ. Enforcement authority, [discussed in Chapter 16, "Enforcement of MSPB Decision"] is one source of jurisdiction for a regional office or judge after an initial decision becomes final. Counsel fee petitions and requests for awards of compensatory or consequential damages in EEO and whistleblowing reprisal cases are other sources of authority for the AJ when the final decision favors the appellant. Those matters are discussed in Chapter 12 (EEO cases), Chapter 13 (whistleblowing cases), and Chapter 16 (Board remedial authority, damages, compliance and enforcement).

The AJ's authority to modify an initial decision is narrowly cabined by the Board's regulation. The AJ lacks authority to correct a remedial order after the initial decision issues. Interpretation of remedial rights contained in the initial decision should await enforcement proceedings following finality of the Board's decision. See Jackson v. USPS, 73 MSPR 512, 517 (1997). Reviewing an OPM annuity overpayment determination, the AJ issued an initial decision stating that the repayment schedule should be reduced, but the AJ issued an erratum to correct an error, raising the monthly payment for the appellant. The appellant timely filed a PFR after the erratum issuance, but his PFR was not timely as to the underlying initial decision. The appellant explained that he could accept the initial determination, but the erratum set an overpayment rate that caused him to object. The Board determined that the AJ had no authority to issue the erratum, allowed the PFR, and remanded the case for additional consideration. On the AJ's lack of authority to issue the erratum, Carroll v. OPM, 114 MSPR 310, 314 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 131 (2010), determined:

We find that the administrative judge lacked the authority to issue the January 12, 2010 erratum. The Board's regulations provide that once an administrative judge issues an initial decision, he retains jurisdiction "only to the extent necessary to" correct the transcript, adjudicate addendum proceedings (which is not at issue here), or vacate an initial decision before it becomes final to accept a settlement agreement into the record. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.112(a). Further, administrative judges lack the authority to reopen or reinstate appeals in which there has been a final Board decision; that authority is reserved to the Board. See, e.g., Robey v. U.S. Postal Service, 105 M.S.P.R. 539, ¶ 10, aff'd, 253 Fed. Appx. 933 (Fed. Cir. 2007), cert. denied, 128 S. Ct. 1925 (2008); McNeil v. U.S. Postal Service, 98 M.S.P.R. 18, ¶ 8 (2004). Because the initial decision became the final decision of the Board on January 4, 2010, the administrative judge no longer retained jurisdiction over the appeal when he issued the erratum on January 12, 2010.

See Stewart v. Dept. of Transp, 2023 MSPB 18, ¶ 9 (2023) (AJ lacked authority to issue an erratum correcting the failure to order interim relief in the initial decision).

Linares-Rosado v. USPS, 112 MSPR 599, 606-07 ¶ 15, 2009 MSPB 219 (2009), declared:

An AJ lacks authority to reopen or reinstate appeals in which there has been a final Board decision; only the Board has such authority. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(e)(1)(B); Nahoney v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 93, ¶ 9 (2009). When a post-ID pleading can be construed as a PFR seeking to reopen and reinstate an appeal, the proper course is to forward the pleading to the Board. See, e.g., Carlson v. General Services Administration, 101 M.S.P.R. 70, ¶ 5 (2006) (the AJ correctly forwarded a case challenging the validity of a settlement agreement to the Board); Beyer v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 672, 675 (1988) (the appellant's letter was a PFR that should have been forwarded by the AJ to the Board).

What occurs when the law changes after the decision becomes final? That occurred in Stephens v. OPM, 30 MSPR 680 (1986). The appellant won a retirement appeal and her attorney petitioned for fees. The fees were denied under the law governing at the time the fee motion was decided. Many months after the fee ruling became final, appellant's counsel asked the AJ to order fees to be paid on the basis of an intervening change in the law. The Board ruled that the AJ lost jurisdiction of the case, with the exception of the limited circumstances identified by 5 CFR 1201.112, following finality of the decision. The Board reopened the case on its own motion, however, and directed the parties to brief and argue retroactive application of the decisions that permitted awards of fees in retirement cases.
