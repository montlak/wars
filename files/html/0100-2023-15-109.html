a. Reporting, Approval Requirements

Outside jobs are regulated, directly or indirectly, when agencies impose restrictions on the work and then require employees either to report or to obtain approval for the outside employment. Each agency has its restrictions and procedures designed to avoid conflicts, apparent conflicts, and conflicted, exhausted workers. These regulations may be strictly enforced. A 60-day suspension was sustained against an employee who failed to report outside employment. The employee was aware of the requirement, and the offense was not trivial in terms of the negative reflection upon the employee's character or availability for overtime employment. The possibility of an appearance of impropriety was present. The employee worked for a trucking company subject to agency investigation. Meyers v. Customs Serv., 18 MSPR 545, 547 (1984). The Board sustained provisions of the Postal Service Employee and Labor Relations Manual requiring an appellant to obtain prior approval before working at a second job on days he took sick leave from the agency, even though the hours at the second job did not overlap with his regular tour of duty at the agency. Rivoire v. USPS, 103 MSPR 643, 649 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 313 (2006). Adverse action was sustained as to an employee who engaged in outside employment beyond the scope of the approval requested from the agency. Prather v. DOJ, 117 MSPR 137, 143 ¶ 13, 2011 MSPB 100 (2011). Gonzalez v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 646, 651 (1991), sustained the charge that, although the appellant was authorized to work part-time as an income tax preparer, he did not report that he had set up his own business as a CPA. He violated an agency regulation requiring that he obtain approval before engaging in "any off-duty employment." See Rivoire v. USPS, 244 Fed. Appx. 351 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (interpreting § 513.312 of USPS Employee and Labor Relations Manual requiring approval for employees to engage in any gainful employment while they are in sick leave status).

Before an employee can be charged with violating an agency requirement for reporting and securing approval of outside work, the activity must be identified in the regulation so that the employee is on fair notice of what is required of him. Forrester v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 450, 456-57 (1985). If an employee is required to report outside employment, the agency directive should be clear of ambiguity before the employee is responsible for its violation. Dumont v. VA, 6 MSPR 284, 286, 6 MSPB 242 (1981). The failure by a supervisor to properly process an appellant's application for outside employment is a factor relating to the reasonableness of the penalty. Weighing against the mitigating influence of the supervisor's error is the fact that the appellant worked without permission while cognizant of the requirement that written approval was required. Corbett v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 544, 546 (1984); see VanFossen v. DHUD, 26 MSPR 563, 565 (1985), post.

An obligation to request approval for outside work is not implied in law. The obligation is imposed by agency policy or directive, although an employee need be mindful of conflicts or apparently conflicts of interest, Ryan v. DHS, 123 MSPR 202, 210 ¶ 20, 2016 MSPB 7 (2016):

Employees are required to obtain prior approval before engaging in outside activities including employment when required by agency supplemental ethics regulations. 5 C.F.R. § 2635.803. The agency has not submitted any evidence or argument establishing that it has a regulation requiring that employees obtain prior approval before engaging in all outside activities. The agency instead relies on a portion of its ethics training concerning the Government-wide standards of ethical conduct, specifically financial conflicts of interest. The applicable regulation requires that an employee who becomes aware of the need to be disqualified from participating in a matter due to a financial conflict of interest should notify the person responsible for assigning him to the matter. 5 C.F.R. § 2635.402(c)(1). An employee is required to disqualify himself from matters that will have a direct and predictable effect on his interests or any person whose interests are imputed to him. 5 C.F.R. § 2635.402(a)-(b). A matter will have a predictable effect if there is a real, as opposed to a speculative, possibility that the matter will affect the financial interest. Id.

Informal discussions with a supervisor did not equate to the formal approval from an agency deputy counselor required to hold outside employment. Currie v. DHUD, 21 MSPR 720, 727 (1984) (relying on United States v. Kenealy, 646 F.2d 699 (1st Cir. 1981) (a real estate appraiser's disclosure of outside employment to a supervisor failed to meet the agency requirement of obtaining approval from a HUD deputy counselor)). In a somewhat similar situation, however, the Federal Circuit reversed a Board decision, remanding for reassessment of the penalty for a DHUD appraiser who obtained permission to engage in outside employment from the wrong official. VanFossen v. DHUD, 748 F.2d 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1984). On remand, VanFossen's removal was mitigated to a 30-day suspension. Appellant made a good faith effort to follow regulations; there was no indication that proper authority would have withheld permission; the appellant reasonably relied on the authorization for more than four years; there was no monetary loss to the government and no actual conflict of interest; and the appellant cooperated fully with the investigation and terminated the outside work on the demand of his supervisor. There was no public awareness of the conduct and appellant had a long, clear record of prior satisfactory service. VanFossen v. DHUD, 26 MSPR 563, 565 (1985); cf. Hollister v. DOJ, DE-0752-14-0040-I-2 (NP 9/1/2022) ("In addition, although he sought approval for his activities and/or advice from agency ethics counsel and a former supervisor, their positive responses were based on the incomplete and incorrect information that the appellant provided. It appears that the appellant knew, or strongly suspected, that he would not get a favorable answer to his ethics questions if he told them the truth. As the administrative judge correctly found, the reasonable inference to be made from the appellant's false statements is that he did not want to give up his business or the money flowing from it and he did not want to be disciplined.").
