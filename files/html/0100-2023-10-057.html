a. Stipulations

Stipulations are useful. Description of the events leading to an adverse or performance-based action encompasses hundreds of facts. Many of those facts are undisputed. At times no facts are really in dispute; the real question is the reasonableness of the penalty or the rationality of a decision that off-duty conduct is connected with the agency's mission or with the employee's job performance. Prehearing orders from AJs generally require the parties to state whether there are any stipulations. 5 CFR 1201.63 (2022), provides:

The parties may stipulate to any matter of fact. The stipulation will satisfy a party's burden of proving the fact alleged.

It saves the AJ's time if the parties stipulate to undisputed facts. Yet identifying and agreeing to material facts, and then putting them all down on paper, may take counsel and client far more time than would be taken if the party with knowledge of the facts recited them during the normal flow of testimony. It is often more economical for all concerned if testimony is taken, subject to reasonable constraints as to materiality and relevance, to permit the development of undisputed facts. Nonetheless, under 5 CFR 1201.41, granting to AJs the "powers necessary" to adjudicate cases, the Board gives its AJs the authority to attempt to compel the parties and their counsel to develop factual stipulations.

A stipulation satisfies a party's burden of proving the fact stipulated. Hyatt v. USPS, 8 MSPR 362, 364 (1981); 5 CFR 1201.63 (2022). Under Grassell v. Dept. of Transp., 40 MSPR 554, 559 (1989), involving a challenge to factual stipulations developed during a telephonic prehearing conference:

...Administrative judges have the authority to hold conferences for the settlement and the simplification of issues under 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(12).... Further, administrative judges have the authority to direct parties to stipulate to facts not in dispute at a telephonic conference. See D'Iorio v. DHUD, 34 MSPR 351, 354 (1987) (administrative judges have the authority to compel stipulations and such authority promotes the mandate of 5 CFR 1201.41(b) that administrative judges shall take necessary action to avoid delay in the disposition of all proceedings)....

Cf. Akosile v. Armed Forces Retirement Home, DC-0752-10-0885-I-1 (NP 1/19/2012) ("This summary included a statement that "[T]he appellant stipulated to specification 2 of the charge." "The appellant failed to file any objections. Accordingly, the administrative judge did not err in determining that this specification was proven by preponderant evidence.").

"Except for the parties' stipulations of fact, statements made in a status conference or prehearing conference are not evidence." Spradlin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 279, 282 ¶ 8 (1999); see Madison v. DOD, 111 MSPR 614, 617 ¶ 8, 2009 MSPB 132 (2009) (matters discussed at conferences do not constitute evidence). Although the Board may accept a stipulation, the AJ may neither expand the scope of a stipulation beyond the purpose of its offer nor relieve the agency of its overall burden based on a stipulation that does not prove the essential elements of the charge. Davis v. USPS, 54 MSPR 374, 377 (1992), citing D'Iorio v. DHUD, 34 MSPR 351, 354-55 (1987). If the appellant agrees to forego testimony of a witness at a hearing based on a stipulation, the appellant will not be heard to complain on PFR that he needs to introduce as new evidence additional information that could have been secured through testimony from that witness. See Malizia v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 513, 514 (1980). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Stipulations," for the authority of the AJ to direct the parties to stipulate undisputed facts and for additional cases describing the evidentiary effect of stipulations.]
