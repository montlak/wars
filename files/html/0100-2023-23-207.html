b. Amendment to Agreement; Counterproposal

A distinction is drawn between parol evidence used to interpret an ambiguous term and evidence showing that the contract framers changed the agreement after it was signed. Parol evidence is inadmissible to vary an unambiguous term, but evidence is admissible to show that the parties subsequently modified their agreement, Rothwell v. USPS, 64 MSPR 473, 477 (1994):

...In construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the words of the agreement itself are of paramount importance, and parol evidence will be considered only if the written agreement is ambiguous. See Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). The quotation of the agreement set forth above reveals that the term "unscheduled absence" was given an explicit definition and that it is not reasonably susceptible of more than one interpretation. Because the term is not ambiguous, parol evidence is thus unnecessary and inadmissible to clarify the intent of the parties when they entered into the agreement. See Greco, 852 F.2d at 560; Romano v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 319, 323 (1991).

Any contract, including one which is unambiguous, may be modified after it is executed, however, and an agreement to modify existing obligations after formation of the contract may be shown by proof of an oral agreement. See Hutchison v. US Postal Service, 39 MSPR 327, 330-31 (1988). The admission of evidence on this question is not barred by the parol evidence rule, because that rule precludes only the admission of prior or contemporaneous evidence to vary the terms of the final written agreement. See id. at 330 n.4 (emphasis in original). The burden of proving that a modification of the agreement has occurred rests on the party asserting that the original agreement has been altered. See id. at 331.

As the administrative judge suggested, the record (while not conclusive) indicates that agency officials may have orally agreed to modify the definition of "unscheduled absence" in the agreement. Evidence regarding their discussions with the appellant after the formation of the agreement is relevant to a finding on the issue of whether the parties had subsequently agreed to modify the definition of "unscheduled absence." See Hutchison, 39 MSPR at 330-31.

Cf. Lee v. Dept. of Labor, 110 MSPR 355, 361-62 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 252 (2008) (citing Rothwell: "To the extent that the appellant now complains that the agency's actions identified in the request for review may have departed from the procedures set forth in the CBA, we note that these actions occurred after the agency had reached an agreement with the union to hold the PIP in abeyance in order to afford the appellant an additional 60-day period to demonstrate her ability to perform at an acceptable level, and did not deprive her of the process to which she was due.")

It may occur that one party creates an agreement, obtains the necessary signatures, and sends it to the opposing party for signature. The opposing party then finds the agreement acceptable for the most part, but still requires a change or addition to the agreement. Rather than having the agreement retyped and resubmitted for the other side's signatures, the recipient of the agreement signs it and returns it with changes or an addendum to the agreement. The additions essentially constitute a counterproposal. The Board recognizes that suggested changes, if accepted, or not opposed, become part of the agreement ultimately submitted to the AJ, Moore v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 237, 241-42 (1988):

[T]he Board has examined the record and sought additional evidence from the parties to clarify the meaning of the agreement.

Two items in the record are especially helpful in shedding light on the meaning of the settlement agreement. The first item is a letter of modification dated October 1, 1986, which the appellant's representative submitted with the settlement agreement to the agency representative. The letter states that the settlement agreement is to be executed subject to certain modifications. Paragraph 1.b. of that letter states:

The agency will appoint appellant to the first vacant position for which she qualifies at the same grade and step as the position from which she resigned, but the agency does not have to appoint appellant to any position requiring the care of patients. This modification would meet the agency's objection to allowing appellant to care for patients while at the same time not excluding appellant from non-care nursing positions. The modification also corrects the omission that appellant be reappointed to a position at the same...grade as the one she left.

The letter ends by stating:

If the agency does not agree to these modifications, please inform me so that I can file the necessary compliance motion with the Board. The signatures of the agency officials on the agreement will indicate the agency's consent to these modifications.

Significantly, the modification uses the term "agency," rather than the term "Commander" as the party agreeing to the settlement, the signed agreement, on the other hand, states that the Commander of the hospital is the party entering into agreement with the appellant.

On August 31, 1987, by Board order, the agency was allowed to respond to the appellant's argument concerning the October 1, 1986, letter. The agency responded that the letter was not part of the agreement. The Board is not persuaded by this argument. As the modification directs, the signatures of the agency officials on the agreement would indicate the agency's consent to the modifications. Because the settlement agreement was enclosed in the same envelope as the modification, the agency must have been aware of it; and, significantly, the agency does not deny being aware of it. It follows that the agency must also have been aware when it signed the settlement agreement that it was consenting to the modifications. Having thus acquiesced in that interpretation, the agency is bound by it. Perry and Wallis, Inc. v. United States, 427 F.2d 722, 725, 192 Ct. Cl. 310 (1970); Lykes-Youngstown Corp. v. United States, 420 F.2d 735, 745, 190 Ct. Cl. 348, cert. denied, 400 U.S. 865, 91 S. Ct. 102, 27 L. Ed.2d 104 (1970). Furthermore, the agency's response to the petition for enforcement contains a tacit admission that the modification is part of the agreement, because the agency quotes from the modification in defining its obligation under the agreement.
