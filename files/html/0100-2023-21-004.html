2. Conversions

At the onset of the FERS retirement system, in the 1980s, there were many appeals involving conversion from CSRS to FERS and attempts to undo the conversions. Conversion issues should be in the past. A few decisions are noted here.

Addressing the problem of placement of an employee into the wrong retirement systems, under the Federal Erroneous Retirement Coverage Corrections Act (FERCCA), according to Brown v. OPM, AT-0841-14-0541-I-1 (NP 8/4/2015):

FERCCA addresses the problems created when employees are in the wrong retirement plan for an extended period. Poole v. Department of the Army, 117 M.S.P.R. 516, ¶ 13 (2012); 5 C.F.R. § 839.101(a). An employee may seek relief under FERCCA if the employee experienced a "qualifying retirement coverage error," defined as an "erroneous decision by an employee or agent of the Government as to whether Government service is CSRS covered, CSRS Offset covered, FERS covered, or Social Security-Only covered that remained in effect for at least 3 years of service after December 31, 1986." Poole, 117 M.S.P.R. 516, ¶ 13; 5 C.F.R. § 839.102. An employee who has been the subject of a qualifying retirement coverage error under FERCCA may be entitled to various forms of relief, including a choice of retirement plans. Poole, 117 M.S.P.R. 516, ¶ 13.

Additional information concerning the two principal retirement systems, and the means for correcting errors in converting from one system to another, was provided in Wible v. Dept. of Army, 120 MSPR 333, 336-37 ¶¶ 6-7, 2013 MSPB 87 (2013):

Congress enacted the CSRS in 1920, and federal employees hired after its effective date and before December 31, 1983, were eligible for this retirement plan. See Office of Personnel Management (OPM), CSRS and FERS Handbook for Personnel and Payroll Offices (Handbook), § 10A1.1-2 Individuals hired after December 31, 1983, became eligible for a new retirement plan, the Federal Employees' Retirement System (FERS). Id. Individuals hired after December 31, 1983, were also required to begin making Old Age, Survivor, and Disability Insurance payments into the Social Security system. Id. If an individual, who was covered under the CSRS plan with at least 5 years of federal service at the end of 1986, had a break in service of greater than 1 year, then that individual would be placed in a separate plan known as CSRS Offset. Handbook, § 10A1.2-1. Rules covering individuals in CSRS Offset are found in 5 C.F.R. Part 831, Subpart J. Individuals in CSRS Offset are covered by both CSRS and Social Security. Office of Personnel Management, Retirement FAQs-What is CSRS Offset?, http://www.opm.gov/faq/retire/What-is-CSRS-Offset.ashx (last visited Oct. 31, 2013). An individual in CSRS Offset earns credits in both CSRS and Social Security. Id. An employee in CSRS Offset pays the same amount into the retirement plan as a CSRS employee does; however, 6.2 percent of such an employee's salary is paid into Social Security and the balance of the retirement contribution is paid into CSRS Offset. Id. When an employee in CSRS Offset retires, OPM calculates the retirement annuity using the same method as a CSRS employee, but, when the individual becomes eligible to draw Social Security, the CSRS retirement payment is reduced by the amount of Social Security benefit attributed to the covered government service. Id.

Limitations on attempts to undo a FERS election were described by Deveney v. OPM, DC-0846-13-0070-I-1 (NP 3/21/2014):

On review, the appellant makes several legal challenges to the administrative judge's findings. Underlying many of the appellant's challenges is the assertion that he was not actually eligible to elect FERS under 5 C.F.R. § 846.201(a). The appellant asserts that he had no basis to elect FERS under 5 C.F.R. § 846.201(a), which confers FERS eligibility upon "employees and Members subject to CSRS on June 30, 1987" because "the record contains conflicting evidence as to whether he was covered by CSRS at that time." Although it appears that the appellant's eligibility for CSRS Offset and his ability to apply for a CSRS refund are in question, his retirement records indicate that he was covered by CSRS, notwithstanding any incorrect information concerning CSRS Offset or a refund. Thus, we find unpersuasive his argument that he was not eligible to elect FERS because he was not, at that time, covered under CSRS.

The appellant argues that Office of Personnel Management v. Richmond, 496 U.S. 414 (1990), does not apply because there is no statutory provision that unequivocally renders the appellant ineligible for retirement benefits under CSRS. The Federal Employees' Retirement System Act of 1986 unequivocally states that any election to transfer to FERS is irrevocable. Pub. L. No. 99-335, § 301(c), 100 Stat. 514; 5 C.F.R. § 846.201(f). Both the Board and the Federal Circuit have applied Richmond in analogous appeals. See Springer v. Office of Personnel Management, 911 F.2d 675, 676-77 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (assertion of estoppel in FERS election appeal rejected as a matter of law); Tanaka v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 M.S.P.R. 296, 299 (1994) (rejecting equitable claims where the agency failed to inform the appellant of her eligibility for FERS); Ades v. Office of Personnel Management, 48 M.S.P.R. 151, 154-55 (1991) (finding that, even if the appellant's election was a result of misinformation, OPM was not estopped from denying her request to re-enroll in CSRS); see also Martinez v. Office of Personnel Management, 104 F.3d 376 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table) (nonprecedential) (appellant was statutorily barred from changing her election notwithstanding her allegations that she relied upon misinformation by the government). Contrary to the appellant's argument, we do not see a distinction between the facts here, as set forth in the initial decision, and a situation in which an individual is misinformed about his retirement eligibility and elects coverage on the basis of such misinformation.

The appellant cites to Hathaway v. Office of Personnel Management, 118 M.S.P.R. 678, ¶ 14 (2012), McDonald v. Office of Personnel Management, 115 M.S.P.R. 236, ¶¶ 10-12 (2010), overruled on other grounds by Cerilli v. Office of Personnel Management, 119 M.S.P.R. 404, ¶ 10 (2013), and Perez Peraza v. Office of Personnel Management, 114 M.S.P.R. 457 (2010), for the proposition that an election can be voidable due to "affirmative misconduct." For the following reasons, we disagree.

Perez Peraza was an appeal involving the waiver of the filing deadline to make a survivor annuity election, and the Board specifically found Richmond inapplicable because the appellant was permitted by law to change or revoke a survivor annuity election within 30 days of the first regular monthly payment. Perez Peraza, 114 M.S.P.R. 457, ¶¶ 6-7 & n.1. The Board remanded on the question of whether the appellant could establish equitable estoppel, i.e., affirmative misconduct and reasonable reliance on that misconduct, to waive the 30-day deadline. Id., ¶¶ 6-14. Similarly, in McDonald, the Board remanded on the issue of affirmative misconduct concerning the election of a survivor annuity because OPM had a statutory obligation to notify the annuitant annually that he had 18 months after retirement to provide or increase a spouse's survivor annuity, and the record was not clear on the question of whether OPM satisfied its obligation.[3] 115 M.S.P.R. 236, ¶¶ 6, 10-12; see 5 U.S.C. § 8339(o)(1). In neither of these cases did the Board actually apply equitable estoppel, and at issue in both of these cases was the waiver of a filing deadline to make or change an election concerning a spouse's retirement annuity. These cases do not concern an appellant's irrevocable election of a retirement system,[4] and we decline to extend the Board's reasoning in those cases to the facts presented here.

[3] Hathaway does not support the appellant's proposition because the Board found that the appellant's assertions regarding the applicability of McDonald were not supported by the record. 118 M.S.P.R. 678, ¶ 14.

[4] As discussed above, a FERS election is irrevocable. There is, however, the option of revoking or changing a survivor annuity election. 5 U.S.C. § 8339(o)(1); Perez Peraza, 114 M.S.P.R. 457, ¶ 5.

Furthermore, the Board has made it clear that when an agency unknowingly or negligently gives incorrect information, such action does not rise to the level of "affirmative misconduct." Perez Paraza, 114 M.S.P.R. 457, ¶¶ 11-13; see McDonald, 115 M.S.P.R. 236, ¶ 12. The appellant here was informed of the correct standard for establishing equitable estoppel, and we agree with the administrative judge's conclusion that the appellant did not prove affirmative misconduct by OPM or the appellant's employing agency at the time of his FERS election.
