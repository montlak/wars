16. Severity of Misconduct

When it evaluates a penalty, the Board principally evaluates the nature and seriousness of the misconduct relative to the employee's duties and responsibilities. Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 184-85 ¶ 16 (2002); see Thomas v. Dept. of Army, 2022 MSPB 35, ¶ 20 (2022) ("It is well established that the most important factor in assessing whether the agency's chosen penalty is within the tolerable bounds of reasonableness is the nature and seriousness of the misconduct and its relation to the employee's duties, position, and responsibilities. Downey v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 302, ¶ 9 (2013); Kirkland-Zuck v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 90 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 19 (2001), aff'd, 48 F. App'x 749 (Fed. Cir. 2002)."); Purifoy v. VA, CH-0752-14-0185-M-1 (NP 5/16/2022) ("In assessing the reasonableness of a penalty, the Board places primary importance upon the nature and seriousness of the offense and its relation to the appellant's duties, position, and responsibilities, including whether the offense was intentional or was repeated frequently. Batara v. Department of the Navy, 123 M.S.P.R. 278, ¶ 8 (2016). The Board also will consider how the appellant's misconduct affected the agency's mission. See Jones v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 561, ¶ 21 (2006), aff'd, 214 F. App'x 984 (Fed. Cir. 2007)."); Rosario-Fabregas v. Dept. of Army, NY-0752-13-0142-I-2 (NP 7/1/2016) ("The seriousness of the appellant's offense is always one of the most important factors considered by the Board in assessing the reasonableness of the agency's penalty determination. Rosenberg v. Department of Transportation, 105 M.S.P.R. 130, ¶ 30 (2007)."); Doe v. DOJ, 107 MSPR 397, 404 ¶ 10, 2007 MSPB 282 (2007) ("In evaluating the penalty, the Board will consider, first and foremost, the nature and seriousness of the misconduct and its relation to the employee's duties, position, and responsibilities, including whether the offense was intentional or was frequently repeated."); Thomas v. USPS, 96 MSPR 179, 182-83 ¶ 5 (2004) ("The Board places primary importance upon the nature and seriousness of the offense and its relation to the appellant's duties, position, and responsibilities."); cf. Barboza v. Dept. of Labor, SF-0752-11-0053-I-1 (NP 9/1/2011) ("[t]he appellant's 5-year tenure is not especially lengthy, and the lack of any actual harm to the agency's mission or reputation cannot negate the potentially serious harm that could have resulted if the Infusion Organics case had proceeded to prosecution and the appellant's falsifications came to light at that time. See Chandler v. Social Security Administration, 80 M.S.P.R. 542, ¶ 12 (1999) (the Board considered the 'potential for harm to the agency's basic mission' as an aggravating factor).").

Whether an appellant's errors are de minimis is not an element of a charge but a factor to be considered in assessing the penalty if the charge is sustained. Stromfeld v. DOJ, 21 MSPR 428, 430 (1984). However, a penalty that is grossly disproportionate to the offense is an abuse of discretion. Howard v. USPS, 72 MSPR 422, 425 (1996). For an example of a case involving mitigation partially due to the overreaction of the agency to charges that were sustained, see VanFossen v. DHUD, 748 F.2d 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1984) [discussed in Chapter 8, "Outside Employment"]. Abrigo v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 88-3390), reversed removal of a letter carrier for unauthorized entry into a private residence. The employee accepted a glass of soda from a child who invited him in to drink it. The charge against him was technical because the entry was not sanctioned by an adult. At worst, the decision to enter the house reflected a momentary lapse of judgment. The agency and the Board gave no consideration either to the rehabilitation and counseling the employee underwent or to the mitigating circumstances surrounding the infraction, including the apparently technical nature of the infraction and the fact that it was not committed maliciously or repeatedly. Removal was an abuse of discretion. The case was remanded for the imposition of a lesser penalty.

The Board occasionally finds a violation of some regulation to be trivial, and then imposes what it thinks could be the maximum penalty. A technical violation of a contracting regulation by an employee with an unblemished, long past record warranted a 60-day suspension rather than removal absent intentional wrongdoing or malfeasance. Mauro v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 86, 95-96 (1987). Given the Board's continual recitation of its deference to agency management's decisions about penalties, rather than impose a long suspension for some trivial problem, the Board would serve everyone better to remand the case to the agency concerned. Most cases of this nature would probably be closed out with a reprimand rather than a long-term suspension.
