i. Prior "Bad Faith" Implementation Standard

Until the 1994 Federal Circuit decision in King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1994), the standard for assessing the propriety of the agency's interim relief order was "bad faith," a burden borne by the appellant. The "bad faith" standard was further defined to mean that evidence should be offered showing that the relief afforded by the agency was discriminatory, demeaning, or inherently unsafe. Perry v. USPS, 54 MSPR 481, 484 (1992). The appellant was required to allege and provide evidence of bad faith for the Board to undertake review of the legitimacy of the agency's interim relief. See Cobb v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 47, 51 n.3 (1993). The evidence was to take the form of an affidavit or sworn statement. It helped if the appellant's allegations were supported by documentary evidence. See Garcia v. DLA, 64 MSPR 145, 149 (1994).

Among the "bad faith" disputes reviewed by the Board, and generally resolved in favor of the agency, were those involving restrictions of duties or details to other jobs. See Coates v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 362, 367 (1997) ("The Board will not review matters such as training opportunities in the context of a motion to dismiss for alleged noncompliance with interim relief."); Heining v. GSA, 61 MSPR 539, 546 (1994) (agency could first determine that an employee's return to a position at her former duty station would be disruptive and later detail her to another position; Browning v. Dept. of Army, 61 MSPR 232, 235 (1994) (dismissing objections by the appellant that organizational changes adversely affected his stature or managerial control within his organization); Chauvin v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 675, 678 n.1 (1993) (relatively minor changes in a position and a shift assigned to the appellant upon restoration did not suggest bad faith); Radcliffe v. Dept. of Transp., 57 MSPR 237, 240 n.5 (1993) (agency did not fail to provide interim relief when it restricted the appellant's duties because of limitations on the appellant's training); Crawford v. Dept. of Treasury, 56 MSPR 224, 229 (1993) (appellant did not make out a case of bad faith by showing that a detail during a period of interim relief was discriminatory, demeaning, or inherently unsafe); Brown v. USPS, 54 MSPR 275, 277 (1991) (reviewing an allegation of a "make work" assignment); Caryl v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 202, 205 (1992). The Board also considered allegations that interim assignments were unsafe. See Wilson v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 303, 308 (1995) (agency did not err in granting leave requested by the appellant because of a job-related injury); Costin v. DHHS, 64 MSPR 517, 527 (1994) ("The appellant need not work in the Maintenance Mechanic position until he sustains a permanent debilitating injury before the agency's continued insistence that he work in that position will be deemed to be in bad faith."). Also reviewed were allegations that assignments and detail were tinged with personal hostility arising from an appellant's involvement in Board litigation. See Jerome v. SBA, 56 MSPR 181, 186 (1993), recons. denied, 59 MSPR 134 (1993), rev'd, King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1994); Rogers v. DOJ, 60 MSPR 377, 385 (1994) (no bad faith on the part of an agency that placed the appellant in another office, as a component of interim relief, when "all of the individuals involved in the hostility that resulted in the demotion of the appellant are still employed at the Salt Lake City office"); Bonggat v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 402, 406 (1993) (adjustments in the position held by the appellant during interim relief, even if it was based on perceptions of the incompatibility between the appellant and his previous chain of command, did not automatically suggest bad faith).

The evaluation of discriminatory job placement did not come into play if the appellant was not placed back into a job but, instead, was kept out of the workplace as unduly disruptive and was provided appropriate pay and benefits while the agency PFR was pending. See Hanner v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 113, 117 n.2 (1992); Bell v. Dept. of Treasury, 54 MSPR 619, 624 (1992) (declining to adjudicate a claim of discrimination in the application of interim relief when the appellant was restored to appropriate pay and benefit status). If the employer attempted to remove the employee during the interim relief period, bad faith was not necessarily demonstrated. Neither the WPA nor an interim relief order insulated the appellant from a subsequent adverse action, as long as that action was not inconsistent with the initial decision. Shumate v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 288, 290 (1994) (suspension of security access).
