8. Discrimination Against Veteran; 5 USC 2302(b)(11)

Under 5 USC 2302:

(b) Any employee who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action, shall not, with respect to such authority--

...

(11)(A) knowingly take, recommend, or approve any personnel action if the taking of such action would violate a veterans' preference requirement; or

(B)  knowingly fail to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action if the failure to take such action would violate a veterans' preference requirement[.]

The Veterans Employment Opportunities Act of 1998 (VEOA), Pub. L. No. 105-339, amended 5 USC 2302(b) to add to the constellation of prohibited practices the knowing taking or failure to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action if to do so would violate a veterans preference requirement. 5 USC 2302(b)(11). The 1998 legislation went on to amend the prohibited personnel practice provisions of the Reform Act to add subsection (e)(2), providing:

Notwithstanding any other provision of this title, no authority to order corrective action shall be available in connection with a prohibited personnel practice described in subsection (b)(11). Nothing in this paragraph shall be considered to affect any authority under section 1215 (relating to disciplinary action).

Was § 2302(e)(2) intended to bar OSC corrective actions but to establish or continue OSC disciplinary action authority for violations of veterans preference laws and regulations? What does the section mean?

Report 105-340 of the Senate Committee on Veterans Affairs (Sept. 21, 1998), stated, at p. 17:

Section 5. Prohibited Personnel Practices. Currently, the actions of Federal employees, as they apply to personnel decisions, may not deny fair treatment on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, marital status, age, handicap or political affiliation. Acts which deny fairness on these grounds are "prohibited personnel practices." 5 USC § 2302. Persons found by the MSPB to have knowingly committed such practices may be removed or suspended from Federal employment, or fined. 5 USC § 1215.

Section 5 of the Committee bill would amend 5 USC § 2302 to add violations of veterans' preference laws to the listing of prohibited personnel practices. Thus, persons who knowingly fail to comply with veterans' preference requirements could be disciplined in accordance with standards applicable to prohibited personnel practices.

Examination of the Senate committee hearings of March 24, 1998, provides no particular insight into the meaning of § 5 of VEOA. Senator Hagel, a principal sponsor of the legislation, remarked:

S. 1021 makes violation of veterans preference law a prohibited personnel practice: Today violations of veterans preference are not a prohibited personnel practice, nor can the Special Counsel punish veterans preference violations as a disciplinary action. S. 1021 will make violations of a veteran's employment rights a prohibited personnel practice, triggering a series of remedies that exist in current law and putting "teeth" into the veterans preference statute.

During the congressional hearing, the representative of OPM gave an example of a referral by OPM to OSC of an agency that manipulated the examining system to avoid veterans preference entitlements. The VFW's representative testified that "[d]isciplinary action against a manager or any federal employee who knowingly violates veterans' preference is the surest means of truly enforcing veterans preference." Representatives of AFGE and NTEU had little to say on the meaning or intent of the provision.
