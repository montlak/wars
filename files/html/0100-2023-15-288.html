2. Alcohol

If an individual is charged with being under the influence of alcohol on the job, the Board will not presume that the appellant was "under the influence" if blood-alcohol tests show a level of alcohol below .10%, the standard permitting a presumption of intoxication in most states. The Board noted the particular circumstances: the appellant explained that he drank too much the night before and reported to work with a hangover but worked for several hours without incident, leaving the record devoid of evidence of impairment. Henry v. TVA, 21 MSPR 496, 498 (1984); cf. Parker v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 621, 623 (1984) (when the agency charged the appellant with being under the influence, it was required to show that he was unfit to work, since that is how the charging terminology was defined by agency regulations). With an employee charged with reporting to work under the influence, although no actual harm was done by the appellant, the agency was properly concerned with what might have been done by the appellant--a truck driver--and the deterrent effect of his removal upon others. Moore v. TVA, 23 MSPR 357, 360 (1984); cf. Peeples v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 129, 130 (1981) (removal of an appellant for reporting for duty under influence of an intoxicant). But see Williams v. GSA, 14 MSPR 281, 282-83 (1982) (mitigating removal of a federal protective officer to suspension for an incident of being unfit for duty because of intoxication; various mitigating factors included the appellant's efforts to rehabilitate himself and a serious family illness).

Removal was warranted of an electronics mechanic accused of reporting for duty under the influence of intoxicants and unauthorized possession and consumption of intoxicants on agency premises. The employee had prior discipline and had been recommended to seek rehabilitative assistance. See Damstrom v. USPS, 11 MSPR 214, 215 (1982) (removal for intoxication on agency premises); Henderson v. Coast Guard, 9 MSPR 610, 613-14 (1982) (removal of an electronics mechanic for reporting for duty under the influence of intoxicants as well as for unauthorized possession and consumption of intoxicants on agency premises); Lott v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 513, 515, 7 MSPB 367 (1981) (removal for reporting to duty under the influence was mitigated based on factors including efforts to obtain help from a treatment center); cf. Coates v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 362, 371 (1997) (maintenance worker fired for causing an accident in a government van while driving under the influence of alcohol: "It is undisputed...that there is a nexus between the sustained charges, DUI and failure to maintain a condition of employment, and the efficiency of the service."); Wiecek v. DOJ, 3 MSPR 462, 464, 3 MSPB 532 (1980) (service detention officer removed for being involved in an altercation, while on duty, with local police while intoxicated). Removal was sustained for an individual who, while employed at a student residential facility as a supervisor for and role model to students, where there was a zero tolerance policy as to students' involvement with alcohol or drugs, twice was disciplined for reporting to the facility under the influence of alcohol, and was fired for a DUI conviction that led to the loss of a commercial driving license required by the job. Kugler v. USDA, 397 Fed. Appx. 634 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP). [Refer to Chapter 12 on "Agency Defenses" for accommodation defenses and refer to Little v. USPS, 66 MSPR 574 (1995), discussed in Chapter 12, concerning current drug use, under "Current Substance Abuser."]

Removal was affirmed on evidence that a heavy equipment mechanic position was under the influence of alcohol at work. Mitigation was rejected based on past alcohol issues and treatment, Callicutt v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-12-0514-I-1 (NP 8/9/2013):

With respect to the penalty of removal, our function is only to ensure that management's judgment has been properly exercised and that the agency-selected penalty does not exceed the maximum limits of reasonableness. Parker v. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 510, ¶ 9, aff'd, 355 F. App'x. 410 (Fed. Cir. 2009). Given the circumstances in this case, we affirm the administrative judge's findings that Lockard gave proper consideration to the Douglas factors and that the penalty of removal was within the bounds of reasonableness. Lockard considered the appellant's length of service, including military service, and that his work performance had been "exceptional." He also considered mitigating circumstances, such as the appellant's medical conditions and medications, various challenging circumstances in the appellant's personal life, and difficulties the appellant experienced with his supervisor. Lockard concluded, however, that the agency "must take a hard position when it comes to alcohol and drug abuse within the workforce." Lockard believed the appellant could not likely be rehabilitated, given that he had completed an alcohol rehabilitation program within the prior two years. In addition, the appellant had previously entered into a last-chance agreement and had been suspended for fourteen days for reporting to work under the influence of alcohol, so he was clearly on notice that such behavior is unacceptable. At the hearing, Lockard reiterated that the appellant had previously been offered an opportunity for rehabilitation via the last-chance agreement and stated that it was not in the government's best interests to continue the appellant's employment because he posed a threat to the safety of others and could not remain safely in an industrial environment while under the influence of alcohol.

Possession of alcoholic beverages on agency premises was not a sufficiently serious offense to warrant dismissal. A 30-day suspension was appropriate given the nature of the offense, the slight connection between the offense and the appellants' responsibilities as food service workers, and the lack of evidence that the appellants were on actual notice of the rule that was violated. Edwards v. VA, 7 MSPR 458, 460 (1981); see Gleim v. USPS, 47 MSPR 502, 507 (1991) (mitigating removal to a demotion out of supervision for drinking on the job and improperly discarding mail on the same day; a long work record and evidence of condonation of conduct; the employee sought help for alcohol abuse).

Alcohol dependence may lead to inability to work, and inability to work may lead to removal for medical unfitness for duty, Stevens v. Dept. of Air Force, 81 Fed. Appx. 342, 344 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP):

Given (i) the fact that Mr. Stevens was diagnosed as being alcohol dependent and that he did not present an evaluation from a licensed professional who considered all the facts and found him fit for duty; and (ii) the fact that Mr. Stevens refused to enter a treatment program (which would have allowed him to remain at work), the agency had no choice but to remove him from his position. Quite simply, given Mr. Stevens' condition and his refusal to be treated, the agency was not required to wait and take the chance that at some point, while under the influence of alcohol, Mr. Stevens would harm himself or others or impede the mission of his organization.
