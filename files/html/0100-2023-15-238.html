a. Overtime

A fraudulent claim for overtime supports discipline. Ahles v. DOJ, INS, 768 F.2d 327 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (distinguishing Wise v. United States, 603 F.2d 182 (Ct. Cl. 1979)); see Broughton v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 357, 360 (1987) (removal for failing to obtain authorization for 573 hours of overtime, ignoring time and attendance regulations, and reporting more overtime than actually worked); Rohn v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 157, 159 (1986) (removal for charges including the receipt of pay for overtime not worked). If an employee is charged with willful efforts to defraud the government by filing false claims for overtime compensation, the determinative issue is not whether overtime was properly authorized, but whether the appellant, who put in a claim for overtime, worked it in good faith. Beer v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 53, 58, 2 MSPB 226, 229 (1980); see Mendelson v. Macy, 356 F.2d 796, 798 (D.C. Cir. 1966) (administrative finding that the failure to claim compensation for some time worked does not exclude the possibility of a fraudulent claim for time not worked; excessive claims may have been deliberately made to offset unclaimed overtime). Under the False Claims Act, the focus of the inquiry is upon the risk of wrongful payment, not the result achieved. The public fisc need not be impaired. "[I]n deciding whether a given false statement is a claim or demand for payment, a court should look to see if, within the payment scheme, the statement has the practical purpose and effect, and poses the attendant risk, of inducing wrongful payment." United States v. Rivera, 55 F.3d 703, 709-10 (1st Cir. 1995).

Leatherbury v. Dept. of Army, 105 MSPR 405, 410-13 ¶¶ 6-10, 2007 MSPB 107 (2007) (reversed and remanded, Leatherbury v. Dept. of Army, 524 F.3d 1293 (Fed. Cir. 2008), post) (following remand, Leatherbury v. Dept. of Army, 110 MSPR 308, 2008 MSPB 308 (2008)), sustained discharge of an employee for a false claim of overtime, with discussion of the circumstantial evidence:

Under Charge 1, the agency alleged that the appellant filed a false claim against the government regarding alleged overtime accrued between January 1996 and December 2001 while transporting mail between The Dalles Dam, which was the administrative office for The Dalles-John Day-Willow Creek Project, and John Day Dam, which was his permanent duty station of record. A substantial majority of the travel between The Dalles Dam and John Day Dam consisted of the appellant's normal daily commute between his home and permanent duty station of record. According to the agency, the appellant, with the assistance of administrative personnel, submitted a "Time History for Dick Leatherbury" to his supervisor, Terry Armentrout, purporting to show that the appellant was owed "$24,422.43 for time spent outside the regular duty day transporting mail between the two dams between January 1996 and December 2001." Shortly after the "Time History" was submitted to Mr. Armentrout, he submitted a memorandum to the Portland District Resource Management Office (RMO), dated July 29, 2002, supporting the appellant's overtime claim. The agency supported Charge 1 with 3 specifications: (1) that the appellant "knew or should have known that Mr. Armentrout included this false information in his memorandum when he transmitted [the appellant's] overtime claim to the RMO," and that the appellant "allowed Mr. Armentrout to submit this false information to the RMO with the intent of misleading the Agency into paying [the appellant] the amount claimed in reliance on the false information"; (2) that the appellant "knew or should have known that the Time History falsely represented that [he] transported the mail during overtime hours an average of 155 hours per year," and that he "provided the Time History to Mr. Armentrout with this false information with the intent of misleading the agency into paying [him] the amount claimed in reliance on the false information"; and (3) that the appellant "did not reveal that [he was] transporting the mail during [his] normal daily commute to/from [his] duty station, and [he] represented the activity as work that required payment, with the intent of misleading the agency into paying [him] the amount claimed in reliance on the false information."

A false claims charge is similar in nature to a charge of falsification. Dogar v. Department of Defense, 95 M.S.P.R. 52, ¶ 6 (2003), aff'd, 128 Fed. Appx. 156 (Fed. Cir. 2005); see also 18 U.S.C. § 1003 (criminal statute prohibiting the making of false or fraudulent statements in order to obtain federal funds). To sustain such a charge, the agency must prove by preponderant evidence that the employee knowingly supplied incorrect information with the intention of defrauding the agency. Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 782 F.2d 975, 977 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Falsification and fraud cases often turn on proof of the element of intent, and whether intent has been proven must be resolved from the totality of the circumstances. Gager v. Department of Commerce, 99 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 7 (2005); Blake v. Department of Justice, 81 M.S.P.R. 394, ¶ 27 (1999). Although circumstantial evidence is generally used to establish intent, Riggin v. Department of Health & Human Services, 13 M.S.P.R. 50, 52 (1982), the Board must also consider the appellant's plausible explanation as an element of the totality of the circumstances, Nelson v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 314, ¶¶ 6-7 (1998). Indeed, it is the lack of any other plausible explanation that gives circumstantial evidence its probative effect. Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 M.S.P.R. 211, 226 (1995), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996). The appellant's intent may be inferred from evidence of his reckless disregard for the truth or for ascertaining the truth, though the falsity of his statements alone is not enough to sustain a charge of falsification or fraud. Dogar, 95 M.S.P.R. 52, ¶ 7. When more than one event or factual specification supports a single charge, the agency need not prove all of the specifications; rather, proof of one or more of the supporting specifications is sufficient to sustain the charge. Burroughs v. Department of the Army, 918 F.2d 170, 172 (Fed. Cir. 1990).

In the initial decision, the AJ found that, with respect to Specifications 1 and 2, the misrepresentations included in the "Time History" "were estimates, and...this was clear on their face." In making this finding, the AJ reasoned:

It would be virtually impossible for an employee to transport mail daily for more than ten years, and always do so exactly between the hours stated in Mr. Armentrout's memorandum; or to take annual, sick and holiday leave on exactly the same number of days each year for six consecutive years, and be prevented from transporting mail for "miscellaneous" reasons on the same, round number of days (20) in each of those years, as stated in Ms. Mincks' "Time History." Absent further inquiry it would be unreasonable to take such assertions as anything other than estimates.

The AJ also cited the hourly overtime pay rates set forth in the "Time History" for the period in question, noting, for example, that the hourly rate in 1996 was listed as greater than the hourly rates in effect between 1997 and 1999, an anomaly that the AJ deemed "nonsensical on its face."...

Based on the evidence of record, we find that the AJ erred in the reasoning which led to his findings on Specifications 1 and 2 of Charge 1, and find that the agency has proven these specifications by preponderant evidence. "Protection of the public fisc requires that those who seek public funds act with scrupulous regard for the requirements of law." Heckler v. Community Health Services, 467 U.S. 51, 63 (1984). Here, it is apparent from the hearing testimony and evidence of record that the appellant, in conjunction with his supervisor, Mr. Armentrout, decided that the agency owed the appellant substantial compensation, likely motivated by ongoing "[u]nion games" wherein the union made "a severe insult to [the appellant's] integrity." Despite having not kept time and attendance records by which he could have calculated the amount of overtime incurred over the preceding years, the appellant nonetheless set out to formulate a claim against the government. However, rather than calculating the time figures himself, the only person in a position to possibly know the precise amount of overtime worked, the appellant transferred the task of completing the "Time History" to administrative personnel, who would have had no way of knowing how much time he actually spent working overtime. The appellant stated at the hearing that he merely reviewed the figures included in the "Time History" to determine if the figures were "reasonable," not to determine if they were "accurate," believing that calculating accurate figures is what the agency "hire[s] the clerical people to do." However, the appellant also noted in his telephone interview with Ms. Deborah Chenowith, the proposing official, that he "wanted to leave it to the [administrative] people...so [he] can say they did it." Such actions evince, at the least, a reckless disregard for the truth or for ascertaining the truth. Moreover, nowhere in the "Time History" did the appellant, or anyone else, note that the figures and calculations presented in the document, apart from perhaps the interest rates cited and the appellant's 1996 salary, were mere estimates; instead, the "Time History" appears to represent an exact accounting of the overtime compensation owed to the appellant between 1996 and 2001. The dollar figures presented in the "Time History" were calculated to the exact penny, without any hint or acknowledgment that the figures were anything less than a complete, accurate, and truthful representation of the overtime compensation owed to the appellant.

In sum, the appellant sought overtime compensation despite having kept no record of the overtime worked, which would seem the only reasonable way by which he could have based his overtime claims. Lacking the guidance of records, the appellant sought the assistance of third parties in formulating his claim, even though he was the only person in a position to know the precise amount of overtime worked. Whether the appellant sought the assistance of administrative personnel to promote the "accuracy" of the calculations, or to shield himself from potential criticism for wrongdoing, the particular circumstances of this appeal support a finding that it "is more likely to be true than untrue" that the appellant acted with reckless disregard for the truth or for ascertaining the truth with respect to Specifications 1 and 2. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(c) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(c)] ; see also Dogar, 95 M.S.P.R. Thus, because the agency proved by preponderant evidence that the appellant knowingly supplied incorrect information with the intention of defrauding the agency, we find that the agency has proven Specifications 1 and 2 of Charge 1.

The Federal Circuit disagreed with the Board's analysis in Leatherbury, finding no fault with the submission of an estimate of overtime, in Leatherbury v. Dept. of Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1301-02 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (following remand, Leatherbury v. Dept. of Army, 110 MSPR 308, 2008 MSPB 308 (2008) (mitigating removal to a reprimand)):

We are at a loss to understand how an employee could be said to engage in misconduct by submitting an overtime claim based on an estimate so long as the estimate was reasonable. In a related context, courts have approved of estimates to satisfy an employee's burden of proof in cases brought under the Fair Labor Standards Act ("FLSA") seeking unpaid wages or overtime compensation. See, e.g., Anderson v. Mt. Clemens Pottery Co., 328 U.S. 680, 687-88 (1946) superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in United States v. Cook, 795 F.2d 987, 990-91 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Arias v. U.S. Serv. Indus., Inc., 80 F.3d 509, 511 (D.C. Cir. 1996). In cases where an employer has failed to keep accurate records in accordance with FLSA requirements:

an employee has carried out his burden if he proves that he has in fact performed work for which he was improperly compensated and if he produces sufficient evidence to show the amount and extent of that work as a matter of just and reasonable inference. The burden then shifts to the employer to come forward with evidence of the precise amount of work performed or with evidence to negative the reasonableness of the inference to be drawn from the employee's evidence. If the employer fails to produce such evidence, the court may then award damages to the employee, even though the result be only approximate.

Anderson, 328 U.S. at 687-88 (emphases added). We conclude that Leatherbury was entitled to submit an estimate for his overtime claim.
