a. Disability Discrimination; Impairment

An allegation that a discharge resulted from disability discrimination is not to be taken into account by an AJ as a mitigating factor; it is to be treated as an allegation of an affirmative defense to be established by the appellant and supported by a preponderance of the evidence. McClarty v. USPS, 15 MSPR 199, 201 (1983). If an appellant establishes discrimination as an affirmative defense, the action is to be set aside, not mitigated. See Sublette v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 82, 86 (1995), followed in Lewin v. DOJ, 74 MSPR 294, 300 n.4 (1997). Although an allegation of discrimination is not, a mitigating factor, the Board considers for purposes of mitigation factors such as mental condition or potential for rehabilitation that are bound up in the affirmative defense, Walsh v. USPS, 74 MSPR 627, 638-39 (1997):

We find some merit, too, to the claim that the appellant's alcohol-related "stress" should have been considered. The CAJ cited McClarty v. USPS, 15 MSPR 199, 201 (1983), for the proposition that "the Board will not consider as a mitigating factor an allegation connected to an affirmative defense of discrimination based on alcoholism." This overreads the case. In McClarty, the administrative judge did not consider the claim that the appellant's absences were due to alcohol problems as an affirmative defense of disability discrimination. Instead, he discussed it solely in his analysis of the penalty. It was this narrow focus that the Board held to be error. Moreover, to the extent that the case may correctly be read as the CAJ did, we note that in the intervening years since it was issued, under the appropriate circumstances, the Board has often considered unsuccessful claims of discrimination as mitigating factors.

For example, in Colon v. Dept. of the Navy, 58 MSPR 190, 204 (1993), the Board held that the appellant's emotional condition caused the misconduct for which she had been removed, and although it could not be accommodated as a disability, it was a relevant consideration in the penalty determination. In Thompson v. Dept. of Justice, 51 MSPR 43, 49-50 (1991), we found that the appellant's conduct was so egregious that he was not "qualified" for his position under the Rehabilitation Act, but even his disqualification for retention in his position did not mean that he could not be retained in federal service. Under the facts of Slaughter v. Dept. of Agric., 56 MSPR 349, 354-56 (1993), where the appellant's emotional problems did not rise to the level of a disability, and while they were not found to be a sufficient basis for mitigation, we held that they were, nonetheless, to be considered in mitigation. Similarly, in Ajanaku v. Dept. of Defense, 44 MSPR 350, 356 (1990), although the appellant's emotional disorder did not provide a sufficient basis for a finding of disability discrimination, it was [,] nonetheless, "entitled to considerable weight as a mitigating factor." Finally, in Sublette v. Dept. of the Army, 68 MSPR 82, 89-90 (1995), while the appellant did not show that he was mentally disabled, he did prove that before it removed him, the agency was aware that he had some degree of mental impairment; that fact, along with his rehabilitation efforts and other factors should have been considered in mitigation of the proposed action. But see Barry v. Dept. of the Treasury, 71 MSPR 283, 286-87 (1996) (holding that stress per se should not generally be found a mitigating factor as to a charge of drug-related misconduct).

Although none of these cases involves an alcoholic employee, what each of them has in common is a finding that even an unsuccessful claim of discrimination need not be considered only in the context of the appellant's affirmative defense, and that the facts underlying it may properly be reviewed under the criteria of Douglas, 5 MSPR at 306. To the extent that they are relevant to a Douglas factor, and were a causative factor in the charged misconduct or poor performance, they remain relevant in the penalty analysis.

See Bal v. Dept. of Navy, 729 Fed. Appx. 923, 929-30 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) (Judge Mayer dissenting) (improvement in medical condition is a factor showing potential for rehabilitation); Bishopp v. Dept. of Air Force, 75 MSPR 33, 40 (1997) (appellant's mental condition did not qualify as a disability sufficient to establish an affirmative defense to the charged misconduct; however, the appellant's mental state was "[a] factor weighing heavily in favor of mitigation.").

The Board has held that evidence that an employee's medical condition or mental impairment played a part in the charged conduct is ordinarily entitled to considerable weight as a mitigating factor. See Walker v. Dept. of the Navy, 59 MSPR 309, 324-25 (1993). Even where the condition does not rise to the level of a disability, if the agency knew about it before taking the action at issue, we may consider the condition in determining the appropriate penalty. See Sublette v. Dept. of the Army, 68 MSPR 82, 89 (1995). The appellant presented unrebutted evidence that he made the agency aware of his conditions before both of the actions at issue in his appeal. While the appellant's physical and mental conditions obviously led to the deteriorating relationship between him and his supervisor, there is no evidence that the appellant was unable to understand or follow the directions that he is charged with ignoring, and therefore his conditions cannot account for his refusal to access the IDRS system or to make the field calls as directed. We find that the evidence that the appellant willfully chose not to follow these directions, coupled with the fact that he had been issued a letter of reprimand and a three-day suspension for other failures to follow directions shortly before this action was brought, outweighs the appellant's evidence that he was suffering from a back problem and various psychiatric problems. See Redfearn v. Dept. of Labor, 58 MSPR 307, 316 (1993) (an employee's deliberate refusal to follow supervisory instructions constitutes serious misconduct that cannot properly be condoned). Accordingly, we agree with the administrative judge that a 30-day suspension was reasonable under the circumstances.
