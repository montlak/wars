III. TRAINING

Proposals requiring management to train employees affect management's right to assign work. PASS and Dept. of Transp., FAA, 61 FLRA 97, 99 (2005) (determining to be nonnegotiable, as infringements on right to assign work and nonnegotiable as arrangements proposals precluding use of arguably unqualified personnel as trainers and precluding cancelation of employee's leave to attend training course). Proposals requiring management to provide formal training, or to assign employees periodically to specific types of training programs, are outside the duty to bargain. AFGE Local 2094 and VAMC, New York, N.Y., 22 FLRA 710, 718-19 (1986) (Proposal 6). Proposals for agencies to make specific training assignments on employee request are outside the duty to bargain; the assignment of training constitutes an assignment of work and its negotiation is inconsistent with management's rights under § 7106(a)(2)(B). A proposal precluding training for fully satisfactory employees is nonnegotiable. NFFE and Dept. of Army, Ft. Monmouth, 13 FLRA 426, 429-30 (1983). Qualifying phrases, e.g., "where appropriate," "insofar as possible," and "normally," do not make training requirements negotiable. The qualifiers still impose conditions on management's exercise of its reserved right to assign work. Illinois Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, N. Chicago, 27 FLRA 714, 729-30 (1987) (Proposal 5). Provision 12, calling for agreement of the employer to provide training fairly and equitably to all employees, infringed the right to assign work. It was nonnegotiable when the union did not advance the proposal as an arrangement or procedure. AFGE Local 2119 and Army, Mfg. & Tech. Center, Rock Island, 72 FLRA 706, 709 (2022) (Member Abbott concurring). AFGE Local 1625 and Navy Fleet Combat Training Ctr., Dam Neck, 28 FLRA 1134, 1136 (1987), adopting the language of the earlier decisions, also concluded that statutory and regulatory provisions concerning training do not create the basis for bargaining over training proposals that go beyond the statutory requirements. It was also determined in AFGE Local 1625 that training proposals were not appropriate arrangements. The proposals did not address adverse effects on employees directly resulting from the exercise of a management right. Training proposals may be nonnegotiable for other reasons, for example, because they relate to military aspects of National Guard employment, not within the ambit of conditions of employment. Delaware Chap., ACT and Delaware NG, 28 FLRA 1030, 1035-36 (1987).

FLRA decided a proposal was nonnegotiable that appeared to require that the agency provide training to unit employees as well as to other employees. Under FEA Stateside Region and DODEA, 58 FLRA 465, 467-68 (2003), the proposal was not permissively negotiable and it was not negotiable as a procedure or as an arrangement:

In NAGE, Local R5-184, 51 FLRA 386, 393 (1995), the Authority set out the sequence of analysis it would follow in cases where a union does not dispute that a proposal affects management's rights under § 7106(a) of the Statute, but claims that the proposal is bargainable under § 7106(b)(1) of the Statute. Under that sequence, the Authority first examines the contention that a proposal is bargainable under § 7106(b)(1). If the matter concerns a subject set forth in § 7106(b)(1), then the Authority does not address § 7106(a) further, because subsection (b)(1) is an exception to subsection (a). Id. See also Professional Airways Systems Specialist, 56 FLRA 798, 802-03 (2000). The Union does not dispute that the proposal affects management's rights under § 7106(a) or claim that the proposal constitutes a procedure or an appropriate arrangement within the meaning of § 7106(b)(2) or (b)(3). Consequently, the issue before us is whether, as argued by the Union, the proposal is a "means of performing work" under § 7106(b)(1).

By its terms, the proposal would permit the Union to select one-half of the committee/training members who are to come from the bargaining unit." The committees and training sessions are those established by the Agency, an organizational element with which the Union has no representational rights. The committees include those involved with curriculum development, as well as those addressing other personnel policies, practices and/or terms and conditions of employment.

In support of its assertion that the proposal is a means of performing work under § 7106(b)(1), the Union relies on Proposal 18 in OEA [Overseas Education Ass'n., Inc., 29 FLRA 734 (1987)] which, according to the Union, allowed the union "to select at least one member per committee" and involved the union and employees "in the development of curriculum changes." Response at 14.

Briefly stated, Proposal 18 in OEA required the agency to make every reasonable effort to involve unit employees in the development of curriculum changes. Where curriculum changes were accomplished with the involvement of unit employees, the proposal further required the agency to consider union nominees for committee representation and to permit the union to select one member per committee. The Authority found that the proposal would involve unit employees and the union in the development of curriculum changes. See OEA, 29 FLRA at 765. The Authority also found that the proposal was negotiable at the agency's election under § 7106(b)(1) insofar as "the development of the curriculum constitutes the means by which the [a]gency's mission of providing a quality education is performed." Id. However, the Authority determined that the portion of the proposal giving the union "the right to select at least one (1) member per committee[,]" violated the right to assign work under § 7106(a)(2)(B) of the Statute by requiring the assignment of specific duties to particular employees. Id. At 767.

We agree with the Union that insofar as the proposal would permit the Union to select employees to serve on curriculum development committees, the proposal concerns the means of performing work under § 7106(b)(1) of the Statute. See id. However, we conclude that the proposal is nonetheless outside the duty to bargain as explained below.

The Union's contention that the proposal concerns the means of performing work under § 7106(b)(1) is limited to committees concerned with curriculum development. The Union makes no similar claim with respect to committees that involve other personnel policies, practices, and/or terms and conditions of employment, despite the fact that those committees are also encompassed by the proposal. Likewise, the Union makes no claim under § 7106(b)(1) with respect to the training sessions that are encompassed by the proposal. Additionally, we note that the Union makes no request for severance of the proposal as permitted under § 2424.2(h) of the Authority's Regulations. See, e.g., AFGE, Local 1985, 55 FLRA 1145, 1149 (1999).

As we stated above, the Union does not dispute the Agency's contention that the proposal affects the exercise of management's rights. In particular, the Union does not challenge the Agency's assertion that the proposal would impermissibly allow Union participation on committees used by management as an integral part of its deliberative process. Furthermore, the Union does not dispute the Agency's contention that the proposal would not allow management to consider its operational needs in assigning work to employees. Under these circumstances, we find that the proposal affects the exercise of management's rights under § 7106(a) of the Statute. See, e.g., Nat'l Assn of Agriculture Employees, 48 FLRA 599, 605 (1993) (proposal allowing union to select employees to serve on panels affected right to assign work); AFGE, Local 1923 and U.S. Dep't of Health and Human Services, HCFA, Baltimore, Md., 44 FLRA 1405, 1442 (1992) (Proposal 10) (where union participation on selection panels and other joint labor-management committees allows the union to participate in the discussions and deliberations leading to decisions involving the exercise of management's rights, the union's participation interferes with those rights).

As portions of the proposal affect the exercise of management's rights, and in the absence of a request to sever the portion of the proposal that concerns the means of performing work under § 7106(b)(1) of the Statute, we conclude that the proposal is outside the duty to bargain in its entirety. See, AFGE, Local 1985, 55 FLRA 1145 (Proposals I and 3) (1999) (absent request to sever, proposals that, in part, affected management's § 7106(a) rights, or were otherwise nonnegotiable, were found outside the duty to bargain in their entirety). Accordingly, we will dismiss the petition for review.

Although FLRA took pains to reach the result, AFGE Local 3407 and DOD, DMA, 39 FLRA 557, 566-67 (1991), determined to be negotiable Proposal 2, calling on the agency to agree that it would provide classes for unit members subject to drug testing (the classes were to explain drug testing as well as the accuracy and nature of the testing), and to provide a question and answer session. FLRA noted its decisional line reserving the employer's exclusive right to determine instruction for employees concerning how they should perform their duties and responsibilities. Those proposals directly interfered with the right to assign work. Another group of decisions determined to be negotiable proposals calling on management to provide employees with information or provide to them notice of various actions. The classes encompassed by the proposal in AFGE Local 3407 had nothing to do with the manner that the employees performed their work. Instead the classes were designed as means to convey information to employees. Although the classes might, if conducted during duty time, interfere with the assignment of work, that alone did not make the proposals nonnegotiable; many substantive proposals require the use of some duty time. Another "informational" training program--concerning the resources of the agency's EEO Department--was negotiable in NFFE Local 1430 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Facilities Eng'g Command, N. Div., Philadelphia, 39 FLRA 581, 588-89 (1991) (Proposal 3), for the same reasons outlined in AFGE Local 3407. See NAGE, FUSE, Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 42 FLRA 730, 773 (1991) (Proposals 10 and 11 were negotiable: providing for drug-related training of union officials and stewards on drug testing program; providing training for employees on evils of drug abuse). Proposals that skirt the issue of required training may be negotiable. In NFFE Local 1430 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Facilities Eng'g Command, N. Div., Philadelphia, 39 FLRA 581, 586-87 (1991), Proposals 2 and 5 defined the employees who would be eligible for training if management decided to offer training. The proposals were negotiable. They did not require training. They did not prevent management from assigning someone to training who was not encompassed within the definitional language of the proposals. Purely procedural proposals concerning training should be negotiable. A proposal was within the scope of bargaining that required the employer to record completed training in employees' official personnel files. NFFE, Council of VA Locals and VA, 31 FLRA 360, 442 (1988). A proposal that permitted "non-examining time" for training on how to fill out agency reports was negotiable as an arrangement. The agency determined whether to provide the training, how much training to provide, and the type of training to provide. POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 56 FLRA 69, 85 (2000).
