b. Relevance, Overbroad Request

Motions to compel discovery will not be granted if the information sought is irrelevant to issues under appeal. Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 170, 174, 7 MSPB 649 (1981); cf. Gromo v. OPM, 42 MSPR 530, 532 (1989) (directing OPM to produce to the appellant its "Policy Guidelines" providing instructions governing the recovery of annuity overpayments); Tinsley v. OPM, 34 MSPR 70, 75 n.8 (1987) (internal OPM issuances were discoverable; they are not publicly available; failure to produce them was not harmful, however, since the appellant did not show how the documents would aid evaluation of OPM's suitability determination). The requirement of relevancy should be tempered with the caution that under Board rules, the scope of discovery includes information that appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. 5 CFR 1201.72(b) (2022). See SSA v. Anyel, 58 MSPR 261, 270-73 (1993) (citing Board rule; directing production of case records and decisions in an action against the ALJ for alleged adjudication deficiencies). Johnson, supra, also determined that discovery requests are not to be evaluated against the right to review material relied upon by an agency to support a proposed adverse action. Evidence assisting in case planning may or may not be admissible, but under Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b), it is discoverable. Nevertheless, for a discovery request to be enforced, there must be some threshold appearance that it is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Without that showing, the AJ will not be faulted for declining to direct discovery that the agency resists. See Wagner v. EPA, 54 MSPR 447, 452 (1992) ("The appellant has not even described the evidence he had hoped to obtain on discovery or explained how his rights were prejudiced by the alleged denial of this evidence."); Nortz v. VA, 47 MSPR 526, 532 (1991) ("A review of the appellant's discovery requests shows that those requests were general in nature and did not elicit specific information that would lead to admissible evidence of the Board's jurisdiction.").

The Board is occasionally fuzzy concerning the showing required to establish the right to discovery. Consider a passage from Fletcher v. USPS, 39 MSPR 380, 384 (1989):

...The appellant's motion did not show with any specificity the relevancy and materiality of the information sought, as required by 5 CFR § 1201.73(c)(2)(i). A mere assertion of a general, rather than a particularized, need for information is inadequate. Cf. Carter v. Dept. of Labor, 29 MSPR 500, 502 (1985) (employee not entitled to have agency produce documents absent showing by employee how such information was relevant to specific issues); Cornelius v. OPM, 30 MSPR 149, 151-52 (1986) (motion to compel denied where request was overly broad, burdensome and immaterial).

Although the appellant indicates in his petition for review that he might have been able to prove his allegation of the involuntariness of his resignation if the motion to compel had been granted, he has still not explained with specificity what evidence would enable him to meet his burden of proof of jurisdiction. Thus, the appellant has not shown how the absence of the requested information prejudiced his substantive rights.

On occasion, Board rulings are more attenuated. See, e.g., Trotter v. USPS, 91 MSPR 282, 286 ¶ 14 (2002) ("Some or all of the documents and requested answers to interrogatories might lead to discovery of additional information regarding the jurisdictional issues. We therefore find that the appellant is entitled to discovery, and that his motion to compel discovery should be granted.").

A showing of error concerning a discovery request may depend on how well the AJ is informed about the request. In Leftridge v. USPS, 56 MSPR 340, 346 (1993), the appellant charged that the AJ failed to compel the agency to produce rules and regulations governing its investigations. The appellant explained in a motion to compel that he needed the information to show inconsistencies or inaccuracies in the investigation leading to charges against him. The AJ denied the motion, "absent any allegation that the investigative process used here denied the appellant due process rights." The appellant failed to make corrections or additions to the AJ's order or to clarify his request. The Board saw no error.

Kiser v. Dept. of Educ., 66 MSPR 372, 381-82 (1995), conducted a point-by-point analysis of discovery requests determined to have been improperly denied by the AJ in an appeal alleging marital status discrimination against a probationary supervisor:

On March 25, 1994, the appellant filed a motion to compel responses to various discovery requests, including interrogatories, requests for documents, and deposition requests. Interrogatories four and five asked the agency to identify each individual who had a discussion with Van Riper concerning the appellant's performance during his probationary period and the nature of the discussion. The administrative judge denied the motion as to these interrogatories on grounds of relevance, but she did not explain why Van Riper's discussions about the appellant's performance were not relevant. Because the agency's primary defense to the appellant's claim of marital discrimination was that he had been demoted because of poor performance, the administrative judge's ruling is erroneous.

The administrative judge also denied the appellant's request for a copy of OPM's background investigation on relevancy grounds. Again, since Van Riper stated that the information in that investigation concerning the appellant's alleged propensity for the pursuit of women raised concerns about his performance, caused her to request a supplemental OPM investigation, and also motivated her own personal investigation, the administrative judge's denial of this motion is unsupportable, even given the wide degree of discretion afforded administrative judges in discovery matters. See D'Elia v. Department of the Treasury, 60 MSPR 226, 236 (1993). We also find that the administrative judge's denial of the appellant's motion to compel the deposition of a number of witnesses--Kinney, Biehl, Bass, Thomas, Eager, Eppinger, Wiley, and Purdey--was unduly restrictive given the circumstances of this case. Although the administrative judge stated that the appellant's motion did not set forth any facts or basis upon which she could make a ruling concerning the relevancy of any of the proposed deponents, the appellant did discuss the relevance of the testimony of each of these individuals.

On March 30, 1994, the appellant filed a motion to compel Van Riper to respond to the following questions and document requests: (1) how she had rated Phillips for his 1993-94 midyear rating; (2) whether Van Riper was aware of Phillips's marital status; (3) whether Van Riper was aware of allegations concerning other employees in the Office of Investigations who were engaged in intimate personal relationships; (4) questions concerning the appellant's relationships which were set forth in OPM's background investigation reports; (5) questions concerning Van Riper's handwritten notes of her meetings with Phillips and the appellant, as well as meetings with other employees which involved her own personal investigation of the appellant; (6) that the agency provide copies of the handwritten notes; and (7) that Van Riper identify with whom she discussed the allegation that the appellant was engaged in a personal relationship with an informant.

In an order issued April 7, 1994, the administrative judge denied the appellant's request for items 1-5 and 7 above as not "in any way relevant." This conclusion, also given with no explanation, is simply not supportable. Those requests are obviously ones that are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. See 5 CFR § 1201.72(a). As to the appellant's request for copies of Van Riper's handwritten notes of conversations with various employees regarding the appellant, the administrative judge accepted the agency's documents for in camera review and, finding "only" two discrepancies between the handwritten notes and the typewritten transcriptions, ordered the agency to provide to the appellant only those two notes. We find this ruling to be an abuse of discretion. On remand, the administrative judge shall direct the agency to provide copies of the remaining notes.

Broad discovery requests to nonparties may not find favor with the Board, D'Elia v. Dept. of Treasury, 60 MSPR 226, 236-37 (1993):

The appellant filed a motion to compel OSC to produce nonprivileged, but unspecified, "factual material" from its complaint file. The administrative judge found that the appellant's motion lacked sufficient specificity. We find no abuse of discretion in the administrative judge's decision to deny the motion on the basis that the appellant failed to specifically identify the "factual material" he sought. See Head v. OPM, 53 MSPR 421, 422 (1992) (appellant failed to identify "relevant material" he sought from the Office of Personnel Management; administrative judge's failure to rule on his discovery request did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights).

Further, under 5 CFR 1201.72(b), discovery requests directed at a nonparty Federal agency must be "limited to information that appears directly material to the issues involved in the appeal." On petition for review, the appellant argues that documents in OSC's file could show that his complaint to OSC disclosed violations of law, rule or regulation, gross mismanagement, and gross waste of funds, in addition to abuse of authority. The complaint submitted by the agency to OSC on the appellant's behalf, however, is in the record and shows that the appellant asserted only abuse of authority in that complaint. The appellant does not contend that he submitted any supplemental complaints to OSC, and copies of other letters he filed with it that he included in his appeal also fail to assert additional bases. Thus, the discovery request to OSC does not concern documents that appear directly material to determining what the appellant alleged in his complaint to OSC, seeing as the relevant documents are already in the record. Thus, we find no abuse of discretion by the judge in denying the appellant's motion to compel discovery of unspecified "factual material" in the Special Counsel's file. See Nortz v. VA, 47 MSPR 526, 532-33 (1991) (discovery requests were general in nature and did not elicit specific information that would lead to admissible evidence of the Board's jurisdiction).

For routine discovery requests challenged as too broad by the agency, the approach should not be to provide no response, Ryan v. Dept. of Air Force, 113 MSPR 27, 33 ¶ 17, 2009 MSPB 235 (2009):

The agency objected to each of the appellant's 21 requests for admission and 22 requests for the production of documents. It appears the administrative judge denied the appellant's motions to compel in their entirety. However, at least some of the appellant's discovery requests clearly meet the standard of seeking "information that appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence." See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.72(a). For example, the appellant's first document request seeks "all e-mails or any other correspondence related to or referring to [the]appellant (directly or indirectly) or related to one of the appellant's disclosures from 01 August 2002 to current date. The agency might argue that this request is overly broad, but that would not excuse the agency from providing documents that are clearly relevant to the matters at issue in this appeal.

The AJ need not require production through discovery of material concerning general agency management unless there is a showing that the information is relevant and material. Carter v. Dept. of Labor, 29 MSPR 500, 502 (1985); cf. Harper v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3471) (the Board did not abuse discretion in denying discovery on a whistleblowing claim when the appellant could not establish he made a protected disclosure); Fairfield v. OPM, SF-0845-15-0738-I-1 (NP 6/28/2016) ("The appellant claimed below that the requested material would show that OPM was careless in reaching its reconsideration decision. The Board reviews OPM's reconsideration decision on its merits, rather than on the background of the individual who prepared the decision, and, therefore, we agree with the administrative judge that the appellant failed to demonstrate that the information sought was relevant to the issues in his appeal.").
