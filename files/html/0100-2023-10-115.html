4. Unofficial Transcript

There is only one official record of the hearing proceedings--the one taken through the Board's official reporter or the official recording contractor, however denominated. 5 CFR 1201.53(a) (2022).

Current Board practice generally results in posting on the e-filing system audio recordings of a hearing. In the past, some parties came to the hearing wired for sound. On the subject of unofficial records, the Judges' Handbook states at Chapter 10, section 18.c.(3)(a):

Recording of Proceedings by Individuals. At the discretion of the AJ, if special circumstances appear to warrant it, participants may be permitted to record the hearing. However, they must be advised in writing or on the record at the hearing that their recording is not an official record. In light of the Board's general policy that electronic devices are not to be operated during hearings, such exceptions should be rare.

The Board tolerated unofficial transcripts to be used in PFRs, presumably if the appellant desired to show that an AJ ignored some testimony or made an erroneous credibility determination. Although the Board did not specify the nature of the "unofficial" transcript, it would likely be the result of an attempt by an appellant or counsel to transcribe the recording of a hearing, as opposed to a transcript produced by a "court reporter." If an unofficial transcript is used in Board proceedings, the opposing party may attempt to demonstrate inaccuracies in the transcription as part of the reply offered by that party. See Fidelibus v. DLA, 21 MSPR 280, 281 (1984), and 24 MSPR 198, 201 n.2 (1984). The Board may compare an unofficial transcript with its audio recording of the hearing. See Smith v. DHS, PH-0752-12-0506-I-2 n.3 (NP 10/22/2014) ("We have compared portions of the quoted testimony to the recording of the proceedings on the hearing compact disc and note that his transcription of the testimony is not a verbatim record of the proceedings. In any event, for purposes of our review, we have accepted the appellant's transcription of the testimony as accurate and considered the testimony as quoted by the appellant in our analysis.").

Of unofficial transcripts, the Board admonished in Marotta v. DHHS, 34 MSPR 252, 257 (1987):

[W]e cannot rely on the appellant's unofficial transcript of portions of the hearing testimony. The appellant's submission consists of selected excerpts, carefully drawn to support his arguments, and is not necessarily an accurate reflection of the testimony as a whole. In discussing the witnesses' testimony, we rely on tape recordings, which are the official, verbatim record of the hearing....

The Board appeared to ignore an unofficial transcript with the observation in Johnson v. DLA, 61 MSPR 601, 603 n.1 (1994):

With its petition, the agency submits portions of the hearing transcript.... See Marotta v. Department of Health & Human Services, 34 MSPR 252, 257, aff'd, 837 F.2d 1096 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table).... In the instant appeal, there is no showing that these excerpts are the official transcript of the hearing. Thus, we have not considered them.

A written transcript of the audio recording of the hearing was accepted and relied upon by the Board, although the transcript was not prepared by the Board's contract reporter, in Grant v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-14-0237-I-1 n.5 (NP 5/27/2015):

Generally, the oral recording made by a court reporter is the official transcript of a hearing. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.53(a). A written transcript will be accepted as the official hearing record when prepared by the court reporter. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.53(b). Here, the agency obtained the oral recording of the hearing and retained a second company to prepare a written transcript. Although the recording prepared by the court reporter retained by the Board remains the official hearing record, we cite the written transcript prepared by the second company for convenience. We have verified the accuracy of the portions of the written transcript cited by listening to the relevant portions of the oral recording.

In Morrison v. NASA, 65 MSPR 348, 353 (1994), the Board declined to consider a segment of a transcript offered by the appellant that the Board characterized as "incomplete and unverified." See Briones v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 8, 10 (1997) (declining to consider representative's tapes and unofficial transcript; it is unclear whether the Board had available the AJ's recording of prehearing conference); Moore v. Dept. of Transp., 64 MSPR 298, 301 (1994) ("We have, however, considered the portion of the hearing transcript in the record below which is signed and certified by the court reporter.").
