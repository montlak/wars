6. Issues Raised Through Exceptions Must First Be Raised With the Judge

5 CFR 2429.5 (2022) provides:

The Authority will not consider any evidence, factual assertions, arguments (including affirmative defenses), requested remedies, or challenges to an awarded remedy that could have been, but were not, presented in the proceedings before the Regional Director, Hearing Officer, Administrative Law Judge, or arbitrator. The Authority may, however, take official notice of such matters as would be proper.

FLRA will not consider any issue not presented in the proceedings before the ALJ. DOJ, INS and AFGE Local 2509, 14 FLRA 578, 579 n.4 (1984); see Long Beach Naval Shipyard and FEMTC, 44 FLRA 1021, 1036 (1992) ("as these arguments were not previously raised before the Judge in this proceeding, they will not be considered"); Marine Corps, Camp Butler and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, Pac. Region, 29 FLRA 1068, 1069 (1987) ("Under section 2429.5 of our Rules and Regulations, we will not consider issues which have not been presented in proceedings before a judge."). An exception is made if the issue initially raised before FLRA concerns its jurisdiction over the case. Long Beach Naval Shipyard and FEMTC, 25 FLRA 1002, 1002 n.1 (1987); see VA, VAMC Asheville and AFGE Local 446, 57 FLRA 681, 683 (2002) ("Parties may raise arguments regarding the Authority's jurisdiction at any stage of the Authority's proceedings.").

FLRA will generally not consider evidence or contentions presented for the first time with exceptions to an ALJ Decision. USDA, APHIS, Plant Protection and Quarantine and Nat'l Ass'n of Agric. Employees, Branch 15, 26 FLRA 630, 636-37 (1987). The exclusionary rule was applied in VAMC Phoenix and AFGE, 60 FLRA 405, 407 (2004):

The rule barring consideration by FLRA of exceptions not preserved through presentation of issues to the ALJ has an exception: a party cannot fairly be charged with having to anticipate a point of law that did not exist at the point the record closed with the ALJ and before issuance of new law from FLRA. See DHS, Border & Transp. Security and NTEU, 61 FLRA 272, 275 (2005). But see DOJ, Bureau Of Prisons, FCI Coleman and AFGE Local 506, 67 FLRA 632 (2014), post. According to SSA and AFGE Council 220, 69 FLRA 363, 365 (2016) (Member Pizzella concurring), the regulation does not preclude consideration of "Union's arguments...in response to the Judge's findings and, thus, could not have been raised prior to the Judge's decision."

In a refusal to bargain case, FLRA would not consider management's negotiability contentions raised for the first time in the agency's exceptions to the decision of the ALJ. VA and AFGE, 24 FLRA 9, 11 (1986); see Dept. of Transp., FAA and PASS, 55 FLRA 951, 956 (1999) (agency could not first raise issue of management rights in exceptions); US Penitentiary, Leavenworth and AFGE Local 919, 55 FLRA 704, 716 (1999) (FLRA will not consider party's argument that it had no bargaining obligation prior to instituting change because subject matter was "covered by" parties' negotiated agreement if that defense was not raised before judge); DOJ, Fed. Bureau of Prisons and AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 1661, 55 FLRA 201, 204 (1999) (agency precluded from raising management rights issue not raised with judge; agency could not reverse position on issue it argued to judge: "changes of position on an issue dealt with below are covered under the general rule prohibiting the raising of new issues on appeal"); Indian Health Serv. and Navajo Nation Health Care Employees, 53 FLRA 1161, 1162 (1998) (declining to consider affirmative defense of bargaining waiver raised for first time in exceptions).

The same holds true for newly raised issues in ULP litigation over union information requests. FDA, Mid-Atl. Region and AFGE Council No. 242, 48 FLRA 424, 438 (1993) (also declining to consider through exceptions matter admitted by respondent in answer to ULP complaint); see Nat'l Park Serv., Nat'l Capital Region, Park Police and Police Ass'n of Dist. of Columbia, 48 FLRA 1151, 1163 n.10 (1993) (FLRA refuses, during adjudication of exceptions to ALJ recommended decision, to take official notice of agency regulations because they were not cited or addressed in prior proceedings). Objections to a remedy should apparently also be raised with the judge and not for the first time with FLRA. See DHS, Border & Transp. Security Directorate and AFGE Local 2012, 60 FLRA 993 (2005); cf. DOD, Defense Distribution Depot and AFGE Local 1945, 61 FLRA 108, 109 (2005) (FLRA would not consider agency's objection to a status quo remedy when objection was not first raised with judge in opposition to General Counsel's unopposed summary judgment motion).

Dept. of Air Force, Davis-Monthan AFB and AFGE Local 2924, 64 FLRA 355, 358 (2009), declined to consider an election of remedies issue that was not first raised with the ALJ in a contract repudiation case that had remedial elements involved in MSPB appeals by the adversely affected employees:

In its opposition to the GC's cross-exception, the Respondent argued that the Judge erred in failing to dismiss the ULP complaint on the grounds that it was barred by § 7116(d) of the Statute. This claim relates to the validity of the underlying decision and, as such, we construe it as an exception to that decision. See Soc. Sec. Admin., Office of Labor Mgmt. Relations, 60 FLRA 66, 67 (2004) (Authority construed as exceptions arguments raised in union's opposition to agency exceptions). Under the Authority's Regulations, exceptions to a Judge's decision must be filed within twenty-five days after service of that decision. 5 C.F.R. § 2423.40. The Respondent did not raise the § 7116(d) argument until its opposition, which was filed well after the twenty-five-day filing limit.[6] As the exception was not timely filed, we dismiss it.[7] See Fort McClellan Educ. Ass'n, 56 FLRA 644, 645 n.3 (2000) (dismissing argument raised by the agency for the first time in opposition to union exceptions).

[6] We note that the Authority has resolved jurisdictional issues raised in exceptions under § 7116(d) even though the issues were not properly raised before the judge. See U.S. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, Veterans Affairs Med. Ctr., Coatesville, Pa., 57 FLRA 663, 666 (2002) (Coatesville VA). This is similar to the Authority's treatment of the jurisdictional issue of sovereign immunity. See United States Small Bus. Admin., Wash, D.C., 51 FLRA 413, 423 n.9 (1995). See also Dep't of the Army, United States Army Commissary, Fort Benjamin Harrison, Indianapolis, Ind. v. FLRA, 56 F.3d 273, 275 (D.C. Cir. 1995). This precedent provides that jurisdictional arguments may be raised without regard to whether they were raised below. That is, the arguments may be raised without regard to exhaustion requirements. See Coatesville VA, 57 FLRA at 663. However, this precedent provides no basis for concluding that such arguments may be raised without regard to procedural requirements.

Failure to engage in full questioning of a witness may be deemed to be a failure to raise an objection with the ALJ to the termination of the testimony of that witness. FLRA will not consider that issue on an exception to the ALJ's decision. See Dept. of Labor, OSHA Region 1 and AFGE Local 948, 58 FLRA 213, 215 (2002). If an issue is raised before the regional director (through a motion to dismiss the complaint), FLRA will consider that issue through an exception even if it was not litigated before the ALJ. See VAMC, Veterans Canteen Serv., Lexington and NAGE Local R5-184, 44 FLRA 179, 186 (1992).

The Respondent argues that the status-quo remedy is inappropriate because, after the ULP hearing and the issuance of Local 506, relevant circumstances changed (the changed-circumstances argument). Specifically, the Respondent argues that it bargained over, reached agreement on, and implemented the one proposal that the Authority found negotiable in Local 506. The Respondent acknowledges that it is raising this argument for the first time in its exception. But the Respondent contends that this is because "[t]he parties were not given the opportunity to brief the [Judge] on the effect, if any, of the Authority's decision in" Local 506 or "on what[,] if any[,] actions had been taken by the parties subsequent to the issuing of [that] decision." The Respondent argues that, while the Judge took "official notice" of Local 506, he did not "re-open the record" or "request briefs on the effect of that decision." Consequently, the Respondent argues, the Judge failed to consider that it had implemented the one proposal found negotiable in Local 506, and that any return to the pre-change status quo would "disrupt the efficiency and effectiveness" of its operations and not be in the public interest.

Section 2429.5 of the Authority's Regulations states that "[t]he Authority will not consider any...arguments...or challenges to an awarded remedy that could have been, but were not, presented in the proceedings before the...Judge." Thus, if events occur after a ULP hearing that may alter the judge's decision, such as a settlement or other intervening events, a party should file a motion with the judge under § 2429.26 of the Authority's Regulations, requesting that the record be reopened to receive the new evidence.[31] In a recent decision, the Authority held that a respondent was barred from presenting an argument that the parties had settled a ULP matter prior to the issuance of the judge's decision because the parties had not informed the GC or the judge of the settlement agreement.[32]

[31] Id. § 2429.26 (permitting a judge to grant leave, upon a party's request, to file post-hearing documents).

[32] U.S. Dep't of the Air Force, Sheppard Air Force Base, Wichita Falls, Tex., 67 FLRA 509, 510 (2014) (Air Force) (Member Pizzella concurring).

Here, as stated above, the Respondent acknowledges that it did not raise its changed-circumstances argument before the Judge. And, because all of the alleged changed circumstances occurred before the Judge issued his decision, the Respondent could have raised them below. The Respondent's arguments--that the parties were not "given" an opportunity to present supplemental evidence and that the Judge erred by failing to, on his own, reopen the record and request briefs from the parties after Local 506--do not support a contrary conclusion. The Authority's precedent and Regulations support finding that the Respondent should have filed a motion requesting that the Judge reopen the record to consider its evidence of alleged changed circumstances.[34] As the Respondent failed to do so, and did not otherwise raise its changed-circumstances argument below, we find that § 2429.5 bars that argument.
