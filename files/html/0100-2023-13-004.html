a. Entry on Duty; Action by Appointing Authority; Reliance; SF-50

The question may be whether the employee actually entered on duty before the appointment was canceled. Rather than relying on the absence or presence of an SF-50 to determine whether an appointment was made, the Board looks at the totality of circumstances surrounding the alleged appointment to determine if the required last act of an official with appointment power occurred. If the appellant has not entered on duty, the individual who set the reporting date also has the authority to cancel the reporting date. Then, if the reporting date is canceled, the appointment is not effective and the individual does not qualify as an employee under 5 USC 2105(a)(3). He never engaged in the performance of a federal function at the agency that initiated the action appealed. Collazo v. Community Services Admin., 15 MSPR 188, 190 (1983) (citing Scott v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 282, 288 (1981), and NTEU v. Reagan, 663 F.2d 239 (D.C. Cir. 1981)); see Johnson v. GSA, 9 MSPR 510, 511 (1982) (withdrawn selection by another agency did not constitute an adverse action because the appellant "never engaged in the performance of a federal function at the agency which took the action being appealed"); cf. Gajete v. OPM, 180 Fed. Appx. 974 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) (entry on an SF-50 showing a tenure group is not conclusive; the Board properly considers other evidence to determine the appellant's proper employment status for purposes of retirement entitlements); Nasuti v. Dept. of State, 112 MSPR 587, 596 ¶ 19, 2009 MSPB 218 (2009) (approval of an SF-50 by an agency is a clerical documentation task that customarily occurs after the effective date of a personnel action).); cf. Luna v. VA, DA-1221-16-0223-W-2 (NP 4/12/2023) ("As the Federal Circuit has stated, an SF-50 is not a personnel action in itself, but at most is merely an after-the-fact record of a personnel action already taken. Nasuti, 376 F. App'x at 33; Scott v. Department of the Air Force, 113 M.S.P.R. 434, ¶ 8 (2010) (stating that, although the issuance of an executed SF-50 is the customary documentation for a Federal personnel action, it does not constitute the personnel action itself). Moreover, an SF-50 is not a legally operative document controlling on its face an employee's status and rights. Scott, 113 M.S.P.R. 434, ¶ 8 (citing Grigsby v. Department of Commerce, 729 F.2d 772, 776 (Fed. Cir. 1984))."); Coleman v. DHS, DA-3443-17-0273-I-1, n.5 (NP 4/6/2023) ("Although 'the SF-50 is not a legally operative document controlling on its face an employee's status and rights,' it still can be considered as evidence when determining the nature of an action. Grigsby v. Department of Commerce, 729 F.2d 772, 776 (Fed. Cir. 1984)."); Lee v. VA, DC-0752-17-0452-I-1 (NP 12/15/2022) ("Relatedly, an SF-50 does not constitute a personnel action itself and does not on its face control an employee's status and rights. Hunt-O'Neal v. Office of Personnel Management, 116 M.S.P.R. 286, ¶ 10 (2011); see, e.g., Arrington v. Department of the Navy, 117 M.S.P.R. 301, ¶ 13 (2012) (finding that the appellant suffered an appealable reduction in grade even though there was not an SF-50 denoting any reduction).").

An individual who was promised a job that was withdrawn after the individual took steps in reliance on the job offer is not an employee pursuant to 5 USC 2105(a)(3). He is not performing a federal function. The individual has no appeal right to the Board. Johnson v. EEOC, 11 MSPR 115, 117 (1982). But the Board had jurisdiction over and reversed the removal of an individual who was given and accepted a commitment from an authorized hiring official, who reported to duty, and whose appointment was subsequently canceled without adverse action procedures. Dyer v. Dept. of Commerce, 15 MSPR 271, 275 (1983) (determining that an appointment occurred prior to a presidentially-mandated hiring freeze). For an interesting case leading a federal district court to direct EEOC to employ an attorney who was given a firm offer that he accepted and who then wound down his law practice before learning that EEOC would not place him in the position because of a hiring freeze, refer to Beacom v. EEOC, 500 F. Supp. 428 (D. Ariz. 1980). Oram v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-3443-17-0035-I-1 (NP 9/8/2022), explained as to a job offer:

Regarding the appellant's claim that the agency's tentative job offer was "firm" and thus irrevocable after he accepted it, the appellant has not submitted anything to support this contention. Rather, in its correspondence extending the offer, the agency clearly identified the offer as "tentative" and conditioned finality of the offer on a number of additional requirements, including the establishment of an EOD date, which the appellant failed to meet. Appointment to a civil service position requires "definite, unconditional action by an authorized [F]ederal official designating an individual to a specific civil service position." Horner v. Acosta, 803 F.2d 687, 693 (Fed. Cir. 1986); cf. National Treasury Employees Union v. Reagan, 663 F.2d 239, 248 n.14 (D.C. Cir. 1981) (determining that a subset of the class of appellants became "employees of the [F]ederal government" when they were administered the oath of office and their offers were not revoked prior to their entrance on duty). There is no evidence of such action here. Furthermore, the Board has held that it lacked jurisdiction over an appeal when an agency withdrew a tentative job offer after an appellant had accepted it, but before she actually performed in the position. Sapla v. Department of the Navy, 118 M.S.P.R. 551, ¶¶ 9-12 (2012).[5]

[5] To the extent the appellant is claiming that the withdrawal of his job offer constituted an unfair employment practice, that argument is similarly unavailing. The appellant has not alleged that his appeal concerns an invalid employment practice that was applied to him by OPM (or, a valid one that was misapplied by the agency), or that any agency employment practice violates one of the basic requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 300.103. See Sauser v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 403, ¶¶ 6-7 (2010); 5 C.F.R. § 300.104(a).

The appellant also argues on review that in rescinding his job offer, the agency engaged in a prohibited personnel practice. It is well settled that absent an otherwise appealable matter, the Board does not have jurisdiction to consider such claims. Davis v. Department of Defense, 105 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶¶ 15-16 (2007) (explaining that absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board cannot consider prohibited personnel practice claims); Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980) (holding that 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b) is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982). The appellant has not identified an otherwise appealable action. See Davis, 105 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 15 (stating that a nonselection is not an otherwise appealable action). Likewise, the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appellant's job offer rescission claim as a mixed-case appeal. A mixed-case appeal involves an action that is appealable to the Board and an allegation that the appealable action is based on prohibited discrimination. Perry v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 582 U.S. __, 137 S. Ct. 1975 (2017); Lethridge v. U.S. Postal Service, 99 M.S.P.R. 675, ¶ 9 (2005) (citing 5 C.F.R. § 1201.151(a)(1); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(a)(2)). As explained above, the appellant has not alleged that he was subjected to an action that is otherwise appealable to the Board.

The Board extended its hiring and appointment analysis in Rossebo v. DLA, 20 MSPR 447, 450 (1984), involving the withdrawal of a hiring action effected through transfer. The employee was transferred and promoted from one agency to another. The action was effective on February 13, the reporting date was February 16. On February 15, while the appellant prepared to travel to the new job, the employment offer was orally withdrawn. A follow-up letter was sent from the agency on February 16. Meanwhile, the hapless appellant's first employer--the agency he left--filled his job with another employee. The issue was whether the cancelation of the appointment was an adverse action affecting the employee, providing appellate jurisdiction to the Board. The Board determined that the employee, at the time of the cancelation, was on official travel time, so that he was engaged in a federal function. He was supervised by the agency personnel office that granted the official travel status, placing him under the direction and authority of a federal official. As a result, the individual was an employee who was separated without the benefit of adverse action procedures. The separation was reversed. If an individual is offered a job, however, and fails to accept it in time because the job offer is delayed in the mails, the applicant has not become an employee. The Board lacks jurisdiction over the appeal from the failure to secure the desired appointment. See Phillips v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 620, 622-23 (1989); cf. Sapla v. Dept. of Navy, 118 MSPR 551, 554 ¶ 11, 2012 MSPB 109 (2012) ("In any event, as the administrative judge noted, it is undisputed that the agency rescinded its offer before the appellant actually performed in the position."). In Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 266 Fed. Appx. 948, 951 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), the appellant held a series of temporary appointments, each not exceeding a year, when she applied for and received a permanent appointment that was not open to temporary appointees. Upon discovery of the problem, the agency told the employee that she was given an emergency temporary appointment and that the position would be readvertised and that she would be eligible for it if she took and passed a civil service examination. Ms. Robinson resigned, maintaining that she should have received a permanent appointment. She then appealed from what she asserted was an improper termination of her permanent position. The court determined she was properly terminated as a probationer:

First, it is clear that her temporary appointments did not provide the one year of continuous service required under clause (ii) of section 7511(a)(1)(A). That clause specifically states that the service must be "other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less." Because she had previously worked exclusively under such appointments, she is not entitled to "employee" status under that clause.

Second, even if Ms. Robinson's mistaken appointment made her the lawful incumbent of a permanent appointment for the two days before the agency discovered the mistake in the appointment process, she was, as a matter of law, serving a one-year probationary period during her tenure in that position. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.801(a)(1) ("The first year of service of an employee who is given a career or career-conditional appointment under this part is a probationary period when the employee...[w]as appointed from a competitive list of eligibles...."). Ms. Robinson's suggestion that the agency waived the probationary term because it did not mention it in the letter regarding her appointment is incorrect, as the agency lacks authority to waive that requirement. See Phillips v. Dep't of Hous. & Urban Dev., 44 M.S.P.R. 48, 52 (1990). Nor is the agency required to inform an employee who is appointed for the first time to a career position that her status is initially that of a probationary employee. Park v. Dep't of Health & Human Servs., 78 M.S.P.R. 527, 534-35 (1998); Phillips, 44 M.S.P.R. at 52. Accordingly, we agree with the Board that Ms. Phillips was, at best, in probationary status at the time her appointment was revoked and thus was not an "employee" under section 7511. For that reason, the Board correctly held that she was not entitled to appeal to the Board from the adverse action of removal under the authority of 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d).
