10. Interpretation of Agreement: External Law; Extrinsic or Parol Evidence; Ambiguous Term; History of Performance; Hearing

According to Hardy v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-14-0344-I-1 (NP 12/5/2014):

...The Board interprets settlements pursuant to principles of contract law and examines whether the written understanding is clearly stated and, if ambiguous, ascertains the parties' intent at the time the agreement was made. See King v. Department of the Navy, 130 F.3d 1031, 1033 (Fed. Cir. 1997)....

A settlement may be deemed to incorporate extrinsic law, Young v. USPS, 113 MSPR 609, 626 n.4, 2010 MSPB 92 (2010):

...It is a well-settled principle in contract law, however, that because parties are presumed to be aware of applicable statutes and regulations and intend to incorporate them, the law existing at the time a contract is executed becomes a part of the contemporaneous circumstances of the contract's execution and is incorporated, without reference, into the agreement itself. See, e.g., Miura v. Department of the Navy, 39 M.S.P.R. 663, 669 n.5 (1989) ("[a] contract is deemed to incorporate all the rights conferred upon the parties by the laws of the state in which the contract was executed") (citing N.C. Freed Co. v. Board of Governors of Federal Reserve System, 473 F.2d 1210, 1215 (2d Cir.), cert. denied, 414 U.S. 827 (1973)); Ocean View Towers Associates v. United States, 88 Fed. Cl. 169, 176 (2009) (the parties' failure to specifically incorporate a federal regulation into the contract had no bearing on the regulation's applicability to that contract because "the law becomes a part of the contemporaneous circumstances of the contract's execution and is incorporated, without reference, into the agreement itself"); Dart Advantage Warehousing, Inc. v. United States, 52 Fed. Cl. 694, 700 (2002) (same). Thus, the appellant was not required to expressly bargain for the agency's compliance with the Privacy Act, or with any other laws, rules, or regulations that the agency is otherwise bound to obey.

See Day v. OPM, DA-0841-15-0596-C-1 (NP 11/21/2016) ("[i]t is presumed that the parties are aware of the applicable retirement statutes and regulations and intend to incorporate them. Young v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶ 39 n.4 (2010)").

When parties seek to compel compliance with a settlement, questions may arise concerning the meaning of the agreement. Buzitksy v. USPS, 82 MSPR 388, 390 ¶ 5 (1999), explained the Board's approach to proof involving interpretation of settlements:

A court-approved settlement agreement is subject to the general rules of construction and enforceability regarding contracts. Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 MSPR 366, 371 (1988), modified by Jones v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 MSPR 252 (1994). The ultimate burden is on the appellant to prove that the agency has not complied with the settlement agreement. Id. Once the appellant asserts an allegation of noncompliance, the agency must produce relevant, material, and credible evidence of its compliance with the agreement. Jones, 61 MSPR at 254. In construing the terms of a written settlement agreement, the words of the agreement itself are of paramount importance, and parol evidence will be considered only if the written agreement is ambiguous. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). A contract is ambiguous when it is susceptible to differing, reasonable interpretations. Gullette v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 569, 572-73 (1996). When a term of an agreement is ambiguous, the administrative judge should resolve the ambiguity in a manner consistent with the purpose and effect of the agreement and with the intent of the parties. Vaughan v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 MSPR 541, 546 (1998).

See Lanclos v. United States, 40 F.4th 1352, 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2022) (settlement is applied according to the plain meaning of all of its terms); Azmat v. DHS, PH-0432-12-0020-C-1 (NP 12/18/2014) ("In interpreting a settlement agreement, the Board looks to the language used by the parties, and will not interpret the language in a way that unilaterally modifies the agreement's material terms. See, e.g., Zamora v. Department of Defense, 58 M.S.P.R. 544, 546 (1993)."); cf. Brown v. GSA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP) (determining the meaning of what an agency had to do to "initiate" a payment of back pay: "We agree with GSA and hold that the Board did not err in reading the term ‘initiate' in the Settlement Agreement to mean ‘taking steps to initiate payment.' The normal meaning of initiate is to begin, not to complete."; dissent by Judge Chen: "initiation of payment must mean initiation of the transfer, not, as the majority holds, initiation of internal and preliminary steps, like, say, opening up a file, which may be necessary to eventually effectuate a transfer").

"Ambiguity exists if the terms of a settlement agreement are reasonably susceptible to more than one interpretation. Johnson v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 8 (2008); Romano v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 M.S.P.R. 319, 323 (1991)." Urena v. USPS, SF-0353-09-0650-C-1 (NP 7/12/2011). "If ambiguity is found, the fact finder's role is to implement the intent of the parties at the time the agreement was made," Raymond v. Dept. of Army, 102 MSPR 665, 669-70 ¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 224 (2006) (citing King v. Dept. of Navy, 130 F.3d 1031, 1033 (Fed. Cir. 1997)), giving the words of the contract their ordinary meaning. Leeds v. USPS, 111 MSPR 172, 175-76 ¶ 8, 2009 MSPB 65 (2009); cf. Webster v. DOD, DC-0752-13-0446-I-1 (NP 9/23/2013) ("It is well settled that, in construing the terms of a written settlement agreement, the centerpiece of the Board's analysis is the plain language of the agreement. See Martin v. Department of Justice, 99 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶ 20 (2005), aff'd, 188 F. App'x 994 (Fed. Cir. 2006).").

The Board distinguished between latent and patent ambiguities in Bables v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 171, 175 ¶ 14 (2000):

In construing the terms of a written settlement agreement, the words of the agreement itself are of paramount importance, and parol evidence will be considered only if the written agreement is ambiguous. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). "An ambiguity may be patent or latent. A patent ambiguity is one that is so glaring as to raise a duty to inquire. A latent ambiguity, on the other hand, is not glaring, substantial, or patently obvious." Massie v. United States, 166 F.3d 1184, 1189 (Fed. Cir. 1999). Where there is a latent ambiguity courts construe the ambiguous term against the drafter of the contract if the nondrafter's interpretation is reasonable. See Hills Materials Co. v. Rice, 982 F.2d 514, 516 (Fed. Cir. 1992)...

When possible, the Board will determine the meaning of a settlement solely through consideration of its terms. The reasoning is that the agreement is a contract interpreted as a matter of law. To determine the meaning of the contract, "the Board must first look to the terms of the agreement to determine the intent of the parties at the time that they contracted." Dati v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 397, 400 (1989); see Rivas v. USPS, 72 MSPR 383, 389 (1996) ("The Board will not imply a term into an agreement that is unambiguous."); Gallagher v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 379, 383 (1993) ("The plain meaning of those terms controls, absent ambiguity."); Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 69 MSPR 398, 402 (1996) ("[b]ecause a settlement agreement is a contract, the Board will adjudicate an enforcement proceeding relevant to a settlement agreement in accordance with contract law."), and Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 684, 696 (1997) (citing Miura v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 663, 668 (1989), for the rule that the words of the agreement are of paramount importance in determining the intent of the parties when they contracted); Gallagher v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 379, 383 (1993) ("We must give the terms of the agreement primary consideration in determining the intent of the parties at the time they entered into the agreement."); Perry v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 45, 48 (1992) (Board interprets agreement based on its language; Board supplied its general understanding of the term "priority consideration"); Mavronikolas v. USPS, 53 MSPR 113, 116 (1992) ("the Board will not imply a term into an agreement that is unambiguous");Shafer v. Dept. of Air Force, 46 MSPR 164, 169-70 (1990) ("In construing a settlement agreement, the Board looks first to the terms of the agreement itself to determine the intent of the parties at the time they contracted. See Wethington v. Department of the Army, 39 M.S.P.R, 285, 287 (1988). The words of the agreement are of paramount importance in determining this intent. See Miura v. Department of the Navy, 39 M.S.P.R. 663, 668 (1989). The Board will consider parol evidence only if the terms are ambiguous. Id. at 668."); Smith v. DOJ, 37 MSPR 374, 377 (1988) (agreement by agency not to attempt to collect what it considered an overpayment of a real estate income tax allowance did not obligate agency to recalculate the allowance); Putnam v. The Board determined the meaning of terms without further evidence in Soffer v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 402, 405 n.1 (1990), interpreting a clause that required the agency to expunge all of its records of a removal to mean that all agency records had to be expunged, not only the official personnel file (Board did not reach the question of what records in a security clearance file had to be expunged). See Sternberg v. Dept. of Transp., 56 MSPR 520, 528 (1993) (similar result: based on settlement, expungement was required of not just OPF but of all agency records). The date of a resignation was determined from a settlement agreement, rather than from a resignation form that was a document separate from the settlement agreement. Alexander v. USPS, 94 MSPR 237, 241 ¶ 8 (2003).

The distinction between settlement terms that are clear, and those requiring interpretation through extrinsic or parol evidence (evidence extrinsic to the language of the agreement, e.g., testimony or negotiation notes, that explains what the parties meant by the disputed term), was developed by the Federal Circuit in Melton v. DHHS, 212 Fed. Appx. 988, 990 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

A settlement agreement is a contract, and its construction is a question of law which this court reviews de novo. Conant v. Office of Pers. Mgm't, 255 F.3d 1371, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2001). When interpreting a contract, we first ascertain whether the contract clearly states the parties' understanding. Id. To the extent the contract is clearly stated and understood by the parties, it is enforced according to its terms. Pagan v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 170 F.3d 1368, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1999). If the language of the contract is sufficiently clear, the inquiry ends there. Textron Def. Sys. v. Widnall, 143 F.3d 1465, 1469 (Fed. Cir. 1998); see Greco v. Dep't of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988) ("Only if there is ambiguity should parol evidence be considered."). If any ambiguity is found, our role is to implement the intent of the parties at the time the contract was made. Pagan, 170 F.3d at 1371.

In this case, the settlement agreement is silent about Ms. Melton's AWOL debt. The most relevant sections are paragraphs 1 and 9, and these paragraphs specify a lump sum payment to Ms. Melton in exchange for her removal from the Agency. Paragraph 14 adds that "[n]o other promises or agreements shall be binding unless signed by all parties and expressly designated as an amendment to this agreement." Nothing in the settlement agreement addresses whether the Agency may or may not collect debts duly owed.

Therefore, parol evidence on the matter of Ms. Melton's AWOL debt is inapposite. In the unambiguous language of the agreement itself, the Agency did not agree to waive its right to collect Ms. Melton's AWOL debt nor is there any implication of such a waiver. In a petition for enforcement of a contract, only the terms of the contract may be enforced. We cannot rewrite the contract or insert words to which a party has never agreed. Am. Capital Corp. v. Fed. Deposit Ins. Corp., No. 05-5150, 2006 WL 3055912, at *3 (Fed. Cir. Oct. 30, 2006). Because the settlement agreement is silent on the matter, Ms. Melton cannot now procure a waiver of her debts through a petition for enforcement.

The Federal Circuit found another agreement without ambiguity, Ammons v. VA, 476 Fed. Appx. 270, 272-73 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP):

We have reviewed the settlement agreement and agree with the Board's conclusions that it was unambiguous and provided that Ms. Ammons would withdraw all of her causes of actions, including her EEOC complaint. Ms. Ammons's reliance on parol evidence is misplaced. We have previously held that parol evidence is admissible only if there is ambiguity in the words of the agreement. Greco v. Dep't of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Here, paragraph one of the settlement agreement, in which Ms. Ammons unmistakably withdrew "all appeals and complaints," and "waive[d] her right to pursue future causes of action arising out of facts in existence as of the date of [her] execution of this Agreement[,]" was unambiguous and does not warrant consideration of parol evidence. Indeed, paragraph seventeen stated that the settlement agreement constituted "the entire understanding between the [p]arties" and that "there were no other terms or commitments, oral or written." Notably, other portions of the settlement agreement explicitly rely on the waiver provisions of paragraph one. For example, paragraph eight of the settlement agreement provided the VA permission to submit a copy of the settlement agreement to the Board, EEOC, Office of Resolution Management, "or any other third party as evidence of withdrawal or waiver of any claim to be withdrawn or waived hereunder." Even more to the point, it makes no sense to remove paragraph one of the settlement agreement because Ms. Ammons's "entire consideration is encompassed in that paragraph." In other words, under Ms. Ammons's asserted modification, her record at the VA would be wiped clean in exchange for nothing. Because the terms of the agreement were clear and comprehensive, we agree with the Board's determination that parol evidence was not permissible.

Ms. Ammons also asserts that the Board committed harmful error by failing to make notes of off the record discussions regarding the deletion of paragraph one from the parties' settlement agreement and further requests that the video of the parties' discussions be made part of the record. However, as discussed above, the terms of the settlement agreement were clear and unambiguous. Accordingly, we conclude that the Board properly determined that parol evidence, such as the alleged discussion between the parties in which they agreed to remove paragraph one, was not permissible.

Of parol evidence, the Federal Circuit remarked, Dobyns v. United States, 915 F.3d 733, 740 (Fed. Cir. 2019):

...Parol evidence cannot be used to "add to or otherwise modify the terms of a written agreement in instances where the written agreement has been adopted by the parties as an expression of their final understanding." TEG-Paradigm Envtl., Inc. v. United States, 465 F.3d 1329, 1338-39 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (quoting Barron Bancshares, Inc. v. United States, 366 F.3d 1360, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2004)); see Barron Bancshares, 366 F.3d at 1375 ("The rule thus renders inadmissible evidence introduced to modify, supplement, or interpret the terms of an integrated agreement.").[3]

[3] Although parol evidence may be useful to determine party expectations relating to particular contract provisions, it cannot be the source of an additional duty. See, e.g., Centex Corp., 395 F.3d at 1288 (looking to the government's representations to understand plaintiff's reasonable expectations relating to the anticipated benefit of contracted government payments).

Some circumstances call for development of parol evidence. When the terms of the settlement are ambiguous, reliance on parol evidence interpreting the agreement is appropriate. Hill v. USPS, 49 MSPR 464, 466 (1991); see Foster v. DOJ, PH-1221-08-0386-C-2 (NP 9/10/2012) ("The terms of an agreement are ambiguous as a result of differing interpretations as to the meanings or intent given to those terms by the parties only when the respective interpretations are both reasonable. See Alexander v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 237, ¶ 7 (2003)."); Cianteo v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 40, 44 n.3 (1993) ("In construing settlement agreements, the Board looks first to the terms of the agreement itself in order to determine the intent of the parties at the time that they entered into the settlement agreement.... In this case, we find that the terms of the settlement agreement are not ambiguous, and that consideration of the agency's proffered parol evidence is not necessary."); cf. Abram v. Dept. of Treasury, AT-0752-16-0589-C-1 (NP 1/23/2023) ("When interpreting a settlement agreement, we first ascertain whether the agreement clearly states the parties' understanding. Conant v. Office of Personnel Management, 255 F.3d 1371, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Any remaining ambiguities are resolved by implementing the parties' intent at the time the agreement was made. Id.").

A settlement agreement is ambiguous if it is susceptible to differing, but reasonable, interpretations, Shaw v. Dept. of Air Force, 79 MSPR 446, 450 (1998). Under Wells v. USPS, 52 MSPR 497, 500 (1992):

In construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the Board looks first to the words of the agreement, which are of paramount importance in determining the intent of the parties at the time that they entered the agreement. See Romano v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 319, 323 (1991). Evidence that is not contained in the body of the document ("parol evidence") will be considered only where the terms of the agreement are ambiguous. See Romano, 49 MSPR at 323 (citing Greco v. Department the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988)). Ambiguity exists if the terms of the agreement are reasonably susceptible of more than one interpretation. See Burger King Corp. v. Horn & Hardart Co., 893 F.2d 525, 527 (2d Cir. 1990). When the parties' intended meaning is not apparent from the face of the agreement, it is appropriate to examine extrinsic evidence of intent. See id. at 528.

Williams v. USPS, 95 MSPR 145, 150-51 ¶¶ 14-17 (2003), determined an agreement to be ambiguous (in part, the result of poor drafting), and the Board suggested the necessity for a hearing to take evidence that would permit resolution of the ambiguities. In Williams, the Board also considered the effect on interpretation of the agreement of the identity of the drafter of the agreement, declaring that ambiguities may be construed against the party that drafted the agreement:

At best, the waiver language is ambiguous and the administrative judge should have conducted a hearing at which the parties could have offered parol evidence concerning the proper interpretation of the settlement agreement. See, e.g., Gullette v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 569, 573 (1996) (where a settlement agreement is susceptible of two differing reasonable interpretations, it is ambiguous and extrinsic (parol) evidence must be considered in order to construe it). Generally, if the parties' interpretations were reasonable and neither party had reason to know the materially different meaning attached to the agreement by the other party, the agreement would have to be set aside for lack of mutual assent if the parties' differences went to the heart of the contract. Gullette, 70 M.S.P.R. at 576. If neither party had reason to know of the different, but reasonable, meaning that the other party attached to the agreement and their differences did not go to the heart of the contract, the agreement would still be in effect, but neither party would be bound by the meaning attached by the other. Restatement (Second) of Contracts, § 2010) (1979).

In addition, to the extent that the settlement agreement contained a latent ambiguity, i.e., an ambiguity that is not glaring, substantial, or patently obvious, the administrative judge could construe the ambiguous term against the drafter of the contract if the nondrafter's interpretation is reasonable. See Bables v. Department of the Army, 86 M.S.P.R. 171, ¶ 8 (2000). In this case, it appears that the agency may have drafted the agreement. IAF, Tab 13 ("Before appellant had an opportunity to appeal to the Board from that action the agency presented to the appellant a settlement agreement for his signature.")

If, however, the appellant had reason to know the meaning that the agency attached to the agreement, the agreement could be interpreted in accordance with the agency's interpretation. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, § 20(2) (1979) ("The manifestations of the parties are operative in accordance with the meaning attached to them by one of the parties if (a) that party does not know of any different meaning attached by the other and the other knows the meaning attached by the first party; or (b) that party had no reason to know of any different meaning attached by the other and the other has reason to know the meaning attached by the first party.").

There is some parol evidence in the record to suggest that the appellant may have known of the interpretation that the agency attached to the agreement....

In Benfield v. Dept. of Army, 347 Fed. Appx. 586 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), the question, resolved against the appellant, was whether a settlement agreement required the agency to lift an order barring the appellant from the military installation where he worked:

...The AJ properly concluded that the Settlement Agreement plainly and unambiguously set out the obligations of the Army and that those obligations had been fulfilled. As explained by the AJ, the Settlement Agreement does not require the Army to terminate the Bar Order because it is contrary to the plain language of the agreement and because during negotiations the Bar Order was addressed, discussed, and specifically excluded from the Settlement Agreement.

The settlement in Young v. USPS, 113 MSPR 609, 613 ¶¶ 10, 15-20, 2010 MSPB 92 (2010), called for an independent psychiatric evaluation of the appellant as a condition to her return to work. The agreement required the appellant to release medical documentation for the use of the examining psychiatrist. The settlement did not specifically allow or preclude the agency from supplying documentation to the psychiatrist. The Board, outlining possible outcomes resulting from a possible determination that the settlement was breached, concluded that the settlement required additional evidence to be taken concerning the intent of the parties:

The administrative judge found that, although the settlement agreement did not specify that the agency would provide documents to the examining psychiatrist, the terms of the agreement did not specifically preclude the agency from submitting such documentation either. CID at 11; see Galatis v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10 (2008) (the Board will not read a nonexistent term into a settlement agreement that is unambiguous). The absence in the agreement of any specific terms prohibiting the agency from disclosing documents to the examining psychiatrist is not, however, dispositive because contract provisions must be read "as part of an organic whole, according reasonable meaning to all of the contract terms" to identify and give weight to the "spirit" or essence of the contract as intended by the parties. Lockheed Martin IR Imaging Systems, Inc. v. West, 108 F.3d 319, 322 (Fed. Cir. 1997); Allen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 17 (2009). The Board is responsible for ensuring that "the parties receive that for which they bargained." Pagan v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 170 F.3d 1368, 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Principe v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 66, ¶ 8 (2005).

In the instant case, however, the term "independent medical examination" appears to be ambiguous for the reasons discussed more fully below. When a settlement term like here is ambiguous, it is appropriate to consider extrinsic evidence. Extrinsic evidence of intent should be considered only if the terms of the agreement are ambiguous. Brown v. Department of the Interior, 86 M.S.P.R. 546, ¶ 17 (2000); see also Greco, 852 F.2d at 560. A contract is ambiguous when it is susceptible to differing, reasonable interpretations. Johnson v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 8 (2008), aff'd, 315 Fed. Appx. 274 (Fed. Cir. 2009). If ambiguity is found, then the fact finder's role is to implement the intent of the parties at the time the agreement was made. King v. Department of the Navy, 130 F.3d 1031, 1033 (Fed. Cir. 1997); Raymond v. Department of the Army, 102 M.S.P.R. 665, ¶ 8 (2006).

As stated above, the term "independent medical examination" is not defined in the settlement agreement. In construing a settlement agreement, words are assigned their ordinary meaning, unless it is shown that the parties intended otherwise. Weber v. Department of Agriculture, 86 M.S.P.R. 25, ¶ 10 (2000). Black's Law Dictionary defines the term "independent" as "[n]ot subject to the control or influence of another." Black's Law Dictionary 617 (7th ed. 2000). Thus, one reasonable interpretation of the term "independent medical examination" is, as the appellant contends, that the examination is wholly independent from any involvement or influence by the agency. Under this interpretation of the term "independent medical examination," the agency's disclosure of documents to Dr. Ruth concerning the appellant would likely constitute a breach of the agreement.

However, the agency appears to contend that "independent medical examination" has a specific meaning within this context like a term of art, a meaning different from the one proffered by the appellant. Specifically, the agency's representative asserted during a status conference below that it is the agency's pattern and practice to submit relevant medical information, and any relevant information concerning the employee's job and his ability to perform his job, with or without reasonable accommodation, to the doctor conducting the fitness for duty examination for his review and consideration. The agency's representative further contended during the status conference that the physician conducting an independent medical examination is "independent" in that the physician does not work for either of the parties and has not evaluated the employee in the past, and the physician should take into consideration any materials submitted for his review, and giving consideration to such materials does not compromise the independence of the physician's examination. While the statements of the agency's representative during the status conference below do not constitute evidence, the agency's disclosure of documents to Dr. Ruth is consistent with its apparent understanding of what constitutes an independent medical examination. See Spradlin v. Office of Personnel Management, 84 M.S.P.R. 279, ¶ 8 (1999); McDavid v. Department of the Army, 58 M.S.P.R. 673, 678 (1993) (the parties' conduct after they have entered into a settlement agreement may be relevant to an interpretation of terms in the agreement). Thus, it appears that the agency interprets the term "independent medical examination" as an examination conducted by a physician who is "independent" of the parties, i.e., not employed and/or under the "control" or "influence" of the agency or the appellant, but the agency does not interpret this term as prohibiting it from providing additional documentation to the examining physician for his consideration.

We find that, because the term "independent medical examination" is susceptible to more than one reasonable interpretation, it is ambiguous. See, e.g., Hammons v. Department of the Air Force, 68 M.S.P.R. 54, 57-58 (finding the term "correspondence relating to this case" ambiguous and relying on parol evidence to determine the intent of the parties as to the meaning of this term), review dismissed, 66 F.3d 346 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Therefore, to interpret this term of the agreement, which is pertinent in resolving whether the agency materially breached the agreement, extrinsic evidence should be used to determine the intent of the parties at the time of the agreement. The record as developed to date, however, does not contain the evidence necessary to resolve this issue. Accordingly, this case must be remanded to allow the parties to submit extrinsic evidence into the record regarding what each party meant by the term "independent medical examination." This will enable the administrative judge to determine the intent of the parties at the time they entered into the settlement agreement. Specifically, the administrative judge should consider whether the parties understood and agreed that: (1) the agency would have no involvement with the examining psychiatrist, i.e. that it would not communicate with the examining psychiatrist or provide him with additional documentation concerning the appellant; or (2) the agency could release the types of materials it routinely releases in fitness for duty examinations, consistent with its own rules or established practice, to provide the examining psychiatrist a complete understanding of the nature of the agency's concerns in ascertaining the employee's psychiatric fitness for duty. While the record as developed to date suggests that these are the prevailing interpretations of the parties with regard to the term "independent medical examination," the administrative judge is not precluded from finding yet another interpretation based on extrinsic evidence provided by the parties on remand. See Sweet v. U.S. Postal Service, 89 M.S.P.R. 28, ¶ 15 (2001). The administrative judge may hold a hearing if he finds it necessary to resolve credibility issues concerning these issues. See Madison v. Department of Defense, 111 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 8 (2009) (although an employee is not entitled to a hearing to establish his allegations in an enforcement proceeding, the administrative judge may grant one in his discretion, if necessary to resolve disputed facts), aff'd, No. 2009-3285 (Fed. Cir. Jan. 22, 2010) (NP); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)(3). If the administrative judge finds that one meaning of the term prevails over the other, then the administrative judge shall further adjudicate the appellant's petition for enforcement as instructed below.

If the administrative judge finds, however, that the parties did not have a meeting of the minds with respect to the meaning of the term "independent medical examination," then there is no reason for choosing one interpretation rather than the other, as no party has reason to rely on the understanding of the other party. See Gullette v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 569, 575-77 (1996). The Board has held that, if there is failure of mutual assent to a particular term of the contract, and the term in question goes to the heart of the contract, then the agreement should result in the invalidation of the entire agreement. Id. at 577. Therefore, if the administrative judge finds that: (1) the parties had differing, but reasonable interpretations of the term "independent medical examination"; (2) neither party had reason to know of the meaning attached by the other; and (3) the term in question is an essential part of the settlement agreement, then the administrative judge should allow the appellant the opportunity to: (1) set aside the settlement agreement and reinstate her enforced leave appeal; or (2) accept the settlement agreement under the agency's interpretation of the term "independent medical examination." See, e.g., Gullette, 70 M.S.P.R. at 576-77 (where the parties had differing and reasonable views of the term "anniversary date" in an settlement agreement, and neither party knew or had reason to know of the meaning attached by the other, the Board gave the appellant the opportunity to reinstate her removal appeal or to accept the settlement agreement under the agency's interpretation of the term because the term in question was an essential part of the agreement).

See Lanier v. VA, NY-0752-14-0309-C-1 (NP 5/18/2022) ("In response to a petition for enforcement claiming breach of a settlement agreement, the agency should submit evidence of the measures it took to comply, but this is a burden of production only; the overall burden of persuasion on the breach issue remains with the appellant. Turner v. Department of Homeland Security, 102 M.S.P.R. 330, ¶ 5 (2006). An administrative judge should hold an evidentiary hearing if there is a genuine issue of fact concerning the agency's breach of a settlement agreement. Id.; see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)(3).").

Addressing extrinsic evidence of intent, concerning a claim related to continuation of health insurance benefits, under Hines v. VA, CH-1221-13-0543-C-1 (NP 4/3/2015):

On review, the appellant argues that the agency failed to submit competent evidence that it complied with the settlement agreement because it did not establish that it transmitted to her insurance carrier the Standard Form (SF)-2801 showing that her benefits were reinstated and that her evidence that the agency did not transmit the form is undisputed.

A settlement agreement is a contract and the interpretation of its terms is a question of law. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Under settled contract law, the party alleging breach of a settlement agreement has the burden of proving such breach. See Reniere v. Department of Agriculture, 62 M.S.P.R. 648, 651 (1994). However, the agency has a heavy burden of production regarding compliance. Id.

In response to the appellant's petition for enforcement, the agency submitted the following evidence: (1) an SF-50 showing the appellant on leave-without-pay status effective December 1, 2012, not to exceed February 28, 2014; (2) SF-50s showing her within-grade increase to step 2, effective December 16, 2012, and to step 3, effective January 12, 2014; (3) an SF-50 showing her resignation, effective February 28, 2014, for personal reasons; and (4) SF-2810s showing the reinstatement of her health benefits, effective November 30, 2012, and the termination of those benefits, effective March 8, 2014. These documents show that the agency met its obligations under the terms of the settlement agreement. As the administrative judge found, the appellant does not dispute these documents. Rather, she contends that the agency breached the agreement by failing to transmit to the carrier the SF-2810 showing the reinstatement of her health benefits, as evidenced by the carrier's statement that it has not received the appellant's health benefit enrollment forms. She repeats her claim that her receiving health benefits for as long as possible was a key component of the agreement and that the agency breached the agreement by not showing that it acted promptly in transmitting to her carrier the SF-2801 showing the reinstatement of her benefits.

In construing a contract, one looks first to the terms of the agreement to determine the intent of the parties at the time they contracted, as evidenced by the contract itself. Sweet v. U.S. Postal Service, 89 M.S.P.R. 28, ¶ 9 (2001). Extrinsic evidence of intent should be considered only if the terms of the agreement are ambiguous; a contract is ambiguous when it is susceptible to differing, reasonable interpretations. Id.

We find that the settlement agreement in this case is not ambiguous. No provision addresses the appellant's health benefits or imposes upon the agency any obligation to communicate with the appellant's carrier regarding those benefits. Cf. Landrith v. Office of Personnel Management, 99 M.S.P.R. 76, 79 (2005) (an expression of legal rights, such as a statute, court order, or settlement agreement is ambiguous if it is susceptible of differing reasonable interpretations; however, "silence is not ambiguity"). Because parol evidence is not used to interpret the terms of an unambiguous agreement, Grubb v. Department of the Interior, 76 M.S.P.R. 639, 643 (1997), we give no weight to the appellant's claim as to what she bargained for. See Weithoner v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 45, ¶ 15 (2006), aff'd, 244 F. App'x 332 (Fed. Cir. 2007). The Board has no authority to unilaterally modify the terms of the parties' settlement agreement, Galatis v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10 (2008), or to read a nonexistent term into an agreement that is not unambiguous, Hamilton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 92 M.S.P.R. 467, ¶ 6 (2002).

See Wilson v. USDA, CH-0432-14-0835-C-2 (NP 12/20/2016) ("The provisions of a settlement agreement must be read 'as part of an organic whole, according reasonable meaning to all of the contract terms' to identify and give weight to the 'spirit' or essence of the agreement as intended by the parties. Allen, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 17 (quoting Lockheed Martin IR Imaging Systems, Inc. v. West, 108 F.3d 319, 322 (Fed. Cir. 1997)). The Board will give a reasonable interpretation to the terms of the agreement to carry out the parties' intentions and to avoid absurd results. Donahue v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 488, ¶ 12 (2003)."); cf. CSI Aviation v. DHS, 31 F.4th 1349 (Fed. Cir. 2022) (considering how a contract incorporates material by reference).

Circumstances may discourage use of parol evidence. Quoting language in a settlement that the agreement "constitut[ed] the complete understanding of the parties," a reviewing court concluded that the appellant "carries an extremely heavy burden in overcoming this attestation as to the agreement's completeness." Soto v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3340). Munoz v. SSA, 97 Fed. Appx. 910, 912 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP), concluded that "an omitted draft clause cannot contradict an included final clause." If a settlement presents an ambiguity not properly resolved by the AJ, remand may be avoided if the record establishes agency compliance with the agreement on an alternative basis. See Outlaw v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 302, 306 ¶ 11 (2005). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Integration or 'Zipper' Clause."]

When used to construe ambiguous agreements, parol evidence has the same meaning as extrinsic evidence, i.e., any evidence that is not contained in the body of the contract itself. Parol evidence may include documentary evidence as well as oral testimony. Romano v. USPS, 49 MSPR 319, 323 n.4 (1991); see Thomas v. DHUD, 71 MSPR 181, 187 (1996) ("In construing the terms of the MOU, the Board will look first to the words of the MOU itself, which are of paramount importance in determining the intent of the parties at the time that they contracted. See Miura v. Department of the Navy, 39 M.S.P.R. 663, 668 (1989). If the terms of the MOU are ambiguous, however, the Board will consider parol evidence. See Greco, 852 F.2d at 560. In reference to contracts, parol evidence has the same meaning as extrinsic evidence, which here includes any evidence that is not contained in the body of the MOU itself. See Romano v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 M.S.P.R. 319, 323 n.4 (1991). Parol evidence thus can include documentary evidence as well as oral testimony. See id. Ambiguity exists if the terms of the agreement are reasonably susceptible of more than one interpretation. See Wells v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 M.S.P.R. 497, 500 (1992)."), rev'd and remanded on other grounds, Thomas v. DHUD, 124 F.3d 1439 (Fed. Cir. 1997). Moore v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 237, 241 (1988), explained:

Here, the language of the signed agreement concerning the area of consideration for job placement is ambiguous; therefore clarification of the terms of the agreement is necessary. A settlement agreement is a contract, and enforceability of settlement agreements is governed by principles of contract law. Village of Kaktovik v. Watt, 689 F.2d 222, 230 (D.C. Cir. 1982). As a general rule, extrinsic evidence is admissible to clarify ambiguous terms in a contract. See United States v. Human Resources Management, Inc., 745 F.2d 642, 648 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Accordingly, the Board has examined the record and sought additional evidence from the parties to clarify the meaning of the agreement.

If the meaning of the agreement cannot be determined from its terms, the Board considers evidence from individuals knowledgeable of the settlement agreement as to the purpose and meaning of those terms. A hearing was directed so the parties could place into the record specific evidence concerning alleged noncompliance with the settlement. Greco v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 408, 409 (1984) (issues concerning entitlement to back pay, priority placement program participation; disposal of personal property); see Buzitksy v. USPS, 82 MSPR 388, 392 ¶ 10 (1999) (hearing was directed on disputed meaning of a phrase in settlement--"indefinite saved grade and pay"--that had no statutory or regulatory meaning). But see Special Counsel v. Nielson, 71 MSPR 161, 177-78 (1996) (although the resolution of a contractual ambiguity may require extrinsic evidence, no evidence need be taken if resolution of the ambiguity would not be dispositive of the parties' dispute).

In Putnam v. DHUD, 44 MSPR 533, 535 (1990), construing an oral settlement agreement, the Board looked to the verbal exchanges of the parties' representatives during the settlement conference that apparently merged into the settlement agreement. From those remarks the Board determined that there was no meeting of minds as to a particular provision. In another case involving an oral settlement, Brown v. Dept. of Interior, 86 MSPR 546, 557 ¶ 18 (2000), the parties attached different meanings to the words of the appellant agreeing to "settle her MSPB complaint." The appellant intended that she would discontinue the Board litigation and continue with challenges to other matters. The agency was of the opinion that the term required the appellant to drop her EEO complaints as well as the Board appeal. Exploring the doctrine of "mutual assent"--an analysis related, but not identical, to the rules governing parol evidence--the Board remanded the case for development of additional evidence:

On remand, the administrative judge shall determine whether the appellant reasonably interpreted the settlement agreement as allowing her to relitigate the merits of the suspensions in the event that the agency was relieved of the obligation to permanently expunge these actions from her record. If the administrative judge determines that the appellant's interpretation was reasonable, he shall determine if the agency had reason to know of the appellant's interpretation. If he determines that the agency did not have reason to know of the appellant's interpretation, he shall determine whether the parties' differences went to the heart of the contract. If the parties' differences went to the heart of the contract, he shall afford the appellant the option of accepting the settlement agreement under the agency's interpretation or setting the settlement agreement aside and reinstating the appeal. See Weber v. Department of Agriculture, 86 MSPR 25 ¶ 12 (2000); Gullette, 70 MSPR at 577. If the administrative judge determines that the appellant's interpretation was reasonable, and further determines that the agency had reason to know of the appellant's interpretation, he shall inform the parties that the settlement agreement will be interpreted according to the appellant's interpretation.

Weber v. USDA, 86 MSPR 25, 28, 30-31 ¶¶ 6, 11-12 (2000), summarized the Board's approach concerning parol evidence. The decision noted that if parol evidence demonstrates that the parties did not reach agreement on an ambiguous term that goes to the "heart" of the settlement, the appellant may not be able to achieve what he thought he bargained for and may end up having to rescind the agreement (for lack of mutual agreement on a material term) and return to the appeal:

A settlement agreement is a contract, the interpretation of which is a question of law. The Board looks first to the terms of the agreement itself in order to determine the intent of the parties at the time they contracted. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Parol evidence is considered only if the language of the contract is ambiguous.[2] Id.

...

When a term in an agreement is ambiguous, the administrative judge should resolve the ambiguity in a manner consistent with the purpose and effect of the agreement and with the intent of the parties. Vaughan v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 MSPR 541, 546 (1998). The Board has consistently held that if a written agreement is ambiguous, parol evidence may be considered. Gullette, 70 MSPR at 573. Because the AJ did not attempt to interpret the specific language discussed above, however, he did not find that the agreement was ambiguous, and thus he did not accept parol evidence on the issue. See Buzitsky v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 MSPR 388, 392 (1999). Consequently, we must remand this case so that the parties may be afforded an opportunity to present evidence of their understanding of the meaning of the word "any" in the phrase "any vacant position" in the agreement.

In adjudicating this appeal on remand, the AJ should also note that, if the parties give substantially different meanings to the words of a contract, and neither party knew or had reason to know what the other meant or understood by the words, there is no reason for choosing one interpretation rather than the other. 3 Arthur L. Corbin, Corbin on Contracts § 538 at 64-65 (1960); Restatement (Second) of Contracts §§ 20, 201 (1979). In such a case, neither party has reason to rely on the understanding of the other party, and there is no question of one party materially changing its position in reliance on a different meaning of a term in the contract. See Corbin, supra, § 538 at 63 (the testimony of a party as to his own meaning becomes immaterial as soon as it is established that he knew or had reason to know that the other party had a different meaning and has materially changed his position in reliance thereon). Under these circumstances, and where each party's interpretation is reasonable, there is no basis for choosing one party's interpretation over the other party's. Gullette, 70 MSPR at 576. If the term in question "goes to the heart of the contract," the entire agreement must be set aside and the underlying appeal will be reinstated. Id. at 576-77. If the aforementioned situation arises, however, the AJ should afford the appellant the opportunity to accept the settlement agreement under the agency's interpretation, in lieu of reinstating the appeal. Id. at 577.

The Board was previously reluctant to accept evidence concerning the meaning of settlement agreements. The Board's current approach to the problem of an ambiguous settlement agreement may have been aided by resolution of a case, unfortunately nonprecedential, decided by the Federal Circuit. Of the proper method of analysis of settlement agreements, the court decided in Vaughn v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3388):

The scope of this court's review of board decisions is limited by statute, 5 USC 7703(c). A settlement agreement, however, is a contract, and it is well settled that the interpretation of a contract is a question of law and that an administrative board's interpretation of a contract is not binding on the court. Bonner v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 202, 205 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Fortec Constructors v. United States, 760 F.2d 1288, 1291 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Here the board stated that because it found "the settlement agreement not ambiguous as to the nature of Mr. Vaughn's reinstatement," no clarification of the terms of the agreement was necessary. We cannot agree.

The dictionary definition of the word "reinstate" is "to place again...in a former position" or "to restore to a previous effective state."...In view of this definition, there was ambiguity whether the settlement agreement entitled Mr. Vaughn to be returned to his former position or at least to a position of the same type, i.e., another full-time permanent position in the Motor Vehicle Craft. As a general rule, extrinsic evidence, including testimony regarding the intentions of the parties, is admissible to clarify ambiguous terms in a contract. United States v. Human Resources Management, Inc., 745 F.2d 642, 648 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The board, in stating that no clarification of the settlement agreement was necessary, appears to have refused to consider evidence proffered by Mr. Vaughn in an attempt to show that the Postal Service's representative verbally agreed to return him to his previous status as a regular employee. Because there is at least an ambiguity in the contract as to the use of the term "reinstate," the board erred in not considering testimony of the parties or their representatives, or other extrinsic evidence, to determine the intent of the parties.

The Federal Circuit declared in Harris v. VA, 142 F.3d 1463, 1467 (Fed. Cir. 1998), that "[i]f there is an ambiguity in the formation of the agreement or during its performance, we implement the intent of the parties at the time the agreement was struck." Although the Board recognizes that it should consider parol evidence if the terms of the settlement are ambiguous, Shafer v. Dept. of Air Force, 46 MSPR 164, 169-70 (1990) (nonetheless denying a hearing), the Board continues to insist, when it can do so, that extrinsic evidence should not be adduced to interpret a settlement. As to a settlement agreement specifying an amount of back pay to be received by the appellant but not specifying what deductions the agency might set off against that amount, Baughman v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 580, 585-86 (1990), explained:

The appellant contends that the settlement agreement is silent as to the issue of unemployment compensation, but that parol evidence indicates that the parties discussed this matter and intended that unemployment compensation would not be deducted from the back pay award. A settlement agreement is a contract between the parties, the interpretation of which is a question of law.... In construing a settlement agreement, the Board looks first to the terms of the agreement itself in order to determine the intent of the parties at the time that they entered into the contract.... The Board will consider parol evidence only if the agreement is ambiguous. Silence in a contract, however, does not necessarily create an ambiguity. See, e.g., Richen-Gemco, Inc. v. Heltra, Inc., 540 F.2d 1235, 1239 (4th Cir. 1976).

In the present case, there is no ambiguity present in the settlement agreement. The parties agreed that the appellant would receive $4,500 in back pay with adjustments made to that sum as required by the regulations governing back pay. The mere fact that the agreement is silent as to unemployment compensation does not create an ambiguity requiring the Board to consider parol evidence. Further, at the end of the hearing at which the settlement was read into the record, the appellant's representative stated that he was satisfied with the terms of the settlement agreement....

If an agreement is silent on a point, e.g., registration in a priority placement program, the appellant cannot complain that his rights were violated when he was not registered in the program. See Carroll v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 239, 242 (1992) (citing Richen-Gemco, Inc. v. Heltra, Inc., 540 F.2d 1235, 1239 (4th Cir. 1976); De Luna v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 526, 530 (1993) (written agreement was unambiguous; it did not include reference to a retirement incentive bonus and did include a clause that there were no other agreements, "oral, express or implied"); cf. Boutin v. USPS, 70 MSPR 376, 378 (1996) (agreement that called for the payment of counsel fees did not cover back pay entitlements; agreement construed to preclude award of back pay).

Evidence will be taken if a term, e.g., the date of reinstatement, is implicitly an element but is not included in the agreement. See McDavid v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 125, 128 (1992). The decision following remand, McDavid v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 673, 676-79 (1993), determined it was appropriate to examine the parties' conduct after the settlement to determine what they meant in the settlement:

The sole issue before us is the meaning of the term "reinstate," as the parties used that term in paragraph 2a of the settlement agreement. As we indicated in our previous decision, consideration of parol evidence in resolving this issue is appropriate. See McDavid, 54 MSPR at 128; see also Romano v. United States Postal Service, 49 MSPR 319, 323 (1991) (a contract is said to be ambiguous when it is reasonably susceptible of more than one interpretation); Miura v. Department of the Navy, 39 MSPR 663, 668 (1989) (if the terms of the agreement are ambiguous, parol evidence may be admitted).

The administrative judge stated in the remand compliance initial decision that the use of the term "reinstatement" in the settlement agreement, instead of "language that the removal would be cancelled or the action rescinded," supported the agency's position regarding the effective date. This statement appears to indicate that the administrative judge believed the term at issue here generally was not used in referring to restorations retroactive to the effective dates of removals. He cited no authority in support of that view, however. Furthermore, even if the term usually has the connotation suggested by the administrative judge, we note that the meaning of a term used in a settlement agreement is the meaning the parties intended it to convey. See 17A C.J.S. 300 Contracts (1963).

The appellant contends that he was told by the agency that, if he were reinstated under the agreement, "it would be 'as though [he] were never terminated' and 'as though [he] had never left [his] desk.'" The only support for this allegation that is properly before the Board, however, consists of the appellant's counsel's assertions [on] behalf of the appellant. In the absence of any indication that the appellant's counsel had any personal knowledge of the agency's alleged statements to the appellant, his assertions do not constitute evidence that the statements were made....

The appellant also argues that a second settlement agreement, into which he and the agency entered in an unsuccessful attempt to resolve their differences regarding the agreement at issue here, indicates that the parties intended that his reinstatement would be retroactive to the date of his removal.[6] We agree.

We note first that the parties' conduct after they have entered into a settlement agreement may be relevant to an interpretation of terms in the agreement. See Laborers Health & Welfare Trust Fund for Northern California v. Kaufman & Broad of Northern California, 707 F.2d 412, 418 (9th Cir. 1983) (in determining the intent of parties to a contract whose meaning is not clear on its face, the trier of fact may consider the parties' conduct subsequent to contract formation); see also Noell v. American Design, Inc., Profit Sharing Plan, 764 F.2d 827, 832 (11th Cir. 1985), quoting Flagg-Utica Corp. v. City of Florence, 275 Ala. 475 (1963) ("[d]ealings of parties subsequent to the written contract are important as going to show construction by parties themselves while friendly"); Lex Tex Ltd. v. Avtex Fibers, Inc. (In re Yarn Processing Patent Validity Litigation), 462 F. Supp. 340, 342 (S.D. Fla. 1978), quoting Brown v. Financial Service Corp., International, 489 F.2d 144, 151 (5th Cir. 1974) (an "established rule of contract interpretation...is that courts may look to subsequent action of the parties to determine the interpretation that they themselves placed on the contractual language").

In the present case, the second agreement, like the first, provided for the appellant's reinstatement without defining the term "reinstated." It also included a separate paragraph related to deductions that the agency was to make from the appellant's salary. In that paragraph, the parties agreed that the appellant would "drop his health plan and life insurance retroactive to July 23, 1987, and...that no health plan or life insurance deductions [would] be offset (not deducted from gross pay)."

Neither the provision quoted above nor any other provision in the second agreement specifically identified the date on which the appellant was to be reinstated. The statement that the appellant's health benefits plan and life insurance were to be cancelled retroactive to the date of his removal, however, along with the statement that payments for those purposes were not to be deducted from the appellant's pay, indicates that the parties contemplated that the appellant's reinstatement would entitle him to receive back pay dating back to his removal. Furthermore, we see nothing in the agreement indicating any basis for payment of this back pay other than the provision that the appellant would be "reinstated to his former position." We therefore find that the parties intended, when they entered into the second settlement agreement, that the term "reinstated" would mean the appellant's restoration to his position retroactive to the effective date of his removal.

We note further that both agreements concern the same general subject matter--i.e., the appellant's entitlement to be put back in his former position pursuant to a USAAMC determination. Evidence regarding the meaning of the term "reinstated" in the second agreement therefore would appear to be highly relevant to the interpretation of the term "reinstate" in the first agreement.... In addition, there is no indication that the parties intended, in entering into the second agreement, to modify their interpretation of the term "reinstate." In fact, there is no indication in the record that the parties were aware that any disagreement regarding the effective date of the appellant's reinstatement would arise. Instead, the purpose of the second agreement was to resolve a dispute over whether the appellant was entitled to be reinstated under the first agreement.
