II. PENALTIES

Assuming that an offense is established by preponderant evidence and that nexus is demonstrated through presumption or proof, there is a third element of an adverse action: the reasonableness of the penalty imposed by the agency. In the Board's analytical chain of being, the merits of the charges and defenses are first examined. If the charges are not sustained or if an affirmative defense is sustained, there is no reason to discuss the reasonableness of the penalty. See Salazar v. Dept. of Energy, 88 MSPR 161, 166 ¶ 16 (2001); Aliota v. VA, 60 MSPR 491, 497 (1994) ("[n]ormally, the merits of the charges and defenses should be considered first. If either the charges are not sustained or an affirmative defense is sustained, there would be no reason to discuss the reasonableness of the penalty"); cf. Bryant v. VA, 10 MSPR 391, 393 (1982) (factual decision concerning whether a charge is proved by preponderant evidence is distinct from the appropriateness of the penalty imposed by the agency, involving a standard of review of "arbitrary or capricious" or "abuse of discretion"). Many appeals devolve to examination of the penalty imposed by the agency. The Board considers the penalty issue under the rubric of mitigation. The focus is on whether the agency's choice of penalty is unreasonable, not upon what penalty might have been imposed were it the Board's choice. In essence, the review standard is whether the penalty is clearly excessive, disproportionate to the sustained charges, or arbitrary, capricious or unreasonable. What this means in practice is that few agency penalty determinations will be disturbed if the charges are sustained and if the agency deciding official took some care to determine and explain how the penalty selected relates to the severity of the offense and its impact on the agency mission.

When proposing and deciding officials and adjudicators speak of mitigation (or aggravation) of a penalty, under consideration are the factors that determine the severity of a penalty, that is, factors that tilt toward a lesser or greater penalty than might ordinarily be applied in the absence of mitigating or aggravating factors. A few hours of AWOL would not ordinarily result in a removal if a penalty table for a first offense of AWOL is consulted. But if there is a long history of past AWOL, the penalty might be removal: that is aggravation of a penalty--the aggravating factor being the past record of AWOL. Or, if the AWOL resulted from circumstances beyond the appellant's control, a reprimand or admonishment or no action at all might be appropriate if the employee has an otherwise clean record, despite a penalty table that suggests a short suspension: that's mitigation of a penalty--the mitigating factor being the absence of any disciplinary record. In civil service practice, mitigating and aggravating factors are usually considered by proposing and deciding officials, and then the resulting penalty choice is reviewed by the Board when the penalty is challenged. What follows is a discussion of various mitigating or aggravating factors and the degree of deference shown by the Board to agency penalty choices.

Deciding officials must consider mitigating factors and their decisions should reflect that consideration. The antonym to mitigation is aggravation. Adverse action proposals must identify aggravating factors. Division between aggravating and mitigating factors is a matter of degree. Discipline for an offense might be mitigated based on a long service record with but a reprimand or two; the same discipline may be subject to enhancement based on a short period of service punctuated by a series of warnings, reprimands, and short suspensions. Some factors involving mitigation or aggravation of penalties also are discussed as factors affecting nexus determinations. In practice AJs mitigate relatively few penalties, and the Board itself mitigates penalties in only a figurative handful of cases each year. A review of the annual reports of the Board from FY 1999 to FY 2020 show a range of mitigation running from 1-4% in appeals, although it is not clear in some reports how the Board calculates its statistics (e.g., whether mitigation is being counted when the Board issues a final decision sustaining or overturning mitigation, or whether the figure includes cases involving mitigation ordered by AJs but not further appealed to the Board through a PFR). More adverse actions are reversed outright, according to the reports, than are mitigated.
