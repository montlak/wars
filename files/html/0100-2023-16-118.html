b. Waiting Period

The interval between step increases for employees with regular schedules is set by 5 CFR 531.405 (2022):

(a) Length of waiting period. (1) For an employee with a scheduled tour of duty, the waiting periods for advancement to the next higher step in all General Schedule grades (or the next higher rate within the grade, as defined in § 531.403) are:

(i) Rate of basic pay less than the rate of basic pay at step 4-52 calendar weeks of creditable service;

(ii) Rate of basic pay equal to or greater than the rate of basic pay at step 4 and less than the rate of basic pay at step 7-104 calendar weeks of creditable service; and

(iii) Rate of basic pay equal to or greater than the rate of basic pay at step 7-156 calendar weeks of creditable service.

A determination to withhold a step increase "shall be based on a current rating of record made under part 430, subpart B, of this chapter." 5 CFR 531.409(b) (2022). As a result of the regulatory change, an agency need not base its negative determination on the appellant's performance during the entire waiting period for the step increase; it is instead required to base its negative determination on the appellant's current rating of record. Nevertheless, the evidence should show that the performance during the period covered by the rating warranted an unacceptable rating. Hudson v. Dept. of Army, 49 MSPR 202, 205-06 (1991). But see Bowden v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 662, 666-69 (1993) (Board followed OPM guidance that placement of the appellant on a PIP demonstrated a lack of successful performance without regard to the prior rating at a successful level), supra. Under preexisting law, there was no legal requirement making an unsatisfactory annual performance appraisal a prerequisite to withholding a step increase. Chiao v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1266); Ross v. Dept. of Army, 3 MSPR 93, 95, 3 MSPB 208 (1980) (a "[s]tep increase must be denied when the agency finds that the employee's performance does not meet the statutory standard for the granting of such an increase"). Conversely, a satisfactory evaluation did not mean that the appellant had performed at an acceptable level of competence for the waiting period. Stephens v. DHHS, 23 MSPR 302, 305 (1984) (citing Van Winkle v. DHUD, 19 MSPR 393 (1984)). For decisions concerning prior reliance on evaluations of performance during the entire waiting period, refer to: Mullins v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 362, 364 (1986); Lance v. Dept. of Energy, 28 MSPR 467, 471 (1985) (citing Whitehead v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 8, 11 (1984)); Chesis v. Dept. of Treasury, 20 MSPR 383, 387 (1984); Whitehead v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 8, 11 (1984); Harris v. IRS, 15 MSPR 382, 385 (1983); Mattersdorf v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 456, 460 (1982); Lloyd v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 417, 420 (1982); Jimerson v. Dept. of Treasury, 10 MSPR 486, 489 (1982); Silverman v. DHHS, 8 MSPR 574, 577 (1981); Zaph v. Federal Maritime Comm'n, 6 MSPR 619, 623 (1981); Brandt v. GSA, 6 MSPR 550, 554, 6 MSPB 463 (1981); Bergman v. DHHS, 4 MSPR 396, 398, 4 MSPB 452 (1980); Hurt v. DHHS, 3 MSPR 434, 437, 3 MSPB 507 (1980).

If an employee's performance is considered over a period extending beyond the close of the waiting period, the error is harmless if the WIGI decision is supported by evidence of substandard performance during the properly defined waiting period. Hurt v. DHHS, 6 MSPR 238, 240-41, 6 MSPB 206 (1981); see Turnage v. USDA, 10 MSPR 62, 65 (1982) ("The presiding official held that the agency had indeed considered work performed after the close of the sixty-day redetermination period in making its determination, and found this to have been a procedural violation of 5 C.F.R. § 531.407(c)(2)(iii). Appellant did not however, show how he was harmed by this error. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(b)(1) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1)]."). No harmful error occurs by including work in an evaluation leading to a WIGI decision over a period that is a week longer than the waiting period, despite regulations calling for the evaluation to be made as of completion of the waiting period. Overton v. IRS, 6 MSPR 439, 444-45, 6 MSPB 373 (1981).
