a. Personal Service Agreement

The right to make a redeposit into the CSRS may depend on whether the appellant is engaged in a personal or nonpersonal service agreement with the federal government, Brown v. OPM, 9 MSPR 122, 124-27 (1981) (involving a teacher):

Appellant's Form SF-2803, Application to Make Deposit or Redeposit to the Civil Service Retirement System, was denied on reconsideration by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM). The denial was based on OPM's determination that, for the period of service for which the application was made, appellant was employed as a teacher under a nonpersonal services contract pursuant to Army Regulation (AR) 62-5. OPM concluded that appellant was therefore not deemed an employee for Civil Service retirement purposes.

Appellant then appealed to the Atlanta Regional Office of the Board, contending that she satisfied the requirements for a federal employee as provided by "Federal Personnel Supplement section 831-1 subchapter 3-3" in that she was engaged in a federal function, was appointed or employed by a federal officer, and served under the supervision or direction of a federal officer. The presiding official, relying on AR 621-5 and 5 C.F.R. part 831-B, found that appellant was employed under nonpersonal services contracts and was therefore excluded from coverage under the Civil Service Retirement System.

In her petition for review, appellant contends that the presiding official's decision was erroneous and not based on a preponderance of the evidence. Specifically, appellant alleges that the presiding official relied substantially on an agency addendum which, in fact, was a contract between the Army and another individual, and not appellant. Appellant also challenges the presiding official's statement that she has not disputed that she was employed under a series of nonpersonal contracts. Instead, appellant strongly contends that the contracts were personal and that she satisfied the three-part test for a federal employee as set forth in the FPM Supplement.

The addendum to which appellant refers is a copy of a "Contract for Counseling Students and Administering USAFI Tests in Army Education Centers." In its reconsideration decision, OPM stated its belief that that contract, which was entered into with another contractor in a different Army Education Center, was similar to the one entered into with appellant. A copy of appellant's contract has not been produced by either party in this case. While we agree that the copy of the contract submitted does not constitute evidence of appellant's contract, we find that there is no evidence that the presiding official relied on the disputed contract in arriving at her decision. In fact, the presiding official made no mention of the contract in her decision. Consequently, we find no basis for that allegation.

...Appellant insists that she was employed under personal service agreements. A personal service agreement has been defined as an agreement between "[p]ersons authorized to be and who are employed under an instrument designated as a 'contract' and who perform their work under the supervision and control of Government officers...." 23 Comp. Gen. 425, 429 (1943) (emphasis supplied). A nonpersonal services agreement is one in which "contractors [are] engaged--not employed--on other than a personal service basis (generally those who do not perform their work under the supervision and control of the Government)."Id. Thus, the key element in differentiating an employee from an independent contractor (or, in this case, a personal service from a nonpersonal service contract) is the "degree of control or supervision" involved. Lodge 1858, AFGE v. Webb, 580 F.2d 496, 504, cert. denied, 439 U.S. 927 (1978).

In support of her contention that she served under personal service contracts and was subject to supervision, appellant submitted an affidavit stating that, in the performance of her duties, she was supervised by a Dr. Eber, an agency official. She stated that Dr. Eber "held teachers [sic] meetings, observed classes, and handled the discipline of the classroom." She further stated that students were required to obtain Dr. Eber's permission in order to be excused from class and that "Dr. Eber instructed the teachers in methods of instruction, policy matters, and [their] responsibility to the Education Center and to the military."

...

Even assuming, arguendo, that appellant's allegations are true, though, we find that Dr. Eber's actions went no further than mere oversight of classroom decorum and performance. There is no evidence, or even allegation, that Dr. Eber in any way directed appellant's performance or assumed responsibility for the manner in which she performed. AFGE v. Webb, supra, at 505. See also 44 Comp. Gen. 761 (1965), requiring as proof of a personal services contract that "the Government provides detailed instructions and close supervision" to the contractor. In fact, appellant stated that Dr. Eber instructed the teachers on "their" responsibility to the agency and only on instructional "methods." Consequently, we find that appellant has failed to show, by a preponderance of the evidence, that she was employed under personal service contracts because she was subject to the supervision and control of a government officer. For this reason, we hold that appellant is not an employee of the Federal government under the test of 5 U.S.C. § 2105(a).

We find, too, that other evidence supports our conclusion that appellant failed to carry her burden of proof that her service was performed as an employee rather than on the basis of non-personal service contracts. First, OPM stated, as noted above, its belief that appellant's contract was similar to that of others found to have performed under non-personal services contracts. Its belief was based on information on the first page of certain contracts which appellant submitted in support of her claim. While we have already held that the contracts of others are not material to this case, and while appellant disputes OPM's assertion, the Board notes that she has never submitted the portions of any of those contracts beyond the first page which could clarify this matter.

Second, retirement deductions were not taken for the period in question.

Third, and more important, Army Regulation 621-5, pursuant to which appellant worked, provides that "civilian teachers...will be paid on the basis of a specific fee for each regularly scheduled Army Education Center Session, purely on a performance basis without any bulk, annual, or periodic method of computation." Only three of the nine vouchers for service which appellant submitted fail to list a "unit price" for each session she contracted to teach, but each voucher refers to payment being for a specific number of such sessions. An individual who is hired for specific transactions and paid on a fee basis without regard to the time element (e.g. one who is not paid on an hourly or annual basis) is not a Federal employee. See Dismuke v. United States, 297 U.S. 167, rehearing denied 297 U.S. 728 (1936); FPM Supplement 831-1, S3-3(c). See also 5 C.F.R. § 831.201(a)(5), excluding from retirement coverage those contractors who are not "paid on the basis of units of time."

In consideration of these findings, the Board concludes that appellant has failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that she was entitled to make deposit into the Civil Service Retirement System.
