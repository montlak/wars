a. Stay of Enforcement Proceeding

Compliance issues arising from multiple, related appeals may also bring stay requests to the Board, but the Board is not inclined to grant the requests and delay compliance without reasonably compelling circumstances. Sink v. USPS, 66 MSPR 325, 326-28 (1995). Sink involved a motion that the Board "suspend for 120 days 'all processing of appeals in which current preference eligibles claim to have been demoted during the agency's 1992-1993 restructuring, and all proceedings regarding compliance where final decisions have been issued in such appeals.'" The stay was declined:

The Board has twice previously granted agency motions to stay proceedings in the appeals arising from the restructuring. See Korthof v. USPS, MSPB Docket No. SF-0351-93-0436-I-1, 60 MSPR 38 (Jan. 26, 1994) (cases stayed pending disposition of motion to intervene filed by Office of Personnel Management on issue of whether restructuring constituted a reorganization that could lead to reduction in force (RIF) actions); Sink v. USPS, 65 MSPR 628 (1994) (suspending processing of all appeals in which claims of demotion by RIF during the restructuring were made, pending Board's decision on compliance issues in Sink). In Korthof, the Board noted that an agency may seek a stay when subsequent cases might have an effect on a previously issued final judgment under the following criteria: (1) The agency could not have avoided being placed in the situation; (2) there is a strong likelihood of success on appeal; (3) irreparable harm will result if a stay is not issued; (4) other interested parties would not be substantially harmed by the stay; and (5) a stay is in the public interest. Id.; see also Jenkins v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 693, 695 (1986). Compare Alfaro v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 127 (1984), and Parton v. Federal Communications Commission, 9 MSPB 258, 9 MSPR 638 (1982) (denying stays of agency duty to comply with final decisions pending OPM requests for reconsideration, and based on similar criteria).

In its current motion, the agency cites "the interest of conserving the resources of the Postal Service, appellants, and the Board," and it asserts that as a result of its efforts to comply with Board decisions reversing restructuring-related RIF actions, and the Board's December 19, 1994 Opinion and Order in the instant case, "there is an adequate system in place for the Board to monitor the Postal Service's progress so that the interests of all appellants can be safeguarded during the suspense period." The appellant responds in opposition that there is not an adequate system to assure compliance for any appellant but himself, since his appeal was not filed as a class action. He notes, inter alia, that there are many appellants who have yet to receive an initial decision from the Board on the merits of their RIF-based claims, and that even those who have received final decisions have occupied demoted positions for approximately two years while awaiting final compliance.

We find that the agency has failed to meet its burden of proving that proceedings should be suspended on the bases stated in Korthof or any other. There is no question currently pending before the Board that must be resolved before adjudication of the remaining categories of appeals cited by the agency can be completed. Indeed, the merits issues in most of these cases were finally decided more than six months ago when the Board issued its decisions on the cases in which OPM intervened. See White v. USPS, 63 MSPR 299 (1994), and Robinson v. USPS, 63 MSPR 307 (1994). Further, the agency has not asserted, much less shown, that it will suffer irreparable harm without a stay, and has only argued by implication but without analysis that there will not be substantial harm to the appellants and that the public interest will be served. It has not argued that the effect of a further suspension of proceedings on any individual appellant would not be harmful. We find that compliance in the instant case will neither effect nor substantially affect compliance in the remaining cases.

Further, we agree with the appellant that the December 19, 1994 Opinion and Order in his case does not directly provide a mechanism by which the rights of any other appellant will be protected. Although the agency has stated that it is making efforts to comply in this and other cases, and although, too, the Board has recognized the magnitude of the tasks now facing the agency, we conclude that the best way to assure that all appellants are provided their rights to a final decision on the merits of their RIF claims and, if successful, a method for assuring agency compliance with any final decision so finding, is through the expeditious processing of all appeals involving both aspects.
