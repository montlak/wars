3. Position Last Occupied

To qualify for the retirement benefit, the underlying disability must prevent the employee from working in the position last occupied. See Dunn v. OPM, 60 MSPR 426, 433 (1994) ("The preponderance of the evidence of record does not establish that the appellant was suffering from disabling pain at the time he last occupied his position."); Norvell v. OPM, 79 MSPR 350, 352 (1998) ("The relevant position for determining whether an appellant is entitled to disability retirement benefits is the position to which he was last officially assigned before filing his application for disability retirement. See Eshelman v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 M.S.P.R. 173, 175-76 (1996)."). The Board may have to take evidence to determine what position was last occupied, as for example whether the position held prior to the filing of the disability retirement application was held through permanent reassignment. See Cohen v. OPM, 65 MSPR 133, 136 (1994).

MSPB review is limited by the determination made by OPM concerning a particular position. If OPM reviewed a disability application as to one position held by the appellant, the Board will not consider whether the appellant is disabled for a different job later secured through demotion, Briese v. OPM, 8 MSPR 298, 299-300 (1981) (CSRS):

Appellant, Arthur M. Briese, filed an application for disability retirement from the position of Supervisory Technical Information Specialist, GS-12, which was denied by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM). On appeal from OPM's reconsideration decision, the Board's St. Louis Regional Office sustained the denial.

By letter of October 2, 1980, appellant submitted a petition for review to the Board, which he supplemented on several occasions. In one of his supplementary submissions, dated December 24, 1980, appellant stated that he had requested a demotion to a lower graded position. Documentation attached to that letter showed that pursuant to the December 23, 1980, request, he was to be changed to the position of Technical Information Specialist, GS-11, effective December 28, 1980.

In order to qualify for a disability retirement annuity, an employee must, inter alia, meet the test of 5 U.S.C. 8331(6), which defines "disabled" and "disability" as:

totally disabled or total disability, respectively, for useful and efficient service in the grade or class of position last occupied by the employee or Member because of disease or injury not due to vicious habits, intemperance, or willful misconduct on his part within 5 years before becoming so disabled. (Emphasis added.)

The Board holds that appellant's petition for review has been mooted by his change to a nonsupervisory position. In accordance with 5 U.S.C. § 8347(d), the Board's review authority in retirement cases extends only to determinations "affecting the rights or interests of an individual or of the United States." Because the OPM decision at issue in the instant appeal relates to appellant's ability to perform in a position other than the one he now occupies, any Board decision on the propriety of OPM's determination would not affect the rights or interests of the parties. Absent an OPM decision concerning an application for retirement from the position appellant now occupies, the Board lacks jurisdiction to consider the appeal.

If a disability application is filed as to a job that is not substantially different from the position held at the time of adjudication, the Board will not require a new retirement adjudication through OPM, stating in Gatiss v. OPM, 9 MSPR 328, 331 (1981):

Appellant was employed as a Clerk-Typist in the Engineering & Services Branch, Peterson Air Force Base, Colorado, when she filed her application for disability retirement. This Branch moved to Langley Air Force Base, Virginia, during a reorganization and appellant was reassigned to a Clerk-Typist position in another branch, where she started work on May 12, 1980. Although appellant's request for reconsideration was filed with OPM after that date, the record does not indicate that she notified OPM of the position change. Since the new position is in the same "grade or class of position last occupied" by the appellant, 5 U.S.C. § 8331(6), the differences in content or physical requirements of the new position as compared to that held at the time appellant filed her application for disability retirement are insufficient to require the filing of a new application, especially since her medical condition is the same. See Chavez, supra, at 350, n.14. Cf. Briese v. Office of Personnel Management, 8 MSPB 1 (1981).

That the appellant could work after separation from federal service does not preclude grant of a disability retirement application, McLaughlin v. OPM, PH-844E-20-0282-I-1 (NP 2/27/2023):

Subsequent work history is relevant to whether an individual's condition is confined to a single work environment. Confer, 111 M.S.P.R. 419, ¶ 16. One is not entitled to a disability retirement annuity when one's medical condition is based on a single work environment, e.g., because it grew out of a personal conflict with a supervisor or resulted from a perceived hostile work environment due to workload or understaffing. Id. However, an appellant is not required to show that her disability rendered her incapable of working all positions. Angel v. Office of Personnel Management, 122 M.S.P.R. 424, ¶ 14 (2015). The relevant position for determining the appellant's qualification for disability retirement benefits is the position she last held before filing her application. Id. Here, the appellant testified that although the consulting job involved similar responsibilities, it was less demanding and involved less pressure and scrutiny than her Consumer Safety Officer position with the FDA because she worked from a checklist, which the FDA does not use or provide to Consumer Safety Officers. She also stated that observation work in the consulting job was less intense because people were not as nervous to see a consultant as they were to see an FDA Consumer Safety Officer. Most importantly, the consulting job did not require her to drive because she flew to India and had an assigned driver while there.
