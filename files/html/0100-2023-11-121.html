b. WIGI/Removal

In Payer v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 534 (1984), the appellant challenged a removal action under Chapter 43. He received a prior favorable initial decision in a within-grade increase appeal. The agency had not petitioned for review of that decision. The appellant moved for and received summary disposition on the basis of a collateral estoppel argument. Although the Board upheld the use of collateral estoppel, it remanded the case. Only four of the six specifications in the removal case were litigated in the WIGI appeal, leaving two specifications for adjudication; those two specifications were remanded for an initial decision. As to the issues actually litigated, it was inconsequential that there was no hearing in the first case. Of importance was the opportunity to prove the claim. The Board determined that the issues in the prior WIGI litigation were essential elements of the removal action and that, as a result, the issues in the second case were determined in the first case. The WIGI/removal connection was again explored in Boles v. Dept. of Transp., 27 MSPR 686, 688 (1985):

...The presiding official relied on certain findings from the within-grade increase appeal to support the agency removal action.

Appellant's main contention in this regard is that a removal action is much more serious a matter than the denial of a within-grade increase, and thus collateral estoppel cannot be applied. However, as noted by the presiding official, the performance appraisal which served as a basis for the denial of appellant's within-grade increase also served as the basis for the removal action. The record clearly establishes appellant's knowledge of this fact, and also his knowledge that a finding on the validity of the denial of his within-grade increase would affect the removal action. The record in this matter establishes that the presiding official properly used the doctrine of collateral estoppel in the instant case as the issues under consideration were the same, appellant had the opportunity to previously litigate the issues, and the findings adopted by the presiding official as proven were necessary to the outcome of the within-grade appeal. Payer v. Department of the Army, 19 MSPR 534, 536-537 (1984).

Gonzalez v. Dept. of Transp., 109 MSPR 250, 255-56 ¶¶ 7-10, 2008 MSPB 143 (2008), applied collateral estoppel in a Chapter 43 removal appeal when the validity of performance standards and the adequacy of communication of those standards were established in a prior WIGI appeal:

Although the required elements for the application of collateral estoppel are present in this case, the Board and the courts have found it unfair to apply the doctrine of collateral estoppel where the party that would be bound lacked the incentive to litigate the issue in the first proceeding because his stake in that proceeding was minimal in comparison with his stake in the second. Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239-40 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Because the appellant's prior appeal concerned only the denial of a WIGI, as opposed to his removal from federal employment, one might argue that he lacked the incentive to litigate the validity of his performance standards in that earlier appeal. However, we find that the record in that appeal demonstrates that, despite the relatively minor financial stakes involved, the appellant fully litigated the issue in the WIGI appeal. We note that a hearing was held in that case, and that the appellant, through his counsel, filed a petition for review in the WIGI appeal that dealt primarily with the validity of the performance standards. In light of those facts, we agree with the administrative judge that the application of collateral estoppel was appropriate with respect to that issue. See Otherson v. Department of Justice, 711 F.2d 267, 276-77 (D.C. Cir. 1983).

If an agency official deciding a reconsideration request following denial of a within-grade increase finds satisfactory performance as to one of the bases for denying the step increase, and that same charge is reflected in an adverse action decision later heard by the Board, the charge cannot be considered to be supported by a preponderance of the evidence. Collateral estoppel is applied to the common performance issue. See Trueheart v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 191, 194 (1983).
