a. Transaction With Patron, Vendor, Inmate

Acceptance of Christmas gifts from postal patrons seems to be the accepted practice, although contrary to regulations. Acceptance of gifts on other occasions by postal employees may result in removal. Familio v. USPS, 24 MSPR 47, 49 (1984); see Cabrera v. USPS, 333 Fed. Appx. 559, 565 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (removal sustained for inappropriate sexual contact with a postal customer and deviation from a postal route); Abrigo v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 88-3390) (reversing the removal of a letter carrier who made the technical error of accepting soda from a minor and drinking it within a residence). Improper receipt of money from patrons over a considerable period of time justifies removal. Edwards v. USPS, 26 MSPR 85, 87 (1985); see Hayes v. Dept. of Labor, 65 MSPR 214, 219-20 (1994) (removal of a physical fitness program director for using an official position to take membership funds for personal gain); cf. Pelletier v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 411, 418-19 (1984) (mitigating removal to a demotion of an assistant commissary manager for charges that included violating policies by condoning the consumption by commissary employees of vendors' food samples).

Sher v. VA, 97 MSPR 232, 241-43 ¶¶ 19-23 (2004), sustained suspension and demotion of a pharmacy service chief for soliciting and receiving free pharmaceuticals from a manufacturer's representative:

The regulation at issue, Standards of Ethical Conduct for Employees of the Executive Branch, 5 C.F.R. § 2635, Subpart B, Gifts from Outside Sources, under which the agency charged the appellant, provides that "an employee shall not, directly or indirectly, solicit or accept a gift...[f]rom a prohibited source."[3] 5 C.F.R. § 2635.202(a)(1). A "gift" is defined as "any gratuity, favor, discount, entertainment, hospitality, loan, forbearance, or other item having monetary value." 5 C.F.R. § 2635.203(b). A "prohibited source" includes "any person who...does business or seeks to do business with the employee's agency." 5 C.F.R. § 2635.203(d)(2).

[3] An employee may accept an unsolicited gift of $20 or less in value "per source per occasion, provided that the aggregate market value of individual gifts received from any one person under the authority of this paragraph shall not exceed $50 in a calendar year." 5 C.F.R. § 2635.204(a). The appellant has not denied that he solicited the sample Lipitor tablets. Thus, the $20 exception does not apply.

The record shows that the appellant attended a training session on the Standards of Ethical Conduct and received a pamphlet explaining those Standards generally in August 2000. The agency submitted a copy of the pamphlet that was distributed at the training session. Among the fourteen Principles of Ethical Conduct for Federal Employees set forth in the pamphlet is the following: "An employee shall not, except as permitted by the Standards of Ethical Conduct, solicit or accept any gift or other item of monetary value...." The pamphlet describes a gift as "[g]enerally, anything of monetary value." The pamphlet further gives examples of unsolicited gifts that may be accepted, including a $15 pen from a person whose license application the employee is processing, a birthday gift from a brother, and tickets worth $16 to a show from a company that applied to the agency for a grant. The pamphlet does not specifically mention drug samples or samples of any kind from a company doing business with the agency. Additionally, witnesses testified without exception that they did not recall the ethics training session including any information that such gifts were prohibited. Even so, we find that the plain language of the Standards and the explanatory pamphlet are sufficient to put employees on notice that they were not to solicit items of monetary value from companies doing business with the agency, including pharmaceutical companies.

Several witnesses also testified that they were aware of occasions when employees accepted sample drugs, and did not believe doing so was prohibited if the drugs were for personal use, rather than for patients. However, Doctor Philip Amidon, formerly a gastroenterologist at Togus, testified that he could not imagine someone receiving enough drug samples for a 6-month supply, which is approximately the amount the appellant received.

The agency could have done more to prevent the occurrence of this misconduct by more explicitly informing its workforce that soliciting sample drugs was prohibited. Nevertheless, the appellant was properly charged, the charges were correctly sustained, and as a supervisor, he must be held to a higher standard than other employees. See Myers v. Department of Agriculture, 88 M.S.P.R. 565 ¶ 34 (2001).

The agency's table of penalties provides that a penalty from a reprimand to a removal is appropriate for a first offense of accepting gifts or gratuities from individuals or firms doing business or having contractual relations with the agency. Additionally, the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has held that removal is warranted when an employee fails to participate in an agency investigation when, as here, criminal prosecution had already been declined. Weston, 724 F.2d at 946-48. Further, the Board has found removal to be a reasonable penalty where the appellant improperly accepted cash payments and interfered with an official investigation, despite his 17 years of federal service and lack of prior discipline. Hayes v. Department of Labor, 65 M.S.P.R. 214, 219-20 (1994). Considering the circumstances of this matter, and the relevant case law, we find that a demotion and 45-day suspension is a reasonable penalty for the appellant's misconduct.

Cf. Aliota v. VA, 65 MSPR 644, 653 (1994) (following remand from 60 MSPR 491 (1994)) (discipline included suspension and a geographical reassignment, following the receipt of a consultation fee by the appellant from a pharmaceutical firm that contracted with the VA to conduct a clinical study).

Receipt of gratuities and knowingly processing false claims from Social Security claimants warranted removal of a claims representative in Herrera-Martinez v. SSA, 84 MSPR 426, 432-33 ¶¶ 15-16 (1999), despite evidence of condonation by a supervisor:

The appellant's position is one requiring a high degree of trust and integrity. The Claims Representative is the primary contact person with the claimant. The agency relies on the honesty of the Claims Representative to keep the program free of abuse. Thus, the appellant's knowing falsification of Griego's application and the failure to correct false information on Esquibel's application are very serious offenses. See Chandler v. Social Security Administration, 80 MSPR 542, 548 (1999). Also, accepting gifts from claimants is a violation of agency regulations because, even without a quid pro quo, it gives an appearance of corruption.

Whether Espinoza engaged in similar misconduct himself or encouraged the Claims Representatives under his supervision to do so does not excuse the appellant's actions. Although condonation may be a mitigating factor, the Board has not always found that condonation warrants mitigation. See Barrett v. Department of the Interior, 81 MSPR 691, 692-93 (1999) (the Board reinstated the agency's penalty after finding that all charges were sustained), modifying Barrett v. Department of the Interior, 65 MSPR 186, 202 (1994) (the Board mitigated the penalty after sustaining only one charge and finding that the appellants' supervisor condoned the sustained misconduct). Furthermore, the nature of the misconduct upon which the decision to mitigate in Barrett, 65 MSPR at 202, was based was much less serious than the misconduct in this case. There, the appellants falsified their time and attendance reports to indicate that the 2 hours they spent working on a project at their supervisor's residence was time on the job. Here, the appellant's misconduct compromised the integrity of the Social Security Administration system. We also note that in Barrett, 81 MSPR at 691, when, like here, all of the charges were sustained, the Board did not mitigate the penalty. We further note that deciding official Gonzalez considered at length the appellant's argument regarding the effect of Espinoza's alleged actions but determined that removal was the appropriate penalty because the appellant had made no attempt to report actions that she knew to be illegal. We agree that Espinoza's actions do not warrant mitigating the removal penalty.

DHHS, 15 MSPR 291, 295 (1983).
