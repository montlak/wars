broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 07: Leave Arrangements, Section E: Maternity, Paternity, and Family Leave, Subsection 01: Maternity and Paternity Leave

1. Maternity and Paternity Leave

Provisions 15 and 16, requiring maternity or paternity leave for purposes of adoption, care of children, or for the care of the mother of a newborn child, were nonnegotiable in NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 730-32 (1992), as infringements upon management's right to assign work. The proposals were not advanced as arrangements. A proposal requiring the agency to grant maternity leave, by permitting use of all available leave accrued and then providing up to six months of leave without pay, was not negotiable because it interfered with the right of the agency to assign work by precluding the agency from requiring the employee to return to work at any time covered by the requested leave. AFGE Local 3804 and FDIC, Madison Region, 21 FLRA 870, 873-74 (1986). A paternity leave provision--that a male employee may be on annual leave or LWOP for up to 30 consecutive days to aid in the care of his wife or minor children--was nonnegotiable because it limited the right of management to assign work by requiring it to grant leave. AFGE Int'l Council of Marshals Serv. Locals and Marshals Serv., 15 FLRA 333, 333-34 (1984).

Negotiable in IAM and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 33 FLRA 711, 735-36 (1988) (Proposal 12), was an article that "[m]aternity leave shall be provided new fathers which shall include grants of annual leave, sick leave and leave without pay to the maximum extent permitted by statute and regulation":

Applicable regulations do not establish maternity leave as a distinct leave category. Absence from duty for birth of a child, or maternity leave, is a combination of sick leave, annual leave, and leave without pay. See FPM Chapter 630, subchapter 13. FPM Chapter 639 states that sick leave appropriately may be used to preserve the health of the mother and the unborn child and to cover the period of incapacity caused by delivery and recuperation. Subchapter 13-5. Leave for matters relating to child care, for example, well-baby care, illness of the infant, adoption or foster care are chargeable to either annual leave or leave without pay. FPM Chapter 630 subchapter 13-6 and 7. FPM Chapter 630, subchapter 4-6 lists the circumstances for which the use of sick leave is authorized. None of the enumerated circumstances includes care of the child or mother, except when there is a contagious disease in an employee's immediate family requiring the employee's personal care.... Hence the governing regulations do not provide for any situation in which a new or expectant father may be authorized sick leave for child care. Consequently, any grant of sick leave to an expectant or new father in connection with the birth or adoption of a child is inconsistent with governing regulations, and would not be authorized by Proposal 12.

Additionally, the proposal states that "[m]aternity leave [sic] shall be provided new fathers[.]" As such, the proposal appears to require granting the requested leave without regard to management's need for the employee's services during the period covered by the leave request. However, the proposal limits the grants of leave "to the maximum permitted by statute and regulation." Therefore, Proposal 12 does not interfere with the Agency's discretion to deny leave, because management may deny leave when denial is based on its right to...assign work.

We agree with the Union that the proposal's reference to grants of leave "to the maximum extent permitted by statute and regulation" supports a finding that it is negotiable. This reference indicates that the Agency would be free to deny leave to expectant or new fathers if the granting of the leave was inconsistent with applicable law or regulation. Similarly, requests for leave could be denied by management when the requester's services are required during the period covered by the request.

NFFE and Dept. of Interior, BLM, 29 FLRA 1491, 1513-14. (1987), considered maternity-related proposals. A proposal was negotiable that employees who are pregnant be allowed to work as long as they and their doctors feel is wise, prior to the delivery of the child. The provision did not address the duties that may be assigned. FLRA determined that "this sentence is simply an attempt to prevent the Agency from unlawfully discriminating against employees on the basis of pregnancy." Another provision was bargainable that maternity leave, through sick and annual leave and leave without pay, will be granted during delivery, confinement, and for a period of no more than the period of absence established by the employee, her physician, and her supervisor. The proposal contemplated that the total period of absence be no more than necessary and provided for the supervisor to participate in that determination. A provision was nonnegotiable that called on the employer to return an employee to her position or a like position at the end of maternity leave. The provision was more liberal than the return rights defined by government-wide regulation, FPM Chapter 630, subch. 13-4 (now defunct), allowing an agency to avoid the return right if termination is required by expiration of an appointment, RIF, for cause, or for similar reasons unrelated to the maternity absence.

A negotiable proposal required the employer to agree that an employee returning from maternity leave may request to work part time or to participate in a job-sharing program on a temporary or permanent basis, a request that would be granted after the employer made favorable determinations concerning its need for the employee's services, the suitability of the position for conversion, and impact of the conversion on service efficiency, NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 31 FLRA 181, 193-94 (1988):

We find that Proposal 1 concerns a negotiable procedure to be followed in connection with Customs' implementation of its part-time employment program, pursuant to the Federal Employees' Part-Time Career Employment Act of 1978, 5 U.S.C. §§ 3401....

...In view of this wording, we find that the proposal would not prevent management from denying an employee's request to work part-time or requiring an employee to return to full-time work if management determined that the assignment would have an adverse effect on the efficiency of the service. We find, therefore, that the proposal does not interfere with management's right to assign work....

[T]he proposal would not directly interfere with management's right to determine the numbers and types or employees or positions assigned to a work project, tour of duty, or organizational unit....

Proposals that essentially restated agency obligations governing the use of sick or other types of leave for maternity purposes were approved in NFFE, Council of VA Locals and VA, 31 FLRA 360, 392-93 (1988) (Proposal 14); see NTEU and PTO, 53 FLRA 539, 593-95 (1997) (Article 18, § 3(A) negotiable: pressing for the availability of advanced sick leave for maternity purposes; the proposal was consistent with the requirements of 5 USC 6307(d) and 5 CFR 630.403, limiting requests for advanced sick leave to 30 days and allowing management to require administratively acceptable evidence in support of request).
