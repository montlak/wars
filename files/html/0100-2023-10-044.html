h. Missing or Unavailable Witness

As to an agency request for a continuance due to witnesses who become unavailable, factors properly considered by the AJ include: "[w]hy the agency considers the witness's testimony essential; how long the agency was aware that the witness would be unavailable before informing the administrative judge and the appellant; whether the agency could have anticipated the delay and preserved the testimony through a deposition or affidavit; whether alternative sources of proof are available; the length of the delay sought; and the extent of the financial burden placed on the appellant." Pidock v. Dept. of Treasury, 54 MSPR 28, 31 (1992) (continuance was necessary because of short notice to the agency that its witnesses would be required at a related grand jury proceeding while the case was set for a Board hearing); cf. Casimier v. USPS, 78 MSPR 35, 37 (1998) (AJ should have informed the appellant of the ability to secure a postponement or continuance of hearing when the witness under subpoena failed to attend the hearing because of scheduled surgery).

If an agency is on long-standing notice of a hearing and fails to keep track of its witnesses, the denial of a continuance will not be overturned, particularly if the agency neither took steps to preserve evidence in advance of the hearing nor asked the AJ to keep the record open for inclusion of affidavits or depositions. Ceja v. DLA, 27 MSPR 531, 534 (1985); cf. Harrison v. USPS, 14 MSPR 476, 477-78 (1983) (agency should have requested a continuance during the week before the hearing, when it knew that a hospitalized witness would be unavailable and it was made aware of the process for requesting a postponement, rather than waiting until the day of the hearing to request the continuance). As to nonfederal personnel, subpoenas are available, and when they are made available, they must be properly used to claim harm for the no-show witness, Agnew v. USPS, NY-0353-14-0337-I-1 (NP 12/22/2016):

...We also find that the appellant's inability to obtain testimony from D.B. and M.E. is no fault of the administrative judge. The administrative judge approved both individuals as witnesses for the appellant, but the appellant failed to ensure their appearance. He did not seek a subpoena for D.B., and although he did seek and receive a subpoena for M.E., he failed to serve it.
