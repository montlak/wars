a. Motion to Dismiss Filed Before Hearing

The motion to dismiss is most frequently employed as a prehearing litigation device intended to derail an appeal on the theory that the case is not timely filed or is not within the Board's statutory or regulatory jurisdiction. Filed by the respondent, the motion seeks immediate dismissal of the appeal if it is untimely or if a statutory or regulatory condition or prerequisite is not met.

The jurisdictional objections available are as many as there are types of appeals. An appeal could be premature, and therefore subject to dismissal on motion. An appeal could be moot if the underlying action was canceled. A constructive adverse action (discussed in Chapter 6) could lack the requisite nonfrivolous allegations necessary to entitle the appellant to a hearing. An IRA appeal (discussed in Chapter 13) could founder for lack of allegations of a protected disclosure or exhaustion of OSC procedures. A step increase appeal might be challenged because the required agency reconsideration process was not fulfilled. The retirement appeal could also be dismissed, on motion, if the required OPM reconsideration process was not followed. The appeal could be subject to dismissal for technical reasons, e.g., lack of the appellant's signature on the appeal. The Board implicitly encourages jurisdictional motion practice by allowing preliminary disposition of cases--particularly those involving constructive adverse action and IRA appeals--based on argument that the facts or legal elements of the case are not pleaded with requisite specificity or sufficiency. An AJ may be inclined to favor dismissal of the appeal. The result may be one less case--for the appellant may give the whole affair up--or it may mean just one more PFR.

The approach of the Board toward motions to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction was explained in Young v. FMCS, 93 MSPR 99, 102 ¶ 5 (2002):

An appeal that is within the Board's jurisdiction can be dismissed, without a hearing, for failure to state a claim upon which relief can granted if the appellant cannot obtain effective relief before the Board even if his allegations are accepted as true. See McVay v. Arkansas National Guard, 80 M.S.P.R. 120, 122, 125 (1998); McGowen v. Department of the Air Force, 72 M.S.P.R. 601, 607 (1996), aff'd, 135 F.3d 777 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table). In appraising the sufficiency of an appeal, the Board follows the accepted rule that an action should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the appellant can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief. See Special Counsel v. Starrett, 28 M.S.P.R. 60, 72-73 (1985), rev'd on other grounds, 792 F.2d 1246 (4th Cir. 1986); see also Conley v. Gibson, 355 U.S. 41, 45-46, 78 S. Ct. 99, 2 L. Ed.2d 80 (1957).

The Board's explanations of what is required to demonstrate nonfrivolous allegations sufficient to withstand a motion to dismiss a complex appeal are convoluted. Some decisions suggest that allegations alone are sufficient; other decisions suggest the necessity of both allegations and evidence; still other decisions distinguish the levels of allegations. Duncan v. USPS, 73 MSPR 86, 92 (1997), noted distinctions among pleadings in different types of appeals:

In this respect, then, a nonfrivolous allegation under USERRA may be compared to a nonfrivolous allegation of discrimination under Title VII rather than to a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction over an allegedly involuntary resignation or retirement, and we analyze an appellant's claim of a violation of USERRA accordingly. See Crawford v. USPS, 70 MSPR 416, 420-24 (1996) (an appellant's right to present evidence on any discrimination claim is of cardinal importance, and his claim should be broadly and liberally construed in determining whether it is nonfrivolous); Ferdon v. USPS, 60 MSPR 325, 329-30 (1994) (a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction must include specific factual assertions underlying the jurisdictional claim).

The combination of allegations and evidence considered in the disposition of preliminary jurisdictional motions was alluded to in Juffer v. USIA, 80 MSPR 81, 86-87 ¶¶ 13-15 (1998), a whistleblower reprisal case:

An appellant, however, is not required to prove jurisdiction to entitle her to a hearing; she need only make a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over her appeal. Bump v. Department of the Interior, 64 MSPR 326, 332 (1994). A nonfrivolous allegation of Board jurisdiction is an allegation which, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter at issue. Dumas v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 789 F.2d 892, 893-94 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

In this regard, the appellant contends that the administrative judge erred in denying her a hearing on her claim that the agency perceived her as a whistleblower. We agree.

We first note that we agree with the appellant that the administrative judge improperly weighed evidence and resolved disputed facts in determining whether the appellant had made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction. The administrative judge weighed the credibility of the appellant's supervisor's statements that he did not perceive the appellant as a whistleblower, and accepted his statements as persuasive. The administrative judge may consider the agency's documentary evidence, but may not weigh evidence to resolve conflicting assertions. It was error to do so here. See Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994).

To the extent that evidence comes into play at all in a preliminary dispositive motion, the motion is really a form of summary judgment. Discovery is often blocked on jurisdictional motions either because the AJ (with or without a motion from the agency) stays discovery pending disposition of the motion, or because the time for responding to the motion is so short that discovery is not feasible (unless an extension for that purpose is obtained). The dispositive jurisdictional motion becomes a summary judgment motion--masquerading as a motion to dismiss. The Board's practice is to the disadvantage of appellants, who bear the burden of proof on jurisdictional issues. As long as the Board permits the use of a motion to dismiss to force the appellant into a preliminary submission of evidence, the Board should recognize that under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, motions to dismiss (filed under Fed. R. Civ. P. 12) that present matters outside the pleadings are often treated as motions for summary judgment. Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(d). Under Federal Rules 12 and 56(c), governing summary judgment motions, a motion to dismiss that is treated as a summary judgment motion is granted only after discovery has been completed. [Refer to Chapter 1, "Jurisdictional Issue; Right to Hearing"; Chapter 6, "Jurisdictional Showing"; and Chapter 13, "Jurisdictional Issue."]

The motion to dismiss (or the equivalent in the form of an AJ's show cause order) should be adjudicated so as to permit each party to reply to the other's evidence and argument. If a record closing date is set for responses to a show cause order, the appellant should be permitted to respond to the agency's submission, Wilborn v. DHS, SF-0752-16-0033-I-1 (NP 7/1/2016):

...In his second and final order issued on November 19, 2015, the administrative judge advised the parties that the record on jurisdiction would close on the date the agency's response was due, or on December 9, 2015, and that he would not accept any more evidence or argument on the jurisdictional issues unless the party submitting it showed that it was not readily available prior to the close of the record. Notwithstanding, the administrative judge acknowledged that, under the Board's regulations, a party may respond to new evidence or argument submitted by the other party just before the close of the record. The agency submitted its final response on December 9, 2015, and, in that response, addressed for the first time the appellant's claims of USERRA discrimination and whistleblower retaliation. Because the response included new argument, the appellant was entitled under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.59(b)(2) to submit additional evidence or argument in rebuttal....

When a motion to dismiss based on jurisdictional arguments is denied, the appellant has not won his case. The AJ has likely done no more than determine that appellant's nonfrivolous allegations are sufficient to carry him to a jurisdictional hearing. Nunez v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 424, 425-26 (1987).
