b. Placement on Administrative Leave Pending Subsequent Removal Attempt

Under Noble v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 524, 527 (1995):

...placing the appellant on administrative leave upon his reporting for duty pending the outcome of a proposal to remove does not constitute complete rescission of the agency action and thus a return to the status quo ante. Rauccio v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 M.S.P.R. 243, 245 (1990); Hudson v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 54 M.S.P.R. 139, 142 (1992). Therefore, the agency has not complied with the Board's decision reversing the appellant's removal.

Similarly, from DeLuca v. USPS, 76 MSPR 487, 488 (1997):

The agency...asserted that it had not returned the appellant to his position nor did it intend to because it was contemplating reinstating charges against the appellant. However, the agency asserted it had placed the appellant on administrate leave and the appellant was being paid overtime. The agency further claimed that the appellant's back pay award worksheet, including overtime hours, was sent to the agency's processing center.

The appellant filed a reply asserting that while the agency had sent the appellant a check, there was no accounting to explain the amount paid. Furthermore, the appellant contended that the agency did not establish compliance by placing the appellant on administrative leave following the cancellation of his removal.

In order to be in compliance with the Board's decision, the agency is required to return the appellant to the status quo ante. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The Board has held that placing an appellant on administrative leave following the cancellation of an adverse action does not constitute complete rescission of the agency action and thus a return to the status quo ante. See Noble v. Department of Justice, 68 M.S.P.R. 524, 527 (1995); Hudson v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 54 M.S.P.R. 139, 142 (1992); Rauccio v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 M.S.P.R. 243, 245 (1990). The agency has provided no legitimate basis for its refusal to return the appellant to duty, and therefore the agency has not complied with the Board's decision reversing the appellant's removal. Cf. Shelton v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 483, 485 (1992) (failure to reinstate an employee to his former position must be supported by a strong overriding interest).

Coan v. VA, NY-0752-14-0300-C-1, n.8 (NP 7/27/2022), added:

Certainly, an agency may institute a second adverse action based on different charges from those that formed the basis of the adverse action at issue and place the appellant in a paid, non-duty status pending the resolution of the second action, but here the agency has not asserted that it had taken such an action. See Nelson v. Veterans Administration, 27 M.S.P.R. 133, 135 & n.2 (1985) (noting that the agency's decision to place the appellant in a paid, non-duty status pending the disposition of another removal action against him did not, by itself, show the agency had failed to comply with the Board's order to reinstate him).

See Coan v. VA, NY-0752-14-0300-C-1 (NP 7/27/2022) ("Although the agency alleges that it complied with the settlement agreement by reinstating the appellant to his Police Officer position and retaining him in a paid, non-duty status, placing an appellant in an administrative leave status following the cancellation of an adverse action does not constitute complete rescission of the agency action and thus a return to the status quo ante. See De Luca v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 487, 488 (1997) (determining that the agency's explanation that it had placed the appellant in an administrative leave status and had not returned him to his position because it was contemplating reinstating charges against him was not a legitimate basis for its refusal to return him to duty)."); Hudson v. DHUD, 54 MSPR 139, 142 (1992) ("However, an agency does not return an employee to the status quo ante when it rescinds the appealed action and then, instead of returning the appellant to duty, places her on administrative leave pending a second proposed removal action. See Rauccio, 44 M.S.P.R. at 245-46."); Rauccio v. USPS, 44 MSPR 243, 245 (1990) ("We find that the appellant's placement on administrative leave pending another removal action is not placement which is substantially similar to his situation in his former position. See Burzinski v. Veterans Administration, 39 M.S.P.R. 561 (1989) (agency's cancellation of enforced leave accompanied by substitution of administrative leave, which was immediately followed by the imposition of indefinite suspension with pay, for the same reasons it placed employee on enforced leave, did not return employee to the status quo ante)."). If the action reversed by the Board is an indefinite suspension, administrative leave prior to the suspension is not a matter for enforcement proceedings, Brown v. DOD, SF-0752-13-0336-C-1 (NP 5/15/2015):

We agree with the administrative judge that the agency has demonstrated compliance with the Board's final order reversing the appellant's indefinite suspension and making the appellant whole between April 1, 2013, and June 26, 2013, by paying him the correct amount of back pay. We further concur with the administrative judge that the appellant cannot challenge the agency's actions prior to the effective date of the indefinite suspension in the instant petition for enforcement because the Board's final order in this appeal only ordered the appellant to be reinstated and made whole effective April 1, 2013. The appellant's challenge to his placement on administrative leave prior to the effective date of his indefinite suspension is thus outside the scope of the instant petition for enforcement.

But, under Brown, as to administrative leave following the suspension, enforcement review was appropriate:

We disagree with the administrative judge, however, that the appellant's challenge to the agency's actions after the end of the appellant's indefinite suspension on June 26, 2013, is also outside the scope of this proceeding. CID at 2 (finding that the appellant's challenges to the agency's acts following the end of his indefinite suspension are part of the petition for enforcement based on the reversal of the removal action). In his initial decision reversing the appellant's indefinite suspension, the administrative judge ordered the agency to provide interim relief pending a final decision from the Board. See MSPB Docket No. SF-0752-13-0336-I-1, Initial Decision at 9 (July 17, 2013); see also 5 U.S.C. § 7701(b)(2)(A). Generally, an administrative judge's order of interim relief is in effect pending the disposition of a petition for review, see Garcia v. Department of State, 106 M.S.P.R. 583, § 7 (2007), and we disagree with the administrative judge that the agency's actions following the reversal of the indefinite suspension should be part of the compliance proceeding based on the reversal of his removal. The agency's actions between the reversal of the indefinite suspension and the effective date of the subsequent removal action properly fall within the instant petition for enforcement as allegations of failure to comply with an order for interim relief under section 7701(b)(2)(A). See Sanders v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 144, §§ 7-8 (2015) (interim relief covers the date of the administrative judge's initial decision and continuing until the Board issues a final decision); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116....
