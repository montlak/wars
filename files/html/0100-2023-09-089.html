l. Enforced Leave

Greek v. USPS, 78 MSPR 470, 472-76 (1998), explained fairly directly that if the appellant is placed on enforced leave, the agency is required then to provide notice of appeal rights. The right to appeal begins after the employee is off duty without pay for 14 days, that is, when the employee is subjected to a suspension greater than 14 days. Lacking proper notice, the appellant is not required to demonstrate diligence in attempting to discover his appeal rights:

The appellant, a preference-eligible PS-5 Mailhandler Equipment Operator, began wearing what the parties call a "respirator" to protect himself from an alleged sensitivity to paper dust and other substances in the workplace. He filed this appeal on May 9, 1997, claiming that the agency constructively suspended him when it confiscated his respirator and sent him home from work on March 10, 1997....

...

In an appropriate case, an administrative judge may assume arguendo that an appellant was subjected to an appealable action and has standing to appeal, and go on to dismiss an appeal as untimely filed, if the record on timeliness is sufficiently developed. See Popham v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 193 (1991). Such an approach is not appropriate if the jurisdictional and timeliness issues are "inextricably intertwined," that is, if resolution of the timeliness issue depends on whether the appellant was subjected to an appealable action. See Romine v. U.S. Postal Service, 64 MSPR 68, 72-73 (1994) (an appeal alleging constructive suspension could not be disposed of on timeliness grounds without addressing jurisdiction, since resolution of the timeliness issue was dependent on whether the agency constructively suspended the appellant). As explained below, in this case the issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are inextricably intertwined, so the appeal cannot be disposed of on timeliness grounds.

An employee who is absent from work involuntarily, i.e., at the agency's insistence, for more than 14 days, has been subjected to a constructive suspension, which is appealable as an adverse action. See 5 USC §§ 7512(2), 7513(d); Baker v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 MSPR 680, 691-92 (1996); Rivas v. U.S. Postal Service, 61 MSPR 121, 126 (1994), overruled on other grounds, 72 MSPR 383 (1996). An appeal from an adverse action must be filed within 30 days of the effective date of the action. See 5 CFR § 1201.22(b). When an agency takes an appealable action, it must advise the affected employee of his right to appeal to the Board, including the applicable time limit and the address of the appropriate Board office for filing. 5 CFR § 1201.21. The Board ordinarily will excuse untimeliness in filing a petition for appeal if an agency should have given notice of a right of appeal but did not, as long as the appellant "acted promptly and within the allowable time limits once he was aware of the basis of his claim." Gordy v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 736 F.2d 1505, 1508 (Fed. Cir. 1984); see also Herring v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 MSPR 438, 443 (1996).

Turning to the present case, the appellant argued below that for purposes of timeliness, the action taken against him (if it was appealable) should be deemed to have taken effect on March 25, 1997, 15 days after the date he alleges he was barred from duty.... When an agency makes an express decision to suspend an employee for more than 14 days, the filing period begins to run as soon as the decision goes into effect, that is, at the outset of the suspension. Here, though, the appellant could not have filed an appeal before March 25, because before then he had been absent for less than 15 days, and there was no express decision from the agency that he was to remain absent for more than 14 days. It would be unfair to hold that the appeal period began to run before an appeal could have been filed. It thus appears that the petition for appeal, filed May 9, 1997, was 15 days late.

It is undisputed that the agency did not give the appellant specific notice of his right to appeal his absence from duty as a constructive suspension. Contrary to the implication in the initial decision, the publication What You Need to Know About EEO, which the appellant acknowledged receiving on March 10, 1997, did not constitute an effective notice of appeal rights under 5 CFR § 1201.21. Although the publication does state that preference-eligible employees can appeal certain actions to the Board, it does not explain what a constructive suspension is, nor does it provide the address of the appropriate Board office for filing an appeal. cf. Nicoletti v. Department of Justice, 55 MSPR 557, 559 (1992) (information about appeal rights is not an effective notice if it does not say where and how to file an appeal). We also reject, as unsupported by any record evidence, the agency's claim that the appellant had such specialized knowledge from his work as a union steward that he can be expected to have known all of the rules regarding constructive suspensions and when, where, and how to file a Board appeal.

Of course, whether the agency was required to give specific notice of a right of appeal depends on whether the agency took an appealable action. The administrative judge assumed for purposes of argument that the agency should have given the appellant appeal rights, but found no basis for excusing the appellant's belated filing because the appellant did not explain when he learned of his potential right of appeal. The appellant argues on review that he first learned that the agency definitely would not allow him to return to duty on April 10, 1997--30 days before he filed his petition for appeal--and that it was not until then that he had reason to believe that he might be able to file a Board appeal alleging constructive suspension. The appellant's argument is supported by the record. See letter from the agency to the appellant dated April 10, 1997, stating that, "based on the information provided by your physician, we cannot allow your return to work."...

If, as appears from the record, the appellant attempted to reach an agreement with the agency concerning his returning to work with a respirator, and if, as the appellant claims, it was not until April 10, 1997 that it became clear to him that the agency would not permit him to return, then it appears that the appellant's late filing should be excused under Gordy because he filed his appeal within 30 days of April 10. Popham holds that a jurisdictional determination may be avoided only if an appellant's well-pleaded allegations going to jurisdiction are taken as true and it is nevertheless clear that the appellant's delay in filing should not be excused. In this case, taking the appellant's allegations as true leads to the apparent conclusion that there was good cause for his untimely filing, making it impossible to avoid a jurisdictional determination. In other words, resolution of the good cause issue depends on resolution of the jurisdictional issue. Accordingly, this appeal cannot be disposed of on timeliness grounds without addressing jurisdiction. See Romine, 64 MSPR at 72.

The Popham decision itself cautions that a jurisdictional determination cannot be avoided if timeliness and jurisdiction are "inextricably intertwined." 50 MSPR at 198 n.5. Whereas in Popham the agency had no obligation to advise the appellant of his appeal rights when he resigned because under the appellant's own version of events it had no reason to know that he considered his resignation involuntary, id. at 199, here the agency should have advised the appellant of his appeal rights if, as the appellant alleges, he was "ordered off work" and not allowed to return for more than 14 days. The decision in Jones v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 594 (1995), relied upon by the administrative judge in dismissing this appeal, is like Popham. In Jones the agency was not required to provide notice of appeal rights because it had no reason to know that the appellant considered his retirement involuntary; the appeal could be disposed of on timeliness grounds without addressing jurisdiction because the appellant did not show that he was diligent in discovering and pursuing his potential right of appeal. By contrast, the appellant herein is not required to show that he was diligent in attempting to discover his potential right of appeal because, under his version of events, he was entitled to a notice of appeal rights from the agency. See Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR 583, 588 (1995). Popham and Jones thus differ from this case in a basic way.
