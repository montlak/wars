15. Remedy

The remedy of choice in successful employment practice challenges is generally priority consideration. Vesser v. OPM, 65 MSPR 282, 285 (1994), involving a successful challenge to an employment practice that otherwise barred a candidate for an ALJ position, explained:

We can order that a Federal employee be restored to his position only if he was duly appointed to it. See, e.g., Lewis v. GSA, 54 MSPR 120, 122 (1992); Schaefer v. INS, 28 MSPR 566, 568 (1985). Thus, we have held in negative suitability cases that, where there has been no appointment to a position, the proper remedy is to order the agency to cancel the unsuitability rating and to return the employee to the eligibility list for employment. Lewis, 54 MSPR at 123. We have found further that we are without authority under the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596, to order back pay and benefits to an applicant for employment. Id., citing Schaefer, 28 MSPR at 568. We find that the same reasoning is applicable here since the appellant was not appointed to an ALJ position.

Because an agency exercises discretion in appointment to a position and the SSA is not the agency which erred here, we cannot order the SSA to hire the appellant, see, e.g., Petrecz v. OPM, 33 MSPR 21, 23 (1987), even if we could assert jurisdiction over the appellant's nonselection for the position. Our authority is limited to ordering OPM to afford the appellant appropriate relief by placing him in the position where he may again be considered for a vacant ALJ position.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant is entitled to reinstatement to his rightful place on the OPM roster of eligibles for appointment as an ALJ; the appellant should be given priority consideration for certification as a candidate for any future vacancies at the sites he has indicated would be acceptable to him. See, e.g., Baxter v. OPM, 44 MSPR 125, 133 (1990); Petrecz, 33 MSPR at 23.
