1. Res Judicata

Res judicata means a claim once litigated is final as to the issues that were litigated or could have been litigated between the parties. Res judicata was defined in its most straightforward application in Petty v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 92-3476):

Res judicata, Latin for "the thing has been decided," developed in the law to prevent the re-litigation of an issue or claim that has already had its day in court. By affording a claimant only one opportunity to obtain redress, the doctrine conserves the resources of the judicial system and prevents the defendant from being harassed by repeated lawsuits asserting the same claim.

Summarized in Pangilinan v. OPM, 245 Fed. Appx. 956, 958 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

...A petitioner may be barred by res judicata "if (1) the prior decision was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases." Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

From Maksimuk v. Connor Sport Int'l., LLC, 771 Fed. Appx. 1001, 1004 (Fed. Cir 2019):

...Claim preclusion, historically known as res judicata, prevents a party from litigating a matter that should have been litigated in an earlier proceeding. See generally 18 Charles Alan Wright et al., Federal Practice and Procedure § 4402 (3d ed. 2018). Claim preclusion applies when three elements are met: "(1) there is identity of parties (or their privies); (2) there has been an earlier final judgment on the merits of a claim; and (3) the second claim is based on the same set of transactional facts as the first." Nasalok Coating Corp. v. Nylok Corp., 522 F.3d 1320, 1324 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (quoting Jet, Inc. v. Sewage Aeration Systems, 223 F.3d 1360, 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2000))....

In a contract dispute, First Mortgage Corp. v. United States, 961 F.3d 1331, 1338 (Fed. Cir. 2020), explained:

"The doctrine of res judicata involves the related concepts of claim preclusion and issue preclusion." Phillips/May Corp. v. United States, 524 F.3d 1264, 1267 (Fed. Cir. 2008). "[I]ssue preclusion operates only as to issues actually litigated, whereas claim preclusion may operate between the parties simply by virtue of the final judgment." Young Engn'rs, Inc. v. U.S. Int'l Trade Comm'n, 721 F.2d 1305, 1314 (Fed. Cir. 1983). Claim preclusion "foreclose[es] any litigation of matters that...should have been advanced in an earlier suit." Phillips/May Corp., 524 F.3d at 1267 (internal quotation marks and citation omitted). "A final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." Federated Dep't Stores, Inc. v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 398 (1981) (citing Commissioner v. Sunnen, 333 U.S. 591, 597 (1948) and Cromwell v. Cty. of Sac, 94 U.S. 351, 352-53 (1877)).

On an appeal from the Court of Federal Claims, Shell Oil Co. v. United States, 7 F.4th 1165, 1171 (Fed. Cir. 2021):

"Under res judicata, a final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980) (citing Cromwell v. County of Sac., 94 U.S. 351, 352 (1876)). Res judicata applies where "(1) there is identity of parties (or their privies); (2) there has been an earlier final judgment on the merits of a claim; and (3) the second claim is based on the same set of transactional facts as the first." Levi Strauss & Co. v. Abercrombie & Fitch Trading Co., 719 F.3d 1367, 1372 (Fed. Cir. 2013). "The doctrine of res judicata rests at bottom upon the ground that the party to be affected...has litigated or had an opportunity to litigate the same matter in a former action in a court of competent jurisdiction." Richards v. Jefferson Cty., 517 U.S. 793, 797 n.4 (1996). A judgment is generally res judicata "not only as to all matters litigated and decided by it, but as to all relevant issues which could have been but were not raised and litigated in the suit." Heiser v. Woodruff, 327 U.S. 726, 735 (1946).

See Sowinski v. Calif. Air Resources Board, 971 F.3d 1371, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) (patent infringement: "Res judicata arises when the prior case or claim was previously tried and the merits were adjudicated. 'In civil cases, a claim generally may not be tried if it arises out of the same transaction or common nucleus of operative facts as another already tried.' Currier v. Virginia, 138 S. Ct. 2144, 2154 (2018).").

Because of the necessity for analysis of the facts as well as claims, the application of res judicata or collateral estoppel is generally a fact-dependent undertaking. See Mycka v. OPM, 56 MSPR 675, 679 n.4 (1993) (citing United States v. Stauffer Chemical Co., 464 U.S. 165, 169-71 & n.3 (1984)).

In an enforcement and IRA proceeding, the Federal Circuit noted, Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 357 Fed. Appx. 293, 293-94 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP):

The doctrine of res judicata serves to "relieve parties of the cost and vexation of multiple lawsuits, conserve judicial resources, and, by preventing inconsistent decisions, encourage reliance on adjudication." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980). The doctrine is applied when: (1) a prior decision was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction, (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits, and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties were involved in both cases. Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005) ("2005 Carson Decision"). Since all three criteria are satisfied here, the board correctly determined that Carson's appeal is barred by res judicata.

...

...Res judicata serves to bar claims that were not, but should have been, advanced in an earlier proceeding:

The preclusive effects of former adjudication are...referred to collectively by most commentators as the doctrine of "res judicata." Res judicata is often analyzed further to consist of two preclusion concepts: "issue preclusion" and "claim preclusion." Issue preclusion refers to the effect of a judgment in foreclosing relitigation of a matter that has been litigated and decided. This effect is also referred to as direct or collateral estoppel. Claim preclusion refers to the effect of a judgment in foreclosing litigation of a matter that never has been litigated, because of a determination that it should have been advanced in an earlier suit. Claim preclusion therefore encompasses the law of merger and bar.

Migra v. Warren City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Educ., 465 U.S. 75, 77 n.1 (1984) (internal citations omitted).

The Federal Circuit more elaborately described res judicata, Whitby v. OPM, 559 Fed. Appx. 1037, 1040-42 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP):

The Board's decision to dismiss Whitby's appeal was based on the doctrine of res judicata, in which "a judgment on the merits in a prior suit bars a second suit involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action." Jet, Inc. v. Sewage Aeration Sys., 223 F.3d 1360, 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (quoting Parklane Hosier Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322, 326 n.5 (1979)). In effect, an earlier judgment forecloses litigation of a matter that never has been litigated if the claim should have been raised in the earlier suit. Id. (quoting Migra v. Warren City Sch. Dist. Bd. Of Educ., 465 U.S. 75, 77 n.1 (1984)). Thus, a subsequent suit will be barred by res judicata if: "(1) there is identity of parties (or their privies); (2) there has been an earlier final judgment on the merits of a claim; and (3) the second claim is based on the same set of transactional facts as the first." Those factors notwithstanding, however, the doctrine applies only if the litigant had a "full and fair opportunity" to litigate his case. Poyner v. Murray, 508 U.S. 931, 933 (1993).

...

Res judicata "forecloses matters that, although never litigated or even raised, could have been advanced in an earlier suit." Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 n.8 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (citing Migra, 465 U.S. 77 n.1). Evidence of Whitby's PTSD and its effects was available while the record was open during his prior appeal. Whitby has known since at least 2007 that his PTSD causes "significant distress" in "important areas of functioning." He has known since at least 2009 that his PTSD symptoms had been present since his service in the Vietnam War, and thus would have existed in 1993, and that the symptoms were "daily and severe." At the July 8, 2010 hearing during the initial appeal, the AJ agreed to leave open the record until July 15, 2010, "for the parties to submit additional documentary evidence." Whitby could have submitted evidence concerning his PTSD during that time but did not. Though he was not rated 100% disabled by the VA until 2011, that rating itself is immaterial here because it reflected his condition in 2011 rather than 1993 when he submitted the refund application. The evidence relevant to his current argument, whether his PSTD [sic] rendered him mentally incompetent in 1993, was known and available to him during the time the record in the prior appeal was open.

Whitby cites to Litton Indus., Inc. v. Litronix, Inc., 577 F.2d 709, 711 (C.C.P.A. 1978), for the proposition that "res judicata and collateral estoppel are not applicable where 'it is apparent that all the questions of fact and law involved...(in the second proceeding) were not determined in the previous proceedings.'" (quoting Universal Overall Co. v. Stonecutter Mills Corp., 310 F.2d 952, 956 (C.C.P.A. 1962)). The "questions of fact and law" referenced by Litton, however, are the relevant transactional facts and the underlying claim. Litton does not hold that res judicata is avoided by simply raising new factual issues. Here, Whitby's current claim, that he is entitled to FERS retirement for the period covered by the refund that he requested, is the same as the claim that previously was adjudicated on the merits. To the extent Whitby is attempting to argue a new claim that his refund request should be considered "voidable" based on "mental incompetence" related to his PTSD, that argument was not raised before the Board and will not be considered on appeal. We generally do not consider issues that were not raised in the proceedings below. Oshiver v. Office of Pers. Mgt., 896 F.2d 540, 542 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (quoting Rockwell v. Dep't of Transp., 789 F.2d 908, 913 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("Our precedent clearly establishes the impropriety of seeking a reversal of the board's decision on the basis of assertions never presented to the presiding official or to the board."). Moreover, the argument and evidence could have and should have been raised during the prior appeal.

...

Whitby also contends that the Board has denied him due process in the prior appeal by conducting a hearing not longer than 15 minutes and by making credibility determinations without considering evidence of his PSTD [sic] or its effect on his mental capacity. Due process requires "a fair hearing on the merits" of a claim. Cushman v. Shinseki, 576 F.3d 1290, 1299 (Fed. Cir. 2009). Though he indicates that he believes the hearing was too short, he does not contend that he was prevented from presenting any argument or evidence he desired to make. Significantly, Whitby made no attempt to raise the PTSD issue he now asserts at the hearing or afterward during the time that the AJ left open the record. On this record, there is no merit to Whitby's claim that the Board denied him due process.

Trufant v. Dept. of Air Force, 578 Fed. Appx. 982, 983-84 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), added:

..."A final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." Federated Dep't Stores, Inc. v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 398 (1981); see also Ford-Clifton v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 661 F.3d 655, 660 (Fed. Cir. 2011); Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005). Res judicata thus applies "not only as to every matter which was offered and received to sustain or defeat [a] claim or demand, but as to any other admissible matter which might have been offered for that purpose." Salazar v. Buono, 559 U.S. 700, 713 (2010) (citations and internal quotation marks omitted); see Migra v. Warren City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Educ., 465 U.S. 75, 77 n.1 (1984) ("Claim preclusion refers to the effect of a judgment in foreclosing litigation of a matter that never has been litigated, because of a determination that it should have been advanced in an earlier suit.").

Of res judicata, Schoening v. OPM, 43 MSPR 6, 10 (1989), explained:

The doctrine of res judicata applies to prevent repetitious litigation on the same cause of action. See Wilson v. Turnage, 791 F.2d 151, 156 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 988, 107 S. Ct. 580, 93 L. Ed.2d 583 (1986). Under the doctrine, when a right, question, or fact that is a ground for recovery, and that was distinctly put in issue is directly determined by a court of competent jurisdiction, such right, question, or fact cannot be disputed in a subsequent suit between the same parties or their privies. Even if the second suit is on a different cause of action, the right, question, or fact determined in the first action must be taken as conclusively determined between the same parties and their privies, as long as the judgment in the first suit remains unmodified. In this case, in which both the parties and the issues decided below are identical, we apply the doctrine of res judicata to prevent relitigating the merits of the earlier claim. See Aceto, 40 MSPR at 161; Davis v. Department of the Navy, 35 MSPR 107, 109 (1987).

Under Campos v. OPM, DC-831E-14-0903-I-1 (NP 3/23/2015):

Res judicata, or claim preclusion, prevents parties from litigating issues that could have been raised in a prior action. Carson v. Department of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005). Under the doctrine of res judicata, a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action. Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 337 (1995). The general rule of res judicata rests upon considerations of economy of judicial time and public policy favoring the establishment of certainty in legal relations. The rule provides that when a court of competent jurisdiction has entered a final judgment on the merits of a cause of action, the parties to the suit and their privies are thereafter bound "not only as to every matter which was offered and received to sustain or defeat the claim or demand, but as to any other admissible matter which might have been offered for that purpose." Commissioner of Internal Revenue v. Sunnen, 333 U.S. 591, 597 (1948) (quoting Cromwell v. County of Sac, 94 U.S. 351, 352 (1876)). Res judicata applies if: (1) the prior decision was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases. Carson, 398 F.3d at 1375.

Res judicata applies to prevent parties from repetitious litigation of the same cause of action if there exists identity of cause of action as well as parties. Cody v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 572, 573 n.3 (1983). The cause of action is the set of facts giving the appellant the right to seek relief from the agency. Frias v. USPS, 63 MSPR 276, 280 (1994); see Giove v. Dept. of Transp., 89 MSPR 560, 566 ¶ 14 (2001) (same holding as Frias), aff'd, Giove v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-3045). An EEOC determination that an appellant could not pursue a claim of discrimination because of a Board settlement was not res judicata as to the appellant's assertion that the agency violated the settlement agreement. The causes of action before the Board and the EEOC were not the same. Bables v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 171, 174-75 ¶¶ 11-12 (2000); Hanna v. Dept. of Labor, 80 MSPR 294, 302 ¶ 12 (1998) ("Since the cause of action in the two cases, a proposal to demote and a demotion, is not the same, res judicata is not applicable."). A decision of the Civil Service Commission precluded relitigation of the claim before the Board. Aceto v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 159, 161 (1989) (res judicata applied to an appeal of an agency action brought before the Board that was previously reviewed by the former Civil Service Commission Board of Appeals and Review). Res judicata also applies to initial decisions that become final through the passage of time. Judicial review initiated in the Federal Circuit does not alter the finality of the Board's decision. See Deloach v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 520, 524 (1994) (citing Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980)); cf. Adams v. DOD, DC-0752-14-1033-I-1 (NP 4/30/2015) ("Federal Circuit decisions were final judgments on the merits for purposes of res judicata.").

Res judicata bars relitigation of claims that were, and of claims that could have been, litigated in the first action, Reyman v. OPM, 237 Fed. Appx. 619, 621 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

...Feliciana seems to confuse res judicata (claim preclusion) with collateral estoppel (issue preclusion). A prerequisite to the application of collateral estoppel is that the issue was actually litigated in the prior case. See Thomas v. Gen. Serv. Admin., 794 F.2d 661, 664 (Fed. Cir. 1986). By contrast, "[u]nder res judicata, a final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980) (emphasis added). Thus, application of res judicata only requires: (1) a final judgment on the merits; (2) the same parties or their privies; and (3) an issue that could have been raised in the prior proceeding.

There is no dispute that the parties involved in this appeal--Feliciana and OPM--are the same as were involved in the prior action involving the guaranteed minimum annuity. Moreover, OPM issued a decision on the merits regarding Feliciana's claim to the guaranteed minimum annuity, and the Board affirmed. Finally, there is no reason why Feliciana could not have raised her claim with OPM for an annuity payable immediately from the date of retirement at the same time that she raised her claim to the guaranteed minimum annuity. Both claims relate to the same cause of action--the appropriate amount of Hilarion's annuity--even though they are based on different legal theories. See Alyeska Pipeline Serv. Co. v. United States, 688 F.2d 765, 769 (Ct. Cl. 1982) ("[C]laim splitting cannot be justified on the ground that the two actions are based on different legal theories."). Although Feliciana belatedly raised the claim of an immediately payable annuity with OPM after it agreed to pay her the guaranteed minimum annuity, she failed to do so in the original OPM proceedings. The Board therefore properly concluded that this claim was barred by res judicata. See Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Ass'n v. Solimino, 501 U.S. 104, 107 (1991) ("When an administrative agency is acting in a judicial capacity...the courts have not hesitated to apply res judicata to enforce repose." (quoting United States v. Utah Constr. & Mining Co., 384 U.S. 394, 422 (1966)) (emphasis in original)).

Nobles v. OPM, 281 Fed. Appx. 972, 974 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP), restated the doctrine in terms of claims that could have been brought in earlier litigation:

...Under the doctrine of res judicata, a final judgment on the merits precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action. Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980). Claim preclusion prevents Mr. Nobles from raising a new claim that could have been advanced in Nobles-I. Pactiv Corp. v. Dow Chemical Co., 449 F.3d 1227, 1230 (Fed. Cir. 2006). For claim preclusion to bar a new claim, it must be based on the same set of transactional facts as the earlier one. Int'l Air Response v. U.S., 302 F.3d 1363, 1368 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Jet, Inc. v. Sewage Aeration Sys., 223 F.3d 1360, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (explaining that "courts have defined 'transaction' in terms of a 'core of operative facts,' the 'same operative facts,' or the 'same nucleus of operative facts,' and 'based on the same, or nearly the same, factual allegations.'").

See Phillips/May Corp. v. United States, 524 F.3d 1264, 1267-70 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (restating elements of res judicata; foreclosure of litigation of matters that never have been litigated, because of a determination that they should have been advanced in an earlier suit; recognizing exception to the doctrine under the Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 26(1)(c) when "plaintiff was unable to rely on a certain theory of the case or to seek a certain remedy or form of relief in the first action because of the subject matter jurisdiction of the courts or [other] restrictions on their authority"); cf. Schattenkerk v. USDA, 30 MSPR 93, 94 n.2 (1986) ("A presiding official of the Board has no authority to consider a matter which was fully adjudicated by the former U.S. Civil Service Commission.").

Res judicata bars either factual or legal arguments that were brought, or could have been brought, before the Board in the earlier appeal. See Bodine v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 91-3462). Examples of the application of res judicata are many. See Winston v. Dept. of Labor (Fed. Cir. 1997 NP No. 96-3245) ("Just as a plaintiff in a tort case cannot bring successive actions based on different theories of liability, Winston cannot bring successive administrative actions based on different theories of LEO coverage."); Guzman v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 92-3050) ("Under principles of res judicata, Guzman is now barred from attempting to relitigate the merits of his removal action. See Nevada v. United States, 463 U.S. 110, 129-30 (1983) (res judicata includes not only matters expressly raised to sustain or defeat a cause of action, but also matters that were not, but might have been, raised for that purpose)."); Donahue v. USPS, 100 MSPR 387, 395 ¶ 20 (2005) (constructive adverse action: the appellant's later appeal was not barred by res judicata because, in an enforced leave situation, he could not have known at the time that he filed earlier appeals that he would remain in a nonduty, nonpay status for so lengthy a period); Austria v. OPM, 77 MSPR 287, 289 (1998) ("Assuming that the argument is new, the doctrine of res judicata precludes parties from relitigating issues that could have been raised in a prior action."); Cooke v. USPS, 69 MSPR 259, 262 (1996) ("Under the doctrine of res judicata, the parties are precluded from relitigating any argument which was raised or could have been raised in the prior proceeding."); Seltzer v. OPM, 62 MSPR 493, 496 (1994) ("[C]onsideration of evidence or argument which is inconsistent with a finding in a prior appeal constitutes relitigation of a matter resolved, and is barred by the doctrine of res judicata. Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980)."); Lopez v. Dept. of Labor, 57 MSPR 163 (1993) (concerning successive claims to restoration for a partially recovered employee: "[F]inal judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." (relying on Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980); Guzman v. OPM, 51 MSPR 1, 4 (1991)); cf. Tyler v. USPS, 90 MSPR 545, 548 ¶ 10 (2002) (same as Cooke, supra). In Lopez, supra, the Board precluded litigation of a restoration claim that varied from, but could have been raised during, an earlier appeal concerning restoration rights. But see Hicks v. USPS, 66 MSPR 137, 141 (1995) (res judicata did not bar an ensuing restoration appeal based on a subsequent change in the Labor Department's determination of an appellant's worker's compensation status); cf. Donahue v. USPS, 100 MSPR 387, 392 ¶ 12 (2005) (litigation over an employment dispute in federal district court does not preclude the application of res judicata to an otherwise final Board decision).

As to enforcement petitions, the Board appears to permit litigation of multiple complaints as long as the incidents complained of are not the same. See James v. USPS, 64 MSPR 376, 379 (1994); cf. Detherage v. But res judicata may bar successive petitions when the matters raised in a later petition could have been raised in an earlier enforcement proceeding. See Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375-76 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (discussed in Chapter 16). Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 679, 683 (1996) (in an enforcement proceeding, the doctrine of res judicata bars further litigation of the underlying decision on the merits); Frias v. In the most common application of res judicata, an employee who appeals an adverse action and loses through a final decision will receive a dismissal on the basis of res judicata if he again files the same appeal. The Board provided additional explication of its conception of res judicata in Roque v. OPM, 55 MSPR 50, 52 (1992), leading the Board to distinguish an appeal seeking reconsideration of an OPM decision denying a retirement annuity and another appeal seeking refund of retirement contributions:

The doctrine of res judicata applies to prevent repetitious litigation of the same cause of action.... An issue is res judicata only if there exists identity of cause of action as well as identity of parties.... Thus, a final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action and the doctrine of res judicata applies only with regard to matters that were actually adjudicated in prior appeals.

In Peartree v. USPS, 66 MSPR 332, 336-41 (1995), the Board explained that the dismissal of an appeal as untimely filed under an MSPB regulatory time limit is not entitled to res judicata effect because it is not a decision "on the merits." In Peartree, the issue was whether the appellant, whose case was once dismissed for untimeliness, could litigate the same case before the Board after obtaining a subsequent decision from EEOC stating that the case could be pursued to the Board. There was no bar to the initiation of the second appeal:

The term "res judicata" is sometimes used broadly to encompass not only the doctrine of "claim preclusion" but also the doctrine of "issue preclusion," also called "collateral estoppel." See Lane v. Peterson, 899 F.2d 737, 741 n.3 (8th Cir.), cert. denied, 498 U.S. 823, 111 S. Ct. 74, 112 L. Ed.2d 48 (1990); 18 Charles A. Wright, Arthur R. Miller & Edward H. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure § 4402 (1981). The doctrines of claim preclusion and issue preclusion both concern the preclusive effects of a prior adjudication and are based on similar policy concerns--to "relieve parties of the cost and vexation of multiple lawsuits, conserve judicial resources, and, by preventing inconsistent decisions, encourage reliance on adjudication." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94, 101 S. Ct. 411, 415, 66 L. Ed.2d 308 (1980). However, there are significant differences in their prerequisites for application, as well as in their preclusive effects, as further discussed below. The Board's practice, in general, has been to use the term "res judicata" in the narrow sense, to refer solely to the doctrine of claim preclusion, and we will do so here.[4] See, e.g., Lopez v. Department of Labor, 57 MSPR 163, 166 (1993) (citing Allen, 449 U.S. at 94, 101 S. Ct. at 414).

[4] On the other hand, there are Board cases that appear to use res judicata in the broad sense, or appear to commingle the res judicata and collateral estoppel doctrines. See Roque v. Office of Personnel Management, 55 MSPR 50, 52 (1992) (stating that res judicata applies only to "matters that were actually adjudicated in prior appeals," whereas claim preclusion applies also to matters that were not, but could have been, raised and adjudicated in prior appeals); Schoening v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 MSPR 6, 10 (1989) (stating that res judicata applies "[e]ven if the second suit is on a different cause of action," whereas claim preclusion applies only if the second "suit" is on the same cause of action); see also Wexler v. Department of the Interior, 48 MSPR 513, 517 (1991), aff'd, 954 F.2d 733 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); Wilson v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 MSPR 244, 247-488 (1987).

Under the doctrine of res judicata, a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action. See Federated Department Stores, Inc. v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 398, 101 S. Ct. 2424, 2427, 69 L. Ed.2d 103 (1981); Lopez, 57 MSPR at 166; see also Lane, 899 F.2d at 741. The doctrine precludes the parties from relitigating issues that were, or could have been, raised in the prior action, see, e.g., Federated Department Stores, Inc., 452 U.S. at 398, 101 S. Ct. at 2427, and is applicable if: (1) The prior judgment was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction;[5] (2) the prior judgment was a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases. See Lane, 899 F.2d at 742; Nilsen v. City of Moss Point, Miss., 701 F.2d 556, 559 (5th Cir. 1983).

[5] The subsequent forum has a limited scope of review in determining whether the prior forum possessed competent jurisdiction to render its decision; the scope of review is generally limited to ascertaining whether the issue of jurisdiction has been fully and fairly litigated and finally decided in the first forum. See Underwriters National Assurance Company v. North Carolina Life and Accident and Health Insurance Guaranty Association, 455 U.S. 691, 704-07, 102 S. Ct. 1357, 1365-67, 71 L. Ed.2d 558 (1982).

Because we find that the second criterion is not satisfied here, we find it unnecessary to address the first and third criteria. With respect to the second criterion, the decision in the prior Board appeal unquestionably became "final" on July 31, 1992. The remaining issue is whether that decision was "on the merits." This issue is one which the Board has not heretofore addressed in depth, although the Board has suggested in Caple v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 MSPR 262, 265-66 (1992), and other cases that the prior decision need not be "on the merits" for res judicata to apply.

In Caple, 52 MSPR at 265-66, the Board refused to hear an appeal where it had dismissed a prior appeal from the same personnel action as not within its jurisdiction, stating that "the doctrine of res judicata [applies] to questions of jurisdiction." Accord Byrd v. Department of the Air Force, 56 MSPR 629, 632 (1993); Schoening v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 MSPR 6, 10 (1989). The Board thus gave res judicata effect to its prior dismissal of an appeal for lack of jurisdiction, citing Underwriters National Assurance v. North Carolina Life and Accident and Health Insurance Guaranty Association, 455 U.S. 691, 706-07, 102 S. Ct. 1357, 1366-67, 71 L. Ed.2d 558 (1982); Durfee v. Duke, 375 U.S. 106, 111, 84 S.C. 242, 245, 11 LED.2d 186 (1963); and American Surety Co. v. Baldwin, 287 U.S. 156, 165-66, 53 S.C. 98, 101-02, 77 LED. 231 (1932). Caple, 52 MSPR at 265. However, these cases merely hold that, where the criteria for the application of res judicata are satisfied, the first forum's decision on all issues, including its finding that it had jurisdiction over the case, should be given full faith and credit by the second forum and not relitigated upon a party's claim that the first forum lacked competent jurisdiction to render its decision.[6] They do not hold that res judicata is applicable to a prior decision that dismissed the case for lack of jurisdiction.

[6] The second forum may, however, conduct a limited inquiry into whether the issue of the first forum's jurisdiction was fully and fairly litigated and finally decided in the first forum. See supra n.5.

In fact, it is "well settled" that a "dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction does not preclude a second action on the same claim...." 18 C. Wright, A. Miller & E. Cooper, supra, § 4436 at 339; see International Philanthropic Hospital Foundation v. United States, 621 F.2d 402, 405, 223 Ct. Cl. 587 (1980); see also Smith v. Pittsburgh Gage and Supply Company, 464 F.2d 870, 874 (3rd Cir. 1972). A second action on the same claim would be viable if the prior jurisdictional defect is cured by, e.g., filing the second action in a forum that does have jurisdiction. See 18 C. Wright, A. Miller & E. Cooper, supra, § 4435 at 330 n.7; Smith, 464 F.2d at 874. Of course, a second action in the same forum would generally be precluded by the doctrine of collateral estoppel which, as discussed below, would preclude relitigation of the same jurisdictional issue. See 18 C. Wright, A. Miller supra, § 4436 at 338-39. Hence, to the extent that Caple and other similar cases relied on the doctrine of "res judicata," we clarify them to find that those appeals were instead barred by the doctrine of collateral estoppel. Cf. Deckert v. Wachovia Student Financial Services, Inc., 963 F.2d 816 (5th Cir. 1992) (majority and dissenting opinions addressing the terminology of res judicata and collateral estoppel).

Although it is thus well settled that a dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction cannot be given res judicata effect, it is not as well settled whether other types of dismissals prior to an actual adjudication on the merits, such as the Board's dismissal of this appellant's prior appeal as untimely filed, are entitled to res judicata effect. See 18 C. Wright, A. Miller supra, § 4435; cf. Federated Department Stores, Inc., 452 U.S. at 399 & n.3, 101 S.C. at 2428 & n.3 (a dismissal for failure to state a claim could be a "judgment on the merits," for res judicata purposes).

We are aware that in Grasty v. Department of the Navy, 41 MSPR 234, 237-38 (1989), the Board found that the appellant's appeal from the agency's final EEO decision was barred by the doctrine of res judicata because the Board had previously dismissed as untimely filed the appellant's request for review of the final grievance decision regarding the same personnel action. The Board thus gave res judicata effect to a prior Board decision dismissing an appeal as untimely filed. Relying on Detherage v. Department of the Air Force, 30 MSPR 411, 413 (1986), and 18 C. Wright, A. Miller supra, § 4441, the Board stated that a final Board decision dismissing an appeal as untimely filed "disposes of the entire claim for res judicata purposes." Id. at 237 n.4.

Detherage does not support the finding in Grasty because it did not involve the doctrine of res judicata. In Detherage, the Board implicitly applied the "law of the case" doctrine in refusing to reconsider an issue that it previously adjudicated against the agency (based on the agency's failure to timely raise the issue) in its prior remand decision in the same appeal. See Detherage, 30 MSPR at 413; Detherage v. Department of Air Force, 29 MSPR 45, 47 n.* (1985). Law of the case rules were developed to "maintain consistency and avoid reconsideration of matters once decided during the course of a single continuing lawsuit." 18 C. Wright, A. Miller & E. Cooper, supra, § 4478 at 788. These rules "do not involve preclusion by final judgment" but, instead, "regulate judicial affairs before final judgment." Id.; see Harris v. Department of Agriculture, 53 MSPR 78, 81 (1992), aff'd, 988 F.2d 130 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).

Nor does 18 C. Wright, A. Miller & E. Cooper, supra, § 4441 support the finding in Grasty. Section 4441 explains that a dismissal of a case as barred by the applicable statute of limitations should be given res judicata effect, noting that "[a] plea of the statute of limitations is a plea to the merits...." Id. at 366 & n.1 (quoting United States v. Oppenheimer, 242 U.S. 85, 87, 37 S.C. 68, 69, 61 LED. 161 (1916)). Unlike statutes of limitations, however, which have long and consistently been deemed part of the merits, see, e.g., Oppenheimer, 242 U.S. at 87, 37 S.C. at 69, regulatory time limits[7] for initiating a proceeding are not deemed part of the merits, see Fay v. South Colonie Central School District, 802 F.2d 21, 29 (2nd Cir. 1986). Unlike a statute of limitations, which is part and parcel of a particular claim to the extent that the statute must be satisfied to prevail on the claim, a regulatory time limit for filing a Board appeal is not similarly part and parcel of a particular claim before the Board, notably in mixed cases for which a number of procedural choices and concomitant time limits are involved. See 5 CFR § 1201.154. We therefore find that a regulatory time limit for filing a Board appeal constitutes merely a "precondition requisite to the [Board's] going forward to determine the merits of [a] substantive claim," i.e., merely an "initial bar to the [Board's] reaching" the merits, and is not equivalent to a statute of limitations for res judicata purposes. Costello v. United States, 365 U.S. 265, 267-68, 285-86, 81 S.C. 534, 535-36, 544-45, 5 L. Ed.2d 551 (1961) (for the quoted reasons, the Court found that a dismissal of a case for failure to file an "affidavit of good cause, which [was] a prerequisite to the initiation of [the prior] proceedings" was not an adjudication on the merits for res judicata purposes).

[7] By "regulatory time limits," we mean time limits set forth in regulations that are not based on statutory time limits. Cf. 5 CFR § 1209.5 (based on 5 USC § 1214(a)(3)).

We therefore find that a dismissal of an appeal as untimely filed, under a regulatory time limit of the Board, is not entitled to res judicata effect because it is not a decision "on the merits." See Fay, 802 F.2d at 25, 28-29 (because a regulatory time limit for filing administrative appeals does not constitute a statute of limitations for res judicata purposes, a dismissal based on the time limit does not bar a subsequent action); see also Costello, 365 U.S. at 267-68, 285-86, 81 S.C. at 535-36, 544-45. Hence, we modify Grasty consistent with the analysis set forth above,[8] and conclude that the Board's dismissal of the appellant's prior appeal as untimely filed is not entitled to res judicata effect so as to bar the present appeal. See Fay, 802 F.2d at 25, 28-29; see also Costello, 365 U.S. at 267-68, 285-86, 81 S.C. at 535-36, 544-45; cf. Spears v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 766 F.2d 520, 522-23 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (a mixed case Board appeal was barred by res judicata where a prior Board appeal from the same personnel action was adjudicated on the merits); Golden v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 268, 272-73 (1994) (notwithstanding the doctrine of res judicata, the Board reopened the mixed case appeal, which was erroneously dismissed as untimely filed but should have been dismissed without prejudice as premature, on the basis that only four months had elapsed from the issuance of the erroneous prior Board decision).

[8] The ultimate disposition of Grasty was correct, based on the alternative grounds that the appeal was barred by the election requirement of 5 USC § 7121, which precludes the direct pursuit of a statutory procedure (such as the Board procedure) where the employee has previously elected the grievance procedure. See Grasty, 41 MSPR at 237.

As noted above, a decision that is not "on the merits," such as the dismissal of the appellant's prior appeal involved here, could be given collateral estoppel effect. The Board may apply the doctrine of collateral estoppel if: (1) The issue previously adjudicated is identical to that now presented; (2) that issue was actually litigated in the prior case; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded by the doctrine was fully represented in the prior case. See Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Mycka v. Office of Personnel Management, 56 MSPR 675, 678-79 (1993). Collateral estoppel does not apply here, however, because the timeliness issue involved in the present appeal--whether the appeal was timely filed within 20 days after the appellant received the agency's final EEO decision, under 5 CFR § 1201.154(b) (1993)--was not involved in the prior Board appeal. See Berkey v. U.S. Postal Service, 42 MSPR 24, 28 (1989).

See Moore v. DOJ, 112 MSPR 382, 389 ¶ 22, 2009 MSPB 190 (2009) (earlier decision dismissing an appeal as untimely was not res judicata as to a merits determination on a later appeal that was timely filed under mixed-case processing procedures); Peltier v. DOJ, 79 MSPR 674, 677 ¶ 6 (1998) ("[T]he appellant is correct that res judicata would not bar this appeal...because the now-final decisions dismissing the earlier appeal without prejudice to refiling and as untimely refiled were not on the merits."); Pashun v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 374, 380 n.2 (1997) (because an earlier IRA appeal was dismissed as untimely rather than adjudicated on the merits, the doctrine of res judicata does not apply); Nebblett v. OPM, 73 MSPR 342, 347 (1997) ("While a dismissal under a statute of limitations is a ruling on the merits, and can therefore be given res judicata effect, the Board's regulatory time limit for filing an appeal is not a statute of limitation, but rather a precondition requisite to the Board's reaching the merits of a claim, and a dismissal for failing to file within the Board's regulatory time limit is therefore not entitled to res judicata effect.").

Rogers v. OPM, 67 MSPR 251, 256 (1995), distinguished procedural and merits adjudication for purposes of res judicata:

The purpose of the doctrine of res judicata is to prevent repetitious litigation of the same matter. Department of the Interior, 48 MSPR 513, 517 (1991), aff'd, 954 F.2d 733 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). The doctrine applies only where there is an identity of parties and cause of action, and its application is limited to issues that were or could have been actually adjudicated in prior appeals. See, e.g., Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 337 (1995); Holloway v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 56 MSPR 422, 425 (1993); Wexler, 48 MSPR at 517-18. The Board's decision in an appeal from an agency action is res judicata only where there has been a final Board decision on the merits of the appeal. See, e.g., Peartree, 66 MSPR at 337-38, 339-40; Bullitt v. U.S. Postal Service, 10 MSPB 816, 12 MSPR 294, 296 (1982); see also Cavanagh v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 MSPR 485, 487-90 (1990). Thus, we find that because the Board previously decided this appeal on procedural grounds, i.e., timeliness, and there was no final Board decision on the merits, OPM is not barred by the doctrine of res judicata from reopening or reconsidering its reconsideration decision.

See Baker v. DHS, 111 MSPR 277, 282 ¶ 14, 2009 MSPB 83 (2009) (dismissal for failure to state a claim is a decision on the merits, not a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction); Lubert v. USPS, 110 MSPR 430, 435 ¶ 12, 2009 MSPB 13 (2009) (dismissal for failure to state a claim is a decision on the merits); Holloway v. Dept. of Interior, 82 MSPR 435, 440 ¶ 11 (1999) ("[A]ppellant's claim for reinstatement was raised but dismissed as beyond the Board's enforcement authority in the prior compliance appeal and was not adjudicated on its merits.... [I]t is not barred by...res judicata."); cf. McGrath v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 500, 503 (1981) (if an initial decision is vacated, it serves neither side by way of res judicata or collateral estoppel).

Although res judicata applies to bar appeals litigated on the merits and there is authority stating that it does not bar relitigation of cases dismissed for jurisdictional deficiencies, collateral estoppel may prevent relitigation of jurisdictional issues. Four conditions must be present: the issues previously and currently litigated are the same; the dispositive issue was actually litigated in the earlier case; the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and the party precluded by the doctrine was fully represented in the prior case. Gregory v. OPM, 66 MSPR 691, 700 (1995); see Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 92 MSPR 370, 375 ¶ 12 (2002) (collateral estoppel may preclude a second action in the same forum that dismissed a prior appeal for lack of jurisdiction). If the first Board disposition is res judicata as to the second appeal, the bar is not avoided by an allegation of inadequate representation in the first case. Grande v. Dept. of Transp., 47 MSPR 403, 405-06 (1991). Pro se status does not defeat collateral estoppel. Jackson v. VA, 95 MSPR 152, 156 ¶ 11 (2003).
