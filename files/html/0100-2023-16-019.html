b. Draft; Intermediate Work Product; Revisions or Rewrites

Posing a problem for midlevel managers and professional employees is the performance standard that requires work product, draft or final, that is supposed to meet requirements of timeliness, accuracy, or completeness. Difficulties develop as the supervisor receives the work, announces it to be deficient in some manner, and then returns it to the employee for a rewrite. Recognizing that most work is susceptible to some revision, the Board appears to take a conservative view of standards calling for work to be produced first in final form and always in final form, i.e., with no need for revision or correction. In Thompson v. Farm Credit Admin., 51 MSPR 569, 574 n.1 (1991), the standard for a GS-14 Financial Analyst was:

C. Prepares a complete and adequately supported work product which:

1. requires no major changes because it is clear, concise, understandable, and targeted for the appropriate audience;

2. addresses and appropriately resolves all pertinent issues; establishes processes to verify the accuracy of final data in most critical projects;

3. contains sound, reasonable, and logical conclusions and recommendations.

The Board considered the Thompson standard unreasonable, 51 MSPR at 578:

In this case, Standard C of the element refers to a "complete" work product and "the accuracy of final data." The language of the standard does not imply that it applies to drafts. Further, the agency did not introduce evidence to indicate that it applied the standard to drafts of other employees. In fact, the record shows that it was not normal to require error-free first drafts...and it was not unusual for first drafts of employees comparably situated to the appellant to be incomplete in some ways or to have errors in some respect.... For these reasons and, as explained below, in light of the retaliatory motive that infused the appellant's removal, the Board finds that the agency, by imposing the standard applied to final work products on the appellant's drafts, improperly imposed a higher level of performance than was required by the Data Bases element.[7]

[7] The performance standard...applies only to final work products and not to draft work products. An agency could take a performance-based action under a performance standard that pertains to unsatisfactory draft work products.

But if rewrites are required, the Board may defer to the agency's views concerning their necessity. See Malcolm v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-0432-15-1154-I-1 (NP 10/6/2022) (patent examiner: "we find it appropriate to defer to the agency's determination that revisions to the appellant's office actions were necessary before they were approved by his supervisor," citing Greer v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 477, 485 (1998); cf. Vitatoe v. GSA, 131 Fed. Appx. 277 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (misconduct case: "even if a mere draft, the communication contained fabricated scores and false attribution").
