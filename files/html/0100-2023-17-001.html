CHAPTER 9

REVIEW OF ARBITRATION AWARDS

I. STATUTORY AUTHORITY FOR REVIEW

As with other components of FLRA jurisdiction, so too with its authority to review arbitration awards: jurisdiction is complex. The labor relations sections of the Reform Act provide and limit review authority of FLRA to review certain arbitration awards, and the CSRA precludes arbitrators from reviewing certain matters, leaving FLRA to define limits on the types of cases subject to their review.

FLRA review of arbitration awards is governed by 5 USC 7122:

(a) Either party to arbitration under this chapter may file with the Authority an exception to any arbitrator's award pursuant to the arbitration (other than an award relating to a matter described in section 7121(f) of this title). If upon review the Authority finds the award is deficient--

(1) because it is contrary to any law, rule, or regulation; or

(2) on other grounds similar to those applied by Federal courts in private sector labor-management relations;

the Authority may take such action and make such recommendations concerning the award as it considers necessary, consistent with applicable laws, rules, or regulations.

(b) If no exception to an arbitrator's award is filed under subsection (a) of this section during the 30-day period beginning on the date the award is served on the party, the award shall be final and binding. An agency shall take the actions required by an arbitrator's final award. The award may include the payment of backpay (as provided in section 5596 of this title).

"Even where an arbitrator may lack jurisdiction, that lack of jurisdiction does not divest the Authority's jurisdiction to resolve exceptions to an award." NFFE Local 479 and Dept. of Air Force, NAS Pensacola, 67 FLRA 284, 285 (2014) (citing VA Canteen Serv. and AFGE Local 85, 66 FLRA 1007 (2012)). Review of an arbitration award is not conducted under the standards of the Arbitration Act, 9 USC 10(a). DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 27 FLRA 706, 708 (1987). [Refer in this chapter to, "Section 7121(f) Award," concerning enforcement of arbitration awards that 5 USC 7121(f) places beyond FLRA's direct exceptions review authority.]

FLRA provides a description of the arbitration review process in its Arbitration Appeal Procedural Review Checklist, http://www.flra.gov/arbitration_appeal_procedural_review_checklist. Guidance is also provided in the updated (2016) Guide to Arbitration Under the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute https://www.flra.gov/system/files/webfm/Authority/AR%20Forms,%20Guide,%20Other/Arbitration%20Guide%209.30.16.pdf, and in the forms provided by the Authority (http://www.flra.gov/arbitration_forms), and in a brief overview of the process (http://www.flra.gov/arbitration).

The statutory review scheme mirrors the "deferential standard of review the Federal courts use in reviewing awards in the private sector." Ostensibly, FLRA and the courts defer to arbitrators' judgments because the parties have bargained for the arbitrator's construction of the contract. SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 1923, 57 FLRA 690, 693 (2002). That deference may be declining as a result of decisions issued since 2017.

FLRA members review a lot of arbitration awards, in comparison with the number of cases percolating through exceptions to ALJ awards involving ULPs, negotiability, and representation disputes. Former Member Pizzella was critical of what he believes to be unnecessary use of the exceptions process. Among his several dissenting or concurring opinions, typical of his expressions of concern is the brief passage in his concurrence to DHHS, CM&MS and AFGE Local 1923, 67 FLRA 665, 669 (2014):

It is surprising that the Agency did not agree to settle this case, given that the Union had a plausible claim and that the arbitration costs surely exceed the value of any cash award to which the grievant may have been entitled. Parties should remember that "[t]he better part of valor is discretion," and that they should consider the costs and benefits before commencing or advancing litigation, not only in dollars or their representatives' time, but also in terms of the effect on their labor-management relationship and employee morale. Moreover, the Authority's role is "to review the award of the arbitrator on very narrow grounds similar to the scope of judicial review of an arbitrator's award in the private sector," not to be an audience for parties wishing to vent their frustrations or a showcase for representatives to demonstrate their tenacity to their leadership or members.

As in the private sector, federal sector arbitration awards may have significant implications for employers--all the more reason for the parties to present cases to arbitrators with care in both factual and legal submissions, and to ensure that all arguments necessary to an arbitrator's award are presented to the arbitrator and not for the first time to FLRA. Former Member Pizzella expressed his concerns about the significance of some awards in the federal sector in a dissenting opinion in NTEU Chapter 83 and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 68 FLRA 945, 959 (2015) (argued by the majority to be hyperbolic):

It is inconceivable to me that Congress ever intended for a single arbitrator to have such expansive power to directly undermine the mission readiness of an entire federal agency and usurp fundamental management prerogatives. Congress certainly did not intend for the Authority to simply rubberstamp, and expand upon, such overreach.

Here, there is no other appeal for the IRS because, as in most arbitration cases, the Authority is the last level of review. Thus, this ill-conceived award is bound to go into effect.

FLRA considers and decides issues that are material, and may decide to avoid resolution of other issues, as it explained in an opaque passage from DHS, C&BP and NTEU Chapter 154, 69 FLRA 412, 414 (2016) (Member Pizzella concurring):

We disagree with our colleague's assertion that "there is no justification" to only assume, without deciding, that the Agency's response is properly before us. To the contrary, consistent with established tenets of judicial decision making "general[ly,]...an opinion should address only the issues that need to be resolved to decide the case."[23] Consequently, as here, "[t]hat an issue has been raised by the parties does not mean that it must be addressed in the opinion if it is not material to the outcome" of the decision.[24] Moreover, because the Authority in appropriate circumstances "assumes without deciding" matters without using the particular phrase "assume without deciding,"[25] our colleague's assertion that the Authority has assumed, without deciding, facts or matters only "forty nine...times"[26] in its history greatly understates the Authority's adherence to this widely recognized judicial practice.
