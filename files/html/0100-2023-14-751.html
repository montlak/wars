broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 22: Grievance Procedures, Section B: Level of Union Grievance Representation, Subsection 01: Requirement of Union Involvement

1. Requirement of Union Involvement

Section 7114(a)(5) of the Statute provides that in any grievance or appeal procedure not negotiated under the Statute, e.g., adverse action appeal procedures under 5 USC 7501-04, employees are guaranteed the right to select their personal representatives. A contract may not require that the union approve the representative who will aid an employee in a statutory appeal. AFGE Local 1858 and Army Missile Command, Redstone Arsenal Commissary, 27 FLRA 69, 70-72, 82 (1987) (Provisions 1 and 7). The contract may not require that employees secure union concurrence before they file grievances. Section 7121(b)(3)(B) gives the employee the right to file a grievance on his own. AFGE Local 3486 and New Jersey Air Nat'l Guard, 177th Fighter Interceptor Group, 5 FLRA 209, 214 (1981). But the employee's right to select his own representative is limited to a statutory appeal or noncontract (administrative or agency) grievance procedure. The union controls the appointment of representatives in the negotiated grievance procedure, Sheet Metal Workers Int'l Ass'n Local 97 and Cosden, 7 FLRA 799, 815 (1982) (ALJ Decision):

The right of unions to control the grievance procedure is an important one. A private attorney acts only in interest of his client, and may negotiate a settlement that is contrary to the interest of other bargaining unit employees. If individual employees were allowed to cut their own deals, it would dilute and diminish the right of unions to act as exclusive bargaining agents, for employers would undoubtedly prefer to play one employee against another, or buy them off, one by one.

FLRA pointed out in Sheet Metal Workers that if, during a grievance case, the union appoints a representative for an employee who has a conflict of interest, the employee has the right either to present his grievance directly to management under § 7121(b)(3)(B), or the employee may lodge a ULP charge against the union if the representation he receives violates the duty of fair representation.

A contract may not provide an employee with the right to be represented by an individual, including an attorney, of the employee's own choosing. The employee may represent himself or the union may provide the representation. 5 USC 7114(5)(A). NFFE Local 1001 and Dept. of Air Force, Vandenberg AFB, 15 FLRA 804, 806 (1984) (Provision 1). Under AFGE Local 1858 and Army Missile Command, Redstone Arsenal Commissary, 27 FLRA 69, 71-72, 82 (1987) (Provisions 1 and 7), supra, however, nothing precludes negotiation of a procedure allowing the employee three options as to grievance representation: by a union official; by a representative approved by the union president; or by the grievant himself. In Vandenberg, the representative approved by the union would have been the employee's personal representative but would not have served as the union's representative. In Redstone Arsenal, the representative approved by the union would serve as the union's representative as well as the representative of the employee. Although § 7114(a)(5) precludes the employee from designating his own representative in a grievance proceeding, the law does not preclude the union from designating its representative to act for the employee and the union in the same type of case. See Colorado Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, Ft. Lyons, Colo., 25 FLRA 803, 811-12 (1987).
