24. Recovery From Disability: Burden of Proof

Under 5 USC 8337(d):

If an annuitant receiving disability retirement annuity from the Fund, before becoming 60 years of age, recovers from his disability, payment of the annuity terminates on the reemployment by the Government or 1 year after the date of the medical examination showing the recovery, whichever is earlier. If an annuitant receiving disability retirement annuity from the Fund, before becoming 60 years of age, is restored to an earning capacity fairly comparable to the current rate of pay of the position occupied at the time of retirement, payment of the annuity terminates on reemployment by the Government or 180 days after the end of the calendar year in which earning capacity is so restored, whichever is earlier. Earning capacity is deemed restored if in any calendar year the income of the annuitant from wages or self-employment or both equals at least 80 percent of the current rate of pay of the position occupied immediately before retirement.

5 USC 8337(e) provides:

If an annuitant whose annuity is terminated under subsection (d) of this section is not reemployed in a position in which he is subject to this subchapter, he is deemed, except for service credit, to have been involuntarily separated from the service for the purpose of this subchapter as of the date of termination of the disability annuity, and after that termination is entitled to annuity under the applicable provisions of this subchapter. If an annuitant whose annuity is heretofore or hereafter terminated because of an earning capacity provision of this subchapter or an earlier statute--

(1) is not reemployed in a position in which he is subject to this subchapter; and

(2) has not recovered from the disability for which he was retired;

his annuity shall be restored at the same rate effective the first of the year following any calendar year in which his income from wages or self-employment or both is less than 80 percent of the current rate of pay of the position occupied immediately before retirement. If an annuitant whose annuity is heretofore or hereafter terminated because of a medical finding that he has recovered from disability is not reemployed in a position in which he is subject to this subchapter, his annuity shall be restored at the same rate effective from the date of medical examination showing a recurrence of the disability. The second and third sentences of this subsection do not apply to an individual who has become 62 years of age and is receiving or is eligible to receive annuity under the first sentence of this subsection.

5 CFR 831.1208 (2022) (CSRS) provides as to recovery or reemployment by the federal government of an individual who has retired with a disability annuity:

Termination of disability annuity because of recovery.

(a) Each annuitant receiving disability annuity from the Fund shall be examined under the direction of OPM at the end of 1 year from the date of disability retirement and annually thereafter until the annuitant becomes 60 years of age unless the disability is found by OPM to be permanent in character. OPM may order a medical or other examination at any time to determine the facts relative to the nature and degree of disability of the annuitant. Failure to submit to reexamination shall result in suspension of annuity.

(b) A disability annuitant may request medical reevaluation under the provisions of this section at any time. OPM will reevaluate the medical condition of disability annuitants age 60 or over only on their own request.

(c) Recovery based on medical documentation. When an examination or reevaluation shows that a disability annuitant has medically recovered from the disability, OPM will terminate the annuity effective on the first day of the month beginning 1 year after the date of the medical examination showing recovery.

(d) Recovery based on reemployment by the Federal Government. Reemployment by an agency at any time before age 60 is evidence of recovery if the reemployment is in a permanent position at the same or higher grade or pay level as the position from which the disability annuitant retired. The permanent position must be full-time unless the position the disability annuitant occupied immediately before retirement was less than full-time, in which case the permanent position must have a work schedule of no less time than that of the position from which the disability annuitant retired. In this instance, OPM needs no medical documentation to find the annuitant recovered. Disability annuity payments will terminate effective on the first day of the month following the month in which the recovery finding is made under this paragraph.

(e) Recovery based on a voluntary request. OPM will honor a written and signed statement of medical recovery voluntarily filed by a disability annuitant when the medical documentation on file does not demonstrate that the annuitant is mentally incompetent. OPM needs no other documentation to find the annuitant recovered. Disability annuity payments will terminate effective on the first day of the month beginning 1 year after the date of the statement. A disability annuitant can withdraw the statement only if the withdrawal is received by OPM before annuity payments terminate.

(f) When an agency reemploys a recovered disability annuitant at any grade or rate of pay within the 1-year period pending termination of the disability retirement benefit under paragraph (c), (d), or (e) of this section, OPM will terminate the annuity effective on the date of reemployment.

Burdens of proof involved in discontinuation of a disability annuity upon medical recovery of the appellant are described by Heiter v. OPM, 107 MSPR 514, 516-17 ¶ 4, 2007 MSPB 305 (2007):

OPM may terminate a disability retirement annuity when an examination or re-evaluation shows that a disability annuitant has medically recovered from the disability. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1208(c). In an action brought by OPM to discontinue a voluntary disability retirement annuity due to an annuitant's alleged recovery from disability, the annuitant bears the burden of proving, by preponderant evidence, continuing entitlement to disability retirement. See Rountree v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 M.S.P.R. 480, 483 (1988). The annuitant may meet this burden by presenting probative evidence of: (1) objective clinical findings; (2) diagnoses and expert medical opinions; and (3) subjective evidence of pain and disability together with: (4) all evidence relating to the effect of the appellant's condition upon his ability to perform in the grade or class of position last occupied. Id.

Prestien v. OPM, 8 MSPR 698, 703-05 (1981), placed the burden of proof on the annuitant to demonstrate a continuing disability if OPM discontinues the annuity because it determines the appellant recovered:

Under the Reform Act the administrative and appellate functions of the former Commission were bifurcated and devolved upon OPM and this Board respectively. OPM was granted the authority to determine civil service retirement applications, including questions of disability retirement, under 5 U.S.C. § 8347(a), (b), and (c) and this Board was empowered, by Section 8347(d), to review appeals from an administrative action or order of OPM affecting the civil service retirement rights or interests of an individual or of the United States.[6]

[6] A complete discussion of the legislative history of 5 U.S.C. § 8347, and the former Commission's procedures for determining civil service retirement applications is set forth in Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPR 404 (1981).

II. BURDEN AND STANDARD OF PROOF

In order to make a final determination of appellant's right to continued receipt of a disability retirement annuity we need here to address two issues of first impression before the Board: (1) who must bear the burden of proof in an appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 8347(d) of an action brought by OPM to discontinue a voluntary disability retirement annuity due to an annuitant's alleged recovery from disability; and (2) the standard of proof in such appeals.

In Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPR 404 (1981), the Board decided similar issues in appeals arising from OPM's disallowance of voluntarily filed applications for disability retirement. In Chavez the Board held that the applicant had the burden of proving entitlement to disability retirement. That holding was primarily based on two grounds: 1) that in passing the CSRA, Congress did not intend to disturb the practice of the former Civil Service Commission in placing the burden of proof on the claimant; and 2) that placing the burden of proof on the claimant was consistent with the established principle of law that one who asserts entitlement to a statutory benefit bears the burden of demonstrating that he or she meets the statutory criteria for such entitlement. The Board also noted that such an allocation is fair because the claimant under normal circumstances either possesses or has readier access to the evidence relating to the disability and because it would be unfair to require OPM to prove a negative, i.e., that appellant is not disabled.

These same considerations are applicable in the present case. Not only did the former Civil Service Commission place the burden of proof on the annuitant to demonstrate his or her continuing entitlement for disability retirement, but such an election is also fair and consistent with the established law. In view of these considerations the Board finds that the burden of proof is properly placed on the annuitant.

The remaining question concerns which standard of proof should be applied. In Chavez the Board held that the preponderance of the evidence is the proper standard. We so held because that is the standard under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c); it was the standard consistently applied by the Commission in such cases; and it is the one most commonly applied in agency proceedings and best suited for cases coming before the Board. These factors are equally applicable to cases involving recovery from disability, and we find no countervailing considerations leading to a different conclusion. Accordingly, the Board holds that the appellant's burden of proof in these cases is by a preponderance of the evidence.

Finally, in Chavez we held that the ultimate determination of disability is to be based upon the probative value of all of the evidence, taking into account: (1) objective clinical findings: (2) diagnoses and expert medical opinions; and (3) subjective evidence of pain and disability together with; (4) all evidence relating to the effect of the appellant's condition upon his or her ability to perform in the grade or class of position last occupied. Id. at 358-59, 6 M.S.P.R. at 422-24. We find that the elements of proof set forth in Chavez in determining disability are equally applicable in determining the fact of continuing disability for civil service retirement purposes. We turn, therefore, to the application of the foregoing standards and elements of proof in the instant case.

To continue the disability annuity, the standard is the same for the initiation of the annuity: whether the condition prevents the appellant from performing useful and efficient service. Rodriguez v. OPM, 32 MSPR 325, 328 (1987). The AJ should inform the appellant of the type and sufficiency of evidence that must be produced for the appellant to prove continuing entitlement to a disability retirement annuity. Hill v. OPM, 91 MSPR 566, 569-70 ¶ 9 (2002) (citing Goodnight v. OPM, 49 MSPR 184, 189 (1991)); see Ballenger v. OPM, 101 MSPR 138, 143-44 ¶¶ 11, 14, 2006 MSPB 24 (2006) ("The fact that the appellant submitted evidence that he suffered from other medical conditions, however, does not show that the conditions were necessarily a basis for his disability-retirement application. See, e.g., Licausi v. Office of Personnel Management, 350 F.3d 1359, 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (explaining that, under the comparable Civil Service Retirement System statutes and regulations, an employee must prove not only the existence of a medical condition, but also that the medical condition prevented efficient and useful service in the employee's position)"; "To satisfy his burden of proving entitlement to disability retirement in a case involving reinstatement of benefits under FERS, the appellant must show that he still suffers from the same disability upon which his annuity had been based. Hill v. Office of Personnel Management, 91 M.S.P.R. 566, ¶ 8 (2002).").

If OPM discontinues the annuity because of recovery of the appellant, in the ensuing Board appeal, the appellant may raise a claim of disability discrimination that the AJ must resolve. See Caldarone v. OPM, 36 MSPR 544, 546 (1988).
