15. Explanation of Reasons for Testing

AFSCME Local 3097 and DOJ, Justice Mgmt. Div., 42 FLRA 412, 432-33 (1991), pronounced negotiable the portion of Proposal 5 calling on the employer to agree that it provide the employee with a written statement of specific reasons forming the basis for a referral to drug testing because of an accident or reasonable suspicion. The proposal would do no more, as to reasonable suspicion testing, than require the statement then required by then-applicable FPM Letter 792-19; as to the referral for testing due to an accident, the preparation of the notice was not shown to entail substantial delay. It could consist of a statement that "the accident is the type for which a drug test is required under Agency policy." AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 843, 844-45 (1991), approved for the same reasons Proposal 2B, concerning "reasonable suspicion testing," that a supervisor document his or her suspicions and have them approved in writing by the next higher level of supervision. Similar in effect were Proposals 1 and 6 in AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council and DOJ, INS, 42 FLRA 599, 603-04, 610-11, 614-17 (1991), essentially compelling the employer to agree to supply employees with justification for testing within a day, that the decision to direct an employee to report for reasonable suspicion testing be based on "articulable information, facts, and circumstances," and that employees be provided copies of the factual observations leading to the testing. The proposals were consistent with Exec. Order No. 12654. They did not interfere with the agency's ability to control internal security, assign work, or impose discipline. NTEU and NRC, 43 FLRA 1279, 1308-09, 1314, 1315-16 (1992), approved as negotiable a provision of Proposal 4, that the employer give written notice to an employee directed to appear for drug testing as a result of reasonable suspicion and requiring that, to the extent practicable, the agency inform the employee of the basis for the reasonable suspicion. The proposal did not call for disclosure of evidence or sources of information if disclosure would frustrate the drug program's purpose of detecting users of illegal drugs or interfere with the agency's ability to summon employees for reasonable suspicion testing. A proposal was negotiable that the agency provide to the union a copy of the document given to an employee explaining the reasons for requiring the employee to undergo drug testing based on the agency's reasonable suspicion that the employee uses drugs. No bar to the proposal was presented by the Privacy Act. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, BATF, 41 FLRA 1106, 1129-30, 1132-33 (1991) (Provision 7).
