2. Apparent Conflicts

The Supreme Court informs us that statutes regulating conflicts contain a congressional intent to prevent apparent as well as real conflicts. Under Crandon v. United States, 110 S. Ct. 997, 1005 (1990) (concerning severance payments from a private employer to employees leaving to accept temporary government positions):

Congress appropriately enacts prophylactic rules that are intended to prevent even the appearance of wrongdoing and that may apply to conduct that has caused no actual injury to the United States.... Legislation designed to prohibit and to avoid potential conflicts of interest in the performance of governmental service is supported by the legitimate interest in maintaining the public's confidence in the integrity of the federal service.

Benavides v. DHS, DA-0752-10-0279-I-1 (NP 7/14/2011), described the analysis of an apparent conflict of interest:

...It is well-established, however, that the agency is not required to prove the appellant actually engaged in improper conduct to establish that he created the appearance of a conflict of interest. See, e.g., Neuman v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶¶ 14-16 (2008). Further, the agency need not establish that specific individuals perceived the impropriety, but rather that the appellant's "interests or duties in one capacity would 'reasonably create an appearance' of having an effect on his interests or duties in the other capacity." See Fontes v. Department of Transportation, 51 M.S.P.R. 655, 664 (1991).

See Reeder v. SSA, DE-0752-20-0346-I-1 (1/24/2023) ("The Board has also explained that fundamental fairness precludes disciplining an employee for creating the appearance of an ethical violation unless she should have known it would appear improper to a reasonable observer under the circumstances. Ryan v. Department of Homeland Security, 123 M.S.P.R. 202, ¶ 13 (2016). Hence, as the agency states, it is appropriate to apply a 'reasonable person' standard in determining whether the appellant violated section 2635.101(b)(14) by creating the appearance that she was not impartial when she hired R.N.")

"Creating the appearance of a conflict of interest constitutes a serious breach of trust. The Government clearly has an interest in prohibiting such conduct, and in ensuring that its agents and employees are not compromised in the performance of their duties as a result of any outside influences." Coons v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 1, 5 (1983). To prove the existence of an appearance of a conflict of interest, an agency must show that the individual's interests or duties in one capacity would "reasonably create an appearance" of having an effect on his interests or duties in the other capacity. Fontes v. Dept. of Transp., 51 MSPR 655, 664-65 (1991) (acceptance of a reduced room rate from the operator of a hotel who was also involved in a business being inspected by the appellant violated 5 CFR 735.202(a), a provision pertaining to the acceptance of a gratuity, favor, or thing of value; acceptance of lodgings at the ordinary rate would not present a real or apparent conflict). Proof of an appearance of a conflict is insufficient under Fontes when the agency charges an employee with an actual conflict. Properly charged, however, the Board sustains adverse action for creating an appearance of conflict of interest, considered a serious breach of trust in derogation of the government's need to ensure that its agents and employees are not compromised in the performance of their duties as a result of outside influences. A supervisor was demoted for creating the appearance of a conflict in Coons v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 1, 6 (1983) (mitigated from removal). A realty specialist employed by an agency responsible for regulation of Indian affairs was removed for buying property with his wife from a relation who was an Indian and a client of the agency. The situation created the appearance of a conflict. Moffer v. Dept. of Interior, 8 MSPR 453, 455 (1981), aff'd, Moffer v. Watt, 690 F.2d 1037 (D.C. Cir. 1982). But see Reeder v. SSA, DE-0752-20-0346-I-1 (1/24/2023) ("The agency contends that a reasonable person with knowledge of the relevant facts would find that the appellant's involvement in the hiring of her son's girlfriend created the appearance of lack of impartiality. In support of its assertion, the agency lists a number of facts that were available to the appellant. However...the appellant's first-level supervisor, R.C., had specifically advised her that her participation in the hiring process for R.N. did not raise any ethical concerns....Hence, we find that fundamental fairness precludes disciplining the appellant for creating the appearance of an ethical violation.").

A housing specialist was removed, despite a long and exemplary past record, for engaging in a real estate transaction contrary to agency regulations governing disposition of its property, and for doing so in a manner that appeared to attempt to avoid discovery of the transaction. Suarez v. DHUD, 96 MSPR 213, 223-25 ¶¶ 30-34 (2004) (removal of an employee with an excellent and long record for engaging in a prohibited transaction involving agency housing property; the transaction, entered into with a non-agency individual with whom the appellant had a close relationship, involved an apparent conflict of interest as well as violation of agency and government-wide standards of conduct); see Schumacher v. USPS, 52 MSPR 575, 582 (1992) (concerns of Postal Service about an employee also working for United Parcel Service); Delovich v. Dept. of Labor, 19 MSPR 155, 155-56 (1984) (mitigating removal to suspension of a coal mine inspector based on a charge of apparent conflict of interest resulting from writing a letter to a coal company criticizing them because, in the appellant's opinion, the firm refused to hire the appellant's son due to the appellant's position and his issuance of citations to the firm). Another decision implied that an individual who accepted gratuities (fare reductions) from a grant recipient of the appellant's employing agency was involved in either a conflict of interest or the appearance of a conflict. Krbec v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 239, 245 (1984) (sustaining removal).

Removal of a long-term bank examiner was sustained because the employee engaged in a minor credit transaction with a bank he was auditing in violation of the agency's regulations precluding examiners from taking any form of loan from the bank. The agency regulations required employees to maintain unusually high standards of honesty and integrity, and to avoid conflicts or the appearance of conflicts of interest. Kluge v. FDIC (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1490). Solicitation of a loan by an employee from an individual who had some type of interest in agency operations affected by the employee's work was an apparent conflict in Stanek v. Dept. of Transp., 805 F.2d 1572 (Fed. Cir. 1986). As to apparent conflict charges arising through the hiring of friends, the Board announced it would analyze cases on a case-by-case basis. See Special Counsel v. Nichols, 36 MSPR 445, 455 (1988).
