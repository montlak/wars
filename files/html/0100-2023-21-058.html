c. Objective Medical Evidence

Bradfish v. OPM, 7 MSPR 492, 494 (1981), defined the concept of "objective medical evidence" to include clinical findings in addition to judgments or opinions concerning those findings:

The term objective medical evidence has been defined as "the clinical findings of treating or examining physicians divorced from their expert judgments or opinion as to the significance of those clinical findings." Underwood v. Ribicoff, 298 F.2d 850, 851 (4th Cir. 1962). It is basically that evidence which is perceptible to the external senses of the examining physician, or which can be proved to exist through medically acceptable clinical and laboratory techniques. See Strickland v. Harris, 615 F.2d 1103 (5th Cir. 1980); Johnson v. Weinberger, 525 F.2d 403 (7th Cir. 1975); Dillon v. Celebreeze, 345 F.2d 753 (4th Cir. 1975).

Upon review of the medical evidence of record, the Board agrees with the presiding official's finding that appellant's treating physician, Dr. Fredrick P. Wesbrook, did not submit objective medical evidence in support of his opinion that appellant was totally disabled. In fact, Dr. Wesbrook's examination report of January 31, 1979, makes it clear that the extensive laboratory work-ups and physical examinations of appellant were all essentially normal. The report is also replete with statements that no etiology or disease had been found to account for appellant's symptoms of chronic muscle and joint pains. Of note, the only other medical report of appellant in the record, submitted by Dr. Kenneth P. Wolski, states:

None of us here at the Marshfield Clinic, after extensive interaction over a period of years, have [sic] been able to identify any active rheumatic disease as a cause for the patient's multiple joint and muscle pains.

Thus, however one characterizes the medical evidence in this case, it does not reveal any clinical or laboratory findings in support of appellant's claim of total disability.

Miller v. OPM, 8 MSPR 137, 138-39 (1981), stressed the importance of medical reports based on frequent medical observations:

In support of her application, appellant submitted a letter from her physician stating that the "chronic and acute stress" was of a disabling severity. However, our review of this statement reveals not only that the objective clinical findings fail to support her claim of total disability but that, as the presiding official found, they were based principally on her report of her symptoms rather than on frequent medical observations. Further, appellant elected not to have a hearing below and did not otherwise present subjective evidence of pain and disability. Appellant's superior officer's statement states that "because of nervousness and tension" she is "unable to concentrate on her responsibilities" and that her "recent physical appearance and behavior indicate that she is in poor health." It, nevertheless, does not support a finding that she cannot perform in her position because of injury or disease.

See Hunt v. OPM, 105 MSPR 264, 275 ¶ 32, 2007 MSPB 77 (2007) ("In addition, none of the psychologists or psychiatrists had a long-term therapeutic relationship with the appellant. The appellant only saw Dr. Ewing for 2 months, the last visit occurring nearly 2 years prior to the hearing. The appellant saw Drs. McLeod, Del Rio, Hedgepath, and Fago only once each. The probative value of their opinions is diminished because medical conclusions based on a long familiarity with a patient are of greater weight than those based on a brief association or single examination. See Anderson, 96 M.S.P.R. 299, ¶ 14; Tan-Gatue v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 11 (2001), aff'd, 52 F. App'x 511 (Fed. Cir. 2002).").

Of the weight of medical evidence, Rapp v. OPM, 108 MSPR 674, 680-81 ¶ 11, 2008 MSPB 107 (2008), commented:

...We also note in this regard that the probative value of medical opinions may depend in part upon such factors as the qualifications of the expert rendering the opinion, and the extent and duration of the expert's familiarity with or treatment of the applicant's condition. Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 M.S.P.R. 404, 423 (1981). Here, Dr. Adams testified, and OPM does not dispute, that she has been treating the appellant for several years. Medical conclusions based on a long familiarity with a patient are of greater weight than those based on a brief association. Tan-Gatue v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 11 (2001), aff'd, 52 F. App'x 511 (Fed. Cir. 2002). "While OPM and the MSPB may give only limited weight to seemingly strong medical evidence..., it typically does so only in the face of factors such as doubts about professional competence, contrary medical evidence, failure of the professional to consider relevant factors, [or] lack of particularity in relating diagnosis to nature and extent of disability." Vanieken-Ryals v. Office of Personnel Management, 508 F.3d 1034, 1042 (Fed. Cir. 2007); see, e.g., Heiter v. Office of Personnel Management, 107 M.S.P.R. 514, ¶ 7 (2007) (finding medical opinion testimony probative where such testimony is unrefuted by countervailing testimony). In the absence of such factors, the administrative judge incorrectly substituted his views as to what pattern of behavior could or could not result from the appellant's mental condition for those of Dr. Adams and Dr. Jimenez.

Of psychiatric evidence offered by the appellant, Gergawy v. OPM, SF-844E-13-1821-I-1 (NP 1/7/2015), noted:

The appellant argues that the administrative judge erred by ignoring the medical evidence from his personal psychiatrist without finding that he did not use established diagnostic criteria, per Vanieken-Ryals v. Office of Personnel Management, 508 F.3d 1034 (Fed. Cir. 2007). Pursuant to Vanieken-Ryals, OPM and the Board may give limited weight to medical evidence "in the face of factors such as doubts about professional competence, contrary medical evidence, failure of the professional to consider relevant factors, lack of particularity in relating diagnosis to nature and extent of disability, etc." 508 F.3d at 1042. In the instant case, we find that the administrative judge correctly weighed the evidence to find that the appellant did not establish by preponderant evidence that he was entitled to disability retirement.
