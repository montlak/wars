d. Deposition

A deposition is sworn and more probative than an affidavit, for the opposing party is present and may question the deponent. Although the deposition is widely used as a discovery device, and as a means to impeach a hostile witness, it may serve as affirmative evidence if it can be placed into the record. [The use of depositions is also described in Chapter 2, "Deposition."] The normal rule in litigation is that depositions may be entered into evidence if they constitute admissions (the party offering the deposition in evidence is adverse in interest to the deponent, e.g., the agency offers the deposition of the appellant, or the appellant offers the deposition transcript of the deciding official in an adverse action) or if there are other special circumstances, e.g., geographical unavailability, sickness or death of the deponent. Kranz v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 630, 635-36 (1994), approved the introduction into the hearing record of a deposition of a witness who could not attend the hearing because he was in prison. Chambers v. SSA, PH-0432-10-0170-I-1 (NP 5/17/2011), permitted the introduction of a deposition transcript of an individual who was unable to attend a hearing for medical reasons.

...Contrary to the appellant's assertion in his petition for review, there is nothing in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41 that indicates that an administrative judge may not admit the deposition of a witness into evidence in lieu of live testimony. Further, the Board's Judges' Handbook states that "depositions may be accepted into evidence at a hearing, despite objections, upon a reasonable showing by the offering party of the unavailability of the affiant to testify." See MSPB Judges' Handbook, Chap. 10, § 14(a)(2).

Ordinarily a party does not introduce in evidence the deposition of a witness who will be testifying at the hearing. The deposition of the witness would be used for impeachment. If deposition transcripts are to be entered into the record as evidence, they (or portions of them) would be included as exhibits in the prehearing submission. If the transcripts are used only for impeachment, they would not ordinarily be entered as hearing exhibits. If a deposition transcript is submitted into the Board's record, it should be made clear, or soon clarified, whether the deposition is meant is potential impeaching evidence or whether the deposition is direct evidence for party making the submission. See Greer v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-15-0869-I-1 (NP 10/6/2022) ("The appellant further argues that the administrative judge previously accepted the deposition transcripts into the record. However, the appellant does not dispute the agency's contention that the administrative judge only intended to allow the appellant to use the deposition transcripts for impeachment purposes during the hearing.").

In Special Counsel v. Dept. of Army, 16 MSPR 178, 182 (1983), a corrective action case brought by OSC to remedy whistleblowing reprisal, the administrative record filed with the Board prior to referral of the case to an AJ for hearing apparently included depositions. Of them the Board said:

Depositions of persons not called as witnesses would be part of the decisional record; but the hearing testimony of deponents would succeed their depositions.[5]

[5] The latter position would give the parties fair opportunity to examine and cross-examine anew on all matters considered by them significant at the time of the witnesses' appearance at hearing, and would enable questions of credibility to be resolved if necessary.

See Proos v. USDA, 22 MSPR 541, 544 n.3 (1984) (impliedly disfavoring the admission, in lieu of testimony, of a deposition transcript based on leading questions or not taken with the specific purpose of substituting a deposition transcript for hearing testimony).

The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure provide some guidance as to the procedures to be followed if a party taking a deposition desires to use it in evidence. Because the Board accepts hearsay, the technical requirements concerning how depositions are put into evidence are not controlling. Essentially, the deposition is an affidavit, assuming that it is signed (unless there is an affirmative or constructive waiver of the signature) and sworn, as required under Fed. R. Civ. P. 30(c). Hightower v. Dept. of Commerce, 31 MSPR 1, 2 (1986), observed that the deposition is valuable only if it offers probative evidence:

The evidence in the deposition is unpersuasive, because the deponent--who was also listed as a witness by the appellant--was not shown to be unavailable to testify at the hearing, the deposition, as submitted by the appellant, was unsigned and incomplete, and the deponent's testimony was in direct conflict with the sworn testimony of an agency witness whose testimony the presiding official found supported by documentary evidence.... Moreover, the deponent's knowledge...is unclear from both the deposition and the hearing testimony.

A few words of explanation are necessary on the subject of signing depositions. The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure call for depositions that may be reviewed and signed by the deponent after the transcript is prepared. FRCP 30 provides:

(e) REVIEW BY WITNESS; CHANGES; SIGNING. If requested by the deponent or a party before completion of the deposition, the deponent shall have 30 days after being notified by the officer that the transcript or recording is available in which to review the transcript or recording and, if there are changes in form or substance, to sign a statement reciting such changes and the reasons given by the deponent for making them. The officer shall indicate in the certificate prescribed by subdivision (f)(1) whether any review was requested and, if so, shall append any changes made by the deponent during the period allowed.

When a deposition is recorded stenographically by a professional reporter, the deponent will normally be asked at the conclusion of the deposition whether she desires to review and sign the transcript or whether that opportunity will be waived. If the deponent seeks to review the transcript, the reporter will normally state that choice at the conclusion of the deposition transcript. The deponent is allowed 30 days or some other time agreed upon to make the changes and notify the other parties of the changes. That time period is calculated from the date the deponent is given the transcript or from the date he is given access to the transcript. The deponent makes minor corrections on an "errata" sheet that is placed at the end of the deposition. The review process allows the transcript to be corrected for typographical, spelling, or minor errors. Substantive changes are not to be made. The review process is not to be a rewrite of the deposition. If the deponent does not purchase a copy of the transcript, the reporter who prepares the deposition may request that the deponent come into her office for the correction session, to avoid providing the deponent a copy of the transcript that, in turn, may be copied by the deponent sans fee to the reporter. If the deponent does not review and sign the transcript within the time allowed, the deposition is deemed signed. Board rules do not incorporate the review process, but Board practice tends to follow the Federal Rules. To be considered relative to review and signature of a deposition is the time available prior to the hearing. All this has no bearing on a deposition that is recorded in an audio format but not transcribed.
