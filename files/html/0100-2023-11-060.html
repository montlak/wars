7. Prior Test: Relevance; Presumptive Relevance

Before adoption of the particularized need test, the locus of focus was whether the union's request for information demonstrated relevance to a purpose within the union's representational responsibility. An information request would not be deemed presumptively relevant. FLRA occasionally put itself in the position of an arbitrator, determining what he or she would receive in evidence. Given the diversity among arbitrators' opinions concerning what is admissible in a particular case, agencies used FLRA as an evidentiary screen, a practice that delayed the resolution of grievances.

The concept of presumptive relevance was reviewed and rejected in AFGE v. FLRA, 811 F.2d 769, 772 (2d Cir. 1987), involving requests for employees' time and attendance reports, progress reviews, and performance appraisals, sought by a union to demonstrate whether there was validity to complaints of unfair treatment by particular employees. The reasons for an information request were either to be apparent from the circumstances or the union was required to respond to managerial requests for clarification. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 21 FLRA 253, 255-56 (1986). The union was to do more than provide a bare request for information. The union was required to explain why it sought the information, so that the agency could make an informed judgment as to whether and to what extent the information was necessary for the collective bargaining process. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 21 FLRA 517, 520 (1986); see DOJ, INS, Border Patrol, El Paso and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 43 FLRA 697, 711 n.* (1991) (information request was not moot because of employee's departure from the employer: requested information "is necessary for the Union to fulfill the full range of its obligations as exclusive bargaining representative."); DHUD and AFGE Local 2403, 40 FLRA 1116, 1121-22 (1991) (obligation of employer to provide information to union about particular adverse action did not depend on possibility of representation of employee by union); Dept. of Labor, Wash., DC and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE, 39 FLRA 531, 538 (1991) (same), complaint dismissed on other grounds following court remand, Dept. of Labor, Wash., DC and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE, 51 FLRA 462 (1995); DOJ, INS, Border Patrol, El Paso and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 37 FLRA 1310, 1319-22 (1990) (information is "necessary" when used to determine whether to file grievance); EPA Health Effects Research Lab., Cincinnati and NFFE Local 801, 16 FLRA 52, 54-55 (1984) (analysis of relation between information request and possible theories of grievance advanced by union); Army Hq., XVIII Airborne Corps and Ft. Bragg and AFGE Local 1770, 34 FLRA 461, 462 (1990) (agency could not unilaterally determine arbitrability or decide what union needed to determine whether to pursue grievance).

As an example of material requested but determined to be irrelevant, in DOJ, Office of Justice Programs and AFSCME Local 2830, 45 FLRA 1022, 1024 (1992), the agency was not required to produce requested investigations of the union and its officers because there was no demonstration that the employer was currently investigating them. VA, Washington and AFGE Local 2250, 42 FLRA 333, 343-44 (1991), explained that a union had the right to minutes of meetings of staff members not within the unit when information disclosed in those minutes might reveal some type of violation of representational rights that might be grieved. The D.C. Circuit declined to enforce Local 2250: the union did not make a showing of "particularized need" for the information sought. VA v. FLRA, 1 F.3d 19, 23 (D.C. Cir. 1993). At about the same time, the Authority's test equating "necessary" information with "useful" information came under scrutiny in the Fifth Circuit in DOJ v. FLRA, 991 F.2d 285, 290-91 (5th Cir. 1993).
