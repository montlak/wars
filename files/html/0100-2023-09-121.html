broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section H: Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals, Subsection 01: Mootness of Appeal, Part d: Procedure Followed if Action Not Completely Rescinded
d. Procedure Followed if Action Not Completely Rescinded

If the agency does not fully restore the status quo, the proper course is for the appeal to proceed: the agency has not truly canceled the action. See Fairley v. USPS, 63 MSPR 10, 13 (1994). If the agency does not follow proper procedures in effecting the cancelation of the adverse action so that cancelation is not bona fide, the Board's jurisdiction over the appeal is not divested. See Johnson v. USDA, 14 MSPR 181, 183-84 (1982) (if the removal was canceled, the AJ did not have the authority to direct the agency to correct its paperwork, but if the cancelation did not remove unfavorable agency comments in the appellant's retirement forms, it was not bona fide and the AJ retained jurisdiction over the appeal).

If the appellant disputes the completeness of his restoration, the AJ is to determine if the status quo has been restored. If there is a challenge concerning the alleged status quo restoration, the AJ usually starts interacting with the parties to develop evidence on whatever is in dispute or to urge the agency on to proper restoration. Without restoration, the AJ is to adjudicate the case on the merits, assuming no settlement agreement. Sherrod v. Dept. of Navy, 90 MSPR 347, 353 ¶ 15 (2001) (citing El-Amin); El-Amin v. USPS, 46 MSPR 367, 369 (1990). The Board determines whether or not the status quo was restored. If it has been restored, the appeal is dismissed as moot. If it has not been restored, adjudication of the appeal continues. Roja v. Dept. of Navy, 53 MSPR 326, 328 (1992) (citing El-Amin v. USPS, 46 MSPR 367, 369 (1990), supra). A settlement may call for cancelation of an underlying adverse action but require an approach to an appellant's future employment that does not restore the status quo. If the appellant then complains that the status quo was not restored, the AJ determines jurisdiction and relief by reference to the validity or waiver of the Board appeal rights in the settlement. The AJ may need to determine if the agreement was coerced or breached. See Szetela v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 3, 7 (1992).

The appellant proceeds at least to the point of a jurisdictional hearing if she makes a nonfrivolous allegation concerning restoration of the status quo. Fairley v. USPS, 63 MSPR 10, 13 (1994); McCulley v. USPS, 54 MSPR 207, 210 (1992) ("The agency argues that the appellant's allegations are either too vague or address matters that are not part of the status quo ante. However, if the appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation of Board jurisdiction, she is entitled to an opportunity to submit evidence in support of that allegation and a jurisdictional hearing."); Palmer v. USPS, 50 MSPR 552, 556 (1991) (requiring a hearing to determine if the appellant was restored to duties and responsibilities either the same or equivalent to those held before the agency action). If the Board decides that the action appealed is not within its jurisdiction, e.g., a short-term suspension, the Board does not become involved in an analysis of whether that action, if canceled, completely restored the status quo. See Fidler v. USPS, 53 MSPR 440, 443 (1992).

If there is no hearing request, the Board examines the documentary evidence and resolves conflicts on mootness as best it can. See, e.g., Frias v. USPS, 57 MSPR 691, 697 (1993) ("Although the appellant asserts generally in his petition for review...that the agency has not rescinded the suspension action, his statements are not in the form of an affidavit or declaration made under penalty of perjury, and he does not rebut the specifics of Mr. Schreck's sworn statement and supporting documentation."); Hudson v. DHUD, 55 MSPR 639, 642-43 (1992) (as to a dispute over the restoration of health benefits following rescission of an adverse action, the Board credited the sworn statement of a personnel assistant, supported by government forms, over the allegation of the appellant, unsupported by evidence, that the agency did not restore her benefits); Krisovitch v. USPS, 46 MSPR 611, 614 (1991) (remanding to an AJ following disputed restoration of status quo when the agency, despite a letter stating that it notified the appellant of her status, had "not presented any evidence, such as a copy of the appellant's personnel records, which would show that this has been effected").

There is at least a suggestion that the appellant preserves Board jurisdiction because of incomplete rescission of the action only if the appellant affirmatively objects to the divestiture of jurisdiction. When an agency incompletely rescinds an action, appellant should address the AJ by letter or motion objecting to divestiture of Board jurisdiction and stating how the status quo was not restored. If the AJ dismisses the appeal after learning of the supposed rescission and before the appellant has a chance to object, it appears sufficient for the appellant to use his PFR to object to the divestiture of jurisdiction. See Brown v. USPS, 81 MSPR 668, 671 ¶ 5 (1999) (appeal was not mooted by retroactive conversion of the status to administrative leave if the appellant was on enforced leave for more than 14 days and enforced leave was not completely rescinded); Burzinski v. VA, 39 MSPR 561, 564 (1989) (appellant objected to the AJ concerning dismissal of the appeal as moot and the appellant raised the same objection through a PFR as to enforced leave that was not completely rescinded because the appellant was not returned to his position; he was placed on administrative leave, immediately followed by an indefinite suspension, characterized as "non-duty pay status," for the same reason he was placed on enforced leave; references to the alleged suspension were not deleted from his personnel file).

If the AJ improperly dismisses the appeal as moot, challenge to that decision is through a PFR. See McCulley v. USPS, 58 MSPR 303, 305-06 (1993) (suggesting that a PFR rather than an enforcement petition is appropriate when an AJ dismisses an appeal as moot due to the agency's cancelation of an adverse action, but the appellant believes the action was not completely rescinded; holding the appellant to timeliness requirements governing PFRs; appellant's objection, concerning references remaining in the OPF, should have been developed while the appeal was pending with the AJ). Under Gonzales v. USPS, 44 MSPR 517, 520 (1990), if the AJ improperly dismissed the appeal before the status quo was restored, on PFR the Board reopens the appeal and remands for adjudication. Some decisions spoke to the use of an enforcement petition when restoration of relief was inadequate following cancelation of an action. See Cupp v. USPS, 66 MSPR 4, 6 (1994) ("We find that the appellant's assertion that he has not received all of the back pay to which he is entitled is a petition for enforcement of the June 3, 1994 initial decision, based upon the agency's rescission of the appellant's removal. Matters relating to enforcement are initially to be adjudicated by the administrative judge."). Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616 ¶¶ 17-18, 2007 MSPB 234 (2007), overruled prior law (Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 322, 324 (1980)), decided that the Board since the Board has no jurisdiction over a moot appeal, the judge has no enforcement authority flowing from the initial decision dismissing the appeal as moot. The challenge to that decisions is through a PFR seeking reopening of the appeal.

A new appeal is not the proper process for responding to an initial decision dismissing an appeal as moot, Joyner v. USPS, 76 MSPR 473, 476 (1997):

On occasion in similar appeals, the Board has denied the petition for review of the initial decision dismissing the appeal as moot, but forwarded the appellant's newly raised allegations that he was not returned to the status quo ante to the regional office for docketing as a new petition for appeal. See Ray v. VA, 73 MSPR 321, 324-25 (1997); Day v. Dept. of the Air Force, 67 MSPR 395, 396-97 (1995). We find that the better procedure is to grant the petition for review on the basis of new evidence and remand the appeal for further adjudication. The appellant is not seeking to enforce a Board order or to file an appeal of a new adverse action. Rather, the appellant's allegation on petition for review amounts to a challenge to the administrative judge's determination below that the removal action was rendered moot. Under these circumstances, it is neither fair nor efficient to place the burden of filing a new appeal on the appellant, who is only seeking review of the initial decision, and to the extent that our previous decisions such as Ray and Day found otherwise, we overrule them.

We note that, in some cases with a similar fact pattern to this appeal, remand will not be necessary, when the record is sufficiently developed to permit the Board to determine on petition for review whether the personnel action being appealed was rendered moot. See, e.g., Frias v. USPS, 57 MSPR 691, 696-97, aff'd, 11 F.3d 1073 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). Here, however, there are factual questions that cannot be resolved on the existing record, i.e., whether the appellant is entitled to the bonus under the terms of the collective bargaining agreement and whether he received what he was entitled to as status quo ante relief.

Joyner added, as to the timing of receipt by the Board of the appellant's evidence that the status quo was not restored, id. at 476:

At the time that the administrative judge issued the February 19, 1997 initial decision, shortly after receiving the agency's motion to dismiss the appeal on the basis of its February 10, 1997 rescission of the removal action, the appellant could not have known whether the agency would return him completely to the status quo ante. Thus, his newly raised allegation and evidence in support of it were not available before the close of the record below despite his due diligence, and we grant his petition for review based on this new evidence.

Some cases decided before Joyner called for a new appeal rather than an enforcement petition to restore the status quo, McLanahan v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 266, 269 (1991), Yuni v. SBA, 38 MSPR 574 (1988), some decisions allowed for the use of an enforcement petition, Cupp v. USPS, 66 MSPR 4, 6 (1994), although the Board also considered mootness issues raised by the appellant on PFR and remanded, without elaboration, to the AJ for continued processing of the appeal, Burns v. USPS, 49 MSPR 234, 236 (1991). See Ray v. VA, 73 MSPR 321, 325 (1997), overruled by Joyner v. USPS, 76 MSPR 473, 476 (1997); Forrest v. USDA, 70 MSPR 539, 543 (1996); Day v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 395, 396 (1995), overruled by Joyner v. USPS, 76 MSPR 473, 476 (1997); cf. Sellman v. USPS, 63 MSPR 145, 151 (1994) (delay in filing a new appeal is excused if the appellant was not advised by the AJ of a right to refile); Thompson v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 192, 194 n.5 (1993) (examining the timeliness of a PFR challenging restoration of the status quo). Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616, 624-25 ¶¶ 15-18 (2007), 2007 MSPB 234 (2007), apparently rejected the use of an enforcement petition to seek relief following a mootness dismissal when restoration of the status quo was inadequate. The result of Haskins is that the initial decision dismissing the appeal as moot is properly challenged through a PFR seeking reopening of the appeal.

The appellant may not learn until after an AJ dismisses an appeal as moot that some working condition or benefit was not restored. The appellant raises the problem to the Board's attention through a PFR. If the PFR is late, the Board determines whether the filing is based on "new evidence" and, if it is, reopens the appeal. If an action is dismissed as moot through an initial decision, the appellant properly protects his interests by filing a timely petition through a PFR and not by awaiting compliance by the agency. The Board noted in Randazzo v. USPS, 108 MSPR 258, 260 ¶¶ 8-9, 2008 MSPB 47 (2008) (over the better-reasoned dissent of Member Sapin):

We next find that the appellant's reason for the delay, waiting for the agency to complete the actions required to make him whole, does not constitute a reasonable excuse. Indeed, the Board has held that any delay by an agency in complying with a final Board order granting relief to a party does not affect the finality of the Board's decision or show "good cause" for an untimely motion. See Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 14 M.S.P.R. 343, 344 (1983), aff'd, 726 F.2d 730 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

We further note that this reasoning is consistent with other precedent finding that an appellant's decision to wait for various events to unfold does not constitute good cause for a filing delay. For example, an appellant's decision to wait for a determination on his grievance before filing his petition for review does not constitute good cause to waive the regulatory time limit for filing a petition for review. See Eaglehart v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 672, ¶ 9 (2006). Similarly, an appellant's decision to wait for a decision by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission before filing a petition for review, and a delay to determine whether the agency followed required procedures, did not constitute good cause to waive the regulatory time limit for an untimely petition for review. See Zamot v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 475, ¶ 6 (2002), aff'd, 332 F.3d 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Williamson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 66, 68-69 (1994)....
