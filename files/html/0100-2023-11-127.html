h. Prior Action Reversed for Lack of Due Process

Henson v. Dept. of Treasury, DA-0752-13-0519-I-1 (NP 7/31/2014), presented the interesting situation of a removal, reversed for lack of due process, that was accompanied by a defense of discrimination that was heard and decided against the appellant. The agency removed the appellant a second time, and the appellant again appealed. The AJ, who considered the merits of the underlying charges when adjudicating the discrimination claim in the earlier appeal, held against the appellant on the merits in the second appeal based on collateral estoppel. The Board remanded the case, determining that collateral estoppel did not control the outcome of the second appeal, although the evidence heard in the earlier appeal could be considered by the AJ:

In an initial decision dated February 25, 2013, the administrative judge reversed the appellant's removal upon finding that the agency violated her right to constitutional due process. The decision stated "the appellant is entitled to a new constitutionally correct removal decision," and it ordered the agency to cancel the removal and restore the appellant to duty with appropriate back pay, interest, and benefits. Because the appellant raised claims of race and color discrimination and retaliation for protected equal employment opportunity activity, the administrative judge took evidence and argument concerning the discrimination and retaliation issues, including evidence and argument concerning the merits of the underlying removal. The administrative judge found that the agency proved the merits of the removal action and that the appellant did not prove discrimination or retaliation. The initial decision became the final decision of the Board when neither party petitioned for review.

Thereafter the agency instituted a second removal action against the appellant for the same misconduct charged in the first removal action after purporting to afford her a constitutionally correct procedure. The appellant appealed the second removal action. The administrative judge found that collateral estoppel precluded the relitigation of the merits of the charges of misconduct and, applying collateral estoppel, he sustained the charges. He also found that the appellant failed to prove her affirmative defenses of age discrimination, retaliation for filing a Board appeal, denial of due process, or harmful error, and that the penalty of removal was reasonable.

The Board has long recognized that an agency can renew an adverse action based on charges brought in a previous proceeding where the previous action was overturned solely on procedural grounds. See, e.g., Strope v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 539, 542 (1997); see also Noble v. Department of Justice, 68 M.S.P.R. 524, 528 (1995) (finding that an agency may institute a second personnel action after the Board reverses a prior action as long as the second action is taken for legitimate reasons). An appellant who disagrees with a second decision to remove her under similar circumstances is entitled to appeal the second removal to the Board.

When the Board reverses a case on due process grounds without reaching the merits of the underlying action, the Board is nevertheless required to render a decision on any allegations of discrimination. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1); Schibik v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 M.S.P.R. 591, ¶ 11 (2005); Marchese v. Department of the Navy, 32 M.S.P.R. 461, 464 (1987). In an effort to follow this long-standing precedent, the administrative judge in the first removal appeal made findings on the merits of the charges and on the appellant's affirmative defenses. In the initial decision now under review, he applied collateral estoppel to the findings in the earlier decision.

Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when: (1) the issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action. McNeil v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 15 (2005). We find that the administrative judge's merits determinations in the first initial decision were not necessary to the resulting judgment. The administrative judge properly examined the agency's evidence in support of its charges because such evidence generally bears on the question of whether discrimination is the real reason for the action. The evidence about the appellant's alleged misconduct adduced in the first hearing is relevant, but the legal conclusion that the charge is sustained is not. The requirement that the Board address allegations of discrimination even when the charges are reversed on other grounds is based in 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a), which does not contain a similar requirement that the Board address the merits even when it reverses an action on procedural grounds. Therefore, the doctrine of collateral estoppel does not apply in this case.
