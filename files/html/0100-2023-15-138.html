a. Difficulty Following Reinstatement

An employee's belief that, following reinstatement, his work assignments are "make work" does not justify the employee's refusal to perform assigned tasks. "Management may not be the wisest: it is always incompetent at best in the eyes of a target for adverse action. This is no excuse for violating the first law of the work place, namely that it is a place for doing work. Petitioner's failure to perform assigned duties clearly justifies adverse agency action, and the choice of an appropriate 'penalty' is a matter of agency discretion." Nagel v. DHHS, 707 F.2d 1384, 1387 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (involving an employee who felt that he was receiving insignificant assignments after he was reinstated to a job); Boyle v. United States, 515 F.2d 1397 (Ct. Cl. 1975), supra. Nagel v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 538, 540-41 (1982), leading to the 1983 circuit court decision, explained that the appellant could not use his whistleblower status to avoid following legitimate supervisory directives:

For the eight months preceding his removal, appellant inexcusably and consistently refused to perform work which his supervisor assigned to him. Despite written instructions, reminders, and warnings, appellant steadfastly refused to submit requested work products.... Further, the Board finds the conclusion inescapable that appellant was goading the agency into taking action against him....

The Board, upon its consideration of pertinent, relevant factors, including: the nature and seriousness of the offense, and its relation to the employee's duties, position, and responsibilities, including whether the offense was intentional, or was frequently repeated; and, the effect of the offense upon the employee's ability to perform at a satisfactory level and its effect upon supervisors' confidence in the employee's ability to perform assigned duties, see Douglas, supra, 5 MSPB at 332, 5 MSPR at 305, concludes that sustained obstinate refusal to recognize supervisory authority or to perform assigned duties so seriously undermines the capacity of management to maintain employee efficiency and discipline, that no agency should be expected to exercise forbearance for such conduct. Few other types of misconduct go so directly to the heart of maintaining the "efficiency of the service."

Appellant had no right to refuse to abide by legitimate supervisory authority. See Bolling v. Department of the Air Force, 8 MSPB 658, 661, 9 MSPR 335, 341 (1981); Stevenson v. U.S., 155 Ct. Cl. 592, 596 (1961), cert. denied, 371 U.S. 835, 83 S. Ct. 58, 9 L. Ed.2d 272 (1962). Nor does appellant's belief that management was out to get him as a reputed "whistle-blower" lessen the gravity of his misconduct. See the legislative history of 5 USC § 2302(b)(8), making it clear that the section "should not be construed as protecting an employee who is otherwise engaged in misconduct...from appropriate disciplinary action."... See also, Prescott v. Department of Health and Human Services, 6 MSPB 216, 6 MSPR 252 (1981). In Church v. Department of the Army, 6 MSPB 614, 6 MSPR 737 (1981), the Board, despite finding evidence of agency harassment, upheld the penalty of removal where the employee did little work and spent most of his time preparing numerous grievances and law suits against his supervisors. See also, Hernandez v. Alexander, 607 F.2d 920, 924 (10th Cir. 1979). And, in Marty v. Center for Disease Control, 7 MSPB 700, 8 MSPR 231 (1981), again despite agency harassment, the Board found removal the proper penalty for an employee's refusal to return to work to do the jobs assigned her. Finally, the Board has also noted the futility of returning employees to duty who refuse to accept or obey legitimate supervisory authority. Bolling, supra; Mitchell v. United States Postal Service, 7 MSPB 62, 7 MSPR 141 (1981); [c] f. Jefferson v. Veterans Administration, 6 MSPB 297, 6 MSPR 348 (1981).

The factors relied on by the presiding official to mitigate the penalty are worthy of consideration. Nonetheless, we do not find them sufficient to outweigh eight months of self-imposed idleness. The Board, therefore, finds that the penalty of removal is reasonable in this case.

Reinstatement is not a license to defy the reasonable expectations of supervisors and to cultivate disharmony with supervisors and fellow employees. See Hawes v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 591, 594 (1983), aff'd, Hawes v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-992) (refusal to accept a telephone call from a supervisor at home after office hours to discuss an office problem and insistence that all directions be made in writing), supra.

In a situation similar to Nagel, supra, an employee was ordered reinstated to his position by the Board following a successful RIF appeal. The agency offered a position that the employee felt was not the same as the one he lost by separation. He filed an enforcement petition, arguing that he lacked the qualifications for the position he was assigned. The Board determined that he should take the position and raise lack of qualifications if the agency later tried to fire him. When the appellant refused to do so, the agency fired him for AWOL. Gammal v. MSPB, 671 F.2d 654, 656-57 (1st Cir. 1982), determined that the discharge was proper:

...We cannot say that it was an abuse of discretion for the Board to find that appellant should have asserted a defense of inadequacy during a removal proceeding after he has started working rather than during a removal proceeding instigated because he refused to work for fear of inadequacy. Cf. Rotolo v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 636 F.2d 6, 8 (1st Cir. 1980); Hurley v. United States, 575 F.2d 792, 793 (10th Cir. 1978). Although the Board places the burden on the employee to try to perform, to allow the employee to protest without first trying would severely impair the agency's ability to plan for and to proceed with its normal functions. While we can understand that appellant's confidence in the intentions of HUD and its willingness to comply in good faith with the reinstatement order may have been shattered by his past experiences, his suspicion of wrongdoing in this instance cannot justify a finding that the Board must reach a conclusion other than one it has chosen.

Gammal, remarked, id. at 656 n.2:

Although we note that we do not think that protest through passive resistance of the type in which appellant engaged promotes an orderly system for the resolution of employment disputes, we need not decide whether an employee is ever justified in asserting the lack of compliance as a defense for failure to report to duty when the opportunity exists to seek a determination on whether the agency has complied....
