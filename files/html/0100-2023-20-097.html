broida mspb Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices, Subchapter 03: Individual Right of Action, Section B: Jurisdictional Issue, Subsection 09: Status of Particular Agencies and Employees, Part a: Postal Service
a. Postal Service

39 USC 201 defines the Postal Service:

There is established, as an independent establishment of the executive branch of the Government of the United States, the United States Postal Service.

One agency of considerable size that is not covered through Individual Right of Action processes is the Postal Service. Under Butler v. USPS, 10 MSPR 45, 48 (1982):

We concur with the Postal Service's contention that it is excluded from the provisions of § 2302(b) and therefore from direct application of those provisions by virtue of the fact that it is not an executive agency pursuant to § 2302(a)(2)(C)....

The Board explained this result, as well as the analytical method that would be applied to CSRA reprisal cases involving the Postal Service that come to the Board as direct appeals, Mack v. USPS, 48 MSPR 617, 620-22 (1991):

A preference-eligible in the United States Postal Service who has completed one year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions is an "employee" under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B) who is entitled to appeal certain agency adverse actions to the Board.... It is undisputed that the appellant here is such an "employee."

In Butler v. United States Postal Service, 9 MSPB 322, 10 MSPR 45, 48 (1982), the Board concurred with the agency's contention that it was excluded from the provisions of 5 USC 2302(b) because it was not an "agency" under section 2302(a)(2)(C). See 39 USC 201; 5 USC 104, 105. We concluded, however, that this had no bearing on the right of a covered employee, entitled to appeal to the Board, to present an affirmative defense to the agency action under 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(B).... In making that determination, the Board noted that 5 USC 7701 gives all employees with appeal rights the same rights and that, while 5 USC 2302(b) does not "refer" to 5 USC 7701, section 7701 refers to section 2302(b) and to the prohibited personnel practices described therein. Id. Consequently, we found that employees entitled to appeal to the Board are entitled to raise the affirmative defenses set forth in 5 USC 7701, including an allegation of a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b).

We conclude similarly here that, since the appellant, as an "employee," has the right to appeal his removal to the Board under 5 USC 7513(d), he has the right to raise the affirmative defenses set forth in 5 USC 7701, including an allegation of a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b).

The Board's authority to grant corrective action is based upon 5 USC 1221. Section 1221 specifies that an employee may seek corrective action, including a stay, from the Board with respect to any "personnel action" that results from a "prohibited personnel practice" taken in retaliation for whistleblowing. See 5 USC 1221(a) and (c)(1). Because a "personnel action" constitutes a "prohibited personnel practice" under 5 USC 2302 only if it occurs in an "agency," the provisions of 5 USC 1221 are not applicable to employees who are not in a covered agency. We therefore find that, because the Postal Service is not a covered "agency" under 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(C), an employee of the Postal Service cannot be subjected to a "personnel action" constituting a "prohibited personnel practice." Accordingly, we must conclude that Postal employees are not entitled to seek "corrective action" under section 1221 in connection with their whistleblowing claims, and that the administrative judge in the instant case consequently erred in considering the appellant's stay request.

We further find that the lower "contributing factor" standard of proof set forth at 5 USC 1221(e)(1) does not apply to employees who are not in a covered agency. Section 1221(e)(1) provides that this standard of proof applies only "in any case involving an alleged prohibited personnel practice as described under section 2302(b)(8)." As we discuss above, however, a "personnel action" constitutes a "prohibited personnel practice" under 5 USC 2302 only if it occurs in an "agency." We therefore conclude that the administrative judge improperly applied the lower standard to the appellant's whistleblowing reprisal allegation.

Thus, the higher standard of proof applicable to all other claims of reprisal, as set forth in Warren v. Department of Army, 804 F.2d 654, 656-58 (Fed. Cir. 1986), applies in Postal Service cases to show reprisal such as that prohibited under both 5 USC 2302(b)(8) and 2302(b)(9).[4] In order to prove reprisal under this standard, the appellant has the burden of showing that: (1) A protected disclosure was made; (2) the accused official knew of the disclosure; (3) the adverse action under review could have been retaliation under the circumstances; and (4) there was a genuine nexus between the alleged retaliation and the adverse action. Because we find that the appellant's evidence, which was insufficient under the lower standard of proof, fails to meet the fourth requirement under Warren, we conclude that the application of the incorrect standard by the administrative judge does not alter the outcome of this appeal and does not prejudice the rights of either party....

[4] Because 5 USC 2302 is not applicable to the Postal Service, the Board hears allegations that an appellant was adversely affected for one of the reasons set forth therein not as prohibited personnel practices per se but as affirmative defenses. The Board, therefore, will analyze such defenses in the manner generally applicable to them. See, e.g., Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 101 S. Ct. 1089, 67 L. Ed.2d 207 (1981); Mt. Healthy City Bd. of Educ. v. Doyle, 429 U.S. 274, 97 S. Ct. 568, 50 L. Ed.2d 471 (1977) (setting forth the test for proof of allegations of reprisal).

Walls v. USPS, DC-0752-21-0100-I-1 (NP 4/28/2023), also explained the analysis for Postal Service employees who complain of whistleblowing reprisal:

[T]he appellant's employer is the U.S. Postal Service, and the whistleblower protection statutes do not apply to the U.S. Postal Service.[5] See Greenlee v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 323, ¶ 7 (2006) (stating that the Whistleblower Protection Act does not apply to the U.S. Postal Service); Mack v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 617, 621 (1991) (reasoning that the U.S. Postal Service is not an "agency" as defined under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(C) and its employees are, therefore, not covered under 5 U.S.C. § 1221). Specifically, the Board has found that the lower "contributing factor" standard of proof set forth in the whistleblower protection statutes does not apply to employees who are not in a covered agency, such as the U.S. Postal Service. See Mack, 48 M.S.P.R. at 621. Rather, the Board has explained that the "higher standard of proof applicable to all other claims of reprisal," as set forth in Warren, applies to U.S. Postal Service cases to show reprisal such as that prohibited under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), (9). Id. Accordingly, to establish this affirmative defense, the appellant must show that his OSHA and OIG complaints constituted protected activity, that the proposing and deciding officials were aware of that protected activity, that the appellant's demotion could, under the circumstances, have been retaliation, and that there was a genuine nexus between the retaliation and the demotion. See Warren, 804 F.2d at 656-58; Mattison v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 123 M.S.P.R. 492, ¶ 8 (2016).

Summarizing the jurisdictional conclusion in Mack, Postal Service employees cannot appeal alleged violations of 2302(b)(8) to the Board under the IRA provisions of the WPA, 5 USC 1221. Instead, they may raise whistleblowing only as an affirmative defense to an otherwise appealable action. Whisnant v. USPS, 83 MSPR 588, 589-90 (1999); Booker v. USPS, 53 MSPR 507, 509-10 (1992), followed in Mendenhall v. USPS, 74 MSPR 430, 440 (1997); Joosten v. USPS, 52 MSPR 198, 201 & n.2 (1992) (although a USPS employee did not have WPA protection, whistleblowing reprisal would still serve as an affirmative defense to an adverse action). The Federal Circuit agreed with the Board, Booker v. MSPB, 982 F.2d 517, 519 (Fed. Cir. 1992):

Booker's further allegations that Postal Service employees committed prohibited personnel practices in violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(D) and (b)(9) by discriminating against her because of her injuries and in reprisal for her complaints to the inspector general are also beyond the board's jurisdiction. As a Postal Service employee, Booker could not seek corrective action from the board under 5 USC 1221.

Section 1221(a) allows an employee to bring an IRA seeking corrective action from the board with respect to any "personnel action" that results from a "prohibited personnel practice" taken in retaliation for whistleblowing. According to section 2302, a "personnel action" may be considered a "prohibited personnel practice" only if it occurs within an "agency" as that word is defined. The definition of "agency" in section 2302(a)(2)(C) does not include the United States Postal Service, which is specifically excluded from the generally applicable definition of "executive agency" in 5 USC 105 (1988), by virtue of its exclusion from the definition of "independent establishment" in 5 USC 104 (1988). It is a unique entity. Where Congress intended the Postal Service to be included within the definition of "agency" or to be subject to particular provisions of Title 5, it explicitly so provided. See, e.g., 5 USC 3371(3), 4103 note, 5514(a)(4)(B), and 5520(c)(4) (1988). Its absence from the section 2302 definition could only have been intentional in light of this congressional practice. Cf. Dept. of Energy v. Ohio, 503 U.S. [607], 112 S. Ct. 1627, 1634, 118 L. Ed.2d 255 (1992) (omission of the United States from the term "person" in certain sections of the Clean Water Act and Resource Conservation and Recovery Act was significant given the specific inclusion of the United States in other definitions of "person" found elsewhere in the statutes).

Howell v. USPS, CH-0752-22-0077-I-1, n.2 (NP 5/21/2022), summarized the decisions:

Postal Service employees are not covered by the whistleblower protection statutes and thus are not entitled to seek corrective action under those statues. Hess v. U.S. Postal Service, 123 M.S.P.R. 183, ¶ 8 (2016) (finding that, as a Postal Service employee, the appellant was not entitled to an award of attorney fees or damages for alleged whistleblower reprisal under 5 U.S.C. § 1221, one of the provisions authorizing corrective action); Mack v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 617, 621 (1991) (finding that Postal Service employees are not entitled to seek corrective action under 5 U.S.C. § 1221 in connection with their whistleblower reprisal claims); see Booker v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 982 F.2d 517, 519 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (finding that Postal Service employees cannot seek corrective action under 5 U.S.C. § 1221).

The exclusion of Postal Service employees from IRA protection also excludes employees of the Postal Service Office of Inspector General, Gaston v. MSPB, 2006 WL 3779754, 2006 U.S. App. LEXIS 31615 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP No. 2006-3264):

We have held that employees of the Postal Service cannot appeal alleged prohibited personnel practices under 5 U.S.C. § 2302 directly to the Board under the individual right of action ("IRA") appeals provided for by the WPA. See Booker v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 982 F.2d 517, 519 (Fed. Cir. 1992).

Gaston is incorrect in asserting that the OIG is not part of the Postal Service. Section 410(b)(10) of Title 39 of the United States Code makes the Inspector General Act of 1978, Pub. L. No. 95-452, 92 Stat. 1101 (codified at 5 U.S.C. app. 3, § 1 et seq.), applicable to the Postal Service. Section 8G(a)(2) of the Inspector General Act ("IGA") defines the Postal Service as a "designated Federal entity" within the meaning of that section. See 5 U.S.C. app. 3, § 8G(a)(2). According to section 8G(b), the OIG was to be "established and maintained in each designated Federal entity." 5 U.S.C. app. 3, § 8G(b). The IGA requires that the "Inspector General...report to and be under the general supervision of the head of the designated Federal entity, but shall not report to, or be subject to supervision by, any other officer of employee of such designated Federal entity." 5 U.S.C. app. 3, § 8G(d). Both the Postmaster General and the USPS Inspector General are subject to appointment and removal by the Postal Board of Governors, which serves as the "head of department" for the Postal Service. See 39 U.S.C. § 202(a),(c),(e) (2000).

Therefore, Gaston was a Postal Service employee. Because employees of the Postal Service cannot appeal violations of the WPA directly to the Board under IRA provisions, we affirm the Board's dismissal because it lacked jurisdiction over Gaston's WPA appeal.
