b. Restoration Appeal Involving Employee Partially Recovered From Compensable Injury

For partially recovered employees asserting disability discrimination, Maiorana v. USPS, 38 MSPR 665, 688 (1988), explained the convoluted nature of Board jurisdiction:

...It is well settled that the Board does not have jurisdiction over the claims of improper restoration by employees who have partially recovered from compensable injuries. See Carter v. United States Postal Service, 27 MSPR 252, 254 (1985) [1]....

[1] The right of appeal to the Board for all partially recovered employees is limited to claims that the agency acted arbitrarily and capriciously in denying them restoration. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 353.306 and 353.401(a)(3) (regulations regarding restoration which do not differentiate between those employees who partially recover within one year of the date compensation began, and those who partially recover more than one year thereafter). Thus, to the extent that Carter v. United States Postal Service, 27 MSPR 252, 254 (1985), may be read to suggest that the Board may have jurisdiction over claims of improper restoration by employees who partially recover from compensable injuries within one year of the date that compensation began, the case is hereby clarified.

From the limited nature of appeal rights, Maiorana determined, Id. at 668:

We note initially our disagreement with EEOC's finding that the appellant's appeal is a mixed case. Under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.151(a), a mixed case is defined as "any case where an employee or applicant for employment alleges that a personnel action appealable to the Board was taken, in whole or in part, on the basis of prohibited discrimination." (Emphasis added.) In order for an action to be appealable to the Board, it must be a matter over which the Board has jurisdiction. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.3. It is well settled that the Board does not have jurisdiction over the claims of improper restoration by employees who have partially recovered from compensable injuries. See Carter v. United States Postal Service, 27 MSPR 252, 254 (1985).... The appellant's recovery was partial and he was restored by the agency more than four years after his injury and separation from the service. Thus, the Board has no jurisdiction over the appellant's appeal.

Because the appellant's allegation that the agency improperly restored him to the position of Mail Distribution Clerk is not a matter within the Board's appellate jurisdiction, his case is not a mixed case appealable to the EEOC. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.158 and 1201.165 (a decision appealable to the EEOC from the Board is defined as a decision in which issues of discrimination are addressed). The appellant's claim of handicap discrimination was not addressed by the administrative judge because of his determination that the Board had no jurisdiction over the underlying matter. See Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 MSPR 1, 2 (1980), aff'd sub nom. Wren v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (a claim of discrimination is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction).

Nor is the case appealable to the Special Panel. In Ignacio v. United States Postal Service [Special Panel No. 1, 30 MSPR 471, 476 (Spec. Pan. 1986), the Special Panel outlined the following four prerequisite steps necessary for the establishment of its jurisdiction. First, the Board must have decided an issue of discrimination law. Second, EEOC must have decided that the Board's interpretation of discrimination law was in error and reversed the Board's decision. Third, the Board must review EEOC's decision and reach the conclusion that EEOC's decision reversing the Board's decision is in error because it has misinterpreted civil service law, rule, regulation or policy directive. Finally, the Board may then certify the case to the Special Panel for a resolution of the matter.

In this case, the first two steps have not occurred. As noted above, because the administrative judge determined that the appellant's restoration was not an appealable matter, he did not address the discrimination issue.[3] Moreover, the EEOC's disagreement with the Board is not on an issue of discrimination but on an issue of jurisdiction. Thus, the prerequisite conditions for submission of an appeal to the Special Panel are not present in this case. Therefore, the administrative judge was correct in dismissing the appellant's first appeal for lack of jurisdiction....

[3] Although this case is factually distinct from our decision in O'Neal v. United States Postal Service, 37 MSPR 125 (1988), that decision is supportive of the conclusion we reach here. In O'Neal, the administrative judge found the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal and went on to find that the appellant had not established his affirmative defense of discrimination on the basis of physical handicap. The initial decision became the final decision of the Board and the action was appealed to EEOC under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(b)(1). EEOC found that the agency's action was the result of discrimination.

Upon referral from EEOC, the Board found that the Board had no jurisdiction over the appeal. O'Neal, 37 M.S.P.R. at 127-28. It found that the appellant had not been affected by an appealable action and therefore, the Board could not consider his claim of handicap discrimination. Id. The Board further found that because the appeal did not meet the requirements for using mixed case procedures, the appellant's appeal to the EEOC under 5 U.S.C. § 7702, and EEOC's issuance of a decision in his case based on that authority, were improper. Id. at 128. Under these circumstances, the Board found that it was without statutory authority either to adopt the EEOC decision or to certify the case to the Special Panel and accordingly vacated the final order and dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. Id.
