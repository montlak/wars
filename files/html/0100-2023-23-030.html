d. RIF Benefit

Gutierrez v. DOD, 66 MSPR 403, 406-07 (1995), discerned nothing amiss about an agreement that waived pay and grade retention rights of an appellant who settled an adverse action case by accepting a demotion through a reduction in force:

In support of his contention that pay retention rights under a RIF cannot lawfully be waived in a settlement agreement, the appellant relies on dicta in McCall v. US Postal Service, 839 F.2d 664 (Fed. Cir. 1988). The court there held that a waiver of appeal rights to the Board in a "last-chance" settlement agreement is not void as a matter of public policy. Id. at 668. In so holding, the court distinguished cases in which the Supreme Court had held that the availability of a labor grievance procedure under the Railway Labor Act does not deprive an employee of his right to bring an action under the Federal Employers' Liability Act,[3] and that certain rights found in the Fair Labor Standards Act were not waivable in the context of a collective bargaining agreement.[4] Id. The McCall court stated that these cases "have no application here because they involved statutes designed to provide minimum substantive guarantees for individual employees. Those portions of the Civil Service Reform Act, with which we are concerned, are directed primarily to the procedures by which civil servants are hired and fired." Id. (emphasis in original). The appellant reasons that grade and pay retention rights are substantive, not procedural, and therefore may not be waived.[5]

[3] Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe Railway Co. v. Buell, 480 U.S. 557, 107 S. Ct. 1410, 94 L. Ed.2d 563 (1987).

[4] Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., 450 U.S. 728, 101 S. Ct. 1437, 67 L. Ed.2d 641 (1981).

[5] The appellant also cites statements of agency personnel officials during settlement negotiations as indicating their belief that such a waiver is unlawful. Whether grade and pay retention rights can be waived in a settlement agreement is a question of law. Agency officials' beliefs about this legal issue would be irrelevant to its resolution. Moreover, the appellant could not reasonably have relied on any agency "misrepresentations" in this regard because the evidence shows that there was disagreement among agency officials about this question and, as discussed below, the appellant and his attorneys had the opportunity to review regulations indicating that such rights can be waived.

We note firstly that McCall neither held nor stated that there can be no waiver of any right that might be characterized as substantive rather than procedural. Secondly, the statutes governing grade and pay retention rights specifically provide that such rights can be waived. The statute governing grade retention states that its provisions "shall cease to apply to an employee who...elects to have the benefits of this section terminate." 5 USC § 5362(d)(4). The statute governing pay retention similarly states that its provisions "shall cease to apply to an employee who...is demoted for personal cause or at the employee's request." 5 USC § 5363(c)(3) (emphasis added).[6]

[6] The Office of Personnel Management's implementing regulations also provide that pay and retention rights can be waived. See 5 CFR § 536.105(a)(3).

In the settlement agreement, the appellant "request[ed] and consent[ed] to" the reduction in grade, and stated that he "understands that by consenting to this action, he will be excluded from receiving grade and pay retention benefits." Accordingly, we find that the appellant's waiver of grade and pay retention rights came within the purview of the exceptions contained at sections 5362(d)(4) and 5363(c)(3), and was lawful.
