B. BACK PAY AND RELATED REMEDIES

Nothing in the federal personnel constellation is simple: interim earnings, overtime, insurance benefits, retirement contributions, differentials. We explore here some complexities of returning employees to their financial status quo.

To the many decisions that discuss monetary relief (including overtime payments and other premiums) for Postal Service employees who prevail in Board appeals or settlements, we add a few more. Back pay calculations in Postal Service cases depend upon whether the employee has veterans preference status. According to Andress v. USPS, 56 MSPR 501, 501-508 (1993), the Back Pay Act governs entitlements of preference eligible postal employees. For nonpreference eligible Postal Service employees, back pay is governed by agency regulations, by the agency Employee and Labor Relations Manual, and by the national labor agreement. See Hargrove v. USPS, 37 MSPR 75, 77 (1988) (relying on Frazier v. USPS, 26 MSPR 584, 585 (1985)); cf. Gribcheck v. USPS, 87 MSPR 473, 479-80 ¶¶ 11-14 (2001) (exploring, as to OPM regulations governing psychiatric fitness examinations, the interplay of the Postal Reorganization Act, 39 USC 1005(a)(2), Veterans Preference Act, and CSRA). The Board's analysis in Andress was questioned but not overruled as to back pay remedies in Erickson v. USPS, 759 F.3d 1341, 1348-49 (Fed. Cir. 2014), suggesting that the Back Pay Act did not authorize counsel fee awards (on a fee petition in court, but more broadly for fee petitions before the Board) for Postal Service employees:

In Andress v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 501 (1993), the Merit Systems Protection Board held that "the Back Pay Act is a provision of title 5 relating to preference eligibles which, because of section 1005(a)(2), continues to apply to preference eligible employees of the Postal Service." Id. at 508. Accordingly, the Board held that a preference eligible employee of the Postal Service is entitled to back pay under the Back Pay Act following the reversal of an adverse action on appeal to the Board.

...

The Board's decision in Andress did not address the right to an attorney fee award. But the Board's ruling that the Back Pay Act is a provision "relating to a preference eligible" would lead to the conclusion that a Postal Service preference eligible would be entitled to an attorney fee award under the attorney fee provision of that Act. However, the Board's analysis in that regard is far from airtight.

Back pay entitlements may be affected by agency regulations or collective bargaining agreements. Postal Service calculations are complex. If Postal regulations provide more extensive entitlements than the Back Pay Act, covered employees may secure relief under those regulations in addition to entitlements secured under the statute. See Campbell v. USPS, 75 MSPR 273, 279 (1997); cf. Wilson v. USPS, 38 MSPR 156, 157-60 (1988) (under Postal Service regulations, no obligation exists to make the appellant whole for the higher taxes he pays when a back pay award is received in a lump sum).
