2. Notice of Disability

There must be some knowledge by the employer of the employee's problem. The obligation to make accommodation for an alcoholic arises only if the agency has knowledge of the disabling condition or if, because of circumstances known to it, the agency should have been aware of it. If the agency does not know of the condition, it cannot discriminate against the appellant on that basis. MacCormac v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 611, 613 (1985); see Parker v. Dept. of Transp., 9 MSPR 488, 489 (1982) ("[n]othing was presented to show that the agency should have regarded this as evidence of the disease of alcoholism and not an isolated incident of drunkenness[.] "). The information can be indirectly conveyed. See Swafford v. TVA, 18 MSPR 481, 483 (1983) (supervisor should have acted upon information received indirectly from a union official concerning the appellant's alcoholism).

The agency's required accommodation for alcoholism and alcohol abuse also extends to the effects of drug abuse. As with alcoholism, however, the employee's problem must be known to the agency for there to be a requirement to extend accommodation. McGilberry v. DMA, 18 MSPR 560, 563 (1984). The appellant bears the burden of proving notice to the agency; the employee must at least provide evidence of bona fide attempts to notify the agency of the problem and to seek rehabilitative assistance. See Coombs v. USPS, 41 MSPR 604, 609 (1989). Once the agency is placed on notice of the possibility of alcoholism and after inducing an employee reasonably to believe that it will undertake some independent investigation of an employee's allegation of disability, the agency cannot later argue that the employee failed to produce evidence supporting his assertion. If information available to the agency would have supported the appellant's defense, the agency's failure to inquire into the appellant's condition is harmful error going to the heart of the removal action because the agency action substantially impaired the appellant's presentation of an affirmative defense before the agency and likely affected the outcome before the agency. Sloan v. IRS, 28 MSPR 77, 79 (1985) (reversing removal; appellant raised affirmative defense of alcoholism in oral reply to removal proposal). But see Bacote v. Dept. of Navy, 12 MSPR 627, 628 (1982) (suggesting that it is insufficient to notify the agency only following the issuance of a proposed removal).

The knowledge of an employee's drinking problem by an individual in the agency chain of command is knowledge of the disability attributed to the agency. Booth v. DHHS, 23 MSPR 353, 355 (1984). The appellant does not place the agency on notice of the problem if he says nothing about it to agency officials, if he offers explanations for leave problems unrelated to alcoholism, if the employee always appears in full control of his faculties in the presence of supervisors and other officials, and if the supervisor provides uncontradicted testimony that he did not know or guess that the appellant had an alcoholism problem. Ferguson v. Dept. of Commerce, 29 MSPR 287, 288 (1985) (citing McGilberry v. DMA, 18 MSPR 560, 563-64 (1984)). Member Devaney dissented in Ferguson, arguing that the Board was not following the precedents of Brink v. VA, 8 MSPR 168, 170 (1980), and Ruzek v. GSA, 7 MSPR 437, 444, 7 MSPB 307 (1981).

The question of an agency's knowledge of an employee's disability is separate from whether or not the employee is a qualified disabled individual. An employee may not bootstrap his proof regarding the first requirement--the agency's knowledge of his disabling condition--to establish the second requirement--that he is a qualified disabled individual. Wilber v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 582, 587-88 (1989).
