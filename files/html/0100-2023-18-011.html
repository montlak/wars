b. Privacy Act; Disclosure of Confidential Information

The Privacy Act, 5 USC 552a--counterpart to the Freedom of Information Act--designed to ensure privacy (and other protections) in federal records systems, including personnel records, complements the Reform Act merit system principle requiring that personnel administration be conducted with due regard for personal privacy. 5 USC 2301(b)(2). Although the Privacy Act provides for monetary damages for willful violations and criminal penalties for certain types of violations, it lacks any sanction applicable to a civil service appeal.

The Board does not allow appellants to use the Privacy Act to preclude agencies from introducing evidence (although the Privacy Act is often invoked as an excuse for agencies not to produce evidence to appellants in discovery [an issue discussed in Chapter 2]). In the first case to consider the implications of the Privacy Act on adverse actions, Barnett v. VA, 4 MSPR 393, 4 MSPB 450 (1980), the employee objected to the introduction into evidence of notes offered by the agency. The appellant challenged the maintenance of the notes under 5 USC 552a. The Board determined that it would not consider an agency's compliance with the Privacy Act. This result followed because the statute grants an appellant judicial recourse to resolve the issue and the outcome of the judicial proceedings "would have no effect on the merits of the removal action presently before us." Battaglia v. DHHS, 5 MSPR 152, 153-54, 5 MSPB 212 (1981), considered an assertion that the agency maintained certain records in violation of the Privacy Act. The Board found no substance to the defense and no harmful error, reasoning that the Act provides judicial recourse to resolve disputed unlawful maintenance of records, citing Barnett, supra. A similar result was reached concerning an allegation that the Freedom of Information Act was violated by maintenance of records on the appellant. Cortright v. Dept. of Transp., 37 MSPR 565, 570 (1988).

Martin v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3302), rejected an argument that the agency violated the Privacy Act, 5 USC 552a(e)(2) by conducting its investigation without first collecting information from the appellant, who was the subject of the investigation. The court determined that the Board did not have jurisdiction to consider Privacy Act violations. Jurisdiction for those violations was vested in district courts. 5 USC 552a(g)(1). See Calhoon v. Dept. of Treasury, 90 MSPR 375, 381 ¶ 5 (2001) ("The Board lacks jurisdiction to review such [Privacy Act] claims; federal district courts are the appropriate forum."). Kent v. SSA, DE-0752-16-0391-I-1 (NP 10/27/2022), said of a Privacy Act objection concerning the agency's misconduct investigation:

The appellant alleged that the agency violated Article 3-6(G) of the CBA, which provides that, "[i]n conducting investigations regarding a non-criminal matter that may result in an adverse determination...the parties are reminded that the Privacy Act requires that, to the extent practicable, information should be initially collected directly from the subject employee." It is unclear whether the agency conducted an "investigation" as contemplated by Article 3-6(G) prior to proposing the appellant's 30-day suspension, but the question of whether the agency complied with the Privacy Act is not one over which the Board has jurisdiction. Social Security Administration v. Carr, 78 M.S.P.R. 313, 333 n.3 (1998), aff'd, 185 F.3d 1318 (Fed. Cir. 1999); see also Martin v. Department of the Army, 251 F.3d 170 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table) (holding that alleged violations of the Privacy Act properly lie in district court)....

Emanuele v. Dept. of Transp, PH-0752-15-0539-B-3, n.2 (NP 2/2/2023), discounted the effect of the Privacy Act on agency investigations used in adverse actions:

The appellant argued below that the agency violated the Privacy Act and that such a violation constitutes a violation of her due process rights. The administrative judge did not address this argument in the initial decision; thus, we address it here. Specifically, the appellant states that 5 U.S.C. § 552a(e) requires an agency to "collect information to the greatest extent practicable directly from the subject individual when the information may result in adverse determinations about an individual's rights, benefits, and privileges under Federal programs." (citing 5 U.S.C. § 522a(e)). Based on this provision, the appellant argues that the agency was required to interview and question her prior to taking the removal action against her and that its failure to do so constituted a violation of her due process rights. We are not persuaded by the appellant's argument. The section of the statute relied upon concerns record creation and keeping and imposes the above-stated requirements for those purposes. The statute does not indicate that these requirements are a matter of due process for an adverse action taken against an employee. The appellant does not cite any authority to support her contention. Accordingly, the appellant's argument is unconvincing.

See Huffman v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0752-14-0047-I-1, n.3 (NP 4/5/2023) ("The Privacy Act concerns maintenance and disclosure of, and access to, Federal agency records about an individual that contain an 'identifying particular assigned to the individual.' 5 U.S.C. § 552a. It does not address how an agency conducts disciplinary investigations.").

The appellant failed to convince a court that information discovered on his computer, used against him in an adverse action, was incorrectly obtained by the agency following a third party's FOIA request. The procedural protections cited by the appellant did not protect agency employees from the agency gathering information in response to a FOIA request. Winters v. Dept. of Navy, 242 Fed. Appx. 701 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP).

The Board addressed Privacy Act violations in a footnote in Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 170, 177 n.5, 7 MSPB 649 (1981). The agency was charged with releasing information to other employees and persons outside the agency about the proposed removal of the appellant and his interim detail to another position. The Board offered some analysis of the Act and its routine user exception, and then went on to state:

Assuming arguendo that a Privacy Act violation had occurred, we need not address the question of whether a personnel action based in whole or in part on a violation of the Privacy Act constitutes a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(12). This is because it is clear that appellant's allegations concerning a violation are a result of, rather than a factor in, the taking of the removal action.

See Grant v. USPS, NY-0752-15-0234-I-1, n.6 (NP 2/28/2023) ("The Board has considered alleged violations of the Privacy Act when the Act is directly implicated in matters over which it has jurisdiction. See Herman v. Department of Justice, 115 M.S.P.R. 386, ¶ 10 (2011) (determining that the appellant raised a nonfrivolous allegation of a protected disclosure based on an alleged violation of the Privacy Act); Gill v. Department of Defense, 92 M.S.P.R. 23, ¶¶ 21-24 (2002) (finding in a demotion appeal that the agency failed to prove its charge that the appellant violated the Privacy Act).").

If an employee raises an issue concerning improper disclosure of information about him, but does not allege that the release of the information contributed to his discharge, the privacy invasion is unrelated to the appeal and is not properly before the Board. Noah v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 383, 385, 5 MSPB 399 (1981); see Lewis v. SSA, PH-0752-10-0029-I-1 (NP 2/24/2011) ("The appellant asserts that the agency committed a prohibited personnel practice by disclosing information regarding the appellant to her co-workers. Because the disclosure of personal information is not delineated as a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1)-(12), we construe the appellant's claim to mean that the agency's disclosure of her personal information to her co-workers constituted a violation of the Privacy Act, 5 U.S.C. § 552a. The Board does not have jurisdiction over Privacy Act claims, as the statute requires such claims to be initially raised before the agency alleged to have committed the violation, 5 U.S.C. § 552a(d)(3), and then in the United States District Courts, 5 U.S.C. § 552a(g)(1). See Bush v. Office of Personnel Management, 91 M.S.P.R. 415, ¶ 8 (2002); see also Young v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶ 40 (2010)."); cf. Phillips v. GSA, 917 F.2d 1297 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (in compliance proceedings, an apology or retraction was not warranted for agency publicity of an adverse action case).

An appellant contended that management unduly influenced witnesses against him by corralling them after it made its decision to remove the appellant and telling them about the removal. The Board determined there was no undue influence, noting that the meetings held by the agency were "voluntary," the meetings were necessary to dispel rumors and to keep informed those employees who had to inform outside groups about the publicity attendant to agency operations that resulted in the appellant's dismissal (a sting operation), and appellant's counsel was invited to a meeting to present the appellant's side of the controversy. Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 355-56 (1987).

As to submissions to an AJ from an agency in alleged violation of the Privacy Act, Dosh v. Dept. of Army, DE-3330-10-0180-I-1 (NP 9/9/2010), decided there was no basis for sanctions:

On review, the appellant alleges that the administrative judge failed to rule upon her motion for sanctions, in which she alleged that the agency submitted her personnel records (i.e., her SF-50s and DD Form 214) to the Board in violation of the Privacy Act, and asks for money damages for the alleged violations. We conclude, however, that the judge's failure to rule on the appellant's motion for sanctions did not prejudice her substantive rights. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984). We note that a violation of an appellant's Privacy Act rights is not one of the limited situations in which Board regulations authorize the use of sanctions, and in none of those situations do the regulations authorize the imposition of money damages. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.43 (failure to comply with an order, failure to prosecute or defend an appeal, or failure to make a timely filing), 1201.74(c) (non-compliance with discovery), 1201.103(c) (ex parte communications), 1201.183(b)(1) (petitions for enforcement). Perhaps more importantly, the Board does not have jurisdiction to adjudicate a claim for violation of the Privacy Act. See Young v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶ 40 (2010); Calhoon v. Department of the Treasury, 90 M.S.P.R. 375, ¶ 15 (2001). Thus, although the administrative judge erred in not ruling upon the appellant's motion, he had no authority to impose sanctions.

There are restrictions concerning disclosures of information gained by drug and alcoholism counselors, but the defensive value of improper disclosures of information appears limited. As to disclosure of information by an official of a rehabilitation center, Goode v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 91-3317), determined:

Finally, Goode contends that the use of his disclosure made at the treatment center violated the Confidentiality Act, 42 USC 290ee-3(c) (1988), which prohibits the use of drug rehabilitation records, except to the extent authorized by court order, "to initiate or substantiate any criminal charges against a patient or conduct any investigation of a patient." The statute's implementing regulations prohibit the use of information received through proper consent in order to "criminally investigate or prosecute." 42 CFR 2.32 (1990). The proceedings that resulted in Goode's removal were administrative, not criminal.
