broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part n: Effect of Judge's Remand to Agency for Redetermination
n. Effect of Judge's Remand to Agency for Redetermination

Santillan v. DHS, 98 MSPR 78, 80-81 ¶¶ 6-7 (2004), involved an appeal from a negative suitability determination. The AJ sustained some, but not all of the charges, and the AJ issued an initial decision affirming the negative suitability determination but remanding the case to the agency under 5 CFR 731.501(a) for a determination whether the action was still appropriate based on the single sustained charge. The AJ informed the parties that there would be no further right to appeal the agency's final determination. The appellant awaited the agency's action, believing, appropriately, that the agency might well withdraw the negative determination and there would then be nothing to appeal to the Board. When the result was adverse, however, the appellant filed a PFR that was timely from the agency's redetermination but that was not timely when measured from the date of the initial decision. The Board dismissed the PFR as untimely, in a poorly-reasoned decision that did not resolve the inherent issue of the impact of a remand on the effectiveness of an initial decision:

The appellant bears the burden of establishing good cause for the untimeliness of his petition for review. To establish good cause for an untimely filing, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980).

It appears from the appellant's petition for review and from the request for an extension that he submitted with his petition for review, that the appellant decided not to file a petition for review until he saw what the results of the agency's determination on remand would be; once he learned that the agency's determination was not favorable to him, he waited nearly three weeks, and then he decided to file his petition for review. However, the initial decision clearly put the appellant on notice of the deadline for filing a petition for review and it further informed the appellant that there would be no right of review of the agency's final determination. That the appellant disregarded the initial decision's clear instructions and chose to wait until he knew the outcome of a decision from which he was told there would be no appeal before deciding to file a petition for review does not reflect due diligence or ordinary prudence. Schoenherr v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 73 M.S.P.R. 99, 102 (1997) (the appellant's decision to deal directly with the agency's officials and to pursue his appeal through other Federal agencies and through Congressional intervention rather than timely filing a petition for review did not warrant a finding of good cause); Shimmin v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 435, 438 (the appellant's claim that he did not file his petition for review earlier because he was awaiting decisions from five other offices or agencies did not establish good cause), aff'd, 43 F.3d 1486 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Nemoga v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 439, 440 (1991) (the appellant's claim that his petition for review was untimely because the agency took another adverse action against him did not constitute good cause); Carpenter v. Department of Health & Human Services, 40 M.S.P.R. 55, 56 (1989) (the appellant's decision to forego the timely filing of his petition for review pending the outcome of other matters in another case did not constitute good cause for waiver of the filing deadline); see also Hayes v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 448, 451 (1995) (the appellant's assumption that he could file a petition for review after OPM issued a decision concerning his retirement claim, despite the instructions in the initial decision regarding the deadline for filing a petition for review, did not constitute the exercise of due diligence or ordinary prudence).

Cf. Shave v. DOD, SF-531D-18-0469-I-1 (NP 5/9/2023) (implying that a PFR could have been filed as to disallowance of witnesses and other evidence when a WIGI denial appeal was remanded by the AJ to the agency for a redetermination; alternatively, upon appeal from that redetermination, appellant could have again requested the witnesses or sought to introduce the same documents).
