5. Notoriety

Notoriety is relied on by agencies (and the Board) as an aggravating factor. Judge Nichols of the Federal Circuit offered a dissenting opinion in Case v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3003), suggesting that bad publicity is properly to be considered an aggravating factor only when it relates to the offense charged, not to something else; and when it states the case fairly as it is known to the MSPB as it considers the appeal. Notoriety appears with some regularity as an aggravating factor in adverse actions. E.g., Sheiman v. Dept. of Treasury, SF-0752-15-0372-I-2 (NP 5/24/2022) ("As the deciding observed, the appellant's misconduct came to light through an anonymous source, and if the media found out, they would have a 'field day.' We agree with the administrative judge that the deciding official properly considered this potential notoriety as a valid concern, especially for a politically sensitive organization like the IRS."); SSA v. Boini, CB-7521-13-0192-T-1 (NP 2/22/2016) (ALJ removal case: "Portions of the incident, moreover, took place in the public area of the petitioner's office, and several of the respondent's decisions have been appealed specifically referencing this incident. See Taylor v. Department of the Navy, 35 M.S.P.R. 438, 442, 444 (1987) (finding general employee awareness of the charged misconduct to be an aggravating factor), aff'd, 867 F.2d 728 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table). We find that there was general awareness of the respondent's misconduct and that these actions have impaired both his ability to perform his duties and the petitioner's operational mission, thus supporting his removal for good cause. See Black, 29 M.S.P.R. at 137."); Rosario-Fabregas v. Dept. of Army, NY-0752-13-0142-I-2 (NP 7/1/2016) ("In addition, the agency has not identified any evidence showing that the actions at issue in this case were notorious. The deciding official testified that the appellant's actions 'could have' brought notoriety upon the agency if the public had known about the letters that the appellant edited. To the extent that individuals outside the agency were aware of the appellant's actions, they viewed the actions as positively impacting the agency's reputation."); Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 185 n.5 (2002) ("There is no evidence that the minor traffic incident actually created any notoriety or caused any adverse effect to the agency's public image. See O'Keefe v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 475, ¶ 17 (2001) (considering notoriety in the local community in determining a penalty)."); O'Keefe v. USPS, 88 MSPR 475, 482 ¶ 17 (2001) ("[t]he record shows that the appellant's misconduct, which was known to the credit company, and the builder, was reported in a local newspaper, giving notoriety to the incident."), vacated and remanded, O'Keefe v. USPS, 318 F.3d 1310 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (requiring reanalysis of facts and charges); cf. Gumbs v. DHHS, 620 Fed. Appx. 945 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) (Judge Dyk dissenting) (sustaining removal of a physician whose license briefly lapsed; the doctor treated one patient during the lapse; the agency concluded that the incident, had it become known outside the facility, could have compromised the trust of the patient community in the agency's health care system); Knight v. USDA, SF-0752-15-0309-I-1 (NP 4/27/2016) ("Regarding the eighth Douglas factor, the notoriety of the offense or its impact upon the reputation of the agency, the deciding official found that it was known among the appellant's coworkers and some individuals in the local community from whom the appellant solicited letters of support in response to the proposed removal. Notwithstanding, it does not appear to us that this incident was publicized, widely known, or otherwise had a significant effect on the agency's reputation. Although the deciding official's assessment of this factor was ambiguous, we agree with the appellant that it was not an aggravating factor."); cf. Crombie v. DHS, AT-0752-10-0412-I-1 (NP 9/14/2010) ("[i]t is still proper for the agency to consider the harm that the appellant's misconduct could have caused even if the misconduct did not actually result in such harm. See Jones v. Department of the Navy, 67 M.S.P.R. 6, 9 (1995) (the potential for harm due to the appellant's misconduct was an aggravating factor); Moore v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 23 M.S.P.R. 357, 360 (1984) (same).")
