a. Annuity Appeal

Clark v. OPM, 40 MSPR 517, 520 (1989), stated of OPM's obligations its administration of the retirement system:

OPM has an affirmative duty to fully and accurately inform claimants of their rights and obligations. See Potter v. Office of Personnel Management, 21 MSPR 476, 478 (1984); Keefe v. Office of Personnel Management, 19 MSPR 609, 611 (1984). In fact, OPM's own guidelines to agencies provide, in pertinent part, that agencies must fulfill their general responsibilities, including "counseling and informing employees on their rights and obligations under the law, giving assistance to claimants, including survivors of deceased employees...." FPM Supp. 831-1, Section S1-4(d) (Sept. 21, 1981).

The Board, expressing displeasure at the presentation of a retirement overpayment case by OPM, stated that the AJ is to assist in the development of the record, but the AJ is not to become OPM's advocate or establish its affirmative burden of proof, Sansom v. OPM, 62 MSPR 560, 564, 567 (1994):

Upon OPM's presentation of evidence, the administrative judge must exercise the authority granted to her at 5 CFR § 1201.41(b)(8) and (9) to ensure that the initial decision contains the necessary findings on all material issues of law and fact presented by the record, as required under 5 CFR § 1201.111(b)(1). West v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 MSPR 355, 358-59 (1985). If the evidence presented by OPM is technically difficult to comprehend, or is extraordinarily cumbersome, the administrative judge must direct OPM to provide explanatory material to enable her to make the required findings of fact. Id. See also, Harris v. Office of Personnel Management, 55 MSPR 21, 23 (1992); Hansen v. Office of Personnel Management, 35 MSPR 1, 4 (1987). Similarly, the administrative judge may call outside witnesses if necessary to resolve an issue; for example, employees of the Social Security Administration may be called to verify an annuitant's eligibility for social security benefits. West, 29 MSPR at 349, n.2.

In West the Board entered a caveat, however, noting that "the administrative judge is not to become the advocate of one party or the adversary of the other" when directing the production of additional explanatory evidence. Id. Thus, if OPM presents evidence that is sufficient to prove its allegation but technically difficult to understand or physically cumbersome to analyze, the administrative judge's duty requires that she direct the parties to provide additional explanatory material to allow her to make all necessary findings of fact and conclusions of law. If, however, OPM presents evidence that does not show all of the elements necessary to prove that an overpayment has occurred, it is not the responsibility of the administrative judge to continue to prod OPM into disgorging sufficient evidence. For example, in Owens v. Office of Personnel Management, 45 MSPR 86 (1990), the Board found that OPM had not presented evidence sufficient to prove that the appellant was eligible for OASI and, given conflicting evidence in the record, the reconsideration decision finding the existence of an overpayment could not be affirmed. Id. at 90.

...

Finally, OPM argues that the administrative judge had a duty to elicit additional information if she found the evidence "obscure or otherwise difficult to understand." In those cases where the Board has required the administrative judge to elicit explanatory material or evidence, the evidence presented by OPM was either technically difficult, confusing or cumbersome. As discussed above, we find that the evidence in this case was not obscure or incomprehensible. Rather, the record did not reflect sufficient evidence to prove the existence and the amount of the overpayment at issue. The administrative judge is required to seek clarification of evidence, but not to press OPM for additional material when the evidence presented is insufficient. In this instance, the evidence is wholly insufficient to constitute proof of an overpayment....

Following Sansom, the Board restated its approach concerning development of evidence in annuity overpayment appeals, Levine v. OPM, 70 MSPR 433, 435-36 (1996):

OPM bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, the existence and amount of an annuity overpayment. See Bacani v. Office of Personnel Management, 64 MSPR 588, 594 (1994). "[I]f OPM presents evidence that is sufficient to prove its [overpayment claim] but [that is] technically difficult to understand or physically cumbersome to analyze, the administrative judge [must] direct the parties to provide additional explanatory material to allow [the administrative judge] to make all necessary findings of fact and conclusions of law." Sansom v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 MSPR 560, 564 (1994) (citation omitted). Where the Board cannot determine on the record before it whether an appellant received an overpayment in the amount asserted, but where OPM's evidence, if clarified as described in Sansom, could establish the existence and amount of the overpayment, the appeal will be remanded to provide OPM an opportunity to clarify its evidence. See Harris v. Office of Personnel Management, 55 MSPR 21 (1992); Hansen v. Office of Personnel Management, 35 MSPR 1 (1987); West v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 MSPR 355 (1985).

The Board will "not...become the advocate of one party or the adversary of the other," however. Sansom, 62 MSPR at 564 (citation omitted). If OPM's evidence "does not show all of the elements necessary to prove that an overpayment has occurred," OPM will not be "prod[ded] ...into disgorging sufficient evidence." Id. Thus, in Owens v. Office of Personnel Management, 45 MSPR 86 (1990), the Board did not remand the appeal to allow OPM to clarify its evidence, where the record affirmatively showed that there had been no overpayment.

If technical evidence concerning retirement contributions cannot be understood, OPM must be directed to provide an explanation. West v. OPM, 29 MSPR 355, 358-59 (1985); see Clinton v. OPM, 38 MSPR 221, 223 (1988) (AJ in an overpayment case should request "such additional information whenever information submitted by the appellant is confusing or incomplete"); Juve v. OPM, 33 MSPR 17, 19-20 (1987) (AJ may not issue an initial decision without consideration of the record submissions of one party for the sole reason that he cannot comprehend the technical nature or mathematical computations in the evidence presented; instead, the proper course of action is for the AJ to direct the agency to provide explanatory material that will assist him in making the required findings of fact); cf. Chambers v. OPM, SF-831M-21-0376-I-1 (NP 9/16/2022) ("Although the administrative judge based his finding that OPM established the existence and amount of the overpayment on his finding that they were not disputed, this was an error, as OPM's burden of proof is not met simply based upon the absence of an objection from the appellant, Sansom, 62 M.S.P.R. at 567 (citing 5 C.F.R. § 831.1407(a)). The Board will require OPM to meet its burden of proof, regardless of whether the appellant objects specifically to elements of that proof, and to produce all of the relevant, material, and credible evidence bearing on the amount and existence of the overpayment. Id.").).

The Board's frustration with OPM's unwillingness or inability properly to present technical evidence became evident in Hansen v. OPM, 35 MSPR 1, 3-4 (1987). OPM determined that the appellant received overpayment of an annuity. The appellant contended that the overpayment should have been waived, and he questioned the computations used by OPM. In response to the appeal, OPM presented computer printouts that were incomprehensible to the AJ. OPM did not appear at the hearing, submit additional evidence, or offer additional explanation. In any court, OPM would have lost its case for failure to present evidence that could be understood. In a rather forced attempt to rescue OPM from its negligence, the Board essentially blamed the AJ for failing to require of OPM the information necessary for a decision:

The Board is sympathetic to the administrative judge's dismay at the complex task OPM foisted upon him, and we certainly do not condone the agency's cavalier approach to the Regional Director's order. However, the Board has repeatedly held that administrative judges cannot escape their duty to render decisions that address the factual and legal issues presented. This responsibility is not abrogated when a party's evidence is highly technical or difficult to comprehend.... Even when confronted with such situations, an administrative judge "must exercise the authority granted to him or her at...[5 CFR 1201.41(B)(9), (10)] to insure, when necessary, that the initial decision contains 'findings of fact and conclusions, as well as the bases therefor, upon all material issues of law and fact presented on the record'...." West [v. OPM] , 29 MSPR [355,] at 359 [(1985)] . This the administrative judge did not do.

In West, the administrative judge "could not verify the existence of an annuity overpayment, the accuracy of OPM's calculation of the alleged overpayment, or recalculate the correct amount of any overpayment he found."...The Board remanded with instructions to request the parties to provide whatever technical or explanatory evidence the administrative judge deemed necessary.... We can do no less here, where the existence of an overpayment is clear, and only the amount is in question.

The administrative judge must order OPM to provide the necessary explanation of its evidence within whatever time period he finds appropriate, keeping in mind that a prudent agency already would have furnished it. If the administrative judge determines that it is necessary, he may order a hearing, within the limits prescribed by Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984), "[t]o insure that the record is fully developed on significant issues,"...and he may order the appearance of any employee of OPM "whose testimony would be relevant"....

We leave it to the administrative judge to determine, in his sole discretion, whether to order an OPM employee to appear in person at a hearing to furnish the explanation or whether to permit the agency to submit a sworn statement.... If OPM does not comply with the administrative judge's order, it will be subject to a suitable sanction, including, where appropriate, striking OPM's evidence and response to the petition for appeal....

...

We cannot believe that OPM's lackadaisical approach to the case is what Congress expected when it entrusted the administration of the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund to that agency.... Many annuitants, both present and prospective, look to the fund as their sole source of post-employment income. Poor administration of any aspect of the fund, therefore, both deprives these former civil servants of benefits earned, and ultimately exposes taxpayers to an undue risk. Thus, it is incumbent upon OPM to see that those who are eligible enjoy the benefits they deserve and to defend the fund from those who have taken more than the law allows and to recoup such overpayments. Fidelity to those obligations is sorely lacking in this case.

The suggestion by MSPB that a "sanction" might include striking OPM's pleadings would place OPM where it was before: with insufficient evidence to sustain its defense to the appeal. That, in turn, would put the Board right back to the point it wanted to avoid: defaulting OPM. The solution was for the Board to exercise its statutory authority under 5 USC 1204(e)(2)(A), to stop payment of salaries to OPM officials for the period of noncompliance with the Board's case processing orders. Compliance would likely have followed in short order. Another possible remedy would have been an immediate award of the fees of appellant's counsel--as a sanction, and not under authority of 5 USC 7701(g), a route often used by federal courts that become fed up with the dilatory tactics of parties and their lawyers. Vice Chairman Johnson was somewhat more realistic in her appraisal of OPM's conduct. She stated in Hansen, 35 MSPR at 5: "The majority's criticism of the agency's lackadaisical approach to this case may effect a temporary improvement in agency case preparation. However, I believe that only by denying the agency a second opportunity to cure its negligently-prepared and delay-causing submissions will such improvement be permanent."
