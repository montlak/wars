a. Ensuring Notice of Record Closing

If there is a hearing, the record generally closes at its conclusion. If the appellant waives a hearing, the record is closed on a date set by the AJ as the final date for receipt of the parties' submissions. The Board will remand an appeal for the development of further evidence if the AJ prematurely closes a record and denies a party the right to submit evidence for Board consideration. See Meehan v. USPS, 7 MSPR 193, 195-96, 7 MSPB 106 (1981). But see Konieczko v. USPS, 51 MSPR 398 (1991) (appeal will not be remanded if a submission on PFR demonstrates that material that the AJ failed to review did not pertain to the basis for disposition of a case, e.g., a fee petition was dismissed as untimely and a submission that the AJ failed to consider pertained to the merits rather than timeliness of the fee petition).

The parties must be notified of the close of record date in all cases. Judges' Handbook, Chapter 10, section 16.a. The Handbook adds, at Chapter 10, § 16(b):

Reopening the Record. Once the record is closed, no additional evidence or argument may be accepted into the record except upon a showing that new and material evidence has become available that despite due diligence was not readily available prior to the closing of the record. Of course, the AJ may reopen the record on his or her own motion prior to issuing of the initial decision. For example, when a party has raised new issues or new evidence in a timely, but last-minute submission, the AJ must reopen the record to afford the other party(ies) an opportunity to respond. See Schucker v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, supra, chapter 10, section 1c(3); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.59(c). After the initial decision is issued, requests for reopening may not be granted. The AJ retains only the very limited authorities set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.112(a).

An AJ may announce at a hearing, or send a notice in an appeal decided without a hearing, that the record will close on a certain date. What is meant by "closing of the record," as a careful AJ will state, must be precisely determined. Normally, the date named will be the last for submission by a party of argument or evidence. If the record closes after the hearing concludes, the closing date may refer to the last day for filing briefs. An AJ's directive should clearly state the deadline for receipt of submissions. See Blackmer v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 571, 574 (1992) (Board implied that a "receipt" date would be construed from a AJ's order stating she would not "accept evidence or argument after the record closed on September 12"; there was no prejudice to the appellant if the record was prematurely closed); Jones v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 241, 243 (1985) (confusing notices). But see Montreuil v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 685, 692 (1992) (nonprejudicial error by the AJ who specified a receipt date and who considered evidence from the agency that arrived a day later). To avoid misleading appellants about the last date for receipt of submissions, AJs must exercise due care if the closing date is unspecified and occurs by inference. Meehan v. USPS, 7 MSPR 193, 194-95, 7 MSPB 106 (1981), remanded an appeal because the AJ failed to provide a specific date for the close of the record. To avoid problems caused by ambiguity, ask the AJ what is meant if the order or statement concerning closing of the record is unclear. The order should state whether the closing date is for the mailing of the submission or for its receipt in the regional office.

A formal notice of the closing of the record may not be mandatory if the closing date can be readily understood from other actions of the AJ. Pittman v. OPM, 2 MSPR 546, 549, 3 MSPB 68 (1980); see Martinez v. DLA, 4 MSPR 477, 479, 5 MSPB 16 (1981) (same as Pittman). In Martinez, it should have been clear to the agency that the record was closing: the agency was given 15 days to submit any additional evidence on material issues raised by the appellant. Under Martinez, the appeal will not be reopened if an agency fails to file its submissions in a timely fashion, unless there is a bona fide reason demonstrating that the agency was precluded from submitting the material on time. Once it is clear that the record will close on a certain date, an unexplained administrative "mix-up" by the agency and its representatives will not excuse late submission of evidence. Steel v. VA, 21 MSPR 224, 226 (1984). Orders must be studied to ensure that their implications are understood. The AJ may give the parties a date that constitutes a deadline for an event, e.g., submission of a settlement agreement. If the parties earlier submit their agreement and the AJ then closes the record, a party cannot complain that the record was not held open past the date of the event that led to the close of the record. See Souza v. VA, 54 MSPR 107, 109 (1992).

It is within the AJ's discretion to order or permit the record to remain open after a hearing for submission of evidence or argument, and the parties must remain alert to submissions (or orders from the AJ documenting those submissions) to preserve their reply rights. See Walton v. TVA, 48 MSPR 462, 468-69 (1991). Extensions may be requested. See Dougherty v. OPM, 36 MSPR 117, 120 (1988) (noting the appellant's failure to request that the AJ extend the time to permit filing of a submission after the date set by the AJ for closing the record). Rulings should be issued on extension requests. It is inappropriate for a AJ to receive and grant a request for additional time to submit evidence or argument without notifying the party and counsel that the request was granted. If notice is not provided, the harmful error rule will be applied. Long v. Dept. of Navy, 32 MSPR 438, 440-41 (1987). If a party inquires whether documents submitted at the last moment by the other party will be considered, the AJ should respond to the inquiry and, if the material is to be considered, allow for a response. Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 412, 418-19 (1991) (determining, however, that the agency's submission and the appellant's reply only summarized and referred to the record of evidence and argument).

Guidance about closing the record was provided to the regions in Field Office Memo 170 of July 31, 1981. The Board advised that no action to close the record is needed if the appellant withdraws his appeal in writing; the appeal may be dismissed if the withdrawal is unequivocal and unconditional. If an agency rescinds an action, an order to the appellant should point out that the documentation offered by the agency moots matter appealed moot, and that the appeal will be dismissed unless the appellant shows good cause before a certain date why the appeal should be maintained. If it appears that an appeal is premature or otherwise not ready for processing, the appellant should be informed that the appeal will be dismissed on the basis of the information indicating its prematurity, unless the appellant shows before a certain date good cause for maintaining the appeal. If a motion to dismiss is filed by the appellant or by the AJ on his own initiative, the parties should be given the chance to oppose the motion within a specified period of time. If the AJ errs concerning notice of the close of the record, MSPB reviews the error but may decide the error was harmless. See Williams v. DOD, DC-3330-18-0427-I-1 (NP 8/26/2022) ("Because of that error, we have considered all of the appellant's submissions from below and on review, including his May 7, 2018 response that he submitted to the administrative judge after the initial decision's issuance. Accordingly, the appellant has not shown how, under these circumstances, the administrative judge's procedural errors prejudiced his substantive rights; therefore, those errors do not provide a basis for review. See Heckman v. Department of the Interior, 106 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶ 13 (2007) (finding no reversible error because the appellant did not show that the cancellation of his requested hearing prejudiced his substantive rights under VEOA); see generally Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981) (finding that an administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown to have adversely affected a party's substantive rights).").
