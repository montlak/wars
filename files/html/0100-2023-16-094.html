d. Other Documentation; Testimony

Supervisors' memoranda to an employee describing her work deficiencies are evidence to be considered by the AJ, even if the memos do not attach pages of the work product. These memos may support a prima facie case of unacceptable performance even if they are not accompanied by affidavits from the supervisors. See Stephens v. DHHS, 23 MSPR 302, 304 (1984) (WIGI appeal). On the appellant's side, testimony from a prior supervisor who evaluated the appellant under the same standards and found his work acceptable is relevant and should be accepted by the AJ. The testimony is not duplicative of the appellant's subjective assertions that he was performing acceptably; the witness was proffered as an objective expert. Franco v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 653, 660 (1987). Opinions of coworkers concerning the reasonableness of the appellant's performance standards are not entitled to great weight. Poe v. USDA, 45 MSPR 678, 686 (1990); Benton v. Dept. of Labor, 25 MSPR 430, 434 (1984). Quizzes on standard operating procedures, administered to screening or security managers, appeared to be evidence of performance (albeit under Chapter 75 standards) in Winlock v. DHS, 110 MSPR 521, 530-31 ¶ 22, 2009 MSPB 23 (2009).

A formal written declaration from a supervisor may establish substantial evidence of substandard performance, Henderson v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0432-10-0374-I-1 (NP 6/8/2011):

...For the Board to sustain an agency's action in a chapter 43 case, the agency must show by substantial evidence that, among other things, the appellant's performance was unacceptable in at least one critical element. See Gonzalez v. Department of Transportation, 109 M.S.P.R. 250, ¶ 6 (2008). Substantial evidence is the "degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, might accept as adequate to support a conclusion, even though other reasonable persons might disagree." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(c)(1) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(p)] . Here, the administrative judge relied upon documentary evidence, as well as a declaration made under penalty of perjury from the appellant's supervisor, to find that the agency met its burden of proof by substantial evidence. Upon review of the record, we find that the appellant has not shown error in the administrative judge's findings.

Decided without a hearing, Punch v. NASA, DA-0432-13-4323-I-2 (NP 2/9/2016), commented on documents, including deposition transcripts and declarations, sustaining a Chapter 43 removal:

The agency witnesses who worked with the appellant during the PIP testified at great length on the errors the appellant committed. See (deposition of the appellant's supervisor); (deposition of the appellant's team lead). Given that the agency bears the burden of proof in this matter by substantial evidence, we find that the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge's reliance on the agency officials' affidavits and depositions was improper. Even if, as the appellant asserts, the individuals provided inconsistent testimony on certain points,[10] she has not shown that any such inconsistencies or any other factors were sufficient to render them incredible. Further, to the extent that the agency witnesses' testimony regarding the errors the appellant committed during the PIP is contradicted by her own testimony, as we have pointed out, substantial evidence is only that degree of evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, might accept as adequate to support a conclusion, even though other reasonable persons might disagree. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(p). Finally, while the appellant argues that the administrative judge did not take into account her own affidavit regarding the cited errors, we find that the administrative judge did, in fact, consider that the appellant disputed the errors attributed to her. In any event, an administrative judge's failure to mention all of the evidence of record does not mean that he did not consider it in reaching his decision. Marques v. Department of Health & Human Services, 22 M.S.P.R. 129, 132 (1984), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1062 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table). In sum, we find that the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge erred in finding that the agency proved by substantial evidence that her performance was unacceptable in at least one critical element.[11]

[10] For example, the appellant asserts that her supervisor testified during his deposition that, based on the appellant's assignments during the PIP, she did not need technical experts but that he also testified that such projects sometimes require extensive interaction with external parties.

[11] The appellant also challenges certain agency officials' qualifications to evaluate her performance, claiming that, because the deciding official relied heavily upon the proposing official and another subject matter expert, he did not make an independent determination. Although the deciding official acknowledged not being a "practitioner in cost estimating or cost assessment," he carefully explained how, in reaching his determination, he relied on his management team to provide the details, and reviewed the cited errors along with the appellant's "rebuttals," and the other pertinent documents, including emails between the appellant and her supervisor. The appellant has pointed to no requirement in law or regulation that a deciding official in a performance-based action under 5 U.S.C. chapter 43 must possess specific subject matter expertise, and we are not aware of any such requirement.

The supervisor's evaluation of the appellant's work is likely to carry more weight than is the absence of customers' complaints. See Thurman v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0432-13-0524-I-1 (NP 9/4/2014) ("Contrary to the appellant's contention, neither the absence of customer complaints nor the fact that his supervisor did not inform him that he had failed the PIP until several weeks after the PIP ended demonstrate that his performance was acceptable.").
