CHAPTER 17

JUDICIAL REVIEW

I. OVERVIEW OF THE APPELLATE PROCESS

"When shall we three meet again? In thunder, lightning, or in rain? When the hurlyburly's done. When the battle's lost and won." (Macbeth, I, i).

The witches' incantation could as readily be repeated at the end of Board litigation. The antagonists may yet again meet before the magistrates of our judiciary. Judicial review of MSPB cases not involving discrimination issues is conducted in the Federal Circuit, with review available in that and other circuits for whistleblower cases. Federal district courts are available for judicial review involving Board cases associated with EEO claims that appellants desire to pursue as plaintiffs in court. [Refer later in this chapter to "Discrimination Issues."] With the exception of whistleblowing cases reviewed by any circuit court under 5 USC 7703(b)((1)(B), and EEO cases heard by district courts under 5 USC 7703(b)(2), the Federal Circuit has exclusive jurisdiction under 28 USC 1295(a)(9) to hear an appeal pursuant to 5 USC 7703(b)(1) and 7703(d). Hagmeyer v. Dept. of Treasury, 757 F.2d 1281, 1283 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Williams v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3126) ("We have exclusive jurisdiction of an appeal from the final decision of the Board under 28 U.S.C. § 1295(a)(9) (1994)."); Cross v. Dept. of Transp., 127 F.3d 1443, 1446 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Federal Circuit has jurisdiction under 28 USC 1295(a)(9) to review Board's final decision in RIF appeal). Unified review in one circuit court is a product of statutory revisions to a system that previously allowed for review of decisions of the Civil Service Commission in federal district and then appellate courts throughout the country. See Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 270-71 nn.11, 15 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (criticizing the preexisting system). The Board looks to the law of the Federal Circuit for its governing guidance on civil service issues. See Schultz v. USPS, PH-0752-94-0233-M-1 (NP 12/16/2016) ("District court decisions also are not binding on the Board. See Cook v. Department of the Interior, 74 M.S.P.R. 454, 459-60 (1997) (citing Ruiz v. U.S. Postal Service, 59 M.S.P.R. 76, 79 (1993)). Also, other than decisions of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, the decisions of the circuit courts are not binding on the Board. See Bowman v. Small Business Administration, 122 M.S.P.R. 217, ¶ 13 n.8 (2015)."). An exception occurs in mixed cases, when a district court reverses a Board decision and remands the case to the Board--as would have occurred if the decision were not mixed and instead been reversed by the Federal Circuit.

Judicial review adds a measure of fairness in administration of our complex civil service law. Judge Newman, in her dissenting opinion in a patent case, provided perspective in Atlanta Gas Light Co. v. Bennett Regulator Guards, Inc., 33 F.4th 1348, 1360 (Fed. Cir. 2022):

The right of judicial review of decisions of administrative agencies has long been recognized. See, e.g., St. Joseph Stock Yards Co. v. United States, 298 U.S. 38, 84 (1936) (Brandeis, J., concurring) ("The supremacy of law demands that there shall be opportunity to have some court decide whether an erroneous rule of law was applied and whether the proceeding in which facts were adjudicated was conducted regularly. To that extent, the person asserting a right, whatever its source, should be entitled to the independent judgment of a court on the ultimate question of constitutionality.").

Today there is rich precedent treating access to judicial review of agency action, amid the complexities and congestion of the administrative state....

To this judicial precedent we turn.
