a. Ramspeck Act

Congressional employees may transfer to the competitive service, and if they do so they acquire appeal rights under provisions of the Ramspeck Act, Bickmore v. Dept. of Interior, 61 MSPR 241, 243-45 (1994):

The pertinent provision of the Ramspeck Act provides as follows:

[A]n individual who served--

(1) for at least 3 years in the legislative branch in a position in which he was paid by the Secretary of the Senate or the Clerk of the House of Representatives; or

(2) for at least 4 years as a secretary or law clerk, or both, to a justice or judge of the United States;

acquires a competitive status for transfer to the competitive service if he is involuntarily separated without prejudice from the legislative or judicial branch, passes a suitable noncompetitive examination, and transfers to the competitive service within 1 year of the separation from the legislative or judicial branch.

See 5 USC § 3304(c).

The Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) provides guidance regarding the Act's requirement that the separation must be involuntary and without prejudice. It states that the employee's record must be good, and that final separation must be due to circumstances beyond the employee's control. Such circumstances include the death, defeat, or resignation of the employee's employer, lack of work, or lack of funds. See FPM Chapter 315, Subchapter 6, 6-1(e).

This interpretation is consistent with the Act's legislative history. In approving the Act's provision permitting transfers to competitive service positions, the Senate's Committee on Civil Service explained its concern for employees in the legislative branch. The Committee stated that this provision gives such employees, who have "rendered long and faithful service to their Members and who have acquired valuable experience in Government, an opportunity to transfer to a position in the executive service should their positions 'on the Hill' terminate." S. Rep. No. 1865, 76th Cong., 3rd Sess. 6 (1940). The Committee Report further stated that "[e]very Member of Congress is familiar with cases where the death, resignation, or defeat of some Member has left his office force without jobs and the assistance these employees have asked of you in their attempts to get placed elsewhere. Some Members have lost valuable employees who have resigned to take positions offering a more permanent future. This provision is in a measure an assurance to these employees that they will have an opportunity to obtain employment in a field where they are experienced and qualified in the event their Member ceases to remain in Congress." Id.

In the present case, we agree with the administrative judge's conclusion that the appellant was not involuntarily separated within the meaning of the Ramspeck Act. As the administrative judge found, the appellant accepted a temporary appointment lasting five days, and her separation was due to the expiration of that appointment.

Although the appellant asserts that she had to accept the temporary position because she had already resigned her previous job, her reasons for accepting the position do not pertain to the circumstances of her separation. Further, to the extent the appellant is arguing that her separation was involuntary because her acceptance of the position was also involuntary, we find that such a choice between unemployment or a five-day appointment does not render her acceptance of the position involuntary. Cf. Schultz v. U.S. Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136-37 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (the fact that an employee is faced with the unpleasant choice of either resigning or opposing a potential removal action does not rebut the presumed voluntariness of the ultimate choice to resign). Further, while the appellant's appointment's limited duration may have been due to a lack of work, or a lack of funds, her separation, as stated by Senator Glenn, was based upon the completion of her assignment at the end of her appointment.

The appellant further argues that the record established that there is no minimum duration of legislative service for appointment under the Ramspeck Act, and that her service for just one day in her appointment would have satisfied the requirements for her Civil Service appointment. As the administrative judge also correctly found, the length of service necessary to reestablish Ramspeck eligibility for an individual who has previously satisfied the three-year requirement has no bearing on the involuntary separation requirement.

Finally, the appellant asserts that OPM's interpretation of the Act here is inconsistent with its longstanding practice, and cites to evidence of other employees whose Ramspeck appointments were not challenged despite the limited duration of their last legislative positions. This assertion, however, was also considered and properly rejected below. As the administrative judge found, these other employees were separated for involuntary reasons, such as the abolishment of their job, lack of funds, the defeat or retirement of their Congressman, or a reorganization. There is no indication that their terminations occurred because of the expiration of their appointments.
