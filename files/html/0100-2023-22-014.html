1. Mitigation

Mitigation from removal to a 120-day suspension made an appellant a prevailing party. It was immaterial that he asked for but did not receive greater mitigation. Shelton v. OPM, 42 MSPR 214, 224 (1989). In Depte v. VA, 20 MSPR 362, 363-64 (1984), a party prevailed whose appeal reduced a removal to a 90-day suspension. An appellant also prevailed whose removal was reduced to a 30-day suspension after the agency proved only part of one of the six reasons stated for the removal. Froehlich v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 265, 268 (1984); cf. VanFossen v. MSPB, 788 F.2d 748 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (considering fee entitlement following substantial mitigation of a penalty through a Board appeal); Boese v. Dept. of Air Force, 784 F.2d 388 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (subsequently discussed concerning the substantial innocence criterion).

An appellant did not prevail whose demotion was canceled only to be substituted by a removal issued for the reasons supporting the demotion, plus some additional reasons. Roth v. USPS, 54 MSPR 298, 301-02 (1992); cf. Smith v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 240, 243 (1990) (appellant did not prevail who settled a removal appeal for priority consideration for a GS-5 or lower-graded position). But see Alexander v. Dept. of Army, 80 MSPR 350, 353-55 ¶¶ 4-10 (1998) (appellant prevailed by a settlement changing the basis of removal from a physical inability to perform work and three misconduct charges, to only the charge pertaining to the ability to work; resolution of the appeal directly benefited appellant by affecting his ability to secure employment; the legal relationship was changed because appellant could ensure the changes through Board enforcement proceedings; fees were denied because appellant was not substantially innocent of the charges; that the case was settled was not evidence that the agency lacked a substantial basis for its action). An appellant prevailed whose removal was mitigated to a demotion, despite the agency's argument that the appellant was originally offered, and declined, demotion in lieu of removal. Hutchcraft v. Dept. of Transp., 55 MSPR 138, 142 ¶¶ 2-11 (1992); see Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 40 MSPR 638, 640 (1989) (appellant prevailed when the two-grade demotion imposed by the agency was mitigated to a one-grade demotion by the Board).

Under earlier decisions, a party who prevailed in a minor way could be considered not to have prevailed at all. In Loftin v. USDA, 45 MSPR 630, 634 (1990), the appellant was demoted from a GS-11/7 to a GS-7/1 position. The AJ, although finding the demotion a reasonable penalty, mitigated the penalty to the highest step of the GS-7 grade:

...Although some specifications of each of the three charges were not sustained on appeal, the administrative judge nevertheless sustained significant elements of each charge and found that the appellant's serious misconduct warranted the agency's selected penalty of reassignment and demotion to a nonsupervisory position at the GS-7 level. Under the circumstances, we cannot find that the administrative judge's decision to mitigate the financial impact of the penalty by ordering the agency to place the appellant in the top step of the GS-7 grade constituted a significant part of the relief that the appellant sought before the Board.
