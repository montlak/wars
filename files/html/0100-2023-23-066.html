1. Statutory Basis for Remedial Authority

The Board itself does not cancel an improper personnel action; it orders the agency to do so. The agency is then to pay benefits and back pay in accordance with the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596, and OPM implementing regulations, 5 CFR 550.801 (2022) et seq. The Board's remedial authority order stems from 5 USC 1204(a)(2) [formerly § 1205], giving the Board jurisdiction to require an agency to take any action necessary to effectuate a Board decision. An order by the Board under § 1204 gives the agency head the legal authority contemplated by 5 USC 302 to take the personnel action. Meier v. Dept. of Interior, 3 MSPR 247, 257-58, 3 MSPB 341 (1980). Aside from its authority to direct an agency to reverse an appealed action, the Board has the authority to order the award of back pay and benefits. Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 270, 272-73 (1984). When the Board orders an agency to cancel a personnel action or take any action necessary to give effect to a Board decision, it does not interfere with the delegations of authority established within the agency to effectuate the Board's decision; conversely, an agency may not decline to comply with a Board order by stating that some component of the agency is without the authority to do what the Board has ordered. Matthews v. OPM, 4 MSPR 431, 436-37, 4 MSPB 482 (1980). Under Meier, because an improper or unwarranted personnel action is void, the most effective and usual method to ensure that all of the ill effects of the improper action have been undone is to render the action null and void by cancelation. It is insufficient to appoint the employee to another position of like pay and grade, even if the appointment is made retroactive to the date of the improper action. The improper action would remain a part of the record and may prejudice the employee in the future.
