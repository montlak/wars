17. Failure to Honor Prior Identical Negotiability Determination

Once a proposal has been determined to be negotiable through the processes of the Statute, it is a violation of § 7116(a)(5) for any agency to refuse to negotiate in good faith on that proposal or on a proposal without material differences, in similar circumstances. Air Force Academy and AFGE Local 1867, 6 FLRA 548, 557-58 (1981) (ALJ Decision); see Office of Adjutant Gen., N.H. NG and Granite State Chap., ACT, 54 FLRA 301, 305 (1998) (comparing negotiable proposal in VA decision: "It is an unfair labor practice for an agency to refuse to bargain over a proposal that is substantively identical to one previously found negotiable by the Authority."); AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, Local 2544 and DOJ, INS, Border Patrol, Tucson, 49 FLRA 545, 550 (1994) ("Authority negotiability orders are binding on all Federal agencies faced with the same proposal."); Nat'l Guard Bureau and NFFE, 47 FLRA 1175, 1176 (1993) (ALJ Decision) ("an agency violates section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute when it refuses to bargain over a proposal that is the same or substantially identical to a proposal the Authority has previously determined to be negotiable"); DODDS, Mediterranean Region and Overseas Fed'n of Teachers, 38 FLRA 755, 759 (1990) (imposing retroactive bargaining order); Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 15 FLRA 829, 845-46 (1984) (ALJ Decision) (refusal to bargain based on assertion of nonnegotiability constitutes violation of § 7116(a)(1) and (5) if FLRA previously determined essentially identical proposals are negotiable). This principle was recognized in FLRA v. OPM, 778 F.2d 844, 848 (D.C. Cir. 1985). It was immaterial that a substantially similar proposal was being challenged in court in another case. IRS and NTEU, 28 FLRA 14 (1987).

When the negotiability issue was decided as to the similar proposal, the agency will not avoid ULP liability by injecting, but not prevailing on, new issues or arguments into its ULP defense in support of its contention of nonnegotiability. Dept. of Army, Ft. Stewart Sch. and Ft. Stewart Ass'n of Educators, NEA, 37 FLRA 409, 417-18 (1990); see Dept. of Interior and NFFE Local 1309, 52 FLRA 475, 479 (1996) (comparing proposal determined negotiable involving MSPB bargaining unit: if agency refuses to bargain over proposal identical to one previously determined negotiable by FLRA, it is immaterial whether respondent raises new or old arguments if the proposal is again found negotiable). The rule adhered to by FLRA is that an agency "acts at its peril when it refuses to negotiate about a proposal which is substantially identical to a proposal previously found negotiable, without regard to whether [the agency] raises 'new' or 'old' arguments." Dept. of Army, Ft. Stewart Sch. and Ft. Stewart Ass'n of Educators, NEA, 37 FLRA 409, 420 (1990). It was at least suggested, however, that because every negotiability determination must be based on the record established by the parties, a determination of nonnegotiability on a proposal submitted by two parties will not preclude resubmission and reargument of the same proposal by other parties, based on a different record. See NFFE Local 1167 v. FLRA, 681 F.2d 886, 892 n.10 (D.C. Cir. 1982); compare, e.g., AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., Ft. Rucker, 28 FLRA 1172 (1987), with NFFE Local 1429 and Dept. of Army, Letterkenny Army Depot, 23 FLRA 117 (1986) (different results on similar proposals were justified due to differences in records submitted by unions).

Although an agency acts at its peril when refusing to negotiate on proposals similar to those previously deemed negotiable by FLRA, a ULP will not be found if subsequently established law supports the agency's position. DOD, Dept. of Army, Hq. XVIII Airborne Corps and AFGE Local 1770, 20 FLRA 811, 817 (1985). If at the time the agency declines to bargain the law supports its determination of nonnegotiability, a ULP is not committed if FLRA later changes its position and decides that the subject of the proposal is negotiable. See Panama Canal Comm'n and Int'l Org. of Masters, Mates and Pilots, 52 FLRA 747, 752-53 (1996).

Assuming a proposal of settled negotiability, if a party attempts to bargain and a higher authority directs it to declare the proposal nonnegotiable, the party willing to bargain will not likely be found to have committed a ULP. See DHHS, Pub. Health Serv. and Ctrs. for Disease Control, 39 FLRA 1306 (1991). If charged under § 7116(a)(5), the higher-level respondent will be responsible for a ULP; if the higher-level activity is not charged, the employing activity will be responsible for the ULP violation. DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau and AFGE Local 2986, 47 FLRA 1213, 1219-20 (1993) (imposing retroactive bargaining order).

An agency's declaration of nonnegotiability does not constitute bad faith bargaining if, at the time of the declaration, no established precedent existed dispositive of the negotiability issue. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 23 FLRA 738, 740 (1986). If a ULP charge and negotiability appeal are filed simultaneously, and the union elects to proceed with the negotiability appeal and stay the ULP case, the agency commits a ULP if, before the negotiability appeal is decided but after it was filed, a negotiability appeal involving a similar proposal is decided in favor of negotiability and the agency still fails to bargain. Dept. of Army, Ft. Stewart Sch. and Ft. Stewart Ass'n of Educators, NEA, 37 FLRA 409, 420 (1990), supra. [For the relationship of negotiability and ULP procedures, refer to Chapter 4.]
