14. Condonation

Condonation is a possible basis for penalty mitigation, not outright reversal. Howard v. Dept. of Air Force, 114 MSPR 482, 485 n.2, 2010 MSPB 154 (2010) (supervisor failed to take action as to threatening statements that surfaced in a removal proposal several years later). Mitigation is warranted if regulatory infractions (permitting commissary employees to drink beer on duty at an office party and while on duty) were condoned by agency management. Pelletier v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 411, 418 (1984); see Canada v. DHS, 113 MSPR 509, 518-19 ¶¶ 19-21, 2010 MSPB 69 (2010) (remanding for consideration of the mitigating effect of whether a supervisor condoned consumption of alcohol by subordinates in a government vehicle while on duty); Wells v. DOD, 53 MSPR 637, 645 (1992) (condonation by supervisors of the appellant's disregard of timekeeping regulations); cf. Brown v. United States, 377 F. Supp. 530, 534 (N.D. Tex. 1974) (setting aside a dismissal based on a charge of improper investigative techniques: "It is elementary that an arm of the Federal Government cannot acquiesce, let alone train, employees for certain conduct, direct them to enter into it, praise them in some instances for such conduct, then turn around and in another instance discharge an employee for that conduct."); Els v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 27, 34 ¶ 15 (1999) (loan of a trailer to another government agency: "[S]ince the appellant was operating under the express authority of his supervisor, this factor weighs heavily against a severe penalty for the misconduct."); Frickey v. Dept. of Army, 61 MSPR 475, 481-82 (1994) (mitigating was the agency's failure to advise the appellant of a possible conflict of interest after the appellant requested guidance); Aliota v. VA, 60 MSPR 491, 497 (1994) (AJ should have resolved an issue of condonation before passing on the propriety of mitigation); Rogers v. DOJ, 60 MSPR 377, 388 (1994) (appellant could be disciplined for using a government fax machine for personal business, an improper use of the equipment, but that the discipline would be mitigated because of a policy that allowed employees to reimburse the government for occasional personal use of the fax machine or phones); Wusstig v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 335, 339 (1991) (mitigation was appropriate when a machinist foreman permitted subordinates to use government equipment on a personal project used for training purposes; the practice was of long standing); Rose v. DHUD, 26 MSPR 356, 360 (1985) (it would be grossly inequitable to suspend an employee for responding to a supervisor's directive, regardless of the employee's perception of the propriety of the order); cf. Avant v. Dept. of Air Force, 71 MSPR 192, 201 (1996) ("Although it is not a defense to the charge, the appellants' condonation argument is relevant to penalty.").

"[t]he Board has held that, although an employee's supervisor's comments may lead the employee to believe that he might get away with a falsification, this does not justify the employee's actions." Simon v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 482, 489 (1994) (citing Fuqua v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 173, 177 (1986)); see Herrera-Martinez v. SSA, 84 MSPR 426, 433 ¶ 16 (1999) (sustaining removal of a claims representative for the acceptance of gifts from claimants and falsification of or failure to correct false information on claimants' applications, despite the apparent condonation by a supervisor); cf. Tudor v. Dept. of Treasury, 639 F.3d 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (analysis of testimony concerning condonation of some actions relied on to discipline the appellant; remanding for further consideration of other elements of the appeal).

Condonation is not to be implied by every management action suggesting that a lesser penalty might be tolerated for misconduct. Removal is not unreasonable only because the agency offered the employee a demotion for his misconduct before the penalty was imposed. Lucas v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1679). As to the related argument that management is estopped from taking action for misconduct if management has taken actions encouraging that conduct, the reliance by the employee on management's statements must be reasonable under the circumstances. See Lebeau v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 160, 169 (1983). Condonation may be rejected as a mitigating factor by the Board if the charged misconduct is sufficiently serious. See MacCormac v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 611, 614 n.6 (1985) (distinguishing VanFossen v. DHUD, 748 F.2d 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1984)).
