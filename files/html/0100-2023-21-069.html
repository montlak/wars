n. Loss of Military Status

Civilian technicians who lose their military status and, as a result, their associated civilian positions, by reason of disability that disqualifies them for military status have the right to consideration for disability annuity from OPM, according to the statutory scheme described in Shaughnessy v. OPM, 15 MSPR 562, 563-64 (1983):

Bernard P. Shaughnessy, (appellant) has petitioned for review of the initial decision of the Board's Atlanta Regional Office which affirmed the reconsideration decision of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) denying his application for disability retirement.

Appellant was employed as a Civilian Technician (Production Controller) with the Kentucky Air National Guard, and as a condition of employment was a military member of the Kentucky Air National Guard as well. He had, because of his physical disabilities, been held to be ineligible for world wide duties with the Air National Guard and in accordance with the provisions of 32 U.S.C. § 709(e) was also ineligible for employment as a civilian technician.

Appellant applied for disability retirement, listing his disability as hiatus hernia, diverticulitis, gastritis, erosion of the esophagus and an ulcer of the esophagus. OPM held that while appellant was ineligible for his duties with the National Guard, he had not shown that he was totally disabled for useful and efficient service in the Federal service.

Appellant appealed to the Board and did not request a hearing. Upon review of the record, the presiding official issued an August 26, 1982 initial decision, finding that the appellant had not met his burden of establishing by a preponderance of the evidence that he is unable to render useful and efficient service in the position of Production Controller. Thus, he affirmed the reconsideration decision.

In his petition for review, appellant requests the approval of his disability retirement in view of the passage of legislation applicable to Guard technicians found disabled for their military position.

Appellant apparently refers to the changes in 5 U.S.C. § 8337, passed by Congress in September, 1982 as a result of Pub. L. 97-253, Title III, § 302 (a), 96 Stat, 792. In pertinent part, the new law states as follows:

(2)(A) Except as provided in subparagraph (B) of this paragraph, an individual shall be retired under this section if the individual--

(i) is separated from employment as a technician under section 709(e)(1)of title 32 by reason of a disability that disqualifies the individual from membership in the National Guard or from holding the military grade required for such employment;

(ii) is not considered to be disabled under the second sentence of subsection (a) of this section;

(iii) is not appointed to a position in the Government (whether under paragraph (3) of this subsection or otherwise); and

(iv) has not declined an offer of an appointment to a position in the Government under paragraph (3) of this subsection.

* * *

(3) Any individual applying for or receiving any annuity pursuant to this subsection shall, in accordance with regulations prescribed by the Office, be considered by any agency of the Government before any vacant position in the agency is filled if--

(A) the position is located within the commuting area of the individual's former position;

(B) the individual is qualified to serve in such position, as determined by the head of the agency; and

(C) the position is at the same grade or equivalent level as the position from which the individual was separated under section 709(e)(l) of title 32.

Congress further provided that this amendment is applicable to any technician separated on or after December 31, 1979 if he or she applies for its benefits before September 8, 1983. Appellant has recently submitted to the Board evidence that on February 9, 1983, he applied to OPM for disability benefits under the law, OPM has submitted no representations to the Board concerning this case.

In view of the above, appellant's petition for review is hereby GRANTED, and the case is REMANDED to the regional office for further processing, including the supplementation of the record by the parties as to appellant's eligibility for disability retirement benefits under 5 U.S.C. § 8337, as amended, and the issuance of a new initial decision.

See 5 USC 8337(h) for the current form of the provision cited in Shaughnessy.

That a medical condition makes one unsuitable for a military appointment does not alone establish a right to disability retirement when the civilian position is lost as a result of the loss of the military appointment, Cotney v. OPM, 7 MSPR 549, 550-51 (1981):

Appellant was discharged from the Alabama Air National Guard because his physical condition of epilepsy, which was controlled by medication, could create a security compromise. Because he lost his military position, appellant also lost his civilian position as a Communications Manager. He then applied for civilian disability retirement, but OPM disallowed the claim, asserting that appellant was not totally disabled within the meaning of the act. As noted in the initial decision, appellant's supervisor indicated that his epilepsy did not directly affect his job performance but created a potential security risk. The medical evidence submitted to OPM indicated that appellant's condition was currently under control with medication. No further medical evidence was submitted with appellant's letter of appeal.

The Court of Claims in Polos, supra [Polos v. United States, 621 F.2d 385, (Ct. Cl. 1980)], held that a determination of physical disability for military purposes does not necessarily render one disabled for purposes of the civilian position, and that this determination must be made by the Office of Personnel Management, not the military. In the present case, OPM determined that appellant was not disabled for his civilian position. However, the presiding official overruled this determination solely on the basis of Allen; he did not review the record in terms of appellant's civilian position to make an independent determination of appellant's condition.

Forde v. OPM, 7 MSPR 559, 560-61 (1981), added as to loss of a military position:

The Court of Claims in Polos, supra, considered the National Guard Technician's Act and its effect on civilian disability retirement. The court held that failure to meet military physical standards to retain membership in the National Guard did not necessarily establish that a civilian employee was disabled for the purposes of qualifying for civilian disability retirement. Civilian disability was a separate determination to be made by OPM and may or may not be the same as the military determination. The Polos court ordered the case returned to OPM for a determination. In the present case, OPM has made their determination. Therefore, for purposes of this adjudication, concerning civilian disability retirement, the Board must review the record in terms of appellant's disability for his civilian position, not his military position.
