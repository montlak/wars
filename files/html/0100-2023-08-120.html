G. LOSS OF APPEAL RIGHTS BY CHANGE IN POSITION

Individuals may switch from one job status to another, e.g., full-time to part-time, or from competitive service to excepted service, and by making the switch, they may lose appeal rights from otherwise appealable actions. See Haley v. Dept. of Treasury, 977 F.2d 553 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (implying that although the appellant was in the competitive service before his agency appointment, that appointment was in the excepted service and that status governed his appeal rights); Horne v. United States, 419 F.2d 416, 419 (Ct. Cl. 1969) (implying that a switch to a different type of position in another agency required another probationary period in the latter position); cf. Phillips v. DHUD, 44 MSPR 48, 52 (1990) (immaterial that an employee was erroneously told that an appointment would be by transfer). But see Grigsby v. Dept. of Commerce, 729 F.2d 772, 773 (Fed. Cir. 1984) ("As a transferee, Grigsby would retain his permanent status and would not be required to serve a new probationary period"); Saltzman v. United States, 161 Ct. Cl. 634 (1963) (suggesting that if an employee is in the competitive service, his employing agency may not divest him of competitive status when the agency obtains statutory authority to make appointments without regard to civil service laws); Bander v. United States, 141 Ct. Cl. 373, 378, 158 F. Supp. 564 (1958) (rejecting that "any change by a Government employee in his agency or line of work means automatic loss of his rights or status"; but an individual had to serve another probationary period after acquiring tenure in a prior but different position with one government agency, followed by the resignation from that position and appointment to a new position with another agency by means other than reinstatement) (a different approach was argued in a dissenting opinion by Judge Madden)); cf. Niles v. DOJ, 9 MSPR 63, 66-67 (1981) (agency should have treated the acquisition of the employee as a transfer; the employee was not appointed as a probationer). The Board ordinarily looks to the provisions of statutes in force at the time an action is taken to determine if the employee has appeal rights. Stewart v. DOD, 82 MSPR 649, 654 ¶ 12 (1999) [discussed later in this chapter, "Department of Defense Education Activity"] .

The Federal Circuit's decision in McCormick v. Dept. of Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339 (Fed. Cir. 2002), reh'g en banc den., 329 F.3d 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2003), and the Board's decision applying McCormick, Stoute v. Dept. of Navy, 98 MSPR 409 (2005), recognize appeal rights for employees who move to new positions, are incorrectly treated as probationers, but who previously completed probationary periods in the competitive service. [Refer to these decisions in this chapter, "Counting Service Time in a Prior Position or Appointment; McCormick," and "Appointment From Register."]
