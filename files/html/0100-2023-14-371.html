3. Competition; Selection Criteria

There was no bar to the negotiation of a proposal that competitive promotion procedures be used for details exceeding 60 days to higher-graded jobs or jobs with promotion potential. AFGE, Nat'l INS Council and DOJ, INS, 8 FLRA 347, 382-83 (1982). The provision of the former FPM at Chap. 300, Subch. 8-4e, that details involving higher-graded jobs or promotion potential be made competitively when exceeding 120 days, did not prohibit agencies from using those procedures for shorter details. The proposal did not interfere with the right of an agency to assign employees through details. Management could detail employees for periods of less than 60 days without competition. For longer periods the proposal preserved the right of management's discretion to identify and select employees based on job-related characteristics. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Promotions, Vacancies, and Appointments," "Ranking, Rating, and Qualifications."]

A proposal that did not qualify as an appropriate arrangement called on the agency to agree that "[n]o employee will be detailed to a regular vacancy or a new position under the negotiated Merit Promotion Program if there is an adequate register for the position in question." The intent of the proposal was to prevent the agency from detailing employees to provide an advantage in later competition for permanent promotion and to prevent the use of details to avoid paying employees at levels commensurate with the work assigned. The need expressed by the union was insufficient to overcome the limitation on details irrespective of management's need to use them in meeting work requirements. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Mare Island Naval Shipyard, 38 FLRA 1410, 1414, 1415-16 (1991) (Provision 2); see NAGE Local R14-52 and Dept. of Army, Red River Army Depot, 44 FLRA 738, 754-55, 759 (1992) (nonnegotiable: section of Provision 5 requiring agreement that "[d]etails will not be used in lieu of other appropriate personnel actions such as recruitment, promotion, or transfer").

A proposal was negotiable that did not call for competition but that made the agency set objective and job-related qualifications and performance attributes necessary for performance of a detail, AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, HCFA, Baltimore, 41 FLRA 618, 623-24 (1991):

The Union argues that Section 5 as a whole, including subsection 1, constitutes an appropriate arrangement.... [T]he Union claims that the section would provide employees with a fair opportunity to compete for career-enhancing details and would minimize the adverse effects of involuntary details. Based on the Union's statements, we find that the disputed sentence in subsection 1 constitutes an arrangement, within the meaning of section 7106(b)(3) of the Statute.

...

We find that subsection 1 would provide significant benefits to employees. The subsection would provide employees with specific information about possible details. The subsection also would assure employees that position qualifications are not tailored to specific employees, thereby denying other employees opportunities to qualify for details.

Subsection 1 clearly limits management's right to determine the qualifications it deems necessary to perform a detail by requiring the qualifications to be objective and job-related. The standards are general, however, and afford the Agency flexibility in determining qualifications.

Moreover, the Agency has identified no interest, and none is apparent to us, in establishing qualifications that are not objective and job-related. We note, in this regard, that the Agency does not object to the requirement in subsection 1 that position qualifications "be necessary to perform the detail." A requirement that qualifications also be objective and job-related does not appear to be substantially more restrictive than the requirement to which it has agreed.

On balance, we find that the benefits afforded employees by subsection 1 outweigh the negative impact on management's right to assign employees. Accordingly, we find that subsection 1 does not excessively interfere with the Agency's right to assign employees and is a negotiable appropriate arrangement under section 7106(b)(3) of the Statute.
