c. Expungement of Record

The Board has not approved expungement of its records. CFTC v. Duncan, 62 MSPR 143, 144 (1994), a proceeding against an ALJ, settled by a confidential agreement. The Board dismissed with prejudice the parties' complaint and answer, but the Board refused to expunge those pleadings from the record:

[T]he parties jointly filed a motion to expunge the complaint and answer that were filed in this case. The parties state that the joint motion to expunge the complaint and answer was made to give full effect to the confidentiality provisions of the Agreement, pursuant to which the parties agreed that public disclosure of the record and/or the terms of the Agreement would constitute an invasion of respondent's privacy and would otherwise be inconsistent with the public interest. The parties also agreed that "the MSPB's disposition of the joint motion to expunge...shall not affect any of the undertakings of either party to this Agreement."

The Board is required to keep an official record of proceedings before it. See 5 CFR 1201.54 [now 1201.53(e)] . The complaint and answer are part of the record in this case. To expunge the record means to erase, strike out wholly, blot out or destroy it so as to leave no trace. Black's Law Dictionary 582 (6th ed. 1969). Because MSPB records are used to document and adjudicate appeals and other matters arising under MSPB jurisdiction, and because other law protects confidentiality and personal privacy, the joint motion to expunge the complaint and answer is DENIED.

Dowell v. Dept. of Interior, AT-0432-20-0015-X-1, n.2 (NP 1/25/2023), denied appellant's request to partially redact records in the Board's appeal file:

In response to the agency's submission, the appellant requests a protective order or that the Board allow her access to the documents filed by the agency so that she can redact or delete the documents that contain her birthdate, address, and social security number. The appellant's request is denied. First, the Board does not allow one party to alter or delete the submissions of another party. Second, with the exception of the Board's decision in this matter, which is available on the Board's website, the case file from this appeal is protected by the Privacy Act of 1974 and is not available to the public through e-Appeal Online or the Board's website. While MSPB's adjudication records may be requested under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), all records are reviewed in accordance with the FOIA and may be withheld from release if warranted by a FOIA exemption.
