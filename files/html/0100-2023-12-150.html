broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section F: Review of New Issues or Issues Not Raised With Judge, Subsection 02: Determining if Issue or Argument Was Raised Below, Part c: Statutory, Constitutional Interpretation
c. Statutory, Constitutional Interpretation

Loube v. OPM, 5 MSPR 531, 533-34, 5 MSPB 521 (1981), precluded consideration of a statutory argument first made by the appellant in a PFR. The argument could have been raised in an earlier stage of the proceeding, the appeal to the regional office. The same result was reached in Bacon v. DHUD, 20 MSPR 408, 413 (1984). The Board declined to consider in a PFR an allegation not previously made that a RIF violated a statutory bar. The Board does not care that the new issue raises constitutional concerns. The Board will not consider a constitutional argument raised for the first time in a PFR. Rasmussen v. USDA, 44 MSPR 185, 189 n.4 (1990); see Lavan v. VA, DA-0752-11-0629-I-1 (NP 8/17/2012) ("The appellant asserts for the first time on review that: (1) the deciding official violated her due process rights by considering ex parte communications in her penalty determination; and (2) the deciding official violated agency procedures by failing to include all the evidence she considered in reaching her decision in the letter of proposed indefinite suspension. She may not raise such claims for the first time on review. See Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980) (the Board will not consider an argument raised for the first time in a petition for review absent a showing that it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite the party's due diligence)."); Woodard v. VA, DA-0752-10-0775-I-1 n.3 (NP 11/8/2011) ("Further, the record did not reveal any assertion of a First Amendment violation by the appellant before the administrative judge. Accordingly, because the appellant raised this defense for the first time on review, we have not considered it."); Chandler v. DHS, DC-0752-10-0368-I-1 (NP 6/30/2011) ("Although the appellant claims that some of the agency's rules regarding government credit card usage violated his constitutional rights, this assertion was not raised below, and therefore may not be raised for the first time on petition for review, see Crowe v. Small Business Administration, 53 M.S.P.R. 631, 634-35 (1992)."). [Refer to the treatment in the Federal Circuit of constitutional issues in Chapter 17, "Exception to Exhaustion Requirement."]
