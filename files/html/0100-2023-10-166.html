f. Prior Ruling Against Party

A party claiming bias must show that the bias constitutes extrajudicial conduct rather than conduct arising in the administrative proceedings before the AJ; that an AJ ruled against a party is not sufficient evidence to show bias. Hicks v. USDA, 65 MSPR 679, 683 (1994); see Haley v. Dept. of Treasury, 977 F.2d 553, 558 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (AJ was not predisposed to the agency because he initially ruled against the appellant on stay request in an IRA claim before reaching a subsequent unfavorable disposition of the IRA appeal); Gregory v. FCC, 84 MSPR 22, 31-32 ¶¶ 18-19 (1999) (earlier ruling against the appellant, coupled with the AJ's statement that she could not imagine that any testimony by the appellant's witnesses would alter prior findings, did not constitute bias; the AJ was permitted to inform the parties of the relative strengths and weaknesses of their positions, as she perceives them); Chianelli v. EPA, 81 MSPR 141, 145-46 ¶ 10 (1999) (denying a request for a different AJ on remand; the appellant's claims "are based on the administrative judge's perceived adjudicatory errors."), aff'd, Chianelli v. EPA (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3010); Jones v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 508, 509 (1990) (bias was not established by a bare allegation that the AJ was biased against the appellant because of the appellant's prior involvement with the AJ as a union representative in other cases); Ragland v. IRS, 6 MSPR 674, 677, 6 MSPB 565 (1981) (referring to a presumption of honesty and integrity that accompanies administrative adjudicators).

Of an unsubstantiated recusal request, Smith v. USPS, 81 MSPR 443, 446-47 ¶ 7 (1999), observed:

Questioning the presumption of integrity without supporting evidence "is totally unwarranted." Montgomery v. Department of the Army, 80 MSPR 435, 441 (1998). We note that the courts have consistently held both that a determination of bias may not be based on conduct arising in a trial setting, and that litigants are entitled to an unbiased judge, not one of their choosing; a "judge is as much obligated not to recuse himself when it is not called for as he is obliged to when it is." In re Drexel Burnham Lambert, Inc., 861 F.2d 1307, 1312 (2d Cir. 1988). As the Supreme Court has cautioned, "judicial rulings alone almost never constitute a valid basis for a bias or partiality motion...and can only in the rarest circumstances evidence the degree of favoritism or antagonism required...when no extrajudicial source is involved. Almost invariably, they are proper grounds for appeal, not for recusal." Liteky v. United States, 510 U.S. 540, 555, 114 S. Ct. 1147, 127 L. Ed.2d 474 (1994), citing United States v. Grinnell Corp., 384 U.S. 563, 86 S. Ct. 1698, 16 L. Ed.2d 778 (1966).

Formal adverse rulings do not demonstrate bias. See Matosian v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 689, 696 (1993) (prehearing rulings, if erroneous, and hearing rulings affecting development of evidence did not establish bias). Nor, perforce, will prehearing comments by an AJ concerning burdens of proof or evidence presented by the parties, but not rising to the level of formal rulings, demonstrate prejudice or bias. Ragolia v. USPS, 52 MSPR 295, 302 (1992). An AJ's ruling in another appeal did not demonstrate bias. See Caracciolo v. Dept. of Treasury, 105 MSPR 663, 670 ¶ 14, 2007 MSPB 139 (2007) ("The appellant's claim that the administrative judge was "unresponsive" in another appeal and the fact that the administrative judge made rulings in that appeal with which the appellant does not agree does not form a sufficient basis to require the administrative judge to recuse herself."); MSPB officials need not disqualify themselves because they have ruled adversely against a party's attorney on a previous occasion involving a different client. Hawes v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-992), aff'g Hawes v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 591, 593 (1983).

The Board will not disqualify an AJ for bias because it is remanding to that AJ a case he earlier decided against the appellant. Gipson v. VA, 7 MSPR 168, 7 MSPB 86 (1981); see Gipson v. VA, 682 F.2d 1004, 1008 (D.C. Cir. 1982) ("Agency officials are not disqualified from conducting a second proceeding involving a party merely because they have ruled against that party previously. NLRB v. Donnelly Garment Co., 330 U.S. 219 (1947)"); Whitmer v. OPM, 48 MSPR 312, 316 (1991) ("The fact that the Board remanded the appeal to the administrative judge does not establish that she was disposed to finding, as she did before, that the appellant failed to prove by preponderant evidence that she was disabled."); Barthel v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 245, 250 (1988) (that the presiding official previously ruled against appellant does not provide a basis for recusal); Bergstein v. USPS, 28 MSPR 495, 496 (1985) ("the Board has held that it will not disqualify a presiding official for bias simply because it is remanding a case to that official who previously held against the appellant"); Daniells v. Dept. of Treasury, 23 MSPR 266, 268 (1984). The AJ will not be considered biased even if he makes comments that reflect his opinion concerning the soundness of the result he originally reached prior to remand. See Niswonger v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 665, 672 (1994).
