G. AGENCY ORGANIZATION; ALTERNATIVES TO RIF

The union could not negotiate to preclude a RIF, downgrading of employees, or transfer of employees. The proposal interfered with the right of the agency to lay off employees under § 7106(a)(2)(A). AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 26 FLRA 768, 774 (1987) (Proposal 11). A decision to reorganize, relocate, and redistribute duties among office staff is a nonnegotiable management right. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 16 FLRA 217, 227 (1984) (ALJ Decision). During a transfer of function or reorganization, the union may not compel management to negotiate the number and grade of positions to be transferred. The numbers, types, and grades of employees or positions assigned to any organizational subdivision is negotiable only at the election of the agency under § 7106(b)(11). AFGE Local 236 and GSA, 9 FLRA 825, 826 (1982). A proposal interfered with management's right to lay off employees that required an equal portion of supervisory and nonsupervisory positions to be abolished during a RIF. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 18 FLRA 418, 426 (1985) (Proposal 8). A union could not require that a RIF not be used to reduce employment levels below the congressionally authorized ceiling. Management had the right to lay off employees for reasons such as a lack of work, shortage of funds, or as a result of a reorganization. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 18 FLRA 418, 424 (1985) (Proposal 5).

Nonnegotiable in IFPTE Local 3 and Dept. of Navy, Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, 51 FLRA 451, 460 (1995), was Proposal 4, establishing the organizational structure in a post-RIF organizational structure by specifying the number of supervisory and management personnel to be assigned:

Proposals that directly determine the working conditions of supervisory and management personnel do not concern conditions of employment of unit employees and are not, therefore, within the duty to bargain. See U.S. Department of Defense, Defense Contract Audit Agency, Central Region and American Federation of Government Employees, Local 3529, 47 FLRA 512, 523-24 (1993) (Defense Contract Audit Agency). See also United States Department of the Navy, Naval Aviation Depot, Cherry Point, North Carolina v. FLRA, 952 F.2d 1434, 1442 (D.C. Cir. 1992).

Proposal 4 contractually determines, among other things, the conditions of employment of supervisors and managers by fixing the number of those personnel to be retained in each listed organizational element. Accordingly, Proposal 4 does not concern a condition of employment of unit employees and is not within the Agency's duty to bargain under section 7117 of the Statute. See Defense Contract Audit Agency, 47 FLRA at 523-24.

Another proposal was nonnegotiable as either a procedure or appropriate arrangement that required reassignments to reduce staffing imbalances that had the potential to cause RIFs. Decisions were distinguished that determined to be negotiable proposals for arrangements for post-RIF reassignment rights. IBEW Local 2080 and Dept. of Army, Army Eng'r Dist., Nashville, 32 FLRA 347, 354-60 (1988). Even relatively broadly stated proposals, e.g., that the employer make every reasonable effort to retain unit employees affected by contracting out, were nonnegotiable infringements upon management's right to determine who should be laid off. AFGE Local 1808 and Dept. of Army, Sierra Army Depot, 30 FLRA 1236, 1249-50 (1988) (Provision 7).

But a demand was negotiable that before conducting a RIF management conduct a cost study that considered but did not require various options involving furloughs of groups of employees at various grade levels. The proposal constituted an appropriate arrangement for employees adversely affected by the agency's right to conduct a RIF. Because the proposal did not interfere at all with the right of the agency to conduct a RIF, it was not necessary to apply the "excessive interference" test as to the relevant management right under § 7106(b)(3). AFGE and DHUD, 21 FLRA 354, 355-57 (1986); see NAGE Local R1-203 and Dept. of Interior, Fish and Wildlife Serv., 55 FLRA 1081, 1094-96 (1999) (Proposal 8) (negotiable proposal required management to conduct cost study prior to RIF and consider options that included furloughs of groups of employees). Another proposal took the cost study methodology a step further. FLRA approved negotiability of an agreement that the cost study be conducted to determine whether instituting a furlough or retraining program would be more cost effective, that before the initiation of the study the union be consulted as to methodology of the study, that a copy of the study be provided to the union within three days of its completion, and that the union be afforded an opportunity to comment on the study at least 30 days before announcement of a RIF. NTEU and NRC, 31 FLRA 566, 576-77 (1988). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Contracting Out."]
