3. Timing of Action

Without evidence that the agency would have acted against the employee without reprisal, a dual-motivation penalty will not be sustained. Spadaro v. Dept. of Interior, 18 MSPR 462, 466 (1983), following remand from 10 MSPR 12 (1982). Although a removal proposal soon following testimony by the appellant in a different Board hearing could lead to an inference of reprisal, the inference can be dispelled through evidence showing that removal was being considered prior to the protected activity. Boomer v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 636, 640 (1987); Ketchum v. FAA, 28 MSPR 268, 274 (1985) (difficulties with the appellant's performance predated his protected activities); Dinkins v. USPS, 5 MSPR 110, 112 (1981) (some preparations for discipline were in process prior to the whistleblowing). If protected activity occurs after the issuance of a proposed removal, the chances for a finding of reprisal based on the timing of events is remote. See Marable v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 622, 631-32 (1992) ("Because the charge was filed after the letter of proposed removal was issued, however, the proposed removal could not have been in reprisal for the unfair labor practice charge."); Berube v. GSA, 30 MSPR 581, 601-02 (1986) (activity commenced on preparing a removal action on various charges before the agency added a charge that was based on the exercise of protected activity), rev'd and remanded, 820 F.2d 396 (Fed. Cir. 1987), rev'g removal on remand, 37 MSPR 448 (1988), post.

If the agency acts to resurrect or to solicit allegations of misconduct long past, but following an appellant's protected activity, an inference of reprisal may arise. See Mendez v. Dept. of Air Force, 38 MSPR 659, 663-65 (1988). But see Cates v. USDA, 24 MSPR 468, 471 (1984) (delay between an investigative report and the initiation of adverse action relatively soon after the appellant filed a grievance was explained by "continuing turmoil" resulting from personnel changes in the management of the organization; the proposing official was unaware of the appellant's grievance). A long lapse between protected action and allegedly prohibited action may also dispel a suggestion of causation. The causal connection is lacking if five years elapse between the filing of an EEO complaint and the adverse action and the appellant fails to establish knowledge of the protected activities by the officials taking the action. Harrison v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 294, 295 n.1 (1985); see Mumford v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 579, 581 (1989) (six years between the protected activity and the RIF); cf. Clark County School Dist. v. Breeden, 121 S. Ct. 1508, 1510-11 (2001) (suggesting that the temporal connection between protected activity and the alleged prohibited personnel action should be close in order to support an inference of reprisal).

The Board may be disinclined to litigate about events long past, particularly if the past actions claimed to have resulted in present reprisal were litigated. If the employee was informed of the prior action in writing, if the action is a matter of record, and if the employee was given an opportunity to dispute the charges at a higher level, additional evidence on the past incident may be excluded. Lewis v. Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 26 MSPR 164, 167 (1985) (following remand, 29 MSPR 447 (1985)). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Agency Consideration of Past Discipline," for limitations on examination of past discipline and exceptions to those limitations.]

Analysis of timing of actions was provided in Cooney v. Dept. of Air Force, 37 MSPR 240, 242-43 (1988), applying the causation analysis of the Federal Circuit decisions in Stanek v. Dept. of Transp., 805 F.2d 1572 (Fed. Cir. 1986), supra, and Warren v. Dept. of Army, 804 F.2d 654 (Fed. Cir. 1986), supra:

Upon consideration of the fourth factor in the Federal Circuit's formulation [after careful balancing of the intensity of the motive to retaliate against the gravity of the misconduct, a nexus is established between the adverse action and the motive], we find that the appellant has failed to establish a nexus between the adverse action and the motive. The sustained misconduct of disregard of supervisory authority is sufficiently grave to outweigh the agency's motive to retaliate [despite mitigation of the penalty]. As the administrative judge found, the appellant had a history of filing grievances against the supervisor who later served as the deciding official in her removal. Despite grievances filed in February, June, and July of 1985, the agency took no action against the appellant until after she first ignored supervisory authority in February 1986. The agency's lengthy twelve-month tolerance of the appellant's multiple filings demonstrates an absence of retaliatory motive and supports our finding that it was the serious misconduct that promoted the agency's decision to remove the appellant. Further, although a pattern developed where the appellant's grievance filings were followed by proposals of adverse actions, the first adverse action was not preceded by a recently filed grievance, the appellant engaged in additional misconduct that correlated to each additional agency proposal, and none of the new grievances were related to the misconduct or disciplinary proposal issued at the time in question. We, therefore, find that the intensity of the agency's motive to retaliate, if any, is outweighed by the seriousness of the appellant's disregard for supervisory authority, and conclude that the appellant has failed to satisfy her burden of proving the affirmative defense of reprisal under the standard set forth in Stanek and Warren.

The Board may decline to draw an inference based upon the timing of events if there is another explanation for the sequence of events. In King v. Dept. of Labor, 37 MSPR 110, 113 (1988), the appellant attempted to link congressional testimony and later discipline for parking violations he contended were earlier condoned or overlooked by his employer. The Board pointed out that the action against the employee was triggered by the complaint of another agency, presumably GSA, concerning the appellant's use of parking facilities. The intervention of the other agency was sufficient to dispel the evidence of retaliation. King considered insufficient to warrant an inference of reprisal a demonstration that the appellant filled out official documents in a particular way without discipline prior to his congressional testimony and could only have been disciplined in reprisal for that testimony when he was suspended for filling out documents in the same way after the testimony. The agency assigned the appellant a new supervisor shortly before his protected activity. The new supervisor took a more active role in supervising her subordinates than did her predecessor, and the new supervisor obtained evidence of the falsification shortly before the congressional interviews occurred.
