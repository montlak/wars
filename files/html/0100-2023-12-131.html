broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section D: Review of New Evidence, Subsection 02: What Evidence Is New, Part d: Due Diligence
d. Due Diligence

New evidence in a PFR must be evidence that could not be obtained at the hearing stage in the exercise of reasonable diligence. Fritz v. OPM, 5 MSPR 28, 30, 5 MSPB 108 (1981) ("However, because appellant has not made a showing that despite due diligence this evidence was previously unavailable, he is precluded from presenting the evidence by way of a petition for review under 6 C.F.R. 1201.115(a)."); see Sanders v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 136, 140 (1994) (declining to consider evidence submitted after the close of the record when the appellant did not comply with the Board order concerning discovery and failed timely to initiate discovery); Szczerbiak v. USPS, 2 MSPR 561, 563, 3 MSPB 82 (1980) (evidence supporting a disability discrimination claim should have first been presented to the AJ); cf. Cap Export, LLC v. Zinus, Inc., 996 F.3d 1332 (Fed. Cir. 2021) (considering due diligence and newly discovered evidence as to civil procedure rules applied to a patent dispute). But see Mendez v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 596, 602 ¶ 13 (2001) (considering with a PFR relevant evidence that the agency should have produced in response to a discovery request).

The Board elaborated on due diligence, Avansino v. USPS, 3 MSPR 211, 214 (1980):

Rule 60(b) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure provides, similarly, for relief from a final judgment or order by a court when a party establishes, inter alia, that there is "newly discovered evidence which by due diligence could not have been discovered in time to move for a new trial under Rule 59(b)." That "due diligence" requirement for newly discovered evidence may be analogized to the Board's "due diligence" requirement for new and material evidence because both relate to new evidence warranting reconsideration of an adjudicatory disposition at the first level. In construing Rule 60(b), the courts have required that the party offering the newly discovered evidence at least offer a reasonable explanation as to why the additional material or testimony could not have been supplied earlier. See, e.g., United Medical Laboratories, Inc. v. Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc., 258 F. Supp. 735, 747 (D. Ore. 1966), aff'd, 404 F.2d 706 (9th Cir. 1968), cert. den., 394 U.S. 921, 89 S. Ct. 1197, 22 L. Ed.2d 454 (1969).

The Board's regulations provide at 5 CFR § 1201.57(a) [now 5 CFR § 1201.58(a)] that "[w]hen there is a hearing, the record shall be closed at the conclusion of the hearing." In the instant case the presiding official notified the parties of this rule shortly before concluding the hearing. The record shows further that appellant was notified more than two weeks before the hearing that the agency's medical officer was scheduled to testify regarding appellant's fitness for duty. When appellant did not then produce his own physician at the hearing to counter testimony by the agency's medical officer, he was precluded from arguing that testimony his physician could have proffered at the time of the hearing was "new and material evidence" that was not available when the record was closed.

Since the basis of appellant's appeal was that the agency should have reassigned him to a position within the agency for which he was qualified, namely, that of Distribution Clerk, it is reasonable to expect appellant to exercise "due diligence" in preparing to counter the agency's evidence that he was not physically qualified for it, by oral testimony or otherwise. If presenting appellant's physician as a witness would have been too expensive for appellant to afford, appellant could have introduced his physician's written statement at the hearing. Appellant has offered no reasonable explanation as to why such a statement could not have been supplied at that time. Thus, the written statement of appellant's physician dated October 23, 1979, relating to appellant's ability to lift up to 70 pounds, cannot be deemed "not available when the record was closed," as required of "new and material evidence" by the Board's regulations. 5 CFR § 1201.115.

See Rivoire v. USPS, 103 MSPR 643, 646-47 ¶ 5, 2006 MSPB 313 (2006) ("The appellant has failed to show that he informed the administrative judge that he was having difficulty in obtaining this evidence or that he requested an extension of time in order to introduce it into the record below."); Conner v. OPM, 84 MSPR 680, 683 ¶ 7 (2000) (evidence requested from the OWCP prior to, and obtained after, the issuance of an initial decision was not considered when the appellant neither informed the AJ of the difficulty in securing evidence nor requested more time to present the evidence in the record after being warned that her evidentiary presentation would be limited to prehearing submissions); Treptow v. OPM, 32 MSPR 387, 388 (1987) (implying that inability during an appeal to afford the costs of medical evidence would not serve to permit belated submission of that evidence with PFR). Medical reports submitted with a petition for review are not new if they could have been obtained at an earlier stage of the litigation. Winkelvoss v. OPM, 19 MSPR 585, 588 (1984). But see Patillo v. OPM, 40 MSPR 452, 455 (1989) (in an annuity overpayment case, the Board accepted information as "new" when it concerned military retirement status that contradicted earlier information presented to and relied upon by the Board).

Diligence is due from both parties. The agency's failure to use discovery, or acquire a hearing continuance, to obtain or supply additional evidence prevents evidence submitted with a PFR from being considered "new," Nelson v. VA, 22 MSPR 65, 68-69 (1984):

In order to meet the new evidence standard, the agency must show that the evidence was previously unavailable despite due diligence. The agency's failure to do this will preclude it from presenting the evidence by way of a petition for review. 5 CFR § 1201.115(a); Beer v. Department of the Army, 2 MSPB 226, 228 (1980). In the present case, the agency could have filed a motion to compel discovery or for the issuance of a subpoena with the presiding official after appellant and Mr. Yan refused to answer its questions. 5 CFR §§ 1201.73(c)(2) and 1201.81(a). Because it did not avail itself of these simple procedures, it may not now claim that appellant's refusal to voluntarily assist it prevented it from obtaining the evidence despite due diligence. See Fuiava v. Department of Justice, 3 MSPB 217, 218 (1980). In addition, Mr. Yan testified at the hearing and was cross-examined by the agency representative. The record does not indicate that the agency objected to Mr. Yan's testimony or asked for an extension of time to rebut it. Therefore, it has failed to show that it exercised due diligence in obtaining the evidence before the record closed at the end of the hearing. Wakeland v. National Transportation Safety Board, 6 MSPB 44, 45 (1981). Moreover, regardless of Mr. Yan's testimony, the agency had the burden of proving its charge that appellant used the credit card on December 27, 1982, and March 11, 1983, to pay for gasoline for his personal vehicle. The additional evidence obtained by the agency concerning those dates and others, even if it was gathered in response to Mr. Yan's testimony, was clearly available to the agency prior to the closing of the record. Therefore, it does not warrant granting the agency's petition for review on the basis of new and material evidence.

The Board would not accept the agency's proffer of evidence in a PFR that suggested, for the first time, that a disciplinary action was undertaken before a protected disclosure, a fact that would have negated a whistleblowing reprisal determination by the AJ. The evidence could have been located earlier with proper litigation management, a conclusion reached in Duggan v. DOD, SF-1221-10-0159-W-1 (NP 9/6/2011) (aff'd, Duggan v. DOD, 484 Fed. Appx. 533 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP)):

The agency has asked the Board to consider newly submitted evidence in this case. The newly submitted evidence relates to the agency's argument that it initiated the action prior to the appellant's whistleblowing. The agency asserts, and supports through affidavits, that the agency representative was a relatively new attorney in the office and did not discover the document until after the record closed.

Citing King v. Office of Personnel Management, 114 M.S.P.R. 181 (2010), the agency argues that when the Board has found that "the appellant's submission of new evidence consisted of records that pre-dated the close of the record, [and] the documents lent credibility to the appellant's claims," the Board will remand the matter for review. The agency argues that the Board should apply such reasoning here because the agency's new evidence would change the outcome of the initial decision. See Russo v. Veterans Administration, 3 M.S.P.R. 345, 349 (1980) (the Board will not grant a petition for review unless it is of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from that of the initial decision). However, King does not stand for a general proposition that all evidence that could change the outcome of an appeal will be considered for the first time on review. Instead, King specifically explains that in annuity cases, unlike the case at bar, our paramount concern is whether the appellant is entitled to the benefit sought, and we resolve any doubt as to whether an appeal should be reopened for adjudication on the merits in favor of the appellant. King, 114 M.S.P.R. 181, ¶ 26.

On the contrary, the general rule pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(d)(1), is that the Board will not consider evidence submitted for the first time with the petition for review absent a showing that it was unavailable before the record was closed despite the party's due diligence. Avansino v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 M.S.P.R. 211, 214 (1980). We do not find the agency's argument persuasive. The agency should have anticipated that the timing of the proposed suspension was a significant issue, for circumstantial evidence regarding the timing of personnel actions normally is at issue in whistleblower appeals. 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(1); Kewley v. Department of Health & Human Services, 153 F.3d 1357, 1361-63 (Fed. Cir. 1998); see, e.g., Peterson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 116 M.S.P.R. 113, ¶ 15 (2011); cf. Daigle v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 84 M.S.P.R. 625, ¶ 9 (1999) (the Board accepted agency's evidence that the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) had approved its performance appraisal system, submitted after the record closed, because the agency had not been aware that OPM's approval would be an issue).

Even if the agency representative was a relatively new employee and did not know the potential location for all relevant evidence, agency managers in the legal office would have known the location of the necessary files and had a duty to assist the representative in finding them. The agency has failed to show how it would not have discovered this material in the exercise of due diligence or ordinary prudence. Cf., e.g., Zieff v. Department of Homeland Security, 98 M.S.P.R. 156, ¶¶ 11-12 (2005) (the agency failed to establish good cause for untimely petition for review based on newly discovered evidence that the appellant's concurrent case before another agency was not final, as the appellant had represented); Ernst v. Department of the Treasury, 69 M.S.P.R. 133, 137 n.2 (1995) (the Board rejected the appellant's claim that he had not discovered newly-submitted evidence in support of his case before the record closed because it had been buried under 200 pounds of documents), aff'd, 92 F.3d 1208 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

Due diligence is demonstrated by an appellant who makes a timely but unsuccessful request for assistance from the AJ to obtain medical records maintained by the Labor Department; those records will be reviewed with the PFR when they are finally obtained after the close of the record before the AJ. See White v. OPM, 24 MSPR 351, 353 (1984); Waite v. OPM, 9 MSPR 533, 534 (1982) (considering an OPM reconsideration file submitted with the PFR by the appellant after OPM failed to produce a file pursuant to the AJ's order); Mead v. DOJ, 9 MSPR 283, 290 (1981) (considering material obtained under the FOIA when the appellant made an unclear and unsuccessful attempt to obtain documentation production prior to the hearing); cf. Allen v. Dept. of Labor, 19 MSPR 80, 81 (1984) (evidence offered by the agency to the AJ after the issuance of the initial decision was "new" when it corrected erroneous information originally supplied by the agency concerning the nature of appointments material to a RIF; the appellant "had no reason to question the accuracy of this information and challenge it by discovery.").

The Board evaluated a report that the agency previously failed to produce pursuant to a timely discovery request. Blevins v. TVA, 46 MSPR 239, 243 n.3 (1990); see Granger v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 487, 491 (1996) (considering jurisdictional evidence as "new" when the AJ closed the record before the appellant could obtain discovery from the agency and after the appellant had initiated a FOIA request to the agency that eventually resulted in the production of new evidence). A transcript of proceedings in a criminal case that cannot with due diligence be obtained prior to the Board hearing may qualify as new evidence. See McCaffrey v. USPS, 36 MSPR 224, 227 (1988). But cf. Anderson v. USPS, 28 MSPR 58, 59 n.2 (1985) (declining to treat as new evidence a representation by the appellant that his transcript copy failed to include the testimony of some witnesses; the appellant could have obtained extension of the time to obtain the transcript earlier; tapes of the hearing provided a verbatim record even if the written transcript was incomplete).

Due diligence requires effort. Evidence may be received with a PFR as "new" if its production was improperly blocked by the agency when the case was before the AJ. But the rule is that if one party refuses to provide information during discovery, the party seeking discovery must pursue it through a motion to compel production of evidence. The Board will not grant a PFR based on new evidence if that evidence could have been secured through discovery. See Madrid v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 418, 421 (1988); Cody v. DOJ, 7 MSPR 218, 222, 7 MSPB 127 (1981) (FOIA materials offered with PFR were not new; the appellant failed to show interference with discovery proceedings available during the appeal). If one avenue of discovery is unsuccessful, evidence will not be considered "new" if another discovery route might have secured the desired information earlier, Davenport v. OPM, 59 MSPR 370, 373-74 (1993):

With its petition for review, OPM submits, as new evidence, copies of the appellant's Federal tax returns for the years from 1983 through 1991, alleging that it had specifically asked during a prehearing telephone conference whether the appellant had those documents, but that it had not pursued its attempts to obtain them because the appellant had asserted that her copies of the returns had been destroyed during a fire.

Assuming arguendo that the appellant's alleged assertion that she did not have copies of her returns excuses OPM's failure to pursue a discovery request for the documents before the record closed below, we find that OPM has still failed to establish a proper basis for considering the documents in question in connection with this proceeding. A party seeking to submit, on petition for review, documents that are not in the record must show that the information contained therein, not just the documents themselves, was unavailable despite due diligence when the record closed. See Grassell v. Dept. of Transp., 40 MSPR 554, 564 (1989). Here, although copies of the appellant's tax returns may not have been available to OPM when the record closed below, OPM could have obtained information about the appellant's earnings during the years in question through interrogatories. Neither OPM's Request for Response to Written Interrogatories and Request for Production of Documents, however, nor its subsequent Motion to Compel Complete Response to Interrogatories, reflects any inquiries regarding the appellant's non-Federal earnings.

See Brown v. USPS, 62 MSPR 76, 79-80 (1994) ("[T]he record below does not contain any discovery requests or motions seeking to compel discovery by the appellant"; "the appellant has not demonstrated how his FOIA request affected his ability to timely file his petition for review.").

The Board will not extend the time for filing a PFR on the basis of unsupported allegations that the other party has, allegedly in bad faith, withheld evidence from the petitioner. Even assuming that an appellant exercised due diligence in obtaining evidence, he is obligated further to exercise due diligence in pursuing his appeal to the Board in a timely manner once he had good reason to believe he had a basis to petition for review. Roberts v. OPM, 40 MSPR 443, 447 (1989); see Williams v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 262, 264 (1990) (evidence of attempts to secure material must be in the record compiled before the AJ; that evidence cannot be presented for the first time with a PFR). If the appellant does not receive evidence, later discovered, that he requested during discovery and that the agency then objected to producing, the appellant essentially waives whatever fault he later assesses against the agency if the appellant did not timely move to compel production of the evidence. See Cassidy v. USPS, 65 MSPR 86, 90 (1994). Cassidy was followed in Dull v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 31, 34-35 (1997):

His apparent decision to delay filing a petition for review in the hope of procuring additional evidence demonstrating his 'innocence' also does not constitute good cause for his untimeliness....

...Instead of filing a motion to compel the agency to produce the documents containing the information he needed, as was his responsibility, the appellant chose to enter into a settlement agreement and withdraw his appeal. The appellant may not now correct his judgmental error after the fact....

The Board reiterated the necessity to pursue elusive evidence during regional proceedings, Pittman v. Dept. of Interior, 60 MSPR 365, 369-70 (1994):

The Board will not consider evidence submitted for the first time with a petition for review absent a showing that it was unavailable before the record was closed despite the party's due diligence. 5 CFR § 1201.115; Avansino v. USPS, 3 MSPB 308, 3 MSPR 211, 214 (1980). The appellant says the information in Behr's statement was not produced in the regional office proceeding because Kathy Eaton, Deputy State Director, Administration, told Behr and other employees not to provide the appellant with any information or statements that would aid him in his appeal to the Board. The appellant's unsworn statements in his petition for review cannot prove the truth of this assertion, see Valverde v. Dept. of the Army, 40 MSPR 380, 386 n.8 (1989), which is not confirmed by Mr. Behr's statement. Even if we were to accept the appellant's assertions as true, we note that he made no allegation below that he was prevented from adducing relevant evidence, and he did not request a hearing at which he could have questioned Mr. Behr under oath. Under these circumstances, we conclude that the appellant has not demonstrated that the evidence in Mr. Behr's statement was previously unavailable despite due diligence.

Regarding the documents alleged to show that it is common agency practice to complete paperwork after the fact, the appellant gave no explanation with his petition for review of why he could not have adduced these or similar documents in the regional office proceeding. In a pleading filed one day after the record on review had closed, the appellant argued that these documents were not previously available because he had been denied access to property management files and other materials. This allegation is itself not properly before the Board because the appellant has made no showing that he could not have made it before the record on review closed. See 5 CFR § 1201.114(i). Even if we were to consider the appellant's explanation of why the documents were previously unavailable, it would not establish that he exercised due diligence in the regional office proceeding. A review of the initial appeal file does not indicate that the appellant availed himself of the Board's discovery procedures to obtain the documents in question, or that he complained to the administrative judge about being prevented from adducing relevant evidence. Cf. Wagner v. EPA, 54 MSPR 447, 453 (1992) (an appellant cannot wait until after adjudication is complete to object for the first time to an administrative judge's hearing-related rulings), aff'd, 996 F.2d 1236 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).

The proponent of evidence to be secured through a subpoenaed witness must protect the record through a request for a continuance if that witness does not appear at the hearing. The proponent must not rest his case until arrangements are made to secure the evidence while the record remains open. See Mojica-Otero v. Dept. of Treasury, 30 MSPR 46, 48 (1986); cf. Larry v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 348, 354 (1997) (failure of an attorney to submit the statement of a witness prior to the close of the record does not make the evidence "new" when the appellant submits it at the PFR stage); Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 426, 427 (1980) (appellant's counsel did not request a subpoena for a witness or request a postponement to obtain the witness; no effort was made to secure the testimony of the witness through a deposition).

A PFR was denied despite the agency's claim that it had a new witness. The agency did not state in its petition whether it attempted to locate the witness prior to submitting its witness list to the AJ. The agency did not demonstrate it told the AJ that it desired the witness but could not locate him. Due diligence was not shown. Bowman v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 353, 354 (1984). If a party needs more testimony from a witness who has testified and who has been excused from the hearing following his testimony, the party should request the AJ to recall the witness rather than seek to reopen the case to obtain that testimony following the initial decision. See Vick v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 364, 365 (1980) (discounting an argument that the witness could not be recalled because the AJ was "pushing for time").

If witnesses can be located through a diligent inquiry before a hearing, their discovery after the initial decision does not make their statements new evidence. See Mitchell v. VA, 28 MSPR 533, 534 n.* (1985). Evidence is not new because witnesses who were known by the agency but unwilling to testify earlier for fear of reprisal are, after the hearing, willing to come forward. See Williams v. GSA, 22 MSPR 476, 479 (1984); cf. Gonzalez v. Dept. of Navy, AT-0752-15-0228-I-1 (NP 9/21/2022) ("The appellant has failed to show, however, that he undertook any efforts below, much less exercised due diligence, to obtain the information presented for the first time on review, His mere assertions that employees were too fearful to provide statements in support of his appeal and that he was unaware of a Government website containing publicly available information are insufficient to meet his burden of showing that he exercised due diligence."). There was a contrary result: an agency managed to persuade the Board that a recently discovered witness had "new" evidence when the agency established that it could not reasonably have known the witness had information--essentially the appellant's admission of wrongdoing--before the close of the record. The individuals did not work together and the witness was on maternity leave at the time that the case was being prepared for the Board. Priddy v. VA, 40 MSPR 42, 45 (1989).

One agency alleged that as the appellant left the hearing room, he admitted to the agency representative that he lied at the hearing. The record was left open for six days following the hearing for submission of additional argument and other submissions. The agency representative did not bring the allegation to the attention of the AJ, but he did file an affidavit with the Board in the agency PFR. The Board declined to consider the allegation of perjury, stating that the evidence could have been presented to the AJ and was, therefore, not new. Thomson v. Dept. of Navy, 16 MSPR 474 (1983). Similarly, the agency attempted to convince the Board through affidavits that a witness lied at a hearing concerning the timing of events. The Board determined that the affidavits did not constitute new evidence. The agency could have cross-examined the witness about the events at the hearing (and presumably could have offered rebuttal evidence). Journiette v. VA, 21 MSPR 103, 106 (1984). [Refer later in this chapter to "Impeachment, Rebuttal Evidence."]

An appellant is responsible for the actions of his representative. The Board will not reopen for admission of new and material evidence that could have been offered earlier but for the failure of the appellant's attorney to realize the importance of requesting a hearing and presenting witnesses. Nathan v. OPM, 15 MSPR 359, 361 (1983); see Garner v. OPM, 22 MSPR 446, 447 (1984) (declining to reopen the case because the appellant complained of ineffective assistance of counsel "who indicated that he would request a hearing on her behalf and would help her with her case"; according to the appellant, "the attorney subsequently was indicted on various charges of fraud, was sentenced to prison, and had lost his license to practice law"). Nor will the appeal be reopened for the analogous claim of a breach of the duty of fair representation by a union or its representative. Cockrell v. Dept. of Air Force, 58 MSPR 211, 217 n.6 (1993). An appellant will not avoid an adverse result below through counsel's argument that appellant did not cooperate with his counsel without an allegation that the appellant was incompetent or could not otherwise maintain the appeal. MacLeod v. OPM, 27 MSPR 627, 627-28 n.* (1985); see Kennedy v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 266, 267 n.2 (1985) ("[C]ounsel's objections based on appellant's actions or inactions in developing the case before the presiding official are without merit as the right of appeal is personal to appellant and thus appellant is held accountable for its previous prosecution and development.").

The fact that appellant did not have counsel during the appeal does not support a claim that previously available evidence is new and material. McReynolds v. USDA, 23 MSPR 428, 429 (1984). Evidence or defenses raised for the first time in a PFR will not be considered for the reason that the appellant lacked counsel during the hearing stage and did not know enough to raise the issues then. Kehrier v. DOJ, 27 MSPR 477, 481 (1985). If an appellant knows that his representative has withdrawn from the case, it is the appellant's responsibility to determine the status of his case or secure alternate representation. The appellant cannot stand by and allow his appeal to lapse, attempting to rescue the effort through submission of "new" evidence in a PFR. Geren v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 722, 724 (1983).

Pro se appellants occasionally find the Board willing to accept evidence belatedly offered with a PFR. See McLoughlin v. Dept. of Treasury, 78 MSPR 150, 155 n.2 (1998) (considering evidence from a pro se appellant concerning appointment status when an AJ failed to advise the appellant of a dispositive jurisdictional issue); McIver v. OPM, 70 MSPR 103, 106-07 (1996) (pro se appellant appeared to suffer from a stress-related disorder that excused his belated filings with the AJ). An appellant could present evidence with a PFR if she demonstrated that during the underlying resignation and appeal at the regional level she was suffering from a mentally disabling condition. See Lewis v. USPS, 35 MSPR 77, 80 (1987) (appellant was not represented before the AJ but she was represented at the PFR stage); cf. Connelly v. USPS, 35 MSPR 614, 617-18 (1987) (appellant, who was removed for a mental condition, was not represented before AJ but was represented at the PFR level).

If a pro se appellant serves evidence on the respondent and reasonably expects that the respondent will supply it to the AJ, the evidence may be considered during the PFR stage after the respondent failed to produce the evidence to the AJ, Pritchard v. OPM, 70 MSPR 453, 457-58 (1996):

The Board will not consider evidence submitted for the first time with the petition for review unless it satisfies the Board's criteria for "new and material" evidence. See 5 CFR § 1201.115(d)(1).... In this case, we note that the appellant is pro se and apparently confused regarding the Board's service procedures under 5 CFR § 1201.26. As noted above, the appellant mailed the SSA award to OPM when she received it. Moreover, through her telephone conversations with OPM's representative below, she reasonably believed that OPM would consider the award and submit it to the Board's regional office as part of its file. See 5 CFR § 1201.25. OPM acknowledges that it received the award on June 29, 1995, and considered it at that time. As discussed below, OPM was obligated to consider the SSA award and was remiss in not submitting it to the Board as evidence it considered in support of its final decision. 5 CFR § 1201.25(c); see Garduno v. OPM, 43 MSPR 3, 5-6 (1989).

Under the circumstances of this appeal, we find that the appellant exercised due diligence in attempting to submit the SSA award into the record below. Further, in view of our discussion below, we find that the SSA award presents a significant challenge to the issues that determine the outcome of this appeal and thus is material evidence. See, e.g., Patillo v. OPM, 40 MSPR 452, 454-55 (1989); Lee v. OPM, 26 MSPR 391, 392 (1985). Therefore, we will consider this evidence on review.

But pro se status may not excuse lengthy delays in reopening for new evidence, as the Board noted as to an appellant's effort to resume an appeal based on evidence that he thought rendered his settlement agreement invalid, Marrero-Camacho v. Dept. of Army, 101 MSPR 362, 365 ¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 57 (2006):

Where an initial decision dismisses the appeal as settled, newly-discovered evidence may constitute good cause for the untimely filing of a petition for review, if the newly discovered evidence establishes that the settlement agreement was invalid. Dickson v. General Services Administration, 85 M.S.P.R. 7, ¶ 6 (1999), aff'd, 243 F.3d 566 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table). The appellant contends that, in negotiating and accepting the settlement agreement, it was his understanding that his retirement annuity would include credit for military service, and that he did not learn until May 1998 that an award of military retired pay would result in the reduction of his monthly annuity. Assuming, arguendo, that this discovery would serve as a basis for invalidating the settlement agreement, the appellant provides no explanation for why he waited an additional 7 years before filing a petition for review. While we note the appellant's pro se status, we find that he has not shown good cause for the lengthy delay.

Particularly when the appeal does not involve a hearing, problems may occur when an AJ permits the parties to simultaneously file evidence on a record closing date, without a right of either party to file evidence responsive to the evidence that goes then goes into the record. There have been a variety of approaches of parties who, when they receive the evidence filed by the other party on the closing of the record, seek to rebut that evidence.
