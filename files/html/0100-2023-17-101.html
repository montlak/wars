4. Classification Dispute

The Board does not consider grade classification in a RIF case, as the OPM does in classification appeals; however, grade-level issues are examined to the extent necessary to determine whether a RIF is bona fide. Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 278, 280, 4 MSPB 350 (1980); see Dunagan v. TVA, 14 MSPR 634, 634 n.* (1983) ("Appellant's proper classification ordinarily will not be reviewed in a reduction in force appeal."). Under Lewis, supra, if a RIF is properly conducted, and if the appellant feels that her grade is too low following the RIF, the remedy is an appeal through the OPM classification process. If an individual receives grade and pay retention benefits following a RIF downgrading, he may not appeal the downgrading to the Board or assert that the new position was improperly classified. Holbrook v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 247, 250, 4 MSPB 322 (1980). If the basis of a RIF appeal is only to challenge the classification assigned to an employee following a RIF, the Board dismisses the appeal, leaving the employee with a classification appeal to his own agency or to OPM. Kummer v. Dept. of Treasury, 4 MSPR 209, 211 (1980).

MSPB does not determine whether the grades assigned to positions are correct under classification standards. This is the function of OPM in a classification appeal. See Knab v. NASA, 13 MSPR 509, 512 (1982). The Board's review "is limited to determining whether there has been a bona fide reduction in force," a process that will at least examine whether a substantive change in duties has occurred. Decker v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 119, 126 n.5 (1989). Clark v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3029), reviewing a RIF, added:

Appellants also contest the agency's use of what they allege to be improper criteria for reclassifying the positions of Scott and Kelso [additional appellants in the RIF appeal], as well as the criteria used to fill all of the permanent positions. Because these allegations contest the classification, selection, and appointment process employed by the agency, they are beyond the jurisdiction of both the board and this court. See, e.g., Ellison v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 7 F.3d 1031, 1034 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (non-selection from promotion); Sanders v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 757 F.2d 1288, 1290 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (classification)....

Cf. Nichols v. DOD, 19 MSPR 471, 474 (1984) (agency cannot rely on OPM recommendations based upon a verbal description to OPM of the circumstances (for example, whether a particular action is a reclassification or a RIF)).

An appellant challenged his RIF because an employee who received a higher-level assignment right was improperly classified at the appellant's grade level according to unimplemented agency survey recommendations. Neither under OPM regulations postdating 1981 nor under the (late) FPM was an agency required to classify an employee's position properly before determining retention standing. Placement of the other employee in the same competitive level was not an error. Apodaca v. Dept. of Educ., 19 MSPR 540, 544-45 (1984). During another RIF, a competing GS-15 employee should have been, but was not, reclassified to GS-14. It was determined that the proper competition was at the level of the GS-15 job. The determining factor was the officially assigned and paid position. The GS-15 who lost out in the competition against the improperly classified employee could not successfully challenge the classification of his competitor. See Bjerke v. Dept. of Educ., 25 MSPR 310, 313 (1984).

Reviewing courts confirm that the Board correctly determines that the question of the classification of the position held by the displacing employee is not within its jurisdiction. Madsen v. VA, 754 F.2d 343 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Hollowell v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 7, 7 n.* (1986) (citing Madsen as to the conclusion: "Appellant does not have standing to question the classification of a position outside her competitive level."). Menoken v. DHHS, 784 F.2d 365, 369 (Fed. Cir. 1986), observed:

It would be almost impossible to determine the retention rights of employees affected by a reduction in force or transfer of function if the correctness of the classification of the positions the employees held had to be reexamined. Reductions in force deal with actual and not theoretical or possible situations.

The Board summarized its holdings regarding classification issues raised in RIF defenses in Haataja v. Dept. of Labor, 25 MSPR 594, 601 n.11 (1985):

...The Board has previously determined that the classification of a position is not reviewable in a RIF. See, e.g., Schneider v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 9 MSPR 121 (1981); Holbrook v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPR 247 (1980). The question of whether an appellant's position is properly graded, however, is reviewable if necessary to determine that the reason for the RIF is bona fide. Lewis v. Department of the Army, 4 MSPR 278 (1980). The Board, further, has determined that an appellant affected by a RIF through the displacement of a qualified higher subgroup employee lacks the standing to question the propriety of that displacement, Lester v. Department of Education, 18 MSPR 63, 67 (1983), but that the Board may review an appellant's challenge of the qualifications of the displacing employee to determine whether the agency showed by preponderant evidence that the displacing employee qualified for assignment to the appellant's position. Graziano v. Department of Defense, 9 MSPR 180 (1981). Under 5 CFR § 351.702, an agency is permitted to waive qualifications for a position to which an employee is offered reassignment pursuant to a RIF where it determines that the employee satisfies "any minimum educational requirements for the position" and "has the capacity, adaptability, and special skills needed to satisfactorily perform the duties and responsibilities of the position," but such determination is subject to review by the Board. Ratkus v. General Services Administration, 12 MSPR 464 (1982).
