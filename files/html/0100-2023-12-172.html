broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part b: Mailroom Delay in Posting PFR
b. Mailroom Delay in Posting PFR

The party filing a PFR must ensure that it is timely placed into the U.S. Postal Service mails. Earlier discussion explained the proof of mailing when a postmark is not affixed until after the envelope is placed in the mails. The significant date is the date that the PFR is placed into the mails of the U.S. Postal Service. An affidavit attempting to establish a date of mailing will not show that mailing occurred on the date that the PFR was placed in "the outgoing mail," for that may only be another way of stating that the petition was placed in the office mail system or unit. See Garrett v. USPS, 52 MSPR 32, 34 (1991) ("[B]ecause the appellant's representative does not state that he deposited the petition with the U.S. Postal Service on the deadline date, we conclude that the appellant has not established good cause for waiving the regulatory deadline[.]"). A delay of just a day due to the tardy operation of a mail room was not excused. Armstrong v. OPM, 36 MSPR 37 (1987); see Hall v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 161, 163 (1993) (PFR was deemed mailed on the date it was marked with a postage meter, not when it was set out for collection by an agency mail carrier); Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 592, 595 (1988) (deposit of a letter with a military postal unit was not the same as placing it into U.S. mails; the agency PFR was one day late and was rejected as untimely); Winslow v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 140, 142 (1989) (same analysis as Davis).

In Falkenstrom v. Dept. of Navy, 54 MSPR 85, 87 (1992), the agency representative handed the PFR to a mail room clerk and the representative observed the mail being taken to the agency van for a mail run to the Naval Station Post Office. The late postmark on the PFR was not excused: "That the agency representative has filed pleadings in this last-minute fashion in the past and, because they were postmarked promptly, has not had any rejected as untimely does not alter the fact that, in this case, the agency has not established that the petition was timely placed in the Postal Service mail stream." See Defreitas v. DMA, 45 MSPR 55, 58 (1990) (Board did not excuse the agency's delay in moving the PFR from the agency mailroom to the USPS truck); Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 295, 299 (1989) (agreement between USPS and DOD authorizing an agency mail unit to perform various postal functions did not make the mail unit the equivalent of USPS). But see Fayhee v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 131, 132 (1989) (accepting a cross-petition timely filed and returned for insufficient postage placed on a PFR by the agency "base post office," a factor found "beyond the agency's control").

Blevins v. Dept. of Air Force, 37 MSPR 154-55 (1988), rejected a PFR postmarked a day late. The explanation that the PFR was placed in the office mail depository for mailing on the due date was met with the comment that "ordinary prudence under those circumstances demands that counsel assure that the petition be posted on that date, not merely placed in the office mail depository." Another mailroom snafu netted the observation in Gill v. Dept. of Treasury, 41 MSPR 267, 269-70 (1989), that: "By waiting until the last moment to submit its petition for review, a party risks the possibility that its petition will not be filed on time. When it assumes this risk, it must make a deliberate effort to assure that it meets the Board's timeliness requirements, and its failure to do so, as in this case, will not excuse its untimely filing...." Gill deemed it immaterial that the day-late hand-delivered PFR arrived at the Board sooner than the same document would have arrived through the mails.
