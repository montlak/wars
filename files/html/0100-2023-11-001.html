CHAPTER 4

EVIDENCE

I. FUNDAMENTALS

"I'll see their trial first. Bring in the evidence." "Thou robed man of justice, take thy place." Shakespeare, King Lear (III, vi). "Take nothing on its looks; take everything on evidence. There's no better rule." Dickens, Great Expectations. "People in general have no notion of the sort and amount of evidence often needed to prove the simplest matter of fact." Lathan, Collected Works.

A competent representative in an administrative proceeding understands the procedural elements of the case, is familiar with governing substantive law, knows the facts, and is conversant with rules of evidence. Because the Board is not bound to rules governing hearsay or the Federal Rules of Evidence, most cases will not stand or fall upon the knowledge or application of formal rules of evidence. This chapter summarizes Board guidance on the limits of evidentiary submissions. The chapter is no more than an introduction; guidance should be obtained from the Federal Rules and the many legal treatises on the law of evidence.

We consider the law of evidence developed by the Board because of its essential relation both to the hearing, the opportunity for the introduction of evidence into the record, and to adjudication, when record evidence is assessed. Evidence is developed through several means. Appellants may submit documents with their appeals, and agencies reply in kind when they file their appeals responses. If no hearing is requested by the appellant, both parties will have the opportunity to submit additional evidence before the record closes and adjudication begins. If the appellant requests a hearing, as the appellant has the right to do, both parties may submit evidence at the hearing. Nothing in the law or in Board regulations restricts an agency to reliance on its documentary, administrative record, or withholds from that agency a full and fair opportunity to cross-examine or otherwise rebut an appellant's evidence presented for the first time at the Board's hearing. Zeiss v. VA, 8 MSPR 15, 17-18, 7 MSPB 516 (1981). The agency may prove its own case through evidence supplied for the first time after the appeal is filed and before the record closes on the appeal, whether or not there is a hearing. See Hawkins v. VA, 46 MSPR 484, 486 (1990). The Board considers the evidence whether it is submitted at the hearing or through other means. Precious v. TVA, 15 MSPR 176, 178 (1983). It is not the quality of the agency investigation but the quality of evidence that controls the result in an adverse action appeal. See Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 550 (1994) ("[T]he thoroughness or lack of thoroughness of the agency's investigation of the appellant's alleged misconduct is not a proper basis for not sustaining the agency's charge."). As to the role of evidentiary rules in the development of credibility findings, the Board cited Weinstein's Evidence ¶ 607[02] (1985): "the basic aim of all credibility rules is to admit evidence which better enables the trier of fact to determine whether it is reasonable to conclude that the witness is telling the truth or not." Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 601 ¶ 10 (2001). An AJ's findings do not of themselves constitute evidence. Vargas v. Dept. of Treasury, SF-0752-11-0626-I-1 n.5 (NP 6/22/2012). [Refer to Chapter 3, "De Novo Hearing," and to later in this chapter, "De Novo Review."]

As a result of the various methods and opportunities for the introduction of evidence, the Board develops its own law of evidence through decisions; it disclaims formal reliance on rules of evidence. See Sanders v. DHS, 625 Fed. Appx. 549, 552 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) ("While the Rules of Evidence may be 'a helpful guide to proper hearing practices,' they do not control Board proceedings. Yanopoulos v. Dep't of Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986)."); Brown v. USPS, PH-0752-11-0254-I-1 (NP 3/29/2012) ("Although the Federal Rules of Evidence are not applicable to Board proceedings, they may provide helpful guidance to proper hearing practice. Yanopoulos v. Department of the Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986)."); Stiles v. Dept. of Navy, 36 MSPR 646, 655 (1988) ("Strict adherence to the Federal Rules of Evidence is not mandatory in administrative proceedings...and the assessment of the probative value of hearing evidence necessarily depends on the circumstances of each case."); Bearden v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 217, 218 n.1 (1983) ("[W]hen dealing with cases of first impression, the Board has the inherent authority to establish evidentiary standards for the application of pertinent Federal law.").
