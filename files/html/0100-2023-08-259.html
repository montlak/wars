f. Premature Elections

Modifying preexisting law, Kirkwood v. Dept. of Educ., 99 MSPR 437, 438-41 ¶¶ 2-15 (2005), determined that the filing of a grievance challenging an adverse action precludes a Board appeal even though the grievance is filed before the effective date of the adverse action decision:

It is undisputed that the appellant, a GS-13 General Attorney, was a bargaining unit member covered under the provisions of a collective bargaining agreement (CBA) between the agency and the American Federation of Government Employees (Union). On June 12, 2003, the agency notified the appellant that she would be removed from her position effective June 20, 2003. The decision letter, advising the appellant of her appeal rights, stated in pertinent part that she must elect between the grievance and the Board appeal procedures and that whichever procedure she elects first, by timely filing under the applicable procedure, will be deemed her election, precluding her from pursuing the other procedure. The decision letter also informed the appellant of the time limits under both procedures.

On June 19, 2003, the appellant filed a grievance under the CBA's expedited grievance procedure "pursuant to the Notice of Appeal Rights set forth in the Notice of Final Decision to Remove...." The appellant also requested that Enforcement Director Susan Bowers (Bowers) stay the effective date of her removal pending a ruling on the grievance. Because the appellant filed a timely grievance under the CBA, within 5 work days after receipt of the agency's decision notice, Bowers stayed the removal pending a decision on the grievance. On June 26, 2003, the appellant filed an amended grievance.

By memorandum dated July 9, 2003, Bowers rendered her grievance decision in which she concurred in the assessment of the appellant's performance and found no basis to modify the removal. She indicated that the additional matters raised in the appellant's June 26, 2003 amended grievance were not properly before her for consideration because the amended grievance was untimely. Nevertheless, she addressed the issues contained therein. Bowers informed the appellant that the removal would be effective at the close of business on July 11, 2003. Bowers further advised the appellant:

In accordance with Article 42, Section 42.10, and Article 43, Section 43.07, of the Collective Bargaining Agreement, if the Union is dissatisfied with this decision, the matter may be referred for arbitration.

Id.

By letter dated July 25, 2003, the appellant's representative, on behalf of the union, invoked arbitration of the appellant's grievance. On August 7, 2003, the agency advised the appellant's representative that the invocation of arbitration was procedurally defective and untimely under the CBA, and that it would raise those issues if the appellant pursued arbitration.

On August 8, 2003, the appellant filed the instant appeal with the Board. In her appeal, the appellant admitted that she had filed a grievance under the negotiated grievance procedure. However, she asserted that, because her grievance served to stay the effective date of her removal and because her arbitration request was untimely, she "is now able to appeal her removal to the Board." On August 11, 2003, the appellant's representative advised the agency that she was withdrawing the arbitration invocation and appealing to the Board.

...

Pursuant to the statute governing grievance procedures, 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e)(1):

Matters covered under sections 4303 and 7512 of this title which also fall within the coverage of the negotiated grievance procedure may, in the discretion of the aggrieved employee, be raised either under the appellate procedures of section 7701 of this title or under the negotiated grievance procedure, but not both.... An employee shall be deemed to have exercised his option under this subsection to raise a matter either under the applicable appellate procedures or under the negotiated grievance procedure at such time as the employee timely files a notice of appeal under the applicable appellate procedures or timely files a grievance in writing in accordance with the provisions of the parties' negotiated grievance procedure, whichever event occurs first.

(Emphasis added.)

"Generally, an appellant's election under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e) to first pursue a grievance precludes a subsequent Board appeal, even if the appellant withdraws the grievance before appealing." Hardy v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 93 M.S.P.R. 63 ¶ 7 (2002). However, there is some conflict in the case law regarding whether an effective election of the negotiated grievance procedure may be made under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e)(1) before the effective date of the appealable action.

In Riggs v. Department of Defense, 48 M.S.P.R. 652, 656 (1991), the Board held that an employee's timely grievance filed under the CBA after he received the agency's removal decision, but before the effective date of the removal, constituted an election of the grievance procedure under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e). Similarly, in the instant case, the appellant filed her grievance after she received the agency's final decision notice, but before the effective date of her removal. The decision notice made the appellant fully aware of the consequences of making an election. Under the Riggs approach, the appellant made a valid election of the grievance procedure, thereby depriving the Board of jurisdiction.

On the other hand, in another line of cases, the Board has held that no election under section 7121(e) can occur before the effective date of the appealable action. See, e.g., Jackson v. Department of the Army, 94 M.S.P.R. 320 ¶¶ 8-9 (2003); Hardy, 93 M.S.P.R. 63 ¶ 7; Sylvester v. Veterans Administration, 34 M.S.P.R. 215, 216 (1987); Battle v. Department of Labor, 29 M.S.P.R. 15, 16 (1985). "Instead, an employee's election under section 7121 is considered to be the first timely filing (either grievance or Board appeal) after the effective date of the action." Hardy, 93 M.S.P.R. 63 ¶ 7 (emphasis added).

Upon further consideration of these divergent lines of cases, the Board finds that Riggs sets forth the better approach. It is consistent with the language of 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e), which does not indicate that a valid election may only be made after the effective date of the appealable action. Instead, the statute provides that the election is effective if the employee timely filed her grievance, which the appellant in this matter did. See 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e).

The contrary line of cases, which includes some cases decided after Riggs, does not overrule Riggs, or attempt to distinguish that case. Further, the cases in that line do not explain why a grievance filed prior to the effective date of the action can never constitute an election under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e) and thereby preclude Board jurisdiction. Accordingly, the Board adopts the Riggs approach as set forth above. The Board overrules any cases holding that no election under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e) can occur before the effective date of the appealable action. Instead, consistent with the relevant statute, an election to file a grievance is effective, and deprives the Board of jurisdiction over the grieved action, if the employee receives adequate notice of her election rights and timely files her grievance, which the appellant in this matter did. See 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e).

Before the 2005 Kirkwood decision, there were decisions that examined the first action of the appellant after the effective date of the action appealed. Under § 7121(d) or (e), no election could occur before the effective date of the appealable action. See Jackson v. Dept. of Army, 94 MSPR 320, 322 ¶ 9 (2003) (union's grievance of an employee's termination prior to the effective date of the termination did not preclude the employee from appealing the termination to the Board after the effective date of the action); Hardy v. VA, 93 MSPR 63, 65 ¶ 8 (2002) (grievance filed, then withdrawn, before the effective date of a suspension did not bar a Board appeal of the suspension); Sylvester v. VA, 34 MSPR 215, 216 (1987) ("The filing of the petition for appeal was the appellant's first action after the effective date of the removal and constituted a timely election under section 7121(d) or (e)."); Carreno v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 515, 518 (1984) ("The decision to remove appellant was not made until December 30, 1983. Thus if appellant filed a grievance on December 16, 1983, it could not have been a grievance of the removal action."); cf. Landsiedel v. Dept. of Air Force, 89 MSPR 256, 258-59 ¶¶ 5-7 (2001) (union's grievance of "certificates of expected separation" is insufficient to divest the Board of jurisdiction of an appeal of an actual RIF six months later raising EEO issues; appellants did not file a written grievance following the effective date of the RIF); cf. Williams v. GPO, 86 MSPR 583, 586 ¶ 10 (2000) (EEO complaints, referring to events occurring between July and September 1998, did not preclude the grievance of a removal effective November 22, 1998). If a grievance was filed on December 16 attacking a proposal to remove the grievant, and the removal was effective on December 30, the removal was not grieved. The removal was subject to Board jurisdiction unless there was a clear showing by the parties of an intention that the grievance also applies to the removal. Riddick v. OPM, 28 MSPR 581, 584 (1985) (on OPM motion for stay); Riddick v. OPM, 27 MSPR 590, 592 (1985) (no bar to a Board appeal when the removal decision was issued October 23, the grievance was filed on October 31, the effective date of removal occurred on November 2, and the Board appeal was filed November 22: "Her filing of...appeal was the first action...after the effective date of removal and constituted her timely election under section 7121(d).").

Before Kirkwood, the election to pursue a Board appeal rather than a grievance was demonstrated by the appellant who grieved before the effective date of the removal and then filed a timely Board appeal, followed by a withdrawal of the grievance conditioned upon the Board's acceptance of the appeal. Battle v. Dept. of Labor, 29 MSPR 15, 16 & n.1 (1985). But see Duncan v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3024) (withdrawal of a written grievance does not affect the election of a grievance over an MSPB appeal right); Jones v. DOJ, 53 MSPR 117, 120 (1992) (as to an appellant who filed a grievance after removal, later withdrawal of the grievance was ineffective to permit the Board to assume jurisdiction over the appeal; the grievance caused the election of remedies that could not be defeated by its withdrawal); cf. Szajkovics v. Dept. of Transp., 90 MSPR 643, 647 n.2 (2001) (reciting the reasoning of Jones, and noting the holding of Angel v. Bullington, 330 U.S. 183, 189 (1947), that "[i]f a litigant chooses not to continue to assert his rights after an intermediate tribunal has decided against him, he has concluded his litigation as effectively as though he had proceeded through the highest tribunal available to him"). The terms of the negotiated agreement were material in determining an employee's intent when pursuing a grievance under a negotiated agreement and in then determining the existence of Board jurisdiction. The intent to grieve a removal could be demonstrated if under the contract procedures the grievance must be filed within 20 days after receipt of the notice of decision to remove, a time that may not necessarily extend beyond the effective date of the removal. Riggs v. DOD, 48 MSPR 652, 657-58 (1991) (the effective date of removal stayed once the grievance was filed). A grievance concerning a proposed action ought not bar a Board appeal challenging a decision. The situation could change, however, if the grievance was expanded, amended, or treated to include a subsequent adverse action decision. See Anthony v. DOD, 13 MSPR 632, 635 (1982) (grievance protesting proposed removal was ineffective to preclude Board jurisdiction over a Board appeal from the removal decision when the appellant "has shown a preference for Board adjudication of the adverse action"); Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 15 MSPR 288, 290 (1983) (issuance of a notice of proposed removal is not appealable to the MSPB). The question may be one of the intent of the filer and scope of the filing. The issue crops up when a proposal is the subject of an EEO complaint and the decision is appealed to the Board. The scope of the EEO complaint, and any amendments or supplements, is considered.
