6. PFR Not Mooted by Overbroad Interim Relief

Agencies occasionally go overboard in their efforts to provide interim relief, and through 1996 they could be penalized in the process. Rather than protecting their right to file a PFR, they defeated it. Moscato v. Dept. of Educ., 72 MSPR 266, 270-72 (1996), ruled the Board would no longer automatically dismiss an agency's PFR when the agency in good faith inadvertently exceeded the requirements of an interim relief order (a modification later reflected in 1997 changes to the governing regulation, 5 CFR 1201.115(b)(1)):

The Board has consistently found that, where an agency exceeds the order for interim relief and cancels an action that is the subject of an appeal in compliance with the administrative judge's order for final relief, the matter is effectively removed from controversy, and the agency's petition for review is rendered moot. See, e.g., Kilgour v. VA, 67 MSPR 544, 548 (1995). This has been true regardless of the agency's intent. Kilgour, 67 MSPR at 548; see also, e.g., Archuleta v. Dept. of the Air Force, 59 MSPR 202, 205 (1993); Edney v. Dept. of the Treasury, 56 MSPR 248, 249-50 (1993).

Upon further consideration of this issue, however, we have decided that we will no longer dismiss an agency petition for review as moot where the agency has in good faith inadvertently exceeded the requirements of an order for interim relief. We find that the Board and prudent policy are ill-served by such an automatic dismissal, where the agency attempted to comply with an order of interim relief, mistakenly exceeded the Board's requirements, did not abandon its intent to go forward, and then took steps to correct its mistake in a timely manner.

In this regard, we note that appellate courts have declined to dismiss a case as moot even when one of the parties apparently provides relief or complies with a judgment, if the party does not intend to forego further legal proceedings. For example, in a land condemnation action, the government complied with a lower court judgment by paying the landowner the amount ordered. Although the government did not seek a stay of the judgment pending appeal, the appellate court found that there was no evidence that the government intended to make the payment as a final settlement of the landowner's claims, or that it intended to abandon its appeal. See United States v. 3,317.39 Acres of Land, 481 F.2d 417, 419 (8th Cir. 1973). The court found instead that the government made the payment, which it deposited on the same day that it filed its appeal, for the purpose of stopping interest from accruing. See id. Accordingly, it did not accept the landowner's contention that the government's action had rendered the appeal moot. See id. at 419. Similarly, in a case involving the improper discharge of a state employee, when the state "obviously did not intend to abandon its right to appeal," its payment of after-tax wages and other items to the employee did not render its appeal moot. See Barnes v. Bosley, 828 F.2d 1253, 1257 n.4 (8th Cir. 1987).

Here, the agency clearly did not intend to abandon its right to review. It consistently represented to the Board and to the appellant that it was attempting to comply with the Board's interim relief order, and that it was only providing relief pending a final decision by the Board on petition for review.

The United States Supreme Court has found that a case becomes moot when it can be said with assurance that there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur, and interim relief or events have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged violation. Los Angeles County v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 631, 99 S. Ct. 1379, 1383, 59 L. Ed.2d 642 (1979). In the current appeal, the relief provided to the appellant did not "completely and irrevocably" eradicate the effects of the underlying action, as the appellant did not receive full back pay. Moreover, the agency corrected its compliance error within a reasonably short time after making it, and provided the Board with evidence of this correction prior to the appellant's filing of the motion to dismiss, or the Board's issuance of a show-cause order.

Because the agency clearly sought only to provide interim relief, and soon corrected its error of providing more relief than that required by the administrative judge's interim relief order, we find that it is not necessary to issue a show-cause order to determine the agency's intent. Cf. Avant v. Dept. of the Navy, 60 MSPR 467, 473 (1994) (where, in response to the appellant's nonfrivolous allegation that the agency was not in compliance with the interim relief order, the agency submitted evidence and argument concerning its compliance before a show-cause order could be issued, it was not necessary to issue a show-cause order). We further find that the agency's good faith, inadvertent error in exceeding the requirements of the administrative judge's interim relief order does not render its petition for review moot. Accordingly, we DENY the appellant's motion to dismiss the petition for review as moot.

Our current interim relief regulations provide, in pertinent part: "Cancellation of the appealed action or relief effected retroactively to the date of the action will result in dismissal of the agency's petition [for review] for mootness." 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(1). This regulatory provision is contrary to the rule of law we announce today. To ensure consistency between our regulations and case law and to avoid confusion, pursuant to our authority under 5 CFR § 1201.12, we SUSPEND application of the last sentence of 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(1). See Hammond v. Dept. of the Navy, 37 MSPR 531, 535-36 (1988), vacated in part on other grounds, 50 MSPR 174 (1991). We will apply the rule we announce today, i.e., that an agency's petition for review will not be automatically dismissed as moot where the agency has in good faith and inadvertently exceeded the requirements of an interim relief order, in all cases relating to this regulation, and we will amend our regulations to reflect the new rule.

Luciano v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 441, 450 (1997), declined to dismiss the agency PFR when the appellant complained the agency restored him to his position effective four days prior to the issuance date of the initial decision. See Lavette v. USPS, 96 MSPR 239, 246 ¶ 14 (2004) (excusing the agency's failure to certify interim relief when it filed its cross-PFR when a subsequent certification showed that the agency met and exceeded its interim relief requirements), followed, Hardison v. Dept. of Navy, 103 MSPR 147, 2006 MSPB 240 (2006) (declining to dismiss agency PFR: "There are some indications that the agency may have exceeded the requirements of the administrative judge's interim relief order by canceling the appellant's removal altogether, and by effecting her demotion retroactive to the date of the removal rather than to the date of the initial decision. There also are indications that a mistaken belief by agency officials that these actions were required caused a delay in completing actions that in fact were required by the interim relief order."); Campbell v. USPS, 94 MSPR 646, 649-50 ¶ 6 (2003) (PFR not mooted when, in good faith and inadvertently, the agency exceed the interim relief order by paying back pay and interest to the appellant); Nanette v. Dept. of Treasury, 92 MSPR 127, 131 n.1 (2002) (applying Moscato); Carter v. DOJ, 88 MSPR 641, 645 n.1 (2001) ("By canceling the removal action and reinstating the appellant as of May 11, 1997, the agency exceeded the requirements of the interim relief order. The agency's actions in this regard, however, do not require dismissal of its petition for review.").

Before Moscato, a problem typically arose when, in response to the initial decision, the canceled the adverse action rather than directing interim relief. An agency's cancelation of an action could moot its PFR regardless of the agency's intent to effect interim relief. See Edney v. Dept. of Treasury, 56 MSPR 248, 249-50 (1993). In McElrath v. VA, 53 MSPR 569, 571 (1992), following an adverse initial decision, the agency issued an SF-50 canceling the demotion. The agency also restored the appellant effective the date of the demotion rather than the date of the initial decision. The agency essentially rescinded the adverse action. The controversy was moot; the agency's compliance with the initial decision essentially removed the matter from controversy. See Gevaert v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 65, 67 (1994) (agency mooted its own PFR by canceling an adverse action rather than supplying interim relief; the Board would not consider evidence of the agency's correction of its error submitted after the date allowed for the agency PFR); Cottman v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 362, 364 (1994) (PFR mooted when the agency attempted to provide interim relief but instead canceled a demotion as of its effective date; it was immaterial that the appellant claimed he was placed at step 2 of his grade rather than at step 4--that was a matter for enforcement); Cain v. Defense Commissary Agency, 60 MSPR 629, 631 (1994) (PFR mooted by overbroad interim relief: after the appellant successfully appealed the RIF, the agency canceled the demotion as of the date it was first imposed). The same result occurred in Moorer v. DOD, 53 MSPR 581, 584 n.2 (1992), the Board adding there that if the adverse action is canceled, the Board still retains jurisdiction to ensure restoration of the status quo, i.e., to examine any reassignment of the appellant out of his initial position.

Other decisions predating Moscato demonstrated that if the agency issued paperwork canceling the action as it attempted to confer interim relief, the cancelation mooted the PFR despite the subjective intention of the agency managers not to cancel the action. See Guidry v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 453, 455-56 (1994); Archuleta v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 202, 205 (1993) (agency canceled a removal that formed the basis of the appeal; the failure of the agency to provide back pay is an issue for enforcement rather than question of interim relief); Cooper v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 120, 121 (1993); Hunter v. VA, 55 MSPR 545, 547 (1992) (agency canceled the removal, retroactive to the effective date, removing the removal from controversy); O'Hara v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 234, 235 (1992) (agency mooted its PFR by canceling the removal retroactive to its effective date); Flowers v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 103, 106 (1992) (granting of a retroactive WIGI). In an appeal challenging an OPM-directed suitability-based removal, OPM managed to moot its own PFR when, having lost at the regional level, it directed the employing agency to place the appellant back on the job. By canceling the action at issue, OPM exceeded the requirements for interim relief and fully complied with the AJ's order for final relief. Brackins v. OPM, 60 MSPR 260, 262 (1993). [Refer later in this chapter to "Cancelation of Adverse Action; Agency Compliance With Initial Decision," on the effect upon PFRs of cancelation by the agency of the matter appealed.]

If the appellant complained of inadequate restoration through interim relief in a cross-PFR and the agency managed to moot its own PFR by canceling the action against the appellant, the Board remanded the appellant's cross-PFR to the region for adjudication as an enforcement petition. See Gevaert v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 65, 69 (1994); Lucas v. DOD, 58 MSPR 471, 473 (1993) (order to the agency concerning appellant's complaints about interim relief when the agency mooted its PFR by canceling the action instead of reinstating the appellant prospectively with a demotion ordered by the AJ). But if the appellant petitioned for enforcement of interim relief and if the underlying appeal was not moot, the Board treated the enforcement petition as a request to dismiss the agency's PFR for failure to provide evidence of interim relief. See Storm v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 14, 19 n.1 (1994). Even though the Board could remand a case for further adjudication, e.g., of an affirmative defense, it could retain jurisdiction over the appeal to the extent necessary to resolve an interim relief issue. See Russell v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 144, 149 (1995) (supplemental findings on interim relief and an order directing corrective action to provide proper interim relief).

If the AJ does not order interim relief and the agency provides it, the Board will not deny the agency's PFR for a technical error in interim relief that is provided. See Thompson v. Dept. of Air Force, 104 MSPR 529, 532 ¶¶ 7-8, 2007 MSPB 22 (2007). [Refer later in this chapter to "Technical Error; Supplemental Evidence of Interim Relief."]
