b. By MSPB

The Board may be accused of failing to consider mitigating circumstances. Who is charged with the responsibility of considering all the relevant Douglas factors? According to Kumferman v. Dept. of Navy, 785 F.2d 286, 291 (Fed. Cir. 1986), the primary responsibility is the agency's, subject to Board review:

Kumferman also argues that the Board abused its discretion in sustaining his removal because it failed to consider all of the factors set forth in Douglas.... It is not reversible error if the Board fails expressly to discuss all of the Douglas factors. Nagel v. Department of Health and Human Services, 707 F.2d 1384, 1386 (Fed. Cir. 1983). The Board need only determine that the agency considered the factors significant to the particular case. Hayes, 727 F.2d at 1540. Connolly v. Department of Justice, 766 F.2d 507, 514 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

The Federal Circuit expects, or assumes, that the Board considers mitigating factors, as the Board must, on penalty challenge by the appellant, to ensure the penalty is not unreasonable. Under Purifoy v. VA, 838 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (following remand, sustaining discharge, Purifoy v. VA, CH-0752-14-0185-M-1 (NP 5/16/2022))

Neither party challenges the sustained charges on appeal. Mr. Purifoy argues, however, that the Board's treatment of the mitigation analysis under the Douglas factors was inadequate. We agree. Even though the Board was required to consider each relevant Douglas factor, it failed to properly consider two factors relevant here. Specifically, the Board (1) did not consider the adequacy and effectiveness of alternative sanctions to deter similar misconduct in the future, and the Board (2) erred in its analysis of the potential for the employee's rehabilitation. See Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305-06.

The Board is to consider mitigating circumstances if the agency does not articulate them. See McClaskey v. Dept. of Energy, 720 F.2d 583 (9th Cir. 1983); Cavanaugh v. USPS, 34 MSPR 670, 674 (1987) (citing Sisemoore v. Dept. of Navy, 13 MSPR 560, 563 (1982)); cf. VanFossen v. DHUD, 748 F.2d 1579, 1581 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (Board's analysis of mitigation is erroneous if it does not consider relevant mitigating factors). If the agency position is that an offense automatically warrants a discharge penalty, the Board is reluctant to accept the argument that the agency seriously considered the Douglas factors. The Board then expects the AJ to closely examine the reasonableness of the penalty rather than deferring to the agency determination. See Parsons v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 438, 440-41 (1984). The Board is to consider mitigation evidence not earlier presented to a deciding official, although the Board need only consider relevant mitigating Douglas factors. Cf. Koester v. U.S. Park Police, 758 Fed. Appx. 925 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP) (remanding for arbitrator to consider evidence not earlier provided to the deciding official). Remanding to the Board, Bal v. Dept. of Navy, 729 Fed. Appx. 923, 929-30 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) (Judge Mayer dissenting), stated:

[T]he Board's final decision contains no analysis of Mr. Bal's arguments regarding Douglas factors seven (consistency of the penalty with any applicable agency table of penalties) and twelve (the adequacy and effectiveness of alternative sanctions to deter such conduct in the future by the employee or others). Although the Board expressed satisfaction that the Administrative Judge "reviewed the factors the deciding official considered in reaching her decision," Douglas factors seven and twelve, and the evidence regarding them, were not considered. The Board's mere statement that it considered all the relevant Douglas factors gives insufficient consideration of the evidence relevant to these additional mitigating Douglas factors. See Malloy, 578 F.3d at 1357 (remanding after finding that "[a]lthough the [Administrative Judge] stated that all of the Douglas factors were considered, the eleventh Douglas factor...is not mentioned and is not shown to have been considered").

Cf. Higgins v. VA, 955 F.3d 1347, 1356 (Fed. Cir. 2020) ("Here...both the agency's deciding official and the Board expressly considered Mr. Higgins's PTSD as a mitigating factor in assessing the reasonableness of his removal. Additionally, whereas Mr. Bal was 'neither a danger to himself or to others,'...Mr. Higgins presented a threat to other VAMC employees. And it is undisputed that unlike Mr. Bal, Mr. Higgins did not improve with continued treatment of his condition.").

Some agencies do not spell out the Douglas factors in the decision notices; some AJs do not provide extensive analysis of those factors in their initial decisions; and the Board picks and chooses among the factors it deems important to arrive at a result. If an agency or appellant argues strenuously for or against a particular factor identified as relevant in Douglas, the Board may dismiss the argument by saying that it is unconvinced that the factor is significant. The Board occasionally provides more extensive analysis, most often to justify the reversal of an AJ's mitigation of an agency penalty. Agencies arguing to the Board that AJs have overstepped their authority by mitigating penalties meet with some success. Appellants meet with some success when they demonstrate the agency officials did not carefully evaluate mitigating factors.

It has been argued in court that neither the employing agency nor the Board follows Douglas if the agency fails to specify how a given penalty determination was made and fails to recite the Douglas factors relied on by the agency, and if the Board then fails to state what effect it will give to the agency failure to articulate the basis for its penalty determination. If, under Douglas, the Board is supposed to defer to the agency decision on the penalty and if, under the Reform Act, the courts defer to the Board, the chain of deference is unfounded if the basic penalty decision lacks reasoned support. Despite this argument, the courts defer to the penalty determination without any apparent regard for the lack of reasoned analysis in the agency decision letter. The penalty determination is validated because the Board conducts a hearing and undertakes its own evaluation of the penalty, curing any earlier defect in the decisional process. See Trent v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 83-1317). This cure affords cold comfort to the appellant, for the Board created the elaborate deferral process because of agency expertise with its own operations. The Board may have been close to the mark: most AJs have come to the Board right out of law school or a few years in a government law office and function as hearing officers; they often have no managerial or supervisory experience. They are in no position independently to accurately assess the impact of a particular set of circumstances on an agency operation, the morale of coworkers, or the ability to maintain discipline. They rely on testimony from managers, the appellant, and others in the workplace with knowledge of the events and their impact. Assuming then that the doctrine of agency expertise is correct and forms a proper basis for deferral, it seems unwise to abandon deferral because the agency with supposed expertise has not done its job. This results in a penalty decision lacking the expertise deemed so important by the Board--a determination that goes to the heart of the adverse action case. It would be better either to require the agency to provide a reasoned decision regarding the penalty or, lacking that analysis, to reverse the agency determination and allow the agency to reinitiate the action to cure the fundamental defect in its decisional process. As will be seen from the discussion of the Ward decision that follows, penalty determinations are subject to due process considerations. The due process should be accorded to the employee by the agency.
