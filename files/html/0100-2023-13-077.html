d. Qualification for Higher-Graded Position

Entitlement to promotion through a Russell appeal assumes the appellant is qualified for the position sought through promotion. See Bittner v. NCUA, 76 MSPR 380, 383 (1997) (the appellant must meet legal and qualification requirements for promotion to a higher grade); Crum v. Dept. of Navy, 75 MSPR 75, 80-81 (1997) (summarizing cases). Physical or medical qualifications may be disputed, as in Walker v. Dept. of Navy, 106 F.3d 1582, 1584-85 (Fed. Cir. 1997), involving an appellant whose job was upgraded through a change in a classification standard at the same time the appellant was reassigned out of the job because he lacked physical qualifications to continue in the position:

Mr. Walker asserts that the second requirement of a constructive demotion claim does not encompass an employee's physical qualification for the position; instead, that requirement concerns only technical matters such as time in grade. He argues that it is inappropriate to make physical qualification a jurisdictional requirement because it deprives the petitioner of a hearing at which he could challenge the agency's action. We disagree with Mr. Walker's arguments.

The Board's decision in Russell, with which we agreed in Artmann, requires a petitioner making a constructive demotion claim to establish, inter alia, that the petitioner met "the legal and qualification requirements for promotion at the time of his reassignment." Russell, 6 MSPR at 711. There is no reason to believe that this requirement is limited to technical matters such as time in grade, and, indeed, Mr. Walker cites no legal authority for this proposition. An interpretation of "qualification requirements" that excludes physical capacities required by the position would be unduly narrow. All of the pertinent qualification requirements of any particular position must be met by one claiming a constructive demotion. Accordingly, we conclude that a showing of physical qualification is a component of this jurisdictional requirement.

Contrary to Mr. Walker's argument, such a requirement does not eviscerate an employee's right to challenge agency action taken on the basis of physical inability to perform. This requirement does not force the petitioner to prove conclusively, without the benefit of a hearing, that he was physically (or otherwise) qualified for the position. It simply requires the petitioner to respond to the jurisdictional challenge by making a nonfrivolous allegation of facts which, if true, would clearly establish the Board's jurisdiction....

The need for a security clearance in the position sought by the appellant may leave him unqualified for the position allegedly lost through a constructive grade reduction, and the Board will not second-guess the agency's determination that a clearance is necessary for the position. Solamon v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-0752-13-0180-I-1 (NP 5/6/2014).

Qualifications may be deemed conceded by the agency if the agency explicitly denies that it is challenging the appellant's qualifications or if, in response to discovery requests, the agency has not disputed the appellant's legal and qualification requirements for promotion. See Ellis v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 102, 108 (1997); cf. Hogan v. Dept. of Navy, 218 F.3d 1361, 1364-66 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (briefly discussing that appellant must be able to meet the physical qualifications requirement in a Russell-style appeal).
