c. Mitigation; Disparate Treatment

Information prepared for a deciding official, memoranda that might reflect consideration of mitigating factors, and internal reports that might lead to exculpatory evidence or other witnesses, are discoverable. Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 170, 175-76, 7 MSPB 649 (1981); cf. Bonds v. USPS, 38 MSPR 80, 83 n.3 (1988) (given the appellant's contention that his demotion resulted from his supervisor's unreasonable work demands and lack of interpersonal skills, discovery should have been permitted of documents concerning whether the supervisor was investigated or was the subject of disciplinary action involving his inability to deal with supervisors and coworkers).

Although in some cases discovery is proper as to disciplinary actions taken in other organizations or parts of the country to support a disparate treatment defense, the information will not be relevant if the removal decision is made regionally, even if an agencywide table of penalties is consulted and the regional official has consulted with central office officials. Yiakis v. GSA, 25 MSPR 661, 669 (1985). Discovery of disparate treatment information is limited to the appellant's organizational unit. Phillips v. GSA, 38 MSPR 206, 310 (1988); Mills v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 403, 407 (1986) (citing Carroll v. DHHS, 703 F.2d 1388, 1390-91 (Fed. Cir. 1983)). The Board's treatment of disparate treatment or comparable penalty defenses is, however, in a state of flux. [Refer to Chapter 7, "Disparate, Comparable Penalties."]

At times agencies seem reluctant to release information permitting appellants to compare penalties meted out to supervisors and unit employees for the same offense. Although there does not appear to be a Board case on point, noted here is a judicial decision involving an attempt by a union to secure that type of comparative information in an arbitration case. The court implied that, lacking some very cogent explanation, comparative information should be released. The Federal Labor Relations Authority ruled that the informational request was properly denied by management. The court, reversing and remanding for reconsideration, determined in North Germany Area Council, Overseas Educ. Ass'n v. FLRA, 805 F.2d 1044, 1047-49 (D.C. Cir. 1986):

The Authority's ruling seems to suggest that the standards of expected behavior for unit employees and managerial employees might differ. But the Authority offers no guidance for determining why, in this case, such standards differ. In support of its holding, the Authority does not cite any FLRA cases which hold that information on falsification by supervisors is irrelevant to the disciplining of unit employees for the same offense.

[I]t is for an arbitrator or deciding authority to take this factor into account when assessing what penalty, if any to impose on the grievant.... It is generally accepted in arbitration practice that enforcement of rules and assessment of discipline be exercised in a consistent manner unless a reasonable basis exists for the variation. Elkouri & Elkouri, How Arbitration Works, 684-85 (4th ed. 1985).

...Arbitrators routinely consider such evidence as relevant to determining whether a unit employee has been disciplined for just cause. See, e.g., Kast Metals Corp., 70 LA 278, 283-84 (Roberts 1978); Peerless Laundry Co., 51 LA 331, 335 (Eaton 1968); Potlatch Forests, Inc., 51 LA 685, 688 (Williams 1968); Lukens Steel Co., 42 LA 849, 850 (Crawford 1964); Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co., 39 LA 823, 825 (Turkus 1962).

...

[C]ases before the...MSPB suggest that agencies must establish consistent reasons for disciplining a particular employee more severely than other employees charged with the same offense.

[A]s cases before the MSPB suggest, the Authority has not explained adequately why supervisors might be held to a different standard of trust than are unit employees. If anything, supervisors might be held to an even higher standard of conduct because of the greater fiduciary nature of their duties....

An AJ properly denied a discovery request for records of all employees charged with AWOL and failure to comply with instructions but not removed. Baldwin v. USPS, 26 MSPR 383, 385-86 (1985). The request covered three years. The denial was warranted by the burden of obtaining the information and the lack of any evidence concerning racial, age, or disability discrimination other than the appellant's own allegations. Another AJ correctly limited discovery of information to two years prior to the incident when the requested material was about incidents similar to that involved in the appealed adverse action. No explanation was provided for the limitation other than "the appellant has not suggested any specific harm to him based on the time limitation." Boddie v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 607, 613 (1986).

In an appeal involving a last chance agreement, the Board commented briefly on attempts to discover other LCAs, Jacobs v. USPS, PH-0752-14-0736-I-1 (NP 7/10/2015):

Further, we find that the appellant's second discovery requests were not reasonably calculated to discover evidence relevant to the central issue in this appeal; namely whether he complied with the LCA as he asserts. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.72(b); see, e.g., DIRECTV, Inc. v. Puccinelli, 224 F.R.D. 677, 686 (D. Kan. 2004) (recognizing that some courts have held a party must make a heightened showing of relevance in order to discover information related to settlement agreements).
