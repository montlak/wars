a. Prolonged AWOL

Unauthorized absence from duty is a proper ground for removal. By its very nature, it disrupts the efficiency of the service. Prolonged absence with no foreseeable end is just cause for removal. Guerra v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-844); see Bailey v. VA, 10 MSPR 229, 230-01 (1982); Mason v. USPS, 9 MSPR 191, 193 (1981) ("There is no question that an employee absence for which no foreseeable end is in sight is a burden which no employer can efficiently endure."); Desiderio v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 84, 86, 4 MSPB 171 (1980) (rejecting an argument on PFR that the appellant's condition improved to a point that he could return to work: "[A]t the time of removal, the agency did not know appellant's condition could be remediated or that he would ever be a fully productive, dependable employee. Prolonged absence with no foreseeable end is deemed to be just cause for removal."); cf. Pake v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 436, 438 (1984) (although an agency cannot base adverse action on approved leave, an unauthorized absence can be grounds for removal since it disrupts service efficiency). Two months of intentional AWOL in the Postal Service constituted a serious impediment to the agency mission warranting removal and was not outweighed by 17 years of service. Hennessey v. USPS, 28 MSPR 127, 129-30 (1985); see Gerges v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 669, 683 ¶ 34 (2001) (sustaining removal of an employee who "had been absent from work continually for more than 8 1/2 months by the time his removal was proposed, had expressed his unwillingness to return to his position, and efforts to locate a suitable other position for him had been unsuccessful."); Staten v. USPS, 22 MSPR 238, 240 (1984) (sustaining removal of an employee, despite a good record of 25 years of service, for approximately two months of AWOL); Smith v. DLA, 15 MSPR 611, 613 (1983) (mitigation of removal to a nine-month suspension was inappropriate: "The agency should not be forced to carry appellant on its rolls for nine months when she has already been absent without excuse for 34 days and offers no evidence that she will be a dependable employee upon her return to work."). Forty-one hours of AWOL supported discharge when the absence accumulated during a period of a little over a month. The appellant's absences increased the costs to the agency for payment of overtime and shortages in other departments called on to supply substitute employees. The misconduct was not outweighed by 17 years of satisfactory service. Young v. USPS, 14 MSPR 549, 551 (1983). Two or three months of absence that should have been excused did not justify removal. There was no evidence, other than the specific period of illness, that there was a history of leave abuse or lack of dependability. Loots v. Dept. of Air Force, 42 MSPR 571, 577 (1989).

If an employee is absent for a prolonged and indefinite period, the proper practice is to charge the absence as AWOL only through the date of the letter proposing removal, not through the date of the final agency decision. Any absence that is not part of the current charge and occurs after the charge has been brought is not properly the subject of the removal action based on the charge. Allen v. VA, 38 MSPR 31, 33 (1988) (citing Smith v. Dept. of Labor, 25 MSPR 102, 103-04 (1984)).
