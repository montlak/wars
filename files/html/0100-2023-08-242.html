1. Postal Service Employees

Because 5 USC 7121 and its implementing regulation, 5 CFR 1201.3(c)(1) (2022), are inapplicable to the Postal Service as a result of the Postal Reorganization Act, 84 Stat. 719 (1970), preference eligible employees in the Postal Service have the right, under a process that may become analytically and practically complex, to appeal an adverse action to the Board, as well as the right to invoke the grievance provisions of a collective bargaining agreement described in 39 USC 1206(b) (describing requisites of USPS labor contracts). Lucas v. USPS, 39 MSPR 459, 461 (1989); see Young v. USPS, 26 MSPR 248, 249 (1985) (nonpreference eligible could not appeal to the Board in an effort to overcome an arbitration award). Hall v. USPS, 26 MSPR 233, 235-36 (1985), explained the statutory scheme:

The Postal Reorganization Act[1] (PRA) established the Postal Service as an independent establishment of the Executive Branch[2] with very limited application of federal employee law. Bredehorst v. United States, 677 F.2d 87, 88-89, 230 Ct. Cl. 399 (1982). Rather, the PRA was designed to bring postal labor relations within the same structure as exists in the private sector[3] and provided that employee-management relations were subject, to the extent not inconsistent with the PRA, to the provisions of the National Labor Relations Act, 29 USC § 151 et seq.[4]

[1] Pub. L. 91-375, 84 Stat. 719 (1970).

[2] 39 USC § 201 (1980).

[3] See 1970 U.S. Code Cong. & Ad.News 3649, 3662.

[4] See 39 USC § 1209(a).

Thus, the PRA provided methods for the resolution of labor disputes which incorporated grievance-arbitration procedures as determined by collective bargaining agreements.[5] Because certain rights were available to postal employees who were preference eligibles as defined by 5 USC § 2108(3) under the Veterans Preference Act of 1944, ch. 287, 58 Stat. 387, two types of adverse action procedures were created. Bredehorst, id. at 89. Under 39 USC § 1005(a)(1), non-preference eligible postal workers who are covered by a collective bargaining agreement, are limited to grievance procedures provided by the agreement. Winston v. United States Postal Service, 585 F.2d 198, 206 (7th Cir. 1978). Preference eligibles, however, are granted the right to appeal adverse actions listed in 5 USC § 7511(2) to the Board under 5 USC § 7512. Bredehorst, supra at 89. The preference eligible Title 5 rights are not derived from the Civil Service Reform Act; rather they are based on the PRA and the Veterans Preference Act. Pursuant to 39 USC § 410(a), federal labor laws are not applicable to the Postal Service absent a specific provision in the PRA or other statute. We find that no provision in the PRA makes 5 USC § 7121 applicable to the Postal Service. Likewise, 5 USC § 7121 is not by its own terms applicable to the Postal Service. Section 7101(a)(3) of Title 5 defines "agency" for purposes of Chapter 71 of Title 5 as an "executive agency." The Postal Service is not an executive agency; it is an independent establishment of the executive branch. 39 USC § 201. See 5 USC §§ 104-105. Thus, we find that 5 USC § 7121 and its implementing regulation, 5 CFR 1201.3(b)(1), are not applicable to the Postal Service.[6] Preference eligible employees in the Postal Service thus have the right to appeal an adverse action to the Board as well as the right to invoke the grievance procedures provided by the collective bargaining agreement specified in 39 USC § 1206(b). The collective bargaining agreement may provide that once an appeal to the Board is initiated, the right to elect the grievance procedure is waived.[7] Malone v. United States Postal Service, 526 F.2d 1099, 1103, 1105 6th Cir. 1975). The right to appeal to the Board, however, cannot be limited by the collective bargaining agreement. 39 USC § 1005(a)(2). Appellant is a Postal Service preference eligible and therefore is entitled by statute and regulation to appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board. See 5 USC § 7511(a)(1)(B), 5 USC § 7512(3), 5 USC § 7513(d), 5 USC § 7701, 5 CFR § 752.401(a), 5 CFR 752.405(a).

A nonpreference eligible Postal Service employee is limited to the remedies of the collective bargaining agreement. Banks v. USPS, 31 MSPR 307, 308 (1986).

The labor agreement may provide that once an appeal to the Board is initiated, the right to elect the grievance procedure is waived, but the right to appeal to the Board cannot be limited by the collective bargaining agreement. Hill v. USPS, 38 MSPR 339, 341-42 (1988) ("The appellant notes that the letter stated that he would waive access to any procedure beyond Step 3 of the grievance-arbitration procedure if he appealed to the Board. Under the standard set forth in Hall v. United States Postal Service, 26 M.S.P.R. 233, 236 (1985), however, the agency may limit an employee's use of the grievance procedure; it just may not limit his right to appeal to the Board. Thus, the appellant's mere disagreement with the administrative judge's conclusion on this issue does not provide a basis for Board review.").

Under Lucas v. USPS, 39 MSPR 459, 461-62 (1989), timeliness of the Board appeal is measured from the date of the agency's action. If a Postal employee is not covered by § 7121, his appeal to the Board, despite use of EEO and contract grievance procedures, must still adhere to the timeliness constraints normally applicable to those procedures. Shrider v. USPS, 26 MSPR 238, 239 (1985). The employee's pursuit of a grievance does not furnish good cause for delay of his Board appeal. See Garrett v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3401) (decision letter advised the appellant of the right to file a grievance and the right to file an MSPB appeal, but if the appellant pursued both, an MSPB appeal might affect grievance rights; the letter did not state or imply that filing a grievance would excuse an untimely MSPB appeal); Mitchell v. USPS, CH-0752-13-1706-I-1 (NP 7/10/2014) ("As the administrative judge found, the appellant's election to pursue his grievance through the negotiated grievance procedure before filing his appeal does not constitute good cause for waiving the time limit for filing a Board appeal. Nahoney v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 93, ¶ 14 (2009)."); cf. Zuhlke v. USPS, 74 MSPR 401, 404 n.3 (1997) ("[A]ny provision in the collective bargaining agreement limiting his right to pursue the negotiated procedure based on his having filed a Board appeal does not directly affect the Board's jurisdiction."). [Refer to Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases," and in this chapter, "Effect of EEO Allegation or EEO Complaint," for the timing of Board appeals when the EEO process is first invoked as to the matter ultimately appealed to the Board.]

A postal employee may not await the outcome of prior, related adverse action before filing an appeal to the Board from a more recent adverse action. See Muse v. USPS, 82 MSPR 342, 344-46 ¶¶ 3-11 (1999) (while appellant was carried on employer's rolls during processing of grievance challenging his AWOL-based removal, he was issued another removal for different misconduct; he should have timely appealed the AWOL removal rather than awaiting disposition of the later removal). Assuming that the case does not involve an EEO complaint, the appeal must be filed within the usual 30 [formerly 20] days from the event leading to the appeal; filing a grievance does not toll the period for the Board appeal. Moten v. USPS, 42 MSPR 282, 285-86 (1989) (appellant received notice of Board appeal rights and time limits in the decision notice; it was not required that the agency grievance decision also advise him of those rights); see Moore v. USPS, 91 MSPR 277, 280-81 ¶ 8 (2002) (remand to determine the timeliness of an EEO complaint). A postal employee who pursues both a grievance and a Board appeal may decide to unconditionally withdraw the Board appeal, and once that is done that appeal may only be reinstated in unusual circumstances, e.g., when there has been misinformation or where there is new and material evidence. See Brakas v. USPS, 90 MSPR 332, 336 ¶ 12 (2001), aff'd, Brakas v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-3068). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Withdrawal of Appeal."]

USPS, 58 MSPR 555, 559-60 (1993):

Appeals of Postal Service employees are not covered under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(d), which implements 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) and allows an appellant who files under negotiated grievance procedures, and who raises issues of unlawful discrimination in connection with a matter otherwise appealable to the Board, to seek Board review of a final arbitration decision within twenty-five days of the issuance of the arbitration decision. See Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 57 M.S.P.R. 57, 59 (1993). We also find that the appellant's pursuit of his grievance does not constitute good cause for his untimely filing. See Lewis, 47 M.S.P.R. at 232.

If the Postal Service employee ends up with both an arbitration award and a timely Board appeal, the Board addresses the weight of the award. See Jackson v. USPS, 57 MSPR 57, 59 (1993) ("The Board has held, however, that 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) does not apply to Postal Service employees with Board appeal rights who may file both a grievance and a Board appeal at the same time. Lucas v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 M.S.P.R. 459, 461 (1989); Hall v. U.S. Postal Service, 26 M.S.P.R. 233, 236 (1985). Correspondingly, the Board may only review Postal Service arbitration decisions within the context of a timely filed appeal of the agency action."). The Postal preference eligible employee, like an ordinary appellant, files an appeal directly with the regional office of the Board (unlike other employees who use arbitration and whose Board appeal rights are limited to review by the Board's headquarters establishment through a review request filed with the Board's Clerk). Under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.157, the presiding official in an appeal of a decision under the procedures in 5 U.S.C. § 7121 shall be an administrative law judge, the Board, or a Member of the Board."). Williams v. USPS, 35 MSPR 581, 587 (1987) (on PFR from initial decision).
