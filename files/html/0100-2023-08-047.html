1. Lack of Jurisdiction; Failure to State a Claim

"An appeal that is within the Board's jurisdiction may be dismissed for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted if the appellant cannot obtain effective relief before the Board even if his allegations are accepted as true. Alford v. Department of Defense, 113 M.S.P.R. 263, ¶ 11 (2010), aff'd, 407 F. App'x 458 (Fed. Cir. 2011)." Beyers v. Dept. of State, DC-3330-11-0538-I-1 (NP 1/30/2012) (same); see Strickland v. DHS, PH-3330-12-0536-I-1 (NP 7/19/2013) ("Dismissal for failure to state a claim is appropriate if, taking the appellant's allegations as true and drawing all reasonable inferences in his favor, he cannot prevail as a matter of law. Patterson v. Department of the Interior, 424 F.3d 1151, 1160 (Fed. Cir. 2005); Haasz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 108 M.S.P.R. 349, ¶ 8 (2008)."); Grey v. DOD, 102 MSPR 458, 461 ¶ 7, 2006 MSPB 204 (2006) ("[a]n appeal within the Board's jurisdiction may be properly dismissed for failure to state a claim if the appellant cannot obtain effective relief before the Board even if his allegations are accepted as true."); cf. Boston v. Dept. of Army, 122 MSPR 577, 581 ¶ 7, 2015 MSPB 47 (2015) ("The Board has the authority to decide the merits of a VEOA appeal without a hearing if there is no genuine dispute of material fact and one party must prevail as a matter of law. Haasz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 108 M.S.P.R. 349, ¶ 9 (2008); see 5 C.F.R. § 1208.23(b)."). Whether allegations are sufficient to state a claim within the Board's jurisdiction requires analysis of the type of case--USERRA or IRA appeal, constructive removal through resignation or retirement that was coerced or the product of misleading information. [Refer in this chapter to discussion of USERRA, VEOA, and restoration appeals; to Chapter 6 for constructive adverse actions; to Chapter 13 for appeals arising from reprisal for protected activity.]

Jurisdictional analysis is never far from the surface of an appeal, Vaughn v. USPS, DC-0752-11-0964-I-1 (NP 11/8/2012):

Moreover, because the Board has limited jurisdiction based on statutory and regulatory grants, the Board must satisfy itself that it has authority to adjudicate any matter that comes before it. Metzenbaum v. General Services Administration, 96 M.S.P.R. 104, ¶¶ 1, 15 (2004). The issue of jurisdiction is thus always before the Board and may be raised by either party or by the Board sua sponte at any time during a proceeding. Lovoy v. Department of Health & Human Services, 94 M.S.P.R. 571, ¶ 30 (2003). For instance, the Board will even consider an argument implicating the Board's jurisdiction raised for the first time on review, without a showing that the argument is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite due diligence. Id.

Further, an agency is not estopped from shifting its position on jurisdiction, nor do equitable considerations relieve the appellant of her burden of proof. See Reedy v. U.S. Postal Service, 84 M.S.P.R. 453, ¶ 4 (1999) ("[T]he agency cannot be estopped from arguing for the first time on petition for review that the Board lacks jurisdiction, even if this position is contrary to the agency's position below."); Shaw v. Department of the Navy, 39 M.S.P.R. 586, 593 (1989) (the Board's "jurisdiction is statutorily and regulatorily-based, and does not arise from any inherent equitable powers federal courts may have"), overruled on other grounds, Joyce v. Department of the Air Force, 74 M.S.P.R. 112 (1997); Kaapana v. Department of the Interior, 1 M.S.P.R. 556, 557 (1980) (the Board is "without authority either to broaden or narrow its appellate jurisdiction through the exercise of...'inherent power'").

If the Board lacks jurisdiction over an appeal, it does not review the allegations to determine whether a claim is stated. The distinction between the Board's treatment of adequacy of jurisdiction and adequacy of a claim was explained in Spruill, supra, and in a USERRA/VEOA appeal, White v. USPS, 114 MSPR 574, 578-79 ¶ 11, 2010 MSPB 172 (2010):

To the extent that the appellant failed to establish jurisdiction over her appeal, dismissal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted was not a proper disposition. See Goldberg v. Department of Homeland Security, 99 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 10 (2005) (where the appellant failed to establish that she exhausted her administrate remedy with DOL, the proper disposition was dismissal for lack of jurisdiction--not dismissal for failure to state a claim); Young v. Federal Mediation & Conciliation Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 99, ¶ 5 (2002) ("An appeal that is within the Board's jurisdiction can be dismissed, without a hearing, for failure to state a claim upon which relief can [be] granted if the appellant cannot obtain effective relief before the Board even if his allegations are accepted as true.") (emphasis added), aff'd, 66 Fed. Appx. 858 (Fed. Cir. 2003). Whether the appellant stated a claim upon which relief can be granted goes to the merits of her case, Goldberg, 99 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 10; see Lackhouse v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 773 F.2d 313, 316-17 (Fed. Cir. 1985), and the Board cannot assume for purposes of analysis that all jurisdictional requirements have been met and resolve a case on the merits, Schmittling v. Department of the Army, 219 F.3d 1332, 1336-37 (Fed. Cir. 2000); Goldberg, 99 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 10 n.2. If the appellant establishes jurisdiction over her appeal on remand, the administrative judge may revisit the issue of whether the appellant has stated a claim upon which relief can be granted. A dismissal for failure to state a claim upon which relief may be granted is based solely on the appellant's allegations, to the exclusion of any record evidence. See Haasz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 108 M.S.P.R. 349, ¶ 8 (2008) ("Disposing of a claim in favor of a defending party, without an evidentiary hearing, and based on matters beyond the claimant's allegations is summary judgment, not dismissal for failing to state a claim upon which relief may be granted."); Williamson v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 9 n.* (2007) ("Because we are considering the documentary evidence, dismissal for failure to state [a] claim upon which relief may be granted would be inappropriate."); Young, 93 M.S.P.R. 99, ¶ 5 (a VEOA appeal should be dismissed for failure to state a claim only if "it appears beyond doubt that the appellant can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief").

Peterson v. VA, 116 MSPR 113, 116-17 ¶¶ 6-7, 2011 MSPB 38 (2011), a whistleblower reprisal (IRA) case, amplified upon White, supra:

On appeal below, the administrative judge found that the appellant failed to establish jurisdiction over her IRA appeal. However, he dismissed the appeal on the ground that "the appellant has failed to state a claim of [sic] upon which relief can be granted." A dismissal for failure to state a claim was not the proper disposition for several reasons. First, whether the appellant stated a claim upon which relief can be granted goes to the merits of her case, and the Board cannot assume for purposes of analysis that all jurisdictional requirements have been met and resolve a case on the merits. White v. U.S. Postal Service, 114 M.S.P.R. 574, ¶ 11 (2010). The Board must determine whether it has jurisdiction over the appeal prior to proceeding to the merits. Schmittling v. Department of the Army, 219 F.3d 1332, 1336-37 (Fed. Cir. 2000). Thus, the administrative judge's determination that the appellant failed to establish Board jurisdiction over her appeal is at odds with his dismissal of the appeal on the merits.

Second, it was inappropriate for the administrative judge to dismiss the appeal for failure to state a claim when he relied upon his review of the appellant's evidentiary submissions and concluded, "I find that it is unclear from the record, if any, protected disclosures the appellant may have made." Dismissal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted is based solely on the appellant's allegations, to the exclusion of any record evidence. White, 114 M.S.P.R. 574, ¶ 11; Haasz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 108 M.S.P.R. 349, ¶ 8 (2008). Disposing of a claim in favor of a defending party, without an evidentiary hearing, and based on matters beyond the claimant's allegations is summary judgment, not dismissal for failing to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. Haasz, 108 M.S.P.R. 349, ¶ 8. Based on the foregoing, we find that the administrative judge erred in dismissing this appeal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. See id.

Dept. of Air Force, SF-3330-13-1464-I-1 (NP 9/23/2014) ("We also note, without making any jurisdictional findings, that the administrative judge's determination that the Board lacks jurisdiction is inconsistent with her finding that the appellant's VEOA appeal fails to state a claim upon which relief can be granted.
