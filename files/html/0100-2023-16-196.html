b. Work Monitoring

An examination does not include undercover telephone surveillance, dependent on the employee not knowing of the surveillance. There is no direct questioning by management, whose role is that of a passive listener rather than participant in the conversations. Requiring notice to the employee would destroy the effectiveness of the surveillance and directly interfere with management's internal security practices. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Jacksonville Dist. and NTEU, 23 FLRA 876, 880 (1986). The right to union representation does not extend to surveillance through telephone monitoring as an investigative technique. Dept. of Treasury, BATF and NTEU, 24 FLRA 521, 524 (1986), aff'd, NTEU v. FLRA, 835 F.2d 1446, 1449 (D.C. Cir. 1987). FLRA explained, however, in BATF, involving an employee who was instructed to relay questions to another employee during a monitored conversation:

The Agency placed Ms. Then in a very awkward situation in order to obtain her cooperation in the investigation of Ms. Franken. Although the Agency's actions did not violate the Statute, we encourage the Agency and others in like circumstances to seek other means, whenever possible, to accomplish their internal security and law enforcement functions in the future. It is our view that actions which foster employee management relations based on trust and cooperation are preferred for the most effective and efficient conduct of the Agency's business.

On appeal, the D.C. Circuit commented in the BATF case, NTEU v. FLRA, 835 F.2d 1446, 1447 (D.C. Cir. 1987):

This case presents a narrow issue: did the questioning of one federal employee under disciplinary investigation, by another federal employee at management's behest, abridge the questioned employee's right under 5 U.S.C. § 7114(a)(2)(B) to have a union representative present during interrogation, even though the questioned employee was unaware that the questions were devised by management and that certain responses might result in disciplinary action or criminal penalties?

...We agree with the FLRA's conclusion, although we phrase the analysis somewhat differently. We hold that, although an examination occurred, the questioned employee had no right to union representation during interrogation because she could not reasonably have believed that her responses might lead to disciplinary action against her. Therefore, under the plain terms of the statute, no violation can be found....
