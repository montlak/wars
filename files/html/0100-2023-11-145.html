1. Objection, Motions to Strike or in Limine

Evidence may be excluded by objection or, once submitted and tentatively accepted (as, for example, documents submitted with an appeal or response to an appeal), by a motion to strike, a procedure discussed in Calhoun v. Bailar, 626 F.2d 145, 149 (9th Cir. 1980). Another procedural device used to exclude evidence is the motion in limine, ordinarily made before the hearing. A motion in limine is a request to the AJ for an advance ruling that a certain type of evidence will not be admitted, e.g., in a whistleblower case testimony would not be admitted concerning racially discriminatory hiring practices. The motion is used when there is good reason to believe that the opposing party will seek to introduce the evidence that is the subject of the motion. The motion saves hearing and hearing preparation time because it narrows the scope of the hearing. The same evidence could, however, be excluded through use of objections made during the prehearing conference or during the hearing. If no objection is made to admission of evidence at a hearing, the appellant is precluded from making the objection in a PFR. See Kent v. SSA, DE-0752-16-0391-I-1 (NP 10/27/2022) ("The appellant did not object below to the authenticity of the recordings, and his failure to lodge such an objection below precludes him from doing so on review. See McCarthy v. International Boundary & Water Commission, 116 M.S.P.R. 594, ¶ 25 (2011) (holding that the appellant's failure to timely object to rulings during the hearing precluded him from doing so on petition for review), aff'd, 497 F. App'x 4 (Fed. Cir. 2012).").

The Board's Hearings Procedures Guide (still used by the Board for training and orientation although it has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook) provides some guidance on the motion, at 33-34:

1. Motions in limine

This type of motion is designed primarily to keep a jury from hearing prejudicial information that they will not be able to disregard.

For guidance on this issue, AJs should consult the comments to Fed. R. Evid. 105, Limited Admissibility, and Fed. R. Evid. 403, Exclusion of Relevant Evidence on Grounds of Prejudice, Confusion or Waste of Time.

a. Procedures

The motion may be made orally or presented in writing. The movant will typically be the appellant, seeking to have potentially prejudicial material excluded in advance of introduction.

On occasion, an agency may make the motion in an effort to keep the appellant from mentioning issues of national security, or disclosing details of ongoing criminal investigations, or revealing the terms of a settlement offer.

To ensure that the record is complete, the moving party should be asked to give an offer of proof as to the general nature of the evidence he is seeking to have excluded. The opposing party must then be given the opportunity to either deny that it intends to introduce such evidence, or argue that the challenged material (testimony or documentary evidence) is relevant and material to the issues before the AJ.

Under 5 CFR § 1201.62, only evidence that is irrelevant, immaterial or unduly repetitious should be excluded. Thus, if the proffered evidence is not excludable under the Board's regulations, the prudent course is to admit it into the record, and assign it only the weight that it deserves in the decision making process.

However, remember that "marginally useful information is not merely useless; it is positively harmful because it inflates the record which the parties, the Judge and then the [reviewing authority] must examine." Union Stockyard Co. v. United States, 308 U.S. 213, 223-24 (1939).
