b. Burden of Proof; Proof of Compliance

The burden of proof when a compliance issue is presented was explained, Heath v. USDA, 111 MSPR 434, 436 ¶ 5, 2009 MSPB 107 (2009):

An agency bears the burden of proving its compliance with a final Board order, and compliance must be supported by relevant, material, and credible evidence in the form of documentation or affidavits. See New v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 106 M.S.P.R. 217, ¶ 6 (2007); Donovan v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 628, ¶¶ 6-7 (2006), review dismissed, 213 Fed. Appx. 978 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (Table); Brownlow v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 223, ¶ 7 (2001). The appellant may rebut that evidence by making specific, nonconclusory, and supported allegations to the contrary. See New, 106 M.S.P.R. 217, ¶ 6; Donovan, 101 M.S.P.R. 628, ¶ 7.

The burden and order of proof were restated, Chen v. USPS, SF-0353-13-0150-C-1 (NP 4/29/2016):

When the Board finds that an appellant has been the victim of an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action, it orders that the appellant be placed, as nearly as possible, in the situation she would have been in had the personnel action not occurred. King v. Department of the Navy, 100 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 12 (2005), aff'd per curiam, 167 F. App'x 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006). The agency bears the burden to prove compliance with the Board's order. Vaughan v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 319, ¶ 5 (2011). An agency's assertions of compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance actions supported by documentary evidence. Id. The appellant may rebut the agency's evidence of compliance by making specific, nonconclusory, and supported assertions of continued noncompliance. Id.

See Brown v. DOD, SF-0752-13-0336-C-1 (NP 5/15/2015) ("In proceedings on a petition for enforcement of a Board order, the agency bears the burden of proving that it has complied with the final order. Lua v. Office of Personnel Management, 100 M.S.P.R. 431, ¶ 8 (2005). Mere assertions of compliance are insufficient to meeting this showing; rather, the agency must produce documentation or affidavits showing compliance. Id. The appellant must offer evidence in the form of documentation or affidavits in order to rebut the agency's claim of compliance. Spates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 438, 443 (1996)."); Noppenberger v. USPS, PH-0752-11-0216-X-1 (NP 8/12/2014) ("When the Board finds a personnel action unwarranted or not sustainable, it orders that the appellant be placed, as nearly as possible, in the situation he would have been in had the wrongful personnel action not occurred. House v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 530, ¶ 9 (2005). The agency bears the burden to prove its compliance with a Board order. An agency's assertions of compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance actions supported by documentary evidence. Vaughan v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 319, ¶ 5 (2011). The appellant may rebut the agency's evidence of compliance by making 'specific, nonconclusory, and supported assertions of continued noncompliance.' Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 113 M.S.P.R. 325, ¶ 5 (2010)."); Robledo v. USPS, NY-0752-07-0236-X-1 (NP 7/30/2014) ("An agency's assertions of compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance actions supported by documentary evidence. Vaughan v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 319, ¶ 5 (2011). The appellant may rebut the agency's evidence of compliance by making 'specific, nonconclusory, and supported assertions of continued noncompliance.' Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 113 M.S.P.R. 325, ¶ 5 (2010).").

Once enforcement proceedings are initiated after a Board final decision, the agency has the burden of proof to demonstrate compliance, and the agency must, if there is a compliance issue, submit evidence of actions completed, a statement of actions in process and actions remaining, and a reasonable schedule for full compliance. 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1)(ii) (2022). See Woodson v. USDA, 94 MSPR 289, 292 ¶ 6 (2003) ("It is well settled...that an agency's evidence of compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance efforts supported by understandable documentary evidence."); Popichak v. USPS, 88 MSPR 623, 625 ¶ 5 (2001) ("The burden is on the agency in an enforcement proceeding to establish compliance with a final Board order."); Payne v. USPS, 82 MSPR 298, 301 ¶ 8 (1999) ("In proceedings on petition for enforcement of a final Board order, the agency bears the burden of proving that it has complied with the order."); Vargo v. USPS, 78 MSPR 66, 73 (1998) ("In proceedings on petition for enforcement of a Board order, the agency bears the burden of proving that it has complied with the final order."); Welber v. USPS, 76 MSPR 601, 604 (1997) ("Here, the appellant has raised a specific issue concerning an element of the back pay to which he is entitled under the Board's orders. The agency therefore has an obligation to produce relevant evidence which is in its possession."); Welber v. USPS, 76 MSPR 601, 603 (1997) ("It is the agency's burden to establish that its actions are in full compliance with the Board's orders."); Spaulding v. USPS, 63 MSPR 612, 615 (1994) ("The agency bears the burden of proving that it is in compliance with a final Board decision."), overruled on other grounds by Mattern v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 65, 72 ¶ 16 (2001); Rodewald v. OPM, 59 MSPR 332, 334 (1993) (AJ erred by placing the burden of proving compliance on the appellant; it was harmless error because the record showed that the agency complied with the final decision); Zuniga v. USPS, 56 MSPR 572, 575 (1993) ("The burden is on the agency to prove that it has complied with the Board's final decision."); Gonzales v. Dept. of Treasury, 46 MSPR 75, 78 (1990) (burden is on agency to submit to the Board proof of compliance with its order); Rickels v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 596, 601 (1989) (agency has the burden of proof to show compliance with Board order; appellant has the burden of proof to show noncompliance with a Board-accepted settlement); Dacey v. USPS, 36 MSPR 198, 200 (1988); cf. Thomas v. OPM, 95 MSPR 695, 698 ¶ 7 (2004) ("OPM bears the burden of establishing that it has complied with the Board's final order by properly calculating and providing the benefits owed to the appellant."); Henderson v. OPM, 92 MSPR 383, 387-88 ¶¶ 9, 10 (2002) (burden of demonstrating compliance with a Board order extends to OPM in annuity determination cases). Evidentiary burdens in compliance cases can be met through stipulations. See Doe v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 198, 200-01 ¶¶ 5-6 (2003).

Once the agency meets the compliance challenge, the appellant is obliged to come up with specific rather than generalized objections to the agency's explanation of how compliance was achieved. See Foster v. USPS, AT-0353-12-0677-X-1 (NP 8/15/2013) ("On April 26, 2013, the agency submitted a statement and evidence purporting to show full compliance....On June 13, 2013, the Clerk's Office ordered the appellant to respond to the agency's evidence of compliance by June 20, 2013. The order advised the appellant that if he failed to do so, the Board might assume he was satisfied and dismiss the petition for enforcement. The appellant did not respond to the June 13, 2013 order. Accordingly, we assume he is satisfied with the agency's evidence of compliance; find the agency compliant; and dismiss the petition for enforcement."); Bowden v. USPS, 95 MSPR 505, 508 ¶ 4 (2004) ("However, in the face of an agency's evidence of compliance, to show noncompliance an appellant must make more than unspecific and unsupported general allegations."); Payne v. USPS, 82 MSPR 298, 301 ¶ 8 (1999) (specific explanations by the agency concerning compliance were not countered when the appellant "merely conclusorily alleges that the agency is still not in compliance"); Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 62 MSPR 212, 217 (1994) (back pay and overtime disputes). [Refer later in this chapter to "Enforcement and Interpretation of Settlement Agreements" and "Burden of Proof."]

After the Board resolves legal and factual issues presented by an enforcement petition, the Board will instruct the employee's agency to calculate and pay the employee the amount of back pay that may be due as a result of the Board's decision. Harmon v. VA, 25 MSPR 340, 342 n.* (1984). It is a final Board order that provides the predicate for an enforcement petition. If the AJ's initial decision was vacated by a subsequent Board order and remand, the Board will not entertain enforcement proceedings because there is no final order. See Flaherty v. USPS, 68 MSPR 637, 638 (1995).

Issues of compliance with adverse action decisions issued by arbitrators present a problem. The General Counsel of the Federal Labor Relations Authority takes the position that an agency commits an unfair labor practice, cognizable by the FLRA, if it does not obey an arbitrator's adverse action reinstatement order. Army Adjutant Gen. Publication Ctr., FLRA G.C. No. 7-CA-40064 (3/31/84). This view appears to have been adopted by the FLRA. DOJ, Bureau of Prisons and AFGE Local 3882, 22 FLRA 928 (1986); Army Adjutant Gen. Publications Ctr. and AFGE Local 2761, 22 FLRA 200 (1986). The Board lacks the authority to enforce arbitration awards. See Kotwitz v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3386). If the appellant grieves an adverse action and obtains mitigation but does not get back pay, the Board does not have jurisdiction to order the agency to award back pay, since the appellant's entitlement arises from an arbitration award and not from a Board decision. See Blackiemore v. USPS, 65 MSPR 424, 426 n.* (1994). These cases suggest that compliance with an arbitrator's award may be achieved through a ULP charge filed with the FLRA. A compliance issue may also be addressed through grievance arbitration. If an arbitration award is appealed to the Board based on EEO issues raised during the arbitration process, the Board will issue a final order on the review request, and that order could then be subject to Board enforcement proceedings if the final Board decision provides affirmative relief. These choices present problems of some complexity and, beyond noting them, they are outside the scope of our discussion. [Refer to Chapter 5 for appeals to the Board from arbitration awards in litigation raising EEO issues; refer to Chapter 8 of Broida's A Guide to Federal Labor Relations Authority Law and Practice (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2023).]

The parties may settle their compliance disputes and submit a settlement agreement to the Board for approval and enforcement. The Board accepts the agreement if the underlying dispute is within the Board's jurisdiction, the agreement is lawful on its face, the agreement recites that the parties have voluntarily entered into the settlement, and the terms of the agreement demonstrate that the parties intend the settlement to be accepted into the record and enforced by the Board. Moore v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 414, 415-16 (1992) ("We find that the subject matter of the settlement, the petition for enforcement, is within the Board's jurisdiction. See Richardson, 5 M.S.P.R, at 251. In addition, we find the written agreement to be lawful on its face. Its execution by the parties documents that they voluntarily entered into a settlement agreement and that they understood its terms. See Perham v. Office of Personnel Management, 45 M.S.P.R. 551, 553 (1990). Further, we find that the terms demonstrate that the parties intend the settlement to be accepted into the record and enforced by the Board."); Gay v. USPS, 51 MSPR 29, 30 (1991) (settlement agreement arising out of an enforcement petition will be accepted by the Board if it appears that it was reached freely and is lawful).

Agencies are responsible for processes within their control. The employing agency will not be held in noncompliance because OPM has not quickly completed a recomputation of the appellant's annuity, if the appellant does not demonstrate that his agency caused OPM's delay. See Carpenter v. DHHS, 36 MSPR 496, 504 (1988).

The Board is supposed to take care of its own compliance problems. Existence of a Claims Court suit does not deprive the Board of jurisdiction to enforce its orders and award back pay. Williams v. DLA, 27 MSPR 393 (1985) (Table).
