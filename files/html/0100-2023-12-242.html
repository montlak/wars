a. Request for Supreme Court Review

If the Federal Circuit remands a case and the government seeks Supreme Court review of the circuit decision, the Board is not inclined to grant a stay. The Board implied that the stay should come from the Federal Circuit, Pitsker v. OPM, 89 MSPR 252, 254-56 ¶¶ 2-6 (2001):

The Board may exercise its discretion to stay the enforcement of a decision pending judicial review or reconsideration. The Board determines whether to grant a stay after considering four criteria: (1) whether the stay applicant has made a strong showing that he will prevail on the merits; (2) whether the applicant will be irreparably harmed absent a stay; (3) whether the issuance of the stay will substantially harm the other parties interested in the proceeding; and (4) where the public interest lies. The Board balances the likelihood of success on appeal or reconsideration with the last three criteria. If support for a stay based on the last three criteria is slight, however, the Board will grant a stay only if the possibility for success on appeal or reconsideration is strong. The Board will not address the first criterion if the applicant fails to demonstrate any support for a stay based on the last three criteria. See, e.g., Rogers v. Office of Personnel Management, 77 M.S.P.R. 626, 628 (1998); Caponio v. Department of the Treasury, 73 M.S.P.R. 243, 246 (1997).

We note initially that OPM has addressed none of the criteria against which a stay request is generally measured. There are no arguments with regard to whether OPM would prevail on the merits or whether OPM would suffer harm absent a stay. Likewise, OPM did not address the third consideration of whether other interested parties would suffer harm, and we note that the appellants have objected to further delaying the implementation of the court's ruling that they are entitled to enhanced benefits (they indicated that Pitsker retired in 1989 and Rogers retired in 1993). OPM also did not consider where the public interest lies on this matter. The lack of argument on these factors alone would be a sufficient basis upon which to deny the agency's stay request. See, e.g., Sawyer v. Office of Personnel Management, 73 M.S.P.R. 174, 176 (1997) (even assuming that there is a strong possibility of success on reconsideration, an agency's stay request will still be denied when there is no support for the last three criteria).

The posture of this case, however, does not precisely fit into the category of cases where a judicial review or reconsideration is pending. Upon the appellants' petitions to the court, the Federal Circuit reviewed the Board's decisions and found them to be based on an error in the interpretation of the governing statute. Pitsker v. Office of Personnel Management, 234 F.3d at 1379. Because the Board could not calculate the appellants' retirement annuities at a higher rate, it had to remand the case to OPM to correct the retirement annuities at issue. OPM, in asking the Board to stay the proceedings, technically is asking the Board for a stay of the court's decision. However, it is well settled that decisions of the Federal Circuit constitute precedent that is binding on the Board. See, e.g., Fairall v. Veterans Administration, 33 M.S.P.R. 33, 39, aff'd, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987). Thus, because the matter is no longer pending before the court, and because the court's decision is binding on the Board, we find that the Board is without any discretionary authority to stay the enforcement of the order during the period of time when the Department of Justice is considering whether to file a petition for issuance of a writ of certiorari.

For the following reasons, we also find that the Board lacks the authority to consider the stay request even if the Justice Department were to file a petition with the Supreme Court. Stays pending the disposition of an application for certiorari are governed by 28 U.S.C. § 2101(f), which provides, in pertinent part:

In any case in which the final judgment or decree of any court is subject to review by the Supreme Court on writ of certiorari, the execution and enforcement of such judgment or decree may be stayed for a reasonable time to enable the party aggrieved to obtain a writ of certiorari from the Supreme Court. The stay may be granted by a judge of the court rendering the judgment or decree or by a justice of the Supreme Court.

The great weight of authority holds that section 2101(f) does not authorize an inferior tribunal considering a case on remand from a court of appeals to stay the case pending the Supreme Court's decision on a petition for certiorari challenging the court of appeals' decision.... Only the court of appeals or a justice of the Supreme Court may grant a stay pending a decision on certiorari. Id. See also Magnum Import Company v. Coty, 262 U S. 159, 163-64, 43 S. Ct. 531, 67 L. Ed. 922 (1923) (a stay pending a decision on certiorari should be sought first from the court of appeals, and then from the Supreme Court). These cases recognize that the inferior tribunal's stay of the remand proceedings would effectively stay the mandate of the court of appeals. Mister, 680 F. Supp. at 298; Hovater, 669 F. Supp. at 393. They also recognize that the court of appeals is in the best position to determine the appropriateness of a stay, particularly when the court of appeals overturned the decision of the lesser tribunal. Mister, 680 F. Supp. at 298. See also Magnum Import, 262 U.S. at 163-64, 43 S. Ct. 531 (the court of appeals appropriately determines whether to grant a stay pending certiorari in the first instance).

Cf. Murphy v. USPS, 89 MSPR 436, 438 ¶ 2 (2001) (declining to place a case "in abeyance" pending Supreme Court review of the adverse decision of the Federal Circuit).
