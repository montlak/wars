19. Reduction in Force

Carter v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 393 (1994), reversed a determination in Carter v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 321 (1993), that a RIF (and a refusal by the agency to waive qualification requirements), leading to separation from the service, was not among the personnel actions enumerated in 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A). The 1994 Carter decision determined the separation resulting from the RIF was properly subject to an IRA appeal, 62 MSPR at 398-400:

Under 5 USC § 1221, an employee, former employee, or applicant for employment may seek corrective action from the Board regarding any "personnel action" which is proposed or taken against him or her as a result of whistleblowing as described at 5 USC § 2302(b)(8). Section 2302(a)(2)(A) describes the personnel actions covered under section 2302(b)(8). A reduction in force is not specifically listed among the personnel actions set out in that provision. However, the Board has not, in the past, deemed this list exhaustive and has treated a reduction-in-force action as a covered personnel action.

A reduction in force is a method of fulfilling management's responsibilities for planning work programs and organizing the workforce to accomplish agency objectives within available resources. Federal Personnel Manual Supplement (FPM Supp.) Chap. 351-1, section 1-2a. See Huber v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 793 F.2d 284 (Fed. Cir. 1986). A RIF occurs when employees are released from their competitive levels because of a lack of work, shortage of funds, insufficient personnel ceiling, reorganization, exercise of reemployment or restoration rights, and reclassification of a position under narrowly defined circumstances. 5 CFR § 351.201(a)(2). Employees are released from their competitive levels through separations from employment, furloughs for more than 30 days, demotions, and reassignments. Id. Unlike an adverse action, a RIF may not be aimed at removing a particular individual, but must be directed solely at eliminating a position. See Cobb v. Department of Labor, 774 F.2d 475, 477 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Grier v. Department of Health and Human Services, 750 F.2d 944, 945 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

In most cases, an agency need only establish that the RIF was taken for bona fide management reasons in order for the action to be sustained. See Anderson v. Department of Defense, 48 MSPR 388 (1991). However, an otherwise bona fide RIF affecting several employees becomes invalid with respect to a specific individual when he or she is included in the RIF solely for reasons which are personal to the employee. Gandola v. Federal Trade Commission, 773 F.2d 308, 312 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

If an otherwise bona fide RIF is aimed at removing a particular individual, the action would not be directed solely at eliminating positions. Rather, the separation, demotion, or furlough pursuant to the RIF would be in the nature of an adverse action, i.e., a removal, reduction in pay or grade or suspension within the meaning of 5 USC § 7512. See Gandola, 773 F.2d at 312; Fitzgerald v. Hampton, 467 F.2d 755, 758, 759 (D.C. Cir. 1972). Because an adverse action is a personnel action set out at section 2302(a)(2)(A)(iii), i.e., an action under Chapter 75, the Board would have the authority to adjudicate such an appeal.

Accordingly, we find that a RIF action that is taken for reasons personal to an individual employee results in a personnel action in the nature of those covered by 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(iii). Accord, Gandola, 773 F.2d at 312 (a reduction in force may not be used as a disguised adverse action); Losure v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 2 MSPB 361, 2 MSPR 195 (1980) (a RIF conducted for improper motives of any kind is, in effect, an adverse action). Of course, an appellant must present more than a conclusory allegation that the implementation of the RIF was personal to him to invoke the Board's jurisdiction over the matter as an adverse action. See Wagner v. Environmental Protection Agency, 53 MSPR 300 (1992).

Here, the parties stipulated to the bona fides of the RIF. However, the appellant alleged that his position was targeted for separation by reduction in force as the result of his protected whistleblowing disclosures. He listed several disclosures which he asserted were protected whistleblowing activities under the WPA. Notably, the appellant alleged that, in 1989, he made protected disclosures to the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) during OSHA's investigation of health and safety violations in the appellant's division. An action taken because of whistleblowing activities is personal to an individual and thus is not taken for one of the legitimate management reasons for invoking a RIF set out at 5 CFR § 351.201(a)(2). We find that the appellant has raised a nonfrivolous allegation of facts which, if proven, would establish that the implementation of the reduction in force was personal to him and thus in the nature of an adverse action.

The legislative history of the Whistleblower Protection Act indicates that the intent of Congress was to provide a broad bulwark to protect federal employees from any reprisal following disclosures of waste, fraud, abuse or threats to public health and safety through good faith whistleblowing. The legislative history also establishes that Congress was aware that whistleblowers faced a variety of retaliatory actions and sought to protect them from any retaliation:

Federal employees are faced with extraordinary pressures not to expose waste and mismanagement by agencies. Those who do "blow the whistle" may be threatened with on-the-job harassment, negative job ratings, unfavorable transfers, denial of promotions, and even dismissal.

We will send a strong, clear signal to whistleblowers that we intend to protect them from any retaliation related to their whistleblowing and an equally clear message to those who would discourage whistleblowers from coming forward that reprisals of any kind will not be tolerated.

135 Cong. Rec. S2779, 80 (daily ed. March 16, 1989) (statements by Sen. Levin). In the past, the Board has not restrictively interpreted the meaning of "personnel action" as set forth at section 2302(a)(2)(A). Thus, in Marren v. Department of Justice, 50 MSPR 369 (1991), the Board determined that an agency's denial of a request for annual leave was a personnel action because the denial concerned a benefit under section 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix). Similarly, the appellant in this case has asserted that the agency's failure to waive qualification requirements resulted in his separation from federal service, rather than obtaining a position. Because the agency's decision resulted in the appellant's loss of pay, we find that it indisputably concerned pay or benefits. Thus, the agency's failure to waive qualification requirements was a personnel action within the meaning of 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix).

Schmittling v. Dept. of Army, 92 MSPR 572, 578 ¶ 15 (2002), determined that an IRA case properly encompassed the allegation that the agency reassigned another employee to a position in order to block the assignment of the appellant to that position during a RIF. Moran v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 77, 87 (1994), and Marchese v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 104, 107 (1994), noted that if reprisal is alleged in a RIF case, a determination must be made whether the reprisal would qualify for consideration under the WPA's contributing factor analysis, or whether the higher burden of proof under the CSRA and Warren v. Dept. of Army, 804 F.2d 654, 656-58 (Fed. Cir. 1986), applies.

An appellant was not sufficiently threatened with a personnel action to invoke IRA jurisdiction when the agency and the union were engaged in negotiations after the agency "informally proposed abolishing the appellant's position and realigning him to a position with a lower grade." Niswonger v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 655, 657 (1994), following remand from Federal Circuit review proceedings, Niswonger v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 665 (1994) (no violations). Under Special Counsel v. Spears, 75 MSPR 639, 667 (1997), a recommendation to place an employee on a "decrement list," leading to eventual ranking of positions were there budget reductions controlled by others, was not a recommendation "sufficiently decisive to constitute the taking of a personnel action."

Predating the 1994 Carter decision, Dean v. Dept. of Army, 57 MSPR 296, 306 (1993), determined that the threat to abolish the appellant's position did not constitute a threatened personnel action within the ambit of the WPA:

We find that the threatened abolishment of the appellant's position does not constitute the threat of a personnel action for purposes of the Whistleblower Protection Act. The "personnel actions" that are appealable to the Board are codified at 5 USC 2302(a)(2). See Weber v. General Services Administration, 54 MSPR 444, 445 (1992). The abolishment of a position is not among the specific personnel actions enumerated in the statute. The statutory provision does include a "catchall provision" covering "any other significant change in duties or responsibilities which is inconsistent with the employee's salary or grade level." See 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(x). However, the wording of this provision does not encompass an abolishment of a position. The Board has rejected the assertion that any order or action made in reprisal for whistleblowing activities is appealable in an IRA action, even if it does not fall within the actions specified in 5 USC 2302(a)(2).

Moreover, even assuming that the appellant's position is subsequently abolished pursuant to a reduction in force and that this would constitute a personnel action under the statute, we do not find that the threatened abolishment of the appellant's position is itself a personnel action. The Board has declined to find that it may assert jurisdiction over any stage in the stream of proceedings leading to a personnel action, as opposed to the personnel action itself. It has found that to do so would be to extend its reach beyond the congressionally mandated limits set forth in 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A). See Slake v. Department of Treasury, 53 MSPR 207, 215-16 (1992)....
