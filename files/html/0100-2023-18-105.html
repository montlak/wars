6. Proposing and Deciding Official Confer; Same Proposing and Deciding Official

Another straightforward situation involves the proposing and deciding officials conferring without the knowledge or consent of the appellant. Assume no vendetta. Before the Civil Service Reform Act, Civil Service Commission regulations required that the final decision concerning an adverse action be made by a higher-ranking official than the proposing official. Regulations implemented after the Reform Act eliminated that requirement. Most of the decisions that follow in this section predate the Federal Circuit's 1999 Stone and subsequent Ward decisions, and the earlier decisions of the Board must be considered against the subsequent analysis by the Federal Circuit. [For decisions involving discussions between proposing and concurring officials in Chapter 43 performance cases, refer in this chapter to "Performance Cases."]

In cases decided before Stone and Ward, aside from the types of prohibited involvement of individuals who were adversaries to the employee, described by Camero, Ryder, Sullivan, and similar decisions, there was no general proscription against communication by one involved in the agency decisional process with a deciding official once a personnel action began, unless there was a showing that the deciding official considered any new charges or specifications not listed in the notice of proposed removal. Neither the Civil Service Reform Act nor its implementing regulations prohibited the same agency official from participating in both preparation of a proposal notice and making a final decision to remove an employee. Carr v. DOD, 61 MSPR 172, 177-78 (1994) (no per se statutory or regulatory prohibition existed against ex parte communications between the proposing and deciding officials or other officials or persons during the agency decisional process; the appellant did not show that the decision to remove him was made by someone other than the deciding official); Masood v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 399, 402 (1991) ("[n]o statute or regulation prohibits ex parte communications between the proposing and deciding officials and any other officials or persons during the agency's decisionmaking process, and...such communications are proper absent a showing that they are prohibited by statute, Office of Personnel Management regulation, or agency internal regulation."); Brennan v. Dept. of Treasury, 31 MSPR 571, 574 (1986) (no violation of law for the deciding official to direct the proposing official to sign a proposal notice (citing DeSarno v. Dept. of Commerce, 761 F.2d 657 (Fed. Cir. 1985))); Pinto v. Dept. of Labor, 11 MSPR 422, 425 (1982) (the law does not prohibit the same agency official participating in both the preparation of a proposal notice and making the final decision to remove an employee). The rule applies to both Chapter 75 (5 USC 7513) and Chapter 43 (5 USC 4303(b)(1)(D)(ii) (calling for higher-level concurrence with a proposal)) procedures. Chappelle v. Dept. of Labor, 27 MSPR 352, 354 (1985) (no evidence that the deciding official considered any new charge or specifications, that the proposing official was "out to get" the appellant, or that the proposing official retaliated for the appellant's filing of a grievance over a performance rating). No harmful error occurred when the proposing and deciding official (neither of whom necessarily qualifies as an "adversary") conferred before, but not after, the adverse action commences. Sweeney v. IRS, 21 MSPR 675, 678 (1984). No harmful error resulted from the proposing official telling the deciding official of the action he was going to take against the employee. Malone v. DHHS, 6 MSPR 209, 211-12, 6 MSPB 184 (1981); Vick v. USPS, 5 MSPR 392, 5 MSPB 407 (1981) (citing Sibert v. DHEW, 4 MSPR 41, 44 (1980), for the proposition that "ex parte communications between the deciding official and other agency officials involved in the adverse action are not prohibited by the regulations implementing the Civil Service Reform Act.").

In an appeal decided before Stone and Ward, Crumpton v. SSA, 6 MSPR 479, 482-83, 6 MSPB 405 (1981), rejected a defense based on allegedly improper communications between the deciding official and others, including the proposing official:

Appellant's next contention is that the deciding official, Mr. J. C. White, had pre-judged his case because of his communications with both Hooks and the proposing official, and should, therefore, have disqualified himself as the deciding official. At the hearing, Hooks testified that following the May 20 meeting he discussed the reassignment of appellant with White and they agreed that a reassignment would probably be best for appellant. The proposing official, Mr. Joe Fondaw, testified that White initially made him aware of the situation following White's discussion with Hooks. He also testified that he informed White of his decision to propose appellant's demotion prior to issuing the notice.

Since the proposed and final agency decisions may be issued by the same person, there is no violation of appellant's procedural rights resulting from communications between the proposing and deciding officials in a case where the agency has adopted a two-stage decision making process not required by statute or regulation. Sibert v. Department of Health, Education, and Welfare, 4 MSPB 132, 4 MSPR 41 (1980).

Similarly, no violation of appellant's procedural rights resulted because of the communications between the deciding official and Hooks. Appellant's petition for review merely alleges that because Hooks spoke negatively of appellant to White, White was incapable of rendering an impartial decision. Appellant did not provide any evidence to support this assertion. The Board notes that the agency decision met the OPM regulatory requirements. The deciding official considered only the reasons specified in the notice of proposed demotion and carefully considered the arguments contained in appellant's written response. 5 CFR § 752.203(e).

Similarly, Miller v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 102, 105 (1981), declined to fault an agency for discussions between proposing and deciding officials:

The petition also contains extensive discussion of certain communications among the agency official who proposed appellant's removal, the official who made the final decision on that action, and the respective superiors of those two officials. Appellant believes that these communications, by which those officials concurred in the charges and penalty proposed against him, were prejudicial ex parte discussions which rendered the decision to remove him a fait accompli when the Notice of Proposed Removal was issued. In support of this argument appellant cites Camero v. United States, 375 F.2d 777, 179 Ct. Cl. 520 (1967), and United States Civil Service Commission Board of Appeals and Review Case No. BR752B40385 (February 22, 1974). However, at issue in both of those cases, unlike the instant case, were ex parte communications between the employing agency's deciding official and the person who had previously represented the agency at an adversarial trial-type hearing on the merits. Thus, in those cases, the ex parte communications deprived appellant of an impartial hearing. The appellant received such a hearing before our presiding official. Moreover, there was no representative trial-type setting at the agency level and, inasmuch as the proposal and final decision may be issued by the same person, it cannot be said that appellant's opportunity to reply was jeopardized by communications between the proposing and deciding officials, and their respective superiors, in a case where the agency has adopted a two-stage process not required by law. Sibert v. Department of Health, Education and Welfare, 4 MSPB 132, 4 MSPR 41 (1980).

The Board restated its view of the law, pre-Stone and Ward, in Gordon v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 322, 324 (1987):

The Board has held that there is no statutory or regulatory prohibition on ex parte communications between the proposing and deciding officials or any other agency officials or persons during the agency's decision-making process. See Andersen v. Department of State, 27 MSPR 344, 348 (1985). Even where an appellant is able to show that these ex parte communications involved the transmission of new information or allegations to which he did not have the benefit of responding, the agency's action will not be reversed unless the appellant demonstrates that his ability to defend against the action was impaired thereby; that is, that he was harmed by the agency's procedure. To do so, the appellant must establish that the deciding official's consideration likely had a harmful effect upon the outcome before the agency. Andersen, 27 MSPR at 349.

A proposing and deciding official may be the same, unless a contrary agency regulation exists. Teichmann v. Dept. of Army, 34 MSPR 447, 449 (1987), aff'd, Teichmann v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3552), followed in Botto v. USPS, 75 MSPR 471, 475 (1997); see Robinson v. VA, 923 F.3d 1004, 1022 (Fed. Cir. 2019) ("we have previously noted that '[a]t the pretermination stage, it is not a violation of due process when the proposing and deciding roles are performed by the same person.' DeSarno v. Dep't of Commerce, 761 F.2d 657, 660 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see also Holton v. Dep't of Navy, 884 F.3d 1142, 1150 (Fed. Cir. 2018). Thus, the fact that Mr. Gibson was both the proposing official and the deciding official does not show that Mr. Robinson did not receive pretermination due process."). The same person may direct an investigation into misconduct, initiate a proposal, and issue the removal decision. Hanley v. GSA, 829 F.2d 23 (Fed. Cir. 1987). The investigating officer may serve as the proposing official. See Hidalgo v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 645, 654 ¶ 16 (2003). Under DeSarno v. Dept. of Commerce, 761 F.2d 657, 660 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

At the pre-termination stage, it is not a violation of due process when the proposing and deciding roles are performed by the same person. The law does not presume that a supervisor who proposes to remove an employee is incapable of changing his or her mind upon hearing the employee's side of the case....

Nor does any Civil Service law or agency regulation support the present allegations of harmful procedural error.... [n]o statute, regulation, policy, directive, Board decision, or court decision [requires] an agency to assign separate persons to perform the proposing and deciding roles.

In decisions predating Stone and Ward, because the proposal and final decision could be issued by the same person under Chapter 75, Davis v. FAA, 39 MSPR 470, 478-79 (1989), there was no violation of an appellant's procedural rights by communications between the proposing and deciding officials after the adverse action process commences, even if the agency had a two-stage decisionmaking process not required by statute or regulation. Cross v. VA, 16 MSPR 429, 431 (1983); McGhee v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 122, 125 (1982) ("Where an agency has adopted a two-stage decision making process not required by statute or regulation, no violation of an appellant's procedural rights results from communications between the proposing and deciding officials. Sibert v. Department of Health, Education, and Welfare, 4 MSPB 132 (1980)."); see Belanger v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 304, 309 (1983) ("The Office of Personnel Management regulations governing adverse actions contain no requirement that the deciding official be a different individual than the proposing official."); cf. Sibert v. DHEW, 4 MSPR 41, 43, 4 MSPB 132 (1980) (appellant did not show harmful error when the deciding official did not disqualify himself after he had been the deciding official in an earlier action suspending the appellant). If the deciding official showed that he exercised independent judgment, the result would not be set aside for harmful error despite the suggestion that the deciding official might not have come to the same conclusion had a superior come to a contrary conclusion; the two officials were of the same opinion concerning the adverse action. Fontes v. Dept. of Transp., 51 MSPR 655, 668 (1991).
