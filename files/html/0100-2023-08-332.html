c. Related EEO Violation, Prohibited Personnel Practice, Other Challenges

An individual who brings a VEOA appeal may not tack on an EEO claim. According to Ruffin v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 396, 400-01 ¶¶ 11-12 (2001) (following remand, 93 MSPR 369 (2003) (agency found the appellant ineligible to compete for the position), aff'd, Ruffin v. Dept. of Treasury, 148 Fed. Appx. 938 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP)):

Under the Veterans Employment Opportunities Act of 1998 (VEOA), Pub. L. No. 105-339, 1998 U.S.C.C.A.N. (112 Stat.) 3182, a preference eligible who alleges that an agency has violated his rights under any statute or regulation relating to veteran preference, and who has exhausted his rights under that section before DoL, may file an appeal with the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(1), (d). The matter at issue in a VEOA appeal, however--like that at issue in an IRA or USERRA appeal--is not whether a particular personnel action is proper and should be sustained. The provisions of VEOA give the Board no authority to adjudicate the merits of any personnel action. Instead, they authorize the Board only to determine whether an agency has, in connection with the action that is the subject of an appeal, violated a statutory or regulatory provision relating to veteran preference. See 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(1)(d). VEOA does not expressly describe or otherwise limit the actions the Board is authorized to take if it determines that there has been a substantive violation; however, the Board has jurisdiction over claims arising under VEOA only after those claims have been reviewed by DoL, the other agency with authority to review VEOA claims, and DoL has only limited authority to correct actions at issue in these allegations. That is, VEOA authorizes DoL to attempt, on finding a violation, "to resolve the complaint by making reasonable efforts to ensure that the agency specified in the complaint complies with applicable provisions of statute or regulation relating to veterans' preference." 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(c)(1). We see no basis for finding that the Board has the authority to order actions correcting violations of provisions unrelated to veteran preference when DoL clearly does not have that authority. Cf. Bodus, 82 M.S.P.R. at 514-15 (Board noted, in concluding that it had no jurisdiction to review age discrimination claims in USERRA appeals, that its remedial authority in such appeals was limited and could be exercised only on basis of USERRA violations).

For the reasons stated above, we conclude that VEOA does not provide the Board with authority to review the merits of any personnel action, and that it instead provides the Board only with the authority to determine whether an agency has violated a statutory or regulatory provision relating to veteran preference. In other kinds of appeals in which the Board's authority is similarly limited, i.e., in IRA and USERRA appeals, the Board has no authority to adjudicate claims of discrimination covered under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1). See Metzenbaum, at 8-9; Bodus, 82 M.S.P.R. at 514-17 (1999); Marren, 51 M.S.P.R. at 638-42. Nothing in any statutory or regulatory provisions relevant to this case suggests that the Board has the authority to review such claims in VEOA appeals. Accordingly, we hold that the Board has no authority to review, in a VEOA appeal, a claim of discrimination covered under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1). On remand, the administrative judge shall not address the merits of the appellant's claim of discrimination based on age or disability.

Light v. SBA, 208 Fed. Appx. 819, 821 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP), endorsed the Board's position declining to permit an EEO claim to be added to a VEOA appeal:

We also agree that the Board lacked jurisdiction to entertain Light's discrimination claim. The Board lacks jurisdiction over a discrimination claim except where it is accompanied by an adverse action claim over which the Board does have jurisdiction. See 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1); Cruz v. Dep't of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1245 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc). Non-selection for employment is not an independently appealable action under 5 U.S.C. § 7702. See Prewitt v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 133 F.3d 885, 886 (Fed. Cir. 1998). Because the Light's discrimination claims are not otherwise appealable, and because the provisions of VEOA give the Board no authority to adjudicate the merits of any personnel action; rather, they authorize the Board only to determine whether an agency has violated a statutory or regulatory provision relating to veteran preference, the Board may not employ VEOA to attain jurisdiction over Light's discrimination claim. See 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(1)(d); See Metzenbaum v. Gen. Servs. Admin., 96 M.S.P.R. 104, ¶ 5 n.3 (M.S.P.B. 2004), aff'd, 122 Fed. Appx. 476 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (unpublished). Accordingly, because the Board has no authority to review, in a VEOA appeal, his claim of discrimination, Light has failed to satisfy his burden of showing that the Board has jurisdiction over his non-selection.

See Jones v. DHHS, DE-3330-12-0338-I-2 (NP 9/15/2014) ("VEOA authorizes the Board to determine only whether an agency, in connection with the action that is the subject of an appeal, has violated a statutory or regulatory provision relating to veterans' preference. Villamarzo v. Environmental Protection Agency, 92 M.S.P.R. 159, ¶ 5 (2002). The citizenship and age of the individuals selected for a position are not material to that issue and, therefore, the administrative judge properly did not address these matters in the initial decision. Moreover, to the extent that the appellant is attempting to raise an age discrimination claim, it is well settled that the Board has no authority to adjudicate discrimination claims in connection with VEOA appeals. See Ruffin v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 396, ¶ 12 (2001).").

Collective bargaining contract violations are not adjudicated in a VEOA appeal, Barnett v. USPS, AT-3330-15-0453-I-1 (NP 3/9/2016):

To the extent the appellant also challenges the CBA's provisions that supposedly preclude him from being considered for the Full-time Regular Mail Handler position, we agree with the administrative judge that VEOA does not grant the Board the authority to address the appellant's allegations that the agency violated the CBA when it failed to consider him for a Full-time Regular Mail Handler position. Ruffin v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 396, ¶ 11 (2001) (explaining that VEOA does not grant the Board authority to consider violations of provisions unrelated to veterans' preference appeals). Accordingly, we conclude that the appellant has provided no basis upon which to disturb the initial decision.

Roesel v. Peace Corps, AT-4324-10-0726-I-1 (NP 3/3/2011), determined that an alleged prohibited personnel practice in violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(9) cannot be adjudicated with a VEOA claim:

In addition to his VEOA claim, the appellant claimed that the agency violated 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(9), presumably because he had filed prior Board appeals on similar grounds. The Board lacks jurisdiction under the VEOA to consider this claim, however, and the appellant has not alleged any otherwise appealable action under which the Board may exercise jurisdiction. See Slater v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 28, ¶ 8 (2009). The appellant, however, may bring such a claim before the Office of Special Counsel, which is charged with investigating allegations of prohibited personnel practices. See 5 U.S.C. § 1214.

Daiello v. Dept. of Army, DC-3330-10-0784-I-1 (NP 5/13/2011), declined to consider in a VEOA appeal, an assertion that a decision to hire another individual was in violation of statutory restrictions:

The appellant also argues that the agency committed a prohibited personnel practice by hiring a retired member of the armed forces in a Department of Defense civilian position during the 180-day period immediately after his retirement in violation of 5 U.S.C. § 3326. Absent a viable VEOA claim or other appealable action, the Board cannot consider the appellant's allegations of prohibited personnel practices. Haasz, 108 M.S.P.R. 349, ¶ 10; Davis v. Department of Defense, 105 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 16 (2007). Further, to the extent that the appellant argues that 5 U.S.C. § 3326 is a statute relating to veterans' preference, he has not made a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency violated his rights under a statute relating to veterans' preference, and the Board lacks jurisdiction over such a claim. See Jones v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 385, ¶ 9 (2010); Letchworth v. Social Security Administration, 101 M.S.P.R. 269, ¶ 7 n.5 (2006).

Of an allegation of preselection, Miller v. Dept. of Navy, PH-3330-13-0042-I-1 (NP 11/27/2013), determined:

Finally, the appellant reasserts on review his claim that the agency committed a prohibited personnel practice (PPP) by preselecting candidates to fill the position in question... .Thus, the administrative judge erred by failing to address the appellant's PPP claim. Nevertheless, the administrative judge's error is not prejudicial to the appellant's substantive rights; thus it provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984). Specifically, the Board cannot obtain jurisdiction over the appellant's PPP claim through the VEOA, and the appellant has not alleged any otherwise appealable action under which the Board may exercise jurisdiction. See Slater v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 28, ¶ 8 (2009); Coster v. Department of Agriculture, 103 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 9 (2006). Accordingly, the administrative judge correctly denied the appellant's request for corrective action.
