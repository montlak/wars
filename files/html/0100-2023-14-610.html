broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 19: Critical Elements and Performance Standards, Section C: Broad Criteria, Subsection 02: Relating Standards to Position Description and Assigned Duties

2. Relating Standards to Position Description and Assigned Duties

Within the duty to bargain are proposals limited to requiring consistency between position descriptions and performance standards, requiring that standards be established in accordance with applicable laws, requiring negotiation over the manner that employees participate in establishing standards to the extent it would not prevent an agency from establishing standards, prohibiting secret studies bearing on standards, requiring that studies be conducted on average workers under normal working conditions, and requiring that information derived from studies be provided to the union within a specified time. AFGE Local 1940 and USDA, Plum Island Disease Ctr., 16 FLRA 816, 820 n.6 (1984) (nonnegotiable: Proposal 2, but stating what could be negotiated); see ACT Roughrider Chap. and DOD, N.D. NG, 56 FLRA 256, 257 (2000) (Proposal 3 negotiable: permitting employee to grieve content of performance standard that does not accurately reflect actual duties performed); AFGE Council of Prison Locals and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 47 FLRA 836, 841-44 (1993) (Provision 2 negotiable: standards and elements as applied be fair and based on objective criteria and job-relatedness); UPTO and Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs, Walla Walla, 44 FLRA 1145, 1155-56 (1992) (negotiable portion of Proposal 2: standards be "reasonably related" to duties in position descriptions). A proposal was negotiable that required that work based on the words "other duties as assigned" shall not be used for performance appraisal. It did no more than require specification of duties in a position description before those duties could be used to assign performance standards. IFPTE Local 25 and Dept. of Navy, Mare Island Naval Shipyard, 13 FLRA 433, 435 (1983).

NTEU and USDA, Food and Nutrition Serv., W. Region, 42 FLRA 964, 979-80 (1991), summarized the law governing provisions concerning consistency of position descriptions with performance standards as it discussed proposals that:

(3) They [performance elements] will be consistent with the duties and responsibilities contained in the employee's position description.

(4) They will be consistent with the grade level of the position.

Of these FLRA explained, 42 FLRA at 981-82:

Turning to subsection 3, the Authority has previously found that proposals that only require consistency between position descriptions and performance elements, like subsection 3, do not restrict an agency's choice of performance elements. For example, FNS, Midwest Region, 25 FLRA at 1071-74, affirmed as to other matters sub nom. National Treasury Employees Union v. FLRA, 848 F.2d 1273 (D.C. Cir. 1988). The Authority has found that under such proposals an agency can achieve the required consistency by amending the position description and that the right of an agency to direct employees and assign work through establishing performance elements and standards remains unaffected, subject to the procedural requirement that the position description involved must accurately reflect the work assigned. Id. Such proposals are negotiable procedures under section 7106(b)(2). Id. For the reasons expressed in conjunction with Proposal 4 in FNS, Midwest Region, we conclude that subsection 3 of Provision 5 is negotiable.[5]

[5] Using phrasing similar to that contained in subsection 3, subsection 4 requires that performance elements be consistent with the grade level of the position. However, grade level is fundamentally different from the contents of a position description and, consequently, changing a grade level is not merely procedural in nature as is amending a position description. "Grade level" can entail matters such as position classification. See, for example, Social Security Administration and American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1923, AFL-CIO, 31 FLRA 933 (1988). It can also involve management's right to determine its organization under section 7106(a)(1). See, for example, American Federation of Government Employees, Local 32, AFL-CIO and Office of Personnel Management, 26 FLRA 452, 455-57 (1987). Also, there are regulatory requirements that govern an employee's eligibility for promotion to a different grade level and that could put significant limitations on an agency's ability to change an employee's grade level. See, for example, 5 C.F.R. §§ 300.601-300.605 (time-in-grade restrictions); 5 C.F.R. § 335.104 (eligibility for career ladder promotion).

A proposal was negotiable that standards and critical elements be consistent with responsibilities contained in a properly classified position description. Management could always revise the position description to achieve the requisite consistency. The agency's rights to direct and assign work and create standards and elements were unaffected. AFGE Local 2849 and OPM, New York Reg'l Office, 7 FLRA 571, 571-74 (1982). FLRA reaffirmed the position that a union may compel negotiations on a proposal requiring that employees be evaluated for performance appraisal purposes only on duties in their job descriptions. AFGE Council of Social Security Dist. Office Locals and DHHS, SSA, 11 FLRA 608, 611-12 (1983); see NTEU and USDA, Food and Nutrition Serv., 25 FLRA 1067, 1074 (1987) (language was not intended to cover specific details); see POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 24 (1993) (Provision 7: a negotiable proposal that performance elements "must be consistent with the duties and responsibilities covered in the employee's position description"); NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 741-42 (1992) (Provision 20 negotiable: elements and standards be consistent with duties and responsibilities contained in position description).

Nonnegotiable was a proposal that management agree that performance standards and critical elements would be significant in terms of title, series, and grade, and that standards and elements would be consistent with the employee's official duties and responsibilities. AFGE Local 1458 and DOJ, U.S. Attorney, S. Dist. of Florida, 29 FLRA 3, 10-11 (1987) (Provision 4). A proposal was nonnegotiable that "critical elements and performance standards shall be fair, reasonable, and realistic within the duties and responsibilities stated in the employee's position description," DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 28 FLRA 871, 875, 878-79 (1987):

[P]roposals which prescribe the content of performance standards rather than relating to the application of those standards directly interfere with management's rights under section 7106(a)(2)(A) and (B). Specifically, the Authority has found nonnegotiable proposals which prescribe that performance standards themselves, rather than merely their application must be fair and reasonable. For example, American Federation of Government Employees, AFL-CIO Local 1603 and U.S. Naval Hospital, Patuxent River, Maryland, 22 FLRA No. 60 (1986); American Federation of Government Employees Local 32 and Office of Personnel Management, 16 FLRA 948 (Proposal 3)....

Proposals were not negotiable that required critical elements of a position to be based only on the grade-controlling factors and to be weighted in direct proportion to their relative importance in grade determination. The proposals precluded the agency from designating as a critical element any component of a job other than a grade-controlling element. The proposals did not set a nonquantitative standard for review of the application of a standard to an employee. They precluded the use of certain types of standards. AFGE Local 1968 and Dept. of Transp., St. Lawrence Seaway Dev. Corp., Massena, 5 FLRA 70 (1981). A reviewing court opined, AFGE Local 1968 v. FLRA, 691 F.2d 565, 572-74 (D.C. Cir. 1982):

The construction of § 7106(b) petitioner advances here is, in our view, unduly literal and completely insupportable. Initially, we reject outright petitioner's characterization of Proposal I, restricting critical elements to grade-controlling factors, as procedural. The only possible basis for that interpretation is the claim...that standards of any sort are inherently procedural, a position we believe to be untenable. Indeed, acceptance of that thesis would eviscerate all of the management rights that Section 7106(a) is designed to protect.

...

...Proposal I restricts the agency's choice of critical job elements to those factors which are grade-controlling. Its effect, therefore, is indistinguishable from that wrought by the proposals involved in National Treasury Employees Union: imposition of a limitation upon management's ability to identify and select those elements. Proposal III, requiring as it does formal negotiations on measures of performance and further requiring referral of hopelessly-mired disputes to the Federal Service Impasses Panel, would similarly restrict management's authority in that area by insisting that it be exercised only with union approval....

...

...The proposal in AFGE Local 32, in the Authority's words, merely "established a general non-quantitative requirement by which the application of critical elements and performance standards established by agency management could subsequently be evaluated...." Proposal IV, in stark contrast, would sanction direct challenges to performance standards and critical job elements, and would allow arbitrators to substitute their notions of proper standards and elements for those of management....

FLRA reiterated that negotiations could not be required on a proposal that required management, to the extent practicable, to relate critical elements to grade controlling duties of a position. The proposal was a substantive limitation on the authority of management to define critical elements. A broad standard, e.g., "fair and equitable" could be used as to the application of the standard and would leave the standard undisturbed when reviewed in arbitration, but a limitation on the nature of the standard itself was substantive, not procedural, and nonnegotiable. AFGE Local 3483 and Fed. Home Loan Bank Bd., New York Dist. Office, 13 FLRA 446, 452 (1983).

Not negotiable was Proposal 2 in AFGE Local 1164 and DHHS, SSA, Dist. Office, Worcester, 49 FLRA 1408, 1412, 1420 (1994), seeking agreement that reception duties will be given only "the weight due when compared to time spent performing other duties" in appraisal of claims representatives. FLRA disapproved the proposal as a procedure and as an arrangement, but its opinion on negotiability was split. Member Talkin considered the proposal negotiable. Chairman McKee thought the proposal was nonnegotiable because it was insufficiently tailored to meet the needs of employees adversely affected by the assignment of reception duties. Member Armendariz rejected the proposal as nonnegotiable because it did not sufficiently address only the needs of adversely affected employees. It was his assessment that "[b]ecause Proposal 2 includes within its scope employees who would not be adversely affected as a consequence of being subjected to performance expectations that do not make allowance for competing demands on their time, it is not tailored to apply to only those employees who will be adversely affected by management's actions."
