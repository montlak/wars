b. Bargaining History

In Horner v. Bell, 825 F.2d 382 (Fed. Cir. 1987), the court was called on by OPM to review an arbitrator's award mitigating a Chapter 43 demotion through interpretation of a contract clause stating that "when the employee is capable of performing another position of the same grade, the supervisor may propose to reassign the employee to such a position." The arbitrator interpreted the contract clause to impose a duty to reassign rather than demote. The court disagreed. Reviewing the bargaining history, the court determined that the contract clause was developed as a compromise; it was not intended to be construed as a requirement. Going beyond the review of the bargaining history, the court suggested that if the clause was intended to be a binding requirement, it would run afoul of the management rights clause of CSRA, 5 USC 7106, and that the contract clause would not be rescued as either a negotiable procedure or as an appropriate arrangement under 5 USC 7106(b)(2), (3). The decision of the court was unusual, because it is normally the arbitrator's job to construe the law of the shop; courts rarely interfere with that construction unless it cannot possibly be said that the result draws its essence from the contract. The secondary conclusion of the court concerning the negotiability of the contract clause (and by implication, its enforceability) was closer to the mark. The D.C. Circuit found a similar proposal to be neither a negotiable procedure nor a negotiable arrangement. AFGE Local 1923 v. FLRA, 819 F.2d 306, 307 (D.C. Cir. 1987).
