3. Mandamus Act

The Mandamus Act, 28 USC 1361, places in federal district courts jurisdiction over actions seeking to compel any agency officer or employee to perform a mandatory duty owed to the plaintiff. The Act itself does not impose any duties upon agency officers or employees. A plaintiff must rely on some other statute imposing a duty to act on the officer or employee. Monetary relief is unavailable under the Mandamus Act. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Administrative Procedure Act."] The Declaratory Judgment Act, occasionally employed to secure redress for a change in civil service status, similarly confers no independent jurisdiction upon a federal district court. See Hills v. Eisenhart, 156 F. Supp. 902, 905 (N.D. Cal. 1957), aff'd on other grounds, 256 F.2d 609 (9th Cir. 1958) cf. Warsaw Orthopedic, Inc. v. Sasso, 977 F.3d 1224 (Fed. Cir. 2020) (discussing Declaratory Judgment Act in litigation over payment for patent rights).

A court may order a federal employee to perform a mandatory act, or refrain from performing an act, that does not involve the exercise of discretion. There are few personnel actions that do not require the exercise of discretion, Ryon v. O'Neill, 894 F.2d 199, 205 (6th Cir. 1990):

Clearly, the reinstatement requested by Ryon cannot be characterized as a ministerial act. Decisions of the DMA concerning where to assign personnel are highly discretionary, requiring supervisors to determine how best to deploy employees in light of the myriad objectives of the agency. Factors too numerous to mention here must all be taken into account by experienced experts in formulating assignment decisions, making mandamus improper under these circumstances....

But see Young v. Hampton, 420 F. Supp. 1358 (S.D. Ill. 1976) (exercising mandamus jurisdiction to review and sustain a federal employee's dismissal for off-duty misconduct); cf. Beacom v. EEOC, 500 F. Supp. 428 (D. Ariz. 1980) (without jurisdictional analysis, directing EEOC to employ an attorney who was given a firm offer that he accepted and who then wound down his law practice before learning that EEOC would not place him into the position because of a hiring freeze).

The Mandamus Act is not available to enforce a contract obligation, e.g., a grievance settlement, according to Bobula v. DOJ, 970 F.2d 854, 860 (Fed. Cir. 1992); see Blaney v. United States, 34 F.3d 509 (7th Cir. 1994) (declining to apply the Mandamus Act to enforce a settlement that was not filed with the Board). Under pre-Reform Act law, the Act could be used to prevent clearly unconstitutional actions impacting federal employees. See Robinson v. Reed, 566 F.2d 911 (5th Cir. 1978) (mandamus could be used to challenge the federal agency's requirement that an employee must attend race relations seminars having nothing to do with her job responsibilities). But see Walpin v. Corp. for Nat. & Community Services, 630 F.3d 184 (D.C. Cir. 2011) (mandamus jurisdiction not established when the appellant failed to demonstrate a clear and indisputable right to relief resulting from his removal as an inspector general, allegedly in violation of the congressional notification requirements of the Inspector General Act); McGrimley v. Foley, 89 F. Supp. 10 (D. Mass. 1950) (mandamus unavailable to compel an appointment not required by statute), aff'd, 180 F.2d 1022 (1st Cir. 1950). Mandamus has been used to clear court records of unfounded allegations against a federal official who, although named in those records, has no other way to clear his name. See In re Smith, 656 F.2d 1101 (5th Cir. 1981). [Refer earlier to "Privacy Act," and later to "Tucker Act"; "EEO Settlement"; "MSPB Settlement"; "Liberty Interest; Name Clearing Hearing."]

Distinct from the mandamus statute is a petition for mandamus filed with an appellate court under the All Writs Act, 28 USC 1651, seeking the appellate court to direct that an action be taken by a lower court or tribunal. In re Impax Laboratories, Inc., 171 Fed. Appx. 839, 840 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP), explained that the remedy of mandamus is available only in extraordinary situations to correct a clear abuse of discretion or usurpation of judicial power; a party seeking the writ bears the burden of proving that it has no other means of attaining the relief desired and that the right to issuance of the writ is clear and indisputable. Mandamus was denied to an appellant who sought the aid of the Federal Circuit in compelling the Board to decide his case. In re McCarthy, 227 Fed. Appx. 904, 904 (Fed. Cir. 2007).
