c. Conditions of Discharge From Military Service

A dishonorable discharge disqualifies the appellant from USERRA protection, Tootle v. MSPB, 559 Fed. Appx. 998, 1001-02 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP):

The Board likewise committed no error in finding no jurisdiction on a second ground--that, even if Mr. Tootle had made the required discrimination allegation, he still could not pursue a claim under USERRA. Under the plain language of 38 U.S.C. § 4304(1), "[a] person's entitlement to the benefits [of USERRA] ...terminates upon the occurrence of...[a] separation of such person from such uniformed service with a dishonorable or bad conduct discharge." Despite Mr. Tootle's three honorable discharges, his one dishonorable discharge takes away any standing he would otherwise have had to bring his USERRA claim. Downs v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 139, 146-47 (2008); Whisnant v. U.S. Postal Serv., NY-3443-99-0131-I-1, 1999 WL 803850 (M.S.P.B. Sept. 29, 1999) (affirming a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction under section 4304 where the appellant's discharge from the military was dishonorable).

In response, Mr. Tootle argues that section 4304, in terminating USERRA rights for those dishonorably discharged, is unconstitutional on equal protection, due process, or cruel and unusual punishment grounds. This argument, even if it had merit, would not undermine the correctness of the Board's dismissal, because it has no bearing on the independent ground that he did not present necessary allegations under USERRA. In any event, we see no merit in the argument. Mr. Tootle has not pointed to anything in the cited constitutional standards that prevented Congress from excluding those dishonorably discharged from the special benefits it provided to those who served in the military and completed such service honorably. Mr. Tootle was entitled to pursue remedies available to those not granted the special benefits of USERRA, which is a step that he, in fact, took.

An employee discharged from military service has a limited period of time to apply for reemployment, and that time is not extended because the employee later upgrades the conditions of the military discharge that originally barred his reemployment, Malko v. USPS, 3 MSPR 42, 44-46 (1980):

Title 38, U.S. Code, Section 2021 et seq., provides, in pertinent part, for the right of reemployment of an inducted person who:

"(1) receives a certificate described in section 9 (a) of the Military Selective Service Act (relating to the satisfactory completion of military service), and

(2) makes application for reemployment within ninety days after such training and service...."

As mentioned above, appellant was not reemployed by the agency after completing military service because he received an undesirable discharge in December 1969. His discharge was subsequently upgraded to honorable, apparently sometime in October 1978, although appellant was given unofficial notification of the upgrade by letter dated August 29, 1978, from the Army Discharge Board, and received final, official notice in April 1979.

The clear intent of the above-cited law and the Office of Personnel Management regulations and guidelines (Part 353 of Title 5 of the Code of Federal Regulations; Federal Personnel Manual Chapter 353) is that once a person meets the criteria for restoration, then he or she is entitled to be restored to employment to the position left, or to a position of like seniority, status, and pay, as a matter of right. An agency must consider him or her to have been on furlough or leave of absence during that duty; and must restore the individual with the status he or she would have had if the individual had never left for military duty. See Fishgold v. Sullivan Drydock & Repair Corp., 328 U.S. 275 (1946); Hilton v. Sullivan, 334 U.S. 323 (1948); Tilton v. Missouri Pacific R.R. Co., 376 U.S. 69 (1964); Accardi v. Pennsylvania R.R. Co., 383 U.S. 225 (1966); Alabama Power Co. v. Davis, 431 U.S. 581 (1976). This is to be contrasted to a reinstatement action wherein an agency may exercise a substantial measure of discretion in reemploying the individual. See generally, 38 U.S.C. 2014 and 5 C.F.R. 307 regarding noncompetitive veterans readjustment appointments, in which the character of an acceptable discharge now "is other than dishonorable conditions."

The crux of the matter revolves around the bringing of proceedings for an upgraded discharge within the prescribed 90-day period for seeking restoration. There is no dispute that appellant made timely application for restoration in December 1969. However, he did not take any action to upgrade his undesirable discharge during the 90-day period. Rather, the record reflects that his first attempt for an upgraded discharge, which was unsuccessful, was not made until early 1976, a period of over 5 years. It would nullify the specific requirements of the law if a former employee who had no certificate of satisfactory service could compel reemployment upon the ground that approximately 10 years later such evidence of satisfactory completion of service was secured and presented. No authority has been cited which holds that an upgraded discharge reverts back in time after the 90 days has elapsed when no timely action has been taken regarding the nature of the discharge.

The Board noted but did not resolve whether restoration rights attached to an employee who apparently lost reserve membership as a result of military retirement. Pence v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 198 (1993).
