b. Leave-Reporting Requirement

An injured employee may be required to follow leave-reporting requirements. An individual who is injured at work and who is then off duty for a period of recovery must either elect continuation of pay status when injured and file duty status reports, or properly request leave if unable to work. The employee may be disciplined for failure to do either, since his inaction leaves his agency in doubt as to his whereabouts or status. Salazar v. United States, 775 F.2d 284, 286 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Dunn v. USPS, 9 MSPR 652, 655-56 (1982) (agency removed an employee who failed to keep the agency apprised of his availability for work after OWCP benefits were suspended and after the agency attempted to notify the employee that he could be removed if he failed to contact his supervisor immediately concerning his physical condition; the supervisor testified that progressive discipline, e.g., reprimand or suspension, was unavailable for an employee in a nonduty, nonpay status).

Agencies must take care to distinguish treatment of employees who take leave because of compensable injuries from those who, although entitled to leave, do not follow proper leave-reporting procedures, as Atchley v. Dept. of Army, 46 MSPR 297, 301-02 (1990), explained in an appeal involving an employee's assertion that he should have been placed on sick leave following an incapacitating on-the-job injury:

Under regulations promulgated by the Office of Personnel Management, an agency must grant sick leave to an employee who has provided the agency with administratively acceptable evidence that he is incapacitated for the performance of his duties due to illness or injury. See 5 CFR 630.401(b), 630.403. Section 630.402 provides, however, that an employee must submit a written application for sick leave within the time limits set by the agency. The employee's duty to comply with the agency's leave procedures is not, however, a condition precedent to the agency's duty to grant sick leave when presented with proper evidence of illness or injury. If an employee has provided administratively acceptable evidence of incapacity, prior to the agency's decision to remove him on AWOL charges, the agency must grant sick leave, regardless of the employee's failure to timely comply with the agency's sick leave procedures. Nash v. United States Postal Service, 8 MSPR 307, 310 & n.10 (1981). The administrative judge therefore erred in sustaining the AWOL charge solely on the basis of the appellant's failure to follow the agency's leave procedures, without deciding whether the appellant had provided administratively acceptable evidence of incapacity.

An agency may discipline an employee for failure to follow its leave procedures, but only if it separately charges and proposes disciplinary action on this basis.... The Board is required to review the employing agency's decision on an adverse action solely on the grounds invoked by the agency; it may not substitute what it considers to be a more adequate or proper basis....

If the employee is removed for AWOL and failure to follow leave-requesting procedures, a defense that the Board will analyze relative to mitigation is whether medications or other medical problems interfered with the ability of the employee to follow those procedures. See Sherlock v. GSA, 103 MSPR 352, 2006 MSPB 277 (2006). But see Burton v. Dept. of Army, 60 MSPR 392, 394 (1993) (charges of AWOL, failure to keep management informed of leave status and failure to follow leave procedures: "The Board has held that removal actions based on AWOL and other leave-related charges cannot be sustained when OWCP determines that the employee was entitled to compensation benefits as a result of a work-related injury for the entire time period charged as AWOL. See Richardson v. Smithsonian Institution, 48 M.S.P.R. 546, 548-49 (1991); Green v. Department of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 52, 54 (1988), appeal after remand 49 M.S.P.R. 289, 291-92 (1991).").
