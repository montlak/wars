A. RIGHT TO AND NECESSITY FOR A RECORD OR TRANSCRIPT

The statute underlying Board operations, 5 USC 7701, provides, as to transcripts:

(a) An employee, or applicant for employment, may submit an appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board from any action which is appealable to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation. An appellant shall have the right--

(1) to a hearing for which a transcript will be kept;...

The Board avoids the use of "transcript," implying a paper recording of hearing testimony, and instead refers to the "record" of an appeal. The official record of the case is defined by 5 CFR 1201.53(e) (2022):

Official record. Hearing exhibits and pleadings that have been accepted into the record, the official hearing record, if a hearing is held, and all orders and decisions of the judge and the Board, make up the official record of the case. Other than the Board's decisions, the official record is not available for public inspection and copying. The official record is, however, subject to requests under both the Freedom of Information Act (5 U.S.C. 552) and the Privacy Act (5 U.S.C. 552a) pursuant to the procedures contained in 5 CFR parts 1204 and 1205.

5 CFR 1201.53 (2022), adds:

Record of proceedings.

(a) Recordings. A recording of the hearing is generally prepared by a court reporter, under the judge's guidance. Such a recording is included with the Board's copy of the appeal file and serves as the official hearing record. Judges may prepare recordings in some hearings, such as those conducted telephonically.

(b) Transcripts. A "transcript" refers not only to printed copies of the hearing testimony, but also to electronic versions of such documents. Along with recordings, a transcript prepared by the court reporter is accepted by the Board as the official hearing record. Any party may request that the court reporter prepare a full or partial transcript, at the requesting party's expense. Judges do not prepare transcripts.

(c) Copies. Copies of recordings or existing transcripts will be provided upon request to parties free of charge. Such requests should be made in writing to the adjudicating regional or field office, or to the Clerk of the Board, as appropriate. Nonparties may request a copy of a hearing recording or existing transcript under the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) and Part 1204 of the Board's regulations. A nonparty may request a copy by writing to the appropriate Regional Director, the Chief Administrative Judge of the appropriate MSPB Field Office, or to the Clerk of the Board at MSPB headquarters in Washington, DC, as appropriate. Nonparties may also make FOIA requests online at https://foia.mspb.gov.

(d) Corrections to transcript. Any discrepancy between the transcript and the recording shall be resolved by the judge or the Clerk of the Board, as appropriate. Corrections to the official transcript may be made on motion by a party or on the judge's own motion or by the Clerk of the Board, as appropriate. Motions for corrections must be filed within 10 days after the receipt of a transcript. Corrections of the official transcript will be made only when substantive errors are found by the judge or by the Clerk of the Board, as appropriate.

(e) Official record. Hearing exhibits and pleadings that have been accepted into the record, the official hearing record, if a hearing is held, and all orders and decisions of the judge and the Board, make up the official record of the case. Other than the Board's decisions, the official record is not available for public inspection and copying. The official record is, however, subject to requests under both the Freedom of Information Act (5 U.S.C. 552) and the Privacy Act (5 U.S.C. 552a) pursuant to the procedures contained in 5 CFR parts 1204 and 1205.

Of particular importance, both for analysis of the case and the expense of its preparation, is the hearing record, previously called a transcript, now called a "record" or "verbatim record." The Board noted that the provisions of 5 USC 7701(a)(1) are "perspicuous" in that in any action appealable to the Board, "the appellant shall have the right to a hearing for which a transcript shall be kept." Webb v. USPS, 3 MSPR 389, 392, 3 MSPB 469 (1980) (citing Murray v. DMA, 1 MSPR 350, 357, 1 MSPB 338 (1980)). The FLRA noted, as to a printed transcript in an arbitration case, "[u]nquestionably, verification of the accuracy of an arbitration transcript and a careful analysis of that transcript in preparation for an appeal or the response to an appeal could be critical to a union's ultimate success in arbitration." VAMC, Ft. Lyon and AFGE Local 2430, 41 FLRA 1091 (1991).

Until it changed its procedures, the Board provided a written transcript with its initial decision. If a hearing was long and continued over a period of time, if a new representative took over in the middle of a hearing, or if the parties wished to submit a posthearing brief referring to the transcript, the AJ could release it before the decision. The Board's practice of providing a transcript was essentially the same as that of other agencies, e.g., EEOC, that provided the parties with their transcripts at public expense.

Times changed. Practices changed. Now the Board' practice is that there is no requirement, statutory or regulatory, that the AJ obtain (or that the Board provide the parties) a written transcript prior to issuing an initial decision. Gurkin v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 95, 98 (1989); see Reid v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 212, 216 (1983) ("All that is required is that there be a verbatim record of the proceedings. This requirement is met here since the hearing was tape recorded."). The official Board record of the hearing is now a digital audio recording. If a party wants a written transcript, the party pays the hearing reporter to get it.

Transcripts are an aid to briefing an appeal, preparing a PFR, or writing a brief and assembling an appendix during judicial review. But possession of a transcript is not a prerequisite for filing a PFR. Walton v. TVA, 48 MSPR 462, 469 (1991). Davis v. Dept. of Education, 13 MSPR 575, 576 (1982), pointed out:

Appellant states that without the transcript, he will be unable to comply with the first paragraph of 5 CFR § 1201.115, and will therefore be denied effective review by the Board. The cited regulation requires that a petition for review set forth, inter alia, "specific reference to the record." In the absence of a transcript, it is not necessary to quote testimony verbatim. Rather, such specific reference need only be sufficient to inform the Board of the identify of the witness, the subject matter of the testimony and the context in which it was given, the content of the testimony in summary form, its relevance to the matter at issue, and any other pertinent factors concerning the testimony which would assist the Board in reaching its determination.[*]

[*] See Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 MSPB 297 at 299, 2 MSPR 129, 133 (1980), which sets out the type of showing the petitioner must make in order to justify the Board's review of the record. Such a showing need not cite a specific page of a transcript in those cases where there is none, but must provide the Board with as many facts concerning the testimony as will "enable the Board to ascertain whether there may be a serious evidentiary question" and "explain...why the challenged factual determination is incorrect."

The Board came close to modifying the rule concerning the need for a transcript, or at least specific reference to the hearing record, Reeves v. USPS, 117 MSPR 201, 206 n.1, 2011 MSPB 102 (2011):

The Board's regulations require that a petition for review "state objections to the initial decision that are supported by references to applicable laws or regulations and by specific references to the record." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(a). The appellant argues that she lacks the financial resources to purchase the hearing transcripts or tapes, and is therefore unable to provide specific citations to the record. However, the Board's regulations provide procedures through which a party may request an exception to paying the cost of providing a hearing tape recording or written transcript. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.53(c). There is no indication in the record that the appellant, who is represented by counsel in this appeal, requested such an exception. In any event, we have reviewed the hearing transcript and we discern no error in the administrative judge's evidentiary rulings. Specifically, the administrative judge correctly excluded testimony about conversations that took place during a confidential mediation session, while allowing testimony about conversations that took place after the mediator left.

As to judicial review, a transcript may be helpful, or necessary. [Refer to Chapter 17, "Record and Issues Below."]

The Board has been known to deny extensions of time for filing a PFR when the extension is requested to enable a party to obtain copies of the hearing recordings from the Board. This is an unreasonable result, but rather than litigate the issue in an appeals court, if the recordings may be needed, they should be requested as soon as the hearing is through. Alternatively, because the record of the case will sit in the regional office of the Board prior to the filing of a PFR, arrangements could be made to review the audio record at the regional office soon after the hearing.

When a transcript is created, it is usually ordered by the agency rather than by the appellant. The (often unemployed) appellant may be without the funds to pay for a transcript, and Board law on counsel fees generally precludes recovery of the costs of a transcript as part of a fee petition filed following successful prosecution of the appeal. When an agency orders the transcript, the stenographic reporting firm under contract with the Board will usually supply a copy of the transcript to the regional office that heard the appeal, or to the Board if the case is at the PFR stage. The result is that the agency and the Board (or its AJ) have a transcript, and often cite to it, while the appellant has no more than his or her notes or recollection of testimony or the opportunity to listen (and cite) to the Board's audio recording. If an appellant becomes aware (or thinks that it is a fair possibility) that an agency ordered a transcript, the appellant may wish to submit a Privacy Act request to the regional office of the Board for a duplicate of the courtesy copy of the transcript that the Board receives, to be paid for by the appellant at the copying rate the Board charges for responding to FOIA requests. If the case is on PFR, the request for the transcript should go to the Clerk of the Board at its headquarters establishment. Submission by the agency of an accurate copy of a Board hearing transcript, without a copy being provided to the appellant, does not constitute an ex parte communication. Lewis v. USDA, 268 Fed. Appx. 952, 960-61 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP). Depending on the speed of production of a written transcript, when one is ordered, it may be posted on e-filing repository.

What then is to be done about the transcript? One may pay a lot of money to get the recording transcribed or, if the agency has ordered the transcript, one may request it from the Board, along with a request for an extension of the time to brief or file a PFR until the transcript is in hand. Requests for copies of the recordings (to be copied onto CD-ROMs) should be directed to the regional office that heard the case, or if the case record has already made its way to the Board headquarters, to the Board's Clerk. If a PFR is due, an extension of the filing date should be requested from the Board's Clerk to allow for the time that it will take to obtain and review copies of the hearing recordings.
