p. Other Problems

When an appellant was demoted, filed an appeal, and ultimately resigned, and the agency for reasons not clear from the Board decision then converted the demotion to a removal, the Board determined that the appellant did not waive his appeal right; the resignation might be contested as involuntary. Howard v. Dept. of Navy, 23 MSPR 438, 440 (1984) (citing Gonzalez v. Dept. of Transp., 701 F.2d 36 (5th Cir. 1983)). If the appellant is demoted and later resigns, the demotion appeal is not mooted by the resignation; the Board can award a remedy at least through the effective date of the resignation. Stringer-Earnest v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 533, 536 (1993). A resignation on November 13, effective November 9, did not serve to cancel a November 9 removal that led to an MSPB appeal. Effective management action was not taken to cancel the removal and the appellant was not notified by any agency official that his removal was rescinded. Tennyson v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 416, 419 (1980) ("Absent notification by an authorized official, we find that the simple act of placing a new punch card into the computer was ineffective to stop the removal from becoming effective in accordance with the terms of the November 2 decision notice.").

Resignation, if otherwise incontestable, does not moot a whistleblowing appeal based on other actions, e.g., a reprimand. The remedies remain open of rescission of the action and damages. But the resignation, if it cannot be challenged, limits the appellant's relief. See Wood v. DOD, 100 MSPR 133, 141 ¶ 15 (2005) (Board determined in an earlier appeal that the appellant's resignation was not involuntary); Sirgo v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 261, 267 (1995) (threat to terminate a probationer was within the scope of the IRA; the probationer's resignation did not moot the appeal; there at least remained the possibility that the appellant could prevail upon the Board to direct abatement of the threat). But see Occhipinti v. DOJ, 61 MSPR 504, 508 (1994) (mooting an IRA appeal when the employee was removed subsequent to the events leading to the IRA appeal; the removal appeal was dismissed without prejudice and, without employee's reinstatement, no meaningful remedy could be provided in IRA case). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Involuntary Resignation."]

If an agency removes an employee for one set of circumstances and, under peculiarities of the Postal Service disciplinary system, then places him on indefinite suspension based on different charge involving suspected criminal actions, the appeal from the indefinite suspension is moot when the removal is affirmed by the Board. Harrison v. USPS, 27 MSPR 535, 536 (1985). If an employee appeals a RIF and, during the pendency of that appeal, also appeals from a denial of reemployment priority rights, the second appeal is moot if the first appeal is won. Bragg v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1171). However, a RIF appeal from abolition and loss of a position is not moot when the appellant is later selected for another, higher-graded position through repromotion eligibility. See Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 511, 514 (1982). An appeal from a within-grade increase denial is moot if the agency retroactively grants the increase. Sterner v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 447, 448 n.* (1984). A restoration rights appeal is not necessarily mooted by OPM's grant of a disability retirement application. See Urena v. USPS, SF-0353-11-0566-I-1 (NP 7/20/2012) ("First of all, we are not convinced that the Office of Personnel Management's (OPM) disability retirement determination necessarily forecloses the appellant from establishing that she is capable of performing in a modified assignment. OPM will grant disability retirement when an employee is incapable of rendering useful and efficient service in a 'position,' but a modified assignment is not a 'position.' Bracey v. Office of Personnel Management, 236 F.3d 1356, 1358-63 (Fed. Cir. 2001)"; also noting the possibility of some backpay and leave restoration through successful pursuit of the disability component of the restoration rights appeal).
