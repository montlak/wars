b. Merit Promotion Grievance

The Board will not relitigate issues decided between parties to previous Board proceedings or other administrative appeal systems unless there is new or material evidence that was unavailable at the time the record in the prior proceedings was closed. See McKenzie v. USPS, 1 MSPR 496, 497-98, 1 MSPB 480 (1980). For an earlier decision to have binding effect, the individual so bound must have been a party to that proceeding or his interests must otherwise have been protected. In Aulik v. USPS, 1 MSPR 501, 501-02, 1 MSPB 485 (1980), the appellant was demoted as a result of a grievance brought by another employee. Appellant challenged the propriety of the grievance decision and claimed that his interests were not adequately represented during the grievance process. The AJ did not relitigate the issues behind the grievance. He determined the issue to be whether the grievance decision constituted a proper basis for downgrading the appellant. The Board reversed and remanded the case: the burden was on the agency to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that the downgrading was supported. To the extent that meeting the burden required a decision on the issues involved in the prior grievance, the burden was on the agency to reestablish those matters, or otherwise establish that the appellant was estopped from challenging the grievance decision. See Truan v. TVA, 15 MSPR 505, 507 (1983) ("[T]here is no allegation of any misconduct or inadequate performance. Rather, the agency demoted appellant solely to remedy its own alleged erroneous selection decision. Thus the agency has not demonstrated that its action promotes the efficiency of the service."). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Collateral Estoppel."]
