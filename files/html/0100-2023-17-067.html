c. Substantive Review

The employer bears the burden of proving that the appellant's competitive level is correct. Oxley v. Dept. of Commerce, 24 MSPR 398, 400 (1984) (SES RIF); Johnson v. TVA, 8 MSPR 135, 137 n.2, 7 MSPB 619 (1981) ("The presiding official incorrectly characterized appellant's argument as an affirmative defense. It is the agency's burden to establish that the appellant's competitive level was correct."). But if an employee seeks to show that a competitive level was improperly established and if the employee seeks to challenge the record containing position descriptions on the theory that the agency should consider what work is done, the employee must supply evidence of the actual duties performed. Reid v. Dept. of Commerce (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2011). Should a new position be essentially identical to the old position, it is considered in the same competitive level as the position abolished. See Wright v. Dept. of Commerce, 9 MSPR 472, 474 (1982) ("Because of the agency's failure to provide an official position description and in light of the agency's concessions that although the focus was different the qualifications needed to perform the new position were to be nearly identical to the old position, the Board agrees with appellant's contention that the new position was in fact a continuation of the former position and should have been considered at the same competitive level as the position abolished."). The RIF of the appellant is then improper if the agency removed the appellant while retaining another employee in the same competitive level as the appellant but with lower retention standing. Lewis v. Dept. of Commerce, 10 MSPR 496, 497 (1982) (citing Wright v. Dept. of Commerce, 9 MSPR 472 (1982)).

An employee may face a heavy burden when challenging the assignment to a particular competitive level if the argument is that other employees should have been placed in that same competitive level. It is the function of the employing agency, not of a court, to determine whether employees are qualified for particular agency positions and to decide what reassignments would unduly interrupt the work program. Agencies are given wide discretion in conducting RIF programs. Administrative decisions are presumed to be correct, subject to reversal only for abuse of authority, violation of law, or lack of substantial evidence. Breauleux v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-860) (citing Wilmot v. United States, 205 Ct. Cl. 666, 682 (1974)).

If RIF dates change, so too should computations of retention standing. See Bilanow v. United States, 309 F.2d 267, 270 (Ct. Cl. 1962) (when the effective date of a RIF was moved back, credit for service, including an application of the formula for employees whose service credit should be calculated on the basis of fractions of a year, should have been recomputed: "A man is going to lose his job, his means of support. At such times it is not unreasonable that he ask his superiors to exercise care and use every available and reasonable means open to secure a just and honorable decision.").

To ascertain whether a one-person competitive level is appropriate, it is necessary to exercise sound discretion and careful judgment when examining the characteristics of other positions. A competitive level cannot be determined through use of classification standards; duties must be examined. A competitive level is improperly structured if it does not include other positions that are basically the same as appellant's. Foster v. Coast Guard, 8 MSPR 240, 244, 7 MSPB 707 (1981). But specialized technical skills and training requirements may place similar engineering jobs into different competitive levels. Trahan v. TVA, 31 MSPR 391, 393 (1986) (distinctions in civil engineer positions were demonstrated through a comparison of position descriptions and affidavits). Supervisory requirements or policy-making responsibilities of one job may justify a separate competitive level for another position having only a supervisory role. See Cox v. TVA, 41 MSPR 686, 691 (1989) (supervisory positions were properly placed in a different competitive level than nonsupervisory positions); Gurkin v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 95, 100 (1989) ("The administrative judge correctly found that the GM-14 position that the appellant believed should have been included in his competitive level was graded at that level because of its supervisory responsibilities.").
