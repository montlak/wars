broida mspb Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses, Subchapter 02: Relationships, Section B: Sexual Misconduct, Subsection 04: Penalties for Sexual Misconduct, Part b: Removal
b. Removal

An agency has an obligation to create and maintain a work environment free of sexual harassment. A supervisor should neither sexually harass employees nor permit others to do so, Snipes v. USPS, 6 MSPR 1, 3, 6 MSPB 16 (1981):

...The agency has an obligation to its employees to create and maintain a work environment free of sexual harassment. Necessarily, the agency must accomplish this important goal through its supervisors. As a result, appellant had two significant duties: 1) not to engage in sexual harassment himself; and 2) to ensure that sexual harassment did not occur among subordinate employees. By sexually harassing his subordinate employees repeatedly, appellant violated the trust the agency placed in him as a supervisor to treat his subordinates with respect and to ensure that the work place was free of sexual harassment. In addition, under certain circumstances, such behavior could constitute discrimination on the basis of sex for which the agency would be responsible. See 29 CFR §§ 1604.11(a)(3) and 1604.11(c). See also Bundy v. Jackson, 641 F.2d 934 (D.D.C. 1981); Hayes v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 4 MSPB 466, 4 MSPR 411 (1980).

Board Member Wertheim dissented in Snipes, stating that there was nothing in the record, considering the otherwise good record of the employee, demonstrating that a penalty less than removal would not have served as well; the agency did not consider a lesser penalty and there was no warning to the employee. Snipes was affirmed in Snipes v. USPS, 677 F.2d 375 (4th Cir. 1982). The court concluded that the offense of abuse of supervisory authority on its face satisfied the requirement that the discharge promote the efficiency of the service. The Board reaffirmed that sexual harassment is intimidating, hostile, offensive conduct that cannot be condoned on the part of a supervisor, lest the agency be said to condone the conduct and ultimately assume liability. Removal was appropriate for kissing one employee and soliciting kisses from another employee. Jackson v. VA, 21 MSPR 63, 67 (1984). But Jackson did not survive judicial review. The evidence of the kiss was unpersuasive. As to the solicitation to kiss, the appellate tribunal determined that for one sustained incident of harassment involving no physical contact and no "quid pro quo," removal of the offending supervisor was too severe, even with unrelated past minor disciplinary infractions. Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Jackson implied that on remand, a demotion to a nonsupervisory position and counseling would be in order. On remand the Board directed demotion to a nonsupervisory position at the next lowest level within the appellant's qualifications. Jackson v. VA, 30 MSPR 240, 242 (1986); see Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 23 MSPR 174, 176 (1984) (removal of a support services supervisor for charges that included sexual harassment; the Board noted: "[T]he agency is entitled to hold him to a high standard of conduct since he was responsible for, among other things, evaluating personnel and reviewing disciplinary cases, and coordinating efforts with equal employment opportunity officials in developing policies affecting minority and female personnel.").

Two consecutive, unwelcome kisses of a coworker warranted removal of a mail clerk. The coworker was upset; the appellant had ready access to young female employees; the appellant was on notice of the agency's sexual harassment policy and had received counseling about it; the appellant demonstrated no remorse (other than concerns about what might occur to his job); he did not show an appreciation for the harm caused by his actions. Viens v. Dept. of Interior, 92 MSPR 256, 266-67 ¶¶ 18-23 (2002). A supervisor, an assistant commissary officer, was removed for improper conduct toward subordinates in Cisneros v. DOD, 83 MSPR 390, 397-98 ¶¶ 17-20 (1999). The Board set aside mitigation directed by the AJ and reinstated the removal:

We find that the seriousness of the appellant's misconduct outweighs the favorable penalty factors discussed by the administrative judge. In this regard, we note that the three female employees concerned testified regarding the impact of the appellant's sexual misconduct on their lives. Yingling testified that the appellant's conduct affected her to the extent that she deliberately changed the way she dressed to be "less dressed" in an effort to deter his behavior but that her efforts were unsuccessful. Merrill testified that the appellant's conduct upset her and adversely affected her performance on the job. She testified that male employees in the meat department tried to persuade her not to complain about the appellant's behavior and that when she complained to the Grocery Manager about the appellant's behavior, the Grocery Manager accused her of not performing her, work, which left her "in tears." She also testified that the appellant "[sa[id] that anyone who ma[de] trouble would pay like hell.] " In addition, she testified that the appellant's conduct affected her personal life. Jefferson testified that she attempted to avoid being in the appellant's presence, that the appellant's conduct almost caused her to cry, that it made her feel uncomfortable at work, that it kept her "scared" that he would make inappropriate remarks to her, and that he made her "scared" that she would lose her job.

The deciding official testified that he removed the appellant because his misconduct was serious and involved many instances of sexual harassment and inappropriate contact over a period of time that caused the female employees involved "to change the way they acted in terms of dress and speech and schedules of work hours." He testified that he took into consideration the fact that the appellant was a supervisor, that he was trained regarding sexual harassment, and that he was responsible for ensuring that the agency's sexual harassment policy was followed. He stated that the appellant's position as a supervisor made his misconduct unacceptable.

We find that the appellant's acts of sexual misconduct were not isolated but were continual over a period of approximately 18 months and that the appellant completely ignored the requests of the female workers concerned to cease his behavior. We further find that the appellant's behavior adversely affected these employees' lives, including their work environment. The Board has recognized that supervisors are responsible for maintaining a work environment free of sexual harassment. See Evans v. Department of the Army, 22 MSPR 340, 342 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 180 (Fed. Cir.) (Table), cert. denied, 474 U.S. 979, 106 S. Ct. 382 (1985). As a supervisor, the appellant exhibited poor judgment in engaging in the charged acts of sexual misconduct. An agency is entitled to hold a supervisory employee to a higher standard of conduct than other employees. Fischer v. Department of the Treasury, 69 MSPR 614, 619 (1996); Smith v. Department of the Navy, 62 MSPR 616, 620 (1994).

Thus, we find that, notwithstanding the favorable penalty factors upon which the administrative judge relied, removal is a reasonable penalty in view of the seriousness of the appellant's sexual misconduct, particularly its continual, unrelenting nature, its pervasiveness, its perpetration on several female employees, and his position as a supervisor. See Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 MSPR 419, 427-30 (1997) (despite supervisory employee's 8 years of satisfactory service with the agency and his prior clean disciplinary record, removal was a reasonable penalty for sustained charges of conduct unbecoming a supervisor, striking another supervisor, and sexual harassment of a supervisor), aff'd, 135 F.3d 776 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table); Alexander v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR 183, 186-92 (1995) (despite, inter alia, supervisory employee's 31 years of combined military and civilian service and numerous letters of commendations, awards, and compliments, removal was a reasonable penalty for his violation of agency's sexual harassment policy by verbal and physical sexual misconduct, including exposing himself to subordinate female employee and indicating that she could receive a promotion by having sex with him); Kirk v. Department of the Navy, 58 MSPR 663, 672 (1993) (citing Vakili v. Department of Agriculture, 35 MSPR 534, 539 (1987)) (physical contact was an aggravating factor that justified a more severe penalty than might otherwise have been warranted against nonsupervisory employee); Pugh v. U.S. Postal Service, 20 MSPR 326, 327 (1984) (removal was a reasonable penalty for sexual harassment involving a single incident of physical contact).

Offensive comments by an administrative law judge to female employees were cause for removal. The comments were disruptive and eroded confidence in the administrative process. SSA v. Davis, 19 MSPR 279, 282 (1984); Davis v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1047); see Dolezal v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 64, 71 (1993) (removal when the relationship between two employees was apparently consensual, but the relationship adversely impacted the ability of another individual to supervise one party to the relationship); Winebarger v. TVA, 22 MSPR 635, 636 (1984) (the removal of a warehouse supervisor for offensive sexual comments or conversations). Prior counseling following incidents of sexual harassment is a factor relevant to the determination of whether removal should be imposed for subsequent incidents. Tyler v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 85, 88-89 (1988).
