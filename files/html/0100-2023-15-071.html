broida mspb Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses, Subchapter 01: Status, Section C: Failure to Maintain or Obtain Condition of Position, Subsection 02: Failure to Complete Training Course; Validity of Examination, Part a: Air Traffic Controller
a. Air Traffic Controller

The Board considers appeals involving air traffic controller trainees who are demoted or removed when they fail their developmental training courses. An introduction to the training scheme was offered by the FLRA in NATCA and Dept. of Transp., FAA, 51 FLRA 192, 193 (1995):

The pay of air traffic control specialists (controllers or ATCSs) depends on the level of difficulty of their work. Air traffic control facilities are classified from level 1 to level 5, with level 5 handling the greatest volume of traffic and having the most complex operations. The highest grade for a controller at a level 1 facility is GS-10 and at a level 5 facility is GS-14.

A controller who has not satisfied all the training and qualification requirements for a particular facility is called a developmental controller (DC). A controller who has completed the required training program and thus can perform the full range of work at a particular facility is called a full performance level (FPL) or journeyman controller. If an FPL controller applies for and is chosen for a higher-graded position at a higher-level facility for which he or she is not fully qualified, the controller becomes a DC at that facility and must complete all phases of training to become an FPL controller at that facility. If a DC does not complete a training program, he or she may be removed from employment or reassigned and demoted to a lower-level facility. A demoted DC must requalify as an FPL controller at the lower-level facility in order to retain employment. A DC who gains or regains FPL status again becomes eligible for promotion and may repeat the training program process to become an FPL controller at a higher-level facility.

Air traffic controller trainees are removed for failing to complete developmental training. Rogers v. Courts approve FAA's practices concerning required advancement of controllers in developmental positions. Haje v. Hampton, 549 F.2d 275, 276 (2d Cir. 1977):

...The FAA acknowledges the extraordinary nature of this requirement, but points out that since the agency is the sole civilian employer of air traffic controllers, it must train its own personnel. To assure a continuous supply of qualified air traffic controllers, the FAA has made it a part of the lower-level specialists job to learn the skills required for the next higher level.

...Without a forced advancement system, there might not be room at the lower grades for persons willing and able to qualify for the essential high level positions. Moreover, while the loss of their jobs is unfortunate, appellants were not treated unfairly. Each received explicit notice that if he accepted the position he could be required to gain additional expertise or be subject to dismissal.

For those who fail training, the associated removal scheme was approved, Sullivan v. United States, 416 F.2d 1277, 1283 (Ct. Cl. 1969):

...Whether it is proper to measure a Government employee's performance by the degree to which he has learned the skills of the next higher position depends upon whether he has been hired as a trainee whose primary duties are to learn such skills, or as a journeyman. As defendant succinctly states it, "a distinction may validly be made between Government employees who have been employed in order to learn (such as plaintiffs) and those who have learned in order to be employed at a certain level (such as the ordinary nontrainee)."

It now need only be noted that plaintiffs' dismissal also did not infringe their rights under the Civil Service Commission regulations setting up a probationary period after which plaintiffs were entitled to be free from further examination of their qualifications, 5 CFR §§ 315.801-.807 (1969), since such regulations, which were designed to afford the Government a limited period during which it might peremptorily dismiss new employees, were not intended to preclude a post-probationary dismissal for failure to complete successfully up-grade training where such requirement has a direct bearing on promoting "the efficiency of the service," 5 CFR § 752.104 (1969). See Preble v. United States, 150 Ct. Cl. 39, 43 (1960). See also Burkett v. United States, 402 F.2d 1002, 1004, 185 Ct. Cl. 631, 634-635 (1968); Greenway v. United States, 175 Ct. Cl. 350 (1966), cert. denied, 385 U.S. 881, 87 S. Ct. 167, 17 L. Ed.2d 108; Meyers v. United States, 169 Ct. Cl. 1, 6 (1965).

To the above, this caveat is added: this opinion necessarily deals only with the unusual, Commission-approved program whereby the Agency trains journeymen controllers; more particularly, with the GS-8 airways operations specialist (center) position. It must be made clear that there is no intent to adversely affect the tenure rights of employees with permanent status in the classified competitive Civil Service, even though they may not have qualified for their superiors' jobs.

Although an air traffic controller trainee may raise as a defense the refusal of the agency to retrain or retest the appellant when the agency allowed others that privilege, the employee must show that the charges and circumstances of the other individuals are similar to those of the appellant. Even then, "we have recognized the paramount importance of a mandatory training program for individuals seeking to work in the critical field of air traffic control" and "the Board gives due weight to the agency's primary discretion in exercising the managerial function of maintaining employee discipline and efficiency." Grassell v. Dept. of Transp., 40 MSPR 554, 561-62 (1989). Radcliffe v. Dept. of Transp., 57 MSPR 237, 241-42 (1993), involving removal of an air traffic control specialist for failure to complete training requirements, rejected mitigation ordered by the AJ:

...The Board has not previously answered the question of whether the Douglas mitigation analysis applies where the agency has taken a removal action after the employee failed to progress satisfactorily in an "up-or-out" training program.... See Hutchcraft v. Department of Transportation, 55 MSPR 138, 147 (1992); Pawlak v. Department of Transportation, 40 MSPR 546, 554 (1989) (remand of case was warranted for consideration of whether the Board had authority to mitigate removal penalty imposed pursuant to "up-or-out" training program). We do so now.

In Griffin v. Defense Mapping Agency, 864 F.2d 1579, 1581 (Fed. Cir. 1989), the Federal Circuit held that, where a security clearance is a condition of employment, an employee has lost that clearance, and there is no agency policy of reassigning employees who unsuccessfully seek security clearances to nonsensitive positions, the Board has no authority to inquire into the feasibility of a transfer to an alternative position. Griffin, at 1581; Lyles v. Department of the Army, 864 F.2d 1581, 1583 (Fed. Cir. 1989). Similarly, we find that, where the satisfactory completion of training is a condition of employment, as it is here, and there is no agency policy manifested by regulation obligating reassignment, the Board has no authority to determine whether reassignment or a lesser penalty would be appropriate under the Douglas factors. Consequently, we find reasonable, and well within the agency's authority, the removal of an employee who has to progress satisfactorily to the next stage of a training program as a condition of his employment and fails to do so. We further find here, however, that since the FAA had an existing policy regarding reassignment for failed developmentals, it was appropriate for the administrative judge to review whether the agency properly had applied its existing policy regarding reassignment.

We note that the administrative judge referenced FAA Order 3330.30B, which provided that failed ATCS developmentals could be assigned to other positions within the agency. That order, established in 1971, was cancelled and replaced by FAA Order 3330.30C, dated September 27, 1984. FAA Order 3330.30C provides that the basic premise of the 1971 order remains, i.e., "failure to progress successfully in the training program may be the basis for separation from the occupation." Id., at paragraph 5. FAA Order 3330.30C also expressly provides that exceptions to separation from the ATCS occupation are permitted for developmentals who show potential for work at the full-performance level in different facilities if general criteria are met. Id. at paragraph 8(c).

The general criteria for a position change include a recommendation by the manager of the "losing facility" and a suitable available vacancy. Id. at paragraph 8(c)(1). The record simply contains no recommendation by the manager of the losing facility. Although the administrative judge found it significant that the appellant's training supervisor recommended (to the Facility Manager) that the appellant be considered for reassignment, we find that this recommendation did not constitute the requisite recommendation by a facility manager. More importantly, there was no obligation by the agency to find another job for the appellant. Consequently, we need not reach the question of whether there was a suitable available vacancy since there was no requirement that the agency find another job for failed developmentals such as the appellant. See Griffin, 864 F.2d at 1581. In the absence of any duty to find alternative employment for an employee who has failed an "up-or-out" training program, we find that the Board may not mitigate the agency's removal penalty....

See Hanratty v. Dept. of Transp., 819 F.2d 286 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (removal of air traffic control specialist for unsatisfactory training progress); cf. Thompson v. Dept. of Air Force, 289 Fed. Appx. 406 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (removal of air traffic control specialist for failure to maintain an Air Traffic Control Specialist certificate as required by agency regulations).

Through 1993 the Board considered some mitigating factors, including the failure to provide a performance improvement period, in appeals from adverse actions based upon the failure to complete training. Davis v. FAA, 39 MSPR 470, 480 (1989) (demotion; "the fact that the agency afforded the appellant a reasonable opportunity to improve militates against our mitigation of the selected penalty") (relying on Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 45-49 (1987), aff'd, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1988)); see Pawlak v. FAA, 40 MSPR 546, 554 (1989) (remanding for consideration of the interplay of the Douglas mitigation factors with the "up or out" requirement of the training program); Cupido v. Dept. of Transp., 12 MSPR 553, 554 (1982) (discounting the stress inherent in the training and examination process required for air traffic control positions); Bryant v. GSA, 12 MSPR 270, 272-73 (1982) (discounting appellant's testimony that he was under great stress as a result of family difficulties when he failed to pass tests associated with his training).

Although it suggested that failure to properly train an employee is a mitigating factor rather than a prohibited personnel practice action under 5 USC 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)], the Board determined in Wright v. FAA, 40 MSPR 355, 363 (1989), rev'd, Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 900 F.2d 1541 (Fed. Cir. 1990), post, that an allegation that an agency violated a merit system principle by ineffectively training an employee may be sustained if the appellant demonstrates that the agency abused its discretion by failing to allocate resources to provide additional or different training that would have provided the appellant with skills sufficient to pass developmental requirements. Regarding position changes, rather than removal, for air traffic controller trainees, the Board interpreted FAA Order 3330.30C, "Employment Program for Developmental Air Traffic Control Specialists," to mean:

[T]hat order provides that when an employee fails training, the only alternative to removal is, under certain limited circumstances, one position change, i.e., a reassignment or demotion to a less complex facility. This exception to separation is permitted for developmentals who do not progress to the full performance level, but who have successfully completed enough of the training program to demonstrate that it would be beneficial for the agency to retain them in the program. All such position changes are contingent upon available vacancies, recommendation of the employee by the manager of the losing facility, indication of acceptance by the manager of the receiving facility, and voluntary acceptance by the employee. Therefore, in keeping with the unique nature of the agency's mission, penalties less severe than removal are not involuntarily imposed.... [T]he agency offered appellant two positions at lower level facilities, both of which she declined. We find, therefore, that the agency did meet its burden of showing that removal was, in this case, a reasonable penalty.

On appeal the Federal Circuit reversed the result in Wright, concluding in Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 900 F.2d 1541, 1545 (Fed. Cir. 1990), that the Board improperly analyzed the case of FAA's self-imposed obligation to employees undergoing training. Ms. Wright had not received proper training in one phase of air traffic control work. The implication was that successful training at the lower rung of the training ladder was necessary for successful completion of the training step that she failed. The court determined that the agency had the burden of showing that it offered the necessary training to the appellant:

The Board held that the trainee had the burden to establish that "had she received all eight problems in Phase VIII, she would have passed Phase XI."... Not only did the Board set up an impossible standard for Wright to meet, i.e., to show she "would have passed," but also the Board committed an egregious error of law in misplacing the burden of proof.... [T]he agency bears the ultimate burden to prove by a preponderance of the evidence throughout the case that the trainee was properly removed. No reason in law or logic warrants shifting the burden of proof (or persuasion) to the trainee to prove that, but for the omitted training, he/she would have passed to the next level. The agency set up the training requirements which, in its judgment, were a necessary foundation for achievement of successive levels of performance. In some instances, the burden of proof is properly shifted to a defendant with respect to matters peculiarly within his/her knowledge. However, the proof of the significance of specified training is a matter peculiarly within the knowledge of the FAA, not the trainee. Having specified that a prescribed course of instruction is necessary, the agency is hard pressed to say, in a removal proceeding, that all of the training is not necessary. We do not say that the FAA may not, in appropriate circumstances, urge that omitted training has no nexus to a trainee's failure, but we do hold that the FAA bears the burden of proof of that fact, not the trainee.

Having concluded that the Board erred as a matter of law in its placement of the burden of proof, nevertheless, we conclude that a remand for reconsideration by the Board on the merits under the proper standard is neither warranted nor necessary. The error in result here clearly rests on the legal error....

We conclude that the FAA failed to establish that Phase VIII radar training had no nexus to Wright's failure at Phase XI. Accordingly, substantial evidence does not support that Wright's removal was for the efficiency of the service.

Wright was applied in Grant v. Dept. of Transp., 54 MSPR 285, 287 (1992) (removals were reversed because the agency failed to prove that instructors properly monitored the appellants). LeBlanc v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 405, 414-15 (1994), analyzed an appellant's progress through air traffic control developmental training:

In cases involving the removal of an employee for failing to complete the requirements of an "up or out" training program such as the National En Route Training Program at issue in this appeal, the agency makes a prima facie case for removal by showing that the developmental ATC "received the established training course and failed." If the agency did not provide all of the required training, it must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the omitted training bears no nexus to the developmental ATC's failure of the program. The ultimate burden of proof remains on the agency to show that it properly removed the developmental ATC. In other words, an appellant is not required to show that, but for the omitted training, he would have passed and moved on to the next phase of the training.

Moore v. Dept. of Transp., 64 MSPR 298, 301-02, 303-04 (1994), evaluated the burdens of proof in cases involving removal for failure to complete air traffic control training, and the associated defense of inadequate or omitted training:

The Board has held in the context of the ATCS training program that, if the agency fails to provide a trainee all of the required training, it must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the omitted training bears no nexus to the trainee's failure of the program. LeBlanc v. Department of Transportation, 60 MSPR 405, 414 (1994); see also Wright v. Department of Transportation, 900 F.2d 1541, 1545 (Fed. Cir. 1990). The appellant reasserts on review that the agency violated its training instructions by assigning him at least twenty on-the-job training instructors (OJTIs) during Phase XI of his training and that the violation had a nexus to his training failure. The specific agency training instruction that the agency allegedly violated states that "[n]o more than three additional OJTIs should be assigned to a developmental [ATCS] for a given position of operation."

The training instruction set forth above limits the number of additional OJTIs that "should be assigned" (emphasis supplied), but does not set an absolute upper limit on the number of additional OJTIs that may be assigned. "Should" is generally understood to denote a permissive act that involves the exercise of discretion.... Thus, the agency was not required to assign the appellant no more than three additional OJTIs. Accordingly, we find that, contrary to the appellant's assertions, the agency did not violate this training instruction by assigning the appellant at least twenty OJTIs. See LeBlanc, 60 MSPR at 415-16.

The appellant also reasserts on review that the agency erred by failing to designate a primary OJTI during Phase XI of his training. The agency's training instructions state that a developmental ATCS's first-level supervisor "shall...[a] ssign one primary OJTI per developmental [ATCS] who should provide a large portion of the developmental's [on-the-job training] on the position involved." "Shall" is generally understood to denote a mandatory action....

...

On remand, the administrative judge shall make a reasoned finding regarding whether the appellant was assigned a primary OJTI under the agency's training instructions after Grebenschioff departed. In making this finding, the administrative judge shall consider the evidence set forth above, as well as any additional evidence he finds relevant. If the administrative judge finds that the agency failed to meet its burden of showing that the appellant received all of the required training, including the assignment of a primary OJTI, he shall determine whether the agency proved by a preponderance of the evidence that the omission had no nexus to the appellant's Phase XI training failure. See Wright, 900 F.2d at 1545; LeBlanc, 60 MSPR at 414, 416-17. If the administrative judge finds that the appellant did not receive all of the required training and also finds that the agency failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence and argument that the omission had no nexus to the appellant's Phase XI training failure, the administrative judge shall order the agency to cancel the removal action....

Moore was followed in Zepmeisel v. Dept. of Transp., DE-0752-12-0098-I-1 (NP 8/14/2013):

"Unlike the vast majority of government positions, developmental air traffic controllers enter an 'up or out' training course which they must pass through to become a Full Performance Level controller." Wright v. Department of Transportation, 900 F.2d 1541, 1544 (Fed. Cir. 1990). "In cases involving the removal of an employee for failing to complete the requirements of an 'up or out' training program...the agency makes a prima facie case for removal by showing that the developmental ATC 'received the established training course and failed.'" LeBlanc v. Department of Transportation, 60 M.S.P.R. 405, 414 (1994), aff'd, 53 F.3d 346 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). The agency bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that any omitted training, including any failure to adhere to its training instructions, bears no nexus to the employee's failure of the training regimen. Id.; see Moore v. Department of Transportation, 64 M.S.P.R. 298, 302-03 (1994) (remanding for an assessment of whether the agency failed to assign an employee a primary on-the-job instructor as required by its training instructions). As with all chapter 75 removals, the ultimate burden of proof rests with the agency to show that it properly removed the developmental ATCS. LeBlanc, 60 M.S.P.R. at 414.

Both before the administrative judge and on review, the appellant has only offered generic statements that the agency failed to comply with its training program. Upon review of the record, we agree with the administrative judge that the agency established a prima facie showing that it complied with its training program which requires, inter alia, the assignment of on-the-job training instructors and 160 hours of training for a developmental ATCS such as the appellant. As the administrative judge found, the appellant was assigned the required instructors during his training period and received the requisite number of training hours prior to the cessation of his training.

On review, the appellant suggests that he should have been granted additional time to complete the training program before being removed. Nothing in the agency's training program, however, requires the agency to offer the appellant additional time to complete his training; the agency's training instructions make clear that additional on-the-job training hours are not guaranteed and that the agency "may assign additional OTJ [on-the-job] hours in cases where there is an expectation that certification will be accomplished within the additional period." In Moore, the Board held that an agency's failure to abide by permissive training requirements, as opposed to mandatory ones, does not evidence the agency's failure to honor its training program or instructions. 64 M.S.P.R. at 301-02. The appellant's argument that the agency erred when it did not afford him additional, optional training hours is unsubstantiated. The appellant's remaining arguments on review--that he should have been shown leniency due to personal issues and that his prior service as an air traffic controller outside of the agency should have been credited--also fail to establish a violation of the agency's training program or instructions which would warrant the reversal of his removal. See Moore, 64 M.S.P.R. at 302-03.
