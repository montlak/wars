a. Current Spouse; Duration of Marriage; Widow: Issue of Marriage; Children

Under 5 USC 8341, a "widow" includes the surviving wife married to an employee for at least nine months immediately before his death or who is the "mother of issue by that marriage." Clark v. OPM, 40 MSPR 517, 519-20 (1989), explained the availability of a survivor annuity to a current spouse when the federal employee dies:

The appellant, however, may be eligible for a survivor annuity as a "current spouse." OPM failed to consider this issue in its reconsideration decision. Under 5 C.F.R. § 831.603, a "'[c]urrent spouse' is defined as a living person who is married to the employee, Member or retiree at the time of the employee's, Member's or retiree's death." A "current spouse annuity" may be awarded if any one of the following three requirements set forth at 5 C.F.R. § 831.618(a) is met: (1) The surviving spouse and the retiree had been married for at least 9 months; (2) a child was born of the marriage; or (3) the death of the retiree was accidental. Section 831.618(b) provides that the 9-month marriage requirement can be satisfied by including the aggregate of all the marriages between the surviving spouse and the decedent. Section 831.618(c) provides, inter alia, that a child born out of a previous marriage between the same parties satisfies requirement (2) stated above. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8341(a)(1) and 8341(i).

While the appellant appears to qualify under these latter two provisions, she asserts that the decedent did not elect a reduced annuity to provide for a survivor annuity. As noted above, OPM failed to consider whether the appellant qualified for a survivor annuity as a "current spouse," and, therefore, it made no determination on the question of the decedent's election or nonselection of a reduced annuity.

The appellant may also be entitled to a lump-sum benefit under 5 U.S.C. § 8342(c) as the widow of the decedent. Section 8342(c) provides that, if the employee or Member dies without designating a beneficiary, lump-sum payments shall be made, in the order of precedence, first to the widow or widower. In the present case, if the decedent did not designate a beneficiary, the appellant, as his widow, may be entitled to a lump-sum benefit.

As to the required duration of a marriage, Beckett v. OPM, DC-0831-13-0639-I-1 (NP 7/7/2014):

An individual seeking retirement benefits bears the burden of proving entitlement to those benefits by preponderant evidence. Jordan v. Office of Personnel Management, 100 M.S.P.R. 623, ¶ 7 (2005). Under FERS, if an employee dies after completing at least 18 months of creditable civilian service and is survived by a widow or widower, the widow or widower is entitled to death benefits under 5 U.S.C. § 8442(b)(1). For purposes of this appeal, "widow" means the surviving wife of an employee who was married to the employee for at least 9 months immediately before the employee's death. 5 U.S.C. § 8441(1)(A). To satisfy the 9-month marriage duration requirement, the aggregate time of all marriages between the surviving spouse and the deceased employee is included. 5 C.F.R. § 843.303(b); see 5 U.S.C. § 8442(e)(2) (when the employee dies within the immediately preceding 9-month period, the requirement is satisfied if the surviving spouse of the individual had previously been married and subsequently divorced, and the aggregate time married is at least 9 months).

With consideration of state law, the Board held that the granting of benefits furthered the purpose behind survivor benefits.

"Stepchild" was defined, in a unique set of facts, to recognize the relationship, for purpose of survivor benefits, rather than to rely on a precise definition of the marital relationship between the annuitant and his wife. Salazar v. OPM, 31 MSPR 248, 249 (1986).

Resolving whether children were adopted, to resolve a survivor annuity claim, Trembles v. OPM, 58 MSPR 40, 41-43 (1993), explained what is necessary to prove adoption in order to qualify for the benefit:

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(e)(2), minor dependent children are eligible to receive survivor annuities under the Civil Service Retirement Act (CSRA). The definition of "child" under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(a) includes "an adopted child," as well as "a child who lived with and for whom a petition of adoption was filed by an employee or Member, and who is adopted by the surviving spouse of the employee or Member after his death...."

Children not otherwise eligible for survivor benefits, who likewise fail to fall within this definition, are barred from receiving such benefits. See Cleland v. Office of Personnel Management, 984 F.2d 1193, 1194-95 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (grandchildren ineligible for survivor benefits under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(a) in absence of evidence of adoption, or filing of petition of adoption prior to death of Federal worker, even though they had been placed in his and his widow's custody and were deemed "children" under state law).

The appellant asserts, however, that her children are entitled to receive benefits through equitable adoption, a doctrine recognized under SSA regulations. Equitable adoption, as set forth at 20 C.F.R. § 404.359, provides as follows:

You may be eligible for benefits as an equitably adopted child if the insured had agreed to adopt you as his or her child but the adoption did not occur. The agreement to adopt you must be one that would be recognized under State law so that you would be able to inherit a child's share of the insured's personal property if he or she were to die without leaving a will.

Accordingly, the appellant argues that since her children have satisfied these requirements, they are eligible for survivor benefits as "adopted children" under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(a).

We recognize that the Board has looked to the provisions of the SSA for guidance in interpreting terms of the CSRA where the meaning of such terms is unclear. For example, in Salazar v. Office of Personnel Management, 31 MSPR 248, 250-51 (1986), the Board affirmed the administrative judge's reliance upon an SSA provision in interpreting the term "stepchild" as used under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(a)(4). See also Sawyer v. Office of Personnel Management, 36 MSPR 201, 205-06 (1988) (in determining whether physically disabled son of annuitant was entitled to continuation of survivor benefits, Board applied standard of "substantial gainful activity" from SSA to interpret meaning of phrase "incapable of self-support" under 5 U.S.C. § 8341). We find, however, that SSA provisions are not helpful in this case in light of Congressional intent limiting the definition of "child" in the context of adopted children.

Pub. L. No. 92-243 amended the definition of "child" under the CSRA to include one "who lived with and for whom a petition of adoption was filed by an employee or Member, and who is subsequently adopted by the surviving spouse of the employee or Member after his death." Civil Service Retirement Program--Survivor Annuities--Adopted Child, Pub. L. No. 92-243, 86 Stat. 56 (1972). The legislative history of this amendment reflects a considered judgment as to both the current and intended future scope of the phrase "adopted child":

The term "adopted child" has been interpreted to mean only a child who has been legally adopted by the issuance of a final decree of adoption before the death of the employee or annuitant. The law [providing for a survivor annuity] does not apply to cases where the death of the employee or annuitant occurs before the final decree is issued....[This Bill] proposes to include for survivorship purposes a "child" who lived with an employee and for whom a petition of adoption was filed before the employee's death, and who is, in fact, adopted by the employee's surviving spouse subsequent to his death.

H.R. Rep. No. 92-811, 92nd Congress, 2d Session (1972), reprinted in 1972 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1988, 1989 (emphasis supplied).[4]

[4] In his official recommendation to the Congress, the Chairman of the U.S. Civil Service Commission explained the purpose of the amendment:

[A] stepchild who lives with a Federal employee in a regular parent-child relationship is immediately recognized for survivor benefits. It appears reasonable to assume that the parent-child relationship is at least as strong in the case of a child where adoption proceedings have been undertaken....The Commission believes this legislation is necessary to provide equitable relief to the deceased's surviving spouse in the relatively rare case where a Federal employee had initiated adoption proceedings, died before the adoption could become final, and his widow did finally adopt the child.

H.R. Rep. No. 92-811, 92nd Congress, 2d Session (1972), reprinted in 1972 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1991.

This history reveals the judgment of Congress as to the degree of formal proof of a parent-child relationship necessary to support an award of survivor benefits for adopted children. We therefore find that the equitable adoption doctrine does not apply to determine coverage under the statute, and we conclude that children who are not legally adopted and for whom an adoption petition was not filed before the employee's death are not entitled to a survivor annuity. To find otherwise would constitute an amendment of this statute in the face of its contrary legislative intent, a power only the congress has authority to exercise. Cleland, 984 F.2d at 1195.

Accordingly, since the appellant's children were not legally adopted by Trembles, nor was a petition for adoption filed before his death, we find that they are not entitled to a survivor annuity under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(e)(2).
