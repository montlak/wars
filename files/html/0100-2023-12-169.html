c. Retirement Appeal

Former Board Chairman Erdreich dissented in some decisions rejecting appeals and PFRs as untimely. He was particularly concerned about the rejection of appeals involving vested retirement benefits. Echoing sentiments he expressed in decisions mentioned in Chapter 2, "Negative OPM Reconsideration Decision Appeal," concerning rejection of untimely appeals from denials by OPM of applications for vested benefits, the former Chairman expressed the better, albeit dissenting, view of a comparable rejection of a PFR, Berry v. OPM, 64 MSPR 610, 610-11 (1994):

I respectfully dissent from the majority's denial of the appellant's petition for review. In my opinion, the Board should excuse the untimely filing in this survivor annuity appeal.

In Alonzo v. Dept. of the Air Force, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980), the Board held that an untimely appellant only need give a "reasonable excuse" for the delay "in light of the particular facts and attendant circumstances where diligence or ordinary prudence has been exercised." In announcing the Alonzo standard, the Board discussed the need to balance the right of employees to an independent decision on the merits with the interest of the agency in finality of personnel actions. As part of that discussion, the Board noted the observation of the Senate that the changes in the appeals procedures incorporated into the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 were intended to protect the right of employees to an independent review of agency actions while also giving agencies a greater ability to remove or discipline expeditiously employees who engage in misconduct or whose work performance is unacceptable. See Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 183 (citing S. Rep. No. 969, 95th Cong., 1st Sess. 51 (1978)).

Where an action has been taken against an employee, the timely filing of the appeal enables the agency to move forward more quickly with the management of its programs and its work force. This competing agency interest in finality is not a part of the assessment in a retirement case, however, where the primary interest involved is the appellant's entitlement under law to a particular benefit. Accordingly, any doubt regarding whether the appellant has shown good cause for the untimely filing in a retirement appeal should be resolved in the appellant's favor.

Here, the appellant stated that she did not understand the procedure for filing a Board appeal and that she sought assistance in filing from several sources after the Office of Personnel Management denied her claim. She has thus given a reasonable excuse for her delay. Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 184. Her right to review should not be denied based simply on the late filing of her appeal. I would thus find that the appellant has shown good cause for her delay, and I would remand the case for a hearing on the merits.

The Board subsequently intimated that retirement cases, involving vested benefits, may suggest a more lenient approach to excusing short delays in the filing of PFRs in Thomas v. OPM, 95 MSPR 695, 697 ¶ 5 (2004). Cf. Baugh v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 01-3297) (affirming dismissal of an untimely appeal from the denial of disability benefits: "refusal to grant a waiver of the regulatory timeliness requirement in this case is admittedly harsh"). In Kjeldsen v. OPM, CH-831M-07-0395-I-4 (NP 3/28/2012), involving a belatedly refiled appeal, the Board distinguished its approach toward showing some leniency in retirement cases, differentiating appeals involving entitlement to an annuity and those involving a collateral component of an annuity already granted, e.g., an overpayment dispute:

The appellant also argues that the Board has made an exception for retirement cases, which are substantially different from adverse action cases as they are less adversarial, citing Edney v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 60, ¶¶ 5-6 (1998). While it is true that the Board has made a distinction between retirement cases and adverse actions in determining whether to find good cause for an untimely filing, the reasons for doing so are not present here. In Edney, the Board noted that, in a retirement appeal, the primary concern is whether the appellant is entitled under law to a benefit. Edney, 79 M.S.P.R. 60, ¶ 6. The Board then concluded that any doubt as to whether the Board should reopen a retirement appeal for an adjudication of its merits should be resolved in favor of the appellant to determine whether the appellant is, in fact, entitled to the benefit sought. Id. Here, however, OPM has not denied the appellant any benefit to which he is entitled because OPM has already granted the appellant a Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS) annuity. Thus Edney is inapposite.
