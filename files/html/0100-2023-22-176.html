6. Fee for Expert, Consultant

Witness fees (including expert witness fees) are not ordinarily recoverable by a prevailing party in an appeal not involving a finding of discrimination. Ramirez v. EEOC, 32 MSPR 228, 231 (1987). In a patent case, the Federal Circuit suggested that expert witness fees are not attorney fees, although they may be reasonably necessary to the defense. Mathis v. Spears, 857 F.2d 749 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (expert fees may be recoverable under statutes awarding costs in litigation; however, those statutes are not applicable to MSPB litigation); cf. Hyatt v. PTO, 9 F.4th 1372, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2021) (considering the "American Rule" generally precluding fee-shifting and several statutory fee shifting provisions relative to recovery of expert witness fees when a statute allows "all expenses of the proceedings").

The Board reaffirmed its disinclination to award fees for expert witnesses as part of counsel fees under § 7701. Willis v. USPS, 89 MSPR 85, 87 ¶ 5 (2001) (citing Fishback v. USPS, 54 MSPR 257, 260 (1992), for the proposition that the "Board lacks authority to award fees for work performed by experts in either a testimonial or nontestimonial capacity"); Russell v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 157, 165-66 (1989) (modified in part on other grounds, Garstkiewicz v. USPS, 50 MSPR 476, 478-79 (1991)) ("In International Woodworkers v. Champion International, 790 F.2d 1174 (5th Cir. 1986), aff'd sub nom. Crawford Fitting Co. v. J.T. Gibbons, Inc., 482 U.S. 437, 107 S. Ct. 2494 (1987), the court addressed the issue of whether federal courts may tax as costs the fees that are paid to expert witnesses who are not appointed by a court, and that are in excess of the amount set forth in 28 U.S.C. § 1821. In doing so, it characterized those fees as a cost. The court described this cost as one between solicitor and client, rather than a taxable cost or a cost of litigation, but it nonetheless recognized the amount as a cost."). But SSA v. Goodman, 33 MSPR 325, 336-37 (1987), implied the availability of reimbursement, as part of counsel fees, for certain types of expert assistance:

In his schedule of attorney fees, the appellant claims hourly rates...for other personnel, including a librarian, statisticians.... See also Bennett v. Department of the Navy, 699 F.2d 1140, 1145 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (an attorney fee award under 7701(g)(1) may include reasonable and necessary out-of-pocket expenses not covered by the attorney's hourly rate which are routinely billed to the client, not taxable costs or prohibited by statute or authorized regulation, and not expenses incurred for the mere convenience of counsel). The rationale for including charges for paralegals and law clerks in fee awards is that such personnel "provide necessary services which, were they performed by attorneys, would be more costly." Keith v. Volpe, 501 F. Supp. 403, 413 (C.D. Cal. 1980). To the extent the charges for the librarian and statisticians are not part of counsel's overhead and are normally charged to fee-paying clients, we hold that such services are comparable to those provided by paralegals and may be properly included in a fee award under section 7701(g). Cf. id. at 414 (services of urban planner are comparable to those of law clerk and may properly be included in a fee award under 42 USC 1988).

See Fishback v. USPS, 54 MSPR 257, 259-60 (1992) (denying, as part of counsel fees, payment for work "performed by a non-attorney labor consultant employed by appellant's counsel," relying on West Virginia University Hospitals, Inc. v.
