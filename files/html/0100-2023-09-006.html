4. MSPB Processing of Appeal

The processing of an appeal begins with its filing and ends with the issuance of an MSPB decision and closure of the administrative record for purposes of judicial or EEOC review or file storage. There are two basic forms of Board decisions: "initial" and "final" decisions. The initial decision is issued by an administrative judge (AJ) at the Board's regional or field office designated to adjudicate the appeal. The initial decision becomes a final Board decision 35 days after it is issued unless before it becomes final either party files a petition for review. If a PFR is filed by one party or both parties, the initial decision becomes final when the Board issues its decision on the PFR. If both parties file a PFR, the Board ordinarily issues one decision addressing both PFRs. If a PFR is denied, the Board issues a final decision. If the PFR is granted, the Board may modify the analysis or result of the initial decision and the Board issues a final decision. Or the Board may grant a PFR and remand the case to the AJ for further proceedings, and at that stage there is no final Board decision until the AJ issues another initial decision with the opportunity for another PFR. The process is tersely described by the Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.113 (2022), providing, in part:

The initial decision of the judge will become the Board's final decision 35 days after issuance. Initial decisions are not precedential.

(a) Exceptions. The initial decision will not become the Board's final decision if within the time limit for filing specified in 1201.114 of this part, any party files a petition for review or, if no petition for review is filed, files a request that the initial decision be vacated for the purpose of accepting a settlement agreement into the record.

(b) Petition for review denied. If the Board denies all petitions for review, the initial decision will become final when the Board issues its last decision denying a petition for review.

(c) Petition for review granted or case reopened. If the Board grants a petition for review or a cross petition for review, or reopens or dismisses a case, the decision of the Board is final if it disposes of the entire action.

The regulation speaks of a case that is "reopened." The reopening process is described at 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022):

Regardless of any other provision of this part, the Board may at any time reopen any appeal in which it has issued a final order or in which an initial decision has become the Board's final decision by operation of law. The Board will exercise its discretion to reopen an appeal only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances and generally within a short period of time after the decision becomes final.

5 CFR 1201.117 (2022) states the Board's authority for a case reviewed through the PFR process or reopened:

(a) In any case that is reopened or reviewed, the Board may:

(1) Issue a decision that decides the case;

(2) Hear oral arguments;

(3) Require that briefs be filed;

(4) Remand the appeal so that the judge may take further testimony or evidence or make further findings or conclusions; or

(5) Take any other action necessary for final disposition of the case.

(b) The Board may affirm, reverse, modify, or vacate the initial decision of the judge, in whole or in part. The Board may issue a final decision and, when appropriate, order a date for compliance with that decision.

The reopening process allows the Board to consider requests to address otherwise final decisions for a variety of reasons that will be subsequently discussed. Almost all decisions from the Board result from the PFR process challenging initial decisions before they become final through the passage of the 35 days allowed for filing a PFR. A late PFR may be treated as a reopening request. A pleading styled as a PFR or request to reopen a final decision may be filed upon the occurrence of an event that may alter the basis for disposition of an appeal, e.g., reversal of an underlying criminal conviction or a change in the law of possibly retroactive application. The Board may on its own initiative reopen a case to correct what it feels is a manifest injustice or clear error of law in the otherwise final decision. For an appeal arriving at the Board's headquarters establishment for review or reopening of an initial decision, the Board's final decision is generally labeled as an "Opinion and Order" for a precedential decision or as an "Order" or "Final Order" for a nonprecedential decision. The distinctions between precedential and nonprecedential decisions are subsequently discussed.

The finality date of the Board's decision (whether the initial decision becomes final through the passage of time if no PFR is filed or if a final decision is issued after a PFR is filed) triggers review rights with the Federal Circuit or another court, a process described in Chapter 17, or through the EEO review process, described in Chapter 12. Final decisions may be augmented by addendum decisions on matters pertaining to compliance issues, claims for compensatory or consequential damages, and counsel fee petitions. [Refer to Chapter 1 for the types of decisions issued by the Board; Chapter 15 explores counsel fee determinations; Chapter 16 considers compliance and enforcement issues.] Initial decisions and final decisions are issued with statements of review rights and deadlines for seeking further review.

Most energy in the MSPB litigation process is spent processing the appeal at the regional or field office. To better understand that component of Board operations, it is helpful to examine a portion of a 1994 MSPB internal "Process Team" review of regional operations. The description is dated, because it does not provide for processing under the e-filing system. But the basic steps are outlined. The Board's internal review summarized the steps in the appellate process, as seen from the perspective of the AJ responsible for adjudication of the appeal:

Initial Receipt, Review and Assignment

-- A new petition for appeal is date-stamped the day it is received.

-- If there are significant deficiencies that cannot be readily corrected by the regional office staff, the appellant is notified of the deficiencies and afforded an opportunity to correct them.

-- The appeal is screened to determine the nature of the action being appealed and to note several items of information which will be entered into the Case Management System (CMS).

-- The appeal is assigned to an AJ.

-- The appeal is docketed, and information about the case entered into CMS.

-- A case file is prepared.

-- A check is made through CMS to determine if the same appellant has filed other appeals

Processing the Appeal Through Hearing or the Close of the Record

-- An acknowledgment order is prepared, usually on CMS, sometimes from a shell document on MS Word.

-- All pleadings received from the parties, as well as the orders, notices, and summaries signed by the AJ, are date stamped, tabbed, and filed.

-- A hearing order is issued setting up the prehearing conference and hearing date.

-- Settlement, status, and prehearing conferences are conducted.

-- Show-cause orders are issued where appropriate, e.g., where the agency has moved to dismiss the appeal on grounds that it has become moot.

-- Rulings are made on discovery motions and other procedural matters.

-- Rulings are made as to the evidence and witnesses to be allowed at hearing.

-- Legal research is conducted as needed.

-- Arrangements are made for travel and lodging, a hearing site, and a court reporter when a hearing is to be held.

-- The hearing is held.

Issuance of the Initial Decision and Case Close-out

-- The AJ reviews the written and testimonial record, conducts additional research as necessary, and writes the initial decision.

-- The Regional Director reviews the case prior to issuance in all cases handled by a GS-14 or lower-graded AJ, and in all "complex" cases, regardless of the AJ's grade level.

-- Court and Board cases cited in the initial decision are insta-cited on Westlaw, except for those on the Board's Standard Insta-Cite List.

-- Close-out information is entered into CMS.

-- The RD reviews every decision that he or she did not review prior to issuance. This post-issuance review has become optional, however.

-- The case files remain in the regional or field offices until a petition for review or appeal is filed with the Board or court, in which case the file is forwarded to Headquarters or the clerk of court. If there is no court or Board review (which is about 80% of the time), the cases are retired to storage.

In this chapter and those that follow, the significant steps in Board adjudication are reviewed from the perspective of the parties who appear before the Board.
