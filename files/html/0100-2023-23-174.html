d. Detail or Other Variation of Permanent Position

Reinstatement followed immediately by a detail or reassignment does not satisfy the requirement that the employee be placed in the status quo ante without a strong overriding interest by the agency to assign the appellant to a different position. See Jones v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 183, 187 (1997) (to restore the status quo, the agency must cancel, not just end a detail; the agency must amend the records to reflect that the employee was not detailed but held a permanent position); O'Connell v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 399, 402 (1995) (improper for agency to detail appellant, successful in a RIF appeal, when agency did not prove that the detail resulted from a lack of work; evidence supporting the detail that agency submitted to the Board after AJ made his initial compliance recommendation could not be considered); Funk v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 52, 55 (1991) (placing a supervisor into a nonsupervisory position). Unless circumstances otherwise justify the reassignment of an employee following reversal of a discharge, the employee's position of record, not duties or a position held during detail immediately prior to removal, constitutes the position that the employee should be restored to in compliance with the Board's order canceling the removal and directing the employee's reinstatement. See Miller v. Dept. of Army, 109 MSPR 41, 45 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 104 (2008).

Bullock v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 361, 367 ¶ 7 (1998), noted as to an improper detail that the appellant refused to accept (and who then took leave to cover the absence), challenged through compliance proceedings:

When a final Board order has issued, we have previously held that where an appellant absents himself from a detail that does not comply with that order, the administrative judge may find the agency in noncompliance and order an award of back pay and benefits for the period during which he is absent from the detail. Gevaert v. Department of the Navy, 74 MSPR 303, 308 (1997); see also Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 62 MSPR 14, 18 (1994) (awarding back pay and benefits to an appellant upon finding that the agency's removal of the appellant for failure to report for a reassignment that did not comply with final Board orders must be canceled).

Without mention of Bullock, Castellanos v. DOJ, NY-0752-09-0107-X-1 (NP 9/25/2014), suggested that if the employee is dissatisfied with the position held the reinstatement, the route is an enforcement petition, not failure to accept the responsibilities pending resolution of the challenge:

Upon reversing an action, the Board requires the agency to restore the appellant as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. Tubesing v. Department of Health & Human Services, 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶¶ 5-6 (2009). If the agency refuses to do so, it must demonstrate that it has "a strong overriding interest or compelling reasons for not doing so." Id., ¶ 6. However, an appellant is not entitled to refuse to report for work in every instance in which he disagrees that he has been restored to the status quo ante.

In Cooke v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 401, 408, aff'd, 73 F.3d 380 (Fed. Cir. 1995), the Board affirmed a removal for AWOL charges stemming from the appellant's refusal to report to a position to which he had been improperly reassigned. The Board held that unless the appellant's refusal to report is "protected by either privilege or a legitimate concern that it would cause him irreparable harm," his "only proper recourse...[is] to obey the order and...challenge its validity on appeal." Id. In Williams v. Department of the Air Force, 89 M.S.P.R. 484 (2001), the Board applied this reasoning to an appellant who refused to report to one of two positions offered as a result of a settlement agreement. Although the appellant argued that neither position complied with the settlement agreement, the Board held that he was required to report to one or the other, and could challenge the suitability of the positions on appeal. Id., ¶ 19.

Applying this reasoning to the instant situation, it is plain that, under our case law, the appellant had no justifiable reason to refuse to report for duty on November 9, 2009, the first date offered by the agency. Although the appellant believed that the conditions imposed (appointment as a new employee, training, background check) and the salary offered did not satisfy the agency's obligation to restore him to the status quo ante, he has not shown that his refusal was protected by privilege or that he had a "legitimate belief that reporting [to duty] would cause him irreparable harm," see Williams, 89 M.S.P.R. 484, ¶ 19. Any harm he might have suffered could have been "cured during the course of" an appeal of the agency's actions or conditions. See Cooke, 67 M.S.P.R. at 408. Accordingly, the appellant should have reported to work, performed his assigned duties, and litigated these issues in a petition for enforcement. See id. at 407; see also Williams, 89 M.S.P.R. 484, ¶ 19. We therefore find that the agency reinstated the appellant as ordered; that the appellant should have reported to work on November 9, 2009; that the agency reasonably carried him as AWOL from that date until when he actually reported to work in July 2011; and that the agency correctly determined that the back pay period ran from August 15, 2006, to November 7, 2009.

An agency failed to comply with a reinstatement order when it failed to return an employee to full supervisory responsibilities in Yetman v. Dept. of Army, 36 MSPR 425, 429 (1988). Although the number of subordinate positions increased after the appellant's removal, her position description clearly encompassed supervision of the unit; augmentation of that unit after her dismissal was insufficient reason to remove her supervisory responsibilities. If an agency claims that the appellant's position substantially changed through accretion of duties, the agency must support that argument, based on undue disruption of agency operations through restoration of the appellant, by submitting position descriptions establishing the duties and responsibilities of the old and new positions. See Hoover v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 545, 556 (1993). Under Jones v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 183, 188 (1997), "[w]hen the agency upgrades an appellant's former position, the Board analyzes the reasons for the reclassification in order to determine if the appellant would have remained in the position at the higher grade level if the unwarranted personnel action had not occurred." The AJ must consider whether the new position was not the clear successor to the appellant's job, whether the new position resulted from accretion of duties, and whether the position had to be filled competitively. Jones noted that the Board generally has no jurisdiction to review position classification and will not entertain a claim during compliance that positions should have been reclassified at an earlier date.

The employee need not be restored to a status better than what existed at the time of the original removal proceedings, Grant v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-11-0884-C-1 (NP 2/10/2014):

...It is true that an employee's restoration to the position that he occupied before an invalid adverse action, without being allowed to perform the full range of his former duties, does not constitute being returned to the status quo ante. Calarco v. U.S. Postal Service, 58 M.S.P.R. 483, 486 (1993). However, a status quo ante remedy does not require that the appellant be placed in a better position than he was in at the time of the agency's action. Hagan v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 8 (2005). Here, we find that the appellant's return to duty in a TRD status constitutes a return to the status quo ante because the appellant was in a TRD status at the time of his December 2010 removal and had been in that status since June 2010. Cf. Sellman v. U.S. Postal Service, 63 M.S.P.R. 145, 154 n.3 (1994) (the agency was not required to return the appellant to active duty in his position when it cancelled its removal action; it was "merely required to place him in an approved leave status, the same status he was in prior to his removal").
