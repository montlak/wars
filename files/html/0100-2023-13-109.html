E. INDEFINITE SUSPENSION

The indefinite suspension places an employee in nonduty and nonpay status pending inquiry into a condition that the agency believes may impair its operations or public confidence in the agency. If the indefinite suspension exceeds 14 days, it is appealable as an adverse action. The indefinite suspension was most frequently used in the early years of the Board "to allow 'examination of charged criminal involvement' if the agency has reasonable cause to believe an employee has committed a crime by which imprisonment may be imposed." Canevari v. Dept. of Treasury, 50 MSPR 311, 315 (1991). The indefinite suspension is ordinarily imposed, like other adverse actions, after a 30-day notice period unless the notice period is itself shortened because there is reasonable cause to believe a crime has been committed supporting a possible sentence of imprisonment. See Pararas-Carayannis v. Dept. of Commerce, 9 F.3d 955, 957 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Other decisions in this section will consider indefinite suspensions during investigations of employees whose security clearances are suspended. As in other Board appeals, affirmative defenses may be raised, and then must be decided in appeals of indefinite suspensions. See Jones v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 398, 402 (1995) (the AJ erred by not deciding a racial discrimination claim asserted in the appeal of an indefinite suspension). The evidence examined by the Board as to the propriety of the indefinite suspension is the information before the agency when it made the indefinite suspension decision. See Rittgers v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-11-0212-B-1 (NP 8/14/2013) ("It is well-settled that the Board must look at the information relied upon by the agency at the time it made the decision. See Barresi v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 M.S.P.R. 656, 663 (1994) (explaining that the relevant evidence in an indefinite suspension is 'the record that these officials have before them when the action is proposed and before it is effected').").

Once an appeal is filed from an indefinite suspension, the appeal should not be dismissed without prejudice pending some other development. A continuance or alternative hearing arrangement should be requested if the appellant is incarcerated. See Moore v. Dept. of Treasury, 52 MSPR 362, 363-64 (1992). In practice AJs can and do DWOP indefinite suspension appeals. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')" to refile and to Chapter 3, "Postponement of Hearing."]

Nothing in the statutes directly authorizes an indefinite suspension. OPM defines the indefinite suspension through regulation, 5 CFR 752.402 (2022):

Indefinite suspension means the placing of an employee in a temporary status without duties and pay pending investigation, inquiry, or further agency action. The indefinite suspension continues for an indeterminate period of time and ends with the occurrence of the pending conditions set forth in the notice of action which may include the completion of any subsequent administrative action.

The genesis of the indefinite suspension was described in McClure v. USPS, 83 MSPR 605, 608 ¶ 6 (1990):

We find, first, that the confusion in this case has at least in part been engendered by the appellant's and the administrative judge's consideration of the action as something other than an adverse action taken under 5 USC Chapter 75. The administrative judge stated that there is no statutory right of appeal from an indefinite suspension, and while we agree that the term "'indefinite' suspension" is not used in the law, and such an action is peculiar in that it is imposed with no definitive end date, nonetheless it is nothing other than a Chapter 75-based adverse action. From the start, the Board has treated indefinite suspensions as "suspensions" under Chapter 75. See Martin v. Department of the Treasury, 10 MSPB 568, 12 MSPR 12, 17 (1982), aff'd in part and rev'd in part sub nom Brown v. Department of Justice, 715 F.2d 662 (D.C. Cir. 1983), and aff'd sub nom. Otherson v. Department of Justice, 728 F.2d 1513 (D.C. Cir. 1984). Our main reviewing court, the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has consistently done so, as well. See, e.g., Dunnington v. Department of Justice, 956 F.2d 1151, 1153 (Fed. Cir. 1992), noting that while it was unclear whether Congress intended that only suspensions imposed for a definite period were to be encompassed by 5 USC §§ 7511-7514, the court, its predecessor court--the Court of Claims, and the Board have applied those requirements, and "[w]e see no reason to stray from this course." Thus, the matter is more simply decided because, pursuant to Chapter 75, only suspensions of more than 14 days are appealable. We conclude that since this appellant's suspension lasted only 14 days, the Board did not have jurisdiction over it at any time. There was and is, therefore, no question to be resolved as to whether the agency insufficiently rescinded the action or whether the appellant filed his appeal prior to the rescission.
