1. Scope of MSPB Review

Under Board regulations, cases brought pursuant to the statute are referred to an MSPB-designated ALJ to conduct a hearing and to issue an initial decision. 5 CFR 1201.140(a) (2022). See SSA v. Burris, 39 MSPR 51, 53 (1988) (discussing decisional process under prior regulations calling for issuance of recommended decision). The agency may not take action proposed against an ALJ until that action has been authorized by the Board. Charges against ALJs must be supported by preponderant evidence. 5 USC 7701(c)(1)(B); Brennan v. DHHS, 787 F.2d 1559 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Charges must be sufficiently detailed to inform the ALJ of the acts he must refute or justify. The decision of the Board ALJ, now an initial decision (previously a recommended decision), must conform with 5 USC 557, the provision defining how agencies are to conduct adjudicative proceedings under the Administrative Procedure Act. Board review is obtained by filing a PFR from the initial decision (previously, by filing exceptions to a recommended decision). The Board addresses the PFR (previously the exceptions) in its final decision, although the respondent's concerns will not be addressed if they cannot be understood by the Board. See SSA v. Burris, 39 MSPR 51, 54 (1988).

The Board possesses the sole authority to establish the level of penalty that an agency may impose against an ALJ. A suggestion of a penalty by the employing agency is not given much deference by the Board. Nor does the Board appear to give much deference to the recommendations by its own judges concerning the penalty that should be sustained. See Dept. of Commerce v. Dolan, 39 MSPR 314, 317 (1988). [Refer later in this chapter to "Penalty Determination."] The Board does not conduct a separate nexus determination in ALJ cases; instead, the Board considers evidence that relates to nexus under the "good cause" standard. See SSA v. Long, 113 MSPR 190, 2010 MSPB 19 (2010), post. Long was followed in SSA v. Round, CB-7521-03-0012-M-1 (NP 1/28/2011) ("[N]exus is established because the misconduct occurred in the workplace and involves work-related matters.... Parker v. U.S. Postal Service, 819 F.2d 1113, 1116 (Fed. Cir. 1987). In any event, the Board has recently stated that it does not engage in a separate analysis of nexus per se in cases involving disciplinary actions brought against administrative law judges. See Social Security Administration v. Long, 113 M.S.P.R. 190, ¶ 46 (2010).").

Cases will ordinarily arise by the employing agency filing a complaint. Additionally, ALJs file appeals from actions--in the nature of constructive adverse actions--taken against them without compliance with statutory and regulatory procedures. Judicial review is in the Federal Circuit, although if there is an affirmative defense of discrimination, review would be in federal district court. See Tinsley v. Astrue, 501 Fed. Appx. 295 (4th Cir. 2012 NP) (also considering a claim of whistleblowing reprisal). [Refer later in this chapter to "Constructive Action Against Judge: Tunik."]
