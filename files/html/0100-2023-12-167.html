broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 08: Procedure on Late Submission, Part a: Affidavit or Sworn Statement Required
a. Affidavit or Sworn Statement Required

For either an extension of time to file a PFR or for a request to waive an expired time limit for good cause, the regulation requires that an extension request be supported by an affidavit or sworn statement that "must include a specific and detailed description of the circumstances alleged to constitute good cause, and it should be accompanied by any available documentation or other evidence supporting the matters asserted." 5 CFR 1201.114(f, g) (2022). Decisional law enforces the regulation. An explanation for the extension must be provided through either an affidavit or a declaration signed under the penalty of perjury in accordance with 28 USC 1746. Poynton v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 192, 195 (1991); see Smith v. Dept. of Army, 110 MSPR 50, 55 ¶ 14, 2008 MSPB 220 (2008) (noting requirement of affidavit or sworn statement to support reasons offered to excuse a late PFR). Some degree of formality should be observed: an extension is not obtained because counsel calls up the Clerk's office and leaves a message that there has been a "snag" preventing the preparation of a PFR. See Siler v. USPS, 76 MSPR 10, 14 (1997); see McNevin v. Dept. of Air Force, 77 MSPR 108, 112 (1997) ("Moreover, his statement that he left a voice mail request for an extension with the Clerk of the Board does not provide a basis for waiving the filing deadline"); Harris v. DMA, 40 MSPR 231, 232 (1989) (appellant submitted a letter explaining the delay, but the failure to submit an affidavit or sworn statement warranted dismissal of the late PFR). If an affidavit is submitted, it should be that, not just a submission labeled as an "affidavit." See Kennedy v. USPS, 55 MSPR 368 (1993) (Table). Documentation supporting the reasons offered in the affidavit may be provided along with the affidavit. But the reference in the regulation, 5 CFR 1201.114(f) (2022), to documentation is meaningful only if there is documentation that is pertinent. The statement of good cause may and should include factual information; if documentation exists, that is fine; if it does not exist, it cannot be produced. What is essential is the sworn declaration or affidavit demonstrating the good cause, supported--if possible--by documentation.

To be in proper form, the justification for the filing delay should be submitted in an affidavit or in a statement under oath pursuant to 28 USC 1746, should be complete and consistent with other facts of record, and should be accompanied by a certificate of service. The Board finds that completion of the form it supplies, "Motion to Ask Board to Accept Filing as Timely or to Set Aside the Deadline" complies with the necessary formalities, when properly completed, with the Board's requirement for an affidavit or sworn statement for a late PFR. Santiago v. USPS, 98 MSPR 179, 182 n.1 (2005).

Submissions by the appellant will not likely be given much weight if they are internally inconsistent on points material to the timeliness of the filing or the cause asserted for delay in the filing. See Winfree v. USPS, 98 MSPR 614, 617 ¶ 9 (2005) ("Further, the appellant has not provided an affidavit or statement signed under penalty of perjury as directed and required, and his unverified statement does not constitute good cause."); Lands v. Dept. of Air Force, 95 MSPR 593, 595 ¶ 6 (2004) ("Under Board precedent, any explanation for the untimeliness of a petition for review that is not submitted in the form of an affidavit or a statement signed under penalty of perjury is insufficient to establish the assertions it contains."); Ramos v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 462, 464 (1994) (submissions from the appellant concerning the timeliness of the refiling of a PFR were partially sworn, partially unsworn, and inconsistent: "The inconsistencies in the appellant's submission on timeliness thus cast doubt on the reliability of his statements concerning when he refiled his petition for review."), supra, aff'd, Ramos v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 94-3558); Labinghisa v. OPM, 52 MSPR 69, 74 (1991) (no good cause for delay of a PFR shown when the only mention by counsel of a medical problem was in the motion papers rather than in an affidavit or a sworn statement; additionally, the time pressure of counsel's practice was an insufficient reason for delay); Gendron v. Dept. of Army, 33 MSPR 100, 103 (1987) (letter from the appellant explaining a belated PFR did not meet the requirement of an affidavit or statement signed under penalty of perjury; citing Cantrell v. USPS, 32 MSPR 248, 250 (1987); Jacobo v. OPM, 32 MSPR 219, 220 (1987)).

The Board has been known to refuse to credit a justification that might have passed muster were it in proper form and consistent with other record evidence. See Williamson v. VA, 40 MSPR 4, 7 (1989). If the party seeking to excuse the delay files only an unsworn statement, the Board is likely to hold that the explanation does not constitute the good cause needed to waive the deadline. See Beckley v. USPS, 43 MSPR 397, 399 (1990); Dela Cruz v. OPM, 77 MSPR 311, 313 (1998) ("Where a party's explanation for the untimeliness of a petition for review is not submitted in the form of an affidavit or a statement signed under penalty of perjury, it is insufficient to establish the assertions it contains."); cf. Kolb v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 100, 103 (1990) (although the appellant did not provide an affidavit or sworn statement, the Board looked to other circumstances concerning the issuance of the initial decision to determine whether the appellant had good cause for delayed filing of his PFR).

Near misses may not count for much. See Bohannon v. USPS, 80 MSPR 447, 449 ¶ 6 (1998) ("This unsworn assertion [concerning date of receipt of initial decision]...does not meet requirements of 5 CFR 1201.114(f) because it was not accompanied by a motion for waiver or either an affidavit or statement signed under penalty of perjury"); Oliveras v. USPS, 64 MSPR 74, 76 (1994) (same result as Ellerbee, post); Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 63 MSPR 28, 30 (1994) ("Although the appellant's waiver request was notarized, it was not a sworn statement or a statement made under penalty of perjury."); Purk v. NASA, 58 MSPR 139, 141 (1993) ("Although the appellant asks the Board to 'consider' his response to the Clerk's order to be his 'affidavit or signed statement,' his statement does not meet the requirements of the regulation that the Clerk expressly cited in the order."); Ellerbee v. USPS, 58 MSPR 48, 49 (1993) ("Although the appellant's response is notarized, it is not in affidavit form nor signed under penalty of perjury."); Labelle v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 465, 467 (1993) ("Although the appellant's responses are notarized, neither is in affidavit form nor signed under penalty of perjury."); Kelly v. USPS, 57 MSPR 268, 270 n.3 (1993) (not deciding whether the requirements of § 1201.114(f) are met by a declaration by the appellant and counsel that "by their signatures they 'swear' that their statements" are true), overruled on other grounds by Gaydon v. USPS, 62 MSPR 198, 202 (1994); Menchaca v. USPS, 56 MSPR 479, 482 (1993) (even if the appellant's unsworn assertions were accepted, they would not have established good cause for the untimely filing of the PFR); Hall v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 169, 171 (1992) ("The appellant's later submission was apparently notarized, but it too was neither sworn nor made under penalty of perjury."); White v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 376, 378 (1992) ("Although this letter [explaining delay] was notarized, it was not a sworn statement or a statement made under penalty of perjury."); Headley v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 660, 662 (1992) (discounting as insufficient a statement that was called an affidavit but that was neither an affidavit nor a statement signed under penalty of perjury); Jackson v. DHHS, 51 MSPR 502, 504-05 (1991) (notarized cover letter but an unsworn statement was insufficient to meet Board requirements; neither was signed under the penalty of perjury); Murray v. Dept. of Army, 48 MSPR 338, 340 (1991) (declining to give weight to an unnotarized motion for a waiver characterized as a "sworn statement").

The sworn statement should comply with minimum requirements of an affidavit or the comparable statute, 28 USC 1746. Under that statute, the attestation is sufficient if it states as to the facts presented that: "I declare (or certify, verify, or state) under penalty of perjury under the laws of the United States of America that the foregoing is true and correct. Executed on (date). (Signature)."

The statement "I swear under God the above statement is truthful," was insufficient, according to Pope v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 352, 355 n.1 (1990). If a party offers a sworn statement, or affidavit, it should be on the basis of personal knowledge or it will be given little weight as to matters beyond the personal knowledge of the affiant. Reece v. OPM, 44 MSPR 345, 348 (1990) (Board accepted an affidavit from the attorney for the appellant explaining the circumstances of mailing performed by his legal assistant). The affidavit should address the reasons for the untimely PFR rather than the merits of the appeal. See Miller v. USPS, 54 MSPR 328, 330 (1992). The Judges' Handbook notes at Chapter 7, section 8: "Any time an AJ requires an affidavit or sworn statement from a party, he or she should refer the party to Appendix IV to part 1201 of the Board's regulations for a sample declaration under 28 U.S.C. § 1746."

As to presentation of sworn evidence supporting a request for waiver of a late PFR, Brown v. OPM, 31 MSPR 164, 166 (1986), explained:

In Ceja v. United States,...the Court held that unsworn declarations are equivalent to an affidavit and are entitled to great weight when unrebutted. In the present case, appellant presents his two unsworn declarations as proof that good cause exists for waiver of the regulatory time limit. We find that the facts in the present case are distinguishable from Ceja. In Ceja, the Court considered the unsworn statement of petitioner along with the unsworn corroborative statements of two co-workers. Appellant provides no such corroborative evidence. Second, appellant was inconsistent in his explanation for the delay. In his first letter, appellant states that the delay was due to the fact that he was unable to secure representation. In his second submission, appellant states that the delay was due to the fact that he "was sick and unable to get out of bed and get a letter written." We find that this inconsistency further weakens a case which does not present very strong evidence to support waiver of the regulatory time limit for filing a petition for review....
