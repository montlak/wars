9. Public Hearing; Closing the Hearing

For the record, the regulation, 5 CFR 1201.52 (2022), states:

(a) Closing the hearing. Hearings are generally open to the public; however, the judge may order a hearing or any part of a hearing closed when doing so would be in the best interests of a party, a witness, the public, or any other person affected by the proceeding. Any order closing the hearing will set out the reasons for the judge's decision. Any objections to the order will be made a part of the record.

(b) Electronic devices. Absent express approval from the judge, no two-way communications devices may be operated and/or powered on in the hearing room; all cell phones, text devices, and all other two-way communications devices shall be powered off in the hearing room. Further, no cameras, recording devices, and/or transmitting devices may be operated, operational, and/or powered on in the hearing room without the consent of the judge.

Chapter 4 of the Judges' Handbook adds:

11. PUBLIC HEARINGS. Generally, the Board's hearings are open to the public. However, the AJ may order a hearing or any part of a hearing closed when doing so would be in the best interests of the appellant, a witness, the public, or any other person affected by the proceeding. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.52. See also chapter 10, section 3 of this Handbook. If an AJ closes a hearing or a part of it, the AJ or court reporter must annotate the hearing transcript or CD to indicate that the testimony was taken during a closed hearing. A brief explanation setting forth the basis for closing the hearing should be included in the record. Any objections to the order should be made part of the record as well. In Wallace v. Department of Health & Human Services, 89 M.S.P.R. 178 (2001), the Board held that the AJ's authority to close all or part of a hearing and to offer other methods of assuring privacy renders invalid a claim that a witness was not called because of privacy concerns. Thus, such a witness's written statement in lieu of testimony is assigned little probative value. The AJ, therefore, should inform a part who interposes an objection based on privacy of the option of closing the hearing during that witness's testimony.

If a hearing is open but the appeal has attracted the public's attention, the AJ may be able to avoid the possibility of disruption by having the public attendees view the hearing on video from another room at the RO or FO, if one is available.

Absent express approval from the AJ, no electronic device such as cell phones, text devices, or any other two-way communications device may be operated and/or powered on in the hearing room. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.52(b). Further, cameras, recording devices and/or transmitting devices are not to be operated, operational and/or powered on in the hearing room without the consent of the AJ. Absent the AJ's approval, all such electronic devices must be turned off in the hearing room. Id.

If the hearing is open despite the appellant's request that it be closed, the Board does not set aside the AJ's decision if the appellant does not demonstrate that his ability to make out his case was prejudiced by the AJ's denial of a closed hearing. Copeland v. OPM, 9 MSPR 127, 128-29 (1981). An initial decision is not likely to be set aside if the public hearing is curtailed, unless the appellant can somehow show that his case was impaired as a result of the closure or partial closure of the hearing. See Hijar v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 133, 134 n.1, 6 MSPB 121 (1981). An open or public hearing may create problems concerning privacy, security, or tranquility of the process if there are interruptions because of people traipsing in and out of the hearing room, noise created by conversations among spectators, or distraction caused by media involvement. Control of the hearing rests with the AJ.

Although records developed in Board hearings are considered covered by the Privacy Act [refer to Chapter 1, "MSPB Records"], the hearings permitting discussion of those records are open to the public unless a party requests that the hearing be closed. If a session is closed, nonparticipants are asked to leave. Those leaving will be told when the hearing may again be open to the public, if that time can be estimated by the AJ. Sequestration orders may be entered to ensure that witnesses do not listen to other witnesses testify and protective orders may be entered to exclude specific individuals for good reason. The legal underpinnings of open hearings in civil service cases are explored in Fitzgerald v. Hampton, 467 F.2d 755, 762-68 (D.C. Cir. 1972); see Fitzgerald v. Hampton, 329 F. Supp. 997 (D.D.C. 1971). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Witness Control; Sequestration."]

There is no exhaustive list of reasons for closing a hearing. A hearing, or a portion of it, could be closed to protect the privacy of individuals (for example, when the appeal involves sexual harassment, patient abuse, conduct involving children), or in matters involving confidential information (national security, taxpayer, patient records, or descriptions of certain medical conditions), or some other sensitive situation (a witness whose testimony might be used in some form of civil litigation against the witness, making the witness reluctant to testify).
