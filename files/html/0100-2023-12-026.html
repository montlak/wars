e. Self-Serving Testimony; Bias; Criminal Conviction; Past Misconduct

"Self-serving testimony and documentary evidence is entitled to weight and must be evaluated for credibility in the same manner as all other testimony presented by the parties." Baldwin v. VA, 111 MSPR 586, 594 ¶ 23, 2009 MSPB 126 (2009); prior decision, Baldwin v. VA, 109 MSPR 392, 2008 MSPB 169 (2008), Baldwin v. VA, 115 MSPR 413, 418-19 ¶ 13, 2010 MSPB 221 (2010), on further review following counsel fee petition); see Hemby v. United States, 185 Ct. Cl. 140, 147-48 (1968) ("It was arbitrary and grossly unfair to reject this evidence out of hand, and not even consider it merely because it was 'self-serving.' It had to be considered and weighed. The fact that the evidence was self-serving can only go to the probative weight of the testimony."); Henderson v. OPM, 109 MSPR 529, 538-39 ¶ 18, 2008 MSPB 191 (2008) (an appellant's testimony should not be discredited as self-serving, since most testimony that an appellant is likely to give, other than admissions, can be said to be self-serving); Bennett v. Dept. of Air Force, 84 MSPR 132, 138 ¶ 10 (1999) ("Although it is true that the self-serving nature of a witness' testimony may be considered as a factor in assessing the probative weight of the evidence, it does not by itself provide sufficient grounds for disbelieving the testimony."); Mancini v. VA, 84 MSPR 70, 74 ¶ 10 (1999) (AJ erred in discounting the appellant's testimony solely because it was self-serving); Nicoletti v. DOJ, 60 MSPR 244, 249 (1993) ("[A]n appellant's testimony should not be discredited as self-serving because most testimony that he is likely to give, other than admissions, can be characterized as self-serving[.]"); McKoy v. DOD, 33 MSPR 195, 199 (1987) ("It is well established that an appellant's testimony should not be discredited simply because it is self-serving, since most testimony that an appellant is likely to give, other than admissions, can be characterized as self-serving."); LaMonnier v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 453, 454 n.1 (1984) ("We are not much impressed by the school of thought which discounts testimony because it is self-serving."); Graber v. OPM, 20 MSPR 267, 269 (1984) ("[T]he fact that testimony may be self-serving does not necessarily render it untrue."); Amell v. GSA, 7 MSPR 531, 534, 7 MSPB 382 (1981) ("Moreover, we reject the presiding official's characterization of the appellant's denial as 'self-serving.' As we recently noted, 'Most testimony is self-serving to some degree.' Gamble v. U.S. Postal Service, 6 MSPB 487, 488 (1981)."); Hall v. VA, 7 MSPR 161, 162-63, 7 MSPB 79 (1981) ("Obviously her testimony was intended to defend herself against the charge, but the fact that it may have been 'self-serving' does not automatically render it untrue. This is especially so in this case because appellant's testimony was substantially corroborated by statements of eye-witnesses to the incident[.]"); cf. Elder v. Dept. of Air Force, 124 MSPR 12, 2016 MSPB 41, ¶ 25 (2016) ("However, the Board will not discredit an appellant's testimony solely because it can be characterized as self-serving. See Thompson v. Department of the Army, 122 M.S.P.R. 372, ¶ 25 (2015) (observing that most testimony that an appellant is likely to give can be characterized as self-serving and finding that it is improper to discredit it solely on that basis). Further, we decline to find that other witnesses, such as the appellant's supervisor, the deciding official, and staff from the agency's Legal Office, had no interest in the outcome of the appeal. See id. (observing that a supervisor who proposed an appellant's removal may have an interest in the outcome of the resulting Board appeal). All of these individuals would, for different reasons, have an interest in ensuring the agency's success in this appeal."); Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 414-16 (1997) (testimony of a victim of sexual harassment was credited as a result of her complaints of the conduct made to others as the conduct occurred).

That self-serving testimony need not be rejected does not mean that testimony not impeached through cross-examination must be credited. See Render v. VA, 90 MSPR 441, 444 ¶ 7 (2001) (that the appellant's testimony was in narrative form and not subject to cross-examination did not mean that the AJ was precluded from discrediting it based on baseless charges by the appellant against other agency officials). Despite holdings that testimony is not to be discounted only because it is self-serving, the Board used this reasoning to resolve a credibility dispute without offering any other explanation for its result. See Nordell v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 276, 282 (1983). There is no error if an AJ discredits an appellant's testimony as self-serving if the finding is made as part of a more complete analysis of the testimony, inconsistencies, and credibility evaluations. Woody v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 547, 549-50 (1984); see Doe v. USPS, 95 MSPR 493, 498 ¶ 10 (2004) ("Although it was error to reject the appellant's testimony as self-serving...the AJ's credibility determination was otherwise and on the whole consistent with Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453, 458 (1987).").

In Gamble v. USPS, 6 MSPR 578, 580-81, 6 MSPB 487 (1981), involving a charge of welfare fraud against a postal employee, the testimony of the appellant and his wife contradicted statements they made in affidavits obtained by postal investigators. The appellant explained that he was told by the investigators that if they signed the statements prepared for him and his wife, he would not lose his job. The investigators denied that the statements were obtained through coercion or false pretenses. The AJ discredited the appellant's testimony because it was self-serving and contradicted by his earlier sworn statement. In its review the Board determined that the AJ's analysis was logically deficient, stating that "[m]ost testimony is self-serving to some degree, and we think that an investigator would have a substantial interest in rebutting any claim that he had used improper methods to obtain a confession."

One factor in determining credibility is whether the witness is biased. "While bias is an important factor, it is only one part of a credibility determination." Mays v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3241). A particular employment status does not imply bias. Special Counsel v. Spears, 75 MSPR 639, 672 (1997), suggested that "skills such as managerial ability have little bearing on a witness's credibility." If there is evidence that a witness referred to the appellant in disparaging, racially biased terms, the AJ should specifically assess the existence and weight of those remarks to determine the bias of the witness and the effect of that bias on his credibility. Chin v. Dept. of Treasury, 44 MSPR 201, 204 (1990); see Edmond v. TVA, 23 MSPR 489, 491 (1984) (AJ should have permitted testimony offered to show that the proposing official falsely charged the appellant because of a supervisor's dislike of the appellant); Paniagua v. GSA, 23 MSPR 229, 232 (1984) (discounting the testimony of an estranged spouse: "[She] has been attempting to get him fired for a number of years, and has unjustly accused him of theft, lying, and engaging in various improprieties while employed by the agency[.]").

Although a witness may have a bias against the appellant, demonstrated, for example, through civil litigation against the appellant, it does not necessarily follow that the whole of the testimony of that witness will be discredited. See Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 551-52 (1994). Nor, as pointed out in an Opinion (concurring and dissenting in part) of Judge Nichols of the Claims Court does bias suggest that the entire adverse action be thrown out on that basis alone, Reynolds v. United States, 454 F.2d 1368, 1378 (Ct. Cl. 1972):

Suppose Cole's role be assimilated to that of a complaining witness. What the court is holding, sub silentio, is that an adverse action is invalid if a complaining witness is biased. We hear a lot about courts favoring the criminals, these days, but I never heard of the most anti-police tribunal ordering a new trial because a prosecution witness was biased. If defense counsel was prevented from cross-examining to show bias, that would of course be another matter. Here, the court has considered the scope of cross-examination that was allowed and has no criticism to offer. Thus, if our trial commissioner reports that Cole was indeed biased, I shall (a) not be astonished, and (b) ask what of it? If the hearing tribunals and adjudicating officials here involved were incapable of discounting a complainant's bias and deciding fairly despite it, our whole machinery to secure fairness in adverse action cases is useless. We might as well junk it and substitute something else, trial by battle, possibly.

The AJ should permit a party to introduce the testimony of an individual who can show that the testimony of significant witnesses may be of inappropriate motivation or doubtful credibility. Testimony should have been permitted to show the agency investigator promised an individual (who became a witness) a job if he cooperated with the investigation. Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 54 MSPR 356, 363 (1992); see Garst v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 371, 380 n.3 (1993) ("the administrative judge abused his discretion in ignoring and disallowing the appellant's evidence of bias on the part of agency witnesses and then discounting on the basis of their friendship with the appellant, his witnesses' testimony"); Bonds v. USPS, 38 MSPR 80, 84 (1988) (of the testimony of a husband and wife working in the same location: "[T]he possibility of bias based on certain relationships and circumstances is a significant factor bearing on a witness' credibility which must be addressed."). An individual's dual role as both the sole complaining witness and the deciding official raises concerns about the credibility of his account of disputed events. See Eichner v. USPS, 83 MSPR 202, 208 ¶ 12 (1999).

The AJ may credit testimony from a disinterested third party, not employed by the agency, that supports the agency's case. See Bonsu v. VA, 3 MSPR 125, 127 (1980). The AJ may favor testimony from someone who is placed at risk by that testimony. See Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 186, 196 (1994) ("Ben's and Slim's testimony is compelling because it was contrary to their interests to admit involvement in misconduct and thus they would have little motivation to fabricate."). Just as evidence of bias is admissible, lack of evidence of bias is probative, Cheng v. USDA, 84 MSPR 144, 146 n.1 (1999):

Four of the appellant's co-workers testified that they heard the appellant make the alleged comments on one or more of the three occasions specified under the agency's charge. The appellant, in turn, testified that she did not make the comments on any of the three occasions. She also suggested that her co-workers were either untruthful or misunderstood her. The testimony of all four of the agency's witnesses, however, was consistent, whereas the appellant's testimony was uncorroborated. Further, it is unlikely that all four of the witnesses would misunderstand the appellant's words, and the appellant failed to show that they had a motive to lie.

See Davis v. Dept. of Navy, DA-0752-14-0285-I-1 (NP 7/5/2016) ("Considering that Maj M.H. was a disinterested observer and that her account is consistent with the signed statement provided by Ms. X, we discern no error in the administrative judge's decision to credit Maj M.H.'s sworn statements over those of the appellant."); Lowe v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 299, 304 (1988) (crediting the testimony of a witness who did not know the appellant and had no bias against him).

A criminal record may be considered as a negative factor in a credibility analysis. See Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 554 ¶ 14 (1999) (as to alleged involvement of the appellant in criminal scheme, AJ could discredit testimony of principal supporting witness who "had served time in both federal and state prisons for credit card fraud, passing counterfeit currency, burglary, and forgery"). Yet past misconduct should not influence a credibility determination unless the misconduct involved the appellant's propensity to tell the truth, Eichner v. USPS, 83 MSPR 202, 207 ¶ 10 (1999):

...An administrative judge may consider an employee's prior misconduct in order to impeach his credibility only when the prior misconduct involved the employee's propensity for telling the truth. See Hawkins v. Smithsonian Institution, 73 MSPR 397, 403 (1997). Here, however, the prior misconduct relied on by the administrative judge was the appellant's deliberate disruption of Postal operations, distracting employees, and disobeying direct orders to return to his work unit on February 27, 1996, for which he received a letter of warning and a 14-day suspension. Since this prior misconduct did not involve the appellant's propensity for telling the truth, the administrative judge improperly considered it in determining the credibility of the appellant's testimony. See Hawkins, 73 MSPR at 403.
