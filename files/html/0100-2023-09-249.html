6. Interrogatories

Rule 33 of the FRCP defines the use of interrogatories (Board regulations establish the time for response).

Interrogatories to Parties

(a) In General.

(1) Number.

Unless otherwise stipulated or ordered by the court, a party may serve on any other party no more than 25 written interrogatories, including all discrete subparts. Leave to serve additional interrogatories may be granted to the extent consistent with Rule 26(b)(1) and (2). [MSPB regulation 5 CFR 1201.73(e)(1) states "Absent prior approval by the judge, interrogatories served by parties upon another party or a nonparty may not exceed 25 in number, including all discrete subparts."]

(2) Scope.

An interrogatory may relate to any matter that may be inquired into under Rule 26(b). An interrogatory is not objectionable merely because it asks for an opinion or contention that relates to fact or the application of law to fact, but the court may order that the interrogatory need not be answered until designated discovery is complete, or until a pretrial conference or some other time.

(b) Answers and Objections.

(1) Responding Party.

The interrogatories must be answered:

(A) by the party to whom they are directed; or

(B) if that party is a public or private corporation, a partnership, an association, or a governmental agency, by any officer or agent, who must furnish the information available to the party.

(2) Time to Respond.

The responding party must serve its answers and any objections within 30 days after being served with the interrogatories. A shorter or longer time may be stipulated to under Rule 29 or be ordered by the court. [MSPB regulation 5 CFR 1201.73(d)(1) sets a 20 day response time.]

(3) Answering Each Interrogatory.

Each interrogatory must, to the extent it is not objected to, be answered separately and fully in writing under oath.

(4) Objections.

The grounds for objecting to an interrogatory must be stated with specificity. Any ground not stated in a timely objection is waived unless the court, for good cause, excuses the failure.

(5) Signature.

The person who makes the answers must sign them, and the attorney who objects must sign any objections.

(c) Use.

An answer to an interrogatory may be used to the extent allowed by the Federal Rules of Evidence.

(d) Option to Produce Business Records.

If the answer to an interrogatory may be determined by examining, auditing, compiling, abstracting, or summarizing a party's business records (including electronically stored information), and if the burden of deriving or ascertaining the answer will be substantially the same for either party, the responding party may answer by:

(1) specifying the records that must be reviewed, in sufficient detail to enable the interrogating party to locate and identify them as readily as the responding party could; and

(2) giving the interrogating party a reasonable opportunity to examine and audit the records and to make copies, compilations, abstracts, or summaries.

Interrogatories are written questions or, as often, directives (e.g., "state the following") posing as questions. They should not be argumentative, nor should they be so complex that they cannot be understood. Pages of preliminary instructions and definitions, much in vogue, like books on grammar, are much ignored. Interrogatories should be answered directly and honestly after conducting whatever investigation is necessary to obtain the requested information. In a government agency the representative responsible for preparation of the answers provides information for the whole organization; a government representative must interview those persons who have the knowledge to answer questions or find records to provide answers. Although an agency is to respond to an interrogatory as an organization, an interrogatory may be directed toward a specific agency employee for response. See Kiser v. Dept. of Educ., 66 MSPR 372, 381-82 (1995). Those providing information do so on official time. 5 CFR 1201.73(a) (2022). An appellant provides information or documents under his control, including those nonprivileged documents in the possession of counsel.

Board regulations governing discovery do not require that interrogatories be answered under oath. Matson v. OPM, 105 MSPR 547, 553 n.3, 2007 MSPB 122 (2007), explained that the requirement of sworn answers under the FRCP is instructive rather than controlling in proceedings before the Board. It is ordinary practice, however, as the Judges' Handbook, post, suggests, to provide sworn answers to interrogatories.

Current Board regulations apparently contemplate the use of interrogatories to nonparties. 5 CFR 1201.73 (2022) provides, in part:

(e) Limits on the number of discovery requests.

(1) Absent prior approval by the judge, interrogatories served by parties upon another party or a nonparty may not exceed 25 in number, including all discrete subparts.

Or do they?

In April of 2008, the Board amended the discovery regulations to allow interrogatories only between the parties. The Federal Register notice accompanying Board regulatory revisions on April 3, 2008, 73 Fed. Reg. 18149, stated in the comments section of the notice:

5 CFR 1201.72(c) is clarified by adding the words "to parties" after "interrogatories'" in response to a comment received from a practitioner suggesting that there may be some confusion about whether interrogatories may be served on nonparties.

Before then the regulation, 5 CFR 1201.72(c) provided:

Methods. Parties may use one or more of the methods provided under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. These methods include written interrogatories, depositions, requests for production of documents or things for inspection or copying, and requests for admission.

The regulatory language as of April of 2008 provided, 5 CFR 1201.72(c) (2022), and still provides:

Methods. Parties may use one or more of the methods provided under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. These methods include written interrogatories to parties, depositions, requests for production of documents or things for inspection or copying, and requests for admission.

The inconsistency as to use of interrogatories for nonparties needs correction.

Consider the possibilities of interrogatories. Assume an appellant was discharged for stealing office supplies. An appropriate question to the agency is:

State the following information as to every individual in the Appellant's Division who during the past three years has been charged with removal of or theft of government property or funds:

a. Name and address;

b. Date of the incident and nature of the charge;

c. Penalty proposed or, if no penalty was proposed, the reason that no penalty was proposed;

d. Nature of the defense raised by the employee, if any;

e. Final disciplinary action that was taken against the employee following the reply, if any;

f. If disciplinary action was proposed but not taken, the reason that no action was taken;

g. If the disciplinary action taken was less than that proposed, the reason that the penalty was reduced following the proposal;

h. Whether and when any complaint, appeal or grievance was filed from the disciplinary action taken and the nature and date of the result of any grievance, appeal, or complaint.

With the answer to this one question, an appellant determines if there is disparate application of penalties. If there appears to be some likelihood of a successful defense on that basis, another discovery device is used concurrently or consecutively--a request for documents pertaining to similar cases.

Assume an employee was discharged for absence without leave and files an appeal. The agency suspects that the employee may defend the case on the theory that he was entitled to sick leave and that he requested sick leave from a supervisor who informally approved the request. The agency may question the employee, assuming this information was not forthcoming in the employee's reply to the proposed action or in the appeal filed by the employee:

State the nature of your defense to the removal action, describing in particular:

a. Where were you on the days covered in the removal proposal charging you with AWOL?

b. For what reason were you absent from work?

c. Who else (providing names, addresses and telephone numbers) is aware of your whereabouts on those days?

d. If you sought medical treatment for any illness or injury you suffered during the time you were charged AWOL, state the name, address, and telephone number of the physician or other individual from whom you sought treatment, describe the treatment you received, and explain whether you informed any employee of the agency of that medical treatment prior to your dismissal.

e. Did you seek leave from the agency for any period of time charged as AWOL? If so, state when that leave was sought, what type of leave was sought, from whom it was sought, how the request was made, and the nature of the response from the individual that you contacted concerning the leave.

Interrogatories have their limits. Although the answers to them should be produced by witnesses and in federal litigation practice are provided under oath (although that requirement does not seem to be adhered to by the Board, there is still the Federal False Statement Act that arguably applies), the response will ordinarily be prepared by counsel. In Tinsley v. OPM, 34 MSPR 70, 75 (1987), a suitability case, the Board went so far as to conclude that agency attorneys could sign substantive interrogatory answers directed to their clients:

[T]he Federal Rules of Civil Procedure do not require that an attorney certify answers to interrogatories addressed to a government agency. See Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Rules 26(g) and 33(a). Under Rule 33(a), an attorney of a government agency, such as the OPM attorney who signed the interrogatories in the present case, is permitted to sign answers to interrogatories addressed to an agency, regardless of whether the attorney has first-hand knowledge of the facts contained therein. See J. Moore, J. Lucas, D. Epstein, Moore's Federal Practice, § 33.07 (2nd Ed. 1982).

The Board's rules do not specifically provide for an appellant's interrogatories to be designated for response by particular individuals. See Webster v. Dept. of Energy, DC-0752-13-0280-I-2 (NP 7/6/2015) ("We know of no authority, nor does the appellant cite any, which would allow her to require that a particular agency office provide answers to her discovery requests.").
