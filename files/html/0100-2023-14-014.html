broida mspb Chapter 07: Nexus and Penalty Determination Chapter 07: Nexus and Penalty Determination, Subchapter 01: Concept of Nexus, Section B: Specific Nexus Determinations, Subsection 04: Off-Duty Drug Offense, Part a: Drug Activity Involving Agency Time or Facilities
a. Drug Activity Involving Agency Time or Facilities

Activities involving drugs on agency premises during work hours bear clear nexus to actions taken to enhance service efficiency. See Zimmerman v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 454, 456-57 (1984). The off-duty possession of drugs in a car that was on agency property affected the agency's ability to maintain discipline and order; one offense warranted removal. McClain v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 464, 466 (1984). Nexus was established by drug-related activity planned on agency premises. Taylor v. USPS, 23 MSPR 48, 50 (1984); see Ingram v. Dept. of Air Force, 53 MSPR 101, 105 (1992) ("at least some of the arrangements for the off duty drug transfers occurred on duty"). Nexus was established as to a drug sale that was at least partially arranged on the premises of the facility. Nexus was shown when an employee engaged in illegal conduct that related to or exacerbated an existing condition that could severely affect the mission of the agency. Hernandez v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3444). A 90-day suspension of a mechanical engineer who possessed and sold drugs on a military base was based on sufficient nexus. Jaworski v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 499, 501 (1984). The appellant, a program manager, had an important and sensitive professional position. His involvement with drugs showed a lack of judgment. A supervisor said he lost confidence in the appellant. Additionally, as a result of the incident, the appellant's security clearance was revoked; work assignments had to be readjusted.

Campbell v. DLA, 37 MSPR 691, 696 (1988), determined that, as to a head accounting technician in whom supervisors and coworkers professed a loss of confidence, nexus existed because the off-duty possession and sale of drugs occurred after the appellant had marijuana in her possession on agency premises and prior to the arrest of the appellant on agency premises with drugs in her possession. The Board observed in Campbell that "[t]here is sufficient nexus between misconduct and the efficiency of the service where the misconduct occurs in part at work." Nexus was established by showing that drug sales were made to agency employees and that the appellant made arrangements for the sales while on duty. Scott v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 113, 114 n.* (1985); see Moe v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0285-I-1 (NP 10/21/2015) (drug use five years earlier sustained discharge: "Specifically, the administrative judge found that the appellant's misconduct--'using and transacting in cocaine with coworkers--was intentional and repeated, and it was extremely serious, particularly because his job required him to work with heavy machinery in dangerous conditions.'...Additionally, the administrative judge considered the agency's evidence that it has a consistent practice of removing employees who have engaged in illegal drug activities and that it removed at least two other employees for off-duty drug use, even though they had more seniority than the appellant, less recent drug use, and no prior discipline.").

Harding v. U.S. Naval Academy, 567 Fed. Appx. 920, 925, 928 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), found nexus and declined to overturn a removal upheld by a labor arbitrator involving an employee who drove onto an agency facility under some degree of influence of unlawful drugs:

...there has never been any dispute that Ms. Harding acted intentionally in using cocaine and in driving her vehicle onto the NSA grounds while in an impaired state.... Indeed, in her response to the notice of proposed removal, she effectively admitted that she had driven onto the NSA facility intentionally, i.e., with the purpose of retrieving a key for the banquet room she intended to use that night.

...

...We conclude that substantial evidence supports the arbitrator's conclusion that the agency proved the requisite nexus between Ms. Harding's conduct and her duties to justify disciplinary action.

The USNA work rules and Executive Order No. 12564 make clear that even off-duty drug use is prohibited conduct for employees. The arbitrator found that the proof of nexus was clear in light of those rules. He further found that the evidence of nexus was buttressed by the fact that Ms. Harding's off-duty misconduct of driving under the influence of drugs or alcohol occurred on the NSA grounds, which are Navy property.

In Executive Order No. 12564, the President made an express finding directed to the nexus between illegal drug use and federal employment, concluding that "[t]he use of illegal drugs, on or off duty, by Federal employees impairs the efficiency of Federal departments and agencies." 3 C.F.R. 224, 225(1987). Case law from this court likewise supports the arbitrator's conclusion that there is a sufficient nexus between illegal drug use, even off duty, and federal employment. See Rice v. Dep't of the Treasury, 998 F.2d 997, 999 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("The law is well settled that an employee can be removed for off-duty possession and use of...illegal drugs."); Sanders v. U.S. Postal Serv., 801 F.2d 1328, 1332 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (citing cases). Ms. Harding argues that the principle of those cases does not apply to circumstances such as hers, in which only a single use of drugs was charged. In fact, however, this court has applied the principle set forth in those cases to sustain removal actions in other cases involving charges of only a single off-duty use of illegal drugs. See Stump v. Dep't of Transp., 761 F.2d 680 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Peterson v. Dep't of the Navy, 268 F. App'x 961 (Fed. Cir. 2008). In light of those authorities and the evidence relating to Ms. Harding's impaired driving on Navy property, we sustain the arbitrator's conclusion that the USNA proved a nexus between Ms. Harding's misconduct and her job responsibilities. Accordingly, we uphold the arbitrator's decision sustaining Ms. Harding's removal.
