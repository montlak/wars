1. Burden

An AJ erred by denying a request for the production of payroll records requested that were by the appellant to demonstrate inaccuracies in relevant time and attendance records. Golden v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 462, 465, 7 MSPB 326 (1981). The AJ thought that the time and attendance records would be more accurate, rendering the payroll records repetitious. As to burden, the Board found unpersuasive the agency argument that the records would be difficult to produce because they were not located at the establishment where the appellant was employed. The Board concluded: "Generally, an appellant's request for production of evidence, if related to a material issue in the case, should be granted in the absence of a positive showing by the agency that it would be unduly burdensome, repetitious or irrelevant." The appeal was remanded for production of the records and an opportunity for the parties to further supplement the record.

The Board's regulations allow the AJ to limit or preclude burdensome discovery, 5 CFR 1201.72(d) (2022):

(d) Limitations. The judge may limit the frequency or extent of use of the discovery methods permitted by these regulations. Such limitations may be imposed if the judge finds that:

(1) The discovery sought is cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive;

(2) The party seeking discovery has had sufficient opportunity by discovery in the action to obtain the information sought; or

(3) The burden or expense of the proposed discovery outweighs its likely benefit.

Discovery may be burdensome. A disciplinary action taken for AWOL at one Army base should not require the Army, during discovery, to list every AWOL action it has taken worldwide. See, e.g., Hatley v. VA, 9 MSPR 585, 589 (1982) (requested nationwide compilation of adverse action statistics involving the review of 1,600 files: an undertaking that was "not warranted in the absence of any evidence concerning age discrimination other than appellant's own allegations"). An appellant who defends an action for disrespectful speech should not have to list and describe every conversation that he has had with each supervisor at an installation over a period of years. The discovery should have reasonable boundaries regarding the time, events, and organizational components covered. If an interrogatory is posed that is burdensome rather than inconvenient, the objecting party must enter an objection and should supply an affidavit by someone (other than counsel) who can state the degree of burden that would be involved in preparing the response to the question. The party claiming burden has the burden of proving it through specifics rather than by generalized protestations of "oppression" or "burden."

Limits were placed on the length of a deposition of a high-ranking agency official in SSA v. Manion, 19 MSPR 298, 301 (1984) (respondent had the choice of a three-hour in-person deposition or a one-hour telephonic deposition). Reasonableness of discovery was examined as to an appellant who requested that the agency provide information for a period of four years concerning actions against other employees who had taken unscheduled leave due to sickness, and concerning individuals who had taken more than 48 hours of leave during a fiscal quarter, excluding vacation time. The agency asserted, and the AJ agreed, that the requests were burdensome and too broad. But the Board determined that discovery was improperly restricted, Abbott v. USPS, 27 MSPR 442, 444 (1985):

In making the determination whether an interrogatory is so broad and all inclusive as to be burdensome, the courts balance the burden on the interrogated party against the benefit that having the information would provide to the party submitting the interrogatory.... However, the courts have also recognized that the scope of discovery in Title VII cases is extensive.... Thus, an allegation of discrimination is a factor to be balanced on the benefit side.

Considered in light of the above principles, we do not find that the reasons set forth by the agency for refusing to answer the interrogatories constitute an adequate basis for denying appellant's motions....

Discovery is burdensome when the requestor already has the information sought. In Zimmerman v. DHUD, 61 MSPR 75, 81-82 (1994), the Board replied to the appellant's protest over denied discovery by directing, on remand, that the AJ order the appellant to produce whatever documents he had that fell within a specific discovery request. The Board referenced the authority for its directive at n.26 of its decision:

See United Cigar-Whelan Stores Corp. v. Philip-Morris, Inc., 21 F.R.D. 107 (S.D.N.Y. 1957) (discovery proceedings should not be utilized to cast upon the defendant the burden of establishing the plaintiff's case when the plaintiff can at least as readily establish the requested facts); United States v. Renault, Inc., 27 F.R.D. 23 (S.D.N.Y. 1960) (objections to interrogatories seeking lists of all documents bearing in any way on business practices would be sustained when most of the information requested was readily available to the defendants from their own file)....

Although parties have the right to notice the place of depositions, an issue of practicality may arise. If the appellant cannot afford to travel, and if the agency will not make alternative arrangements, it may be unreasonable for an AJ to draw an adverse inference against the appellant who cannot afford to attend the deposition. See Swafford v. TVA, 30 MSPR 130, 134 n.3 (1986).

Practitioners on both sides cast objections as to burden through discovery responses. If an objection is made as to burden, proper practice is to support the objection with a description of the time and expense or other problem associated with production of the requested information. Then the recipient of the discovery response may be in a position, before filing a motion to compel, to reframe or adjust the parameters of the request to meet the objection, or at least to lessen the asserted burden. [Refer to Chapter 17, "Ruling on Discovery."]

Watkins v. DOD, DC-1221-13-0230-W-3 (NP 1/31/2023), an IRA case, found unobjectionable a requirement that the appellant bear the cost of a forensic examination of an agency email system. The Board noted that the AJ "granted the appellant's request for a forensic examination of the agency's email system, provided that any such examination would take place at the appellant's expense, and would be a recoverable cost should he prevail in his appeal."
