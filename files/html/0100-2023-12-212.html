5. Reply to Response

5 CFR 1201.114 (2022) results from a 2012 regulatory amendment allowing the following pleadings, including a reply to a response to a PFR:

Petition and cross petition for review--content and procedure.

(a) Pleadings allowed. Pleadings allowed on review include a petition for review, a cross petition for review, a response to a petition for review, a response to a cross petition for review, and a reply to a response to a petition for review.

(1) A petition for review is a pleading in which a party contends that an initial decision was incorrectly decided in whole or in part.

(2) A cross petition for review has the same meaning as a petition for review but is used to describe a pleading that is filed by a party when another party has already filed a timely petition for review.

(3) A response to a petition for review and a cross petition for review may be contained in a single pleading.

(4) A reply to a response to a petition for review is limited to the factual and legal issues raised by another party in the response to the petition for review. It may not raise new allegations of error.

(5) No pleading other than the ones described in this paragraph will be accepted unless the party files a motion with and obtains leave from the Clerk of the Board. The motion must describe the nature of and need for the pleading.

Page limits apply to the reply. Under 5 CFR 1201.114 (2022):

(h) Length limitations. A petition for review, a cross petition for review, or a response to a petition for review, whether computer generated, typed, or handwritten, is limited to 30 pages or 7500 words, whichever is less. A reply to a response to a petition for review is limited to 15 pages or 3750 words, whichever is less. Computer generated and typed pleadings must use no less than 12 point typeface and 1-inch margins and must be double spaced and only use one side of a page. The length limitation is exclusive of any table of contents, table of authorities, attachments, and certificate of service. A request for leave to file a pleading that exceeds the limitations prescribed in this paragraph must be received by the Clerk of the Board at least 3 days before the filing deadline. Such requests must give the reasons for a waiver as well as the desired length of the pleading and are granted only in exceptional circumstances. The page and word limits set forth above are maximum limits. Parties are not expected or required to submit pleadings of the maximum length. Typically, a well-written petition for review is between 5 and 10 pages long.

Before the regulatory revision, replies were prohibited or, if submitted, ignored. There were some individuals or agencies that, having filed a PFR and read the response, desired to file a reply to the response, an administrative last word. In judicial proceedings, replies are generally permitted, but it was not so in Board litigation. The policy was that replies would be ignored by the Board. The PFR was to be complete when filed--no further submissions or addenda were allowed, White v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 672, 674 n.2 (1984), unless the Board granted leave to submit additional evidence or unless there was a showing that evidence was unavailable earlier. Miller v. DLA, 29 MSPR 205, 205 n.* (1985); see Barratt v. USPS, 68 MSPR 592 (1995) (Table) ("We have not considered the appellant's reply to the agency's response to the petition for review because it was submitted after the record closed and he has not shown that it was based on evidence that was not previously available."); Marley v. USPS, 20 MSPR 555, 556 n.1 (1984) (stating the rule against responsive briefs); Atkinson v. Dept. of Air Force, 18 MSPR 691, 692 n.1 (1984) ("The Board's regulations do not provide for the submission of pleadings beyond the response to the petition."); cf. Santella v. Special Counsel, 86 MSPR 48, 54 ¶ 10 (2000) ("Thus, since there is no provision in the Board's regulations for a reply to responses to a petition for review, and since we do not believe the Board would derive a significant benefit from making an exception to that rule in this case, we deny the motion as to the reply.").

The practice before 2012 having been described, there were occasional exceptions, as in Hasanadka v. OPM, 116 MSPR 636, 640 n.1, 2011 MSPB 77 (2011), when the Board allowed a reply when OPM raised a dispositive issue in its opposition to the appellant's PFR: "The Board's regulations do not provide for a reply by the party filing a petition for review. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201. 114(d). However, because the issue of res judicata was not specifically raised until OPM filed its response to the petition for review, we have considered the appellant's arguments on that issue." When a reply brief was accepted, it should only respond to new arguments raised in the opposing party's principal brief. The reply brief was not to raise new arguments. See Special Counsel v. Kehoe, 46 MSPR 112, 118 (1990).
