d. Denial of Union Representation

5 USC 7114(a) provides:

(2) An exclusive representative of an appropriate unit in an agency shall be given the opportunity to be represented at--

...

(B) any examination of an employee in the unit by a representative of the agency in connection with an investigation if--

(i) the employee reasonably believes that the examination may result in disciplinary action against the employee; and

(ii) the employee requests representation.

(3) Each agency shall annually inform its employees of their rights under paragraph (2)(B) of this subsection.

The nature of the statutory rights was explained in Howard v. OPM, 31 MSPR 617, 621 (1986):

Appellants...argue that they were denied due process rights under...National Labor Relations Board v. Weingarten, Inc., 420 U.S. 251, 260, 95 S. Ct. 959, 965, 43 L. Ed.2d 171 (1975), throughout OPM's investigation.... Weingarten rights, which involve a private sector employee's right to request union representation at an investigatory interview that the employee reasonably believes might result in disciplinary action, are comparable to the provision found at 5 USC § 7114(a)(2)-(3). The appellants have submitted no evidence that they requested representation at their investigatory interviews and that their requests were denied. We note that 5 USC § 7114(a) does not require that the agency inform the employee of his or her right to have a union representative at each individual interview. O'Kane v. Department of the Air Force, 23 MSPR 25, 28 (1984); Sears v. Department of the Navy, 7 MSPB 290, 7 MSPR 417 (1981), aff'd, 680 F.2d 863, 866 (1st Cir. 1982).

The Board considered the right of an employee to union representation at an investigatory interview in Sears v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 417, 7 MSPB 290 (1981). The appellant was removed, after a long record of good service, for theft of less than $100 of government property. He admitted the facts supporting the charge during an interview that provided Miranda warnings. He signed a release waiving his rights and stating that he signed the waiver without coercion or promises of leniency. The agency investigators did not inform the appellant at the interview of any rights that he may have had to union representation under 5 USC 7114. The agency contended that it was the employee's responsibility to request representation; the agency's only obligation was to provide annual notice to its employees of their rights generally. That notice was not circulated, the investigatory interview having taken place only a day after the effective date of the Reform Act.

Sears determined that § 7114 rights are triggered by the request of the employee to have a labor organization that represents him present during an investigatory interview. These rights are different than those guaranteed by Miranda to deter police misconduct. The right to representation by a labor organization is based on the responsibility of the union to represent in good faith all employees in the bargaining unit. The agency need not individually advise employees of their rights under § 7114 at the time of an interrogation; the annual notice of rights is sufficient. In Sears the annual notice, given after the date of the investigation, was not determined to be untimely as to the appellant or to establish harmful error, although the Board did not discuss in detail the effect of the timing of the annual notice. The Board's result was affirmed in court, Sears v. Dept. of Navy, 680 F.2d 863 (1st Cir. 1982); see O'Kane v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 25, 28 (1984) ("Section 7114(a)(3) requires that employees be informed of the right to have a union representative present at investigatory interviews on an annual basis; employees need not be so informed at each individual interview."); Cogman v. DLA, 12 MSPR 569, 572 (1982) ("[E]mployees need only be informed of the right to have a representative present at investigatory interviews on an annual basis. The statute does not require that the employee be so informed at each individual interview."); Anderson v. USPS, 8 MSPR 686, 688 (1981) (same).

Biddle v. Dept. of Treasury, 63 MSPR 521, 529-30 (1994), noted of representational rights during an investigatory interview:

[T]he appellant's rights under the sixth amendment to the Constitution are not implicated because he was not subjected to a custodial interrogation. See Howard v. Office of Personnel Management, 31 MSPR 617, 621 (1986), aff'd, 837 F.2d 1098 (Fed. Cir. 1987). Under 5 USC § 7114(a), an employee is entitled to have a representative present when he is being questioned in connection with an investigation if he reasonably believes that disciplinary action will result and he requests the presence of a representative. The statute imposes no Miranda-like requirement to inform an employee of his right to representation other than to require an agency to inform employees annually of their right to be represented. The appellant does not assert that he requested a representative and that the agency refused his request. Further, the appellant does not contend that the agency failed to provide the annual notice of a right to representation. Therefore, there is no basis upon which to suppress any evidence gathered in the interviews.

Robinson v. USPS, 28 MSPR 681, 684-85, 687 (1985), involved an employee, a union steward, whose locker was opened for the purpose of locating a health insurance form; the form was apparently not in the locker, but 1,314 pieces of undelivered mail were. Removal followed. The use of evidence obtained from the locker was explored as to a provision of a collective bargaining agreement requiring the employer to provide notice to and allow the presence of the employee and a union representative during locker inspections unless the employer had reasonable cause to suspect criminal activity. Because there was no original suspicion of criminal activity, the provision was violated by the search, but the removal remained undisturbed:

Before an area may properly be held to be free of "governmental intrusion," there must be a "reasonable expectation" of such freedom. Katz v. United States, 389 U.S. 347 (1967).... More specifically, the Postal Service has a valid interest in ensuring the safety of the mails and in discovering theft, which does not end at the door to the lockers it provides to employees. Thus, courts have recognized that the public's interest in the integrity of the mail greatly outweighs the Postal Service employee's private interest in the "very restricted and regulated employment related use" of his locker.... We conclude, therefore, that in the absence of some limiting provision, an agency has the right to enter its employees' lockers for a proper reason.

With respect to the particular contract provision at issue...the agency committed error and we must decide whether that error was harmful....

...

...Under the circumstances of this case, we find that appellant has not met this burden because he has not demonstrated that, had the agency followed the procedures called for by the contract, the outcome of this case would have been different.

Had the agency complied with that procedure, appellant or a union representative would have been able to be present when the locker was opened. There is no indication in the contract or elsewhere in the record that they could have prevented the agency from opening it, or even delayed or impeded in any way the agency's right to do so....

See Giove v. Dept. of Transp., 230 F.3d 1333, 1340-43 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (interpreting specific provisions of a contract between a union of air traffic controllers and FAA concerning the right to representation during investigatory interviews); Callery v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 158, 163 (1991) (no harmful error when the agency arguably violated the negotiated agreement by directing the appellant to write a memo of his version of events without granting his request for the presence of a union representative).

As to the right to a non-union representative, Thatcher v. VA, DA-0752-13-0277-I-1 (NP 10/17/2014), cryptically commented, at n.6:

The statute applying Weingarten in the federal workplace allows bargaining unit employees to have non-union representation during a grievance or appeal. 5 U.S.C. § 7114(a)(5). The right to be represented by non-union representatives, however, does not appear to extend to investigative interviews. Cf., e.g., Gonzales v. Department of Health & Human Services, 22 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1984) (an employee's right of representation at a proposed meeting to discuss his compliance with his supervisor's instructions was not violated where a local union representative was available to represent him, but he unreasonably insisted on being represented by the union president, who was unavailable), aff'd, 770 F.2d 181 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Because the statute specifically allows non-union representation during grievances and appeals, it is unlikely that such representation also would be allowed for investigative interviews. See Edwards v. Department of Homeland Security, 110 M.S.P.R. 243, ¶ 13 (2008) (where a statute enumerates certain exceptions to a general rule, it is preferable not to interpret the statute as containing additional exceptions that have not been enumerated).
