6. Adjudication After Waiver, Failure to Attend Hearing

The Judges' Handbook provides guidance to AJs concerning the consequences of a party's failure to show up at a hearing, at Chapter 4:

13. FAILURE OF A PARTY OR REPRESENTATIVE TO APPEAR.

a. Appellant. If the appellant and the appellant's designated representative (if any) fail to appear for the scheduled hearing, the hearing cannot proceed. The AJ should try to call the appellant, and if unsuccessful in making contact, wait a reasonable time before cancelling the hearing in case the appellant is merely tardy.

If neither the appellant nor the appellant's representative appear, the AJ must issue a show cause order that requires the appellant to show good cause for his or her absence. The AJ must then follow up with a second order either resetting the hearing if the appellant establishes good cause, or rescheduling the date for the close of the record if the appellant fails to respond to the order or if the response fails to show good cause. In the latter instance, the appeal must be adjudicated on the basis of the written record only. See Callahan v. Department of the Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

If the show cause order has informed the appellant that failure to respond may result in the dismissal of the appeal for failure to prosecute, the AJ may also consider dismissing the appeal on that basis. However, because a single failure to comply with an AJ's order is not a sufficient reason to dismiss an appeal, dismissal should only be considered if the failure to appear at the hearing is part of a broader pattern of neglect by the appellant and the dismissal is based on the entire pattern of that behavior. Cf. Talbot v. Department of the Interior, 83 M.S.P.R. 325 (1999) (then-Vice Chair Slavet dissenting), which upheld the cancellation of the hearing and the ultimate dismissal of the appeal for the appellant's failure to prosecute it, based on his several failures to comply with the AJ's prehearing orders and to show good cause for such failures.

b. The Appellant's Representative. If the appellant fails to appear for the hearing, but the appellant's representative does appear, the AJ must inform the representative that the following alternatives are available: (1) proceeding with the hearing; (2) having a decision on the written record; or (3) requesting a continuance. See Sparks v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 M.S.P.R. 422 (1987). The appellant's representative must show good cause to obtain a continuance.

c. Agency or Intervenor. If either the agency or an intervenor fails to appear, the hearing, absent extraordinary circumstances, will proceed as scheduled after the AJ has waited a reasonable time for the absent representative to appear and has attempted to contact him or her.

See Oram v. DHS, DC-4324-18-0042-I-1 (NP 2/15/2023) (remanding appeal when the protective provisions of the AJ Handbook were not followed as to an appellant who did not appear for a hearing).

The problem of the no-show appellant was reviewed in Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 590, 592 (1984), on remand, 28 MSPR 41 (1985). The appellant had an experience then not uncommon with the Washington Regional Office: he could not find it. The regional office was then in Falls Church, Virginia, in an isolated office complex unknown by address to many local residents and seemingly impossible to find without some advance scouting or detailed directions (referencing a nondescript strip mall). The Board concluded that the AJ did not err by proceeding with the hearing without the appellant, who, the Board decided, should have requested directions before the hearing. The failure to take reasonable steps to find the hearing location was waiver of the right to a hearing.

The AJ in Callahan convened the hearing and took evidence to support the agency position, a practice that was unfavorably reviewed by the Federal Circuit in Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 748 F.2d 1556, 1558-59 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The court declared the appellant's failure to attend the hearing to be inexcusable. There was no evidence that he asked for directions or looked at a map. In the court's words, "an MSPB hearing is not a mere social occasion for which unjustified lateness might be acceptable. It was his responsibility--which he did not fulfill--to use reasonable efforts to get there on time." But as to the Board's adjudication of the appeal:

In the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (Reform Act), Congress granted employees subject to adverse personnel actions the right to a hearing before the Board.... Significantly, the agency has no statutory right to a hearing....

...

In this case, however, the Board went to the opposite extreme. The presiding official heard testimony from agency employees regarding facts disputed by petitioner, and apparently accepted new affidavits from other employees not in attendance. Her decision indisputably reflects her strong reliance on oral testimony that petitioner never heard at the time, never had a real chance to contradict or explain, and never had the opportunity to inquire into by way of cross-examination. This is precisely contrary to Congress' purpose when it created the hearings procedure so that employees could rebut agency charges personally. It is also plainly contrary to the Board's established position that the hearing is not to be an opportunity for an agency to buttress a weak or inconclusive record.

...If the employee forfeits the opportunity for a hearing,...a decision on the record alone is required.

[T]he congressional purpose to have the hearing held for the employee's benefit (not the agency's), taken together with the fundamental values incident in the opportunity to confront and cross-examine witnesses, strongly suggest that a hearing with live witnesses should never be forced upon an employee who has forfeited or abandoned his right to a hearing.

The court further noted, as to the right of the agency to buttress its case without a hearing, 748 F.2d at 1560 n.7:

We do not pass on the MSPB's right in this or other cases, if the Board regulations permit, to supplement the written record by further affidavits or documents, provided that the employee has access to that new material and can similarly supplement the record by written material....

Callahan was embraced by the Board. In earlier cases, if a continuance was denied, the hearing continued in the appellant's absence, perhaps with the opportunity for the appellant to submit evidence or argument later in response to agency evidence. Hendel v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 186, 188 (1981); see Gould v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 306, 307 (1984) (appellant's representative did not receive an order denying a request for postponement of the hearing); Lyons v. Dept. of Air Force, 14 MSPR 448, 450 (1983) (AJ could hold a partial hearing to take the testimony of agency witnesses, provided that a period of time was allowed for the appellant to respond to hearing testimony). Now, if an appellant is absent from a Board hearing without justification, the proper procedure is for the Board to consider the case on the record alone. Best v. USPS, 26 MSPR 379, 381 (1985); see Daniels v. USPS, 30 MSPR 552, 553 (1986) ("[I]f an employee forfeits the opportunity for a hearing by inexcusably failing to appear, the Board and its presiding officials must render a decision based upon the record evidence alone."). The AJ is not to dismiss the appeal as a sanction. Gamble v. VA, 31 MSPR 649, 652 (1986). Dismissal for failure to prosecute is inappropriate if an appeal is filed, the agency files a response, and the appellant then fails to respond to inquiries from the Board about postappeal submissions or procedures. The AJ should adjudicate the case on the record. Montgomery v. VA, 28 MSPR 386, 388 (1985) (appeal should not have been dismissed for failure to respond to the AJ's orders when the appellant claimed that he stopped work because he was suffering from paranoia and stress). Should the appellant's representative attend and participate in the hearing without the appellant in tow and without objection, the hearing may proceed. Darby v. USPS, 29 MSPR 582, 582 n.1 (1986); cf. Atilano v. McDonough, 12 F.4th 1375, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2021) (determining in a veterans benefits appeal whether the claimant's personal absence from his hearing is a withdrawal of his hearing request: "a party can be given a 'hearing' solely for the party's counsel to argue or for presentation of testimony by a person other than the party, with the party not present but represented in person by counsel"); Gelb v. VA, SF-1221-21-0267-W-1, n.2 (NP 9/14/2022) ("We have considered the appellant's argument that, even though she was absent, the hearing could have proceeded with her representative acting in her stead. See Sparks v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 M.S.P.R. 422, 425 (1987). In this case, however, unlike Sparks, the appellant's representative requested that the hearing be rescheduled. Moreover, as discussed above, the administrative judge had explicitly ordered the appellant herself to appear by video and advised her that failure to comply with that order could result in sanctions under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.54 (which may include cancellation of a hearing)."). The Board still maintains discretion to provide a hearing for the no-show appellant if the Board and the appellant both so desire. See Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 41, 42 (1985) (on remand from the court's decision). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Failure to Attend Hearing," concerning sanctions imposed for failure to attend the hearing.]
