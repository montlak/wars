B. JURISDICTIONAL ISSUES

It is the appellant's burden to prove that the Board has jurisdiction in an IRA case; it is not the agency's burden to prove that the Board lacks jurisdiction. Czarkowski v. Dept. of Navy, 93 MSPR 514, 517-18 ¶ 12 (2003). Jurisdiction is an essential finding by an AJ in an IRA appeal, and the basis for that jurisdictional determination, including whether IRA allegations were timely and specifically pleaded, must appear in the AJ's initial decision or the Board may remand the case for development of evidence concerning jurisdiction. See Fritz v. DHHS, 87 MSPR 287, 291-92 ¶ 8 (2000) (AJ erred by only identifying claims properly referred to OSC; AJ should have analyzed jurisdiction over each IRA claim; jurisdictional determinations required analysis of elections of remedies and coverage of either 5 USC 2302(b)(8) or 2302(b)(9)); Dempsey v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 375, 377 (1993) (OSC closeout letter was insufficiently specific; AJ failed to advise the appellant of the requirements to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue).

Whether a disclosure is protected is not subject to stipulation. See Wojcicki v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 628, 634, 640 (1996) (notwithstanding the parties' stipulation that the appellant made protected disclosures, the Board must resolve whether the appellant had a reasonable belief that the condition he reported established a substantial and specific threat to public health or safety). IRA jurisdiction involving an allegedly protected disclosure is not established by concession, default, or failure to respond to an AJ's order, Skubik v. Dept. of Army, PH-1221-16-0347-W-1 (NP 2/9/2023):

As to the May 27, 2015 nonselection, the administrative judge, citing to the agency's failure to object to her prehearing order, found that the agency conceded the appellant made what he believed to be a protected disclosure and met the knowledge/timing test. However, regardless of any concessions by the agency, "[t]he question of whether a disclosure is protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) is a matter of mixed fact and law and stipulations relating to such matters are not binding." Jensen v. Department of Agriculture, 104 M.S.P.R. 379, ¶ 7 (2007) (quoting Schneider v. Department of Homeland Security, 98 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 17 n.4 (2005)); cf. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.63 (providing that parties may stipulate to matters of fact). The administrative judge thus erred in failing to independently analyze whether the appellant proved by preponderant evidence that he made a protected disclosure, and that the protected disclosure was a contributing factor in a personnel action taken against him.

Because there are multiple elements to an IRA cases (e.g., a covered employee, a defined personnel practice, a disclosure that meets each of the several statutory criterion, actual or constructive knowledge of the disclosure by the proper agency official, prior resort to OSC, proper pleading to OSC, timely filing with MSPB through nonfrivolous allegations of fact), jurisdiction is a complex matter that is ordinarily raised through an agency motion to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdictional or the functional equivalent of that motion, an order from the AJ to show cause why the appeal should not be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction.

The AJ must make a jurisdictional determination in an IRA appeal before proceeding to any other aspect of the case. Under King v. VA, 105 MSPR 21, 24-25 ¶¶ 8-9, 2007 MSPB 48 (2007) (affirming dismissal for lack of jurisdiction following remand, King v. VA, 296 Fed. Appx. 40 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)):

Before adjudicating an IRA appeal on the merits, the Board must make a threshold finding of jurisdiction. The Board may not assume that the appellant has established jurisdiction over her appeal, and then proceed to reject her whistleblower reprisal claim on the merits; rather, the Board must first address the matter of jurisdiction before proceeding to the merits of the appeal. Schmittling v. Department of the Army, 219 F.3d 1332, 1336-37 (Fed. Cir. 2000). Absent jurisdiction, the Board is without authority to decide the issues presented by the appellant. Id. at 1337. If there is no jurisdiction over this appeal, the administrative judge's findings on the merits of the appeal are a nullity. Id.

The administrative judge made no finding of jurisdiction below. He simply proceeded to the merits of the appeal, apparently on the assumption that the Board has jurisdiction. This procedure is prohibited under Schmittling and numerous Board decisions interpreting Schmittling. See, e.g., Hagen, 103 M.S.P.R. 595, ¶ 10; Santos v. Department of Energy, 102 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 6 (2006); Smart v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 566, ¶ 5, aff'd, 157 Fed. Appx. 260 (Fed. Cir. 2005), cert. denied, 126 S. Ct. 1663 (2006); Keefer v. Department of Agriculture, 92 M.S.P.R. 476, ¶¶ 17-18 (2002)....

See McCarty v. EPA, 105 MSPR 74, 77 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 59 (2007) ("Although administrative judges have great discretion in determining which issues in an IRA appeal to consider first, the Board may not assume in an IRA appeal that the appellant has established jurisdiction and then proceed to reject the whistleblower claim on the merits. Rather, the Board must first address the jurisdictional issue before proceeding to the merits of the appeal.").

Following a remand and another PFR, McCarty v. EPA, 108 MSPR 45, 49 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 21 (2008), considered the availability of relief as an element of jurisdiction:

A dismissal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted is appropriate in an IRA appeal when the appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction but, even assuming that the appellant can show that she was subjected to a retaliatory personnel action, the Board would be unable to grant effective relief. McVay v. Arkansas National Guard, 80 M.S.P.R. 120, 125 (1998). In this appeal, if the appellant is able to prove the merits of her claim, the Board will be able to issue an enforceable order granting effective relief because the relief that the appellant seeks includes reinstatement to her FCIP position, which is within the Board's authority to order. See O'Brien v. Office of Independent Counsel, 79 M.S.P.R. 406, 413 (1998) (if the AJ on remand found that the appellant's termination was taken in reprisal for whistleblowing and reversed the agency's action, the appellant would be entitled to status quo ante relief, including reinstatement). Thus, the AJ should not have dismissed this appeal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. When an appellant does not prevail on the merits of an IRA appeal over which the Board has jurisdiction, and when the remedy she seeks is within the Board's authority to grant, the proper disposition is to deny corrective action.[2]

[2] A dismissal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted is appropriate only if, taking the appellant's allegations as true, he cannot prevail as a matter of law. Davis v. Department of Defense, 105 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 11 (2007). Where an AJ does not take the appellant's allegations as true but, instead, finds in the agency's favor based on his assessment of the evidence, it is not appropriate to dismiss the appeal for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. See Williamson v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 9 n. (2007); Davis, 105 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 11.

To avoid serial remands involving components of an IRA case, once jurisdiction has been established, each element of the IRA case on the merits should be analyzed in the Board decision. Kahn v. DOJ, 618 F.3d 1306 (Fed. Cir. 2010). [Kahn is discussed later in this chapter, "Variations in Order of Proof; Necessity to Develop Evidence, Consider Entire Record; Whitmore."] An IRA jurisdictional determination may be revisited. See Kaplan v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-1221-15-1065-W-1 (NP 10/3/2022) ("Although the administrative judge originally assigned to these appeals found that the appellant met her jurisdictional burden, there is no procedural rule to prevent that order from being reconsidered and reversed. See, e.g., Van Lancker v. Department of Justice, 119 M.S.P.R. 514, ¶ 3 (2013); Williams v. Defense Logistics Agency, 34 M.S.P.R. 54, 57-58 (1987).").

As an AJ evaluates jurisdictional averments in pleadings, the AJ is to bear in mind the pro se status of an appellant, who will not be expected to frame issues "with the precision of a common law pleading." Becker v. VA, 76 MSPR 292, 298 n.4 (1997). If the AJ questions whether the allegations contained in an IRA appeal are within the Board's jurisdiction, the AJ should ensure that the appellant is specifically informed as to what is required to bring the case within the Board's jurisdiction. Informing the appellant that there is a jurisdictional issue is not sufficient without also telling the appellant what he must allege or prove to demonstrate jurisdiction. Laity v. VA, 54 MSPR 51, 53-54 (1992). Laity came to the peculiar conclusion, however, that if the appellant manages to allege facts that would create a case within the Board's WPA jurisdiction, the AJ is then to conduct a hearing on jurisdictional issues, much the same as if the appellant alleged an involuntary adverse action. There is no point to a jurisdictional hearing if facts are alleged to place the case within the Board's jurisdiction. The hearing should be on the merits, and this is the approach that now appears to be adopted by the Board under Rusin v. Dept. of Treasury, 92 MSPR 298 (2002), discussed below. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Whistleblower Protection Act: Claims for Damages, Other Relief," concerning mootness of appeals.]

Many, many cases define the jurisdictional elements of an IRA case. One reason for the proliferation of the cases is that the parties, counsel, and AJs do not follow or understand the matrix of decisions that have poured out of the Board and the Federal Circuit since the WPA 1989.
