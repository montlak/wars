E. OPINION AND IMPEACHMENT EVIDENCE

Witnesses testify as to facts and as to their own opinions, and sometimes it is hard to discern whether the testimony is one of fact or opinion. If a witness states that he saw a tree, he is only offering his opinion that what he saw was a tree; perhaps what he saw was a bush. With any witness, the Board expects that there will be some foundation for the testimony, that is, some basis for what the witness will say that relates to the observations and experience of that witness. See, e.g., Harriss v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 427, 435 (1995) ("At most, they testified that they believed that it was her responsibility but did not testify as to the basis for their belief or when they came to believe it."). The Board offers some commentary in the Hearings Procedures Guide concerning the development of what it calls "foundational facts" supporting testimony. Although the Hearings Procedures Guide has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook, it is still used by the Board for training and orientation. The Guide states at 26-28:

2. Lay Opinion Witnesses

a. When Permitted

Opinion testimony by lay witnesses is limited to those opinions or inferences that are rationally based on perception and helpful to a clear understanding of the witness' testimony or the determination of a fact in issue. Fed. R. Evid. 701; Fund of Funds, Ltd. v. Arthur Andersen & Co., 545 F. Supp. 1314 (S.D.N.Y. 1982).

A lay witness can testify that an employee appeared intoxicated. Lesley v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 38, 40 (1981).

b. Basis of Opinion--Firsthand Knowledge

In laying a proper foundation, the examiner must show that the witness had an adequate opportunity to observe the event.

Opinion can be based on matters involving sense recognition, simple odors, taste, sound, and observed emotional states. For example, a lay person could testify that a person appeared to be angry, happy, or joking.

A lay person may offer his/her opinion as to identity and likeness of appearance, voice or handwriting, Paccon, Inc. v. United States, 399 F.2d 162, 185 (Ct. Cl. 1968) (identity of handwriting).

A lay person may offer his/her opinion as to the speed of a moving vehicle. Attal v. Pennsylvania Railroad Co., 212 F. Supp. 306, aff'd, 323 F.2d 363 (3rd Cir. 1963); Gilliland v. Ruke, 280 F.2d 544 (4th Cir. 1960).

c. Opinion on Sanity

Where a lay witness expresses an opinion as to the mental competency of another, he must base his opinion upon the facts actually observed by him. See John Hancock Mutual Life Insurance Co. v. Dutton, 585 F.2d 1289 (5th Cir. 1978) (witness may have personal knowledge of the state of mind of another through observation of that person); Kaufman v. United States, 350 F.2d 408 (8th Cir. 1965) (two FBI agents who spent 10 hours and four hours with defendant respectively, were qualified to testify as to the defendant's mental capacity).

Fed. R. Evid. 701 provides that if the witness is not testifying as an expert, the witness' testimony in the form of opinions or inferences is limited to those opinions or inferences that are rationally based on the perception of the witness and helpful to a clear understanding of the witness' testimony or the determination of a fact in issue.

Like Fed. R. Evid. 702 regarding experts, this rule is broad because of the "helpfulness" standard. It is stated as a general principle, leaving the specific application to the judge. It may cover almost any area not requiring specialized expertise.

d. Board Cases

Lesley v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 38, 40 (1981) (lay witnesses properly allowed to testify that appellant appeared intoxicated).

Moen v. Dept. of Transp., 28 MSPR 556, 559 (1985) (statement by co-worker familiar with marijuana from past experience was properly relied on to conclude appellant was smoking marijuana).

Boyling v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 276, 278 (1981) (Board held that identification of handwriting is determined by trier of fact based on lay or expert opinion).

Aside from testimony of witnesses concerning their opinions of the physical or mental conditions of parties or witnesses, testimony may be offered concerning the personal attributes of character, reputation, and habit of individuals who figure in the factual scenario. Added to those opinions are efforts to introduce evidence that the appellant committed prior acts of a nature similar to the acts charged in the appeal before the Board.
