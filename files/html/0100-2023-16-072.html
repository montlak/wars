broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section D: Unilateral Changes in Employment Conditions and Past Practice, Subsection 04: Absence of Change; Impact on Working Conditions, Part a: Determining When Impact Occurs
a. Determining When Impact Occurs

Before determining whether matters are weighty enough to require bargaining, one first determines when impact occurs. A good example of the FLRA’s analysis occurred in a RIF case. The union learned that a RIF would be conducted and sought impact bargaining. The employer desired to defer bargaining until after specific RIF notices were issued. FLRA favored earlier bargaining, Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 35 FLRA 844, 852, 854-57 (1990):

An agency is obligated to bargain...before implementing a change in the conditions of employment....

...

The issuance of a specific RIF notice changes the employee's status. An employee who has received a specific RIF notice has been identified for separation, downgrading, or reassignment.... [E]mployees who have been issued specific notices must decide during the notice period whether to accept the reassignments or other actions to which they are subject pursuant to those notices.

Because the issuance of specific RIF notices identifies particular employees who will be affected by the RIF, subjects those employees to changes in pay status...and requires employees to make decisions which affect significantly their employment...an agency's issuance of specific RIF notices to employees constitutes a change in the employees' conditions of employment. Because the RIF notices implemented a change in the...conditions of employment, the Respondent was obligated to notify the Union and fulfill its statutory obligation to bargain before the issuance of those notices.

...

Some aspects of the RIF process that occur before the issuance of specific notices are negotiable. For example, the determination of the competitive area is negotiable....

[P]roposals concerning employees' assignment rights are, in some circumstances, negotiable.... [I]t would be anomalous, at best, for the Authority to find that although an agency is obligated to bargain over the content of the RIF notice, the agency is not obligated to bargain until after the RIF notice has been issued.

...

[T]he Union requested bargaining over any change in conditions of employment which resulted from the decision to contract out....

...

...We believe that the "effective" date of the RIF does not control whether the Respondent was required to bargain with the Union before issuing the specific RIF notices in this case. Under the Statute, an agency is obligated to bargain over "conditions of employment" "affecting" bargaining unit employees. 5 U.S.C. § 7104(a)(12) (emphasis added). This obligation encompasses bargaining over procedures by which management rights are exercised and appropriate arrangements for employees who are adversely "affected" by the exercise of those rights.... Nothing in the dictionary or the Statute would lead to the conclusion that an employee is "affected" by a RIF only after the effective date of the RIF.

The same result was reached as to notices of proposed furloughs. The proposal notices changed employees' conditions of employment by placing personnel in a status that allowed them to become subject to actions described in the notices. The agency was obliged to provide the union with adequate notice of the proposed changes and an opportunity to bargain over negotiable aspects of those changes. Hq., Dept. of Air Force 375th Combat Div., Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 44 FLRA 117, 125 (1992). The results, however, are not always consistent. An agency announced that it would likely be relocating an office. The union requested impact bargaining. The agency responded by stating that no final decision was made as to relocation. The union filed a ULP when the agency did not agree to bargain over the impact of the move. The union's bargaining request was premature, although FLRA seemed to agree that in some circumstances a contemplated change may be so close to implementation that the agency's silence or refusal to bargain may be inconsistent with its bargaining responsibilities. See DHHS, SSA, Region I and AFGE Local 1164, 47 FLRA 322, 329-30 (1993) (ALJ Decision).

An agency should have engaged in impact bargaining when it decided to reduce personnel ceilings in its headquarters and field offices. It was immaterial that no RIF immediately occurred and that the agency told the union it would reduce staffing levels through attrition. There was no assurance that a RIF would not occur in the future. Because there would be fewer employees after the implementation of the reduced personnel ceilings, the reduction of personnel ceilings had a reasonably foreseeable effect on the performance appraisals of the remaining employees by increasing their workloads. EEOC and Nat'l Council of EEOC Locals #216, AFGE, 40 FLRA 1147, 1154-55 (1991). As to drug testing, FLRA viewed practices as changed when the program was announced with an effective date and the agency did not clearly state that it was delaying the program for purposes of bargaining. Not determinative was not the date that employees actually were first tested. Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 702-05 (1991).
