a. Transfer to Another Agency

Should an individual transfer to another agency after being notified that he will fall victim to a RIF by his original employer, and the transfer precedes the effective date of the RIF, the Board lacks jurisdiction over a RIF appeal unless there is evidence that the transfer was involuntary. The appeal can only be taken from a RIF action. If the transfer, coupled with a demotion, occurs on the same date that the RIF was effective, the employee lost his position by RIF and could appeal on that basis. Hyter v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 678 (1981) (employee accepted a downgrade through the exercise of reemployment rights, resulting in reassignment to another component of the agency, on the same date that the RIF was effective). Hyter and its kin were, however, subsequently held to have been "decided without benefit of an analysis of the voluntariness of the effected action...." Koop v. FEMA, 16 MSPR 605, 607-08 & n.2 (1983). In Koop, involving a transfer to another agency after the RIF notice but before the effective date of the RIF, the Board observed:

Nothing in law or regulation confers upon the Board jurisdiction over transfer actions per se. If however, the transfer action is found to be involuntary, it is of no effect and operates to give effect to the underlying RIF action.[2]

[2] See Knox v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 12 MSPB 58 at 60 (1982) where the Board held that it lacked jurisdiction over a RIF action where the voluntary resignation was effective the same day as the effective date of the RIF; Butler v. Department of the Interior, 9 MSPB 305 at 305 (1982), where the Board took jurisdiction over a RIF action after determining that a resignation effective the same day as the RIF action was involuntary. Also see, Tennyson v. Department of the Air Force, 3 MSPB 491, 493 (1980) and Hyter v. U.S. Department of the Army, 6 MSPB 568 (1981). Insofar as these cases were decided without benefit of an analysis of the voluntariness of the effected action, the analysis was incorrect and incomplete, and they are to that extent hereby overruled.

As in the analogous situation involving an employee initiated action such as a resignation or a retirement, transfers are presumed to be voluntary, and that presumption will prevail unless the appellant presents sufficient evidence to establish that the resignation was obtained through duress or coercion, or shows that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency's statements. See Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d 584, 585, 587-88, 207 Ct. Cl. 333 (1975) and Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572 at 1575 (Fed. Cir. 1983). The test for duress is:

...(1) [t]hat one side involuntarily accepted the terms of another; (2) that circumstances permitted no other alternative; and (3) that said circumstances were the result of coercive acts of the opposite party. Fruhauf Southwest Garment Co. v. United States, 126 Ct. Cl. 51, 62; 111 F. Supp. 945, 951 (1953)

In the instant case, while appellant may have believed that he had no alternative but to transfer, he did have a choice and he chose to transfer rather than challenge the validity of the RIF action. Merely because appellant was faced with a situation arguably limited to two unpleasant alternatives, the voluntariness of his action is not obviated. See Christie at 587-88, 207 Ct. Cl. 333 and Knox, supra.

Moreover, there is no evidence in the record of the proceedings on the merits before the presiding official that the RIF proceedings were not undertaken in good faith by the agency, or that appellant's transfer was a product of agency misrepresentation or deception which meet the standard for involuntariness set forth in Scharf, supra.

Koop was limited in Talley v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 261, 263 (1991): the Board does not exercise jurisdiction over appeals from otherwise unadorned transfers or reassignments, whether they are characterized as voluntary or involuntary. Hyter apparently retains some validity, however, for it was cited with approval in Price v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 516, 518 (1984), involving the employee's geographic reassignment to another location within the agency after the employee received a RIF notice, for the principle that "an employee's release from his or her competitive level triggers the employee's RIF appeal rights under 5 CFR 351.901 even if the employee accepts another position contemporaneously with the effective date of the RIF action." In Price, the effective date of the reassignment postdated the effective date of the RIF. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action."]
