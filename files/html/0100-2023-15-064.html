broida mspb Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses, Subchapter 01: Status, Section C: Failure to Maintain or Obtain Condition of Position, Subsection 01: License, Certification, Part a: Lawyer
a. Lawyer

To retain federal employment as an attorney, an employee must retain state bar membership. If that membership or license is suspended, the employee is terminated for failure to adhere to qualifications requirements for the position. McGean v. NLRB, 15 MSPR 49, 53-54 (1983) (suspension from bar membership for a year and a day permitted removal from position as attorney-advisor for failure to maintain a basic qualification for service in the position and for unprofessional conduct; the Board declined to reexamine a basis for bar discipline once it was determined that the discipline was imposed by an entity with subject matter jurisdiction over the appellant's bar license), aff'd, McGean v. NLRB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 83-1184). Inability to maintain a license to practice law has a direct impact on agency operations. Without the license the attorney does not meet the condition of employment of holding licensure in a state, territory, the District of Columbia, or Puerto Rico. The attorney has a duty to notify the agency of the suspension of his license to practice law. Failure to notify the agency is a violation of trust. Currie v. DHUD, 21 MSPR 720, 724 (1984) (appellant knew he was suspended and did not notify the agency; the suspension effectively lasted more than a year; bar membership was a requirement stated in the appellant's position description; another sustained charge included unauthorized outside employment); see Dept. of Labor v. Slattery, 87 MSPR 528 (2000) (Table) (nonprecedential final decision; compare the separate opinions of Acting Chairman Slavet and Member Marshall concerning the indefinite suspension of an ALJ who was investigated for misconduct that could, but did not at the time, lead to a revocation of his license to practice law); cf. SSA v. Price, 94 MSPR 337, 343 ¶¶ 13-15 (2003) (attorney fee case: noting that the agency made a procedural error when it placed an ALJ on AWOL (enforced leave) for a period that the judge's bar license was suspended). Laycock v. Dept. of Army, 97 MSPR 597 (2004), skirted the issue of whether an agency could remove an attorney based upon the agency's withdrawal of its approval of the attorney's qualifications to practice law for the agency; instead, the case was resolved against the appellant based on factual issues concerning the attorney's performance.
