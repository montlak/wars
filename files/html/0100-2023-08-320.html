j. Retirement Benefits

The Board analyzed the relation between a retirement plan and its sponsor, the Tennessee Valley Authority, in Hereford v. TVA, 88 MSPR 201, 205-07 ¶¶ 10-16 (2001), and determined that USERRA does not provide Board jurisdiction over a claim of military discrimination in a retirement plan that is an entity maintained separately from the sponsoring employer:

The Board's jurisdiction under USERRA[1] is limited to complaints against Federal executive agencies. See 38 U.S.C. § 4324(b) (permitting the filing with the Board of USERRA complaints against "any Federal executive agency or the Office of Personnel Management"). USERRA defines Federal executive agencies to include executive agencies as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 105, other than intelligence agencies. 38 U.S.C. § 4303(5). Those agencies include executive departments, government corporations, and independent establishments. 5 U.S.C. § 106. TVARS is not one of the executive departments listed in 5 U.S.C. § 101.

[1] The provisions of the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994 (USERRA) are codified in Chapter 43 of Title 38 (38 U.S.C. § 4301-33).

To constitute a government corporation subject to the Board's jurisdiction, the TVARS would have to be considered part of the TVA because the TVARS itself is not incorporated. We note that the administrative judge found that the TVARS is not part of the TVA because the TVARS funds are maintained separately from the TVA, the Board of Directors of the TVARS exercises exclusive control over its pension funds, And the TVA appoints a minority of the TVARS's Board members. We find the administrative judge's conclusion consistent with court decisions submitted into the record by the TVARS. (for example, Tennessee Valley Trades & Labor Council v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 151 F. Supp. 135, 141-42 (M.D. Tenn. 1990)).

Nor does TVARS constitute an independent establishment. An independent establishment is an establishment in the executive branch "which is not an Executive department, military department, Government corporation, or part thereof, or part of an independent establishment." 5 U.S.C. § 104(l). It appears that an independent establishment must be created by statute or Executive order. See Energy Research Foundation v. Defense Nuclear Facilities Safety Board, 917 F.2d 581, 582-83 (D.C. Cir. 1990) (finding agency to be an independent establishment based on language in enabling statute describing agency as "an independent establishment in the executive branch" and noting that other enabling statutes include similar language). Cf. Katsiavelos v. Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago, 859 F. Supp. 1183, 1185 (N.D. Ill. 1994) (finding Federal Reserve Bank not an independent establishment because privately owned and controlled although regulated by federal statute and regulation). The TVARS was established by action of the TVA, a Government corporation. We find no authority that indicates that a Government corporation possesses the authority to create wholly independent federal agencies.

Section 4303(4) of Title 38 provides that an employee pension benefit plan is considered an "employer" for only limited purposes under USERRA.

Except as an actual employer of employees, an employee pension benefit plan described in section 3(2) of the Employment Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (29 U.S.C. 1002(2)) shall be deemed to be an employer only with respect to the obligation to provide benefits described in section 4318.

38 U.S.C. § 4303(4)(C). Section 4318 governs the pension benefits of employees who have been reemployed following uniformed service. 38 USC 4318. Thus, an employee pension benefit plan, as that term is defined in the Employment Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), is not considered an employer for the purposes of USERRA's antidiscrimination provision. 38 U.S.C. § 4310.[2]

[2] Section 4311 prohibits "an employer" from denying a benefit of employment based on an employee's military service. 38 U.S.C. § 4311(a).

Section 3(2) of ERISA defines "employee pension benefit plan" to include:

any plan, fund, or program which was heretofore or is hereafter established or maintained by an employer or by an employee organization, or by both, to the extent that by its express terms or as a result of surrounding circumstances such plan, fund, or program--

(i)  provides retirement income to employees, or

(ii) results in a deferral of income by employees for periods extending to the termination of covered employment or beyond, regardless of the method of calculating the contributions made to the plan, the method of calculating the benefits under the plan or the method of distributing benefits under the plan.

29 U.S.C. § 1002(2). The TVARS meets this definition of an employee pension benefit plan. It is a plan or program established by TVA, an employer, to provide retirement income to its employees. See Tennessee Valley Authority v. Kinzer, 142 F.2d 833, 836 (6th Cir. 1944). Consequently, we find that, under USERRA, the TVARS is not an employer of the TVA retirees for purposes of USERRA's anti-discrimination provision.

In so finding, we recognize that the TVARS is not governed by the substantive provisions of ERISA because it is a plan established by an instrumentality of the Federal government. 29 U.S.C. §§ 1002(32) and 1003(b)(1). Section 4303(4) of Title 38 defines USERRA's coverage over pension plans by reference to ERISA's definition section and not by reference to plans covered by ERISA's substantive provisions. Further, ERISA's provision describing its coverage recognizes that its definition of "employee pension benefit plan" includes governmental retirement plans, specifying that: "The provisions of this subchapter shall not apply to any employee benefit plan--if (1) such plan is a governmental plan." 29 U.S.C. § 1003(b)(1).

We also conclude that Congress's inclusion of "rights and benefits under a pension plan" in USERRA's definition of "benefit of employment" does not compel a finding that an employee can file a military discrimination claim against a pension plan. Section 4311 of Title 38 permits an employee to bring a claim against an employer that denies him a benefit of employment. Thus, an employee could bring an action challenging an employer's refusal to permit him to join a pension plan. However, USERRA does not permit an action against a pension plan claiming discrimination in the administration of the plan, and we find nothing which would support a contrary position. Accordingly, we find that the appellant has failed to state a claim under 38 U.S.C. § 4303(4)(C) and other pertinent provisions of USERRA and ERISA.
