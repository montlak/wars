A. SOURCES OF JURISDICTION

The scope of Board subject matter jurisdiction is defined by 5 USC 7701:

(a) An employee, or applicant for employment, may submit an appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board from any action which is appealable to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation....

Under 5 USC 1204:

(a) The Merit Systems Protection Board shall--

(1) hear, adjudicate, or provide for the hearing or adjudication, of all matters within the jurisdiction of the Board under this title, chapter 43 of title 38, or any other law, rule, or regulation, and, subject to otherwise applicable provisions of law, take final action on any such matter[.]

See 5 CFR 1201.3 (2022) (enumerating sources of appellate jurisdiction). [The regulation is recited later in this chapter, "Appealable Actions."]

A statute, rule, or regulation must provide an affirmative grant of jurisdiction in order for the Board to hear a case. 5 USC 7701 provides that an employee, or an applicant for employment, may appeal to the Board from any action that is appealable to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation. That statute does not itself confer jurisdiction. An appeal may not be made to the Board under 7701 unless the action complained of is appealable under another law, rule, or regulation. Umshler v. Dept. of Interior (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 90-3517). The most frequent source of Board jurisdiction is through a statute. OPM regulations provide some appellate rights, e.g., of probationary employees under limited circumstances, or in suitability, reduction in force, or restoration appeals. There do not appear to be any "rules" creating Board jurisdiction, and it does not appear that agencies other than OPM have created regulatory rights of Board appeal divorced from specific statutory jurisdictional grants.

As to 5 USC 7701, the statute permits an appeal under another law (e.g., statute), rule, or regulation, Hill v. Dept. of Treasury, AT-3443-17-0371-I-1 (NP 1/27/2023):

On review, the appellant argues that, under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a), an employee or applicant may submit an appeal to the Board from any action which is appealable to the Board. This is true. However, it does not establish that the appellant's nonselection is, in fact, an action appealable to the Board. Section 7701 is not itself a grant of jurisdiction. Rather, it sets forth the procedures for adjudicating appeals that are within the Board's jurisdiction. Belhumeur v. Department of Transportation, 104 M.S.P.R. 408, ¶ 9 (2007). Therefore, notwithstanding this provision, the appellant must still establish that she has been subjected to an action "which is appealable to the Board."

Board jurisdiction involves two elements: first, there must be a personnel action (or failure to act) within the cognizance of the Board, e.g., a demotion, termination, or reduction in force; second, the individual bringing the appeal must be the type of employee, e.g., competitive service, who is authorized to appeal the action. The jurisdictional elements are interrelated: appealable actions, defined by statutes and regulations, are appealable only when they are taken against and by defined groups of employees, applicants for employment, annuitants, or individuals with claims for OPM-administered benefits (discussed in Chapter 14). Speaking generally, Flickinger v. VA, SF-3443-13-3781-I-1 (NP 7/31/2014), observed:

The Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). In order to establish Board jurisdiction under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75, an individual must, among other things, show that she satisfies one of the definitions of "employee" in 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1). Walker v. Department of Army, 119 M.S.P.R. 391, ¶ 5 (2013); 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d). An individual in the competitive service, like the appellant, must either: (1) not be serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or (2) have completed one year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less. Walker, 119 M.S.P.R. 391, ¶ 5; 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A).

The Board lacks the authority to broaden its statutory authority, no matter how compelling the injustice advanced. "The Board does not have jurisdiction over all actions that are alleged to be incorrect." Preece v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 222, 226 (1991); see Miller v. DHS, 111 MSPR 325, 332-33 ¶ 14, 2009 MSPB 85 (2009) (in an appeal indirectly challenging the return of an employee from an overseas assignment: "The Board does not have jurisdiction over all matters that are alleged to be unfair or incorrect, such as whether the agency should not have decided to eliminate his position") (aff'd, Miller v. MSPB, 361 Fed. Appx. 134 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)); cf. Lundberg v. Dept. of Air Force, CH-3443-14-0011-I-1 (NP 4/29/2014) (that "the terms of the regulation plainly indicate that the fact that a particular appeal may be excluded from an agency's negotiated grievance procedure does not provide the Board with the authority to hear a matter that is not otherwise appealable under the Board's appellate jurisdiction").

The Federal Circuit joins the Board in strict construction of Board jurisdiction. See Noble v. TVA, 892 F.2d 1013, 1014 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (compare majority and dissenting opinions on Board jurisdiction over a claim of denial of reemployment rights of a TVA employee). That the Board has jurisdiction over one agency does not empower the Board to expand its jurisdiction to a nonparty to an appealable action. Viola v. OPM, 33 MSPR 474, 477 (1987) (when MSPB found no error concerning retirement calculations by the OPM, it declined to interplead the past employer, USPS, to resolve a dispute with the USPS concerning retirement deductions). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Collateral Result of Restoration Order; Action Related to Case Under Appeal," and Chapter 2, "Class Action, Consolidated Appeals, and Joinder; Bifurcated Appeals," for decisions permitting permit joinder of other agencies in retirement appeals when joinder is necessary to full relief.] The Board shares with agencies the responsibility for adjudicating EEO issues involving actions within the Board's appellate jurisdiction. [The "mixed case" procedure is discussed in Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases"; review of certain arbitration award based on EEO challenges is discussed in Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards."]

The Board catalogues its subject matter jurisdiction at 5 CFR 1201.1-.3 (2022). That regulation, reprinted later in this chapter, "Appealable Actions," provides but half the analysis, for it does not state the types of employees and agencies whose employees have the right to invoke the Board's subject matter jurisdiction. Because the Board's jurisdiction derives from statutes, regulations, and Reorganization Plan No. 2, the regulation is a nonbinding catalogue of the types of actions within the Board's jurisdiction. See Woodington v. Dept. of Navy, 88 MSPR 689, 690 ¶ 15 (2001); Buscher v. USPS, 69 MSPR 204, 207 n.2 (1995) (the Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.3 (1995), described its jurisdiction as, "not an independent source of jurisdiction itself, but is merely descriptive of jurisdiction found in other laws, rules, and regulations."); Utterback v. Dept. of Transp., 10 MSPR 168, 170 (1982) ("Section 1201.3 of 5 C.F.R. is merely a non-exclusive summary of the types of actions made appealable to the Board by statute or jurisdiction over any action. Rather, the Board derives its jurisdiction from Reorganization Plan No. 2, from the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 and from appropriate provisions of statutes and regulations.").

One might think, mistakenly, that a statute that has been on the books since 1978, applied by an administrative agency that has decided cases since 1979, would have been fully interpreted, explained, and applied to every conceivable situation. But no. Jurisdictional complications daily confront AJs and parties. The statutory sources of Board jurisdiction are broadly-stated, ambiguously phrased--applicable to the nation's largest workforce serving the public through more than a hundred agencies and in many types of appointments. The permutations of jurisdictional issues, seemingly innumerable, support a fair number of lawyers earning passably respectable incomes in private practice, union law offices, and government service, as well as Board AJs.
