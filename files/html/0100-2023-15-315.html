7. Failure to Follow Procedure

Neglect of work may include failure to follow instructions, but a defense to the charge of neglect may be established if the appellant's conduct was consistent with his training and routine agency practice, Mendez v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 596, 606-07 ¶¶ 24-28 (2001):

We also find that the agency has not established that the appellants negligently performed their duties by failing to ensure that all the marijuana was destroyed. The agency's two specifications underlying its negligence charges alleged that, based upon their training and instructions in the Seized Property Handbook, the appellants did not reasonably perform their duty to ensure that all of the marijuana had been destroyed when they left the Tucson facility, and that they, therefore, breached their duty of witnessing and ensuring the destruction of all the marijuana as set forth in the Seized Property Handbook.

With respect to the negligence charge, we first find that the agency alleged but one valid specification, i.e., that the appellants negligently failed to ensure the destruction of the contraband. The second "specification" merely states that the appellants were aware of the requirements set out in the Seized Property Handbook applicable to the destruction of the marijuana placed in the care of the appellants. The allegation that they failed to follow the policy in that document is no more than an allegation of the standard of care breached by the actions referred to in the first specification, not a separate specification.

Culpable negligence in the performance of official duties is a failure to exercise the degree of care required under the particular circumstances, which a person of ordinary prudence in the same situation and with equal experience would not omit. See, e.g., Williams v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 65 MSPR 612, 614-16 (1994) (negligence established on showing of duty, the employee's knowledge, and breach of duty of health care worker's responsibility for surgical instrument sterilization), aff'd, 69 F.3d 554 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); see generally Black's Law Dictionary at 1184-85 (Rev. 4th ed. 1968). The question of whether the appellants breached their duty of care turns on whether the Seized Property Handbook, under all the circumstances, established the applicable standard of care required of a person in the same situation and with the same experience as the appellants, or whether instead the applicable standard of care was established by other factors, including training, knowledge, customary agency practice, and adequacy of agency procedures. Whether the standard was breached is an objective inquiry, not based on the appellants' personal belief as to the adequacy of their performance.

Contrary to the agency's assertion and the administrative judge's finding, we find that the applicable objective standard of care under all the circumstances was not the apparently unequivocal standard set out in the Seized Property Handbook. The appellants' training, knowledge, and experience, as well as the agency's demonstrated accepted and customary practices for the destruction of contraband at the Tucson incinerator, lead us to conclude that the appellants did not breach their duty of care under applicable procedures. The Grassfire Report and evidence below establish the following: It was physically impossible to destroy the marijuana in the limited amount of time that the agency allocated for the project; the appellants' official training instructed them to follow the procedures at their particular duty Port-El Paso; the appellants' on-the-job training in El Paso did not instruct them about the length of time needed to ensure the marijuana's destruction, and the agency and the responsible supervisory personnel were in fact unaware of that time requirement or the mechanical limitations of the contractor's incineration facility; Port of El Paso had no established rules regarding how long the appellants were required to wait at the burn facility to ensure that the marijuana was destroyed; El Paso's established procedures and practices were fundamentally flawed; the appellants followed established procedures during the Tucson burn operation; and the responsibility for these flawed procedures rested with the El Paso Port Director.[3]

[3] Cf. Blake v. Department of Justice, 81 MSPR 394 ¶ 25 (1999) (where employee followed supervisors' apparently lawful directives, blame for the consequences should properly be imputed to the one responsible for such directives, citing Rose v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 26 MSPR 356, 360 (1985))....

Under these circumstances, we find that the agency failed to prove that the appellants were negligent under the applicable standard of care in performing their duties by failing to ensure the marijuana's complete destruction. Therefore, the charges are not sustained, and the agency's actions are REVERSED.
