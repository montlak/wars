a. Erroneous Exclusion of Evidence

The general rule is that "[a]n administrative judge has wide discretion to control the proceedings, including authority to exclude testimony she believes would be irrelevant or immaterial." McCauley v. Dept. of Interior, 116 MSPR 484, 488-89 ¶ 8, 2011 MSPB 59 (2011) (citing Guerrero v. VA, 105 MSPR 617, 625-26 ¶ 20 (2007); Miller v. DOD, 85 MSPR 310, 313 ¶ 8 (2000)). Although an AJ is given broad discretion to exclude irrelevant evidence, see Special Counsel v. Rivera, 61 MSPR 440, 444 (1994), the AJ will not likely err by allowing too much evidence; but the opposite approach may give cause for a remand from the Board. In Powell v. Dept. of Army, 7 MSPR 199, 202, 7 MSPB 111 (1981), the appellant sought to establish that his removal was in reprisal for his activities as a union president. The AJ refused to allow the testimony of the witnesses requested, ruling that the appellant's status as union president was irrelevant. Reversing and remanding the case for the development of the excluded evidence, the Board decided that although evidence may be excluded if it is immaterial or unduly repetitious, the AJ's anticipation that proffered evidence may be unpersuasive or inadequate to fully establish a defense does not make the evidence immaterial. It was error to exclude evidence in Powell directly relating to a material issue of retaliation and pretextual dismissal. In another appeal, testimony from a former supervisor that was offered to show that the appellant's performance was satisfactory was improperly excluded; the AJ erred when he concluded that the testimony would repeat that offered by the appellant. The supervisor's testimony could be considered more expert and more objective. Harms v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 165, 166 (1986).

It is not just the appellant who must be fairly treated in the introduction of evidence. The AJ errs by requesting or directing the agency to present evidence on only the most serious charge. Unless there is a ruling excluding unduly repetitious evidence, the agency has the right to present evidence on all the charges. Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 119, 122 n.2 (1986); see Myers v. USDA, 81 MSPR 496, 507 ¶ 26 (1999) (AJ erred by permitting evidence on only one charge when other charges were not the same as the charge relied upon and when an analysis of other charges was necessary to determine whether the appellant was negligent in the performance of official duties). But see Wildberger v. SBA, 63 MSPR 338 (1994) (allowing a selective group of charges to be developed in the agency case). [Refer to Wildberger in Chapter 3, "Bifurcated Hearing."] If the AJ excludes evidence from a witness that could also come from the appellant concerning harassment on the job, there is no error. Thurston v. VA, 254 Fed. Appx. 811, 814 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (relying on Tiffany v. Dept. of Navy, 795 F.2d 67, 70 (Fed. Cir. 1986), affirming the authority of the AJ to exclude witnesses whose testimony is repetitious).

The Board does not often articulate the basis for its determinations that AJs improperly exclude (or admit) evidence. Objections are deflected with occasional comments that a PFR presents no more than disagreements with the findings of the AJ. Or the Board may decide that the appellant has not established that the AJ's adjudicatory error requires reversal unless the appellant demonstrates prejudice by the error. See Hanna v. Dept. of Labor, 80 MSPR 294, 303 ¶ 13 (1998); Brame v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 188, 189 (1983) (exclusion of the appellants' statements going to mitigation of the removal penalty for engaging in a strike against the government was not prejudicial given the nature and effect of the appellants' offense). However, in Roth v. USPS, 54 MSPR 290, 295-96 (1992), the Board offered a semblance of a review standard, stating that the "appellant has not shown that the allowance of any of the excluded testimony would have likely resulted in findings contrary to those made by the administrative judge." See Scovell v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-16-0355-I-1 (NP 1/31/2023) ("To obtain reversal of an initial decision on the basis of the exclusion of a witness, the appellant must show that a relevant witness or evidence, which could have affected the outcome, was disallowed. See Thomas, 116 M.S.P.R. 453, ¶ 4.").

The possibility of an erroneous ruling merited a remand when an AJ excluded the testimony of an individual who would have testified that he was offered a job in exchange for cooperation with the investigation leading to charges against the appellant. Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 54 MSPR 356, 364 (1992) (regarding a further witness, if the testimony "could have been probative," it "should not have been excluded"). But in Higgins v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 167, 169-70 (1982), the Board considered and rejected an objection to the exclusion of evidence that could have demonstrated a supervisor's animosity:

The Board recognizes the apparent inconsistency in the presiding official's restriction of certain evidence purportedly demonstrating the supervisor's alleged animosity toward appellant and his subsequent reference to lack of evidence in deciding that issue against the appellant. However, we have reviewed appellant's assertions, the agency's responses, and the portions of the record cited by the parties, and conclude that the presiding official's evidentiary rulings were not improper. Moreover, even if those rulings were unduly restrictive, we can discern no prejudice to appellant's substantive rights because the charges of insubordination and AWOL were supported by preponderant evidence, warranting removal despite any purported animosity for appellant on the part of his supervisor.

See Vaughn v. Dept. of Treasury, 119 MSPR 605, 612 ¶ 14, 2013 MSPB 50 (2013) ("With respect to the appellant's argument that the administrative judge improperly disallowed some of her exhibits, the appellant does not specify which of her exhibits were disallowed. Our review of the record shows that the administrative judge issued an order dated August 30, 2012, granting the agency's motion to strike as untimely several exhibits that the appellant submitted after the hearing. An administrative judge may refuse to consider any motion or other pleading that is not filed in a timely fashion. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(c). Moreover, the appellant has not explained how the disallowed exhibits would affect the result reached in this appeal. Thus, she has not established that the administrative judge abused his broad discretion in excluding evidence or that any such error denigrated her substantive rights. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981)."); Thomas v. USPS, 116 MSPR 453, 456-57 ¶ 4, 2011 MSPB 62 (2011) ("The Board has held that in order to obtain reversal of an initial decision on the ground that the administrative judge abused his discretion in excluding evidence, the petitioning party must show on review that relevant evidence, which could have affected the outcome, was disallowed. Sanders v. Social Security Administration, 114 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 10 (2010); Jezouit v. Office of Personnel Management, 97 M.S.P.R. 48, ¶ 12 (2004), aff'd, 121 Fed. Appx. 865 (Fed. Cir. 2005). At the opening of the hearing, the administrative judge refused to permit the appellant to introduce exhibits that had not been approved during the pre-hearing conference, that had been in his control prior to the pre-hearing conference, and that she determined were not relevant to the case at hand. We find nothing in these documents that would have led the administrative judge to reach a different conclusion regarding the charge or the penalty. The appellant has therefore failed to make the required showing that the outcome was affected by the administrative judge's decision."); Armorer v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 103, 106 (1982) (excluding the EEO counselor's report was not erroneous; the appellant could not have established pretext through the document); cf. Fellhoelter v. USDA, 568 F.3d 965, 972 (Fed. Cir. 2009) (not error for the AJ to exclude duplicative evidence in an IRA appeal).

If the AJ excludes evidence as part of a prehearing order or during the hearing, the Board will not consider a claim of error as to that exclusion unless the proponent of the evidence objects to the AJ's evidentiary ruling. It is not enough to offer the evidence and have it rejected. Once it is rejected, an objection to the rejection must be lodged. See Whitehurst v. TVA, 43 MSPR 486, 491 (1990). On PFR, the party complaining of excluded evidence must describe the testimony it sought to elicit and explain how its inclusion in the record would affect the result reached. See Nuss v. OPM, 57 MSPR 451, 454 (1993); cf. Ziegler v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-11-0645-I-1 (NP 2/14/2013) (it appeared that the appellant's counsel may have objected to exclusion of evidence when he thought he was on the record but was not; the Board considered the nature of the evidence excluded and considered its exclusion to be harmless because it was cumulative).
