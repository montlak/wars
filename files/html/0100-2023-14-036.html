D. OVERTIME and PREMIUM PAY ASSIGNMENTS

Overtime may be claimed under both the Federal Employees Pay Act and the Fair Labor Standards Act, depending on the classification of the employee. Some employees do not qualify for any overtime under either statute. A proposal for overtime for National Guard technicians is nonnegotiable. By law, 32 USC 709(g), technicians may only receive compensatory time. NFFE Local 1655 and Illinois NG, 26 FLRA 654, 665 (1987); cf. NFFE Local 405 and Dept. of Army, Army Info. Sys. Command, St. Louis, 42 FLRA 1112, 1141 (1991) (Proposal 24: law prohibits compensatory time off to WG employees; as to GS employees, negotiable to call for grant to employees, at their request, of compensatory time off in lieu of overtime pay).

OPM regulations implementing the Federal Employees Pay Act, and requiring that overtime be official ordered or approved under that Act to be compensable, were reviewed in Doe v. United States, 372 F.3d 1347 (Fed. Cir. 2004), denying claims of overtime compensation by Justice Department lawyers. [Refer later in this chapter to "Compensatory Time."] Other statutes may affect overtime for employees of some agencies. See, e.g., Bull v. United States, 479 F.3d 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (affirming FLSA judgment for Customs and Border Protection Service canine enforcement officers). But cf. Almanza v. United States, 935 F.3d 1332 (Fed. Cir. 2019) (affirming denial of FLSA overtime for canine handler instructors engaged in voluntary, post-class activities).

The parties can negotiate for a provision requiring management to pay overtime if law or regulation requires that payment when employees perform work during their lunch hour. The proposal did not require a duty-free lunch hour and it did not require the agency to compensate employees for work during lunch hours in circumstances that would not otherwise justify the compensation. AFGE Local 727 and Court Serv. & Offender Supervision Agency, 59 FLRA 674, 677 (2004) (Proposal 5). The parties may bargain for a clause in their contract that will require the employer to remedy an unlawful deprivation of overtime through a back pay award. NAGE Local R1-109 and VAMC, Newington, 38 FLRA 928, 932-33 (1990). [But see discussion later in this chapter, "Settlement of Pay Claims."] Without statutory authorization, however, the scheduling of overtime does not entitle an employee to overtime compensation unless the hours were actually worked. Nat'l Ass'n of Agric. Employees and USDA, APHIS, Plant Protection and Quarantine, 48 FLRA 639, 644-45 (1993), reconsideration denied, 48 FLRA 914 (1993) (nonnegotiable: proposal that apparently required continuation of overtime pay, despite discontinuation of Sunday overtime shift), aff'd following remand, 51 FLRA 843, 845 (1996) (deferring to an OPM interpretation that 5 USC 6101 and 5 CFR 610.121 require employees to perform work to be entitled to overtime compensation); see FOP Lodge 1F and VAMC Providence, R.I., 57 FLRA 373, 383 (2001) (Proposal 10(a) (proposal nonnegotiable requiring officers receiving training to be paid overtime irrespective of whether overtime actually worked); USDA, APHIS, Plant Protection and Quarantine and Nat'l Ass'n of Agric. Employees, 51 FLRA 975, 978 (1996) ("[S]ection 6101 and section 610.121 do not apply to, and thus do not constrain, an agency's ability to terminate, due to lack of work, a Sunday overtime shift before its scheduled end. [U]nder 5 USC section 5542, PPQ officers could not be paid for overtime in situations where they are released from work in a manner that does not comply with section 6101 and section 610.121."), reconsideration denied, 52 FLRA 971 (1997); cf. IAM Lodge 2261 and Dept. of Army, Tooele Army Depot, 47 FLRA 427, 436-37 (1993) (arbitrator could not award Sunday premium pay under 5 USC 5546(a), allowing premium pay for Sunday work only if work is part of employee's regularly scheduled period of service and not on overtime, when work performed by the grievants would have been on Sunday and compensated on an overtime basis).
