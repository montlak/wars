3. Untimely Hearing Request

If a hearing request is untimely filed, the AJ should expressly rule on it. Spradlin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 279, 284 ¶ 11 (1999). The Alonzo standards, discussed concerning late appeals, also govern acceptance of untimely hearing requests. In addition to these standards, the Board considers the time remaining within the 120 days allowed under Board practice following filing of the appeal for issuance of an initial decision. Beaudette v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 394, 398, 5 MSPB 409 (1981). Beaudette considered how to deal with the purported negligence of an attorney who was on vacation and had difficulties with temporary office staff when the AJ's notice about hearing rights arrived at his office. The Board concluded that the appellant should not suffer for the attorney's minimal neglect. There was no showing of prejudice to the agency, the hearing request was only 10 days late, and there remained at least 90 days for the hearing and issuance of an initial decision. The appeal was remanded for a hearing.

Glover v. OPM, 9 MSPR 177, 179 (1981), discussed factors influencing whether an AJ should waive an untimely hearing request:

It is the Board's policy that its rules shall be applied in a manner which expedites the processing of each case, but with due regard for the rights of all the parties. 5 CFR § 1201.11. It is also the Board's policy that initial decisions in all appeals under 5 USC § 7701 will be issued within 120 days of the filing of the appeal. Gipson v. Veterans Administration, 1 MSPB 420, 1 MSPR 435 (1980). In deciding whether good cause exists for waiving the deadline for requesting a hearing, a presiding official should consider whether there is time remaining, after the request is filed, to reasonably permit the arrangement of a hearing and the issuance of the initial decision before the 120-day time limit expires. See Webb v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 MSPB 469, 3 MSPR 389 (1980). In this case, 61 days remained in the 120-day limit after appellant's request for a hearing was received.

Another factor to be considered by the presiding official, as set forth in Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 264-65, 4 MSPR 180, 184-85 (1980), is the degree to which the agency would be prejudiced as a result of a waiver of the time limit for filing a hearing request. In this regard we note the agency's failure to show that granting the hearing would prejudice it in any manner.

Further, a presiding official might reasonably take into account the type of case being appealed when entertaining a tardy request for a hearing. See Reaudette v. Department of the Navy, 5 MSPB 409, 5 MSPR 394 (1981). In a disability retirement appeal, such as this one, in which relatively few witnesses are normally required and in which the presiding official's analysis of subjective evidence of physical disability is mandated, the convening of a hearing is particularly important and comparatively not burdensome.

The factors were reiterated, Watty v. VA, NY-4324-14-0152-I-1 (NP 4/12/2016):

An appellant must submit any request for a hearing with the appeal or within any other time period the administrative judge sets for that purpose. 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.24(e), 1208.13(b), 1208.23(b). Failure to timely request a hearing will result in a waiver of that right when the appellant has not shown good cause for the failure. Robinson v. Department of the Army, 50 M.S.P.R. 412, 417 (1991). The factors to be considered when deciding to waive the deadline for requesting a hearing include those typically considered in connection with the waiver of the deadline for filing a petition for review: the length of the delay; whether the appellant was notified of the time limit or was otherwise aware; the existence of circumstances beyond the control of the appellant that affected his ability to comply with the time limit; the degree to which negligence by the appellant is shown to be present or absent; circumstances showing that any neglect involved is excusable neglect; a showing of unavoidable casualty or misfortune; and the extent and nature of the prejudice to the agency that would result from waiver of the time limit. Beaudette v. Department of the Navy, 5 M.S.P.R. 394, 398 (1981). In addition to these factors, the administrative judge should consider whether the time remaining, as of the date the request for a hearing was filed, reasonably permits the convening of a hearing and the rendering of a decision on the appeal within 120 days from the date on which the appeal was filed. Id.

The misdirected mailing of the acknowledgment order weighs in favor of waiving a filing deadline. See Birdsong v. Department of the Navy, 75 M.S.P.R. 524, 527 (1997). Further, although the appellant's request for a hearing was late, the delay was not lengthy under the circumstances. There was sufficient time, more than 90 days, for the administrative judge to convene a hearing after the hearing request and render a decision within 120 days. The agency has not established that any prejudice would result from waiving the time limit. Based on these facts, we reverse the administrative judge's finding that the appellant waived his right to request a hearing. See Shadrick v. Office of Personnel Management, 89 M.S.P.R. 357, ¶ 8 (2001) (finding that service at an incorrect address constitutes good cause for failure to respond to an administrative judge in a timely manner).

A delay of eight days in requesting a hearing, following the entry of appearance of retained counsel, resulted in disapproval of the belated request, Ramos v. DHS, AT-0752-13-0637-I-1 (NP 11/21/2014) (Vice Chairman Wagner dissenting):

The appellant argued below that good cause existed to excuse the untimely filed request for a hearing based on her prior pro se status, lack of understanding of the importance of a hearing, the failure of the administrative judge to explain the importance of the right to testify and cross-examine adverse witnesses, the risk of "significant" prejudice to her case if she was denied a hearing, absence of prejudice to the agency, and the fact that there were 147 days left until the initial decision deadline as, at the time, case processing had been suspended for 30 days to allow the parties to conduct discovery. Further, she noted that she had requested a hearing within 1 business day of retaining counsel. The administrative judge denied her request on the grounds that the appellant's prior pro se status and lack of understanding of the importance of requesting an evidentiary hearing did not constitute good cause for her untimely request.

On review, we discern no reason to disturb the administrative judge's denial of appellant's untimely hearing request. The appellant does not allege that she was unaware of her hearing right or that she was otherwise unable to understand and respond to the Board's acknowledgment order. See Robinson v. Department of Army, 50 M.S.P.R. 412, 417 (1991). The fact that she was pro se at the time of the filing deadline does not constitute good cause; the Board has long held that a party's lack of legal counsel or inability to obtain legal counsel does not establish good cause for an untimely filing. McCoy v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 256, ¶ 8 (2009), aff'd, 360 F. App'x 132 (Fed. Cir. 2010). Similarly, an appellant's inexperience with legal matters and unfamiliarity with Board procedures do not warrant waiver of the Board's deadlines. See Brady v. Department of Labor, 57 M.S.P.R. 341, 344 (1993), aff'd, 26 F.3d 138 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Although the 8-day delay was not particularly significant, we have consistently denied a waiver of a filing deadline if good cause for the delay is not shown, even where the delay is minimal and the appellant is pro se. See, e.g., Moorman v. Department of the Army, 68 M.S.P.R. 60, 64 (1995) (declining to excuse a 3-day delay in filing an appeal where the appellant failed to establish good cause for the delay), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). Further, prejudice to another party is only one factor to be considered in determining the existence of good cause and, in any event, will not be considered until the appellant establishes good cause for the untimely filing. See Miller v. Department of Army, 43 M.S.P.R. 228, 232 (1990). Likewise, the number of days remaining until the initial decision deadline is also only one factor to be considered and, moreover, does not establish good cause for an untimely filing. See Beaudette, 5 M.S.P.R. at 398; see also Alonzo, 4 M.S.P.R. at 184.

Implying that requests to waive deadlines for hearings should be supported by sworn statements, Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 412, 417 (1991), noted that "[appellant's] representative's mere allegations that the 'yes' block was checked on his copy of the appeal petition and that its omission from the copy forwarded to the Board was a 'clerical error' are insufficient to establish the truth of the assertions." Cf. Spradlin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 279, 283-84 ¶ 11 (1999) (AJ was obliged to inform the appellant that a late filed hearing request must be accompanied by a showing of good cause for delay). Should the AJ excuse a late hearing request without an explicit ruling on a show cause order, there is no error if the hearing is granted and the record contains evidence supporting a waiver of the deadline for the hearing request. See Parker v. VA, 30 MSPR 109, 110 n.1 (1986). If it is unclear when the appellant received an acknowledgment order setting out the process for requesting a hearing, lacking other evidence the Board may attempt to determine for itself when the order was received by the appellant. The Board may then determine when a reasonably prudent appellant would have requested a hearing. See Delacruz v. OPM, 37 MSPR 71, 73-74 (1988).
