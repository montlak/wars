l. Eleventh Circuit

Stephens v. DHHS, 901 F.2d 1571 (11th Cir. 1990), resolved that the existence of CSRA remedies and OSC precluded access to courts as to a promotion claim based upon alleged constitutional violations. The court affirmed a district court ruling that the plaintiff was not entitled to veterans preference. See Gibbs v. United States, 517 Fed. Appx. 664, 668 (11th Cir. 2013) (CSRA remedies are government employee's exclusive remedy when challenging federal personnel action taken against him); Hardison v. Cohen, 375 F.3d 1262, 1264-66 (11th Cir. 2004) (monetary remedy was not available for VA Title 38 medical resident terminated without an available administrative procedure; Congress declined to provide protections and court would not imply protection through monetary damages under Bivens; equitable relief was precluded); Miller v. USDA Farm Servs. Agency, 143 F.3d 1413 (11th Cir. 1998) (precluding Bivens remedy for ASCS county executive director; APA review of agency termination procedures was sufficient remedy); Ferry v. Hayden, 954 F.2d 658, 661 (11th Cir. 1992) (equating, for purposes of analyzing preclusion of civil lawsuit, violation of merit system principle protecting constitutional rights with prohibited personnel practice; no consideration of § 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)], calling for violation of a law, rule, or regulation implementing or concerning a merit system principle); Wells v. FAA, 755 F.2d 804, 810 (11th Cir. 1984) (applying preclusion analysis to cases arguably invoking First as well as Fifth Amendment rights: "We should never suppose that in providing more effective remedies for some grievances than for others, the Congress intended that those grievants who were left in the second group were really meant be better off than those in the first.").
