i. Clear Legal Error; Manifest Injustice

The Board may, under appropriate circumstances, reopen an appeal to correct a clear and material legal error, Guenther v. USPS, 68 MSPR 667, 670-71 (1995) (still dismissing an untimely PFR), or to prevent a manifest injustice to the appellant's substantive rights, Fields v. OPM, 79 MSPR 659, 661 ¶ 6 (1998), and Morris v. OPM, 85 MSPR 128, 129 (2000) (although the PFR was late, the Board reopened the appeal sua sponte to avoid the manifest injustice in the adjudication of the appellant's annuity entitlement). See Sellers v. USPS, 98 MSPR 44, 48 ¶ 7 (2004) (restating manifest injustice rule and reopening because the AJ erred in dismissing the appeal for lack of jurisdiction).

In Gordon-Cureton v.USPS, 107 MSPR 79, 83-84 ¶ 11, 2007 MSPB 239 (2007), the Board was confronted with the need to review an appeal that had been remanded when the AJ failed to properly apply remand instructions and when the agency representative failed to provide information essential to the determination of the appellant's claim to veterans preference status, a jurisdictional issue:

...Parties who appear before the Board are expected to adhere to filing deadlines, and ordinarily the Board will not reopen an appeal to cure the untimeliness of a petition for review. Still, "the Board will not turn a blind eye to clear and material errors" that have prejudiced a party's rights. Sutcliff v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 101, ¶ 7 (2000). Notwithstanding the untimeliness of a petition for review, the Board has the discretion to reopen an appeal to prevent a "manifest injustice" when an error implicates a party's "basic procedural rights." Trachtenberg v. Department of Defense, 104 M.S.P.R. 640, ¶ 12 (2007). As explained below, under the particular circumstances presented, we find it appropriate to reopen this appeal.

See Rothlisberger v. Dept. of Army, 111 MSPR 662, 666 ¶ 8, 2009 MSPB 141 (2009) (noting the "manifest injustice" criterion in an indefinite suspension case involving due process considerations). Reopening was employed to permit OPM to provide its views on a Board decision relating to reviewability of agency decisions to remove employees deemed unable to hold noncritical sensitive positions, and the due process required to make those determinations, in Crumpler v. Dept. of Defense, 113 MSPR 94, 2009 MSPB 233 (2009).

The Board summarized its law concerning petitions to reopen that are belated (by nine weeks) but granted by the Board sua sponte, McVicker v. SSA, 77 MSPR 324, 328 (1997):

[W]e note that the Board has broad discretion in deciding whether to open and reconsider a case on its own motion. See 5 CFR § 1201.118; Azarkhish v. Office of Personnel Management, 915 F.2d 675, 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990). Although the Board generally will not reopen an appeal to cure the untimeliness of a party's petition for review, the Board will not turn a blind eye to clear and material errors that appear to have prejudiced an appellant's substantive rights. See, e.g., Robinson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 72 MSPR 444, 447-50 (1996); McFalls v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 MSPR 252, 258-59 (1996). When cognizance of an error that implicates an employee's basic procedural rights is necessary to prevent manifest injustice, the Board will reopen a case sua sponte to address that error. See, e.g., Robinson, 72 MSPR at 447-50 (reopening the case sua sponte to address the administrative judge's clear error of law that prevented the appellant from showing that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal); Carey v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 359, 361 (1991) (reopening the case sua sponte to address claim of new evidence that would provide the Board with jurisdiction over the appeal).
