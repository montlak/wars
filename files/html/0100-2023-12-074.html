F. LEVEL OF BARGAINING

Determination of disputes concerning the level of bargaining is for ULP proceedings, not negotiability appeals. NTEU Chap. 71 and IRS, Philadelphia Serv. Ctr., 4 FLRA 796, 797 (1980); see AFGE Council of Soc. Sec. Dist. Office Locals and DHHS, SSA, San Francisco Region, 4 FLRA 584, 587 (1980) (ULP case, not a negotiability appeal, is proper means to resolve whether, subsequent to unit consolidation, bargaining obligation exists at local level); AFGE Local 2879 and SSA, San Diego, Cal., 2 FLRA 733, 734 (1980) (question of whether bargaining obligation existed after unit consolidation is appropriate for ULP case but not negotiability appeal). But the union is entitled to a negotiability determination even though the agency disputes the proper level of bargaining. Nat'l Ass'n of Agric. Employees, Local 39 and USDA, APHIS, Elizabeth, N.J., 49 FLRA 319, 320 (1994), provides an example of FLRA's approach:

The Agency contends that the Authority lacks jurisdiction to consider the proposals contained in the petition for review because the Agency had no obligation to bargain below the level of recognition. Specifically, the Agency claims that the title of exclusive representative and "accompanying rights are reserved for NAAE, National, which has not delegated any type of bargaining authority to NAAE Local 39."

The Union argues that "Local #39 is...recognized by both the National Union and the Agency as an appropriate local bargaining unit." The Union further argues that the proposals that are the subject of negotiation at the local level were presented in response to policy changes that were made or announced by the Agency at the local level. Therefore, the Union maintains that it was the appropriate entity to engage in impact and implementation negotiations.

We reject the Agency's argument that we lack jurisdiction to consider the petition for review. The Agency's contention concerns the parties' duty to bargain in this case. It is well established that the Authority will consider a petition for review of a negotiability issue where the parties disagree over whether a proposal conflicts with law, rule, or regulation.... In this case, the Agency declared the proposals nonnegotiable on the basis that they interfere with the exercise of various management rights. Where, as here, the conditions governing review of negotiability issues have been met, a union is entitled to a decision by the Authority as to whether its proposals are within the duty to bargain under the Statute, notwithstanding the fact that additional issues may exist. See American Federation of Government Employees, AFL-CIO, Local 2736 v. FLRA, 715 F.2d 627, 630-31 (D.C. Cir. 1983). To the extent that there are additional issues regarding the duty to bargain in the specific circumstances of this case, those issues should be resolved in other appropriate proceedings....

FLRA's earlier decisions were not always consistent. FLRA determined that if a matter is covered at a higher level of negotiations and is not within the purview of local negotiations, a negotiability appeal as to the proposal will be dismissed as moot. NFFE Local 1979 and Forest Serv., San Dimas Equip. Dev. Ctr., 16 FLRA 369, 376 (1984). But FLRA decided both duty to bargain and scope of bargaining in its negotiability decisions. In a negotiability appeal over a proposal concerning distribution of paychecks, FLRA resolved a dispute over whether the agency had the authority to enter into the agreement requested by the union, resulting in a determination that the agency improperly foreclosed bargaining by its particular manner of delegating authority over the matter at issue. AFGE Local 1409 and Army Adjutant Gen. Publications Ctr., 18 FLRA 508, 509 (1985).
