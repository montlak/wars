1. Method of Filing

5 CFR 1201.22 and 5 CFR 1201.4 (2022), identify the processes for filing an appeal:

§ 1201.22(d) Method of filing an appeal. Filing of an appeal must be made with the appropriate Board office by commercial or personal delivery, by facsimile, by mail, or by electronic filing under § 1201.14.

§ 1201.4(l) Date of filing. A document that is filed with a Board office by personal delivery is considered filed on the date on which the Board office receives it. The date of filing by facsimile is the date of the facsimile. The date of filing by mail is determined by the postmark date; if no legible postmark date appears on the mailing, the submission is presumed to have been mailed five days (excluding days on which the Board is closed for business) before its receipt. The date of filing by commercial delivery is the date the document was delivered to the commercial delivery service. The date of filing by e-filing is the date of electronic submission.

Although the Board's regulation establishes the postmark as the date of filing for an appeal filed by mail, the discussion that follows demonstrates that the postmark creates a presumption of the date of mailing, and that presumption is subject to rebuttal by evidence concerning the actual date and circumstances of the mailing. See Wilkins v. Dept. of Army, DC-0752-17-0238-I-1 (NP 10/7/2022) (certification on appeal form of date of receipt of removal decision: "Although the Board generally presumes that documents placed in the mail are received in 5 days, that presumption was rebutted here by the appellant's certified statement that he received the decision later. See Cabarloc v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 695, ¶¶ 7-8 (2009); 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.22(b)(3), 1201.23.").

Because the Board is so rigid in its insistence upon timely filing, and because things do go wrong in the filing process, it is recommended that the appeal always be filed on time, and if possible a few days before the deadline. For paper filings, two methods of filing can be used, for example, filing by both fax and mail, on the theory that two filing methods provide a greater chance of avoiding an appeal lost in the mail. Cf. King v. VA, CH-0752-12-0039-C-1 (NP 6/16/2014) ("When an appellant delays the filing of his petition for review until the eleventh hour, the appellant bears the risk that unforeseen circumstances could prevent the timely filing of his petition."). E-filing (should) eliminate the possibility of misdelivery of a mailed or faxed appeal. The e-filing system provides a (printable) receipt on final electronic submission of the appeal, and the system can print out a copy of the appeal as it is electronically recorded (with the filing date imprinted).
