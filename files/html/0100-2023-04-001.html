HOW TO USE THE FLRA GUIDE

Over the years, this Guide has adopted conventions of its own. Citations are generally to the Decisions of the Federal Labor Relations Authority, provided on-line by FLRA at www.flra.gov and printed by the Government Printing Office and identified by volume and page number as "FLRA". Block quotations from the FLRA and court decisions may omit footnotes, and if that occurs, we do not use the "[Footnote omitted]" signal. Additionally, block quotations will omit references to portions of the administrative record, e.g., "Agency Response to Petition, p. 40." No ellipses are used when record references are dropped. Dissenting and concurring opinions are usually not noted in discussion or quotation of FLRA and court cases. Readers are cautioned to read full opinions, available through printed text, CYBERFEDS, LEXIS, PERSONNET/WESTLAW, and, often, through the FLRA Internet website, www.flra.gov.

There are references to ALJ decisions throughout the Guide. Those references are usually to decisions by the Authority that incorporate an ALJ decision. Decisions of ALJs that are not reviewed by the Authority, for lack of exceptions by a party, are generally not referenced in the Guide because they lack precedential value.

Many references are made to the Code of Federal Regulations and to the United States Code. When references to the CFR and USC are made in the text (as opposed to quotations or parenthetical explanations following citations), an effort is made to keep the citations current, or to show whether the CFR or USC reference was only valid for a past year. The regulations and statutes change, and not all references mentioned in the Guide are checked each year. Readers should check the current edition of the CFR and USC to ensure the validity of a citation.

There are many references throughout the Guide to the FPM or Federal Personnel Manual. That publication, once OPM's mainstay of federal personnel regulation, was abolished in 1993. Some elements of the FPM were incorporated into Title 5 of the CFR. Other components have reappeared in various OPM guidelines to agency personnel officials. To the extent FLRA decisions directly depend upon the FPM then extant, research should be conducted to determine whether the operative FPM provision has been continued in some other form or not at all.

In past years the Guide included the full text of some of the FLRA General Counsel's legal guidance memoranda summarizing areas of Authority jurisprudence. These memoranda are becoming dated. We are now referencing them in the Guide by title, with the Internet website address where the memoranda can be found, but we have deleted them as appendices to the Guide.

This Guide reflects the author's interpretation of the importance, organization, and content of decisions from the FLRA and its reviewing courts. Any decision cited in the Guide should be read in its entirety. Any issue in federal sector labor relations entails some complexity. Readers should conduct their own research, using this Guide as a starting but not concluding point for analysis.
