s. Attempt to Obtain Congressional Assistance

One example of the Board's insistence that parties read and follow Board directives is Montoya v. USPS, 34 MSPR 610, 610-11 (1987), declaring that an untimely PFR will not be accepted because the appellant professes that he could not get information from a congressional office concerning the date for filing a PFR; the initial decision stated the due date. See Fredrick v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 477, 480 (1997) ("The appellant's decision to deal directly with agency officials, or to seek Congressional intervention, instead of filing a petition for review, does not establish a good cause for waiver."); Swift v. OPM, 68 MSPR 411, 413 (1995) (that the appellant was unable to afford a representative and that congressional offices and the White House could not assist her did not constitute good cause for her untimely PFR); Jackson v. OPM, 55 MSPR 105, 108 (1992) (three month delay was not excused on the basis that the appellant supplied her documentation to a state senate office); Styskal v. VA, 54 MSPR 270, 272 (1992) (decision to seek congressional intervention, rather than Board review, does not excuse an untimely PFR); Montoya v. OPM, 53 MSPR 387, 389 (1992) (appellant's appointment with a senator's staff to obtain assistance with a PFR was more than a month after the PFR was due; her inability to secure assistance because the senator's office was being picketed was insufficient to excused a belated filing); Necessary v. Dept. of Interior, 49 MSPR 356, 358-59 (1991) (declining to extend the period for filing a PFR when the appellant could have followed directions containing information for filing and instead enlisted the assistance of a congressman); Oestreich v. Dept. of Air Force, 7 MSPR 355, 356, 7 MSPB 237 (1981) (delay in the filing of a PFR for the reason that the appellant has "been gathering information and documents for my Congressman" was not sufficient to excuse the late filing); cf. Chiarella v. USPS, 41 MSPR 16, 18 (1989) (appellant "has not described the nature of the information that he received from the different agencies and organizations, or from the Congressman's office, and why that information may be considered to constitute new evidence that was not otherwise available with due diligence before the expiration of the filing period"). These cases notwithstanding, the Board docketed a PFR that was initiated as a request for assistance sent by the appellant to a congressman who, in turn, sent the letter on to the Board. Rodriguez v. USPS, 96 MSPR 189, 190 ¶ 3 (2004).
