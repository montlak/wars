III. JURISDICTIONAL OVERVIEW

5 USC 1204 recites the Board's jurisdiction under its enabling statute:

(a) The Merit Systems Protection Board shall--

(1) hear, adjudicate, or provide for the hearing or adjudication, of all matters within the jurisdiction of the Board under this title, chapter 43 of title 38, or any other law, rule, or regulation, and, subject to otherwise applicable provisions of law, take final action on any such matter[.]

"Jurisdiction is 'a tribunal's "power to hear a case," a matter that "can never be forfeited or waived."' Union Pac. R.R. Co., 558 U.S. at 81 (quoting Arbaugh, 546 U.S. at 514)." May v. McDonough, 61 F.4th 963 (Fed. Cir. 2023 NP) (from dissent of Judge Newman).

The Reform Act, its amendatory and supplementary statutes, and its predecessor statutes create procedural and substantive entitlements, and appellate and remedial rights to enforce those entitlements. The Board operates through affirmative grants of jurisdiction through statutes or implementing regulations. Unless there are statutory (or regulatory) restrictions, or benefits or restrictions contained in a collective bargaining contract, the government is generally free to withhold benefits from a variety of federal positions. See Fowler v. United States, 633 F.2d 1258, 1260 (8th Cir. 1980). "The Board does not have jurisdiction over all matters involving a federal employee that are allegedly unfair or incorrect." Mendelson v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3061); see Sato v. Dept. of Army, SF-3443-16-0122-I-1 (NP 7/19/2016) ("The Board's jurisdiction is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule, or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Thus, it follows that the Board does not have jurisdiction over all matters involving Federal employees that are alleged to be unfair or incorrect. Johnson v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 573, 577 (1995)."); Hipona v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 522, 525 (1989) (5 USC 7701(c)(2)(C) authorizes the Board to overturn agency actions within its jurisdiction that are not in accordance with law, but that section does not provide the Board jurisdiction over all actions alleged to be incorrect); cf. Van Gilder v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 337, 412 (1982) (the Board lacks authority to create a remedy for an appellant who has exhausted all judicial appeals and courts have found adequate grounds for an agency action). If the Board lacks jurisdiction over a matter, it lacks authority to refer the matter to another forum that has jurisdiction. The Board ordinarily looks to the provisions of statutes in force when an action is taken to determine if the employee has appeal rights. Stewart v. DOD, 82 MSPR 649, 654 ¶ 12 (1999) [discussed later in this chapter, "Department of Defense Education Activity"]; cf. Bishop v. USDA, 2022 MSPB 28., ¶ 10 (2022) (defining the scope of "civil service law").

If the Board has jurisdiction--authority to decide--an appeal, it does not easily yield that authority. Brady v. Dept. of Treasury, 94 MSPR 439, 442 ¶ 7 (2003). Jonson v. FDIC, 121 MSPR 56, 61-62 ¶¶ 7-9, 2014 MSPB 22 (2014) (dissent by Member Robbins), said of Board jurisdiction, contrary to an argument that the agency's organic statute limited Board review rights in an adverse action:

In 1978, Congress enacted the Civil Service Reform Act (CSRA) to replace the prior "patchwork system" of laws that had governed federal employment with "an integrated scheme of administrative and judicial review, designed to balance the legitimate interests of the various categories of federal employees with the needs of sound and efficient administration." United States v. Fausto, 484 U.S. 439, 445 (1988). The Board has jurisdiction under chapter 75 of the CSRA to review agency actions removing federal employees and to examine the reasons on which those actions are based. Siegert v. Department of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 684, 686 (1988); see 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d); see also Elgin v. Department of the Treasury, 132 S. Ct. 2126, 2130 (2012) (discussing both an employee's rights and the scope of Board review under the CSRA). As the Supreme Court has explained, "the CSRA makes [Board] jurisdiction over an appeal dependent only on the nature of the employee and the employment action at issue." Elgin, 132 S. Ct. at 2137 (citations omitted); see Fausto, 484 U.S. at 448-49 (holding that, given the "comprehensive nature" of the CSRA, the exclusion of certain employees from the protections of chapter 75 was deliberate). Here, neither party disputes that the appellant meets the definition of an employee for purposes of chapter 75 jurisdiction. Instead, the FDIC argues that section 19 of the Resolution Trust Corporation Completion Act (RTCCA), Pub. L. No. 103-204, § 19, 107 Stat. 2369, 2402-04 (1993) (codified as amended at 12 U.S.C. § 1822(f)), limits the Board's authority under the CSRA by empowering the FDIC to "prescribe regulations establishing procedures for ensuring that any individual who is performing, directly or indirectly, any function or service on behalf of the Corporation meets minimum standards of competence, experience, integrity, and fitness," including prohibiting individuals who have engaged in a "pattern or practice of defalcation...from performing any service on behalf of the [FDIC]." 12 U.S.C. § 1822(f)(4)(A), (E).

We cannot agree with the FDIC that the Board's jurisdiction is limited by section 19 of the RTCCA. There is nothing in the statute that suggests that Congress intended to circumscribe the reach of the CSRA. 12 U.S.C. § 1822(f). In addition, the FDIC cites to no legislative history that supports its position, and we can locate none. See IAF, Tab 10 at 13-14 (failing to provide any such history); H.R. Rep. No. 103-103(I)-(II) (1993), reprinted in 1993 U.S.C.C.A.N. 3040, 3051, 3091-92 (discussing section 1822(f), identified as section 19 of the proposed legislation, without reference to the CSRA); H.R Rep. No. 103-380 (1993) (Conf. Rep.), reprinted in 1993 U.S.C.C.A.N. 3098, 3101 (same). We are mindful of the mandate that, when possible, statutes should be read to "foster harmony" with each other. Digital Equipment Corp. v. Desktop Direct, Inc., 511 U.S. 863, 879 (1994) (citing, among other cases, Ruckelshaus v. Monsanto Co., 467 U.S. 986, 1018 (1984) ("[W]here two statutes are 'capable of co-existence, it is the duty of the courts, absent a clearly expressed congressional intention to the contrary, to regard each as effective.'") (citation omitted)). Applying this principle, we read 12 U.S.C. § 1822(f) as empowering the FDIC to regulate the conduct of those providing services on its behalf, while still providing qualified employees with the full review of the Board for adverse employment actions under chapter 75 of the CSRA. We do not view these two principles as mutually exclusive.

In this regard, we decline to follow the regulations at 12 C.F.R. Part 336, Subpart B to the extent that they mandate the appellant's removal and limit Board review of that removal. Instead, we agree with the administrative judge's conclusion regarding issue 6, that the Board has jurisdiction over the adverse action and the penalty.

There are statutes, and there are circumstances--e.g., national security--that limit or block Board jurisdiction and remedial authority, and they are discussed in portions of this and other chapters of this Guide. Bottom line: the Board is the creation of statute, and statutes provide its jurisdiction. If there is no statutory (or in some cases regulatory) authority for the exercise of Board authority, the Board lacks jurisdiction over the dispute. The analysis of the Board's jurisdiction is an analysis of the statutes establishing limitations on defined government actions affecting enumerated groups of employees. [Refer to the Board's brief description of its jurisdiction at https://www.mspb.gov/appeals/jurisdiction.htm and to its 2007 "Information Sheet No. 1: Jurisdiction" at https://www.mspb.gov/appeals/infosheets/Jurisdiction.htm.]

Most actions appealed to the Board present no jurisdictional quandary. Adverse and performance-based actions appeals from OPM's retirement decisions, and reductions in force by tenured employees (those with the type of appointment and sufficient time in the federal service to qualify for appeal rights) move to adjudication, assuming a timely appeal, without complication. In other appeals, including those filed by whistleblowers and individuals seeking to negate resignations and retirements, the Board's jurisdictional analysis is elaborate and abstruse. Instead of accepting those appeals and adjudicating them on the merits, the Board requires that appellants make an initial showing through vaguely-stated pleading standards that the cases are within its jurisdiction. Appellants must then plead their cases with particularity and legal refinement to establish a prima facie showing of Board jurisdiction. What then ensues is a battle between the parties and the AJ over whether or not the appellant's allegations demonstrate Board jurisdiction to the AJ's satisfaction under subjective pleading standards. If so, the case moves to a hearing; if not, the case is dismissed as beyond the Board's jurisdiction. Heightened pleading requirements for atypical cases equate to the Board's use of summary judgment without application of that appellation to the process. The result is that advocates must, in any but routine appeals, scrutinize the case to determine, and then present to the Board, arguments and evidence demonstrating that the facts alleged in an appeal do or do not make out a case within the Board's jurisdiction. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action"; Chapter 13, "Overview" (describing appeals presenting prohibited personnel practice issues); and Chapter 17, "Judicial Relief Outside the Reform Act" (considering judicial remedies outside the appellate processes of the Reform Act).]

Most Board cases fall within several categories of appeals, each receiving chapter-length treatment in this Guide: adverse actions [Chapter 6], performance actions [Chapter 9], reductions in force [Chapter 10], disputes over entitlement to retirement and restoration benefits [Chapter 14], and Individual Right of Action claims undertaken by whistleblowers who allege reprisal [Chapter 13] . Other classes of appeals are derived from statute or regulation. These include appeals challenging OPM regulations and agency actions implementing the regulations; appeals challenging employment practices established by OPM; appeals involving suitability determinations; actions against administrative law judges; requests for review of arbitration awards involving actions and employees otherwise within the Board's jurisdiction coupled with allegations of violation of civil rights laws; and appeals under the 1994 Uniformed Service Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA) and its counterpart, the 1998 Veterans Employment Opportunities Act (VEOA) (appeals that are increasing in number as servicemen and servicewomen in overseas conflicts return from military service and seek positions or reinstatement in the civil service). [For editorial convenience, discussion of appeals under USERRA and VEOA is found later in this chapter, "Uniformed Service Employment and Reemployment Rights Act" and "Veterans Employment Opportunities Act." Discussion of appeals involving OPM employment practices, regulations, and regulatory implementation is also found later in this chapter, "OPM Regulation Review; Challenges to Agency Implementation of OPM Regulations; Challenges to OPM Employment Practices"; and suitability actions are discussed later in this chapter "Suitability Determination."]

Board jurisdiction is generally granted and limited by statute, Godfrey v. VA, 40 MSPR 438, 442 (1989), or by OPM regulation, including appeals from reductions in force and limited appeal rights for probationary terminations. Because the Board is a limited-jurisdiction tribunal whose authority to act is derived from statute and regulation, it is obligated to satisfy itself that it has jurisdiction over the action. Costin v. DHHS, 64 MSPR 517, 528 (1994). The existence of Board jurisdiction is a threshold adjudication issue; the appellant has the burden of establishing jurisdiction by nonfrivolous allegations, defined by Board regulation. See Massimino v. VA, 58 MSPR 318, 322 (1993) (jurisdiction is a threshold issue to be established by preponderant evidence); cf. Fidler v. USPS, 53 MSPR 440, 442 (1992). "[I]t is the nature of the action, and not the agency's characterization of the action, that determines the Board's jurisdiction." Czarkowski v. Dept. of Navy, 87 MSPR 107, 115 ¶ 20 (2000).

The Board has plenary statutory authority to hear, adjudicate, or provide for the hearing or adjudication of all matters within its jurisdiction. It also has authority to order any federal agency or employee to comply with any order or decision issued and to enforce compliance with the order or decision. 5 USC 1204(a)(1), (2). The Board has authority under 5 USC 1204(e)(2)(A) to enforce compliance with any order issued under § 1204(a)(2) by ordering that any employee, other than a presidential appointee, shall not be entitled to be paid during the period of noncompliance with the order--authority the Board has threatened but not exercised. See Stone v. OPM, 5 MSPR 68, 70, 5 MSPB 142 (1981) (referring to the predecessor statute, 5 USC 1205). [Refer to compliance remedies in Chapter 16, "Enforcement."]

The corollary of the rule that the Board has plenary authority to hear matters within its jurisdiction is the rule that "[t]he Board's jurisdiction is not plenary but is limited to that granted by statute, rule, or regulation." Monser v. Dept. of Army, 67 MSPR 477, 479 (1995); see James v. USPS, CH-3443-13-0332-I-1 (NP 1/22/2014) ("The Board does not have jurisdiction over all matters involving a federal employee that are allegedly unfair or incorrect. Miller v. Department of Homeland Security, 111 M.S.P.R. 325, ¶ 14 (2009), aff'd, 361 F. App'x 134 (Fed. Cir. 2010)."). A petitioner seeking Board review of agency action must establish that the Board has jurisdiction to hear the appeal. Paye v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3718) (citing Minor v. MSPB, 819 F.2d 280 (Fed. Cir. 1987)). Unless a court order pertains directly to an appeal before the Board, the Board does not have general jurisdiction to enforce civil court orders pertaining to federal employment status. See Thomas v. Dept. of State, 12 MSPR 555, 559 (1982); cf. Weaver v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 465, 466-67 (1983) (Board lacks jurisdiction to direct the VA or a state agency to grant the appellant a pension). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Jurisdictional Issues," and Chapter 14, "Annuity Calculations Issues," concerning court-ordered division of retirement benefits.]

The Federal Circuit noted of Board jurisdiction, King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 1994):

[T]he board has no plenary jurisdiction. Cowan v. United States, 710 F.2d 803, 805 (Fed. Cir. 1983). We construe its jurisdiction narrowly; it includes only those actions specifically granted by some law, rule, or regulation. Id.; Van Werry v. MSPB, 995 F.2d 1048, 1050 (Fed. Cir. 1993). If we believe the board's interpretation of section 7701(b)(2) is "contrary to the intent of Congress, as divined from the statute and its legislative history," we owe the board's statutory interpretation no deference. Muwwakkil v. OPM, 18 F.3d 921, 925 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

Discussion in this chapter will focus on the scope or limits of Board jurisdiction. The Board defines and strictly abides by the limitations on its authority. Under McNeese v. OPM, 61 MSPR 70, 73 (1994), "It is well settled that the Board's jurisdiction is not plenary, but is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by statute or regulation. Moreover, the Board is without authority to broaden or narrow its appellate jurisdiction through the exercise of inherent power." An agency's mistaken notice to an employee of Board appeal rights does not confer jurisdiction. See Boyles v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3327); Hunter v. DOJ, 73 MSPR 290, 294 (1997) ("the mere fact that the agency informed the appellant in its termination notice that he had a right of appeal to the Board does not confer jurisdiction on the Board"); Miles-Townsend v. USPS, 37 MSPR 405, 407 (1988) (affirming AJ's ruling that an agency's misstatement of an appeal right does not confer Board jurisdiction); Morris v. Dept. of Interior, 11 MSPR 126, 128 (1982) ("[T]he fact that an agency improperly advises an employee of a right of appeal which does not, in fact, exist, is insufficient to confer jurisdiction on the board. 5 U.S.C. 1205 (a)(1) and 7701; O'Halloran v. Department of Agriculture, 3 MSPB 66 (1980).").

"Jurisdiction may not be conferred on the Board by estoppel based on an appellant's reliance on an agency's misrepresentation." Colbert v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 492, 497 (1992) (citing Walton v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 244, 249-50 (1989)); see Annunziata v. USPS, 36 MSPR 192, 196 (1988) ("Notwithstanding the fact that appellant was apparently misled by the administrative judge's erroneous statement, such reliance cannot create a right of appeal to the Board where none exists."); cf. Williams-Hargraves v. HUD, 88 MSPR 176, 181 ¶ 12 (2001) (error in vacancy announcement did not convert an excepted temporary appointment into competitive service appointment); cf. Smith v. MSPB, 813 F.2d 1216 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (OPM's approval of a performance appraisal system did not establish an appeal right for a nonpreference eligible employee). The erroneous description of appeal rights by an AJ does not create jurisdiction. Nor may jurisdiction be established through estoppel based on an agency's misconduct. See Walton v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 244, 249-50 (1989) (agency's misrepresentation as to the rate of pay upon an employee's exercise of return rights did not confer jurisdiction on the Board: "the law is well settled that subject matter jurisdiction cannot be conferred by estoppel based on misrepresentations, as a sanction, or based on any other actions by the parties" (citing Supreme Court cases)). If the Board lacks jurisdiction over an action, it cannot correct procedural errors by the agency. See Zamora v. DOD, 62 MSPR 338, 340 (1994) (because the Board did not have jurisdiction over "suspension" while the appellant was otherwise in a nonduty and nonpay status, it could not correct errors in that suspension).

The Board must determine its own jurisdiction in an appeal. McCray v. Dept. of Army, 2023 MSPB 10, ¶ 23 (2023). Parties cannot confer jurisdiction on the Board by agreement, although they can stipulate to facts that establish Board jurisdiction. Compare Murdock-Doughty v. Dept. of Air Force, 74 MSPR 244, 248 (1997) (agency's statement in response to an acknowledgment order that the appellant was an "employee" as defined by statute did not serve to confer jurisdiction on the Board; the AJ was not precluded by the agency's statement from considering Board jurisdiction), and Godfrey v. VA, 40 MSPR 438, 441 (1989) ("Even though the appellant and the agency argue that the Board has jurisdiction over this appeal, an agreement between the parties cannot serve to confer jurisdiction on the Board."), with Altman v. Dept. of Navy, 72 MSPR 656, 660 (1996) ("Here, the agency in its own decision admitted that it had constructively demoted the appellant. Although the Board must determine its own jurisdiction, we find that the agency's admission constitutes a sufficient factual basis for finding that we have jurisdiction over the appellant's appeal."), and Granger v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 487, 490 (1996) ("Although parties cannot confer jurisdiction on the Board by stipulating to the Board's jurisdiction...they can stipulate to facts that establish our jurisdiction over the underlying action....").

Unless a statute dictates a different result, an employee's status when the agency effected its action controls the Board's appellate jurisdiction. Godfrey v. VA, 40 MSPR 438, 441 (1989); see Stewart v. DOD, 82 MSPR 649, 654 ¶ 13 (1999) (citing Allen v. MSPB, 127 F.3d 1074 (Fed. Cir. 1997).). If the Board decides that it lacks jurisdiction over a case or class of cases, its ruling is retroactive: the ruling divests the Board of jurisdiction as to all matters pending in that case or related cases. See Williams v. DOD, 53 MSPR 23, 26 (1992).

Jurisdiction is always before the Board and may be raised by any party or sua sponte by the Board at any time during Board proceedings. Coradeschi v. DHS, 109 MSPR 591 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 199 (2008); see McDermott v. District of Columbia Gov't, 13 MSPR 345, 347 (1982) ("the Board recognizes that subject-matter jurisdiction may be raised at any time in the course of a legal proceeding. See Knee v. Chemical Leaman Tank Lines, Inc., 293 F. Supp. 1094, 1095 (D.C. Pa. 1968)."); cf. Hamilton v. MSPB, 75 F.3d 639, 642-45 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (MSPB may sua sponte raise timeliness of an appeal). Under Schwartz v. USPS, 68 MSPR 142, 144-45 (1995):

As a limited-jurisdiction tribunal whose authority to act is derived from statute and regulation, the Board is obligated to satisfy itself that it has jurisdiction over the action before it. It will not act where it lacks statutory or regulatory authority, and may raise the matter of its own jurisdiction sua sponte at any time. See O'Clery v. USPS, 67 MSPR 300, 302 (1995).

Even after a final Board decision determined the appellant to be an employee within its jurisdiction, a jurisdictional issue could be raised during compliance proceedings. See Murdock-Doughty v. Dept. of Air Force, 74 MSPR 244, 248-49 (1997); cf. Brewer v. USPS, 75 MSPR 234, 236 (1997) ("Because the appellant was not affected by an appealable action in the 1995 compliance RIF, we cannot consider his discrimination claim."); Rogers v. USPS, 59 MSPR 647, 651 (1993) ("The agency did not argue below that the appeal was beyond the Board's jurisdiction. Indeed, it advised the appellant of his right to file an appeal with the Board when it reinstated the earlier removal pursuant to the settlement agreement. Nevertheless, the Board, as a limited-jurisdiction federal tribunal, has an obligation to inquire into its own jurisdiction sua sponte. Cf. Hambsch v. United States, 857 F.2d 763, 764 (Fed. Cir. 1988), cert, denied, 490 U.S. 1054 (1989)."); Godfrey v. VA, 40 MSPR 438, 440 (1989) ("subject matter jurisdiction may be raised at any time in the course of a legal proceeding"; agreement between the parties cannot serve to confer jurisdiction on the Board); Holden v. OPM, 32 MSPR 367, 368 (1987) ("although the appellant contends that the agency's motion to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction was filed untimely, we note that the issue of subject-matter jurisdiction may be raised at any time").
