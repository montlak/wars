c. Dismissal of OSC Action

The Board may dismiss with prejudice an OSC disciplinary or corrective action if the employing agency takes an adverse action against the employee based on the same charges contained in the OSC complaint and OSC is satisfied with the corrective action taken by the agency. Special Counsel v. Woods, 28 MSPR 490, 494 (1985); see Special Counsel v. Julian, 71 MSPR 512, 513 (1996) ("When the OSC files a motion to dismiss a complaint, the Board will ordinarily dismiss the complaint, absent objection from the respondent."); Special Counsel v. VA, 67 MSPR 19, 20 (1995) (settlement of a corrective action complaint: "When the OSC files a notice of withdrawal of a complaint, the Board will ordinarily dismiss the complaint, absent objection from the respondent."); Special Counsel v. Lynn, 29 MSPR 666, 669 (1986) (approving OSC's request to dismiss its complaint when the agency-employer imposed penalties for the same conduct leading to the OSC complaint; the respondents' interests were unaffected by the dismissal since they could appeal their suspensions to the Board). This disciplined employee can make use of the Board's appellate jurisdiction to contest his removal. The Board may also, on motion of OSC, dismiss a complaint without prejudice if events following the filing of the complaint (the respondent's resignation and imposition of a criminal sanction) vitiate any purpose served through the continuation of administrative litigation. See Special Counsel v. Tedder, 8 MSPR 333, 334-35 (1981).

The denial of a respondent's motion to dismiss an OSC prosecution following presentation of the OSC case in chief does not conclusively establish that OSC has proved a prima facie case. The denial amounts only to "a tentative and inconclusive ruling on the quantum of [the Special Counsel's] proof" and "amounts to nothing more than a refusal to enter judgment at that time." Special Counsel v. Brown, 28 MSPR 133, 141 (1985).
