3. Destruction or Loss of Property

Vick v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 364, 366 (1980), considered appropriate removal of an electric power controller charged with endangering ship safety by failing to properly execute operational procedures regarding dock drainage valves. See Gober v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 354, 357-58 (1983) (removal of a machinist whose work resulted in sustained excessive levels of rejection and scrap). Removal was sustained, despite the AJ's initial mitigation of the penalty, for a medical supply technician charged with carelessness in following procedures required to sterilize medical equipment. The problem involved waste of approximately $1,000 of medical supplies. Of the employee's remorse as a mitigating factor, the Board concluded, without supporting citations, Williams v. VA, 65 MSPR 612, 615 (1994):

[A]lthough an employee's expression of remorse bears on the determination of an appropriate penalty for deliberate misconduct, whether the employee showed remorse is of little relevance where, as here, an adverse action is based on negligence. That the appellant is quite sorry that surgical instruments were contaminated does little to less the possibility of a recurrence of his negligence.

Negligent creation of a fire by a firefighter on private land warranted demotion rather than removal, even though there was property damage and involvement of agency personnel. The appellant had a long record of good service and potential for rehabilitation. Horney v. Forest Serv., 29 MSPR 543, 546-47 (1985); see Riley v. USDA, 20 MSPR 32, 34 (1984) (demotion and suspension of a supervisory official involved in fire control for setting an eight-acre fire to forest land that cost $3,500 to suppress). Jacoby v. USPS, 85 MSPR 554, 562-63 ¶¶ 15-17 (2000), mitigated to a suspension the removal of a postal supervisor who failed to set a station alarm on the day of an after-hours theft of $8,000 from the postal station. The appellant, who also unintentionally failed to "volunteer" information to agency investigators, had 22 years of service with adequate performance and no prior discipline. The agency did not show that the appellant's failure to set the alarm contributed to the perpetrator's ability to commit the robbery.

Careless operation of a fire truck, resulting in some property damage and slight injuries, sustained adverse action. The Board mitigated demotion to a suspension because of the agency's undue reliance on a past warning concerning another aspect of firefighting operations, the appellant's otherwise good record, and stress relating to personal and family problems. Sadorf v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 178, 181 (1993); see Stevenson v. DOJ, 14 MSPR 263, 267 (1982) (suspension for negligence in causing damage to a government helicopter and to a government vehicle).
