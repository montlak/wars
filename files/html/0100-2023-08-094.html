F. APPEAL RIGHTS OF PROBATIONER

Having doubtless confused the reader with case law that defines competitive and excepted service employees, how they avoid probation, and, when they cannot, how probationary or trial periods are calculated, we boldly advance to more straightforward discussion of the limited appeal rights available to employees who well and truly are probationers--often individuals serving in their very first appointment in the federal service. The limited appeal rights of probationers are collectively stated at 5 CFR 315.804-06 (2022), discussed later. Keep in mind that probationer's close relative, the trial period, defined by 5 CFR 316.304 (2022) as to the first year of service of a term employee in the competitive service:

Trial period.

(a) The first year of service of a term employee is a trial period regardless of the method of appointment. Prior Federal civilian service is credited toward completion of the required trial period in the same manner as prescribed by § 315.802 of this chapter.

(b) The agency may terminate a term employee at any time during the trial period. The employee is entitled to the procedures set forth in § 315.804 or § 315.805 of this chapter as appropriate.

To place in perspective discussion that follows, it should be understood that probation is meant to provide great latitude to managers in deciding to remove employees before they acquire Board adverse action appeal rights, Jones v. DOJ, 524 Fed. Appx. 660, 664 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP):

Generally, the agency uses the probationary period "to determine the fitness of the employee...for continued employment." § 315.803. As the Board has previously found in discussing suitability under 5 C.F.R. § 731.202, "misconduct in prior employment that would have a bearing on efficient service in the position in question...may be the basis for finding an individual unsuitable." LaPre v. Dep't of Justice, 62 M.S.P.R. 329, 335 (1994). Additionally, as BOP correctly notes, this court has recognized the "great latitude [of federal managers] in removing probationary employees before they bec[ome] vested with the rights afforded tenured employees." Bante v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 966 F.2d 647, 650 (Fed. Cir. 1992)....

Aside from the limited probationary appeal rights described in this section, probationers may have recourse through Individual Right of Action and prohibited personnel practice complaints, EEO complaints processed under the civil rights laws, unfair labor practice procedures of the federal-sector labor laws, possible constitutional challenges in court, appellate procedures used to challenge discrimination based on past or present military service, and the processes for unemployment and workers compensation claims. We discuss here only the limited appeal right to the MSPB conferred on probationers by OPM regulations. The appeal rights of competitive service probationers are summarized, Yancey v. DOD, DC-315H-11-0305-I-1 (NP 9/29/2011):

A probationary employee in the competitive service may only bring an appeal of her termination to the Board in three very limited circumstances: (1) The employee was discriminated against on account of her marital status; (2) the employee was discriminated against based on partisan political affiliation; or (3) the agency based its action, in whole or part, on issues that arose pre-appointment and did not follow the required procedures. Niemi v. Department of the Interior, 114 M.S.P.R. 143, ¶ 9 (2010); 5 C.F.R. §§ 315.805, 315.806.

[Refer to the Board's "Information Sheet No. 4: Probationary Employees" at https://www.mspb.gov/appeals/infosheets/Probationary_Employees.pdf.]

The analytical chain in a probationer's appeal was explained by Spain v. DHS, PH-315H-17-0448-I-1, n.3 (NP 6/9/2022):

If an appellant establishes Board jurisdiction by proving that the agency based a probationary termination on marital status or partisan political reasons, only then does the Board proceed to the merits of the case. Burton v. Department of the Air Force, 118 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶¶ 8-11 (2012); 5 C.F.R. § 315.806(b). In the event that an appellant establishes jurisdiction over a termination for preappointment reasons, the Board does not reach the merits of the agency's decision; rather, the Board's jurisdiction is limited to whether the agency failed to afford the appellant the procedural protections of 5 C.F.R. § 315.805, and, if so, whether the procedural error was harmful, such that the action must be set aside. West v. Department of Health & Human Services, 122 M.S.P.R. 434, ¶ 7 (2015). Finally, if an appellant establishes jurisdiction by showing that he is an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1), the Board will not reach the merits of the termination unless the agency's procedures for effecting the termination afforded the appellant minimum due process of law, including prior notice and an opportunity to respond. See Gadsden v. Department of State, 102 M.S.P.R. 79, ¶¶ 16-17 (2006) (reversing the agency's removal action when the appellant established that he met the definition of an "employee" with Board appeal rights, but the agency failed to provide him minimum due process of law under Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532 (1985)).

A terminated probationer's lot is not always a happy one. This maxim is confirmed by OPM regulations that grant probationers' termination appeals under only limited circumstances. The theme is developed in many Board and judicial decisions denying review of termination of probationary appointments, no matter how egregious or unfair the circumstances. The Board, dedicated to the preservation of probationary status. advises that the federal government has never found a more reliable test for predicting employee performance than a one-year on-the-job demonstration of competence, citing FPM Ch. 315-8-1(a) (1980). Doe v. NSA, 6 MSPR 555, 571, 6 MSPB 467, 480 (1981) (dissenting opinion of Member Wertheim). The courts sustain, but do not expand upon, regulatory and express or implied statutory limitations upon probationers' appeal rights. See Bante v. MSPB, 966 F.2d 647 (Fed. Cir. 1992). Citing Mastriano v. FAA, 714 F.2d 1152 (Fed. Cir. 1983), Brown v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3574), noted that probationers are not employees within the meaning of 5 USC 7511(a)(1) and lack statutory appellate protection. See Bartholomew v. United States, 740 F.2d 526, 530 (7th Cir. 1984) ("probationary federal employees have only the weakest claims of entitlement to continued employment and may be discharged within the broad discretion of the employing agency"). But see McGuffin v. SSA, 942 F.3d 1099, 1102 (Fed. Cir. 2019) ("An employer may terminate an individual during his probationary period if the individual 'fails to demonstrate his fitness or his qualifications for continued employment....' 5 C.F.R. § 315.804(a). The employer, however, 'must honestly be dissatisfied with the probationer's conduct or performance after giving him a fair trial on the job.' Shaw v. United States, 622 F.3d [sic: F.2d] 520, 544 (Ct. Cl. 1980) (quotation omitted) (discussing 5 C.F.R. § 315.804(a) (1975))."). "Probationary employees are most vulnerable to dismissal shortly before the end of their probationary period" and dismissal at that time is not uniquely suspect or unjust and it does not confer upon the probationer the rights of a regular employee generally or a property right in his employment in particular. Pharr v. MSPB, 173 Fed. Appx. 817, 819 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP). [Refer to Chapter 17, "Discharge of Probationer," concerning judicial review of probationers' appeals.]

Flowers v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 167, 171 (1993), distinguished appeal rights for probationers and those who qualify for ordinary Board appeal rights upon successful completion of probation:

If, on remand, the administrative judge determines that the appellant completed her probationary period, then she would be an "employee," as defined under 5 USC § 7511(a)(1), and the Board would have jurisdiction over her appeal under Chapter 75. If, on the other hand, the administrative judge determines that the appellant did not complete her probationary period, then she would have a regulatory right of appeal to the Board only if she had made a nonfrivolous allegation that she was terminated due to discrimination based on partisan political reasons or marital status, or that her termination for pre-appointment reasons was taken without the procedural requirements afforded under 5 CFR § 315.806(c). See 5 CFR §§ 315.805-.806; Burke v. Department of Justice, 53 MSPR 372, 375 (1992).

Under Gaxiola v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 515, 519-20, 6 MSPB 432 (1981), procedures for probationary termination and appeals pass constitutional muster:

Our recognition of the limited appeal right created by OPM for probationers is consistent with the statutory scheme of distinguishing between permanent and probationary employees for appeals purposes. The courts have also recognized and supported the distinction between the rights of permanent and probationary federal employees. In Sampson v. Murray,[11] the Supreme Court stated with respect to probationary employees: "We are dealing in this case not with a permanent government employee, a class for which Congress has specified certain substantive and procedural protections, but with a probationary employee, a class which Congress has specifically recognized as entitled to less comprehensive procedures." The Court cited 5 USC § 3321 as well as other provisions that show that Congress intended probationary employees to have fewer procedural rights than permanent employees in the competitive service. The Court noted that even preference eligibles are required to complete a probationary or trial period before they are entitled to hearing procedures afforded persons in the competitive service. Although decided prior to enactment of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, the Supreme Court's statement in Sampson regarding probationary federal employment is still significant because the 1978 Act continues to distinguish between the protections granted permanent employees and probationary employees.

[11] 415 U.S. 61, 80, 94 S. Ct. 937, 948, 39 L. Ed.2d 166 (1974).
