e. Classified Information

Classified information may find itself knocking at the Board's door. Internal guidance for handling classified documents is in the Judges' Handbook, Chapter 17:

2. NATIONAL SECURITY (CLASSIFIED) INFORMATION.

a. Definition. Classified information is information that needs protection, in the interest of national security, from unauthorized disclosure. Executive Order 12356 and Information Security Oversight Office (ISOO) Directive No. 1 govern classification. The protection of classified information is the responsibility of each individual who possesses or has knowledge of such information, regardless of how it is obtained. There is no current Board order or directive that sets forth policy and procedures for handling classified information.

b. The AJ's Responsibility When a Party to an Appeal Attempts to Introduce Classified Information into the Record. When a party attempts to introduce classified information into the record, the AJ must immediately make a ruling on its admissibility. If one or both parties agree that classified information will be necessary for the proper adjudication of an appeal, or if the AJ cannot determine its relevance without having knowledge of the classified material, the AJ must immediately notify ORO, which will locate an employee with the appropriate level of security clearance and provide for the adjudication of the appeal if it appears that classified information will be introduced or examined in camera. Given the government-wide trend of limiting the number of security clearances, an AJ with the requisite level of clearance may not be available. Further, the Board lacks the facilities necessary to handle classified information, such as secured phone lines, locked storage areas, etc. All support staff who assist a properly cleared AJ must also have the necessary level of clearance. Therefore, it is important for the AJ to determine if the appeal may proceed without the introduction of classified information, both documentary and testimonial. If it cannot, ORO will attempt to assign the case to an AJ but if no one is available, the appeal may be assigned to a properly cleared ALJ from outside the MSPB. Thus, it is important to determine as early in the proceedings as possible whether there is a likelihood that classified information will have to be introduced.

Board Order 1550.2 (September 7, 1994) provides policy and procedures for access to classified material:

1. PURPOSE. This Order revises MSPB policy and procedures for granting access to classified information.

2. POLICY. An employee may access classified information only if he or she has been granted a security clearance for access to a given level of classified information, and had a "need to know" (need for the classified information). A security clearance may be granted after a personnel investigation has been completed with favorable results. Before access is granted, the individual must receive a security briefing and sign Standard Form (SF) 312, Classified Information Nondisclosure Agreement. A person is granted a security clearance to access information at a specified classification or lower, i.e., a person with a Top Secret clearance can access Top Secret, Secret, and Confidential information, and a person with a Secret clearance can access Secret and Confidential information but not Top Secret information.

3. RESPONSIBILITIES. The protection of classified information is the responsibility of each individual who possesses or has knowledge of such information, regardless of how it is obtained. Any violation of Executive Order 12356, or the regulations implementing it, may subject the violator to disciplinary action or criminal penalties under Sections 793, 794, 798, and 1001 of Title 18 USC.

a. Office Heads. Office heads are responsible for safeguarding all classified information within the custody or control of their offices, maintaining a high degree of security consciousness among their employees, initiating employee clearances and requests for access to classified information.

b. Senior Agency Official for Information Security. The Clerk of the Board is the Senior Agency Official for Information Security and is responsible for ensuring the promulgation and administration of policies, granting clearances, standards, and procedures that ensure effective compliance with Executive Order 12356 and Information Security Oversight Office (ISOO) Directives.

c. Chief Administrative Law Judge. The Chief Administrative Law Judge (CALJ) is responsible for providing for the adjudication of appeals requiring the submission of classified information. The CALJ must maintain an appropriate classified information log concerning the receipt and handling of classified documents or other material or data submitted during the course of an adjudication. The CALJ shall inform the Clerk of the Board whenever classified information is received.

d. Administrative Judges. When a party to an appeal attempts to introduce classified information into the record, the Administrative Judge (AJ) must immediately make a ruling on its admissibility. If the AJ cannot make a determination as to relevance without having knowledge of the classified material, the AJ must immediately notify the CALJ. The CALJ will provide for the adjudication of the appeal if it appears that classified information will be introduced or examined in camera.

e. Office of Appeals Counsel. Responsible for processing petitions for review in cases where classified information has been submitted. The classified information will be segregated from the case file and retained by the Office of the Clerk and made available to persons having need for access to such information as necessary.

f. Director of Planning and Resource Management Services. Responsible for implementing the Board's personnel security program to ensure that incumbents have been cleared to occupy a critical sensitive position and for access to classification information. An individual may be cleared for access without actually having access; access is only granted when the employee has been cleared and has a "need to know."

4. REQUESTS FOR ACCESS TO CLASSIFIED MATERIAL. Office heads may request that an employee be granted access to classified material by completing and submitting MSPB Form 176 to the Clerk of the Board. A copy of the form is attached to this Notice. Following a determination by the Personnel Security Officer that a favorable personnel investigation has been completed for the employee, the Clerk will forward security briefing tapes to the employee and a copy of SF-312 for signature. After the SF-312 has been returned to the Clerk, appropriate access will be granted.

5. ADMINISTRATIVE WITHDRAWAL OF ACCESS. The Senior Agency Official for Information Security will administratively withdraw access from any person who no longer requires access to classified information in connection with the performance of the person's official duties. Where access is needed to adjudicate a case, access will normally expire upon issuance of the initial decision or final Board decision.

The practice now is for the Board to seal records containing documents when national security interests are involved. See Smit v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 612, 615 n.1 (1994). Previously, 5 CFR Part 1208, "National Security Information Procedures," defined MSPB procedures for safeguarding classified information. Through a notice in the Federal Register of November 22, 1993, the Board amended its regulations to delete Part 1208. The Board decided that its procedures duplicated Executive Order 12356, NARA Information Security Oversight Office Directive 1, and an internal Board directive. See 58 Fed. Reg. 61611 (11/22/93).
