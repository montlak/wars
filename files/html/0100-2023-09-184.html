2. Signature

The MSPB regulation provides, 5 CFR 1201.24 (2022):

Content of an appeal; right to hearing.

(a) Content. Only an appellant, his or her designated representative, or a party properly substituted under § 1201.35 may file an appeal. Appeals may be in any format, including letter form. An appeal may be filed in electronic form provided that the requirements of § 1201.14 have been satisfied. All appeals must contain the following:

(1) The name, address, and telephone number of the appellant, and the name and address of the agency that took the action;

...

(9) The signature of the appellant or, if the appellant has a representative, of the representative. If the appeal is electronically filed, compliance with § 1201.14 and the directions at the Board's e-Appeal site (https://e-appeal.mspb.gov) satisfy the signature requirement.

Under 5 CFR 1201.31 (2022):

Representatives.

(a) Procedure. A party to an appeal may be represented in any matter related to the appeal. Parties may designate a representative, revoke such a designation, and change such a designation in a signed submission, submitted as a pleading.

Electronic filing processes for appeals and designation of representatives are at 5 CFR 1201.14 (2022).

The regulations at 5 CFR 1201.24(a)(9) (2022) no longer require the "original" signature of an appellant or the appellant's representative to perfect the appeal. Judges' Handbook Chapter 2. A signature is required, but apparently it may be a fax or photocopy of the signature. Electronic filing procedures establish a presumption of sorts of the existence and legitimacy of signatures. Under the Board's electronic filing procedure, 5 CFR 1201.14(k) (2022), "[e]lectronic documents filed by a party who has registered as an e-filer pursuant to this section shall be deemed to be signed for purposes of any regulation in part 1201, 1203, 1208, or 1209 of this chapter that requires a signature." [Refer to this chapter, "Dates of Filing."]

The designation of representative must be by "pleading," including electronic filing. 5 CFR 1201.31(a) (2022). Board practice is for representatives to be designated in writing and the written designation must be signed by the party designating that representative. In practice, an appellant may supply a simple written, signed designation to his representative and for the representative to prepare, sign and file the appeal, accompanied by the written designation. An electronically filed appeal allows for incorporation of a PDF file of a signed designation. [Refer later in this chapter to "Appeal Filed Through Representative."]

An AJ may waive the appellant's failure to sign an appeal or to file a written designation of a representative. Behrendt v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 72, 73-74 n.* (1984); 5 CFR 1201.12 (2022). Although repeated failures to submit an appeal signed by the appellant would be good cause for dismissal of the appeal for failure to prosecute, one quickly remedied failure would not result in dismissal. Wright v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 403, 406 (1987); see Simmons v. OPM, 20 MSPR 225, 227 (1984) (failure to sign an appeal did not warrant dismissal after the appellant made a good faith effort and sent the signed document to the agency rather than to the Board). Considerations of waiver aside, the AJ must reject an appeal that does not contain a signature, as directed in Board Order 1050.10 (Oct. 27, 1988). The appeal will then be returned to the appellant with leave to cure the defect in 15 days (25 days for overseas cases). The signature requirement should not be carried to the point of absurdity. One appellant did not sign in the space on the Board appeal form designated for his signature, but he did sign in the space assigned for designation of a representative. Over the agency's objection, the Board accepted the appeal for adjudication. Steel v. VA, 21 MSPR 224, 225-26 (1984). The Federal Circuit determined the Board abused its discretion when, in what amounted to a class action appeal, the union filed appeals for many employees but was unable to get employees' signatures on several appeals until after the deadline established by the Board. Processing of the appeals when the signatures were obtained did not prejudice the agency. Young v. Dept. of Commerce, 737 F.2d 1029 (Fed. Cir. 1984). [Refer later in this chapter to "Class Action, Consolidated Appeals, and Joinder; Bifurcated Appeals" for decisions discussing signature requirements.] According to an MSPB Internal Memo of December 2, 1988, from Headquarters to the regions, an appellant's original signature is not required on a counsel fee or compliance petition.
