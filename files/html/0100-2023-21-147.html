2. FERS Minimum Retirement Age Annuity

Connolly v. OPM, 96 MSPR 584, 586-89 ¶¶ 4-11 (2004), briefly explained the nature of a deferred FERS minimum retirement age annuity, noting that an employee with five years of civilian service may receive service credit for qualifying military service if a deposit for that service is completed before the employee's separation from the civil service:

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8410, an employee must have completed at least 5 years of creditable civilian service to be eligible for an annuity. Section 8413 sets forth the requirements for a deferred annuity. An employee who separates from Federal service with at least 5 years of service is entitled to an annuity when he reaches age 62. 5 U.S.C. § 8413(a). Read together, it is clear that, to be eligible for a deferred annuity under FERS, an employee must have 5 years of civilian service. There is no dispute that the appellant has 5 years of civilian service under FERS. The statute further provides:

An employee...who is separated from the service...after completing 10 years of service but before attaining the applicable minimum retirement age under section 8412(h) is entitled to an annuity beginning on the date designated by the employee...in a written election under this subsection. The date designated under this subsection may not precede the date on which the employee...attains such minimum retirement age and must precede the date on which the employee...becomes 62 years of age.

5 U.S.C. § 8413(b). The minimum retirement age (MRA) for employees born before January 1, 1948, like the appellant, is 55 years of age. 5 U.S.C. § 8412(h). The appellant became 55 on December 4, 2001, and would be eligible for a deferred annuity at that time if he had completed 10 years of service. OPM denied his application for a deferred annuity because it found that he had 9 years, 8 months, and 2 days of creditable service when he resigned from his position with the Department of the Army effective March 15, 1990.

The appellant argued below that he should receive credit for his three years of military service. Under 5 U.S.C. § 8411(c), an employee is entitled to credit for

military service performed after December 31, 1956, and before the separation on which title to annuity is based, if a deposit (including interest, if any) is made with respect to such [service] in accordance with section 8422(e).

In its reconsideration decision, OPM based its finding that no deposit was made on the Department of the Army's certification to that effect, and the appellant does not dispute this finding. He asserted below, however, that he was willing to make the deposit if that would make him eligible for an annuity.

The AJ did not consider the appellant's claim regarding the deposit. Rather, relying on 5 U.S.C. § 8413(b), the AJ found that, because the appellant is not yet 62, he was not eligible for a deferred annuity unless he had "10 years of creditable civilian service" (emphasis added), prior to his reaching the applicable minimum retirement age.

In requiring the appellant to have 10 years of creditable civilian service, the AJ misstated the requirements of the statute. As noted, subsection (b)(1) of section 8413 requires that the employee have "10 years of service." OPM has never asserted in this appeal, let alone cited any authority for the proposition, that the appellant could not receive a deferred annuity under 5 U.S.C. § 8412(b) as long as he had 5 years of creditable civilian service and makes a deposit for his post-1956 military service. In fact, in its reconsideration decision, OPM stated that the appellant's military service could not be used to compute his annuity, not because all of the required service had to be civilian service, but because he did not make a deposit for it. Moreover, Chapter 42, section 42A1.1-2E of OPM's CSRS [Civil Service Retirement System] and FERS Handbook for Personnel and Payroll Offices (1998) (Handbook) states that to receive a deferred annuity of the kind for which the appellant has applied, the "employee must have at least 10 years of creditable (civilian and military) service...." Section 45B1.1-2E further provides that "...former FERS employees are eligible for a deferred annuity as early as their MRA if they have 10 or more years of creditable service, including 5 years of civilian service." Thus, the 10year requirement can be met by using post-1956 military service if the required deposit is made.

Under OPM's implementing regulation,

[a]n employee's...deposit for military service must be completed before separation from service. ...A person who was eligible to make a deposit for military service but failed to complete the deposit within the time limits provided in this paragraph, may complete the deposit in a lump sum within the time limit set by OPM when it rules that an administrative error has been made.

5 C.F.R. § 842.307(a)(3); see Gilliam v. Office of Personnel Management, 91 M.S.P.R. 352, ¶ 9 (2002) (if a separated employee failed to make or complete a deposit for military service prior to his separation due to an administrative error, he may complete the deposit in a lump sum within a time period set by OPM).

An employing agency's failure to inform an employee of the need to make a deposit for his military service, when the agency is obligated to provide this information, constitutes an "administrative error" within the meaning of 5 C.F.R. § 842.307(a)(3). See, e.g., Nunez v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 326, 329-34 (1996). It therefore may warrant waiver of the requirement that the deposit be made before separation. See id. Accordingly, if the Department of the Army was obligated to provide this information, and it failed to do so, then the appellant may be entitled to another opportunity to make the deposit. See Gilliam, 91 M.S.P.R. 352, ¶ 9.

We note that the Department of the Army may have been required to tell the appellant, before it separated him, of the consequences of his failure to make a deposit covering his military service. Chapter 45 of OPM's Handbook describes advice that should be provided to an employee who is separating and who is not entitled to an immediate annuity. See Handbook, Chapter 45, section 45B2.1-2; see also id., section 45A1.1-1B. That advice includes information about the requirement that an employee make a deposit for military service performed after 1956, if he is to receive credit for that service. Id., sections 45A2.1-2B; 45B2.1-2.

The AJ made no finding in regard to whether an administrative error was made and if so, whether that error caused the appellant's failure to make the deposit prior to his separation. OPM has not submitted evidence and argument on the issue. Nor are the appellant's assertions sufficient evidence upon which to make a finding. Although he states on petition for review that he was never told of the necessity for making a deposit before he separated from the Department of the Army, that statement is unsworn. We find that remanding this appeal to the AJ is necessary so that the parties may have an opportunity to develop the record more fully.
