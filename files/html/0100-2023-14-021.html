c. On-Duty or Job-Related Offense

Removal of an employee for falsification of medical records was sustained in Gipson v. VA, 682 F.2d 1004 (D.C. Cir. 1982), finding justifiable the VA's concerns about the possible effect of falsified medical forms on medical care. The falsification could have a significant effect on the employee's reputation for honesty and integrity and a significant effect on service efficiency. A similar result was reached in Crofoot v. GPO, 21 MSPR 248, 253 (1984), involving the removal of a photocomposition keyboard operator following problems involving his worker's compensation claim:

[T]he Board has held that deliberate falsification of documents for the purpose of defrauding the government is sufficient to raise a presumption of nexus. Appellant's conviction for false pretenses casts doubt on his honesty, reliability and ethical conduct and undermines his trustworthiness, an essential ingredient of the employer-employee relationship [appellant was a Photocomposition Keyboard Operator]. This is especially true where, as in the present case, the fraudulent conduct is perpetrated directly against the employing agency, and the appellant is claiming a benefit for an alleged on the job injury....

Crofoot was reviewed in the Federal Circuit. Less than enthusiastic about sustaining the discharge, the court noted that the employee's removal was supported only by the use of the doctrine of collateral estoppel, involving a guilty plea to a criminal charge differing from the charge supporting his removal from employment. Although the court declared that "if it had been found that the petitioner committed the offense of defrauding the United States Government, the Board would have been correct in drawing a presumption of nexus from this offense," fraud was not established by collateral estoppel. As a result, the court concluded in Crofoot v. GPO, 761 F.2d 661, 664 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

We think that the Board's holding on the nexus issue constituted an error of law which requires reversal pursuant to 5 USC 7703(c)(1)....

Raising the presumption of a nexus places an extraordinary burden on an employee, for it forces him to prove the negative proposition that his retention would not adversely affect the efficiency of the service.... The principal problem we are confronted with is the closely connected issue of whether, absent any presumption in favor of the GPO, there was a nexus between the sole sustained charge of notoriously disgraceful conduct and the efficiency of the service. Because neither the Board nor the presiding official made any findings of fact on the question, we believe a remand of this case to the Board for its determination is the only appropriate remedy....

On remand, the Board sustained the agency once again, Crofoot v. GPO, 31 MSPR 442, 445 & n.2 (1986), deciding that the misconduct was established by a nolo contendere plea, that Crofoot was convicted for an offense involving dishonesty, that the conviction arose from his employment with the agency (the problems related to a claim for workmen's compensation), and that the agency expressed a concern about the appellant's trustworthiness. The Board concluded that the conviction alone could justify a loss of confidence in the appellant's trustworthiness, but the Board was careful to distinguish Gloster v. GSA, 720 F.2d 700 (D.C. Cir. 1983), involving a determination by the court that there was no nexus between the removal of a custodial worker and a finding of guilt as to welfare fraud. Crofoot, it was pointed out, involved a charge relating to a program within the direct administrative responsibility of the employing agency.

Reviewed again, the Board's decision was sustained, Crofoot v. GPO, 823 F.2d 495, 498 (Fed. Cir. 1987):

The record shows that the agency spent substantial sums on its worker's compensation program and was faced with an increasing number of fraudulent claims. It also shows that the petitioner's conviction was well-known within the agency. Cf. Gloster v. GSA, 720 F.2d 700, 704 (D.C. Cir. 1983) (nexus did not exist where employee's conviction was not common knowledge in agency). According to one of the petitioner's supervisors, if the petitioner were allowed to return to work after his conviction, other employees might be encouraged to submit false claims. The petitioner's removal was therefore an appropriate measure to deter fraudulent claims and to ensure the continued integrity of the agency's worker's compensation program.

The Board likewise properly concluded that the agency justifiably lost confidence in the petitioner's trustworthiness as a result of his guilty plea to and conviction of the crime of false pretenses. That crime is intimately connected to an employee's trustworthiness and reliability. The petitioner's conviction for false pretenses could reasonably cause the agency to lose confidence in his ability to perform his duties in a trustworthy manner....

See Brooks v. USPS, 14 MSPR 305, 306 (1983) ("obvious" nexus between a charge of improper receipt of continuation of pay and service efficiency).

Nexus supported removal of an engineer involved in falsification of records and unauthorized possession of government property. The employee did not overcome a presumption of nexus created by the conduct. Kumferman v. Dept. of Navy, 785 F.2d 286 (Fed. Cir. 1986), aff'g Kumferman v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 5, 7 (1984); see Hanna v. Dept. of Labor (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3240) ("Filing a false claim for workers compensation benefits based on an injury sustained in the course of federal employment has a sufficient nexus to the employee's work to serve as a basis for disciplinary action"); Els v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 27, 32 ¶ 11 (1999) (nexus existed between service efficiency and a charge of misuse of government property); Scott v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 187, 188 (1984) (nexus was established and a removal warranted as to a mechanic charged with the theft of roofing material belonging to a civilian construction company working at an agency shipyard; the conduct impaired the working relationship with a contractor). Misappropriation of funds by a postal window clerk had an obvious nexus to work: "The nature of the misconduct inherently prejudices the Postal Service and relates, without question, to the faithful and loyal performance by an employee of his duties." Walsh v. USPS, 53 MSPR 478, 481 (1992). An appellant was properly removed who, while on duty and on the agency's premises, attempted to remove private property from a military installation. The agency's military police intervened to prevent the theft. Sufficient nexus existed between the appellant's misconduct and service efficiency. See Gray v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 639, 640 (1982).
