e. Improper Reassignment

An appellant is entitled to a hearing as to voluntariness when he alleges that an agency used a geographical reassignment to force him either to resign or to accept a demotion. See Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 20 MSPR 478, 479 (1984), following remand, 26 MSPR 250, 253 (1985) (directing the agency to cancel the demotion). The circumstances surrounding the reassignment must be examined to rule on the appellant's allegation of an involuntary downgrading. Kinsella v. DHHS, 41 MSPR 643, 648 (1989) (considering the assertion that the agency improperly forced appellant to accept a demotion in lieu of an undesirable relocation through a mobility agreement). There is a difference between nonfrivolous allegations, warranting a hearing, and proof of involuntariness. Unsupported assertions of unwarranted threats to force an employee to accept a downgrade do not establish involuntariness. See Cooley v. USPS, 68 MSPR 353, 358 (1995) (appellant alleged that threats were made to reassign her out of state, but she waived a hearing and apparently supplied no evidence that the threats were made).

The Board revised the analysis of appeals involving employees removed for refusal to accept geographical reassignments, deciding in 2013 that an agency must prove "that the reassignment was required because the agency had eliminated or had no need for the appellant's continued performance in her former position or because it needed to address the appellant's performance problems in her former position." Miller v. Dept. of Interior, 119 MSPR 438, 441-44 ¶¶ 6-11, 2013 MSPB 35 (2013) (replacing the opinion at 119 MSPR 331, 2013 MSPB 27 (2013)), recon. den., Miller v. Dept. of Interior, 120 MSPR 426, 2013 MSPB 94 (2013)). The reasoning of Miller was extended, in reverse, to involuntary retirements by Welhouse v. USDA, CH-0752-12-0387-I-2 (NP 8/19/2014):

When a retirement involves a directed reassignment, the Board has held that the involuntariness of the retirement may be established by showing that the reassignment had no solid or substantial basis in personnel practice or principle. Caveney v. Office of Administration, 57 M.S.P.R. 667, 670 (1993). A reassignment must be "properly ordered due to bona fide management considerations in the interest of promoting the efficiency of the service." Miller v. Department of the Interior, 120 M.S.P.R. 426, ¶ 11 (2013)....

Whereupon, Miller was reversed by the Federal Circuit in Cobert v. Miller, 800 F.3d 1340 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (Judge Wallach concurring), reinstating a traditional burden shifting analysis involving adverse actions based on refusal to accept geographical reassignment in Ketterer v. USDA, 2 MSPR 294, 299, 2 MSPB 459 (1980).

Law developed before the 2013 Miller decision explained the order and burdens of proof in an involuntary downgrading case involving allegations of improper reassignment, McMullen v. USDA, 33 MSPR 13, 15-16 (1987):

...In cases...where the appellant has the burden of proving that a seemingly voluntary action was in fact involuntary because it was the result of an improper reassignment, the analysis to be used is that set forth in Rayfield v. Dept. of Agric., 26 MSPR 244 (1985)...

In Rayfield, the appellant alleged that his retirement was involuntary because a directed transfer was intentionally designed to force him to retire. The Board held that although the appellant bore the ultimate burden of proving that his retirement was involuntary, the agency must refute the allegation that the transfer was intended to coerce retirement by showing that it was based on a legitimate management reason. The appellant is then allowed an opportunity to rebut the averred legitimate reason by demonstrating that the transfer had "no solid or substantial basis in personnel practice or principle." If the appellant can make that showing, the Board may conclude that the reassignment was not a valid discretionary management determination but was either an improper effort to pressure the appellant to retire or was at least an arbitrary and capricious adverse action....

Using the Rayfield analytic framework, in this case, we find that the agency showed that the reassignment was based on legitimate management reasons and that appellant did not rebut this showing....

If the appellant requests a geographical reassignment and takes a pay cut, he will not have suffered a constructive adverse action if the pay cut was taken knowingly and if the reduction is in accordance with, or not contrary to, statutes and applicable provisions of a negotiated agreement. See McGarigle v. USPS, 904 F.2d 687 (Fed. Cir. 1990); cf. Sweeney v. MSPB, 776 Fed. Appx. 788, 795 (4th Cir. 2019) (downgrading was not involuntary: "Although Appellant had to choose between the unattractive options of participating in termination proceedings or being reassigned to Harrisburg, he was still presented with a choice."; relying on Gaudette v. Dept. of Transp., 832 F.2d 1256, 1259 (Fed. Cir. 1987)). If an agency enters into a mobility agreement with an employee, and if that agreement contains restrictions on the employee's geographical reassignment, the Board examines allegations and evidence demonstrating, as to a claim of involuntary downgrading, that a reassignment was ordered in violation of the agreement. If the appellant complains that a reassignment was ordered despite a medical condition that he believes should have allowed him to avoid the reassignment under the mobility agreement, the agency should be given medical evidence demonstrating that its decision to reassign the employee is unreasonable. Kinsella v. DHHS, 41 MSPR 643, 648-49 (1989).
