a. Permissive Subjects of Bargaining

Although management must maintain working conditions negotiated or developed under past practice that are mandatory subjects of bargaining, even after the expiration of an agreement or change in bargaining representative, matters stand otherwise as to permissive subjects of bargaining, i.e., those matters excepted from the obligation to negotiate under § 7106(b)(1) and those matters outside the required scope of bargaining under the Statute, DOJ, INS and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 52 FLRA 256, 260 n.3 (1996):

In particular, terms and conditions of employment embodied in an agreement as a result of mandatory bargaining continue, to the maximum extent possible, absent express agreement to the contrary or unless modified in a manner consistent with the Statute; terms and conditions resulting from permissive bargaining may be unilaterally terminated by either party. E.g., U.S. Department of the Air Force, HQ Air Force Materiel Command and American Federation of Government Employees, Council 214, 49 FLRA 1111, 1121 (1994)....

NTEU and Customs Serv., 59 FLRA 217, 220 n.3 (2003) (remanded on other grounds, NTEU v. FLRA, 399 F.3d 334 (D.C. Cir. 2005)), added:

It is also well established that, even if parties have negotiated over a permissive subject in the past, there is no basis to conclude that such previous bargaining renders future bargaining mandatory. In Allied Chemical & Alkali Workers of Am., Local Union No. 1 v. Pittsburgh Plate Glass Co., Chem. Div., 404 U.S. 157, 187 (1971), the Supreme Court considered the effect of an employer's prior agreement to a contract provision that constituted a permissive subject of bargaining, on the employer's duty to bargain under section 8(d) of the National Labor Relations Act. The Court held that by once bargaining and agreeing on a permissive subject, the parties do not make the subject a mandatory topic of future bargaining. Because the duty to bargain under the Statute parallels the duty to bargain under the Act, the Authority adopted the rationale of the Court in that case with respect to the duty to bargain over permissive subjects. See NATCA, 56 FLRA at 291. See also AFGE, Local 225, 56 FLRA 686, 689 (2000).

NTEU and DHS, C&BP, 59 FLRA 978, 978 n.1 (2004), noted:

An agency may elect to bargain over matters set forth in § 7106(b)(1) of the Statute, but is not required to do so. Following the expiration of a collective bargaining agreement, either party may notify the other that it will no longer be bound by the provisions in the expired agreement concerning § 7106(b)(1) matters. United States Dep't of Commerce, Patent and Trademark Office, 53 FLRA 858, 873 n.13 (1997) (citing Federal Aviation Admin, 23 FLRA 209 (1986). The Agency notified the Union that it was employing "its statutory right to terminate and no longer be bound by the provisions of the national agreement in which [the Agency] agreed to bargain over matters covered by 5 U.S.C. 7106(b)(1)."

If a party does not desire to be bound to a provision of an expired agreement involving a permissive subject of bargaining, it must notify the other party of its intention no longer to be bound. If the agreement is recognized by the parties as continuing with full force and effect pending renegotiation, the provisions relating to permissive subjects of bargaining continue in effect as well. Hq., Ft. Sam Houston, Dept. of Army and Local 2154, AFGE, 15 FLRA 974, 975 (1984). Either party may reassert its right not to negotiate regarding the permissive subject of bargaining once the applicable agreement has expired. A waiver of bargaining rights is a permissive subject of bargaining; once the agreement expires and the union tells management it no longer wishes to be bound by the provision, management is then obliged to bargain and cannot insist upon a continuation of the waiver in the old agreement. FAA, Northwest Mountain Region, Seattle and PASS, 14 FLRA 644, 647-49 (1984); see MSPB Professional Ass'n and MSPB, 30 FLRA 852, 861 (1988) ("Provisions concerning matters over which an agency is required by the Statute to bargain continue to bind the agency after expiration of the agreement in the absence of an express agreement to the contrary or a modification of those provisions in a manner consistent with the Statute. Federal Aviation Administration, Northwest Mountain Region, Seattle, Washington, and Federal Aviation Administration, Washington, D.C., 14 FLRA 644, 647 (1984). However, neither party is required, upon an agreement's expiration, to continue adherence to provisions concerning matters which are negotiable only at the election of either party."); cf. DOJ, INS and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 55 FLRA 892, 990 (1999) (terms included in contract negotiated under Exec. Order 11491 did not waive party's bargaining rights under Statute).

A contract provision allowing the employer to set and change shifts, tours of duty, and hours of work was a waiver of bargaining rights that did not survive the expiration of the contract once the union indicated a desire to bargain over a change in duty hours and luncheon breaks. Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. Region IX, Chicago and NTEU, 17 FLRA 221, 223 (1985); cf. DOJ, Fed. Bureau of Prisons and AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 1661, 55 FLRA 201, 205-06 (1999) (management was required to restore shift set by local agreement that was repudiated without notice before contract expiration). If an expired contract contains permissive limitations on bargaining rights, and the union representative states an interest in negotiations, that request operates as a termination of the permissive limitation in the old agreement. Management is no longer free to refuse to bargain. FAA and PASS, 17 FLRA 142, 143 (1985). But DOJ, Fed. Bureau of Prisons and AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 1661, 55 FLRA 201, 205-06 (1999), declared that notice of termination of a permissively negotiated provision must be explicit, not implicit:

Authority precedent suggests that to be effective, a party must give notice that explicitly contains a statement of intent to terminate a provision dealing with a permissive bargaining subject.[9] No case has been found where, as here, intent to terminate must be inferred from other actions, such as through the issuance of an operations memorandum by higher authority during the term of the agreement.

[9] See, e.g., Federal Aviation Administration, 23 FLRA 209, 211 (1986); United States Department of Transportation, Federal Aviation Administration, 20 FLRA 548, 560 (1985), remanded on other grounds sub nom. Professional Airways Systems Specialists v. FLRA, 809 F.2d 855 (D.C. Cir. 1987); United States Department of Transportation, Federal Aviation Administration, Los Angeles, California, 15 FLRA 100, 103 (1984); Federal Aviation Administration, Northwestern Mountain Region, Seattle, Washington, 14 FLRA 644, 649 (1984). In these cases, the Union notified management, after expiration of a negotiated agreement, that the Union no longer intended to be bound by an agreement provision dealing with a permissive bargaining subject.

Moreover, sound policy reasons support the General Counsel's claim that notice of intent to terminate an agreement provision on a permissive bargaining subject should be express, not implied. A contrary rule encourages confusion in the parties' bargaining relationship. A party should not have to guess whether negotiated agreement terms are in effect or not. Such matters are of critical importance in governing the parties' day-to-day labor-management relationship.

Bureau of Prisons added that no bargaining obligation accompanies termination of a permissive bargaining subject at the conclusion of an agreement, id. at 206:

...A party's right to terminate unilaterally a permissive bargaining subject is not contingent on first satisfying a bargaining obligation as to the substance, impact or implementation of the change. No precedent to the contrary was cited by the Judge or the parties. Moreover, this conclusion is supported by at least two policy reasons. First, since the termination of the permissive subject can only become effective on the expiration of an agreement, the Union would have the opportunity to bargain on the matter as part of the renegotiation of the expired agreement. Second, attaching bargaining obligations to termination of permissive bargaining provisions may discourage parties from engaging in bargaining on permissive subjects....

Because the right to designate representatives is statutory, any waiver or limitation of that right in a contract does not survive the expiration of the contract if the union then seeks to reassert its statutory right to designate representatives. Dept. of Transp., FAA, San Diego and PASS, 15 FLRA 407, 410 (1984). Following expiration of an agreement, the union may discontinue a memorandum of understanding that delays access to ULP procedures pending informal attempts to resolve disputes over management responses to union information requests. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 3937, 43 FLRA 549, 550 (1991). See AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 22 FLRA 478, 483 (1986) ("[I]n the Federal as in the private sector, previous bargaining over a permissive matter would not prevent an agency negotiating a new agreement from changing its position and refusing to bargain over an elective subject."). If the bargaining relation is at the national level, the agency may discontinue employment practices at both the national and local levels if those practices are the subject of permissive bargaining and the underlying negotiated agreement terminated. See NTEU Chap. 137 and DHS, C&BP, 60 FLRA 483, 486-88 (2004) (reconsideration denied, 61 FLRA 60)) (overtime assignment practices under National Inspectional Assignment Policy and Local Inspectional Assignment Policy); DHS, C&BP and NTEU, 60 FLRA 496 (2004) (another determination that local inspectional arrangements, permissive in nature, concluded with the expiration of the parties' national labor agreement); cf. DHS, C&BP, Seattle and NTEU Chap. 139, 60 FLRA 490 (2004) (setting aside award of back pay for violations of inspectional policies addressed in NTEU Chap. 137, supra), reconsideration denied, 61 FLRA 85 (2005).
