broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 21: Union Rights, Bargaining Rights and Procedures, Section F: Joint Committees, Subsection 01: Incentive Awards Program

1. Incentive Awards Program

A proposal required establishment of a joint labor-management committee to design, develop, and administer an incentive awards program. A committee would have the limited authority to make recommendations, subject to final approval by the director of the activity. The proposal was negotiable because management of awards was within the discretion of the agency and did not involve the manner that the activity, a medical center, performed its work. That the proposal compelled appointment of union members to the committee did not interfere with management's right to assign work. The committee did not involve the official duties of the individuals involved. The proposal was distinguishable from one that required EEO counselors to be selected from among union nominees. EEO counseling responsibilities were officially designated tasks, defined by regulations, and involved the assignment of work. NFFE Local 541 and VA Hosp., Long Beach, 12 FLRA 270, 273-74 (1983). Union participation in an incentive awards program, including the suggestion program, was negotiable to the extent that proposals were to include two union representatives on the committee and that those members would fully participate on the committee that made recommendations to the activity director. No law or regulation vested solely in management the exclusive right to establish the program or otherwise preclude negotiation over the program. NFFE Local 1579 and VA Reg'l Office, Louisville, 12 FLRA 600, 602 (1983) (Proposal 1); see NFFE Local 1256 and Sawyer AFB, 29 FLRA 171 (1987) (Provision 2) (negotiable: proposal for union representation on incentive awards committee).

A decision distinguished committees that recommend employees from those that decide on employees who will receive incentive awards, and the distinction remained valid through 1987. A contract provision permitted a union observer to be on an incentive awards committee that made recommendations to a commanding officer. It was within the officer's sole discretion whether to grant or deny awards, including quality step increases, sustained superior performance ratings, and outstanding awards. When the union learned that the command intended to delegate certain award decisions to department heads, removing from the committee authority to recommend those awards, the union demanded bargaining on the decision and its impact. Although the agency was willing to negotiate over impact, it would not do so as to the decision. FLRA agreed with the agency: the committee was involved in a substantive role in determining whether performance warranted an award, authority that was part of the management right to direct employees and assign work. Even though the union was but an observer, the union role in that function directly interfered with reserved management rights. Dept. of Navy, N. Div., Naval Facilities Eng'g Command and NFFE Local 1430, 19 FLRA 705, 707-08 (1985). Other proposals permitting union participation in joint committees did not involve committees that functioned as part of the decisionmaking process involving reserved rights. Dept. of Navy, N. Div., Naval Facilities Eng'g Command and NFFE Local 1430, 19 FLRA 705 (1985) (ALJ Decision), rev'd and remanded sub nom. NFFE Local 1430 v. FLRA, No. 85-1648 (D.C. Cir. Nov. 6, 1986), on remand, Dept. of Navy, N. Div., Naval Facilities Eng'g Command and NFFE Local 1430, 24 FLRA 907, 907-08 (1986) (remanded based on NTEU v. FLRA, 793 F.2d 371, 374-76 (D.C. Cir. 1986), rejecting FLRA's conclusion that the determination of whether a particular level of job performance warrants granting incentive award is within management's right to assign work and direct employees).

FLRA's rulings on matters concerning incentive awards committee membership were reconsidered in AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., Ft. Rucker, 28 FLRA 1172, 1179, 1180-81 (1987) (Provision 9). The proposal required the agency to select one employee to serve as union representative on the incentive awards committee, a group that reviewed proposals for awards concerning performance recognition and that made recommendations for approval or disapproval of those awards to the activity chief. The proposal was negotiable:

...During the pendency of this case before the Authority, the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit rejected the Authority's reasoning in Internal Revenue Service, 14 FLRA 463 (1984), relied on by the Agency, that the management right to assign work includes the management right to reward superior performance of what has been assigned. NTEU v. FLRA, 793 F.2d 371, 374 (D.C. Cir. 1986).

Thereafter, based on its decision in NTEU v. FLRA, the D.C. Circuit in an unpublished memorandum decision reversed and remanded Dept. of Navy, Northern Division, Naval Facilities Engineering Command, 19 FLRA 705 (1985)--the other Authority decision principally relied on by the Agency.... In that decision the Authority had determined that the agency had not committed an unfair labor practice when it unilaterally changed the functions of the incentive awards committee on which the union had a role as an observer. In reaching that outcome, the Authority had relied on Internal Revenue Service and reasoned that the determination of whether a particular level of job performance warrants granting an incentive award is within management's authority to assign work and direct employees. In its order reversing the decision, the court noted specifically that the basis for the Authority's decision had been rejected by the court in its decision in NTEU v. FLRA.

FLRA explained the change of law concerning union participation in incentive awards programs in a ULP case, Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr. and FUSE, NAGE, Local R1-144, 30 FLRA 697, 700-01 (1987):

We turn first to the Respondent's argument that the Union's representation on the Joint Management/Union Incentive Awards Committee, as described in the Memorandum of Understanding, is contrary to the Statute....

...

...Respondent relied on the Authority's decision in NAFEC to repudiate the Memorandum of Understanding. That decision, however, was reversed. In our decision on remand, 24 FLRA 907, we adopted the court's view that section 7106(a)(2)(A) and (B) of the Statute does not confer on management the sole prerogative to determine whether to reward superior performance. Furthermore, in our decision and order on remand in IRS, 27 FLRA 132, we found that the proposal determining the level or rate of incentive pay to be awarded to employees under the agency's productivity plan did not interfere with management's rights under section 7106(a) of the Statute.

Because of AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., Ft. Rucker, 28 FLRA 1172 (1987), FLRA declared negotiable a proposal that the union shall have one fully participating member on the incentive awards committee. NFFE Local 797 and Dept. of Navy, 29 FLRA 333, 335 (1987) (Provision 2). Relying on NTEU v. FLRA, 793 F.2d 371, 374-76 (D.C. Cir. 1986), a decision determined to be negotiable a proposal that the union have four representatives on the incentive awards committee, that they participate in deliberations and discussions, and that, during evaluations and voting with respect to nominees for awards, the union representatives serve as participating members of the committee. Indep. Letterman Hosp. Workers Union and Dept. of Army, Nutrition Care, Letterman Army Med. Ctr., 29 FLRA 456, 459 (1987) (Proposal 2).

Reviewing an arbitrator's award that the agency improperly unilaterally failed to bargain over a change in a joint committee approach to awards nominations, DHS, C&BP and NTEU, 63 FLRA 505, 508-09 (2009), concluded:

The Agency's contention that the Union's proposal to retain Article 17 is outside the duty to bargain because it affects management's right to assign work is not supported by precedent. In NTEU v. FLRA, the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit held that "the terms 'assign work' and 'direct employees' were not meant to be so expansive" as to include the "right to reward [the] performance of what has been assigned." NTEU v. FLRA, 793 F.2d at 374. Adopting the court's rationale, see NTEU, 27 FLRA 132, 135 (1987), the Authority has subsequently and consistently held that proposals requiring that employees participate in making recommendations regarding performance awards do not affect management's rights to direct employees and assign work under § 7106(a)(2)(A) and (B). See NAGE, Local RI-203, 55 FLRA 1081, 1083 (1999) ("management's rights to direct employees and assign work do not extend to the decision to grant an award"); see also NAGE, Local R1-144, FUSE, 38 FLRA 456, 487 (1990) (citing NFFE, Local 1256, 31 FLRA 1203, 1206-07 (1988); Int'l Org. of Masters, Mates and Pilots, 36 FLRA 555, 565 (1990); Local 1815, 28 FLRA at 1179-81). Consistent with this principle, the Authority has held that joint labor-management awards committees that make recommendations regarding the granting of awards do not affect management rights under § 7106 of the Statute. See Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., 30 FLRA at 700 (among other things, committee made recommendations with respect to proposed awards for unit employees); see also NFFE, Local 797, 29 FLRA 333, 335 (1987); NFFE, Local 1256, 29 FLRA at 173-74; Local 1815, 28 FLRA at 1180 (union membership on committee which makes recommendations with respect to proposed awards is negotiable).

The Agency's argument that the Union's proposal is outside the duty to bargain because it would potentially permit an arbitrator to review management's exercise of its rights under § 7106 is unpersuasive. The Authority has consistently held that such arguments are not a basis for finding a proposal outside the duty to bargain. See, e.g., NTEU, 46 FLRA 696, 708 (1992); NTEU, 45 FLRA 696, 709-10 (1992); AFGE, Local 1923, 39 FLRA 1197, 1200 (1991); NTEU, 39 FLRA 346, 350 (1991); see also Newark Air Force Station, 30 FLRA 616, 635-36 (1987) (the question of any impermissible interference with management's rights must be directed to the merits of an arbitration decision, including the remedy). Moreover, the Agency's attempt to distinguish National Border Patrol Council and NTEU, upon which the Arbitrator relies, is also unpersuasive. The distinctions relied on by the Agency are irrelevant with respect to the issue of whether arbitral review of limitations on the exercise of a management right is a ground for finding a proposal nonnegotiable. See NTEU, 46 FLRA at 708 (possibility of arbitral review is not a basis for precluding negotiation of a proposal). Moreover, because the decision to grant or deny an award does not constitute the exercise of a management right, the absence of standards governing awards decisions does not constitute an impediment under § 7106 to finding the Union's proposal negotiable.

We reject the Agency's argument that an arbitrator cannot review whether union representational activities constitute the work of the Agency. In this regard, as the Agency acknowledges, the Authority has long held that the "question of any impermissible arbitral interference with management's rights must be directed to the merits, including remedy, of an arbitration decision relating to performance standards' consistency with law." Exceptions at 20 (quoting NTEU, 39 FLRA at 350 (citation omitted)). As applied here, the Authority has reviewed several awards resolving disputes as to whether various representational activities constitute the work of the agency. See, e.g., United States Dep't. of Transp., FAA, 60 FLRA 20, 22-23 (2004) (arbitrator's award granting holiday pay for representational work on Columbus Day held contrary to law); see also Warner Robins Air Logistics Ctr., Warner Robins, Ga., 23 FLRA 270, 271-72 (1986). Therefore, the fact that, in applying the Union's proposal, an arbitrator may potentially be required to distinguish representational activities from agency work is not a basis for finding the proposal outside the duty to bargain or the award contrary to law.

Based on the foregoing, the Agency's exception does not provide a basis for finding that the Arbitrator's determination that the Union's proposal is substantively negotiable is contrary to law. Accordingly, we deny the Agency's exception.
