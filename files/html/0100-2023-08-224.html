c. Lower Graded Position Through Return Right--Overseas Employee

Return rights for DOD employees working in foreign areas were described in Prather v. Dept. of Army, DC-0752-14-0327-B-1 (NP 11/22/2022):

Under Department of Defense (DOD) regulations, civilian employment in the competitive service in foreign areas generally is limited to a period of 5 continuous years. DOD Instruction (DODI) No. 1400-25, Volume (Vol.) 1230, ¶ 4.h.(1)2; see 10 U.S.C. § 1586 (generally authorizing the establishment of rotation programs to facilitate the interchange of civilian DOD employees between posts of duty in and outside the United States). An employee who is covered by the 5-year limitation, and who does not have a statutory right to return to a position in the United States,[3] must, as a condition of employment, agree to register in the agency's Priority Placement Program (PPP) before the end of the 5-year period or before the end of an approved extension. DODI No. 1400-25, Vol. 1230, Enclosure (Encl.) 2, ¶ 5.a.(2). The employee also must agree that failure or refusal to register in the PPP is a basis for separation. Id., Encl. 2, ¶ 5.a.(3)(c).

[3] Generally, competitive service career and career-conditional employees who are employed in the United States or another nonforeign area and who accept an assignment in either a foreign area or in a nonforeign area different from the one where they are employed currently shall be granted statutory return rights. 10 U.S.C. § 1586(b); DODI No. 1400-25, Vol. 1230, ¶ 4.i.

The DOD PPP Handbook, which sets out standard operating procedures for the PPP, provides that PPP registration will be denied when the registering activity--in this case, the agency's Civilian Personnel Advisory Center (CPAC)--has knowledge of performance or conduct that directly and negatively affects the employee's qualifications, eligibility, or suitability for placement. DOD PPP Handbook, chapter 3, ¶ C.16. In the case of "substantiated conduct or performance problems," regardless of whether formal disciplinary action is taken, eligibility will be withheld for a period of time to ensure that the necessary corrective measures have had their intended effect. Id., chapter 3, ¶ C.16.(a). Local agency policy further specifies that when CPAC has knowledge of a conduct or performance problem, PPP registration must be withheld for a period of at least 90 days....

If an employee returning from an overseas assignment in accordance with the terms of a preestablished agreement receives a lower-graded position resulting in pay retention, the employee is not entitled to appeal--even if the return under 10 USC 1586 is involuntary. Fromer v. DOD, 29 MSPR 481, 483 (1985) (remanding for a determination of whether pay retention was provided and not reaching jurisdiction if there was no pay retention). Walton v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 244, 248-50 (1989), added:

...The Board has held that an employee who claims that his exercise of return rights from an overseas assignment to his original, lower graded position under 10 USC 1586 was involuntary and an adverse action, but who has received the benefit of pay retention under § 1586, has not been affected by a reduction-in-grade action that is appealable to the Board. See Fromer v. Dept. of Defense, 29 MSPR 481, 483 (1985); Nightingale v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 560, 562-63 (1982)....

The administrative judge, furthermore, correctly found that the agency's termination of the appellant's term promotion after completion of the 2-year rotation period and his return to the position from which he was promoted, in accordance with 5 CFR 335.102(g), is specifically excluded from adverse action coverage by 5 CFR 752.401(c)(8)....

The appellant contends that his appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction because his exercise of return rights was based on misinformation and thus was involuntary. An agency action, however, is not within the Board's jurisdiction merely because it is involuntary. The involuntariness of an action does not establish the Board's jurisdiction if the employee's status or some other reason takes the case out of our purview.... The appellant's return from the overseas assignment...with the benefit of pay retention, even if it had been ordered by the agency contrary to the appellant's desires, is not an adverse action appealable to the Board.... Regardless, therefore, of whether his exercise of return rights was involuntary, based on his alleged reliance on agency misinformation as to his rate of pay on return to his former position, his appeal is not within the Board's jurisdiction.

Further, the appellant's contention that jurisdiction may be conferred on the Board by estoppel, based on his exercise of return rights in alleged reliance on the agency's misrepresentation of his rate of pay, lacks merit. The law is well settled that subject matter jurisdiction cannot be conferred by estoppel based on misrepresentations, as a sanction, or based on any other actions by the parties. See, e.g., Insurance Corp. of Ireland v. Compagnie Des Bauxites de Guinee, 456 U.S. 694, 702, 102 S. Ct. 2099, 2104, 72 L. Ed.2d 492 (1982); Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger, 437 U.S. 365, 377 n.21, 98 S. Ct. 2396, 2404 n.21, 57 L. Ed.2d 274 (1978); Schwartz v. Gordon, 761 F.2d 864, 869 (2d Cir. 1985); Rubin v. Buckman, 727 F.2d 71, 72 (3d Cir. 1984); Warren v. Department of Transportation, 19 MSPR 560, 563-64 (1984).
