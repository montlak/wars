2. Protective Order for Appellant

For appellants (those who are still employed during the Board appellate process), guidance concerning protective orders was offered in In re Uriarte v. USPS, 93 MSPR 183, 186 ¶¶ 8-10 (2002):

During the pendency of any proceeding before it, the Board may grant an order protecting a witness or other individual from harassment. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(B)(i); Ernst v. Department of the Treasury, 69 M.S.P.R. 133, 137 (1995), aff'd, 92 F.3d 1208 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). The purpose of this authority is to protect witnesses in order to aid the fact-finding process. Garst v. Department of the Army, 60 M.S.P.R. 514, 520 (1994). A motion for a protective order must be made as early as possible in the proceedings and must include a concise statement of reasons for the motion and any relevant documentary evidence. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.55(d); Oates v. Department of Health & Human Services, 64 M.S.P.R. 349, 351 (1994). The request must describe the allegedly unfair manner in which agency officials have treated the named employee. Oates, 64 M.S.P.R. at 351. Mere speculation that the agency took improper actions in reprisal for a protected activity does not establish harassment warranting a protective order. See Ernst, 69 M.S.P.R. at 137; Leaton v. Department of the Interior, 65 M.S.P.R. 331, 341 (1994), aff'd, 64 F.3d 678 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Instead, the movant's evidence must show that he has suffered harassment because of his involvement or potential involvement in the Board appeal. Heining v. General Services Administration, 61 M.S.P.R. 539, 557 (1994).

We first find that Mr. Uriarte satisfied the requirement that he seek a protective order as early as possible in the proceeding. The record establishes that Mr. Uriarte testified at the Board hearing on August 8, 2000, and that he filed his motion for a protective order on August 28, 2000, upon believing that the agency took acts of reprisal against him following his testimony.

We also find, however, that the question of whether Mr. Uriarte has satisfied the test for obtaining a protective order is best decided by the administrative judge who conducted the hearing in this appeal. The administrative judge is in the best position to determine whether Mr. Uriarte's testimony gave rise to a need for such an order. Mr. Uriarte also had unresolved discovery requests for evidence that he claimed supported his protective order request pending with the administrative judge at the time the administrative judge denied the protective order request on jurisdictional grounds as well as agency responses to Mr. Uriarte's request. The Board's regulations also anticipate that motions for protective orders are within the administrative judge's province. 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.41(b)(14), 1201.55(d), 1201.182(c)(3). Further, Mr. Uriarte filed his protective order motion with the administrative judge. In light of these factors, we find that the administrative judge should have ruled on Mr. Uriarte's pending motion before issuing the initial decision that divested him of jurisdiction over this matter. See Spithaler v. Office of Personnel Management, 1 M.S.P.R. 587, 589 (1980)...We, therefore, find it appropriate to remand this matter to the administrative judge for adjudication of Mr. Uriarte's motion for a protective order.

A motion for a protective order must be specific, Oates v. DHHS, 64 MSPR 349, 351 (1994):

In her petition, the appellant asks that a protective order be issued for one of the witnesses who testified on her behalf at the hearing before the administrative judge. Under certain circumstances, the Board may grant an order protecting a witness or other individual from harassment, but a motion for a protective order must be made as early as possible in the proceedings and must include a concise statement of reasons for the motion and any relevant documentary evidence. See 5 CFR §§ 1201.41(b)(14), 1201.55(d). Even assuming that the motion here was timely and properly made, we deny it. The appellant's motion fails to describe the allegedly unfair manner in which agency officials have treated the named employee.... As the appellant has failed to set forth any clear basis upon which we can find that the named employee has suffered harassment because of her involvement in this appeal, we deny the appellant's motion for a protective order. See 5 CFR § 1201.55(d).

Pumphrey v. DOD, 122 MSPR 186, 192 ¶ 14, 2015 MSPB 10 (2015), also emphasized the need for specificity as to an appellant's motion for a protective order:

[T]he Board and its administrative judges are authorized to issue any order that may be necessary to protect a witness or other individual from harassment in connection with a pending Board matter. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(B); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(14). A request for such a protective order will not be granted unless the moving party makes a concise statement of the reasons, together with any relevant documentary evidence. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.55(d). The Board will not grant such an order based on mere speculation. Leaton v. Department of the Interior, 65 M.S.P.R. 331, 341 (1994), aff'd, 64 F.3d 678 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Neither the appellant's petition for review, nor submissions before the administrative judge, contain sufficient information from which the Board could conclude that he has been, or may be, subjected to the sort of harassment from which 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(B)(i) was meant to afford protection. Thus, we decline to issue any order under that authority regarding the appellant's unsupported claim of retaliation in connection with the scheduling of his furlough days. See Leaton, 65 M.S.P.R. at 341; Heining v. General Services Administration, 61 M.S.P.R. 539, 556-57 (1994).

The existence of the authority to issue a protective order was noted in a constructive adverse action appeal by an ALJ in Lawson v. DHHS, 61 MSPR 313, 315-16 (1994); see Ernst v. Dept. of Treasury, 69 MSPR 133, 137 (1995) (as to a "constructive request for a protective order," the Board recognized its authority to grant an order to protect the appellant but denied it under the circumstances; reliance was placed upon Leaton v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 331, 341 (1994), stating that "mere speculation that the agency reprimanded the appellant for improper use of government property and equipment in reprisal for filing his appeal does not establish harassment warranting a protective order"). In Lawson, the AJ hearing the case used the protective order authority to direct the employer to provide the AJ with information relating to the appellant's appeal from his constructive removal as an ALJ. The Board took the unusual step in Lawson of vacating the protective order on the agency's motion to the Board to rescind that order, apparently a type of interlocutory appeal (the Board's decision did not state whether the AJ hearing the case granted an interlocutory appeal, but the decision suggests that the employer simply filed a motion with the Board to vacate the protective order). The Board vacated the protective order with the observation that the order was more in the nature of a discovery order than a protective order against harassment of the appellant.

Leaton v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 331, 341 (1994), denied a protective order to an appellant who complained of harassment following his adverse action appeal:

The appellant has filed a "Motion for Protective Order," in which he alleges that the agency recently reprimanded him for improper use of government property and equipment, in reprisal for his filing of this appeal. He requests that: The reprimand be set aside; the agency be enjoined from further "harassment"; the agency remove references to a 1991 disciplinary action from his file; the agency be enjoined from disciplining him for participating in appeals and grievances; and he be awarded attorney fees.

A reprimand taken in reprisal for an employee's exercise of his right to appeal an adverse action to the Board is a prohibited personnel practice. See 5 USC 2302(b)(9)(A). Section 2302 is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction, however. See Fogerty v. Department of the Treasury, 53 MSPR 168, 170 (1992). Although the Board is authorized to adjudicate a claim of reprisal for activities protected under section 2302(b)(9) in an action to enforce a final Board order, Gaydon v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 MSPR 276, 279 (1988), or when such a claim is interposed as an affirmative defense to an otherwise appealable action, 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(B), neither circumstance is present here.

We also decline to order the agency not to "harass" the appellant or to take future actions in reprisal for his filing of appeals and grievances. While we do have the authority to issue an order "necessary to protect a witness or other individual from harassment," 5 USC § 1204(e)(1)(B)(i), a party moving for such an order "must include a concise statement of reasons justifying the motion," 5 CFR § 1201.55(d). Here, the appellant merely speculates that the agency reprimanded him for improper use of government property and equipment in reprisal for his filing of this appeal; without more, we cannot conclude that he has been, or may be, subjected to the sort of "harassment" from which 5 USC § 1204(e)(1)(B)(i) was meant to afford protection.

Further, given that the propriety of the 1991 disciplinary action is not properly before us, we lack the authority to order the agency to remove from the appellant's records references to that action.

If the appellant seeks protection from agency harassment and if his appeal alludes to whistleblowing activities, the AJ must inform the appellant of what is required to establish Board jurisdiction if the jurisdictional basis is not otherwise apparent from the appeal.
