6. Communication of Standard

Papritz v. DOJ, 31 MSPR 495, 497-98 (1986), endorsed flexible means of communicating performance standards:

An agency is required to communicate to its employees, at the beginning of each appraisal period, the performance standards and critical elements of their positions. 5 USC § 4302(b)(2). When an appellant contends that an agency violated this requirement, the agency must prove by substantial evidence that the appellant was made aware of and understood the performance standards and critical elements in question at the beginning of the appraisal period that forms the basis of the adverse action. Cross v. Department of the Air Force, 25 MSPR 353, 357 (1984). Communication of those standards may occur in the performance improvement period, in counseling sessions held with the appellant, in written instructions, or "in any manner calculated to apprise the employee of the requirements against which he is to be measured." See Donaldson v. Department of Labor, 27 MSPR 293, 298 (1985).

Recognizing that under Papritz and Donaldson performance standards may be communicated through counseling sessions or through other discussions between a supervisor and an employee, the performance standard is generally set in a performance appraisal plan. It may be supplemented (generally through the injection of a standard at the minimally successful level, if required) through the PIP. An agency cannot communicate standards to the employee and, following a PIP, reach back to rely on unsuccessful performance during the time that the employee was unaware of those standards. See Jimenez-Howe v. Dept. of Labor, 35 MSPR 202, 208 (1987); cf. Feesago v. DOD, SF-0432-16-0458-I-1 (NP 8/10/2022), post.
