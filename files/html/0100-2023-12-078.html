1. Effective Date of Interim Relief

Interim relief is available only when a PFR has been filed. Laviene v. USPS, 53 MSPR 238, 242 (1992); cf. Arellanes v. DOD, DE-0752-15-0021-C-1 (NP 2/21/2023) (Vice Chairman Harris concurring and dissenting in part) (interim relief not a component of an AJ's compliance decision). If a PFR is not filed by an agency from an initial decision favoring the appellant, barring other unusual developments, the initial decision becomes final and the agency is required to implement the relief directed by the AJ, with enforcement proceedings (discussed in Chapter 16) available to the appellant to ensure the relief directed by the AJ is provided to the appellant. If the agency files a PFR but does not provide interim relief directed by the AJ, the Board issues a decision dismissing the PFR and making final the initial decision that granted a remedy to the appellant.

Interim relief is "effective upon the making of the [initial] decision." 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(A). When it files its PFR, the agency should show that the appellant has been on the rolls and paid since the date of the initial decision. A later date of restoration to duty will result in dismissal of the PFR. See Ricciardi v. USPS, 51 MSPR 622 (1991) (Table); Stevenson v. DOD, 51 MSPR 622 (1991) (Table). The agency's PFR will be dismissed if it restores the appellant, but the restoration is not effective until after the date of the initial decision. See Hurlburt v. DOJ, 52 MSPR 221, 223 (1992).

If an appellant protests that he has not received his "back pay" to the date of interim relief and the agency does not demonstrate its prompt correction of the problem, the agency's PFR is subject to dismissal for failure to provide interim relief. See White v. USPS, 60 MSPR 314, 317 (1993). But actual payment of amounts due for interim relief is not required by the date the agency files its PFR. The only requirement by that date is documentation of the administrative action necessary to secure the issuance of a paycheck. See Johnson v. DOJ, 67 MSPR 494, 497 (1995) (relying on an SF-50). Although interim relief must be provided effective on the date of the initial decision, as a practical matter, documents will be backdated. This practice was approved in Johnson v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 386, 389 (1993), as long as the evidence of the interim relief is supplied to the Board not later than the date the agency is permitted to file its PFR:

Contrary to the appellant's assertion, the fact that the agency did not take the appropriate personnel action until November 25, 1992, does not render the action insufficient. Interim relief must be effective as of the date of the initial decision, 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(A); Moore v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 73, 74 (1992), but there is no requirement that the agency take the appropriate personnel action on the very day that the initial decision is issued. Indeed, such a requirement would impose upon the agency the impossible burden of anticipating which day an initial decision will be issued, whether it will order interim relief, and, if so, what is required by the interim relief order. Thus, the appellant's suggestion that the agency improperly "back-dated" his personnel records is misplaced.
