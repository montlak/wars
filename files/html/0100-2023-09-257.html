B. STRATEGIC CONSIDERATIONS

The appellant will normally be in a position to make better use of discovery than the agency. Most appeals involve adverse or performance-based actions. The agency should have investigated the adverse action or prepared the documentation supporting the performance case before taking action against the appellant. Without regard to formal agency investigations, agency representatives have access to information (data, files, and people) pertaining to the appellant and similar disciplinary cases without going through discovery. Nevertheless, the agency can put discovery to good use, particularly if the appellant is planning to use expert witnesses, e.g., physicians, to obtain information about expected testimony. Both the agency and the appellant can use interrogatories to require the opponent to state who the witnesses will be, the nature of their testimony, their addresses and phone numbers. Requests will be made for documents bearing on disputed issues. Admission requests may be used to narrow the scope of a factual controversy. Physical and mental examinations may be required in cases involving claims for compensatory damages. Depositions elicit all sorts of information and may lock witnesses into versions of events that, when varied without good reason, cast doubt on veracity.

Some lawyers, particularly those with little litigation experience in personnel cases, seek to avoid disclosures or obstruct the process through needless objections. It is simpler, and usually produces a more reasonable result, if discovery is offered in a responsive manner without evasion or acrimony. Normally the material, if discoverable, will be produced in the end, following litigation over discovery issues. Discovery disputes prolong the proceeding and increase the parties' expenses. If the disputes prolong the adjudication sufficiently, the agency may face increased back pay and counsel fee exposure. If the AJ errs by denying discovery, a PFR may result in a remand for yet another period for discovery and another hearing if anything material is discovered. Failure to comply with discovery requests may result in sanctions. There will be times that objections can and should be entered, but they should be sparingly employed.

Some AJs take the most restrictive view of discovery. Their rulings demonstrate time and again that what would be discoverable under the Federal Rules in a district court becomes irrelevant or immaterial when the information is requested and opposed before the MSPB. As a result, the parties are shortchanged in the hearing process. Low-level rulings by AJs on discovery disputes are insulated from judicial review because it cannot be clearly demonstrated that the case turned on information withheld. Few Board decisions remand cases for improprieties during discovery; the Board is reluctant to prolong a case and require a second hearing on the possibility that discovery would produce information that would change the initial decision. If remand is ordered for additional discovery, the party should be able to submit at another hearing, or supplement the existing record with, additional evidence that he was prevented from filing by the other party's earlier lack of compliance. The AJ must then reconsider the evidence of record. See Belcher v. Dept. of Air Force, 82 MSPR 230, 235 ¶ 10 (1999).
