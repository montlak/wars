a. Suitability

Mattson v. OPM, 8 MSPR 159, 161-64 (1981), provides an early and extensive analysis of the interplay between past alcoholism and OPM suitability determinations involving future employment:

I. BACKGROUND

When the appellant originally applied for the position of Keypunch Operator, he indicated on his application form that he had received a suspended sentence for public drunkenness and that he had been forced to leave past employment because of a "chemical dependency." Based on these disclosures, the former Civil Service Commission undertook, and OPM continued, a suitability investigation that revealed the appellant had a long history of alcoholism.

In 1967, the appellant was discharged from the Air Force as a result of his dependency on alcohol. He later resided at and participated in the programs of at least four alcohol rehabilitation centers, but he left them all without successfully completing the programs. In 1975, the appellant resigned from the GS-3 position of Card Punch Operator with the Veterans Administration after receiving notice of termination for poor attendance caused by excessive drinking. He was then employed for less than a year with the State of Minnesota, where he developed an unsatisfactory attendance record. In 1978, the appellant's wife, who originally met the appellant at an alcoholic rehabilitation center where she was employed, divorced him because he could not stay sober. The appellant's former supervisors, who were interviewed during the investigation, stated that the appellant had been an excellent worker when he reported for duty, but that they could not recommend him for Federal employment until he overcame his alcohol problem.

When OPM informed the appellant of the findings of its investigation, the appellant responded with a letter of recommendation from a supervisor in the rehabilitation center where he had been residing for the last 3 1/2 months, stating that the appellant was a very productive, above average worker who had no problems with his attendance. OPM concluded that the appellant was unsuitable for Federal employment, nonetheless, because of the facts disclosed in the investigation and because the appellant's improvement over the last 3 1/2 months, though commendable, was achieved in a totally structured institutional environment.

II. DISCUSSION

The regulations of OPM concerning suitability determinations provide that it may deny an applicant examination or deny an eligible appointment if it determines that "this action will promote the efficiency of the service." 5 CFR 731.201. They provide, further, that the determination should be based on:

[1] Whether the conduct of the individual may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent effective performance in the position applied for or employed in; or

[2] Whether the conduct of the individual may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent effective performance by the employing agency of its duties and responsibilities.

5 CFR 731.202(a). "Delinquency or misconduct in prior employment" and "[h]abitual use of intoxicating beverages to excess" may be considered a basis for disqualification. 5 CFR 731.202.

The Comprehensive Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism Prevention, Treatment, and Rehabilitation Act of 1970, as amended (Alcohol Abuse Act), provides that "[n]o person may be denied or deprived of Federal civilian employment or a Federal professional or other license or right solely on the ground of prior alcohol abuse or prior alcoholism." 42 USC 4561(c)(1). It provides further, however, that "[T]his...shall not be construed to prohibit the dismissal from employment of a Federal civilian employee who cannot properly function in his employment." 42 USC 4561(d).

In accordance with its mandate under the Alcohol Abuse Act to develop and maintain appropriate prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation programs for alcohol abuse and alcoholism among Federal civilian employees, 42 USC 4561(a), OPM's predecessor promulgated the following rule concerning the employment of alcoholics:

In considering applicants for Federal employment who have a history of alcoholism or drug abuse, the Commission will make its determination on the basis of whether or not the applicant is a good employment risk. In such cases, the length of time since the last abuse of alcohol or drugs is less important than the steps taken by the applicant to obtain treatment of his or her illness through medical care, rehabilitation, and similar actions.

FPM Supp. 792-2, subch. S6-5 (April 1976) (emphasis added). When OPM published the current version of the FPM Supp. 792-2 on February 29, 1980, that provision was revised only by substituting its name for that of the Commission. In the brief filed in response to our reopening order, OPM asserted that the "good employment risk" standard established by FPM Supp. 792-2 is simply a restatement of the suitability guidelines set forth at 5 CFR 731.202. In other words, OPM urged, a determination of whether an applicant's "[d]elinquency or misconduct in prior employment" and "[h]abitual use of intoxicating beverages to excess" may "reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent effective performance..." is the same as a determination of whether, based on the applicant's past conduct, he or she is a "good employment risk." We defer to OPM's eminently reasonable construction of a rule that it promulgated. See National Distributing Co. v. U.S. Treasury, 625 F.2d 997, 1019 (D.C. Cir. 1980).

OPM asserted further in its brief that, if an applicant has a history of alcohol abuse and related employment delinquency or misconduct, and if he or she cannot demonstrate the successful completion of a rehabilitation program or sufficient progress toward rehabilitation, that applicant may be found ineligible for Federal employment as a poor "employment risk." It contended that this view is supported by the language of 42 USC 4561(c)(1), prohibiting the denial of such employment "solely on the ground of prior alcohol abuse or prior alcoholism." (Emphasis added.) Since the ineligible rating is based on employment delinquency or misconduct as well as alcoholism, OPM argued, it is not grounded "solely" on alcoholism;[2] and since the appellant has not established that he is rehabilitated or is soon likely to be rehabilitated, the problem of alcohol abuse or alcoholism appears to be current, not "prior."

[2] See Spragg v. Campbell, 466 F. Supp. 658 (D.S.D. 1979), upholding the removal of a Federal employee for crashing a government vehicle while intoxicated on duty. The court found in that case that the employee's removal was not predicated "solely" on his alcohol abuse. Id. at 660-61.

We find that this view is consistent with OPM's lawfully issued regulations at 5 CFR 731.202 and that it is not inconsistent with the Alcohol Abuse Act. We must still consider, however, whether application of that view in a case such as this results in handicap discrimination. See Ruzek v. General Services Administration, 7 MSPB 307, 309 (1981). The appellant claimed before the presiding official that he was being discriminated against because of his alcoholism, but the presiding official failed to consider that claim as he was required to under 5 CFR 1201.111(b). We shall remedy that error here.

The appellant established a prima facie case of handicap discrimination by showing that, as an alcoholic, he was a "handicapped person" under 29 CFR 1613.702(a), and as a person who can perform the essential functions of the position of Keypunch Operator without endangering the health and safety of himself or others, he was a "qualified handicapped person" under 29 CFR 1613.702(f). The appellant established, further, that OPM failed to accommodate his handicapping condition reasonably by rating him eligible for Federal employment as a rehabilitated alcoholic. See Stalkfleet v. U.S. Postal Service, 6 MSPB 536, 543 (1981). OPM then countered this showing by demonstrating that its action was based on a legitimate non-discriminatory reason, namely: the appellant had participated in rehabilitation programs in the past, but his alcoholism continued to affect his attendance at work; and although the appellant was participating in another rehabilitation program at the time he applied for Federal employment and was doing well in a related work program, there was no credible evidence that the appellant was rehabilitated or was sufficiently rehabilitated to be a "good employment risk."

The appellant did not rebut OPM's showing of a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for his ineligibility rating with any argument or facts tending to establish that the agency's stated reason for failing to accommodate his handicap is pretextual for handicap discrimination. We find, therefore, that the appellant has not carried his burden of proving that his ineligible rating was based on the prohibited personnel practice of handicap discrimination. 5 USC 2302(b)(2) and 7701(c)(2)(B); Stalkfleet, 543. We find instead that OPM has supported its decision to rate the appellant ineligible by preponderant evidence. 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(B).
