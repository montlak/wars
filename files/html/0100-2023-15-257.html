1. Service Efficiency

Theft is prejudicial to the federal service and without question is related to the faithful and loyal performance of an employee's duties. Cook v. VA, 9 MSPR 392, 394 (1981). Theft warrants discharge, with consideration of mitigating factors, if the appellant would have continued access to government property, and if the agency's ability to deter other employee theft would be lessened, particularly if the agency is experiencing significant problems with theft. Douglas v. VA, 5 MSPR 280, 305-06, 5 MSPB 313, 345 (1981); see Fox v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 342, 345 (1981) (minor thefts warranted a demotion from WG-13 inspector to WG-10 pipefitter: "Considering appellant's conduct and the fact that, in his present position, appellant had access to large quantities of valuable and sensitive material vital to the nuclear propulsion program, his integrity and trustworthiness in handling such materials were severely diminished in the eyes of the agency."). Removal is reasonable for an employee's first-time offense of theft or unauthorized possession of government property if the value of the property taken was not de minimis. Vannoy v. OPM, 75 MSPR 170, 175, 177 (1997); see O'Connell v. DOD, 374 Fed. Appx. 30 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) ("Theft of a significant amount of property by a person occupying a position of trust is a serious offense for which an agency can reasonably remove an employee even in the face of other factors favoring the employee."). Repeated theft of gasoline from motor pool vehicles by a motor vehicle operator justified discharge. The penalty of removal for an employee with a good reputation in the community and a long and otherwise satisfactory service record in other respects was not unduly harsh. The agency warned him to avoid the conduct resulting in removal. Lancaster v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 627, 630, 6 MSPB 528 (1981).

Theft of money from a coffee fund for employees led to removal, sustained despite a long and good work record. The theft of property, whether it is government property or the property of coworkers, demonstrates an employee's dishonesty and untrustworthiness. To impose a lesser penalty than removal limits the agency's ability to fulfill its duty to provide a safe workplace for its employees. Wanner v. USPS, 6 MSPR 648, 649, 6 MSPB 434 (1981) ("Appellant's offense in this case was serious, as 'the crux of the matter is the question of reliability.' See Rodriguez v. Seamans, 463 F.2d 837, 843 (D.C. Cir. 1972)."); see Scott v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 187, 188-89 (1984) (removal for the unauthorized possession of five rolls of roofing material belonging to a civilian construction company working in the shipyard where the appellant was on duty; removal of the appellant, an air conditioning equipment mechanic, was warranted because the appellant's work brought him into contact with the property of civilian construction firms). Removal for attempted theft of government property was proper when the employee, a commissary sales store checker, was in a position giving her responsibility for handling of government funds. O'Kane v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 25, 28-29 (1984) ("That appellant was prevented from completing the act of theft does not detract from the seriousness of the offense or the reasonableness of the penalty of removal."); see Sanders v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 88, 92 (1984) (dismissal of a store checker for defrauding the agency by failing to properly charge customers). In Sears v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 417, 421, 7 MSPB 290 (1981), involving removal of less than $100 of government property by an employee with 25 years of service, a purchasing agent responsible for procuring material for public use was expected to adhere to high standards of honesty. The agency was justified in concluding that it could not trust the appellant and that the conduct severely impaired his usefulness in any position. See Rizzo v. VA, CH-0752-15-0104-I-2 (NP 8/5/2022) (tossing some documents from a shared printer into the garbage: "A charge of inappropriate conduct does not generally require an element of intent. See Crouse v. Department of the Treasury, 75 M.S.P.R. 57, 63 (1997). An agency may incorporate an element of intent by claiming that the employee engaged in intentional misconduct or that the conduct was improper because of the employee's intent. Id.").
