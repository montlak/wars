2. MSPB Review Procedure

The process for seeking Board review of arbitration awards, referred to as "decisions" by the Board, is 5 CFR 1201.155 (2022), "Requests for review of arbitrators' decisions":

(d) Contents. The appellant must file the request with the Clerk of the Board, Merit Systems Protection Board, 1615 M Street NW., Washington, DC 20419. The request for review must contain:

(1) A statement of the grounds on which review is requested;

(2) References to evidence of record or rulings related to the issues before the Board;

(3) Arguments in support of the stated grounds that refer specifically to relevant documents and that include relevant citations of authority; and

(4) Legible copies of the final grievance or arbitration decision, the agency decision to take the action, and other relevant documents. Those documents may include a transcript or recording of the hearing.

(e) Development of the Record. The Board, in its discretion, may develop the record as to a claim of prohibited discrimination by ordering the parties to submit additional evidence or forwarding the request for review to a judge to conduct a hearing.

(f) Closing of the Record. The record will close upon expiration of the period for filing the response to the request for review, or to the brief on intervention, if any, or on any other date the Board sets for this purpose. Once the record closes, no additional evidence or argument will be accepted unless the party submitting it shows that the evidence was not readily available before the record closed.

The 2012 regulatory changes did not change the preexisting scope of review; instead the regulation now requires that the allegation of discrimination be first raised with the arbitrator if the Board is going to review the arbitration award based on an EEO allegation. When review is requested, the Board then reviews the EEO issue as well as other civil service issues not involving EEO, as the Board noted in its Federal Register notice accompanying the 2012 rule changes at 77 FR 62,350, 62,360 (10/12/2012): "An appellant who raises a discrimination claim to the arbitrator in addition to the Title 5 or other employment claim will be entitled to an adjudication of both."

In cases other than those of Postal Service preference eligibles, who appeal directly to the Board's regional offices, e.g., Jones v. USPS, 31 MSPR 130, 131 (1986), the review of an arbitration award is conducted in Washington, meaning that the petition must be filed with the Board's Clerk, with an explanation of the case and description of the specific circumstances constituting discrimination. The governing regulation, 5 CFR 1201.155 (2022), was set out earlier in this discussion. Regional offices have no jurisdiction to issue decisions on the petitions. Oliphant v. USPS, 18 MSPR 468, 468-69 (1983). The appellant may seek review of the arbitration award. The Board's regulations do not provide for a cross-request for review by the agency. See Benson v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 548, 553 (1994) (citing then 5 CFR 1201.154(d)). If an appeal from an arbitration award is directed to an AJ, the AJ should forward it to the Board's Clerk for docketing. See Moore v. SSA, AT-0752-11-0881-I-1 (NP 2/28/2014). The exception to this procedure lies with appeals from Postal Service employees; 5 USC 7121 is not applicable to those individuals. Appeals by postal employees within the Board's jurisdiction are processed and decided by Board regional offices. Williams v. USPS, 35 MSPR 581, 583 (1987), supra; see Jones v. USPS, 35 MSPR 612, 613 (1987) (forwarding to the region an appeal filed with the Board's Clerk in Washington); Jones v. USPS, 31 MSPR 130, 131 (1986). For Postal Service employees, the employee's case is treated as an original appeal, not as a request for review of an arbitrator's decision. [Refer to Chapter 1, "Effect of Collective Bargaining Contract Grievance or Related Procedures; 5 USC 7121," concerning the limitations in the Postal Service negotiated agreements involving initial processing of cases under grievance procedures and later attempts to shift to the Board appeal process; refer later in this chapter to "Postal Service Employees."]

If the Board needs to conduct factfinding on an arbitration review request, as occurred with a claim for compensatory damages that an arbitrator failed to resolve despite a finding of discrimination, the Board may forward the matter to one of its AJs to conduct a hearing, develop a record, and issue a recommended decision to the Board for the Board to evaluate in issuing its final decision on the arbitration review request. See Sadiq v. VA, 119 MSPR 450, 2013 MSPB 36 (2013) (referral to AJ to make determinations as to issues involving religious discrimination and disparate penalties when the arbitrator did not make those findings, following remand to consider allegations of discrimination, Sadiq v. VA, CB-7121-12-0004-V-1 (NP 3/13/2015)); Pace v. Dept. of Treasury, 118 MSPR 542, 2012 MSPB 108 (2012) (referral to AJ for analysis of disability discrimination claims); Hollingsworth v. Dept. of Commerce, 115 MSPR 636, 638 ¶ 5, 2011 MSPB 26 (2011) (citing then 5 CFR 1201.204(b)(3)) (following remand, Hollingsworth v. Dept. of Commerce, 117 MSPR 327, 2012 MSPB 12 (2012) (awarding pecuniary and nonpecuniary damages)), supra; cf. Dennis v. DHUD, CB-7121-12-0024-V-1 (NP 4/16/2014) (Board analysis following referral of an arbitration appeal to an AJ for consideration of a disparate penalty claim). Or if the arbitrator used the wrong legal standard to evaluate an EEO claim, the Board may remand the case to an AJ. See Huynh v. SSA, CB-7121-14-0023-V-1 (NP 1/22/2015) ("To the extent that the appellant may not have been afforded proper notice of her burdens and the elements of proof during arbitration, the administrative judge shall provide such notice and afford the parties the opportunity to submit evidence and argument under the proper standards before making recommended findings on the merits of those claims. See Pace v. Department of the Treasury, 118 M.S.P.R. 542, ¶ 11 (2012). ...The administrative judge shall conduct such further proceedings as necessary and make recommended findings to the Board regarding the retaliation claim."). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Remand for Resolution of EEO Issue."]

To receive Board consideration during the review process, an appellant's arguments must pose specific objections to the arbitrator's interpretation of civil service law (including EEO laws), rules, or regulations. Guhl v. SSA, 41 MSPR 556, 559 (1989); see Means v. Dept. of Labor, 60 MSPR 108, 112 (1993) ("The appellant's appeal form and...letter express disagreement with the arbitrator's decision, but they do not set forth any reasoned basis for disturbing any of the arbitrator's conclusions.").

Wright v. Dept. of Commerce, 110 MSPR 349, 352-53 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 251 (2008), outlined the review process and explained that the review request must specifically direct the Board to the arbitrator's errors:

A request for review of an arbitrator's decision must contain:

(1) A statement of the grounds on which review is requested;

(2) References to evidence of record or rulings related to the issues before the Board;

(3) Arguments in support of the stated grounds that refer specifically to relevant documents, and that include relevant citations of authority; and

(4) Legible copies of the final grievance or arbitration decision, the agency decision to take the action, and other relevant documents.

5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(d). The appellant has submitted legible copies of the arbitration decision, and the agency's decision removing him. He has also stated, in general terms, the grounds on which review is requested, i.e., that the arbitrator's decision exceeded his authority, was arbitrary and capricious, and was inconsistent with prior decisions of other arbitrators, the Board and the EEOC. However, he has not provided references to evidence of record or rulings related to the issues before the Board. Nor has he provided arguments in support of his stated grounds. Instead, the appellant has simply refiled the closing brief he submitted before the arbitrator. In effect, the appellant is requesting de novo review of the arbitrator's decision, which is beyond the scope of the Board's role in this matter. See Robinson, 30 M.S.P.R. at 392. We therefore find that the appellant has not met the Board's criteria for review of an arbitrator's decision. Accordingly, we sustain the arbitrator's decision. See Cook v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 50 M.S.P.R. 660, 661-62 (1991) (an appellant's bare assertions of error by the arbitrator, unsupported by references to the record or even by argument, constitute mere disagreement with the arbitrator and do not justify modifying or setting aside the arbitration decision).

It is the parties' responsibility to determine what documentation is necessary and then to supply it to the Board. See Carr v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 665, 670 n.5 (1987). Although the Board has reviewed deficient petitions, at least to the extent of determining whether jurisdiction existed over the petitions, see Cantrell v. DHHS, 55 MSPR 600, 603 (1992), the materials that must be submitted are now codified at 5 CFR 1201.155(d) (2022), supra, roughly paralleling the regulation, 5 CFR 2425.2 (2022), of the Federal Labor Relations Authority governing the structure of exceptions to arbitration awards reviewable by the Authority. Denson v. VA, 30 MSPR 383, 385-86 (1986), set out the materials that the Board expected to be produced, prior to the regulation on the subject, on review of an arbitration award:

...We find that appellants are not required to submit a transcript or verbatim record of the arbitration hearing with a request for review. Our determination whether the arbitrator erred as a matter of law, however, will be made on the basis of the documents submitted with the request and the agency response. In reaching this conclusion, we reject the contention that lack of a transcript or tapes of the arbitration hearing entitles appellant to a new hearing before the Board. Such a hearing would, in effect, provide an appellant with a de novo review, contrary to 5 USC § 7121(d) and the policy considerations and legislative history of the Civil Service Reform Act. See Robinson, supra.

In order to assure, however, that a sufficient record exists for determining whether an arbitrator has erred as a matter of law, we will require that all requests for review include a statement of the grounds on which review is requested, evidence of record or rulings bearing on the issues before the Board, arguments in support of the stated grounds with specific reference to the pertinent documents and citations of authority, and legible copies of the award and other pertinent documents.[2] The agency may also submit pertinent documents and citations with its response to the request for review. The Federal Labor Relations Authority currently requires similar submissions with exceptions to review of arbitration decisions, and we find that these requirements would also facilitate requests for Board review. See 5 CFR § 2425.2. We, therefore, adopt these requirements for requests for review under 5 USC § 7121(d).[3]

[2] A transcript or hearing tape may be submitted as a pertinent document.

[3] In support of his request for review, appellant has submitted the arbitrator's award, both parties' briefs before the arbitrator, the rules allegedly violated by the agency, the negotiated agreement, and other documents purportedly supporting his contentions. Along with its response to appellant's request, the agency has submitted the documentary record developed at the grievance proceeding. Appellant has, therefore, satisfied the above requirements, and a sufficient record exists to decide the questions raised in appellant's request for review.

As to the responsibility of the parties to present to the Board information about the arbitration to support arguments on review, under Avent v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-14-0008-V-1 (NP 9/10/2014):

In addition to the deference afforded arbitration awards, we note that an appellant seeking review of an arbitration decision has the burden of providing the Board with materials necessary to support matters raised on review. Holly v. Department of Health & Human Services, 92 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 10 (2002); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.155(d)(4). Here, the only evidence the appellant provided was the final arbitration decision. In its response, the agency provided its removal letter, and the arbitrator's denial of summary judgment. However, the record does not include the proposal to remove the appellant, a transcript or recording of the arbitration hearing, or any other supportive evidence.

If the Board's Clerk requests the appellant to supplement the review request with materials required under the Board's regulations, the Board may dismiss the review request if the response from the Appellant is not supplied with due diligence. See Serkin v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-14-0006-V-1 (NP 7/17/2014) ("Now, several months later and well past the deadline set by the Clerk of the Board, the appellant, despite the fact that he received both the Clerk's notice and the agency response, has still failed to supplement his request for review. A prudent person in the appellant's position would not have neglected to respond for this length of time. See Campa v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 M.S.P.R. 321, 323-24 (1994) (allowing an inordinate period of time to elapse without inquiring about the status of one's appeal is not the act of a reasonably prudent person).").

The appellant is not required to submit a transcript or verbatim record of the arbitration hearing with his review request, although he may do so (or he may submit a hearing recording), and lack of a transcript does not entitle the appellant to de novo review by MSPB. The Board's determination will be made on whatever documents are submitted with the request for review. Denson v. VA, 30 MSPR 383, 385 (1986); see Colon v. VA, 73 MSPR 659, 664 (1997) (declining to review objections to an arbitration award when the objections were based on materials apparently considered by the arbitrator but not provided to the Board); Cosio v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 691, 695 (1994) ("The determination of whether an arbitrator erred as a matter of law is made on the basis of the documents submitted with the appellant's request and the agency response."). But in Hidalgo v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 645, 656 ¶ 22 (2003), the Board asserted that the appellant could not demonstrate the existence of procedural objections or requests during the arbitration hearing without submitting a copy of the transcript to the Board.

Although a transcript or the hearing recordings are not an absolute prerequisite to a petition seeking review of an arbitration award, if a party seeks review of an arbitration award based on credibility determinations, some record of the proceedings may be necessary, as the Board pointed out in Chandler v. SSA, 80 MSPR 542, 547 ¶ 9 (1999):

Further, although the appellant questions the arbitrator's credibility determinations, we are also unable to assess such determinations without access to tapes or a transcript of the hearing. The appellant seeking review has the burden of providing the Board with materials necessary to support matters raised on review, including such transcripts or tapes. See 5 CFR 1201.154(d)(4).

Emphasizing that it reviews the arbitrator's determination of EEO claims based on the portions of the record presented to the Board, Avent v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-14-0008-V-1 (NP 9/10/2014), noted the hazard associated with a review request that depends on analysis of testimony but that lacks a transcript:

The appellant alleges that the arbitrator erred by finding that removal was a reasonable penalty when compared to similarly-situated white individuals. We find that the appellant failed to prove this claim.

After weighing witness testimony, the arbitrator dismissed the allegation that the appellant was subjected to a disparate penalty when compared to white individuals. He reached this conclusion by finding that there was insufficient evidence of the claim, "especially when considering the sparse testimony of the [appellant] and the straight forward [sic] testimony of [another witness]."

Because the appellant failed to present the Board with a transcript of the hearing and the arbitrator relied on testimony in dismissing the appellant's allegation, we are unable to assess the determinations at issue. See Holly, 92 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 10; 5 C.F.R. § 1201.155(d)(4). Absent any supporting evidence, the appellant's claim constitutes mere disagreement with the arbitrator's finding, which is entitled to deference. See Holly, 92 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 10. Therefore, we decline to consider this matter further.

After a Board decision is issued, the appellant may petition the EEOC to consider the Board's decision as to EEO issues. See 29 CFR 1614.401(d) (2022). The EEOC may concur with the Board or it may render a different decision on the discrimination issue; if the EEOC issues a different opinion, the Board must then adopt the decision or reaffirm its prior decision, with modifications if necessary. If the Board does not adopt the EEOC decision, a Special Panel composed of one Board member, a Commission member, and a Chairman appointed by the President considers and decides the case. Consideration of the "mixed case" under 7702 balances the respective expertise of the Board and the EEOC in an effort to protect the integrity of each. The administrative process may be bypassed by a lawsuit at various stages described at 5 USC 7702(e).
