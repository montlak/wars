m. Denial of Leave

Vitale v. VA, 107 MSPR 501, 509-12 ¶¶ 19-27, 2007 MSPB 300 (2007), suggested the possibility that a retirement is coerced if the agency places the employee in an intolerable situation by improperly denying leave:

In order to overcome the presumption that a resignation or retirement was voluntary, an employee must show that: (1) The resignation or retirement was the product of misinformation or deception by the agency; or (2) the resignation or retirement was the product of coercion by the agency. Terban v. Department of Energy, 216 F.3d 1021, 1024 (Fed. Cir. 2000). To establish involuntariness on the basis of coercion, an employee must show that the agency effectively imposed the terms of the employee's resignation or retirement, the employee had no realistic alternative but to resign or retire, and the employee's resignation or retirement was the result of improper acts by the agency. Id. The touchstone of the "voluntariness" analysis is whether, considering the totality of the circumstances, factors operated on the employee's decision-making process that deprived him of freedom of choice. Coufal v. Department of Justice, 98 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 22 (2004); Heining v. General Services Administration, 68 M.S.P.R. 513, 519-20 (1995).

In cases such as this one, where the employee alleges that the agency took actions that made working conditions so intolerable that the employee was driven to an involuntary resignation or retirement, the Board will find an action involuntary only if the employee demonstrates that the employer engaged in a course of action that made working conditions so difficult or unpleasant that a reasonable person in that employee's position would have felt compelled to resign or retire. Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 577-78 (1996). The Board addresses allegations of discrimination and reprisal in connection with an alleged involuntary resignation or retirement only insofar as those allegations relate to the issue of voluntariness. Pickens v. Social Security Administration, 88 M.S.P.R. 525, ¶ 6 (2001); Markon, 71 M.S.P.R. at 578.

On June 1, 2005, the agency issued the appellant a letter informing him that, because he had used 77 hours of sick leave since December 15, 2004, he was being placed on sick leave certification and would be required to submit an acceptable medical certificate within 5 workdays upon his return to duty whenever he used sick leave. On November 9, 2005, in response to the appellant's grievance of his placement on sick leave certification, the agency rescinded the sick leave certification letter and instead issued the appellant a written "sick leave warning notice" reminding the appellant that sick leave should be "judiciously utilized and only when necessary. The warning letter also stated that the agency "hope[d] that [the appellant would] improve [his] leave usage so that it [would] not be necessary to resort to sick leave certification." The appellant retired effective January 3, 2006.

The appellant asserted that agency officials coerced his retirement by placing him on sick leave certification despite their awareness that he had a medical condition (diabetes) that required him to use sick leave frequently. The appellant also submitted evidence of alleged discriminatory statements made by Human Resources Director George Pearson to support his assertion that the agency was trying to coerce his retirement. Id. at 4-22. According to the appellant's first-line supervisor, Supervisory Social Worker for Mental Health Joseph O'Rourke, and former Social Work Executive/Supervisory Social Worker Rose Ann Schultz, prior to the issuance of the June 1, 2005 sick leave certification letter to the appellant, Mr. Pearson stated during a meeting discussing sick leave abuse in the Medical Center Social Work department: "Is [the appellant] trying to retire on the job? Shouldn't he be retiring--he's 70." Mr. O'Rourke stated that, when he informed Mr. Pearson at this meeting that "the appellant was 66" years old and "protected under [the Age Discrimination In Employment Act] ...and ADA [Americans with Disabilities Act] regulations," Mr. Pearson implied that "disability retirement" was an option for the appellant. Also, according to both Ms. Schultz and Mr. O'Rourke, Mr. Pearson stated during this meeting that, "if you don't place [the employees] on sick leave certification, you can't NAIL them for excessive leave use."

The appellant asserted that, because the agency issued him the written sick leave warning notice even after he had supplied doctors' certificates for his months on sick leave certification, he was "certain" that the agency would have returned him again to a sick leave certification status the following month based on his need to attend 3-5 doctor appointments per month. Based on the agency's actions and the statements made by Mr. Pearson, the appellant allegedly believed that the agency was trying to force him to retire and that, if he did not retire, the agency would "build the case to...'nail' [him]." Id. He also asserted that he "retired January 3, 2006, concerned about [his] deteriorating health" because the hostile work environment that resulted from the agency's actions caused him great stress, which, in turn, had detrimental effects on his health, including his blood sugar and blood pressure levels.

Based on the record evidence, we find that the totality of the circumstances does not establish that the appellant's retirement was involuntary. We note initially that an agency is authorized to request its employees to provide medical certification for the use of sick leave "when the agency determines it is necessary." 5 C.F.R. § 630.403(a); Fuqua v. Department of the Navy, 31 M.S.P.R. 173, 174 n.1 (1986); see Jones v. Department of Justice, 98 M.S.P.R. 86, ¶ 15 (2004), review dismissed, 125 Fed. Appx. 1006 (Fed. Cir. 2005). The appellant asserted that he was "arbitrarily" placed on sick leave certification on June 1, 2005, for "sick leave abuse. The Board may find retirements to be involuntary based on coercion where the employee shows that "the agency has taken steps against the employee, not for any legitimate purpose, but simply to force the employee to quit." Staats, 99 F.3d at 1124. Here, however, the record establishes that, after a Medical Center-wide initiative was conducted to review its employees' sick leave use, numerous employees, including the appellant, received letters addressing their sick leave use. The appellant did not dispute that he took 77 hours of sick leave during the sick leave use review period as asserted by the agency in the June 1, 2005 sick leave certification letter. Cf. Caveney v. Office of Administration, 57 M.S.P.R. 667, 670-71 (1993) (finding a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness where the appellant alleged that his retirement in lieu of a directed reassignment was involuntary because the directed assignment had "no solid or substantial basis in personnel management or management principles").

Additionally, while a resignation or retirement procured in violation of the regulations for granting leave may be coercive, the appellant did not assert that he was denied any request to use sick leave after he was placed on sick leave certification. Cf. Landahl v. Department of Commerce, 83 M.S.P.R. 40, ¶¶ 7-11 (1999) (finding a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness based on the appellant's claim that the agency coerced his resignation by violating the regulations for granting leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993). The appellant also has not alleged that he was subject to any disciplinary action for his use of sick leave from the issuance of the June 1, 2005 sick leave certification letter until his retirement. See Pope v. Department of the Navy, 44 M.S.P.R. 289, 293 (1990) (an agency's requirement for medical documentation to document sick leave is not itself a disciplinary action). Further, the evidence establishes that the agency rescinded the appellant's sick leave certification letter in response to his grievance and replaced it with a November 9, 2005 written sick leave warning notice, nearly 2 months before he decided to retire.

Although the appellant's placement on sick leave certification, coupled with the statements of Mr. Pearson, may have caused the appellant apprehension and exacerbation of his medical ailments, he has not established that his working conditions were so intolerable that a reasonable person in his position would have felt compelled to retire. See Miller v. Department of Defense, 85 M.S.P.R. 310, ¶ 32 (2000) (a feeling of being unfairly criticized or difficult or unpleasant working conditions are generally not so intolerable as to compel a reasonable person to resign).

Considering the totality of the circumstances, we find that the appellant has not established by preponderant evidence that the agency engaged in a course of action that made working conditions so difficult that a reasonable person in his position would have felt compelled to retire. See id. It is therefore unnecessary to remand this appeal for further adjudication on the timeliness issue because we find that the Board does not have jurisdiction over the appeal.
