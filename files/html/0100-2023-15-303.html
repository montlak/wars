e. Lack of Training

A mitigating factor is the agency's failure to provide training or counseling on work assignments leading to disciplinary action. Removal is appropriate in a Chapter 75 performance case if, despite training and a learning period, the appellant cannot perform under established criteria. See Lofton v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 91, 92 (1982) (oral counseling was sufficient; there was no regulatory requirement of written counseling); Frisby v. IRS, 11 MSPR 15, 18 (1982) (appellant could not be reassigned elsewhere, as no vacancies existed). A lack of cautions or training and an unblemished prior record of 14 years of service suggested that the agency was remiss in failing to apply progressive discipline. Removal of an aviation safety inspector was reduced to a demotion to the next lower nonsupervisory position. Drake v. Dept. of Transp., 28 MSPR 253, 255 (1985); cf. Wright v. FAA, 40 MSPR 355, 358, 362-63 (1989) (citing Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 42-45 (1987), suggesting that lack of training is not a prohibited practice but it can be a mitigating factor; noting, however, the unique circumstances of an air traffic controllers' developmental program requirements). The alleged failure to train will, however, be discounted as a mitigating factor if the appellant has declined the offered training or failed to request specific training. See Bryant v. Dept. of Army, 84 MSPR 202, 209 ¶ 17 (1999).

Yet it was broadly stated that in a Chapter 75 case, the agency is not obligated to provide training before removing an employee for unsatisfactory performance. Thompson v. Dept. of Treasury, 16 MSPR 294, 298 (1983). Willis v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 9, 12 n.2 (1991), declared that "there is no law, rule, or regulation that requires an agency to afford an employee training prior to removal for performance deficiencies." Failure to provide training to remedy performance deficiencies is not a prohibited personnel practice. It is a merit system principle that the work force be provided adequate training, but no law, rule, or regulation implementing this merit system principle is violated when the agency fails to provide proper training. 5 USC 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)]; 5 USC 2301(b)(6), (7); Cho v. Dwight David Eisenhower Army Med. Ctr., 8 MSPR 52, 57-58, 7 MSPB 548 (1981); see Thompson v. Dept. of Treasury, 16 MSPR 294, 298 (1983) ("[The] Board has explicitly rejected the contention that an agency is obligated to provide training before removing an employee for unsatisfactory performance."); Bruner v. USDA, 9 MSPR 93, 95 & n.2 (1981) (noting the analysis of the merit system principle and also noting that there was no evidence establishing that the appellant was improperly trained or supervised). But see Wright v. FAA, 40 MSPR 355, 361 (1989), under some circumstances, the failure to train may be a violation of a merit system principle and a prohibited personnel practice [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Failure to Complete a Training Course; Validity of Examination"]). [Refer to Chapter 13, "Failure to Provide Effective Training; 5 USC 2301(b)(7); Training for Government Personnel on Whistleblowing Protections" (statute requiring that "[e]mployees should be provided effective education and training in cases in which such education and training would result in better organizational and individual performance)."]

One issue concerning training is that of discrimination and disparate treatment. If an employee is removed under Chapter 75 and argues that he performed poorly because he was inadequately trained, he may compare the training that he received with the training provided to similarly situated employees. See Savage v. USPS, 5 MSPR 492, 494, 5 MSPB 490 (1981). Yet, training is discretionary. Discrimination as to training depends on a showing of discriminatory purpose. 5 USC 2302(b)(6); Price v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 161, 163, 165 (1982) ("The Board has explicitly rejected the contention that...an agency is obligated to provide training before removing an employee for unsatisfactory performance." Training for a comparator employee in an office was not basic or remedial but was for the purpose of expanding her skills and knowledge.).
