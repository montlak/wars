2. Settlement Offer

Public policy encourages settlements. Negotiations leading to settlements often include express or implied admissions of some degree of wrongdoing by one or both parties. If these concessions were later used as evidence, they would not likely be made and fewer settlements would be reached. Public policy dictates that settlement offers and the discussions concerning them cannot be used in evidence. The Board follows this policy. It will not consider an offer of settlement as evidence, although the internal guidance of the Board found in the Hearings Procedures Guide at 26 is a bit cryptic: because "an Agency's settlement offer is not admissible into evidence the Board looks to Fed. R. Evid. 408 for guidance on matters of settlement offers." (The Hearings Procedures Guide has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook, but it still remains a valuable source of guidance and is used by the Board for training and orientation.) See In re MSTG, Inc., 675 F.3d 1337 (Fed. Cir. 2012) (patent litigation: considering the scope of privilege against disclosure of settlement discussions).

Speaking of the rule of evidence on settlements, Rule 408 of the Federal Rules of Evidence states:

(a) Prohibited Uses. Evidence of the following is not admissible--on behalf of any party--either to prove or disprove the validity or amount of a disputed claim or to impeach by a prior inconsistent statement or a contradiction:

(1) furnishing, promising, or offering--or accepting, promising to accept, or offering to accept--a valuable consideration in compromising or attempting to compromise the claim; and

(2) conduct or a statement made during compromise negotiations about the claim--except when offered in a criminal case and when the negotiations related to a claim by a public office in the exercise of its regulatory, investigative, or enforcement authority.

(b) Exceptions. The court may admit this evidence for another purpose, such as proving a witness's bias or prejudice, negating a contention of undue delay, or proving an effort to obstruct a criminal investigation or prosecution.

An agency offer of settlement is entitled to no weight in determining a penalty. Williams v. DHHS, 29 MSPR 525, 528 (1985). Cheng v. USDA, 84 MSPR 144, 146-47 ¶ 5 (1999), stated:

In finding that removal was within the tolerable bounds of reasonableness, the administrative judge specifically observed that the appellant was offered an opportunity to remain an agency employee if she would seek medical treatment or at least demonstrate that she did not need it, and that she refused. The Board has held that compromise offers and negotiations cannot be used as evidence of liability, Hayden v. U.S. Postal Service, 15 M.S.P.R. 296, 302 (1983), aff'd, 758 F.2d 668 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (Table), and that an agency's offer of settlement is entitled to no weight in considering the propriety of mitigation of the agency-imposed penalty. Mills v. Department of the Navy, 30 M.S.P.R. 403, 407 (1986). Here, the agency's offer was part of a proposed settlement agreement, which would have also required the appellant to waive future Board appeal rights. Thus, the administrative judge erred in considering the appellant's rejection of it. See Pugh v. U.S. Postal Service, 81 M.S.P.R. 313 (1999) (finding that the administrative judge improperly relied on the appellant's refusal to accept the agency's settlement to determine the extent of the agency's liability in restoration case)....

Hayden v. USPS, 15 MSPR 296, 302 (1983), refused to consider, in support of appellant's argument that removal did not promote service efficiency, an offer the agency made to the appellant to reinstate him without back pay if he waived his appeal rights:

Appellant argues that the fact that the agency offered to settle the action by reinstating appellant if he would waive his appeal rights and back pay demonstrates that removal does not promote the efficiency of the service. It is not inappropriate for an agency to make an offer of settlement. See Asberry v. United States Postal Service, 692 F.2d 1378, 1381-1382 (Fed. Cir. 1982). Moreover, it is generally recognized that the use of compromise offers and negotiations as evidence is barred. See Federal Rules of Evidence, Rule 408; United States v. Tuschman, 405 F.2d 688, 690 (6th Cir. 1969); United States v. Shotwell Mfg. Co., 287 F.2d 667, 673 (7th Cir. 1961); Cooper v. Brown, 126 F.2d 874, 878-9 (3rd Cir. 1942). Although the Federal Rules of Evidence, which specifically bar the use of such evidence, do not apply to Board proceedings, the Board will look to them for guidance. See Remy v. Veterans Administration, 7 MSPB 624, 629 (1981). Thus, we conclude that the agency's offer of settlement is entitled to no weight in determining whether removal is appropriate.

See Guirguess v. USPS, 32 Fed. Appx. 555, 563 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP) ("[B]ecause the proposed settlement is not admissible for the purpose of determining whether the agency properly removed Guirguess, the Board did not err by not considering it.").

Pugh v. USPS, 81 MSPR 313, 323 ¶ 17 (1999), explained that an appellant's rejection of a settlement offer may not be considered as evidence in abatement of the agency's liability:

...The administrative judge improperly relied on the appellant's refusal to accept the agency's settlement offer to determine the extent of the agency's liability. See Special Counsel v. Costello, 75 MSPR 562, 609 (1997) (under Rule 408 of the Federal Rules, an unaccepted settlement offer is ordinarily not admissible evidence to establish the existence or extent of liability); Hayden v. U.S. Postal Service, 15 MSPR 296, 302 (1983) (although Board proceedings are not bound by the Federal Rules of Evidence, the Board looks to these rules for guidance), aff'd, 758 F.2d 668 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (Table)....

But cf. Spahn v. Dept. of Justice, 93 MSPR 195 (2003) (under Title VII, the Board considered whether the appellant demonstrated dissimilar treatment through evidence of settlements reached with other employees involved in substantially similar misconduct).

The Board declined to consider evidence of the agency's treatment of a settlement offer in a corrective action case prosecuted by the Special Counsel. "[i]f the Board were to permit OSC to charge respondents with an additional violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(8) using evidence that respondents rejected an offer of settlement, future litigants might well be unwilling to engage in settlement discussions with OSC." Special Counsel v. Costello, 75 MSPR 562, 609 (1997); see Wheeler v. DVA, 88 MSPR 236, 241 ¶ 13 (2001) (an agreement for corrective action between the agency and the OSC did not prove the appellant's allegation in an IRA appeal that the agency committed abuse of authority); cf. Graham v. USPS, 4 MSPR 47, 49, 4 MSPB 137 (1980) ("Testimony as to what occurred in an informal stage of an agency grievance proceeding is not relevant to the question of either the legality of the agency's action that is the subject of the grievance, or the agency's ultimate disposition of the grievance at its final stage.").

Tarr v. Dept. of Transp., DC-0752-10-0910-I-1 (NP 4/19/2012), although determining that a settlement offer could not be used as evidence to support a charge, suggested the settlement offer was evidence of remorse as a mitigating factor:

The only document in the record that includes any estimated calculation of the appellant's unauthorized use is the written admission/agreement that the appellant drafted pursuant to settlement discussions. This document reflects that the appellant offered to repay the agency $5,000 as part of a potential settlement. The agency cannot rely upon statements provided by the appellant during settlement discussions as a basis for imposing discipline.[6] See Frank v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 90 M.S.P.R. 458, ¶ 4 n.2 (2001).

[6] Furthermore, the amount the appellant proposed to repay as part of a potential settlement further supports the finding that he was remorseful and willing to accept responsibility for his actions.

An agency's settlement offer does not operate as an estoppel precluding the agency from later arguing the propriety of its actions should the settlement efforts fail. Martin v. Dept. of Educ. (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-811). The Board seems disinclined to accept the argument that an appellant is a victim of reprisal for the exercise of an appeal right based on an agency's offer to reduce the severity of an adverse action if the appellant does not appeal to the Board. The offer is taken as a settlement overture. See Marler v. VA, 58 MSPR 116, 122-23 (1993). The Board considers evidence of settlement discussions to be inadmissible on the merits of a case. See Frank v. EEOC, 90 MSPR 458, 460 n.2 (2001) (the Board declines to consider statements of the appellant's counsel made during the course of settlement discussions); Spates v. USPS, 68 MSPR 9, 12 (1995).
