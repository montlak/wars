e. Past Discipline Reversed After Initial Decision

There is little law on the effect of reversal of past discipline on a currently sustained charge. In Lindo v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 90-3207), removal was supported by a prior letter of warning, a seven-day suspension, and a 14-day suspension. The initial decision sustaining the removal was issued on August 22, 1989, and a PFR from that decision was denied on February 6, 1990. In the interim, on December 4, 1989, an arbitrator concluded that the seven-day suspension was not for good cause. The Board was unaware of the arbitrator's decision. On June 4, 1990, the 14-day suspension was reduced to seven days. The court remanded the appeal to the Board to allow it to reconsider whether the removal should be mitigated in light of the two arbitration decisions. The court noted but did not expressly hold that the proper procedure would have been for the appellant to request the Board to reopen and reconsider its decision pursuant to 5 CFR 1201.117 (1990) (currently 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022)). On remand from the court, the Board sent the case back to its regional office for reconsideration. Lindo v. USPS, 43 MSPR 316 (1990) (Table); see Singleton v. VA, 62 MSPR 424, 427 (1994) (when a settlement required the agency to expunge some records of past discipline from the OPF, the agency was then required to reconsider a past penalty, a suspension, without regard to the expunged penalty, a reprimand); cf. DHHS, SSA and AFGE Local 2369, 23 FLRA 304 (1986) (FLRA remand to agency to reconsider penalty partially based on a past reprimand that was set aside by an arbitrator after the employee was suspended).

Merten v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3284), also considered the problem of agency reliance on past discipline that was vacated. The court sent the case back to the Board with the admonition that the error would compel complete reevaluation of the penalty by the agency rather than assessment by the Board of whether the penalty was reasonable under all the circumstances:

Mr. Merten's theory is that an otherwise neutral decision (in this case, the agency decision to remove him) may be tainted by a recommending official's error. In particular, Mr. Merten points to the recommendation of his acting supervisor (Ms. Alice Ortner) that he be removed. That recommendation lists a prior discipline on "11/29/95--letter of warning." Ms. Ortner may have been unaware that the prior discipline had been expunged by an arbitrator. Mr. Merten contends that the expunged prior discipline concerned his general lack of availability to serve working routes.

Ms. Ortner's recommendation as acting supervisor went to the agency's recommending official, who was the actual supervisor, Jane Dietrich. She endorsed Ms. Ortner's recommendation. The reviewing official, Mrs. Davis, also endorsed the recommendation. She testified that she considered Mr. Merten a marginal employee, in part because of his limited availability. Finally, the deciding official, Postmaster Ron Schilling, endorsed and implemented the recommendation of removal.

Mr. Merten proffers a "cat's paw" view of what occurred regarding the reason for his removal. He asserts, correctly, that he could not be cited for a prior disciplinary action, since none existed. Nevertheless, the initial recommendation cited a prior disciplinary action as support for the removal recommendation. This erroneous cat's paw, according to Mr. Merten, underlies the up-the-chain endorsement decisions, affirming the original recommendation. Were it so, that those above Ms. Ortner simply rubber-stamped the initial, tainted recommendation without independent review of the facts, a prima facie case of harmful procedural error would surely be made.

The Board, however, did not assess the evidence in the light of the alleged cat's paw theory. Instead, the Board dismissed this claim of alleged harmful procedural error, stating that "appellant's claims go to the appropriateness of the penalty rather than to any failure to follow required procedures."

The Board's view of Mr. Merten's claim of harmful procedural error is legally incorrect. If Mr. Merten is correct on the facts, a matter on which we express no opinion, then the agency's decision to remove him cannot stand, because it would be infected with the harmful error of a false allegation of prior discipline. Such an error has nothing to do with whether removal, or some lesser penalty, is warranted under all the Douglas circumstances. Instead, the error, if proven by the facts, would compel the agency to retreat to square one, to decide as a matter of first impression whether the facts concerning Mr. Merten's accident--a single incident for the occasion of discipline--warrant a recommendation and decision of removal.

For the foregoing reasons, we conclude that the Board acted arbitrarily in discarding Mr. Merten's claim on the ground that it could be fully rebutted as part of the "reasonable penalty" determination. Mr. Merten is entitled to have his harmful error claim adjudicated on its own bottom, as a challenge to the Postmaster's removal decision. To the extent that further testimony is required to afford Mr. Merten a full and fair hearing on this claim, we expect the Board to afford the requisite opportunity for such further testimony.
