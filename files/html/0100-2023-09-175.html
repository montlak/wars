broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section H: Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals, Subsection 06: Waiver of Appeal Through Last Chance or Other Preappeal Agreement, Part o: Discrimination, Reprisal
o. Discrimination, Reprisal

Although an LCA will waive prior EEO claims or defenses, after the LCA is in place and invoked, the appellant may assert, and the Board will adjudicate, assertions that the LCA was administered in a discriminatory or retaliatory manner. See Whittaker v. VA, 695 Fed. Appx. 557, 560 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP) ("Ms. Whittaker alleges that the VA removed her 'because [she]filed an [Equal Employment Opportunity Commission ("EEOC")] case' against her VA supervisor. 'Because it is an implied term of every agreement that each party will act in good faith..., a party may breach an agreement by acting in bad faith,' which includes retaliation by a supervisor. Posey v. Dep't of Def., 180 Fed. Appx. 931, 935 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (citation omitted)."); Posey v. DOD, 180 Fed. Appx. 931 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) ("Mr. Posey could overcome the waiver of appeal rights in the LCA if he established before the Board that DeCA breached the agreement. Link v. Dep't of Treasury, 51 F.3d 1577, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Because it is an implied term of every agreement that each party will act in good faith toward the other, a party may breach an agreement by acting in bad faith. Id. Obviously, if Mr. Posey's supervisors retaliated against him while he was subject to the LCA, they acted in bad faith, and DeCA thereby breached the agreement. For these reasons, the AJ erred in ruling that he lacked jurisdiction to consider this part of Mr. Posey's appeal."); Barber v. USPS, CH-0752-10-0277-I-1 (NP 12/17/2010) ("The LCA also provided that the penalty for any violation of the LCA would be removal, that there would be no mitigation of the penalty, and that any subsequent appeal would be limited solely to the question of whether the appellant violated the LCA....Here, the Board is not foreclosed from considering the merits of the charges--e.g., did the appellant violate the LCA. Therefore, the Board may also consider the appellant's affirmative defense of racial discrimination. See generally Martin, 70 M.S.P.R. at 657 (a complete waiver of Board appeal rights to challenge the merits of the underlying action necessarily deprives the Board of jurisdiction to consider an appellant's affirmative defenses)."); Jackson v. DOJ, 96 MSPR 498, 503 ¶ 14 (2004) ("We also agree with the AJ's finding that the appellant failed to prove his affirmative defense of race discrimination. The appellant provided no evidence that other employees who violated a LCSA or committed misconduct similar to the appellant's misconduct received favorable treatment. In fact, the parties stipulated that no employees had been disciplined for refusal to work mandatory overtime since at least 1995. The appellant's mere disagreement with the AJ's findings does not warrant full review of the record by the Board. Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 M.S.P.R. 129, 133-34 (1980)."); cf. McCall v. USPS, 839 F.2d 664, 667 (Fed. Cir. 1988) ("There is nothing, McCall tells us, to prevent management from acting in bad faith, abusing its discretion, engaging in prohibited personnel practices or discrimination, or in any other way violating employees' substantive rights. We agree that the term 'acceptable to management' does introduce an element of subjectivity into the agreement, but this does not mean that agencies would have a free hand to take arbitrary action against employees. We think it is implicit in the agreement here that the agency must abide by it in good faith. Thus, the agreement itself serves as a check on arbitrary agency action. If an agency acts in bad faith or takes other arbitrary and capricious action, as a breaching party it would not be able to enforce the agreement.").

If discrimination is not an affirmative defense to the agency's use of an LCA to take action against the appellant, the Board will not adjudicate an independent EEO claim raised in the appeal from the otherwise valid LCA-based removal. See Smitherman v. DLA, 56 MSPR 626, 628 (1993) (the AJ should not have evaluated a claim of disability discrimination based on the appellant's post traumatic stress syndrome, learned of by the agency "after the removal action."); cf. Johnson v. VA, PH-0752-17-0017-I-1 (NP 3/17/2023) ("The appellant also argues that she was removed because of her race. This allegation, however, is irrelevant to the dispositive issue, i.e., whether the Board may exercise jurisdiction over the removal appeal notwithstanding the appellant's waiver of her appeal rights in the LCA. See Easterling, 110 M.S.P.R. 41, ¶ 12. Moreover, absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board lacks an independent source of jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's discrimination claim. See Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982).").

The Board implies into the "good faith" administration of an LCA the absence of whistleblower reprisal. See Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 621, 626-27 (1993) ("In concluding that there was no basis for the appellant's claims of reprisal for whistleblowing and general animosity towards him, the AJ found, in effect, that the appellant failed to make a showing that the agency had acted in bad faith in enforcing the agreement against him."); see Hamlin v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0752-15-0696-I-1 (NP 7/15/2016) (alleged whistleblower reprisal: "Here, although the appellant has alleged that the agency was motivated by retaliatory animus when it removed him pursuant to the LCA, he has not alleged that the agency's purportedly retaliatory actions amounted to an unjustified deprivation of his rights. Specifically, as discussed above, the appellant does not dispute the administrative judge's finding, with which we agree, that he violated the LCA by exhibiting unacceptable conduct and performance on two occasions during the last chance period. Because the appellant violated the LCA, the agency was justified in removing him pursuant to the LCA and it did not act in bad faith by removing him pursuant to the LCA.").

Boland v. VA, CH-0752-11-0537-I-1 (NP 3/4/2014), discounted whistleblowing claims raised to deflect action based on an LCA violation:

Even assuming that the nurse only forwarded the appellant's emails to agency management in reprisal for his report about her, and that the nurse's action started the chain of events that led to the agency's review of these emails and his removal, the appellant has failed to allege facts that could show that the agency acted in bad faith in effectuating the last-chance agreement based on the inappropriate emails that came to light during that process. See Clark v. Department of Health & Human Services, 21 M.S.P.R. 348, 351 (1984) ("Appellant's final legal argument, that the action must be reversed because the agency's decision to investigate was discriminatory, is without merit. The agency's reasons for conducting the investigation which led to appellant's removal are outside the scope of this proceeding."); see also Carr v. Social Security Administration, 185 F.3d 1318, 1324-26 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (finding that the Board was not required to account for the petitioner's allegations regarding the alleged retaliatory motive of the persons whose complaints initiated an inquiry that brought the petitioner's misconduct to light, when the decision makers themselves were not improperly motivated; to hold otherwise would improperly allow the employee to use the Whistleblower Protection Act as a shield to protect her own misconduct). For instance, there is no allegation, or evidence in the record suggesting, that the nurse had any influence over or role in the administrator's decision-making, see Carr, 185 F.3d at 1325-26, or that the agency conducted a deficient investigation into the appellant's conduct, cf., e.g., Social Security Administration v. Carr, 78 M.S.P.R. 313, 335 (1998) ("If an agency fails to investigate a charge sufficiently before bringing an action, such a failure might indicate an improper motive."), aff'd, 185 F.3d 1318 (Fed. Cir. 1999).

See Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 621, 626-27 (1993) ("In concluding that there was no basis for the appellant's claims of reprisal for whistleblowing and general animosity towards him, the judge found, in effect, that the appellant failed to make a showing that the agency had acted in bad faith in enforcing the agreement against him.").
