b. Time Spent on PFR

The Board attempted to deal with the problem of the PFR from an initial decision awarding fees that consumed disproportionately large amounts of legal time in comparison to the monetary gain achieved. In Stein v. USPS, 65 MSPR 685, 688-91 (1994), the PFR resulted in a minor upward adjustment of the fees awarded by the AJ. Counsel then sought from the AJ the full amount of fees associated with litigation of the PFR. The AJ could not determine from the documentation how many hours were spent on unsuccessful components of the PFR. The AJ's solution was to award fees based on the percentage of the additional fees recovered through the PFR compared with the fees claimed. If the amount at issue in the PFR was $5,000, the amount awarded was $1,000, and the amount spent litigating the PFR was $4,000, the resulting award for the work on the PFR would be 20%, or $800. The Board agreed with the AJ's approach, with the exception of its conclusion that the AJ should have asked appellant's counsel for additional information to permit identification of the number of hours spent on unsuccessful components of the PFR before the AJ employed the percentage allocation methodology. The Board explained the appropriate methodology: an award may be reduced either by identifying the hours associated with unsuccessful claims or by reducing the award to account for limited success. If the former route is chosen, the AJ should attempt to identify the precise number of hours to disallow for the unsuccessful claims:

The Court has held that once a plaintiff meets the threshold requirement of being a prevailing party, the extent to which he prevailed is the most critical factor to consider in determining reasonable attorney fees. It has concluded that a trial court, in fixing fees, is obligated to give primary consideration to the amount of damages awarded as compared to the amount sought, where recovery of monetary damages is the purpose of the litigation. Depending on the circumstances of the case, such a comparison may properly result in the award of low fees or no fees at all. See Farrar, 113 S. Ct. at 574-75. Relying on this precedent, the Board has held that it will consider whether a fee award should be reduced because the relief ordered was significantly less than what the appellant sought. See Smit v. Department of the Treasury, 61 MSPR 612, 619 (1994).

On petition for review of the September 24, 1993, addendum initial decision, the appellant sought reimbursement for the expenses incurred in pursuing the two petitions for enforcement ($13,217.03) and for the expenses deemed inflated by the administrative judge ($357.54). The Board rejected his claim regarding the former set of expenses, but he prevailed with respect to the latter. See Stein II, 63 MSPR at 540-44. The relief ordered thus was significantly less than what the appellant sought, and the administrative judge properly found that a reduction in the fee award was warranted. See Farrar, 113 S. Ct. at 574-75; Smit, 61 MSPR at 619.

Such an award may be reduced either by identifying the hours associated with unsuccessful claims or by simply reducing it to account for the limited success. See Smit, 61 MSPR at 619. In making such a reduction, an administrative judge should attempt to identify the precise number of hours to disallow for the unsuccessful claims, after providing the parties an opportunity to supplement the record; if that attempt fails, the administrative judge reduces the award in an otherwise appropriate manner to account for the limited success. See id. at 619-20. More generally, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has held that, before an administrative judge decides on his own to reject claims in an attorney fee application, he should communicate his concerns to the appellant and allow him an opportunity to correct the specific deficiencies identified. See Wilson v. Department of Health and Human Services, 834 F.2d 1011, 1012 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

As noted above, however, the administrative judge did not communicate his doubts regarding the adequacy of the billing information submitted, nor did he allow the parties to supplement the record in order to identify the hours spent on the appellant's unsuccessful claim for fees incurred in litigating the two petitions for enforcement. Instead, he stated in his June 15, 1994, addendum initial decision that such a determination could not be made based on the record before him and then set forth his own method for reducing the award. See Stein IV, slip op. at 3-4. This was error. See Wilson, 834 F.2d at 1012; Smit, 61 MSPR at 619-20. Remand of the fee motion is appropriate here, moreover, because the existing record is not specific enough for us to identify precisely the hours associated with the unsuccessful claim. The administrative judge shall make that determination after allowing the parties to supplement the record regarding this issue; if a precise number of hours spent on the unsuccessful claim cannot be identified after the parties have supplemented the record, the administrative judge then may reduce the fee award by an otherwise appropriate amount. See Smit, 61 MSPR at 619-20.

As noted above, the appellant has filed two supplemental requests for attorney fees for time spent litigating the instant petition for review. Because we are unable at this time to determine the extent to which the appellant will prevail on the basis of this petition for review, a ruling on his entitlement to an award of attorney fees for the time spent on the petition must await the outcome of the case on remand. See Hand v. Department of the Army, 56 MSPR 334, 338-39 (1993). Therefore, after reducing the amount of the fee award in the manner described above, the administrative judge shall then evaluate the degree of success obtained by the instant petition for review and determine whether or not the amount sought in the supplemental requests needs to be reduced. If it does need to be reduced, the administrative judge shall apply the procedure set forth in Smit, 61 MSPR at 619-20, for reducing the amount requested.
