f. OWCP Finding; Social Security Determination

Decisions of OWCP do not ordinarily bind the Board, although they may constitute persuasive guidance. Brewer v. OPM, 75 MSPR 163, 168-69 (1997). "While not binding, OWCP decisions constitute evidence that merits consideration." Camenisch-Felts v. USDA, 47 MSPR 493, 496 (1991); see Daniels v. USPS, 57 MSPR 272, 279 (1993) ("OWCP's determination as to whether an employee is entitled to FECA benefits does not preclude the Board from addressing the specific issue of whether an agency has sustained a charge of falsification."); Chamberlain v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 626, 634 n.4 (1991) ("Although the law provides that OWCP's determination that an employee is entitled to benefits for a compensable injury or disability is final, the Board may still hear and decide collateral matters not expressly decided by OWCP.... In this case, one such matter not directly decided by OWCP was whether the appellant's compensable injury was responsible for his resignation."); Bahm v. Dept. of Air Force, 38 MSPR 627, 631 (1988) (employer was not bound by the OWCP result in taking action against an employee on the charge of obstructing a medical exam to obtain OWCP benefits). [For extensive treatment of the workers' compensation system, refer to Eleanor Laws' Federal Sector Workers' Compensation (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2022).]

According to the Federal Circuit, an "OWCP decision is not the result of an adversarial adjudication and, in any event, does not bind the Board." Cox v. MSPB, 817 F.2d 100, 101 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (citing Miller v. USPS, 26 MSPR 210, 213 (1985)). Cox involved a dispute over the nature of the employee's termination (by reason of misconduct or disability). The employee was discharged for participation in the air traffic controllers' strike, but he sought to show through an OWCP determination that he had a compensable injury during the work stoppage. The Board determined that the employee was not ill during the strike. OWCP later decided, apparently, that he was, and the court's judgment essentially concluded that the Board did not have to provide the OWCP finding preclusive effect. The scope of the decision, although broadly stated, must be considered rather limited given the peculiar nature of the facts. OWCP findings are the result of investigation and careful review (of evidence both from the appellant and agency) by the Department of Labor.

As to the legally, as opposed to factually, preclusive nature of some OWCP findings, if OWCP concludes that an appellant is entitled to worker's compensation benefits, the employing agency cannot challenge appellant's restoration rights by seeking to prove that the underlying injury or illness was not work-related. See Gavette v. Dept. of Treasury, 44 MSPR 166, 175 (1990). If OWCP determines that a position is suitable for an employee who is being restored to the workplace, MSPB will not review OWCP's suitability determination. See McLain v. USPS, 82 MSPR 526, 530 ¶ 9 (1999) (an OWCP finding, in an enforced leave case, of whether an agency provided employment within the employee's limitations, was conclusive); Miller v. USPS, 82 MSPR 170, 174 ¶ 8 (1999) (citing 29 CFR 10.124 (1999)). [Refer to Chapter 8, "No Foreseeable End to Absence; Absence When Compensation Ends," for additional cases analyzing the significance of OWCP determinations.]

An OWCP determination that an injury is work-related does not resolve whether the agency offered or could offer sufficient accommodation to an employee who appeals an adverse action, Kirkland v. DHS, 119 MSPR 74, 83-84 ¶¶ 16-17, 2013 MSPB 3 (2013) (involving review of an arbitrator's award):

Furthermore, to the extent that OWCP's September 13, 2011 decision should now be considered as new and material evidence in support of the appellant's discrimination claims, we find that it is not of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from the arbitrator's decision. See Russo v. Veterans Administration, 3 M.S.P.R. 345, 349 (1980). Before the arbitrator, the agency did not dispute the appellant's evidence that she had been diagnosed with bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome, and the arbitrator found that the appellant had this medical condition in his analysis of the evidence. The primary issue in dispute concerned whether the agency had provided sufficient reasonable accommodation for this condition to allow her to successfully perform her duties requiring keyboarding on a computer. In resolving this factual conflict, the arbitrator made credibility determinations that the appellant had overstated her medical restrictions and the volume, complexity, and amount of keyboarding required for her WebTELE duties. Specifically, the arbitrator found that the appellant was not credible because evidence showed that she regularly performed other computer activities that required the same abilities as her computer duties, and, therefore, he concluded that she was not truthful at hearing when she described her physical capabilities and limitations.

The new evidence presented by the appellant on review shows that the OWCP has now determined that the appellant's carpal tunnel syndrome is a work-related injury, which was not disputed below. More importantly, OWCP's decision does not prove the appellant's medical condition was of such severity that she was disabled from working during the timeframes relevant to the charges. An OWCP determination that an employee is entitled to receive medical benefits for a work-related injury does not necessarily mean that the Department of Labor has found that the employee is disabled and cannot perform gainful employment. See 20 C.F.R. §§ 10.0(b); 10.5(f); 10.310(a); 10.400(b). Therefore, this new evidence is insufficient to materially affect the arbitrator's factual and credibility determination that the appellant was more capable at performing computer keyboarding than she contended and that the accommodations provided by the agency for the appellant's medical conditions were reasonable accommodations and sufficient to allow her to perform the essential functions of her position.

In a disability retirement appeal, the Board is not bound to a determination of the Social Security Administration granting the appellant disability insurance benefits under the Social Security Act. Trevan v. OPM, 69 F.3d 520 (Fed. Cir. 1995); see Royster v. OPM, 68 MSPR 655, 661 (1995) (citing Wilmot v. OPM, 35 MSPR 238, 240 (1987)). But the Board cannot ignore the SSA award, Pritchard v. OPM, 70 MSPR 453, 458-59 (1996):

In Trevan v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 F.3d 520 (Fed. Cir. 1995), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that, even though an award of social security benefits does not necessarily require that an applicant be granted a FERS disability retirement, the award of such benefits must be considered when determining FERS benefits entitlement:

In light of Congress' express intent to coordinate FERS and Social Security benefits, OPM and the Board must consider an award of Social Security disability benefits...in determining entitlement to FERS benefits.

Trevan, 69 F.3d at 526.
