C. ADMISSIONS

An admission is a statement by a party offered in evidence against that party: any statement by any party qualifies. The theory is that if a party states something that could adversely affect his interests, the statement will likely be true and will have probative value. The use of the admission is one-sided: the admission is offered against the maker.

An appellant's admission to a charge suffices as proof of the charge without additional proof from the agency. See Mascol v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 565, 567, 7 MSPB 408 (1981); see SSA v. Boini, CB-7521-13-0192-T-1 (NP 2/22/2016) ("The Board has held that an agency can establish its charge through evidence of an employee's admission of relevant facts supporting the charge. See Hoofman v. Department of the Army, 118 M.S.P.R. 532, ¶ 9 (2012), aff'd, 526 F. App'x 982 (Fed. Cir. 2013)."). An extrajudicial admission by an appellant is admissible in a Board proceeding. See Baldwin v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 107, 108 n.2 (1983) (statement made in a state unemployment benefit application). Statements made in answer to a Special Counsel complaint are admissions. See Special Counsel v. Winfield, 18 MSPR 402, 407 (1983). Admissions may be adoptive. Should an attorney for the appellant make an admission of substandard performance during the personal reply to a denial of a step increase, while the appellant is present and makes no effort then or later to deny the truth of the statement, the statement becomes an admission supporting the agency's case against the appellant. McCloskey v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 680, 682, 6 MSPB 570 (1981). If, during a hearing, the agency offers testimony through a management witness of what an employee's representative said, an objection should be made at the time the testimony is offered if there is a question as to whether the representative had the authority to speak for the employee. See Tesanovich v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3269) ("[i]t is well settled that a person is bound by the consequences of his attorney's conduct. Mr. Tesanovich cannot now disavow the letter, which he previously acknowledged was his response to the charges against him."). If a representative is clearly representing the union rather than the appellant, the adoptive admission rule does not apply. If an admission is ambiguous, the author of the admission should be permitted to testify concerning its meaning. See Halperin v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 89-3075). The statement of an agency representative, made during discovery, cannot bind the agency to a position contrary to the plain language of an agency regulation. See Moulding v. Dept. of Air Force, 52 MSPR 19, 22 (1991).

An admission is not established by the failure to deny charges, Barnett v. Dept. of Interior, 6 MSPR 4, 6, 6 MSPB 19 (1981):

This Board has previously held that the purpose of an advance notice is to apprise an employee of the charges against him/her and does not constitute evidence of the charges contained therein. Powell v. Department of Interior, 3 MSPB 35, 2 MSPR 513 (1980).

The fact that appellant failed to deny the charges in the advance notice is inconsequential. Appellant had no burden to disprove the charges against her and did not relieve the agency of its burden of proof by stipulating to the truth of the charges. 5 CFR § 1201.66. The agency was obligated to submit a well developed record to the presiding official in order to prove its case by a preponderance of the evidence in accordance with 5 USC § 7701(c)(1)(B). Powell, supra, 37, 2 MSPR 516. Therefore, the presiding official did not err in her determination that the agency did not support its action by a preponderance of the evidence.

See Gary v. VA, 19 MSPR 247, 250 (1984) (appellant did not admit the charges by initialing them). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Evidentiary Value of Charge."]

If the agency's case rests on appellant's admission of misconduct, and if the appellant denies making the admission, the appellant should be permitted to call witnesses to refute the evidence of the admission. Hockaday v. GSA, 9 MSPR 142, 145 (1981), remanded a case to the AJ to receive the testimony of witnesses, initially refused by the AJ, who were offered to provide evidence impeaching the agency investigator who testified that the appellant admitted the charged misconduct. Different rules concerning the failure to deny allegations may be applied in Hatch Act cases, OSC disciplinary cases, and cases against Administrative Law Judges, generally reviewed under the Administrative Procedure Act. Under 5 CFR 1201.124(c) (2022), unanswered allegations contain in a OSC disciplinary action complaint (including Hatch act allegations) may be considered admitted. In actions against ALJs, under 5 CFR 1201.139(c) (2022), unanswered allegations may be "considered true."

Discovery may provide admissions in the form of answers to questions during depositions, responses to interrogatories, and, yes, responses to requests for admissions. These materials, particularly responses to interrogatories and requests for admissions, are placed into the record as exhibits with the prehearing submission. Depositions, or portions of them, may also be included as exhibits with the prehearing submissions, or they can be used to elicit admissions during cross-examination at the hearing.
