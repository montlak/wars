3. Must Prevail Through MSPB Procedures

The Board, interpreting Cuthbertson v. MSPB, 784 F.2d 370, 372-73 (Fed. Cir. 1986), applies a two-factor test to determine prevailing party status: (1) Did the employee obtain all or a significant part of the relief he sought from the Board?; (2) Was the relief causally related to the initiation of the proceedings before the Board? Wade v. Dept. of Navy, 45 MSPR 635, 638 (1990) (agency timely began processing an award of counsel fees before appellant moved for compliance with the decision granting fees), partially overruled on other grounds, Whaley v. USPS, 61 MSPR 340, 344-47 (1994); cf. Curran v. Dept. of Treasury, 26 MSPR 306, 309 (1985) (court reversal of a Board decision favoring the agency would make appellant a prevailing party and, depending on the court's analysis and disposition, control fee entitlement analysis).

Whether relief might have been secured through means other than a Board appeal is immaterial to whether the relief was in fact secured as a result of a Board process. See Foley v. USPS, 59 MSPR 413, 418-19 (1993) (enforcement proceeding: Board discounted agency's arguments that appellant was not the prevailing party because the relief he sought could have been obtained by a phone call to agency counsel rather than through the initiation of enforcement proceedings, relying on Garstkiewicz v. USPS, 981 F.2d 528 (Fed. Cir. 1992))). But when an appellant was suspended pending the outcome of criminal proceedings against him, and was later acquitted on those charges and reinstated retroactively, he was not a prevailing party for the purposes of a fee award. The acquittal, not his appeal, led to reinstatement. Boyett v. Customs Serv., 14 MSPR 433, 436 (1983). To be a prevailing party, the party must prevail before the Board. One cannot, for example, ask the Board to award fees if the relief was obtained in district court. Stevens v. TVA (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-742); cf. Jones v. Dept. of Army, 590 Fed. Appx. 975, 978 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) (if counsel did not contribute in some manner to the appellant's ultimate success, the appellant is not the prevailing party for purposes of a fee award).

If the agency rescinds an adverse action that was appealed to the Board because a disability retirement application was granted retroactively by OPM, it cannot be said that the appellant prevailed through MSPB appeals procedures. Maestas v. SBA, 38 MSPR 354, 356 (1988). In Watts v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 18, 21 (1987), a final decision ordering the relief sought by the appellant, cancelation of his removal, was not rendered by the Board. The appellant's removal was canceled when OPM granted his disability retirement application. To obtain fees, the appellant's relief had to be causally related to the initiation of his appeal. Cancelation of the appellant's removal resulted from OPM's decision to grant his disability retirement application. The retirement process was entirely separate from both the agency's adverse action procedures and the appellant's Board appeal, meaning that the relief secured by appellant from OPM was not the result of a Board appeal and that Watts was not a prevailing party eligible for a fee award. [Refer later in this chapter to "Cancelation of Action Appealed."]

The standards for assessing "prevailing party" status under the CSRA and under the Whistleblower Protection Act are the same. Smit v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 612, 616 (1994); see Hamel v. President's Comm'n on Executive Exch., 987 F.2d 1561 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("Although the WPA and the CSRA attorney's fees provisions differ in some respects, they have in common the threshold requirement that there be an initial determination as to whether the person seeking the fees was a prevailing party in the proceedings before the Board."); Remaklus v. Dept. of Navy, 63 MSPR 443, 445-46 (1994) (IRA appellant did not prevail when the reassignment he appealed was canceled as a result of a reorganization; nothing in the record demonstrated that the reassignment was canceled because of appellant's initiation of MSPB proceedings). If partial relief is obtained for an IRA appellant when the whistleblowing complaint is being processed by OSC, a fee petition for the results obtained with OSC is not ripe for consideration by the Board until there is a final decision in the IRA case; settlement of some issues through OSC does not provide a right to a preliminary counsel fee award. See Mangano v. VA, 109 MSPR 658, 676-77 ¶ 45, 2008 MSPB 202 (2008).

If an individual prevails before the Board but fails to obtain a finding of discrimination, and if the EEO issue is pursued in federal court, a timely fee petition will be adjudicated by the Board as to the time invested in obtaining prevailing party status before the Board. The Board does not lose jurisdiction over the fee petition by reason of resort to court on the EEO claim. Matthews v. SSA, 104 MSPR 130, 133-35 ¶¶ 9-10, 2006 MSPB 325 (2006). Suppose an appellant bifurcates an appeal into a claim of discrimination processed under EEOC procedures and a challenge to the merits of the removal in a Board appeal and then goes on to win the EEO case and lose the Board appeal. The Board may determine that he is not entitled to fees because his reinstatement was not causally related to his MSPB appeal. Young v. MSPB, 776 F.2d 1027 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The Board may look only to its own decisional procedures. But if there is a link between MSPB procedures and another procedure that otherwise would not support a fee recovery, the result may be different. See Cuthbertson v. MSPB, 784 F.2d 370 (Fed. Cir. 1986).
