e. Occupational Groupings

Dept. of Air Force, Edwards AFB and Sport Air Traffic Controllers Org., 35 FLRA 1311, 1314 (1990), commented favorably on functional groupings:

...Authority precedent exists for finding appropriate a unit structured around a functional grouping of employees who possess characteristics and concerns limited to that group. See, for example, Department of the Navy, Naval Station, Norfolk, Virginia, 14 FLRA 702 (1984). Moreover, the Statute itself explicitly recognizes that units established on a functional basis may be appropriate. 5 U.S.C. § 7112(a)(1) [now 5 USC 7112(a)]. As to size of a unit, while the small size of a proposed unit may be one factor to be considered in determining appropriateness, it does not automatically disqualify a unit from being found appropriate.

See Dept. of Navy and IAF Local F-121, 60 FLRA 469, 473 (2004) (approving functional units in place of region-wide units following reorganization: "[t]he Authority has previously found functional units of employees appropriate, including as relevant here, units of firefighters and units of police officers, where the employees in such units shared a community of interest and where the units promoted effective dealings and efficiency of agency operations. See, e.g., Dep't of the Navy, Naval Station, Norfolk, Va., 14 FLRA 702, 704 (1984) (unit of firefighters); Panama Canal Comm'n, 5 FLRA 104, 115-16 (1981) (police division).").

A unit structured around the special interests of an occupational or craft grouping of employees can promote effective dealings and efficiency of agency operations. It allows the parties to respond in a more meaningful manner to a functional group of employees who possess characteristics and concerns limited to that group. Firefighters were granted status as a separate unit within a larger security division because their jobs were unique. There was little interchange with employees in other job classifications; they had a unique shift system. Dept. of Navy, Naval Station Norfolk, Va. and Fed. Fire Fighters Ass'n, Local 13, 14 FLRA 702, 704 (1984); see Panama Canal Comm'n, 5 FLRA 104, 114-15 (1981) (firefighter unit), supra. A consolidated unit was appropriate for firefighting personnel throughout the Air Force Logistics Command. All the employees were within the proposed unit, performed a unique job function, worked under common overall supervision at the AFLC level, and shared essentially similar job classifications and organization, uniform program direction, personnel policies, and labor relations practices. They had a clear and identifiable community of interest. Air Force Logistics Command and Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters, AFLC Council, 7 FLRA 210, 213 (1981).

FLRA reconfirmed the use of functional groupings for unit determinations when it remanded a case to the region to consider a unit of special police, Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing and Washington Plate Printers Union, Local 2, 49 FLRA 100, 108 (1994):

...Authority precedent exists for finding appropriate a unit structured around a functional grouping of employees who possess characteristics and concerns limited to that group. See, for example, Department of Navy, Naval Station, Norfolk, Virginia, 14 FLRA 702, 704 (1984) (the Authority found that a unit composed of firefighters was an appropriate unit, noting in this regard the employees' unique position descriptions, job responsibilities, special pay and retirement system, training, shift work, and work location). Moreover, the Statute itself explicitly recognizes that units established on a functional basis may be appropriate. See U.S. Department of Air Force, Edwards Air Force Base, California, 35 FLRA 1311, 1314 (1990) (the Authority affirmed a regional director's decision finding that a group of employees constituted an appropriate unit based upon their unique job classification, specialized and distinct occupational field, their limited interaction with other employees, different work hours, and different retirement provisions and physical examination requirements).

A unit of special police at WCF appears to be a unit structured around a functional grouping. However, the record in this case contains insufficient evidence bearing on the factors relied upon in the cited cases. Thus, we conclude that we cannot determine whether a unit composed of special police at WCF is appropriate.

See Dept. of Army, Hq., Ft. Dix and IBPO, NAGE, SEIU, 53 FLRA 287, 296 (1997) (suggesting that police officers may fall into the category of employees that justify separate bargaining units based on functional groupings). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Firefighters," concerning supervisory status of firefighters.]

FLRA declined to find appropriate units two Justice Department divisions, Antitrust and Civil Rights, consisting of attorneys who, it was concluded, were centrally managed for purposes of personnel administration and who were interchangeable with attorneys in other offices and divisions of the Department, making separate units inappropriate. Antitrust Div., DOJ and NTEU, 16 FLRA 297, 302 (1984). A regional unit of OPM investigators was not appropriate. The employees were not a functional group of employees who possessed characteristics, e.g., frequent travel, and concerns limited to that group. In OPM, the Investigations Division was not "uniquely structured around the 'special interests' of the Investigation Division employees." See OPM, Atlanta Reg'l Office and AFGE, 48 FLRA 1228, 1237 (1993).

FLRA declined to certify as an appropriate a unit of physicians working at an Army hospital. They were General Schedule employees, and although they had specific concerns unique to their profession, they worked under the direction of managers who were not physicians, they worked with other nonprofessionals, and there was no apparent disadvantage to the physicians if they were placed in a unit with other professionals. The creation of two small units of recognition would not foster effective dealings or efficiency of agency operations. Army Med. Dept. Activity, Redstone Arsenal and AFGE Local 1858, 14 FLRA 150, 152 (1984). A separate unit was denied for medical and dental officers who were functionally integrated with other professional employees in the activity and who shared essentially the same working conditions. The distinguishing factors, a slightly different salary scale, some unique professional legal liabilities, and a separate competitive area, were insufficient for the proposed unit to have a clear and identifiable community of interest separate and distinct from other professional employees. DHHS, Pub. Health Serv., FDA Bureau of Drugs and Medical-Dental Officers Soc'y of FDA, 11 FLRA 687, 687-88 (1983).

Although functional groupings are of significance, the "Authority has never held that appropriate units must include only employees who share functions or occupations." Dept. of Air Force, Lackland AFB and AFGE, 59 FLRA 739, 742 (2004).
