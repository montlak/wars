broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 09: Drug Use and Drug Detection, Section B: Negotiability Framework, Subsection 22: Union Representation; Observer; Lab Inspections

22. Union Representation; Observer; Lab Inspections

NFFE Forest Serv. Council and USDA, Forest Serv., 40 FLRA 174, 180 (1991), approved as negotiable Proposal 5, inviting agreement that:

Employees are entitled to Union representation during the collection of urine samples. The representative shall observe all actions of the collection site monitor. Employees shall receive written notice informing them of their right to union representation.

FLRA reasoned, 40 FLRA at 183-85:

The final Guidelines state that "[n]o unauthorized personnel shall be permitted in any part of the designated collection site when urine specimens are collected or stored."... The final Guidelines do not define the term "unauthorized."... [W]e construe the term "unauthorized" to refer to persons who have not been empowered or permitted by proper authority to be at the collection site.... In our view, the Agency, at a minimum, is such a "proper authority."...

By negotiating on this proposal, the Agency would be exercising its discretion under the final Guidelines to "empower" or "permit" persons to be present at the collection site during the collection of urine specimens.... The proposal is consistent with section 2.2(d) of the final Guidelines because, by requiring the Agency to exercise its discretion through negotiations, the proposal itself constitutes the authorization that would allow Union representatives to be present at the collection site to observe the actions of the collection site monitor....

...

...Proposal 5...would have no effect on the Agency's decision to conduct...tests and it would in no way be determinative of the manner in which the tests themselves are administered. The proposal would neither preclude nor restrict the Agency's ability to conduct random drug tests and it would not dictate to management the equipment or techniques used to conduct those tests....

[T]he presence of the Union representative as an "observer" would not in and of itself compromise the security of the testing process. In any event, other than requiring that the Union representative be an "observer," the proposal would not affect the Agency's decision as to the terms under which the Union representative would be present at the collection site. Consequently, the proposal would not prevent the Agency from imposing conditions on the presence of the Union representative to protect the integrity of the specimens or the testing process.

[T]he proposal constitutes a procedure within the meaning of section 7106(b)(2) of the Statute.... [T]o the extent that the proposal would apply to other forms of drug testing, for example, "reasonable suspicion" testing or post-accident testing, we find that, even assuming that those forms of testing constitute an exercise of management's right...to determine its internal security practices, the effect of the proposal on the conduct of those forms of testing would be no different from the effect on random testing....

NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, BATF, 41 FLRA 1106, 1114, 1133 (1991), declared negotiable a proposal, Provision 3, that "would allow a union representative to be present at the collection site when employees are undergoing urine testing, provided the collection facility allows the union representative's presence." NTEU approved as negotiable Provision 8, requiring "[n]otification that the employee is entitled to union representation at all stages of the testing procedure." NFFE Local 2015 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., 41 FLRA 1158, 1172, 1174-75 (1991), approved as negotiable Provision 3, pressing for entitlement to a union representative who "shall observe all actions of the collection site monitor." NFFE Local 2015 disapproved, however, as nonnegotiable a provision (Proposal 7) for up to four hours of official time to arrange for private testing to refute a MRO's finding of a positive test result.

AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 1661 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Danbury, 31 FLRA 95, 97-100 (1988), allowed as negotiable a proposal that gave the agency the option to permit a supervisor and a union representative to accompany an employee being tested to the collection site to verify that the sample was collected. See AFSCME Local 3097 and DOJ, Justice Mgmt. Div., 42 FLRA 412, 435 (1991) (negotiable: portion of Proposal 5 calling on management to give employee reasonable opportunity to contact union official prior to undergoing drug testing); NTEU and Dept. of Energy, 41 FLRA 1241, 1242-48 (1991) (no impediment to proposal that union representation be allowed at every stage where various activities associated with drug testing may be performed by contractors). NTEU and NRC, 43 FLRA 1279, 1308-09, 1314 (1992), determined to be negotiable a provision of Proposal 4 requiring the agency to inform employees subjected to reasonable suspicion drug testing that they may consult with the union before the test is administered. The union's explanation of the proposal stated that the contact with the union could be by telephone and that the test could continue if a union representative was not available within a reasonable period of time.

NFFE Forest Serv. Council and USDA, Forest Serv., 40 FLRA 174, 175-76 (1991), pronounced negotiable proposals that: if agency officials visit the lab for an inspection, the union will be entitled to designate an observer to attend the inspection; and the observer will be on official time and allowed travel and per diem. See NFFE Local 2015 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., 41 FLRA 1158, 1186, 1188-89 (1991) (approving as negotiable Proposal 9, allowing union to designate representative to attend inspections of laboratory). In AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council and DOJ, INS, 42 FLRA 599, 620-22, 625-37 (1991), a negotiable a provision permitted the union to be present, with 10 days' notice, when the agency ran its computer program to make random selections. Another negotiable provision permitted the union opportunities to inspect the lab used by the agency to perform drug testing analyses; the agency did not show how the inspections would interfere with its contractual relationship with the lab. A provision allowing the union to review the software developed by the agency to perform random selections was deferred for better development of the record. FLRA noted, however, in AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council that there would appear to be no risk to internal security "[i]f a verification program could be developed that would accomplish the objective required by the union and would not, by its use, affect or compromise the random selection process."
