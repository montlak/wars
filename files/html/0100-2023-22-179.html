9. Telephone or Facsimile

Telephone toll charges of counsel were awarded as a part of fees in SSA v. Balaban, 39 MSPR 622, 626 (1989), followed in Garcia v. USPS, 75 MSPR 198, 201 (1997), and Garcia v. USPS, 78 MSPR 425, 427 (1998) (awarding long-distance telephone charges and rejecting the AJ's analysis that toll calls were an unreimbursable part of the law firm's overhead). The Board added facsimile transmission costs to the group of compensable expenses. Russell v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 157, 163 (1989), modified in part on other grounds, Garstkiewicz v. USPS, 50 MSPR 476, 478-79 (1991). The Board does not seem inclined to become embroiled in disputes over the necessity of toll charges for faxes that duplicate service by mail, as long as the amount spent on tolls is not unreasonable. See Stein v. USPS, 63 MSPR 537, 542 (1994).

Although the Board allows reimbursement, through fee awards, of toll charges for calls and fax transmissions, it does not permit counsel fee awards to subsidize the costs of the equipment used to make those transmissions, Garcia v. USPS, 75 MSPR 198, 202 (1997):

In addition, the appellant claims that he is entitled to $110.00 for facsimile costs. The Board has the authority to award monies for the costs incurred by an appellant's counsel for reasonable and necessary long-distance facsimile transmissions. See Russell, 43 MSPR at 163. The record evidence, however, does not show that facsimile costs were incurred by the appellant's counsel. Rather, the documentation shows only that the appellant's counsel charged a fixed price for the receipt ($1.00 per page) and sending ($1.50 per page) of facsimiles. Such charges are not an out-of-pocket expense. The "cost" of receiving a facsimile (wear and tear on the facsimile machine and paper) is a part of a firm's overhead, and is not recoverable under section 7701(g)(1), regardless of whether the firm normally charges its clients for receipt of facsimiles. Sending a facsimile via a long-distance telephone call would result in an out-of-pocket expense, but in this case there is no evidence that such expenses were actually incurred, or if they were, what the expenses were. Given that the Board must approach any allowance of expenses with caution and provide for them prudently and with careful respect to the applicable statute, we deny the appellant's request for counsel's facsimile charges....
