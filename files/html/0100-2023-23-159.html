b. Effect of Retirement

An opaque opinion suggested that mandatory retirement at the time of the effective date of a removal decision that is reversed by the AJ may limit the propriety of restoration of the status quo, Morrison v. Dept. of Navy, PH-0752-14-0669-B-1 (NP 6/28/2016):

The agency has argued that the administrative judge erred by ordering that the appellant be restored to the status quo ante. In particular, the agency notes that the appellant is now older than the mandatory retirement age for a firefighter and a proposed removal action was pending at the time of the appellant's retirement. These facts are not a bar to status quo ante relief.

Normally, when the Board finds that an appellant has been subjected to an involuntary retirement, it orders the agency to restore the appellant to the status quo ante by cancelling the retirement, reinstating the appellant, and providing any appropriate back pay and benefits. Baldwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 586, ¶ 46 (2009). As noted by the administrative judge, the relief granted to the appellant should not place him in a better position than he would have occupied absent the involuntary retirement. See Silvestri v. Department of the Army, 60 M.S.P.R. 580, 584 (1994). Here, there is evidence that, had the appellant not retired, the agency would have issued its decision letter and removed him.

In returning the appellant to the status quo ante, the Board's relief order must account for the proposed removal action and the mandatory retirement age; otherwise the appellant could be placed in a better position than he would have been in had he not retired. Sink v. Department of Energy, 110 M.S.P.R. 153, ¶¶ 18-22 (2008) (finding that the appellant's relief for an involuntary retirement was to be restored to the status quo ante taking into account the appellant's impending removal). Ultimately, if the appellant is lawfully removed retroactive to his retirement date, he may not be entitled to reinstatement or back pay, and the order directing the cancellation of his retirement might negatively affect his ongoing receipt of retirement benefits and leave him in a worse position. Thus, we modify the remand initial decision to clarify how the facts of this case may affect the status quo ante relief.

We find that status quo ante relief requires that the agency first determine if and when the appellant would have been removed had he not retired. Then the agency must take the necessary unexecuted steps to issue its decision, including providing the appellant with notice of his appeal rights in accordance with 5 C.F.R. § 1201.21. Only the agency can act on the notice of proposed removal. See Sink, 110 M.S.P.R. 153, ¶ 23. If, as suggested by the unissued decision letter in the record, the agency would have removed the appellant effective July 13, 2012, then the appellant's retirement effective that date would not be cancelled, the appellant would not be entitled to reinstatement or any back pay based on the involuntary retirement action at issue in this appeal, and the agency must follow applicable law and regulation in documenting the separation as when an employee retires in lieu of removal. If the appellant would not have been removed, or the effective date of the removal would have occurred after July 13, 2012, then the appropriate relief in this appeal would be to cancel the involuntary retirement, restore him with appropriate back pay and other benefits from July 13, 2012, until the date he otherwise would have separated from service by either a removal action or the mandatory retirement age, and to adjust his retirement annuity accordingly, i.e., potentially a later retirement date. See Sink, 110 M.S.P.R. 153, ¶¶ 22-26.

Guzman v. DHS, SF-0752-15-0170-C-1 (NP 1/6/2023), should be reviewed for the effect on back pay entitlement of a law enforcement officer who reaches mandatory retirement age under 5 USC 8425 (FERS) or 5 USC 8335 (CSRS) during the pendency of his (successful) appeal and the requirement that the agency provide a 60-day notice to the employee prior to the date of the mandatory retirement (so as to allow the employee to request an exemption from the mandatory retirement provision).
