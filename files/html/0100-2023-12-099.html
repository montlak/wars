broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section B: Interim Relief, Subsection 07: Certification and Proof of Interim Relief, Part b: Technical Error; Supplemental Evidence of Interim Relief
b. Technical Error; Supplemental Evidence of Interim Relief

Decisions of relatively recent vintage show flexibility as to implementation of interim relief and tolerance by the Board for minor agency miscues that do not result in significant harm to the appellant. If an agency timely restores the appellant to the work force but does not begin paying him until a few weeks later, interim relief is provided as long as the agency paid the employee back to the date of the initial decision. See Batten v. USPS, 101 MSPR 222, 226 ¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 35 (2006) ("the appellant has not alleged that the agency has failed to provide him with the pay and benefits appropriate to the EAS-11 position to which it assigned him, even if the agency is not in full compliance with the interim relief order, the Board exercises its discretion not to dismiss the agency's petition for review under these circumstances"); Stewart v. Dept. of Transp, 2023 MSPB 18, ¶ 14 (2023) ("the agency satisfies its obligation by taking appropriate administrative action by the deadline that will result in the issuance of a paycheck for the interim relief period"); Byers v. VA, 89 MSPR 655, 661-62 ¶ 12 (2001) (agency showed it took reasonable steps to insure that the appellant received pay and benefits due to her from the date of the initial decision and it promptly corrected inadvertent errors as soon as it learned of them; noncompliance with an interim relief order was excused under the circumstances); O'Keefe v. USPS, 88 MSPR 475, 479 ¶ 7 (2001) ("The appellant has not offered evidence to show that the agency has intentionally delayed paying him for the interim relief period."); Omites v. USPS, 87 MSPR 223, 227 ¶ 7 (2000) (in an initial decision issued on March 6, the appellant was returned to duty and an administrative action was begun to pay the appellant for the period from the initial decision until returning to duty on March 23, and by May 23 the agency completed the necessary paperwork for back pay to be issued by the agency accounting unit; "[s]uch inadvertent delay in issuing back pay does not show noncompliance with the interim relief order"); Beardsley v. DOD, 52 MSPR 457, 460 (1992) ("because the agency provided proof of compliance with its petition for review and restored the appellant to the payroll as of the date of the initial decision, and has paid him going back to the date of the initial decision, the agency complied with the interim relief order"), followed in Fox v. FDIC, 62 MSPR 447, 452 (1994); cf. Rosario-Fabregas v. Dept. of Army, NY-0752-13-0142-I-2 (NP 7/1/2016) ("Reasonable, inadvertent delays in issuing pay due under an interim relief order do not show noncompliance with the order. The same principle applies here, where the agency has shown a reasonable, inadvertent delay in providing health insurance benefits. See Fox v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 62 M.S.P.R. 447, 452 (1994) (delay in reinstating the appellant's health insurance benefits did not warrant dismissal of the agency's petition for review for failure to comply with an interim relief order).").

Purely procedural problems concerning interim relief are not likely to support rejection of an agency PFR. An appellant's contention that he did not receive the notice from the agency that it would be unduly disruptive to return the appellant to duty was not harmful error. Crespo v. USPS, 53 MSPR 125, 128-29 (1992); see Pencook v. USPS, 67 MSPR 409 (1995) (PFR would not be dismissed when the agency notified the appellant but not the AJ of its undue disruption determination). Not every technical deficiency in an SF-50 documenting interim relief results in dismissal of a PFR, Fletcher v. USDA, 63 MSPR 165, 168 n.2 (1994):

The appellant also argues that the SF-50B the agency has submitted is insufficient evidence of compliance because it lacks an approval date and because of "the lack of any apparent authority of the individual who signed the form." Although block 50 on the form--the block reserved for the "Signature/Authentication and Title of Approving Official"--is signed by an official other than the one whose name is typed in the block, there is no indication that the former official lacked the authority to approve the action (or sign the form) for the latter. Furthermore, we see no basis for finding that the absence of an approval date has the significance attributed to it by the appellant....

The Board declined to dismiss a PFR on the basis that the agency did not return the employee to his position until a week after the end of the PFR filing period when the personnel papers showed reinstatement as of the date of the initial decision, Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 59 MSPR 585, 589-90 (1993):

The appellant has moved that the Board dismiss the agency's petition, contending that the agency has not fully complied with the Board's interim relief regulations. He argues that, absent a specific determination under 5 CFR 1201.115(b)(2) that his presence would be unduly disruptive, the agency was required to return him to his position before it filed its petition. He argues further that, because the agency did not make such a determination, and did not return him to his position until approximately one week after the end of the filing period, the agency did not comply with the Board's interim relief regulations.

The agency in an appeal such as this is required, in the absence of a determination that the appellant's return to duty would be unduly disruptive, to submit proof that it has returned the appellant to duty. We find that the agency in this appeal has done so. By issuing an SF-50 timely reinstating the appellant, and by notifying him of the date on which he was to report to work, the agency completed all the administrative steps necessary to comply with the interim relief order. Furthermore, we see nothing improper in the agency's providing the appellant with a week's advance notice of the requirement that he return to duty, and we note that, because he was restored retroactive to the date of the initial decision, the appellant will receive all pay and other benefits to which he is entitled from that date. Thus, the evidence submitted is sufficient to establish that the agency complied with the administrative judge's interim relief order.

See Stewart v. Dept. of Transp, 2023 MSPB 18, ¶ 14 (2023) ("[T]he record shows that the appellant specifically requested, on May 24, 2019, that the agency delay his return to duty until that date. The appellant cannot argue that the agency's petition for review should be dismissed merely because it accommodated his request.").

If an agency issues an SF-50 stating it placed the appellant on a nonduty but paid status with reinstatement of benefits as of the date of the initial decision, the agency PFR will not be dismissed on the basis of an error concerning reinstatement of health benefits if the agency promptly corrects its error. See Crickard v. VA, 92 MSPR 625, 627 n.2 (2002); Farrelly v. USPS, 86 MSPR 230, 233 ¶ 7 (2000) (inadvertent error and an attempt by the agency to correct an adjustment to interim relief was insufficient to bar an agency PFR; no evidence of intentional delay in a payment); Pezdek v. DOD, 80 MSPR 554, 558 ¶ 9 (1999) (possible error in the classification of a job assigned to the appellant through interim relief was not a violation of agency responsibilities when the error did not affect the appellant's pay); Woodford v. Dept. of Army, 75 MSPR 350, 355-56 (1997) (rule applied to the correction of an error involving withholding taxes and health benefits); Gregg v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 127, 132 (1996) ("[A]gency promptly remedied the defects contained in its documentation of compliance, effectively refuting any inference of noncompliance[.]"); Wilson v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 303, 307 (1995) (payroll error concerning the salary step of an employee, corrected by the agency, was not a basis for dismissing the agency's PFR); Johnson v. DOJ, 67 MSPR 494, 497 (1995) (inadvertent payroll error, promptly corrected, was insufficient to warrant a dismissal of the PFR); Harvey v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 120, 124 (1994) ("the agency...has shown that its noncompliance with the interim relief order was inadvertent and promptly corrected when it discovered the pay error"); Heining v. GSA, 61 MSPR 539, 546 (1994) ("[T]he fact that the agency may not have delivered interim relief payments and information about health benefits to the appellant as quickly as she may have wished does not show bad faith by the agency; as long as the appellant was restored to the payroll as of the date of the initial decision."); Beardsley v. DOD, 55 MSPR 504, 508 (1992) "Although some payments may have been late, the fact that the agency provided proof in the form of an SF-50 that it returned the appellant to pay status with benefits as of the date of the initial decision, is sufficient to demonstrate its compliance with the administrative judge's interim relief order.").

"The Board will accept evidence of compliance with an interim relief order, filed after the deadline for filing a PFR, only if the agency submitted prima facie evidence of compliance with its petition, and if the agency's later evidence shows that any noncompliance was both inadvertent and promptly corrected when its error was discovered." Avant v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 467, 474-75 (1994), followed, Powers v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 256, 260 ¶ 7 (2000). Avant distinguished Mascarenas v. DOD, 54 MSPR 303, 307 (1992), involving an agency's deliberate but incorrect decision to follow erroneous OPM advice concerning the deduction of interim earnings from interim relief pay. See Carter v. DOJ, 88 MSPR 641, 645 ¶ 6 (2001) ("Although the agency submitted this evidence of compliance after the deadline for filing a petition for review, we will accept it because the agency submitted prima facie evidence of compliance with its petition for review, and the agency's evidence shows that any noncompliance was both inadvertent and promptly corrected when its error was discovered."); Fahrenbacher v. Dept. of Navy, 85 MSPR 500, 505 ¶ 8 (2000) (agency evidence showed that noncompliance was inadvertent and was promptly corrected upon the discovery of an interim pay error); Luciano v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 441, 450-51 (1997) (reasonable, inadvertent delays in issuing pay due under the interim relief order did not show noncompliance with the interim relief order); Franklin v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 583, 590 (1996) (same).

The rule concerning supplemental evidence showing prompt correction of an inadvertent interim relief error was restated in Johnson v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 59, 64-65 (1996). See Pezdek v. DOD, 80 MSPR 554, 558 ¶ 7 (1999) (agency provided prima facie evidence of compliance with interim relief requirements when it submitted its PFR and corrected the appellant's pay step when the appellant's motion to dismiss placed it on notice of the salary error); cf. Christopher v. Dept. of Army, 107 MSPR 580, 585 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 2 (2008) (despite the appellant's complaints of errors in the interim relief process, the agency PFR was not dismissed when the evidence showed the appellant was receiving pay and benefits from the agency during the interim relief period).

Not all delays will be excused as inadvertent and inconsequential. Moore v. USPS, 78 MSPR 80, 84-85 (1998), dismissed an agency PFR when an appellant's interim relief pay was delayed for months:

The agency's...petition for review of the May 19, 1997, initial decision was filed on June 23, 1997. As noted, with its petition, the agency submitted a memorandum advising the appellant that he was being placed on administrative leave effective May 19, 1997, that he was not to report to duty because the agency deemed that his return would be disruptive to the operation of the facility, and that he was being placed in a pay, but non-working, status. In the absence of an SF-50 or SF-52 documenting the appellant's interim appointment, it is questionable whether the agency established a prima facie case of compliance with the administrative judge's order. See, e.g., Wilson v. Department of Justice, 68 MSPR 303, 307 (1995). Assuming arguendo that it did, however, the record reflects that the appellant specifically notified the agency on July 10, 1997, in connection with his Motion for Sanctions, that he believed that the agency was not in compliance with the administrative judge's interim relief order because he had not received any pay or compensation. Notwithstanding, the agency did not examine the appellant's claim until it was compelled to respond to the Board's Order of October 8, 1997. When the agency did so, it learned that the appellant had indeed not been paid because the memorandum placing him on administrative leave had not been provided to the Finance/Payroll Office. It was not until then that the agency set about to assure that the appellant received a check for what was, by that time, the rather substantial amount of money due him. Thus, in mid-October, 1997, the agency first did what it was required to do by June 23, 1997, namely, take appropriate administrative action that would result in the issuance of a paycheck.

As described above, there is no indication that the agency took any action to correct its initial error in failing to put the appellant in pay status even after he clearly gave the agency notice that he had not received any money. The agency's failure to act for three months after being put on notice cannot be excused. Moreover, the appellant has well supported his claim that the agency's failure to timely pay him any money for a period of four months was not a "minor" matter, but, in fact, and as might reasonably be expected, caused him and his family to suffer a substantial financial hardship.

Accordingly, because we find that the agency's failure to timely provide the appellant with this most fundamental element of interim relief, the resumption of his pay, was not excusable nor a minor mistake, its petition for review must be dismissed. See, e.g., Bartholomew v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 MSPR 598, 601-02 (1997).

There are older decisions that near misses do not count as compliance with interim relief orders. An agency PFR was dismissed for failure to provide interim relief in Shatzel v. USPS, 51 MSPR 451, 453 (1991), when the agency reinstated the appellant as of June 3. The initial decision called for interim relief effective May 15, the date of the decision. Hamilton v. Dept. of Navy, 64 MSPR 247, 248 (1994) (insufficient to place appellant into interim relief position on date effective two weeks after date of initial decision); Moore v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 73, 74 (1992) (an agency PFR was dismissed when interim relief was provided effective March 22 and the date of the initial decision was March 11). Under law predating the current certification rule, if the agency provided interim relief but failed to properly notify the Board of interim relief, the agency could still be defaulted, and its PFR could be dismissed, if the agency's delay was not excused.
