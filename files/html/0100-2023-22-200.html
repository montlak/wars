6. Supplemental or Corrected Motion

After a fee petition is filed, additional fees will often be incurred as a result of time invested in other continuing tasks. There may be discovery or a hearing on the fee motion. If the fee motion is granted, the appellant may be required to oppose an agency PFR. That will entail additional discussions with the client, research and drafting the opposition. To capture this time, counsel files a supplementary fee motion or petition.

No rules govern the timing of supplemental fee petitions. One does not submit a supplemental fee petition with each new time entry. But the supplemental fee petition should be filed within some reasonable period after services have been performed. If counsel fees are awarded by initial decision and the agency files a PFR, a good time to file the supplemental petition for the time spent on opposing the PFR is when the Board issues an order denying it.

Board regulations do not mention the use of a supplementary fee petition. Typically, one is filed with the region that issued the initial decision on the fees. However, if the agency files a PFR from an initial decision awarding fees, it is proper to state in the response to the PFR the amount (and supporting detail) of additional fees incurred in responding to the agency submission. If the fee award from the AJ is sustained, the Board may then award additional reasonable fees entailed in preparing the PFR response without the need for a supplementary fee motion. Francois v. OPM, 64 MSPR 191, 197 (1994); see Hart v. Dept. of Transp., 115 MSPR 10, 2010 MSPB 183 (2010) (supplemental fee requests); Wilson v. USPS, 58 MSPR 653, 661 (1993) (considering and granting a supplementary fee request filed with a cross-PFR); Johnston v. Dept. of Treasury, 104 MSPR 527, 528 ¶ 3, 2007 MSPB 21 (2007) (sustaining AJ's award of fees; adding fees of $1412 claimed for preparation of a response to the agency PFR challenging the fees; "The agency has not opposed the appellant's supplemental request for fees and costs, and we have no reason to doubt the reasonableness of the hours expended."); Gonyer v. OPM, 41 MSPR 204, 205 (1989) (request of additional fees made and granted with the response to the PFR); Moore v. VA, 22 MSPR 605, 605 (1984) (granting at the PFR stage fees requested for reviewing and preparing a response to the agency's PFR). But see Hand v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 334, 338-39 (1993) (appellant's fees increased through his PFR, remanded for a determination of the exact amount of the final award: "The appellant has also requested additional attorney fees for 15 hours spent in preparing the instant petition for review.... Because we are unable at this time to determine the extent to which the appellant will prevail on the basis of this petition for review, a ruling on the appellant's entitlement to an award of reasonable attorney fees for the time spent preparing the petition must await the outcome of this case on remand."). The better practice, however, is to file a supplemental fee petition with the Board's regional or field office. The PFR scheme is not designed to resolve factual disputes on the new evidence contained in a supplemental fee request. Generally, the Board will not consider evidence submitted by a party for the first time with a PFR unless a showing is made that it was unavailable before the record was closed, despite the party's due diligence. Lehman v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 240, 243 (1987) (on PFR from an initial decision partially denying fees and expenses).

Once the Board issues its final decision on the merits of the underlying appeal, counsel must then request all recoverable fees in an initial fee application and cannot wait until some later decision on another aspect of his appeal to file a supplemental fee petition that reaches back to claim some time spent working on an earlier phase of the case that could have been included in the initial fee petition. Bartel v. FAA, 30 MSPR 451, 456 (1986). There is an exception to Bartel that allows counsel to correct errors or omissions in the original fee petition when necessitated by mistakes in math or documentation (it is not surprising that the many detailed records regarding time entries can become tangled or mislaid during the years that may pass before the issuance of a final Board decision on fees). The exception was described in Brito v. GPO, 31 MSPR 335, 338-40 (1986). The appellant's counsel did not realize he could obtain fees for opposing an agency PFR and counsel did not claim the time in his fee petition. After he learned of his error, he filed a supplemental fee petition that was denied as untimely. Brito reversed the regional office:

The Board's practice is to accept supplemental attorney fee requests and supporting evidence for legal services performed in the case subsequent to the submission of the original fee motion....

[C]ounsel for prevailing appellants should ordinarily be permitted to supplement or amend previously timely filed requests for attorneys fees under 5 CFR 1201.37(a)(2). When a supplemental or amended fee request is accepted for consideration, the agency must be afforded the opportunity to respond to such request. Such a standard is reasonable and necessary in order to afford prevailing appellants a full opportunity to recover the cost of legal representation in opposing unfounded agency actions. An opportunity to supplement or amend an otherwise timely request for attorneys fees should ordinarily be denied only upon: (1) a showing by the agency that such supplementation or amendment would substantially prejudice its right to oppose the fee request; or (2) a finding by the presiding official that acceptance of the supplemental or amended request for fees would unduly delay the proceedings before the Board.

The Board complicated its formulation on amended and corrected fee petitions in Kessler v. USDA, 37 MSPR 471, 473 (1988) (relying on Stephens v. OPM, 30 MSPR 680, 681 (1986)), determining that the jurisdiction of the AJ to entertain a request for amendment or correction of a fee petition ends when the addendum initial decision awarding fees becomes final. Under Kessler, appellant's counsel may not use a second (or supplemental) fee petition to correct omissions in the first fee petition after the initial decision on the first fee petition becomes final. See Wiatr v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 441, 446 (1991). The rule remains that the appellant should be able to supplement his original fee petition unless supplementation would substantially prejudice the agency's right to oppose the fee request or unless the supplementation would only delay the proceeding. Supplementation is appropriate to address new arguments raised by the agency (or by the AJ). Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 5, 16 (1991) (brief filed in response to objections raised by the agency that could not have been anticipated by the appellant before receiving the agency's response).
