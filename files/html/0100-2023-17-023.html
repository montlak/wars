5. Action to Avoid RIF

RIFs are demoralizing. They cause agencies to lose highly trained employees; they result in expenditures for severance pay and early retirement; and they breed litigation by appellants who believe that their procedural rights were violated or, worse, that the RIF was created or manipulated to target them for removal from the federal service for reasons having nothing to do with the legitimate agency organizational goals. Agency directives generally require managers to avoid RIFs by use of reassignments or early retirement packages. Employees attempt to avoid the adverse effects of RIFs through reassignments within their agencies or through transfers to other agencies. Movements of employees through reassignments, transfers, voluntary downgradings, and other stratagems have led to considerable litigation over the scope of Board RIF jurisdiction.

The Board ordinarily lacks jurisdiction to review an employee's voluntary reassignment taken to avoid the consequences of a RIF. Paul v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 174, 178 ¶ 8 (1998) ("A voluntary reassignment accepted by an employee to avoid the consequences of an impending RIF is not appealable."); Talley v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 261, 263 (1991); Kaplan v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 64, 65, 4 MSPB 153 (1980) (notation on a SF-50 notwithstanding, the evidence demonstrated that the agency followed reassignment rather than RIF procedures; the reassignment was not appealable to the Board). RIF jurisdiction may apply, however, depending on the effective date of the reassignment and, particularly for employees affected by the Postal Service reorganization, the inducements and representations made to employees in the process of planning for and attempting to avoid or reduce the size of a RIF. The more closely the reassignment process resembles the execution of a RIF, the more likely the reassignments will be considered part of and contestable as a RIF. If an employee takes a geographical reassignment from one position to another the day after a RIF separation was to be effective, the employee's release from his competitive level by RIF triggers the RIF appeal right. Price v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 516, 518 (1984) (by the time the employee was reassigned, the RIF was effective and the employee had served the entire notice period).
