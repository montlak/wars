A. AGENCY INVESTIGATION

The first concern is with preliminaries: what should the agency be doing before formal adverse action is initiated against the employee? Because the adverse action process is a continuum from preliminary investigations and assessments of employee performance and conduct to the final decision notice, the procedures tend to affect one another and analysis is not as neat as might be convenient. But try we must, and we Begin the Beguine: the agency investigation.

An agency need not formally investigate every charge of misconduct, Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 550 (1994):

The administrative judge's conclusion that the agency failed to show that the photographs were taken on agency premises does not logically follow from his determination that the agency's investigation of the appellant's misconduct was inadequate. Although we make no judgment here as to the quality of the agency's investigation in this appeal, the thoroughness or lack of thoroughness of the agency's investigation of the appellant's alleged misconduct is not a proper basis for not sustaining the agency's charge.

Moreover, the administrative judge erred to the extent that he suggests that the agency had a duty to investigate the appellant's misconduct. An agency is obligated to make reasonable inquiries into exonerating facts brought to its attention by an appellant before removing him. Here, however, the appellant made no reply to his proposed removal, and thus presented no exonerating evidence triggering a duty on the part of the agency to inquire. The appellant did not allege or show below that the agency had any affirmative duty to investigate or that he was harmed by a failure to more fully investigate his conduct.

See Clark v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 348, 351 (1984) (agency decision to investigate is outside the scope of a Board appeal; "[w]hether a prohibited personnel practice unrelated to such action occurred cannot be considered in the context of our jurisdiction over this proceeding"); Schaules v. USPS, 19 MSPR 390, 392-93 & n.2 (1984) (agency was not required to compile an investigative file of evidence supporting a charge; the agency was, however, required to provide the material it assembled as support for the removal action to the appellant).

Many adverse action proposals are issued without any more support than documents gathered by a personnel specialist who is given and concurs with reasons provided by a supervisor desiring to take an adverse action against the employee. Other actions are proposed following extensive internal investigations. Law enforcement agencies tend to rely on more structured investigations. Some organizations, the Department of Veterans Affairs being one, have elaborate administrative procedures to investigate misconduct, professional malpractice, or patient abuse. Inspectors general may look into misconduct allegations, as may military commanders through official processes available to them. Whatever the procedures, the failure of the agency to use them is not sufficient to invalidate the action without a showing of harmful error. The failure of the agency to convene a board of investigation into charges of patient abuse was not harmful error, without a showing of prejudice. The appellant presented no evidence to show that the original evidence presented to the Board, if presented to the agency board of investigation, could possibly have changed the agency's removal decision. Hoffman v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3283). But see Mercer v. DHHS, 772 F.2d 856 (Fed. Cir. 1985) [discussed in this chapter, "Might Have/Would Have Analysis."]. The allegation that the agency failed to follow its own regulations when it did not provide a copy of an internal investigation to the appellant is analyzed under the harmful error rule. See Simon v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 482, 486 (1994) (no error when the deciding official did not state that he relied on the information).

An agency's choice of investigative techniques is not harmful error without a regulatory violation and harm to the appellant, Alsedek v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 229, 240 (1993):

[T]he appellant claims that the agency's decision to conduct an informal, rather than a formal, investigation into the charges prior to taking action against him constituted harmful procedural error and violated his right to due process as guaranteed by the Fifth Amendment to the Constitution. Agency regulations place the decision to convene a formal rather than an informal investigation solely within the discretion of the officer instituting the investigation and further indicate that informal investigations are preferred. The appellant does not dispute this, nor does he claim that an error was made in the conduct of the informal investigation. Thus, he has not established the existence of any procedural error. Moreover, the appellant does not present evidence showing that any error likely had a harmful effect on the outcome of the investigation before the agency. Consequently, the appellant has not established any basis for reversal of the initial decision based upon harmful procedural error by the agency. See Baracco v. Department of Transportation, 15 MSPR 112, 123 (1983) (reversal of an action is warranted only where procedural error, whether regulatory or statutory, likely had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency), aff'd, 735 F.2d 488 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 469 U.S. 1018, 105 S. Ct. 432, 83 L. Ed.2d 358 (1984).

In addition, the appellant has failed to show that his due process rights were violated. As the administrative judge correctly found, an appellant is not entitled to counsel during the investigatory phase of an administrative proceeding. See Cornelius v. Office of Personnel Management, 30 MSPR 149, 151 (1986). Moreover, he is not entitled to a hearing before the decision unless the agency's regulations require it. See Mercer v. Department of Health and Human Services, 772 F.2d 856, 859-60 (Fed. Cir. 1985). A formal investigation under agency regulations requires a hearing; however, as stated above, convening a formal investigation is entirely discretionary. Finally, the right to be informed of charges is a due process right that attaches to administrative adjudications, but not to administrative proceedings which are merely investigatory. See Flanagan v. Department of the Army, 44 MSPR 378, 381 (1990).

See SSA v. Carr, 78 MSPR 313, 332-33 (1998) (declining to fault the agency for investigating an employee's conduct without notifying the employee of the pendency of an investigation and placing the employee on administrative leave during some periods of the investigation); Antonucci v. DOJ, 8 MSPR 491, 493 (1981) (despite alleged negligent or "heavy handed" investigative procedures, "the opportunity to examine and cross-examine all of the deponents at the hearing overcomes any basis for doubt of the accuracy of the statements made in the course of the investigation").
