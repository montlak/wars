10. Electronic Research

Thomas v. USPS, 87 MSPR 331, 343-44 ¶ 25 (2000), resolved to be compensable, as a component of counsel fees, computerized legal research services:

The appellant requested payment of $494 in fees for the expense of computerized legal research. Attorney fee awards under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) include those reasonable out-of-pocket expenses incurred by attorneys and ordinarily charged separately to their clients. See, e.g., Department of Health & Human Services v. Balaban, 33 MSPR 309, 323 (1987). Recoverable expenses are limited to those normally included within attorney fees and do not include those that may be awarded as costs under statute. 28 USC § 1920; Mitchell v. Department of Health & Human Services, 19 MSPR 206, 215 (1984). Statutorily authorized costs do not include computer research expenses. 28 USC § 1920. We find that the expense of computerized legal research is compensable as an award of attorney fees under section 7701(g)(1). The circuit courts generally agree that the expense of computerized legal research is compensable as attorney fees, not as costs. See, e.g., United States ex rel. Evergreen Pipeline Construction Co. v. Merritt Meridian Construction Corp., 95 F.3d 153, 173 (2d Cir. 1996).[1] A number of circuits have held that separately billed computerized research is compensable as an out-of-pocket expense in addition to the attorney fee lodestar. Case v. Unified School District No. 233, 157 (157 F.3d 1243, 1257-58 Cir. 1998); In re Mullins, 91 F.3d 1516, 1522 (D.C. Cir. 1996); Uniroyal Goodrich Tire Co. v. Mutual Trading Corp., 63 F.3d 516, 525-26 (7th Cir. 1995).[2] Awarding computerized legal research expenses separately from the lodestar is consistent with our practice of compensating, in the attorney fee award, reasonable out-of-pocket expenses incurred by attorneys and ordinarily charged separately to their clients. Balaban, 33 MSPR at 323. Thus, we award the appellant the expense of computerized legal research in addition to the lodestar amount....

[1] In Evergreen, 95 F.3d at 173, the Second Circuit cited Standley v. Chilhowee R-IV School District, 5 F 3d 319, 324 (8th Cir. 1993), for the holding that the expense of computerized legal research is compensable as attorney fees. Although in Standley the Eighth Circuit held that such expense must be factored into the lawyer's hourly rate, the court in Evergreen did not decide whether the expense of computerized legal research is compensable as an out-of-pocket expense not factored into the hourly rate or whether it must be reflected in the hourly rate. In a subsequent case, LeBlanc-Sternberg v. Fletcher, 143 F.3d 748 (2d Cir. 1998), the Second Circuit made clear the limited purpose for which Standley was cited in Evergreen. The Fletcher court held that attorney fee awards include those reasonable out-of-pocket expenses incurred by attorneys and ordinarily charged to their clients, and remanded to a lower court for it to reconsider its finding denying expenses claimed by plaintiffs, including computerized legal research. Fletcher, at 763.

[2] The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, without discussion, has listed the expense of computerized research as an out-of-pocket expense that is not factored into the reasonable hourly rate. Sterling Federal Systems v. Goldin, 16 F.3d 1177, 1189 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

Thomas was followed in Seeley v. OPM, 88 MSPR 424, 426 ¶ 4 (2001); see Ruble v. OPM, 96 MSPR 44, 51 ¶ 14 (2004) (awarding fees for computerized legal research).
