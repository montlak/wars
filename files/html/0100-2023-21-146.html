1. Deferred Annuity

If an employee is not eligible for an immediate annuity, she may be eligible for a deferred annuity, Blando v. OPM, 36 MSPR 102, 104 (1988):

Eligibility for a deferred retirement is governed by 5 U.S.C. § 8338. Pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 8338(a), an employee who is separated from the service or transferred to a position in which he does not continue subject to Subchapter III of Chapter 83 after completing five years of civilian service is entitled to an annuity beginning at the age of sixty-two years. Although OPM erroneously analyzed the appellant's annuity claim as a claim for an immediate annuity, which is governed by 5 U.S.C. § 8336, the statute cited in the reconsideration letter, 5 U.S.C. § 8333(a), applies generally to retirement cases under Subchapter III. This provision of the law requires the completion of five years of civilian service for eligibility for a deferred annuity as well for eligibility for an immediate annuity. Similarly, the regulation, 5 C.F.R. § 831.201, also applies generally to retirement cases.

Service credit does not appear to be allowed for LWOP after separation for an individual seeking a deferred annuity. See Daniel v. OPM, 43 MSPR 599, 603 (1990) ("Finally, the appellant's assertion that he should have been considered to have been in leave-without-pay status until OWCP made its decision and that this time should be considered creditable service is without merit. Cf. Knipple v. Office of Personnel Management, 41 M.S.P.R. 7, 10 (1989) (time spent in leave-without-pay status after separation is not creditable for purposes of completing the 10-year requirement for a survivor annuity benefit).").

As to CSRS, for a deferred annuity, Hayden v. OPM, 58 MSPR 286, 289, 293 (1993), explained the "high-three" calculus and the manner that unused sick and annual leave are treated:

The amount of an annuity is based on, inter alia, an employee's average pay, which is the average of the highest rate of basic pay for three consecutive years, the "high three." See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8339(a) and 8331(4)....

...

The appellant is not entitled to service credit for his unused sick leave and annual leave. Only employees eligible for immediate retirement may receive service credit for unused sick leave. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.302(a). The appellant was separated in 1983, but was not eligible to receive an annuity because he had not met the age and years of service requirements for immediate retirement. Therefore, he is not entitled to credit for his unused sick leave. Upon separation, an employee receives payment for any unused annual leave, and such leave is not counted toward service credit for retirement purposes. See Federal Personnel Manual Supp. 831-1, subchapter S3-4(1). The record indicates that the appellant was paid for his unused annual leave.

CSRS deferred annuities were explored with particular regard to the type of government service last served, Mica v. OPM, 66 MSPR 41, 43-45 (1994):

The appellant served for approximately 9 and one half years as a Congressional employee before he was elected a Member of Congress (Member), effective January 3, 1979. The appellant subsequently completed ten years of Member service, before retiring on January 2, 1989. The appellant then served an additional 6 months and 29 days as a Congressional employee, before separating from the government on August 1, 1989. The appellant turned 50 on February 4, 1994.

The appellant applied for a deferred, reduced annuity commencing at age 50, based on his ten years of Member service, and his 20 years of total government service. OPM found that, although the appellant's ten years of Member service entitled him to an annuity at age 60, he was not eligible for a reduced annuity at age 50 because he had completed only 19 and one half years of government service at the time he, was separated as a Member.

...

The statute governing deferred retirement for Members provides as follows:

A Member who, after December 31, 1955, is separated from the service as a Member after completing 5 years of civilian service is entitled to an annuity beginning at the age of 62 years. A Member who is separated from the service after completing 10 or more years of Member service is entitled to an annuity beginning at the age of 60 years. A Member who is separated from the service after completing 20 or more years of service, including 10 or more years of Member service, is entitled to a reduced annuity beginning at the age of 50 years.

5 U.S.C. § 8338(b) (emphasis added).

As previously stated, it is undisputed that the appellant had 20 years of service, including 10 years of member service, at the time of his separation from the government on August 1, 1989, and that he was 50 years old as of February 4, 1994. However, OPM interprets the third sentence of this subsection as further requiring that a Member have completed his 20 years of service on or before the time he is separated as a Member. Such an interpretation would render the appellant ineligible for a reduced annuity at age 50, as he only had 19 and one half years of service at the time his Member service ended.

The administrative judge found that OPM's position was supported by the plain terms of 5 U.S.C. § 8338(b), noting that the appellant was not a "Member" but rather a "former Member" at the time he separated with 20 years of service. However, as the appellant points out, the first sentence of this same subsection, concerning entitlement to a different annuity, expressly requires that a Member be "separated from the service as a Member," suggesting that when Congress desired to restrict the eligibility of former Members, it knew how to do so. In attempting to refute such an inference, OPM actually concedes the very point, asserting that Congress included the qualifying phrase "as a Member" in the first sentence "for the purpose of preventing former Members who separated earlier [than January 1, 1956] from establishing entitlement" by virtue of their subsequent service. In other words, in the absence of such language, Members whose Member service terminated before January 1, 1956 (i.e., a certain class of former Members) would nonetheless be entitled to an annuity upon their subsequent separation from the service, assuming they possessed the requisite government service at the time of this separation. By the same logic, the absence of this phrase from the third sentence indicates that former Members, like the appellant, who ultimately separate with 20 years of total government service, including ten years of Member service, are entitled to an annuity commencing at age 50.

OPM further argues that any implication resulting from the absence of the phrase "as a Member" from the third sentence of 5 U.S.C. § 8338(b) is outweighed by the explicit language of 5 U.S.C. § 8344(d)(1), which provides as follows:

[T]he retired Member or Member separated with title to immediate or deferred annuity, who serves at any time after separation as a Member in an appointive position in which he is subject to this subchapter, is entitled, if he so elects, to have his Member annuity computed or recomputed as if the service had been performed before his separation as a Member....

OPM asserts that this provision "specifically states how OPM may use post-Member service: it can enhance the computation of benefits; but it does not in any way change the former Member's title to benefits. In fact, 5 U.S.C. § 8344(d)(1) only describes the computational effect of post-Member service, and does not address the question of title. The legislative history of 5 U.S.C. § 8344(d)(1) is similarly limited to a discussion of computational issues. See S. Rep. 1289, 86th Cong., 2nd Sess. (1960), reprinted in 1960 U.S.C.C.A.N. 2773, 2774.

...In the present case, OPM's interpretation of 5 U.S.C. 8338(b) would effectively read the phrase "as a Member" out of the first sentence, violating a basic tenet of statutory construction. See Vesser v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 F.3d 600, 605 n.3 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (it is a settled rule that a statute must, if possible, be construed in such fashion that every word has some operative effect). Moreover, as the court stated in Vesser, "the plain and unambiguous meaning of a statute prevails in the absence of clearly expressed legislative intent to the contrary."Id.
