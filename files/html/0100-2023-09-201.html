4. Amicus Curiae

Board regulations provide at 5 CFR1201.34(e) (2022):

(e) Amicus curiae.

(1) An amicus curiae is a person or organization who, although not a party to an appeal, gives advice or suggestions by filing a brief with the judge or the Board regarding an appeal. Any person or organization, including those who do not qualify as intervenors, may request permission to file an amicus brief. The Board may solicit amicus briefs on its own motion.

(2) A request to file an amicus curiae brief must include a statement of the person's or organization's interest in the appeal and how the brief will be relevant to the issues involved.

(3) The request may be granted, in the discretion of the judge or the Board, if the person or organization has a legitimate interest in the proceedings, and such participation will not unduly delay the outcome and may contribute materially to the proper disposition thereof.

(4) The amicus curiae shall submit its brief within the time limits set by the judge or the Board and must comply with any further orders by the judge or the Board.

(5) An amicus curiae is not a party to the proceeding and may not participate in any way in the conduct of the hearing, including the presentation of evidence or the examination of witnesses. The Board, in its discretion, may invite an amicus curiae to participate in oral argument in proceedings in which oral argument is scheduled.

According to Chapter 10, section 7.d.(2) of the Judges' Handbook, "An amicus curiae is any person or organization who, in the discretion of the AJ or the Board, may be granted leave to file briefs containing advice or suggestions regarding an appeal." "Parties or organizations which fail to qualify as intervenors may be granted permission, in the discretion of the judge, to file an amicus curiae brief." Azdell v. OPM, 88 MSPR 319, 329 (2001). Chapter 3 of the Judges' Handbook notes at section 5.c.:

Any person or organization, including those who do not qualify as intervenors, may be granted permission to file a brief as an amicus curiae, in the discretion of the AJ. See also chapter 10, section 7, infra. E-filing may not be used to file a request to participate as an amicus curiae or file a brief as amicus curiae. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.14(c)(6).

The Handbook explains further, at Chapter 10, Section 7.d.(2):

An amicus curiae is any person or organization who, in the discretion of the AJ or the Board, may be granted leave to file briefs containing advice or suggestions regarding an appeal.

Traditionally, an amicus is a friend of the court. Although the Board is not a court, the amicus is seen as a friend of the Board. There have been few amici noted in Board litigation. Perhaps the Board has few friends. The Board occasionally solicits amici comments on cases presenting difficult issues and invites participation of amici, along with pro bono counsel, in complex PFRs. The Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.34(e) (2022), is directed, however, to those individuals or organizations who wish to present their views in an appeal and who are not solicited by the Board to do so. The regulation states that, in the discretion of the AJ or the Board, any person or organization, including those who do not qualify as intervenors, may be granted leave to file an amicus curiae brief. Theoretically, an amicus is a neutral but helpful expert, offering some friendly and informative advice for the good of the adjudicator. In practice, amici are usually special interest groups with a point of view to espouse. The amicus would not normally be directly affected by the outcome of the case, a distinction in status from a person, agency, or organization seeking to become an intervenor. See Clerman v. ICC, 35 MSPR 190, 191 n.1 (1987). The ACLU, for example, might wish to address a First Amendment issue. The author could present some views on an issue involving counsel fees and affecting members of the appellants' bar. The Board has not ruled out the possibility that a separate component of an agency, e.g., an Office of Inspector General, could file an amicus brief in litigation involving the agency served by the OIG. Kent v. GSA, 56 MSPR 536, 540 (1993). Amicus filings were denied as to organizations addressing an issue that would not be resolved in the Board appeal and as to an individual seeking to present his disagreement with analysis contained in an initial decision and statutory interpretation apparently applied by OSC. Carson v. Dept. of Energy, AT-1221-14-0520-W-1 (NP 5/21/2015).
