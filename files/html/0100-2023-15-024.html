c. Donated Leave

The allowance of donated leave is similar to the discretionary grant of LWOP, Joyner v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 154, 160-61 (1993):

In order to receive donated annual leave under the voluntary leave transfer program, an employee must show that there is a medical emergency. A "medical emergency" is "a medical condition of an employee or a family member of such employee that is likely to require an employee's absence from duty for a prolonged period of time and to result in a substantial loss of income to the employee because of the unavailability of paid leave." See 5 CFR 630.902. Contrary to the administrative judge's finding, the appellant's condition may well represent such a medical emergency. Nonetheless, we find that the agency did not abuse its discretion in declining to permit the appellant to receive transferred annual leave under the voluntary leave transfer program.

As detailed above, the appellant's condition is chronic in that she was permanently unable to report for duty in her current position. Because there was no foreseeable end to her absence and because her absence was a hardship to the agency, the agency was not required to grant her LWOP. Analogously, we find that the agency is not required to prolong the situation by extending the appellant's absence on donated leave.

Under the Office of Personnel Management's regulations establishing the voluntary leave transfer program, each agency is authorized to establish procedures to administer the program. See 5 CFR 630.902.... The agency policy thus mandates that the voluntary leave transfer program is only available where LWOP is given. If it was not improper to deny LWOP, as we find above, it cannot be improper to decline to permit the appellant to receive donated annual leave.

Leave donation or transfer occurs under the Federal Employees Leave Sharing Act of 1988, allowing federal employees to transfer certain unused portions of their accrued annual leave to the leave account of an employee experiencing a medical emergency. 5 USC 6331-34 (2000). In Jones v. Dept. of Transp., 295 F.3d 1298, 1305 (Fed. Cir. 2002), the court held that the Voluntary Leave Transfer Program does not preclude the otherwise-proper removal of an employee, even if the agency approved the employee's participation in the Program and the employee had a positive transferred leave balance. The donated leave could be administered as annual leave. An employee could be removed for absence when there was no foreseeable end to the absence. Jones provides a synopsis of the legislative history and objectives of the Voluntary Leave Transfer Program.
