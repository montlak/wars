11. Knowledge of Disability

If an appellant defends a case on the basis of a medical (or other) condition that he claims the agency failed reasonably to accommodate, it must be demonstrated that the agency knew or reasonably should have known of that condition before it can be charged with failing to make the accommodation. Joshua v. SSA, 2 MSPR 280, 283, 2 MSPB 444 (1980) ("[w]e note that the appellant has the burden of establishing that the agency failed to accommodate his medical condition"); see Vidal v. USPS, 75 MSPR 191, 195 (1997) ("The appellant failed to prove his allegation of discrimination, however, when he introduced no evidence showing that the agency knew of his poor vision at the time it assigned him to the LSMO position."); Leftridge v. USPS, 56 MSPR 340, 345 (1993) (no evidence that agency managers knew of the appellant's condition or that the appellant was perceived as disabled); cf. Hillman v. DOJ, 103 Fed. Appx. 371, 373 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (until the employer is made aware of the disability, the duty to accommodate does not arise; citing Office of Senate Sergeant at Arms v. Office of Senate Fair Employment Practices, 95 F.3d 1102, 1107 (Fed. Cir. 1996); Banks v. VA, CB-7121-15-0006-V-1 (NP 2/27/2015) ("Given the arbitrator's finding that the appellant disavowed her need for an accommodation on the day in question, we find no basis to determine that the agency committed disability discrimination by failing to accommodate her. See Beard v. Department of Energy, EEOC DOC 0120112979, 2013 WL 2146805 *3 (May 9, 2013) (the agency did not discriminate against the complainant by failing to offer him a reasonable accommodation because the complainant informed the agency that he did not need an accommodation).").

The agency need not speculate as to the appellant's condition. It was not notice to the agency that the agency's alcoholism counselor thought that the appellant was having problems with stress when the counselor was not an authority on delayed stress syndrome. See Marshall v. GPO, 18 MSPR 682, 684 (1984) ("Thus, there is no record of a diagnosis of delayed stress syndrome for appellant during his period of employment."). Walker v. Dept. of Air Force, PH-0752-09-0155-I-1, 2011 MSPB 44 (NP 3/22/2011) (Vice Chair Wagner dissenting), noted:

The record thus shows that the appellant has failed to present evidence creating the inference that Captain Kiesow's or Colonel Cole's actions were taken as a result of his claimed disability; they had no knowledge of his claimed disability. See Cordoba v. Dillard's, Inc., 419 F.3d 1169, 1175 (11th Cir. 2005). With regard to claims of disability discrimination, an employer cannot be held liable for firing an employee when it lacks knowledge of the employee's disability. See Cordoba, 419 F.3d at 1175; Singleton-Grant v. Shinseki, 2010 WL 1638821 (EEOC Apr. 15, 2010).

Accommodation is required only if there is knowledge of the employee's disability by agency officials or if those officials are aware of factors that should reasonably lead them to conclude there is a disabling condition. Schulte v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 126, 130 (1991) (appellant did not show by preponderant evidence that the agency "knew or should have known" of his disability at the time of his removal or that the agency officials involved considered the appellant as disabled); Dahl v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 182, 184, 5 MSPB 236 (1981) (agency accommodative responsibility requires knowledge on the part of the agency officials of factors that would lead them to reasonably conclude that an employee suffered from a disabling condition before any agency obligation attached); see Sleboda v. DOJ, 54 MSPR 386, 390, 391 (1992) ("[t]he agency was not aware of his drug addiction before he was removed"; "the agency was not obliged to accommodate an unknown handicap"); Taylor v. USPS, 41 MSPR 374, 379 (1989) (to rebut a charge of failure to perform work, a disability discrimination defense will avail the employee only if he made his supervisor aware of his medical restrictions or made it clear that he was endangered by an assignment); Clancy v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 196, 199, 6 MSPB 173 (1981) (accommodative responsibility exists when the responsible agency officials "are made aware of the handicap or of factors from which such handicap may be inferred").

The agency may not avoid knowledge of a disabling condition by refusing to accept pertinent information from the appellant, Hughes v. Comm'n on Civil Rights, 26 MSPR 277, 278-79 (1985):

The basis of the agency's challenge to the presiding official's handicap discrimination finding is it's [sic] assertion that it was unaware of appellant's "handicapping" condition. We do not find this assertion persuasive particularly since the agency rejected as irrelevant the voluminous medical data submitted by appellant. Moreover, the record reflects that the agency was aware that appellant: 1) had been involved in an automobile accident; 2) had undergone a series of operations; 3) was in a full length cast and used crutches; 4) was under medication, bottles of which cluttered her desk and 5) uncharacteristically had increased difficulty following her supervisor's instructions. See Dahl v. Department of Labor, 5 MSPB 236, 5 MSPR 182 (1981). Thus, the presiding official was correct in concluding that the agency knew or should have known of appellant's handicapping condition and that it had a duty to accommodate it absent a showing that the applicable accommodation, completion of employee performance appraisals by others, would have constituted an undue hardship. Billings v. Department of the Air Force, 24 MSPR 20 (1984).
