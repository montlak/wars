1. Response to Appeal

Agencies submit with their appeal responses documents and written statements that may or sometimes may not have surfaced during the proposal stage of the adverse action. The Board treats these as evidence; the submissions do not deny the appellant a fair hearing: the appellant may challenge the evidence and the AJ's initial decision will be based only on evidence that the appellant has had an opportunity to rebut. Objections to these materials will be entertained as to the authenticity, materiality, and accuracy of the documents. Hutchins v. DOJ, 7 MSPR 645, 647, 7 MSPB 475 (1981). As documents may constitute hearsay, the Board may evaluate the documents under Borninkhof and give the agency submissions little or no weight in the determination of the appeal. Hayden v. USPS, 15 MSPR 296, 300 (1983).

If the agency appeal response (or the appellant's appeal) is overloaded with prejudicial and objectionable material, the record is best protected by a motion to strike the offensive material from the record. Alternatively, a motion in limine may be filed by requesting the AJ to declare that the materials will not be considered in the adjudication of the appeal. Isolated documentation problems need not result in reformation of the appeals file. Should materials be improperly submitted--for example, evidence of prior disciplinary proceedings not cited in the proposal notice--no reversible error occurs unless it is demonstrated that the materials were considered by the proposing or deciding official. Hubbard v. USPS, 32 MSPR 505, 508 (1987).

Agency counsel who prepare narrative responses to appeals should bear in mind that their assertions concerning the facts of the case are not of evidentiary value to the Board, meaning the facts that counsel asserts must be backed up by evidence provided to the Board.
