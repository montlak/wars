7. Compelling Reason for Reassignment; Detail; Different Duties

The restoration of the appellant to the position she occupied before the improper personnel action, without being allowed to perform the full range of her former duties, does not constitute return to the status quo ante. Through enforcement proceedings initiated by the appellant, the Board determines if there are compelling agency reasons to support the changes. Coles v. USPS, 101 MSPR 412, 414 ¶ 5, 2006 MSPB 66 (2006) (citing Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 438, 447 (1988)).

The authority of an agency to reassign an employee following a restoration compelled by Board order was the subject of review as to a reassignment after a successful appeal from a removal mitigated to a suspension. The Board rejected agency arguments that the reassignment was a matter of agency discretion, Mann v. VA, 29 MSPR 271, 274-75 (1985):

...The Board's power to assure compliance with its decisions is far-reaching.... The Board will look carefully at reassignments to determine whether the agency has substantively complied with its order and returned the appellant as nearly as possible to the status quo ante....

...Thus, the obvious first question to be decided is whether the employee was reinstated to his or her former position. Reinstatement followed immediately by a detail or reassignment will not satisfy this test....

The Board's analysis does not end at this point, however. The next question to be answered is whether the appellant's former position still exists at the same grade and classification levels....

However, even if the employee is not returned to the former position which still exists at the same grade and classification levels, it may still be that the agency has returned the employee as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. Kerr does not require that the employee must always be returned to his or her former position. While generally the law requires that the employee be reinstated to the position from which he or she was removed, where the agency has a strong overriding interest for not placing the employee in the identical position, the Board will analyze the legitimacy of the agency's proffered rationale in order to determine if it is sufficient to justify not returning the employee to his or her former position. In such cases, if the Board finds that the interests of the agency are so compelling, then those reasons, in appropriate cases, may be sufficient to override the appellant's entitlement to return to his or her former position. In such cases the Board must, of course, address the question of whether the position to which the appellant has been reassigned is in fact substantially similar to the former position.

In this case the agency claims that it hired appellant's replacement in good faith and that it had a legitimate reason for resolving the problem of having two employees in the same position by reassigning appellant, rather than her replacement. The agency claims that it gave due consideration to the capabilities of both employees and the needs of the agency in making this decision. However, the agency has not offered any evidence that appellant's reassignment to her former position would disrupt the work in the agency's supply service...or of other overriding circumstances which would justify the decision to reassign appellant rather than her replacement.[1] Therefore, the Board finds that the agency has not shown a compelling reason for not reinstating appellant to her former position.

[1] The agency has cited Quinney v. Department of the Army, 3 MSPB 123, 3 MSPR 1 (1980) for the proposition that the agency, not the Board, must resolve the assignment of employees when two people occupy the same position as a result of a Board determination. The Board in Quinney held only that where two parties were victims of wrongful demotions, they both were entitled to the status quo ante, and the agency should sort out the exact assignments on its own.

Even assuming that the agency had satisfactorily met its burden of justifying its failure to reinstate appellant in her former position, the agency would still not be in compliance with the Board's order. As the Regional Director found, appellant's new responsibilities differ in both supervision and budget control from her former responsibilities.... As Program Management Officer, she had no supervisory or budget-management responsibilities. Such a change in supervisory or budgeting responsibilities has been held to be a significant factor in making determinations of compliance with a Board order....

Berteletti v. USPS, 45 MSPR 1, 6-8 (1990), involving cancelation of a demotion and restoration of the appellant to a disputed status quo, reiterated the Board's criteria for considering the validity of a challenge to a reassignment following appellant's successful appeal of an adverse action:

[I]n Mann...the Board set out a four-part progressive test to determine whether the employee had been returned as nearly as possible to the status quo ante: (1) Was the employee reinstated to his former position; (2) does the former position still exist at the same grade and classification level; (3) does the agency have an overriding interest in not returning the employee to his former position; and (4) is the position to which the employee was reassigned substantially similar to the former position[17] ....

[17] While the agency is generally required to reinstate the employee to his former position, where the agency claims it has a strong overriding interest for not placing the employee in the identical position, the Board will analyze the legitimacy of the agency's proffered rationale in order to determine if it is sufficient to justify not returning the employee to his former position.... In such cases, if the Board finds that the interests of the agency are so compelling, then those reasons, in appropriate cases, may be sufficient to override the appellant's entitlement to return to his former position.... The Board must address in such cases the question of whether the position to which the appellant has been reassigned is in fact substantially similar to the former position.

...

Although the Board does not have jurisdiction to review a detail of an employee, it does have authority to review whether the appellant has been returned as nearly as possible to the status quo ante in the context of determining jurisdiction over an allegedly cancelled appealable action.
