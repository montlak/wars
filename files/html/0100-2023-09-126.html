i. EEO Allegation; Claim for Compensatory Damages

Mootness is avoided, even if an agency rescinds an action, if an appeal includes a claim for compensatory damages based on a nonfrivolous affirmative defense of discrimination, Currier v. USPS, 72 MSPR 191, 195-99 (1996):

The mootness doctrine, which stems from the "case or controversy" requirement of Article III of the Constitution, is also jurisdictional in nature. See Nasatka v. Delta Scientific Corp., 58 F.3d 1578, 1580 (Fed. Cir. 1995), cert. denied, 116 S. Ct. 912, 133 L. Ed.2d 843 (1996). Although the Board is not an Article III court, it is governed by an analogous statutory provision that prohibits it from issuing advisory opinions. See 5 USC § 1204(h). A case is moot when the issues presented are no longer "live" or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the case. Powell v. McCormack, 395 U.S. 486, 496, 89 S. Ct. 1944, 1950-51, 23 L. Ed.2d 491 (1969); Horner v. MSPB, 815 F.2d 668, 670-71 (Fed. Cir. 1987); see Occhipinti v. Dept. of Justice, 61 MSPR 504, 507 (1994). Mootness can arise at any stage of litigation, and an appeal will be dismissed as moot when, by virtue of an intervening event, the Board cannot grant any effectual relief whatever in favor of the appellant, viz., when the appellant, by whatever means, obtained all of the relief he could have obtained had he prevailed before the Board, and thereby lost any legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the appeal. See Calderon v. Moore, 116 S. Ct. 2066, 135 L. Ed.2d 453 (1996); Perisho v. USPS, 69 MSPR 55, 58 (1995); Gonzalez v. Dept. of Justice, 68 MSPR 439, 441 (1995); Mascarenas v. Dept. of Defense, 60 MSPR 320, 323 (1993).

Put another way, once Board jurisdiction has attached, it cannot be divested based on mootness unless the appellant has received all of the possible relief he sought before the Board, i.e., unless it is impossible for the Board to grant any further effectual relief. See Nasatka, 58 F.3d at 1580; see also Dalton v. Dept. of Justice, 66 MSPR 429, 434 (1995); Hatler v. Dept. of the Air Force, 3 MSPB 406, 3 MSPR 322, 325 (1980) (stating that mootness is determined by reference to the allegations of the complaining party (citing Network Project v. Corp. for Public Broadcasting, 561 F.2d 963, 968 (D.C. Cir. 1977), cert. denied, 434 U.S. 1068, 98 S. Ct. 1247, 55 L. Ed.2d 770 (1978)). The available remedy need not be "fully satisfactory" to avoid mootness: a partial remedy is sufficient. Calderon, 116 S. Ct. at 2067.

Before the Civil Rights Act of 1991 (CRA), compensatory damages[4] were not available in Board appeals. See Nordhoff v. Dept. of the Navy, 64 MSPR 478, 485 (1994); Waidzunas v. Dept. of Veterans Affairs, 56 MSPR 529, 534 (1993); Wilson v. USPS, 38 MSPR 156, 158 (1988). The CRA authorized, for the first time, an award of compensatory damages in Board appeals based on claims of intentional discrimination prohibited by the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, or the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.[5] 42 USC § 1981a (Supp. IV 1992); see Hocker v. Dept. of Transp., 63 MSPR 497, 504-05 (1994), aff'd, 64 F.3d 676 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), cert. denied, 116 S. Ct. 918, 133 L. Ed.2d 848 (1996); see also Nordhoff, 64 MSPR at 485. Such damages could include "future pecuniary losses, emotion pain, suffering, inconvenience, mental anguish, loss of enjoyment of life, and other nonpecuniary losses...." 42 USC § 1981a(b)(3); Hocker, 63 MSPR at 504.

[4] For purposes of the CRA, the term "compensatory damages" excludes back pay, interest on back pay, or any other type of previously available relief, 42 USC § 1981a(b)(2), and the term is so used throughout this Opinion and Order. See Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U.S. 244, 253, 114 S. Ct. 1483, 1491, 128 L. Ed.2d 229 (1994).

[5] The CRA does not authorize compensatory damages for age discrimination prohibited by the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA). See Walker v. Dept. of Agric., EEOC Appeal No. 01943190 (May 3, 1995); Taylor v. Dept. of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05930633 (Jan. 14, 1994). Thus, the holding here does not preclude a dismissal for mootness where the action appealed is completely rescinded, the appellant's only discrimination claim is under the ADEA, and there are no other viable claims for relief.

Prior to the CRA, when a personnel action was appealed to the Board, the available relief was limited to complete rescission of the action appealed, consisting of cancellation of the action; the appellant's restoration (if applicable) to his former or other substantially equivalent position, as appropriate; back pay; interest on back pay; and other employment benefits that he would have received had the action not occurred. See e.g., Green v. USPS, 61 MSPR 34, 35 (1994). This type of relief has been described as status quo ante relief. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The Board has therefore stated that an appeal must be dismissed as moot when the agency completely rescinds the action appealed, or when the appellant has been returned to the status quo ante. E.g., Ferguson v. Dept. of Justice, 23 MSPR 55, 56 (1984); Himmel, 6 MSPR at 486; see Bruning v. Veterans Administration, 834 F.2d 1019, 1021 (Fed. Cir. 1987). None of these cases, however, involved the issue of potential compensatory damages. As further discussed below, we find that such descriptions of the mootness standard--i.e., descriptions in terms of "complete rescission" or "return to the status quo ante"--are no longer apt in those mixed-cases where the issue of potential compensatory damages is raised. This is because such descriptions were based on the pre-CRA limits on the Board's remedial authority, and do not reflect all of the relief now available before the Board in mixed cases.

To reiterate, the standard for mootness is whether the appellant has received all of the possible, effectual relief. See Church of Scientology of California v. United States, 506 U.S. 9, 12-14, 113 S. Ct. 447, 450, 121 L. Ed.2d 313 (1992) ("[t]he availability of [a] possible remedy is sufficient to prevent [a] case from being moot"). Where, as here, an appellant has outstanding, viable claims of compensatory damages before the Board, the agency's complete rescission of the action appealed does not afford him all of the relief available before the Board, so that the mere rescission does not render the appeal moot. See Consolidated Rail Corp. v. Darrone, 465 U.S. 624, 630, 104 S. Ct. 1248, 1252, 79 L. Ed.2d 568 (1984) (the potential for money damages prevents the case from being dismissed as moot); Powell, 395 U.S. at 495-500 (dismissal is unwarranted despite the mootness of the primary issue and attendant claim for relief, where a subsidiary claim for relief is outstanding, regardless of whether the subsidiary claim may have been pursued in another forum); see also Firefighters Local Union No. 1784 v. Stotts, 467 U.S. 561, 571, 104 S. Ct. 2576, 2584, 81 L. Ed.2d 483 (1984) (as long as the parties have a concrete interest in the outcome of the litigation, the case is not moot notwithstanding the size of the dispute); cf. Cruz v. Dept. of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1245-46 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc) (where the Board never had jurisdiction over the action appealed, a claim that the action was based on prohibited discrimination does not bring the appeal within the Board's jurisdiction).

The Board is without authority to broaden or narrow its appellate jurisdiction through the exercise of inherent power. See McNeese v. OPM, 61 MSPR 70, 73, aff'd, 40 F.3d 1250 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Moreover, the Board has held in pre-CRA cases that it is required to review discrimination claims in mixed cases, such as this, even where the discrimination claims could not afford any further relief because the Board had already determined that the action appealed should be reversed for other reasons. See, e.g., Marchese v. Dept. of the Navy, 32 MSPR 461, 464 (1987); see also Totten v. USPS, 68 MSPR 255, 257 (1995) (post-CRA case relying on the Marchese line of cases). For the foregoing reasons, we find that this appeal is not moot. Nor has the appellant consented to the divestiture of Board jurisdiction; in fact, he has vigorously opposed it. See, e.g., Himmel, 6 MSPR at 486. We therefore find that this appeal may not be dismissed.[8]

[8] We note that an AJ is not precluded from striking a discrimination claim as frivolous, even before a hearing. See Crawford v. USPS, 70 MSPR 416, 423-24 (1996). Once any discrimination claims have been properly stricken as frivolous, and assuming there are no other types of outstanding, viable claims for relief before the Board, cf. supra n.5, the AJ may dismiss an appeal as moot upon the agency's complete rescission of the action appealed. Cf. Powell v. McCormack, 395 U.S. 486, 498-99, 89 S. Ct. 1944, 1951-52, 23 L. Ed.2d 491 (1969) (where one claim has become moot, and the plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to show that he is entitled to any other remedy, the action should be dismissed as moot); Hoever v. Dept. of the Navy, 70 MSPR 386, 388-89 (1996) (dismissing as moot an IRA appeal in which the appellant was afforded an opportunity to prove his claim for consequential damages but failed to do so).

We further note that where an appellant has raised a nonfrivolous claim of discrimination, but has not specifically raised any claim for compensatory damages, the AJ should afford him a specific opportunity to raise such a claim before dismissing his appeal as moot based on the agency's complete rescission of the action appealed. Cf. Yates v. USPS, 70 MSPR 172, 180 (1996).

Regarding the appellant's argument below and on review that his claim for attorney fees also precluded the dismissal of this appeal as moot, we find that it is without merit. The Board has held that outstanding attorney fee issues do not prevent the dismissal of an appeal as moot. Koerner v. OPM, 51 MSPR 365, 367 (1991); Garstkiewicz v. USPS, 46 MSPR 689, 690 (1991); Young v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 541, 543 (1984). In addition, the Board has held that attorney fees may be awarded in an appeal that is dismissed as moot. Garstkiewicz, 46 MSPR at 690. Unlike attorney fees, compensatory damages could not be awarded in an appeal such as this, if it is dismissed as moot without addressing discrimination issues. Although an AJ has the discretion to bifurcate the compensatory damage issues for administrative convenience[9], such a bifurcation should not have the effect of depriving the appellant of his right to compensatory damages based on his discrimination claims. We therefore conclude that attorney fee issues are distinguishable from compensatory damages issues for purposes of determining mootness.

The AJ here ruled that she would bifurcate the compensatory damage issues, but ultimately did not reach those issues based on her dismissal of the entire appeal, including the discrimination claims, as moot.

A case is moot when the issues presented are no longer "live" or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the case. Wrighten v. Office of Personnel Management, 89 M.S.P.R. 163, ¶ 5 (2001). An appeal will be dismissed as moot if, by virtue of an intervening event, the Board cannot grant any effectual relief in favor of the appellant. Id. Thus, an agency's complete rescission of the action appealed, and an appellant's restoration to the status quo ante, may render an appeal moot. Id., ¶¶ 6-8. However, if an appellant raises a claim for compensatory damages that the Board has jurisdiction to adjudicate, the agency's complete rescission of the action appealed does not afford her all of the relief available before the Board and the appeal is not moot. Id., ¶ 9.

Thus, because the agency rescinded the appellant's removal and returned her to status quo ante, we must determine whether the Board has authority to award compensatory damages for discrimination and EEO reprisal claims. The Board's authority to adjudicate such claims arises from the "integrated scheme of administrative and judicial review" contained in the Civil Service Reform Act (CSRA) of 1978. United States v. Fausto, 484 U.S. 439, 445 (1988) (finding that a former Federal employee without Board appeal rights was not entitled to seek judicial review of his suspension), superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in Kaplan v. Conyers, 733 F.3d 1148 (Fed. Cir. 2013); CSRA, Pub. L. No. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111 (codified as amended in various sections of title 5, including as pertinent here, 5 U.S.C. §§ 7701-7703).

The CSRA does not address the Board's authority to award compensatory damages in connection with discrimination claims. See generally CSRA, Pub. L. No. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111. This omission is not surprising because such damages were not available until more than 10 years after the CSRA was enacted.[1] Cf. West v. Gibson, 527 U.S. 212, 217 (1999) (observing that it was "not surprising" that the statutory authority of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) does not specifically enumerate compensatory damages because such damages were not available when the statute in question was enacted). With the Civil Rights Act of 1991, Pub. L. No. 102-166, § 102, 105 Stat. 1071, 1072-74 (codified at 42 U.S.C. § 1981a), Congress authorized compensatory damages for both title VII and disability discrimination claims under 42 U.S.C. § 1981a(a)(1)-(2). Neither the Civil Rights Act of 1991, nor its legislative history, addresses whether the Board has the authority to award compensatory damages. 42 U.S.C. § 1981a; H.R. Rep. Nos. 104-40(I)-(II) (1991), reprinted in 1991 U.S.C.C.A.N. 549; see Savage, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 45 (observing that title VII does not provide the Board with enforcement authority). However, because the Board is charged with applying substantive discrimination law, we have long awarded compensatory damages. See Savage, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 45 (observing that the Board adjudicates substantive discrimination issues under the standard set forth in 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16(a)); Southerland v. Department of Defense, 122 M.S.P.R. 51, ¶ 12 (2014) (observing that the Board generally defers to the EEOC on substantive discrimination law).[3]

The Board previously has awarded compensatory damages in mixed cases, and our holding in Savage was not intended to disrupt the status quo on this issue.

This practice of awarding of damages is consistent with the structure of the CSRA. The CSRA provides for a complex interplay between the Board and the EEOC. An employee or applicant alleging discrimination in conjunction with an otherwise appealable action initially may elect either to file an EEO complaint with her agency or proceed directly to the Board. Lott v. Department of the Army, 82 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 6 (1999); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(a); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(b); see 5 U.S.C. §§ 7701(a), 7702(a)(1)-(2). However, regardless of the avenue chosen, the complaining individual's only right to an evidentiary hearing in such mixed cases is before the Board, not the EEOC. Rosso v. Department of Homeland Security, 113 M.S.P.R. 271, ¶ 11 (2010); see 5 U.S.C. §§ 7701(a)(1), 7702(a); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(d)(3) (providing that an agency issuing a final decision on a mixed-case complaint "shall advise the complainant of the right to appeal the matter to the [Board] (not EEOC)"); see also Gubisch v. Department of the Treasury, 36 M.S.P.R. 634, 637 (1988) (observing that the EEOC has no role in a mixed-case appeal unless and until the Board has issued a decision that complies with 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1)). In such appeals, "the Board shall...decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action in accordance with [its] appellate procedures under [5 U.S.C. §§ 7701 and 7702]." 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1); Savage, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 45.

An appellant who disagrees with the Board's decision may seek review from the EEOC. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(b)(1). The EEOC may decline to "consider" the Board's decision, consider and concur in the decision, or consider and disagree with the decision. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(b)(2)-(3). If the EEOC disagrees, it must refer the matter back to the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(b)(3), (b)(5)(B). The Board then reviews and either "concur[s] and adopt[s]" the EEOC's decision or reaffirms its own decision. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(c).

If the Board reaffirms its own decision, it is required to refer the matter to a Special Panel, consisting of one member each from the Board and the Commission, and a Presidential appointee. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(c)(2), (d)(1), (d)(6)(A). The Special Panel then issues a decision, "giv[ing] due deference to the respective expertise of the Board and [EEOC]." 5 U.S.C. § 7702(d)(2)(A)-(B).

At various stages in the process, if the employee has exhausted her administrative remedies, or if she has attempted to do so and sufficient time has elapsed, she has the right to proceed in Federal district court. 5 U.S.C. §§ 7702(a)(2)-(3), (b)(5)(A), (d)(2)(A), (e)(1), 7703(b)(2). There, she is entitled to de novo review. 5 U.S.C. §§ 7702(e)(3), 7703(c).

A Senate Report on the CSRA expressed the intent that the Board consider discrimination claims together with "the employee's inefficiency or misconduct" as "two sides of the same question." S. Rep. No. 95-969, at 53 (1978), reprinted in 1978 U.S.C.C.A.N. 2723, 2775. This would permit a "single unified personnel policy which took into account the requirements of all the various laws and goals governing Federal personnel management." Id. The mixed-case appeal process was intended to streamline adjudication, avoid "forum shopping and inconsistent decisions," and ensure that "the Board and the [EEOC] work together to resolve any differences." Id.

Similarly, a joint statement by a Senate and House conference committee expressed the goal to "maintain[ ] the principle of parity between the MSPB and EEOC" in the mixed-case appeal process. H.R. Rep. No. 95-1717, at 139 (1978) (Conf. Rep.), reprinted in 1978 U.S.C.C.A.N. 2860, 2873. Therefore, the Board's decision in a mixed-case appeal was to "include[ ] any remedial order the [EEOC]...may impose under law." H.R. Rep. No. 95-1717, at 140, reprinted in 1978 U.S.C.C.A.N. at 2873. The EEOC was to determine whether the Board correctly interpreted the laws "over which the EEOC has jurisdiction" and awarded an appropriate remedy. H.R. Rep. No. 95-1717, at 140, reprinted in 1978 U.S.C.C.A.N. at 2873-74.

Consistent with their relative roles in adjudicating appealable actions, the Board generally defers to the EEOC on issues of substantive discrimination law unless the EEOC's decision rests on civil service law for its support or is so unreasonable that it amounts to a violation of civil service law. Southerland, 122 M.S.P.R. 51, ¶ 12. The Civil Rights Act of 1991 provides for compensatory damages in matters brought pursuant to 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16. 42 U.S.C. § 1981a(a)(1); West, 527 U.S. at 217-23. Further, the EEOC has the authority to award compensatory damages pursuant to the Civil Rights Act of 1991. West, 527 U.S. at 217-20, 223. In finding such authority appropriate, the U.S. Supreme Court reasoned that it is consistent with a remedial scheme that requires exhaustion of administrative remedies to "encourag[e] quicker, less formal, and less expensive resolution of disputes within the Federal Government outside of court." Id. at 218-19.

This reasoning is equally applicable here, particularly because, as discussed above, an employee's only right to an administrative hearing on a discrimination claim related to an otherwise appealable action is before the Board. Further, because such cases necessarily contain allegations that discrimination prohibited by 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16 occurred, we find the reasoning in West extends to the Board's adjudication of these claims. See 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1)(B)(i); see also 5 U.S.C. § 7703(b)(2) (stating that "[c]ases of discrimination subject to the provisions of section 7702 of this title shall be filed under [42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16(c)] "). Although the Board stated in Savage that mixed-case appeals are decided using the Board's appellate procedures, it also recognized that the substantive standard for title VII claims in the Federal sector is set forth in 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16. Savage, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶¶ 44-45. Therefore, we find that Savage did not affirmatively diminish the Board's previous practice of awarding compensatory damages.

The EEOC believes that the Board is required to adjudicate an appellant's claim for any compensatory damages resulting from a discriminatory or retaliatory adverse action. Martin v. Department of the Air Force, 73 M.S.P.R. 590, 593-94 (1997). Although it reasonably may be argued that the question of whether compensatory damages are available in the administrative process involves the Board's jurisdiction, and therefore is a matter of civil service law, the Board previously has found that, at base, the issue is one of discrimination law. Crosby v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 263, ¶ 8 (1998). The Board reasoned that the authority for compensatory damages derives from the Civil Rights Act of 1991, which is discrimination law, and implicates not just the Board's authority, but that of the EEOC. Id. Thus, if an appellant prevails in an appeal before the Board based on a finding of discrimination, she may recover compensatory damages from the agency pursuant to the Civil Rights Act of 1991. Hocker v. Department of Transportation, 63 M.S.P.R. 497, 505 (1994), aff'd per curiam, 64 F.3d 676 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.202(c) (reflecting the Board's authority to award compensatory damages under the Civil Rights Act of 1991). We continue to defer to the EEOC's interpretation of the Board's ability to award compensatory damages, which we find is not so unreasonable as to amount to a violation of civil service law.

In light of our finding that Savage does not alter the Board's previous practice of awarding compensatory damages, we find that the appeal is not moot.

Finally, the administrative judge also correctly determined that the appellant's affirmative defense under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(9) does not preclude dismissal of the instant appeal as moot. When an appellant has raised what appears to be a further claim for compensatory damages before the Board, the agency's complete rescission of the adverse action does not afford the appellant all the relief that he could receive if the matter had been adjudicated and he had prevailed. See Antonio v. Department of the Air Force, 107 M.S.P.R. 626, ¶ 13 (2008). Although the appellant raised an affirmative defense of reprisal under section 2302(b)(9), it would provide no further relief, even if proven. Alleged violations of section 2302(b)(9) are not an independent source of Board jurisdiction. Booker v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 507, 510, aff'd, 982 F.2d 517 (Fed. Cir. 1992). Although the Board is authorized to adjudicate a claim of reprisal for activities protected under section 2302(b)(9) when such a claim is interposed as an affirmative defense to an otherwise appealable action, see Moss v. Department of the Air Force, 82 M.S.P.R. 309, ¶ 10, aff'd, 230 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table), it may not award compensatory damages under those circumstances, see Diehl v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 11 n.2 (1999). Further, the Board lacks the statutory authority to award consequential damages in cases where, as here, a claim of reprisal for activities protected under section 2302(b)(9) is interposed as an affirmative defense. Such relief may only be awarded in the context of a petition for corrective action brought by the Office of Special Counsel under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(g).
