b. Sale or Distribution

Involvement in on-duty sales of drugs may warrant removal, as in Parker v. USPS, 31 MSPR 58, 59-60 (1986), involving a GS-5 Clerk:

The appellant was charged with...criminal, dishonest, notoriously disgraceful or immoral conduct, or other conduct prejudicial to the agency. Specifically, the appellant arranged for another agency employee to purchase an ounce of cocaine from his stepbrother at the appellant's home. The appellant pled guilty...to a felony charge of possession and distribution of one ounce of cocaine, aiding and abetting....

...

We find that the presiding official erred in mitigating the penalty. The sustained charge, aiding and abetting the sale of drugs to another employee, is a serious offense, and the appellant's involvement with illegal drugs reflects adversely on his honesty and integrity....

The agency proved that it had a serious drug problem. The charge against the appellant was the result of an undercover drug investigation at the facility which resulted in the discovery that the appellant and nine other employees were involved in drug transactions. The other employees were all removed or resigned in the face of removal. Although the other employees were charged with selling drugs, the appellant's misconduct was sufficiently similar in that he also was directly involved in the sale of drugs. The record reflects that the transaction was initiated at work, involved several telephone conversations between the appellant and the employee arranging for the transaction, and terminated at the appellant's home where the employee, accompanied by the undercover inspector, purchased the cocaine for $2,500.00.

On appeal Parker was affirmed. Parker v. USPS, 819 F.2d 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1987). The court noted that some of the appellant's activities were conducted on-duty, observed that the appellant was convicted of a drug offense in criminal court as a result of his dealings, with sentence suspended, and concluded that nexus was established by a direct connection to service efficiency. The appellant aided and abetted the sale of drugs to another employee at his facility through a transaction that was arranged at least in part at work. Other involvement in drug sales warranted discipline. The Board sustained removal of a customs employee who assisted her husband in a large-scale drug importation scheme. McCants v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 125, 131 (1987). The Board did not find an agency in error for distinguishing its treatment of drug sellers and distributors, who were removed, and individuals found to be users only, who were suspended. Mutch v. USPS, 26 MSPR 224, 225 (1985).

The sale or distribution of illegal drugs is a serious offense permitting an inference of untrustworthiness. Dealers in controlled substances are often subject to demands for money not likely obtainable through lawful commercial channels, demands that may cause supervisors to legitimately question an employee's ability to safeguard pilferable items. Dietz v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 13, 14 (1985); see Rusnack v. Dept. of Commerce, 36 MSPR 551 (1988) (removal of a clerk responsible for accountability and inventory control of over $100,000,000 in equipment was justified, despite the employee's service in the position for more than a year following an indictment for cocaine possession). Possession of marijuana and its sale by a VA claims clerk to a patient suffering from alcohol and drug abuse warranted removal in Burrell v. VA, 14 MSPR 519, 520 (1983). Removal of a mailhandler was sustained for the on-duty distribution of one marijuana cigarette, characterized by the Board as a serious offense. Although the incident did not have an impact on his job, the agency mission was undermined by the damage to the employee's reputation for honesty and integrity. Paglia v. USPS, 23 MSPR 192, 194 (1984); see Finnegan v. USPS, 23 MSPR 94, 96-97 (1984) (removal of a distribution clerk for on-duty sales of drugs to postal inspectors). On-base solicitation to sell marijuana supported the nexus between discharge and service efficiency when there was evidence of a drug abuse problem on base among civilian and military personnel. Sayers v. Marine Corps (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-743); see Bernal v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 283, 284 (1984) (meatcutter removed for the sale of marijuana to another agency employee on agency premises during duty hours).

Mitigation was not in order for an individual who sold marijuana to an informant: some of the transaction occurred at work, the appellant was involved in a job that required great care, and the incident resulted in publicity. Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 572, 575-76 (1990) (machinist at a naval shipyard removed for the possession and sale of marijuana off duty and off premises; mitigating factors were overcome by the nature of the job and the risk that drug use could impair performance in the overhaul of nuclear submarines; drug transactions were initiated at work and the incident received publicity in the local press, "thereby harming the agency's reputation and causing a negative impact on the public trust in the agency's mission"); see Grady v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 426, 429 (1984) (GS-5 Secretary removed for selling $25 worth of marijuana to an investigator; the appellant's access to classified information was revoked as a result of the incident and the agency would be unreasonably burdened if it had to create a nonsensitive position for the employee). An employee convicted of a felony and a misdemeanor for distribution of a controlled substance, "speed," was properly removed. His cooperation with law enforcement authorities did not demonstrate rehabilitative potential because the cooperation was to assist the appellant in his criminal charges. Stephens v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 234, 237-38 (1987) (decision does not state whether the involvement with drugs was on or off duty); see Campbell v. DLA, 37 MSPR 691, 696-97 (1988) (removal of a head accounting technician for the off-duty sale of marijuana as well as possession of the drug on agency premises; the agency considered the viability of alternative sanctions; removal was chosen because of the "nature of the sustained charges and the appellant's fiduciary role."); Person v. USPS, 22 MSPR 618, 621 (1984) (city carrier removed for a conviction resulting from the off-duty nonprofit sale of cocaine to a friend).
