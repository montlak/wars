Denial of restoration of law enforcement duties was justified in Revak v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-12-0265-C-1 (NP 9/24/2013):

On petition for review, the appellant contests the administrative judge's finding that the agency complied with the Board's order to cancel the appellant's demotion and restore him to his permanent position. The purpose of a Board order cancelling an adverse action is to restore the appellant as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). This principle applies not only where the Board has ordered an action cancelled but also where, as here, the Board has mitigated the penalty. Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 97, 101 (1997). Return to the status quo ante requires that the appellant be placed back in his former position or in a position substantially equivalent in scope and status to his former position. Id.

The Board may not base a finding of compliance with a reinstatement order solely upon a restoration to title and grade. Doe v. Department of Justice, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 7 (2003). Rather, the Board must conduct an assessment of the scope and responsibilities of the new position compared with the appellant's former position. Id. The appellant's duties and responsibilities may be changed only if the agency has demonstrated a compelling reason for doing so. Id. For example, the Board has held that where an individual loses a security clearance required for the position he held prior to a reversed removal action, the agency may comply with a reinstatement order by assigning the employee to a position with similar duties that does not require a security clearance. See, e.g., LaBatte v. Department of the Air Force, 58 M.S.P.R. 586, 594 (1993). Similarly, in Marren v. Department of Justice, 32 M.S.P.R. 285 (1987), the Board found the agency in compliance where a border patrol agent was not restored to his former position because he lacked a government driver's license. Id. at 287. In Doe, the agency reassigned the appellant, a criminal investigator with the Immigration & Naturalization Service, to duties that would not require him to testify in any criminal prosecution, based on letters from the U.S. Attorneys Offices expressing their concern that the appellant had perjured himself during a suppression hearing and therefore could not be called as a witness absent exceptional circumstances. Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 9. The Board concluded that these concerns were similar to the denial of a security clearance and the failure to maintain a driver's license, as in the cases cited above, and constituted a compelling reason for not returning the appellant to the exact duties he had performed prior to his removal. Id., ¶¶ 15, 17. The common thread in such cases is that an outside event or determination rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of his former position and that as long as that incapacity exists, the agency is not obligated to restore the appellant to his prior position. Id., ¶ 13.

The Board may reach a different conclusion, however, where the agency exercises its own discretion in altering the appellant's duties. For example, in Black v. Department of Justice, 85 M.S.P.R. 650 (2000), the agency argued that it was not required to restore the appellant's special agent badge and credentials because granting those items was discretionary with the District Director, but the agency cited no external determination, such as failure to obtain a security clearance, as a basis for the District Director's position. The Board ruled the agency was not in compliance. Id. at 653-54. Similarly, in Clemons v. Smithsonian Institution, 50 M.S.P.R. 74 (1991), where the Board reversed a removal for theft of a can of soda, the Board rejected the agency's contention that it had a compelling reason not to restore the appellant to the night shift on its belief that the appellant was not sufficiently trustworthy to work nights when close supervision was not available. Id.; see also Briggs v. National Council on Disability, 68 M.S.P.R. 296, 302 (1995) (an agency cannot refuse to comply with a Board reinstatement order based on reasons that were rejected by the Board in the decision reversing the action).

Here, it is undisputed that the agency complied with the Board's order to the extent it canceled the appellant's demotion and restored the appellant to his permanent Zone Officer position. However, it is also undisputed that following the appellant's 14-day suspension, the agency immediately detailed him to a Park Ranger position which, unlike his permanent Zone Officer position, does not include law enforcement duties. The appellant does not assert that the duties of the Zone Officer and Park Ranger positions are otherwise dissimilar. The matter in dispute is whether the agency had a compelling reason for not returning the appellant to law enforcement duty despite his lack of a current, favorably adjudicated Single Scope Background Investigation (SSBI).

The appellant contends that similarly situated Federal Wildlife Officers (FWOs) were permitted to retain law enforcement duties despite having expired SSBIs. In his petition for enforcement, he identified 43 agency law enforcement officers whom he alleged were exercising law enforcement duties despite having expired SSBIs, and 16 more whom he alleged were exercising law enforcement duties despite having SSBIs that had either expired or were due to expire "within a few months." According to the appellant, the agency improperly singled him out by not permitting him to perform law enforcement duties. Id

As the administrative judge noted in the initial decision, the unsworn statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence. Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995). However, the appellant correctly observes that the October 12, 2012 declaration by James Hall, Chief of Law Enforcement, National Wildlife, does constitute evidence. In his declaration, Hall averred that of the first 59 people or positions listed in the appellant's petition, 35 were FWOs within the National Wildlife Refuge System, of whom 27 had current, favorably adjudicated SSBIs, 6 were undergoing the investigative process, and 2 were no longer employed with the FWS. Hall further declared that the remaining 24 individuals were not law enforcement officers, but supervisors, of whom 15 had a current favorably adjudicated SSBI, 8 were in the investigative process, and one was "currently arranging for an investigation." The record therefore shows that at least six FWOs were permitted to perform law enforcement duties without having a current, favorably adjudicated SSBI.

However, we do not agree that these FWOs were similarly situated to the appellant. It is the practice of the agency that, if an SSBI reinvestigation is initiated before the current SSBI is due to be renewed, the FWO will be allowed to maintain law enforcement authority until the new SSBI is completed and favorably adjudicated. There is nothing in the record to suggest that reinvestigations for the six FWOs in question were not timely initiated prior to the expiration of their current SSBIs. By contrast, it is undisputed that a reinvestigation of the appellant had not been initiated before his SSBI expired. Consequently, the agency was neither required nor permitted to delegate law enforcement authority to the appellant prior to the completion of a new, favorably adjudicated SSBI.

The appellant also argues that the agency was responsible for failing to timely initiate a reinvestigation prior to the expiration of his SSBI. In that respect, he argues, this case differs from Doe, in which the assignment of the appellant to different duties was entirely the result of an outside event or determination. This distinction does not warrant a different result. Assuming arguendo that the agency improperly failed to initiate a reinvestigation before the appellant's SSBI expired, that error occurred before the demotion action for which status quo ante relief was ordered, and therefore is not relevant to the issue of compliance. We further note that, while the appellant did not have a current, favorably adjudicated SSBI at the time of his reinstatement, and therefore could not be given law enforcement duties at that time, the agency has since initiated the process of obtaining a new SSBI for the appellant.

We conclude that the facts of this case are akin to those in Doe, LaBatte, and Marren, and that the agency had a compelling reason not to return the appellant to law enforcement duties upon his reinstatement. Moreover, as noted above, the Zone Officer and Park Ranger positions are not substantially different except insofar as the latter position does not involve law enforcement duties. We therefore affirm the finding of the administrative judge that the agency is compliance with the reinstatement order. See Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 19.

We turn now to the central issue presented by this case, the effect of the appellant's duty assignment on the question of compliance with the Board's final order. The Board has held that, where an individual loses the security clearance required for the position he held prior to a reversed removal action, the agency properly assigned the employee to a different position with similar duties that did not require a security clearance. LaBatte v. Department of the Air Force, 58 M.S.P.R. 586, 594 (1993); Gray v. Department of the Navy, 29 M.S.P.R. 281, 283-84 (1985). Likewise, in Marren v. Department of Justice, 32 M.S.P.R. 285, 287 (1987), the agency was found in compliance where a border patrol agent was not restored to his former position because he lacked a government drivers' license. In that case, the agency would not reissue a license upon the appellant's reinstatement because he was serving probation following a conviction for driving under the influence of alcohol. See also Burrell v. Department of the Navy, 43 M.S.P.R. 174, 176 (1990) (agency justified in assigning a motor vehicle operator to other duties because his poor driving record caused the agency to withhold certification for him to transport explosives).

The common thread in these cases is that an outside event or determination rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of his prior position. Thus, the Board found that, as long as that incapacity existed, the agency was not obligated to restore the appellant to his prior position.

In contrast to the cases cited above, in Black v. Department of Justice, 85 M.S.P.R. 650, 653-54 (2000), INS argued that it was not required to restore Ms. Black's special agent badge, and credentials because granting those items was discretionary with the District Director. The agency in Black cited no external determination, such as a failure to obtain a security clearance, as a basis for the District Director's position. The Board ruled that the agency was not in compliance. Similarly, in Clemons v. Smithsonian Institution, 50 M.S.P.R. 74, 79-80 (1991), the agency argued that it had a compelling reason not to restore the appellant to the night shift after the Board reversed a removal for theft of a can of soda. The agency in that case opined that Mr. Clemons was not sufficiently trustworthy to work nights when close supervision was not available. The Board, however, found that the agency's argument regarding the appellant's trustworthiness did not justify failing to restore him to the status quo ante. In Briggs v. National Council on Disability, 68 M.S.P.R. 296, 302 (1995), the Board summarized this area of the law by stating that "[i]t is well established that an agency cannot refuse to comply with a Board reinstatement order based on reasons that were rejected by the Board in the decision reversing the action."

In the instant case, the concerns of the United States Attorneys Offices regarding the appellant are similar to the denial of a security clearance and the failure to obtain a drivers' license in the cases discussed above. The agency's decision regarding the appellant's duties, while related to the matters addressed in the removal appeal, is based on concerns of the United States Attorneys Offices and not the matters that were adjudicated before the Board. In other words, the agency does not rely on the charges that the Board did not sustain, but on an independent event--the concerns of the United States Attorneys Offices.

While the appellant essentially wants the Board to order the United States Attorneys Offices to allow him to testify, the Board lacks the authority to do so. Nor does DHS have any authority over the United States Attorneys Offices. Because INS has been moved to DHS, the appellant's employing agency and the United States Attorneys Offices are now part of two separate cabinet level departments. Thus, the Justice Department, although still listed in the case caption, is no longer a party to this dispute. The Board, therefore, can not address the limitations that have been placed on the appellant by a component of the Justice Department, and the relevant inquiry in this case is the agency's compliance obligations in light of the limitations imposed by the United States Attorneys Offices.[4]

There is no hint that such a high level official was involved in this dispute. Nor is there any suggestion that INS officials pressured the United States Attorneys Offices into making the statements they made. In fact, the record reflects that the United States Attorneys Offices were concerned about the appellant testifying before the Board appeal arose.

In his petition for review from the administrative judge's initial decision on the merits, the appellant disputed the administrative judge's findings regarding the sustained charge. The Board denied the appellant's petition by Final Order, thus leaving undisturbed the administrative judge's factual findings and her conclusion that the agency proved the first charge.

The agency did not reassign the appellant to a new position. Rather, the agency returned him to his former position as ordered by the Board. The agency decided, based on the sustained charge, that it is best at this time not to assign the appellant to refrigerant duties. Thus, it detailed him to a position without refrigerant duties in the same shop, under the same supervisor, and at the same grade level as his Air Conditioning Equipment Mechanic position. For the following reasons, we find that the agency's action does not place it in noncompliance with the Board's Final Order.

In Doe v. Department of Justice, 95 M.S.P.R. 198 (2003), the Board considered the effect of a change in duties on the question of compliance with a Board final order. In that case, the appellant, a criminal investigator for the Immigration & Naturalization Service, was assigned duties that he alleged were inconsistent with his pre-removal duties and which would not involve testifying in court. The agency argued that it changed the appellant's duties because two United States Attorneys Offices had informed the agency that they would not call Mr. Doe as a witness absent exceptional circumstances because of a belief that he had committed perjury. Based on these facts, the Board found that the agency had a compelling reason for the reassignment.

In Doe, the Board reiterated its precedent that "[i]t is well established that an agency cannot refuse to comply with a Board reinstatement order based on reasons that were rejected by the Board in the decision reversing the action." Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198 ¶ 14 (emphasis added), quoting Briggs v. National Council on Disability, 68 M.S.P.R. 296, 302 (1995); see also Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 55 M.S.P.R. 317 (1992) (an agency may not contest a Board decision on the merits in the context of a compliance proceeding), vacated on other grounds, 69 M.S.P.R. 503 (1996). The Board discussed a number of cases where it found that an agency had shown a compelling reason for not restoring an individual to his former position and identified the common element that existed in the cases as "an outside event or determination [that] rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of his prior position." Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198 ¶ 13.

This case is the reverse of Doe. Here, the agency has not given the appellant refrigerant duties based on reasons that were accepted by the Board in its final decision. The Board found that the appellant committed serious misconduct in not keeping accurate records of the freon inventories entrusted to him. It found that the appellant's shortcomings resulted in a $21,000.00 fine from the EPA and possible future fines as well if he were to continue handling refrigerants, as implied in the initial decision's conclusion that the appellant's failure to maintain proper records of his freon inventory posed a "serious risk to the agency of being found in violation of EPA regulations." Before the Board, the agency avers that "'[i]n the interest of maintaining a safe environment,' avoiding substantial and foreseeable regulatory penalties, and fulfilling the agencies [sic] obligations to the local community, appellant cannot be authorized to perform refrigerant duties." In his response, the appellant does not dispute this statement, arguing only the back pay issue.

Under the circumstances, we find that the agency has articulated compelling reasons, unrebutted by the appellant, for detailing the appellant to a position where he does not have refrigerant duties. Indeed, the "outside event or determination [that] rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of his prior position," Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198 ¶ 13, was the Board's final determination that the agency proved the charge that the appellant had continually, and after repeated counseling and ongoing training and supervision, failed to maintain proper and correct records of the freon inventories for which he was responsible. The fact that the agency did not file a petition for review of the initial decision is of no consequence since the charge at issue was sustained. The only thing that happened was that the administrative judge mitigated the demotion penalty to a 14-day suspension. The mitigation does not change the fact that charge one was sustained. Thus, the agency exercised its management discretion in assigning the appellant to duties in his shop other than duties involving refrigerants. See, e.g., D'Leo v. Department of the Navy, 53 M.S.P.R. 44, 48 (1992) (the Board does not have independent jurisdiction over matters concerning an agency's authority to make decisions to assign duties to its employees); Taylor v. Department of Health & Human Services, 40 M.S.P.R. 106, 111 ("the agency has discretion to assign personnel and use its staff in the most efficient manner"), aff'd, 891 F.2d 299 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table).

When a penalty was mitigated although charges of inappropriate personal conduct were sustained, the agency could reassign the employee out of a position with the OIG office that required a high level of personal deportment, but the agency had to insure that the employee's assigned job was substantially similar to the job she held prior to the reassignment, Miller v. Dept. of Army, 109 MSPR 41, 45-46 ¶¶ 13-15, 2008 MSPB 104 (2008):

The agency next argues that the appellant is unfit for her prior position based on the misconduct for which she has been disciplined. According to the agency, such misconduct precludes the appellant from contributing to the OIG's mission, which involves "constantly look[ing] over everyone else's shoulder" and "remain[ing] above reproach in their own deportment." The agency's position is consistent with the administrative judge's findings in the initial decision. The administrative judge found as credible the agency's testimony and documentary evidence reflecting its view that OIG personnel are held to the highest personal standards. The administrative judge determined that the appellant's conduct was unsuitable, tended to detract from her character, and reflected a betrayal of trust. The agency's reason for reassigning the appellant out of OIG was accepted by the Board in what became the final decision. See, e.g., House v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 530, ¶ 22 (2005) (The agency's reasons for not restoring the appellant to his former position were accepted by the Board in its final decision). The "outside event or determination [that] rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of [her] prior position," Doe v. Department of Justice, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 13 (2003), was the Board's determination that, by her actions, the agency proved the charge that she committed conduct unbecoming a federal employee. That the penalty was mitigated does not change the fact that the first charge was sustained. Under the circumstances, the record reflects that the agency has articulated an overriding interest or compelling reason for not returning the appellant to her position in the OIG.

Nevertheless, to be in compliance with the Board's order, the agency must also show that it has reassigned the appellant to a position which is substantially similar to her former position. Here, the responsibilities of the appellant's new position are not substantially similar to those of her former position. See Kerr, 726 F.2d at 733 (the Board must determine whether the actual duties and responsibilities to which the employee was returned are either the same as or substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties and responsibilities held prior to the wrongful discharge); Mann, 29 M.S.P.R. at 274-75 (where the Board finds that the interests of the agency are so compelling that they override the appellant's entitlement to return to his former position, the Board must address the question of whether the position to which the appellant has been reassigned is in fact substantially similar to the former position).

While the Support Specialist position appears to include a diverse list of duties, the appellant alleges that there was no work available that pertained to the position and that she was instead asked to clean, shred documents, and run errands. The appellant attached an e-mail in which she requested more work and a reply to her e-mail indicating there was simply not enough work for the position. The agency concedes that the two positions are not similar and states that "it is illogical to expect similar tasking duties in a non-IG office." Based on these submissions, we conclude that the agency did not reassign the appellant to a position which is substantially similar to her former OIG position. In order to be in compliance with the Board's order, the agency must return the appellant to her former position of Inspector General Investigative Specialist or reassign her to a substantially similar position.

DHHS, 115 MSPR 327, 2010 MSPB 242 (2010), post), considered an agency's justification, based on an agreement or relationship between the agency and a state organization, for reassigning the appellant, who prevailed in his appeal to the Board from termination from the position of GS-13 public health advisor:

When the Board finds a personnel action unwarranted, the aim is to place the appellant, as nearly as possible, in the situation he would have been in had the wrongful personnel action not occurred. See House v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 530, ¶ 9 (2005); Mascarenas v. Department of Defense, 57 M.S.P.R. 425, 430 (1993); see also Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). This is called status quo ante relief. It is the agency's burden to prove its compliance with a Board order. See New v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 106 M.S.P.R. 217, ¶ 6 (2007), aff'd, 293 Fed. Appx. 779 (Fed. Cir. 2008); Donovan v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 628, ¶¶ 6-7, review dismissed, 213 Fed. Appx. 978 (Fed. Cir. 2006).

The agency has failed to demonstrate compliance with regard to the appellant's position.

Part of status quo ante relief requires an agency to, among other things, reinstate the employee to his former position. Miller v. Department of the Army, 109 M.S.P.R. 41, ¶ 11 (2008); Bullock v. Department of the Air Force, 80 M.S.P.R. 361, ¶ 5 (1998); see Kerr, 726 F.2d at 733. Where an agency has not reinstated the appellant to his former position and duties, the agency must have a strong overriding interest or compelling reasons for not doing so. Miller, 109 M.S.P.R. 41, ¶ 11; Walker v. Department of the Army, 90 M.S.P.R. 136, ¶ 16 (2001); Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361, ¶ 5.

In the instant case, it is undisputed that the agency has not placed the appellant in the position he occupied at the time of his termination. As discussed above, however, the administrative judge found that the agency had placed the appellant in a position similar in responsibility and required expertise to the position he occupied at the time of his termination. Nevertheless, the administrative judge concluded that the agency was in noncompliance because it had failed to show a strong overriding interest or compelling reasons justifying its assignment of the appellant to a different position.

In its submission to the Board, the agency explains that it accomplishes its mission by, among other things, assigning agency employees to work with the various state public health departments. Prior to his termination, the appellant was assigned to work with the Louisiana Public Health Department. According to the agency, assignees work in state public health departments at the request of the state government, and a state public health department can request that an agency assignee be reassigned out the state. With regard to the appellant, the agency provided a copy of a letter from Dr. M. Rony Francois, Assistant Secretary of the Louisiana Department of Health and Hospitals, specifically stating that the State of Louisiana "elects not to accept [the appellant]" as an assignee.

The question before us is whether the agency has now shown a strong overriding interest or compelling reasons for not restoring the appellant to his public health advisor position in Louisiana. In Doe v. Department of Justice, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, VII 9, 16-17 (2003), the Board found that there was a compelling reason for reassigning a criminal investigator because the offices of two United States Attorneys indicated that they would not allow him to testify in criminal proceedings absent exceptional circumstances. In that case, the Board discussed a number of other cases where it had found that an agency had shown a compelling reason for not restoring an individual to his former position and identified the common element that existed in those cases as "an outside event or determination [that] rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of his prior position." Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 13. For example, the Board noted that it had held that where an individual loses the security clearance required for the position he held prior to a reversed removal action, the agency may properly reassign the employee to a different position with similar duties that does not require a security clearance. LaBatte v. Department of the Air Force, 58 M.S.P.R. 586, 594 (1993); Gray v. Department of the Navy, 29 M.S.P.R. 281, 283-84 (1985). Similarly, in Marren v. Department of Justice, 32 M.S.P.R. 285, 287 (1987), the Board found that an agency was in compliance where a border patrol agent was not restored to his former position because he lacked a government drivers' license.

We discern little difference between the circumstances in Doe and the other cases discussed above and the facts of the instant case. As in those cases, an outside event or determination has rendered the appellant incapable of performing the duties of his prior position. Accordingly, based on Dr. Francois's letter, we find that the agency has shown a compelling reason for reassigning the appellant and is in compliance in that regard.[2]

[2] In his submission to the Board, the appellant argues that, because Dr. Francois lacked personal knowledge of his work performance with the State of Louisiana, the letter was not sworn nor made under penalty of perjury, and it does not cite any evidence, Dr. Francois's letter was insufficient to establish a strong overriding interest or compelling reason for not restoring the appellant to his position in Louisiana. The appellant's argument is misplaced. Dr. Francois's letter establishes that the State of Louisiana did not want the appellant to serve as an agency assignee. The appellant has provided nothing questioning the agency's assertion that public health advisors serve in a particular state only with the consent of the state government.

Where a compelling reason exists for reassigning the appellant to a position other than the one he encumbered at the separation, the agency must establish that the duties and responsibilities of the position the employee has been assigned to are substantially equivalent in scope and status to those of the position the employee previously held. Miller, 109 M.S.P.R. 41, ¶ 11; Walker, 90 M.S.P.R. 136, ¶ 16; Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361, ¶ 5. In analyzing such an issue, the Board must look beyond the title and grade of the positions involved and must conduct an assessment of the scope of the duties and responsibilities of the new position compared with the scope of the duties and responsibilities of the appellant's former position. See Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 7; Joos v. Department of the Treasury, 79 M.S.P.R. 342, 347 (1998).

As mentioned above, the administrative judge reviewed the position descriptions of the public health advisor position occupied by the appellant at the time of his termination and the public health analyst position he currently occupies and found that they were similar in responsibility and expertise. Before the Board, the appellant objects to the administrative judge's determination and asserts that the position he has been placed in is a "sham teleworking" position. In a statement made under penalty of perjury submitted with his objections, the appellant essentially states that he has been placed in a full-time involuntary telework status, he has not been to his assigned duty station nor met face-to-face with an agency employee for a year and a half, and on an average day he speaks with his supervisor for about five minutes and does little or no work commensurate with his grade and position.

As set forth above, the agency bears the burden of proof regarding compliance with the Board's final order. See New, 106 M.S.P.R. 217, ¶ 6; Donovan, 101 M.S.P.R. 628, ¶¶ 6-7. An appellant may rebut the agency's evidence of compliance by making specific, nonconclusory, and supported assertions of continued noncompliance. See New, 106 M.S.P.R. 217, ¶ 6; Donovan, 101 M.S.P.R. 628, ¶ 7.

The appellant's statement made under penalty of perjury regarding the purported nature of his position and duties constitutes the required specific, nonconclusory, and supported assertions of continued noncompliance. The agency has offered nothing to rebut the appellant's assertions. Thus, we conclude that the agency has failed to demonstrate compliance with the Board's final order.

It argues that the night shift does not afford the necessary supervision provided during the day shift and, consequently, that the night shift security guards must be beyond reproach in regard to integrity and honesty. The agency further asserts that the appellant does not meet this standard because he committed a theft, and argues that it should be permitted to reassign him to another shift.

The administrative judge found, and we concur, that although the appellant did in fact take the property, the seriousness of the offense was mitigated by several factors: (1) the value of the item was de minimis: (2) the appellant reported the break-in to his supervisor; (3) the appellant did not take more valuable property which was available; (4) the appellant admitted the misconduct; and (5) the appellant did not attempt to deceive agency investigators. In other Board cases involving more egregious conduct by security guards, the Board has found employees sufficiently trustworthy to be returned to their former positions.... While the theft of a soda from an agency cafeteria cannot be condoned, it is hardly on a scale of larcenous conduct that warrants close and continuing supervision. The factors outlined above as well as the fact that the record does not show any prior instances of misconduct all indicate that the appellant is sufficiently trustworthy to be placed on the night shift. Consequently, we reject the agency's arguments regarding the appellant's trustworthiness.

The agency asserts that it made a legitimate, nondiscriminatory management decision in not reinstating the appellant to his former night shift position. It asserts that the reassignment was not done to circumvent compliance because the appellant was placed in the same position at the same grade and base pay as his former position. In support of its argument that the Board may not review its failure to return the appellant to the night shift, the agency cites to Spinks v. United States Postal Service, 621 F.2d 987 (9th Cir. 1980). In that decision, the court held that reduction of an employee's night differential pay due to a shift change does not constitute a reduction in pay necessitating the application of grievance procedures reserved for adverse actions. However, the agency's reliance on this decision is misplaced. Spinks involved an alleged adverse action in which the appellant specifically brought the case to appeal his loss of shift differential pay. In contrast, the issue in this case concerns compliance with a previous Board order in an enforcement proceeding brought under 5 USCA § 1204(a)(2) (West. Supp. 1990). While the appellant's reassignment and resulting loss of pay differential would generally not have been appealable as adverse actions, they are within this Board's jurisdiction to consider in a compliance case....

The agency also cites to Gray v. Department of Navy, 29 MSPR 281 (1985), in support of its position. In that case, the Board found that reassignment of the appellant to a position that did not require a security clearance restored him as nearly as possible to the status quo ante where the appellant's security clearance had been revoked. In that case, however, the Board held that it had no authority to order reinstatement of a security clearance. It further held that because the appellant required a security clearance to perform his former job, the agency's reassignment of the appellant to an equivalent position not requiring a security clearance was sufficient to comply with the Board's order. This is not the case here. The agency does not assert that the appellant's alleged unsuitability for night shift work is in any way related to security clearance considerations.

...In its response to the appellant's motion for compliance dated September 24, 1990, the agency alleged that there were no positions available for the appellant on the night shift. This issue was not addressed by the administrative judge, and the agency did not subsequently raise it in its brief supporting its disagreement with the administrative judge's recommendation. The agency may therefore be deemed to have abandoned this claim. In any event, the agency's claim that no night shift positions were available is not dispositive in this case. The fact that the agency filled the appellant's former position does not establish a compelling reason for failing to reinstate the appellant to a position on the night shift. See Williams v. Department of Health and Human Services, 32 MSPR 259, 262 (1987), citing Mann, 29 MSPR at 275.

Agency justifications for changing jobs or duties of an appellant who is ordered restored to the position may fail for lack of proof supporting the justification offered. In Vincent v. DOJ, 32 MSPR 263, 267-68 (1987), an appellant's removal from the position of correctional officer was mitigated to a suspension. The charges were that he had an inappropriate relationship with an inmate, failed to report contact with a former inmate's friend, breached security, concealed a fact during an investigation, and provided a false statement during an agency investigation. On his return to duty, he was reassigned from the low-security installation he worked at prior to removal, the Allenwood prison camp, to a nearby maximum-security installation, Lewisburg Penitentiary. The agency did not comply with its reinstatement order, i.e., the agency did not present sufficient reasons for the reassignment. The pre- and post-reassignment positions were compared in detail. The positions at Allenwood and Lewisburg were the same, but the duties were somewhat different, and the type of inmate was different at the two institutions: the risk of working at the maximum security installation was greater. The agency sought to justify its decision by arguing that the employees at the prior location would not trust the appellant. The Board responded: "We note that the agency's argument that it could no longer trust appellant in his position is identical to its argument for removing appellant from his position. This argument was rejected by the administrative judge in the initial decision." In Williams v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 259, 262 (1987), it was noted--with significance as to proof in compliance proceedings--that the agency's reasons for its actions were stated only in its response to the petition for enforcement. Statements made in the response are not evidence. See Gamel v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 168, 172 (1989) (discounting allegations of untrustworthiness and rejecting as speculative agency concerns about the possibility of future violations of security regulations); cf. Nielson v. Dept. of Navy, 32 MSPR 332, 336-37 (1987) (remanding for regional determination whether agency had a strong, overriding interest for placing the appellant in a policymaking position with limited supervisory responsibility rather than returning him to his original operational position with extensive operational and budgetary responsibilities).
