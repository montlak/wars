2. Timeliness

Timeliness of a submission is controlled by FLRA regulations governing service of documents. See 5 CFR 2424.2(g) and 5 CFR 2429.27 (2022). Timeliness determinations depend on the version of FLRA regulations effective when a case originated. Those regulations have been revised over time. The time for filing a statement of position begins to run from the date of receipt by the agency head of the completed union negotiability appeal. AFSCME Local 2027 and ACTION, 12 FLRA 643, 643 (1983); cf. NTEU and DHS, C&BP, 72 FLRA 752, 753 (2022) ((Chairman DuBester concurring in part, dissenting in part) (untimeliness not excused if service is timely on agency head and delayed filing of the agency's submission occurred when transmittal of a copy to the agency representative was delayed); SEIU Local 200-B and VAMC, Syracuse, 44 FLRA 821, 823 (1992) (agency has 30 days to file from receipt of union's corrected petition for review). If there is a conflict as to when the agency received the union's petition, the union should supply FLRA with the postal receipt showing the date of agency receipt. Int'l Org. of Masters, Mates and Pilots and Panama Canal Comm'n, 36 FLRA 555, 558 (1990). If the response to the agency statement of position is filed by personal delivery, the filing date is the date FLRA receives the paperwork. 5 CFR 2429.21(b) (2022); SEIU Local 150 and VAMC, Milwaukee, 35 FLRA 521, 523 (1990). The agency's 15-day reply limit runs from receipt of the union's response by the agency, not its representative, and a delay caused by the agency's internal mail system does not excuse untimely filings. AFGE Local 520 and VA Reg'l Office Columbia, SC, 60 FLRA 615, 615-16 (2005). Service by commercial delivery is effective on deposit with that service. 5 CFR 2429.27(c-d) (2022). Under earlier regulations, Federal Express was not a component of the Postal Service. If the document was served by Federal Express, it was deemed filed on the date it was received by the Authority. AFGE Local 3434 and NASA Marshall Space Flight Ctr., 49 FLRA 382, 384 (1994). An affidavit coupled with additional evidence may establish the date of service of a statement of position, when the filing party contends that the metered postmark does not show the correct filing date. Hawaii FEMTC and DOD, Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard, 57 FLRA 450, 452 (2001). During the Pandemic starting in early 2020, FLRA is not accepting in-person filings. The FLRA website should be checked to determine when in-person filings will again be accepted.

An untimely agency statement of position on a negotiability issue will not be considered, unless there are extraordinary circumstances warranting a waiver of the time limits under 5 CFR § 2429.23(b). See IAM Lodge 2135 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 43 FLRA 1202, 1204 (1992) (as to response to agency statement of position, "Delays resulting from a party's internal administrative or clerical procedures do not establish extraordinary circumstances sufficient to warrant Authority reconsideration of its decision to decline to consider a document because it was untimely filed"); cf. NFFE Local 2015 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., 53 FLRA 967, 969-70 (1997) (late union response was not waived; despite failure of FLRA case management order to state a date for union response to agency's statement of position, information should have been apparent from structure of statute, regulation, and receipt of agency position statement). "Where a party does not respond to a show-cause order, the Authority has held that it will not consider the deficient filing that prompted the order." AFGE Local 1547 and Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB, 64 FLRA 642, 642 (2010) (vacated on other grounds, Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB v. FLRA, 844 F.3d 957 (D.C. Cir. 2016)). That does not mean, however, that the agency suffers a default. FLRA will look to the rationale that the agency offered to the union in the declaration of nonnegotiability. Even if no rationale was offered, FLRA still analyzes the proposals to determine if they are barred from negotiation by law. AFGE, Gen. Comm. of AFGE for SSA Locals and SSA, 23 FLRA 329, 329 (1986); see NTEU and DHS, C&BP, 72 FLRA 752, 753 (2022) ((Chairman DuBester concurring in part, dissenting in part) (if the SOP is barred as untimely, FLRA will consider rationale offered in the agency's nonnegotiability declaration, e.g., the assertion that the proposal violates management rights); AFGE Local 3172 and DHHS, SSA, Vallejo Dist. Office, 35 FLRA 1276, 1277-78 (1990) (FLRA will decide case if agency articulates allegation of nonnegotiability to union, but agency then fails to file timely statement of position).

AFT Indian Educators Fed. and Dept. of Interior, BIA, 63 FLRA 585, 585 (2009), noted FLRA's treatment (or lack of treatment) of a belated agency statement of position:

The Agency's SOP was due on January 22, 2007. On February 6, 2007, the Agency filed a motion to waive time limits and requested an extension of time to file its SOP, asserting that waiver was warranted because Agency counsel was not notified by the Agency of the Union's petition until after the time to file an SOP had expired. The Agency filed an SOP on February 20, 2007 and the Union filed a timely response.

Requests for waivers of time limits may be granted only in "extraordinary circumstances." 5 C.F.R. § 2429.23. The Agency's failure timely to notify its counsel of the Union petition does not constitute an extraordinary circumstance. In this regard, it is well-established that parties are "responsible for being knowledgeable" of statutory and regulatory filing requirements. AFGE, Local 2065, 50 FLRA 538, 539-40 (1995). Accordingly, we do not consider the SOP. However, in accordance with Authority precedent, we consider the Union's reply to the SOP, because it complied with all filing requirements. See Marine Eng'rs' Beneficial Assoc, Dist. No. 1-PCD, 60 FLRA 828, 829 (2005). As they are properly part of the record, we consider the Agency's allegation of nonnegotiability (Allegation) attached to the Union's petition, and the record of the post-petition conference (Conf. Report). Id.

If the union's response to an agency statement of position on a negotiability appeal is not submitted on time, FLRA decides the appeal without considering the response. NFFE Local 1167 and Dept. of Air Force, 31st Combat Support Group, Homestead AFB, 6 FLRA 574, 574 (1981). AFGE Local 1547 and Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB, 64 FLRA 642, 642 (2010) (vacated on other grounds, Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB v. FLRA, 844 F.3d 957 (D.C. Cir. 2016)), added:

[W]hen a union does not respond to an SOP, and the petition for review does not contest certain assertions in the SOP, the Authority will find that the union concedes those assertions. See, e.g., AFGE, Local 801, 64 FLRA 62, 64 (2009) (citing 5 C.F.R.§ 2424.32(c)(2) [2]) (Local 801). Therefore, in determining the negotiability of the proposals, any of the Agency's assertions in the SOP that are not contested in the petition will be treated as undisputed.

[2] Section 2424.32(c)(2) states, "Failure to respond to an argument or assertion raised by the other party will, where appropriate, be deemed a concession to such argument or assertion."

When an appeal is considered without a response from the union, FLRA examines the union's petition for review and the agency's statement of position. See AFGE, Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals and Dept. of Labor, Office of Ass't Sec'y, 37 FLRA 828, 831 (1990). If FLRA issues a show cause order on timeliness and receives no response, the untimely submission will not be considered. See VA and AFGE, 67 FLRA 152, 153 (2013) (citing AFGE Local 1547 and Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB, 64 FLRA 642, 642 (2010) (vacated on other grounds, Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB v. FLRA, 844 F.3d 957 (D.C. Cir. 2016))); cf. NLRB Professional Ass'n and NLRB, 62 FLRA 397, 398 (2008) (if union's response is rejected as untimely, FLRA does not consider agency's reply to that response). If the agency's position statement is not timely and not considered, FLRA will not consider the agency's reply to the union's response, although it will consider that reply as to new arguments in the union response. NTEU and DHS, C&BP, 72 FLRA 752, 753-54 (2022) (Chairman DuBester concurring in part, dissenting in part); see AFGE Local 1748 and Dept. of Labor, Kansas City, 73 FLRA 233, 234 (2022).

A court affirmed FLRA's rigid insistence on its time limits governing submissions in negotiability proceedings, NLRB Union v. FLRA, 313 Fed. Appx. 328, 328-29 (D.C. Cir. 2009):

The FLRA treats a filing as timely if received by the Authority or postmarked by U.S. mail on or before the due date; See 5 C.F.R. § 2429.21(b). Here the unions sent their filing using Federal Express and it was received one day late. The FLRA thus ignored the late-filed paper and ruled against the unions, finding many of their points waived or unsupported. The unions concede that theft filing was late, and that the principal reason was that counsel misread the rule. Nonetheless, they argue that the Authority abused its discretion in strictly applying its timely filing rule because their lateness should have been excused due to "extraordinary circumstances." 5 C.F.R. § 2429.23(b). This argument is unpersuasive.

The unions argue that three factors, taken together, create the "extraordinary circumstances" required for a waiver of the time limits. These are: (1) delays counsel had experienced using the U.S. mail because of anthrax-related security measures; (2) the fact that the filing was actually received on the earliest possible date that it would have been received had the unions used the U.S. mail; and (3) the fact that the Authority itself took three years to decide this matter on a motion for reconsideration. Of these, the only one relating to the "circumstances" of the filing is the first, as the lack of delay caused by the unions' lateness and the Authority's own foot-dragging after the fact create no extraordinary reason justifying the unions' failure to adhere to the rule in the first place. See, e.g., Nat'l Treasury Employees Union, 60 F.L.R.A. 226, 226 n.1 (2004) (refusing to consider lack of delay caused by one day late courier filing as even presenting a claim of extraordinary circumstances).

We see no abuse of discretion in the Authority's rejection of the anthrax excuse as an "extraordinary circumstance." These fillings occurred years after the anthrax scare, this problem with the U.S. mail does not explain why counsel failed to deposit the filing with Federal Express at a time when it could have been delivered before the deadline or why a courier was not used, and counsel could have used both Federal Express and the mails to ensure both rapid delivery and timely filing under the rule.

Nor is there any merit to the unions' back-up argument that the Authority arbitrarily abandoned its prior practice of considering negotiability issues even absent arguments by the parties. As the Authority makes clear, it promulgated a new rule on this matter in 1998, see Negotiability Proceedings, 63 Fed. Reg. 66,405, 66,411 (Dec. 2, 1998) (codified at 5 C.F.R. § 2424.32(c)) (failure to raise or rebut arguments will be deemed a waiver), and it has consistently refused to consider arguments not raised by the parties since that time, see, e.g., Nat'l Treasury Employees Union, 60 F.L.R.A. 219, 222(2004). Moreover, the Authority notes that the unions never raised this argument in the administrative proceeding, and by failing to contest this point, the unions compel us to agree with the Authority that the argument is therefore waived. See 5 U.S.C. § 7 123(c) ("No objection that has not been urged before the Authority, or its designee, shall be considered by the court....").

FLRA may waive the time limit for a reply if the prior statement of position was not delivered through the mails. See AFGE Local 1770 and DOD Domestic Dependent Elementary and Secondary Sch., 64 FLRA 953, 954-55 (2010) (union response to agency statement of position was returned to union by Postal Service; upon explanation from agency, FLRA indicated it would waive time limit for agency reply).
