broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 19: Critical Elements and Performance Standards, Section C: Broad Criteria, Subsection 03: Incorporation of Organizational, Other Goals

3. Incorporation of Organizational, Other Goals

NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 38 FLRA 456, 466-67 (1990), resolved as negotiable the first sentence of Proposal 4, requiring bargaining over a provision that:

Accomplishment of organizational objectives will be included in performance plan by incorporating goals, program plans or other measures of program results. The performance plan will include the supervisor's and employee's conditions and assumptions required to meet the performance plan's standards....

Concerning the first sentence of the proposal, requiring management to be specific as to goals and measures in a performance plan, FLRA viewed the proposal as "prescribing that the specified matters be included in performance plans for informational purposes only," adding in NAGE Local R1-144, 38 FLRA at 467-68:

...The proposed first sentence would only require the Agency to set forth information concerning the context in which performance standards and critical elements apply. It does not establish criteria that would govern the content of those standards and elements: that is, the level of job performance expected of employees with regard to selected duties and responsibilities of their jobs for purposes of appraising their performance. In our view, a proposal that only requires that information be included in performance plans and does not specify criteria that would govern the content of performance standards and critical elements does not interfere with the Agency's right to determine such content.

...

The Authority has previously held that proposals that merely require an agency to provide information concerning conditions of employment do not directly interfere with management's rights but are negotiable procedures....

The second sentence of the proposal in NAGE Local R1-144 was nonnegotiable because it interfered with the right of management to assign work. By connecting statements of goals in performance plans to the content of performance standards, the sentence permitted employees to negotiate over the content of the standards. FLRA noted, 38 FLRA at 470, "[i]f the sentence was revised to require that employees be provided with information as to what management expected of them in order to meet performance plan requirements, the sentence would likely be negotiable." Nonnegotiable as a substantive limitation on management's discretion to set standards was Provision 6 in NTEU and USDA, Food and Nutrition Serv., W. Region, 42 FLRA 964, 982-83, 984 (1991), mandating that performance elements be: regular and recurring parts of the employee's job; primary responsibilities of employees; components constituting a significant amount of employees' time; directly related to mission accomplishment; and components "on which unacceptable performance will have serious consequences to accomplishing the work."

FLRA declared negotiable Provision 13 in POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 39-40 (1993), that "[n]o performance standard shall refer to a criterion or policy which is not in written form and attached to the performance appraisal plan or to a criterion or policy which is to be specified at a later time":

Provision 13 prevents reference in performance standards to criteria or policies that are not in writing and attached to performance appraisal plans. In addition, the provision prevents management from referring in performance standards to criteria or policies that are not yet promulgated.

The Union states that Provision 13 is intended to assure that employees have "adequate, written notice of the standard of performance that is expected of them." The Union further asserts that its intent is "only to preclude management from holding employees retroactively accountable for standards of performance before the substance of those standards were actually established and communicated to the employee." According to the Union, the provision would, for example, "preclude the issuance of a performance standard on October 1 which reads 'Employee will comply with [A]gency regulation on timeliness of case closures to be issued on December 1.'" The Union adds that the Agency "may, on December 1, amend the previously established appraisal plan for the employee by the inclusion of a standard requiring compliance with the newly established [A]gency regulation on timeliness, which would have to be attached to the appraisal plan."

Based on the Union's explanation, which is consistent with the provision's plain wording, we find that Provision 13 addresses the content of performance standards only insofar as it prevents management from referring to unpublished criteria and policies in them. In other words, the provision requires the Agency to include in its performance standards the policies and criteria against which employees' performance will be evaluated. As the Union explains, management may subsequently act to incorporate any later published criteria or policies the Agency chooses to include. In this regard, we note that any change in the current performance standards to incorporate new criteria or policies would be subject only to impact and implementation bargaining. See, for example, National Weather Service Employees Organization and U.S. Department of Commerce, National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, National Weather Service, 37 FLRA 392, 395 (1990). As such, Provision 13 does not establish substantive criteria governing the content of performance standards. That is, management retains discretion to determine the expectations or requirements employees must satisfy to attain a particular level of performance. Accordingly, Provision 13 is negotiable....

Management could not be required to negotiate a proposal that production studies or goals could not be translated into performance standards, e.g., work units per person, unless the work was repetitive and could be done uniformly; the job content was constant throughout the appraisal period; the method of operation, service, and work unit produced could be measured objectively; and the work units were equivalent. AFGE Local 1708 and Military Ocean Terminal, 15 FLRA 3, 5-6 (1984) (Proposal 2).
