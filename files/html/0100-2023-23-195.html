a. Treatment of Enforcement Issue; Evidence on PFR

The review procedure for enforcement cases is similar to that established for initial decisions in appellate cases to the extent that allegations or arguments relating to noncompliance must first be submitted to the AJ; they cannot be raised for the first time in a PFR, unless it is shown that the new argument is based on new and material evidence. Shafer v. Dept. of Air Force, supra. However, it is the AJ's responsibility to inform the appellant that he bears the burden of proving noncompliance with a settlement; if that guidance is not provided, the Board may consider evidence that otherwise should have first been presented by the appellant to the AJ. See As'Salaam v. USPS, 78 MSPR 480, 482 ¶ 6 (1998). The Board may examine the pleadings that develop during the PFR stage to determine if the parties have sorted out their disputes over the course of time, from the filing of the PFR through the last submission to the Board. See Kolassa v. Dept. of Treasury, 59 MSPR 151, 151 & n.2 (1993). An agency may demonstrate compliance with a settlement agreement even after the filing of a PFR. Copher v. USPS, 91 MSPR 235, 237 ¶ 10 (2002).

It is not proper to file a PFR to obtain review of the agency's compliance with a settlement. Doster v. AID, 10 MSPR 521, 522 (1982). A PFR that essentially seeks settlement compliance will be treated as an enforcement petition for processing by the regional office. Stokes v. USPS, 30 MSPR 101, 102 (1986); see Rivera v. USPS, 107 MSPR 542, 548 ¶ 10, 2007 MSPB 312 (2007) ("Where claims of noncompliance are raised for the first time before the Board, they are generally forwarded to the administrative judge for docketing as a new petition for enforcement."); Turner v. DHS, 97 MSPR 607, 608 (2004) (remanding case to region: "[w]hen a settlement agreement is entered into the record for enforcement, an appellant's allegation that the agency has not complied with that agreement is properly a matter for initial consideration by the administrative judge as a petition for enforcement, rather than by the Board as a petition for review"); cf. Goodson v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3356) (lack of a final Board decision on compliance led to a dismissal of the judicial petition for review of the Board decision on the PFR seeking enforcement of the settlement; the Board had dismissed the PFR and remanded the case to the region for adjudication of the enforcement dispute).

If the agency complains that the appellant has not complied with a settlement, the enforcement issue should be raised through enforcement proceedings at the regional office, not through a PFR. The petition should state what actions it expects the Board to take to secure compliance. Willis v. USPS, 32 MSPR 580, 582 (1987). [Refer to the earlier discussion of Bales v. Dept. of Transp., 54 MSPR 187, 191 (1992), under "Appellant's Agreement to Resign or Take Other Actions; Agency Self-Help."]
