9. Involuntary Resignation

If a probationer's resignation is coerced, the Board declines jurisdiction if it lacks jurisdiction over a straightforward termination of the probationer. There must be a claim of discrimination based on partisan politics or marital status. Guy v. Dept. of Energy, 6 MSPR 62, 63, 6 MSPB 63 (1981); see Baldwin v. SBA, DC-0752-16-0464-I-1 (NP 9/29/2016) ("In the context of a probationary employee's alleged involuntary resignation, the Board will consider allegations of discrimination based on partisan political reasons or marital status only insofar as those allegations relate to the issue of voluntariness and not whether they would establish Board jurisdiction over the termination action."); Nogami v. Dept. of Interior, DC-315H-15-0134-I-1 (NP 5/20/2015) ("Accordingly, we agree with the administrative judge's finding that the Board has no jurisdiction to review the appellant's claim that his resignation was involuntary. See Link v. Department of the Navy, 3 M.S.P.R. 187, 189 (1980) (the appellants' alleged involuntary resignations during their probationary periods provided them with no greater right of appeal to the Board than they would have had if they had been terminated). We also agree with the administrative judge's finding that, absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board has no jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's claim of race discrimination. See Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd sub nom. Wren v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 681 F.2d 867 (D.C. Cir. 1982)."); Fox v. DOD, CH-0752-11-0659-I-1 (NP 1/6/2012) ("The appellant's arguments regarding the merits of his involuntary resignation claim are irrelevant to the issue of the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal and, thus, they fail to show that the administrative judge committed an error of law in dismissing this appeal for lack of jurisdiction. See Rivera v. Department of Homeland Security, 116 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 13 (2011) (finding the appellant's merits arguments to be immaterial to the jurisdictional issue); Kennedy v. National Aeronautics & Space Administration, 84 M.S.P.R. 103, ¶ 3 n.1 (1999) (the Board did not address appellant's arguments regarding the merits of his claim, since they were irrelevant to dispositive issues, including those of collateral estoppel and jurisdiction)."), aff'd, Fox v. DOD, 549 Fed. Appx. 969 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP); Holland v. Dept. of Treasury, 2 MSPR 492, 493, 3 MSPB 15 (1980) (dismissing probationer's appeal from coerced resignation for lack of an allegation of partisan political reasons or marital status).

Under Chaiyes v. DHS, SF-315H-12-0618-I-1 (NP 5/3/2013), only a nonfrivolous allegation supporting a probationer's appeal permits the Board to consider an allegedly involuntary resignation that occurred before an announced effective termination date:

...An employee-initiated action, such as a retirement or resignation, is presumed to be voluntary, and thus outside the Board's jurisdiction. Carey v. Department of Health & Human Services, 112 M.S.P.R. 106, ¶ 5 (2009). An involuntary resignation, however, is equivalent to a forced removal and therefore, within the Board's jurisdiction. Id. As the administrative judge correctly found, the appellant did not make a nonfrivolous allegation that his resignation was involuntary. Moreover, to qualify as an "employee" with appeal rights under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75, the appellant, as a competitive service employee, must show that he either is not serving a probationary period or has completed one year of current continuous service under an appointment other than a temporary one limited to a year or less. Baggan v. Department of State, 109 M.S.P.R. 572, ¶ 5 (2008). The appellant here was serving a probationary period at the time of his separation and he has not made a nonfrivolous allegation that he has any prior service or that he has completed one year of current continuous service. Thus, the appellant has not established either that he is an "employee" with chapter 75 appeal rights or that he has been affected by an action that is appealable to the Board. We find, therefore, that the administrative judge properly dismissed this appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

To the extent that the appellant is attempting to appeal his termination during his probationary period, the evidence shows that his termination never took effect. In any event, however, the appellant has not established that any termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status discrimination. Therefore, even if we deemed the termination to have taken effect (and we discern no basis for doing so), we would lack jurisdiction over that action as well. See Stokes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 761 F.2d 682, 685-86 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

If the employee separates during probation by resignation instead of termination for postappointment reasons, an appeal of the voluntary resignation is not within the Board's appellate jurisdiction. Related allegations of marital status discrimination are not reviewable. See Dennis v. OPM, 27 MSPR 185, 186 n.* (1985) (determining the resignation was voluntary; then determining there was no jurisdiction over an appeal from the voluntary resignation since the appeal was not from a probationary termination for post-appointment reasons). The Federal Circuit implied in Winn v. MSPB, 335 Fed. Appx. 970, 972-73 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), that if an employee faced with removal during probation for preappointment reasons resigns instead of being separated, the employee may be able to challenge the resignation as involuntary for reasons that could include whether the employee had insufficient time to decide whether to resign:

...It is undisputed that Dr. Winn was not given a notice of proposed removal and time to file a response; instead, he was summarily removed as of midnight of the day of the notice of actual removal. Accordingly, if Dr. Winn had presented a prima facie pleading that he was removed for pre-employment conditions, he would have been entitled to challenge whether his resignation was voluntary, or whether it must be deemed involuntary because of the short time in which he had to decide whether to resign. However, the reasonable and fair reading of the removal notice is that Dr. Winn was being removed for inadequacies that were manifested during his actual employment, and not for anything that happened before his employment. Substantial evidence supports the AJ's conclusion that § 315.805 was not implicated.
