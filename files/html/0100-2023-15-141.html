a. Abuse of Coworker, Subordinate

Employees are expected to treat each other with a modicum of courtesy. See Fonville v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 351, 355 (1986) (citing Glover v. DHEW, 1 MSPR 660, 662-63 (1980), aff'd, 705 F.2d 442 (4th Cir. 1983)); Murphy v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 333, 338 (1984) ("An agency is entitled to expect its employees to deport themselves in conformance with accepted standards. Abusive language and behavior are not acceptable or conducive to a stable working atmosphere."); Kullenberg v. VA, 21 MSPR 513, 516 n.4 (1984) (vulgarity toward a coworker is not condoned, but it would have a more disruptive effect when directed at a supervisor). Inappropriate language toward a coworker may constitute a violation of agency ethical standards, Kramer v. VA, 23 MSPR 271, 275 (1984) (sustaining removal--a history of similar past incidents and related discipline), although the infractions need not be classified as a violation of an agency regulation. Abusive language, including a racial slur, toward coworkers may be treated in a fashion similar to insubordination. It can support discharge of an employee with relatively short tenure and a poor past record. Faul v. Dept. of Air Force, 30 MSPR 37, 38-39 (1986). An ALJ was removed for charges that included repeated vulgar and personally demeaning comments to and about other employees. See SSA v. Carr, 78 MSPR 313, 340-41 (1998).

Disrespectful behavior toward a peer, consisting of screaming and a display of anger in a patient care area, warranted removal of an employee who had some past discipline for disrespectful conduct and who was told to resolve his differences with his peer by seeking assistance from a designated manager. Tobochnik v. VA, 9 MSPR 82, 84-85 (1981). "[E]ven a single instance of indecent and disgraceful conduct toward a coworker can support a penalty of removal; there need not be a pattern of such behavior before discipline is reasonable. See Teichmann v. Department of the Army, 34 M.S.P.R. 447 (1987), aff'd, 854 F.2d 1327 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table)." Garner v. USPS, DA-0752-08-0575-I-2 (NP 7/19/2010); cf. Arellanes v. DOD, DE-0752-15-0021-I-1 (NP 8/10/2015) ("The appellant's disrespectful conduct toward his supervisor and colleagues is a clear basis for agency discipline. See Beaudoin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 99 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶ 10 (2005)."). Demotion from a GM-14 Supervisory Employee Development Specialist to a GS-12 Employee Development Specialist was sustained because, among other reasons, the appellant called a subordinate a liar in front of three people. Wirth v. SSA, 21 MSPR 691, 694 (1984). Disrespectful comments toward coworkers led to adverse action in Hamilton v. VA, 115 MSPR 673, 2011 MSPB 35 (2011).

Davis v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 360, 370-71 (1994), mitigated demotion of a supervisor to a suspension based on some verbal abuse of an inmate. The decision recited case law concerning penalties against supervisors and others who engage in rude or abusive language:

In White v. Department of Justice, 35 MSPR 684, 689-90 (1988), the Board found that a 30-day suspension of a Correctional Officer, and not removal, was the maximum reasonable penalty for the sustained charges of involvement in illegal activity, i.e., entering U.S. Customs with firecrackers and using insulting, abusive, and demeaning language towards constituted law enforcement authority. In that case, although the appellant had a prior disciplinary record that included a 5-day suspension for harassment and disrespectful conduct and a 2-day suspension for theft of government property, he had 9 years of service, and the Board determined that there was no indication that a 30-day suspension would not be sufficient to deter similar misconduct in the future.

In Ballentine v. Department of Justice, 30 MSPR 652, 655-61 (1986), the Board held that a 60-day suspension and reassignment, rather than a four-grade demotion and reassignment, was the maximum reasonable penalty to be imposed on a Chief Deputy U.S. Marshall for the sustained charges of use of profanity and misuse of a government vehicle. Even though the appellant was a high-level supervisor and was charged with a correspondingly high degree of responsibility, the Board found that his approximately 20 years of service, his 9 years as a supervisor, his commendations and awards, and his clean prior disciplinary record weighed in favor of mitigation.

Likewise, in Armstrong v. US Postal Service, 28 MSPR 45, 47-50 (1985), the Board determined that a 60-day suspension, rather than a demotion, of a supervisor was the maximum reasonable penalty for his use of profanity and rude and argumentative behavior in an agency-sponsored training session. The Board noted that the supervisor had 18 years of apparently satisfactory service, and the instructors at the training session did not inform him that his speech and behavior were inappropriate or advise him to refrain from such conduct. Finally, in Franco v. US Postal Service, 27 MSPR 322, 324-27 (1985), the Board held that a 60-day suspension, and not an eleven-grade demotion to a nonsupervisory position, was the maximum reasonable penalty to be imposed on a supervisor for rude and discourteous behavior towards managers. In that case, the supervisor had more than 15 years of satisfactory service with the agency, the last 8 of which he had served in a management capacity, and although his past disciplinary record contained two previous letters of warning for abusive language, he received those less than 2 weeks before the charged misconduct.

Cf. Collins v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-13-0281-I-1 (NP 7/31/2014) ("The special combination of circumstances at play in this appeal including the extreme nature of the appellant's profanity and the hostile environment that the appellant created by the daily use of this profanity in the presence of her coworkers support the deciding official's determination that the appellant's misconduct was extremely serious and reveal her lack of potential for rehabilitation, outweighing her many years of unblemished service.").

Demotion was sustained of a supervisory correctional officer who made racially disparaging comments about a coworker. The appellant's First Amendment defense was not credited in Johnson v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 46, 50-51 (1994), a decision that also noted the adverse impact of the statements on the appellant's ability to serve as a mentor for younger supervisors:

...Although the appellant claims that the agency's regulation violated his right of free speech under the First Amendment, he does not argue, and there is no indication, that his insulting, abusive, or obscene comments about a coworker relate to a matter of public concern. See Means v. Department of Labor, 60 MSPR 108, 113 (1993); Sigman v. Department of the Air Force, 37 MSPR 352, 355 (1988) (speech that involves only internal agency grievances rather than matters of concern to the community does not involve a matter of public concern), aff'd, 868 F.2d 1278 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table). Even assuming arguendo that the appellant's statements did address a matter of public concern, we find that the disruptive effect of the appellant's racially derogatory comments, which were made in the presence of other agency personnel while on duty, outweighs any public interest in such comments. See Mings v. Department of Justice, 813 F.2d 384, 388-89 (Fed. Cir. 1987).
