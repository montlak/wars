A. NATURE OF REVIEW

The action reviewed by an appellate court is the Board's, not the original agency action. See NTEU v. MSPB, 743 F.2d 895, 912 n.22 (D.C. Cir. 1984). Federal district courts hear "mixed" cases appealed from the Board involving issues of discrimination, and the plaintiff may have the right to a de novo trial (bench or jury) on EEO issues. The 1989 Whistleblower Protection Act vests review authority in the Federal Circuit for Special Counsel corrective and disciplinary action cases leading to final Board decisions. 5 USC 1214(c); 5 USC 1215(a)(4). Board final decisions involving Individual Right of Action cases are also appealable to the Federal Circuit. 5 USC 1221(h). The 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act, as amended, allows for Board decisions in whistleblower cases to be reviewed in the Federal Circuit or in other circuits.

The Federal Circuit may review a Board decision on the merits of a litigant's challenge to an OPM rule or regulation. Clark v. OPM, 95 F.3d 1139, 1143 (Fed. Cir. 1996); see Delos Santos v. OPM, 289 F.3d 1382, 1385 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (determination of whether to grant regulation review is within sole discretion of the Board; it is only when Board decides merits of a petition that court has jurisdiction to consider Board's decision); Manning v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3075) (Board determination not to conduct implementation review because petitioner's claims are weak is a determination on the merits of the claim sufficient to vest review authority in Federal Circuit); Ward v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3044) ("We have jurisdiction to review the Board's discretionary decision not to review a challenged OPM rule or regulation only if in making its decision the Board considered the merits of the challenged OPM rule or regulation" (citing Clark v. OPM, 95 F.3d 1139, 1142 (Fed. Cir. 1996)); NTEU v. MSPB, 743 F.2d 895, 901-04 (D.C. Cir. 1984) (tracing right of judicial review in regulation implementation cases before and after the Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1982); cf. Siwa v. OPM, 206 Fed. Appx. 985 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) (under Delos Santos, although the Board does not make a merits determination when it dismisses a regulation implementation review petition based on res judicata, the court is without jurisdiction to review the Board's determination); Frederick v. OPM, 186 Fed. Appx. 988 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) (same holding as Siwa).

To determine whether the Federal Circuit has jurisdiction, it may be significant to first determine whether the Board properly has jurisdiction. In cases brought under 5 USC 7701, the scope of the subject matter jurisdiction of the Federal Circuit is no broader than the scope of the MSPB's jurisdiction. If the Board has no jurisdiction, neither does the court, other than its inherent authority to determine whether it or the Board has or lacks jurisdiction. Manning v. MSPB, 742 F.2d 1424, 1427 (Fed. Cir. 1984); see Leichtman v. DOJ, 404 Fed. Appx. 482 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) (court lacked jurisdiction to review arbitrator's decision to deny counsel fees following arbitration award setting aside three-day suspension; Board would not have had jurisdiction over the short suspension); Noble v. TVA, 892 F.2d 1013, 1014 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (court may review Board's decisions regarding only matters within Board's subject matter jurisdiction). If the Board concludes that it does not have jurisdiction over a case--as, for example, in Manning, a reassignment--counsel must carefully consider whether an appeal to the Federal Circuit is worthwhile. The court will not reach the substantive dispute if the Board had no jurisdiction to consider the appeal. Sometimes it is difficult to determine who, if anyone, has jurisdiction to hear a case. The circuit court is not in the business of offering legal advice, declining, for example, to suggest a forum to an employee whose appeal was outside the jurisdiction of the Board because she was the employee of a nonappropriated fund facility. Henault v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-785).
