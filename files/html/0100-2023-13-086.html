b. EEO Allegation

Anderson v. Dept. of Navy, 45 MSPR 136, 142 (1990), overruled in part, Rivas v. USPS, 61 MSPR 121 (1994), noted that if, with a claim of enforced leave or constructive suspension, the appellant raises an allegation of disability discrimination, the presence of the discrimination allegation does not alone confer jurisdiction on the Board. The AJ must analyze the constructive suspension issue to determine if the Board has jurisdiction over the case and then, after determining that a constructive suspension has occurred and has vested jurisdiction in the Board, decide the discrimination claim. What Anderson did not state was that the concurrent resolution of jurisdictional and discrimination allegations may be required when the employee claims that she could not work because the agency would not accommodate a disability. The Board addressed disability discrimination in another case of enforced leave. As to an appellant placed on enforced leave, a machinist with a back problem, Pledger v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 325, 331-32 (1991) (following earlier remand, 34 MSPR 456 (1987)), determined that accommodation may require consideration of reassignment and job restructuring:

We find that the appellant has established a prima facie case of handicap discrimination. The record reflects that he has a long and persistent history of recurrent and continuous back pain, and that he has been placed on medical restrictions which include the avoidance of heavy lifting and excessive climbing, stooping, or bending. Thus he has shown that he has a physical impairment that substantially limits one or more of his major life activities, specifically, working.... The appellant has further shown that his enforced leave was based on his inability to perform the duties of his position. Furthermore, by indicating that he might be able to perform the duties of the Laborer position offered by the agency if it were restructured, he articulated a method by which he believed the agency could reasonably accommodate his handicap.

See Tardio v. DOJ, 112 MSPR 371, 380 (2009) (constructive suspension claim: "It is well settled that, absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board lacks jurisdiction over discrimination claims. See Carlisle v. Department of Defense, 93 M.S.P.R. 280, ¶ 9 (2003); Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980) (allegations of discrimination are not an independent source of appellant jurisdiction, and an underlying appeal within the Board's jurisdiction must first be presented for such allegations to be considered), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982)."); cf. Parks v. USPS, 110 MSPR 64, 2008 MSPB 213 (2008) (adopting EEOC finding of disability discrimination relative to placement of employee with medical restrictions on enforced leave rather than providing accommodation for the appellant's diabetic condition).
