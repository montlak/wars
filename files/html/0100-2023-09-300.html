b. Failure to Designate Representative

In Bennett v. Dept. of the Navy, 1 MSPR 683, 689, 2 MSPB 93 (1980), the AJ dismissed an appeal filed without a required designation of representative for lack of prosecution because the attorney who filed the case failed to supply the designation after two requests from the AJ. The dismissal was upheld. If an appeal is not submitted directly by an appellant, an immediate designation of representative must be filed to validate the appeal. Failure to supply the form after two requests in the Bennett appeal warranted dismissal for failure to prosecute, with the same effect as an adverse adjudication on the appeal's merits. But see Morris v. OPM, 37 MSPR 401, 405 (1988) (appeal was reinstated when the record demonstrated good faith efforts to comply with the designation requirements). In Bennett, the appellant was charged with the error of his attorney. The right of appeal is personal to an appellant and the responsibility for the prosecution of an appeal remains with an appellant, whether or not he is represented by counsel. Another appeal was dismissed after counsel withdrew; the appellant was instructed by the AJ to file a new designation of representative and he did not do so after being warned that the appeal would be dismissed for noncompliance. The Board did not state why the appellant was not permitted to proceed without a representative. Driggs v. FAA, 15 MSPR 597, 599 (1983).

A different result was reached in Carrier v. USPS, 65 MSPR 54 (1994). The AJ's initial decision declined to address jurisdictional submissions from appellant's counsel because appellant did not file a written statement or form designating a representative. The Board remanded Carrier's appeal for proper consideration of appellant's appeal: the single failure to comply with the directive in the acknowledgment order calling for designated of a representative did not warrant a sanction without notice to the appellant that his appeal was procedurally deficient. The point that appeals should not be dismissed for an isolated failure to adhere completely to an AJ's order was again made, Gumper v. DOJ, 51 MSPR 394, 396-97 (1991):

...In his petition, the appellant named a representative but provided no address or phone number for the representative. The administrative judge issued an April 4, 1991, acknowledgment order directing the appellant to complete a designation of representative form within 10 days of the date of the order.

On April 11, 1991, the administrative judge issued a second order in which he noted that the appellant appeared to be requesting a Board decision defining his rights to receive a disability retirement or Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP) benefits. Because it appeared that the Board would not have jurisdiction to make such a decision, the administrative judge informed the appellant of the general law applicable to disability retirement and OWCP benefits and ordered him to notify the administrative judge by April 29, 1991, whether he wished to proceed with his appeal....

...On April 25, 1991, beyond the 10-day time period for doing so, the appellant submitted a completed designation-of-representative form. The appellant did not refer to the April 11, 1991, order in his submission of April 25, 1991, and did not specifically state that he wished to pursue his appeal. In his April 30, 1991, initial decision, the administrative judge dismissed the appeal for failure to prosecute.

...

An administrative judge has the authority to impose the sanction of dismissal if a party fails to prosecute or defend an appeal. See 5 CFR § 1201.43(b). The administrative judge should resort to the imposition of sanctions, however, only when necessary to serve the ends of justice. He should impose sanctions only when a party has failed to exercise due diligence in complying with any order, or when a party has exhibited negligence or bad faith in its efforts to so comply. See Benton v. Dept. of the Interior, 47 MSPR 200, 203 (1991); Macon v. Dept. of the Air Force, 46 MSPR 410, 413 (1990). A single failure to comply with a Board order is insufficient to support such a dismissal. See Murdock v. Government Printing Office, 38 MSPR 297, 299 (1988). Good faith attempts falling short of full compliance should be considered in determining whether dismissal is an appropriate sanction. See Morris v. OPM, 37 MSPR 401, 405 (1988).

In his petition for appeal, the appellant informed the administrative judge that he was represented. His appeal was also signed by his named representative. He complied with the acknowledgment order to submit a completed designation-of-representative form, even though the compliance was less than full because he submitted the form 9 days after the deadline set by the administrative judge in the acknowledgment order. His submission of the completed designation-of-representative form was an indication that he wished to pursue his appeal, particularly in view of the fact that he submitted it before the deadline to inform the administrative judge that he wished to proceed with his appeal. Under the circumstances, we find that the appellant made a good faith attempt to comply with the administrative judge's orders and that the administrative judge erred in dismissing the appeal for failure to prosecute. Cf. Hoyt v. Dept. of the Treasury, 19 MSPR 21, 23 (1984).
