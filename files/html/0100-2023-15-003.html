1. Impact on Service Efficiency

AWOL and leave abuse are not well received in the federal service, Davis v. VA, 792 F.2d 1111, 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1986):

Ms. Davis' argument that an employee's AWOL has no effect on the efficiency of the service is frivolous. An essential element of employment is to be on the job when one is expected to be there. To permit employees to remain away from work without leave would seriously impede the function of an agency. Rubin v. United States, 150 Ct. Cl. 28, 38 (1960). An unexcused absence imposes burdens on other employees and, if tolerated, destroys the morale of those who meet their obligations. Id. Moreover, absence without leave is not excused by acceptable performance when an employee does choose to appear. Acceptable performance is a separate element of one's obligations to an employer. For these reasons, we hold that any sustained charge of AWOL is inherently connected to the efficiency of the service....

More succinctly, Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 429 Fed. Appx. 964, 967 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP):

...Regarding the nexus element, "the nexus between the charged offense and the efficiency of the service is automatic when the charged offense is AWOL." Bryant v. Nat'l Sci. Found., 105 F.3d 1414, 1417 (Fed. Cir. 1997).

The theme was reiterated, Ross v. USPS, AT-0752-13-0151-I-1 (NP 4/29/2014):

Regarding nexus, we reject the appellant's suggestion that the agency must establish the actual effect that his repeated unscheduled absences had on its operations. It is well-settled that one of the essential functions of almost every government job is coming to work regularly. See, e.g., Stevens v. Department of the Army, 73 M.S.P.R. 619, 625 (1997). Indeed, in Law v. U.S. Postal Service, 852 F.2d 1278, 1279-80 (Fed. Cir. 1988), our reviewing court rejected the petitioner's argument challenging the required nexus between his misconduct (multiple approved unscheduled absences and one instance of AWOL) and the efficiency of the service. The Federal Circuit noted that "an agency is inherently entitled to require an employee to be present during scheduled work times and, unless an agency is notified in advance, an employee's absence is disruptive to the agency's efficient operation." Id.

Taylor v. USPS, 393 Fed. Appx. 717, 719-20 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), noted of AWOL:

"In order to prove a charge of AWOL, an agency must show by preponderant evidence that the employee was absent, and that his absence was not authorized or that his request for leave was properly denied." Wesley v. U.S. Postal Serv., 94 M.S.P.R. 277, 283 (2003). An AWOL charge automatically satisfies the nexus requirement because "any sustained charge of AWOL is inherently connected to the efficiency of the service." Davis v. Veterans Admin., 792 F.2d 1111, 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

The odium of unexcused absence was restated, Ajanaku v. DOD, 44 MSPR 350, 355 (1990):

There is an inherent relationship between continuous unexcused absences and the efficiency of the service, since an essential element of employment is to be on the job when one is expected to be there. To permit employees to remain away from work without leave would seriously impede the function of an agency, impose additional burdens on other employees, and if tolerated, destroy the morale of those who meet their obligations....

See Jones v. VA, 401 Fed. Appx. 539, 542 (Fed. An agency has a right to have employees in attendance, unless there is a valid excuse for their absence. Giesler v. Dept. of Transp., 3 MSPR 277, 281, 3 MSPB 367 (1980). Under Bryant v. Nat'l Science Found., 105 F.3d 1414, 1416-18 (Fed. Cir. 1997), involving persistent tardiness, often caused by transportation problems:

An agency, however, is not required to accommodate transportation problems. See Roberson v. Veterans Admin., 27 M.S.P.R. 489, 492 (1985). Moreover, the NSF tolerated Ms. Bryant's absences for quite some time prior to instituting more stringent measures. The NSF provided both written and oral counseling, imposed a suspension, and instituted several schedule changes at Ms. Bryant's request. Despite these accommodations, Ms. Bryant failed to report to work on time regularly. Under these circumstances, we cannot say that the Board erred in sustaining the NSF's charges of tardiness.

The agency may charge unsatisfactory attendance, Crawford v. USPS, CH-0752-21-0069-I-1, n.4 (NP 3/29/2022):

When periods of AWOL are included within an agency's charge of unsatisfactory attendance, the Board will consider them as an AWOL charge. See Savage v. Department of the Army, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 32 (2015) (stating that to the extent periods of AWOL are included in an excessive absences charge, the Board will consider them as an AWOL charge).

The serious adverse impact on the agency's mission resulting from an unauthorized absence of several weeks is self-evident and, unless there is rebuttal from the appellant, an agency need not present detailed evidence concerning that impact. Douglas v. VA, 5 MSPR 280, 305-06, 5 MSPB 313, 341 (1981); see Thom v. Dept. of Army, 114 MSPR 169, 173 ¶ 7, 2010 MSPB 110 (2010) ("Removal is warranted in response to the appellant's significant period of unauthorized absence from duty because of its obvious disruption to the efficiency of the service. See Desiderio v. Department of the Navy, 4 M.S.P.R. 84, 85 (1980) ('[U]nauthorized absence from duty has been held to be proper grounds for removal since by its very nature it disrupts the efficiency of the service.')."); Crutchfield v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 444, 450 (1997) ("Her position was the 'only one of its kind in the command,' and required her regular presence at the agency to prepare material for shipping."); Pitsenberger v. Dept. of Navy, 9 MSPR 403, 408 (1982) (appellant held the highest ranking position in the office; a presumption arose that the job required the performance of duties unique in function, responsibility, and trust; excessive unauthorized absence was, by the nature of the job, disruptive to service efficiency).

No agency can tolerate indefinitely the persistent unauthorized absences of an employee. Bryant v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-996). Further proof of nexus is unnecessary in cases of absence without leave. Williams v. Dept. of Army, 24 MSPR 537, 542 (1984); see Bryant v. Nat'l Science Found., 105 F.3d 1414, 1417 (Fed. Cir. 1997) ("[N]exus between the charged offense and the efficiency of the service is automatic when the charged offense is AWOL."); Crutchfield v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 444, 448 (1997) ("Unauthorized absence, by its very nature, disrupts the efficiency of the service."); Leonard v. USPS, 3 MSPR 142, 145, 3 MSPB 251 (1980) (determining that the agency need not excuse a prolonged absence because the agency was considering acquisition of a new work facility and might, in the future, be able to accommodate the appellant by changing his work schedule). Absence is no less subject to sanction if the employee volunteered to work on an overtime assignment. See Farrell v. VA, 14 MSPR 94, 97 (1982) (removal of a housekeeping aide was sustained; a past disciplinary record was considered).

No organization can operate successfully without a reliable work force, nor can it achieve an efficient operation when an employee is frequently absent. Sanders v. VA, 11 MSPR 434, 437 (1982); see Dias v. VA, 102 MSPR 53, 58 ¶ 16, 2006 MSPB 124 (2006) ("The Board has consistently held that AWOL is serious and that removal is reasonable for a sustained charge of AWOL for several weeks, particularly, as here, where the appellant has prior disciplinary actions for similar offenses."); Crutchfield v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 444, 451 (1997) (removal for continual and excessive unscheduled absences over several years prior to removal; agency officials reasonably believed there was no foreseeable end to the absences); Vance v. VA, 9 MSPR 409, 411 (1981) (AWOL: "[I]n appellant's particular field, nursing, this offense is quite serious and detrimental to the efficiency of the service."); Cross v. VA, 8 MSPR 370, 371 (1981) (AWOL charges: "[R]egular attendance by employees was necessary for optimum care to be offered by the nursing service; frequent requests for emergency annual leave create difficulties in the maintenance of adequate staffing to insure proper nursing care.").

The Postal Service, a big agency with a big mission, produces lots of Board appeals from employees who are discharged for AWOL, extensive LWOP, or irregular attendance. Burks v. USPS, 593 Fed. Appx. 988, 990-91 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), summed up some decisions involving Postal employees:

The Postal Service has the burden to prove its charge of "failure to be regular in attendance" by a preponderance of the evidence. See Vaughn v. U.S. Postal Serv., 109 M.S.P.R. 469, 476 (2008). It must also establish that any disciplinary action "promotes the efficiency of the service and that the imposed penalty is reasonable." Id. at 476-77.

To take disciplinary action against an employee for absences, the Postal Service must show each absence "was not requested and approved in advance...." Stratton v. U.S. Postal Serv., 89 M.S.P.R. 337, 342 (2001) (citing Williams v. U.S. Postal Serv., 68 M.S.P.R. 150, 156 (1995)). The Board found, and Ms. Thomas does not contest, that Ms. Thomas was absent for approximately six weeks from February 26, 2012 until April 6, 2012, when the Postal Service issued its Notice of Proposed Removal. There is no evidence in the record that Ms. Thomas's absences were specifically requested or approved in advance. To the contrary, the record shows the Postal Service repeatedly instructed Ms. Thomas, by mail and by phone, to justify her continued absence. The Postal Service warned that failure to do so would result in disciplinary action. Ms. Thomas failed to respond to these requests or to otherwise communicate with the Postal Service. Accordingly, substantial evidence supports the Board's determination that Ms. Thomas's absences were actionable.

The Board has previously held that "numerous instances of absence and tardiness" constitute a failure to be regular in attendance. Brin v. U.S. Postal Serv., 49 M.S.P.R. 549 (1991). It has relied on fewer than six weeks of absences in previous cases. See, e.g., Jones v. U.S. Postal Serv., 110 M.S.P.R. 674, 675 (2009) (eleven unscheduled absences during a three-month period); Stratton, 89 M.S.P.R. at ¶ 10 (sixteen incidents of absence and tardiness); Byers v. U.S. Postal Serv., 78 M.S.P.R. 456, 458, 463-64 (1998) (nine or fewer incidents of unscheduled leave during a four-month period, in addition to a prior disciplinary record based on attendance). Here, we find that Ms. Thomas's absence for at least six weeks provides substantial evidence to support the Board's finding that she failed to be regular in attendance.

Ms. Thomas suggests that even if she failed to be regular in attendance, the penalty of removal was unreasonable. She notes the Administrative Judge misstated her length of service, one of the factors used to determine the appropriate penalty. See Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305 (1981). The Administrative Judge found that Ms. Thomas had twelve years of government service. In fact, Ms. Thomas had approximately twenty eight years of combined civilian and military service in the government.

We find that this error was harmless. "In assessing the appropriateness of the agency's penalty selection, the most important factor is the nature and seriousness of the misconduct...." Jinks v. Dept. of Veterans Affairs, 106 M.S.P.R. 627, 635 (2007). The Administrative Judge properly found that Ms. Thomas's failure to be regular in attendance without any justification for her absences was a serious offense that merits removal. An additional sixteen years to an already long period of service would not have altered this conclusion. Accordingly, we find the Board's affirmance of the penalty of removal was not an abuse of discretion.

Absence without leave and related attendance problems are valid reasons for removal. Huston v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 260, 264, 6 MSPB 223 (1981) (13-day absence was sufficient for removal; "existence of drug dependency does not excuse his admitted failure to call his supervisor during his period of absence"); Clemmons v. USPS, 5 MSPR 363, 365, 5 MSPB 383 (1981) ("The Board agrees with the agency that AWOL and related attendance problems are valid reasons for removal"; mitigating removal based on lengthy service and documented disability). Courts reaffirm the right of an agency to dismiss employees who are AWOL or who take frequent, unscheduled absences and disrupt agency operations. See Baltz v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3129) ("Being absent without leave is a basis for removal without consideration of how essential the employee is to the agency."); Johnson v. Burnley, 887 F.2d 471, 481 (4th Cir. 1989) ("Most telling is the fact that many of her unexcused absences followed closely on the heels of Johnson's reprimands and suspensions for prior attendance problems. The undisputed evidence shows that Johnson had numerous unexcused absences and had flouted clear warnings about prompt attendance."); Law v. USPS, 852 F.2d 1278, 1279-80 (Fed. Cir. 1988) ("First, the record shows that the board considered testimony from agency witnesses stating that it was important that Law be present, on time, and avoid unscheduled absences. The agency provided more than a mere conclusory statement. In any event, an agency is inherently entitled to require an employee to be present during scheduled work times and, unless an agency is notified in advance, an employee's absence is disruptive to the agency's efficient operation. See Washington v. Department of the Army, 813 F.2d 390, 394 (Fed. Cir.) (even prior approved leave may show unreliable attendance and render employee "of marginal value to agency"); ...Second, we have held that 'any sustained charge of AWOL is inherently connected to the efficiency of the service.'
