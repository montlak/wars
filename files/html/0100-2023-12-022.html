a. Some Testimony Discredited

Early on, the Board held that AJ may, but is not required to, distrust the testimony of a witness--the appellant--once his testimony is discredited on other points or charges, Blocker v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 467, 470, 6 MSPB 395 (1981):

In addition, the appellant alleges that the presiding official erred by relying upon his credibility determinations with respect to the first charge to find that the agency's third charge was sustained. However, it was within the presiding official's authority to distrust the testimony of the appellant once his testimony had been discredited as to other charges. This is not to say that the presiding official was required to discredit the testimony, but, in weighing all of the evidence relating to the third charge, he was free to consider his prior credibility determinations and the reasons therefor in assessing the relative weight to be accorded the appellant's testimony relating to the third charge. The Board finds no error inasmuch as appellant fails to show that those credibility determinations were not properly made. See Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 MSPB 297 (1980). See also Carbo v. United States, 314 F.2d 718 (9th Cir. 1963).

See Hawkins v. Smithsonian Inst., 73 MSPR 397, 404 (1997) ("This rule makes sense because a finding that a witness is not credible with respect to some testimony may call into question the witness' character for truthfulness with respect to other testimony."); Bolton v. DOJ, 26 MSPR 658, 660 (1985) ("[W]e rely not on the inmate's credibility for the weight which we accord these statements, but on the extent to which these statements are corroborated by other evidence in the record.").

A witness's lack of credibility on one issue does not necessarily discredit his testimony on other issues. See Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 827 F.2d 1564 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Jenkins v. DHHS, DC-0752-12-0586-B-2 (NP 2/2/2016) ("It is well settled that even if a witness is not credible on one point, it does not necessarily mean that the remainder of his testimony lacks credibility. Craft v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 78 M.S.P.R. 374, 380 (1998); Boscoe v. Department of Agriculture, 54 M.S.P.R. 315, 323 (1992). This is particularly true where, as here, the allegedly inconsistent testimony has no direct relation to the facts surrounding the charged misconduct. See Boscoe, 54 M.S.P.R. at 323."); Craft v. VA, 78 MSPR 374, 380 (1998) ("[E]ven if a witness is not credible on one point, it does not necessarily mean that the remainder of his testimony lacks credibility."); Seas v. USPS, 73 MSPR 422, 430 (1997) ("the fact that a witness may give inconsistent testimony on one issue does not mean that her testimony on another issue cannot be credible"); Cohen v. GSA, 53 MSPR 492, 497 (1992) ("even if the witness was incredible with respect to these other matters, this lack of credibility would not require the administrative judge to discredit his testimony with respect to the critical element at issue before us now"); Luscri v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 482, 486 (1989) ("[a]n administrative judge is not required to discredit the testimony of a witness on all issues once the testimony is discredited on any issue"); Van Valkinburgh v. Dept. of Interior, 31 MSPR 433, 434 n.1 (1986); LaMonnier v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 453, 454 n.1 (1984) ("[a] presiding official may choose to accept portions of a witness' testimony as true and reject other portions"); Pedersen v. Dept. of Transp., 9 MSPR 195, 198 (1981) ("A presiding official is not required to discredit the testimony of a witness on all issues once the testimony is discredited on any issue.").

Verbosely stated in Greer v. USPS, 43 MSPR 180, 188 (1990), "[w]hile an administrative judge is not required to discredit the testimony of a witness on all points, if it is not found persuasive on one, he is free to consider prior credibility determinations in assessing the relative weight to be accorded that witness's testimony on a subsequent charge." That some testimony of a witness is inconsistent does not mean that it must all be disregarded. The rule is permissive, allowing the trier to distrust a witness if the witness is found to have made a false statement under oath. Furlough v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 122, 125 n.3 (1988); Special Counsel v. DeFord, 28 MSPR 98, 101 (1985) ("rule" is flexible and permissive, not mandatory). "Although an AJ is not required to discredit a witness's credibility on all issues once he has found the witness not credible on one issue, the specific instances of lack of credibility is a proper consideration in assessing the witness's overall credibility." Cross v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 62, 70-71 ¶ 14 (2001). Although there is no requirement that all testimony provided by a witness must be discredited if any of it is determined to be not credible, the Board determined that when an appellant initially denied personally falsifying inventory figures, a denial that was ultimately retracted, his denial that he instructed subordinates to do so lacked credibility. Black v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 133, 135 (1985).

The Board summarized its views on the partially discredited witness, Sternberg v. DODDS, 41 MSPR 46, 54 (1989):

...An administrative judge is not required to discredit the testimony of a witness on all issues once the testimony is discredited on any issue.... Once he has discredited the testimony on one charge, however, he must provide some reasoned explanation of why he accepts the testimony as credible on the other charges.

Sternberg was followed in Walsh v. VA, 62 MSPR 586, 590 (1994).
