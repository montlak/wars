1. Time and Place for Filing Appeal

The employment practice challenge is within the Board's appellate jurisdiction, 5 CFR 1201.3(a)(7) (2022), meaning that the case is initiated through appeal to a Board regional or field office. The Board's 30 day appeal deadline applies in employment practice appeals. See Ozier v. MSPB, 400 Fed. Appx. 560 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (declining to overturn the Board's determination that the appellant untimely challenged an employment practice administered by OPM and SSA resulting in her nonselection for an ALJ position; the time to appeal ran from notice of the nonselection; the court did not accept an argument that there was a continuing violation); Clay v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3243) (sustaining a Board order dismissing as untimely an appeal from the OPM's scoring of an ALJ examination); Gingery v. OPM, CH-300A-13-0240-I-1 (NP 2/14/2014) ("The Board has applied the 30-day time limit to employment practices appeals. See O'Leary v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 87, ¶ 7 (2000). Also, the Federal Circuit indicated in an unpublished decision that the 30-day filing period applies to appeals alleging a violation of the requirements set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 300.103. See Clay v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 243 F.3d 565, 2000 WL 1644606, *2 (Fed. Cir. 2000)."); Burroughs v. Dept. of Army, 116 MSPR 292, 300 ¶ 22, 2011 MSPB 31 (2011) (noting the 30 day limit for filing an employment practice claim from the time the appellant learned of his nonselection; remanding for further development of the record on timeliness, including a determination of whether the agency was required to notify the appellant of Board appeal rights when it notified him of his nonselection); O'Leary v. OPM, 86 MSPR 87, 93 ¶¶ 12-14 (2000) (interpreting the regulatory deadline; just cause for a delay in the circumstances of the case: OPM knew that the appellant had considered his ALJ examination scores to reflect an improper employment practice and OPM should have provided a notice of appeal rights; the exact jurisdictional basis for filing an appeal did not become clear to the appellant until he became aware of the initial decision in an appeal brought by other agency employees), on denial of OPM reconsideration petition, 90 MSPR 124 (2001). The appellant asserting a challenge to an employment practice should do so during the time his appeal is before the administrative judge, not for the first time on PFR. See Perez v. DHS, NY-3443-09-0286-I-1 (NP 8/5/2010) (still adjudicating the allegation). As in other appeals, discovery rights apply to employment practice challenges. See Beam v. OPM, 61 MSPR 54 (1994), post.

The necessity for notice of appeal rights as a basis for calculation of the timeliness of an employment practices appeal was noted in Purifoy v. Dept. of Navy, AT-3443-12-0204-I-1 (NP 7/3/2013):

To establish good cause for the untimely filing of an appeal, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980). In O'Leary v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 87 (2000), the Board discussed the relevant factors for determining the timeliness of an employment practice claim in an appeal that involved the Office of Personnel Management's (OPM's) administrative law judge (ALJ) selection process. The Board stated:

An important factor in determining whether good cause exists in this case is whether the appellant was entitled to a notice of appeal rights. An agency is obligated to notify an employee of his right of appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board when it takes an appealable action. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.21. In cases in which the nominal action is an employee-initiated action, such as a retirement or resignation, that is ordinarily not appealable to the Board, the agency is not required to give the employee a notice of appeal rights unless the employee has placed the agency on notice that he considers his separation to have been involuntary, or the circumstances show that the agency was, or reasonably should have been, aware of facts indicating that the separation was involuntary. See, e.g., Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 583, 586 (1995); Jones v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 M.S.P.R. 306, 309 (1994). Absent an obligation on the agency's part to provide appeal rights, the appellant must establish that he exercised due diligence in attempting to discover whatever appeal rights he might have in order to show good cause. Gingrich, 67 M.S.P.R. at 586. If the agency failed to meet its obligation to provide a notice of appeal rights, however, the appellant is not required to show due diligence in discovering appeal rights; the question is whether the appellant was diligent in filing an appeal after he learned that he could do so. Id. at 588.

86 M.S.P.R. 87, ¶ 11. The Board stated in O'Leary that OPM would be required to provide notice of appeal rights to the appellant if "it had reason to know that the appellant believed that its ALJ examination had been applied in a discriminatory fashion or was inherently biased against a certain group of individuals." Id., ¶ 12 (citation omitted). The Board further found that the appellant was entitled to notice of appeal rights because the record showed that the appellant had notified OPM that he thought that its ALJ process was discriminatory. Id., ¶¶ 12-13. Finally, the Board held that the appellant had shown good cause for his delay in filing the appeal because he filed his appeal less than a month after becoming aware that he could file an appeal. Id., ¶¶ 13-14.

In this case, the agency similarly had reason to know that the appellant believed that the selection process for the Program Analyst position was discriminatory; therefore, it was obligated to provide her with notice of the right to file an employment practice appeal. Furthermore, in the absence of evidence that the agency provided the appellant with notice of appeal rights, the administrative judge's determination that the appellant failed to raise an employment practice claim in her EEO complaint is irrelevant to the determination of timeliness. Rather, the question presented is whether the appellant was diligent in filing an appeal after she learned that she could file one. Gingrich, 67 M.S.P.R. at 588. Here, the record is not developed on this issue. Accordingly, it is necessary to remand this issue for a determination of when the appellant became aware of her right to file an employment practice appeal and whether she, thereafter, acted with due diligence and reasonable prudence in filing this appeal.

See Gingery v. OPM, CH-300A-13-0240-I-1 (NP 2/14/2014) ("Although the regulations governing unemployment [sic] practices appeals do not explicitly state that the 30-day filing limit set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b)(1) applies to such appeals, we find that a person of ordinary prudence would not have believed that he could have waited indefinitely to file this appeal. At the very least, due diligence required that the appellant consult with the Board to ascertain whether his interpretation of the relevant Board regulations was correct. See Ford v. Office of Personnel Management, 59 M.S.P.R. 66, 69-70 (1993) (to the extent that the appellant was confused about the filing deadline, ordinary prudence requires that he inquire of the agency or the Board).").

The Board rejected a continuing violation approach to the timing of an employment practices appeal, Chadwell v. OPM, DA-300A-09-0680-I-1 (NP 5/5/2011):

...We agree with the administrative judge, however, that he must identify an "action" appealable to the Board, that the time period for filing an appeal is governed by the date of the action or receipt of the agency's decision concerning the action, and that his "continuing violation" theory does not provide a basis for extending the filing deadline. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a); Ozier v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 400 F. App'x 560, 562 (Fed. Cir. 2010); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b)(1). The appellant also contends that the administrative judge misrepresented his burden of proof in her show-cause order on timeliness by requiring him to show "good cause" for his delay in filing. He asserts that the burden should be "lower" because OPM did not notify him of his right to appeal. Again, however, we agree with the administrative judge that the appellant, who is an attorney, demonstrated that he was aware of the appeal process by filing his previous appeal. To the extent that his delay was caused by his attempts to discover additional bases for appeal or evidence to support an appeal, he has similarly not shown good cause for waiving the filing deadline. See, e.g., Ozier, 400 F. App'x at 562-63.
