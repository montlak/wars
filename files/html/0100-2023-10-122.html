B. FORM AND TIMING OF MOTIONS

The Board's rules call for written motions, with the exception of oral motions made during the prehearing conference or during the hearing. 5 CFR 1201.55(c) (2022) No specific form is required by regulation, although an AJ could set requirements concerning the form or timing of motions through an order. The motion can be made in a letter or through a document bearing the caption of the case with a formal heading "Motion" for whatever is being requested. What is essential is that the party filing the motion state what is wanted and why he or she thinks that the motion should be granted. "A motion's substance rather than its linguistic form determines its nature and thus its legal effect." Intellectual Property v. TCI Cablevision, 248 F.3d 1333, 1340 (Fed. Cir. 2001). The basic purpose of communications from an appellant should be determined regardless of whether they are formally designated as motions. In Joseph v. USPS, 2 MSPR 161, 2 MSPB 327, 162-63 (1980), the appellant made it clear through correspondence that he could not afford to travel to Dallas to attend a hearing concerning the termination of his employment in St. Louis. The AJ decided the case on the record. The Board reversed and sent the AJ to a hearing in St. Louis, stating that the AJ should have construed the appellant's letters about financial hardship as a motion to change the venue of the hearing.

The motion, a certificate of service, or some accompanying document (e.g., a transmittal letter) should state that a copy of the motion was served on the opposing party or counsel if the parties are not using e-filing with all parties immediately served through that system. The regulatory requirements should ordinarily be followed; without a showing of prejudice, however, the granting of a motion made telephonically that should have been made in writing is not cause for reversing the order. Johnson v. VA, 45 MSPR 206, 208 (1990). When the motion is made through e-filing, that system ordinarily automatically accomplishes service, unless some participants in the appeal are not e-filers and must then be served by mail or fax or, if agreed, by email.

At one time the Board's rules called for the submission of a proposed form of order. What is a form of order? It is a written statement of the action that you want the AJ to take. It is simpler to give an example than to describe it. If, for example, you are filing a motion to compel the opposing party, the agency, to produce some documents, you file a motion. With the motion, you file a separate page with a caption on it, with the word "Order" right below the caption, (centered), and beneath it, language like the following: "Upon consideration of the appellant's motion to compel discovery and the opposition to that motion filed by the agency-respondent, it is ordered this ___ day of ____ 2023, that the Agency is to provide the documents enumerated by Appellant's request within ten days from the date of this Order." Beneath that language, put a signature line, and beneath the signature line, put the words "Administrative Judge." If the AJ signs the order, he serves it, or he prepares his own order and serves it. The proposed order provides the AJ with a precise description of what the AJ is requested to direct or approve.

The requirement for the form of order was dropped, probably because no one knew what the Board wanted or when forms were provided they were not used. The form of order can still be used, and its use may be recommended when the advocate wants to present a clear, cogent statement to the AJ of exactly what the advocate wants. Examples of situations suggest forms of orders would be consented discovery schedules in complex cases or consented protective orders covering information produced in discovery.

Procedural motions, e.g., motions for an extension of time or postponement of a hearing, should be accompanied by a statement that the other party was contacted and that that party's consent has either been provided or denied as to the proposed motion (or, the other side states it has no position or it does not respond). 5 CFR 1201.55(a) (2022). Often the exchange between counsel is through email. The email showing a request for consent or opposition can be attached to the motion. The other party need not consent to the motion; if the other party cannot be located or will not return a call, that should be stated in the motion. Motions to compel discovery, discussed in Chapter 2, may require a sworn statement from a party or counsel as to the nature of the response, or lack of a response, to a discovery request (accompanied by the discovery request and any (deficient) response). Motions seeking to excuse a late appeal or a late PFR (or response to a PFR), motions for counsel fees, and motions for subpoenas, may have their own particular requirements, dictated by Board regulations governing those motions. Some motions are unopposed, or they may be joint, e.g., a request for a suspension of proceedings, a motion for a continuance. The party who submits an unopposed or joint motion should make plain the nature of the motion, so that it can be granted without the delay entailed when the AJ waits for a response to the motion.

Motions during the course of the hearing may be oral or in writing. When made orally, the opposing party must be given an opportunity to object to the motion on the record. Although the AJ has considerable discretion concerning treatment of motions, Johnson v. USPS, 37 MSPR 388, 392 (1988), adjudication procedures must comport with the basic requirements of fairness and notice, including an opportunity to respond. Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., 32 MSPR 488 (1987). But see Williams, post. Although motions are supposed to be served upon opposing representatives, it is still the responsibility of the AJ to ensure that service occurred. The AJ errs by assuming, lacking a service certificate, that service was made. The AJ should not grant a motion before he has made a determination concerning service. Rodarte v. OPM, 29 MSPR 224, 225 (1985), followed, Mughal v. OPM, 54 MSPR 554, 556 (1992) (AJ erred in not assuring that OPM received a copy of the appellant's motion, and that it had an opportunity to respond, when the motion had no certificate of service). With electronic filing becoming more common, service of a motion is usually automatically accomplished upon filing (assuming everyone is an e-filer).

The response period to motions now allowed by regulation is ten days from the date of service of the motion. The calculation is from the date of service, i.e., the date the motion was e-filed, put in the mail, hand-delivered, faxed, or delivered to a commercial overnight express service. This leaves only a short period of time for responses, and if the responses are not filed on time, the motion may be treated as unopposed. Although all parties are permitted to reply to motions, if circumstances require action on the motion before a reply can be filed, the AJ may act upon the motion, waiving the regulatory right of a reply. Williams v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 427, 430, 4 MSPB 479 (1980); cf. Gaff v. Dept. of Transp., 45 MSPR 387, 391 (1990) (no reversible error, although the AJ dismissed appeals prior to the appellants' period for responding to agency motions; there was no indication that the AJ did not consider responses to motions and the appellants did not show they were precluded from filing additional evidence pertaining to jurisdiction because they lacked more time to respond). If the party who has not had the chance to reply to the motion is dissatisfied with the result, that party may file a motion for reconsideration of the order, or, lacking that opportunity, the objection may be made in a PFR from the initial decision. The AJ (on his or her own, or at the urging of a party), may eliminate some delay and paperwork entailed with a motion by convening a conference call, listening to the positions from each side of the motion, and ruling on the motion during the conference (with a written order issued after the conference call concludes). As is the case with other Board regulatory deadlines, an extension of time may be requested to respond to a motion.

There is no regulatory time limit for an AJ to decide a motion. Some AJs do not rule on motions at all (an undesirable practice); some wait until the prehearing conference to rule on them. To preserve for PFR an objection to the AJ's failure to rule on a motion, an objection must be filed or otherwise lodged (for example, during the prehearing conference) with the AJ. See Matosian v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 689, 697 (1993). There is no best time to file an objection. If after a week has passed from the time of the response to the motion, or after no response has then been filed, the moving party should write a letter to the AJ, copied to opposing counsel, objecting to the failure to rule on the motion and suggesting a conference call to discuss the motion and obtain a ruling. This may not be endearing to the AJ (who is being called negligent), but it preserves for later PFR the right to object to the AJ's inaction--a matter of importance, for example, in a discovery dispute. For e-filers, letters are difficult to file; instead, file a request for a conference call to discuss the status of the motion.

AJs typically issue prehearing orders calling for motions concerning hearing procedures to be filed several weeks prior to the hearing. Attention must be paid to the prehearing order. If the appellant is granted a continuance for the hearing, but the prehearing order is not modified to extend the time for the filing of motions, an appellant's motion submitted past the deadline may be denied as untimely even though it precedes the hearing. See Cottman v. Dept. of Labor, 23 MSPR 688, 691 (1984) (motion for a subpoena). Yet prehearing orders are subject to a rule of reason. An ambiguous prehearing notice calling for submission of motions prior to the hearing should not bar a pro se appellant's request for documents on the day of the hearing, unless there is reasoned weighing by the AJ of the relevance of the requested documents against the delay caused by directing the agency to produce the documents during the hearing. Although production of documents may not alter the result of the case, it is unjust to deny the appellant the proper presentation of his defense only to save time. Baum v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 492, 494, 6 MSPB 414 (1981).
