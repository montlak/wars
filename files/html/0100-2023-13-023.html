a. Hearing

Under Ragland v. IRS, 1 MSPR 758, 759, 2 MSPB 167 (1980), "[w]hen an employee presents a nonfrivolous argument that his acceptance of a reassignment to a lower grade was coerced, and this argument is based on more than mere conclusory allegations, the employee is entitled to a hearing on the allegation." According to Dvorin v. Dept. of Air Force, 70 MSPR 407, 410-11 (1996):

There is no statute which requires the Board to hold a hearing on the threshold issue of jurisdiction. See Wilson v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 807 F.2d 1577, 1582-83 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Manning v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 742 F.2d 1424, 1427-28 (Fed. Cir. 1984). However, where an appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the jurisdictional question. Dumas v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 789 F.2d 892, 894 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994). An appellant is not required to prove Board jurisdiction to entitle her to a hearing; rather, she need only make a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over her appeal. See Alford v. Department of the Army, 47 MSPR 271, 274 (1991). Nonfrivolous allegations of Board jurisdiction are allegations of facts which, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter in issue. Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894; Ferdon, 60 MSPR at 329.

In determining whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction entitling her to a hearing, an administrative judge may consider an agency's documentary submissions. See Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894; Manning, 742 F.2d at 1428; Ferdon, 60 MSPR at 329. Although the threshold issue of whether the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal may be disposed of on the basis of the documentary record in appropriate cases, such disposition is not always permissible. Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894; Manning, 742 F.2d at 1426. To the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction to the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions of the parties, and the agency's evidence may not be dispositive. See Dumas, 789 F.2d at 893-94; see also Ferdon, 60 MSPR at 329 (the administrative judge may consider the agency's unrebutted or uncontradicted documentary evidence which tends to show that the appellant has no right to appeal to the Board).

The appellant contended below that she had been constructively demoted, in that the agency's creation of the GS-1701-11 Supervisory ESL Instructor position in September 1992, with the same duties and responsibilities as her former GS-1712-9 Supervisory Training Instructor position, constituted a correction of a classification error, which made her former GS-9 position worth a GS-11, and that she should have been selected noncompetitively for the GS-11 position. The administrative judge found the appellant failed to allege sufficient facts to support her claim that she had been constructively demoted, upon considering the evidence submitted by both parties and making factual findings concerning the weight of this evidence. These determinations should not have been made until after a jurisdictional hearing. See Ferdon, 60 MSPR at 329-30; Alford, 47 MSPR at 274. A resolution of these disputed issues requires weighing the evidence and resolving conflicting factual assertions of the parties. Accordingly, the administrative judge erred by dismissing the appellant's appeal without affording her a jurisdictional hearing. See Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894; Ferdon, 60 MSPR at 329-30.

A hearing is warranted on the basis of a specific charge that an employee was induced to accept a reduction in pay through misrepresentations by the agency. McVickers v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 566, 568 (1982) (employee who entered an apprenticeship program with a pay cut brought an appeal based on an allegation that the agency misled him into believing that his previous salary eventually would be reinstated). An allegation of involuntary downgrading is nonfrivolous and warrants a hearing if the appellant states that he was informed that he would have to request a downgrade to continue to work, he did not initiate the request, and he signed the request under protest. Enriquez v. GSA, 15 MSPR 309, 310 (1983). When an argument that the acceptance of the reassignment was coerced is based on more than conclusory allegations, the employee is entitled to a hearing on the allegations. Stepp v. FAA, 7 MSPR 321, 322, 7 MSPB 210 (1981); see Hinton-Morgan v. Dept. of Army, 75 MSPR 382, 393 (1997) ("To the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions of the parties and the agency's evidence may not be dispositive.").

Ultimately the voluntary (or involuntary) nature of the employee's action is an issue of fact. To obtain a hearing, an employee's appeal may contain specific factual allegations concerning communications between the appellant and his supervisor, presenting a case of unwarranted modification of duties, coupled with a threat of removal leading to acceptance of a demotion. At the hearing, the employee must prove the statements attributed to the supervisor, the changes in duties, and the elements of coercion. Heil v. USPS, 5 MSPR 442, 443, 5 MSPB 448 (1981) (citing Ragland v. IRS, 1 MSPR 758, 759-60, 2 MSPB 167 (1980), and Spiegel v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 140, 142, 2 MSPB 307 (1980)). If the allegation is that the downgrading was coerced, the appellant must come forward with sufficient evidence to show that his demotion was involuntarily extracted or coerced; otherwise his requested demotion will be deemed voluntary and not subject to the Board's jurisdiction. Cline v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 102 (1984) (position change was not involuntary when the appellant was dissatisfied with the current position and location of that position; the agency did not coerce the move).
