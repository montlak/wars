b. Discovery During Pendency of Criminal Proceedings

To avoid the possibility of appellants circumventing rules restricting disclosure of information in the criminal cases, the Board fashioned a rule that will call for discovery normally to be stayed when an appellant is also enmeshed in criminal proceedings involving substantially the same conduct leading to the Board appeal, Keay v. USPS, 57 MSPR 331, 355-37 (1993):

At the outset, we note that whether good cause exists for postponement of a hearing is determined by the unique circumstances of each case and rests with the sound discretion of the administrative judge.... Similarly, we have found that administrative judges have broad authority in discovery matters and, absent a showing of abuse of this discretion, we will not substitute our judgment for theirs. See Brumley v. Dept. of Transp., 46 MSPR 666, 681 (1991). Accordingly, the determination of whether an agency's motion to stay all discovery should be granted cannot be done in the abstract but must be made on a case-by-case basis.

The Board has generally stayed its proceedings pending the completion of a criminal trial because its proceedings could interfere with an ongoing criminal case involving the same conduct....

In Afro-Lecon v. United States, 890 F.2d 1198, 1203-04 (Fed. Cir. 1987), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit recognized the potential harm to the prosecution of the criminal case, as well as to the criminal defendant, which may result from the broad scope of civil discovery, in contrast to criminal "discovery." It further noted that postponement of civil proceedings is "desirable...for the protection of the integrity of two separate processes," citing Peden v. United States, 512 F.2d 1099, 1103, 206 Ct. Cl. 329 (1975). 820 F.2d at 1204. We therefore find that such a consideration must enter into the stay determination, even if it does not directly affect the presentation of the agency's case on appeal and there is no specific showing that the related criminal case necessarily will be harmed. See Pidock, 54 MSPR at 32 (Board proceedings "might" constitute improper interference); Wallington, 42 MSPR at 465 (Board proceedings "could" constitute improper interference). We further find, however, that the extent of the potential harm to the related criminal case and the specificity of that showing would affect the weight given to this factor in determining the stay request. Moreover, the Board may allow certain discovery to continue in particular circumstances, such as to preserve evidence which may otherwise be lost, even though a stay is warranted, or consider the issuance of protective discovery orders if necessary to prevent prejudice, where discovery is allowed to continue. See generally 4 Moore's Federal Practice (2nd Ed. 1993) Para. 26.70[2] at 26-463-26-465 [and cases cited therein] .

In Wallington, 42 MSPR at 465, the Board, while acknowledging that it would suspend its proceedings pending the completion of the trial, adopted the standard set forth in Afro-Lecon, 820 F.2d at 1202--"whether the interests of justice seem to require such action"--for determining whether to stay its proceedings until the final resolution of the appellant's criminal proceedings.[2] We find that, regardless of whether the stay is requested by the appellant or the agency, this standard is generally met where a stay is requested for a reasonable period pending the completion of a related criminal trial, it is unlikely that either party would be prejudiced thereby, and prosecuting authorities have concurred in the stay request to prevent undue interference in ongoing criminal proceedings and have provided specific reasons therefor. We further find that this standard has been met in this appeal and that the administrative judge properly exercised her judgment in granting the discovery stay.

[2] In determining whether a stay of proceedings or discovery is appropriate, the courts have considered the following factors:

(1) the interest of the plaintiff in proceeding expeditiously with the civil action as balanced against the prejudice to the plaintiff [from] delay; (2) the burden on defendants; (3) the convenience to the courts; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the public interest. In re Mid-Atlantic Toyota Antitrust Litigation, 92 F.R.D. 358, 359 (D. Md. 1981).

First, the Board proceedings will not be inordinately delayed because of the stay. We note that the appellant has not objected to the postponement of the hearing and thus a further delay in discovery proceedings will not significantly affect the expeditious processing of the appeal. When Board proceedings resume, he will have the opportunity to conduct discovery prior to the hearing. Moreover, the length of the stay is for a reasonable period, i.e., until the completion of the trial, and the appellant is free to request reconsideration of the stay if the trial is not completed within a reasonable time. He has not shown prejudice by this delay. As the administrative judge found, his only claim of prejudice, that the informant's memory of the drug transaction would fade, has no basis in fact. There is no indication in the record that the informant will not be available for discovery proceedings because of advanced age, etc., requiring that her testimony be preserved now. Moreover, if the appellant prevails in his appeal, he will be restored to the status quo ante. Pidock, 54 MSPR at 31-32.

Second, while the agency has not demonstrated that the presentation of its case would be materially affected by the denial of a stay or that third parties would be affected, the public interest will be affected thereby. As we discuss above, the deputy district attorney submitted a sworn statement that his prosecution may be hindered or otherwise impaired by a continuation of the Board's proceedings. He further stated that the appellant would not be allowed to depose its witnesses prior to trial under Pennsylvania's Rules of Criminal Procedure. The appellant has asserted that the Commonwealth would not be affected by proceeding with Board discovery because the witnesses have already testified at a preliminary hearing. But, he does not deny that he can circumvent, in effect, the limitations on criminal discovery by using the Board's discovery proceedings to depose the Commonwealth's witnesses before trial, even if he has a different attorney representing him on the criminal matter. Nor has he offered to limit his Board discovery to that permitted under the Commonwealth's Criminal Procedure Rules. Therefore, instead of the Commonwealth seeking to gain an unwarranted advantage, we find that the appellant is more likely to do so by deposing the agency's witnesses in advance of his criminal trial....

Finally, we note that duplicative efforts in connection with the Board's proceedings may be avoided by the stay and that the appellant will not be burdened by the stay since he will be able to defend himself in the criminal proceedings without prosecuting his Board appeal at the same time. Accordingly, we find that the interests of the parties require the Board to grant the stay.
