a. Use of the APA

Under limited circumstances, the APA can be of use to individual employees seeking to challenge agency personnel actions. See Atterbury v. U.S. Marshals Service, 941 F.3d 56, 63 (2nd Cir. 2019) (labor contract calling for "just cause" for termination of court security officer established a constitutional right to due process under the APA); Hubbard v. EPA, 809 F.2d 1 (D.C. Cir. 1986) (permitting an APA suit to enforce First Amendment rights through an injunction action pursued by an applicant for a federal position); see Hubbard v. MSPB, 205 F.3d 1315 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (back pay was unavailable to applicant for employment under the Back Pay Act or Reform Act in a correction action undertaken by OSC for events occurring prior to the 1989 WPA amendments); Hubbard v. EPA, 982 F.2d 531, 532 (D.C. Cir. 1992) (denying a back pay remedy to Hubbard, an applicant for employment, despite his victory in his constitutional/APA suit).

The Eighth Circuit suggested in Hastings v. FAA, 187 F.3d 938, 940 (8th Cir. 1999), that it is "far from certain" whether, given the preclusive effect of the CSRA, a private cause of action may be brought in district court for violation of the Classification Act. If the MSPB would have jurisdiction over the basic claims presented by a plaintiff to a court, the Reform Act will bar an independent cause of action in district court directly against the government. The plaintiff's remedy is a timely appeal to the Board (or protest to OSC or filing of an EEO complaint) and the availability of judicial review of the administrative decision. See Wilder v. Prokop, 846 F.2d 613 (10th Cir. 1988); Carducci, post. In the Ninth Circuit, the APA provides a basis for reviewing classification decisions issued by the Office of Personnel Management. Burroughs v. OPM, 764 F.2d 1300, 1302 (9th Cir. 1985). But this is not true in the District of Columbia Circuit. Barnhart v. Devine, 771 F.2d 1515, 1523 (D.C. Cir. 1985); see Harrison v. Bowen, 815 F.2d 1505, 1516 (D.C. Cir. 1987) (comprehensive scheme of CSRA precluded APA review of a performance-based action against an excepted service employee); NTEU v. Egger, 783 F.2d 1114 (D.C. Cir. 1986) (direct resort to court to review OPM classification determinations is precluded by CSRA provisions granting the right to petition OSC to remedy prohibited personnel practice provisions that are arguably implicated when the agency violates statutory provisions calling for equal pay for equal work).

Other decisions suggest limited availability of the APA. See Ass'n. of ALJs v. Colvin, 777 F.3d 402, 404, 406 (7th Cir. 2015) (Judge Ripple concurring) (Administrative Procedure Act did not furnish jurisdiction to a district court to evaluate claims that production goals for SSA ALJs interfered with their judicial independence; the concurring opinion by Judge Ripple suggested the possibility that workload requirements on judges could lead to a loss of judicial independence); Graham v. Aschcroft, 358 F.3d 931, 935 (D.C. Cir. 2004) (judicial review was unavailable for a minor disciplinary action despite a claimed violation of agency regulations; the principle that agencies must follow their own regulations was trumped by the inability of agencies to confer rights undermining a comprehensive statutory scheme, established by the CSRA, defining conditions of judicial review); Grier v. Secretary of the Army, 799 F.2d 721 (11th Cir. 1988) (exercising, without discussion of jurisdictional problems, APA authority to review and deny plaintiff's claim of violation of procedural rights when she was not selected for a promotion); Hondros v. Civil Serv. Comm'n, 720 F.2d 278, 293-94 (3d Cir. 1983) (APA jurisdiction to determine whether an agency arbitrarily circumvented legal requirements to avoid using a civil service register referral procedure it earlier promised to employ); Gnotta v. United States, 415 F.2d 1271, 1276 (8th Cir. 1969) (suggesting lack of jurisdiction to review claimed denial of promotion). But see Blue v. Widnall, 162 F.3d 541, 545 (9th Cir. 1998) (following Veit v. Heckler, 746 F.2d 508, 511 (9th Cir. 1984): "the general judicial review provisions of the APA never confer jurisdiction to review federal personnel actions.").

The APA was used to secure review in a district court, and subsequently in a circuit court of appeals, of a decision by OPM affirming an insurer's denial of a federal employee's claim for benefits under a health insurance plan. See Muratore v. OPM, 222 F.3d 918, 920-22 (11th Cir. 2000) (adopting "arbitrary and capricious" rather than a de novo standard of health insurance contract review); Bryan v. OPM, 165 F.3d 1315, 1318-20 (10th Cir. 1999) (APA governs disputes under Federal Employees Health Benefits Act; however, the remedial authority of the court was limited by 5 CFR 890.107(c) (2022) to a court order directing OPM to require the insurance carrier to pay the disputed benefits following the exhaustion of administrative remedies permitting a challenge to full or partial nonpayment of claims); Burgin v. OPM, 120 F.3d 494 (4th Cir. 1997) (court construes an insurance contract rather than deferring to OPM's interpretation of the contract); Nesseim v. Mail Handlers Benefit Plan, 995 F.2d 804, 807 (8th Cir. 1993) (measure of review of an OPM decision is the APA standard of "arbitrary, capricious, abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law"); Harris v. Mutual of Omaha Co., 992 F.2d 706, 712 (7th Cir. 1993) (OPM's denial of coverage was a final decision within the meaning of the APA; the district court applies a deferential standard of review under the APA). The APA was employed to obtain review of OPM wage rate determinations. See Albaniz v. OPM, 728 F.2d 1460, 1463 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (district court had APA jurisdiction to review the methodology used by OPM to determine COLA rates). A claim to promotion was considered in Cominsky v. Rice, 233 F. Supp. 190 (E.D. Pa. 1964) (apparent APA jurisdiction: the court adhered to deferential standard of review relative to the agency's assessment of the plaintiff's qualifications for a position; court noted the extensive grievance proceedings prior to the lawsuit). The APA was not available to review an agency's decision to revoke an employee's security clearance, because that decision was an agency action committed to agency discretion by law. Oryszak v. Sullivan, 576 F.3d 522, 527 (D.C. Cir. 2009);cf. Zummer v. Sallet, 37 F.4th 996 (5th Cir. 2022) (CSRA precluded jurisdiction in district court over suit by employee who lost his job when he lost his clearance and sought to litigate the constitutionality of the clearance revocation). APA review was denied to a national union attempting to challenge agency regulatory requirements that Air Reserve Technicians wear military uniforms while on civilian duty. The availability of FLRA negotiability and unfair labor practice processes, as well as the availability of contract grievance procedures, impliedly precluded a direct APA remedy in district court. See AFGE v. Sec'y. of Air Force, 716 F.3d 633, 636-40 (D.C. Cir. 2013). APA review was unavailable for a VA physician who sought review of a VA grievance decisions sustaining his dismissal. See Hakki v. VA, 7 F.4th 1012 (11th Cir. 2021) (Judge Jordan concurring) (38 USC 7463 and related statutes precluded judicial review absent a statutory grant).
