6. Postal Service Employees

Non-Postal Service employees appeal an arbitration award involving an EEO issue to Board headquarters. For Postal Service employees, the review scheme differs. A Postal employee without ordinary Board appeal rights has no right to MSPB review of an arbitration award, whether or not there is an issue of discrimination. A preference eligible Postal employee may pursue both a Board appeal and a grievance through her collective bargaining contract. If the grievance results in an arbitration award, the Postal employee cannot secure direct Board review of the award. If discrimination is raised for the first time in the Board appeal by the Postal employee, the Board reviews that affirmative defense de novo. [Refer in Chapter 1 to "Effect of Collective Bargaining Contract Grievance or Related Procedures; 5 USC 7121"; "Postal Service Employees."] The Postal manager or personnelist [whose statutory appeal rights are discussed in Chapter 1, "Postal Service"] has no grievance arbitration route; he must pursue an ordinary Board appeal with the usual affirmative defenses.

The analysis of Postal employees' appeal rights when grievance procedures are also invoked is indirect. 5 USC 7121(d) grants a right of Board review of arbitration decisions when EEO issues are raised with the Board. The statute requires the employee to make a binding election between the contract procedure and the Board appellate procedure, but if the contract process is chosen: "[s]election of the negotiated procedure in no manner prejudices the right of an aggrieved employee to request the Merit Systems Protection Board to review the final decision pursuant to section 7702 of this title in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board." But 5 USC 7121, involving election of remedies, and its implementing regulation, 5 CFR 1201.3(c)(1) (2022), are made inapplicable to the Postal Service by the Postal Reorganization Act, 84 Stat. 719 (1970). Those Postal employees who are eligible to appeal to the Board and who are covered by negotiated grievance procedures are not forced to make a statutory election of remedies. Preference eligible employees in the Postal Service have the right to appeal an adverse action to the Board's regional offices, as well as the right to invoke the grievance provisions of a collective bargaining agreement specified in 39 USC 1206(b). Hall v. USPS, 26 MSPR 233, 236 (1985); see Landingham v. USPS, 81 MSPR 77, 81 ¶ 6 (1999) (preference-eligible Postal Service employee may file both a grievance and a Board appeal from the same action, but does not have the right to Board review of an arbitrator's award); Blackiemore v. USPS, 65 MSPR 424, 427-28 (1994) (if a Postal employee elects to file an EEO complaint with his agency, he may file an appeal with Board 20 [now 30] days after he receives agency resolution or a final decision on the discrimination issue, or, if the agency has not resolved the matter or issued a final decision within 120 days, the employee may appeal the matter to the Board at any time after the expiration of 120 calendar days); Jackson v. USPS, 57 MSPR 57, 59 (1993) (Board appeal must be initiated within 20 [now 30] days of the effective date of the adverse action; the employee may not await an arbitrator's decision to seek review, as might an employee of another agency who could not simultaneously file an MSPB appeal and pursue a grievance); Emerald v. USPS, 49 MSPR 586, 589 (1991) (apparently recognizing that the Postal Service employee can initiate the appeal after the agency completes an EEO investigation into his complaint); Epstein v. USPS, 36 MSPR 662, 664 (1988) (MSPB does not have jurisdiction to review appeals from USPS arbitration decisions under 5 USC 7121; rather, preference eligible postal employees may avail themselves of both a Board appeal and the negotiated grievance procedure).

Summarizing the processes for eligible Postal Service employees, Muse v. USPS, 82 MSPR 342, 345 ¶ 8 (1999):

An eligible Postal employee may simultaneously file both a grievance and a Board appeal, each subject to relevant timeliness requirements. See Marjie v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 95, 98 (1996). Only within the context of a timely filed petition for appeal of an agency action may the Board review a Postal employee's arbitration decision on a grievance. See Fedon v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 MSPR 657, 660 (1998).

Moore v. USPS, 91 MSPR 277, 279 n.1 (2002), also explained the availability of Board review for postal employees who would otherwise have appeal rights:

Generally, under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d), an individual affected by a personnel action that is both appealable to the Board and covered by a negotiated grievance procedure may contest the action before the Board or via a grievance, but not both. An individual who chooses initially to grieve may seek Board review of the final decision on the grievance if he or she claims that the personnel action was based on discrimination prohibited by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1). Section 7121(d), however, does not apply to the U.S. Postal Service, and thus Postal Service employees have the right to grieve and to appeal actions directly to the Board. Marjie v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 95, 98 (1996).

In greater detail, the review scheme was explained in Fedon v. USPS, 78 MSPR 657, 660 (1998):

As a general rule, an individual affected by a personnel action that is both appealable to the Board and covered by a negotiated grievance procedure may contest the action before the Board or via a grievance, but not both; an individual who elects to grieve may seek Board review of the final decision on the grievance if he or she claims that the action was based on prohibited discrimination. See 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d). That statutory provision does not apply, however, to a Postal Service employee who has the right to appeal an action directly to the Board. See Lucas v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 M.S.P.R. 459, 461 (1989); Banks v. U.S. Postal Service, 31 M.S.P.R. 307, 308 (1986). An eligible Postal Service employee can file both a grievance and a Board appeal from the same action, but does not have a right of Board review of an arbitration decision. Marjie v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 95, 98 (1996). When the appeal of a Postal Service preference eligible employee is properly before the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d), and the action appealed has been the subject of an arbitration decision, the Board may consider the arbitration decision if the Board otherwise has jurisdiction over the appeal and the timeliness requirements are met. See Lipsky v. U.S. Postal Service, 58 M.S.P.R. 555, 557-58 (1993). The appellant herein, as a preference-eligible, has no right to obtain review of the arbitration decision, as the administrative judge found, but she has a right to appeal her removal to the Board. Id.; see 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511(a)(1)(B)(ii), 7512(1), 7513(d). The initial decision dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, and did not decide the question of the timeliness of the appeal. Since, as set forth above, the Board has jurisdiction over the removal action, the issue of timeliness of the appeal must be addressed.

Postal Service employees may not appeal to the Board for headquarters review of an arbitration decision under 5 USC 7121. Burch v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3046) (citing Bacashihua v. MSPB, 811 F.2d 1498 (Fed. Cir. 1987)); see Clements v. USPS, 101 MSPR 218, 2006 MSPB 37 (2006) (Board lacked jurisdiction to review a grievance decision of a Postal Service employee but the case was remanded to the regional office for docketing as an adverse action appeal); Lipsky v. USPS, 58 MSPR 555, 559 (1993) ("Appeals of Postal Service employees are not covered under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(d), which implements 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) and allows an appellant who files under negotiated grievance procedures, and who raises issues of unlawful discrimination in connection with a matter otherwise appealable to the Board, to seek Board review of a final arbitration decision within twenty-five days of the issuance of the arbitration decision."); Fuentes v. USPS, 54 MSPR 4, 7 (1992) ("Postal Service employees do not have a right of arbitration review under 5 USC 7121"); Banks v. USPS, 31 MSPR 307, 308 (1986) ("Under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) the Board has authority to review final arbitration decisions pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7702 in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board, and which involves a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1). However, the Board has expressly held that 5 U.S.C. § 7121 and its implementing regulation, 5 C.F.R. § 1201.3(b)(1) are not applicable to the Postal Service," citing Winston v. USPS, 585 F.2d 198 (7th Cir. 1978)). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Postal Service."]

Of what significance is an arbitrator's award to a Postal Service employee who can and does file a timely direct appeal to the Board from the same action that was considered in the arbitration? If the Postal employee secures an arbitration award and also pursues a Board appeal, the Board adjudicates the appeal if it is timely and if the employee is within the Board's jurisdiction. Lipsky v. USPS, 58 MSPR 555, 557-58 (1993), stated: "When the appeal of a Postal Service preference eligible employee is properly before the Board under 5 USC 7512 and 5 USC 7701, and the action appealed has been the subject of an arbitration decision, the Board may consider the arbitration decision if the Board otherwise has jurisdiction over the appeal and timeliness requirements are met." Lipsky did not state what the Board meant by "may consider" the arbitrator's award when the Postal Service employee's timely appeal is adjudicated.

Lucas v. USPS, 39 MSPR 459, 461-62 (1989), considering the availability and timeliness of appeals of Postal Service employees, who can file both appeals and grievances, and other employees, who must elect between grievances and appeals, stated "[t]he Board may review a Postal Service arbitration decision only within the context of a timely-filed petition for appeal of an agency action over which the Board has jurisdiction":

The administrative judge first found that it was inappropriate for him to review the arbitrator's decision, citing the Board decision in Leamon v. Department of the Navy, 31 MSPR 189 (1986). He then found that the appellant's appeal was untimely. He noted that it was filed nearly 21 months after the effective date of the appellant's removal, and 5 months after the arbitrator's decision. The administrative judge concluded that the appellant failed to establish good cause for the untimeliness. In this regard, he noted that the appellant had not responded to his order to file evidence and argument on this issue.

...

We have reopened this case, however, to clarify the Board's procedure and scope of review in cases where a Postal Service employee who has received an arbitration decision appeals to the Board. Although the administrative judge correctly concluded that the appellant's petition should be dismissed as untimely, he apparently did so under law that is inapplicable to the Postal Service.

The administrative judge found that it was inappropriate for him to review the arbitrator's decision under Leamon, presumably because of the statement in that case that 5 CFR § 1201.157 precludes initial review of arbitration decisions by administrative judges. Leamon, however, deals with the Board's authority to review an arbitrator's decision under 5 USC § 7121(d). Id. at 192. That statutory provision does not apply to the Postal Service. See Hall v. United States Postal Service, 26 MSPR 233, 236 (1985). Unlike employees of other agencies, Postal Service employees may file both a grievance and an appeal at the same time. Id.

The Board may review a Postal Service arbitration decision only within the context of a timely-filed petition for appeal of an agency action over which the Board has jurisdiction. Such an appeal is properly before the regional office for determinations of timeliness and jurisdiction. See Waters v. United States Postal Service, 36 MSPR 683, 684 (1988); Epstein v. United States Postal Service, 36 MSPR 662, 664 (1988). Therefore, the administrative judge correctly found that he was required to determine whether the appellant's petition was timely from the date of the agency's action....

See Anderson v. USPS, 109 MSPR 558, 560 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 193 (2008) ("Pursuit of a grievance does not extend the time for filing a Board appeal."); Masdea v. USPS, 90 MSPR 556, 559 ¶ 8 (2002) (implying that waiting for resolution of a grievance did not excuse a late Board appeal; finding it "well-settled" that awaiting the outcome in another forum will not provide good cause); Muse v. USPS, 82 MSPR 342, 346 ¶ 11 (1999) ("It is well settled...that pursuit of a grievance in another forum does not constitute good cause for the untimeliness of an appeal."); Allison v. USPS, 81 MSPR 335, 340 (1999) ("[T]he fact that the arbitration decision was unfavorable to the appellant does not establish good cause for the untimeliness."); Zuhlke v. USPS, 74 MSPR 401, 404 ¶ 13 (1997) (pursuit of a grievance does not constitute good cause for an untimely appeal); cf. Bacashihua v. MSPB, 811 F.2d 1498 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (affirming the Board's treatment of election requirements inherent in USPS grievance procedures).

The Federal Circuit provided its view of the review scheme for postal service preference-eligible adverse action appeals, Robey v. MSPB, 253 Fed. Appx. 933, 934-35 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Preference-eligible employees of the Postal Service may: (1) appeal an agency action directly to the Board; (2) pursue grievance/arbitration procedures; or (3) pursue both within certain limitations. Stahl v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 83 F.3d 409, 411 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Although Mr. Robey initially appealed the removal action by the Postal Service to the Board, he later withdrew the appeal and instead pursued grievance/arbitration procedures through the union. Now, Mr. Robey appears to be seeking review of the determination by the arbitrator. As a Postal Service employee, however, Mr. Robey has no right of Board review of the arbitration decision. Fedon v. U.S. Postal Serv., 78 M.S.P.R. 657, 660 (1998); Marjie v. U.S. Postal Serv., 70 M.S.P.R. 95, 98 (1996). Although the Board may take the arbitration decision into consideration when deciding an appeal over which it otherwise has jurisdiction and in which the timeliness requirements have been met, Fedon, 78 M.S.P.R. at 660; Lipsky v. U.S. Postal Serv., 58 M.S.P.R. 555, 557-58 (1993), the Board may only review a Postal Service arbitration decision within the context of a timely appeal of the agency action. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Serv., 57 M.S.P.R. 57, 59 (1993).[1]

[1] The Federal Circuit likewise lacks jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's decision pursuant to a Postal Service agreement with the union. Burke v. U.S. Postal Serv., 888 F.2d 833, 834 (Fed. Cir. 1989). While 5 U.S.C. § 7121 authorizes the same review of arbitration decisions by the Federal Circuit as is available for decisions by the Board, § 7121 and its implementing regulations are not applicable to Postal Service employees. Id.

Jackson v. USPS, 57 MSPR 57, 59 (1993), recapitulated the limitations on review of arbitrators' awards involving Postal employees:

The administrative judge found that he had no authority to review the arbitration decision under 5 CFR § 1201.154(d) because the appellant did not raise an allegation of discrimination. Section 1201.154(d) implements 5 USC § 7121(d) which requires aggrieved employees alleging unlawful discrimination to elect between appealing to the Board and pursuing negotiated grievance procedures; it further provides for Board review of arbitration decisions where such discrimination is alleged. The Board has held, however, that 5 USC § 7121(d) does not apply to Postal Service employees with Board appeal rights who may file both a grievance and a Board appeal at the same time. Lucas v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 MSPR 459, 461 (1989); Hall v. U.S. Postal Service, 26 MSPR 233, 236 (1985). Correspondingly, the Board may only review Postal Service arbitration decisions within the context of a timely appeal of the agency action. Lucas, 39 MSPR at 461. Thus, since the appellant's petition for appeal of his removal was untimely filed and he had failed to show good cause for this delay, we conclude that the administrative judge properly dismissed the appeal.

Waters v. USPS, 36 MSPR 683, 684-85 (1988), spoke of deference to an arbitrator's award during a Postal Service employee's Board appeal:

If the administrative judge finds that this appeal was timely filed and that the Board has jurisdiction, the appellant is entitled to adjudication of the merits of his appeal. In addressing the merits, the arbitrator's decision is to be reviewed under the same standard applicable to preference eligible employees of other agencies. See Williams v. United States Postal Service, 35 MSPR 581, 588 (1987). The Board will, in most cases, defer to Postal Service arbitration decisions and will only set them aside or modify them where the appellant shows that the arbitrator erred in interpreting civil service law, rules, or regulation. Id. at 592. In cases in which a question arises about the appropriateness of deferral, the Board will follow the guidance of relevant Federal court decisions. Id. at 592 n.12. All requests for review must include a statement of the grounds on which the review is requested, evidence of record or rulings bearing on the issues before the Board, arguments in support of the stated grounds with specific reference to the pertinent documents and citations of authority, and legible copies of the award and other pertinent documents. Id. at 592 n.13.

That deference does not extend to collateral estoppel effect. See Kroeger v. USPS, 865 F.2d 235, 238 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (allowing for collateral estoppel effect of an arbitration award when the issue previously adjudicated is identical with the one later presented; the issue was actually litigated in the prior case; the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the end-decision then made; and the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action). The Board defers to the judgment of the arbitrator in most circumstances, but in appeals involving Postal Service preference eligible employees, the Board will not apply collateral estoppel to an arbitrator's findings on discrimination of the kind prohibited under 5 USC 2302(b)(1). Berkey v. USPS, 42 MSPR 24, 28 (1989) (declining to apply collateral estoppel to an arbitration award when the issues before the Board were not the issues actually litigated before the arbitrator).

Stahl v. MSPB, 83 F.3d 409, 411-13 (Fed. Cir. 1996), also explained the review scheme for Postal employees' cases that may travel the route of arbitration rather than an appeal to a Board regional office:

Ms. Stahl, a Distribution Clerk with the Postal Service, was removed from service in March 1992 on the ground that she was medically unfit for duty. Ms. Stahl is a preference eligible veteran as defined by statute, and therefore is afforded certain appeal rights not available to other Postal employees. 5 USC § 2108; 39 USC § 1005. Upon her removal the Postal Service gave Ms. Stahl the written information that she could appeal to the MSPB and could participate in negotiated grievance procedures. She was told that if she chose to appeal to the Board she waived access beyond Step 3 of the negotiated grievance procedures, a step that did not include formal arbitration, and was subject to certain additional limitations not here relevant. Ms. Stahl was given a copy of the MSPB regulations, 5 CFR § 1201 et seq. She then proceeded under the grievance procedures.

Eventually an arbitrator's decision against Ms. Stahl was issued on March 17, 1995. On April 21, 1995 Ms. Stahl filed with the MSPB a petition for review of the arbitrator's decision. The Board points out that the arbitrator's decision is not appealable, for 5 USC § 7121(d), which provides for Board review of arbitration decisions when discrimination is alleged, does not apply to Postal Service employees. Burke v. United States Postal Service, 888 F.2d 833, 834 (Fed. Cir. 1989); see Bacashihua v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 811 F.2d 1498, 1502 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (5 USC § 7121 and implementing regulations do not apply to the Postal Service).

...

In accordance with the Postal Reorganization Act, Pub. L. No. 91-375, 84 Stat. 719 (1970), Postal Service preference eligible employees may appeal directly to the Board or may invoke grievance/arbitration procedures under the agreement between the Postal Service and the American Postal Workers Union. Bacashihua, 811 F.2d at 1502. With limitations, preference eligible employees may participate in both paths of appeal. See Mays v. United States Postal Service, 995 F.2d 1056, 1059-60 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (discussing limitations on appeal procedures).

At the time of her separation Ms. Stahl was told, in the Letter of Decision, that an appeal to the Board would be deemed a waiver of grievance/arbitration procedures beyond Step 3. The letter stated certain limitations to her right to appeal to the Board, and that the time for appeal was "20 calendar days from the effective day of this decision." However, the MSPB regulations she was given at the time of separation stated that an arbitrator's decision can be appealed to the Board when there is an issue of prohibited discrimination.

Ms. Stahl argues that her appeal was filed within 35 days of the arbitrator's decision, as provided in the MSPB regulations she was given:

5 CFR § 1201.154(d). If the appellant has filed a grievance with the agency under its negotiated grievance procedure in accordance with 5 USC 7121, he or she may ask the Board to review the final decision under 5 USC 7702 within 35 days of the date of issuance of that decision....

5 USC § 7121, which is referred to in § 1201.154(d), in turn provides:

5 USC § 7121(d). An aggrieved employee affected by a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b)(1) of this title which also falls under the coverage of the negotiated grievance procedure may raise the matter under a statutory procedure or the negotiated procedure, but not both.... Selection of the negotiated procedure in no manner prejudices the right of an aggrieved employee to request the Merit Systems Protection Board to review the final decision pursuant to section 7702 of this title in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board....

Ms. Stahl alleged discrimination based on handicap and retaliation for EEO activity, thus invoking 5 USC § 2302(b)(1):

5 USC § 2302(b). Any employee who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action, shall not, with respect to such authority--

(1) discriminate for or against any employee or applicant for employment--

(D) on the basis of handicapping condition, as prohibited under section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (29 USC 791);

Ms. Stahl explains that although she has now been made aware that neither 5 USC § 7121 nor 5 CFR § 1201.154(d) applies to Postal Service employees, even preference eligibles, she was misled at the time of her separation. She states that the material she was given did not explain, and she was not told, that the MSPB regulations did not all apply to the Postal Service. Thus she argues that there was good cause for the delay, and that the agency's act in giving her the partly-inapplicable MSPB regulations was sufficiently confusing to warrant waiver of the untimely filing of the appeal.

Ms. Stahl also argues that her interpretation of the information she was given at the time of separation was logical, for the provisions for concurrent grievance and Board appeal are meaningless or unfair if interpreted as the Postal Service interprets them. She points out that a preference eligible Postal employee who wishes to participate in a grievance procedure will find that the time for filing an appeal to the Board--a course that is available, according to the Letter of Decision--will have expired long before resolution of the grievance, even if the employee does not proceed beyond Step 3 of the grievance/arbitration process. Further, as the Board stated in its Initial Decision, "The Postal Service collective bargaining agreement may, however, and does, provide that an employee may not proceed to binding arbitration if the employee has appealed the same matter to the Board."

However, the issue before us is not the balance established in the procedures of the collective bargaining agreement, but whether Ms. Stahl was adequately notified of the correct appeal rights. As we have observed, in the Letter of Decision the Postal Service told Ms. Stahl that she had twenty days within which to appeal to the Board. The statement was plain and unambiguous. This notice, in a letter personal to Ms. Stahl, could not be ignored. It was a clear statement of an imminent time limit, and any apparent discrepancy with the MSPB's regulations should have engendered inquiry at the time.

Despite the complexities of the various appeal rights and procedures, Ms. Stahl was clearly and correctly informed of her choices, by personal letter containing correct time limits. The Board's finding that Ms. Stahl had reasonable notice of her appeal rights is supported by substantial evidence. We sustain the Board's ruling that good cause was not shown for waiver of the time limit and acceptance of the appeal. See Phillips v. USPS, 695 F.2d 1389, 1390-91 (Fed. Cir. 1982) (waiver of time limit for filing appeal is committed to the sound discretion of the Board).

Ms. Stahl also argues that she should be permitted to appeal to the Board because she raised a handicap discrimination claim. For appeals of discrimination claims and "mixed cases" before the Board, the Federal Circuit considers only procedural or threshold issues of jurisdiction. Ballentine v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 738 F.2d 1244, 1246-47 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

The Board has jurisdiction of discrimination claims when an appealable adverse action is alleged to be based on discrimination. 5 USC § 7702(a); Williams v. Dept. of the Army, 715 F.2d 1485, 1487, 1491 (Fed. Cir. 1983). See Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1245-46 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (there must be an appealable adverse action in order to assert discrimination). In this case there was an adverse employment action. However, in order to raise discrimination as a defense to that action Ms. Stahl was required to file an appeal to the Board from the adverse action; such an appeal must meet the condition of timeliness or achieve waiver of untimeliness. See Hopkins v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 725 F.2d 1368 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

See Muse v. USPS, 82 MSPR 164, 167 ¶ 7 (1999) (Board lacks jurisdiction to review a USPS grievance decision); Allison v. USPS, 81 MSPR 335, 340 ¶ 13 (1999) (as to a Postal Service employee, the Board lacks authority to review arbitration award that denies a grievance); Marjie v. USPS, 70 MSPR 95, 98 (1996) ("A Postal Service employee with Board appeal rights may file a grievance and a Board appeal from the same action, but such an employee does not have a right of Board review of an arbitration decision.").

If a Postal employee who may be a preference eligible appeals an arbitrator's award directly to the Board, the Board will dismiss the request for review under 5 USC 7121(d) and remand the case to a regional office for a docketing as a direct appeal from the adverse action. The AJ assigned to the case must then determine whether the appellant is a preference eligible, whether the direct appeal was timely filed, whether relitigation is barred by collateral estoppel, or whether factual findings on the case are appropriate. Newton v. USPS, 98 MSPR 149, 151-52 ¶¶ 3-7 (2005).

It can occur that a Postal employee arbitrates a discharge, obtains reinstatement with a period of retained jurisdiction by the arbitrator, and is fired after the arbitrator loses jurisdiction. At that point the postal employee can take his case to the Board if the employee is within the Board's jurisdiction. See McGrath v. USPS, 22 MSPR 241, 242-43 (1984).
