13. Uncertainty as to Outcome of a Related Case; Pending Settlement Negotiations or Investigation; Case Taken to Court

Resolution of a grievance proceeding does not affect the time to start an appeal, a separate proceeding. See Davis v. MSPB, 221 Fed. Appx. 995 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP); cf. Gingery v. OPM, CH-300A-13-0240-I-1 (NP 2/14/2014) (employment practices appeal: "An appellant's decision to await the outcome of another action before the Board before filing this appeal does not provide good cause for an untimely appeal. See Carpenter v. Department of Health and Human Services, 40 M.S.P.R. 55, 56 (1989) (appellant's decision to forego the timely filing of his petition for review pending the outcome of other matters in another case did not constitute good cause for waiver of the filing deadline)."). Delay in filing an appeal is not excused by the argument that the appellant could await the result in the appeal of a similarly situated employee and file an appeal when and if the other employee was successful. Medina v. Dept. of Air Force, 11 MSPR 646 (1982) (Table); Martinez v. Dept. of Air Force, 11 MSPR 645 (1982) (Table); see McDougall v. United States, 149 F. Supp. 651, 652 (Ct. Cl. 1957) ("Had plaintiff wanted his case held in abeyance until after Mulligan's case had been decided, he should have first secured the consent thereto of the Civil Service Commission."). Jurisdiction was declined as to an appellant who voluntarily withdrew his appeal, but then, seven months later, refiled the appeal after learning that similarly situated employees who appealed fared better than did the appellant in his settlement. Schafer v. DOD, 6 MSPR 307, 309, 6 MSPB 262 (1981). By (broad) analogy, delay is not excused because the appellant is waiting for a statute to be enacted or modified. See Lundberg v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 382, 385-86 (1990) (appellant unsuccessfully argued that his late filing should be excused because he was awaiting enactment of the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989).

A delay in filing an appeal from a removal is not excused by an interest in awaiting the outcome of a disability retirement application. Phillips v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 244, 245 (1985). The same is true as to an OWCP determination, Turner v. Dept. of Army, 31 MSPR 127, 128 (1986)

Under Scott v. USPS, 80 MSPR 581, 583-84 ¶¶ 7-8 4 (1999), involving withdrawal of an appeal to pursue a related proceeding in district court:

...Generally, an appellant's withdrawal is an act of finality that has the effect of removing the appeal from the Board's jurisdiction. See Thomas v. U.S. Postal Service, 71, MSPR 474, 477 (1996); Scarboro v. Department of the Navy, 55 MSPR 494, 496 (102). In the absence of unusual circumstances such as misinformation or new and material evidence, the Board will not reopen or reinstate an appeal once it has been withdrawn merely because the appellant now wishes to proceed before the Board. Zuhlke, 74 MSPR at 404; Thomas, 71 MSPR at 477.

Here, the appellant's petition contains no new and material evidence and otherwise sets forth no basis to reinstate the original appeal. The appellant withdrew his appeal with the clear intention of pursuing his case in district court, and he made a voluntary, unequivocal request to withdraw. his appeal to the Board.... Furthermore, the appellant does not assert or establish that the AJ erred in dismissing his original appeal pursuant to his request, or that the agency or the Board contributed to his decision to proceed to district court. See, e.g., Lewis v. U.S. Postal Service, 47 MSPR 228, 232 (1991); Duncan v. U.S. Postal Service, 29 MSPR 72, 75 (1985), aff'd, 795 F.2d 1000 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

See Smith v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3133) (noting that if the appellant is concerned about losing her right to file suit in district court, she could timely file a protective appeal with the MSPB and request a stay of one of the proceedings); cf. Vaught v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 554, 560 (1993) (undue delay following pursuit of an action in court and following conclusion of OSC investigation). ). [Refer to Chapter 17, "Effect of District Court Review."]

Resort to OSC on a prohibited personnel practice allegation did not toll the time to file an appeal. Layser v. USDA, 8 MSPR 381, 383 (1981) (remanding, however, because of possibly misleading advice from OSC; this result would now be different as to whistleblowing allegations first presented to OSC under the Whistleblower Protection Act, [a matter discussed in Chapter 13, "Whistleblowing Reprisal"]. [Refer to Chapter 1 and Chapter 12, "Jurisdictional Problems with EEO Complaints," for the tolling effect of EEO complaints filed in mixed case appeals.]

An appellant's lengthy delay in pursuing a Board appeal is not excused while he informally attempts to resolve the matter with the agency. Staton v. DHS, 103 MSPR 318, 322 ¶ 12, 2006 MSPB 273 (2006). "[A] decision to deal directly with the agency rather than to file an appeal with the Board does not demonstrate good cause for the delay." Dancy-Butler v. Dept. of Treasury, 80 MSPR 421, 426 ¶ 6 (1998). An appellant fails to exercise due diligence if he allows a known probable appeal period to slip by while he attempts to reach a settlement with the agency. Schrum v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 103, 107 (1989); see Weiss v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3054) ("[T]he conduct of settlement negotiations does not, in general, toll the time for filing an appeal."); Stevenson v. Dept. of Commerce, 15 MSPR 221, 223 (1983); Wall v. DHHS, 10 MSPR 139, 141 (1982) (delay was not excused even though, during the lag time, the appellant tried to negotiate a solution regarding his constructive demotion with agency officials); cf. McCoy/Robinson v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-0752-15-0067-I-1 (NP 11/13/2014) ("Given such clear notice of their appeal rights, the appellants' mistaken belief that the time limit for filing a Board appeal did not begin to run until after the completion of the internal agency investigation does not constitute good cause for their untimely appeals. See, e.g., Waller v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 696, 699 (1998) (the appellant's mistaken belief that he could not file a petition with the Board until after arbitration does not establish good cause where the initial decision properly advised him of his right to file a petition with the Board).").

The Board regulation on governing the timing of appeals, 5 CFR 1201.22(b) (2022) states that "[w]here an appellant and an agency mutually agree in writing to attempt to resolve their dispute through an alternative dispute resolution process prior to the timely filing of an appeal...the time limit for filing the appeal is extended by an additional 30 days--for a total of 60 days." But the narrowly-defined regulatory exception to the general rule requiring a timely filing of an appeal remains the exception. Delay in filing an appeal was not excused when an agency notice correctly stated the appeal deadline, stated that the time for filing the appeal would be extended another 30 days if the parties mutually agreed to use alternative dispute resolution, the appellant waited about 30 days to request ADR, the agency declined to enter ADR by notice 20 days later, and another 35 days or so passed before the appeal was filed. Campbell v. MSPB, 208 Fed. Appx. 866 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP). The total time that elapsed in the Campbell appeal, 60 days, was not excused because the appellant thought the time for an appeal was extended on the basis of his request, as opposed to the necessary mutual agreement, for ADR.

A complex series of litigation events involving an appeal, a settlement, a subsequent performance-based removal case, and related proceedings convinced the Board to waive the appellate deadline based on the Board's conclusion that the appellant acted timely in attempting to use Board's appellate processes to pursue his removal appeal in accordance with the provisions of an AJ's order in a prior appeal. See Jones v. DHHS, CH-1221-10-1030-C-1 (NP 8/4/2014).
