9. Interpretation and Application of Settlement: Legal Principles

A settlement is applied according to the plain meaning of all of its terms, Lanclos v. United States, 40 F.4th 1352, 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2022):

We begin our review with an interpretation of the express terms and plain meaning of the settlement agreement. C. Sanchez & Son, Inc. v. United States, 6 F.3d 1539, 1543 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("A contract is read in accordance with its express terms and the plain meaning thereof."). We give the terms of an agreement their ordinary meaning unless "the parties mutually intended and agreed to an alternative meaning." Harris v. Dep't of Veterans Affs., 142 F.3d 1463, 1467 (Fed. Cir. 1998). Finally, we interpret terms in an agreement "in a manner that gives meaning to all of its provisions and makes sense." McAbee Constr., Inc. v. United States, 97 F.3d 1431, 1435 (Fed. Cir. 1996)....

When a term of an agreement is ambiguous, the AJ should resolve the ambiguity in a manner consistent with the purpose and effect of the agreement and with the intent of the parties. See Thomas v. DHUD, 124 F.3d 1439, 1442 (Fed. Cir. 1997); Vaughan v. USPS, 77 MSPR 541, 546 (1998) (same as Thomas). Wells v. USPS, 52 MSPR 497, 500 (1992), explained the contractual analysis:

In construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the Board looks first to the words of the agreement, which are of paramount importance in determining the intent of the parties at the time that they entered the agreement. See Romano v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 319, 323 (1991). Evidence that is not contained in the body of the document ("parol evidence") will be considered only where the terms of the agreement are ambiguous. See Romano, 49 MSPR at 323 (citing Greco v. Department the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988)). Ambiguity exists if the terms of the agreement are reasonably susceptible of more than one interpretation. See Burger King Corp. v. Horn & Hardart Co., 893 F.2d 525, 527 (2d Cir. 1990). When the parties' intended meaning is not apparent from the face of the agreement, it is appropriate to examine extrinsic evidence of intent. See id. at 528.

Lanier v. VA, NY-0752-14-0309-C-1 (NP 5/18/2022), explained that "The Board will not imply a term into an agreement that is unambiguous and will not hear a party's complaints about matters that were not included in a settlement agreement. Tom, 97 M.S.P.R. 395, ¶ 9."

The Board attempts to avoid illogical constructions of agreements. See Donahue v. USPS, 94 MSPR 488, 492 ¶ 12 (2003) ("To construe the waiver clause as the agency suggests would cause the absurd result of having one provision precluding refiling and another clearly allowing for it."); As'Salaam v. USPS, 78 MSPR 480, 482-83 ¶ 7 (1998) (when an agreement called for the agency to submit the results of a fitness exam to OWCP and OWCP did not receive the report, the agency was in noncompliance when the report was sent to but not received by OWCP; "the plain intent of the settlement agreement was to have the examination results considered by OWCP"); Corsiglia v. USPS, 69 MSPR 5, 9 (1995) (settlement must be construed to give effect to all of its terms, but "it must also be read in context so as to avoid absurd results which the parties could not have intended"); Abernethy v. OPM, 62 MSPR 170, 173 (1994) (rejecting interpretation offered by OPM of a settlement it negotiated because that construction "would create an absurd result and obviously [was] not what the appellant bargained for in the settlement discussions").

"In interpreting the meaning of one term of an agreement, that provision must be read in conjunction with the rest of the agreement." Saunders v. USPS, 75 MSPR 225, 232 (1997) (relying on the Restatement (Second) of Contracts, § 202(2) (1979)). A settlement agreement will be enforced according to its language without parol evidence of the parties' intent if that intent is apparent from the language of the agreement. Parrish-Fishman v. Dept. of Interior, 49 MSPR 179, 182-84 (1991) (Chairman Levinson dissented from the majority opinion; he asserted that an arguably unreasonable result in an agreement should open the door for an explanation of the parties' true intent through parol evidence); see Aylward v. USPS, 85 MSPR 40, 42 ¶ 5 (1999) (when the terms of a settlement are clear on their face, the terms control without a need for further elucidation from parol or other evidence); Mills v. VA, 68 MSPR 392, 393 (1995) (when a settlement called for an exact sum of $20,765.64, rather than a calculation formula, parol evidence as to how the sum was calculated, or should have been calculated, would not be considered).

The Board looks to the intent of the parties when they settled. A later change in law does not change the meaning of the settlement agreement. See Keenan v. USPS, 68 MSPR 407, 409-10 (1995) (agency regulations, rather than the Back Pay Act, controlled computation of the interest required by a settlement agreement that incorporated those regulations by reference even though, after the date of the settlement, the regulations were invalidated by the Board to the extent that they did not provide the rights established by the Back Pay Act to preference eligible veterans; the invalidation of postal regulations governing back pay calculations did not alter the settlement terms or entitle the appellant to more than the agreement provided). The Board avoids a construction or application of a settlement that is contrary to law. As to a reassignment negotiated in an agreement, the agency would not be compelled to circumvent statutes, e.g., Indian Preference laws, to accomplish the purpose of the settlement. See Gallegos v. Dept. of Interior, 63 MSPR 223, 225 (1994) (under the principles of contract law a party may be discharged from an obligation if performance of the obligation is impossible or illegal). If the parties are bound by a collective bargaining agreement, the settlement may be interpreted in accordance with arbitration decisions that are considered precedential as to the points in dispute. See Hicks v. USPS, 52 MSPR 561, 564 (1992).

There are as many phrases to describe the interpretation of contracts, including settlement agreements, as there are to describe the principles of statutory interpretation. The Federal Circuit provided its catalogue of contract interpretation law, Wexler v. Dept. of Interior (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 90-3217):

Principles of settlement contract interpretation: look to the parties' intent as reflected by the terms of their agreement; a settlement agreement is a contract; interpretation is a question of law; when construing a contract, the court looks first to the terms of the agreement itself; language of a settlement agreement must be construed literally, giving full force and effect to each provision; language of a particular section should be read in context of the entire agreement; ordinary and commonly accepted meaning of language without twisted or strained analysis should be followed; interpretation leading to absurd results should be avoided; the party arguing that a contract has been breached has the burden of proof.

Specific terms in agreements may cause difficulties because of linguistic ambiguities. Stewart v. USPS, 78 MSPR 537, 539 (1998), expressed how time is computed "from" a particular day:

...In computing time "from" a day, that day is generally excluded. See Nunley v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 MSPR 442, 445 (1992). Nevertheless, the word "from" a certain day does not have an absolute and invariable meaning, but should receive an inclusion or exclusion construction according to the intention with which the word is used. Black's Law Dictionary at 601 (5th ed. 1979); Nunley, 52 MSPR at 445 (whether the first day should be included or excluded is largely controlled by the intention of the parties as ascertained from the context and subject matter)....

Added here are a few comments from the Restatement of Contracts (Second):

Sec 203 Standards of Performance in Interpretation

In the interpretation of a promise or agreement or a term thereof, the following standards of preference are generally applicable:

(a) an interpretation which gives a reasonable, lawful, and effective meaning to all the terms is preferred to an interpretation which leaves a part unreasonable, unlawful, or of no effect....

comment:

c. Unreasonable and unlawful terms. In the absence of contrary indication, it is assumed that each term of an agreement has a reasonable rather than an unreasonable meaning, and that the agreement is intended to be lawful rather than unconscionable, fraudulent, or otherwise illegal. But parties are free to make agreements which seem unreasonable to others, and circumstances may show that even an agreement innocent on its face has an illegal purpose. If a term or a contract is unconscionable or otherwise against public policy, it should be dealt with directly rather than by spurious interpretation.
