1. Hourly Rate

The Board's pronouncements on hourly rates multiply and sometimes bewilder. The simplest statement of the law is from Swanson v. DLA, 35 MSPR 115, 119 (1987):

In order to establish the appropriate hourly rate, the fee application must contain a copy of the fee agreement, if any, as well as evidence of the attorney's customary billing rate for similar work. 5 CFR 1201.37(a)(2)(ii) and (iii). The customary billing rate may be established by evidence showing the rate the attorney actually billed other clients for similar work during the period for which he seeks compensation. Mitchell v. Department of Health and Human Services, 19 MSPR 206, 210 (1984). If the attorney has not billed sufficient work to establish a customary billing rate, the rate may also be established by affidavits from other attorneys in the community with similar experience stating the rates that they charge fee paying clients in similar cases. Id.

See Neal v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 207, 213-14 (1994) ("Counsel may establish the customary billing rate through evidence showing the rate the attorney actually billed other clients for similar work during the period for which compensation is sought, or, if the attorney has not billed sufficient work to establish a customary billing rate, through affidavits from other attorneys in the community with similar experience stating the rates that they charge fee-paying clients in similar cases.").

So much for simplicity. It would be nice if one could quote Swanson, attempt to ascertain a customary rate, and move on. In many appeals, counsel submits a fee petition, properly documented with an affidavit describing her fee structure, including timesheets and a retainer agreement, and if there is fee entitlement, the amount is either agreed to in a settlement or ascertained by the AJ who reviews the fee petition. But the case law that follows suggests that some fee claims are litigated with as much or more zeal than the underlying merits dispute. The Board has not eschewed the many other decisions on reasonableness of fees that it has generated. Many of those cases essentially concern how to judge the reasonableness of an hourly rate claimed by counsel who either have not established a track record of fees through other fee awards or who do not have a uniform office billing rate. Parenthetically, it is noted that although a uniform billing rate is helpful to establish the reasonableness of the rate, it is not a conclusive measure of reasonableness. It should also be understood that an agency may submit evidence (rather than the customary argument) concerning the reasonableness of a rate. The Board noted the failure of an agency to submit evidence of what it believed to be a reasonable rate. See Ybarra v. DLA, 25 MSPR 419, 423 (1984). But the agency's failure to object to a particular rate does not excuse the AJ's duty independently to assess its reasonableness. See Schultz v. USPS, PH-0752-94-0233-M-1 (NP 12/16/2016) ("Even absent a specific challenge from the agency, the Board must ensure that only reasonable fees are awarded. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(1); Ruble v. Office of Personnel Management, 96 M.S.P.R. 44, ¶ 8 (2004).").

At first it did not seem that the hourly rate issue would pose a problem. The Board declared that if it is agreed that a specific fee be paid to counsel for legal services rendered on behalf of an appellant, "we will presume that the amount agreed upon represents the maximum reasonable fee which may be awarded." O'Donnell, 2 MSPR at 455 & n.11, 2 MSPB at 611, supra. The AJ awarded a fee based on an hourly rate between the amount charged to a union client, claimed to be reduced as a special concession by counsel to the union, and the normal hourly rate. The discounted rate was a normal commercial billing practice for attorneys with labor union clients that are normally expected, if not explicitly understood, to produce a high volume of legal business. Without clear evidence to the contrary, ordinary experience warrants the presumption that private law firms do not operate as charitable institutions, and a bill rendered by such a firm accurately reflects the partnership's fair judgment of the maximum fee that is commercially reasonable under the circumstances. Then hourly rates were addressed in Kling v. DOJ, 2 MSPR 464, 470 n.5, 2 MSPB 620 (1980), resulting in the injection of the Board into the business of ratemaking for attorneys. Initially the Board stated:

Experience thus far in considering motions for fees indicates that agencies regularly oppose any award of fees but fail to address the reasonableness of the particular amount claimed in responding to the motion before the presiding official, as in this case. Since the Board normally does not review issues which have not been raised before its presiding official, especially when such issues turn upon disputed factual matters which have not been presented to that official for resolution, the agency thereby risks waiver of its opportunity to seek Board review of the reasonableness of a fee award. Agencies and their representatives should therefore be cognizant of the need to address all pertinent issues, with rebuttal evidence as well as argument, in responding to motions under 5 CFR 1201.37(a).

Having said this, the Board went on to state that although the agency's failure to contest the amount claimed may relieve an AJ of the need to resolve disputed factual issues, it cannot relieve the Board of the statutory duty to assure that reasonable counsel fees are awarded. See Grant v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-11-0884-A-1 n.11 (NP 2/10/2014) ("The agency did not explicitly raise these arguments. However, even if an agency does not contest the reasonableness of certain fees, the Board is still obligated to comply with its statutory duty to ensure that only reasonable attorney fees are awarded. Kling v. Department of Justice, 2 M.S.P.R. 464, 470 (1980); see 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(1).").

Kling requires that an addendum decision establish a factual predicate for the Board to determine if there has been proper analysis of factors pertinent to a reasonable fee award. The AJ should identify factors relevant to counsel fees and evaluate their impact on the hours and rate claimed and upon any other factors warranting adjustment of the product of the rate and compensable hours.

Kling gave passing recognition to civil rights cases that analyzed a series of factors, as in Johnson v. Georgia Highway Express, Inc., 488 F.2d 714 (5th Cir. 1974), that included:

1. Time and labor required. Hours claimed must be weighed against the judge's own knowledge and experience of the time required for similar activities. The possibility of duplication of efforts should be scrutinized. Legal work should be distinguished from non-legal work, which commands a lesser rate.

2. Novelty and difficulty of issues. A case of first impression requires more research, which deserves compensation, but time merely devoted to learning a new field of law is an investment for future cases.

3. Skill required to perform the legal service properly. Observation of the attorney's work product, preparation, and ability are important to this factor.

4. The preclusion of other employment due to acceptance of the case, through foreclosure of other business due to conflicts of interest or through inability to use time spent on the client's behalf for other purposes.

5. The customary fee for similar work in the community.

6. Whether there is a fixed or contingent fee.

7. Time limitations imposed by the client or circumstances. Some premium should be recognized for priority work which delays the attorney's other work.

8. Amount involved and result. The amount of damages, for example, may be considered, but is not controlling. The effect of the case on the development of the law, or in benefiting similarly situated persons, should be considered.

9. The experience, reputation, and ability of the attorney. Expertise in a specialized field, and demonstrated skill and ability are pertinent.

10. The undesirability of the case. Where an unpopular case has been undertaken which may have an economic impact on the attorney's practice, this factor can be considered.

11. The nature and length of the professional relationship with the client. A lawyer or firm may vary the fee in light of the professional relationship with the client.

12. Awards in similar cases.

Concluding that Board cases are simpler and shorter than most cases in the federal courts resulting in fee awards, the Board opted for a shorter analysis of fees. In earlier cases, the Board suggested that most of the Johnson factors are accounted for by two objective variables: "The lawyer's customary hourly billing rate and the number of hours devoted to the case." Kling, 2 MSPR at 472. However, allowing for the possibility of reference to Johnson factors, Kling suggested an analysis not as complex as Johnson, yet more complex than multiplication of the rate set by the lawyer according to the hours invested, 2 MSPR 464, 472-73:

We caution, however, that the presiding official must scrutinize with due care the hours and the billing rates claimed by counsel in supporting their motions for attorney fees. The presiding official may cut hours for duplication, padding, or frivolous claims, and impose fair standards of efficiency and economy of time. Such evaluation obviously requires adequate information as to the way in which counsel's time was spent (discovery, legal research, interviewing witnesses, appearance at a hearing, preparation of motions or briefs, etc.). Billing rates should be evaluated according to the individual attorney's professional standing, experience, status (general partner, junior partner, associate), and specialized expertise. The presiding official must include in the addendum decision findings adequate to substantiate the determination of the appropriate billing rates and compensable hours.

Any adjustment to the amount derived from multiplying the hourly rate by the number of compensable hours should also be identified in the addendum decision and the basis carefully explained. In this regard, we note that the purpose of 7701(g)(1) is to assure that legal expense is not a barrier to the representation of employees and applicants for employment who have just cause for appeal. Accordingly, a public policy "bonus multiplier" for counsel is not justified in a Board award of attorney fees in an employee appeals case. It may nevertheless be appropriate, when counsel's compensation is contingent on success, to adjust the award upward to compensate for the risk the attorney is accepting of not being paid at all.

Essentially the Board jumped from a simple rate-multiplied-by-hours equation to a complex analysis involving recognition of skill, standing, and experience--the very factors the Board had said were typical of commercial or civil rights cases but too complex for Board work.

For a historical overview of billing practice and evolution of the "lodestar" analysis, refer to Gisbrecht v. Barnhart, 122 S. Ct. 1817, 1824-25 (2002).
