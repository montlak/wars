c. PIP Extension

In Papritz v. DOJ, 31 MSPR 495, 499 (1986), the employee was given a notice of unsatisfactory performance, assigned two projects to perform during a 60-day improvement period. On the day before the end of the period, he was given another project, after satisfactorily completing the others. He was then provided another few weeks to complete the third project, with an extension of the opportunity period. He was discharged after it was determined that his performance on the third project was deficient. The employee argued that his improvement period was completed and should not have been extended. The Board disagreed:

[T]his case differs from Frish in two important respects. First, the performance of the appellant in Frish was rated minimally satisfactory before his performance improvement period was extended, while the agency in the case now before the Board did not provide any such rating. Second, we find that the agency's extension of the appellant's opportunity period was warranted.... [D]ue to unexpected technical problems and budgetary constraints, the appellant was unable to perform the work he had been assigned. The agency therefore was unable to evaluate the appellant's performance during the initial performance improvement period....

The appellant further argues that the extended opportunity period was too short to provide him a reasonable opportunity to improve. We disagree. Even if, as the appellant alleges, he was not allowed all the additional time by which the agency extended the period (i.e., 30 days), there is no evidence in the record that the length of time the appellant was allowed unfairly prevented him from demonstrating acceptable performance. To the contrary, the record shows that the appellant submitted his initial draft at least one day early and that, despite being informed that his performance would be evaluated on the basis of this particular project, he failed to request additional time in which to correct the deficiencies before submitting his final draft.

Fitzsimmons v. Dept. of Treasury, NY-0432-10-0212-I-1 (NP 6/16/2011) (aff'd, Fitzsimmons v. Dept. of Treasury, 476 Fed. Appx. 246 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP)), discerned no impediment to the extension of a PIP for legitimate reasons:

The appellant's claim that the agency improperly extended his performance improvement plan (PIP) is actually part of his harmful procedural error claims. The administrative judge, however, considered and rejected this claim, implicitly crediting testimony that the agency extended the PIP in light of the appellant's requests for reasonable accommodation for his alleged disability and for advanced sick leave. Further, the appellant's reliance on Frish v. Veterans Administration, 24 M.S.P.R. 610, 614 (1984) is misplaced. There, the agency extended the appellant's PIP after he successfully completed it, and the appellant's extensive absences occurred before the PIP. Here, however, the appellant had not successfully completed the initial PIP and the agency appropriately extended the PIP because of the appellant's absences and need for accommodation during the PIP. See Green v. Department of Labor, 26 M.S.P.R. 96, 98 (1985).

See Davis v. SSA, SF-0432-12-0763-M-1 (NP 5/3/2016) ("[S]he asserts that, by failing to extend the OPS by 4 days to account for her absences, the agency failed to comply with its personnel policies, which require an OPS plan to last for a period of 120 days. ...[t]he record reflects that the agency provided the appellant an adequate opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance over the course of approximately 51/2 months."); Koo v. GPO, DC-0432-11-0069-I-1 (NP 9/30/2011) ("As for the duration of the PIP, the appellant has not shown how the alleged extension of the PIP period prejudiced her ability to improve her performance to a minimally successful level."). But see Frish v. VA, 24 MSPR 610, 619 (1984) (the agency did not properly justify extending a PIP for three months after the employee met the PIP's minimum standards).
