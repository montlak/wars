h. Settlement After Initial Decision

Under 5 CFR 1201.112(a) (2022), the AJ maintains jurisdiction to:

(4) Vacate an initial decision to accept into the record a settlement agreement that is filed prior to the deadline for filing a petition for review, even if the settlement agreement is not received until after the date when the initial decision becomes final under § 1201.113 of this part.

If the initial decision has become final, the Board will receive agreements that are reached after a PFR is filed from an initial decision. See Holderbach v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 548, 551 (1989) (recognizing that if a settlement was entered at the PFR stage, there was no opportunity for an AJ to determine that the parties understood their actions; the appeal was remanded for findings concerning a challenge to the settlement). Under Holderbach, concerning the threshold level of review of a challenge to a settlement offered to the Board after a PFR is filed:

[A] challenge to a settlement reached after a petition for review has been filed on the merits requires less of a threshold for inquiry on the validity of the agreement than a challenge to a settlement reached before an initial decision has been issued on the merits.

Before a case may be dismissed as settled prior to an initial decision on the merits, an administrative judge must first document for the record whether the parties actually reached agreement, understood the terms of the agreement, and then agreed whether the agreement was to be enforceable by the Board.... Where a settlement is reached after a petition for review has been filed, however, there is no opportunity for an administrative judge to make these findings that aid in assuring that the parties understood their actions in settling the case.

See Nguyen v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 93-3377) (if the parties reach a settlement they intend to include in the record, but the initial decision is issued first and the appellant does not file a PFR, the Board should make a further inquiry concerning the effect of the settlement if there are enforcement proceedings, with the option of reopening the case to receive the settlement as new and material evidence); Conklin v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 38 (1993) (Table) (accepting the agreement: "The settlement agreement specifies, inter alia, that the parties agree that the agreement will be included in the Board's record for enforcement purposes[.]"); Garrett v. GSA, 59 MSPR 487 (1993) (Table) (accepting a settlement at the PFR stage: "the parties fully understand the agreement, the parties have made the agreement of their own free will, and they request that the agreement be entered into the record[.]"). But cf. Heining v. GSA, 66 MSPR 571, 573 (1995) (if an OPM reconsideration motion has been filed and is pending following a Board final decision, the parties' settlement agreement will not moot the reconsideration request and the Board will not vacate its decision to remove the basis for OPM's reconsideration request).

If an AJ mistakenly adjudicates an appeal after a settlement was reached and the appellant has withdrawn the appeal, to prevent an injustice, e.g., the agency's attempt to withdraw the settlement, the Board will vacate the AJ's decision and adopt the settlement as the disposition of the appeal. Frazier v. USPS, 18 MSPR 310, 311 (1983), enf'd, Frazier v. USPS, 26 MSPR 584, 586 (1985) (Board decided that it had jurisdiction to enforce back pay entitlements arising through the underlying settlement). If the appeal is settled after it has reached the PFR stage, and if a condition of the settlement is the withdrawal of the PFR with prejudice, the parties may have the Board make the settlement a part of the record when the PFR is withdrawn; the effect of the withdrawal of the PFR is that the initial decision becomes the final decision. The Board then has jurisdiction, despite the earlier initial decision, to enforce the subsequent settlement. Sternfeld v. DOJ, 28 MSPR 417, 418 n.1 (1985) (better practice would be for the Board to reopen the case, enter its order accepting the settlement, and dismiss the appeal as settled); see Mills v. VA, 66 MSPR 170, 172-73 (1995) (dismissing an appeal with prejudice based on a settlement submitted after the agency filed a PFR, and remanding allegations of noncompliance to the regional office for adjudication as a petition for enforcement).

Without a filed or unfiled settlement, formal action is required to terminate Board proceedings: the Board may adjudicate a PFR on the basis of the existing record if counsel advises the Board that there has been a settlement but no action is taken to enter the settlement into the record, or if no request is made for dismissal of the appeal after counsel has promised that the settlement would be forthcoming. Sternfeld v. DOJ, 27 MSPR 575, 576 (1985).

The Board has a program to settle cases at the PFR stage with the assistance of headquarters attorneys in the Office of Appeals Counsel. Parties are informed of the program when they file a PFR. Headquarters personnel may select cases for settlement attempts, or the parties may tell the Board that they seek settlement assistance following the filing of a PFR. The PFR settlement program uses a settlement conference to explain the process, to explore substantive bases for settlement, and to advise the parties of enforcement rights should a settlement be negotiated and accepted by the Board. See, e.g., Haley-Wilson v. GSA, 64 MSPR 220 (1994) (Table). Of the cases selected for settlement discussions through the Office of Appeals Counsel, approximately 20% are successfully resolved. [Board guidance is in Chapter 5, "PFR Settlement Program."].
