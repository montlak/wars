E. AGENCY RESPONSE TO APPEAL

After the Board receives an appeal, it sends the appellant an acknowledgment order stating the appeal was received and containing instructions concerning its processing. That order is also sent to an agency representative. In that acknowledgment order, there is a directive to the agency that the agency file and serve a response to the appeal within 20 days from the date of the acknowledgment order. 5 CFR 1201.22(b) (2022). The agency has the right and obligation to respond to an appeal; the AJ should not dismiss an appeal without processing it in accordance with Board regulations requiring notice of the appeal to the agency. See Muttie v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 376, 378 (1982). The acknowledgment order provides instructions as to the documents to be furnished by the agency in response to the appeal.

5 USC 7513(e) defines the basic record of an action to be supplied by an agency to the Board:

Copies of the notice of proposed action, the answer of the employee when written, a summary thereof when made orally, the notice of decision and reasons therefor, and any order effecting an action covered by this subchapter, together with any supporting material, shall be maintained by the agency and shall be furnished to the Board upon its request and to the employee affected upon the employee's request.

The Federal Circuit determined in Porzillo v. DHHS, 369 Fed. Appx. 123, 128 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), that § 7513(e) was of somewhat limited scope:

The use of the phrase "copies of" indicates that an employee's right to review the designated materials does not include the right to examine the original documents or the databases in which they are stored. We are unaware of any authority suggesting that, in addition to furnishing paper copies of documents, an agency must give a former employee access to the agency's computer system to view documents in their original environment and format. Such a policy would be difficult and time-consuming to implement, and would raise security concerns. Ms. Porzillo does not deny that she received (or already possessed) accessible, readable copies of all the supporting materials relied upon by the agency. The agency thus met its burden under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(e), and the administrative judge was justified in relying on those materials.

The regulatory requirements for the appeal response are set out at 5 CFR 1201.25 (2022). These materials should be grouped and provided in a file appropriately tabbed and indexed. For the convenience of everyone involved in the appeal process, and especially for the benefit of the agency representative, it is suggested that the individual documents be paginated if a multitude of papers are filed under any particular tab; this avoids the waste of hearing time as people attempt to find the documents referenced during questions and argument. A numbering machine is an excellent investment. Some copying machines offer a sequential number stamp feature; some versions of Adobe do the same. Electronic filing of the response to the appeal provides automatic page numbering, although it takes some patience to split large files up into groups of documents that do not exceed the digital size limit stated in the e-filing instructions.

5 CFR 1201.25 (2022) outlines the minimum requirements for the agency response:

The agency response to an appeal must contain the following:

(a) The name of the appellant and of the agency whose action the appellant is appealing;

(b) A statement identifying the agency action taken against the appellant and stating the reasons for taking the action;

(c) All documents contained in the agency record of the action;

(d) Designation of and signature by the authorized agency representative; and

(e) Any other documents or responses requested by the Board.

Common to most case files will be the following documentation:

1. Notice of Proposed Action;

2. Designation of Representative;

3. Written Response;

4. Summary of Oral Response;

5. Notice of Decision.

Documentation appropriate to an adverse action case include:

1. Affidavits and witness statements;

2. Investigation reports;

3. Records (memos of counseling, leave requests, timecards or production records, etc.);

4. Regulations and policy statements (agency table of penalties, collective bargaining contract, etc.);

5. Medical evidence as appropriate;

6. Evidence regarding aggravating or mitigating circumstances (e.g., evidence of past discipline).

For an unacceptable performance case, the agency response should include:

1. Performance Standards and critical elements;

2. Position description;

3. Evaluations and appraisals;

4. Work products;

5. Records of training, counseling, warnings;

6. Documentation of an opportunity to improve performance;

7. Performance appraisal regulations and plan;

8. Evidence of OPM approval of performance appraisal plan;

9. Collective bargaining contract, if appropriate.

For a reduction in force case, appropriate documentation comprises:

1. Retention registers, RIF notices;

2. Evidence of veterans preference status, appraisals, as appropriate;

3. Position descriptions and qualification standards;

4. Justifications for findings of undue interruption of agency programs through exercise of assignment rights;

5. Documentation of regulatory basis for RIF.

See Nichols v. MSPB, 625 Fed. Appx. 987, 993 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) ("The Board's statement that Mr. Nichols 'presumably had a copy of the agency's [Report of Investigation] and could have submitted portions of it in response to the administrative judge's jurisdictional order,' ignored 'procedures required by...regulation.' Forest, 47 F.3d at 410 (citing 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c)). In particular, the Agency was required to submit to the Board '[a]ll documents contained in the agency record of the action' under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.25(c) ('The agency response to an appeal must contain...[a]ll documents contained in the agency record of the action.').").

Board regulations do not require the response to the appeal to be under oath. The response is to include a designation of representative and signature by the authorized representative. Rosen v. ICC, 20 MSPR 574, 577 n.4 (1984). Documents submitted by an agency in response to an appeal become part of the official record and need not be formally introduced into evidence during the hearing. Boltin v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2472). This is a significant right. If documents can be submitted in the response, they need not be introduced or reintroduced at the hearing. If they need not be offered at the hearing, there is no risk of their later exclusion if the appellant objects. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Motion Practice."]

Agencies occasionally include in the attachments to their appeals and responses to appeals materials that were not earlier included in agency records, including affidavits or declarations and other documents created for the purpose of the appeal or response. If those materials are objectionable to the appellant, he will need to protect the record by a motion in limine or motion to strike information from the record, although the Board does not appear inclined to force agencies to trim their files in response to appellants' assertions of verbosity. See Price v. VA, 13 MSPR 107, 110 (1982) (in response to the appellant's protest about an agency response of about 600 pages, the Board found no error by the agency "in including all relevant documents in the removal file"). Should the agency fail to include in its response the documents constituting the record of the action, the Board expects the AJ, upon learning of a problem with the record, to ascertain whether the agency abused the administrative appeals process and to determine how the problem should be corrected. See Garduno v. OPM, 43 MSPR 3, 6 (1989); cf. Masson v. OPM, 36 MSPR 244, 247 (1987) ("[A]n agency may more fully document the record during the appeal stage of the proceedings, and respond to allegations raised in the appeal. 5 CFR 1201.25."). In one of the few decisions finding an agency appeal response insufficient, King-Roberts v. OPM, 80 MSPR 431, 433 ¶ 3 (1998), OPM was required, on remand, to furnish to both the appellant and the Board hearing tapes from a removal appeal relied on by OPM to support a subsequent disability retirement determination.

The agency's response to the appeal must be served on the appellant, but delayed service will not result in sanctions without a showing of harm by the appellant, particularly if the appellant should have been on notice that the agency filed the response and failed to inquire of its whereabouts. Fonville v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 351, 353 (1986) ("Additionally, appellant could have asserted that she was surprised by the evidence when she received the file at the hearing and sought a continuance or the right to respond in writing after the hearing."). The failure of the agency to provide a transcript of an oral reply in the appeal response was not harmful error, Tuffy v. DHS, NY-0752-11-0212-I-1 (NP 5/10/2013):

The appellant argues that the agency committed harmful procedural error by not providing him a synopsis of his oral reply. The administrative judge noted that there was no synopsis of the oral reply in the agency file. However, there is no statutory obligation on the part of an agency to transcribe an appellant's oral reply. 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(2). Nor is there any other such requirement. See Melnick v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 42 M.S.P.R. 93, 101 (1989), aff'd, 899 F.2d 1228 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table). Moreover, even if the agency erred in this regard, the appellant bears the burden of proving that any such error was harmful, that is, that it likely caused the agency to reach a different result than it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 M.S.P.R. 211, 242 (1995), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). The appellant failed to set forth what additional information or evidence he provided at the oral reply (which he admits he had and which the deciding official indicates he considered), that would have caused the agency to reach a different result in this case. Thus, the appellant has failed to establish his claim of harmful procedural error.
