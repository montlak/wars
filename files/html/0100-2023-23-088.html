12. Retirement, TSP Contribution or Loan

Niday v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 673, 677 (1990), suggested that computation of back pay is governed by regulations and is not a matter for negotiation between the employee and the agency. The Board will compel proper evidence of agency contributions and calculations of retirement contributions following reinstatement. Tydingco v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 563, 565 (1985); see Gallagher v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 379, 385 (1993) (dispute over a settlement canceling removal: agency was required to ensure appellant's leave and earnings statement reflected the appellant's accrued retirement contributions); Hooks v. USPS, 56 MSPR 655, 659 (1993) (requiring agency to ensure employee receives all retirement allocations he would have accrued had he not been terminated); Greene v. DHHS, 54 MSPR 163, 169 (1992) ("Although the Back Pay Act does not expressly require withholding or deducting income taxes from back pay awards, it is implicit from the words of the statute and its implementing regulations that taxes and retirement contributions that would have been withheld had the improper action not occurred must be deducted from a back pay award." (citing Cortez v. VA, 27 MSPR 648, 651 (1985) (under the Back Pay Act an employee is regarded as never having a break in service, and retirement contributions must be paid unless waived by OPM))). Under Hargett v. Dept. of Navy, 82 MSPR 180, 182 ¶ 8 & n.1 (1999), retirement contributions are calculated on the basis of gross basic back pay, defined as the amount the appellant would have been paid had no personnel actions been taken. Gross basic back pay does not include any amounts paid for lump sum annual leave, and it does not take into account any interim earnings or amounts paid to the appellant in the form of separation incentives or gross annuities.

The procedure for settlement of retirement contribution problems was outlined, Dailey v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 493, 495 (1987) (an enforcement petition):

[C]alculation of the amount of back pay to which appellant is entitled is governed by 5 CFR 550.805(e)(2) and Federal Personnel Manual Supplement 990-2, Subchapter S8-7(d)(2) and (4). Briefly, these provisions provide that a refund of retirement contributions paid to an employee based on a separation that is subsequently found erroneous and canceled by restoring the employee to duty retroactively so that there was no break in service removes the legal basis for the refund. See 5 USC § 8342. Consequently, a refund paid in error represents a debt due the retirement fund that must be deducted from any back pay entitlement. [So must any lump-sum leave payment received by the employee be set off against the employee's back pay award.] Where the employee is entitled to back pay, and where, as here, the employee has obtained a refund of retirement contributions, the agency must contact OPM to determine the amount of the refund (debt) to be offset against the back pay entitlement and submit to OPM a check for the appropriate amount.

The Board will deny restoration of retirement benefits when, because of intervening circumstances, no obligation exists to make those contributions. See Roja v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 618, 621 (1992) (appellant was on OWCP status; because an OWCP recipient is on LWOP, he receives no basic pay and FERS contributions are based on a percentage of basic pay). Should assistance be required from OPM to compute annuity benefits, employing agencies will not be penalized as a result of OPM's delays. Carpenter v. DHHS, 36 MSPR 496, 504 (1988). If both the employing agency and OPM must be joined to adjudicate retirement rights, the AJ may join them. See McLaughlin v. OPM, 62 MSPR 536, 547 (1994) (Postal Service was properly joined because one issue was its special option early retirement with a pay bonus). Driscoll v. USPS, 113 MSPR 565, 570 ¶ 16, 2010 MSPB 82 (2010), suggested that if an appellant, following an adverse action, retired, the employee should address to OPM a concern that the amount of the annuity has not been correctly calculated, although the Board will entertain an enforcement petition to determine if the agency failed to notify OPM of changes in the appellant's salary as a result of a Board order directing back pay.

5 CFR Part 1605 (2022) provides regulations of the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board governing correction of administrative errors (including adjustments of personnel actions through MSPB litigation or arbitration awards) affecting contributions of Thrift Savings Plan participants. See 5 CFR 1605.13 (2022) ("Back pay awards and other retroactive pay adjustments").

Assuming an underlying Board order affecting employment, the Board may order adjustments to be made to TSP accounts, Giove v. OPM, 106 MSPR 53, 56 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 146 (2007) (determining, however, that compliance with the Board order did not require any agency to permit redeposit on funds the appellant withdrew from his TSP account on the facts and assertions involved in the disability annuity case, and without determining when an opportunity to make a redeposit would be appropriate, for example, in an adverse action appeal):

The Board has the authority, under 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2), to order any federal agency to comply with any order or decision issued under its adjudicatory authority, and to enforce compliance with any such order. Under that provision, the Board may review matters related to TSP accounts, including claims that an agency failed to take corrective actions it should have taken with respect to such an account. See, e.g., Shobert v. Department of the Air Force, 90 M.S.P.R. 262, ¶¶ 9-11 (2001) (the Board's order to cancel the employee's suspension required the agency to comply with applicable TSP regulations, including those providing for transfer of funds to the employee's TSP account and for payment of lost earnings on that account). We therefore agree with the appellant that the Board has the authority to review his TSP-related claim, and to determine whether OPM or any other federal agency has failed to take action necessary as a result of the August 2004 order mentioned above.

An agency must fully account for contributions due to the Thrift Savings Plan. Shobert v. Dept. of Air Force, 90 MSPR 262, 266-67 ¶¶ 9-11 (2001) (partially overruled by Bills v. Dept. of Air Force, 122 MSPR 367, 2015 MSPB 25 (2015)), outlined the scheme for recovery of TSP contributions for employees restored to agencies through Board orders:

The TSP was established by the Federal Employees' Retirement System Act of 1986 (FERSA), Pub.L. No. 99-335, 100 Stat. 514 (codified as amended largely at 5 U.S.C. §§ 8351 and 8401-8479), and has been implemented through regulations at 5 CFR, Part 1600. The agency was required to comply with the FERSA and with the applicable TSP regulations in issuing the appellant's back pay. See 5 CFR § 550.805(h) (agencies must correct errors that affect an employee's TSP account consistent with regulations prescribed by the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board).

The agency was required to implement the appellant's TSP elections by deducting his employee contributions from the back pay award and transferring those funds to the TSP. 5 CFR § 1605.4(c)(1). According to a summary prepared by DFAS, the appellant elected to contribute 5% of his basic pay to the TSP. The DFAS report states that the appellant earned $860.96 in wages and that $43.05 in employee contributions was deducted and transferred to the TSP. The report also indicates that the agency transferred to the appellant's TSP account $8.61, representing the agency automatic (1%) contributions, and $34.44, representing the agency matching contributions. Accordingly, we find that the agency has submitted sufficient evidence with respect to the employee and agency contributions made to the appellant's TSP account.

In addition to these contributions, the agency was required to pay an amount, computed by the TSP recordkeeper, that is necessary to restore the TSP earnings lost as a result of the agency action. See 5 CFR § 1606.3. Lost earnings are calculated by the TSP recordkeeper based on "lost earnings record"[2] submitted by the employing agency. Id. at 1606.13. See also TSP Bulletin No. 01-35, Processing Lost Earnings Records (Aug. 15, 2001). Pursuant to 5 CFR § 1606.11(b), the agency was required to provide lost earnings records to the TSP recordkeeper "for each pay period affected by the error." The appellant asserts that the agency must produce these lost earnings records as evidence of compliance, citing Camastro v. Department of Justice, 86 MSPR 267, 273-74 (2000). We agree. While the TSP recordkeeper determines the proper amount of lost earnings to be deposited into the appellant's TSP account, the agency must show that it provided the information and certification that is required to enable the TSP recordkeeper to correctly compute the lost earnings.

[2] A "lost earnings record" is defined as "the data record containing information enabling the TSP system to compute lost earnings and to determine the investment fund in which money would be invested had an error not occurred." 5 CFR § 1606.2.

Following remand, Shobert returned to the Board, Shobert v. Dept. of Air Force, 91 MSPR 572, 575 ¶ 9 (2002). The agency was in compliance with its TSP obligations:

...The TSP regulations define a "lost earning record" as "the data record containing information enabling the TSP system to compute lost earnings and to determine the investment fund in which money would be invested had an error not occurred." 5 C.F.R. § 1606.2 (2001). Lost earnings records have a "beginning date," which is the pay date on which the contribution should have been made; an "ending date," which is the pay date on which the contribution was made; the amount of the contribution by source (employee, agency automatic 1%, or agency matching); and, if the "beginning date" is before May 1, 2001, the amount of the contribution by investment fund (G, F, or C). See TSP Bulletin No. 01-35, Processing Lost Earnings Records (Aug. 15, 2001). The agency has provided a spreadsheet that appears to meet the definition of a "lost earnings record." The spreadsheet shows the dates each contribution should have been made, the dates the contributions actually were made, and an accounting showing the source of the contribution (employee, automatic 1%, and matching), and the allocation among the investment funds. Accordingly, we find the agency in compliance with respect to the appellant's TSP account.

The FRTIB TSP restoration regulation was discussed in Latham v. USPS, DA-0353-10-0408-X-1 (NP 9/11/2013):

The agency was required to comply with applicable TSP regulations in issuing the appellant's back pay. See Shobert v. Department of the Air Force, 90 M.S.P.R. 262, ¶ 9 (2001). As relevant here, TSP regulations provide that:

The employing agency must give a reinstated or retroactively appointed [TSP] participant the following options for electing makeup contributions:

(i) The reinstated or retroactively appointed participant may submit a new contribution election for purposes of makeup contributions if he or she would have been eligible to make such an election but for the erroneous separation or erroneous failure to appoint; or

(ii) If a reinstated participant had a contribution election form on file when he or she separated, the contribution election the participant had on file when he or she separated may be reinstated for purposes of makeup contributions.

5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(a)(2) (2013).

Here, the agency provided the appellant with the opportunity to make a new election for purposes of makeup contributions during the back pay period. The appellant completed the "Employee Form To Recover Back Pay," PS Form 8038, which specifically asked whether he wanted to participate in the TSP during the back pay period. The appellant checked the "no" box. Underneath that box, he wrote: "Not now. Was already placed in taxable distribution. Want money for back pay period." Thus, the appellant clearly indicated that he did not wish to have deductions taken from his back pay for TSP contributions.

Further, the agency provided the appellant with a TSP election form, TSP-1. The appellant contends that this form is "clear evidence" of his intent to participate in the TSP during the back pay period. He points to a comment he wrote at the top of the form: "To be made whole [illegible] during back pay period." This comment does not constitute a TSP election. The form clearly instructed the appellant that "[t]o start or change the amount of your contributions to your TSP account, enter either a whole percentage of your basic pay per pay period (item 6) or a whole dollar amount per pay period (item 7)." The appellant left items 6 and 7 blank. Thus, the record reflects that the agency provided the appellant with the option of submitting a new election form for the back pay period and that the appellant elected not to participate. We therefore agree with the administrative judge that the agency has demonstrated compliance with regard to the appellant's TSP participation during the back pay period.[3]

[3] The appellant states that the agency "touts the ability to change one's TSP contributions at any time." That may well be true with respect to prospective TSP contributions, but makeup contributions must be computed before the back pay award is paid, deducted from the back pay, and submitted to the TSP record keeper. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(c)(1).

Thomas-Jordan v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-16-0343-X-1 (NP 3/20/2023), added as to agency obligations concerning TSP accounts:

Regarding the appellant's TSP account, the regulations implementing the Back Pay Act require that an agency correct errors affecting an employee's TSP account consistent with the regulations prescribed by the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board. Rittgers v. Department of the Army, 123 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 7 (2015); 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(h). Pursuant to these regulations, the employing agency must give a reinstated employee who would have been eligible to contribute to her TSP account but for the erroneous separation the opportunity to submit a new contribution election for purposes of makeup contributions or to reinstate the contribution election she had on file at the time of her separation for makeup contributions. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(a)(2). The regulations also provide the employee's makeup contributions must be accompanied by attributable agency matching contributions and that, even if the reinstated employee does not elect to make up her employee contributions, the employing agency must make all appropriate agency automatic (1%) contributions associated with the back pay award. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(c)(3).

Tubesing v. DHHS, 115 MSPR 327, 335-37 ¶¶ 17-20, 2010 MSPB 242 (2010), addressed a TSP accounting issue:

The regulations implementing the Back Pay Act require an agency to correct errors affecting an employee's Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) account consistent with regulations prescribed by the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board (FRTIB). 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(h); see Madison v. Department of Defense, 111 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 11 (2009). The FRTIB regulations address the process by which a reinstated employee's TSP account should be credited with the contributions that should have been made during the back pay period. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13. Among other things, the regulations require the employing agency to deduct the appropriate TSP contributions from an employee's back pay award and submit the contributions to the "TSP record keeper," a component of the FRTIB. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(c)(1); Madison, 111 M.S.P.R., ¶ 11; see 5 C.F.R. § 1690.1.

In the September 30, 2009 Opinion and Order, the Board directed the agency to "clearly set forth its calculations relating to the appellant's...TSP account." Tubesing, 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶ 22. In its submission in response to the Board's Opinion and Order, the agency provided a spreadsheet purporting to show the amount of the appellant's TSP contributions and the agency's matching payments. The agency, however, offered no explanation regarding its calculations.

In response to the agency's submission, the appellant argued that the agency had not properly restored his TSP contributions. In support of his assertion, the appellant cited an October 14, 2009 statement from the agency's finance and accounting service allegedly showing that nothing had been withheld from the appellant's back pay award for TSP contributions. Contrary to the appellant's assertion, the October 14, 2009 statement appears to show that the agency deducted $892.32 from his back pay award for the TSP. The agency, however, provides no explanation regarding how this figure was calculated and it appears inconsistent with the agency's other documentary evidence regarding the appellant's TSP account.

To be in compliance regarding the appellant's TSP account, the agency must show that it has complied with the FRTIB's regulations regarding back pay awards, including the requirement that the agency inform the TSP record keeper of the back pay award. See Walker, 90 M.S.P.R. 136, ¶ 26. The agency must also show that it requested the FRTIB to provide a computation of interest and lost earnings in accord with the applicable regulations. Id. Additionally, the agency must provide a detailed explanation of its calculations regarding TSP and must show how they demonstrate compliance.

Agency requirements on TSP contributions were modified as a result of a regulatory change that discontinued the requirement of an agency to request a computation of interest and lost earnings from FRTIB, Bills v. Dept. of Air Force, 122 MSPR 367, 371 n.2, 2015 MSPB 25 (2015):

In the compliance initial decision, the administrative judge incorrectly imposed two additional requirements on the agency to show compliance. The administrative judge required the agency to inform the TSP record keeper of the back pay award and to show that it requested FRTIB to provide a computation of interest and lost earnings in accord with the applicable regulations. The administrative judge's authority for these requirements was based on Shobert v. Department of the Air Force, 90 M.S.P.R. 262, ¶ 11 (2001), which in turn derived the requirements from the then-current governing regulations, 5 C.F.R. §§ 1606.3 (2001) and 1606.11 (2001). However, the authority of 5 C.F.R. Part 1606 expired on August 31, 2003. See Various Changes to the Thrift Savings Plan, 70 Fed. Reg. 21290, 21291 (April 25, 2005). At that time, the governing authority for all TSP contributions from back pay awards became 5 C.F.R. Part 1605. 70 Fed. Reg. at 21291. Under 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13, the agency is now no longer required to request a computation of interest and lost earnings from FRTIB, and only needs to notify the TSP record keeper of the back pay award if there are employee make-up contributions. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(c). Therefore, the portion of the compliance initial decision requiring the agency to inform the TSP record keeper of the back pay award and to show that it requested FRTIB to provide a computation of interest and lost earnings is hereby VACATED, and Shobert is OVERRULED to the extent that it requires agencies to comply with 5 C.F.R. §§ 1606.3 (2001) and 1606.11 (2001).

With reference to Bills, Chen v. USPS, SF-0353-13-0150-C-1 (NP 4/29/2016), said of TSP calculations and accounting for those calculations and deposits:

The regulations implementing the Back Pay Act require that an agency correct errors affecting an employee's TSP account consistent with the regulations prescribed by the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board (FRTIB). Price v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 222, ¶ 16 (2012) (citing 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(h)). As explained in the FRTIB's regulations, makeup contributions are contributions that should have been deducted from an employee's basic pay or contributed by the agency on an earlier date, but were not deducted or contributed. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.1. Breakage is "the loss incurred or gain realized on makeup...contributions." Id. Calculating breakage is a function of the TSP, not a function of the employing agency. See 5 C.F.R. § 1605.2(a) (stating that "[t]he TSP will calculate breakage on late contributions [and] makeup agency contributions"). Thus, the agency cannot change the breakage calculated for the appellant's makeup contributions.[5]

The role of FRTIB was also noted in Price v. USPS, 118 MSPR 222, 228-30 ¶¶ 15-17, 2012 MSPB 74 (2012), assessing whether an agency restored the status quo when it canceled an adverse action:

The appellant claims, with respect to the interest paid on his TSP account, that, while the agency corrected the initial contribution to his TSP account for the period of his demotion, it has not paid him for the lost interest on those contributions. He asserts that Tubesing v. Department of Health & Human Services, 115 M.S.P.R. 327 (2010), requires the agency to request a computation of interest from the FRTIB but that the declaration from Russell Teed, Accounting and Control Specialist for the Payroll Benefits Branch, did not aver that he asked FRTIB to calculate interest, and thus the agency failed to prove that it provided him with status quo ante relief with respect to his TSP account.

The regulations implementing the Back Pay Act require an agency to correct errors affecting an employee's TSP account consistent with regulations prescribed by the FRTIB. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805 (h); see Tubesing, 115 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 17; Madison v. Department of Defense, 111 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 11 (2009), aff'd, 363 F. App'x 26 (Fed. Cir. 2010). The FRTIB regulations address the process by which a reinstated employee's TSP account should be credited with the contributions that should have been made during the back pay period. Tubesing, 115 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 17 (citing 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13). Among other things, the regulations require the employing agency to deduct the appropriate TSP contributions from an employee's back pay award and submit the contributions to the "TSP record keeper," a component of the FRTIB. Tubesing, 115 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 17 (citing 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13 (c)(1)). The agency must show that it has complied with the FRTIB's regulations regarding back pay awards, including the requirement that the agency inform the TSP record keeper of the back pay award. Tubesing, 115 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 20. The agency must also show that it requested the FRTIB to provide a computation of interest and lost earnings in accordance with the applicable regulations. Id.

The appellant concedes in his petition for review that the agency corrected the initial contribution amount to his TSP for the period of his demotion, but he asserts that the agency has not paid him for the lost interest on those contributions. As the administrative judge recognized, Mr. Teed explained in his sworn declaration that the TSP calculated the appellant's interest and lost earnings, known as "breakage," based on the "late" TSP contributions resulting from the appellant's back pay award. Mr. Teed noted that the TSP, rather than the agency, calculates breakage and that breakage can be positive or negative given the risk of investments. Mr. Teed attached a computer report and explained that the report is customized to show "any and all periods of employment history TSP deductions" and "the exact breakage calculated by TSP with respect to these periods of back pay for [the appellant]." Mr. Teed explained the breakage calculations for relevant pay dates in relevant funds for employee contributions, agency automatic contributions, and agency matching contributions. Such calculations represent the computation of interest and lost earnings in accordance with Tubesing and the applicable regulations. The appellant's assertion that the agency failed to meet its burden solely because Mr. Teed did not explicitly aver to requesting the FRTIB to calculate interest and lost earnings is without merit as the evidence shows that breakage was calculated and credited to the appellant's TSP account.

Of difficulties restoring the appellant's Roth account within the TSP, Grimsrud v. Dept. of Transp., NY-0752-13-0073-X-1 (NP 6/28/2016), also commented on the roles of the agency and FRTIB:

According to FRTIB regulations, when pay has been contributed to a participant's traditional balance when it should have been contributed to his Roth balance, the agency must promptly submit a redesignation record. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.17(b). That is exactly what the agency did here. (certification of redesignation of records and journal voucher). Once the agency submitted the redesignation records, the FRTIB was responsible for treating "the erroneously submitted contribution (and associated positive earnings) as if the contribution had been made to the correct balance on the date that it was contributed to the wrong balance." 5 C.F.R. § 1605.17(c)(1). The FRTIB was also responsible for changing the tax characterization of the erroneously characterized contribution. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.17(c)(2).

Similarly, once the agency certified the transfer of funds (Form TSP-2-G), the FRTIB was responsible for calculating "breakage," i.e., "the loss incurred or the gain realized on makeup or late contributions." 5 C.F.R. § 1605.1. The regulations make clear that the FRTIB calculates breakage on late contributions and makeup agency contributions. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.2(a). The FRTIB, not the employing agency, must determine the number of shares of applicable investment funds the participant would have received had the contributions been made on time. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.2(b)(1)(ii). The regulations further provide that there is no "breakage" associated with the redesignation of a TSP account from traditional to Roth. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.17(c)(4).

The agency cannot change the breakage calculated for the appellant's makeup contributions.

The appellant appears to be arguing that the agency failed to comply with 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13 by not calculating the breakage on his TSP makeup contributions based on investment in the correct fund. Makeup contributions are contributions that should have been deducted from an employee's basic pay or contributed by the agency on an earlier date but were not deducted or contributed. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.(1) Breakage is the loss incurred or gain realized on makeup contributions. Id. Makeup contributions and the associated breakage are invested according to the employee's contribution allocation on the posting date.[3] 5 C.F.R. § 1605.13(a)(3). Calculating breakage is based on the participant's contribution allocation on file for the "as of" the date. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.2(b). The "as of" date is the date the TSP contribution should have taken place. 5 C.F.R. § 1605.1(b).

[3] Posting date means the date on which a transaction is credited or debited to a participant's account. 5 C.F.R. § 1690.1.

The appellant appears to be alleging that, at the time of his separation, he had a contribution allocation on file investing all of his contributions in the C Fund, and therefore his breakage should have been calculated based on an investment in that fund. The agency states that it has been advised by the TSP and the Defense Finance and Accounting Service that the management of the appellant's TSP account is a matter between the appellant and the TSP. The appellant acknowledges that he received both makeup contributions and breakage. We agree with the administrative judge's finding that the agency is in compliance with its TSP payment obligations under the applicable regulations. Calculating breakage is a function of the FRTIB/TSP, not a function of the employing agency. See 5 C.F.R. § 1605.2 (stating that "[t]he TSP" will calculate breakage on makeup contributions). The appellant's remedy for errors in the breakage calculation is found in the procedures in 5 C.F.R. Part 1605, Subpart C. See 5 C.F.R. § 1605.21(a)(2)(iii).

Mr. Wilkes alleges that the VA did not fully comply with the MSPB's order reinstating his employment because the agency did not reinstate his TSP loan, which had been converted to a taxable withdrawal upon his retirement. The governing law does not support Mr. Wilkes's argument.

Mr. Wilkes, not the VA, had an obligation to seek reinstatement of his TSP loan. The VA may correct errors in Mr. Wilkes's TSP account consistent with the FRTIB's regulations. See 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(h) ("Agencies must correct errors that affect an employee's [TSP] account consistent with regulations prescribed by the [FRTIB]."). The FRTIB's regulations provide that, if an agency reinstates a wrongfully separated TSP participant, the participant must notify the TSP within ninety days of reinstatement to restore any previously withdrawn amount to the TSP account. See id. § 1605.13(d). During the ninety day period, a participant "may also elect to reinstate a loan which was previously declared to be a taxable distribution." § 1605.13(e). However, the regulations do not require the TSP to automatically restore a participant's loan, nor do they require the agency to notify the participant of the right to restore a TSP loan previously treated as a taxable distribution. See id. § 1605.13(d)-(e); see also Crazy Thunder-Collier, 2010 M.S.P.B. 202, at ¶ 13 (explaining that § 1605.13 "does not state any agency duty to provide notice of th[e] right" to reinstate a TSP loan). Thus, because it was incumbent upon Mr. Wilkes to contact the FRTIB to have his TSP loan reinstated, the MSPB properly concluded that the VA had no obligation to reinstate Mr. Wilkes's TSP loan.
