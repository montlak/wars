broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 21: Union Rights, Bargaining Rights and Procedures, Section F: Joint Committees, Subsection 02: Promotion and Staffing

2. Promotion and Staffing

A proposal interfered with management's selection rights under § 7106(a)(2)(C) that provided for a joint committee to develop a promotion evaluation plan determining selective factors to be used in merit promotion. NFFE Local 943 and Dept. of Air Force, Keesler Technical Training Ctr., 12 FLRA 172, 173 (1983); see NFFE Local 1482 and DOD, DMA, Hydrographic/Topographic Ctr., Louisville, 45 FLRA 52, 65 (1992) (also, the type of arrangement expressed by Proposal 2, was nonnegotiable as an arrangement because "[t]he burden imposed on management's ability to determine the criteria used to decide which employees are eligible outweighs the benefit to employees of guarding against abuse and pre-selection").

A proposal was a nonnegotiable interference with management rights under § 7106(a)(2)(C) that established union participation on a rating panel that identified factors used to evaluate candidates, determined criteria used to rate candidates under each factor, and established the methods of combining the ratings to arrive at ranks of candidates. NFFE Local 1745 and VA, 13 FLRA 543 (1983) (Proposal 1). Local 1745 was affirmed in NFFE Local 1745 v. FLRA, 828 F.2d 834 (D.C. Cir. 1987). The court noted that the proposal required a union member to sit on a panel charged with forming the selective criteria to be used for rating candidates for promotion. The essence of the court's approach to the negotiability issue was summarized in note 31 of its Opinion:

...We have found efficiency in government to be a key policy promoted by the exemption from bargaining afforded by management rights enumerated in § 7106. See National Treasury Employees Union v. FLRA, 691 F.2d 553, 560-61 (1982). If Congress deemed it in the interest of "an effective and efficient government" to exclude from bargaining management's authority to make selections for appointment, it is reasonable to assume that Congress intended to insulate not simply the ultimate choice of candidates for promotion but also the judgments implicating the criteria on which promotion decisions are to be based.

In Local 1745, the D.C. Circuit deferred to FLRA's determination that the proposal was not a negotiable procedure. Identification of the qualifications needed for performance of a particular job involved judgments directly affecting the selection ultimately made, 828 F.2d at 841-42:

At bottom, the union would have us characterize its proposal as purely procedural because it prescribes no substantive criteria for management decisionmaking. We are further told that the union member it would have sit on the three-member rating and ranking panel would play merely a "participatory" and "noncontrolling" role, and thus could not possibly interfere with management's reserved authority.

We find this characterization of the union's proposal fundamentally and fatally flawed. The notion that the union member of the panel would play a "non-controlling" role rests on the counterintuitive assumption that the two nonunion panel members will always agree, and could never be persuaded by the union member's "participatory" activity. But it is evident that any participation by the union member portends influence on the nonunion members and, more directly, on the panel's final outcome....

Considered from this vantage point, the infirmity in the union's case is apparent. The proposal's restraint in advocating criteria for management decisionmaking does not save it from trespassing on management authority sought to be immunized by Section 7106(a). The management rights statutorily protected are rights to decision by management, and, the proposal at hand contemplates direct union participation in the decisionmaking process....

A provision was not negotiable that required a union representative on a ranking panel. The evaluation and comparison of employee qualifications is an integral part of the right of management to make selections. AFGE Mint Council 157 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Mint, 19 FLRA 640, 643 (1985) (Provision 3). A proposal for union membership on ranking panels was not saved by an "opt out" clause that did no more than allow management limited flexibility in barring representatives from certain segments of the bargaining unit, depending on the location of the positions being filled. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA 422, 423-24 (1987) (Provision 2).

A proposal for a union observer on a position management committee was nonnegotiable. AFGE Local 2094 and VAMC, New York, N.Y., 22 FLRA 710 (1986). The Local 2094 result was affirmed, AFGE Local 2094 v. FLRA, 833 F.2d 1037, 1042-43 (D.C. Cir. 1987):

...This court has recognized management's right to take certain actions necessary for the exercise of its rights to discuss and deliberate on the various factors on which a determination will be made.... Thus, any claim that management's nonnegotiable rights are confined strictly to those listed or enumerated in section 7106 is without support and contrary to the weight of authority.

...

The evidence in this case indicates that the PMC is responsible for reviewing and recommending approval, prior to implementation, of all changes in organization involving work design, occupational distribution, grade distribution, staffing requirements, and costs. The Authority found that management's discussions and deliberations of those activities is an implied right under section 7106, and this court agrees. A union representative in this all-management planning meeting could well inhibit management's decision-making as to its section 7106 rights. The Authority's finding, therefore, is entirely reasonable and consistent with the guiding purpose of the Act.

...

...The court agrees with the Authority's use of the "direct interference" standard because "the proposal is not merely a procedure to be followed by the Agency in exercising its reserved rights, but rather would inhibit management in reaching decisions and in acting with respect to those rights."
