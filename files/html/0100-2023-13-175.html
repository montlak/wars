e. Prolonged Absence; Irregular Attendance

SSA v. Mills, 73 MSPR 463, 469-73 (1996), approved removal of two ALJs who were incapacitated and on LWOP for several years:

Petitioner has presented sufficient evidence establishing good cause to remove the respondents from the agency's employment rolls. It is undisputed that, because of injuries sustained, respondents will be unable to return to active duty for the foreseeable future, most likely forever. Both the Board and the predecessor to the Board's reviewing court have held that physical incapacitation constitutes good cause for removal of an ALJ. Benton v. United States, 488 F.2d 1017, 1025 (Ct. Cl. 1973); Department of Health & Human Services v. Underwood, 68 MSPR 24, 25-26 (1995).

In Benton, the United States Court of Claims held that the involuntary retirement of a hearing examiner because of physical incapacity was governed by the procedures in section 11 of the Administrative Procedure Act (currently codified at 5 USC § 7521), and that incapacitation satisfied the good cause standard. 488 F.2d at 1025. The Board is bound by the decisions of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, its reviewing court. Boulineau v. Department of the Army, 57 MSPR 244, 249 n.7 (1993). The Court of Claims, which decided Benton, was the predecessor to the Federal Circuit, and was the reviewing court of the Board's predecessor, the Civil Service Commission. Because the Federal Circuit has adopted the case law of the Court of Claims as binding precedent, Court of Claims decisions are also binding on the Board. See South Corp. v. United States, 690 F.2d 1368, 1370-71 (Fed. Cir. 1982); Petersen v. Office of Personnel Management, 56 MSPR 388, 392 n.7, aff'd, 16 F.3d 422 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).

Respondents urge the Board not to rely on the Benton decision because of its age, and because it was decided prior to the 1978 amendment of section 7521. After comparing the language of section 7521 before and after it was amended, we find that the amendments would not have affected the disposition of Benton. In fact, the only differences in the amended version are the substitutions in terminology (the Board instead of the Civil Service Commission, administrative law judge in place of hearing examiner), and the inclusion of additional adverse actions which may only be taken subject to proof of good cause. Compare Pub. L. No. 89-554, 80 Stat. 528 (1966) with 5 USC § 7521 (1994).

Respondents also argue that the Board should not rely on Underwood because the respondent in that case did not contest the removal action. Although it is true that the CALJ in that case entered a default judgment against the respondent, the CALJ also found, based upon the record, that the respondent was in extended LWOP status with no foreseeable return to duty, and that the agency's assertions regarding its increased workload were true. Accordingly, he recommended that the Board find good cause for removal of the respondent. In a Final Decision issued on June 14, 1995, the Board adopted the CALJ's decision. Department of Health & Human Services v. Underwood, 68 MSPR 24 (1995). As a published decision of the Board, Underwood is entitled to significant weight in resolving the question of whether good cause exists in the instant case.

Respondents contend that, absent a showing of misconduct, good cause cannot be found. Because we have found that Benton, which does not involve misconduct, remains precedential, and because there is no support for respondents' contention in either the legislative history of the APA or case law interpretation of section 7521, we hold to the contrary.

Respondents also argue that, because they are currently in approved LWOP status, the agency cannot remove them.[5] They rely on a Board decision stating that, as a general rule, "an agency's approval of leave for unscheduled absences precludes the agency from taking adverse action on the basis of those absences." Young v. Department of the Air Force, 36 MSPR 473, 476 (1988) (citing Williams v. Department of the Army, 24 MSPR 537, 541 (1984)), overruled on other grounds by Wilkinson v. Department of the Air Force, 68 MSPR 4, 7-8 (1995). Young, however, noted the continued existence of a primary exception to this rule. Id. That exception, set forth in Cook v. Department of the Army, 18 MSPR 610, 611-12 (1984), provides that an adverse action may be taken where: (1) The employee's absence was for a compelling reason beyond his or her control so that agency approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial; (2) the absences continued beyond a reasonable time and the employee was warned that adverse action might be initiated unless the employee became available for duty; and (3) the agency showed that the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. Id.

[5] Respondents also take exception to the CALJ's failure to address their "entitlement to leave as injured employees under the Federal Employees Compensation Act [FECA]" The respondents do not cite to any provision of FECA, and we can find no statutory support for the contention that they are entitled to indefinite leave.

In Young, the appellant's absence did not meet these criteria. 36 MSPR at 476. In the instant case, however, the respondents' absence is due to their incapacity to work, a compelling reason beyond their control, so that the agency's approval of leave is immaterial. Further, the respondents have each been absent for over four years, which is beyond a reasonable time, and they were put on notice that they would be removed unless they became available for duty. Finally, as detailed below, the agency has shown that it has a need to fill the respondents' positions with employees that will be available for duty on a regular basis. Thus, the circumstances in this case meet the criteria set forth in Cook, and the appellants' removal is not precluded by the fact that they have been on approved leave. In addition to proving the respondents' incapacity, the agency has demonstrated that its workload is increasing, and that it has a compelling need to fill respondents' positions with active ALJ's. The respondents presented evidence intended to show that an increase in active ALJ's would not necessarily augment productivity, and that as a result, there is no urgent need to replace them.

While we agree that the respondents have shown that there are a number of other factors in addition to the availability of ALJ's that affect OHA's productivity, we believe it is equally apparent that an increase in the number of on-duty ALJ's would very likely result in an overall increase in productivity.... The possibility that this improved production might not manifest itself in a statistically-measurable increase in the number of cases decided does not negate its existence....

The agency has shown that it has a compelling need to replace respondents with active ALJ's as one of several actions taken to keep up with an increasing caseload.... We find that common sense and the evidence presented support a finding that petitioner's action of removal will increase the efficiency of the agency, and promote the ideal of dispensing prompt justice.

Respondents' evidence does not support a claim of disparate treatment and, even if it did, the penalty is appropriate considering the circumstances.

The respondents allege that other agency employees have been absent from duty for extended periods and have not been subjected to removal proceedings. In order to prove disparate treatment, respondents must show that a similarly situated employee received a different penalty. Hawkins v. Department of the Navy, 49 MSPR 501, 506 (1991); see Shepley v. Department of Transportation, 15 MSPR 436, 439 (1983) ("The burden of proving disparate treatment is on the appellant."). The "charges and the circumstances surrounding the charged behavior must be substantially similar." Parker v. Department of the Navy, 50 MSPR 343, 350 (1991).

The respondents must prove that the agency has failed to initiate removal of another ALJ who has been continuously inactive for an extended period of time, and who will not return to active duty in the foreseeable future. While respondents have presented evidence that other, non-ALJ employees have been on extended leave and have not been removed, they have not shown that the petitioner has treated other similarly situated ALJ's differently. In fact, in response to discovery, petitioner indicated that a total of five ALJ's had been in a position similar to that of the respondents. Letters were sent to all five requesting a return to active duty and warning that they would be removed if their return was not possible. Two of the ALJ's returned to work, one was removed, Underwood, 68 MSPR at 26, and the other two are the respondents in this case. Respondents have produced no evidence that any other similarly situated ALJ's exist.
