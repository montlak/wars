4. Cases Submitted on Stipulation

FLRA regulations permit cases to be decided either by the ALJ or by FLRA on stipulations of fact, 5 CFR 2423.26 (2022):

Stipulations of fact submissions.

(a) General. When all parties agree that no material issue of fact exists, the parties may jointly submit a motion to the Administrative Law Judge or Authority requesting consideration of the matter based upon stipulations of fact. Briefs of the parties are required and must be submitted within 30 days of the joint motion. Upon receipt of the briefs, such motions shall be ruled upon expeditiously.

(b) Stipulations to the Administrative Law Judge. Where the stipulation adequately addresses the appropriate material facts, the Administrative Law Judge may grant the motion and decide the case through stipulation.

(c) Stipulations to the Authority. Where the stipulation provides an adequate basis for application of established precedent and a decision by the Administrative Law Judge would not assist in the resolution of the case, or in unusual circumstances, the Authority may grant the motion and decide the case through stipulation.

(d) Decision based on stipulation. Where the motion is granted, the Authority will adjudicate the case and determine whether the parties have met their respective burdens based on the stipulation and the briefs.

In any matter submitted directly to and accepted by FLRA for decision based on a stipulation of facts, FLRA considers only the facts contained in the stipulation. GSA and AFGE, 6 FLRA 430, 432 (1981). Additional information or contradictory information in the parties' briefs will be disregarded. SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 13 FLRA 409, 409 n.1 (1983); see USDA, Forest Serv., Pac. NW Region and NFFE Forest Serv. Council, Region 6, 48 FLRA 857, 857 n.1 (1993) (striking portions of brief referring to document filed in negotiability case because document was not among ULP exhibits stipulated by parties). The same is true with documentation submitted for the first time with briefs. Dept. of Transp., FAA, Airways Facilities Sector, Chicago O'Hare Airport and PASS Local 301, 16 FLRA 1001, 1001 n.1 (1984).

If FLRA finds that the stipulated facts are insufficient for it to issue a decision, or if the parties are at odds on material facts, the case will be remanded to the regional office for further processing. See SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 55 FLRA 246, 250 n.9 (1999) (remanding based on insufficient stipulation to regional director for further proceedings, including possible assignment to ALJ); DOJ, INS and AFGE Local 2718, 52 FLRA 686, 692 n.6 (1996) (ALJ to whom the case is remanded may make findings other than on facts identified by FLRA to be in dispute, if those findings are deemed necessary by judge to case resolution); FAA and NATCA, 52 FLRA 548, 553 (1996) ("While the stipulation explicitly refers to a proposal that was offered in 1992, nothing in either the stipulation or the record establishes whether the same, or a different, proposal was presented to the Respondent after negotiations were moved to the national level. Without this critical factual information, it is not possible to assess whether the Union advanced a proposal that is substantially identical to one that the Authority previously has found negotiable."); Border Patrol, Tucson Sector and Nat'l Border Patrol Council, AFGE Local 2544, 48 FLRA 391, 394 (1993) (remand to regional director: parties' stipulation was insufficient because parties did not agree, and record did not disclose, disputed information involved in a ULP concerning agency's refusal to provide information to union); DOJ, Office of Justice Programs and AFSCME Local 2830, 42 FLRA 371, 375-76 (1991) (remand to regional director: "The parties' stipulation and accompanying exhibits do not contain sufficient information for us to make determinations.... Moreover, it is apparent from the parties' briefs that they disagree over not only the facts but also the issues to be resolved."). A motion to strike evidence or portions of a brief is appropriate if a party recites facts or refers to documents not embraced in the stipulation of facts. See Customs Serv. and NTEU, 39 FLRA 749, 757 (1991).

Under earlier regulations, 5 CFR 2423.26, the ALJ could decide a case on the basis of stipulated facts if the case had not been referred directly to FLRA by the regional director. If the case was referred to an ALJ under that procedure, the ALJ decided the case on the basis of the stipulation. Even if the parties arrived at a stipulated set of facts in a ULP case, the ALJ was required to prepare a decision and could not transfer the case directly to the FLRA without a decision. Army Health Clinic, Ft. Ritchie and NFFE Local 1153, 5 FLRA 490, 490 n.* (1981).
