broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 01: Retirement, Section D: Disability Retirement, Subsection 09: Effect of Disability Accommodation on Annuity Entitlement, Part a: Conflicting Agency Evidence Concerning Accommodation
a. Conflicting Agency Evidence Concerning Accommodation

Bryan v. OPM, 26 MSPR 471, 474 (1985), suggests that if there is not contradictory evidence, the certificate of agency reassignment efforts constitutes preponderant evidence of the lack of an available vacant position as an accommodation. If there is conflicting evidence as to the appellant's ability to perform another position through reassignment, the conflicting evidence is resolved through the Board's credibility analysis under Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453, 458 (1987). See Sucic v. OPM, 64 MSPR 281, 284-85 (1994) (agency's certification of reassignment and accommodation efforts was not conclusive; the Board credited a supervisor's testimony concerning the appellant's ability to perform modified duties based on observations of the appellant and remanded the case to determine if the light duty provided to the appellant was full or part-time); cf. Sharlofsky v. OPM, PH-844E-13-0463-I-1 (NP 8/15/2014) ("In Sucic, 64 M.S.P.R. at 284-87, the Board determined that the testimony of the individuals who worked with the appellant 'was entitled to greater weight than [the] statement in the Agency Certification that the Postal Service could not accommodate the appellant.'"); Prato-Easterling v. OPM, AT-844E-13-0352-I-1 (NP 7/7/2014) (settlement stating the agency would inform OPM that appellant was disabled, unable to perform useful and efficient service in her position, and was not qualified for reassignment, was not controlling; Board considered the testimony of a supervisor concerning the agency's ability to accommodate the appellant; the determinative factor in denying the disability retirement application, however, was the conclusion that the appellant failed to show that her medical condition caused her inability to work).

The Board may disregard an agency's conclusory statement that reassignment is not necessary because the employee's performance is successful and no medical restrictions prevent the employee from performing or attending work, Thomas v. OPM, DE-844E-15-0524-I-1 (NP 5/6/2016):

...In any event, we have considered OPM's argument regarding the agency's certification that reassignment was not necessary and find that it provides no basis to disturb the initial decision. As discussed above, the appellant has shown by preponderant evidence that she is disabled for her position and that she cannot be reasonably accommodated. We find that the agency's conclusory statement that reassignment is not necessary and that the appellant does not have any medical restrictions that would prevent her from performing critical duties of her position or from attending work altogether is not persuasive and does not outweigh the other record evidence discussed above. See Francois v. Office of Personnel Management, 64 M.S.P.R. 191, 195 (1994) (declining to afford weight to the agency's certification that reassignment was not necessary where "OPM has not proffered any explanation for why it credited the administrative officer's conclusory statement and checking off a box on a preprinted form" over more probative, detailed evidence).

Courtney v. OPM, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP), considered a challenge to OPM's determination that the agency adequately accommodated the appellant's disability, but the court affirmed the facts found by the Board:

During this time, Ms. Courtney applied to OPM for disability retirement benefits. She asserted that she suffered from vision-related ailments, asthma, and diabetes. As a result of the vision problems, she said, she was unable to perform her duties, which were all computer-based, and she had difficulty commuting. In her disability retirement application, Ms. Courtney also said that she had requested reasonable accommodations that were not granted. The agency's alleged failure to provide these accommodations, she said, resulted in her missing work and being unable to perform useful and efficient service.

The agency completed its required materials for the disability application, including the Supervisor's Statement, Certificate of Reassignment and Accommodation Efforts, and Disability Retirement Application Checklist. Within those materials, the relevant agency employees indicated that requested reasonable accommodations had been provided and that Ms. Courtney's performance even with her alleged disability "was not less than fully successful" (even if her conduct was unsatisfactory). That is, in the agency's view, her performance had not suffered.

...

The Board considered the evidentiary record before it, reviewed it in detail, and ultimately agreed with OPM that Ms. Courtney's position was not supported. The Board first observed that Ms. Courtney's "performance was highly successful on her last rating of record" before her absence. Further, her supervisor stated that her performance was "not less than fully successful in any critical element." Indeed, the Board observed that "there is no evidence that [Ms. Courtney's] performance was unsatisfactory." And although she was absent from work, the Board found that her "absence from work alone" was "insufficient to support a finding in favor of disability retirement."

Ms. Courtney argued that the agency failed to grant her reasonable accommodations, effectively forcing her out because of her disability. The Board, however, reviewed the record and found that each time Ms. Courtney requested reasonable accommodations, they were granted. Namely, "the agency granted the appellant more than 12 accommodations to include software to help her read and write and otherwise work on her computer, hardware for the same purpose, an adjusted work schedule and telework as appropriate in accordance with the appellant's request, as well as breaks when needed." Ms. Courtney had argued in particular that she was denied telework, a device called a "Reader," and the time to undergo certain software training related to her requested accommodations. But the record showed that (1) she was approved for telework but had withdrawn her own request for it, (2) she had not shown that she had requested a Reader and had failed to establish that the Reader was a necessary accommodation, and (3) the agency had said that her caseload expectations would be considered in light of the time it would take to learn the software and hardware for her accommodations. Finally, the Board concluded that Ms. Courtney had not identified any "medical record, report, or advice that would suggest [that] she was unable to work on a computer at all or that the accommodations provided by the agency were inadequate to perform the duties of her position."
