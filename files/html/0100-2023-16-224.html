7. Questioning of Witnesses

The decisions earlier discussed consider the right to union representation and assistance during investigatory interviews of employees who reasonably believe they may be disciplined as a result of information developed during the interview; the decisions also consider the right of notice to and representation by the union during formal discussions. There is another category of cases that establishes limited rights for unit members who are questioned by agency representatives when those representatives prepare for hearings before third-party adjudicators. These cases involve inquiries that are undertaken after the agency has completed its underlying investigation concerning a personnel matter. The inquiry is essentially prehearing preparation by the agency attorney or representative. Depending on the nature of the inquiry and the type of adjudicator, the employee who is questioned may be entitled to assurances against reprisal, and the employee's participation in the interview with a management representative is to be voluntary. Additionally, if the inquiry takes on the character of a formal discussion, the union has the right to notice of the interview and an opportunity to be present during the interview. The decisions do not always distinguish between the employer's obligation to warn the employee and notify the union.

We return to the Statute, 5 USC 7114(a):

(2) An exclusive representative of an appropriate unit in an agency shall be given the opportunity to be represented at--

(A) any formal discussion between one or more representatives of the agency and one or more employees in the unit or their representatives concerning any grievance or any personnel policy or practices or other general condition of employment[.]

Confusion, or lack of clarity, resulted from FLRA and court decisions involving the rights of employees and the union when management seeks to interview unit employees who may become witnesses in ULP or arbitration cases or statutory appeals brought by unit employees through MSPB and EEOC procedures. Simplified and summarized, IRS, Brookhaven Serv. Ctr. and NTEU Chap. 99, 9 FLRA 930 (1982), the progenitor of this decisional family, determined that when management interviews a unit member who is a potential witness in a ULP or arbitration hearing, the interview is ordinarily not a formal discussion, but the employee must be assured by management that no reprisal (discipline) will be taken if the employee declined to be interviewed. The Brookhaven warning has practical significance: a lot of employees decline to be interviewed. Brookhaven did not address the necessity for management either to notify the union of the interview or permit the union to act as a representative during the interview.

Decisions subsequent to Brookhaven further considered the nature of a grievance and a formal discussion, as those terms are defined by statute. In Bureau of Gov't Fin. Operations, Hq. and NTEU, 15 FLRA 423 (1984), involving a meeting scheduled by management to interview a unit employee for an MSPB hearing, FLRA decided an MSPB appeal did not rise to the level of a grievance under § 7114(a)(2)(A). FLRA also decided that the interview did not meet the criteria necessary to characterize it as formal meeting. An appellate court entered the picture, NTEU v. FLRA, 774 F.2d 1181 (D.C. Cir. 1985), reviewing Government Financial Operations. FLRA was wrong on its restricted definition of a grievance. Remanding the case to FLRA, the court strongly hinted that there were sufficient indicia of formality in the structure of the interview to conclude that the union was entitled to notice and an opportunity to provide some (undefined) level of representation at the interview meeting.

Dept. of Air Force, Sacramento Air Logistics Ctr., McClellan AFB and AFGE Local 1857, 29 FLRA 594 (1987), was next in the lineage. The case involved an interview by management of a unit employee in preparation for an arbitration hearing. FLRA, following the reasoning of the D.C. Circuit decision, determined that the interview was formal and, as a result, the union was entitled to advance notice of the interview and an opportunity to be represented at the meeting.

Dept. of Air Force, Warren AFB and AFGE Local 2354, 31 FLRA 541, 547 (1988), involved a management representative who interviewed a unit employee in preparation for a ULP hearing. FLRA identified the right of an employees to avoid coercive question concerning the exercise of employees' statutorily protected rights. FLRA separately recognized the right of a union to be represented at formal discussions involving grievances and employees' conditions of employment.
