7. Statements of Representative, Party

Counsel occasionally confuse what they say and what they prove. Statements made by an agency representative in a letter arguing the agency case on appeal before the AJ do not constitute evidence of the truth of those statements. Enos v. USPS, 8 MSPR 59, 63, 7 MSPB 554 (1981); see Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 79 MSPR 342, 348 (1998) ("It is well-settled that statements in responses by the agency's representative are not themselves evidence."); Di Sera v. Dept. of Army, 71 MSPR 120, 123 n.3 (1996) (the agency representative could not alone "stipulate" a fact; statements made by an agency representative are not evidence); Leaton v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 331, 337 (1994) ("The appellant's prehearing submission is an unsworn pleading, and as such, it is argument, not evidence."); Perez v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 65 MSPR 287, 289 (1994) ("[T]he agency representative's unsworn statements, contained in the agency's response, do not constitute evidence[.] "); Fontanilla v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 312, 316-17 (1990) ("Statements of a party do not constitute evidence as to the truth of their assertions."); Barger v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 170, 174 (1982) ("[T]he Board would not accept an alleged statement of fact made by any party to a Board proceeding as 'fact' when the record contains evidence which establishes a different conclusion."). Statements made in opening and closing arguments are not evidence, except that the admissions of a representative may constitute evidence. However, an AJ may consider the "consistency of appellants' testimony with respect to allegations made on their behalf." Bury v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 273, 277 n.3 (1984); cf. Borowski v. USDA, 40 MSPR 372, 376 (1989) ("While the agency's arguments, if substantiated, may have satisfied its burden of showing a nondiscriminatory reason for the challenged action, arguments alone do not constitute evidence. See Enos v. U.S. Postal Service, 7 MSPB 554, 8 MSPR 59, 63 (1981) (statements made by an agency's representative in a letter arguing the agency's case on appeal before the administrative judge do not constitute evidence of the truth of those statements). We find that the record is insufficient to resolve the allegation of age discrimination."). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Admissions."]

Counsel's statements concerning the status of jurisdictional issues, e.g., the pendency or status of an EEO complaint, should be backed by evidence to receive credit from the Board, Felton v. Dept. of Air Force, 106 MSPR 198, 200-01 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 170 (2007):

In its motion to dismiss the appeal, the agency contended that the appellant had begun the equal employment opportunity (EEO) process, but it stopped the pre-complaint process when he failed to respond to the agency's notice of final interview. According to the agency, he did not file a formal complaint. The agency thus disputes the appellant's statements, but it has presented no evidence to support its statements. The statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence. Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995). Although the agency included in its response file a statement that the appellant did not file a formal complaint, this submission is not sworn and bears no indication that the author, the agency representative in this appeal, has any personal knowledge of the appellant's EEO activity. See, e.g., Singletary v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 76, 79 (1998) (agency's statement was not in affidavit form and therefore does not constitute evidence).

Hendricks v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 163, 168-69 (1995), added, however, that the assertions of an appellant's representative constitute at least allegations that may be sufficient to raise a factual question as to the timeliness of an appeal from a constructive adverse action. But see Imler v. VA, DE-0752-15-0408-I-1 (NP 9/25/2015) (as to the timeliness of an appeal and medical causes for its delay: "[T]he vague, unsworn descriptions of the appellant's conditions, submitted by her attorney, do not constitute evidence. See Burger v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 582, ¶ 48 (2003), aff'd sub nom. Hayes v. U.S. Postal Service, 390 F.3d 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2004)."). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Representative as Witness."]

If a representative makes a statement in a pleading based on the representative's participation in Board proceedings, the Board may credit the statement, as in Brown v. Dept. of Interior, 86 MSPR 546, 556 ¶ 16 (2000), involving an appellant's challenge to a settlement agreement:

...The agency contends that the appellant then called her father in Nevada and spoke with him for approximately an hour. Although the statements of a party's representative in a pleading generally do not constitute evidence, see Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 MSPR 163, 168 (1995), in this case, the agency representative was a witness to, and a participant in, the settlement discussions, and her statement is consistent with the appellant's statement that "The judge was in the courtroom as I was on the telephone with my father[.] " Despite the appellant's contention to the contrary, it appears that the administrative judge provided the appellant the opportunity to consult with her father for support and advice prior to the appellant's final decision to accept the agreement.

Similar to Brown is Felch v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-07-0550-B-1 (NP 9/15/2010):

...Although statements of a party representative in a pleading generally do not constitute evidence, see Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995), sworn statements submitted as attachments to pleadings do constitute evidence to be afforded whatever weight is appropriate under the circumstances of the case. In this case, Mr. Avery was a witness to, and a participant in, the agency's actions to comply with the terms of the settlement agreement. Therefore, we find that the administrative judge properly considered his sworn declaration in denying the appellant's petition for enforcement. See Brown v. Department of the Interior, 86 M.S.P.R. 546, ¶ 16 (2000) (recognizing that a party representative may provide evidence, in some circumstances, when he was a witness or participant in a matter at issue).

See Cheatham v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-3330-15-0027-C-1 (NP 3/16/2016) (enforcement proceeding: "Here, the agency representative declared under penalty of perjury that the facts contained in the agency's narrative establishing its compliance are true and correct. In addition, the agency's narrative explanation on review is undisputed. See Cox v. U.S. Postal Service, 87 M.S.P.R. 575, ¶ 3 (2001). The agency's narrative also is supported by documentary evidence.").

An appellant's unsworn allegation in an enforcement case is not evidence. Betterly v. VA, 47 MSPR 63, 65 (1991), said of the appellant's contention that he was pressured by the AJ into settling his case:

[T]he appellant's assertions as to the administrative judge's statements were not made in an affidavit or sworn statement and are unsupported by any submission from his representative or the agency. Thus, they remain simple allegations on his part....

Yet assertions by a party or a representative may constitute nonfrivolous allegations supporting Board jurisdiction over an appeal even if those statements are not evidence. Hendricks v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 163, 168-69 (1995). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Jurisdictional Issues" and Chapter 6, "Jurisdictional Showing," for decisions discussing the significance of nonfrivolous allegations in establishing the right to a jurisdictional hearing in constructive adverse action cases.] In Paris v. Dept. of Treasury, 104 MSPR 331, 340 n.2, 2006 MSPB 362 (2006), involving analysis of a claim of disability discrimination and the sufficiency of the appellant's proof concerning accommodation, the Board noted that statements by a party contained in an e-filing, e.g., an appeal, may carry the weight of a sworn statement pursuant to 5 USC 1201.14(j). [Refer to earlier in this chapter, "Unsworn Written Statements."]
