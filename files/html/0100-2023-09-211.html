2. Motion to Dismiss Appeal

"An appeal that is within the Board's jurisdiction may be dismissed for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted if the appellant cannot obtain effective relief before the Board even if his allegations are accepted as true. Alford v. Department of Defense, 113 M.S.P.R. 263, ¶ 11 (2010), aff'd, 407 F. App'x 458 (Fed. Cir. 2011)." Beyers v. Dept. of State, DC-3330-11-0538-I-1 (NP 1/30/2012).

An agency occasionally will seek to short-circuit the appeals process by filing a motion to dismiss the appeal. The motion is usually filed when there is a challenge to jurisdiction or timeliness, but it must be filed with regard for the state of the record before the Board. A motion to dismiss based on a truncated record supplied by the agency can be risky. An agency could find itself in a bad position without a proper response to an appeal. Were the appeal dismissed on the basis of a minimal record, by the time a PFR is decided and the appeal remanded, the lapse of time could eliminate the possibility of securing affidavits or favorable testimony at a subsequent hearing. One agency filed a motion to dismiss an appeal from a constructive suspension following the agency's imposition of enforced leave. Over the appellant's protestations that he was entitled to a hearing, the AJ agreed with the agency and dismissed the appeal. The appellant filed a PFR; the agency opposed the PFR and requested that, if the Board granted the PFR, the agency be permitted 20 days to file its response to the appeal. The Board sustained the PFR and remanded the case for a hearing, denying the agency's request for time to file a response to the appeal because the agency had "not presented any reason why it could not respond to the merits of appellant's petition in a timely fashion...." Woodall v. FERC, 21 MSPR 290, 292 n.1 (1984).

An agency should presume that a case will be adjudicated on the merits and file a proper appeal response even if it simultaneously files a motion to dismiss the case for lack of Board jurisdiction. Alternatively, if the agency objection is on the basis of jurisdiction or timeliness, and particularly if the defect is apparent from the information provided in the appeal, the agency could move to dismiss and move to stay all other proceedings (including filing its response to the appeal) pending the AJ's ruling on the motion to dismiss. If a motion to stay proceedings is filed, it should be ascertained, probably by telephone conference call with the AJ and opposing counsel, whether the AJ will grant the stay motion to avoid missing the deadline for filing the response to the appeal. If a dispositive motion is filed, the agency may move to stay discovery (and the appellant may oppose that motion) for the period that the dispositive motion is being considered by the AJ.

The lack of an agency response to an appeal is not necessarily fatal to an agency's case. In Bynum v. USPS, 112 MSPR 403, 2009 MSPB 194 (2009) (aff'd on other grounds, Bynum v. USPS, 382 Fed. Appx. 934 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)), a restoration appeal, the agency did not submit a response file because, "the AJ granted the agency's motion to stay discovery pending resolution of the jurisdictional issue." The Board noted, id. at 412 n.2:

...In deciding the merits of the appeal, the Board need not review the agency's response file as the appellant has failed to meet her burden of proving that the agency acted arbitrarily and capriciously in denying her request for restoration.

The better or best practice is for the agency to file the response called for by the acknowledgment order or submit a motion to delay the submission of the response pending the AJ's ruling on a motion to dismiss (or order to show cause from the AJ) on an arguably dispositive issue of jurisdiction or timeliness of the appeal.
