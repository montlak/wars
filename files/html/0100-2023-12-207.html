2. Signature, Designation of Representative Required

Under 5 CFR 1201.114(c) (2022), a PFR must contain the signature of the party or the party's representative. To ensure some integrity in the PFR process, a PFR not submitted by the appellant is invalid without a designation of representative. Riddick v. OPM, 5 MSPR 215, 5 MSPB 263 (1981); see Bell v. VA, 46 MSPR 365 (1990) (PFR must be signed by either appellant or a designated representative); cf. Magelitz v. OPM, 118 MSPR 472, 476-77 ¶ 9, 2012 MSPB 99 (2012) ("...Ms. Deursch and her attorney did not submit a properly executed designation of representative, despite notice from the Clerk of the Board of the deficiency of the petition for review. Thus, the petition for review was deficient under the Board's regulations."); Bernard v. OPM, 37 MSPR 486, 492 (1988) (Chairman Levinson dissenting and stating his views concerning the necessity for the signature of the appellant or designated representative). The designation must be written. Eves v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 107, 109 (1992). The Board provides a designation form, but the form is not required. The regulations require only that a representative be designated in writing. Brady v. Dept. of Labor, 57 MSPR 341, 346 (1993). Board practice, however, is to permit a PFR to be filed by an individual designated as representative before the AJ. Factual representations by an individual made in addition to a PFR will not be considered if those representations are not part of the PFR and if the proponent has not been designated to be the appellant's representative. Deery v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 455, 456 n.1, 7 MSPB 321 (1981). The process does not work in reverse: a designation of representative at the PFR stage does not validate the failure to properly designate a representative before the AJ. Desrochers v. USPS, 27 MSPR 137, 139-40 (1985). If there is some confusion as to the status of a representative during a PFR, the Board considers statements made concerning the issue at the PFR stage. See, e.g., Gardner v. Dept. of Treasury, 68 MSPR 258, 261 (1995). If the PFR is submitted electronically, signature requirements are met if the e-filing process is followed. 5 CFR 1201.14(k) (2022).

At times the Board may not be inclined to look behind formalities of the filing of a PFR. While his appeal was pending, and after hearing, an appellant submitted a letter to the AJ stating that the AJ should consider the letter to be a formal request to withdraw the hearing, and in that letter the appellant also told the AJ that the individual who was representing him no longer had the authority to act on his behalf. The appeal was dismissed as withdrawn. The representative filed a PFR. The Clerk of the Board directed the appellant to designate the individual as his representative if he wished the Board to consider the petition. The appellant did not respond. The representative, however, responded by arguing that the appellant's revocation of his designation was ineffective because the appellant was not competent either to discharge counsel or withdraw his appeal. The Board decided that because the appellant revoked the designation of the representative and because the PFR was not signed by either the appellant or a properly designated representative, it was deficient under the Board's regulations and it was dismissed. Scahberg v. USPS, 104 MSPR 621, 2007 MSPB 39 (2007).

The Board's Chairman noted a dissent to Gracik v. OPM, 35 MSPR 392, 393-94 (1987), as to a PFR submitted but not signed by an appellant's representative, his wife:

Our regulations, codified at 5 CFR Part 1201, were established to fulfill the Congressional mandate that employees receive full due process rights in the adjudication of their appeals.... These rules should not be applied woodenly, but they should not be ignored when they are plainly applicable. Similar to the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, one of the purposes of 5 CFR § 1201.114(a) is to make certain that the parties assent to the filings made on their behalf.... Because the appellant's representative not only failed to sign the petition for review, but also failed to respond to the show cause order, the record before the Board lacks any manifestation of assent.

Without some assurance that the appellant is actively pursuing his claim, we cannot be sure there is an active case or controversy before us. Yet, unless there is a contested matter, we cannot act; advisory opinions are prohibited by the Board's organic statute....

The defective filing should not be reviewed by the Board; it should be dismissed for failure to meet the Board's requirements.

Board regulations do not require that changes in designated representatives be filed at the same time as a PFR, and the practice of the Clerk is to allow appellants an opportunity to submit those designations after undesignated representatives have filed PFRs. Coleman v. USPS, 71 MSPR 270, 274 n.2 (1996). An agency may file a proper designation reflecting a change of representative after the new representative files a PFR. See Dodd v. Dept. of Interior, 94 MSPR 174, 177 ¶ 5 (2003). If the Board receives a PFR from an individual who is neither the appellant nor the designated representative, the Board will request the person submitting the PFR to supply a designation. If that individual disclaims representative status, the Board may direct the appellant to show cause why his PFR should not be dismissed as deficient. See Bell v. VA, 48 MSPR 145, 146-47 (1991). Should the Board receive submissions from more than one representative, it will request the appellant to notify the Board whether he is represented and, if so, by whom. See Eves v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 107 (1992). If there is a change in representation, the Board will not dismiss the PFR if the PFR is timely filed but if, unless there is demonstrable prejudice to the agency, the designation form is belatedly filed. See Benson v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 548, 552-53 (1994) (arbitration review proceeding); cf. Davis v. GSA, 32 MSPR 162, 163 n.1 (1987) (declining to consider the agency response to a PFR when the response was filed by an undesignated representative).

A PFR submitted by an agency must be accompanied by a designation of representative. The Board dismissed one agency PFR filed without a proper designation after the agency was given the opportunity, and failed, to cure the deficiency. Yokley v. USPS, 41 MSPR 93, 95 (1989). If an agency makes a good faith but technically deficient attempt to file a designation, and then corrects its error, the Board will not dismiss the otherwise timely-filed agency submission. See Arenz v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 88, 93 n.4 (1991).
