a. Distinction Between Regulation Review, Implementation Review

"The essential distinction between facial invalidity and invalid implementation is that the former relates to prospective application of regulations, whereas the latter relates to implementation which has already taken place in some particular agency resulting in commission of a prohibited personnel practice at that agency." Morales v. OPM, 90 MSPR 631, 632 n.1 (2001); see Avery v. OPM, 94 MSPR 212, 214 n.3 (2003) (same analysis); Nadybal v. OPM, 91 MSPR 485, 489 n.4 (2002) (same analysis).

The Board does not have jurisdiction to review the operation of agency regulations that do not implement OPM regulations. See Sczygelski v. OPM, CB-1205-13-0019-U-1 (NP 8/21/2013) ("The provision of the Standards of Conduct that he challenges is an internal CBP rule, not a regulation promulgated by OPM. The Board does not have jurisdiction to review such an internal agency rule. E.g.,Kligman v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 14 (2006); Welber v. Office of Personnel Management, 52 M.S.P.R. 23, 25 (1991). The petitioner's contention that the internal rule violates 5 U.S.C. § 7323(c), causing the agency to commit a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(12), therefore does not articulate a claim within the Board's regulation review jurisdiction."); Cuffee v. OPM, 74 MSPR 24, 26 (1997) (MSPB lacked regulation review jurisdiction to consider the operation of a DOD priority placement program excluded from the OPM's CTAP placement program established by 5 CFR Part 330, Subpart F); Welber v. OPM, 52 MSPR 23, 25 (1991) (no jurisdiction to review a postal regulation involving employment examination practices).

A challenge to an OPM regulation and an agency's implementation of an OPM regulation was considered in NTEU v. Sugarman, 4 MSPR 510, 511-13 (1981):

On February 7, 1980, Vincent L. Connery, National President of the National Treasury Employees Union (NTEU), filed a petition for review of certain regulations of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), pursuant to 5 USC sec. 1205(e)(1)(B). Specifically, Mr. Connery requested that the Board review Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Supplement 335-1, Subchapter S6, and declare one of its provisions invalid on its face and as implemented by the U.S. Customs Service, an agency of the Department of the Treasury. The Board sought and received the comments of OPM and the Department of the Treasury as to whether it should exercise its discretion to grant review.

At issue is the accessibility to employees and others of "crediting plans," which are used by promotion and selection panels to rate and rank candidates for employment or promotion.

FPM Supplement 335-1, entitled Evaluation of Employees for Promotion and Internal Placement, presents OPM's "instructions for use of evaluation procedures." Subchapter S6 provides in relevant part:

Individuals responsible for the Personnel Management Program must clearly identify those examination materials (such as test materials, rating guides, or crediting plans) which require security and control, and ensure that proper security and control are maintained.

Connery alleges that, relying on this regulation, the Customs Service has denied NTEU, a duly elected exclusive bargaining representative, access to crediting plans which are used by Customs Service Promotion Evaluation Boards to rate and rank employees for promotions. He claims that withholding of crediting plans under this regulation constitutes no less than eight prohibited personnel practices.

The Department of the Treasury does not deny that it withholds some crediting plans, under some circumstances, relying on the subject regulation. It vigorously denies that such withholding constitutes any prohibited personnel practice. To the contrary, it argues, indiscriminate disclosure of such ranking criteria could result in an unfair advantage to some competitors. OPM maintains that the Board has no jurisdiction to review the regulation, arguing that the regulation does not affect or directly relate to any "personnel action" within the meaning of 5 USC sec. 2302(a)(2)(A), and thus cannot on its face cause the commission of any prohibited personnel practice.

For the reasons set forth below, we grant the petition for review of this regulation as implemented by the Customs Service, but decline to grant review of the regulation on its face.

Section 1205(e) provides that the Board shall have "sole discretion" whether to grant a petition such as that presented here. In deciding whether to exercise this discretion, the Board will consider, among other things, the likelihood of a given issue being timely reached through ordinary channels of appeal, the availability of other equivalent remedies, whether the regulation at issue has broad application to the Federal service, and whether the arguments against its validity are such as to create a reasonable likelihood that the rule, regulation, or practice will be found to be invalid under section 1205(e).

We think there is little likelihood that the petitioner would prevail on the merits with respect to his attack on the face of this regulation. There is no showing that the commission of a prohibited personnel practice is a reasonably foreseeable result of the regulation's existence. The rule is rationally related to the purpose of preventing unfair advantage to any competing applicant or employee. Petitioner's speculations about its use to conceal arbitrary actions are too insubstantial in our opinion to warrant review. Accordingly, we decline to review the regulation on its face.

However, petitioner also alleges that implementation of the regulation by the Customs Service has resulted in the commission of prohibited personnel practices. The apparent absence of objective criteria by which the Service determines whether to release crediting plans also raises questions. Petitioner alleges that Customs has more than once refused to release a given plan and then reversed itself after the filing of an unfair labor practice complaint with the Federal Labor Relations Authority. Accordingly, the Board will review the implementation of this regulation by the Customs Service to determine whether it has resulted in the commission of a prohibited personnel practice.

Specific issues which the parties should address include:

1.  Are there specific instances of personnel actions taken by the Customs Service involving the use of crediting plans to which access was improperly denied? What provision of section 2302(b) was violated, and how did withholding of the crediting plan contribute to this result?

2.  What criteria are used by the Customs Service in deciding whether to release a given crediting plan? Has the Service arbitrarily refused access to such plans? What prohibited personnel practice, if any, has resulted?

After reviewing the submissions of the parties, the Board resumed its consideration of the case, described the use of crediting plans, and concluded that the release or refusal to release the plans did not amount to a prohibited personnel practice. See NTEU v. Devine, 8 MSPR 640, 645 (1981).

Conceivably, the Board could review an arbitration award that involves a regulation implementation challenge, as long as the claim before the arbitrator asserts discrimination covered by 5 USC 7702. See Ogden Air Logistics Ctr. v. AFGE Local 1592, 6 MSPR 630, 636, 6 MSPB 531 (1981) ("The union does not assert that Project Mainstream was required or applied by the Office of Personnel Management or CSC, its predecessor. The union complains of implementation of an upward mobility program which management established. Accordingly, implementation of the program is not appealable to the Board since the employment practice complained of was not administered or required by 0PM. Assuming, arguendo, that the subject matter of this case was within the Board's jurisdiction, appellant does not allege a violation of 5 U.S.C. 2302(b)(l). Instead, it is alleged that sections 2302(b)(ll) and 2301 were violated. Therefore, absent a claim of discrimination cognizable under section 7702, the Board could not review this decision even if it involved a matter which could have originally been brought before the Board.").
