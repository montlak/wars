1. Mootness of Appeal

Without a live controversy, an appeal is moot. Frank v. Minnesota Newspaper Ass'n, 490 U.S. 225, 227 (1989). "Mootness can arise at any stage of litigation." Nguyen v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 603, 605 ¶ 4 (1999). See Jordan v. USPS, 90 MSPR 525, 530 n.4 (2002) (same result in a USERRA case); cf. Randolph v. McDonough, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP) ("'If an event occurs while a case is pending on appeal that makes it impossible for the court to grant any effectual relief [whatsoever] to a prevailing party, the appeal must be dismissed as moot.' Nasatka v. Delta Sci. Corp., 58 F.3d 1578, 1580 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (internal quotations omitted)."). Under Confederacion De Asociaciones Agricolas v. United States, 32 F.4th 1130, 1139 (Fed. Cir. 2022):

The mootness doctrine arises from Article III's limit on the exercise of federal judicial power to live cases and controversies. See Campbell-Ewald Co. v. Gomez, 577 U.S. 153, 160-61 (2016). Moot cases are those in which "the issues presented are no longer 'live' or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome." U.S. Parole Comm'n v. Geraghty, 445 U.S. 388, 396 (1980) (quoting Powell v. McCormack, 395 U.S. 486, 496 (1969)). A case becomes moot and must be dismissed only when "'it is impossible for a court to grant any effectual relief whatever' to [the plaintiff] assuming it prevails." Mission Prod. Holdings, Inc. v. Tempnology, LLC, 139 S. Ct. 1652, 1660 (2019) (quoting Chafin v. Chafin, 568 U.S. 165, 172 (2013)). If "there is any chance" a court can grant the plaintiff's requested relief if it prevails on the merits, no matter how "uncertain or even unlikely" that chance may be, the "suit remains live." Id.

The Board is specifically prohibited from issuing advisory opinions, and may not render an opinion on a matter that is moot. USDA v. Palmer, 68 MSPR 586, 588-89 (1995). It is not enough that a case or controversy existed earlier in the proceedings. Roe v. Wade, 410 U.S. 113, 125 (1973); Hornbuckle v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3252). Under Special Counsel v. Malone, 77 MSPR 477, 481 (1998):

Mootness...occurs "where, due to an intervening event, the appellant obtained all the relief he could have obtained had he prevailed before the Board, or where it is impossible for the Board to grant further relief." Absent entitlement to relief, the appellant lacks a legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the appeal and the appeal must be dismissed....

McCauley v. VA, CH-3443-14-0099-B-1 (NP 9/13/2016), added:

...The mootness doctrine, which stems from the "case or controversy" requirement of Article III of the Constitution, is jurisdictional in nature. See Nasatka v. Delta Scientific Corp., 58 F.3d 1578, 1580 (Fed. Cir. 1995). Although the Board is not an Article III court, it is governed by an analogous statutory provision that prohibits it from issuing advisory opinions. See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(h). A case is moot when the issues presented are no longer "live" or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the case. Powell v. McCormack, 395 U.S. 486, 496-98 (1969); Horner v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 815 F.2d 668, 670-71 (Fed. Cir. 1987); see Occhipinti v. Department of Justice, 61 M.S.P.R. 504, 507 (1994). Mootness can arise at any stage of litigation, and an appeal will be dismissed as moot when, by virtue of an intervening event, the Board cannot grant any effectual relief whatever in favor of the appellant, viz., when the appellant, by whatever means, obtained all of the relief he could have obtained had he prevailed before the Board, and thereby lost any legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the appeal. See Perisho v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 55, 58 (1995); Gonzalez v. Department of Justice, 68 M.S.P.R. 439, 441 (1995); see also Gingery, 110 M.S.P.R. 83, ¶ 17.

Put another way, once Board jurisdiction has attached, it cannot be extinguished based on mootness unless the appellant has received all of the possible relief he sought before the Board, i.e., unless it is impossible for the Board to grant any further effectual relief. See Dalton v. Department of Justice, 66 M.S.P.R. 429, 434 (1995). The available remedy need not be "fully satisfactory" to avoid mootness: a partial remedy is sufficient. Calderon v. Moore, 518 U.S. 149, 150, 116 (1996). Thus, the availability of even a partial remedy is sufficient to prevent a case from being moot. Id.

Considerations of judicial efficiency and economy of the Board's resources, as well as potential impact upon parties not before the Board, are relevant in making a mootness determination. Conrad v. DOJ, 24 MSPR 452, 454 (1984) (relying on Wells v. Harris, 1 MSPR 208, 222-23, 1 MSPB 199 (1979)) (suitability determination); cf. Prehoda v. DHS, 98 MSPR 418 (2005) (suitability determination); Brackins v. OPM, 60 MSPR 260, 263 (1993) (suitability appeal was moot when OPM canceled its determination and instructed the employing agency to return the appellant to its rolls); Gaston v. OPM, 34 MSPR 123, 124 (1987) (suitability appeal was moot when OPM notified the Board that it was vacating its negative determination and was further investigating the appellant's case). According to Perisho v. USPS, 69 MSPR 55, 58 (1995):

An appeal is moot where the issues it raises are no longer "live" or the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the litigation. See Occhipinti v. Dept. of Justice, 61 MSPR 504, 507 (1994), citing 6A MOORE'S FEDERAL PRACTICE ¶ 57.13. Thus, mootness occurs where the appellant has already obtained all of the relief that he could have obtained in the appeal under consideration, whether as a result of a separate appeal, see Anderson v. USPS, 64 MSPR 233, 238 (1994); or the decision on a different issue, see Rivas v. USPS, 62 MSPR 484, 486 (1994), or the agency's cancellation of the underlying action that gave rise to the appeal, see Gunuskey v. Dept. of the Army, 68 MSPR 556, 558 (1995). See also Gonzalez v. Dept. of Justice, 68 MSPR 439, 441 (1995) (an appeal is moot where the appellant has already gained the only remedy to which he is entitled); White v. International Boundary & Water Commission, 59 MSPR 62, 65 (1993) (the Board is precluded by 5 USC § 1204(h) from issuing advisory opinions).

To ascertain whether it is presented with a live controversy, the Board determines its jurisdiction by the nature of an agency's action against a particular appellant at the time an appeal is filed with the Board. An agency's unilateral modification of its action after an appeal is filed cannot divest the Board of jurisdiction, unless the appellant consents to the divestiture, or unless the agency completely rescinds the action being appealed. See Stanley v. DOJ, 58 MSPR 354, 357 (1993) (agency could not retroactively modify personnel actions to terminate the appellant during his probationary period when he was terminated shortly after he completed probation). Damages claim may preclude mootness dismissals even when the appealed action is canceled. [Refer later in this chapter to "EEO Allegation; Claim for Compensatory Damages."]

If the question is whether the Board has jurisdiction over an action that an agency contends was rescinded, e.g., a resignation challenged by the appellant as a constructive discharge, the AJ must determine if the Board has jurisdiction over the allegedly involuntary resignation before the AJ can entertain a dispute over the restoration of the status quo. See King v. USPS, 73 MSPR 362, 366 (1997). But assuming a case within the Board's jurisdiction, an appeal will not be dismissed if the agency reduces it after the appeal was filed; it is the nature of the agency action against the appellant when the Board appeal is filed that determines jurisdiction. In Himmel v. DOJ, 6 MSPR 484, 485-86, 6 MSPB 408 (1981), involving a postappeal reduction of a 30-day suspension to 14 days, Board jurisdiction was not divested by unilateral modification of the penalty:

When the appellant filed her petition for appeal, the agency's decision to suspend her was a final decision and the appellant had already served part of the suspension. There is no question that the Board had jurisdiction over her thirty-day suspension at the time she filed her petition. The question is whether the agency's subsequent action in retroactively reducing the suspension to fourteen days divested the Board of its appellate jurisdiction under 5 USC §§ 7512(2) and 7513(d), which restrict the Board's appellate jurisdiction over suspensions to those for more than fourteen days.

Under analogous circumstances, federal courts generally hold that a complaint will not be dismissed for lack of jurisdictional amount if the sum claimed by the plaintiff at the time he filed the complaint was in excess of the statutory minimum and was made in good faith. United States v. City of Chicago, 549 F.2d 415, 424 (7th Cir. 1977). One court specifically held that the defendant could not divest the court of jurisdiction after a complaint had been filed simply by transferring part of the property at issue and thereby reducing the amount in controversy between the parties to less than the statutory minimum. Gulf Refining Co. v. Price, 232 F.2d 25, 30 (5th Cir. 1956), cert. denied, 352 U.S. 981, 77 S. Ct. 380, 1 L. Ed.2d 365 (1957).

Similarly, the nature of the agency's action against a particular appellant at the time an appeal is filed with the Board is determinative of the Board's jurisdiction. The unilateral modification of the action by the agency after the appeal has been filed cannot divest the Board of jurisdiction, we find, unless the appellant consents to such divestiture or unless the agency completely rescinds the action being appealed. See, e.g., Curtis v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 4 MSPB 4, 3 MSPR 481 (1980).

The "case or controversy" must continue throughout the adjudicatory process, Thomas v. USPS, 73 MSPR 120, 125 (1997) ("To present a live case or controversy, there must be a live case or controversy when a case is decided, not merely when the complaint is filed."), and a case can be mooted by developments during the litigation. See Alleman v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 233, 239 (1998) ("Based, as well, on the agency's evidence and argument, we find that the originally-expressed concern that the agency's compliance with the reclassification decision was only temporary is no longer a reason for the continued adjudication of their appeals. They do not assert to the contrary, and the evidence shows that the issue is no longer live."); EPA v. Lotis, 70 MSPR 101, 102 (1996) (as a result of a continuing resolution and OPM's instructions, furloughs were canceled and the respondents received compensation, leaving the case moot); USDA v. Palmer, 68 MSPR 586, 589 (1995) (adjudication involving potential furloughs of administrative law judges was mooted by cessation of the budgetary threat that led to the furlough complaints).

Uhlig v. DOJ, 83 MSPR 29, 31 ¶ 7 (1999), suggested the possibility of mootness through partial relief, but did not expand on that suggestion, other than to observe that the appellant conditionally withdrew EEO claims and that the appellant's appeal would not avoid a mootness dismissal by a claim for counsel fees:

Mootness can arise at any stage of litigation, and an appeal will be dismissed as moot where the appellant has obtained all of the relief he could have obtained had he prevailed before the Board, or where there is no further relief the Board can grant. Currier v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 191, 195 (1996); Perisho v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 55, 58 (1995). The relief obtained need not be "fully satisfactory" to avoid mootness; a partial remedy is sufficient. Currier, 72 M.S.P.R. at 195.

Currier v. USPS, 72 MSPR 191, 197 (1996), cited in Uhlig, did not expand on the notion of a mootness dismissal for partial relief. Currier determined that an unresolved claim for compensatory damages for EEO claims avoids a mootness dismissal for full relief if the agency cancels an appealed action but the EEO claim remains viable, i.e., if it is not settled or withdrawn.

If the Board questions whether there is a live controversy before it, the Board may issue a show-cause order directing the appellant to demonstrate why the action should not be dismissed as moot; if there is then no response, the Board may dismiss the case as moot. See Jose v. OPM, 21 MSPR 400, 401 (1984). The agency may file a motion to dismiss the appeal as moot, and the appellant should respond to that motion within ten days from service of the motion. 5 CFR 1201.55(b) (2022). Because mootness goes to Board jurisdiction, the Board will occasionally accept argument or evidence on PFR that would otherwise be untimely or procedurally inadmissible to resolve the jurisdictional issue. Dellera v. DHUD, 65 MSPR 636, 642 (1994) (Board considered appellant's reply to agency opposition to PFR to determine whether the controversy was moot).

An appeal from a removal is not moot because an appellant does not seek reinstatement to a position, as long as the appellant seeks other remedies within the Board's power to correct. Coleman v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3018), determined that an appeal was not moot just because the employee did not seek reinstatement:

The threshold question raised by the agency is whether there is a case or controversy as required to establish jurisdiction pursuant to Article III of the United States Constitution. See GTE Sylvania, Inc. v. Consumers Union of the United States, Inc., 445 U.S. 375, 382-83 (1980). The agency urges that since petitioner no longer seeks reinstatement in the medical center at North Chicago, there is no actual controversy presented in this suit. We do not agree.

Restoration in the same work unit or location from which an employee is wrongfully terminated is not the only remedy which a petitioner may obtain through a successful appeal. If a petitioner is successful in challenging an agency's action, the Board, and consequently this Court, is authorized to order the following: that petitioner be reinstated in another work area or location of the agency; that petitioner be awarded back pay and benefits under 5 USC § 5596; and that the agency purge its files of all references to the improper action, see Marren v. Dept. of Justice, 55 MSPR 1, 2-3 (1992). Although petitioner has stated that he does not wish reinstatement in the North Chicago medical center, he has not indicated that he no longer seeks reinstatement at another work area or location of the agency. In fact, petitioner has indicated to the contrary by his May 25, 1993 application for federal employment, in which he did not specify any particular agency or geographic location. Further, petitioner has stated that he seeks to have his record cleared of "discriminatory remarks," which we take to include all record of the agency action from which he now appeals. We believe that these objectives of the petitioner are not precisely the same as those wished by the agency. See GTE Sylvania, 445 U.S. at 383. Thus, the requirements of Article III have been satisfied.

The Board seems to discourage dismissal of an appeal as moot when dismissal is based upon interpretation of evidence on contested issues. See McLaury v. Dept. of Interior, 92 MSPR 18, 21 ¶ 9 (2002). The Board need not reach mootness if the Board lacks jurisdiction over the action appealed. Tunik v. SSA, 93 MSPR 482, 493 ¶ 33 (2003); see King v. USPS, supra.
