E. TRAINING AS CONDITION TO WORK EVALUATION

The union could not require negotiations on a proposal that required training in duties not described in position descriptions as a precondition to appraisal or disciplinary action based on performance of those duties. The authority to determine the duties and functions to be included in appraisals is part of management's right to direct work. The proposal interfered with that right by requiring that management provide training before it could rate certain duties. AFGE Local 3004 and Dept. of Air Force, Otis AFB, 9 FLRA 723, 724 (1982). Nonnegotiable as an arrangement was Proposal 1 in NFFE Local 858 and USDA, Fed. Crop Ins. Corp., 48 FLRA 552, 553, 556-57 (1993), that "[i]f an employee does not have sufficient training or experience on an element for the supervisor to objectively rate the employee's performance, the employee will not be rated on that element for that appraisal cycle." The provision was too broad:

Proposal 1 is intended to ameliorate the adverse effects on employees' performance appraisals insofar as those appraisals concern elements for which employees do not have sufficient training or experience for supervisors to objectively rate their performance. The proposal protects employees who do not have sufficient training or experience in elements of their positions from receiving adverse evaluations on those elements by precluding the Agency from evaluating employees on those elements. Performance appraisals are the basis for rewarding, reassigning, promoting, reducing in grade, retraining and removing employees. Performance appraisals also are one of the factors used in determining employees' retention standing for RIF purposes and in selecting employees for promotion and other career enhancement opportunities. Thus, the ratings that employees receive in their performance appraisals have significant consequences for their employment relationships....

Under the proposal, the Agency would be prohibited from evaluating employees for the entire rating cycle on elements for which they do not have sufficient training or experience to be rated objectively. The disputed portion of the proposal precludes management from appraising employees for the entire rating cycle on a particular element unless those conditions are met, without regard to whether defects in performance were attributable to lack of training or experience.

Based on the above, we find that the burden imposed by the proposal on the exercise of management's rights to direct employees and to assign work through the evaluation of employee performance outweighs the benefits afforded to employees under the proposal. We find, therefore, that the proposal excessively interferes with management's rights, under section 7106(a)(2)(A) and (B) of the Statute, to direct employees and to assign work....

A negotiable proposal stipulated that when an employee is reassigned or transferred he will be given no less than 90 days to train so as to satisfactorily perform the duties of the new position. Essentially the proposal precluded evaluation during the training period, delaying, but not preventing, evaluation. The proposal did not mandate that the agency provide any training or prescribe any training that the agency might offer.
