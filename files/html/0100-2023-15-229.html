b. Perjury

Perjury is a particular form of false statement: it involves a false testimony under oath (or the equivalent) as to a material matter. Not any false statement made under oath qualifies. The false statement must materially relate to the main focus of the matter under inquiry. Silva v. USPS, 59 MSPR 268, 271-72 (1993), distinguished perjury from a false statement (made during the course of the appellant's MSPB appeal):

The offense of perjury is generally considered to be the knowing and willful giving of false testimony under oath relating to a material matter. See 70 C.J.S. Perjury § 2 (1987); 18 USC 1621. The allegation of perjury was based on conflicting statements that the appellant made in the course of his appeal of the October 1, 1991 removal action. In an affidavit in support of his appeal of that removal, the appellant stated that he "continue[s] in this effort [to confront and treat (his) alcoholism] to the present with meeting twice a week at the GMF [General Mail Facility]." At the hearing in that appeal, the appellant admitted that he had not attended the EAP meetings regularly. The administrative judge found that the statement in the appellant's affidavit did not meet the elements of perjury because it was not material to the issues decided in the appeal of the October 1, 1991 removal. See U.S. v. Jackson, 640 F.2d 614, 617 (8th Cir.), cert. denied, 454 U.S. 1057 (1981). The agency had not charged the appellant with failing to attend the EAP meetings in that proceeding. The administrative judge made no determination of the truthfulness of either statement.

We see no error in the administrative judge's finding. The statement in the October 24 affidavit may have been false, but it was not material to the matter under adjudication in the appeal of the October 1, 1991 removal, i.e., whether the appellant breached the settlement agreement by his absences.

...

The agency based its perjury charge on a statement by the appellant that the agency had reason to believe was false. Not only did the appellant contradict his earlier statement by his testimony, but attendance records from the EAP program show that the appellant did not attend the twice weekly meetings. As discussed above, the administrative judge did not sustain the perjury charge because she found that the statement was not material, a question of law for the administrative judge to decide. See U.S. v. Raineri, 670 F.2d 702, 718 (7th Cir.), cert. denied, 459 U.S. 1035 (1982). We find that an agency error in this regard does not rise to the level of bringing a charge in bad faith.

That an adjudicator fails to credit an individual's testimony does not establish that individual as a perjurer. See St. Mary's Honor Ctr. v. Hicks, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 2754 (1993) ("But initially we must point out that there is no justification for assuming (as the dissent repeatedly does) that those employers whose evidence is disbelieved are perjurers and liars...."). Prehoda v. DHS, 98 MSPR 418, 425 n.5 (2005), made the same point:

An administrative judge's finding of lack of credibility is not tantamount to a finding of "dishonest conduct" within the meaning of 5 C.F.R. § 731.202(b). "Credibility" is defined as "[t]he quality that makes something (as a witness or some evidence) worthy of belief." Black's Law Dictionary 374 (7th ed. 1999). "It is not exclusively synonymous with veracity, but it encompasses conviction, a lack of uncertainty, and has such weight as to the truth of the fact to leave little room for doubt." Barron's Law Dictionary 111 (3d ed. 1991) (citations omitted). "Dishonest," on the other hand, means "characterized by lack of truth, honesty, or trustworthiness: unfair, deceptive." Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary 332 (10th ed. 2002). The administrative judge in question did not find that the appellant's testimony was perjurious. Rather, he found that, in certain respects, the appellant's testimony lacked credibility in light of other evidence....

"[T]he difference between deliberate lies and honest mistakes is always an issue when perjury is possible." Delgado v. DOJ, 966 F.3d 556, 562 (7th Cir. 2020) (amended, without change to quoted portion, on petition for rehearing, Oct. 29, 2020). Concerning the related charge of giving false testimony under oath, the burden of proving that an appellant's statements are false rests with the agency. Gatling v. NTSB, 17 MSPR 43, 49 (1983).

Of the permissible bounds for lawyers who develop testimony through witnesses and witness preparation, Fields v. DOJ, 452 F.3d 1297, 1304 (Fed. Cir. 2006), noted:

...It is proper and appropriate for an attorney to point out to, and explore with, a witness, any inconsistencies between the witness' proposed testimony and that of others, and to point out other facts that may impact the witness' testimony. That, however, is quite different from an attorney suggesting to a witness to lie or implying that the witness should do so. Here the statements by Pantos upon which Fields relies involved only permissible attorney conduct. We do not read the statements attributed to Pantos as suggesting or implying that she was urging Fields to give false testimony. As the administrative judge correctly recognized, by questioning Fields's statements in view of conflicting statements from other agency witnesses, Pantos "was simply doing her job." She did not "ask him to lie, commit a crime, or violate any law, rule or regulation."

Not rising to the level of perjury, a false police report concerning a supervisor's conduct warranted adverse action in Duckenfield v. VA, DC-0752-12-0677-I-2 (NP 7/17/2014).
