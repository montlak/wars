5. Pregnancy

Wade v. Dept. of Navy, 45 MSPR 595, 599-600 (1990), analyzed a pregnancy discrimination allegation:

...The Pregnancy Discrimination Act, Pub. L. No. 95-555...(codified at 42 USC 2000-e(k)), expands the definition of unlawful sex discrimination to include discrimination "because of or on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions." Thus, the appellant's contention is properly treated as a claim of sex discrimination under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The appellant bears the burden of proof on the claim of sex discrimination....

...

[The Appellant] asserts that she was suffering from post-pregnancy complications which prevented her from returning to work, and that she was removed from her position. She has produced no evidence to suggest that non-pregnant individuals have been treated differently when they have been absent for a lengthy time under similar circumstances. The law requires only that "women affected by pregnancy...be treated the same for all employment-related purposes...as other persons not so affected but similar in their ability or inability to work." 42 USC 2000e(k). See also 1978 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 4753 (the law was intended to prevent employers from treating pregnancy, child birth, and related medical conditions differently than other disabilities are treated).

The fact that the agency removed the appellant during an extended pregnancy-related absence, but did not remove her during her previous absences does not show unlawful discrimination because this action was simply the culmination of a process begun earlier on the basis of unrelated absences. The agency issued a notice of proposed removal in February, 1987, but held that action in abeyance based on a work attendance agreement. The agency had also issued two letters of requirement regarding the appellant's use of leave in February 1988, and February 1989. Moreover, with respect to her last absence, the agency exercised great patience in waiting ten months from the time the appellant went on maternity leave in October 1988 before acting to remove her, despite the demonstrated need to fill her position.

The appellant's allegation that the collective bargaining agreement violates the Pregnancy Discrimination Act because it treats requests for advanced sick leave due to pregnancy and pregnancy-related conditions differently from requests for advanced sick leave for other conditions, is not relevant to the appellant's case. Even if the appellant had been advanced the maximum amount of sick leave, it would not affect the disposition of her appeal. The appellant was removed for being unavailable for duty, and whether she was on sick leave or LWOP is not material; what is material is that she was on leave rather than on duty.

Lofton v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-12-0582-I-1 (NP 1/24/2014), added as to pregnancy and disability discrimination:

Pregnancy, absent unusual circumstances, is not a physical impairment. 29 C.F.R. Part 1630, Appendix § 1630.2(h) ("[C]onditions, such as pregnancy, that are not the result of a physiological disorder are also not impairments."). Further, an individual with pregnancy-related complications that qualify as a physical impairment must also demonstrate that she is substantially limited in a major life activity. See, e.g., Mayorga v. Alorica, Inc., No. 12-21578-CIV, 2012 WL 3043021, at *4-7 (S.D. Fla. July 25, 2012) (discussing how pregnancy-related complications may qualify as a disability under the ADAAA); Wanamaker v. Westport Board of Education, 899 F. Supp.2d 193, 210-12 (D. Conn. 2012) (granting the plaintiff leave to amend her complaint in order to allege how her pregnancy-related complication substantially limited a major life activity)....
