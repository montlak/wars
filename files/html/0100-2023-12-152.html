broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section F: Review of New Issues or Issues Not Raised With Judge, Subsection 02: Determining if Issue or Argument Was Raised Below, Part e: Discrimination
e. Discrimination

The Board shows little inclination to accept allegations of discrimination raised for the first time in a PFR. In Siegelman v. DHUD, 14 MSPR 326, 332 (1983), the Board declined to consider an allegation of disability discrimination in a performance-based downgrading. In Siegelman, the appellant did not assert that she lacked knowledge of the discrimination as she pursued the case in the regional office. See Brzozowski v. USPS, 51 MSPR 196, 199 (1991) (precluding consideration of a disability discrimination allegation raised for the first time on PFR); Cortright v. Dept. of Transp., 37 MSPR 565 (1988) (concerning the newly-raised issue of sexual harassment). Burge v. Dept. of Air Force, 82 MSPR 75, 91 ¶ 31 (1999), stated the Board's position:

With respect to his claim of sex discrimination[,] the appellant did not timely raise it below and it was not included in any of the prehearing conference summaries in the list of issues to be adjudicated. See Wilson v. Department of Justice, 58 MSPR 96, 101 n.4 (1993); Dean v. Department of the Army, 57 MSPR 296, 300-01 (1993). Further, the appellant has not shown that his sex discrimination claim is based on new and material evidence that, despite his exercise of due diligence, was unavailable when the record closed below, or that he was unaware of the basis for this claim at the time he filed his petition for appeal. Therefore, this claim is not entitled to consideration on review. See Marr v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 196, 201 (1991); Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPR 268, 271 (1980); see also Wilson, 58 MSPR at 101 n.4; Dean, 57 MSPR at 300-01.

See Harrup v. DOD, DC-0752-13-5718-I-1 (NP 6/8/2015) ("Finally, we find that the appellant failed to raise an affirmative defense of disability discrimination in the proceedings below, and we find that she cannot raise such a claim for the first time on review. See Ronso v. Department of the Navy, 122 M.S.P.R. 391, ¶ 3 n.1 (2015) (the Board will not consider a new claim raised for the first time on review absent a showing it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite the party's due diligence). The record reflects that the appellant did not raise her disability discrimination allegations in her initial appeal, and that she did not do so in response to the administrative judge's affirmative defenses order. Although the appellant has submitted new evidence on review concerning her challenge to the scheduling of her furlough days in connection with her disability discrimination claim, we find that this information previously was available to her and it does not justify accepting a new claim of disability discrimination for the first time on review."); Vazquez v. USPS, 114 MSPR 264, 267 n.3, 2010 MSPB 125 (2010) ("We also decline to consider the allegations of disability discrimination that the appellant raises for the first time on review. The appellant's sole submission below contains no language that could be construed as raising a disability discrimination claim, and the appellant has not explained why he failed to raise the issue below. We cannot, therefore, find that the appellant's claim of disability discrimination was timely raised. See Abatecola v. Veterans Administration, 29 M.S.P.R. 601, 608, aff'd, 802 F.2d 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table); Anderson v. Veterans Administration, 3 M.S.P.R. 71, 74 (1980) (the Board will not consider an appellant's discrimination allegations made for the first time on review absent a showing that he was previously unaware of the basis for making them)."); Hart v. VA, PH-0752-10-0134-I-1 (NP 10/14/2010) ("With regard to the appellant's assertion on review that the Board should find discrimination on the basis of race, there is no evidence in the record that the appellant ever raised a claim of race discrimination below. Because such a claim was not raised below, the Board need not address it on review. Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980).").

For a time the Board was more amenable to considering allegations of discrimination than other allegations of error initially raised in a PFR. See Mitchell v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 364, 365, 6 MSPB 310 (1981). The Board reopened an appeal on a PFR involving an issue of discrimination raised for the first time "[b]ecause of the seriousness of this claim," but found no evidence of discrimination and affirmed the AJ's decision sustaining the removal. Sorrells v. USPS, 6 MSPR 29, 30, 6 MSPB 38 (1981). Waiver considerations were applied. At times the Board did not accept EEO allegations first presented in a PFR. The Board rejected a discrimination charge raised in a PFR as untimely when, during the adverse action hearing, the appellant said that he did not wish to raise an allegation of discrimination in his appeal. Chandler v. DHHS, 8 MSPR 30, 32 n.3, 7 MSPB 528 (1981) (distinguishing Zidele v. DLA, 6 MSPR 455 (1981), because Mr. Chandler was made aware of his right to make the EEO allegation at his hearing and affirmatively declined to do so). The Board also declined to adjudicate under Title VII when "[a]lthough appellant has obliquely referred to possible racial discrimination throughout this appeal, he has never alleged that the agency action was the result of discrimination and the record reveals no reason to suspect that possibility." Wade v. VA, 8 MSPR 414, 415 (1981); see Wood v. Dept. of Army, 8 MSPR 420, 422 (1981) ("Had appellant alleged below some facts which could have placed the presiding official on notice that a claim was being made or implied that he was being discriminated against based on his handicapping condition, the Board would now be presented with a basis for reviewing the presiding official's initial decision sustaining the agency action.").

Although the Board was occasionally inclined to reopen initial decisions if an allegation of discrimination was raised for the first time in a PFR, the Board applied its usual standard of review to new evidence offered with the PFR concerning the discrimination allegation if that allegation was raised before the AJ. Pender v. USPS, 7 MSPR 403, 405 n.2, 7 MSPB 278 (1981). Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 310, 312-13 (1983), declined to entertain new evidence of discrimination submitted for the first time in a PFR. All evidence offered by the employee was available before the record was closed. Brunton v. USPS, 114 MSPR 365, 372 n.5, 2010 MSPB 139 (2010), permitted an EEO issue first raised by a pro se appellant in a PFR to be heard by an AJ on remand on other issues, to avoid what the Board characterized as a result that would otherwise be "unduly harsh." See McCardle v. EEOC, SF-0752-15-0230-I-1 (NP 1/6/2023) ("Here, however, we are not persuaded that Mitchell warrants a different outcome. There is no mention that the appellant in Mitchell was represented by counsel before the administrative judge. By contrast, the appellant was represented by counsel below, and he is responsible for the errors of his chosen representative. Sofio v. Internal Revenue Service, 7 M.S.P.R. 667, 670 (1981).").

Earlier EEOC regulations provided that an employee who did not raise a discrimination issue in the Board appeal, but who did pursue the issue at the agency level, should be told that he may petition the Board to review the previous Board decision once the agency EEO determination was made. 29 CFR 1613.404, 1613.405(b) (1990). In Compton v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 533, 534 (1984), the Board issued its final decision on July 6, 1982. On June 29, 1983, the appellant petitioned for review of the July 1982 decision based on an EEO determination resulting from agency procedures. The Board decided that it has no jurisdiction over an issue of discrimination raised in the abstract. The agency final decision was not reviewable by the Board, but it was reviewable by EEOC under 29 CFR 1613.414 [now at 29 CFR 1614.303 (2022)]. The Board cited Spears v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 535, 537 (1982), determining that the EEOC regulation (then 29 CFR 1613.404) added a new procedure to the already complex and difficult area of mixed case law. The Board, it decided, did not have jurisdiction to decide discrimination issues not raised in the course of proceedings before it; the Board has jurisdiction over discrimination claims only if they are alleged in an appeal of an action within the Board's jurisdiction. The result in Spears was affirmed in Spears v. MSPB, 766 F.2d 520 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The referral procedures at Part 1613 of the EEOC regulations were not carried forward in the Commission's 1993 regulations codified at 29 CFR Part 1614. [Refer to Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases," concerning mixed case jurisdiction.]

If an appellant appeals to EEOC from an MSPB final decision on a PFR, it appears that EEOC may consider (and resolve in favor of the appellant), issues of discrimination not specifically addressed to the Board through the PFR. Combs v. USPS, 15 MSPR 340, 341 (1983).
