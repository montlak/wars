b. Medical Certificate

Removal was reversed for an employee who signed his physician's name to an accurate but unsigned medical certificate providing the basis for the illness that caused his absence. The employee had 13 years of service and no prior disciplinary record. Black v. USPS, 5 MSPR 120, 120-21, 5 MSPB 186 (1981). An opposite result was reached for a guard who falsified a doctor's certificate. Mitigation was inappropriate because of the need for a high degree of trust in the guard. McGilberry v. DMA, 18 MSPR 560, 564-65 (1984). [Refer to other cases discussed in earlier in this chapter, "Justification for Leave; Medical Certification Requirement; Merger of Charges."] The Federal Circuit noted of a removal based on forged medical certifications, Kleyman v. GSA (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3109):

...Falsification of documents can support a removal action, even for a first-time violator with an otherwise unblemished record. See e.g, Beverly v. United States Postal Serv., 907 F.2d 136, 138 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (noting that "removal is well within the recommended range of penalties for a first offense of falsifying documents."). The fact that the agency's Penalty Guide allows for varying levels of discipline in this circumstance, ranging from "reprimand to removal," does not mean that the agency cannot choose in its discretion a harsh but appropriate penalty. Furthermore, that Kleyman's conduct did not rise to the level of accepting a bribe or falsifying a bid does not mean that her forgery was not a serious infraction warranting removal. An agency's choice of penalty is subject to great respect and is rarely reversed. See Schapansky v. Department of Transp., 735 F.2d 477, 484 (Fed. Cir. 1984) ("Determination of the appropriate penalty is a matter committed primarily and largely to the discretion of the employing agency. Only in the exceptional case, in which the penalty exceeds that permitted by statute or regulations or is so harsh that it amounts to an abuse of discretion, may its imposition be overturned.") (citations omitted).

The fact that the agency did not report her crime to the Office of the Inspector General is irrelevant and, in any event, unhelpful to Kleyman, as it could only have made matters worse for her by subjecting her to possible criminal sanctions....
