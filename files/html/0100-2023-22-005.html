a. Civil Rights Statutes

Arnold v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 17, 23-25 ¶¶ 18-21 (2003), stated the fee criteria applied to appeals involving successful EEO defenses:

The statute at 5 U.S.C. § 7701 describes the procedures that must be followed by the Board in actions appealable to it under any law, rule or regulation. Subsection (g)(2) of section 7701 sets forth the standard for awarding attorney fees where the appellant is the prevailing party in a Board appeal and the Board's decision "is based on a finding of discrimination prohibited under section 2302(b)(1) of [Title 5]." In such a case, "the payment of attorney fees shall be in accordance with the standards prescribed under section 706(k) of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(k))." Section 2000e-5(k) of Title 42 gives federal district courts the discretion to award reasonable attorney fees to prevailing parties in Title VII discrimination cases where there has been a finding of discrimination. See Santella v. Special Counsel, 90 M.S.P.R. 172 ¶ 10 (2001) ("Title VII's attorney fees provision permits a court, in its discretion, to award attorney fees to the prevailing party, either the plaintiff or the defendant" (emphasis added)).

Thus, under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(2), Congress has provided the Board with the same discretion accorded federal courts to award or not award attorney fees to a prevailing party where a Board decision is based on a finding of discrimination. That is to say, the civil-service statute does not make an award of attorney fees automatic because a party prevailed on a claim of discrimination; it only makes the party eligible for an award of fees. By incorporating the discretionary standard of 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(k) into 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(2), Congress expects the Board, as do federal courts, to examine the facts of each case before deciding whether fees are reasonable under the circumstances, that is, whether the appellant is entitled to any fees. As the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, our reviewing court, said in Sterner v. Department of the Army, 711 F.2d 1563 (Fed. Cir. 1983), "if a party in fact substantially prevails in his action, then he is eligible for an award of attorney's fees. Eligibility, however, does not mean entitlement." Id. at 1567 n.15 (emphasis in the original; citation omitted); accord Reinbold v. Evers, 187 F.3d 348, 362 (4th Cir. 1999) (if a determination is made that a party has substantially prevailed so as to be eligible for attorney fees, a court must still go on and determine, based on the appropriate factors, whether he is entitled to an award of fees).

Our application of section 7701(g)(2) to this appeal is also fully consistent with the decision of the Supreme Court of the United States in Farrar v. Hobby, 506 U.S. 103, 113 S. Ct. 566, 121 L. Ed.2d 494 (1992). In that case, the Court held that if a party is found to have prevailed, "the degree of [his] overall success goes to the reasonableness" of a fee award. Id. at 114, 113 S. Ct. at 574, quoting Texas State Teachers Assn. v. Garland Independent School District, 489 U.S. 782, 793, 109 S. Ct. 1486, 1494, 103 L. Ed.2d 866 (1989). The Board has followed that rule with respect to fee awards under both 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(2), which is implicated here, and 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(1). See Kelly v. Department of the Navy, 43 M.S.P.R. 430, 436 n.6 (1990) (the computation of a reasonable attorney fee is the same regardless of whether the award is authorized by 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(1) or (g)(2)); see also Garcia v. U.S. Postal Service, 83 M.S.P.R. 458 ¶ 7 (1999) (once an appellant meets the threshold requirement of being a prevailing party, the Board must determine reasonable attorney fees). Reasonable attorney fees can be nominal or none at all depending on the circumstances. Farrar, 506 U.S. at 115-16, 113 S. Ct. 566 (although the appellant prevailed in his claim, the only reasonable fee recovery was "no fee at all," in view of the minimal success he achieved); Garcia, 83 M.S.P.R. 458 ¶ 7 (depending on the circumstances, a fee award "may properly result in the award of low fees or no fees at all," citing Farrar, 506 U.S. at 114-16, 113 S. Ct. 566).

Thus, not every prevailing appellant in a discrimination case will be entitled to attorney fees. Consequently, although the issue of whether the appellant was a "prevailing party" has been resolved by EEOC under discrimination law, the issue of whether he is entitled to fees under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(2) and the discretionary standard of 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(k) is one that is subject to a factual inquiry under civil-service law. By means of 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(2), Congress entrusted that factual inquiry to the Board's sole discretion independent of EEOC discrimination law. We therefore exercise our discretion under civil-service law to determine whether the attorney fees claimed by the appellant are reasonable.

Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 17, 27-28 ¶¶ 30-33 (2003) [discussed in Chapter 17, "Statutory Basis for Review" and "Attorney Fees Award, Application"], also determined that the Federal Circuit is the exclusive source of review of a Board decision involving a fee award claimed under the civil rights statutes.
