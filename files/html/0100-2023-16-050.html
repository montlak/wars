a. Law Before Guillebeau

Before Guillebeau, under Callaway v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 592, (1984), supra, the Board set aside removal of a receptionist whose performance standard stated that one incident of rudeness constituted unsatisfactory performance. The Board noted that 5 USC 7503(a) discouraged an absolute standard for discourteous conduct; the statute provided for only a suspension of 14 days or less for four instances of such conduct within one year. The removal was reversed because no valid performance standard remained to measure the alleged errors of the appellant. The Board rejected the argument that, lacking a proper agency standard, it was the Board's function to establish a standard for the position.

The use of absolute standards and the application of Callaway were discussed in Komara v. VA, 28 MSPR 239, 242 (1985), involving demotion of a medical technician whose standard called for performance of patient tests within established time frames 100% of the time during the year. Time limits on some tests were established for administrative convenience, not because death or injury could result from untimely processing. The Board said of the agency's objections to an AJ's conclusion that the standard violated Callaway:

While finding that this particular absolute performance standard is improper, we reaffirm our holding in Callaway, supra, that an agency may properly use absolute standards in measuring the performance of employees whose unacceptable performance could result in death or injury. In doing so, however, the agency must tailor the standard to apply only to situations where a single failure to meet the standard could result in death or injury, not merely to encompass such situations. Here, although agency personnel testified that the untimely processing of some tests could harm patients, the agency did not establish this danger with respect to all types of STAT tests. Therefore, we find that the agency abused its discretion in establishing an absolute performance standard which applied to all tests.[3]

[3] Cf. James v. Veterans Administration, 26 MSPR 365 (1985) (a performance standard requiring a Medical Technologist to perform all blood grouping, typing and crossmatching with 100% accuracy is valid because unrebutted testimony reflected that incompatible blood injected into a patient may cause death).

The agency also contends that the presiding official erred in not considering that some tests appellant failed to timely perform could have caused death or injury. The presiding official, however, properly refused to consider appellant's actual performance absent a valid performance standard.[4] ...

[4] As stated in Callaway, the Board will not establish the standard for performance in order to evaluate the charged performance deficiencies because such an action would exceed the scope of the Board's authority.

The Board reiterated in Smith v. VA, 59 MSPR 340, 348-49 (1993), involving action against a pharmacist, the distinction drawn in Komara between standards describing work that, if faulty, could result in harm, and other failings:

As to whether standard D.2 of the critical element "Clinical" is absolute, we have defined an absolute performance standard as one in which a single incident of poor performance could result in an unsatisfactory rating on one job element. See Sullivan, 44 MSPR at 651-52; Russi v. Department of the Army, 40 MSPR 585, 589 (1989).

Standard D.2 of the critical element "Clinical" provides that, to obtain a fully successful rating, an employee must perform as follows:

Upon receipt of a medication order (inpatient) or prescription (outpatient), always reviews and interprets physicians [sic] order to ensure the appropriate dosage and directions for administration have been specified to provide therapeutic efficacy without toxicity due to overdose or drug interaction. Always contacts physician when there is an incompatibility or improper dose, dosage form, route of administration or any other irregularity in physician order. Reviews patient profile to ensure rational drug usage. Shows no hesitancy in contacting physicians.

The standard indicates that a single incident of failure to review and interpret a physician's order as provided, or to contact a physician for clarification of an irregularity in an order, would result in an unsatisfactory rating for that job element. See id. Therefore, we find that the standard is an absolute standard.

Nevertheless, an absolute performance standard may be valid where a single failure to meet the standard could result in death, injury, breach of security, or great monetary loss. See, e.g., Callaway v. Department of the Army, 23 MSPR 592, 599 (1984). Here, the agency contends in its petition for review that "[I]n no instance would there not be some type of injury or adverse reaction if Appellant/pharmacist" failed to properly "review and interpret physician orders" or to contact a physician concerning irregularity in a physician's order.

In establishing an absolute performance standard, "the agency must tailor the standard to apply only to situations where a single failure to meet the standard could result in death or injury, not merely to encompass such situations." Komara v. Veterans Administration, 28 MSPR 239, 242 (1985). Even assuming in this appeal that the agency has shown that the appellant's failure to properly review and interpret physicians' orders could result in injury, it has not shown that the appellant's failure to always contact physicians concerning irregularity in their orders could likewise cause injury. See id. As the appellant has explained, for example, and the agency has not disputed, a mere irregularity in a physician's prescribed medication dosage, while unusual, may be "well within clinical parameters." Therefore, we find that standard D.2 of the critical element entitled "Clinical" is an invalid absolute standard. See Komara, 28 MSPR at 242.

In a mixed case in district court, Cohen v. Austin, 861 F. Supp. 340, 347-49 (E.D. Pa. 1994), concluded that performance standards were unreasonable because they were absolute:

Plaintiff also argues that the performance standards are invalid because they are impermissibly absolute. An absolute performance standard is one that allows for a rating of unsatisfactory performance based on a single incident of poor performance. Smith v. Dept. of Veteran Affairs, 59 MSPR 340, 348 (1993). While absolute performance standards are allowed in cases where a single failure to meet the standard could result in breach of security, injury, death or great monetary loss, absent such circumstances, an absolute performance standard is invalid, and the establishment of such a standard constitutes an abuse of discretion. Id.; Callaway v. Dept. of the Army, 23 MSPR 592, 598 (1984).

In Smith, the court held that the performance standards imposed on a pharmacist were impermissibly absolute. The standards stated that a pharmacist: "Upon receipt of a medication order...or prescription..., always reviews and interprets physicians [sic] order to ensure the appropriate dosage and directions... Always contacts physician when there is an incompatibility or improper dose... Shows no hesitancy in contacting physicians." Id. at 348. The court reasoned that the standards were absolute because a failure to review and interpret a physician's order as provided, or to contact a physician for clarification on a single incident would result in an unsatisfactory rating under the performance standards. Id.

Similarly, in Sullivan v. Dept. of the Navy, 44 MSPR 646, 651-52 (1990), aff'd, 949 F.2d 403 (Fed. Cir. 1991), the court found the standard that "all recurring reports are [to be] accurately prepared and submitted/received by the required dates" to be absolute. The court noted that there was no evidence that failure to meet the standard would cause death, injury, breach of security or great monetary loss to justify an absolute standard. While the court also acknowledged that an absolute standard is not fatally invalid if the position description, written instructions and agency clarify the standard so that the employee knows the standard is not applied in an absolute manner, the court held there was no evidence that the employee was aware of this in Sullivan.

In the present case, the performance standards imposed on plaintiff are absolute. First, the marginally acceptable performance standard, as stated above, requires plaintiff to guarantee award of the solicitation on the target date. Second, the marginally acceptable standard allows for "no mistakes that could result in a solicitation/award being nullified upon protest." Thus, under the marginally acceptable standard, if plaintiff failed to award the solicitation by the targeted award date, or if the solicitation contained errors, plaintiff's performance would be rated as unacceptable. Even under the standards for expected performance, there could not be any major solicitation defects which would render the solicitation package unacceptable. Given that a failure to meet these standards would result in a rating of unacceptable performance similar to the standards in Sullivan, the standards are absolute.

While the MSPB rejected plaintiff's argument that the standards were absolute, the MSPB made this determination by reasoning that plaintiff was fired for committing more than just one error. In so doing, the MSPB considered plaintiff's performance under the standards. Rather than looking at the standards in the abstract, the MSPB reasoned that when plaintiff failed to work on a certain solicitation package assigned by defendant, plaintiff committed several errors under the marginally acceptable performance standards, such as failing to have the solicitation in for review before 105 days, failing to guarantee award on the target date, failing to keep the solicitation open at least 30 days and failing to prevent mistakes that could have resulted in the award being nullified upon protest. Cohen v. General Serv. Admin., 53 MSPR 492, 501 (1992).

However, the MSPB's analysis is faulty because it does not address whether the standards are absolute. Determining whether a standard is absolute and invalid is separate from determining whether plaintiff's conduct as measured by the performance standard is unacceptable. Callaway, 23 MSPR at 600. While plaintiff may have committed several errors under the standards, the number of errors cited by defendants is irrelevant to the question of whether the standards are absolute. If the standards were not absolute, they would have provided some guidelines to plaintiff as to how many mistakes could be made before his performance was unacceptable, such as stating how many solicitations could be submitted late. Rather, under the standards imposed, plaintiff's one failure in any of the tasks resulted in unacceptable performance.

The Board would not construe as permissibly absolute an otherwise poorly written standard, Ortiz v. DOJ, 46 MSPR 692, 696 (1991):

[W]e find that the agency's argument that the administrative judge's issuance of a ruling on the validity of a standard without first giving it the opportunity to argue the merits of an "absolute standard" is without merit. An invalid standard cannot be rehabilitated by calling it an absolute standard. The standard would have to be rewritten to be a valid standard before it could be considered to be a valid absolute standard. Moreover, the agency did not communicate to the appellant that the standard was absolute.

For a close analogue to an absolute standard--a requirement that the work products of an appellant be produced in error-free final form--refer to Thompson v. Farm Credit Admin., 51 MSPR 569, 578 (1991).
