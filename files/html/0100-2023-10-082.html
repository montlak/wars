d. Preserving Review Right Through Objection

Objections will inevitably be raised to various procedures and evidence, and motions will be made orally (or in writing) during the hearing. Whenever an objection or a motion is made, the opponent should be given the chance to respond. Unless he does not have the authority to rule on a motion, the AJ should promptly rule on the motion or objection. 5 CFR 1201.55 (2022); 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(4), (8) (2022).

An objection can play a significant part in the development of a record if review is later requested, for "[A]n appellant cannot wait until after adjudication is complete to object for the first time to an administrative judge's hearing-related rulings." Langford v. Dept. of Treasury, 73 MSPR 129, 138 (1997); see Van Wagenen v. DHS, SF-0432-13-0193-I-1 (NP 2/11/2015) ("The appellant, however, did not object at the hearing to the testimony of H.G., the agency manager who supervised her after the PIP ended, or to the testimony of her other PIP supervisors. She cannot do so now. See Hill v. Department of Health & Human Services, 28 M.S.P.R. 91, 92-93 (1985) (an employee's failure to object at hearing to introduction of allegedly irrelevant evidence precluded her from doing so on review), aff'd, 795 F.2d 1011 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table)."); Miller v. DOD, 85 MSPR 310, 313 ¶ 8 (2000) (Board would not review a limitation on the questioning of a witness during a hearing unless an objection was entered to the AJ's ruling). Consider the appellant who sought testimony from his supervisor during the hearing concerning examples of the appellant's allegedly poor performance. The AJ suggested that the parties wait to develop evidence concerning specific deficiencies from other witnesses. The appellant's representative agreed to wait and, in so doing, was deemed to have acquiesced in the limitation of the cross-examination of the supervisor. Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 37 MSPR 78, 83 (1988). Whitehurst v. TVA, 43 MSPR 486, 491 (1990), noted as to the appellant's failure to object to the AJ's exclusion of hearing evidence on the agency's objection:

The appellant contends that the administrative judge erred at the hearing by excluding two documents that he proffered in support of his arguments. The first document proffered by the appellant consisted of a portion of the agency's regulations pertaining to transfer of function, in support of his argument on that issue. The second document was a letter issued by the agency's management, dated April 29, 1988, prior to the reorganization and the RIF, that directed a freeze on appointments and filling vacancies. The administrative judge sustained the agency's objections to these proffered documents because they were not shown to be relevant to the appeal.

Although the appellant now contends that the administrative judge's exclusion of the documents was error, he failed to object on the record below to the administrative judge's evidentiary rulings. The appellant was obligated to preserve for the Board's review his objection to the administrative judge's conduct of the hearing. He cannot wait until after the adjudication is complete to object for the first time to the administrative judge's hearing related rulings. See, e.g., Twine v. Department of Health and Human Services, 36 MSPR 388, 393 (1988); White v. Department of the Army, 32 MSPR 600, 602 (1987); Hill v. Department of Health and Human Service, 28 MSPR 91, 92-93 (1985).

The Whitehurst approach suggests that if one party objects to the entry of evidence and if, after consideration of the parties' position on the objection, the AJ sustains the objection, the other party is then to object to the ruling on the objection. One would think the second objection would be superfluous. Once the original objection is argued and ruled upon, the point should be adequately preserved for review. However, the Board does not always readily determine that the appellant acquiesced in a ruling that cuts off his right to develop the record during a hearing. Although the appellant's representative acquiesced when an AJ cut off questioning on an EEO issue, the representative reiterated the appellant's discrimination claim to the AJ, essentially preserving his objection to the AJ's limitation of the questioning. See Evono v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 541, 548 (1996). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Conduct of Questioning" and "Control of Questioning."]
