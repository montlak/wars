broida flra Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 06: Mootness; Changed Circumstances, Section B: Withdrawal of Nonnegotiability Allegation

B. WITHDRAWAL OF NONNEGOTIABILITY ALLEGATION

When an agency withdraws its allegation that a proposal is not negotiable, the negotiability appeal becomes moot and will be dismissed. AFGE Local 2840 and Blytheville AFB, 2 FLRA 915, 915 (1980); see AFGE Local 997 and Maxwell AFB, 9 FLRA 39, 39 (1982) ("Since the agency has withdrawn the allegation of nonnegotiability, there is no longer an issue as to whether the provisions in question in this case are within the parties' duty to bargain under the Statute. The dispute involved in the union's appeal therefore has been rendered moot."). FLRA's general practice is to recognize the withdrawal of the allegation and not address the issue further. FLRA does not issue any order concerning that particular proposal. Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 709 (1991). A negotiability petition is moot and will be dismissed if the agency disclaims that there is any dispute that the union proposal is within the duty to bargain. ACT and State of Georgia, 3 FLRA 686, 686 (1980). FLRA will find the parties in agreement on negotiability and implicitly find a negotiability dispute moot if, in the course of the parties' exchange of position statements, it becomes clear that the provision is bargainable upon conditions stated and agreed to by the parties. See AFGE Local 1513 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Air Station, Whidbey Island, 41 FLRA 589, 616 (1991) (Provision 5); see AFGE Local 900 and Dept. of Army, Army Reserve Personnel Ctr., St. Louis, 46 FLRA 1494, 1499-1500 (1993) (Provision 7--union's brief made concession that agency felt was sufficient to remove proposal from realm of nonnegotiable). If an agency withdraws an allegation of nonnegotiability arising from agency head review of a completed contract, but the union believes it and the agency still differ over the meaning of the proposal that became part of the contract, the appeal is moot; the dispute over the meaning of the proposal can be resolved through whatever procedures the parties have adopted in their negotiated agreement. Chicago O'Hare Chap. 10, ACT and Illinois Air Nat'l Guard Technicians, 10 FLRA 261, 261-62 (1982).

As to conditional disapproval of provisions and ongoing negotiation and consideration of negotiability during the course of negotiability appeals, AFGE Local 1900 and Dept. of Army, Ft. McPherson, 51 FLRA 133, 137-38 (1995):

When an agency contends that a provision is negotiable based on a particular interpretation and states that it will withdraw its allegation of nonnegotiability if that interpretation is adopted by the union, there is no longer a dispute as to the negotiability of the provision, if the union agrees with the agency's interpretation. See National Federation of Federal Employees, Local 1263 and Defense Language Institute, Presidio of Monterey, California, 29 FLRA 61, 66 (1987). The Agency states that it will withdraw its disapproval of Provisions 3, 4, 5, and 8 based on interpretations of those provisions with which the Union agrees. Consequently, there is no longer any dispute over the negotiability of these provisions, and we will dismiss the petition for review as to Provisions 3, 4, 5, and 8.

When a union offers to amend the wording of a proposal that is before the Authority to comply with agency objections, the proposal is not ripe for Authority review because the agency has not had the opportunity to review the proposed amendment. See, for example, National Association of Agriculture Employees and U.S. Department of Agriculture, Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service, Hyattsville, Maryland, 48 FLRA 599, 601 (1993). Here, the Agency head declared Provision 10 to be nonnegotiable solely because it did not expressly refer to chapter 75 of title 5 of the U.S. Code. Although the Union offers to amend the provision to include the omitted reference, the Agency has not reviewed the provision as amended. Accordingly, there is presently no dispute over whether the amended provision is within the duty to bargain, and we will dismiss the petition for review of Provision 10 without prejudice to the Union's right to file a negotiability appeal if, upon review, the Agency head declares the amended provision nonnegotiable.

The agency need not make a formal declaration that it is withdrawing its allegations of nonnegotiability for FLRA to bow out of a negotiability appeal. An agency made no response to a union's request to the agency for its position as to the negotiability of various proposals. The union filed its negotiability appeal, the agency responded in a motion to dismiss by stating that it had not bargained or taken any position concerning the negotiability of the proposals, and the union asked that FLRA order the parties to resume bargaining. From all of that FLRA concluded that "it appears from these statements that both parties would prefer to bargain over the proposals." The negotiability petition was dismissed "without prejudice to the Union's right to file another petition for review at such time as the conditions governing review are met..." NFFE Local 2050 and EPA, Washington, 48 FLRA 389, 390 (1993).

"An Agency's withdrawal of its allegations of nonnegotiability in prior cases is not dispositive of whether proposals are within the duty to bargain under the Statute.... [T]he prior inclusion of proposals concerning the same subject matter in a collective bargaining agreement is not dispositive of those proposals' negotiability under the Statute." SEIU Local 556 and Dept. of Navy, Marine Corps Exch., Kaneohe Bay, 26 FLRA 380, 385 (1987).
