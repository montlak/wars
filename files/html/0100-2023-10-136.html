a. Appellant

Although the appellant may elect not to testify on his own behalf, he may not, unless there is a Fifth Amendment challenge, prevent the agency from calling him as a witness. Lateef v. Dept. of Interior, 6 MSPR 664, 667, 6 MSPB 557 (1981); see Nestro v. USPS, 34 MSPR 242, 246 (1987) ("Appellant does not assert any belief that his testimony would be used in a criminal proceeding."); Presley v. VA, 15 MSPR 559, 561 (1983) (a subpoena was not required for the agency to compel the testimony of the appellant in the agency's case). The Board squarely holds that the appellant may be called as an agency witness. O'Neil v. Dept. of Transp., 12 MSPR 212, 216 (1982). Nevertheless, the Board determined in Lateef that the AJ properly declined to allow the agency to call the appellant as a witness after both parties rested their cases. Permitting the requested reopening allows the agency to have a second chance to prove its charges after listening to the appellant's defense. Although administrative hearings are not strictly subject to the formal rules of evidence, it is unfair and unreasonable to permit the agency to present part of its direct case during its portion of the hearing and then to present additional nonrebuttal evidence after the appellant completed his case. The agency could not prevail on the witness issue by a claim that it was assured by the appellant's representative that the appellant would testify; the agency did not inquire, prior to resting its case, whether the appellant would testify. The Board noted that an appellant is not required to call witnesses in response to the agency case. The moving party must call the witnesses it needs.

Reid v. USPS, 54 MSPR 648, 655 (1992), explained as to testimony by the appellant:

An appellant can elect not to testify on his own behalf at a Board hearing, but he does not have the right to prevent an agency from calling him as a witness in its case-in-chief. See, e.g., O'Neil v. Department of Transportation, 10 MSPB 745, 12 M.S.P.R. 212, 216 (1982). We note that the appellant first indicated that he would testify at the hearing but that, during the hearing, the appellant's counsel indicated that he would not testify. The agency then moved to call the appellant as a witness on the basis that it had relied on the appellant's prior representation that he would testify. The administrative judge granted the motion and limited cross examination by the appellant's counsel to those issues raised on direct examination. Because the appellant waited until the hearing began before indicating that he would not testify, we find no abuse of discretion by the administrative judge in limiting his testimony. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b); see also O'Neil, 12 M.S.P.R. at 216-17.

Since the agency may call the appellant as a witness, should the agency do so? It may seem intriguing for the agency to call the appellant during the agency's case, but the technique may be inefficient or counterproductive. Unless the appellant's admission on particular points is essential, trying to make the agency's case through the appellant is likely to result in disjointed testimony. If an agency requests the appellant as a witness, the appellant's counsel can object, allowing the AJ to decide whether the agency will be permitted to call the appellant. The AJ may well direct the agency to conduct its examination of the appellant after the appellant testifies in his own case. Or the AJ may allow the agency to call and examine the appellant and, on request by appellant's counsel, allow the appellant's counsel to forego questioning of the appellant during the agency case and then put the appellant back on the stand to testify fully (and without particular regard to the appellant's scope of testimony during the agency's case) during the presentation of the appellant's case. If this route is followed, the agency will likely be limited during cross-examination of the appellant to matters not already explored during the agency's questioning of the appellant during the agency's case. The sequence of questioning of the appellant who is approved as an agency witness should be explored during the prehearing conference.

When the appeal includes an affirmative defense of discrimination or reprisal, some AJs require that the appellant testify an the onset of the hearing as to the EEO or reprisal claims. The agency can then meet that evidence as it puts on its case. The appellant then responds to the agency's evidence. Otherwise, the agency puts on its case on the merits, the appellant responds to the agency case and presents evidence supporting the affirmative defenses, and the agency then responds through testimony to meet those defenses.
