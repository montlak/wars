5. PFR

Fees are properly awarded for time spent on a PFR. It is immaterial that the attorney is not formally designated as counsel. Langenbach v. USPS, 12 MSPR 411, 414 (1982). Time is also compensable for preparation of the opposition to an agency PFR. Keely v. MSPB, 760 F.2d 246 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Lynch v. Dept. of Educ., 65 MSPR 407, 411-12 (1994) (counsel could be compensated for a successful defense against an agency PFR challenging a fee award but remanding the case to the region to split out the compensable time from time that could not be compensated on appellant's unsuccessful cross-PFR); Alexander v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 569, 571 (1985) (fees awarded for the time of an attorney and an associate attorney who opposed a PFR); Bodrick v. District of Columbia Gov't, 20 MSPR 532, 533 (1984) (opposition to agency PFR of an addendum decision); Koenig v. OPM, 19 MSPR 216, 217 (1984) (fees for the opposition to the agency PFR). But see Vandemark v. Dept. of Navy, 26 MSPR 81, 82 n.3 (1985) (denying fees for the unsuccessful PFR of an addendum decision since appellant did not prevail on that aspect of the case). Compensation was awarded for 20 hours spent on the response to a PFR in Simms v. District of Columbia Gov't, 20 MSPR 486, 488 (1984). In Simms, the additional compensation was requested in the PFR response rather than in a later supplemental fee petition. But cf. Gallegos v. Dept. of Interior, 60 MSPR 5, 9 (1993) ("Although the appellant requests that he be awarded fees allegedly incurred by reason of the agency's petition, the proper method for requesting payment of attorney fees is to file a motion under 5 CFR 1201.37 [1993] with the Board's regional office. See Mayzlik v. OPM, 54 MSPR 331, 334 (1992).").

Counsel may claim compensation for periodic, reasonable conferences with his client concerning the status of a pending PFR. An attorney is under a professional duty to keep a client reasonably informed about the status of a matter and to comply promptly with reasonable requests for information. Hooks v. USPS, 42 MSPR 225, 228-29 (1989) ("Considering that the calls occurred at intervals of several months, we do not find that their frequency was unreasonable."); see Ruble v. OPM, 96 MSPR 44, 49 ¶ 11 (2004) (attorney may bill for keeping a client informed of the case).

Compensable time includes the time spent defending a fee petition by opposing the PFR filed by the agency to contest an addendum decision awarding fees. Howard v. USPS, 76 MSPR 680, 682 (1997) (allowing 2.7 hours reviewing and responding to agency PFR of addendum initial decision); Meadows v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 463, 465 (1995) (five hours awarded for a response to agency PFR from fee decision; one hour was not allowed for the unsuccessful motion to strike agency PFR as untimely); Dishman v. USPS, 37 MSPR 295, 297 (1988) (12 hours). Seven hours were allowed as compensable time to review and respond to the agency PFR of the fee award by the AJ. Erdman v. Dept. of Labor, 6 MSPR 51, 54, 6 MSPB 54 (1981).

The success obtained in the PFR need not be complete (as, for example, in a PFR of an addendum decision concerning fees): the petition need only obtain a significant part of the relief sought. The fee should not be reduced because the appellant did not prevail on every contention raised if the issues were based on the same facts and were not fractionable. Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., 32 MSPR 497, 503 (1987). Conner v. OPM, 76 MSPR 689, 690-91 (1997), awarded $6,745 in fees claimed for a successful response to a PFR challenging a fee award and an unsuccessful cross-PFR when the time could not be allocated between the two:

In addition to the attorney fees awarded by the administrative judge, the appellant requests $6,745.10 for fees incurred in preparing his response to OPM's petition for review. The appellant's response also included his cross petition for review. Attorney time spent preparing a response to an agency petition for review is compensable, but time spent on an unsuccessful petition for review is not. See Freeman v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 66 MSPR 125, 130 (1995). In the present case, we find that the appellant is entitled to all of the time spent preparing his response to OPM's petition for review and his cross petition for review because he has not identified any time devoted exclusively to the unsuccessful cross petition, and it seems that he did not make such a distinction with respect to this time because the tasks necessary for preparing both the response and the cross petition were the same. Further, OPM has not responded to the appellant's request for fees incurred in responding to its petition for review and in preparing his cross petition for review, and we have no cause to doubt the reasonableness of the number of hours expended by counsel in this effort. See Meadows v. Department of the Navy, 68 MSPR 463, 464-65 (1995).

If the appeal is successful but the PFR is not, then counsel will not receive a fee award for the PFR, assuming entitlement to fees for the appeal. See Freeman v. VA, 66 MSPR 125, 130-31 (1995) (attorney time spent preparing a response to an agency PFR was compensable, but time spent on an unsuccessful cross-PFR was not; still awarding fees for time that was not divisible, e.g., review of the initial decision and the evidentiary record, between the PFR and the cross-PFR); Koenig v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 155, 158 n.4 (1992) ("The appellant requests additional attorney fees and costs of $929.25 for his opposition to the agency's petition for review. We deny that request in light of our decision below that the agency's petition is meritorious."); Koerner v. OPM, 55 MSPR 150, 152-53 (1992) ("The Board has held that where it denies a petition for review, the appellant cannot be considered a prevailing party with respect to it, and, therefore, there is no basis for awarding attorney fees for work performed in preparing that document."). If the Board declines the PFR but reopens on another point, it does not appear that the time spent on the PFR will be recovered. See Brown v. OPM, 54 MSPR 415, 419 (1992) (rejecting argument that PFR was necessary to address Board jurisdiction over moot appeal; that issue could have been addressed in the fee petition); Pacheco v. OPM, 68 MSPR 129, 135 (1995) (refer to the concurring opinion of Member Amador).

If a PFR from a favorable but insufficient fee decision is filed by the appellant and the Board does no more than correct some arithmetical errors of the AJ and grants no substantive relief sought by appellant, time will not be awarded for prosecuting the PFR. Blackman v. USPS, 67 MSPR 382, 387 (1995). Under current Board rules, a reply is permitted to a PFR opposition. Under earlier rules, when the reply was not allowed, time was ordinarily not allowed for preparing a reply to a PFR opposition. The Board would not accept the reply as part of its adjudication process. Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 5, 16 (1991); Gavette v. OPM, 31 MSPR 115, 119-20 (1986) (denying time for reply brief: "counsel was specifically advised by the Board that its regulations contemplate that 'the petition for review will be complete when filed,' and that 'there are no regulatory provisions for the acceptance of further materials submitted by the parties'"). But see Bartel v. FAA, 30 MSPR 451, 455 (1986) ("Although a reply to a response is not a submission contemplated by the Board's regulations and presiding officials should carefully scrutinize gratuitous submissions for which fees are sought, in this case counsel is entitled to be paid for that part of the work on the reply that served to clarify her original fee petition for the presiding official.").
