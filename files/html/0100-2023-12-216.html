1. Cancelation of Adverse Action; Agency Compliance With Initial Decision

Mootness is determined solely by reference to the allegations of the complaining party. Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 322, 325, 3 MSPB 406 (1980) (overruled as to the ability of a party to file a petition for enforcement after an appeal has been dismissed as moot, Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616, 2007 MSPB 234 (2007)). But if an action was canceled and the relief is as full as it can be, consistent with the nature of the action and the authority of the Board, the case is moot: the Board does not issue advisory opinions. Malewich v. USPS, 13 MSPR 548, 549 (1982); see White v. Int'l Boundary Water Comm'n, 59 MSPR 62, 65 (1993) (appellant filed a PFR from an initial decision canceling an appeal as moot following agency rescission of the appellant's removal; the agency allegedly reinstated the employee to a lesser position and the appellant filed a PFR to secure compliance; since the appellant was again removed, the compliance issue was moot because "the Board cannot grant effective relief."); cf. Green v. Dept. of Air Force, 114 MSPR 340, 343 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 114 (2010) (USERRA claim for improper charges for military leave mooted by payment for the amount of that leave shortly after the issuance of the initial decision). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals," for decisions concerning mootness of appeals; refer to Chapter 16, "Mootness," for decisions concerning mootness of compliance or enforcement petitions; refer earlier in this chapter to "PFR Not Mooted by Overbroad Interim Relief."]

The initial decision from an AJ is not of sufficient force and effect to require a party to comply with an order contained in it until the decision itself becomes final. If the agency complies with an initial decision reversing a removal but the appellant files a PFR from the favorable initial decision, the PFR may be moot. See Valery v. VA, 60 MSPR 313 (1993) (Table) (PFR was dismissed as moot after the agency canceled removal and provided back pay to the appellant through the day after the initial decision; OPM approved the appellant's disability retirement on the day after the initial decision was issued). Agency compliance with an initial decision moots its PFR. Metsker v. DOD, 5 MSPR 173, 174, 5 MSPB 227 (1981), followed, Ostrout v. USPS, 53 MSPR 586, 588-89 (1992); see Trotter v. DOD, 54 MSPR 563, 564 (1992) ("[W]hen an agency cancels an action in compliance with the initial decision, the matter is effectively removed from controversy."). Acquiescence removes the matter from controversy, Anthan v. Dept. of Transp., 10 MSPR 408, 409 (1982), after the agency was clearly notified by appropriate language in the initial decision that it must comply only with a final decision. McGee v. USPS, 22 MSPR 338, 339 (1984). In Beer v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 53, 55-56, 2 MSPB 226 (1980), the Board was confronted with an employee who appealed his removal and obtained a favorable initial decision. The agency filed a PFR while it concurrently canceled the removal. Cancelation of the action appealed removed the matter from controversy; it could be inferred that the agency acquiesced in the decision of the AJ and, barring action of the Board to reopen the case, the initial decision became a final decision. The Board considered the agency PFR only because the Board concluded that, in some fashion, the agency might have been misled by the information in the initial decision pertaining to appeal rights. Should the appellant and the agency petition and cross-petition for review, and should it appear that the agency canceled the action against the appellant and mitigated the penalty to conform with the initial decision, only the appellant's PFR will be reviewed; the agency's cross-petition will be dismissed as moot. Nickerson v. USPS, 49 MSPR 451, 458 (1991). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Interim Relief" for decisions defining what occurs when an agency grants full rather than interim relief in response to an interim relief order.]

There is an exception to the doctrine that agency compliance with an initial decision moots an agency PFR. If the agency demonstrates that it did not intend to comply, that compliance was an administrative error, and that compliance was brief, the rule will not be applied and the PFR will be adjudicated. See Drappo v. Dept. of Air Force, 44 MSPR 542, 542-43 (1990); Crary v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 226, 227 n.1 (1985) (PFR was not moot when the agency legal representative told a supervisor to reverse the directive to the appellant to return to work; the order was countermanded because the agency intended to file a PFR and although the appellant returned to her job, her supervisor did not assign work to her). However, if the error occurs through the fault of the agency, and no effort is made to correct the error, e.g., by obtaining advice from MSPB over allegedly confusing statutory provisions, the agency acquiesced in the initial decision and its PFR is moot. Kent v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 676, 680 (1990). This applies to disability decisions as well. Francis v. OPM, 36 MSPR 43, 45 (1987). Should OPM reverse its decision on a reconsideration (retirement) decision, mooting the appeal from denial of retirement benefits, MSPB will not entertain OPM's PFR from the resulting dismissal of the appeal without new and material evidence from OPM or OPM's allegation that it made an error in granting benefits. The unilateral modification of the action after the appeal was filed divested the Board of jurisdiction because the action appealed was completely rescinded. Ramirez v. OPM, 44 MSPR 259, 261 (1990).

Incomplete efforts toward, but failure to achieve, compliance may avoid mootness, Crary v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 226, 227 n.1 (1985):

In her response to the agency's petition for review, appellant asserts that her supervisor told her to return to work as a result of the initial decision, she reported as directed, but before her supervisor could assign her work, the agency's legal representative told her supervisor not to put her back to work because the agency was submitting a petition for review of the initial decision. The agency filed a motion to strike this response, asserting that it did not reinstate appellant after the initial decision. The Board has clearly held that an agency's compliance with an initial decision moots the issue and therefore a petition for review of the issue must be dismissed. See Malewich v. U.S. Postal Service, 12 MSPB 111, 13 MSPR 548 (1982); Anthan v. Department of Transportation, 9 MSPB 635, 10 MSPR 408 (1982); Beer v. Department of the Army, 2 MSPB 226, 2 MSPR 226 (1980). We find, however, that appellant's assertions, even if proved, would not show that the agency complied with the initial decision because they indicate that she was not permitted to resume working. Therefore, we find that appellant's supervisor's actions did not moot the petition for review.

Any issue of counsel fees must be treated under an application filed after the final decision dismissing the appeal; a claim for counsel fees does not create a live controversy as to other issues in an appeal from the underlying, subsequently canceled, adverse action. Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 322, 325-26, 3 MSPB 406 (1980) (overruled as to the ability of a party to file a petition for enforcement after an appeal has been dismissed as moot, Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616, 2007 MSPB 234 (2007)).
