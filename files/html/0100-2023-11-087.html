C. STIPULATIONS

5 CFR 1201.63 (2022) provides:

Stipulations.

The parties may stipulate to any matter of fact. The stipulation will satisfy a party's burden of proving the fact alleged.

A stipulation is an agreement between the parties and offered to the Board that certain facts will be considered proved. The stipulation satisfies a party's burden of proving the fact alleged. See Dubiel v. USPS, 54 MSPR 428, 432 (1992) ("This stipulation obviates the agency's initial burden of proving the facts underlying its charge."); Judges' Handbook, Chapter 10, section 14.d. [misnumbering appears on the MSPB website]. Stipulations may be entered before or during a hearing. Board guidance suggests that, whenever possible, the AJ should secure stipulations to narrow the issues and expedite the hearing. If the AJ feels that a stipulation might be reached, he may go off the record and explore the possibility of stipulation with the parties. If a stipulation is obtained, the AJ goes back on the record and recites the stipulation. In an arbitration case, if there is no verbatim transcript maintained, the best practice is to reduce stipulations to writing and make them part of the record. See Sloane v. DLA, 834 F.2d 1006 (Fed. Cir. 1987). Stipulations are often made part of a prehearing submission.

One does not stipulate to disputed facts. One party does not stipulate anything: it takes two. See Di Sera v. Dept. of Army, 71 MSPR 120, 123 n.3 (1996) ("First, it takes both parties to stipulate to a matter of fact. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.63."). Examples of stipulated facts might include the duties assigned to an appellant, the whereabouts of certain individuals at given times and dates, and the organizational structure of the agency. The parties could stipulate to the facts of the charged misconduct and its nexus to service efficiency, leaving in dispute the reasonableness of the penalty and an affirmative defense, e.g., disability discrimination. See Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 180 ¶ 4 (2002).

The parties cannot create or establish jurisdiction of the Board through stipulation because the government (either the agency or the Board) cannot be estopped to deny jurisdiction if none exists. See Bielomaz v. Dept. of Navy, 86 MSPR 276, 280 ¶ 10 (2000) ("[T]he fact that the agency conceded that the appellant was an 'employee' cannot confer jurisdiction on the Board where it does not otherwise exist."); Walton v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 244, 249-50 (1989) ("The law is well-settled that subject matter jurisdiction cannot be conferred by estoppel based on misrepresentations, as a sanction, or based on any other actions by the parties."). [Refer to "Issues of Fact and Law."] Under Granger v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 487, 490 (1996): "Although parties cannot confer jurisdiction on the Board by stipulating to the Board's jurisdiction, see Mas v. Department of the Army, 46 MSPR 46, 47 (1990), they can stipulate to facts that establish our jurisdiction over the underlying action, see Cruz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 49 MSPR 74, 75 (1991)...." See Altman v. Dept. of Navy, 72 MSPR 656, 660 (1996) ("Although the Board must determine its own jurisdiction, we find that the agency's admission constitutes a sufficient factual basis for finding that we have jurisdiction over the appellant's removal.").

A stipulation satisfies the party's burden of proving an issue. If the AJ recites a stipulation reached off the record and the recitation of the stipulation is not later challenged, the matter stipulated is deemed proved. See Mazzarino v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 224, 225 (1985) (agreement that the performance appraisal system was approved by OPM (citing Rea v. DMA, 20 MSPR 262, 265 (1984)). A stipulation should be unambiguous. See Genus v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 345, 349 (1987). The appellant may not challenge the stipulation on PFR if, at the time of the regional adjudication, he did not object to, or seek clarification of, the stipulation. See Taylor v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 471, 474 (1990) (stipulation to limit charges); cf. Ludlum v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 56, 59 ¶ 6 (2000) (by stipulation the parties narrowed charges in the proposal to a single charge).

Ordinarily the Board will not permit the withdrawal of a factual stipulation without an allegation that the stipulation resulted from untrue representations, fraud, accident, surprise, excusable neglect or some other reason justifying relief. See Adakai v. Dept. of Interior, 20 MSPR 196, 200 n.7 (1984). Wright v. Dept. of Labor, 82 MSPR 186, 190-93 ¶¶ 9-14 (1999), analyzed an attempt by an appellant's counsel to withdraw a stipulation to the specifications of unsatisfactory performance entered into by the appellant prior to the counsel's appearance in the Board litigation. The Board employed a "manifest injustice" review standard:

A stipulation reached at a prehearing conference in a Board proceeding, when reduced to a written conference summary, is analogous to an order reflecting the action taken in a final pretrial conference in a federal court proceeding, which "shall be modified only to prevent manifest injustice." Fed. R. Civ. P. 16(e). A court's refusal to allow such a modification is reviewed under an abuse of discretion standard. Charles Alan Wright, Arthur R. Miller & Mary Kay Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure § 1527, at 279 (2d ed. 1990). We find the manifest injustice/abuse of discretion standards to be appropriate in Board proceedings. See Kravitz v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 MSPR 44, 47 (1997) (although the Board is not bound by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, it may look to them for guidance).

As noted above, at the hearing the appellant's attorney sought to withdraw the stipulation that her performance was unacceptable. She urged then, and argues on review, that the stipulation was improper for several reasons: (1) The stipulation went to an "ultimate" issue in the appeal; (2) stipulations are limited to issues of fact, and the issue of unacceptable performance was a question of law or a mixed question of law and fact; (3) the stipulation failed to specify any particular time period involved; and (4) the appellant did not understand what a stipulation was or its legal effect.

The Board's regulation on stipulations provides that "parties may stipulate to any matter of fact." 5 CFR § 1201.63 (emphasis added). The appellant is thus correct in asserting that a stipulation to a matter of law or a mixed question of law and fact would be improper. Cf. 22 Charles Alan Wright & Kenneth W. Graham, Jr., Federal Practice and Procedure § 5194, at 197 (1978) (the court is not bound by stipulations of mixed fact and law). Although the term "unacceptable performance" is a term of art that is specifically defined by regulation, whether a particular employee's performance meets this definition during a specified period is a question of fact, not a question of law or a mixed question of fact and law.[4] Similarly, the appellant has cited no legal authority, and we are aware of none, that indicates that it is inappropriate to accept stipulations as to "ultimate" issues of fact, however that term might be defined.[5]

[4] A mixed question of law and fact is one that depends for its resolution on questions of both law and fact. Black's Law Dictionary 1003 (6th ed. 1990). The appellant has identified no question of law that needs to be resolved in order to determine whether her performance during the PIP was unacceptable, i.e., whether she "fail[ed] to meet established performance standards in one or more critical elements of [her] position" during that period.

[5] Whether the appellant's performance was unacceptable during the PEP was certainly an extremely important issue, but was not the only issue to be resolved in the appeal.

Although the appellant's attorney stated at the beginning of the hearing that the appellant did not understand what a stipulation was, the appellant herself did not testify at the hearing about what her understanding was during the prehearing conference, or how she understood the statement in the AJ's summary of the prehearing conference about the stipulation. Significantly, that summary recited that:

If a party believes that this is an incorrect summary of the prehearing conference, that party must so notify me as soon as possible. In the absence of any such notice, the rulings contained in this Order are final and will not be modified without a showing of good cause.

The appellant did not object to or question the contents of the prehearing conference summary at any time between the issuance of the summary on December 15, 1997, and the hearing on February 26, 1998. Nor did the appellant's attorney object to the stipulation at any point between her designation on February 10 and the hearing on February 26, despite a telephone conversation between the attorney and the AJ on February 17, which the AJ summarized as follows:

On February 17, 1998, I telephoned the appellant's attorney and reminded her of the hearing date in this appeal. The appellant's representative indicated that she had read the file and was familiar with my rulings. I then informed her she should call me if she had any questions regarding the appeal.

The failure of the appellant or her attorney to object to the stipulation prior to the hearing are significant factors militating against a finding that permitting the appellant to withdraw the stipulation was necessary to prevent manifest injustice, or that the AJ abused his discretion by refusing to allow such a withdrawal. Another, equally strong factor militating against such a withdrawal is that the appellant did not point to any evidence suggesting that the stipulation was factually incorrect. The agency's allegation of unacceptable performance was that the appellant failed to meet deadlines for processing certain types of work within the time frames required by her performance standards. The appellant has not contended, either at the hearing, or in her petition for review, that this allegation was untrue. Under these circumstances, we conclude that the appellant has not established that allowing her to withdraw her stipulation is necessary to prevent manifest injustice.

Wright considered a stipulation entered during the course of prehearing proceedings. Except for stipulations of fact, "statements in a status conference are not evidence." Spradlin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 279, 282 ¶ 8 (1999); see Aviles v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-10-0964-I-1 (NP 7/25/2011) ("the administrative judge did not err by considering the stipulation in the initial decision as stipulations of fact made at a status conference are considered to be evidence. See Spradlin v. Office of Personnel Management, 84 M.S.P.R. 279, ¶ 7 (1999).").

The parties may, by their conduct during the hearing, vitiate the stipulation. If the parties at first stipulate to the credibility of a witness, and then proceed to introduce evidence on that individual's credibility, the AJ who undertakes to determine the credibility must weigh all of the evidence offered on the issue. See Taylor v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 495, 498 (1987). [Refer to "Stipulations" in Chapter 3.]
