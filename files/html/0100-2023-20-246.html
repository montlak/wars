6. Termination of Stay

The Board provides a measure of due process, consisting of the appellant's right to comment, when OSC decides it no longer needs a stay because its investigation has not demonstrated the likely existence of a prohibited personnel practice, Special Counsel v. FAA, 60 MSPR 24, 25-26 (1993):

On November 12, 1993, the OSC requested the Board to terminate the stay currently in effect. The OSC states that it has completed its investigation into the matter. Based on the results of the investigation, the OSC further states that it no longer has reasonable grounds to believe that Mr. Jennings' removal constitutes a prohibited personnel practice and that the OSC has closed its file in this case. Mr. Jennings was provided with an opportunity to respond to the OSC's request to terminate the stay, but he did not respond.

Pursuant to 5 USC 1214(b)(1)(A)(i) (Supp. IV 1992), the OSC may request the Board to order the stay of a personnel action if the OSC determines that there are reasonable grounds to believe that a personnel action was taken, or is to be taken, as a result of a prohibited personnel practice. The legislative history establishes that the purpose of the stay provision is to minimize the adverse consequences of a prohibited personnel practice, to provide time for the OSC to conduct a full investigation, and to settle the controversy. H.R. Rep. No. 274, 100th Cong., 1st Sess. 23 (1987). It was not intended that stays of personnel actions should extend for prolonged periods of time. Id.

The OSC states that it has completed its investigation and has concluded that there no longer are reasonable grounds to believe that a prohibited personnel practice has occurred. Mr. Jennings has submitted no information or evidence which demonstrates that the OSC's conclusion is incorrect.

Therefore, in the absence of evidence which tends to refute the OSC's conclusion that it no longer has reasonable grounds to believe a prohibited personnel practice has occurred, or that further investigation is warranted, the Board GRANTS the request of the OSC. The stay is hereby TERMINATED effective the date of issuance of this Order.

An agency may move to terminate a stay. The Board may then join the agency's motion and a motion from OSC to extend the stay. However, under Special Counsel v. Dept. of Transp., 71 MSPR 87, 90 (1996), the agency may not use a motion to terminate a stay as a means of placing before the Board evidence that it could not introduce to oppose OSC's extension request:

The agency's arguments in support of its motion to terminate the stay are merely challenges to the arguments made in the OSC's request for an initial stay. The statute prescribing initial stay procedures makes no provisions for agency comments in opposition, while the section concerning extensions explicitly provides for such agency comments. Compare 5 USC § 1214(b)(1)(A) with 5 USC § 1214(b)(1)(C). A motion to terminate should not be used as a means to circumvent this statutory limitation on the agency's rights to comment. Cf. Special Counsel v. Department of Transportation, 59 MSPR 556, 558 (1993) (in considering motions to terminate an initial stay the Board cannot ignore the statutory scheme providing no opportunity for an agency response). Therefore we DENY the agency's motion to terminate the stay. However, we have considered the arguments made in the motion to terminate as if they were the agency's response to the OSC's request to extend the stay.

A stay proceeding is in no way intended to be a substitute for a complete hearing on the merits of the OSC's claim. Special Counsel v. Department of Commerce, 26 MSPR 280, 281-82 (1985). Rather, the Board considers only whether the Special Counsel's prohibited personnel practice claim is clearly unreasonable. Special Counsel v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 44 MSPR at 546. Viewing the record in the light most favorable to the OSC, we find that it is not clearly unreasonable to believe that a prohibited personnel practice occurred. The agency has provided extensive argument, but nothing that demonstrates that if the OSC proves all its allegations it could not establish the occurrence of a prohibited personnel practice. See Special Counsel v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 44 MSPR at 546. Therefore, nothing submitted by the agency changes the previous determination that the Special Counsel's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable. See Special Counsel v. Department of the Treasury, 67 MSPR 458, 460 (1995).

See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Transp., 74 MSPR 155, 157 (1997) (stay is to minimize the consequences of an alleged prohibited personnel practice; it is not a substitute for a merits hearing on OSC's claim).

In Special Counsel ex rel Kachanes v. USDA, CB-1208-14-0021-U-2 (NP 9/3/2014), an initial stay was terminated by Special Counsel motion when the employee was not able to continue employment with the agency during the period of the stay and when the parties sought to resolve their case through means that did not include a stay:

On August 20, 2014, Chairman Grundmann granted OSC's request for a 45-day stay of the indefinite detail, proposed removal, and constructive removal of Ms. Kachanes. The Department of Agriculture was ordered to reinstate Ms. Kachanes to her position of record, with the same duties and responsibilities that she formerly had, effective August 20, 2014. On August 25, 2014, the Department of Agriculture requested modification of the stay order based in part on Ms. Kachanes's representation that she was unable to work because of her disability. It further noted that Ms. Kachanes was receiving Social Security disability benefits and had a pending application for disability retirement through the Federal Employees' Retirement System.

On August 27, 2014, OSC filed a motion requesting a termination without prejudice of the August 20, 2014 order granting the stay, stating that the parties desired to resolve the matter without the reinstatement of Ms. Kachanes to her former position. OSC also requested that the Department of Agriculture be released from its obligation to submit proof of Ms. Kachanes's reinstatement pursuant to the initial stay order. Consistent with 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(1)(D)(ii), OSC provided Ms. Kachanes with notice and an opportunity for comments prior to requesting termination of the stay. Both Ms. Kachanes and the Department of Agriculture consented to OSC's motion to terminate the stay.

Based on the foregoing, the August 20, 2014 stay is hereby terminated without prejudice effective the date of the issuance of this Order. Further, pursuant to the request of the parties, the Department of Agriculture is hereby released from its obligation to submit proof of its compliance with the August 20, 2014 initial stay order.
