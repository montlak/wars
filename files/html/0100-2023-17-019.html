e. Part-Time

The Part-Time Act, 5 USC 3401, does not overrule, modify, or provide greater rights to part-time employees than are provided by law and regulations governing a reduction in force. Only the agency decides what positions are required, where they are located, and when they are filled, abolished, or vacated. Stern v. FTC, 46 MSPR 328 (1990); Cobb v. Dept. of Labor, 22 MSPR 401 (1984), rev'd, Cobb v. Dept. of Labor, 774 F.2d 475 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (discussed below); Drucker v. Dept. of Interior, 14 MSPR 436, 441 (1983). Drucker noted, 14 MSPR at 441:

...The Part-Time Act does not appear, either explicitly or implicitly, to overrule, modify or provide greater rights to part-time employees than are provided by the law and regulations regarding a reduction-in-force. See 5 USC Chapter 35; 5 CFR part 351. As the Board held in Griffin v. Department of Agriculture, 2 MSPB 335, 337, 2 MSPR 168, 171 (1980), only the agency can decide what positions are required, where they are to be located, and when they are to be filled, abolished, or vacated. See also Federal Personnel Manual, ch. 351, subch. 1-5a (July 7, 1981)....

The Part-Time Act is not violated by a RIF if management eliminates full-time jobs and creates part-time jobs because of decreased allocations for full-time positions. The purpose of the Act is to prevent agencies from abolishing positions held by full-time employees in order to make duties available to be performed on a part-time career employment basis. The question is one of motivation. Matthews v. EPA, 18 MSPR 533, 536 (1984) (RIF was undertaken due to decreased allocation for full-time positions; abolition of the position was not prompted by an intent to make duties available to a part-time employee; no violation of 5 USC 3403). If two positions, both 32 hours per week or less, differ only in the number of hours worked, they are in the same competitive level. An agency improperly RIFs a part-time employee by assigning the job to a former full-time employee whose schedule is reduced to part-time in the new job. See Cobb v. Dept. of Labor, 774 F.2d 475 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (setting aside a sham reorganization involving the abolition of two part-time (job-sharing) positions to make one full-time position); Tucker v. CPSC, 21 MSPR 621, 623 (1984) ("Differences in work schedule among part-time employees who would otherwise be assigned to the same competitive level do not constitute a basis for establishing separate competitive levels.").

Adams v. DOD, 96 MSPR 325, 329-30 ¶¶ 8-12 (2004), determined that RIF procedures were inapplicable to a reduction from full-time to part-time work when the appellants received pay and grade retention benefits:

There is no statutory right of appeal to the Board regarding an agency's RIF actions. Kohfield v. Department of the Navy, 75 M.S.P.R. 1, 4 (1997). Pursuant to its authority under 5 U.S.C. § 3502, the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) has issued regulations governing the scope of the Board's jurisdiction to review an agency's RIF actions under OPM's regulations at 5 C.F.R. part 351. Id. Specifically, OPM's regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 351.901 state: "An employee who has been furloughed for more than 30 days, separated, or demoted by a reduction in force action may appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board."

The Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Thus, to establish the Board's jurisdiction over their RIF appeals, the appellants must show that they were either furloughed for more than 30 days, separated, or demoted by the RIF action, as those actions are defined in OPM's regulations.

The appellants were not separated. The uncontested evidence also supports a finding that the appellants were not demoted. OPM's regulations define demotion as follows:

(4) Demotion means a change of an employee, while serving continuously within the same agency:

(i) To a lower grade when both the old and the new positions are under the General Schedule or under the same type graded wage schedule; or

(ii) To a position with a lower rate of pay when both the old and the new positions are under the same type ungraded wage schedule, or are in different pay method categories.

5 C.F.R. § 210.102(b)(4) [Emphasis added]. All of the appellants retained their respective General Schedule or graded wage schedule grades and rates of pay. Although the number of hours that the appellants worked per pay period was reduced, they did not incur a reduction in grade or rate of pay, and, thus, they were not demoted. See 5 C.F.R. § 210.102(b)(4); Wolf v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 317 F.3d 1395, 1397-98 (Fed. Cir. 2003).

The appellants also were not subjected to a "furlough," as that term is defined in OPM's RIF regulations. OPM's RIF regulations define a "furlough" as "the placement of an employee in a temporary nonduty and nonpay status for more than 30 consecutive calendar days, or more than 22 workdays if done on a discontinuous basis, but not more than 1 year." 5 C.F.R. § 351.203 (emphasis added). The appellants were permanently, not temporarily, assigned to part-time positions. Thus, they were not furloughed under the definition of "furlough" in OPM's RIF regulations.

Because the appellants' RIF assignments did not result in their furlough for more than 30 days, separation or demotion, the Board does not have jurisdiction over these appeals. Accordingly, we VACATE the initial decision and DISMISS these appeals for lack of jurisdiction.[*]

[*] These appeals do not fall within the Board's adverse action jurisdiction because an agency's RIF-based actions are excluded from the Board's adverse action jurisdiction. See 5 U.S.C. § 7512(B); James v. Von Zemenszky, 284 F.3d 1310, 1314 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Huber v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 793 F.2d 284, 286-87 (Fed. Cir. 1986), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 1043 (1987). We also note that OPM's RIF regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 351.704(b)(3) state that an agency is not permitted "to satisfy a full-time employee's right to assignment by assigning the employee to a vacant other-than-full-time position," which appears to have been what happened here. OPM's regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 351.704(a)(2), however, allow an agency the discretion to "choose to offer a vacant other-than-full-time position to a full-time employee.... in lieu of separation by reduction in force," which appears to be what occurred in these cases. The regulations seem inconsistent. We do not need to reach this issue since, as explained above, the agency took no action which is appealable to the Board under any statute, rule or regulation.
