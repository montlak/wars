broida flra Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards, Subchapter 02: Statutory Preclusion of Certain Matters From Grievance Arbitration, Section D: Preclusion of Grievances by Particular Employees, Subsection 01: Grievances by Terminated Probationers or Individuals Who Do Not Meet the Statutory Definition of an "Employee"

1. Grievances by Terminated Probationers or Individuals Who Do Not Meet the Statutory Definition of an "Employee"

The Civil Service Due Process Amendments of 1990 brought into the statutory appeals framework many nonpreference eligible employees earlier excluded from normal MSPB appellate procedures, who serve two year probationary or trial periods. See AFGE Local 916 and Dept. of Air Force, Oklahoma City Air Logistics Ctr., Tinker AFB, 47 FLRA 150, 153-54 (1993) (lack of arbitrator's jurisdiction award directing reinstatement of excepted service employee who was not covered by the Civil Service Due Process Amendments). Terminations of employees serving a probationary period remain excluded from arbitration. See, e.g., DOJ, INS v. FLRA, 709 F.2d 724, 726-29 (D.C. Cir. 1983). [Refer later in this chapter to "Dismissals of Probationers, Term or Temporary Employees; Students"; refer earlier in this chapter to "Statutory Authority for Review," involving actions that can be challenged through statutory appeals; refer to coverage of grievance procedures in Chapter 6, "Grievance Procedures"; grievance rights of nonappropriated fund (NAF) employees are discussed in Chapter 6, "NAF Employees"; refer later in this chapter to "Bifurcated Cases; Issues of Jurisdiction or Arbitrability; Jurisdictional Defect," and Dept. of Labor, BLS and AFGE Local 12, 66 FLRA 282 (2011).]

On occasion FLRA determines when or how an individual became a federal employee for purposes of determining the propriety of an arbitrator's award concerning a claimed benefit. FLRA treats the issue as a matter of interpretation of law, and in the process it reviews the facts and law pertaining to the grievant's appointment. See USDA, Farm Serv. Agency New York State Office and AFGE Local 2831, 58 FLRA 508, 509-10 (2003).

Reflecting decisions of courts denying protections of the CSRA to excepted service employees without veterans preference protection and without the protection of the Civil Service Due Process Amendments of 1990, NLRB and NLRBPA, 35 FLRA 1116, 1124-26 (1990), declared that nonpreference-eligible, excepted service employees (should they now lack the protections of the CSDRA) are precluded by law from challenging an adverse action set forth in 5 USC 7512 or an action based on unacceptable performance set forth in 5 USC 4303(a), through the negotiated grievance procedure:

In [NTEU and DHHS, Region V, Chicago, 25 FLRA 1110 (1987)] HHS I; [NTEU and Dept. of Treasury,] Office of Chief Counsel; [30 FLRA 656 (1987), and NTEU and DHHS, Region IX, San Francisco, 31 FLRA 993 (1988)] HHS II, the Authority held that adverse actions involving nonpreference-eligible, excepted service employees are within the scope of the negotiated grievance procedure. That position has been rejected by the United States Courts of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit, the District of Columbia Circuit, and the Ninth Circuit insofar as it pertains to the adverse actions set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 7512 and to the actions based on unacceptable performance set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 4303(a)....

The courts found that, under the comprehensive remedial framework established by Congress in chapters 43 and 75 of the...(CSRA)...the right to review of major adverse actions...and performance based actions...was limited to competitive service and preference eligible employees.... The courts relied on the Supreme Court's decision in United States v. Fausto, which held that the CSRA: (1) established a preferred position for competitive service and preference eligible employees; and (2) was designed to ensure uniform results in appeals of adverse actions...and performance based actions...involving those classes of employees by restricting review of appeals in such cases to the MSPB and the Federal Circuit....

...

We have reexamined the Authority's position and, based on the courts' rationale and conclusions set forth above, hold that nonpreference-eligible, excepted service employees, such as the grievant, are precluded by law from challenging major adverse actions and performance based actions through the negotiated grievance procedure. To the extent that previous decisions of the Authority are to the contrary, they will no longer be followed.

Because the grievant in this case, a Schedule A attorney, is a nonpreference-eligible, excepted service employee, her grievance contesting her removal--a major adverse action--is a matter which is outside the scope of the negotiated grievance procedure and is not properly subject to arbitral review. In short, the Arbitrator had no jurisdiction as a matter of law to determine the merits of the grievant's removal....

Consequently, we conclude that the Arbitrator's award on the merits of the grievant's removal from the Federal service is deficient because it is contrary to law. Accordingly, we will set aside the award insofar as it pertains to the grievant's removal.

In so finding, we emphasize that we do not hold that nonpreference-eligible, excepted service employees are precluded from challenging disciplinary actions, other than actions covered by 5 U.S.C. § 7512 and 5 U.S.C. § 4303(a), through the negotiated grievance procedure. Moreover, we do not address whether such employees are precluded from filing grievances concerning major adverse actions which involve alleged interference with protected union activity under the Statute or grievances concerning actions based upon unlawful discrimination.

At note 3 of NLRB, 35 FLRA at 1124, FLRA defined the phrase "major adverse action":

The phrase "adverse action" generally includes serious personnel actions, or "major adverse actions," such as removals, reductions in grade, reductions in pay, suspensions for more than 14 days, or furloughs of 30 days or less, see 5 USC § 7512, and "minor adverse actions," such as suspensions for 14 days or less, see 5 USC § 7502. See United States v. Fausto, 484 U.S. 439, 445-47 (1988). Employees may also be removed or reduced in grade for unacceptable performance under 5 USC § 4303(a). Apart from suspensions of 14 days or less under 5 USC § 7502, these are all actions which, if taken against competitive service or preference eligible employees, would be appealable to MSPB and to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. See 5 USC §§ 7701 and 4303(e).

DOD, Dependents Sch. and OEA, 10 FLRA 312, 312 n.1 (1982), determined:

In its opposition, the agency also contends that since the award relates to a removal action, the Authority has no jurisdiction to review the award in this case. However, the only awards to which exceptions may not be filed with the Authority under section 7122(a) of the Statute are those relating to matters described in section 7121(f) of the Statute. This award, involving the removal of a probationary employee, does not relate to any of the matters described in section 7121(f) and therefore this case is properly before the Authority for review.

FLRA broadly construes the types of grievances relating to terminations a probationers. At issue in NIH and AFGE Local 2419, 65 FLRA 823, 824-25 (2011), was a grievance protesting the agency's refusal to permit a union representative to attend a meeting where the employee was informed of his termination during probation. FLRA set aside the award:

The Authority has held that a grievance concerning the termination of a probationary employee is excluded from the scope of negotiated grievance procedures based on the statutory and regulatory scheme for a probationary period of employment set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 3321 and 5 C.F.R. part 315, subpart H. E.g., GSA, Region 2, N.Y.C., N.Y., 58 FLRA 588, 589 (2003) (GSA) (finding that grievance concerning agency's failure to abide by certain contractual provisions when it terminated grievant probationary employee was not substantively arbitrable) (citations omitted); U.S. Dep't of the Air Force, Nellis Air Force Base, Las Vegas, Nev., 46 FLRA 1323, 1326-27 (1993) (NAFB) (same). In so holding, the Authority has consistently noted that parties may not use the collective bargaining process to provide procedural protections for probationary employees facing termination. E.g., AFGE, 58 FLRA at 298 (citation omitted); NAFB, 46 FLRA at 1326-27 (citations omitted). Of particular relevance, the Authority has found contrary to law a bargaining proposal requiring, among other things, that an agency provide a probationary employee with the option to request union representation during a meeting in which he or she faces termination. See NTEU, 39 FLRA at 852-53.

The award is contrary to law. The Union grieved whether the Agency processed the termination of a probationary employee in accordance with the parties' agreement when it denied the grievant's request for Union representation. That portion of the grievance, therefore, concerned the termination of a probationary employee. See, e.g., GSA, 58 FLRA at 589 (grievance claiming that agency failed to follow contractual procedures when it terminated probationary employee concerned termination of probationary employee); AFGE, 58 FLRA at 298 (same). Such grievances are not substantively arbitrable. See id. Indeed, as noted above, contract provisions requiring that an agency provide a probationary employee with the option to request union representation during a meeting in which he or she faces termination are contrary to law. See NTEU, 39 FLRA at 852-53. Accordingly, we find that the portion of the grievance concerning whether the Agency violated the parties' agreement was not substantively arbitrable; because the Arbitrator based his award on that portion, we set aside the award as contrary to law. See, e.g., GSA, 58 FLRA at 589 (Authority set aside award after arbitrator erroneously concluded that a grievance concerning the termination of a probationary employee was substantively arbitrable).

That an administrative grievance process may address some matters pertaining to a probationer's employment does not mean that the probationer's termination may be resolved through a contract grievance process, NTEU Chap. 103 and DHS, C&BP Long Beach, 66 FLRA 416, 418 (2011):

The Authority has held that grievances concerning the termination of a probationary employee are excluded from the scope of the negotiated grievance procedure as a matter of law. E.g., BLS, 66 FLRA at 284. This is based on "Congress's intention to allow summary termination of probationary employees." NTEU v. FLRA, 848 F.2d 1273, 1275 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (NTEU). Further, that certain matters involving a probationary employee's termination may be appealed through an administrative procedure does not mean that those matters may be resolved through the negotiated grievance procedure. See id. at 1276-77. Such a finding would be contrary to "Congress's intention that collective bargaining not supplement probationers' existing procedural protections." Id. at 1276 (emphasis added).

An arbitrator may not consider a challenge to the unacceptable performance rating of a probationer. That result, FLRA explained in Dept. of Air Force, Nellis AFB and AFGE Local 1199, 46 FLRA 1323, 1326 (1993), was essential to permit management the ability to terminate probationers without more protection than is granted to them by OPM regulations.
