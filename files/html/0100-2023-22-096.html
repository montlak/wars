4. Prohibited Personnel Practice Case

In an IRA case a prevailing appellant is entitled to fees under 5 USC 1221(g)(2) if the Board's decision is based on a finding of a prohibited personnel practice. The statute is complemented by 5 USC 7701(g), providing fees to a prevailing party in the interest of justice. Although the latter statute leaves the Board with discretion to award or deny fees, the fee award may be made to the prevailing party without a finding of a prohibited personnel practice, for example, to a party who prevails through settlement, assuming underlying Board jurisdiction over the action leading to the settlement. Application of the two statutes was explained in Auker v. DOD, 86 MSPR 468, 470-74 ¶¶ 4-13 (2000):

Under 5 USC § 1221(g)(2), an appellant in an IRA appeal is entitled to an award of attorney fees and costs "[i]f [he] is the prevailing party before the Merit Systems Protection Board, and the decision is based on a finding of a prohibited personnel practice...." Although the administrative judge in this case found that the appellant was a prevailing party, he did not address the question of whether his initial decision was "based on a finding of a prohibited personnel practice...." Instead, he indicated indirectly, by referring only to other criteria, that such a finding was not necessary. In doing so, he cited two Board decisions, Roman v. Department of the Army, 72 MSPR 409 (1996), aff'd, 129 F.3d 134 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table), cert. denied, 523 U.S. 1052 (1998), and Smit v. Department of the Treasury, 61 MSPR 612 (1994).

In Roman and in Smit, the administrative judges awarded attorney fees under 5 USC § 1221(g)(2). In reviewing those awards, the Board did not directly hold that the authority to grant such an award was limited to cases in which there had been a finding of a prohibited personnel practice. It indicated in each case, however, that the administrative judge had made such a finding. Roman, 72 MSPR at 411; Smit, 61 MSPR at 615. Roman and Smit therefore do not support the proposition that an award of attorney fees may be made under 5 USC § 1221(g)(2) in the absence of a finding of a prohibited personnel practice.

More important, we note that the language of 5 USC § 1221(g)(2) that is quoted above is unambiguous, and that it plainly authorizes an attorney fee award only when the decision on the appeal includes a finding of a prohibited personnel practice. Because the initial decision on the appellant's appeal includes no such finding, the Board has no authority to award fees in this case under 5 USC § 1221(g)(2). See Chevron, U.S.A. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, 467 U.S. 837, 842-43 (1984) (courts and agencies must give effect to unambiguously expressed intent of Congress); cf. Mitchell v. Department of the Navy, 51 MSPR 103, 112-14 (1991) (fees may be awarded under 5 USC § 7701(g)(2), which applies to cases in which the decision is based on a finding of discrimination prohibited under 5 USC § 2302(b)(1), only when there has been a finding of such discrimination).

Finally, we note that 5 USC § 1221(g)(1) provides that, "[i]f the Board orders corrective action under [the] section" authorizing IRA appeals, the "[c]orrective action shall include attorney's fees and costs...." As we have indicated above, the administrative judge did not order the agency in this case to take any corrective action. Accordingly, this provision also provides no basis for granting the appellant's motion for an attorney fee award.

Section 1221(g) of title 5, U.S. Code, is not the only provision authorizing the Board to award attorney fees to appellants in cases it adjudicates. The Board also may make such awards under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1), on which the appellant in this case expressly relied in his motion for an attorney fee award. In his addendum initial decision, the administrative judge did not directly address the issue of whether the latter section was applicable to IRA appeals. He seems to have assumed, however, that the only authority for awarding fees in an IRA appeal that the parties had settled was 5 USC § 1221(g)(2). As we have indicated below, we do not concur in this apparent view.

Under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1), "the Board, or an administrative law judge or other employee of the Board designated to hear a case," may award attorney fees "if the employee or applicant [requesting the award] is the prevailing party and the Board, administrative law judge, or other employee...determines that payment by the agency is warranted in the interest of justice, including any case in which a prohibited personnel practice was engaged in by the agency..." (emphasis added). The U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia has concluded that the "reference [in 5 USC § 7701(g)(1)] to 'any case' involving prohibited practices plainly extends to all proceedings in which action is sought to identify and correct such practices." Frazier v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 672 F.2d 150, 169 (D.C. Cir. 1982).

An IRA appeal is such a proceeding. That is, it is a case in which an appellant is seeking a finding that he has been affected by a particular kind of prohibited personnel practice, i.e., the prohibited personnel practice of reprisal for disclosures protected under 5 USC § 2302(b)(8), and in which he is seeking an order correcting the effects of that practice. 5 USC §§ 1221(a), 2302(a)(1), 2302(b)(8). The language of 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) therefore provides strong support for a finding that attorney fees in an IRA appeal may be awarded under that provision. Moreover, nothing in the language of 5 USC § 1221(g) indicates that that section supersedes 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) or otherwise precludes the application of the latter section to IRA appeals.

We also note that the legislative history of 5 USC § 1221(g), which was enacted more than 10 years after the enactment of 5 USC § 7701(g)(1), indicates that the drafters of section 1221(g) considered section 7701(g)(1) to be a basis for awarding attorney fees in IRA appeals. In an apparent reference to section 7701(g) and the "interest of justice" standard included in that section, the Senate committee considering legislation that eventually led to the enactment of 5 USC § 1221(g) made the following statements:

Current law allows the awarding of attorney's fees only if it is warranted "in the interest of justice." Because this standard makes such awards infrequent, employees who succeed in proving they were subject to a prohibited personnel practice can still end up adversely affected because of the financial burden of attorney's fees. S. 508 would require that the relevant agency be liable for attorney's fees if the employee is the prevailing party and the decision is based on a finding of a prohibited personnel practice.

* * *

Subsection (g) [of the proposed section 1221] requires that when an individual prevails before the Board and the Board's decision is based on a finding of a prohibited personnel practice, the agency involved must be liable to the individual for reasonable attorney's fees and other costs incurred. Current law permits the MSPB to order the agency to pay attorney's fees when the MSPB determines that such payment by the agency is warranted in the interest of justice.

S. Rep. No. 100-413, at 22, 33 (1988) (emphasis added) (footnote omitted).

We note further that 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) does not require the finding of a prohibited personnel practice that is required under 5 USC § 1221(g)(2); that it does not require the order for corrective action that is required under 5 USC § 1221(g)(1); and that fees accordingly may be awarded under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) under circumstances that would not permit an award under 5 USC § 1221(g). Among the cases in which fees may be awarded under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) are those in which the appellant is a prevailing party because of relief he obtained through a settlement agreement. See Vann v. Department of the Navy, 38 MSPR 411, 413-14 (1988). If 5 USC § 1221(g) were construed as precluding an award under 5 USC § 7701, IRA appellants who settle their appeals--such as the appellant in the present case--could be prevented from obtaining fee awards to which they otherwise would be entitled. Construing 5 USC § 1221(g) in this manner therefore would be inconsistent with congressional intent--shown clearly in the legislative history quoted above--that such attorney fee provision make it easier for appellants prevailing in IRA appeals to recover attorney fees.

Finally, we note that other provisions of 5 USC § 7701 have been found to be applicable to IRA appeals. See Costin v. Department of Health & Human Services, 72 MSPR 525, 530-32 (1996) (interim relief provision applicable in IRA appeals), modified on other grounds, 75 MSPR 242 (1997); Marren v. Department of Justice, 51 MSPR 632, 639-40 (1991) (quoting legislative history indicating that IRA appellants would have representational and transcript rights afforded to other appellants under 5 USC § 7701), aff'd, 980 F.2d 745 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

Following remand, the appeal resurfaced in Auker v. DOD, 92 MSPR 279, 283-85, 287 ¶¶ 7-12, 17-18 (2002), and relying on an intervening decision of the Federal Circuit concerning IRA jurisdiction, Yunus v. VA, 242 F.3d 1367 (Fed. Because the appellant obtained substantive relief through his settlement agreement, he was the prevailing party. But fees were denied. The settlement, although reducing the appellant's suspension of one day to a reprimand, did not convince the Board that the appellant was substantially innocent of the charges against him. Nor could it be said that the agency either acted in bad faith or knew or should have known that it would not prevail.

Mr. Carson also argues that Mr. Zabel's fees should be awarded as "other reasonable costs incurred" or "consequential damages" under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1) and (2).
