broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section D: Review of New Evidence, Subsection 03: Materiality of New Evidence, Part d: OPM, SSA, OWCP, VA Determination; Evidence of Medical, Mental Condition
d. OPM, SSA, OWCP, VA Determination; Evidence of Medical, Mental Condition

The materiality of evidence of disability was summed up in Harpole v. OPM, 98 MSPR 232, 238-39 ¶ 14 (2005) (involving submission of materials concerning an adverse security clearance determination):

[O]ur disability cases show that we have frequently accepted as new evidence medical reports that were developed in concurrent proceedings conducted in other fora and unavailable to the appellant when the record closed. The Board, for example, has reviewed, with appropriate caveats, evidence pertaining to disability findings by the Social Security Administration and agency fitness-for-duty findings. See, e.g., Redmond v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 4 ¶¶ 10-15 (2001) (reversing initial decision due to findings by Social Security Administration, which Board accepted as new and material evidence); Furst v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 439 ¶¶ 9, 15 (1999) (reversing initial decision due to findings of agency physician in fitness-for-duty examination, which the Board accepted as new and material evidence); Pritchard v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 453, 457-59 (1996) (remanding appeal to examine Social Security award findings, which the Board accepted as new and material evidence); Biscaha v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 M.S.P.R. 304, 308-09 (1991) (awarding disability retirement after examining employing agency's corrected submission regarding the appellant's job-related medical condition, which the Board accepted as new and material evidence)....

"An OWCP decision containing information that wholly contradicts the basis of the Board's initial decision, and that was unavailable, despite due diligence, before the record closed below, is new and material evidence warranting reversing the initial decision." Smart v. Dept. of Navy, 92 MSPR 120, 124 ¶ 11 (2002) (remanding for a determination of whether the OWCP reviewed the suitability of a position offered to, and declined by the employee); see Bruton v. VA, 109 MSPR 271, 274 ¶ 10, 2008 MSPB 150 (2008) (OWCP decision reinstating benefits is new evidence relative to filing date for a PFR). If an employee is removed for AWOL and the employee is later determined by OWCP to have been entitled to continuation of pay, the evidence of the OWCP determination is new and material: it was not previously available and it demonstrated the error of the agency in removing an employee absent because of a work-connected injury. See Brown v. NARA, 92 MSPR 95, 97-98 ¶ 9 (2002) (reopening to permit the AJ to determine the impact of the OWCP determination on AWOL charges leading to the appellant's removal); Hicks v. USPS, 19 MSPR 18, 20 (1984) (OWCP decision vacating, remanding and reversing an earlier decision was evidence that was new and material since it refuted the agency's charge of submission of a fraudulent claim); McGilberry v. DMA, 18 MSPR 560, 563 (1984) (after the initial decision was issued, OWCP redetermined the appellant's entitlement to continuation of pay for the period of appellant's charged AWOL). The same result was reached concerning the effect of an OWCP decision issued after the initial decision in Guess v. USPS, 29 MSPR 89, 91 (1985). See Satterfield v. USPS, 80 MSPR 132, 135-36 ¶¶ 6-7 (1998) (recognizing OWCP as new evidence; there was no change in the outcome, however, because the appellant had settled his Board appeal through a last chance agreement that was not a product of fraud, coercion, mutual mistake, or a threat of action that the agency knew or should have known could not be sustained; the appellant could have continued to litigate rather than settle by obtaining a dismissal of the appeal without prejudice pending receipt of the OWCP decision); Carter v. USPS, 75 MSPR 51, 54 (1997) (OWCP decision received after the initial decision undercut the basis for an initial decision affirming the appellant's removal for refusing to accept a position); Gucciardo v. USPS, 48 MSPR 420, 422 (1991) (an OWCP determination favorable to the employee was new and material evidence when the initial decision determined that the employee did not receive FECA benefits for an injury leading to a restoration rights claim).

Reversal of a prior OWCP determination may furnish grounds for the agency to seek reopening of a prior Board decision. See Welber v. USPS, 50 MSPR 557, 561 (1991) (noting that the agency is under a duty to submit a new OWCP decision to the Board quickly, requesting, if necessary, that the record in a regional or PFR proceeding be kept open for receipt of the OWCP decision once the agency learns that the decision will soon be issued); cf. Gerges v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 669, 678 n.5 (2001) (OWCP determination offered by the agency was new evidence; it demonstrated that the appellant's medical problems were not related to job difficulties involved in a constructive suspension appeal).

To be considered, the evidence must be material, that is, probative of the issues under appeal. See Szejner v. OPM, 99 MSPR 275, 281 ¶ 13 (2005) (SSA determination of disability was new, because it was received by the appellant after the record on review closed; the SSA determination was not material, because it was limited to a period that the appellant was not employed by the agency); Gregory v. TVA, 27 MSPR 170, 171 (1985) (following an initial decision sustaining a removal for an injury-related inability to perform work, the employee submitted a later finding of the TVA Retirement Board advising him that he did not qualify for disability retirement; the evidence was immaterial because the appellant's medical restrictions could make it impossible for him to work but not render him disabled under the standards of the TVA Retirement Board). Konrad v. Dept. of Treasury, 12 MSPR 533, 534 (1982), said of an OPM decision granting disability retirement after the record closed on an appeal from a downgrading:

During the appeal process appellant did not raise the issue of the effect that her disability may have had on her work, which effect, she now argues, caused her poor performance. Appellant had filed for disability retirement in May prior to the downgrade action, which was proposed in August. She chose to keep the processes completely separate. She made no argument which connected her disability and her poor performance, though clearly she knew the facts and circumstances of her disability at the time of her downgrade. The approval by OPM of the disability retirement application does not constitute new and material evidence to support a petition for review when appellant knew of her disability prior to the agency adverse action and at the time she appealed her demotion.

See Simpkins v. OPM, 113 MSPR 411, 414 ¶ 10, 2010 MSPB 52 (2010) (VA rating decision issued after the date of an initial decision is new evidence when it involves the same condition that formed the basis of the appellant's FERS disability retirement application); Hendrickson v. Dept. of Labor, 85 MSPR 334, 335-36 ¶¶ 2-3 (2000) (Board accepted medical evidence first submitted with the PFR questioning the appellant's mental competence to timely file the appeal); cf. Miller v. OPM, AT-844E-14-0925-I-1 (NP 7/8/2015) (SSA decision bearing on a condition involved in appellant's disability retirement appeal was new evidence, justifying remand for its consideration, when the SSA decision was issued after the close of the Board record); Williams v. OPM, SF-844E-13-0453-I-1 (NP 5/19/2014) ("We find that it is in the interest of justice to permit the appellant to submit the medical evidence considered by SSA to the administrative judge on remand for consideration, with the SSA decision itself, in determining whether the appellant met the requisite burden for establishing entitlement to disability retirement benefits. Givens, 95 M.S.P.R. 120, ¶ 9."); Givens v. OPM, 95 MSPR 120, 122-23 ¶¶ 7-9 (2003) (SSA award issued after the Board decision warranted reopening relative to disability retirement adjudication); Gardner v. OPM, 91 MSPR 391, 394-95 ¶ 8 (2002) (SSA disability determination was sufficiently probative to be considered new evidence in a disability retirement case even though some medical evaluations relied upon by the SSA were conducted prior to the issuance of the initial decision); Barbagallo v. OPM, 83 MSPR 557, 560 ¶¶ 6-7 (1999) (SSA disability determination that became final after the record was closed on the PFR was new (although not dispositive) evidence in an appeal from a disability annuity determination).

A diagnosis following an initial decision of a condition existing before the decision was issued may be new evidence, particularly if the appellant's mental condition impaired his ability to participate in earlier proceedings and to submit medical evidence before the record closed. Beyer v. USPS, 37 MSPR 672, 675 (1988); see Bridges v. OPM, 21 MSPR 716, 717 n.1 (1984) (psychiatric report admitted by the Board following the issuance of the initial decision when the appellant requested the AJ to keep the record open pending receipt of the report; the physician needed additional time to prepare a comprehensive report and evaluate the effect of possible medications on the appellant); Young v. OPM, 7 MSPR 33, 34 (1981) (evidence of a mental condition that could have impaired the appellant's ability to timely file appeal; the employing agency intervened to support a waiver of the time limit; no prejudice to OPM).
