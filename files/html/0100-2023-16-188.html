broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section H: Representation During Investigations; Weingarten Rights, Subsection 01: "Reasonable Belief" Standard, Part a: Immunity; Promise Against Discipline
a. Immunity; Promise Against Discipline

An employee has the right to representation at an interview if he reasonably believes that it is likely to result in discipline. That belief should be objective. If the employee is assured that the imposition of discipline is not contemplated, in the usual case the employee's fear of discipline will be subjective rather than objective. Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Base and Culp, 14 FLRA 731, 733 (1984) (Chairman Mahone Concurring). One FLRA decision concluded that if an employee is given immunity at the onset of an investigative interview, so that the employee has no reasonable fear of any discipline arising from information provided at the interview, or from the use of that information, there is no right to a union representative. INS, San Diego and AFGE Local 2544, 15 FLRA 383, 393 (1984) (ALJ Decision). But that result was reversed because of a determination in AFGE Local 2544 v. FLRA, 779 F.2d 719, 725-27, 729 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (following remand, 21 FLRA 240 (1986)), that, under the standard of "reasonable belief," substantial evidence did not support the determination that the employee could not reasonably fear disciplinary action. Judge Wald writing for the D.C. Circuit, over Judge Starr's dissent, declared:

While in some circumstances an express grant of immunity to an employee might well be expected to remove any fear of disciplinary action, the facts in this record lead us to conclude otherwise, i.e., that the circumstances surrounding the purported grant of immunity here would not negate any reasonable apprehension of discipline. So long as a reasonable fear of disciplinary action remained, the union was entitled...to represent Reed at the investigatory interview.

...Reed could very reasonably have entertained doubts about the ability of the OPR agents to grant him administrative immunity.... [T]he FLRA has not argued that an employee may never question a grant of administrative immunity....

...

...Reed had never heard of the immunity. Neither had...the president of the union local representing all the Tucson border patrol agents. Even more critically,...the station officer normally charged with the responsibility of imposing discipline for misconduct by patrol agents had no knowledge of any such procedure.... There is absolutely no evidence in the record...that demonstrates that the grant of immunity was made pursuant to any formal INS policy or procedure for granting administrative use immunity....

Another factor to consider in evaluating the reasonableness of an employee's fear of discipline is the almost total absence of procedural regularity surrounding the purported grant of immunity to Reed.... [T]he OPR agents in this case clearly were not following any standardized procedures for granting such immunity, but rather were acting in a slipshod and ad hoc fashion....

...There had apparently been no INS attempt to inform employees or supervisors in advance of any policy of granting administrative immunity. And indeed when the OPR agents did attempt to offer immunity to Reed, they gave a totally inadequate explanation of its scope and effect.... Reed had no way of knowing whether OPR was acting in conformity with its own or INS procedures, and thus he did not act unreasonably in continuing to fear disciplinary action despite the purported immunity grant....

These substantial considerations militating toward a reasonable apprehension of discipline on Reed's part are purportedly balanced by the fact that the OPR investigators told Reed that he was only a witness and not the subject of an investigation. But in a prior case the FLRA has itself ruled that this circumstance alone does not provide sufficient evidence to support a finding that an employee would not reasonably fear disciplinary action. See Internal Revenue Service, Washington, D.C. and National Treasury Employees Union, 4 F.L.R.A. 237 (1980).... We consequently conclude that the FLRA's determination that the "grant of immunity" would remove any reasonable apprehension of discipline lacks substantial evidence in the record.

...

Even if the immunity conferred upon Reed turns out in fact to sufficiently protect him from any disciplinary action, that circumstance alone does not alter our conclusion. Because the focus of § 7114(a)(2)(B) is the risk of discipline at the time of an investigatory interview, we need not address the government's argument that the purported grant of immunity could be enforced on estoppel grounds. A violation of § 7114(a)(2)(B) does not turn on equitable considerations that might intervene to shield an employee from the unfairness resulting from a strict application of the law. Reliance on the doctrine of estoppel, in fact, is itself a concession that a violation of 7.1.1.4§ (a)(2)(B) occurred at the time of the interview. Moreover, even if the employee were to succeed on an estoppel claim, this would not restore to the union its right...to be present at an interview "'in which the risk of discipline reasonably inheres.'"...
