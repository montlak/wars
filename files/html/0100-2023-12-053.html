10. Service

Both the petition and cross-petition must be served upon opposing representatives. This should be automatic when all parties and counsel are e-filers. The Board normally does not act as an intermediary for service purposes, although in practice the Board usually sends out a form letter letting the parties know that a PFR/Cross-PFR was filed. See Vaughn v. Dept. of Treasury, 119 MSPR 605, 2013 MSPB 50 (2013) (letter acknowledging the PFR is not required by regulation; if the PFR is received but the acknowledgment letter is not, the duty to timely file the PFR is not likely to be excused). The Board's regulations require that a PFR be served on each party and representative in the appeal and contain a certificate of service stating how and when service was made. Wooten v. VA, 56 MSPR 497, 499 (1993). If everyone is an e-filer, service is when the PFR is placed into the e-filing system, and service is electronic for the e-filers. Service requirements are stated at 5 CFR 1201.114(j) (2022):

(j) Service. A party submitting a pleading must serve a copy of it on each party and on each representative, as required by paragraph (b)(2) of § 1201.26.

5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2) (2022) admonishes:

Service by the parties. The parties must serve on each other one copy of each pleading, as defined by § 1201.4(b), and all documents submitted with it, except for the appeal. They may do so by mail, by facsimile, by commercial or personal delivery, or by electronic filing in accordance with § 1201.14. Documents and pleadings must be served upon each party and each representative. A certificate of service stating how and when service was made must accompany each pleading. The parties must notify the appropriate Board office and one another, in writing, of any changes in the names, or addresses on the service list.

The response to the cross-petition must be filed within 25 days after service of the cross-petition and served upon the opposing party. 5 CFR 1201.114(e) (2022).

Unless there is prejudice to a party, deficient service will not likely result in dismissal of the opposing party's PFR. See Audet v. OPM, 93 MSPR 628, 632 ¶ 9 (2003) (despite late service, the appellant filed a timely response to the OPM PFR; she failed to explain her bare contention that she was denied due process by the OPM delay; and she did not explain why she did not file an extension request with the Board if she needed more time to oppose the OPM PFR); Timberlake v. USPS, 79 MSPR 520, 523 n.3 (1998) ("The agency notes that it was not served with a copy of the appellant's petition for review until after the final date for filing, notwithstanding that an earlier date appears on the certificate of service. The Board's regulations, however, do not require that service be made 'upon filing.' See 5 CFR §§ 1201.26(b)(2), 1201.114(h). The appellant's petition for review was timely filed with the Board. His apparently untimely service of a copy of his petition on the agency, therefore, is of no effect. The agency has not suggested, nor does it appear, that it was prejudiced by the belated service inasmuch as its response was duly filed by the date set forth in the Board's order of November 25, 1997."); Clark v. IRS, 78 MSPR 465, 467 n.* (1998) (declining to dismiss the agency's response to a PFR because the agency did not properly serve the appellant's representative with the response); Foland v. Dept. of Air Force, 75 MSPR 92, 95 n.2 (1997) (agency failed to show prejudice by an irregularity in the service of a PFR on the agency); Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 21 MSPR 370, 371-72 n.2 (1984) (appellant made an inadvertent error in service; the appellant's response to the PFR was forwarded to the agency).

The Board may attempt to correct a service problem if the appellant does not do so. In Costin v. DHHS, 64 MSPR 517, 523 (1994), the Board explained the relation between filing and service, as it also noted a change in prior practice involving petitions filed but not served:

...The agency's petition, filed by U.S. Mail, was postmarked January 4, 1994, and thus was timely filed.

The appellant has submitted evidence showing that the copy of the petition that was served on him was postmarked January 7, 1994. He argues that, if the petition received by the Board was also postmarked after January 4, 1994, it should be dismissed as untimely. He also argues that, if the petition received by the Board was postmarked before January 5, 1994, it should be dismissed because service on him was not made "upon filing."

As noted above, the agency's petition was timely filed. Further, contrary to the appellant's contention, the Board's regulations do not expressly require that service be made "upon filing," see 5 CFR §§ 1201.26(b)(2), 1201.114(h). Indeed, under standard Board practice in effect at the time the agency filed its petition for review, if the agency had not served a copy of its petition for review on the appellant at all, it would have been considered to have filed a timely, but deficient, petition, and it would have been given an opportunity to perfect its petition by serving all parties within a time period set by the Clerk of the Board.[1] The agency's apparently belated service (by three days) of a copy of its petition on the appellant, therefore, does not require dismissal of its petition. Further, the appellant was not prejudiced by the agency's apparently belated service....

[1] The above-described practice is no longer followed. Instead, the Clerk of the Board will serve the petition for review on the other parties.

See Brown v. Dept. of Interior, 86 MSPR 546, 553 ¶ 10 (2000) (if the Board serves the agency with a PFR because the appellant fails to do so, the agency's response time runs from the date of the Board's service); Herring v. VA, 72 MSPR 96, 99 (1996) (noting that the practice at the Board's headquarters is to serve a copy on the other parties when a party fails to comply with Board regulations requiring service). Previously, the Board occasionally dismissed a PFR if the appellant failed to correct deficient service after the Board requested him to do so. See, e.g., Hudgies v. USPS, 55 MSPR 311, 314 (1992).
