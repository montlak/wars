3. Proper Party; Intervenor

Either party to an arbitration award may file an exception to the award. 5 CFR 2425.2(a) (2022). A "party" is any labor organization or agency or activity who participated as a party in a matter involving the issuance of an arbitration award. 5 CFR 2421.11(b)(3)(ii) (2022). A party need not participate in the arbitration hearing to file exceptions to an adverse arbitration award. See Dept. of Navy, Mare Island Naval Shipyard and FEMTC Local 127, 53 FLRA 390, 396 (1997).

Because the statute permits a party to the arbitration to file a grievance, the identity of the union's representative at the time exceptions are filed is not material to the union's standing to file the exceptions, AFGE Nat'l INS Council and DHS, C&BP, 69 FLRA 549, 551 (2016):

Nonetheless, the Agency asserts that the grievant does not have standing to file the exception because the Union was replaced by a different exclusive representative. Thus, the Agency claims that the Union could not file exceptions to the award or give the grievant the authority to do so.

We disagree. The Statute provides, in pertinent part, that "[e]ither party to arbitration...may file...an exception to any arbitrator's award."[33] Further, as relevant here, the Authority's Regulations define "party" as "[a]ny labor organization...[w]ho participated as a party...[i]n a matter where the award of an arbitrator was issued."[34]

[33] 5 U.S.C. § 7122(a).

[34] 5 C.F.R. § 2421.11(b)(3)(ii).

Applying this language to the facts of this case, there is no dispute that the Union filed the grievance pursuant to the parties' agreement and was--along with the Agency--a party to the arbitration. Therefore, under the plain language of the Statute and the Authority's Regulations, the Union is a party permitted to file exceptions to the award.[35] As such, we find that the Union, despite ceasing to be the unit's exclusive representative, is a party that may file exceptions to the award, and had the authority to designate the grievant as its representative to file exceptions on its behalf.[36]

This finding is consistent with private-sector labor-law precedent. The courts have held that decertification does not affect a union's substantive right to act on behalf of employees under the collective-bargaining agreement in existence at the time of a grievance,[37] or "deprive [a union] of its standing to pursue [a] grievance...under and according to the terms of the [a]greement."[38] National Labor Relations Board precedent also holds that a decertified union has the right and duty to represent employees in pre-decertification grievances under the parties' collective-bargaining agreement, despite being replaced by a different exclusive representative.[39]

III. Order to Show Cause

Counsel filed exceptions, and the Agency filed an opposition. Thereafter, the Authority issued an Order directing counsel to show cause why his exceptions should not be dismissed because he lacked standing to file them. The Authority stated that the record indicated that counsel filed the exceptions on his own behalf, rather than as a representative of the Union, which was a party to the arbitration. Based on this, the Authority stated that counsel did not appear to be a party to the arbitration within the meaning of the Authority's Regulations; as such, he appeared to lack standing to file exceptions.

The Authority ordered counsel to provide evidence that he was authorized to file exceptions on behalf of the Union. The Authority stated that a "[f]ailure to comply with this Order...may result in dismissal of [counsel's] exceptions." Counsel did not respond to the Order.

IV. Analysis and Conclusion

The Authority will dismiss a party's filing when the party fails to comply with an Authority order concerning that filing. See, e.g., NAGE, Local R3-32, 57 FLRA 624, 624 n.* (2001) (NAGE) (dismissing opposition because party failed to submit response to Authority's order directing party to show cause why opposition should not be dismissed); U.S. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, Med. Ctr., Coatesville, Pa., 56 FLRA 829, 830 n.1 (2000) (VAMC) (dismissing exceptions because party failed to submit response to Authority's order directing party to show cause why opposition should not be dismissed)

The Authority ordered counsel to demonstrate that the exceptions were filed on behalf of the Union. Counsel filed nothing in response. Counsel was given notice that his failure to respond to the Order could result in dismissal of the exceptions. Because counsel has failed to comply with the requirements of the Authority's Order, we dismiss the exceptions. See NAGE, 57 FLRA at 624 n.*; VAMC, 56 FLRA at 830 n.1.

There is a distinction between the union and the grievant. The union is the party for purposes of seeking review. A union that becomes the exclusive representative may file exceptions in a case arbitrated by a different union that was previously the exclusive representative. See AFGE and VA Automation Ctr., Austin, 57 FLRA 769, 769 n.* (2002). A grievant is not a party and is ineligible to seek review by FLRA of the arbitrator's award. Warner Robins Air Logistics Ctr. and AFGE Local 987, 1 FLRA 95, 96 (1979); see AFGE Local 1960 and Dept. of Navy, Dev. Ctr., 29 FLRA 680, 682 (1987) ("the grievant has no standing to file a request for reconsideration"); Fleet Combat Training Ctr. and AFGE Local 3723, 11 FLRA 284, 285 (1983) (union and activity being parties to arbitration, grievant was not party in proceedings; he was not entitled to file exceptions). But see AFGE and DHS, ICE, 70 FLRA 208, 208 (2017) ("As a preliminary matter, the Agency requests that the Authority dismiss the exceptions because (1) the grievant lacked standing to file the exceptions and (2) it was not properly served with the exceptions' attachments. Regarding the first preliminary matter, the record demonstrates that the grievant is a chief steward of the Union and nothing in the record before us indicates that the exceptions were not authorized by the Union. Thus, we conclude that the Union's exceptions are properly before us.").

An individual or an organization does not become a party by assuming the costs of the arbitration. Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. and NTEU, 40 FLRA 1254, 1255 (1991). A grievant is not made a party because the union states that the union has no objection to the grievant proceeding with exceptions; the record should demonstrate that the grievant participated as a party before the arbitrator or that he was authorized by the union to file exceptions. Dept. of Army, Tooele Army Depot and IAM Lodge 2261, 38 FLRA 454, 455 (1990); see Dept. of Air Force, Tinker AFB and AFGE Local 916, 49 FLRA 968, 969 (1994) ("As the grievant was not a party to the proceeding and was not authorized by a party to file on its behalf, the grievant is without standing...to file exceptions").

Of the grievant's usual lack of standing to file exceptions, DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, MDC Guaynabo and AFGE Local 4052, 66 FLRA 81, 85-86 (2011) (following remand, DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, MDC Guaynabo and AFGE Local 4052, 67 FLRA 417 (2014)), noted:

One of the individual grievants, JM, wrote a letter to the Authority claiming that the "Union failed to list [his] name in the Table of Award correctly." Letter from JM to the Chief Administrative Law Judge. Under the Authority's Regulations, only a "party" to an arbitration may file an exception. 5 C.F.R. § 2425.1(a) (stating that "[e]ither party to an arbitration...may file an exception to an arbitrator's award rendered pursuant to the arbitration"). A "party" to an arbitration is defined in the Authority's Regulations as "(b) Any labor organization or agency or activity...(3) Who participated as a party...(ii) In a matter where the award of an arbitrator was issued." 5 C.F.R. § 2421.11(b)(3)(ii). In this case, the parties who participated in the arbitration were the Union and the Agency. The grievant, who did not participate in the arbitration, may not file an exception. See AFGE, Local 2904, 20 FLRA 3, 3-4 (1985) (finding that an individual who was not a party to the arbitration lacked standing to file an exception). Accordingly, we will not consider JM's letter.

Cf. Broadcasting Bd. of Gov. and AFGE Local 1812, 66 FLRA 380, 383 (2011) ("The Petitioner does not cite a regulation permitting him to intervene in this matter, and nothing in the Authority's Regulations permits such an intervention. Cf. A grievant may prosecute exceptions if the union authorizes the grievant to act as its designee. See NFFE Local 1984 and DOD, DCMD Philadelphia, 56 FLRA 38, 38 n.1 (2000). The designation should be made with some formality. See DOD, Military Entrance Processing Station and AFGE Local 1627, 45 FLRA 976, 976-77 (1992), reconsideration denied, 46 FLRA 101 (1992) (letter from union to grievant stating that union would not file exceptions but that grievant "may always do so on your own behalf" did not show that grievant participated in case as party or that she was authorized by union to file exceptions). But a relatively informal designation by the union to the grievant authorizes the grievant to file the exceptions, AFGE Nat'l INS Council and DHS, C&BP, 69 FLRA 549, 550-52 (2016):

The Agency argues that the grievant lacks standing to file exceptions, because (1) the grievant provided only "an informal email from [the Union] purporting to grant her authority to file her exceptions"; and (2) the Union is no longer the exclusive representative of the grievant's bargaining unit, and thus, may not itself file exceptions.

The Authority has held that grievants are not parties that may file exceptions to arbitration awards.[24] A union, however, may designate a grievant as its representative for purposes of filing exceptions to an arbitration award.[25] But a union's mere acquiescence to a grievant's request to file exceptions on his or her own behalf, does not provide a grievant the necessary authority to file exceptions under the Statute.[26] Thus, where a grievant files exceptions to an award, the Authority will determine whether the record demonstrates that the grievant has been duly designated by a union as its representative for purposes of filing exceptions to the award.[27]

Here, the record shows that the Union designated the grievant to file exceptions on the Union's behalf. The grievant asked for a letter from the Union giving her the authority to file exceptions and "taking [the matter] out of [the Union]'s hands." The Union advised the grievant that it "w[ould] provide a letter giving [the grievant] the power to appeal." When the grievant later followed up with the Union requesting that letter, the Union's attorney replied by email: "Betty, power granted." Thus, undisputed facts in the record clearly indicate that both the Union and the grievant understood, through their email exchanges, that the Union was designating the grievant as its representative to file exceptions.
