18. Specific Conditions

We list here various medical conditions and decisions that can be examined for an understanding of the evidence that supported disability retirement applications. Almost all of the decisions cited are those that approved applications. Appeals resulting in decisions affirming denial of applications would reveal no more than evidence considered insufficient by the Board to warrant findings of disability. A finding of a lack of evidence in so limited that a determination would seem of little benefit to readers.

Alcoholism: Winslett v. OPM, 30 MSPR 293, 296 (1986) (dissent of Member Devaney at 30 MSPR 444 (1986)) (failed to meet burden of showing he could not maintain sobriety for the ensuing year; sober for six months prior to application for disability retirement).

OPM, 50 MSPR 465, 470 (1991) ("We further note that OPM's own regulations suggest that it would not have granted the appellant a disability retirement annuity based solely on a congenital condition. These regulations provide that a claim for disability retirement will not be allowed unless OPM determines that the applicant became disabled while serving under the Civil Service Retirement System. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.502(b)(5). A congenital condition, by definition, is present from birth. Thus, the evidence does not support the administrative judge's speculation concerning OPM's reason for granting the appellant's application for disability retirement.").
