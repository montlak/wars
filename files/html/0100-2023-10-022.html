5. Strategic Considerations--Intentional Waiver

Turnage v. USDA, 230 Ct. Cl. 799 (1982), offered interesting observations concerning the strategy involved in waiver of a hearing. The employee felt that the agency should have proceeded against him with an unacceptable performance action (Chapter 43) rather than an adverse action (Chapter 75), and concluded that the procedural error was sufficient to require reversal of the action. On the strength of the procedural objection, the employee waived a hearing. The Board remanded the case for additional evidence under Chapter 75, concluding that the action could proceed as an adverse action. At this stage, the employee maintained that he had the right to a full hearing, not just the right to provide additional evidence for the record. The employee declined to provide additional evidence, however, resting instead on his belated request for a hearing. The court, on review, decided that the employee should have requested the hearing the first time around, or at least presented evidence in the second proceeding, preserving his statutory argument concerning a hearing for subsequent judicial review. The court refused to allow the employee to forego the opportunity to present evidence, wait until the appeal was decided against him on the basis of a record he could have supplemented, and then claim that denial of a hearing was a procedural error. The court's suggestion was not to place all one's (procedural) eggs in one basket.

Thorne v. DHHS, 79 MSPR 583, 589-90 ¶ 15 (1998), involved an IRA case brought by a terminated probationer who alleged that several protected disclosures led to his separation. The AJ limited the scope of the hearing after incorrectly determining that the appellant did not raise some issues with OSC. The appellant advised the AJ that he would not attend the hearing because, given the restrictions on the issues, his attendance would have been a waste of time and money. The appeal was adjudicated on the record, with a result unfavorable to the appellant. On PFR the Board agreed with the appellant's arguments that the scope of the hearing was unduly limited. The case was remanded for hearing on all matters properly raised initially with OSC and then with the Board through the IRA appeal. Although the Board did not address waiver of the hearing, the result was clear: the improper restriction of the issues to be heard vitiated the voluntariness of the appellant's hearing waiver. But cf. Markland v. OPM, 73 MSPR 349, 354-57 (1997), aff'd, Markland v. OPM, 140 F.3d 1031 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (appellant effectively abandoned the Board hearing when, as a result of discovery disputes that might have been resolved, the appellant declined to proceed with the hearing).

The appellant does not have the option of requesting a favorable decision on the record and then obtaining a hearing if he does not receive the result he desires. The conditional hearing request would result in a summary judgment proceeding. Either the hearing is held, or it is not, and the appeal is decided on the record assembled with or without a hearing. Benson v. OPM, 83 MSPR 549, 551-52 (1999). One AJ allowed or encouraged the conditional withdrawal of a hearing premised on what the appellant apparently believed was a prehearing commitment by the AJ to decide the case in the employee's favor. The Board described the situation without comment in Allen v. Dept. of Army, 76 MSPR 564, 567 (1997), and remanded the case for the hearing the appellant thought he would get on his affirmative defenses if the Board reversed the AJ's initial decision in his favor. Another case noted the appellants' motion for a waiver of a hearing if certain motions were denied. The Board determined that there was some confusion as to whether the hearing was on jurisdiction or the merits of an IRA case and, as a result, the Board determined that the hearing waiver was not informed. Keefer v. USDA, 92 MSPR 476, 481-82 ¶ 12 (2002); cf. Wilson v. USPS, DA-0752-12-0101-I-1 (NP 8/19/2014) ("Contrary to her assertion, nothing in the appellant's August 15, 2012 withdrawal of her hearing request reflects that she made a conditional waiver.").
