e. Significance of Agency's Failure to Prove Charge

If an agency fails to establish its charges against an appellant, who in turn establishes a prima facie case of discrimination, the result may be the conclusion that discrimination motivated the action, particularly if there is collateral evidence of disparate treatment or harassment, as in Wickramasekera v. VA, 21 MSPR 707, 712-14 (1984), involving the discharge of a police officer for sleeping on duty and kindred offenses:

The agency next challenges the presiding official's finding that discrimination on the basis of race and national origin, and reprisal for non-work related reasons, motivated the agency's action against the appellant. The agency argues that nowhere in the initial decision did the presiding official find that discrimination and reprisal were demonstrated as a motive by preponderant evidence. We note, however, that the presiding official correctly recited the applicable standard and burden of proof of such affirmative defenses in the initial decision and, after thorough analysis under that standard, concluded that they had been established. The fact that the presiding official may not have restated the applicable burden in his concluding sentence cannot be considered reversible error.

The agency further argues that the inability of two of the appellant's witnesses to set forth particular instances of agency discrimination against the appellant is fatal to his affirmative defense. However, as noted by the appellant in his response to the petition, other witnesses identified specific acts of agency harassment based on the appellant's race and national origin and instances of inequitable treatment with no apparent legitimate basis. Further, contrary to the agency's claims, the totality of the testimony of the appellant's witnesses established a prima facie case of discrimination from which it could be inferred, if unexplained, that they were taken for prohibited, discriminatory reasons. See Furnco Construction Co. v. Waters, 438 U.S. 567, 576, 98 S. Ct. 2943, 2949, 57 L. Ed.2d 957 (1978). After such a case was made, the agency was properly afforded the opportunity to set forth a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for its action, pursuant to the guidelines set forth by the Supreme Court in McDonnell-Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973), and its progeny. Inasmuch as the charges against the appellant were properly found not to have been sustained, the agency's articulated, non-discriminatory reason for the removal may be deemed questionable. However, in order to find discrimination, the presiding official must determine not merely that the charges were unsustained on the merits, but also that they were not the true reason for the agency's employment decision. The appellant may demonstrate such pretext as follows:

[E]ither directly by persuading the [Board] that a discriminatory reason more likely motivated the employer or indirectly by showing that the employer's proffered explanation is unworthy of credence.

Texas Department of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 256, 101 S. Ct. 1089, 1095, 67 L. Ed.2d 207 (1981).

In this case, we find that the appellant has clearly established not only that the agency's charges against him lacked merit, but also that their proponents were not credible and their motivation was suspect. The appellant's evidence to support his claim of pretext properly included facts as to his supervisors' previous treatment of him and their general attitude toward his employment. See McDonnell-Douglas, supra, 411 U.S. at 804, 93 S. Ct. at 1825. In this regard, the presiding official found evidence of negative feelings, remarks and actions against the appellant and incidents of inequitable treatment of him because of his race and/or national origin. He also found evidence of a prejudicial attitude toward the appellant in general on the part of his second-line supervisor, who had expressed an intention to "get rid of" him.

The agency further challenges the presiding official's statement that the security police force at the facility in question was "98% to 99%, black," asserting that in fact three of the 24 employees were non-black. Assuming the accuracy of that assertion, we believe such a discrepancy to be de minimus, and find that the presiding official properly considered the racial mix of the work force in determining the agency's motivation in this case. Furnco Construction Co., supra, 438 U.S. at 580, 98 S. Ct. at 2951.

The presiding official found that the totality of the appellant's evidence supported a finding of discrimination. Such a determination is within the domain of the trier of fact and, absent a demonstration of error, we will not further review the presiding official's findings in this regard. Person v. United States Department of Agriculture, 5 MSPB 85 (1981). See United States Postal Service Board of Governors v. Aikens, 460 U.S. 711, 103 S. Ct. 1478, 1482-3, 75 L. Ed.2d 403 (1983); and Silberhorn v. General Iron Works Co., 584 F.2d 970, 971 (10th Cir. 1978).

The decision in a performance-based removal appeal, Carter v. SBA, 61 MSPR 656, 665-66, 668 (1994), emphasized that the failure of an agency to prove the action does not necessarily establish discriminatory intent, although rejection of the agency's basis for its action permits intent to be inferred and a finding of discriminatory intent based on inference:

In its petition for review, the agency does not challenge the administrative judge's finding that the appellant made out a prima facie showing below of sex discrimination. The agency asserts, however, that the administrative judge erroneously rested his finding of sex discrimination upon the mere fact that its charges of unacceptable performance were not sustained. Further, the agency maintains that it articulated below a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action, i.e., the appellant's cited performance deficiencies, albeit the administrative judge found that reason to lack credence.

We agree. Although the administrative judge enumerated other factors in addition to the agency's failure to support its charges by substantial evidence in his analysis of the appellant's claim of sex discrimination, the initial decision nevertheless appears to rest its ultimate conclusion of sex discrimination largely, if not exclusively, upon the sole fact that the agency's charges were not sustained. Specifically, the administrative judge stated as follows:

Because the agency's reason for taking this action has not been shown to be worthy of credence, I find that it is more likely true than not that the agency action against the appellant was taken because she is a female.... I find that the removal action was discriminatory against the appellant on the basis of sex.

As the agency correctly asserts in its petition, the administrative judge erred by sustaining the appellant's affirmative defense of sex discrimination based, essentially, upon the mere fact that its charges were not sustained. We find that, while giving lip service to the principle that the burden of proof of prohibited discrimination lies at all times with the appellant, the administrative judge actually required the agency to prove that the appellant's asserted poor performance was the real reason she was removed. To the contrary, however, it was the appellant's burden to prove that her performance was not the real reason.

In St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, __ U.S. __, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 125 L. Ed.2d 407 (1993), which was decided subsequent to the issuance of the initial decision, the Supreme Court clarified the application of the test for intentional Title VII discrimination set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-04, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 1824-25, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973) (to establish a claim of prohibited employment discrimination, the employee first must establish a prima facie case; the burden of going forward then shifts to the agency to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action; and, finally, the employee must show that the agency's stated reason is merely a pretext for prohibited discrimination).

The Court clarified in Hicks that "[i]t is not enough...to disbelieve the employer; the factfinder must [also] believe the [employee's] explanation of intentional discrimination." Hicks, __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2754 (emphasis in original). Because the burden of proof of discrimination lies at all times with the employee, see id., __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2747, the Court explained that:

Title VII does not award damages against employers who cannot prove a nondiscriminatory reason for adverse employment action, but only against employers who are proven to have taken adverse employment action by reason of [race, color, sex, religion, national origin, or retaliation].

Id. __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2756. The Court added, "That the employer's proffered reason is unpersuasive, or even obviously contrived, does not necessarily establish that the [employee's] proffered reason of [discrimination] is correct." Id.

Applying Hicks to appeals before the Board, we find that an agency meets its burden to articulate a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason when it introduces evidence, "which, taken as true, would permit the conclusion that there was a nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse action," regardless of whether the agency actually proves its charges. Id. __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2748 (emphasis in original). Thus, the appellant who asserts an affirmative defense of discrimination in an appeal before the Board must actually show, by a preponderance of the evidence, both that the stated reason was false, or not the real reason for the action, "and that discrimination was the real reason" for the adverse action. Id. __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2752 (emphasis in the original).

...

...In analyzing the matter of the "real reason" for the agency's action, the administrative judge's "disbelief of the reasons put forward by [the agency] (particularly if disbelief is accompanied by a suspicion of mendacity) may, together with the elements of the prima facie case, suffice to show intentional discrimination." Hicks, __ U.S. at __, 113 S. Ct. at 2749. Such "will permit the [administrative judge] to infer the ultimate fact of intentional discrimination," but does not suffice to compel such a finding. Id. (emphasis in original). As the Court noted, "there must be a finding of discrimination." Id. __ U.S. at __, n.4, 113 S. Ct. at 2749 n.4 (emphasis in original).

An example of a Board finding of discrimination on the bases of age and sex involved the demotion of an employee for poor performance who worked with the agency on related duties for 32 years. The employee performed satisfactorily, participated in agency training, and received college degrees while with the agency. The employee, who was selected for her position after filing an EEO complaint protesting the agency preference for younger men to work in the unit where she sought the position, received close monitoring and full documentation of errors as she began work in the new unit. She was reprimanded for errors before her peers, whose supervisor made the comment that hers "was not a woman's job," and was given unsolicited information about retirement benefits. Howard v. Dept. of Commerce, 15 MSPR 23, 27 (1983); see Weiss v. United States, 595 F. Supp. 1050 (E.D. Va. 1984) (employee was reinstated whose job performance suffered because of anti-Semitic remarks and harassment by the supervisor; an employer cannot use an employee's diminished work performance as a legitimate basis for removal if the diminution is the direct result of the employer's discriminatory behavior). Racial slurs and unfair treatment led to a finding of racial discrimination in an adverse action in Cho v. Dwight David Eisenhower Army Med. Ctr., 8 MSPR 52, 56, 7 MSPB 548 (1981). But when a charge against an employee was not sustained and a prima facie case of discrimination was established, the stated basis for the agency action--although not sustained--was not considered pretextual when the only other evidence of discrimination was a supervisor's use of a disparaging remark a year and seven months prior to the proposal of the removal.
