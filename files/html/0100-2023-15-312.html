4. Medical Error

Wynne v. VA, 75 MSPR 127, 136 (1997), upheld removal of a health technician for actions that allegedly jeopardized care of patients entrusted to his care. See Wallace v. DHHS, 89 MSPR 178, 184 ¶¶ 16-17 (2001) (discharge for the unauthorized administration of a medical test that may have involved risk for the patient or supervising clinician); Taylor v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 450, 455-56 ¶¶ 7-8 (1998) (adverse action for, among other reasons, the use of an incorrect laboratory practice that could have resulted in the misdiagnosis or incorrect treatment of patients); McLaughlin v. Dept. of Army, 34 MSPR 334, 344 (1987) (GS-12 Dental Officer was removed for the substandard dental treatment of patients; another charge sustained was that the appellant continued, despite counseling, to make errors on worksheets used for performance evaluation and manpower utilization).

The Board suggested that adverse action could be appropriate for one instance of improper administration of medication. Giltner v. Dept. of Air Force, 39 MSPR 253, 259-60 (1988) (remanding for reconsideration; performance standards permitted no more than one error in drug administration every six months and no error exposing a patient to a potentially life-threatening situation). Removal of a nurse was sustained for a combination of medication errors and failure to provide proper assistance to patients. Ware v. VA, 76 MSPR 427, 435 (1997) (agency could properly consider a prior work record, that the appellant was aware of the rules, that she was on a PIP at the time of the charged misconduct, and that her performance deficiencies were similar to the charged misconduct); see Giltner v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 209, 216 (1991) (removal mitigated to the suspension of a clinical nurse whose technical violation of sterile medication techniques was serious but did not cause harm to the patient; the appellant had a long prior service history with no discipline).

Recordkeeping errors by a nurse, although inadvertent, warranted removal if they concerned patient care and if there was been past counseling; however, given long service and job-related stress, reduction in grade and reassignment to nurse's aide or to a similar paraprofessional position was a more reasonable penalty. Gamble v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 11, 18 (1984). There was no basis for setting aside a suspension of a nurse who improperly failed to provide medication to inmates, even though the lack of medication did not cause harm to patients. The discipline was warranted by the unjustified failure to follow nursing practices and agency procedures rather than by harm caused. Harris v. DOJ, 29 MSPR 332, 333-34 (1985); see Lunny v. VA, 7 MSPR 654, 659 (1981) (removal of a medical technologist for failing to follow procedures applicable to the hospital's blood bank); cf. Rias v. VA, 11 MSPR 545, 547-48 (1982) (removal mitigated to suspension of a medical administrative assistant charged with refusal to assist a veteran in obtaining admission to a facility and failure to respond to a congressional inquiry concerning the veteran; the appellant believed his actions were in the best interests of the parties under the circumstances).
