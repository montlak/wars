3. Adverse Action, Performance Case

The AJ should specifically determine whether an adverse action promotes service efficiency under 5 USC 7513(a). Gantt v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 55, 57, 6 MSPB 57 (1981). The severity of the penalty is another issue requiring distinct analysis. The recitation of the efficiency of the service standard will not justify adverse action; each case must be considered on its own merits based on the evidence of record, including a discussion of the appropriateness of the penalty. Frisby v. IRS, 2 MSPR 146, 146-47, 2 MSPB 312 (1980). Allegations of harmful error require specific discussion. A statement by the AJ that, although it appeared that the agency "made several mistakes in the hiring and firing of" an appellant, "none were prejudicial to his rights" is too cryptic for review. Bilodeau v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3106). The analysis on ultimate issues should proceed in a logical fashion. An AJ should decide the case on the merits before treating an appellant's claim that disparate treatment warrants mitigation of the penalty. See Gadsden v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 74, 75-76 (1985). An AJ (and the Board) may rely upon a ground of decision not raised by the parties without first giving them notice of the proposed reliance and the opportunity to address the issue. Henderson v. Dept. of Interior, 202 F.3d 1356, 1360 (Fed. Cir. 2000).

The Board may relieve its AJs of the necessity to address all issues that may be presented by the appeal with a statement like that in Wagner v. EPA, 54 MSPR 447, 455 (1992), that "there is no requirement for an administrative judge to cite every possible alternative basis for the disposition of an appeal when the basis that he cites is legally sufficient to support his decision." See Schiller v. DOD, PH-0432-13-0143-I-1 (NP 1/28/2015) ("The administrative judge need not respond to every theory and speculation presented by the appellant in reaching her determination. See Marques, 22 M.S.P.R. at 132. Moreover, we find that none of the claimant's arguments are of sufficient weight to overturn the administrative judge's finding that his performance remained unacceptable in at least one critical element."). Although the better practice is for an AJ to state what specific legal factors (statutes or controlling cases) guide her analysis, if the result is correct and the analysis can fairly be discerned from the initial decision, the Board will not vacate the decision because it lacks precise legal citation or analysis. An example of the Board's approach in an adverse action appeal is provided by Coleman v. Dept. of Air Force, 66 MSPR 498, 506 (1995):

In addition, the appellant argues that the administrative judge did not articulate a legal standard that defined misrepresentation of a material fact in his initial decision, and that his failure to do so constitutes reversible error. To sustain a falsification charge, the agency must prove by preponderant evidence that the employee knowingly supplied incorrect information with the intention of defrauding, deceiving, or misleading the agency. See Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., 782 F.2d 975, 977 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Although the administrative judge did not specifically set out any legal standard in the initial decision, he correctly analyzed the evidence supporting the agency's charge of misrepresentation under the correct legal standard, and found that the appellant intentionally misrepresented material facts about his psychiatric history to Dr. Chakraburtty during an agency initiated medical evaluation....

Rejecting an assertion that an AJ did not address all issues presented in a furlough appeal, Snyder v. Dept. of Navy, 854 F.3d 1366, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2017), commented:

We also reject Ms. Snyder's argument that the AJ was required to summarize all of the arguments raised in her briefing. The mere fact that the AJ did not recount these arguments as thoroughly as Ms. Snyder would like does not mean that the AJ did not sufficiently consider them. See, e.g., Synopsys, Inc. v. Mentor Graphics Corp., 814 F.3d 1309, 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (noting that an agency is "not require[d]...to address every argument raised by a party or explain every possible reason supporting its conclusion"); Gonzales v. West, 218 F.3d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2000) ("[A]bsent specific evidence indicating otherwise, all evidence contained in the record...must be presumed to have been reviewed by [the agency]."); Medtronic, Inc. v. Daig Corp., 789 F.2d 903, 906 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("We presume that a fact finder reviews all the evidence presented unless [the fact finder] explicitly expresses otherwise.").

The adjudication of an adverse action requires three distinct determinations: (1) that the misconduct charge is sustained by a preponderance of the evidence; (2) that there is a sufficient nexus between the misconduct and the efficiency of the service; and (3) that the penalty imposed by the agency has been appropriately chosen in light of the specific facts and circumstances. Hall v. Dept. of Air Force, 16 MSPR 341, 342 (1983). If a case involves more than one charge, the AJ should analyze each charge, even though only one charge is considered sufficient to support the penalty. See Martinez v. DOD, 4 MSPR 15, 16, 4 MSPB 109 (1980). There are decisions concluding that secondary issues need not be decided if they are unnecessary to the result.
