2. Settlement

OSC has broad authority to enter into settlement agreements, and the Board defers to those settlements. Special Counsel v. Dept. of Transp., 75 MSPR 486, 488 (1997) (discussing the approval of a settlement in a corrective action case; when OSC enters into a settlement, it effectively requests the withdrawal of the corrective action petition); Special Counsel v. Heyel, 35 MSPR 402, 403 (1987) ($1,000 fine imposed in the settlement of a charge that the respondent influenced a candidate for a position to withdraw in order to improve the prospects of another candidate); Special Counsel v. Parker, 29 MSPR 659, 661 (1986) (settlement of a $1,000 fine and a reprimand against the respondent for influencing a candidate to withdraw from competition for a position); Special Counsel v. Evans-Hamilton, 29 MSPR 516 (1984) (settlement was accepted imposing a suspension for influencing an applicant to withdraw from competition for a position to improve the prospects of another person); Special Counsel v. Falt, 26 MSPR 569, 570-71 (1985) (concerning approval of an arrangement allowing the respondent in a disciplinary case to pay a $1,000 forfeiture in installments; the respondents waived the regulatory period for filing exceptions to an ALJ's recommended decision).

Settlements reached between OSC and the employing agencies may be accepted by the Board on the condition that advance notice of future personnel actions be given to the Board and to OSC to permit the renewal of petitions for stay of a personnel action. See In re Goldschmidt, 1 MSPR 118, 119, 1 MSPB 114 (1979). If a settlement is submitted to the Board's ALJ in an OSC case, the appellant may not withdraw from the settlement without a showing sufficient to vitiate any other Board settlement, e.g., fraud, duress, mutual mistake [the types of problems discussed in Chapter 16, "Setting Aside a Settlement"] See Special Counsel v. Jennings, 44 MSPR 1, 5 (1990) (refusing to permit withdrawal from a settlement that appeared substantively reasonable). A settlement may include a provision that a personnel action will not be the subject of future litigation. That provision will bar a future IRA appeal based on that personnel action. See Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 640 ¶¶ 99-100 (2001). Settlements involving OSC may be sealed pursuant to Board order. Special Counsel v. Woods, HQ12068510008 (1985) (unpublished regional decision). Although OSC has broad authority to enter into settlement agreements, the Board will not approve an agreement contrary to a statutory constraint. Special Counsel v. Doyle, 45 MSPR 43, 49 (1990). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Sealing of Record or Files."]

When considering an OSC settlement, the Board will review any penalty imposed in light of the Douglas factors to determine that it is within the bounds of reason. Special Counsel v. Ponce, 29 MSPR 385, 386 (1985). Other factors considered include the conservation of time, effort, and expense and whether any reasonable purpose is served by continued processing of the case. Special Counsel v. Judson, 43 MSPR 61, 62 (1989) (30-day suspension was imposed for influencing a candidate to withdraw from competition for a clerk typist job for the purpose of improving the prospects of another candidate); Special Counsel v. Wingard, 37 MSPR 179, 180 (1988) (through the settlement of a sexual harassment complaint, the respondent was debarred from federal employment for three years); see Special Counsel v. Hove, 46 MSPR 1, 12 (1990) (approving a settlement imposing a $750 civil fine for recommending the employment of a relative in violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(7)). The settlement may include counsel fees for the complainant's representative. See Special Counsel v. Customs Serv., 76 MSPR 687, 688 (1997) (corrective action case was settled, calling for the cancelation of a personnel action and the payment to complainant's counsel of approximately $55,000 in fees).

If OSC and the respondent reach a settlement, the Board will entertain OSC's motion to dismiss the underlying disciplinary complaint; the dismissal should be granted unless the respondents will suffer clear legal prejudice. Special Counsel v. Parra, 49 MSPR 123, 125 (1991). Cases have been settled through a dismissal with a reservation by OSC of the right to refile a complaint should the respondent fail to comply with the agreement. See Special Counsel v. Goewert, 64 MSPR 320, 321 (1994). As with other cases, settlements of Special Counsel litigation may be filed with the Board for purposes of enforcement, or they may be unfiled. See e.g., Special Counsel v. Catchings, 68 MSPR 593 (1995) (Table) (Hatch Act case: "It appears that the parties submitted the agreement merely to advise the Board they have settled the matter and that the case is now moot. They did not request that the Board adopt the agreement into the record for the purpose of enforcement."); cf. Special Counsel v. Reckard, 77 MSPR 651, 653 (1998) (Hatch Act complaint was dismissed as moot when the penalty imposed by settlement was already served and the Board could impose no greater penalty).

The Board may interpret OSC settlements in a manner designed to achieve fairness rather than literal compliance. In a settlement entered in an OSC disciplinary action by an employee who agreed to a suspension but then retired on disability, the Board accepted the settlement and ordered that the suspension be deferred and served if the employee was restored to duty or became reemployed. Special Counsel v. Julian, 28 MSPR 95, 97 (1985). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Enforcement and Interpretation of Settlement Agreements."]
