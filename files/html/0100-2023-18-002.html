A. HARMFUL ERROR DEFINED

Adverse actions and other cases subject to Board appeal are sufficiently complex, involving the efforts of so many people, some more skilled in legal niceties than others, that close inspection will likely disclose some error in the process. Agencies are expected to conform to the regulatory procedures they adopt, Dewberry v. OPM, 31 MSPR 132, 134 (1986), and Parker v. OPM, 75 MSPR 688, 693 (1997), but civil service law does not mandate reversal of every action involving a regulatory or procedural error. The question of how much error is sufficient to invalidate an action against an employee is the subject of harmful error. The term itself is taken from the Reform Act, 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(A), providing: "[T]he agency's decision may not be sustained...if the employee...shows harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision." The burden of proof is on the appellant, Allred v. Dept. of Army, DC-0752-13-3062-I-1 (NP 6/11/2015):

...An agency error is harmful only where the record shows that the procedural error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 681, 685 (1991). The Board may not assume that an employee has been harmed by a procedural error in the adverse action process; rather, the appellant bears the burden of proving harm. Pumphrey v. Department of Defense, 122 M.S.P.R. 186, ¶ 10 (2015).

...

The appellant's apparent contention that she would have been exempted absent the agency's error is at best speculative, and the Board has held that such speculation does not constitute harm....

The decisions that follow apply the rule in a variety of contexts. Although it has been remarked that the harmful error rule incorporated into the Reform Act was a revision of earlier law (that supposedly permitted the invalidation of removals for minor technical procedural defects), many earlier decisions essentially concluded that removals could be invalidated only if a procedural violation was substantial and prejudicial. E.g., Joyce v. United States, 2 Ct. Cl. 226, 228 (1983); Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 278 (D.C. Cir. 1977); Cruz-Casado v. United States, 553 F.2d 672, 675 (Ct. Cl. 1977); Pascal v. United States, 543 F.2d 1284 (Ct. Cl. 1976); Rasmussen v. United States, 543 F.2d 134 (Ct. Cl. 1976); Bray v. United States, 515 F.2d 1383 (Ct. Cl. 1975); Henderson v. United States, 175 Ct. Cl. 690 (1966); Service v. Dulles, 354 U.S. 363 (1957). Nevertheless, the intent of Congress was to reduce the impact of procedural requirements, and the result is reluctance by the Board to overturn agency decisions on the basis of procedural violations. See Blackford v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 712, 713 (1981) (harmful error language was adopted to avoid the reversal of agency actions because of technical procedural oversights resulting in no substantial prejudice to, or impairment of, an employee's rights).

The harmful error rule is statutorily-based. The statute governing Board adjudication, 5 USC 7701(c)(1), states that the Board will sustain an agency action proved by preponderant evidence, but:

(2) Notwithstanding paragraph (1), the agency's decision may not be sustained under subsection (b) of this section if the employee or applicant for employment--

(A) shows harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision.

The Board implements the statute through its regulation, 5 CFR 1201.4(r) (2022):

Harmful error. Error by the agency in the application of its procedures that is likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. The burden is upon the appellant to show that the error was harmful, i.e., that it caused substantial harm or prejudice to his or her rights. [formerly 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(3) (2014)]

An allegation of harmful error does not create Board jurisdiction. Harmful error is an affirmative defense to an action otherwise within the Board's jurisdiction. See Pinsonneault v. Dept. of Army, NY-315H-17-0203-I-1 (NP 5/19/2022) ("Because the Board has no jurisdiction over the appellant's termination as an otherwise appealable action, the Board has no independent jurisdiction to adjudicate her harmful error claims. Hurston v. Department of the Army, 113 M.S.P.R. 34, ¶ 11 (2010)."). "Harmful error can never be presumed, even when an agency fails to afford the employee any of the procedural protections of section 7513." Drummonds v. VA, 58 MSPR 579, 585 n.6 (1993); see Simmons v. Dept. of Air Force, 99 MSPR 28, 43 ¶ 32 (2005) ("Harmful error cannot be presumed; the burden of showing harmful error is on the appellant."); Hidalgo v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 645, 653 ¶ 13 (2003) (alleged error in designation of investigating officer: harmful procedural error cannot be presumed; an agency error is harmful only if the record shows that the procedural error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error). The burden is on the appellant to prove harmful error by preponderant evidence. 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1) and 5 CFR 1201.4(r) (2022); Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 242 (1995); cf. Shaffer v. DOJ, DA-0752-15-0466-I-2 (NP 4/25/2023) ("We cannot determine on this record whether the agency committed a procedural error. Neither party has submitted a copy of the rules or regulations governing the agency's DRB, and so it is impossible to know whether the agency's decision to close the appellant's DRB appeal violated the rules of the DRB program or was, in fact, consistent with those rules. Because there is no evidence establishing what procedure the agency allegedly violated, we find that the appellant failed to show that the agency committed procedural error. Therefore, the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant failed to prove his affirmative defense of harmful error."). Allegations of harmful error should first be addressed to the AJ. The Board will not consider an allegation of harmful procedural error raised for the first time in a PFR unless there is new and material evidence. Kline v. DHHS, 22 MSPR 326, 328 (1984). But see Lethgo v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-10-1037-I-1 (NP 11/2/11) (appeal remanded when, as material to the claim of a wrongful separation, the appellant noted in his appeal the failure of the agency to place him in a priority placement program but failed to check a box on the appeal form that he was asserting harmful error; the asserted error was not raised in the prehearing conference, but the appellant was not informed of his burden of proof). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of New Evidence," concerning PFRs.]

The Board's harmful error interpretation is generally judicially accepted: for there to be harmful error warranting reversal of a Board decision, the error must substantially impair an employee's rights. Dorrance v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 735 F.2d 516 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In administrative disciplinary proceedings, if a removal is based on substantial evidence and conforms to the law, technical deviations from procedures do not invalidate the action if there is no prejudice shown. Boylan v. USPS, 704 F.2d 573 (11th Cir. 1983). The Supreme Court reviewed the Board's harmful error test in a case that originated in arbitration, Cornelius v. Nutt, 105 S. Ct. 2882 (1985). The Court's discussion of the harmful error provision of the Reform Act suggested that because the provision is to benefit the employer, a construction is not favored that provides particular benefit to an employee who identifies nonprejudicial procedural error:

The Act does not define the term "harmful error,"[9] and the legislative history of § 7701(c)(2)(A) is inconclusive.[10] The Act provides, however, that the Board "may prescribe regulations to carry out the purpose of [§ 7701]," the provision in which the harmful-error rule appears. See § 7701(j). Pursuant to this authority, the Board has promulgated a definition of "harmful error":

"Error by the agency in the application of its procedures which, in the absence or cure of the error, might have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different than the one reached. The burden is upon the appellant to show that based upon the record as a whole the error was harmful, i.e., caused substantial harm or prejudice to his/her rights." 5 CFR § 1201.56(c)(3) (1985).[11]

[9] It would be natural, however, to assume that Congress intended the term "harmful error" in § 7701(c)(2)(A) to have the same meaning that it has in the judicial context, that is, error that has some likelihood of affecting the result of the proceeding. See, e.g., United States v. Hasting, 461 U.S. 499, 507-509, 103 S. Ct. 1974, 1979-1980, 76 L. Ed.2d 96 (1983); Kotteakos v. United States, 328 U.S. 750, 760-762, 66 S. Ct. 1239, 1245-1246, 90 L. Ed. 1557 (1946).

[10] The original Senate version of the bill that became the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 provided that "agency action shall be upheld by Board, the administrative law judge, or the appeals officer unless--(A) the agency's procedures contained error that substantially impaired the rights of the employee." See S. Rep. No. 95-969, p. 224 (1978); see also id., at 179. The Senate Report explains: "Henceforth, the Board and the courts should only reverse agency actions under the new procedures where the employee's rights under this title have been substantially prejudiced." Id., at 51. See also id., at 54, 64. The Senate Report does not refer directly to the application of the harmful-error rule in an arbitration. The Report, however, does state that in "the negotiated grievance procedure an arbitrator must apply the same standards in deciding the case as would be applied...if the case had been appealed through the appellate procedures of 5 U.S.C. section 7701." Id., at 111. Thus, it is clear that the Senate version of the harmful-error rule focused on the rights of the employee and did not suggest affirmatively that the Board or an arbitrator could take into account the rights of the union. The Conference Committee did not adopt the Senate version. Petitioner points out that the Joint Explanatory Statement of the Committee on Conference, which explained "the effect of the major actions agreed upon by the managers" of the two bodies, H.R. Conf. Rep. No. 95-1717, p. 127 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 78, p. 2860, did not note that any substantive change in meaning was intended by the change in language. We decline, however, to infer congressional intent to adopt the substance of the Senate version solely on the basis of this legislative silence.

[11] Similarly, in Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 M.S.P.B. 489, 493, 1 M.S.P.R. 505, 513 (1980), the Board explained: "Unless it is likely that an alleged error affected the result, its occurrence cannot have been prejudicial.... Stated another way, the question is whether it was within the range of appreciable probability that the error had a harmful effect upon the outcome before the agency." See also, e.g., Davies v. Department of the Navy, 4 M.S.P.B. 83, 85, 3 M.S.P.R. 565, 568 (1980); Fuiava v. Department of Justice, 3 M.S.P.B. 217, 218, 3 M.S.P.R. 103, 106 (1980).

The agency's "procedures" considered by the Board in applying § 7701(c)(2)(A) include not only procedures required by statute, rule, or regulation,[12] but also procedures required by a collective-bargaining agreement between the agency and a union.[13] Thus, in an appeal of an agency disciplinary decision to the Board, the agency's failure to follow bargained-for procedures may result in its action's being overturned, but only if the failure might have affected the result of the agency's decision to take the disciplinary action against the individual employee. At least insofar as it applies to proceedings before the Board, this interpretation of the harmful-error rule is entitled to substantial deference.[14] See Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. The burden of establishing harmful error is on the employee. The Reform Act does not impose on the MSPB an affirmative duty to investigate whether an agency has complied with required procedural formalities. A fundamental principle in administrative procedure is that the burden to rebut the presumption of regularity applying to agency actions is with the challenging party. Arterberry v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 83-1202). "Harmful error cannot be presumed; an agency error is harmful only where the record shows that the procedural error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error." Kimm v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 198, 208 (1994); see Salter v. Dept. of Treasury, 92 MSPR 355, 359 ¶ 7 (2002) ("[T]o show harmful error, an appellant must prove that any procedural error substantially prejudiced his rights by possibly affecting the agency's decision[.] "); Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 242 (1995) ("Reversal of an action is warranted only where a procedural error, whether regulatory or statutory, is likely to have had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency."); cf. Kaplan v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-0432-17-0195-I-1 (NP 10/6/2022) ("Thus, procedures unrelated to the processing of an appellant's removal cannot be challenged under the harmful error analysis. Livingston v. Department of the Air Force, 26 M.S.P.R. 273, 275 (1985).").
