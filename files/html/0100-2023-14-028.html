c. Law Enforcement Officer; Giglio Impairment

A law enforcement officer with a position of great trust and responsibility--e.g., a criminal investigator--must conform to a higher standard of conduct than employees who are not responsible for law enforcement. Quander v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 419, 421 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 180 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Rodriguez v. DHS, 314 Fed. Appx. 318, 321 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (noting decisions holding law enforcement officers to a higher standard of conduct than are other federal employees); Wiggins v. Nat'l Gallery of Art, 980 F.2d 1436 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (security guard was held to standards "above those borne by ordinary employees"); Bidnick v. DOJ, AT-0752-14-0060-I-1 (NP 4/15/2015) (criminal investigator: "Regarding the nature and seriousness of the offense and its relation to the appellant's duties, position, and responsibilities, the Board has long held that offenses touching on honesty and integrity are especially serious when it comes to law enforcement officers, who are held to a higher standard of conduct to begin with. E.g., Phillips v. Department of the Interior, 95 M.S.P.R. 21, ¶ 16 (2003), aff'd, 131 F. App'x 709 (Fed. Cir. 2005); Childs v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 348, 356 (1995); Austin v. Department of Justice, 11 M.S.P.R. 255, 259 (1982). The Board places primary importance on this penalty factor. Jones v. Department of the Interior, 97 M.S.P.R. 282, ¶ 13 (2004)."); Cantu v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 253, 257 ¶ 8 (2001) (canine enforcement team leader: "higher standard of conduct and degree of trust are required of an incumbent of a position with law enforcement duties"); Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 241 (1995) (rule applied to an OIG criminal investigator); Beck v. DOJ, 67 MSPR 219, 224 (1995) (correctional treatment specialist); Yeager v. GSA, 39 MSPR 147, 150 (1988) (FPO: "[a]s a law-enforcement officer, he was obligated to conform to a higher standard of conduct than non-law enforcement employees"); Galloway v. DOJ, 29 MSPR 200, 200-01 n.* (1985) (rule applied to a correctional officer); Bragg v. VA, 29 MSPR 61, 63 n* (1985) ("The Board has repeatedly emphasized that a law enforcement officer must conform to a higher standard of conduct than other employees."); Boyd v. DOJ, 14 MSPR 427, 432 (1983) (FBI special agents are held to high standard of conduct).

Of criminal activity by a federal law enforcement official, the Federal Circuit remarked, Martinez v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1269):

We are especially reluctant to interfere with an agency's decision to take adverse action against law enforcement officers charged with committing a crime. See Masino v. United States, 589 F.2d 1048 (Ct. Cl. 1978). The reluctance stems not only from the notion that off-duty criminal misconduct dampens the efficiency of the service in an economic sense, but from the realization that an agency responsible for law enforcement relies heavily on its reputation for honesty and integrity. The agency may reasonably determine that the efficiency of its service to the public would deteriorate should it cast a blind eye on the malefactions of its own employees.

The designation of a position or individual as a law enforcement officer is not dependent on the appellant's eligibility for LEO retirement credit. Jones v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 91, 96 n.2 (2000). Positions not classified as law enforcement positions, but analogous to them, e.g., security officers, may be held to a higher standard as well. Weekes v. DHS, 351 Fed. Appx. 442, 445 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (citing Padilla v. DOJ, 64 MSPR 416, 423 (1994); Jones v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 501, 506-07 (1992) (Customs and Border Patrol officer).

The highest degree of professional conduct and ethics may reasonably be expected of a law enforcement officer, especially one who works undercover. See Dikiy v. Dept. of Treasury, 23 MSPR 454, 457 (1984) (upholding a suspension for, among other charges, maintaining a close personal friendship with a confidential informant even though the appellant knew he was a convicted felon). Unlawful conduct by a law enforcement officer (deputy marshal) during official business has an obvious nexus to disciplinary action. Brown v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-785); see Morones v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 285, 293 (1987) (adverse action against a detention and deportation officer who, in violation of the statute he was supposed to enforce, engaged in an off-duty relationship with an alien). A supervisory police officer has a responsibility both to uphold the law and to maintain appropriate standards of performance and integrity among his subordinates. Morgan v. Dept. of Army, 934 F.2d 310 (Fed. Cir. 1991). The conduct standards for law enforcement officers extend to positions that are analogous to law enforcement positions, e.g., security officers or, in Padilla v. DOJ, 64 MSPR 416, 423 (1994), a Bureau of Prisons electronics technician who was required to follow agency security regulations. Cf. Lindsay v. DOJ, 9 MSPR 55, 57 (1981) (paralegal specialist with the Citizen Complaint Center was removed for failure to cooperate in an agency investigation and for falsifying facts in an investigation concerning the alleged theft of the appellant's privately owned vehicle; the Board accepted a finding of nexus by the AJ "because appellant was in a sensitive position which required the independent exercise of judgment and appellant's misconduct was such as to indicate poor judgment").

Under Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972), exculpatory evidence is to be provided to the defense, including evidence affecting the reliability or credibility of a law enforcement witness. If the witness is a federal law enforcement officer, adverse information concerning that individual's credibility may limit his usefulness as an employee whose duties include testifying in criminal proceedings. The Board described the characterization of a "Giglio-impaired" law enforcement agent, Cameron v. DOJ, 100 MSPR 477, 482 n.1 (2005):

Under Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972), investigative agencies must turn over to prosecutors, as early as possible in a case, potential impeachment evidence with respect to the agents involved in the case. The prosecutor then exercises his discretion as to whether the impeachment evidence must be turned over to the defense. A "Giglio-impaired" agent is one against whom there is potential impeachment evidence that would render the agent's testimony of marginal value in a case, which means, of course, that a case that depends primarily on the testimony of a Giglio-impaired witness, is at risk. See Hathaway v. Department of Justice, 384 F.3d 1342, 1349 (Fed. Cir. 2004).

Prather v. DOJ, 117 MSPR 137, 149 n.3, 2011 MSPB 100 (2011), also characterized Giglio impairment:

Under Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972), investigative agencies must turn over to prosecutors potential impeachment evidence with respect to the agents involved in the case. The prosecutor then exercises his discretion as to whether the impeachment evidence must be turned over to the defense. See Rodriguez v. Department of Homeland Security, 108 M.S.P.R. 76, ¶ 29 n.3 (2008), aff'd, 314 Fed. Appx. 318 (Fed. Cir. 2009), overruled on other grounds by Thomas v. U.S. Postal Service, 116 M.S.P.R. 453 (2011). A "Giglio impaired" agent is one against whom there is potential impeachment evidence that would render the agent's testimony of marginal value in a case. Id.

Agencies may remove a law enforcement officer based on misconduct that may be discovered by defense counsel in criminal proceedings and, upon discovery, may be used to impeach the officer's testimony. See Carlton v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 633, 638 ¶ 8 (2004) (removal of criminal investigator for off-duty misconduct: "the appellant's guilty plea could be used against him if he is called to testify at a trial in which he is involved"); Hidalgo v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 645, 652 ¶ 10 (2003) ("in its decision letter, the agency noted that, because he made false statements under oath during an official investigation, the appellant became vulnerable to impeachment in a legal proceeding, a factor that undermined his ability to testify credibly on behalf of the agency"); Micali v. Dept. of Treasury, 56 MSPR 127, 133 (1992) (removal of GS-13 Criminal Investigator responsible for planning and conducting tax evasion cases: "the appellant's position required him to testify in court against alleged tax evaders, and the charged conduct...would be a basis for impeaching his testimony and compromising such tax cases."); Betts v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 37, 42 (1984) (off-duty offense involving the possession of cocaine and removal were sufficiently linked by the testimony of supervisors that appellant's job as a federal police officer required him to testify in court and that the incident eroded their trust and confidence in him); Antonucci v. DOJ, 8 MSPR 491, 494 (1981) (charge of malicious destruction of a suspect's property during the execution of a search warrant, the Board accepted the agency argument that "appellant, as a law enforcement officer, was frequently called on to testify in federal court and that his integrity and credibility were 'absolutely critical' to effective performance of his duties."); cf. Murray v. DOJ, 821 F. Supp. 94, 106 (E.D. N.Y. 1993) (Title VII suit challenging the dismissal of a FBI agent; the court surveys cases allowing or refusing criminal defense counsel to conduct discovery of law enforcement officers' personnel records to determine whether there is some evidence of perjurious conduct or other dishonesty).

Hernandez v. DHS, 324 Fed. Appx. 908, 910-11 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), stated as to an appeal involving a criminal investigator:

With respect to the removal penalty, the board independently balanced the relevant Douglas factors, including Hernandez's disciplinary history, satisfactory performance reviews, the gravity of her misconduct, and its relation to the efficiency of the service. In determining that the penalty of removal was within the tolerable limits of reasonableness, the board found that Hernandez's lack of prior disciplinary actions and satisfactory performance reviews could not counterbalance the seriousness of her misconduct and her supervisor's loss of trust in her integrity and honesty. A very high standard of honesty and credibility is expected of law enforcement officers because of the public trust and confidence attendant to their job responsibilities. Moreover, Hernandez's record of false statements could affect her credibility if she needed to testify in court as a government witness. See Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150, 154 (1972). Finally, because Hernandez did not acknowledge any wrongdoing or express any regret for her actions, the board found her likelihood for rehabilitation slight. Because the board applied the appropriate legal standards, and substantial evidence supports its conclusions, its decision must stand.

If an agency relies on Giglio impairment to remove a law enforcement officer, the proposal notice should identify the impairment as an aggravating factor. Failure to provide proper notice of the alleged impairment in the proposal, and reliance upon it by the deciding official, resulted in reversal of a removal for lack of minimum constitutional due process guarantees in Solis v. DOJ, 117 MSPR 458, 461-63 ¶¶ 6-10, 2012 MSPB 21 (2012):

On review, the appellant argues, inter alia, that the administrative judge erred when she determined that the appellant failed to establish his affirmative defense of harmful procedural error, especially with respect to his argument that the deciding official improperly relied on Giglio issues in sustaining his removal. However, we find that the appellant's argument with respect to the agency's consideration of Giglio issues is properly analyzed first as a claim that the agency denied him minimum due process. See Silberman v. Department of Labor, 116 M.S.P.R. 501, ¶¶ 4-8 (2011).

When an agency intends to rely on aggravating factors as the basis for the imposition of a penalty, such factors should be included in the advance notice of adverse action so that the employee will have a fair opportunity to respond to those factors before the agency's deciding official. Lopes v. Department of the Navy, 116 M.S.P.R. 470, ¶ 5 (2011). Our reviewing court has explained that, if an employee has not been given "notice of any aggravating factors supporting an enhanced penalty," an ex parte communication with the deciding official regarding such factors may constitute a constitutional due process violation because it potentially deprives the employee of notice of all the evidence being used against him and the opportunity to respond to it. Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274, 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2011). Furthermore, in determining whether a due process violation has occurred, there is no basis for distinguishing between ex parte information provided to the deciding official and information personally known by the deciding official, if the information was considered in reaching the decision and not previously disclosed to the appellant. Lopes, 116 M.S.P.R. 470, ¶¶ 10-13.

However, not every ex parte communication rises to the level of a due process violation; only ex parte communications that introduce new and material information to the deciding official constitute due process violations. Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1999). The question is whether the ex parte communication is "so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that no employee can fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances." Ward, 634 F.3d at 1279 (citations omitted). The Board will consider the following factors, among others, to determine whether an ex parte contact is constitutionally impermissible: (1) whether the ex parte communication merely introduces "cumulative" information or new information; (2) whether the employee knew of the information and had a chance to respond to it; and (3) whether the ex parte communications were of the type likely to result in undue pressure upon the deciding official to rule in a particular manner. See Blank v. Department of the Army, 247 F.3d 1225, 1229 (Fed. Cir. 2001). A due process violation is not subject to the harmful error test; instead, the employee is automatically entitled to a new constitutionally correct removal proceeding. Ward, 634 F.3d at 1279.

Here, the agency did not provide the appellant notice regarding Giglio issues in the notice of proposed removal. In his written evaluation of the penalty determination factors set forth in Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305-06 (1981), however, the deciding official listed the appellant's Giglio issues as an aggravating factor in assessing the removal penalty, which contributed in part to his finding that the appellant had no potential for rehabilitation. In so finding, the deciding official observed that the appellant's attorney failed to set forth any arguments in the appellant's written reply to the proposal notice to explain how the agency would overcome the Giglio issues facing the appellant, despite the agency's failure to provide him proper notice of this matter. The deciding official's hearing testimony corroborated his written evaluation of the Douglas factors.

The agency's reliance on Giglio issues in imposing the appellant's removal without providing him notice and an opportunity to respond to those issues cannot fairly be deemed cumulative or immaterial to the deciding official's decision. See Stone, 179 F.3d at 1376-77. Thus, the agency violated the appellant's due process rights by denying him notice of the specific information considered and an opportunity to respond. See Silberman, 116 M.S.P.R. 501, ¶ 14; see also Gray v. Department of Defense, 116 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶¶ 9-13 (2011) (finding that a Ward due process violation occurred when the deciding official considered the appellant's likely loss of eligibility for a sensitive position as an aggravating factor without notice to the appellant). Consequently, because the agency violated the appellant's due process rights, the appellant's removal must be reversed and he must be afforded a "new constitutionally correct removal procedure." Ward, 634 F.3d at 1280. Based on our disposition, we need not address the appellant's other arguments on review.

Giglio impairment alone supports adverse action, without its consideration as an aggravating factor accompanying another charge, Nguyen v. DHS, 737 F.3d. 711, 715-19 (Fed. Cir. 2013):

In Giglio, the Supreme Court held that criminal defendants have a due process right to be informed of evidence affecting a government witness's credibility. Giglio, 405 U.S. at 154. It required that prosecutors establish procedures to ensure that such exculpatory evidence is identified and disclosed to the defendant.[3] Id.; see also Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83, 87 (1963) (holding that suppression of material exculpatory evidence justifies a new trial "irrespective of the good faith or bad faith of the prosecution"). Suppression of such impeachment evidence requires a new trial if it could "in any reasonable likelihood" have affected the jury's judgment. Giglio, 405 U.S. at 154 (internal quotation marks and citation omitted). The Ninth Circuit has applied Giglio to hold that prosecutors must examine personnel files of a law enforcement witness they intend to call at trial for evidence affecting the witness's credibility. United States v. Henthorn, 931 F.2d 29, 30-31 (9th Cir. 1991); accord Milke v. Ryan, 711 F.3d 998, 1003−07 (9th Cir. 2013).

[3] In Giglio, the Supreme Court reversed a conviction and remanded for a new trial when the prosecutor did not disclose to the defendant that a government witness had been promised he would not be prosecuted if he cooperated with the government. 405 U.S. at 153−54.

The USAO [United States Attorney's Office] is responsible for ensuring compliance with Giglio, and is not required to rely on a law enforcement witness that it deems is Giglio impaired. See Giglio, 405 U.S. at 154 (stating that improper nondisclosure "is the responsibility of the prosecutor"); see also Kahn v. Dep't of Justice, 528 F.3d 1336, 1340 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (characterizing Giglio determinations as the prosecutor's responsibility). In this case, the USAO established procedures pursuant to Giglio and Henthorn requiring that Deportation Officers such as Mr. Nguyen must "be free of any findings or investigations that impair their credibility." The USAO found Mr. Nguyen was Giglio impaired based on his admission during the OPR investigation that he made false statements to the police.

This situation is analogous to cases where a third-party agency revokes a credential or qualification necessary to perform the employee's position. In such cases, the Board takes the independent agency's action at face value, and reviews the employing agency's response to that action. For instance, in Shoffner v. Department of the Interior, 9 M.S.P.R. 265, 267 (1981), the agency removed Mr. Shoffner for failing to remain qualified for his position as a Hydrologic Technician due to a one-year revocation of his driver's license. The Board did not consider whether or not the revocation was correct, but rather reasoned that removal was appropriate because Mr. Shoffner "could no longer operate a government motor vehicle because the possession of a valid state motor vehicle operator's license was a prerequisite to the operation of the government vehicle." Id. This made Mr. Shoffner unable to perform his duties as a Hydrologic Technician, "which required driving a vehicle alone approximately 1,000 miles per month." Id.; see also Penland v. Dep't of Interior, 115 M.S.P.R. 474, 476 (2010) (affirming the agency's removal of a pilot who failed to maintain his pilot authorization).[7]

[7] This case differs from situations where the agency itself decides to revoke a necessary credential. In those cases, the Board typically reviews the underlying decision to, e.g., revoke medical certification or procurement authority. See, e.g., McGillivray v. Fed. Emerg. Mgmt. Agency, 58 M.S.P.R. 398 (1993) (agency revoked procurement authority); Siegert v. Dep't of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 684 (1988) (agency revoked clinical privileges); Cosby v. Fed. Aviation Admin., 30 M.S.P.R. 16 (1986) (agency revoked medical certification); but see Kaplan v. Conyers, No. 2011-3207, 2013 WL 4417583, (Fed. Cir. Aug. 20, 2013) (en banc) (Board lacks jurisdiction to review agency's finding that an employee is ineligible to occupy noncritical sensitive position).

Mr. Nguyen argues that this case is not analogous to a licensing situation, because his position does not require a license. Additionally, unlike the public standards for obtaining a driver's license, Mr. Nguyen argues the USAO's Giglio determination "is devoid of any objective standard and stands essentially without sufficient articulation or explanation." However, Mr. Nguyen's position as a Deportation Officer expressly requires testifying and swearing out complaints, and the USAO's Giglio determination prevents him from performing these duties. As in third-party licensing situations, the Agency lacks authority to contest the USAO's decision. The Board did not err in holding the Agency did not violate Mr. Nguyen's due process rights.

Although Giglio issues ordinarily arise in appeals by law enforcement agents, similar considerations were applied in the appeal of a chemist charged with off-duty misconduct that the agency contended impaired his ability to testify in court, as required in his position description. Bland v. DHHS, CH-0752-13-0078-I-1 (NP 2/7/2014).

The Board has not explained how an agency must prove Giglio impairment. Agencies asserting impairment may try to rely on no more than an opinion of a proposing or deciding official that the impairment would occur if the appellant were retained and later testified in a proceeding presenting the possibility of a credibility challenge to the appellant's testimony. The opinion of the proposing or deciding official is speculative. Stronger evidence is a written statement or testimony from a U.S. Attorney's office that Giglio impairment would likely arise from the actions of the appellant. If that statement is obtained from the federal prosecutor's office, the Board will not reevaluate the prosecutor's opinion, Taylor v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-0752-12-0296-I-1 (NP 5/24/2013):

In her initial decision, the administrative judge sustained both of the agency's charges based upon the appellant's admission that she created and signed the Driving While Intoxicated (DWI) arrest documentation that she knew contained materially inaccurate and untrue statements about how the arrest transpired. The administrative judge also sustained the agency's charge of unacceptable conduct based, in part, upon the determination of the United States Attorney's Office (USAO) that the appellant's aforementioned misconduct prevented the USAO from calling her as a credible government witness in any future case.[3] The administrative judge found no support for the appellant's claim that she was denied due process because she was not allowed to appeal the USAO's determination.

[3] The effect of the USAO's letter was that the appellant was "Giglio-impaired." See Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972) (the testimony of a federal law enforcement officer can be impeached based upon a showing that she possesses poor character for truthfulness, and the government must disclose in any criminal prosecution those officers whose sworn testimony may lack credibility).

On review, the appellant alleges, as she did below, that she was denied due process when she was not allowed to appeal the USAO's determination that she was "Giglio-impaired," which served as a basis for the agency's unacceptable conduct charge against her. When an agency intends to rely on aggravating factors, such as the USAO's Giglio determination, as the basis for the imposition of a penalty, such factors should be included in the advance notice of adverse action so that the employee will have a fair opportunity to respond to those factors before the agency's deciding official. See Lopes v. Department of the Navy, 116 M.S.P.R. 470, ¶ 5 (2011). However, the Board has stated that (1) it lacks authority, as does any agency other than the Department of Justice, over the USAO; (2) it cannot address the limitations that the USAO has placed on an employee in Giglio determinations; and (3) an agency must still comply with the Giglio limitations. Doe v. Department of Justice, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 16 (2003).

The agency was bound by the USAO's decision that the appellant was Giglio-impaired, and it determined that such impairment prevented the appellant from fully performing her duties as a police officer. See Doe, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 16. The agency appropriately notified the appellant in the notice of proposed removal that the USAO had determined the appellant was Giglio-impaired and that the agency viewed such impairment as support for the unacceptable conduct charge. After the appellant was given an opportunity to respond to the charges, the deciding official weighed the evidence and determined that the appellant's removal was appropriate and warranted. Accordingly, because the agency informed the appellant that the USAO's determination was an aggravating factor supporting her removal and provided the appellant with an opportunity to respond to the removal charges, the administrative judge properly concluded that the agency did not violate the appellant's due process rights.[4]

DHS, SF-0752-11-0502-I-1 (NP 8/31/2012):

Albeit in a compliance setting, the Board has stated that it lacks authority, as does the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), over the USAO, that it cannot address the limitations that a component of DOJ has placed on an employee, and that DHS must adhere to those limitations as well. Doe v. Department of Justice, 95 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 16 (2003). The appellant urges that, unlike him, the appellant in Doe retained his grade and that there was no deprivation of his property interests, and that therefore Doe is inapplicable to this situation. The appellant has not shown, however, how that factor detracts from the Board's statement regarding its, and DHS's, authority over determinations of the USAO. As the administrative judge correctly found, neither Conyers nor Northover overruled Doe, and it remains good law. Similarly, the appellant's reliance on Lamour v. Department of Justice, 106 M.S.P.R. 366 (2007), and Siegert v. Department of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 684 (1988), is misplaced in that those cases also involve decisions made by the agency which is a party to the appeal. In sum, the administrative judge properly found that the agency provided the appellant with notice and an opportunity to respond to the charge against him, and did not deny him the essential requirements of due process by not allowing him to contest the USAO's determination. Cf. McGean v. National Labor Relations Board, 15 M.S.P.R. 49, 53 (1983) (even if the District of Columbia Court of Appeals acted on an unverified complaint in suspending attorney, an attorney advisor with the agency, such error did not subject the court's decision to collateral attack, and accordingly the Board was without authority to review the merits of the court's determination in deciding whether agency properly removed employee from his position).

Not all misconduct of a law enforcement officers rises to the level of Giglio impairment, as was explained concerning an incident that occurred during an extramarital affair, Burr v. DOJ, SF-0752-11-0505-I-1 (NP 5/7/2013) (DEA criminal investigator):

The deciding official also claimed that the appellant's misconduct would impair his ability to testify in criminal trials because the agency would be required to disclose this derogatory information to the defense prior to him testifying under Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), and to prosecutors under Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972). The agency's Giglio policy requires that prosecutors be provided with findings, or with credible allegations that are the subject of a pending investigation of misconduct, which reflect on the truthfulness or possible bias of the employee selected to testify; the prosecutor then exercises discretion as to whether this potential impeachment evidence must be turned over to the defense. See Cameron v. Department of Justice, 100 M.S.P.R. 477, ¶ 10 n.1 (2005). Such potential impeachment evidence may render a Giglio-impaired agent's testimony to be of marginal value, placing at risk any case that relies on such an impaired witness. Id.

Although the appellant apparently misled JM about his intent to leave his wife to be with her, the deciding official's testimony indicated that such behavior, as well as the fact of the appellant's illicit relationship, is neither directly actionable nor specifically proscribed under agency rules, and instead involved a personal issue between the appellant and JM. The deciding official also conceded in his testimony that the appellant was not charged with either lying or making false statements, misconduct that would clearly implicate the agency's responsibilities under Brady and Giglio. Indeed, the deciding official acknowledged that not every agent whose conduct the agency has found unbecoming is subject to testimonial impairment under Giglio, and he failed to explain in his testimony exactly what portion of the sustained misconduct would so impair the appellant.

Off-duty actions of a law enforcement officer that interfere with another federal agency's criminal investigation and that disrupt relations between the employing agency and the other investigative agency support a finding of nexus. Rooney v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1544), aff'g Rooney v. USDA, 30 MSPR 663, 664 (1986). Other off-the-job misconduct has serious implications as well. Shoplifting was sufficient to sustain removal of a law enforcement officer--a customs patrol officer--whose job "was to enforce the law, not to break it." Mojica-Otero v. Dept. of Treasury, 30 MSPR 46, 50 (1986); see Cooper v. USPS, 42 MSPR 174, 180 (1989) (USPS police officer removed for shoplifting); Martinez v. DOJ, 20 MSPR 40, 41 n.* (1984) (citing Austin v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 255, 257, 10 MSPB 221 (1982) (shoplifting-related removal was sustained; the amount of goods taken, less than $100, would not ordinarily warrant a presumption of nexus, but nexus was established as to the appellant, a law enforcement officer)); Quander, supra. But see Topper v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 69, 70 (1996) (mitigating to a suspension the removal of a law enforcement officer who shoplifted some clothing; facts taken from the dissenting opinion of Member Amador). Removal of a border patrol agent for off-duty shoplifting was sustained. It was an act of dishonesty by a person in a sensitive position significantly affecting that individual's reputation for honesty and integrity and therefore affecting the efficiency of the federal service. [Refer to adverse actions based on shoplifting charges in Chapter 8, "Petty Theft."]

If an employee is entrusted with a weapon, off-duty incidents reflecting negatively on his judgment in using that weapon are relevant to job performance and may form the basis for an adverse action. McCray v. Dept. of Army, 1 MSPR 460, 462, 1 MSPB 445 (1980) (removal of a lead security guard was sustained when he shot and killed another individual following an off-duty traffic incident; adverse action may be taken when employee is entrusted with a weapon and an off-duty incident reflects negatively on his judgment in using the weapon); see MacDonald v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 403, 404, 4 MSPB 459 (1980) (police officer was removed for being in a post exchange building after closing hours with a concealed firearm; the conduct reflected poor judgment). Johnson v. United States, 628 F.2d 187, 193 (D.C. Cir. 1980), said of misuse of a weapon during an altercation involving a BATF agent:

...Whether on duty or off, a law enforcement officer must maintain a high standard of conduct. Entrusted with special authority and armament, he is expected to remain calm under pressure. The capacity to think rationally and intelligently despite adverse circumstances is more than a desirable trait; its presence could mean life instead of death for the officer himself, his fellow officers, and innocent bystanders. Furthermore, a law enforcement officer must observe the letter of the law he has sworn to uphold. His behavior must be an example for the rest of society.

Nexus was establishing by an off-duty traffic confrontation involving the display of a weapon by a law enforcement officer, Rodriguez v. DOJ, NY-0752-10-0081-I-1 (NP 10/7/2011) (setting aside mitigation and sustaining removal):

[8] The administrative judge correctly determined that the agency proved the existence of a nexus between the appellant's misconduct and the efficiency of the federal service. Given the nature of the appellant's misconduct, his role as a law enforcement officer, the display of a weapon authorized by his position, and the need for the agency to trust the candor of its law enforcement officers, we discern no error in the administrative judge's finding of nexus. Doe v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 128, ¶ 20 (2010); Kruger v. Department of Justice, 32 M.S.P.R. 71, 74 (1987).

Sufficient nexus was shown for disciplinary action against a security police officer who became involved in an off-duty incident involving improper possession of a weapon and misrepresentation of his position with the government. His ability to perform duties involving use of a weapon and a need for honest reporting were placed at issue by the off-duty misconduct. Majette v. USPS, 16 MSPR 419, 425 (1983); see Anderson v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 611, 615 (1994) (sustaining suspension for improper display of law enforcement credentials and an assault on duty involving a private citizen registering a complaint against the appellant); Chandler v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 90, 96 (1982) (display of a weapon and credentials during an off-duty argument led the Board to sustain an agency-imposed suspension). Removal was sustained for "unbecoming conduct" involving a Border Patrol agent accused of unlawful possession of a gun. Kranz v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 630, 635 (1994) (examining Texas state law governing the possession of weapons), aff'd, Kranz v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3448). Nexus supported discipline of a supervisory correctional officer who entered a guilty plea to a disorderly conduct charge involving the display of a weapon that the officer was permitted to carry. There was an impact on the job: the display of a weapon during a dispute with a civilian adversely impacted the agency's confidence in the appellant's ability to deal with prison confrontations without resorting to deadly force. There was also an impact on the public: limited publicity, with a greater impact in a small community, led to a suspicion of authority and a decline in confidence in agency integrity among people who knew the appellant as a law enforcement officer, Stines v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 511, 514 (1984), but that result was reversed by Stines v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-630) (discussed earlier concerning off-duty threats and assaults). [Refer to Chapter 8, "Negligence" and "Mishandling or Improper Possession of Weapon."]

Off-duty use of a weapon that did not violate agency regulations (pertaining only to on-duty use of weapons) and that did not result in any charge by local law enforcement authorities was not worthy of discipline in Grafton v. Dept. of Treasury, 864 F.2d 140 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Removal of a BATF criminal investigator was reduced to a suspension following the Board's analysis of his hobby of buying, selling, and trading firearms. Oddo v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 483, 495 (1982). Although the Board sustained one specification of a technical violation of the Gun Control Act, there was considerable confusion or doubt concerning the meaning of the statutory provision and its application to the appellant. Appellant Oddo had an excellent service record. There was no evidence that the appellant's conduct was publicized or caused any harm to the reputation of the agency.

An off-duty and unauthorized false arrest of private citizens by a deputy marshal warranted dismissal. Connolly v. DOJ, 18 MSPR 39, 42 (1983). Connolly was reviewed in Connolly v. DOJ, 766 F.2d 507, 513 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The court opinion recited the facts: the employee drew a gun to effect an off-duty arrest, had the citizens he arrested jailed briefly pending disposition of the charges against them (apparently tampering with an auto or use of an auto without a proper registration), and verbally abused local police who investigated the incident. Sustaining the discharge, the court said of the incident:

Connolly's conduct in this bizarre fiasco was not only improper and in excess of his authority, but his rash behavior impinged on the working relationship between the Marshals Service and the local authorities and adversely affected the reputation of the Department of Justice and its Marshals Service. He demonstrated questionable judgment in drawing his weapon and in later refusing to back down in pursuing his charges when it should have been apparent to a reasonably prudent man that there were no reasonable grounds to believe the accused were guilty. The local police were called at the request of the accused who readily identified themselves and their conduct was explained by the owner of the car.... Connolly apparently "got his back up," insisted that his suspects be booked and charged, and refused to admit his error until the affair had dragged on unreasonably long, even though the local police were unconcerned with the incident and sought to reassure Connolly by vouching for the suspects. On the evening of the incident, Connolly's supervisor was informed over the telephone by the Falmouth police, that he "was on the borderline of being in protective custody for being intoxicated." This information later entered into the agency's decision to bring the removal charges. Finally, there was no excuse for his use of threatening, abusive, obscene language toward Ptl. Cusolito, the third reason for his discharge.... See Hayes v. Dept. of Navy, 727 F.2d 1535, 1539 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Masino v. United States, 589 F.2d 1048, 1057 (Ct. Cl. 1978).

See Jones v. Secretary, Dept. of Army, 512 F. Supp. 1397 (D. Kans. 1995) (affirming the removal of a security officer for a munitions plant who, among other charges, become embroiled in an off-duty dispute with local police); Haldeman v. Dept. of Treasury, 15 MSPR 482, 484 (1983) (criminal investigator was removed for what appeared to be an off-duty, improper search of an individual following the appellant's identification of himself as a special agent).

Sufficient basis for removing a border patrol agent existed on proof of off-duty obscene phone calls and a threat of violence against an individual. A favorable psychiatric report was insufficient to outweigh the agency's concerns about the credibility of the employee as a witness in law enforcement proceedings and about his future contacts with aliens. Barnhill v. DOJ, 10 MSPR 378, 381 (1982); see Capozzella v. FBI, 11 MSPR 552, 557 (1982) (off-duty misconduct, consisting of dressing up in women's clothing for unauthorized purposes was properly punishable by an adverse action against a counterintelligence agent, but many mitigating factors and a demonstration of disparity in the penalties called for mitigation of the penalty to demotion to the next lower non-special agent position). As to a correctional officer whose position required contact with women in a prison, off-duty misconduct affecting a woman was sufficient to sustain removal of the appellant in Royster v. DOJ, 58 MSPR 495, 499-500 (1993):

The agency next contends that the administrative judge erred in his determination that nexus had not been shown. We agree. An agency may show a nexus between off-duty misconduct and the efficiency of the service by demonstrating by preponderant evidence that: (1) The circumstances are so egregious as to raise a rebuttable presumption; (2) the misconduct adversely affects the appellant's or co-workers' job performance or the agency's trust and confidence in the appellant's job performance; or (3) the misconduct interfered with or adversely affected the agency's mission. Kruger v. Dept. of Justice, 32 MSPR 71, 74 (1987).

The agency argues that the administrative judge viewed the misconduct as a "violent lovers' quarrel" and, therefore, not particularly serious, especially since the appellant and the woman had resumed a cordial relationship. Furthermore, after noting that Barnhill v. Dept. of Justice, 9 MSPB 608, 10 MSPR 378 (1982), is "[t]he Board case most on point," the administrative judge nevertheless found it inapplicable as precedent because the appellant did not exhibit the aberrant behavior shown in Barnhill and because the woman was willing to resume friendly relations with the appellant. The administrative judge thus found that the appellant's misconduct was not serious and, therefore, discounted the testimony by the prison warden that he had lost trust and confidence in the appellant's abilities. Id. The agency contends that Barnhill is directly on point.

We find that Barnhill is applicable to this case. Barnhill dealt with an employee whose duties as a border patrol agent included the apprehension of female aliens. 10 MSPR at 380. The employee had been convicted on a misdemeanor charge of making obscene phone calls and had been charged with making a threat of violence to a female. Despite favorable reports from the employee's psychiatrist and parole officer regarding the employee's progress in his counseling and his potential for rehabilitation, the Board found the required nexus between the misconduct and the efficiency of the service. Id., at 380-381. The Board found persuasive the testimony by an agency official stating that the misconduct had resulted in a loss of trust and confidence in the employee's ability to perform his duties. The official noted that the duties of the position required dealing with female aliens and that claims of abuse by agency employees sometimes arose. Given the nature of the misconduct and the nature of the appellant's position, both with respect to his particular duties dealing with female aliens and his general status as a law enforcement officer held to a higher standard of conduct, the Board found that the required nexus had been established. Id.

In this case, the sustained misconduct involved several threatening and abusive phone calls. In those phone calls, the appellant at times threatened to kill the woman and her house guests. Although the administrative judge found that the appellant did not exhibit the aberrant behavior shown in Barnhill, we do not discern a substantial difference between these actions. The administrative judge placed much emphasis on the relationship between the appellant and the woman. Although the relationship is a factor to be considered, the appellant's repeated threats to the woman must also be considered.

The employee's duties required him to maintain the safety, custody and control of female inmates. The prison warden testified that problems had occurred in the past involving sexual relations between staff members and inmates. He further testified that he had irretrievably lost his belief in the appellant's integrity and could no longer trust the appellant to properly perform the duties of his position. We find that the agency has established by preponderant evidence that the appellant's misconduct adversely affected its trust and confidence in the appellant's ability to perform his duties. See Beasley v. Dept. of Defense, 52 MSPR 272, 275 (1992).

Furthermore, nexus may be proven by showing that the employee's off-duty misconduct is antithetical to the agency's mission. Kruger, 32 MSPR at 75 n.2. The agency is not required to demonstrate a specific impact on the appellant's job performance or the efficiency of the service before taking action. Id. at 75. Based on the above, we find that Barnhill is applicable and that the agency has established the required nexus between the appellant's misconduct and the efficiency of the service. Id.

See Prather v. DOJ, 117 MSPR 137, 149-50 ¶ 32, 2011 MSPB 100 (2011) (off duty misconduct with vulnerable women, involving misuse of appellant's position of authority, reasonably caused the agency to lose trust in the appellant's ability to independently work as a criminal investigator with female informants).

Conviction for distribution of obscene materials supported removal of an immigration inspector. Although the supervisor apparently had not lost confidence in the appellant, the nature of the misconduct irreparably damaged agency confidence in the employee's ability to properly conduct himself in all situations. Ahr v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 238, 243 (1984).

Unauthorized use of a government vehicle, a hit-and-run accident resulting in a fatality and failure to report the accident in a timely manner, justified the removal of a DEA special agent whose responsibility was to uphold the law, Hilderbrand v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 233, 236-37 (1984):

Appellant, as a Special Agent, had the general responsibility to uphold the law, not break it. While holding such a sensitive position, it can hardly be challenged that an agency has the right to expect and hold its law enforcement personnel to a high standard of conduct. See MacDonald v. Dept. of the Navy, 4 MSPB 459 (1980). In considering the penalty, the deciding official stated that it was appellant's "casual disregard for the proper use of official government equipment," his failure to "maintain a high degree of credibility" and "to think quickly and act properly in emergency situations," and his "indecision relative to reporting the accident in a timely manner," which caused the adverse action to be taken against him. He further stated that "[t]he DEA cannot afford to maintain personnel as law enforcement officers who react poorly in stressful situations due to the dangerous nature of narcotics investigations." Id.

Nexus was established in actions against supervisory Border Patrol agents who, while on duty, consumed alcohol in a government vehicle, albeit without damage to themselves or the car, but with resultant loss of management confidence in their ability to perform as supervisors, Canada v. DHS, 113 MSPR 509, 514-15 ¶¶ 10-11, 2010 MSPB 69 (2010):

In addition to the requirement that the agency prove the charge it has brought against the appellants, the agency must also prove that there is a nexus, i.e., a clear and direct relationship between the articulated grounds for the adverse action and either the appellants' ability to accomplish their duties satisfactorily or some other legitimate government interest. Merritt v. Department of Justice, 6 M.S.P.R. 585, 596 (1981), modified by, Kruger v. Department of Justice, 32 M.S.P.R. 71, 75 n.2 (1987). The administrative judge found an "obvious" nexus in these cases, reasoning that the appellants are supervisors and must be prepared to lead by example and exercise good judgment in order to have credibility and be trustworthy. Given the nature of the facts cited in support of the charges in these cases, we deem it appropriate to more fully address this issue.

An agency may establish nexus by showing that the employee's conduct (1) affected his or his coworkers' job performance; (2) affected management's trust and confidence in the employee's job performance; or (3) interfered with or adversely affected the agency's mission. Johnson v. Department of Health & Human Services, 86 M.S.P.R. 501, ¶ 1 (2000), aff'd, 18 Fed. Appx. 837 (Fed. Cir. 2001), and aff'd sub nom. Delong v. Department of Health & Human Services, 264 F.3d 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2001). The deciding official testified in both cases that the incident at issue had caused him to lose confidence in the appellants. He also testified that the mission of the agency is national security, and that it is important for supervisors to make sound decisions that are based on policies, directives, and law. In addition, he noted that the appellants, as first-line supervisors, oversee a young and impressionable workforce, and that such junior employees look to their supervisors for guidance and direction. Finally, the deciding official testified that drinking alcohol in a government vehicle is dangerous, considering the nature of law enforcement work, not only from a liability standpoint, but also from an officer safety standpoint. The deciding official's testimony establishes that the appellants' conduct affected management's trust and confidence in their job performance and adversely affected the agency's mission. Therefore, under the particular circumstances of these cases, nexus is established.

Removal was sustained of a wildlife management biologist who entered a nolo plea to a publicized violation of criminal laws relating to federal wildlife and endangered species laws that he was directly responsible for enforcing. Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 356-57 (1987). Removal was mitigated to a 30-day suspension for a correctional officer who attempted to smuggle a small quantity of fireworks into the country and who became abusive during a confrontation with customs officials. White v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 684, 689 (1988). Mitigation was declined as to a law enforcement officer involved in off-duty drug use.
