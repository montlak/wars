2. Evidence

Formal rules of evidence are not applicable to FLRA proceedings. Dept. of Army, Hq., Ft. Sill and NFFE, 29 FLRA 1110, 1130 (1987); Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and Portsmouth FEMTC, 4 FLRA 619, 625 (1980) (ALJ Decision) (declining to apply parole evidence rule since rules of evidence are not applicable to proceedings before FLRA). See 5 CFR 2423.31(b) (2022). According to VAMC, Veterans Canteen Serv., Denver and AFGE Local 2241, 52 FLRA 16, 22 (1996):

It is well established that the determination of the matters to be admitted into evidence is within the discretion of an Administrative Law Judge under section 2423.19(g) of the Authority's Regulations.... In this regard, the parties are not bound by the rules of evidence, and it is within the broad discretion of a judge to admit evidence and to determine whether such evidence is relevant. See 5 CFR § 2423.17.

Cf. INS and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 55 FLRA 93, 98 (1999) (judge abused discretion by refusing to admit relevant testimony); Dept. of Commerce, NOAA and AFGE Local 2640, 54 FLRA 987, 1010 n.14 (1998); AFGE Local 1345 and Vasquez, 53 FLRA 1789, 1796-97 (1998) (reaffirming discretionary authority of ALJ as to matters to be admitted into evidence).

Discretion must be exercised as to the parties' substantive rights, as was made plain in AAFES, Waco and AFGE Local 4042, 53 FLRA 749, 762-63 (1997), remanding a case to permit the agency to introduce further evidence concerning the disruptive impact of the imposition of a status quo ante order:

The determination of matters to be admitted into evidence is within the discretion of the Judge under the Authority's Regulations. Department of Veterans Affairs Medical Center, Denver, Colorado and Veterans Canteen Service, Denver, Colorado, 52 FLRA 16, 22 (1996) (Veterans Affairs Medical Center) (citing Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, Portsmouth, New Hampshire, 49 FLRA 1522, 1531 (1994). It is also within the discretion of a judge to determine the relevance of evidence presented. Id. However, a judge's exclusion of evidence the judge finds to be immaterial or irrelevant constitutes abuse of discretion where a reviewing court determines that the excluded evidence is relevant. In this regard, courts have held that judges abused their discretion in refusing to admit relevant evidence, but did not abuse their discretion in refusing to admit evidence not shown to be relevant. E.g., Chudson v. Environmental Protection Agency, 17 F.3d 380, 384-85 (Fed. Cir. 1994); Johnson v. Burnley, 887 F.2d 471, 480 (4th Cir. 1989).

Although the Federal Rules of Evidence do not control ULP proceedings, a ULP case is to be decided on record evidence. The requirement that FLRA base its determination on the record presented applies equally to negotiability disputes as well as to ULP cases. A party failing to sustain its burden of proof acts at its peril. FLRA will not make a determination on a ULP allegation based on assumptions regarding factual matters if relevant evidence could have been presented but was not. VAMC, Leavenworth and AFGE Local 85, 40 FLRA 592, 597 (1991). Although the rules of evidence may not be strictly applied, some rules of relatively general application, e.g., exclusion of settlement discussions of evidence of facts in a ULP complaint, will be applied. See 56th Combat Support Group, MacDill AFB and NFFE Local 153, 44 FLRA 1098, 1103 (1992). FLRA may take official notice in a ULP case of other FLRA proceedings. DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Fairton and AFGE Local 3975, 62 FLRA 187, 188 (2007).
