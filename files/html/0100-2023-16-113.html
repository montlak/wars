e. Maintenance of Status Quo During Bargaining and Arbitration Proceedings

Because management is not to change working conditions unilaterally, unless there is a waiver of bargaining rights, it follows that management must maintain the status quo during bargaining. As to obligations during the course of bargaining, DOJ, INS and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 31 FLRA 145, 154 (1988), determined that the suggestion by the union that the agency maintain the status quo as to uniform requirements rather than making a change was a bargaining request sufficient to trigger the requirement that the agency maintain the status quo during bargaining. Implementation may be made prior to the completion of the bargaining process only when the changes are consistent with the necessary functioning of the agency or once the matters before FMCS or FSIP are resolved. IAM Local Lodge 2424 and Dept. of Army, Aberdeen Proving Ground, 31 FLRA 205, 214-15 (1988) (citing Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 734 (1987)). The obligation to maintain the status quo assumes proof that management implemented changes while a negotiable proposal was pending. SSA Malden Dist. Office and AFGE Local 1164, 54 FLRA 531, 538 (1998). The employer was not justified in refusing to stay a decision to reassign employees pending impact bargaining because of an overriding exigency. The employer had two months' notice that the reassignment would be needed. It was not shown that there would be any severe disruption by accommodating the union request for bargaining. Bureau of Gov't Fin. Operations Hq. and NTEU, 11 FLRA 334, 344, 346 (1983) (ALJ Decision). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Maintenance of Status Quo."]

Dept. of Army, Defense Language Inst., Presidio of Monterey and NFFE Local 1263, 43 FLRA 974, 980-81 (1992), decided that management is not obliged to stay its action pending an arbitration proceeding brought to contest the action taken by management. The obligation to maintain the status quo during bargaining does not extend to the period involving arbitration over interpretation of contract rights. FLRA explained as to a contracting-out dispute:

[A]n agency is obligated to stay any management actions pending negotiations on that matter to the extent consistent with its necessary functioning.

We find nothing in the Statute or any applicable law that requires a stay of actions taken pursuant to section 7106(a)(2)(B), pending resolution of the propriety of those actions through the negotiated grievance arbitration procedure, absent a negotiated procedure or arrangement that requires such a stay. The General Counsel argues that the Statute requires such an obligation in order to protect the arbitration process. We disagree. Rather, we conclude that, under the statutory scheme, the grievance arbitration process is a contractual mechanism for resolving disputes that should not place the parties in peril of committing statutory violations unless they repudiate the negotiated grievance arbitration provisions or fail to abide by a binding award. Indeed, the value of arbitration to both management and unions is the speed with which workplace disputes may be resolved and, where appropriate, improper management actions may be remedied. In general, the continuation of an agency action pending arbitration regarding the propriety of that action should have no material effect on the integrity of the process. On the other hand, if unions could force agencies to stay any action merely by filing a grievance disputing that action, the grievance arbitration procedure could become a tool to prevent agencies from carrying out otherwise lawful and appropriate management plans, pending the completion of arbitration. This would, we believe, undermine grievance arbitration as a neutral and expeditious method of dispute resolution in the Federal workplace.
