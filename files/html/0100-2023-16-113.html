2. Substantial Evidence Controls

Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPR 505, 527, 1 MSPB 489 (1980), applied the substantial evidence test to ALOC/WIGI cases, and the test still applies, despite some historical deviations. The Court of Claims affirmed the Board's application of the substantial evidence test to ALOC/reconsideration cases in Meyer v. SSA, 666 F.2d 540 (Ct. Cl. 1981), a result the court found not to be required by the statutory language, but suggested by the general scheme of the Reform Act establishing the substantial evidence test as the standard in performance cases. A contrary result was reached in Schramm v. DHHS, 682 F.2d 85 (3rd Cir. 1982), on remand, Schramm v. DHHS, 18 MSPR 634, 636 (1984) (returning the case to the AJ for a new hearing). The court interpreted the statute 5 USC 7701(c)(1) literally and decided that a preponderance test would be applied to ALOC cases. The same result was reached in Ommaya v. NIH, 726 F.2d 827 (D.C. Cir. 1984) (following White v. Dept. of Army, 720 F.2d 209 (D.C. Cir. 1983)) (on remand, White v. Dept. of Army, 20 MSPR 5 (1984)).

The Board briefly equivocated as to whether Schramm might call for a preponderance of evidence standard in the Third Circuit. See Gordon v. VA, 19 MSPR 526, 528 n.2 (1984) ("In...Schramm v. Department of Health and Human Services, 682 F.2d 85 (3rd Cir. 1982), the Court held that the preponderant evidence standard applies in appeals concerning the denial of a within-grade salary increase, not the substantial evidence standard. See also Stankis v. Environmental Protection Agency, 713 F.2d 1181 (5th Cir. 1983). The Board believes, however, that the appropriate Federal rule is that set out in Meyer and Parker, which was followed in this case."); Munisteri v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 317, 320-21 (1983) (favoring the substantial evidence test). The distinction could make a difference. One WIGI denial was sustained under the substantial evidence test and then the WIGI denial was reversed, following court consideration, under the preponderant evidence test. See Stankis v. EPA, 20 MSPR 250, 253 (1984); Stankis v. EPA, 713 F.2d 1181 (5th Cir. 1983) (applying a preponderance rather than a substantial evidence standard to an MSPB appeal involving a denial of WIGI). If the AJ substituted his own judgment as to the adequacy of an appellant's performance for that of the agency, he improperly disregarded the deference required by the Board to agency expertise in performance evaluation that was to be ensured through application of the substantial evidence standard of review. Nordell v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 276, 280 (1983).

The Federal Courts Improvement Act placed ordinary review of Board ALOC decisions in the Federal Circuit. That court announced soon after its formation that it would follow law developed by the Court of Claims, its predecessor court. The Board decided that the substantial evidence test would be applied to ALOC appeals arising in all regions, for the legal review standards developed by the Federal Circuit would control the disposition of these cases. See Reyna v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 6, 7 n.* (1983). The issue of the controlling evidentiary standard has not been litigated in the Supreme Court; it is unlikely that the Court would find the issue important enough to warrant certiorari, particularly because the Federal Courts Improvement Act eliminated the potential for circuit conflicts. The Meyer decision, supra, was affirmed by the Federal Circuit, with recognition of the conflict in circuit decisions. Romane v. DCAA, 760 F.2d 1286 (Fed. Cir. 1985). There was, however, another development: the Board realized that the presence of discrimination issues precludes Federal Circuit appellate review jurisdiction; in mixed-WIGI appeals, district courts have jurisdiction. Circuit courts other than the Federal Circuit would review the district court decisions. The Board astutely announced that in the Third, Fifth, and District of Columbia Circuits, the preponderance standard yet reigned for mixed case review. See, e.g., Wade v. DHHS, 20 MSPR 683, 685 (1984). Then, however, the Board got cold feet and announced that it was overruling Wade, stating that "we believe that the Board is obliged to follow the precedent of the Federal Circuit Court of Appeals in this and all other mixed ALOC appeals, even though another appellate court may actually review our decision." Afifi v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 282, 284 (1987).

Substantial evidence is not a high bar. But agencies can miss their mark, Ahuruonye v. Dept. of Interior, DC-531D-14-0587-I-1 (NP 12/29/2014):

Additionally, the Board may not sustain an agency's withholding of an employee's WIGI unless that action is supported by substantial evidence. Chaggaris v. General Services Administration, 49 M.S.P.R. 249, 255 (1991). Here, the agency failed to submit any of the appellant's work products that included apparent errors. Cf. id. at 255-56 (the appellant's performance deficiencies were described in considerable factual detail and were corroborated by the affidavits of his supervisors, providing sufficient support for the agency's determination that he was not performing at an acceptable level); Hudson v. Department of the Army, 49 M.S.P.R. 202, 206-07 (1991) (the agency submitted copies of documents prepared by the appellant that included apparent errors of the kind described in the performance standard, but, because that evidence covered only a fraction of the period at issue, the agency failed to present substantial evidence supporting its decision to withhold the appellant's WIGI). Here, the agency failed to issue the appellant a notice that his performance was not at an acceptable level of competence or provide him an opportunity to request reconsideration of that determination under OPM's procedures and, in addition, failed to submit any supporting evidence. Therefore, the action must be reversed.
