a. De Novo Review

The AJ considers de novo all relevant evidence presented by both parties, whether offered at a hearing or transmitted as part of the administrative record. Webb v. USPS, 38 MSPR 35, 38 (1988); see Pardo v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 206, 209 (1982) ("[T]he Board may properly consider evidence relating to a period of time extending beyond the date of the agency's decision to remove appellant."). This means that the appellant (or agency) is not limited to the presentation of evidence that was previously submitted to the employing agency. It is not a proper objection to the evidence offered by the appellant at the hearing that he could have, but did not, offer evidence at the agency reply proceedings. Broadnax v. USPS, 10 MSPR 142, 148 n.4 (1982). In Broadnax, the Board stressed that it must consider de novo all relevant evidence offered at the hearing by either party. If there is no hearing, the parties will be given a chance to file evidence by mail and to respond to the submissions offered by the opposing party. The rule concerning the de novo nature of the Board appellate process extends to appeals from OPM retirement decisions: in a disability retirement case, the Board may consider evidence that was not submitted to OPM if it is related to a medical condition supporting the appellant's application to OPM. Waters v. OPM, 83 MSPR 553, 556 ¶ 5 (1999). A settlement between private parties limiting evidence that may be disclosed as to an individual's prior private sector employment does not bar OPM from introducing evidence it developed prior to the settlement concerning the employee's suitability for federal employment. See Carter v. OPM, 87 MSPR 72, 75 ¶ 8 (2000). [Refer to Chapter 1, "De Novo Review of Agency Actions," and to Chapter 3, "De Novo Hearing."]

In a nonprecedential decision, the Board may have suggested limitations on use by an agency of information tending to prove a charge when that information was collected after the proposal and decision letters were issued, Eagleheart v. USPS, SF-0752-06-0167-B-2 (NP 3/28/2011):

...The Board adjudicates the charge as it is described in the agency's proposal and decision notices. Rackers v. Department of Justice, 79 M.S.P.R. 262, 276 (1998), aff'd, 194 F.3d 1336 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table). Accordingly, the administrative judge erred in considering evidence collected after the appellant's removal in sustaining the specification. This error did not affect the correctness of her conclusion, however, because it is undisputed that the agency was unaware of this evidence and did not consider it in determining to remove the appellant. See Stuhlmacher v. U.S. Postal Service, 89 M.S.P.R. 272, ¶ 14 (2001). Moreover, the error does not provide a basis for disturbing the administrative judge's credibility determinations, which we find are supported by the record below.

The rule concerning de novo review is the same in arbitration of cases otherwise within the Board's jurisdiction. All evidence obtained up to the time of the arbitration hearing is admissible. See Brook v. Corrado, 999 F.2d 523 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (OIG report). [Refer to Chapter 3, "De Novo Hearing."].
