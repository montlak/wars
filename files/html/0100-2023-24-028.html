b. New and Material Evidence

During the preparation of briefs and the appendix, it should be understood that the appeal depends on what evidence was developed before the Board. The record below controls the appeal above. Adding new evidence or documents not already part of the record into the appendix of an appellate brief is improper. Courts of appeal are courts of review. The general rule on supplementing the record with new evidence is that appellate courts cannot act on evidence that is not before the court [Board] below, nor receive any paper that was not used at the hearing. Coplin v. United States, 761 F.2d 688, 691 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (citing Boone v. Chiles, 35 U.S. (10 Pet.) 177, 208 (1836)); see United States v. Miller, 80 U.S. (13 Wall.) 576, 577 (1872). An appellate court will not consider exhibits that were not submitted to the Board and that do not form part of the administrative record. Alston v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 86-1600); Hairston v. VA, 761 Fed. Appx. 1005, 1008 n.3 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP) ("Because this is a court of appeals, we will not evaluate this evidence in the first instance, and a remand for the Board to consider it is not warranted.").

Ordinarily, the Federal Circuit does not review and adjudicate an appeal on evidence presented for the first time to the court and not first presented to the Board. See Maples v. OPM, 347 Fed. Appx. 593 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (court would not consider what was argued as new evidence of disability; "the proper vehicle for Maples to present her new medical evidence is by filing a motion to reopen at the Board"). When a party possesses evidence at the time of an original hearing, the court does not abuse its discretion in refusing to reopen the case for further proceedings based on that evidence. NATCA v. Dept. of Transp., FAA (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 91-3524). Of an attempt to develop additional evidence during the course of an appeal in the Federal Circuit, Pacious v. NASA, 469 Fed. Appx. 874, 876-77 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP), determined:

As an initial matter, while this appeal was pending Mr. Pacious filed two motions asking this court to compel NASA to produce certain information related to NASA's surveillance of Mr. Pacious. The discovery period before the Board is closed, and there is no right to discovery on appeal. See United States v. Hicks, 278 Fed. Appx. 976 (11th Cir. 2008) (denying motions to compel production of documents on grounds that "neither the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure nor the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure authorize discovery on appeal"). Because this court's review is limited to the evidence in the record before the Board, we cannot consider any new evidence presented for the first time on appeal. See Mueller v. U.S. Postal Serv., 76 F.3d 1198, 1201-02 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ("Because we are limited to reviewing decisions of the Board based on the record before the deciding official, we decline to base our judgment on evidence that was not part of the record before the administrative judge.") (internal citation omitted). Moreover, such information is not necessary for resolving the issues presented in Mr. Pacious's appeal. Consequently, those motions are denied.

Belter v. DHS, 565 Fed. Appx. 895, 897 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), said of the appellant's attempt to have the reviewing court send the case back to the Board for evaluation of allegedly new evidence:

... To the extent Mr. Belter asks us to review the information that he received in response to his FOIA request in the first instance, we do not have that power. "Congress specifically limited our appellate review, 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c), to final orders and decisions of the board on the record." Rockwell v. Dep't of Transp., F.A.A., 789 F.2d 908, 913 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (emphasis in original). We also conclude that the Board did not violate 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115 because that regulation only applies to "issues raised in a timely petition... for review" of the AJ's initial decision. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115 (2012). Mr. Belter did not present to the Board the information that he obtained via the FOIA request because Mr. Belter received it long after he filed his petition for review. We cannot reverse or vacate the Board's decision based on the information that was never presented to the Board.

A reviewing court occasionally becomes concerned about the Board's failure to reopen a case for admission and consideration of new evidence. In Phillips v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 91-3311), the appellant was disciplined and the Board upheld the agency. Later, someone else involved in the same incident received a lesser penalty (after the Board decision). Because the second individual was clearly culpable but received a lighter penalty than the appellant, the court concluded that the Board acted arbitrarily by refusing to reopen the record for new evidence. If there is new evidence, the best course is to present it to the Board in a petition for reopening. Do not submit it to the court, whose review is solely on the record, without an explanation of what is being done and why the case should be either remanded to the Board or considered by the court. See Rockwell v. Dept. of Transp., 789 F.2d 908 (Fed. Cir. 1986).
