broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 01: Initial Decision, Section A: Initial Decision Format, Subsection 01: Minimum Requirements, Part b: Notice of Right to Review
b. Notice of Right to Review

The initial decision explains rights of further administrative and judicial review. The explanation of review rights does not ordinarily present a problem. However, some appeals are decided without complete advice. The problem occurred in several mixed cases. Whenever issues of discrimination are raised in the course of an appeal, the appellant should be advised of both his right under 5 USC 7702(b)(1) to petition the EEOC to consider the Board's decision and his right to a trial de novo in the United States District Court on the issues of discrimination. See Ritter v. USPS, 37 MSPR 334, 341 (1988) ("Even though the record supports the administrative judge's finding that the discrimination claim was unsupported by the evidence, the Board has an obligation to inform the appellant of his right to appeal the Board's decision to the EEOC and/or the U.S. District Court."); Perkins v. VA, 3 MSPR 576, 578, 4 MSPB 93 (1980); Abram v. VA, 1 MSPR 299, 1 MSPB 288, 300 (1980) (disability discrimination: remanding for issuance of a decision advising the employee of the right to secure review of an EEO allegation by petition to EEOC and by trial de novo in district court); cf. Irwin v. OPM, 95 MSPR 578 ¶ 2 (2004) (Table) (even when an OPM annuity determination depends on a provision of a nondiscretionary statute allowing for no discrimination, the AJ should include a notice of EEOC appeal rights).

The lack of notice of EEO review rights in an initial decision can be cured in the Board's final decision following a PFR. See Bonggat v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 402, 412-13 (1993) (error cured through a notice in the final decision on PFR); Connor v. USPS, 50 MSPR 389, 396 (1991) (enforcement case: "Since the appellant properly raised...issues of discrimination and reprisal challenging the agency's actions in complying with the Board's order, and they were considered by the...judge in the compliance case...this was a "mixed" appeal, and mixed case appeal rights should have been included in the CID."); Monahan v. USPS, 41 MSPR 153, 158 (1989) (notice in the Board's final decision of mixed-case appeal rights remedied the AJ's failure to provide that notice in the initial decision); Guthrie v. USPS, 41 MSPR 102, 108 (1989) (AJ should have provided notice of rights to appeal to EEOC or to sue in district court; the omission was cured by the Board's notice of those rights in its final decision on PFR); Krishnan v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 572, 574 (1989) (error was remedied by proper notice in the decision on a PFR).

Even if "the record supports the administrative judge's finding that the discrimination complaint was frivolous, the Board has an obligation to inform the appellant of the other avenues by which he could appeal the Board's decision on the discrimination issue." Heller v. Dept. of Army, 36 MSPR 675, 681 (1988); see Moran v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 77, 88 (1994) (even a frivolous allegation of discrimination warrants notice in the initial decision of other avenues of appeal of the MSPB decision on the discrimination issue; the omission was cured by the notice contained in the Board's Opinion and Order on PFR).

If the appellant does not allege an EEO violation in his appeal, the AJ need not provide a notice of EEOC review rights. See Thomas v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 579, 581 (1987). If the Board deems that it lacks jurisdiction over an EEO allegation, its decision need not include notice of EEOC appeal rights. Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 683, 688 n.3 (1989), aff'd, Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 934 F.2d 1240 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc); see Fox v. Dept. of Transp., 69 MSPR 192 (1995) (Table) ("Because voluntary retirements are not appealable actions within the Board's jurisdiction, the appellant is not entitled to mixed-case appeal rights providing a further appeal of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission."); Cook v. CIA, 58 MSPR 542, 543 (1993) (correcting an erroneous issuance in an initial decision of mixed case appeal rights); Connor v. USPS, 52 MSPR 583, 586 n.5 (1992) ("Although the appellant argues that the administrative judge should have given him appeal rights to a federal district court, instead of to the court of appeals, the hearing was properly limited to the jurisdictional issues, and did not reach the affirmative defenses raised by the appellant in his appeals. Therefore, we find no error in the appeal rights provided in the initial decision."); Gust v. OPM, 43 MSPR 31, 33 (1989) (if the Board clearly lacks jurisdiction, e.g., over a classification appeal, the should not provide the appellant with mixed case review rights). If the appellant does not allege discrimination and the AJ issues a decision with a notice of mixed case review rights, the Board may reopen the case, identify the error, and cure the error through a correct notice of appeal rights in its final decision. See Bonney v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 196, 197 (1993).

As to review rights in mixed cases, cases have been tossed out of court because the lawsuit failed to name the agency head as a defendant; instead, the defendant named was the agency or the United States. 42 USC 2000e-16(c), incorporated by reference in 5 USC 7703(b)(2), states that in civil actions filed under the statute, "the head of the department, agency, or unit, as appropriate, shall be the defendant." Dean v. VARO, 943 F.2d 667, 672 (6th Cir. 1991), commented as to notification of the right to sue in mixed cases:

Due to the harsh consequences of inadvertently misnaming the proper defendant, we are compelled to address a recurring problem that arises in many of the cases in which plaintiff mistakenly names the agency itself rather than the head of the agency or department as required by 2000e-16(c). The right-to-sue letters often received by potential plaintiffs most often do not adequately inform them as to who is the proper defendant.... For instance, the letter sent to Dean by the MSPB stated:

If you do not request review of this order on your discrimination claims by the EEOC, you may file a civil action against the agency on both your discrimination claims and your other claims in an appropriate United States District Court. See 5 USC Section 7703(b)(2).

...It would not unduly burden the MSPB to state more specifically in their right-to-sue letters just who should be sued.

Without much success, the United States Courts of Appeal have for several years admonished various federal agencies and departments to make their letters more specific.... To this court's chagrin, our requests appear to have fallen on deaf ears. Relying on the old adage that if at first you don't succeed, try, try again, we hereby do urge the MSPB and other agencies or departments that issue right-to-sue letters to clearly and specifically inform potential plaintiffs of just who it is they should sue. The ambiguity in the letter received by Dean in this case is totally unnecessary and results in the needless dismissal of his complaint.

[Refer to Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases," for judicial review procedures in mixed cases.]

If the AJ reviews a Postal Service arbitration award, he must provide notice in his initial decision of enforcement rights and a statement of further process available to the appellant. See Montalvo v. USPS, 50 MSPR 48, 52 n.4 (1991). The Board sometimes errs in identifying review rights in final decisions. One appeal was sent back to the Board so that it could identify the proper court for review (and restart the period allowed for the appellant to seek judicial review). See Marsh v. Dept. of Army, 9 MSPR 189, 190 (1981).

The notice of review rights is provided by the Board. Although the employing agency must notify the appellant of the right to appeal to the Board, the agency need not explain further review rights following the Board appeal, Evans v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 222, 225 (1992):

...When an agency issues a decision on a matter appealable to the Board, the agency is required to provide the employee with notice of his appeal rights to the Board.... However, once an appeal has been docketed by the Board, the employee must look to the Board for his subsequent appeal rights, and the agency has no further obligation to inform him of his appeal rights....

The initial decision (and final decision) should contain a notice of appeal rights, but the notice is advisory, that is, the Board's notice does not create jurisdiction in another tribunal, Reid v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 354, 357 (1994), rev'd on other grounds, King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215 (Fed. Cir. 1995):

Although the Board provides appellants with notice of appeal rights from its decisions, 5 CFR §§ 1201.111(b)(6) and 1201.157, the notice is not a part of the decision. The Board's decision is restricted to matters within its jurisdiction, 5 USC § 1204(a)(1). As the Board does not have jurisdiction to either confer or determine the jurisdiction of the district courts or the EEOC, the notice of appeal rights is not a part of the Board's decision. Only the EEOC, the district court, and courts with authority over them may make these jurisdictional determinations. The notice of appeal rights is nothing more than a nonbinding, non-precedential statement designed to guide appellants who seek review of final Board decisions....
