A. OVERVIEW

Under 5 CFR 1201.72(a) (2022), discovery is the process permitting a party to obtain information, including documents and testimony, not already provided by another party. That information can then be used by the requesting party to plan and develop his case. Discovery can be used extensively in adverse action, performance-based, and RIF appeals. It is used in challenges to OPM reconsideration decisions concerning retirement benefits. See Gaddas v. OPM, 61 MSPR 650, 652 (1994) (OPM subpoena was permitted for records pertaining to the appellant's earnings). The Board's regulations do not preclude the use of discovery in counsel fee disputes and enforcement proceedings. But cf. Suggs v. VA, SF-0752-09-0734-X-1 (NP 11/20/2013) ("To the extent the appellant is attempting to pursue discovery against the agency, the Board's regulations provide no avenue for him to do so as part of this compliance review proceeding. "). But see Bernard v. USDA, 788 F.3d 1365, 1367-70 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (recognizing ambiguity in Board discovery regulations as they may apply to enforcement proceedings); 80 Fed. Reg. 66787 (Oct. 30, 2015) (implementing regulatory change extending discovery to enforcement proceedings at 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(9)). If the appellant is to obtain information during the appeal, discovery is the means established by the Board to accomplish that end. The Board does not conduct an investigation of the appellant's case on the appellant's behalf. See Guthrie v. USPS, 41 MSPR 102, 105 (1989) ("We note...that the appellant himself, rather than the Board or the agency, is responsible for preparing and presenting his own defense to the action."). [For more detailed discussion of discovery practices, refer to Broida's MSPB Discovery Forms Book for Agency Representatives (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2004).]

Discovery represents one major and beneficial change in civil service practice following enactment of the Civil Service Reform Act and creation of the Board. Yet in many appeals it is probably little or improperly used, particularly in appeals undertaken by unrepresented appellants. Used effectively, discovery can improve and focus case evaluation and presentation. Used improperly, discovery wastes time by consuming the parties and the AJ in unnecessary disputes over its scope. Discovery can be expensive--especially if depositions are used extensively. Discovery can occasionally educate your opponent on points of fact or law that might otherwise remain unnoticed (a result that can be good or bad, depending on your perspective).

Discovery presents some difficulties for the Board. Federal discovery practices and rules were developed for civil litigation in courts. Although the rules of federal procedure governing discovery appear straightforward, their administration requires research and occasionally results in disputes resolved through motions to compel discovery. Yet many individuals and representatives who appear before the Board are not lawyers. Many AJs have not actively litigated cases, although some AJs are prior agency counsel with lots of experience in Board or EEOC work. The lack of familiarity with discovery among many who are involved in Board litigation results in either a complete failure to use discovery or unnecessary objections and disputes among those who do not understand or appreciate the nature of the process. Discovery is a component of Board practice that can be improved through reading and study of Board decisions and legal treatises and practice books.

Discovery in an appeal is undertaken while the case is pending in the Board's regional or field office. Discovery is not initiated through or during a PFR from the initial decision. See Forston v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 154, 158 (1993) (Board regulations do not provide for discovery during the adjudication of a PFR); cf. Mosby v. DHUD, 114 MSPR 674, 676 ¶ 4, 2010 MSPB 198 (2010) (same result as Forston); Powers v. DHHS, 7 MSPR 619, 623 (1981) (declining to consider the appellant's request, during the adjudication of the PFR, that the agency provide documents). Discovery applies to the full range of Board appeals. See, e.g., Beam v. OPM, 71 MSPR 629, 633 (1996) (directing a response to discovery in an OPM employment practice case); Beam v. OPM, 61 MSPR 54 (1994) (discovery in employment practice case). In 2015 Board regulations were changed to make clear that discovery may be used in enforcement proceedings. Earlier practice was inconsistently discretionary. Compare Forston v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 154, 158 (1993) ("The Board's regulations make no provision for discovery in compliance cases"), with Kuykendall v. VA, 68 MSPR 314, 328 (1995) (the AJ has discretion to permit discovery in a compliance proceeding). [Refer to decisions in this chapter and in Chapters 15 and 16 concerning discovery in disputes over compliance and counsel fees.]

Discovery serves several purposes.

Through discovery a party obtains information, some of it useful. Documents that might otherwise never be revealed can be secured. Not all information obtained will be necessary: much will not be relevant or material, and some may be damaging and would harm the party were it introduced. Materials gained in discovery may be used to impeach the credibility of people testifying at the hearing. The most common example is the witness who gives one response in a discovery deposition and another at the hearing (followed by the question implying that the witness must be mistaken or lying at some point). Discovery materials may be used as affirmative evidence, particularly as an admission from the other party or a managing agent of the other party. The question and response need only be read into the record, or introduced as an exhibit, during the case in chief, and it is as if the opposing party gave the response from the witness chair; an admission is often introduced as an exhibit with the prehearing submission. Discovery techniques permit preservation of testimony given by individuals who are dying, who are too ill or too geographically remote to attend a hearing, or whose memory or ability to testify may diminish over time.

Discovery may be conducted as to the employing agency, OPM, and other federal agencies with information pertaining to the appeal. See Templeton v. OPM, 951 F.2d 338, 342 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (permitting "broad" discovery "from the FAA, OPM and any other government agencies that may have records pertinent to his claim"). Discovery may be obtained from persons, corporations, and entities that are not parties to the appeal. Discovery does not require more than production of evidence or information. The Board lacks the authority under its discovery procedures to order an agency to locate information that the agency does not itself possess, Irvin v. OPM, 29 MSPR 511, 511 n.1 (1985), although subpoenas may be used to obtain that information from others agencies, persons, or organizations.

There are several discovery methods or devices. Most commonly used are interrogatories, requests for documents, and depositions. Interrogatories are written questions to individuals or organizations that must be answered in writing and should be answered under oath. Requests for documents are exactly that. Depositions are interviews: a party's representative has a witness appear at an office, where the witness is sworn, and the witness then answers questions by the representative. The questions, answers, and objections, if any, are transcribed or recorded. Some practitioners use requests for admissions to facts or contentions. Admissions may be useful to establish proof of facts or to prepare factual stipulations for use in prehearing submissions.

Discovery requests are made by pleading or letter to the opposing party. No special format is required by the rules, although lawyers will use captioned discovery requests. Should they produce an insufficient response, a motion to compel discovery is filed with the AJ. Once granted, refusal to respond to or comply with the order compelling discovery furnishes a basis for a motion for sanctions (discussed later in this chapter, "Failure to Cooperate in Discovery").
