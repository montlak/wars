7. Involuntary Transfer

Although its reasoning was not clear, the Board determined that an involuntary transfer between agencies may be tantamount to removal and appealable, Colburn v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 257, 259-60 ¶¶ 5-7 (1998):

In Yaksich [v. Dept. of Air Force, 71 MSPR 355 (1996)], the Board found that an employee-initiated action, such as a transfer between agencies, is presumed to be voluntary unless the appellant presents sufficient evidence to establish that the action was obtained through duress or coercion, or was otherwise involuntary, and that an appellant is entitled to a hearing on the issue of Board jurisdiction over an appeal of an allegedly involuntary action if she makes a nonfrivolous allegation casting doubt on the presumption of voluntariness. 71 MSPR at 360. Although Yaksich does not specifically state the relationship between the grades of the position the appellant left at her former agency and the position into which she transferred at the new agency, its holding is not limited to transfers to lower-graded positions, and instead finds that "a transfer between agencies" can be appealable if it is involuntary. Id. This holding applies to the appellant's circumstances here.

In reaching this conclusion, we note that a coerced inter-agency transfer, even a lateral transfer, is analogous to a coerced resignation and is, therefore, within the Board's jurisdiction. Both actions sever the employment relationship between the parties, and to hold otherwise would preclude review of agency attempts to coerce an employee's resignation merely because the employee finds a position at another agency. We, therefore, find that if an agency coerces a transfer, the coercion renders it involuntary, and for that reason the transfer is appealable to the Board as tantamount to a removal. See Ball v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 68 MSPR 482, 484 (1995). See also Koop v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 16 MSPR 605, 607 (1983) (involuntary inter-agency transfer is of no effect and operates to give effect to underlying reduction-in-force action, while a voluntary transfer does not).*

[*] In contrast, a coerced intra-agency reassignment, without a reduction in pay or grade, is not an appealable action because a reassignment, even if involuntary, is not an adverse action. See Talley v. Department of the Army, 50 MSPR 261, 262-63 (1991).

Here, the appellant has alleged that the agency coerced her into obtaining employment at another agency by creating a hostile work environment and retaliating against her for filing an EEO complaint. Moreover, the appellant has presented specific examples of the agency's alleged improper actions. We, therefore, find that the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the issue of whether her transfer was coerced and, therefore, involuntary. See Yaksich, 71 MSPR at 364-65.

Coburn was followed in Roach v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 4, 10 ¶¶ 16-17 (2000):

...The administrative judge reasoned that a transfer does not involve a separation from federal service, and cannot, therefore, constitute, or otherwise be tantamount to, a removal. In reaching this conclusion the administrative judge noted that, while the Board has stated that a coerced transfer is analogous to an involuntary resignation that was within the Board's jurisdiction, an "analogy" is a "far cry from substantial identicalness [sic]" which is required for the transfer to be "tantamount" to a removal. Initial Decision at 2, citing Colburn v. Department of Justice, 80 MSPR 257 (1998).

The administrative judge's analysis is simply wrong. In Colburn, the Board specifically held that an involuntary lateral transfer between agencies is appealable to the Board as tantamount to a removal. Colburn, 80 MSPR 257 ¶¶ 4-7. In arriving at this conclusion, the Board distinguished cases finding that a coerced intraagency reassignment is beyond the Board's jurisdiction, noting that a reassignment, even if involuntary, is not an adverse action. Id., at n.*. Thus, there is no basis for requiring an actual separation from federal service to bring a coerced lateral inter-agency transfer within the Board's jurisdiction. Here, the appellant alleged that various acts of retaliation ultimately rendered continued employment with the agency impossible. While the agency asserts that the appellant had to find new employment because it was abolishing his office, the actual reason for the appellant's transfer is a question of fact to be decided after a jurisdictional hearing.

See Colpitts v. VA, PH-0752-17-0398-I-1 (NP 4/27/2023) (remanding for hearing based on detailed allegations of a hostile environment: "Similarly, an involuntary inter-agency transfer, even without loss of grade or pay, is analogous to a constructive removal. Colburn v. Department of Justice, 80 M.S.P.R. 257, ¶¶ 5-6 (1998); see also Roach v. Department of the Army, 86 M.S.P.R. 4, ¶ 17 (2000)."); Yaksich v. Dept. of Air Force, 71 MSPR 355, 364 (1996) ("[I]f the appellant makes a showing that her transfer was a result of discrimination in denying her request for an extension [of foreign tour of duty], she will have shown that she was 'forced' into an involuntary transfer.").

In Colpitts, supra, a hearing was required when the transferred manager asserted:

[T]hat the Bedford VA Director created hostile and intolerable working conditions from June 2015 until May 2016 by, among other things, pressuring his first-line supervisor to downgrade his performance appraisal and take administrative action against him, removing him as the Bedford VA liaison to the Hanscom Air Force Base Community Partnership Committee, scheduling numerous meetings with him and abruptly canceling them at the last minute, attempting to embarrass him in front of the Veterans Integrated Service Network Director and other senior staff, ordering him to reassign one of her direct reports rather than hold that employee accountable for his performance because he would file an EEO complaint against her, and calling him a "moron" on multiple occasions in front of staff members.

Sherrill v. Dept. of Interior, DC-3443-13-0507-I-1 (NP 3/4/2014), presented the situation of an employee who, while working for the Postal Service, transferred to Interior on what she asserted was the promise of higher pay. She entered on duty at Interior without having resolved her concerns about her pay rate and appealed to the Board from a reduction in pay induced by misrepresentation by Interior. Sherrill determined that the Board lacked jurisdiction to resolve the pay issue at Interior or to return the appellant to the Postal Service:

To the extent that the appellant is alleging that her transfer from the Postal Service to the employing agency was involuntary, a coerced inter-agency transfer, even a lateral transfer, is analogous to a coerced resignation and is, therefore, within the Board's jurisdiction. Colburn v. Department of Justice, 80 M.S.P.R. 257, ¶ 6 (1998). Nonetheless, the Board presumes that an employee-initiated transfer between agencies is voluntary unless the appellant can show that the agency from which she transferred coerced her transfer. See Herrin v. Department of the Air Force, 95 M.S.P.R. 536, ¶ 7 (2004). Here, however, the appellant does not allege that her former agency, the Postal Service, coerced her transfer. Therefore, the appellant has not made a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over her appeal as an alleged involuntary transfer.[3] See generally Park v. Department of Health & Human Services, 78 M.S.P.R. 527, 534-35 (1998) (an employee who leaves one agency to take a position at another agency should be held responsible for determining the consequences of the change of positions because she is generally in the best position to assess the situation, knowing her current employment status, and she may ask the potential employing agency about the terms of the new position).

[3] Moreover, even if the Board were to find jurisdiction over the appellant's allegation that she accepted the transfer in reliance on the alleged misrepresentations of the employing agency, the Board's remedies would be limited. The Board cannot order the employing agency to increase the appellant's pay and grade because, even absent the alleged pay misinformation, she would not have occupied a higher pay and grade level at that agency. An appellant's entitlement upon reversal of a personnel action is to be returned to the status quo ante, not to be placed in a better position than one in which he or she would have been but for that action. See Herrin, 95 M.S.P.R. 536, ¶ 8. Thus, the Board does not have the authority to order the employing agency to place the appellant in a better position than she would have been had she not relied on its misinformation. In addition, the Board does not have the authority to order the Postal Service to reinstate the appellant to her prior position because the Postal Service did not coerce the appellant's transfer and it is not a party in this appeal. See id., ¶ 7 (the proper party respondent in an appeal where the issue is whether the appellant's action was involuntary based on misrepresentation is the agency that allegedly misinformed the appellant).

Because the appellant does not allege that the agency reduced the pay and grade for her current position below the amount stated in the agency's November 2012 offer letter, we find no reason to disturb the administrative judge's finding that the appellant has failed to make a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction based on a reduction in pay and grade. Further, to the extent that the appellant is challenging the process by which the agency classified her position at the GS-12, Step 6 level, upon her transfer from the Postal Service, the alleged classification error provides no basis for a finding of jurisdiction. See Ellis, 117 M.S.P.R. 511, ¶ 10. The Board has not been granted jurisdiction over cases concerning the proper classification of a position, either by statute or regulation. Id. We therefore deny the petition for review.

See D'Angelo v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3069) (when the appellant transferred to Justice from Army under the misapprehension that her tenure remained the same, but was fired by Justice as a probationer, the Board lacked jurisdiction to return her to the Army position).
