r. Security Clearance Determination

Harpole v. OPM, 98 MSPR 232, 237-41 ¶¶ 11, 14, 16-18 (2005), considered as a component of the evidence of disability, a statement of reasons supporting a proposed revocation of a security clearance, likening the statement to a fitness for duty report or the reasons underlying a revocation of a medical certification to perform a job:

In her submission of additional evidence, the appellant has presented us with an apparently novel issue, whether the Board may consider an SOR for a proposed security clearance revocation as evidence in an appeal of a disability retirement decision. We thus dispose of it at the threshold. The Supreme Court has strictly proscribed our authority to review matters related to the denial or revocation of security clearances. Department of the Navy v. Egan, 484 U.S. 518 (1988). The Board may not review the merits of a security clearance denial or revocation in connection with an appeal of an adverse action taken for "cause" related to the clearance. Pangarova v. Department of the Army, 42 M.S.P.R. 319, 321 (1989). In such cases, the Board must limit its analysis to "whether such cause existed, whether in fact clearance was denied, and whether transfer to a nonsensitive position was feasible. Nothing in [5 U.S.C. § 7513], however, directs or empowers the Board to go further." Egan, 484 U.S. at 530. In the instant case, the appellant does not seek to appeal an adverse action, but instead, she appeals OPM's decision denying her a disability retirement annuity. Despite having had ample opportunity to object to consideration of this evidence, OPM has not done so. Further, the appellant does not ask us to look behind the reasons set forth in the SOR. Her sole purpose in submitting the SOR is to provide evidence relating to the effect of her condition upon her ability to perform in the grade or class of position she last occupied. See Dunn v. Office of Personnel Management, 60 M.S.P.R. 426, 432 (1994), dismissed, 91 F.3d 169 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). The SOR plays a role similar to the supervisor's statement that normally accompanies an application for disability retirement. Such use of an SOR, we find, does not offend the Court's mandate in Egan, and we now turn to the second issue here, whether the appellant's submission constitutes new evidence under the Board's standard.

...

Further, our disability cases show that we have frequently accepted as new evidence medical reports that were developed in concurrent proceedings conducted in other fora and unavailable to the appellant when the record closed. The Board, for example, has reviewed, with appropriate caveats,[6] evidence pertaining to disability findings by the Social Security Administration and agency fitness-for-duty findings. See, e.g., Redmond v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 4 ¶¶ 10-15 (2001) (reversing initial decision due to findings by Social Security Administration, which Board accepted as new and material evidence); Furst v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 439 ¶¶ 9, 15 (1999) (reversing initial decision due to findings of agency physician in fitness-for-duty examination, which the Board accepted as new and material evidence); Pritchard v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 453, 457-59 (1996) (remanding appeal to examine Social Security award findings, which the Board accepted as new and material evidence); Biscaha v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 M.S.P.R. 304, 308-09 (1991) (awarding disability retirement after examining employing agency's corrected submission regarding the appellant's job-related medical condition, which the Board accepted as new and material evidence). The appellant's evidence submitted on PFR thus constitutes new evidence under the Board's standard.

[6] See, e.g., Trevan v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 F.3d 520, 526 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (OPM and the Board must consider an award of Social Security disability benefits in a disability retirement application under the Federal Employees' Retirement System (FERS), but may find that this evidence is outweighed by inconsistent and inadequate medical evidence); Wilmot v. Office of Personnel Management, 35 M.S.P.R. 238, 240 (1987) (a determination of entitlement to disability payments by the Social Security Administration is based on different laws and regulations and is thus not binding upon the Board in ruling on a CSRS application).

We find that the first item, the SOR, potentially affects the outcome of the appeal and is thus material. The reconsideration decision rested strongly upon the finding that the appellant's condition resulted only in an attendance deficiency, which alone does not establish disability. See Fisher v. Office of Personnel Management, 85 M.S.P.R. 30, ¶ 6 (1999); Wilkey-Marzin v. Office of Personnel Management, 82 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 11 (1999). The appellant's proposed loss of her security clearance, however, adds a new type of service deficiency. The ability to maintain a clearance is an absolute requirement of her most recent position. Without a clearance, she cannot continue in that position. The new evidence shows that her clearance was in jeopardy at least in part because her cognitive and mood difficulties affected the government's assessment of her reliability and, in the past, as the record suggests, they may have caused her to fail to safeguard classified materials appropriately. The SOR, we note, appears to have been based at least in part upon the findings of a government medical specialist to whom the appellant's employing agency referred her; by virtue of such referrals, the specialist presumably understands the high-security work environment and is qualified to make such determinations.

Additionally, in a number of our cases, we have found that the appellant's loss of a job qualification or credential for medical reasons justifies the award of a disability annuity. See, e.g., Marshall v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 561, ¶¶ 28-35 (2002) (the appellant was entitled to disability retirement; her mental disability precluded her from rendering useful and efficient service as a criminal investigator because she could no longer carry a firearm); Furst, 83 M.S.P.R. 439, ¶¶ 9, 15 (the appellant was entitled to disability retirement because the employing agency's physician found in a fitness-for-duty examination that he had suffered from degenerative joint disease for several years); Greene v. Office of Personnel Management, 12 M.S.P.R. 235, 237-38 (1982) (the appellant was entitled to disability retirement because his employing agency found that his mental disability, which required indefinite use of tranquilizer drugs, rendered him medically unqualified to perform the duties of an air traffic controller). The Board has compared the reinvestigation and adjudication process for security clearances to a fitness-for-duty examination "because both procedures determine whether an employee meets the qualifications for a position." E.g., Roach v. Department of the Army, 82 M.S.P.R. 464, ¶ 16 (1999) (citing Weber v. Department of the Army, 59 M.S.P.R. 293, 297 (1993), aff'd, 26 F.3d 140 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table)); cf. Gibler v. Office of Personnel Management, 39 M.S.P.R. 417, 421 (1989) ("We find that this policy requiring [National Guard civilian technicians appointed under 32 U.S.C. § 709(a)] to meet weight standards or face a military discharge and separation from their civilian positions for a non-disability related reason is directly related to the readiness of the National Guard and thus involves a strictly military matter outside the Board's review authority."). Finally, we note that OPM has not objected to the introduction of this new evidence. See Furst, 83 M.S.P.R. 439, ¶ 7. We thus conclude that the SOR must be weighed with all of the other evidence in the record on remand.

For the same reasons, Major O'Maille's medical report is also material and must be considered. His examination is directly comparable to fitness-for-duty examinations or medical certifications conducted by other agencies and, together with the other evidence in the record, has the potential to change the outcome of this appeal. For example, the record shows that the appellant underwent neurological testing, which turned up unexplained anomalies, and some of her psychological tests showed impaired functioning. Likewise, colleagues, relatives, and friends gave statements as to her mood disorders, forgetfulness, and impaired functioning. Further, to describe her more recent performance reviews as wholly adequate is to mischaracterize them. Her final review in the record clearly states that her supervisor had concerns about her declining performance. For all of these reasons, we find that the evidence in this case should be reexamined in light of the new evidence and any subsequent developments in the appellant's situation that are relevant to her disability application.
