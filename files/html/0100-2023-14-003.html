1. General Approach Toward Proof

Most nexus cases concern off-duty misconduct. Although nexus is required for on-duty incidents, it is generally assumed to be established when a misconduct charge involving on-duty misconduct is sustained. See SSA v. Round, CB-7521-03-0012-M-1 (NP 1/28/2011) (ALJ suspension case: "nexus is established because the misconduct occurred in the workplace and involves work-related matters"). Board decisions are filled with statements like "it is obvious that AWOL impairs service efficiency" or similar conclusions concerning insubordination, fighting, and falsification of employment applications. No close analysis is provided of exactly how service efficiency is impaired, although minor misconduct may warrant mitigation of an unreasonably harsh penalty. This result is characterized as another use of presumptions; although nexus between conduct and efficiency of the agency may not be presumed from off-duty, non-job-related incidents, serious on-duty misconduct raises a presumption that the required nexus exists. The misconduct affects co-employees, agency personnel, and agency property. McClaskey v. Dept. of Energy, 720 F.2d 583 (9th Cir. 1983). Under McClaskey, the presumption is designed to relieve the Board from expending its resources on a particularized inquiry into the nexus between the misconduct and the efficiency of the service when that relation is obvious.

Ellis v. DOD, 114 MSPR 407, 410 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 141 (2010), states the Board's approach on proof of nexus:

An agency may show a nexus between off-duty misconduct and the efficiency of the service by three means: (1) a rebuttable presumption in certain egregious circumstances, (2) preponderant evidence that the misconduct adversely affects the appellant's or co-workers' job performance or the agency's trust and confidence in the appellant's job performance, or (3) preponderant evidence that the misconduct interfered with or adversely affected the agency's mission. Kruger, 32 M.S.P.R. at 74....

Even when the impact of misconduct is unclear, some Board decisions avoid proof of impact on government operations. In Schumacher v. USPS, 52 MSPR 575, 579-80 (1992), involving an appellant who worked as a USPS sorter operator and who, in violation of agency dual employment rules, also worked for a competitor, UPS:

The appellant argues that there was no documentation to support the deciding official's testimony that his dual employment might damage the agency's image and affect his ability to compete with UPS.... [T]he administrative judge found that involvement in a conflict of interest is an offense which inherently impacts on the efficiency of the service, and that the deciding official testified that an individual simply could not be loyal to both employers and that employment with UPS could be damaging to the business interests of the agency.

An appellant's satisfactory performance and the lack of evidence showing that his off-duty conduct was publicized does not rebut the inference arising from the relation between his misconduct and the agency's mission; the agency need not demonstrate a specific impact on the appellant's job performance and the efficiency of the service before taking action against him.... Nor is the agency necessarily required to produce evidence explicitly demonstrating that an employee's off-duty conduct adversely impacts on the efficiency of the service; otherwise, agencies would have to await actual impairment of service efficiency before taking action, which would be contrary to the public interest....

Spotti v. Dept. of Air Force, 49 MSPR 27, 34 (1991), observed that nexus can be demonstrated without proof of direct impact of conduct on a particular job:

An agency need not show an adverse effect on specific duties to show the required nexus between an employee's misconduct and the efficiency of the service. See Robertson v. Department of the Navy, 29 MSPR 466, 470 n.4 (1985), aff'd, 809 F.2d 788 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table). Rather, it is sufficient for the agency to show that a risk exists because of the employee's misconduct. See Averill v. Department of the Navy, 30 MSPR 327, 331 (1986). By using marijuana before coming to work and during his lunch breaks, we find that the appellant here clearly raised the risk that his intoxication might affect his ability to repair the guidance systems of the Minuteman missiles on which he worked.

"An agency may show a nexus between off-duty misconduct and the efficiency of the service by demonstrating, among other things, that the misconduct adversely affects the appellant's or coworker's job performance or the agency's trust and confidence in the employee's job performance." Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 243 (1995). Indirect proof of nexus is permitted through expressions of agency personnel about their trust and confidence--subjective considerations--or about the effect of the appellant's conduct on the agency or workplace operations, as Benton-El v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-09-0709-I-2 (2/24/2011), involving off-duty conduct, implied:

Regarding the existence of a nexus between the appellant's off-duty criminal conduct and the efficiency of the federal service, the Board has held that an agency may show a nexus between off-duty misconduct and the efficiency of the service by, among other things, showing that the misconduct adversely affects the appellant's or co-workers' job performance or the agency's trust and confidence in the appellant's job performance. Ellis v. Department of Defense, 114 M.S.P.R. 407, ¶ 9 (2010); Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 M.S.P.R. 211, 243 (1995), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). In the instant case, the record evidence supports that the appellant's off-duty misconduct, which resulted in his being sentenced to a year's imprisonment, diminished the agency's trust and confidence in his ability to perform his assigned duties, and thereby establishes nexus. See Fouquet v. Department of Agriculture, 82 M.S.P.R. 548, ¶ 18 (1999) (finding that off-duty misconduct involving an offense of theft can reasonably cause an agency to lose trust or confidence in an employee's ability to function in his position). Given the seriousness of the appellant's actions and his position as a contract specialist, we discern no reason to disturb the administrative judge's finding that a nexus exists.

See Pope v. USPS, 114 F.3d 1144, 1147-48 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (sexual misconduct creates a double presumption: a first presumption that the employee's actions created a hostile work environment, and a second presumption that the hostile work environment harmed the efficiency of the service); Jeanquart v. VA, CH-0752-14-0565-I-1 (NP 8/17/2022) (domestic violence: "[E]ven if the rebuttable presumption of nexus were not applicable, the agency established nexus on the basis that: (1) the appellant's misconduct eroded senior management's trust and confidence in his job performance; and (2) when, as here, the appellant's former spouse was also an agency employee stationed at the same facility as the appellant, the appellant's misconduct conflicted with the agency's mission of providing a safe environment to treat sick and injured veterans.").

The problems created by presumptions in other types of litigation were recognized in a concurring and dissenting opinion in a patent dispute, Judge Plager observing in A.C. Aukerman Co. v. R.L. Chaides Construction Co., 960 F.2d 1020, 1047 (Fed. Cir. 1992):

[A] presumption such as that involved here typically is designed to place upon a party with special knowledge of the facts the burden of coming forward with that knowledge. Here...the presumption of harm [ ] does the opposite. The burden of evidentiary production is placed on the plaintiff, the party least likely to know the facts about how and in what ways the defendant was harmed by the delay....

The off-duty conduct cases that follow demonstrate the state of nexus analysis. Although the Board theoretically eschews hypothetical constructions of nexus and prefers proof, the proof it accepts is conclusory and speculative, often no more than a supervisor saying that he no longer has confidence in the appellant; meanwhile, the appellant is performing his job adequately and without complaint. If nexus is established by the speculative opinion of any supervisor that he lacks confidence in an employee who has committed some off-duty offense, the law degenerates into an incantation of magic words. When writing about nexus, AJs and the Board lapse into silence or, worse, weave syntax so complex that neither they nor others understand it. Attempts to synthesize the law leave the author with the same feeling expressed by Learned Hand in remarks attributed to him by Irving Younger. Judge Hand served on a committee that attempted to restate the law governing presumptions. The committee's governing board voted to recommit the restatement to committee for further development. Of this Hand said: "We have been over it ad nauseam. We've spent days on it. Judges and lawyers have mixed it up until no one on earth can tell what it means. I'm beaten, I'm through with it. I'm not going to do anything more with it. You can refer it back to the committee if you want, but you won't get a lick of work out of me."

An appeal may be decided--for example, Stines v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-630), concerning an off-duty threat--that bends the law back to a more rational path. Yet the path of nexus is still strewn with so much debris that establishing a predictable rule of law will likely require intervention by the Supreme Court or statutory amendment.
