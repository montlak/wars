1. Arrest

On one point the Board seems clear: an arrest for off-duty conduct does not alone supply the nexus or evidence of misconduct to support removal. Barber v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 229, 230-31, 7 MSPB 698 (1981), reversed the removal (but sustained the "crime exception" suspension) of a security guard who was discharged based on two arrests for alleged handgun assaults:

The Board agrees with appellant that there is insufficient evidence in the record to sustain his removal. According to the agency, appellant was removed because his arrests raised a "reasonable doubt...as to his innocence." The proposing official testified that he could not trust appellant in his position because "where there is smoke, there must be some fire...." It is well settled, however, that in order to support a removal action an agency must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the individual to be disciplined actually committed the complained of acts. 5 USC § 7701(c)(1)(B); Young v. Hampton, 568 F.2d 1253, 1257 (7th Cir. 1977); Phillips v. Bergland, 586 F.2d 1007, 1010 (4th Cir. 1978). The agency presented no such evidence in this case, and appellant's removal cannot stand.

The Board agrees with the presiding official's findings that a "special trust" is required of security officials such as appellant whose jobs require them to carry handguns, and that the agency "would be remiss in its obligation to protect its employees and other members of the public if it did not exercise care in the choice of persons to whom dangerous weapons were entrusted." However, this "nexus" inquiry into the relationship between off-duty misconduct and the efficiency of the service is only appropriate once an agency has proven, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the alleged off-duty misconduct did in fact occur. That prerequisite step is lacking in this case.

The Board added in Roby v. DOJ, 59 MSPR 426, 428 (1993), sustaining a removal for reasons other than the arrest that constituted one charge against the appellant:

On petition for review, the appellant argues that the administrative judge erred in finding a nexus between the DWI charge and the efficiency of the service. The agency charged the appellant as follows:

Charge I: DWI Arrest

Specifically, on January 6, 1992, you were stopped by the Highway Patrol for a breathalyzer sobriety test which indicated that you were intoxicated while operating a motor vehicle. You were then arrested and remained in custody for approximately four hours. This type of behavior constitutes off-duty misconduct.

The appellant argues that the agency may not remove an employee based upon a charge of being arrested for an offense. He argues that a nexus does not exist between the arrest and the efficiency of the service, because an arrest alone does not provide sufficient evidence that the employee has committed the prohibited acts. Although he admits that he was eventually convicted of driving under the influence, he contends that the agency charged him only with being arrested.

We agree. The Board has previously stated in dictum that "[p]resumably, if an arrest alone is not sufficient cause for an indefinite suspension, it cannot be a sufficient basis for a removal." Lawton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 53 MSPR 153, 156-57 n.7 (1992). Furthermore, other Board cases, primarily dealing with the imposition of indefinite suspensions, state that an arrest by itself is not sufficient cause for such actions and must be accompanied by other supporting circumstances showing that the appellant committed the offense for which he is charged. Dunnington v. Department of Justice, 45 MSPR 305, 306 (1990), aff'd, 956 F.2d 1151 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Larson v. Department of the Navy, 22 MSPR 260, 262 (1984).

Here, the agency has charged the appellant with being arrested for driving while under the influence of alcohol. Both the proposal letter and the decision letter discussed the appellant's subsequent conviction for that offense because it formed the basis for the second charge, and the appellant appears to have admitted his guilt to his supervisor. As noted by the appellant, however, the agency charged him with arrest, not conviction for or commission of the offense. We find that an agency cannot base a removal action upon a charge of arrest. Therefore, we reverse the administrative judge's decision to sustain the charge of "DWI Arrest."
