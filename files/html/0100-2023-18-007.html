a. Mitigation of Penalty

If an action involves harmful error, it must be set aside. If there are mitigating factors, the action may be sustained, it may be reduced, or it may be set aside--depending on the strength of the mitigation advanced by the appellant and the failure of the agency to consider the mitigating factors in its decisional process before the appeal. Sometimes confusion exists as to what constitutes harmful error and what constitutes mitigating circumstances. An example of the confusion is provided by Miguel v. Dept. of Army, 727 F.2d 1081 (Fed. Cir. 1984). An employee was fired for theft of a small quantity of soap from her work site, a commissary. The court considered the penalty excessive and remanded the case for redetermination of the penalty. The court also observed that the employee was denied the union contract right to be advised that she could have a union representative at a meeting convened to confront her with the allegation of theft. The court found this to be error, but seemed unwilling to grapple with its legal effect. Rather than calling the error harmless (given the existence of independent evidence of the theft) or harmful (given the possibility that a union representative might have advanced compelling arguments in favor of mitigation), the court directed the Board to consider the error as a factor in mitigation of the penalty. This analysis makes it difficult to distinguish the effects of error, harmful error, and legal error from standards applied to mitigation of penalties.

One decision that does not confuse the doctrines is Boatman v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 58, 63 (1994). Appellant, a correctional officer, was suspended after she left a weapon in an unattended vehicle in violation of post orders. She argued that her absence from the vehicle was excused by a provision in a collective bargaining agreement. The defense was not within the ambit of harmful error: "While alleged errors such as this--i.e., alleged errors that concern the circumstances under which the charged misconduct occurred--may be relevant to the merits of the action or to the reasonableness of the penalty imposed, they do not concern the procedures the agency applied in arriving at its decision to suspend the appellant for the charged misconduct."

Attention should be paid to a procedural error that substantially impacts a penalty determination. Under Douglas v. VA, 5 MSPR 280, 5 MSPB 313 (1981) [discussed in Chapter 7, "Mitigation or Aggravation of Penalties"], the Board defers to agency penalty choices. The Federal Circuit defers to the Board. To avoid having the harmful error rule completely eviscerated in the area of penalty determination, the Board should undertake a de novo review of the penalty when it appears that the failure of the agency to follow its own or OPM regulations (or statute) may have impaired the agency's ability to determine the penalty fairly.
