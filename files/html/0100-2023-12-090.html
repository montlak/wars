2. Authority of FSIP, Interest Arbitrators to Resolve Duty to Bargain or Negotiability Disputes

FSIP adjudication functions are discussed in Chapter 7. We consider here the Panel's role as to negotiability issues. Initially, FLRA issued Interpretation and Guidance stating that FSIP could not resolve questions concerning the obligation to bargain arising after the Panel asserted jurisdiction in an impasse. Interpretation and Guidance, 11 FLRA 626, 629 (1983). Commander, Carswell AFB and AFGE Local 1364, 31 FLRA 620, 623-25 (1988), then determined that "the Panel can resolve an impasse relating to a proposal concerning a duty to bargain issue by applying existing Authority case law":

...The Panel's role in resolving negotiation impasses is an important one for the effectuation of a successful Federal sector labor-management relations program. It is also important that the labor-management community understand the obligations and limitations relating to the appropriate procedures to resolve impasses which include duty to bargain issues. Accordingly, we set forth here our view as to the Panel's authority in this area.

In Interpretation and Guidance, 11 FLRA 626 (1983), the Authority concluded that the Panel's authority under section 7119 of the Statute to assist the parties in resolving an impasse through whatever methods or procedures the Panel considers appropriate did not include resolving questions concerning the underlying obligation to bargain. Subsequently, the Authority stated that interest arbitrators have the same authority as the Panel in resolving these issues....

The view that the resolution of duty to bargain issues arising in the context of specific proposals is a matter reserved to the Authority under the Statute has been affirmed by the courts.... For the reasons discussed below, we conclude that not every claim in an impasse resolution proceeding that a proposal is outside the duty to bargain must be addressed by the Authority instead of the Panel or an interest arbitrator.

There is now a substantial body of Authority precedent resolving numerous duty to bargain issues. That precedent is intended to provide guidance not only to the parties to the bargaining process, but also to third parties like the Panel and interest arbitrators whose function is to resolve negotiation impasses. In our view, the purposes of the Statute are best furthered by encouraging third party consideration and application of this precedent so as to assist in the resolution of negotiation impasses which raise substantively identical duty to bargain issues to those already decided by the Authority. No useful purpose is served by requiring the Panel or interest arbitrators to refrain from applying precedent merely in order to permit the Authority to address a substantively identical proposal which varies slightly in wording from a proposal previously addressed by the Authority. Accordingly, not every claim in an impasse resolution proceeding that a proposal raises a duty to bargain issue must be brought to the Authority for resolution....

We believe that the Panel and interest arbitrators have the same authority under the Statute to consider duty to bargain issues which arise in a negotiation impasse. That is, if the Panel can resolve an impasse relating to a proposal concerning a duty to bargain issue by applying existing Authority case law, the Panel may do so. In our view, this approach is consistent with the Statute because it encourages prompt resolution of impasses involving duty to bargain issues which have already been ruled on by the Authority. This approach also preserves the Panel's discretion as to whether or not to assert jurisdiction, and, as intended by the Statute, ensures that undecided duty to bargain issues will be resolved by the Authority.

It was also stated, as to the substantive review authority of FSIP and interest arbitrators, 31 FLRA at 622-23:

If a proposal raising a duty to bargain issue is presented to an interest arbitrator and that issue cannot be resolved on the basis of existing Authority case law, the arbitrator may not impose that proposal. If the arbitrator determines that he or she can resolve the duty to bargain issue and does so in an award, we will consider the following questions in order to determine whether the arbitrator applied existing Authority case law in resolving an impasse: (1) was the proposal raising the duty to bargain issue substantively identical to one which was previously addressed by the Authority? (2) were the parties' contentions before the arbitrator similar to ones addressed by the Authority in previous cases? (3) did the arbitrator cite and discuss applicable Authority case law and other relevant precedent? and (4) are there any other considerations which lead to a conclusion that the arbitrator correctly or incorrectly considered the duty to bargain issue?

Before Carswell AFB, FLRA's position was that FSIP did not have the authority to resolve disputes concerning the obligation to bargain. Section 7117(c) of the Statute specifically vested jurisdiction over negotiability issues in FLRA. ULPs based on a dispute over whether there is a bargaining obligation under the particular circumstances leading to a bargaining request were resolved by FLRA under § 7116(a)(5) of the Statute after an investigation by FLRA's General Counsel pursuant to § 7118. Although the Panel could resolve an impasse while avoiding obligation to bargain or negotiability problems, if those matters were presented they could be resolved only by FLRA. POPA and PTO, 21 FLRA 580, 580-81 (1986); see Interpretation and Guidance, 11 FLRA 626, 628 (1983). See, e.g., DODDES and Antilles Consol. Ed. Ass., 71 FLRA 127 (2019) (Member DuBester dissenting) (ULP decision relating to negotiability of FSIP-directed contract article) (recon. den., DODDES and Antilles Consol. Ed. 71 FLRA 359 (2019) (Member DuBester concurring), remanded for consideration of negotiability issue, Antilles Consol. Educ. Assn. v. FLRA, 977 F.3d 10 (D.C. Cir. 2020), FLRA decisions vacated as to remaining negotiability issue involving workday provision, leaving union to initiate negotiability appeal if proposal still relevant but rejected as nonnegotiable by agency, DODDES and Antilles Consol. Ed. Assn., 72 FLRA 414 (2021) (Chairman DuBester concurring; Member Abbott dissenting)). The reconsideration decision in Antilles Consol. Ed. Assn. noted: "because the Authority had not previously determined the negotiability of a 'substantively identical' contract provision, the Authority found that the Panel lacked the authority to impose Article 19, Section 1 on the parties. Further, the Authority addressed the negotiability of that section and found that it interfered with management's right to determine when employees performed certain work, under § 7106(a)(2)(B) of the Statute." 71 FLRA at 359.

Apart from the authority stated in Carswell AFB, an interest arbitrator acting pursuant to a direction of the FSIP may not resolve a dispute over the duty to bargain. The negotiability issue may only be resolved by the FLRA. Dept. of Air Force, Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB and AFGE Council of Locals 214, 18 FLRA 710, 712 (1985). Under SSA and Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, AFGE, 25 FLRA 238, 239-40 (1987):

In this and future cases involving allegations of nonnegotiability made in an interest arbitration proceeding, we will carefully examine the record of the case and the arbitrator's award. This examination will be made to determine whether the arbitrator made a negotiability ruling or whether the arbitrator merely applied existing Authority case law to resolve the impasse. In the event of the former action, the award will be set aside in accordance with Louis A. Johnson V.A. Medical Center [15 FLRA 347 (1984)]. In the latter, we will resolve the exceptions on the merits and sustain the award if existing case law is correctly applied. In this case, the Arbitrator states in his award that the Agency claimed that the proposal concerning accommodations for pregnant VDT operators was not negotiable but he included the proposal in the agreement as an "exception" to management's right to assign work. There is no clear Authority precedent on this issue. Accordingly, since the Agency asserted its claim of nonnegotiability, the Arbitrator did not have the authority to resolve the impasse on this issue and his award is deficient to that extent.

In cases involving allegations of nonnegotiability made in an interest arbitration proceeding, FLRA examines the case record and the arbitration award to determine if the arbitrator made a negotiability ruling or if the arbitrator applied existing precedent to resolve the impasse. In the event of the former determination, the award will be set aside; however, in the latter case, FLRA will sustain the award if the existing case law is correctly applied. SSA and Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, AFGE, 25 FLRA 238, 240 (1987); see Commander, Carswell AFB and AFGE Local 1364, 31 FLRA 620, 623 (1988), supra. To the extent an arbitrator's resolution of an impasse depends on the improper resolution of a negotiability issue, the award is set aside. DHUD and AFGE Local 476, 18 FLRA 783, 785-86 (1985) (dispute over definition of competitive area). The parties are then ordered to comply with the interest award and to resume bargaining to the extent provided for by law. DOJ, INS and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 31 FLRA 1123, 1140 (1988). Refer to Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, Lower Col. Region and NFFE Local 1487, 41 FLRA 3 (1991), discussed below as to the "Role of Arbitration," permitting FSIP-approved, jointly selected arbitrators some flexibility with regard to treatment of negotiability issues arising in the context of interest arbitration. [Refer to interest arbitration in Chapter 9.]

The review process involved in exceptions to interest arbitrators' awards based on negotiability issues was reiterated in DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 28 FLRA 871, 877 (1987), adding as to FLRA jurisdiction to review arguments concerning application of the appropriate arrangements bargaining test:

...As with an allegation of nonnegotiability raised by an agency during the interest arbitration proceeding, we view the question of whether a provision constitutes an appropriate arrangement to be a negotiability issue which must be resolved by the Authority unless the interest arbitrator relied on established precedent to find that a provision is a negotiable appropriate arrangement. In this case it is not apparent that the Arbitrator applied established precedent to find that the provision constituted an appropriate arrangement. Consequently, the award is deficient by directing inclusion of the first disputed sentence of...the collective bargaining agreement and we will strike this sentence.

Whatever the role of the FSIP or interest arbitrators as to negotiability disputes, FLRA exercises its jurisdiction over a negotiability dispute before it--even if FSIP should not have made a referral to an interest arbitrator, or if the interest arbitrator then issued challenged rulings as to negotiability issues, AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council and INS, 51 FLRA 1308, 1311-12 (1996):

...Nothing in section 7117(c) conditions the Authority's jurisdiction to render a decision regarding a duty-to-bargain allegation on the jurisdiction under section 7119 of the Panel or interest arbitrator to impose the provision that is the subject of the petition. See Patent Office Professional Association v. FLRA, 26 F.3d 1148 (D.C. Cir. 1994) (POPA v. FLRA) (in reviewing a decision of the Authority issued under section 7117(c), the court affirmed the Authority's negotiability determinations as to some provisions despite finding that the arbitrator lacked jurisdiction to award those particular provisions). Thus, the Agency's arguments that the Panel improperly referred the impasse to the interest arbitrator and the interest arbitrator exceeded his authority in making duty-to-bargain determinations do not demonstrate that the Authority lacks jurisdiction over the Union's petition.
