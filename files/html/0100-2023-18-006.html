1. Mixing Harmful Error and Other Doctrines

Affirmative defenses in Board cases include discrimination, prohibited personnel practices, and error in the application of law. Added to the affirmative defenses are pleas in mitigation. To these are added objections based on lack of constitutional due process, a defense that becomes particularly important when the reply process is tainted through ex parte communications with the deciding official. The doctrines are interpleaded and sometimes analysis is intermixed. Because the different defenses require proof of different statutory elements and may involve differing burdens of proof, care should be taken to identify the proper nature of affirmative defenses and mitigating circumstances.

Harmful error arises most frequently in adverse actions. Focus in reduction in force cases may be on the complete failure to use RIF processes when they should have been used, or the focus may be on imperfect application of OPM regulations. The Board does not ordinarily couch these analyses in terms of harmful error. Board decisions speak to whether substantive rights have been denied to the appellant. Alternatively, the Board may consider whether the violation of a RIF regulation is immaterial because of the result that is reached when the RIF analysis is reconstructed, as it should have been first applied, through the course of the Board appeal. The resulting analytical framework is, at times, indistinguishable from a harmful error analysis. [Refer to Chapter 10, "Challenge to Competitive Level," "Impact of Error," "Bona Fide RIF: Losure," and "Substantive Rights, Harmful Error."]

Harmful error goes to the procedures used in an appealed action, not ordinarily to the elements of the underlying offense charged, e.g., failure to follow instructions, English v. SBA, DE-0752-16-0306-I-1 (NP 2/3/2023):

The errors identified by the appellant relate to his supervisor's decision to revoke his telework privileges. Thus, such errors predated his failure to follow his supervisor's instruction and concern the circumstances under which the charged misconduct occurred. The Board has held that such errors do not come within the harmful error rule because they do not concern the procedures the agency applied in arriving at its decision to suspend the appellant for the charged misconduct. See Boatman v. Department of Justice, 66 M.S.P.R. 58, 63 (1994); Livingston v. Department of the Air Force, 26 M.S.P.R. 273, 275 (1985). In any event, even assuming that the agency committed a procedural error in revoking the appellant's telework privileges, the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant did not have an unfettered right to disregard his supervisor's instructions; rather, he was required to obey the order, even if he believed it to be improper, and protest its propriety later. See Howarth v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 1, 7 (1997). Thus, he has not shown that the agency likely would have reached a different conclusion in the absence or cure of any error.
