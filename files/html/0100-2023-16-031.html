d. Social Security Claims Representative

For a Social Security claims representative, the Board approved the use in standards of the terms "consistently," "often," "usually," and "generally." These measures were justified for a group of duties varying in complexity and significance; the assessment of the work of a claims representative was necessarily subjective. Seay v. DHHS, 24 MSPR 688 (1984); see Wilson v. DHHS, 770 F.2d 1048 (Fed. Cir. 1985), supra. "Performance indicators," essentially statistical benchmarks for quality of work provided through memorandum, were approved as legitimate extensions of standards, giving "content" to these standards, in Mouser v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 543, 547-48 (1987).

The standard under review in Roberson v. DHHS, 29 MSPR 201, 202 n.2 (1985), for a GS-7 Claims Technician was:

Experiences some difficulty in selecting appropriate data, extracting information from claims material, and encoding it accurately for input via SSA computer systems. Determines the sequence of actions necessary to utilize the computer systems efficiently and determines at what point data is ready to be entered into the system. Often requires technical assistance even on routine cases. Operates teletype and/or other telecommunications equipment, as assigned.

The AJ reversed the removal, finding that the standard was invalid for lack of objective criteria; the terms used--"often," "some," and "efficiently"--were too vague. The Board disagreed, 29 MSPR at 202-03:

...In his remand decision, the presiding official found that the record established that the agency's appraisal system had been approved by the Office of Personnel Management on March 13, 1981. He found further that the agency had proven by substantial evidence that the appellant had committed errors in approximately 22% (38 of 169) of the cases processed under Critical Element 14. Yet, he also found that Critical Element 14 was invalid because it "contains no objective criteria and uses only the vague terms 'some,' 'often,' and 'efficiently' as measures of performance....

...

In Seay v. Department of Health and Human Services, 24 MSPR 688, 691 (1984), the Board held that there is no per se requirement under 5 USC § 4302(b) for an agency to include quantitative criteria in each performance standard, and that, instead, an agency, as in the instant case, may establish different levels of performance based upon such terms as "consistently," "usually," "often," or "generally," even though such standards are to some extent subjective in nature. Moreover, the agency may give content to an employee's performance standard through various methods of informing the employee of specific work requirements.... The presiding official did not examine the record to determine if in fact additional specificity had been given to the appellant's performance standards.

Here, even if the written standard could have been more precise, we find that there could be no doubt that the appellant's error rate was unacceptable. Her supervisor identified her errors and discussed her work with her every 2-3 weeks. Also, the presiding official found that the agency's...60-day notice specified her errors "as well as the general and basic nature of those deficiencies would allow." Nevertheless, during the ensuing 60-day period, she had a 22% error rate. Thus, we find that the agency has proved by substantial evidence that the appellant has failed to perform acceptably in a critical element of the performance standards for her position.

Rupp v. DHHS, 51 MSPR 456, 459-60 (1991), declared adequate standards first reviewed by an arbitrator in considering a claims representative's removal for unsatisfactory performance and step increase denial:

GJT 1: Generally makes accurate entitlement determinations on assigned workload, but often requires technical assistance in making complex determinations. Often fails to process claims expeditiously and frequently requires assistance in order to identify the best available adjudication techniques.

GJT 40: Generally makes accurate and timely decisions on wages, SGA, coverage, age, eligibility, continuing eligibility, etc. but often requires technical assistance on complex cases.

As to the use of "generally" in GJT 40, the Board "conclude[d] that although the present standards may have been somewhat vague, the arbitrator did not err in finding that they were not per se invalid because they were capable of being clarified by subsequent counseling." Id. at 465.
