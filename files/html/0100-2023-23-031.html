e. Limitation on the Right to Seek Future Federal Employment

Thomas v. DHUD, 63 MSPR 649, 658-59 (1994), considered valid provisions in an agreement calling for the appellant's resignation and his agreement not to seek certain positions with the agency for a period of two years following the resignation:

The appellant argued below that the memorandum of understanding was an "illegal supplement" to the settlement agreement that violated 5 USC §§ 3301 and 3302, as well as 5 CFR § 4.3. He asserted that his promise in the memorandum of understanding not to apply for the position of Office Manager or Deputy Regional Administrator for a period of two years after his resignation violated the prohibition against one person's influencing another person to withdraw from competition for a federal position.

...

Section 3301 of title 5 of the United States Code sets forth the President's authority to prescribe regulations for the appointment of individuals to the federal competitive service, and section 3302 provides for the President's authority to prescribe rules governing the competitive service. Section 4.3 of Title 5 of the Code of Federal Regulations, which was promulgated under the authority of 5 USC §§ 3301 and 3302, provides that "[n]o person shall influence another person to withdraw from competition for any position in the competitive service for the purpose of either improving or injuring the prospects of any applicant for appointment." Reading this language literally, we note that there is no allegation that the appellant here had applied for any position at the time he signed the memorandum of understanding. He simply agreed to resign and not to apply for certain positions in exchange for a settlement agreement that cancelled his demotion, assured him a clean record, and afforded him two years with the same pay in another position while seeking employment elsewhere. This, as the administrative judge found, constitutes the lawful waiver of a right in exchange for other rights.

Choroszy v. DHS, PH-315H-16-0458-I-1 (NP 4/27/2022), summarized decisions involving settlements that restrict future employment applications to an agency:

The appellant first argues that the Board should invalidate the settlement agreement because it is counter to public policy, as it contains a provision barring him from seeking employment or working for ICE for a period of 4 years. However, these types of provisions are common in "clean record agreements" and are routinely recognized by the Board as valid. See, e.g., Thomas v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 63 M.S.P.R. 649, 659 (1994) (finding lawful a settlement agreement containing a provision in which the appellant agreed not to apply for certain types of positions in exchange for a clean record and other terms); Roane v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 1, 2-3 (1992) (noting that the valid settlement agreement contained a provision barring the appellant from employment with the agency in the future); Laranang v. Department of the Navy, 40 M.S.P.R. 233, 236 n.4 (1989) (holding that a provision barring the appellant from seeking or accepting employment with the agency for 2 years was a valid part of the agreement). Contrary to the appellant's position, public policy favors settlement agreements in administrative and civil actions. Clay v. Department of the Army, 47 M.S.P.R. 406, 408 (1991). The appellant's argument provides no basis to disturb the initial decision's dismissal of his appeal.

See Joseph v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 371, 375 (1997) (settlement provision restricting the scope of positions that appellant could seek).
