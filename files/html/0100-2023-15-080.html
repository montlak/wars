a. Clearance Process

The authority to grant federal employees access to classified material derives from Article II, § 2 of the Federal Constitution and from Executive Order 10450, reprinted in the note to 5 USC 7311. The head of each department and agency is given responsibility for "establishing and maintaining...an effective program to insure that the employment and retention in employment of any civilian officer or employee...is clearly consistent with the interests of the national security[.]" Executive Order 10450, § 2. The authority of agency heads under the Executive Order is not dependent on a specific delegation to the agency of investigative authority from the Office of Personnel Management. Read v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3437). The Order provides no appeal right or jurisdiction to the MSPB to review a clearance determination. Beagles v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3707). [Refer to Elaine L. Fitch and Mary Kuntz's Security Clearance Law and Procedure (Dewey Publications, Inc.).]

Under Executive Order 10450, the head of each department and agency is responsible for designating as "sensitive" any position that could permit the employee to "bring about, by virtue of the nature of the position, a material adverse effect on the national security...." § 3(b). Executive Order 10450 § 8(a) sets out the criteria governing applications for and restrictions or revocations of security clearances:

(1) Depending on the relation of the Government employment to the national security:

(i) Any behavior, activities, or associations which tend to show that the individual is not reliable or trustworthy.

(ii) Any deliberate misrepresentations, falsifications or omissions of material facts.

(iii) Any criminal, infamous, dishonest, immoral, or notoriously disgraceful conduct, habitual use of intoxicants to excess, drug addiction or sexual perversion.

(iv) Any illness, including any mental condition, of a nature which in the opinion of competent medical authority may cause significant defect in the judgment or reliability of the employee, with due regard to the transient or continuing effect of the illness and the medical findings in such case.

(v) Any facts which furnish reason to believe that the individual may be subject to coercion, influence, or pressure which may cause him to act contrary to the best interests of the national security.

(2) Commission of any act of sabotage, espionage, treason, or sedition, or attempts, threat or preparation therefor, or conspiring with, or aiding or abetting another to commit or attempt to commit any act of sabotage, espionage, treason, or sedition.

(3) Establishing or continuing a sympathetic association with a saboteur, spy, traitor, seditionist, anarchist, or revolutionist, or with any espionage or other secret agent or representative of a foreign nation, or any representative of a foreign nation whose interests may be inimical to the interests of the United States, or with any person who advocates the use of force or violence to overthrow the government of the United States or the alteration of the form of government of the United States by unconstitutional means.

(4) Advocacy of use of force or violence to overthrow the government of the United States, or of the alteration of the form of government of the United States by unconstitutional means.

(5) Knowing membership with the specific intent of furthering the aims of, or adherence to and active participation in, any foreign or domestic organization, association, movement, group, or combination of persons (hereinafter referred to as organizations) which unlawfully advocates or practices the commission of acts of force or violence to prevent others from exercising their rights under the Constitution or laws of the United States or of any State, or which seeks to overthrow the Government of the United States or any State or subdivision thereof by unlawful means.

(6) Intentional, unauthorized disclosure to any person of security information, or of other information disclosure of which is prohibited by law, or willful violation or disregard of security regulations.

(7) Performing or attempting to perform his duties, or otherwise acting, so as to serve the interests of another government in preference to the interests of the United States.

(8) Refusal by the individual, upon the ground of constitutional privilege against self-incrimination, to testify before a congressional committee regarding charges of his alleged disloyalty or other misconduct.

A determination that one or more of these criteria exist during the investigative stage does not per se disqualify the employee from obtaining a security clearance. However, it does act as a type of prima facie case that the employee should not be granted a clearance. The employee must then show during any clearance adjudication process that the information developed is not true, that there is no rational relation between the charges and the protection of classified information, or that there are mitigating circumstances surrounding the conduct. Ultimately, the standard for granting a security clearance is whether to do so is "clearly consistent with the interests of the national security."

Primary responsibility for conducting security clearance investigations for employees in the competitive service falls to the Office of Personnel Management unless the department or agency assumes that responsibility by law or agreement with OPM. The head of each department and agency is authorized to conduct investigations of persons who fill sensitive positions not in the competitive service. Departments and agencies without the facilities to conduct investigations may enter into an agreement with OPM to conduct investigations regarding employees holding noncompetitive positions. See Executive Order 10450, § 8(b) and (c). OPM is given responsibility for monitoring agency security clearance programs to identify "[t]endencies in such programs to deny to individual employees fair, impartial and equitable treatment at the hands of the Government, or rights under the Constitution and laws of the United States or this order." Id. § 14(a)(2).

Executive Order 10450 provides that, at the least, investigations into employees who hold sensitive positions shall include "a national agency check (including a check of the fingerprint files of the Federal Bureau of Investigation), and written inquiries to appropriate local law-enforcement agencies, former employers and supervisors, references, and schools attended" by the employee. § 3(a). If the initial investigation establishes no adverse information, the employee may then have his or her clearance granted or continued. If the initial investigation reveals adverse information, OPM or the agency is then required to conduct a "full field investigation or such less investigation" as is deemed necessary to "determine whether retention of such person is clearly consistent with the interests of the national security." Id.

Full field investigations for critical-sensitive positions focus on the background and activities of the employee over the shorter period of the past 15 years or since the employee's 18th birthday, with the most intensive efforts going into the past five years. The basic elements of the full field investigation are a national agency check, including Immigration and Naturalization Service files on naturalized citizens and State Department files on passports; personal interviews with past and present employers, supervisors, coworkers, school authorities, neighbors and references; a police and credit check; and a military service check. The investigators look at the "character, habits, morals, associations and reputation" of the subject. If adverse information surfaces calling into question a subject's loyalty, the case is referred to the Federal Bureau of Investigation. [Refer to the late FPM, Chapter 736, Subchapter 2.] Once a security clearance is granted to an employee for a critical-sensitive position, the employee is required to file a personnel security questionnaire every five years. The questionnaire is then reviewed along with the employee's personnel file, previous investigation, and any other information that has been developed to determine if further investigation is appropriate. [Refer to Elaine L. Fitch and Mary Kuntz's Security Clearance Law and Procedure (Dewey Publications, Inc.).]
