2. Jurisdiction or Timeliness as Alternate Bases for Dismissal of Appeal

The Board may elect not to decide a timeliness issue if it is simpler to address a jurisdictional issue (or the Board may dismiss the case on a timeliness issue rather than decide a thornier jurisdictional matter). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Timeliness and Jurisdictional Issues," "Timeliness of Appeal," and "Jurisdictional Issue."] The most frequently cited decision on the point, Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193, 197-98 (1991), allows AJs some discretion, depending on the state of the record, to choose among dispositive issues, with a general preference for nonjurisdictional dismissals when timeliness or other threshold reasons for dismissal are presented:

We hold here that, while the existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, a jurisdictional determination would not be in the Board's interest of adjudicatory efficiency and fairness to the parties involved in an untimely appeal in which (a) the Board arguably may have jurisdiction over the appeal and (b) the record developed on the timeliness issue shows no good cause for the untimely filing,[2] or (c) other grounds exist for the dismissal of the appeal.[3] Thus, if the record is sufficiently developed on the issue of timeliness or other grounds to show that an appeal should be dismissed on other than a jurisdictional basis, then an administrative judge may properly determine, by assuming arguendo the appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction (without actually making fact findings and conclusions of law on the jurisdictional issue), that dismissal of the appeal is warranted.[4] In all other circumstances,[5] however, the administrative judge must: (1) Make an actual jurisdictional determination; (2) make a determination on the issues of timeliness or other grounds, if necessary; (3) conduct further proceedings on the merits; and (4) make a determination on all other material issues of fact and law.[6] ...

[2] For example, an appellant, who claims that good cause has been shown for a waiver of the time limit for his untimely appeal of an alleged involuntary resignation or retirement, does not carry his burden of proof on the timeliness issue where the record shows that he failed to assert, much less establish, that the agency knew that he perceived the action as one obtained through duress or coercion and, therefore, had an obligation to inform him of his right to file a timely appeal. See, e.g., Gaynor v. USPS, 43 MSPR 481, 484 (1990). In cases such as Gaynor, the parties' substantive rights would not be prejudiced by not affording the appellant a jurisdictional hearing and a determination on that issue, because the ultimate outcome is the same regardless of any finding on the jurisdictional issue, i.e., the dismissal of the appeal by the Board without any determination on the merits.

[3] See, e.g., Wooten v. Government Printing Office, 21 MSPR 425, 426-27 (1984) (the administrative judge properly dismissed the appellant's petition for enforcement based on the equitable doctrine of laches because the appellant unreasonably and inexcusably delayed for more than 1 year in filing his petition).

[4] See, e.g., Manning v. Trustees of Tufts College, 613 F.2d 1200 (1st Cir. 1980); Hochstadt v. Worcester Foundation for Experimental Biology, 545 F.2d 222 (1st Cir. 1976)....

[5] This would include appeals where the issues of jurisdiction and timeliness are inextricably intertwined so as to require a jurisdictional determination before the timeliness issue can be addressed.

[6] This analysis applies henceforth to all Board appeals in which final decisions have not yet been rendered. See, e.g., Bivens v. Department of the Navy, 38 M.S.P.R. 67, 71-72 (1988); Fairall v. Veterans Administration, 33 M.S.P.R. 33, 39, aff'd, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

Fain v. Dept. of Educ., 98 MSPR 162, 164 ¶ 4 (2005), dismissing as untimely an appeal from an arbitrator's award denying a grievance pertaining to a performance rating, restated the Board's approach on choices among threshold issues of jurisdiction and timeliness:

Board jurisdiction is normally the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, for its jurisdiction is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). When faced with an untimely appeal of questionable jurisdiction, however, the Board may not deem a jurisdictional determination to be in the interest of efficiency and fairness, if the record is sufficiently developed to show that the appeal should be dismissed because no good cause exists for the untimely filing. Higgins v. U.S. Postal Service, 86 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶ 6 (2000). On the other hand, dismissal of an appeal on timeliness grounds may not be appropriate where resolution of the timeliness issue depends upon whether the appellant was subjected to an appealable action, and "the issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are inextricably intertwined." Id. On the issue of timeliness, the appellant bears the burden of proof. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)] .

Petric v. OPM, 108 MSPR 342, 344-45 ¶ 6, 2008 MSPB 59 (2008), explained the approach, with a preference for a jurisdictional determination, in an appeal from a claimed involuntary retirement:

In an appropriate case, an AJ may assume that an appellant was subjected to an appealable action and has standing to appeal and proceed to dismiss an appeal as untimely filed if the record on timeliness is sufficiently developed. Hanna v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 6 (2006). Such an approach is not appropriate, however, if the jurisdictional and timeliness issues are "inextricably intertwined," that is, if resolution of the timeliness issue depends on whether the appellant was subjected to an appealable action. Id. The issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are generally inextricably intertwined in a constructive removal appeal based on an alleged involuntary retirement. See, e.g., Shelton v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 126, ¶ 15 (2005). Moreover, it is undisputed that the agency did not provide the appellant with notice of his right to appeal an alleged involuntary disability retirement to the Board, and, accordingly, any time limits for filing an appeal. Therefore, because the issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are inextricably intertwined, this appeal should not have been disposed of on timeliness grounds without first addressing jurisdiction. Hanna, 101 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 6; Shelton, 99 M.S.P.R. 126, ¶ 15.

See Makse v. VA, 98 MSPR 246, 248 ¶ 8 (2005) ("Although the existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, in an untimely appeal, a jurisdictional determination is not in the Board's interests of adjudicatory efficiency and fairness to the parties involved if the record is sufficiently developed to show that the appeal should be dismissed because no good cause exists for the untimely filing."; an exception occurs when jurisdiction and timeliness are inextricably intertwined.); Labinski v. USPS, 88 MSPR 125, 127 ¶ 4 (2001) ("[T]he Board may dismiss an appeal as untimely filed without adjudicating the issue of its jurisdiction if the Board arguably has jurisdiction over the appeal, and the record developed on the timeliness issue shows no good cause for the untimely filing."); Deloach v. Dept. of Treasury, 58 MSPR 574, 577 n.1 (1993) ("Because she dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, the administrative judge made no finding with regard to the timeliness of the appeal. This was proper under Popham[.] "); Bosworth v. USPS, 51 MSPR 36, 39-40 (1991) (reiterating the Popham analysis); see also Greek v. USPS, 78 MSPR 470, 475 (1998) ("Popham holds that a jurisdictional determination may be avoided only if an appellant's well-pleaded allegations going to jurisdiction are taken as true and it is nevertheless clear that the appellant's delay in filing should not be excused."); Chapman v. GSA, 50 MSPR 204, 207 (1991) ("[W]here the record is sufficiently developed in the issue of timeliness or other grounds to show that an appeal should be dismissed on other than a jurisdictional basis, an administrative judge properly may determine, by assuming arguendo that the appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction, that dismissal of the appeal is warranted.").

Confusing, confused? You are not alone. Here is a statement, taken from Reyes v. Dept. of Navy, DC-315H-10-0222-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010), that says if jurisdiction is in question but the case is untimely, dismiss for lack of timeliness; if there is clearly no jurisdiction, dismiss for that reason:

"The existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal and ordinarily should be determined before reaching the issue of timeliness." Rosell v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 594, ¶ 5 (2005). It is proper to dismiss an appeal for untimeliness where the record is fully developed on that issue, and Board jurisdiction is unresolved, particularly where the Board arguably has jurisdiction. Id. (citing Popham v. United States Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 193, 197-98 (1991)); see also Labinski v. United States Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 125, ¶ 4 (2001). Where the Board clearly lacks jurisdiction, however, the case should be dismissed on that basis. See Rosell, 100 M.S.P.R. 594, ¶ 5; Labinski, 88 M.S.P.R. 125, ¶ 4.

The proper choice of the AJ, when the jurisdictional issue is clear, is to avoid the timeliness problem, Beaudette v. Dept. of Treasury, 100 MSPR 353, 358-59 ¶¶ 10-11 (2005):

In an appropriate case, an administrative judge may assume arguendo that an appellant was subjected to an appealable action and has standing to appeal, and go on to dismiss an appeal as untimely filed. Popham, 50 M.S.P.R. at 197. While the existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, a jurisdictional determination would not be in the interest of adjudicatory efficiency and fairness to the parties involved in an untimely appeal in which (a) the Board arguably may have jurisdiction over the appeal; and (b) the record developed on the timeliness issue shows no good cause for the untimely filing, or (c) other grounds exist for the dismissal of the appeal. Id.

Where the Board's lack of jurisdiction is clear, however, the appeal should be dismissed on the basis of jurisdiction rather than timeliness. See Epps v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 188, 190 (1996) (deciding not to address the timeliness issue found dispositive by the administrative judge after concluding that, regardless of the timeliness of the appeal, the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appeal); Rolon v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 53 M.S.P.R. 362, 365 (1992) (when lack of jurisdiction is clear, the Board may decide to avoid the timeliness issue); cf. Nicoletti v. Department of Justice, 53 M.S.P.R. 610, 615 n.3 (1992) (the Board may dismiss an appeal as untimely filed before determining whether the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal, if it arguably has jurisdiction).

Epps v. USPS, 70 MSPR 188, 190 (1996), applied Popham to avoid a timeliness decision:

We find that we need not address the issue found dispositive by the administrative judge, inasmuch as we conclude that regardless of the timeliness of the appeal, the appellant is not a person who comes within the category of those entitled to appeal RIF actions to the Board. See Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193, 197 (1991) (a jurisdictional determination may not be in the interest of efficiency where alternate grounds for dismissal exist, but jurisdiction nonetheless remains "the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal").

Rosell v. Dept. of Defense, 100 MSPR 594, 597-99 ¶¶ 5-9 (2005), reiterated that if the case can be dismissed based on a clear lack of jurisdiction, timeliness need not be addressed. In Rosell the appellant appealed after being separated for violation of a last chance agreement. The AJ dismissed the case for lack of timeliness, but the Board decided that the better route was to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, there being no assertion that the agency violated the last chance agreement that divested the Board of jurisdiction over the appeal leading to that agreement:

The existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal and ordinarily should be determined before reaching the issue of timeliness. Habdas v. Department of the Navy, 84 M.S.P.R. 412, ¶ 5 (1999); Popham v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 193, 197-98 (1991). Nonetheless, where Board jurisdiction over an appeal is unresolved but the record is sufficiently developed to dismiss the appeal on timeliness grounds, an administrative judge may dismiss the appeal on those grounds without making a determination as to jurisdiction. Popham, 50 M.S.P.R. at 197. Where the Board clearly lacks jurisdiction over an appeal, however, and where the existing record suggests that the question of timeliness is close, the better practice is to address the jurisdictional issue and dismiss the appeal on that ground. See Burger v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 582, ¶ 44 (2003), aff'd sub nom. Hayes v. U.S. Postal Service, 390 F.3d 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2004). We note that our holding here does not reverse the general rule, first set forth in Popham, that an administrative judge may dismiss an appeal on timeliness grounds where the record is sufficiently developed on that issue but where Board jurisdiction remains unresolved. Rather, our holding is limited to the narrow set of cases where uncertainty exists as to the timeliness question but the Board clearly lacks jurisdiction over the appeal.

Here, regarding the timeliness issue, the appellant attributes her two-day delay in filing her appeal to: (1) The confusion caused by her receipt of three separate letters from the agency on April 30, 2004--the decision notice, a letter informing her that her VERA/VSIP approval had been revoked, and a letter informing her that she tested positive for illegal drug use; (2) her mother's sudden bout with cancer; (3) her son's bitter separation from his wife; (4) the incarceration of her youngest son; and (5) the difficulty of moving her daughter home from college and sending her to Africa for a student exchange. The appellant also asserts that another attorney, whom she retained prior to filing this Board appeal with the assistance of different counsel, provided her with incorrect information regarding the time limit for filing the appeal. Given the appellant's good cause arguments, coupled with the minimal length of her filing delay, we acknowledge that the question of timeliness is close. See Walls v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 29 F.3d 1578, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (noting that the Board should consider factors such as the length of the delay and a showing of unavoidable casualty or misfortune in every good cause determination, and finding that good cause existed for the appellant's two-day delay in filing his appeal); Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980) (listing "the length of the delay" among the factors considered in making a good cause determination). Therefore, we reserve judgment on the timeliness of the appellant's appeal and decide the jurisdictional question that it presents.

The appellant bears the burden of proving that her appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)] . The Board does not have jurisdiction over an action taken pursuant to an LCA in which an appellant waives her right to appeal to the Board. Merriweather v. Department of Transportation, 64 M.S.P.R. 365, 375 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 83 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table)....

...The appellant responded to the administrative judge's jurisdictional show-cause order by challenging the validity of the LCA. Specifically, she argued that the precise office of the Department of Defense in which she worked at the time the LCA was executed had been "abolished" prior to her removal, and because her former office did not execute a "memorandum of agreement...to delegate authority of the LCA" to the office to which she was reassigned, the deciding official lacked the authority to remove her pursuant to the LCA. The appellant also disputed the validity of the LCA by arguing that, to her knowledge, her supervisor did not "formally" engage in the decision-making process leading to the removal action, in violation of the agency's Drug Plan. However, neither of these claims undermines the validity of the LCA, and the appellant has not shown that the agency breached the agreement in a material way or otherwise breached the agreement by acting in bad faith. See Link, 51 F.3d at 1582. The appellant likewise has failed to allege that she complied with the agreement, that she did not knowingly and voluntarily enter into the agreement, or that the agreement was the result of fraud or mutual mistake. See Hamiter, 96 M.S.P.R. 511, ¶ 8. Thus, the appellant's arguments are unavailing since she failed to establish any of the jurisdictional factors set forth above.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant waived her Board appeal rights under the LCA, and we dismiss her appeal for lack of Board jurisdiction.

If the determination of jurisdiction and timeliness cannot be separated, a jurisdictional determination is required. Toby v. VA, PH-0752-15-0289-I-1 (NP 9/23/2016), involving the question of whether the appellant had sufficient service to qualify as an employee with appeal rights, arguably untimely exercised, explained:

An administrative judge may not dismiss an appeal as untimely without determining whether an appealable action has occurred when issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are "inextricably intertwined," i.e., when resolution of the timeliness issue depends on whether the appellant was subjected to an appealable action. E.g., Brown v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶ 5, aff'd, 469 F. App'x 852 (Fed. Cir. 2011). One of the circumstances in which timeliness and jurisdictional issues are inextricably intertwined is when the jurisdictional question is whether the appellant was an "employee," as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 7511, who is entitled to appeal a removal action but was not given notice of his right to appeal his termination as an adverse action. See Smart v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶¶ 10-11 (2010), overruled on other grounds by Smart v. Department of Justice, 116 M.S.P.R. 582 (2011). The agency's termination notice did not inform the appellant that he had the right to appeal his termination to the Board as a removal action. Accordingly, it was error for the administrative judge to dismiss the instant appeal as untimely filed without first determining if the appellant had established jurisdiction. The case must therefore be remanded to the regional office to resolve whether the appellant established jurisdiction.

Under Brown v. USPS, 115 MSPR 609, 613 ¶ 5, 2011 MSPB 23 (2011), an involuntary retirement appeal, cited in Toby:

In an appropriate case, an administrative judge may assume that an appealable action occurred and that the appellant has standing to appeal and may proceed to dismiss an appeal as untimely filed if the record on timeliness is sufficiently developed. Petric v. Office of Personnel Management, 108 M.S.P.R. 342, ¶ 6 (2008). Such an approach is not appropriate, however, if the jurisdictional and timeliness issues are inextricably intertwined, that is, if resolution of the timeliness issue depends on whether the appellant was subjected to an appealable action. Id. The issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are typically inextricably intertwined in an appeal based on an alleged involuntary retirement because if the agency has subjected the employee to an appealable action then the agency's failure to inform an employee of her right to appeal may excuse an untimely filed Board appeal. See id.; Hanna v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 6 (2006); Higgins v. U.S. Postal Service, 86 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶¶ 6-10 (2000). Generally, an appellant may establish good cause for an untimely filing of an involuntary resignation or retirement appeal if, at the time of the employee's resignation or retirement, the agency knew or should have known of facts indicating that the action was involuntary but did not inform the appellant of her appeal rights. Shelton v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 126, ¶ 7 (2005). If an agency failed to advise an employee of appeal rights when it should have done so, the appellant is not required to show that she exercised due diligence in attempting to discover her appeal rights; rather, the appellant must show that she was diligent in filing an appeal after learning that she could do so. Id.; Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 583, 588 (1995).

A clearly jurisdictional issue may prove as elusive quarry, and there is another analytical problem posed by the Board in Toby: when timeliness and jurisdiction are "inextricably intertwined": the appeal cannot be disposed of on timeliness grounds without addressing jurisdiction. Parkinson v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 316, 319 (1996) (relying upon Romine v. USPS, 64 MSPR 68, 72 (1994)); Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193, 198 n.5 (1991), supra. This exception is applied in cases involving constructive suspensions. An AJ should not dismiss an appeal as untimely without deciding the jurisdictional issue presented in a constructive suspension appeal, because whether the agency was required to advise the appellant of his appeal rights depends on whether the agency actually took an appealable action. See Romine v. USPS, 64 MSPR 68, 72-73 (1994); Parker v. USPS, 59 MSPR 603, 610 (1993) ("In Popham, the Board stated that in cases where the issues of jurisdiction and timeliness are inextricably intertwined so as to require a jurisdictional determination before the timeliness issue can be addressed, it is not proper for an administrative judge to dismiss the appeal on timeliness grounds without deciding the jurisdictional issue. Popham, 50 M.S.P.R. at 197-98 & n.5. We find that this is such an appeal, because the agency had an obligation to inform the appellant of his appeal rights if--and only if--he was affected by an appealable action. We must therefore remand this appeal for further proceedings."). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action."]

The Board elaborated on intertwined issues of jurisdiction and timeliness in Wylie v. USDA, 99 MSPR 71, 73 ¶ 6 (2005), involving the claim that an involuntary retirement resulted from a RIF that was procedurally deficient and that failed to provide notice of appeal rights:

In an appropriate case, an administrative judge may assume arguendo that an appellant was subjected to an appealable action and has standing to appeal, and go on to dismiss an appeal as untimely filed. Popham v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 193 (1991). Such an approach is not appropriate, though, if the jurisdictional and timeliness issues are "intertwined." Romine v. U.S. Postal Service, 64 M.S.P.R. 68, 72 (1994). Those issues are "intertwined" if the appellant was not advised of his appeal rights, whether he should have been depends on whether the agency took an appealable action, and the agency was in a position to know whether the action was appealable. Id. at 71-73. Here, as explained below, jurisdiction and timeliness are intertwined.

See Parker v. U.S. Postal Service, 59 M.S.P.R. 603, 608-10 (1993); see also Miller v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 417, 420 (1998); Davis v. Department of the Navy, 74 M.S.P.R. 184, 188-89 (1997). Thus, because the issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are inextricably intertwined, this appeal should not have been disposed of on timeliness grounds without first addressing jurisdiction.

Smart v. DOJ, 113 MSPR 393, 397 ¶ 11, 2010 MSPB 49 (2010) (vacated for lack of jurisdiction, Smart v. DOJ, 116 MSPR 582, 2011 MSPB 72 (2011)), stated the decisional alternatives available when jurisdiction and timeliness are both in question:

We have found that where jurisdiction may be lacking, but where the record is sufficiently developed on the timeliness issue, an administrative judge may, in an appropriate case, assume arguendo that an appeal presents a matter within the Board's jurisdiction and dispose of the appeal on timeliness grounds. See Popham v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 193, 197-98 (1991). The administrative judge in the present appeal seems to have taken this approach on remand. However, where the issues of the Board's jurisdiction and the timeliness of the appeal are inextricably intertwined, as in this case, we have found that the administrative judge must first address the jurisdictional issue before addressing the timeliness of the appeal. See Delalat v. Department of the Air Force, 103 M.S.P.R. 448, ¶ 9 (2006). Accordingly, because the issues of timeliness and jurisdiction are inextricably intertwined and because the record is not sufficiently developed on the timeliness issue, this appeal should not have been disposed of on timeliness grounds without first addressing jurisdiction. Hanna v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 6 (2006).
