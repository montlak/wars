h. Scientist

Hsieh v. Defense Nuclear Agency, 51 MSPR 521, 528 n.3 (1991), stated of position standards for a GM-13 Research Physicist:

[T]he appellant's position was a technical, scientific job, in the area of nuclear radiation and biology. Jobs of such a technical nature are not susceptible to performance standards that are strictly objective, and their standards may require a degree of subjective judgment that would not be necessary or proper in a position of a less professional or technical nature.

Greer v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 477, 483-84 (1998), citing Hsieh with approval, noted of a performance action involving a GS-12 Research Chemist:

The general rule is that performance standards must, "to the maximum extent feasible, permit the accurate appraisal of performance based on objective criteria," and must be "reasonable, realistic, attainable[,] and clearly stated in writing." Benton v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR 284, 286 (1988). Performance standards should be specific enough to provide an employee with a "firm benchmark" toward which to aim his or her performance. Smith v. Department of Energy, 49 MSPR 110, 116 (1991). The degree of objectivity and specificity required in performance standards depends on the job involved, however; with greater discretion and independence reposed in the incumbent of a position comes less objectivity and specificity in the position's performance standards. See Graham v. Department of the Air Force, 46 MSPR 227, 236 n.7 (1990); Melnick v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 42 MSPR 93 (1989), aff'd, 899 F.2d 1228 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table). Indeed, "technical, scientific job[s] " are "not susceptible to performance standards that are strictly objective, and their standards may require a degree of subjective judgment that would not be necessary or proper in a position of less professional or technical nature." Hsieh v. Defense Nuclear Agency, 51 MSPR 521, 528 n.3 (1991), aff'd, 979 F.2d 217 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

Considering the scientific expertise required in the appellant's work and the independence and discretion he had in carrying out that work, a reasonable person could find that the appellant's performance standards are valid. In this connection, we note that the appellant conceded that his performance standards were attainable, and he did not argue below that the standards were invalid. It is also significant that the appellant participated in the drafting of his performance standards and did not object at that time that they were unreasonable or unduly subjective. Cf. Siegelman v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 13 MSPB 27, 14 MSPR 326, 331 n.8 (1983). Any lack of specificity in the appellant's performance standards was cured because the agency "g[a]ve content to [the standards] by informing [the appellant] of specific work requirements through written instructions, information concerning deficiencies and methods of improving performance, [and] memoranda describing unacceptable performance." Smith, 49 MSPR at 116....

In Greer, the appellant was removed for failing to meet a critical element calling for preparation of research plans. The agency faulted the plans prepared by the appellant because they lacked accomplishment milestones, did not "address 'performance, ballistic, and mechanical properties goals,' and did not contain matrices of planned mixes." The Board credited the testimony of an agency witness who evaluated the plans, 79 MSPR at 485:

The Director of Propulsion for the agency succinctly summarized the shortcomings of the two sets of research plans the appellant submitted: The plans had "no clear schedule, no obvious milestones, no way to measure accomplishment, no resources identified, [and] no time period" for completion of research. Upon comparing the appellant's plans with examples of what the agency considers satisfactory plans prepared by other researchers--one of which was provided to the appellant during the PIP--we agree with the assessment of the Director of Propulsion that the appellant's research plans were not acceptable. Normally the Board "should give deference to the judgment by each agency of the employee's performance in light of the agency's assessment of its own personnel needs and standards." Lisiecki v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 769 F.2d 1558, 1562 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (quoting S. Rep. No. 969, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 10 (1978)). Such deference is particularly appropriate in a case like this one, where the question is the adequacy of a research scientist's performance. Based on the unrebutted testimony of the Director of Propulsion describing what a research plan should contain, and considering the model research plans submitted by the agency, a reasonable person could find that the appellant's research plans were unacceptable. Accordingly, the agency has shown by substantial evidence that the appellant's performance was unacceptable on critical element three.

Of interest in Greer was the observation that the appellant's research plans were deficient in comparison with those of other researchers. Some Board AJs are reluctant to permit appellants to compare their work with that of their peers in performance cases; however, the Board allowed the agency to prove its case, in part, through comparative evidence.

Standards were approved for a GS-13 scientist requiring, for acceptable performance, that submissions "[be] of high quality requir[ing] few major revisions" and that research papers pass peer review and be approved for submission by management. Stubblefield v. Dept. of Commerce, 28 MSPR 572, 575 (1985). The Board declined to state that the agency acted arbitrarily or unreasonably by establishing performance standards that included the judgment of appellant's fellow scientists regarding his performance. The standards were not absolute because "[t]he appellant could have exceeded the standards with exemplary performance."

Consider Faust v. Smithsonian Inst., 29 MSPR 496, 497 n.1 (1985), involving the performance standard for a microbiologist. The critical element was:

In consultation with the Associate Director for Science, plan, design, coordinate and codirect the Center's long term microbial ecology programs with emphasis on the functional relationship with interactions between phytoplankton and bacteria and on the factors which control the development of microbial communities in a watershed-estuarine ecosystem.

The performance standard was, id. at 497 n.2:

The extent to which research goals for the microbial ecology programs are implemented; the development and funding of proposals for the interdisciplinary research; and the synthesis of program results.

The standard was upheld, id. at 498:

We have previously found that certain professional positions are not susceptible to a mechanical, judgment-free rating system....

...We cannot say that the agency acted arbitrarily or unreasonably in establishing performance standards which included the judgment of appellant's trained fellow scientists regarding appellant's performance.
