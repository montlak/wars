5. Involuntary Retirement

Involuntary retirement is treated as removal. The appellant has the right to a hearing if he makes a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness. See Dodson v. USPS, 67 MSPR 84, 87 (1995) (without specific assertions of fact that, if proven, would demonstrate an involuntary retirement, the appellant has no right to a jurisdictional hearing). Under Jenkins v. MSPB, 911 F.3d 1370, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2019) (Judge Reyna dissenting):

An involuntary retirement is an adverse employment action "where an agency imposes the terms of an employee's resignation, the employee's circumstances permit no alternative but to accept, and those circumstances were the result of improper acts of the agency." Schultz v. U.S. Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (internal quotation marks omitted). "The [MSPB] has jurisdiction over an involuntary retirement, which is treated as if it were a removal." Cooper, 108 F.3d at 326. "An employee thus may seek reinstatement or back pay as a result of an involuntary retirement." Id. There is a presumption that an employee retired voluntarily. Staats v. U.S. Postal Serv., 99 F.3d 1120, 1123 (Fed. Cir. 1996). To overcome this presumption, a petitioner must establish (1) the retirement was the product of misinformation or deception by the agency; or (2) the retirement was the product of coercion by the agency. Terban v. Dep't of Energy, 216 F.3d 1021, 1024 (Fed. Cir. 2000); see Staats, 99 F.3d at 1124 ("[T]he doctrine of coercive involuntariness is a narrow one."). The test for involuntariness is "an objective one" that "considers the totality of the circumstances." Garcia v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., 437 F.3d 1322, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted).

The jurisdictional bases for involuntary retirement claims were summarized, Manley v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3163):

Since employee resignations and retirements are presumed to be voluntary and former employees thus bear the burden of pleading and proving involuntariness, see Middleton v. Department of Defense, __ F.3d __, 1999 WL 600391, at *3 (Fed. Cir. Aug. 10, 1999), in order to be entitled to an evidentiary hearing before the Board on the question of jurisdiction, an employee must allege facts that, if proven, could make out a prima facie case of involuntariness. See id. We have followed a three-part test for determining when coercion or duress may be found to vitiate an apparently voluntary resignation. Coercion is shown by evidence establishing: "(1) that one side involuntarily accepted the terms of another; (2) that circumstances permitted no other alternative; and (3) that said circumstances were the result of coercive acts of the opposite party." Id. (quoting Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d 584 (Ct. Cl. 1975)). "This test is an objective, rather than subjective one; an employee's subjective feelings are irrelevant. The employee must present allegations of fact which, if proven, establish that a reasonable employee confronted with the same circumstance would feel coerced into resigning." Id.

The administrative record here reveals that the AJ stated the proper legal test and correctly applied it to his factual findings, which are supported by substantial record evidence. Manley has not alleged any facts showing that he retired involuntarily due to coercion or duress; specifically, he has not alleged that the agency "effectively imposed the terms of [his] retirement, that [he] had no realistic alternative but to resign or retire, and that [his] retirement was the result of improper acts by the agency." Staats v. United Postal Serv., 99 F.3d 1124 (Fed. Cir. 1996). He has not alleged that he was induced by a threat to take disciplinary action that the agency knew could not be substantiated, see Schultz v. United States Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136 (Fed. Cir. 1987), or that the agency took steps against him without any legitimate purpose, simply to force him to quit, see Caveney v. Office of Admin., 57 MSPR 667, 670 (MSPB 1993). Nor has he alleged that he was induced to resign based on agency misinformation or deception. See Staats, 99 F.3d at 1120; Middleton, 1999 WL 600391, at *4.

At most, Manley alleges that he faced a "hostile, unpleasant, and personally distasteful work environment" and one in which various other individuals were discriminated against. However, those facts, even if proven, do not rise to the level of coercion and thus do not make his retirement involuntary. See Staats, 99 F.3d at 1124. Nor do they justify a hearing. There are no allegations that the agency ever took any detrimental, unauthorized, or discriminatory actions against him.

Contrary to Manley's contentions, neither the governing statute nor the Due Process Clause of the Fifth Amendment guarantees a right to an evidentiary hearing on a question pertaining to the Board's jurisdiction. See id. A hearing is required only if he makes a nonfrivolous allegation that, if proven, would establish Board jurisdiction. See id. at 1125. Substantial evidence supports the Board's decision that he did not.

See Rysavy v. DHUD, 28 MSPR 263, 265 (1985) (hearing was warranted based on misleading advice to the appellant from agency personnel specialists that he could contest a RIF even though he accepted, on their advice, a discontinued service retirement prior to the effective date of the RIF); Condaxis v. Dept. of Commerce, 7 MSPR 467, 469, 7 MSPB 331 (1981) (hearing was warranted based on an assertion that the agency failed to advise the appellant that if he participated in a trial retirement program, he would lose his permanent tenure).

Under Garcia v. DHS, 437 F.3d 1322, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc), "Our cases recognize that most resignations and retirements are not constructive removals, and that 'the doctrine of coercive involuntariness is a narrow one' requiring that the employee 'satisfy a demanding legal standard.'" See Apodaca v. MSPB, 379 Fed. Appx. 986, 989 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (crediting the factual findings of the MSPB judge that the appellant requested retirement and was not forced into retirement, and that he did more than express contemplations of retirement based on the actions of the appellant and his supervisor and email traffic: "Apodaca was at all times in control of his retirement decision.").

Anderson v. DOD, DC-0752-17-0044-I-1 (NP 1/18/2023), summarized factors in the analysis of an involuntary retirement claim:

Retirements are presumed to be voluntary, and the appellant bears the burden of proving otherwise. Axsom v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 605, ¶ 12 (2009). To overcome the presumption that a retirement was voluntary, the employee must show that the retirement was the result of the agency's misinformation or deception, or that the retirement was coerced by the agency. Id. To establish involuntariness on the basis of coercion, an employee must show that the agency effectively imposed the terms of the employee's retirement, the employee had no realistic alternative but to retire, and the employee's retirement was the result of improper acts by the agency. Id. The touchstone of the "voluntariness" analysis is whether, considering the totality of the circumstances, factors operated on the employee's decision-making process that deprived her of freedom of choice. Id. If an employee claims that her retirement was coerced by the agency creating intolerable working conditions, the employee must show a reasonable employee in her position would have found the working conditions so difficult or unpleasant that they would have felt compelled to retire. Id. The Board addresses allegations of discrimination and reprisal in connection with an alleged involuntary retirement only insofar as those allegations relate to the issue of voluntariness. Id.

...

...Although the appellant has alleged unpleasant working conditions, she has not alleged facts to show that her working conditions were so intolerable such that a reasonable person in her position would have felt compelled to retire or resign. See Miller v. Department of Defense, 85 M.S.P.R. 310, ¶ 32 (2000) (explaining that dissatisfaction with work assignments, a feeling of being unfairly criticized, or difficult or unpleasant working conditions generally are not so intolerable as to compel a reasonable person to resign)....

Retirement is not coerced should the appellant lack a desirable choice among alternatives. The appellant is not coerced by the choices of applying for disability retirement, exhausting his leave, or eventually being removed. Davis v. USPS, 6 MSPR 316, 317, 6 MSPB 269 (1981); see Long v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3637) ("That the agency may have considered firing Mr. Long for some activity does not convert his voluntary action of accepting early retirement into an involuntary action."); Silvestri v. Dept. of Army, 60 MSPR 594, 598-99 (1993) (it was not coercive for the agency to offer discontinued service retirement based on a reassignment that the appellant declined because he thought the agency would seek to remove him for unacceptable performance after reassignment); O'Neal v. USPS, 37 MSPR 125, 128 (1988) (appellant whose physical disability precludes him from working and instead accepts involuntary retirement for a source of income does not demonstrate the involuntariness of his decision); cf. Stockton v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-13-0434-I-1 (NP 7/18/2014) ("Even if the appellant genuinely felt that he had no alternative but to retire, in part due to his medical conditions, he has failed to identify any coercive or improper act on the part of the agency that could have left a reasonable person in his position with no other choice but to retire. See Vitale v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 107 M.S.P.R. 501, ¶ 26 (2007) (although an agency official may have caused the appellant apprehension and exacerbation of his medical ailments, he failed to establish that his working conditions were so intolerable that a reasonable person in his position would have felt compelled to retire)."); West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 562 (1990) (discounting the appellant's claims that he was not truly disabled; OPM's allowance of the retirement refuted the appellant's assertion; the treatment of other employees was not determinative since they were not similarly situated). A retirement is not coerced because an agency separates an employee after the agency is notified by OPM that the employee has been granted disability retirement. The agency's administrative response to OPM's grant of the appellant's retirement application is not an agency-imposed separation action, assuming no wrongdoing on the part of the employing agency that led to the retirement application. Mavronikolas v. USPS, 51 MSPR 592, 597 (1991). Retirement is not involuntary because the alternative is unemployment. Malone v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 458, 459 (1985).

Arroyo v. United States, 229 Ct. Cl. 647 (1981), described some standards governing an involuntary retirement claim:

Whether or not a separation is voluntary depends upon all the facts and circumstances. It is the substance of a transaction rather than its form that governs.... Petitioner's retirement may be found involuntary if it was induced by Government action causing duress.... The mere fact that petitioner faced two unpleasant alternatives, however, does not mean that his retirement was coerced.... Petitioner must show that a reasonable person would find that (1) one side involuntarily accepted the terms of another, (2) circumstances permitted no alternative, and (3) circumstances were the result of coercive acts of the opposite party. Even if a petitioner resigns under protest to avoid a threatened termination, the resignation is voluntary.

Arroyo determined that the employee was counseled as to his performance and that adverse action was proposed when there was no improvement. His supervisors never discussed retirement among themselves or with the employee. There was nothing involuntary about his retirement. See Thome v. DOD, 22 MSPR 24, 25 n.* (1984) (retirement was not forced when the appellant returning from overseas assignment faced a choice between termination of employment or registration in an agency priority placement plan.); Langa v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3709) (retirement was not coerced by the choice of accepting the agency's incentive payment and retirement or not retiring and, facing an uncertain future); Allen v. OPM, 77 MSPR 212, 220 n.4 (1998) ("[A]n employee's awareness that the agency was in the process of restructuring and that his future placement was uncertain does not suffice to raise a nonfrivolous allegation of an involuntary retirement under 5 U.S.C. ch. 75 based on agency coercion, misrepresentation, or duress, since he could have resolved the uncertainty by waiting to determine how the reorganization would ultimately affect him. See Staats v. U.S. Postal Service, 99 F.3d 1120, 1125-27 (Fed. Cir. 1996); Taylor v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 67, 71, aff'd, 121 F.3d 727 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table); O'Clery v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 300, 303-04 (1995), aff'd, 95 F.3d 1166 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table); Bissett v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 631, 637 (1995)."); Taylor v. USPS, 73 MSPR 67, 72 (1997) ("[E]mployee's awareness that the agency was in the process of restructuring and that his future placement was uncertain does not suffice to raise a nonfrivolous allegation of an involuntary retirement...since he could have resolved the uncertainty by waiting to determine how the reorganization would ultimately affect him"; no evidence of affirmative misrepresentation by agency); Patrick v. USDA, 72 MSPR 509, 523 (1996) (the loss of annuity income caused by a decision late in the application window for an early retirement added a disadvantage, but it did not compare to the pressure of making a decision to end a career in less than a day), aff'd, Patrick v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3104); Holman v. Dept. of Treasury, 9 MSPR 218, 220 (1981) (retirement was not coerced when management conducted a study of a possible reorganization that could result in the abolition of some positions, but no management decision was ever issued concerning the implementation of the proposal); Aronson v. SSA, 8 MSPR 650, 653 (1981) (an allegation that a retirement was caused by a disagreement with a supervisor is insufficient to meet the test of coercion; the appellant had a grievance pending, the implication being that he could have addressed the problem within the organization); cf. Dougherty v. DOD, 66 Fed. Appx. 872, 873-84 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (depression leading to retirement suggested a choice between the unpleasant alternatives of fighting a removal and accepting retirement).

The bottom line is that retirement is an adverse action if it is coerced. Edgerton v. MSPB, 768 F.2d 1314, 1316-17 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (citing Perlman v. United States, 490 F.2d 928 (Ct. Cl. 1974)). But the bar to proof of coercion is a high one. The test of an involuntary retirement is that the employee involuntarily accepts the terms of the agency, circumstances permit no other alternative, and the circumstances are the result of coercive acts of the agency. Edgerton (citing Scharf v. Dept. of Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574 (Fed. Cir. 1983), and Taylor v. United States, 219 Ct. Cl. 86, 591 F.2d 688 (Ct. Cl. 1979)). Retirement may be coerced through a combination of circumstances, e.g., both time pressure and misleading statements from agency personnel concerning insurance benefits and appeal rights. See Middleton v. DOD, 185 F.3d 1374 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (discussed earlier in this chapter, "Jurisdictional Showing" under "Constructive Adverse Action"); Barry v. DHUD (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3564) (retirement was coerced when the agency first offered a retirement option to an appellant who declined a geographical reassignment and then withdrew the offer and threatened the appellant with a misconduct removal for AWOL that would apparently not qualify the appellant for retirement and would leave him unable to challenge the reassignment).

Because an involuntary retirement is treated as an adverse action, the Board acquires jurisdiction only if it would have the authority to adjudicate an adverse action appeal by the appellant. See Khan v. United States, 201 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (involuntary retirement claim of a VA healthcare professional appointed under 38 USC 7401(1) was not appealable to Board; the claim was within the province of the VA grievance system); Falso v. OPM, 116 F.3d 459, 461 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (remanding the involuntary retirement claim for a determination by the Board of whether the employer, VA, should be joined as a party, avoiding the issue of the timeliness of an appeal as to the VA), following remand, Falso v. OPM, 77 MSPR 207 (1998); Falso v. OPM, 70 MSPR 470, 475 (1996) (remanding for consideration of whether the involuntary retirement claim by a VA health professional appointed under 38 USC 7401(1) would be within the jurisdiction of the Board). USPS, 82 MSPR 327, 331 ¶ 12 (1999), explained the necessity of personal jurisdiction over a Postal Service window clerk who brought a constructive adverse action appeal:

...A Postal Service employee may file a Board appeal under chapter 75 only if he is covered by 39 USC § 1005(a) or 5 USC § 7511(a)(1)(B). 5 USC § 7511(b)(8). To appeal an adverse action under chapter 75 of title 5, an employee of the Postal Service must, among other things, be a veteran with preference-eligible status, a management or supervisory employee, or an employee engaged in personnel work in other than a purely nonconfidential clerical capacity. 39 USC § 1005(a); USC § 7511(a)(1)(B); see Toomey v. When the appellant was a reemployed annuitant terminable at the will of the appointing authority, there is no right to appeal a retirement that was based on allegedly improper information from the agency concerning the amount of future retirement benefits. See Bovay v. SBA, 100 MSPR 175, 177-78 ¶ 7 (2005) ("for even if the appellant were to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that his retirement was involuntary, the Board would nevertheless lack jurisdiction over his appeal. Title 5 U.S.C. § 3323(b)(1) provides that a reemployed annuitant receiving an annuity serves at the will of the appointing authority. Hence, as a general rule, such an employee has no right to appeal his separation to the Board. Hays v. Department of the Air Force, 84 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 14 (1999), review dismissed, 232 F.3d 905 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table); Ochoa v. Department of the Navy, 65 M.S.P.R. 39, 43 (1994).").
