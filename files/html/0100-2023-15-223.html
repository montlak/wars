c. Administrative Law Judge; Judicial Independence

An administrative law judge should be able to bring complaints to his chief judge about support personnel over whom he has no control without risking discipline; although the criticisms were accompanied by vulgar remarks, the remarks were made in private and did not warrant discipline. Offensive remarks made to subordinates or staff members are, however, serious. Inclusion of inappropriate statements in a decision written by an ALJ may be good cause for discipline, but the discipline must not impermissibly impinge on the right of an ALJ to determine the content of his opinion. Discipline is not warranted for isolated statements relating to the substance of the case. SSA v. Glover, 23 MSPR 57, 76-78 (1984).

Another ALJ case decided that disruptive expression of personal dissatisfaction on a matter of office organization was not a matter of public concern and not protected by the First Amendment. But comments expressed in memos from an ALJ were not properly the basis for discipline when the supervisor who was the target of the criticism stated that they were not considered abusive or demeaning. There was no testimony that the supervisor's administrative authority was affected by the remarks or that the comments affected professional relations with the employee or others. Ordinarily, demonstration of disrespect by a subordinate to a supervisor affects the close association between supervisor and subordinate and the need for the supervisor to have confidence in and loyalty from the subordinate in performance of his duties. Disrespectful comments by a subordinate to a supervisor may be cause for discipline because of the effect of those comments on service efficiency. The relation between the ALJ and the Chief ALJ was one of independence. The limited administrative functions of the supervisor did not require personal loyalty from the ALJ or involve a professional relationship damaged by critical comments. SSA v. Brennan, 19 MSPR 335, 340 (1984), and 27 MSPR 242, 249-50 (1985). [The Brennan decisions are discussed in Chapter 6, "Administrative Law Judges."]

The second Brennan decision, 27 MSPR at 250 n.8, observed that in appeals decisions under the efficiency of the service standard, the Board distinguishes critical comments from loud, abusive, threatening, obscene, or sexually suggestive comments:

In appeals decisions under the efficiency of the service standard, the Board has distinguished merely critical comments from loud, abusive, threatening, obscene, or sexually suggestive comments. Compare Wheat v. Veterans Administration, 21 M.S.P.R. 54 (1984) (appellant's telling his supervisor that he was untruthful and that appellant did not trust him was not insulting or insubordinate) with Muldrow v. Veterans Administration, 22 M.S.P.R. 586 (1984) (shouting and making sexually suggestive and threatening comments was insubordinate) and Hemelt v. Veterans Administration, 21 M.S.P.R. 74 (1984) (use of insulting, abusive, and obscene language was insubordinate).
