b. Effect of Grievance or Other Settlement; Collective Bargaining Contract

Bradley v. USPS, 69 MSPR 595, 597-99 (1996), supra, involving an excepted service employee, implied that settlement agreements that create a break in service followed by reappointment may require completion of a new waiting period. A three-day break in service resulting from a settlement agreement reinstating an excepted service employee with the intent to interrupt his service prevented completion of a one-year period of continuous employment essential to Board jurisdiction over the appellant's removal. Hayes v. USPS, 36 MSPR 622, 625 (1988) (service was discontinuous when the employee's indefinite suspension was settled through a last chance agreement placing the employee on a probationary status; "the appellant has failed to establish that the true nature of his employment was anything other than two appointments, separated under an agreement that was properly designed to interrupt his service"); see Stewart v. USPS, 55 MSPR 362, 364 (1992) (15-month break in service based on settlements precluded jurisdiction). When an individual settled an adverse action proposal by resigning and the employee then complained that the settlement was involuntary, the agency argued that the employee did not have a year of current, continuous service at the time of the resignation. That issue, however, would not be reached if the settlement leading to the demotion was set aside. If the settlement was vacated, the employee would be returned to the position he held before settlement, and in that position that appellant met service requirements necessary to support an appeal. See Short v. USPS, 66 MSPR 214, 221 (1994). An individual in a period of probation, who was terminated and later reinstated as a probationer through an EEO settlement, was required to serve a full probationary period following the reinstatement when she did not win retroactive relief through settlement. Good v. Dept. of Navy, 48 MSPR 108, 111-12 (1991) (relying on FPM Ch. 315, subch. 8-a(b)(1)).

Sufficient service may be accrued if under a contract an arbitrator reinstates an employee with full service credit for the time lost through an improper removal. The employee then gains the necessary service credit to appeal to the Board if the employee is again terminated. Sanders v. USPS, 32 MSPR 455, 458 (1987) (whether back pay was awarded by an arbitrator appeared to be immaterial). However, while a collective bargaining contract provides greater than statutory procedural protections to disciplinary action, the negotiated contract will not bestow jurisdiction on the Board. Weinberger v. USPS, 4 MSPR 330, 332, 4 MSPB 395 (1980).
