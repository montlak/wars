broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 01: Settlement, Section B: Setting Aside a Settlement, Subsection 07: Breach of Settlement: Election Between Rescission or Enforcement, Part c: Enforcement Petition to Rescind Settlement; Late PFR Raising Enforcement Issue
c. Enforcement Petition to Rescind Settlement; Late PFR Raising Enforcement Issue

A petition for enforcement (as opposed to a PFR seeking to set aside an initial decision adopting a settlement) is used to set aside the agreement due to a material breach. The procedure for exercising the option is for the nonbreaching party to move in a petition for enforcement to enforce the terms of the agreement or rescind the agreement and go forward with the original appeal. Komiskey v. Dept. of Army, 70 MSPR 607, 609 (1996) (enforcement petition appropriate to seek rescission of agreement based on alleged breach); Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 109, 112 n.2 (1992) ("[w]here one party has breached a settlement agreement, the nonbreaching party may move in a petition for enforcement to enforce the terms of the agreement or rescind the agreement and go forward with his original claim.").

The appellant seeking to set aside an agreement because of an agency breach should not seek that relief initially with the Board through the PFR process; the remedy should be sought by an enforcement petition filed with the regional office. Whiteside v. Dept. of Treasury, 56 MSPR 173, 175 (1992). In Castellanos v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 385, 389 (1991), the appellant filed a PFR claiming that the agency breached an agreement to remove references to his removal action from his official personnel file. Although the Board concluded that the PFR was untimely, the Board continued:

As to the contentions by each party that the other party violated the terms of the settlement agreement, these contentions are properly raised in a petition for enforcement before the regional office, rather than in a petition for review.... The agency requested that the settlement agreement be vacated, the appeal reopened, and the removal action "reinstated." The appellant presented arguments pertaining to the merits of the removal action...and thus also appeared to be requesting that the removal action be adjudicated on the merits.... In this regard, we note that if one party breaches a settlement agreement, the other party has the option of enforcing the terms of the settlement agreement or rescinding the agreement and going forward with the original claim....

Therefore, we FORWARD the appeal to the Dallas Regional office for consideration of each party's contention regarding the other's noncompliance with the settlement agreement and the parties' request that the agreement be set aside based on such noncompliance....

If the contention is not that the agreement was breached, but that it was invalid due to fraud or mutual mistake, an attack on the settlement should proceed by PFR timely filed after the initial decision approving the settlement. Once the initial decision is issued accepting a settlement, the AJ has no authority to consider a request that the settlement be rescinded for fraud or mutual mistake. Breach of the agreement, not its basic invalidity, is raised through an enforcement petition with the AJ. Underlying invalidity is raised through a timely PFR. See Saunders v. Dept. of Interior, 56 MSPR 671, 673-74 (1993) (appellant must file the PFR within the regulatory time limit from the time he knows of the basis for the invalidity of the settlement). The process, and the elections within the process, were summarized by Keith v. USPS, 56 MSPR 668, 669-70 (1993):

When an administrative judge dismisses an appellant's appeal pursuant to a settlement agreement, and the appellant believes that the agreement is invalid because it was obtained by fraud or coercion, or because it was based on mutual mistake, he may file a petition for review of the initial decision dismissing his appeal. See Frizzell v. Department of the Air Force, 53 M.S.P.R. 413, 415 (1992); Strange v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 674, 676 (1991). If the employee is not challenging the validity of the agreement, however, but instead believes that the agency has failed to comply with the provisions therein, he may file a petition for enforcement with the regional office that issued the initial decision on his appeal. See 5 C.F.R. 1201.182(a). If the appellant substantiates his allegation that the agency has breached the agreement, he is entitled to either enforcement of the agreement or rescission of the agreement and reinstatement of his claim. See Murphy v. U.S. Postal Service, 54 M.S.P.R. 202, 206 (1992).

The Board became more direct concerning the proper approach in Evans v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 222, 225-26 (1992) (relying on Castellanos). In Evans the Board dismissed an untimely PFR challenging a settlement, but it sent the case back to the region for adjudication of what became an enforcement issue:

The appellant's contention on petition for review that the settlement agreement should be set aside because the agency violated the terms of the settlement agreement is properly raised in a petition for enforcement before the regional office.... Accordingly, we FORWARD the appeal to the San Francisco Regional Office for consideration of this contention.... The regional office shall determine whether the enforcement claim is timely raised, see Adamcik v. US Postal Service, 48 MSPR 493, 496-98 (1991), and, if so, whether the agency violated the terms of the settlement agreement. If so, the regional office shall afford the appellant the option of enforcing the terms of the settlement agreement or rescinding the agreement and reinstating the appeal for adjudication of the merits of the removal action....

See Strange v. USPS, 48 MSPR 674, 677 (1991) (if the appellant's enforcement petition suggests that he seek both enforcement and rescission, the AJ must determine from the appellant the remedy of choice).

The Board synthesized the law concerning the use of procedures for enforcement and rescission of settlements, and the use of PFRs to set settlements aside for fraud and mutual mistake, Sena v. DOD, 66 MSPR 458, 463-64 (1995):

When one party commits a material breach of a settlement agreement, the other party is entitled either to enforce the settlement agreement or to rescind the agreement and reinstate his or her appeal. Holmes v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 57 MSPR 115, 119 (1993); Betterly v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 47 MSPR 63, 67 (1991). We first consider whether it is appropriate for us to adjudicate this issue on petition for review, or whether the appellant's petition for review should be forwarded to the regional office as a petition for enforcement under 5 CFR §§ 1201.181-.183.

The Board has stated that a pleading which alleges that the other party breached a settlement agreement, but which does not contend that the administrative judge erred in dismissing the appeal pursuant to the agreement, is in substance a petition for enforcement, which should be considered initially by the regional office. See Butanowicz v. Department of the Treasury, 48 MSPR 550, 555 (1991); Willis v. US Postal Service, 32 MSPR 580, 582 (1987). Our compliance regulations do not, however, provide for exclusive jurisdiction by the regional office when a party seeks rescission of a settlement agreement and reinstatement of his or her appeal, rather than enforcement of the agreement. Indeed, those regulations directly address only petitions seeking to enforce Board orders. See 5 CFR §§ 1201.181-.183. We also note that the Board considers petitions for review seeking to set aside settlement agreements on grounds of fraud or mutual mistake. See De Luna v. Department of the Navy, 58 MSPR 526, 528-29 (1993).

Remand to the regional office is appropriate where the factual record is insufficiently developed to enable us to adjudicate the agency's compliance with the settlement agreement. See, e.g., Forston v. Department of the Navy, 60 MSPR 154, 161-62 (1993); Betterly v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 47 MSPR 63, 67 (1991). In the instant case, both parties have filed extensive submissions, including affidavits, documentary evidence, and legal argument regarding the agency's compliance with or breach of the settlement agreement. We therefore find it appropriate under the circumstances to adjudicate the matter here. See De Luna, 58 MSPR at 529. Cf. Forston, 60 MSPR at 162 (Board did not follow usual practice of adjudicating fraud issues and forwarding compliance issues to regional office in light of circumstances and interest in judicial efficiency).

If the appellant files a late PFR challenging a settlement accepted through an initial decision, the Board may deny the PFR as late but remand the appeal to the AJ if the PFR alleges an enforcement issue, that is, that the agency failed to abide by the agreement. See Rotelli v. Dept. of Navy, SF-315H-17-0113-I-1 (NP 7/15/2022) ("Under the circumstances, the appropriate course is to forward the appellant's claim to the regional office for docketing of a petition for enforcement. See Gard v. Department of Education, 97 M.S.P.R. 64, ¶¶ 7-8 (2004) (dismissing as untimely filed with no good cause a petition for review in which the appellant expressed dissatisfaction with the settlement process, but forwarding the appellant's allegations of noncompliance to the regional office for docketing of a petition for enforcement)."); Owen v. USPS, 87 MSPR 449, 453 ¶ 9 (2000) (PFR to set aside an agreement was untimely; remanded for compliance adjudication when the appellant alleged the agency breached a confidentiality agreement, an allegation that, if true, could justify rescission of the agreement); Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 109, 112 (1992) (declining to reopen a settlement on an untimely PFR, but remanding the case to the region to consider the case as an enforcement petition seeking either the enforcement or rescission of the agreement).

If during enforcement proceedings, the agency cures the breach, the Board may decline to honor a request to rescind the agreement, Wine v. Dept. of Interior, DA-0752-18-0116-X-1 (NP 2/10/2023):

Although the appellant seeks to rescind the settlement agreement and reinstate his removal appeal due to the original breach of the agreement, we hold that such relief is inappropriate where, as here, the agency has cured the breach and the appellant has received the full benefit of his bargain. See Tretchick v. Department of Transportation, 109 F.3d 749, 752 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (rejecting suggestion that there is an "absolute right" to rescission in response to a breach and rejecting rescission where any purported breach had been cured); King v. Department of the Navy, No. 98-3342, 1999 WL 37406, at *2 (Fed. Cir. Jan. 12, 1999) (unpublished) (affirming denial of rescission where the agency had cured its breach of the settlement agreement 6 years later, and the appellant provided no evidence that she was harmed by the breach or the delay in curing it); cf. Lutz v. U.S. Postal Service, 485 F.3d 1377, 1381-82 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (finding material breach potentially justifying rescission where "negative statements contained in" the agency forms "prejudiced the disability proceedings" and resulted in denial of benefits). Thus, assuming arguendo that the agency's breach was material--which the administrative judge did not address, and we need not now decide--we find that, as in Tretchick, rescission is inappropriate because the agency cured its breach of the agreement and the appellant received all the benefits to which the agreement entitled him....
