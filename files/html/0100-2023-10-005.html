2. Jurisdictional Hearing; Documentary Evidence

5 USC 7701(a)(1) guarantees the appellant a hearing in an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction. See Staples v. MSPB, 684 Fed. Appx. 951, 954 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP) ("Mr. Staples complains that he was denied a hearing, but the Board's rules permit a hearing on the merits only if the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.24(d). Because the Board lacked jurisdiction here, Mr. Staples was not entitled to a hearing."). Historically, the Board's position is that cases not involving customary removals, reductions in pay or grade, suspensions of more than 14 days, appeals from OPM retirement decisions, and RIF appeals, warrant hearings if the appellant makes nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction. Typical cases that require a preliminary showing of jurisdiction include constructive suspensions resulting from enforced leave and coerced resignations, downgradings, or retirements. Whistleblower reprisal appeals involving personnel actions not normally within the appellate Board's jurisdiction require nonfrivolous jurisdictional allegations for the appellant to obtain a Board hearing. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action" and "Jurisdictional Showing."]

In recent years there has been persistent criticism by the Federal Circuit of the Board's characterization of "jurisdictional" hearings. The Federal Circuit's view, simplified (and not without ambiguity and some internal contradiction in decisions), is that jurisdiction is established by nonfrivolous allegations and supporting evidence from the appellant. When jurisdiction is established, the appeal moves forward to a merits hearing. The distinction is of significance. If a case is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, assuming no impediment based on the timeliness of the appeal, an appellant may be able to bring the case again, with better evidence of jurisdiction, and avoid a bar through res judicata or collateral estoppel. If the appeal is heard on the merits, the final decision should have preclusive effect. The byplay between the Board and the Federal Circuit is reflected in the discussion that follows. The final word on the subject has not been written. What vitality remains in the concept of a jurisdictional hearing remains to be seen. The Board continues to refer to hearings on jurisdiction, as in Funari v. VA, DA-315H-12-0319-I-1 (NP 11/27/2013):

At the outset, we note that the administrative judge inappropriately dismissed the appellant's appeal based upon his failure to prove jurisdiction by preponderant evidence without a jurisdictional hearing. See Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 325, 329 (1994). Rather, an appellant is entitled to a hearing on jurisdiction if he makes a nonfrivolous allegation[3] that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal. See Ferdon, 60 M.S.P.R. at 329....

[3] Nonfrivolous allegations" are allegations of fact that, if proven, could establish that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter at issue. Walker v. Department of the Army, 119 M.S.P.R. 391, ¶ 6 n.2 (2013).

5 CFR 1201.4(s) (2022), defines the sometimes elusive nonfrivolous allegation:

Nonfrivolous allegation. A nonfrivolous allegation is an assertion that, if proven, could establish the matter at issue. An allegation generally will be considered nonfrivolous when, under oath or penalty of perjury, an individual makes an allegation that:

(1) Is more than conclusory;

(2) Is plausible on its face; and

(3) Is material to the legal issues in the appeal.

5 CFR 1201.24(d) (2022), states the right to a hearing:

(d) Right to hearing. An appellant generally has a right to a hearing on the merits if the appeal has been timely filed and the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal.

Given the lack of clarity of the regulations on what it really takes to establish jurisdiction in an appeal not clearly within the Board's jurisdiction, it is no surprise that there have been a lot of decisions attempting to provide definition.

Of hearing entitlement in cases alleging constructive actions, a topic discussed at length in Chapter 6, a Federal Circuit decision of significance in determining sufficiency of jurisdictional allegations, Garcia v. DHS, 437 F.3d 1322, 1344 (Fed. Cir. 2006), determined:

[U]nder 5 U.S.C. §§ 7701 and 7512, once a claimant makes non-frivolous claims of Board jurisdiction, namely claims that, if proven, establish the Board's jurisdiction, then the claimant has a right to a hearing. At the hearing, the claimant must prove jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence. If the Board determines that the claimant fails to prove jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence, then the Board does not have jurisdiction and the case is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction.

See Washington v. MSPB, 492 Fed. Appx. 106, 108 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP) (summarizing Garcia, quoted in Chapter 6.]

Archerda v. DOD, SF-4324-12-0055-B-1 (NP 3/31/2014), applying Garcia, explained when a hearing is waived as to a jurisdictional determination:

If an appellant makes nonfrivolous allegations of fact that, if proven, could establish jurisdiction over his appeal and he has requested a hearing, then he is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing. See Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322, 1344 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc). Where the appellant has not requested a hearing, however, the threshold question is not whether he has raised a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction, but whether he has established by preponderant evidence that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal. See id. Here, the appellant withdrew his hearing request shortly after the administrative judge began a hearing on remand. Furthermore, the appellant was properly apprised of the elements and his burden to establish Board jurisdiction over his alleged involuntary reduction in grade and/or pay claim, and the appellant responded to the administrative judge's orders to submit evidence and argument on the jurisdictional issue. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Thus, because the existing record is fully developed on the jurisdictional issue, the Board may make a jurisdictional determination based on the written record. See Vitale v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 107 M.S.P.R. 501, ¶ 18 (2007) (finding that the Board could make a finding on jurisdiction where the appellant did not request a hearing and the record was fully developed on the jurisdictional issue).

At times the Federal Circuit avoids entangling itself in distinctions between hearings on the merits and hearings on jurisdiction and simply refers to an evidentiary hearing on a disputed jurisdictional issue, Coradeschi v. DHS, 439 F.3d 1329, 1332 (Fed. Cir. 2006):

An appellant to the MSPB must prove jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)] ; however, when jurisdiction is factually disputed and jurisdiction cannot be determined by the documentary evidence, the MSPB should hold an evidentiary hearing to resolve the jurisdictional question once the appellant has made non-frivolous allegations that jurisdiction is proper. See Garcia v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., [437 F.3d 1322] (2006) (en banc); Pervez v. Dep't of the Navy, 193 F.3d 1371, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Manning v. MSPB, 742 F.2d 1424, 1427-28 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Thus, we must review the record de novo and determine whether Coradeschi has made non-frivolous allegations that he was an employee and should therefore be granted a hearing where he may present further evidence that he meets the § 1201.56 jurisdictional burden.

Holloway v. Dept. of Interior, 95 MSPR 650, 654 ¶ 12 (2004), affirmed the use of a jurisdictional hearing. The Board obliquely noted in the IRA case that "the appellant's nonfrivolous allegations, along with his exhaustion of the OSC process, were sufficient to establish the Board's jurisdiction, so that a jurisdictional hearing was not required." In Niemi v. Dept. of Interior, 114 MSPR 143, 146 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 107 (2010), raising the possibility, but resolving against the appellant, the question of whether service prior to his probationary appointment qualified the appellant as an employee with ordinary adverse appeal rights, the Board noted in passing: "[b]ut if an appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the jurisdictional question. Upshaw v. Consumer Products Safety Commission, 111 M.S.P.R. 236, ¶ 13 (2009)." Cf. Pettus v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0353-13-0409-I-2 (NP 11/5/2015) ("Accordingly, we find that the appellant has nonfrivolously alleged facts that, if proven, would establish that she was a physically disqualified individual and that the agency violated the restoration rights to which she was entitled. She therefore is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which she must prove jurisdiction by preponderant evidence."), [Refer to Chapter 13, "Rusin Model: Nonfrivolous Allegations Establish Jurisdiction; Hessami," for the Rusin case and the decline of jurisdictional hearings in IRA cases; refer to Chapter 2 "Hearing on Jurisdictional Issue"; refer to Chapter 1, "Appeal Rights Based on Military Service and Veterans Preference," for discussion concerning the pleading standards to obtain hearings in VEOA and USERRA cases; Chapter 6 should be reviewed concerning pleading requirements in cases involving constructive adverse actions, indefinite suspensions, and enforced leave.]

McCray v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 154, 160 ¶ 11 (1998), involving an alleged coerced resignation, provides a straightforward explanation of the requisites of a jurisdictional hearing:

To establish entitlement to a jurisdictional hearing, an appellant need not allege facts which, if proven, definitely would establish that the resignation was involuntary; she need only allege facts which, if proven, could establish such a claim. See Braun, 50 F.3d at 1008; Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894. As the court stated in Braun:

We further hold that Mr. Braun has made a non-frivolous allegation that, if proven, could establish that his resignation was coerced. This non-frivolous allegation is all that is required to trigger the Board's jurisdiction at this threshold stage. It would be illogical to require a petitioner to prove in advance by preponderant evidence that a resignation or retirement was involuntary to secure a hearing on that very issue. When there is a question as to the voluntariness of the petitioner's resignation or retirement, and the petitioner makes a non-frivolous allegation of the involuntariness of the resignation or retirement, an evidentiary hearing is required to determine whether the resignation or retirement was in fact involuntary.

50 F.3d at 1008 (citations and footnote deleted). See also Hurwitz v. Department of the Army, 61 MSPR 436, 440 (1994) (finding that the AJ erroneously suggested that the appellant needed to prove duress in order to gain the right to a hearing, when she was only required to nonfrivolously allege it).

An appellant who alleges facts sufficient to support a prima facie case is entitled to an evidentiary hearing. Melvin v. USPS, 79 MSPR 372, 376 ¶ 1 (1998) (USERRA case). Stiles v. Dept. of Navy, 36 MSPR 646, 649 (1988), reviewed the relation between jurisdictional issues and Board hearings:

At the outset, there is no statutory authority requiring the Board to hold a hearing on the threshold issue of jurisdiction. See Rose v. Department of Health and Human Services, 721 F.2d 355, 357 (Fed. Cir. 1983). Section 7701(a) of Title 5 provides a right to a hearing on the merits, see Crispin v. Department of Commerce, 732 F.2d 919, 922 (Fed. Cir. 1984), but only after jurisdiction has been properly invoked. See Rose, 721 F.2d at 357. There are certain cases where a hearing on jurisdiction would be appropriate, as where the appellant raises non-frivolous issues of fact relating to jurisdiction which cannot be resolved simply on the submissions of documentary evidence. See Manning v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 742 F.2d 1424, 1427 (Fed. Cir. 1984)....

If the appeal is clearly not within Board jurisdiction, a hearing will not be granted. See Johnson v. MSPB, 431 Fed. Appx. 915, 917 (2011) ("'There is no statutory authority requiring the [Board] to hold a hearing on the threshold issue of jurisdiction.' Manning v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 742 F.2d 1424, 1427 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The right to a hearing applies 'only after jurisdiction has been properly invoked.' Id. at 1428."); Beadle v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 193, 197 (1989) (no error in denying a hearing to a terminated probationer who presented no factual assertions supporting his claim that he was terminated for partisan political reasons); Wizi v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 192, 193 n.2, 6 MSPB 169 (1981) (AJ properly denied a hearing to an employee who claimed agency error when he was not selected for a position; there was no jurisdiction over the agency action).

If jurisdiction is disputed, it is important that the appeal specifically describe events establishing a bona fide claim to Board jurisdiction and to a Board hearing. If there are disputed facts that go to the Board's jurisdiction, the AJ should convene a hearing. In Murray v. DMA, 1 MSPR 350, 350-51, 1 MSPB 338 (1980), the appellant alleged he involuntarily retired, an action considered tantamount to a removal. The AJ reviewed unsworn statements in the record and concluded that the retirement was voluntary. The Board reversed and remanded for a hearing: "Since an involuntary retirement is tantamount to a removal, a hearing is required here to resolve this disputed factual issue. See Bell v. Groak, 371 F.2d 202, 204 (7th Cir. 1966); cf. Goodman v. United States, 358 F.2d 532, 533 (D.C. Cir. 1966); Dabney v. Freeman, 358 F.2d 533, 535 (D.C. Cir. 1965)." Because an involuntary retirement is tantamount to a removal, a hearing is required to determine voluntariness if the facts are disputed. A hearing may be convened to take evidence on a jurisdictional issue and the AJ may then resolve the issue against the appellant without continuing the hearing into other areas. See Carmichael v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 135, 137 (1982) (limiting the hearing to evidence establishing jurisdiction over a probationer's appeal).

The right to a hearing is based on well-founded allegations of fact tied to jurisdiction. The hearing may be denied if the appellant submits only conclusory statements that are rebutted by specific agency submissions on jurisdiction. Wilson v. MSPB, 807 F.2d 1577, 1583 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Nonetheless, the rule is straightforward: the appellant is entitled to a hearing on jurisdiction when he seeks to establish that jurisdiction and when he has raised a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction. Habdas v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 412, 417 ¶ 10 (1999) (appellant was entitled to a jurisdictional hearing based on a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction; the AJ may consider the agency's documentary submissions in determining whether the appellant made a nonfrivolous jurisdictional allegation, but the AJ may not weigh the evidence and resolve conflicting assertions or provide the agency's evidence dispositive weight); Jones v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 310, 311 (1989) (question of whether an appellant who alleged a demotion was in a permanent or temporary position). Welby v. USDA, 101 MSPR 17, 22-23 ¶¶ 12-15, 2006 MSPB 1 (2006), analyzed what the appellant must prove to establish the right to a hearing on jurisdiction and the merits of denial of a reemployment priority right.

There are decisions suggesting that entitlement to a hearing does not require proof; the appellant need only make a nonfrivolous claim of Board jurisdiction to obtain a jurisdictional hearing. Sweeney v. Dept. of Interior, 73 MSPR 329, 332-33 (1997) ("The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Board have held that, where an appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the jurisdictional question. Dumas v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 789 F.2d 892, 894 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 325, 329 (1994). Nonfrivolous allegations of Board jurisdiction are allegations of fact which, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter in issue. Id."). But a jurisdictional hearing is not warranted without an allegation of facts that, if proven, establish a prima facie case of an appealable action. See Nortz v. VA, 47 MSPR 526, 533 (1991). A hearing was required as to whether the employee had sufficient federal service in the same or similar positions to qualify for appeal rights. At issue were the duties performed by the appellant. Schmidt v. USPS, 39 MSPR 188, 191-92 (1988). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action" and "Jurisdictional Showing."]

An example of the Board's analysis of the distinction between an allegation of nonfrivolous jurisdictional facts and a factual showing of jurisdiction is provided in Waldau v. USPS, 54 MSPR 193, 195 (1992), involving whether a Postal Service employee qualified as a management employee statutorily entitled to a hearing:

...An appellant is not required to prove Board jurisdiction to entitle him to a hearing; rather, he need only make a non-frivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal....

We find that the appellant has made such an allegation. The appellant submitted an objection to the agency's motion to dismiss his appeal in which he contested the agency's definition of manager. He set forth specific examples of what he considered to be management functions that he performed. Thus, the appellant's argument concerning jurisdiction was nonfrivolous because it was based on more than mere conclusory allegations. See Alford, 47 MSPR at 274. Furthermore, in her initial decision, the administrative judge proceeded to consider this argument and the evidence and argument submitted by the agency to make factual findings concerning the weight of the evidence. Indeed, as previously stated, she did not conclude based on these submissions that the Appellant failed to make a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction; rather, she concluded that he did not meet his burden of proving that he was a management employee. This determination should not have been made until after a hearing. See, e.g., Alford, 47 MSPR at 274.

If the evidence submitted by the parties on jurisdiction is conflicting, ambiguous, or insufficient to make a solid jurisdictional ruling, the AJ should grant a hearing, Ferdon v. USPS, 60 MSPR 325, 329-30 (1994):

Although the threshold issue of whether the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal may be disposed of on the basis of the documentary record in appropriate cases, such disposition is not always permissible. Dumas v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 789 F.2d 892, 894 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Manning v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 742 F.2d 1424, 1427-28 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Board have held that, where an appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the jurisdictional question. Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894; Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Schmidt v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 MSPR 188, 192 (1988). Nonfrivolous allegations of Board jurisdiction are allegations of fact which, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter in issue. Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894. In determining whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction entitling him to a hearing, an administrative judge may consider an agency's documentary submissions. See Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894; Manning, 742 F.2d at 1428; see also Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 796 F.2d 1469, 1472 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Newman, J., additional views). But, to the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions of the parties and the agency's evidence may not be dispositive. See Dumas, 789 F.2d at 893-94.

The administrative judge may consider the agency's documentary evidence which tends to show that the appellant has no right to appeal to the Board. In Manning, 742 F.2d at 1428, for example, the administrative judge properly considered the agency's unrebutted evidence that Manning was not on forced leave for a period of 14 days or more. Similarly, where an appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation that his resignation was involuntary due to coercion, the administrative judge may consider the agency's uncontradicted documentary evidence that the appellant was a probationary employee who would not be entitled to appeal rights absent a nonfrivolous claim of marital status or partisan political discrimination. To the extent that the Board's decision in Collins v. Defense Logistics Agency, 55 MSPR 185, 189 (1992), held that the agency's evidence may not be considered at all, we modify it here.

See Perry v. Dept. of Treasury, PH-0752-11-0156-I-1 (NP 9/29/2011) ("Furthermore, the appellant's allegation that he was improperly denied the right to a hearing is unpersuasive because he failed to raise allegations of fact which, if proven, could establish that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal. See also Briscoe v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 55 F.3d 1571, 1573 (Fed. "); Kimzey v. In constructive adverse actions (discussed in Chapter 6) there will ordinarily be a hearing concerning the facts supporting the allegation that an adverse action was constructively taken against the appellant, assuming the existence of nonfrivolous allegations of fact supporting jurisdiction. But if the issue is more technical, e.g., the rights secured under a particular form of appointment, no hearing is obtained unless material issues cannot be resolved on documentary materials alone. See Soehngen v. DOJ, 47 MSPR 169, 172 (1991). The same result occurs in stay cases brought under the Whistleblower Protection Act. If it appears that the Board lacks jurisdiction, and if the jurisdictional issue can be resolved on documentation alone, a hearing is not granted. Wagner v. EPA, 53 MSPR 300 (1992). The analysis was restated by Wagner v. EPA, 54 MSPR 447, 451 (1992):

In Alford, 47 MSPR at 274, the Board held that an appellant is not required to prove Board jurisdiction to establish entitlement to a hearing, but rather, need only make a nonfrivolous allegation that the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal. Even after an appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation of the Board's jurisdiction, however, he is entitled to a hearing only if the nonfrivolous allegation cannot be decided based on the documentary evidence of record. See, e.g., Manning v. MSPB, 742 F.2d 1424, 1428 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Jarman v. SBA, 44 MSPR 514, 516 (1990); O'Neal v. USPS, 39 MSPR 645, 649, aff'd, 887 F.2d 1095 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table).

Wagner, supra, 54 MSPR at 452, concluded that the appellant's allegations did not "establish that a preliminary hearing was warranted on the issue of the Board's jurisdiction over his alleged exclusion from training." The reference to a "preliminary hearing" was interesting but otherwise not expounded on by the Board. Are there to be two hearings in doubtful cases: one on jurisdiction, if documentation will not suffice, and then one on the merits? Laity v. VA, 54 MSPR 51, 54 (1992), referenced the concept of a jurisdictional hearing without explaining how it might differ from a merits hearing. The appellant filed an IRA appeal, following resort to OSC, complaining of a reprimand that he received allegedly as a result of his complaints of "obstruction of government efficiency," "goldbricking," and violations of agency policy requiring the cleaning of clinical areas prior to the cleaning of administrative areas. The AJ dismissed the case because the appellant did not cite any law or regulation that was being violated. The Board first determined that the AJ's acknowledgment order should have but did not tell the appellant that he was required to "show that he made disclosures concerning a violation of law, rule, or regulation, or...gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds." The Board then noted that the employee cited specific laws and regulations concerning his allegations, and described in detail the conduct that violated those regulations, before both the AJ and on PFR:

We find that the appellant has made nonfrivolous allegations of fact relating to the Board's jurisdiction over his claim of reprisal for his asserted disclosures of violations of law, rule, or regulation that cannot be resolved based on the documentary evidence of record. Thus, we conclude that the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the jurisdictional issue....

It cannot be fathomed from Laity what the jurisdictional hearing is supposed to accomplish. If the appellant states facts within the Board's jurisdiction, he is entitled to a hearing on the merits, not a hearing on jurisdiction. In constructive adverse action cases the hearing on jurisdiction and the merits merges. According to the Board's view of constructive adverse actions, jurisdiction depends on proof of the constructive adverse action; having demonstrated a noticeless constructive action, the appellant then proves both jurisdiction and entitlement to a remedy by deprivation of the constitutional due process required in an adverse action. Winner takes all. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Jurisdictional Showing," for the interaction of jurisdiction and merits dispositions in constructive adverse action appeals.]

Matters become analytically more complex when the Board attempts to discern whether jurisdictional allegations are sufficient to obtain a hearing. The Board may require, in addition to allegations, detailed information through sworn declarations, possibly statements in pleadings (e.g., the appeal), or documents to convert what the Board sometimes calls a "bald allegation" (the Board's way of characterizing the jurisdictional allegation as insubstantial or frivolous) to an allegation sufficiently complete to warrant a hearing. There are cases, however, that permit an AJ to direct the submission of evidence to allow the AJ to make a more informed jurisdictional determination. Ellis v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 102, 106-07 (1997), provided a concise statement of the AJ's authority to require evidence to support allegations, but Ellis, involving a constructive demotion, supplied no more than an abstruse explanation of how that discretion should be exercised:

...It is true that an allegation, standing alone, may not be sufficient to warrant a jurisdictional hearing, and the Board may request sufficient evidence to determine if, in the first instance, there is any support for what otherwise might be bald allegations. Briscoe, 55 F.3d at 1573; see also Clede v. Dept. of the Air Force, 72 MSPR 279, 284-86 (1996), aff'd, 113 F.3d 1257 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table). The AJ here did not explicitly require the appellant to submit specific documentary evidence, however. Cf. Manzan v. USPS, 68 MSPR 616, 620 (1995) (in remanding the appeal for further jurisdictional development, the Board stated that the AJ may require the appellant to submit documentary evidence in support of his jurisdictional claim before determining whether his allegations are sufficient to entitle him to a jurisdictional hearing). Moreover, unlike in Briscoe and Clede, where the pertinent jurisdictional evidence regarding the appellants' alleged mental incapacity was likely to be within their possession and control, the pertinent evidence here regarding the circumstances surrounding the reclassification of the appellant's former position is likely to be within the possession and control of the agency. Indeed, the appellant contended below that she had "limited access to the paper trail generated by the agency in upgrading her previous position," and requested that, if the AJ determined that her submission was insufficient to afford her a jurisdictional hearing, she be allowed to complete discovery before her appeal was dismissed. Cf. Briscoe, 55 F.3d at 1574 (the court dismissed Briscoe's argument that she was unable to support her jurisdictional allegations because the Board would not allow adequate discovery, finding that her discovery motions were not likely to reveal relevant information and that she did not show why she could not submit statements or affidavits from her doctors or others to support her bald allegations of mental incapacity). Furthermore, the appellant did submit a sworn affidavit, attesting to the alleged jurisdictional facts in a somewhat specific and detailed manner, which could constitute evidence. Cf. Truitt v. Dept. of the Navy, 45 MSPR 344, 347 (1990) (sworn statements that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters asserted therein). We therefore find, under these circumstances, that the appellant raised nonfrivolous jurisdictional allegations regarding the upgrading of her former position.

Requiring the production of evidence in addition to nonfrivolous allegations, or requiring evidence as a component of a nonfrivolous allegation, amounts to the use of summary judgment on jurisdictional issues. The Board does not use the label of summary judgment--to the contrary, the Board asserts that it is precluded from using summary judgment. However it is labeled, the relation between evidence and allegations in the disposition of jurisdictional issues is a matter of some significance. To establish a jurisdictional basis for a hearing in an appeal not indisputably within the Board's jurisdiction, e.g., an adverse action, the appellant's best route is to provide allegations, evidence supporting the allegations, and legal analysis on the jurisdictional issue--like a response to a summary judgment motion.

The Federal Circuit criticized the Board, without much effect, for failing to distinguish a dispositive jurisdictional issue and proof of a claim within Board jurisdiction. The concept of nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction sufficient to warrant an evidentiary hearing, as opposed to a "jurisdictional hearing," was developed in Dick v. VA, 290 F.3d 1356, 1361-62 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (partially overruled on other grounds, Garcia v. DHS, 437 F.3d 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2006)), involving a claimed constructive adverse action:

Confusion arises in cases where the issues before the Board are "at one and the same time jurisdictional issues--requiring a non-frivolous allegation for Board jurisdiction--and merits issues--requiring a determination in favor of the employee if relief is to be awarded." Walley, 279 F.3d at 1019. When confronted with such issues, we have held that the Board need not hold a separate hearing to determine jurisdiction. Yunus, 242 F.3d at 1371. Indeed, in Rose v. Department of Health and Human Services, 721 F.2d 355, 357 (Fed. Cir. 1983), we held that there is "[n]o statutory right to a hearing to contest jurisdiction" because "5 U.S.C. § 7701(a)(1) applies only after jurisdiction has been properly invoked," and that the denial of a hearing on jurisdictional issues is not a violation of due process. In Staats v. United States Postal Service, 99 F.3d 1120, 1124-25 (Fed. Cir. 1996), we again noted that 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a) 'does not provide for a hearing on the question whether the Board has jurisdiction in a particular case, and the Due Process Clause does not mandate a hearing where the statute does not require that one be afforded." There simply is no right to a hearing on the threshold issue of jurisdiction. After making a non-frivolous allegation of the involuntariness of a retirement or resignation, an employee has a right to a hearing on voluntariness, Burgess v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985), but such a hearing is a hearing on the merits, not a jurisdictional hearing.[3]
