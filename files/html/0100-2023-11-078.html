a. Federal Personnel Manual; Agency Handbook, Publication

Hamlet v. United States, 63 F.3d 1097, 1103-04 (Fed. Cir. 1995), commented on agency personnel manuals:

Obviously, not every piece of paper released by an agency can be considered a regulation entitled to the force and effect of law. See Piccone v. United States, 407 F.2d 866, 877, 186 Ct. Cl. 752 (1969) (Nichols, J., concurring); Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 280-81 (D.C. Cir. 1977). However, several courts have held that an agency manual or handbook can be a binding agency regulation. In Service v. Dulles, 354 U.S. 363, 77 S. Ct. 1152, 1 L. Ed.2d 1403 (1957), the Supreme Court held that an agency employee's removal was invalid because it violated the United States Department of State's Manual of Regulations and Procedures. The Court held that the unpublished manual, promulgated in accordance with the relevant executive order and statute, was binding on the Department. Id. at 374-76, 77 S. Ct. at 1158-59. Similarly, in Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535, 538-40, 79 S. Ct. 968, 971-73, 3 L. Ed.2d 1012 (1959), the Court held that the Department of Interior was required to comply with the procedural standards set forth in an internal order before dismissing an employee, even though in the absence of such standards the employee could have been dismissed summarily. The Court treated the provisions of the informal order as "regulations." Id. at 539-40, 79 S. Ct. at 972-73. Moreover, in Thorpe v. Housing Authority of Durham, 393 U.S. 268, 274-76, 89 S. Ct. 518, 522-23, 21 L. Ed.2d 474 (1969), the Court held that a circular distributed by the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) was binding on the Department since the circular was issued pursuant to the agency's general rule-making powers and was intended by the agency to be mandatory. The Court evaluated the circular as "an administrative regulation." Id. at 276, 89 S. Ct. at 523.

The Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) was abolished on December 31, 1994. DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 51 FLRA 210, 211 n.2 (1995). While it was extant, the Board considered FPM as guidance by OPM to the agency rather than as binding law or regulation. The FPM could be entitled to weight if it was consistent with congressional policy on the issue being treated. Brumley v. Dept. of Transp., 40 MSPR 1, 3 (1989); see Horner v. Jeffrey, 823 F.2d 1521, 1530 (Fed. Cir. 1987) ("it is extremely doubtful whether FPM provisions in general can achieve the status of law"); Collins v. MSPB, 978 F.2d 675 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (FPM provides guidance but it did not create rights); Jolly v. Listerman, 672 F.2d 935, 940 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (speaking of the FPM and relying on Piccone v. United States, 407 F.2d 866, 877 (Ct. Cl. 1969): "[n]ot 'every piece of paper emanating from a Department or Independent Agency is a regulation.'"); Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 281 (D.C. Cir. 1977) ("[T]o determine the effect of a Manual provision, a court must determine the Commission's [now OPM's] intent in authoring it, as ascertained by an examination of the provision's language, its context, and any available extrinsic evidence."); Rubel v. OPM, 89 MSPR 247, 251 n.* (2001) (speaking of OPM FERS Handbook: "While the manual does not appear to have been the product of a formal rulemaking or adjudication process, it is nevertheless entitled to some deference."); cf. Adams v. United States, 59 4th 1349, 1358-59 (Fed. Cir. 2023) (en banc; Judges Reyna and Newman dissenting) ("Although the HDP Supplement comes from a Federal Personnel Manual that is no longer in force, we have continued to regard the Federal Personnel Manual as 'a valuable resource for construing regulations that were promulgated or were in effect' before it was discontinued in 1993. See Schmidt v. Dep't of Interior, 153 F.3d 1348, 1353 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 1998)."); Clark v. DOJ, CH-0752-15-0288-I-2, n.2 (NP 10/21/2022) ("The CSRS and FERS Handbook is a public document, of which we take administrative notice. See Azdell v. Office of Personnel Management, 88 M.S.P.R. 319, 323 (2001).").

Whitsell v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3054), spoke of the FPM as guidance:

...Although the Federal Personnel Manual was not in effect at the time of the reduction in force at issue in this case, the Manual was long regarded as a useful source of guidance as to the proper construction of the "competitive area" regulation, which was promulgated while the Manual was still in effect.

A 1971 OPM bulletin entitled "Your Retirement System" was not entitled to official notice. The appellant did not produce a copy of the tome and "its contents are not common knowledge or readily verifiable." Bost v. OPM, 43 MSPR 310, 314 n.5 (1990). Publication of pay information in the Federal Employee News Digest was not entitled to official notice; the document was not printed by government authority. Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 7 MSPR 366, 368, 7 MSPB 246 (1981).
