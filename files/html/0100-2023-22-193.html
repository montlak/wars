a. Hearing Concerning Fee Entitlement in Settled Appeal

Before Buckhannon Bd. v. West Virginia DHHR, 121 S. Ct. 1835 (2001), and Sacco v. DOJ, 90 MSPR 37, 39-42 ¶¶ 4-11 (2001) [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Cancelation of Action Appealed"], hearings were requested to establish the appellant as a prevailing party entitled to fees. The 2001 decisions brought an end to counsel fee requests based upon actions canceled before hearing, and earlier law allowing hearings or other evidentiary review to establish prevailing party status became obsolete. See Schneider v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 462, 465 (1989); Brito v. GPO, 31 MSPR 335, 339 (1986); Payne v. Dept. of Interior, 29 MSPR 625, 627-28 (1986); Wood v. U.S. Mint, 24 MSPR 615, 620 (1984); Kemper v. DHUD, 9 MSPR 231, 233 n.3 (1981); Hodnick v. FMCS, 4 MSPR 371, 376 n.6, 4 MSPB 431 (1980). Hearing requests could be of significant when there is a factual dispute over the reasonableness of a fee petition. [Refer in this chapter to "Jurisdictional to Accept and Enforce Settlement Providing for Fees or Supporting Fee Motion" concerning the process for the appellant who settles an appeal involving a jurisdictional issue, e.g., a constructive removal, and seeks the Board to accept the settlement into the record for purposes of enforcement.]

Under Mitchell v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 103, 111-12 (1991), "[d]eterminations as to whether an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice under 5 USC 7701(g)(1) should be made on the basis of the existing record, even when that record is incomplete because the parties settled the appeal before presenting their evidence":

In Miller v. Department of the Army, 42 MSPR 421 (1989), the Board considered an issue similar to that presented here. In that case, like this one, the parties settled the appeal before they had presented their evidence concerning the merits of the appellant's removal, and the appellant subsequently filed a motion for an attorney fee award. The Board, after finding that the appellant was the prevailing party, remanded the appeal so that the hearing the appellant had requested concerning her entitlement to a fee award could be held.... It held that, in a case settled before the presentation of evidence, an administrative judge should grant an appellant's request for a hearing when an informed finding could not otherwise be made concerning the appellant's entitlement to an attorney fee award....

The appellant in this appeal has asked that a hearing be held if there is any question of fact regarding his entitlement to a fee award.... Under the holding stated above, the appellant's request would be granted. We have given that holding further consideration in this case, however. As the dissenting opinion in Miller indicated, an appellant generally is entitled, on filing a removal appeal, to present evidence concerning his allegations of agency error at a hearing and through documentary submissions. See 5 USC 7701(a)(1); Miller, 42 MSPR at 428 (Levinson, Chairman, dissenting). If the appellant in this appeal had done so, the administrative judge (and perhaps the Board) would have made findings and conclusions concerning the discrimination allegation and concerning the other allegations and arguments presented in the appeal. By settling his appeal without having submitted that evidence, he voluntarily relinquished this right, along with the benefits he might have obtained by exercising the right, in return for the benefits he was assured of obtaining under the settlement agreement.

We note further, as did the dissenting opinion cited above, that the Supreme Court has stated that a request for an attorney fee award "should not result in a second major litigation," see Miller, 42 MSPR at 428 (Levinson, Chairman, dissenting), citing Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424, 437, 103 S. Ct. 1933, 1941 (1983). In a case in which the underlying appeal was settled without a hearing or other significant submission of evidence, a hearing concerning the merits of the appellant's allegations of agency error would be especially inappropriate; the hearing would, in effect, constitute the principal litigation in the case. Under those circumstances, one of the major benefits of settling the appeal--avoiding the time and expense of a complete hearing on the merits of the appeal--would be lost. See Miller, 42 MSPR at 429 (Levinson, Chairman, dissenting).

For the reasons stated above, we hold that determinations as to whether an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice under 5 USC 7701(g)(1) should be made on the basis of the existing record, even when that record is incomplete because the parties settled the appeal before presenting their evidence. To the extent Miller and other Board decisions are inconsistent with this holding, they are hereby overruled.

In light of the holding stated above, we find that the appellant is not entitled to the hearing he requested below. We find further that the appellant has failed to meet his burden of showing that an award of attorney fees in connection with his removal is warranted in the interest of justice. See Blumenson v. Department of Health and Human Services, 30 MSPR 644, 647 (1986) (appellant bears burden of proving entitlement to attorney fee award).

See DiGiulio v. Dept. of Treasury, 66 MSPR 659, 665 n.2 (1995) (fee motion following settlement: "Determinations as to whether an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice...should be made on the basis of the existing record.... Because the record does not require further development and the dispositive issues are legal rather than factual, we resolve them here rather than remanding the appeal for further adjudication."); McIver v. Dept. of Interior, 52 MSPR 644, 647 (1992) ("We have recently held that determinations as to whether an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) should be made on the basis of the existing record, even when that record is incomplete because the parties settled the appeal before presenting their evidence. Mitchell v. Department of the Navy, 51 MSPR 103, 112 (1991).").

Before Mitchell, the hearing procedure was recommended with settlements of cases raising jurisdictional issues, e.g., alleged involuntary resignations. See Shaw v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 588-59 (1989), overruled by Joyce v. Dept. of Air Force, 74 MSPR 112, 115 (1997) (involving the rescission of a constructive suspension and removal) (partially overruled by Joyce v. Dept. of Air Force, 83 MSPR 666, 668 ¶ 10 (1999)); see also Miller v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 421, 427-28 (1989) (overruled in Mitchell, supra); Wilde v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 149, 152 (1989) (fee application arising from a settlement: "[R]emand is warranted to afford the appellant an opportunity to establish that her resignation was coerced and, thus, was within the Board's jurisdiction[.] ").

The Board is unclear about what it takes to demonstrate entitlement to fees if a case is settled before an evidentiary record is compiled. Perhaps allegations, if sufficiently detailed and factually supported, made in the fee petition (or in the underlying appeal and attachments, agency response, and prehearing submissions) will suffice if those allegations establish fee entitlement according to Board case law. It is unknown how the Board will treat agency responses contradicting such factual assertions. See McIver v. Dept. of Interior, 52 MSPR 644, 649-50 (1992) (allegations of error described in fee petition did not rise to the level of gross procedural error).

The parties may agree in their settlement that the AJ will determine the amount of fees, and the Board noted one agreement that was accepted into the record for purposes of enforcement, Munson v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-13-1073-A-1 (NP 7/15/2015):

...In relevant part, the NSA provided that the agency would pay the appellant's counsel "reasonable attorney fees in an amount as determined by the Administrative Judge upon application of a fee motion," and that such amount would be "in full settlement of all claims of attorney fees and expenses related in any way to this case" and would constitute a "full and final resolution of the matter of attorney fees and costs." In a November 20, 2013 initial decision, the administrative judge found Board jurisdiction over the appeal, entered the NSA into the record for purposes of enforcement, and dismissed the appeal as settled. The administrative judge did not make a determination of reasonable attorney fees; rather, in the initial decision, she set out the following course of action, to which the parties had agreed, to determine reasonable attorney fees and costs:

(1) the appellant will submit her request for payment of attorney fees and expenses to the agency; (2) the agency will respond to the fee request within 15 calendar days, stating the amount of attorney fees and expenses that it deems reasonable and intends to pay; and (3) if the appellant disagrees with the agency's response, she may file a petition for enforcement (PFE) asking the Board to enforce [the attorney fee provision] of the settlement agreement.

The parties did not agree on an amount of reasonable attorney fees and the appellant filed a petition for enforcement on December 31, 2013, seeking, in pertinent part, $41,697.41 in attorney fees and costs. In response, the agency asserted that the appellant's petition for enforcement should be dismissed because the agency was in compliance with the NSA and the administrative judge had not yet rendered a decision on the fee motions in the underlying appeal. In a September 19, 2014 compliance initial decision, the administrative judge ordered the agency to pay $41,122.13 in attorney fees and costs, i.e., the requested amount of $41,697.41 less $575.28 in disallowed costs.
