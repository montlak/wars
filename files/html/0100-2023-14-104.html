a. Failure to Include Past Discipline in Proposal: Due Process

For many years the Board applied the harmful error doctrine when the deciding official determined a penalty on factors that included incidents of past discipline that were not included in the proposal notice. As a result of a 2011 decision from the Federal Circuit, Ward v. USPS, 634 F.3d 1274 (Fed. Cir. 2011), the analysis changed. Now, if the deciding official considers in the penalty analysis past misconduct or its relative, past discipline, that was not included in the proposal analysis, the issue becomes complex and twofold: the information considered may constitute an ex parte communication or consideration sufficient to void the adverse action for want of due process. The agency can try again, but only after providing a proper proposal notice. Or, under Ward, if the consideration of information by the deciding official does not rise to the level of a due process violation, the Board still should analyze the error under the harmful error doctrine. The Federal Circuit, deciding the case on the facts before it, did not provide an all-encompassing explanation of the distinction between an error that rises to the level of a due process violation and one that is considered under cases defining harmful error, a concept identified in statute, 5 USC 7701(c)(2), and Board regulation, 5 CFR 1201.4(r) (2022). Following up on Ward, starting in 2011 the Board set aside more than a few removals based upon improper consideration of past misconduct or past discipline by deciding officials when that information was not provided in the proposal notice. The Ward decision and its progeny should be carefully reviewed. [Refer to Chapter 11, "Information Pertaining to Aggravation or Mitigation of Penalty; Ward."]

To the extent a harmful error analysis is applied to agency decisions involving reliance on past discipline not identified in the proposal letter, the Board decisions that follow should be of some assistance. The Board applies the harmful error rule if past discipline is relied upon but not mentioned in the proposal letter. The error is not fatal if the serious nature of the sustained charge justifies the penalty imposed. Gardner v. USPS, 44 MSPR 565, 570 (1990); see Brown v. DOD, 208 Fed. Appx. 854, 857 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) ("Furthermore, Mr. Villeneuve testified that he would have removed Ms. Brown even without knowing about her prior misconduct.... Thus, assuming arguendo that Mr. Villeneuve improperly considered Ms. Brown's past misconduct, this would not have been harmful error because he would have removed her anyway. See Deutsch v. U.S. Postal Serv., 39 M.S.P.R. 386, 390 (1989) (affirming removal despite improper consideration of past disciplinary record because current offense warranted penalty)."); Luongo v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 643, 649 n.2 (2004) (consideration of appellant's earlier counseling on performance was inappropriate to enhance a penalty when the counseling was not cited in the notice of proposed removal or in the decision letter; "[n]evertheless, we find that this error is harmless in light of our current evaluation of the evidence"); Underwood v. DOD, 53 MSPR 355, 360 (1992) ("The appellant has not shown to date what evidence or argument she would have proffered had she known that her prior past record was being considered in assessing the penalty, and how that evidence would have affected the reasonableness of the penalty. Moreover, on the record before us, without considering the past record, the penalty of removal is within the bounds of reasonableness."); Goode v. DLA, 45 MSPR 671, 676-77 n.4 (1990) (agency should not have relied on a past suspension that was not referenced in the removal proposal; the Board found "the error to be harmless, however, in light of our current evaluation of the reasonableness of the penalty"); Deutsch v. USPS, 39 MSPR 386, 390 (1989) (a negotiated agreement limited the consideration of past discipline if there was no intervening discipline occurred within two years; consideration of past discipline was harmless because the charged offense--striking a supervisor without provocation--alone warranted removal); Lavezza v. VA, 27 MSPR 105, 107 (1985) (the deciding official knew of but did not consider past incidents; a single incident of patient abuse warranted removal); Williams v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 277, 278-79 (1984) (it was error to consider past discipline not cited in a proposed removal notice; the error was not harmful because the appellant's offense warranted removal without the consideration of aggravating factors); McClendon v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 708, 709 n.* (1984) (AJ properly declined to consider a past suspension not cited in the proposal letter; although the agency deciding official considered the past action in determining a penalty, the error was not harmful because the penalty was within the parameters of reasonableness); Plath v. DOJ, 12 MSPR 421, 425 (1982) (possible reliance by the agency on past misconduct not referenced in the proposal was harmless based on the serious nature of the sustained charge alone); cf. Hubbard v. USPS, 32 MSPR 505, 508 (1987) (past discipline that was not cited in the proposal was included in the appeal response; it was harmless error when the prior discipline was not considered or relied on by the deciding official or the AJ). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Past Discipline, Performance Record, Aggravating Factors" concerning errors in proposal notices.]

The Board explained application of the harmful error rule to citation of past discipline, Coleman v. Dept. of Air Force, 66 MSPR 498, 505-06 (1995):

To the extent that Hutchinson and the administrative judge might have relied on the appellant's prior threatening statements and attendance-related misconduct, characterizing them as "erratic behavior," to enhance the penalty, we find that reliance on such misconduct was improper because it was not cited in the notice of proposed removal. See Lovenduski v. Dept. of the Army, 64 MSPR 612, 615 (1994); Horn v. USPS, 56 MSPR 511, 513 (1993); Herndon v. TVA, 15 MSPR 660, 661 (1983). Nonetheless, we find that such error did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights because we conclude on the record before us, without considering such alleged prior misconduct, that the removal penalty was reasonable. See Underwood v. Dept. of Defense, 53 MSPR 355, 360-61, aff'd, 980 F.2d 744 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). In this regard, we note that we have sustained the removal penalty under these circumstances. See Tate v. Dept. of Defense, 57 MSPR 180, 190 (1993); Webb v. USPS, 21 MSPR 493, 494-95 (1984), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1060 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table).

In Thompson v. USPS, 50 MSPR 41, 45 (1991), the appellant was removed for not following safety rules. The deciding official upheld the proposal, indicating among other things that he had "given full consideration to all of the evidence of record as well as...your past records." The adverse action decision noted that the appellant had been given letters of warning and had received two suspensions for infractions of instructions. The deciding official improperly relied on past discipline, but the Board found the error of no consequence:

...The agency erred in relying on the appellant's past disciplinary record in imposing the penalty, inasmuch as it did not place the appellant on notice that it was going to rely on his record, and the administrative judge erred in considering it.... Nevertheless, those errors were not prejudicial to the appellant's rights.... Even without considering the past disciplinary actions against the appellant, the record shows that the appellant had a long history of refusing to follow safety procedures and instructions....

...

...The disobedience of a supervisor's orders is done at the risk of being insubordinate and may be sufficient cause for removal.... Therefore, we conclude that the seriousness of the appellant's misconduct warranted the agency-imposed penalty of removal under the circumstances.

Thompson was followed in Horn v. USPS, 56 MSPR 511, 513 (1993) (improper to rely on a past disciplinary record without notice in the proposal letter when determining a violation of the last chance agreement; the removal was sustained without regard to past discipline). See Howarth v. USPS, 77 MSPR 1, 5 (1997) (the agency could not rely on an alleged violation of the last chance settlement agreement to enhance the penalty when the LCA was not cited in the proposed removal; the error was harmless if the penalty was reasonable for sustained current misconduct without considering past misconduct); O'Quinn v. USPS, 66 MSPR 166, 170 n.* (1995) ("If, on remand, the administrative judge determines that one or both charges are sustained, such that a review of the penalty need be made, she should be aware that her consideration below of the appellant's past [disciplinary] record as penalty enhancing was in error inasmuch as the agency did not place him on notice that it was going to rely on his past record."); cf. Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 186-87 ¶ 21 (2002) (mitigating removal for many reasons, but noting that "it was error for the deciding official to consider...prior incidents in determining the penalty because the agency did not state in the proposal notice that they would be considered").

Without relying on past discipline, an agency may rely on past but not currently charged incidents of misconduct to enhance a penalty, as occurred in the removal of an employee who borrowed money from subordinates. The citation of past incidents "show[ed] the serious and repetitive nature of the appellant's conduct." Vargas v. USPS, 83 MSPR 695, 697 ¶ 4 (1999) ("The agency cited the above-mentioned incidents in its penalty assessment as aggravating factors, thereby making it clear to the appellant that it did not rely on the incidents to sustain the charges, but did consider them in determining the penalty to impose upon her.").

The Board carved out an exception concerning the use of prior misconduct when no action has been taken: it will permit evidence of past uncharged misconduct to prove that an employee had prior knowledge concerning agency rules. See Arnold v. Dept. of Energy, 36 MSPR 561, 566 (1988) (use of government vehicles). [Refer to decisions discussing aggravating factors earlier in this chapter, "Articulation of Relevant Factors" and "Progressive Discipline"; refer to Chapter 11, "Past Discipline; Performance Record; Aggravating Factors," for the consequences of an agency's failure to state in the proposal or decision letter the extent of its reliance on past misconduct or performance problems.]

If the agency relies on past discipline based on approved leave, the discipline may be inappropriate but the record of poor attendance may still be a factor in assessment of the penalty under Cade v. USPS, 8 MSPR 717, 720-21 (1981):

Although we have made it clear that a prior disciplinary record based on approved leave may not be treated as a disciplinary record warranting an enhanced penalty, we also point out that this principle need not preclude an agency, in an otherwise appropriate case, from showing that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his unreliable attendance. This evidence would be pertinent to an "employee's past work record, including length of service, performance on the job, ability to get along with fellow workers, and dependability...," which is a factor relevant to the appropriateness of a penalty....
