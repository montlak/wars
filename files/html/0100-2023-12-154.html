G. TIMELINESS OF PFR

5 USC 7701(e)(1)(A), calls for a PFR to be filed within 30 days after receipt of an initial decision. The current regulation makes allowance for mailing time of the initial decision and provides, 5 CFR 1201.114 (2022):

(d) Place for filing. All pleadings described in paragraph (a) and all motions and pleadings associated with them must be filed with the Clerk of the Merit Systems Protection Board, 1615 M Street, NW, Washington, DC 20419, by commercial or personal delivery, by facsimile, by mail, or by electronic filing in accordance with 1201.14 of this part.

(e) Time for filing. Any petition for review must be filed within 35 days after the date of issuance of the initial decision or, if the petitioner shows that the initial decision was received more than 5 days after the date of issuance, within 30 days after the date the petitioner received the initial decision. For purposes of this section, the date that the petitioner receives the initial decision is determined according to the standard set forth at § 1201.22(b)(3) of this part, pertaining to an appellant's receipt of a final agency decision. If the petitioner is represented, the 30-day time period begins to run upon receipt of the initial decision by either the representative or the petitioner, whichever comes first. A cross petition for review must be filed within 25 days of the date of service of the petition for review. Any response to a petition or cross petition for review must be filed within 25 days after the date of service of the petition or cross petition. Any reply to a response to a petition for review must be filed within 10 days after the date of service of the response to the petition for review.

Service on a party's designated representative will be imputed to the party. LaGreca v. USPS, 114 MSPR 162, 164 ¶ 3, 2010 MSPB 109 (2010). If the initial decision is served on the appellant through e-filing and by mail, the e-filing service starts the period for filing the PFR, not the later receipt of the mailed edition of the ID. E.g., Fowlkes v. DOJ, DC-531D-18-0561-I-1 (NP 1/18/2023). [Refer later in this chapter to "Delayed Receipt, Misrouting of Initial Decision, PFR; Constructive Receipt."]

The definitional section of the Board's regulations explains at 5 CFR 1201.4(l) (2022) how the date of filing is determined:

A document that is filed with a Board office by personal delivery is considered filed on the date on which the Board office receives it. The date of filing by facsimile is the date of the facsimile. The date of filing by mail is determined by the postmark date; if no legible postmark date appears on the mailing, the submission is presumed to have been mailed five days (excluding days on which the Board is closed for business) before its receipt. The date of filing by commercial delivery is the date the document was delivered to the commercial delivery service. The date of filing by e-filing is the date of electronic submission.

For purposes of the Board's regulations, "days" are calendar, not business days. Vazquez v. USPS, 43 MSPR 16, 18 (1989); cf. 5 CFR 752.402 (2022) (OPM regulation: "Business day means any day other than a Saturday, Sunday, or legal public holiday under 5 U.S.C. 6103(a).") [On November 10, 2022, OPM issued regulations that, among many other things, removed the definition for "business day" from its regulation at 5 CFR 752.402. 87 Fed. Reg. 67765 (Nov. 10, 2022).

An "erratum" order correcting an error but not changing findings or conclusions of an initial decision does not change the finality date of the original decision, assuming that the erratum order does not contain a new notice of appeal rights. See Andre v. Dept. of Army, 91 MSPR 342, 345 ¶ 7 (2002).

A PFR of the ruling on a counsel fee motion may be submitted to the Board in accordance with the regulations governing PFRs. 5 CFR 1201.203(f) (2022). As to enforcement issues, the regulation allows a PFR on a finding of compliance. 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(4) (2022). PFRs on enforcement decisions must be submitted within the regulatory deadline. Rodgers v. USPS, 33 MSPR 640, 642 (1987). As to findings of noncompliance or partial compliance, the review procedure is more complex. [Refer to Chapter 16, "Enforcement of MSPB Decision."]

Initial decisions provide instructions for filing PFRs, and initial decisions also provide the deadline date for filing. The Board quite naturally considers the instructions contained in the initial decisions to be "straightforward." If an appellant fails to follow those directives, it often concludes that the employee "did not exercise the diligence or ordinary prudence necessary to establish good cause for the untimely filing of his petition for review." Willis v. USPS, 43 MSPR 439, 441 (1990), followed in Stanley v. USPS, 61 MSPR 225, 227 (1994) ("[W]e see no reason to find that the appellant's representative's claim that he misread the deadline for filing a petition for review establishes good cause for the 1-day delay in filing."); see Terry v. DOD, 93 MSPR 53, 56 ¶ 8 (2002) ("failure to follow straightforward instructions in the initial decision for filing a petition for review constitutes failure to exercise due diligence or ordinary prudence"); Jones v. USPS, 86 MSPR 410, 411 ¶ 6 (2000) ("We also find that the appellant's 'confusion' over the filing instructions was not reasonable because those instructions were straightforward."). The regulatory application on the timeliness of PFRs is uniform. Even as to an appeal that has no initial time limit, e.g., a USERRA appeal, once an initial decision is issued, a PFR must be filed within the time allowed by the Board's regulations. See, e.g., Lober v. USPS, 95 MSPR 615, 617 ¶ 6 (2004) (dismissing late PFR in USERRA appeal).

It may occur that an appellant's PFR is late but the appeal was then subject to a dismissal without prejudice to refile. The Board may deny the PFR and send the case back to the region to determine as a refiled appeal, with leave to the AJ to determine the timeliness of the refiling measured from the date of the PFR. See Goodman v. OPM, 112 MSPR 414, 417 ¶ 10 & n.*, 2009 MSPB 195 (2009), on appeal following remand, Goodman v. MSPB, 397 Fed. Appx. 639 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (affirming dismissal of appeal for untimely refiling following initial DWOP). [Refer to DWOPs and belated refiling in Chapter 2, "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')."]
