19. Foreign Service

The foreign service is a world unto itself, a complex one, and it is not covered in the Guide. For the legislative history of statutes governing the foreign service and for discussion of some decisions interpreting whether foreign service or civil service statutes govern employee tenure, refer to James v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3342) (no Board jurisdiction over a veterans preference employee appointed to the Foreign Service prior to 1980 and later separated through involuntary retirement; 22 USC 4156(d) (1990)); cf. Salleh v. Christopher, 85 F.3d 689 (D.C. Cir. 1996) (deciding that the Secretary of State may not review and set aside a decision of the Foreign Service Grievance Board); Olds v. USIA, 896 F.2d 1371, 1372 (D.C. Cir. 1990) (considering a pay claim arising from conversion of an employee from the foreign service pay schedule to the General Schedule, and noting "Unlike other employees of the United States government, most employees of the Foreign Service had been part of a separate Foreign Service (FS) personnel system rather than part of the Civil Service General Schedule (GS) personnel system"); Hall v. Baker, 867 F.2d 693 (D.C. Cir. 1989) (challenge to an involuntary retirement based upon "selection out" of the foreign service based upon performance assessment; question concerning jurisdiction of the Foreign Service Grievance Board); Costello v. AID, 843 F.2d 540 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (a dispute as to whether the Foreign Service Grievance Board had authority to award attorney fees to a foreign service officer who prevailed in an action brought to separate him from the service for cause as defined by the Foreign Service Act of 1980); Coster v. United States, 485 F.2d 649 (Ct. Cl. 1973) (considering whether the Veterans Preference Act controlled the dismissal of Foreign Service Reserve Officer serving under time-limited appointment); Born v. Allen, 291 F.2d 345, 348 (D.C. Cir. 1960) (declining to impliedly repeal Veterans Preference Act protections for foreign service employees in bifurcated agencies, e.g., the U.S. Information Agency, as distinct from career foreign service appointees in the State Department). [Refer later in this chapter to "Statutory Grievance System; Foreign Service Grievance Board."]

The Foreign Service may include individuals employed in agencies other than the State Department, Ang v. Dept. of State & DHS, 103 MSPR 324, 327-28 ¶¶ 6-9, 2006 MSPB 275 (2006):

Employees covered under 5 U.S.C. § 7511 may appeal to the Board from certain adverse actions, including removals and suspensions for more than 14 days. 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511-13. Subsection (b)(6) of 5 U.S.C. § 7511, however, excludes from coverage an employee "who is a member of the Foreign Service, as described in section 103 of the Foreign Service Act of 1980."

The appellant argues, on petition for review, that he was not a member of the Foreign Service because he was employed by DHS, and not by State. He cites no authority, however, in support of his position that all Foreign Service personnel are employed by State, and we are aware of none. Moreover, the United States Code includes references to "agencies utilizing the Foreign Service personnel system," e.g., 22 U.S.C. §§ 3923, 4001 (emphasis added); and the Foreign Service Act itself indicates that, even at the time of its enactment in 1980, agencies other than State could employ individuals under the Foreign Service personnel system, see, e.g., Foreign Service Act of 1980, Pub. L. No. 96-465, § 102, 94 Stat. 2071, 2076 (after defining "Department" generally as meaning the Department of State, this provision indicates that the term also is used to refer to "another agency authorized by law to utilize the Foreign Service personnel system"); id., § 201(b), (reference to "agencies utilizing the Foreign Service system"); id., § 408(b) ("[f]or the purpose of performing functions abroad, any agency...may administer employment programs for its employees who are foreign nationals..."). We find, therefore, that the appellant's employment by an agency other than State does not preclude a finding that he was a member of the Foreign Service.

We also note that the term "members of the [Foreign] Service" is defined, under 22 U.S.C. § 3903(6), as including foreign national employees who provide services at Foreign Service posts abroad, and that the standard form 50 documenting the appellant's appointment indicates that the legal authority for that appointment was the Foreign Service Act of 1980.

Under the circumstances described above, we find that the appellant is a member of the Foreign Service, and that he therefore has failed to establish that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75.[1] See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)] (the appellant has the burden of proving issues of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence).
