14. Miscellaneous Actions

A civil service taxonomy includes many actions, real, threatened, or imagined, visited upon the Board that are not within its jurisdiction.

A letter of reprimand is not directly appealable to the Board, although it could be the subject of an IRA (whistleblower reprisal) appeal. McVay v. Arkansas Nat'l Guard, 80 MSPR 120, 123 (1998). "[T]he Board lacks jurisdiction over a claim of harassment." Washington v. VA, DA-3443-11-0229-I-1 (NP 10/25/2011). [Refer to Chapter 13 concerning hostile environment claims associated with alleged whistleblowing reprisal; refer earlier in this chapter to "Harassment; Hostile Working Environment; Remedy" in connection with past or present military service and USERRA claims.]

An agency's bar to a former employee's presence at the worksite was not a matter appealable to the Board or subject to Federal Circuit review. Shepel v. Dept. of Commerce, 68 Fed. Appx. 200, 202 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP). [For the impact of a bar order on reinstatement rights through enforcement proceedings, refer to Chapter 16, "Compelling Reason for Reassignment; Detail; Different Duties"; refer to Chapter 6, "Bar Order Preventing Entry to Workplace."]

A flexiplace dispute is not properly the Board's issue through an ordinary appeal. Gore v. Dept. of Labor, 101 MSPR 320, 322 ¶ 5, 2006 MSPB 51 (2006). The Board lacks appellate jurisdiction over an agency's decision not to offer living quarters allowance along with a job offer. Oram v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-3443-17-0035-I-1 (NP 9/8/2022).

Suspension of clinical privileges, by itself, is not an appealable adverse action, Sage v. Dept. of Army, 108 MSPR 398, 403 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 68 (2008):

In her petition for review, the appellant contends that because Army Regulation 40-68, 10-6, b(3) expressly provides that "[a] suspension of [clinical] privileges (final determination) is an adverse privileging action," the suspension of her clinical privileges over a 6-month period qualifies as an adverse action that she is entitled to appeal to the Board under 5 C.F.R. § 752.401 and chapter 75 of title 5 of the United States Code. We disagree. An agency cannot through its own action confer or take away Board jurisdiction. Siegert v. Department of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 684, 691 (1988). The suspension of the appellant's medical credentials is not in itself an adverse action appealable to the Board. See 5 U.S.C. § 7512; 5 C.F.R. § 752.401. The appellant relies on Siegert to support her proposition that the Board has "broad jurisdiction" to review the actions of an agency's credentials committee. However, in Siegert, the employee had been subjected to an otherwise appealable action based on his failure to maintain his clinical privileges--a removal, which is a covered action under 5 U.S.C. § 7512 appealable to the Board under 5 U.S.C. §§ 7513(d) and 7701. Siegert, 38 M.S.P.R. at 685, 691. Siegert does not stand for the proposition that the Board has jurisdiction to review decisions of an agency's credentials committee absent an otherwise appealable action. Thus, we agree with the administrative judge's conclusion that the alleged "gap" in the appellant's clinical privileges is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction.

An employee may appeal the decision to deny voluntary early retirement if the agency controls the program. See Cohn v. DOD, DC-0752-14-0966-I-1 (NP 6/25/2015); Dawson v. USDA, 121 MSPR 495, 504-05 ¶¶ 16-17, 2014 MSPB 67 (2014). [Refer to Chapter 14, "Voluntary Early Retirement Authority," for these and related decisions.] Earlier law was that failure of an agency to offer an employee an early retirement option would not be directly appealable. Adams v. DOD, DC-0752-10-0741-I-1 (NP 3/4/2011) ("The appellant also asserts that the agency should have provided him with the opportunity to retire under the Voluntary Early Retirement Authority (VERA) and that the Board must have jurisdiction over the agency's decision not to authorize VERA.... However, the appellant offers no statutes, cases, or regulations to support his proposition that the Board has jurisdiction. In fact, in Johnson v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 573, review dismissed, 65 F.3d 186 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), the Board held that it was unaware of any statute or regulation that gives the Board jurisdiction over such offers. Id. at 577."); see Morano v. Dept. of Army, PH-3443-13-0421-I-1 (NP 12/31/2013) ("Importantly, the appellant has not identified, and we are not aware of, any statutory or regulatory provision or Board or Federal Circuit case law to support his assertion that the Board has jurisdiction over the agency's decision to deny his request to participate in the VERA/VSIP early retirement program.").

The law remains that an employee may not appeal the denial of a voluntary separation incentive payment (VSIP), Dehoyos v. Dept. of Army, DA-3443-15-0182-I-1 (NP 8/5/2015):

The Board's jurisdiction is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule, or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Like the administrative judge, we are unaware of any law, rule, or regulation giving the Board jurisdiction over an appeal of a VSIP denial. There are no provisions for Board appeals in the governing statute and regulations. See 5 U.S.C. chapter 35, subchapter II; 5 C.F.R. part 576. Furthermore, we find that the denial of a VSIP is not an appealable agency action or order affecting an individual's rights under FERS. Cf. 5 U.S.C. § 8461(e)(1) (an administrative action or order affecting the rights or interests of an individual or of the United States under FERS administered by the Office of Personnel Management may be appealed to the Board). As applicable here, VSIPs are governed by 5 U.S.C. chapter 35, subchapter II and 5 C.F.R. part 576, not 5 U.S.C. chapter 84. Furthermore, if VSIPs were retirement benefits under 5 U.S.C. chapter 84, they would be paid from the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund, which they are not. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 3523(b)(7) (reflecting that VSIPs are paid from "appropriations or funds available for...basic pay"), 8348(a)(1)(A) (reflecting that FERS benefits come from the Fund). Nor are VSIPs to be a basis for payment or included in the calculation of any other type of benefit. See 5 U.S.C. § 3523(b)(5). Accordingly, we find that we lack jurisdiction over the appellant's claim that the agency improperly denied him a VSIP.

An error in a service computation date is not directly appealable. Other than in an appealable reduction in force, the appellant is left to resolve the matter when OPM issues a retirement decision, Campbell v. Dept. of Navy, AT-3443-11-0860-I-1 (NP 3/2/2012):

On September 30, 2011, the appellant timely filed this petition for review of a September 14, 2011 initial decision dismissing her appeal for lack of Board jurisdiction, restating the same arguments she made below that her service computation date (SCD) was incorrect and she was missing creditable service time. At the time of her petition, the appellant was currently employed as a federal employee. The Board's jurisdiction is limited to those areas specifically granted by law, rule, or regulation. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a); Cowan v. United States, 710 F.2d 803, 805 (Fed. Cir. 1983). The Board does not have jurisdiction to review the SCD of a current federal employee outside the implementation of a reduction-in-force action which adversely affected the employee. See generally, Redding v. Department of the Interior, 3 M.S.P.R. 414 (1980).

The appellant also raised for the first time on appeal that she contacted the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) about her credible service time and OPM advised her that it would not review her service credit until after she retires. With regard to determinations by OPM, the Board has jurisdiction only after OPM has issued a final reconsideration decision. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)]; Farol v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 M.S.P.R. 606, 608 (1990). A final decision is a decision that OPM issues after a request for reconsideration of an initial decision, or after a decision that OPM designates as final. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.109(f). The Board has jurisdiction to hear an appeal involving retirement matters only after OPM issues such a decision. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.110. The appellant will have an opportunity to seek review of any determination OPM makes after she retires.

An agency error on a pay card, with detrimental effect in securing employment, is beyond the Board's jurisdiction as a de facto suitability action. See Simpkins v. MSPB, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2023 NP).
