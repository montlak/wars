2. Evidence, Findings From First Hearing

Should the Board remand a case on a specific issue, the AJ may implicitly or explicitly incorporate into his second initial decision findings from the first decision that were not disturbed by the Board's action on the PFR leading to the remand. Duke v. Dept. of Air Force, 5 MSPR 433, 436, 5 MSPB 441 (1981); see Salter v. Dept. of Treasury, 92 MSPR 355, 360 ¶ 13 (2002) ("In her decision on remand, to the extent that the administrative judge believes that her analysis of the appellant's claims of discrimination and reprisal remains unchanged, she may incorporate it by reference in her new decision."); cf. Banks v. United States, 741 F.3d 1268, 1278 (Fed. Cir. 2014) ("When a party offers additional evidence that is consistent with previously-offered evidence, but is not new or different in any real sense, a court should decline the invitation to revisit its previous determination. See Intergraph Corp. v. Intel Corp., 253 F.3d 695, 698 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (noting that the district court stated, '[a]lthough Intergraph may have obtained more evidence related to the FTC's actions, the Federal Circuit was certainly aware of the actions'); see also United States v. Bartsh, 69 F.3d 864, 867 (8th Cir. 1995) (the appellant presented 'no new substantive evidence, but merely a recalculation of the same evidence that was offered at the sentencing hearing')."). The AJ need not reconsider the arguments and claims from the first iteration of the appeal; to the extent that they are not disturbed by the remand, those matters are res judicata. See McLaughlin v. OPM, 62 MSPR 536, 546 (1994); McKenzie v. EEOC, 42 MSPR 634, 639 (1989) (AJ did not need to revisit EEO and reprisal claims when a remand order was limited to other, specific issues). A "partial retrial" does not deny the appellant due process. Limiting the scope of the hearing on remand serves the interests of judicial economy. Umshler v. Dept. of Interior, 55 MSPR 593, 597 (1992); Riese v. USPS, 40 MSPR 666, 672 (1989) (AJ could limit a remand hearing to testimony about nexus and penalty and avoid revisiting the substance of the charges, a matter examined during the first hearing); cf. Daniells v. Dept. of Treasury, 23 MSPR 266, 268 (1984) ("Since the Board previously determined that appellant was properly afforded her right to a hearing in this matter, we find that the presiding official did not err when she denied appellant's request for a rehearing."). The AJ properly exercises her discretion by excluding testimony that was not relevant or material to issues outlined in a remand order. Nicoletti v. DOJ, 60 MSPR 244, 251 (1993). If a remand results from a decision of the Federal Circuit, the Board has been known to direct the appellant to provide copies of the Federal Circuit briefs to the AJ if the case is remanded by the Board to the AJ for further analysis or hearing.

When the Board orders an AJ to readjudicate claims or defenses after allowing the parties to introduce further evidence, the AJ is not bound by any finding he made in the first initial decision unless the Board order limits the review of the AJ. The AJ is not required to state a reason for altering his findings on remand, Beamon v. Dept. of Labor, 41 MSPR 525, 538-39 (1989):

In her petition, the appellant argues that the administrative judge erred with regard to this issue because, in his first initial decision, he found that the evidence of record indicated that the appellant's supervisor may have been racially motivated and that the appellant had presented circumstantial evidence of race discrimination, while he found in his remand decision that the same evidence was "equivocal." She further argues that the evidence presented on the record of the first initial decision was sufficient to establish that her removal was motivated by race discrimination, and that the administrative judge's stated reasons for altering his findings on remand are lacking in sound reasoning and evidentiary support. The appellant asserts that the testimony of her co-workers--which the administrative judge found established circumstantial evidence of her claim of discrimination in his first initial decision--was improperly discounted in his remand initial decision.

The administrative judge's findings in the first initial decision concerning the appellant's allegation of discrimination were vacated by the Board in its remand order.... The Board ordered that the administrative judge re-adjudicate the merits of the appellant's allegation of discrimination after allowing the parties the right to introduce further evidence in the record concerning the discrimination claim. Thus, the administrative judge was not bound by any finding he previously made in his first initial decision.

Upon reconsideration of the appellant's claim of discrimination, the administrative judge was free to make different findings of fact, based upon all the evidence of record. Thus, we find no error in the administrative judge's determination, on remand, that the evidence he found was circumstantial evidence of race discrimination in the first initial decision was "equivocal" in his remand initial decision.

We also find that the administrative judge was not obligated to state a reason for altering his findings on remand. All the administrative judge was required to do on remand was to make new findings and conclusions and set forth the reasons upon which he based the resolution of the issues of fact and law presented on the record....

The Board will not overturn an AJ's processing of an appeal, and determinations as to receipt of evidence, in a remand hearing unless there is a showing of harmful adjudicatory error, Alvarado v. Dept. of Air Force, 103 MSPR 1, 13 ¶¶ 25-26, 2006 MSPB 230 (2006):

Addressing the last point first, we see no error in the AJ's ruling that he would not consider testimony proffered at the first hearings. In our prior decision, we noted that the AJ who heard this appeal prior to remand had left the Board. Alvarado, 97 M.S.P.R. 389, ¶ 16. We also noted that, because the case was likely to turn in part on credibility determinations, it was appropriate to convene a new hearing on remand so the new AJ would have the opportunity to observe the witnesses and assess their credibility first-hand. Id. While our prior decision did not preclude the AJ from considering testimony provided at the first hearing, it is clear from that decision that we contemplated that the AJ would rely primarily on the evidence adduced at the later hearing in making his decision. The agency has not shown that the AJ abused his discretion.

In addition, the agency has not shown that the AJ's ruling resulted in any harm. While the agency correctly notes that witness recollection would presumably be fresher at the earlier hearing, the agency has made no specific allegation of harm. Nothing in our review of the record indicates that either party was precluded from using the record of the prior hearing to either refresh a witness's recollection or impeach his credibility. In addition, the agency has identified no instances in which a witness's recollection was impaired by the passage of time.

A party will not be heard to object because during the remand, the other party manages to strengthen its case by learning from its errors in the first round and presenting additional or more forceful evidence, e.g., by presenting as a witness at the hearing an individual who previously testified only by deposition. The appellant was not denied any substantive due process. Errors not cured by PFR from the first initial decision could not, however, be raised for the first time in a PFR from the second initial decision (assuming no similar second error during the second round of regional adjudication). Roach v. VA, 48 MSPR 373, 377 & n.3 (1991). The situation is different if the Board has reviewed and made findings on facts in the decision that remands an appeal to the AJ. Then the AJ is not free to redetermine those facts. The Board then adheres to the law of the case doctrine when it remands an appeal. This means that, without good cause, factual decisions made before the remand and adopted by the Board are not to be redetermined, Williams v. Dept. of Army, 49 MSPR 405, 410 (1991):

Under the law-of-the-case doctrine, courts generally adhere to matters already decided in a previous appeal of the same case.... One aspect of this doctrine requires inferior tribunals to honor the mandate of superior courts within a single judicial system by not [relitigating] issues that were resolved on a prior appeal....

The Board added at footnote 8 of Williams, 49 MSPR at 411:

The law-of-the-case doctrine requires adherence to previously decided matters unless one of three "exceptional circumstances" exists: The evidence in a subsequent proceeding was substantially different; controlling authority has since made a contrary decision of the law applicable to such issues; or the decision was clearly erroneous and would work a manifest injustice....
