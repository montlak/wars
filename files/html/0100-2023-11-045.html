4. Statistics

Statistical evidence is significant in performance cases or in support of EEO affirmative defenses. The AJ may look to the racial mix of the work force, comments by supervisors and coworkers, and past examples of discrimination. See Wickramasekera v. VA, 21 MSPR 707, 713 (1984). [Refer to Chapter 12 on "Discrimination" and Chapter 13 on "Prohibited Personnel Practices" for analysis of burden of proof in discrimination cases.] If the sample used to develop the statistics is too small, conclusions about discrimination will not be drawn from the statistical evidence alone. See Stern v. FTC, 46 MSPR 328, 335-36 (1990) (small group was an inadequate base for a statistical analysis in a RIF [decision discussed in Chapter 10, "Discrimination Defenses"]; Decker v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 119, 134-35 (1989) (RIF appeal: the sample was too small, the positions of employees sampled were not comparable); Drucker v. Dept. of Interior, 14 MSPR 436, 441 (1983) (RIF: statistical analysis did not reveal a sufficient disparity to establish sex discrimination under an adverse impact theory [case discussed in Chapter 10, "Discrimination Defenses"] ); Fuller v. DOJ, 4 MSPR 497, 499, 5 MSPB 31 (1981) (172 employees, eight Black employees: four removals of nonprobationers, three of them Black, during a period of years constituted a sample too small to permit a conclusion of discrimination in a removal case); cf. Georgeoplous v. USPS, 61 MSPR 411, 414 (1994) (bias allegation: a statistical sample was not sufficiently large to establish that male appellants as a class had been discriminated against by an MSPB AJ).

Statistics may prove useful in other types of cases if used with care. SSA v. Brennan, 19 MSPR 335, 338 (1984), involving the removal of an administrative law judge, determined that if an agency's proof of unacceptably low productivity rests almost entirely on statistical evidence, the statistics will not be sufficient evidence of good cause for removal under 5 USC 7521 unless it is established that the statistics validly measure comparative productivity. To be meaningful, statistical evidence used to compare performance of employees must measure the performance of employees in the same positions. See SSA v. Goodman, 19 MSPR 321, 331-32 (1984); Johnson v. SSA, 6 MSPR 207, 208-09, 6 MSPB 182 (1981) (considering statistical analysis of the work of other employees in a performance case). Goodman was overruled or distinguished in 800 F.3d 1332 (Fed. Cir. 2015), allowing productivity statistics to support removal of an underperforming ALJ. [Refer to Chapter 9, "Statistics; Sampling Method," discussing statistical evidence in Chapter 43 performance cases; Chapter 12, "Statistics," discussing statistics in cases involving discrimination issues; and Chapter 8, "Performance," considering performance cases under Chapter 75; refer to Chapter 5 for ALJ removals.]
