12. Debt Recoupment

If a settlement provides for payments to the appellant, and the agency seeks to recover a debt (severance pay) from the employee, statutory debt collection procedures must be followed unless the agreement contains another provision or a reference to a statute, e.g., the Back Pay Act (and implementing regulations), that specifically permits collection of the debt, Tichenor v. Dept. of Army, 84 MSPR 386, 390-91 ¶¶ 7-8 (1999):

[E]ven if the debt was reinstated, there are statutory and regulatory procedures that an agency must follow to offset a debt against the amount the agency owes to an employee. See, e.g., 31 USC § 3716. In its response to the appellant's petition for review, the agency argues that the settlement agreement is governed by the Back Pay Act, 5 USC § 5596, and its implementing regulations at 5 CFR Part 550, Subpart H. These regulations provide that an agency shall deduct the erroneous payment of severance pay received as a result of an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action. 5 CFR § 550.805(e)(2)(iii). Although the Board has the authority to review an agency's award of back pay, see Spezzaferro v. Federal Aviation Administration, 24 MSPR 25, 29-30 (1984), what the Board enforces in a settlement agreement are the contract terms, not the Board's statutory authority. See Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, 56 MSPR 311, 315 (1993); Kelley v. Department of the Air Force, 50 MSPR 635, 639-640 (1991). This case is distinguishable from cases in which the Board has applied the terms of the Back Pay Act to settlement agreements because there is no indication that the parties intended that the agency would pay the appellant according to the terms of the Back Pay Act. See Kelley, 50 MSPR at 642-43 (1991) (the Board will not imply that the parties have intended an award of back pay for an employee when they have merely agreed to the cancellation of a personnel action in a settlement agreement); see also Kellihan v. Department of the Navy, 72 MSPR 47, 50 (1996) (where the parties use a term of art in a settlement agreement without defining it, the Board will apply the regulatory or statutory definition of that term unless it is shown that the parties intended otherwise); Gannon v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR 660, 665 (1995) (a settlement agreement that provided that the agency would pay the appellant "all back pay, benefits, and entitlements" was governed by the Back Pay Act). Because it does not appear that the parties intended payment under the settlement agreement to be governed by the provisions of the Back Pay Act, the agency cannot rely on the regulations implementing the Act to justify the withholding of the amount paid to the appellant as severance pay. Aside from the Back Pay Act, the agency has not identified a statutory or regulatory provision that would justify its offset in this case, and it has not offered any evidence to demonstrate that it complied with statutory or regulatory procedures to implement an offset. See Gannon, 67 MSPR at 664 (although the party asserting noncompliance has the burden of proving a breach, following the appellant's filing of a petition for enforcement, the agency must produce relevant material evidence of its compliance with the settlement agreement). Because it is clear that the agency, by withholding the amount the appellant received as severance pay, did not pay the appellant according to the explicit terms of the settlement agreement, if the administrative judge determines on remand that the appellant owes a debt to the agency, the agency may meet its burden of producing relevant material evidence of its compliance with the settlement agreement by identifying the statutory or regulatory authority that would justify an offset in this case and by establishing that it followed the proper procedure to implement the offset.

We reject the agency's argument the severance pay was withheld by DFAS and was not the result of action by the agency. It appears that the agency uses DFAS as its paying agent, but there is no indication in the record that DFAS would refuse to pay the appellant for her unused sick leave without an offset for the severance pay if the agency instructed DFAS to do so. Restatement (Second) of Agency § 140 cmt. a (1958) ("a principal is subject to liability upon a transaction conducted by his agent, whom he has authorized or apparently authorized to conduct it in the way in which it is conducted, as if he had personally entered into the transaction."); cf. King v. Department of the Navy, 130 F.3d 1031, 1034 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (in the absence of evidence that OPM or DFAS refused to comply with the terms of the parties' settlement agreement, the court rejected the agency's argument that it had no control over government records in the possession of OPM or DFAS for the purpose of purging these records pursuant to the settlement agreement).

But cf. Stephens v. Dept. of Army, 322 Fed. Appx. 989 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (agency made overpayment to appellant during fulfillment of settlement and agency then requested repayment; appellant unsuccessfully sought, through enforcement proceedings, Board determination that she was not overpaid). Disputed in Melton v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-09-0448-C-1 (NP 6/18/2015), was the agency's attempt to obtain payment for health benefits incurred while the appellant was on LWOP, following a broad settlement that contained a provision resolving all employment-related matters between the parties. The agency's debt collection violated Ms. Melton's settlement:

Pursuant to paragraph 2a of the agreement, the agency paid the appellant a lump sum of $35,000 to cover any costs and damages. In exchange, the appellant released the agency from all employment-related claims in paragraph 4. The agreement also included a mutual release of all issues involving the appellant's employment and resignation.

...

Paragraph 1 of the settlement agreement indicates that it is "in full and final settlement of all issues involving the employment and resignation of [the appellant]...as well as any other matters related to [her] employment with the [agency]."

We find that paragraph 1 is unambiguous. It contains a full and final settlement of all employment-related matters. Such terms have been read broadly. See W. Vinten Limited v. United States, 213 Ct. Cl. 759 (1977) (per curiam) (a "full and final settlement" constitutes a general release of all claims between the parties, "which are not expressly reserved or excepted from the scope of the agreement"). In the past, the Board has found similar language expressed the parties' desire to fully resolve their outstanding disputes related to an appellant's employment with an agency. See Laity v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 256, 263 (1994) (a settlement that fully resolved any and all matters related to the issues leading to the issuance of a suspension waived the appellant's appeal rights regarding not only those issues involved in the grievance case he actually brought but also related in any way to the agency's actions leading to the suspension); see also Paderick v. Office of Personnel Management, 54 M.S.P.R. 456, 459 n.2 (1992) (an agreement providing that it is a "full and final settlement of all matters" in the appeal constitutes a waiver of the right to move for payment of attorney fees).

The appellant accrued a debt for health insurance premiums while on LWOP from February 12, 2009, through January 31, 2010, and from May 23, 2010, through August 7, 2010. The agreement does not address this debt. The agency requested payment for this debt after the effective date of the agreement. We find that the agency breached the agreement by requesting this payment because paragraph 1 unambiguously indicates that the agreement resolves all outstanding disputes related to the appellant's employment with the agency. If the agency wanted to exclude this debt from the global settlement agreement, then it should have so indicated in the agreement. See Johnson v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 16 (2008) (regardless of whether he was aware of his Board appeal rights, the appellant waived them when his union entered into a global settlement agreement on his behalf without expressly reserving them), aff'd, 315 F. App'x 274 (Fed. Cir. 2009).

The agency argues that DFAS is a separate entity, DFAS was not a party to the settlement agreement between the appellant and agency, and any dispute between DFAS and the appellant should be settled by those two parties. We reject the agency's arguments because DFAS was acting as an agent of DOD in collecting the debt, and the debt arose out of the appellant's employment with the agency. See CF, Tab 1 at 36 (containing the statement by DFAS in a letter to the appellant that it was the DOD activity responsible for collecting her debt); see also Tichenor v. Department of the Army, 84 M.S.P.R. 386, ¶ 8 (1999) (rejecting the agency's argument that severance pay withheld by DFAS was not the result of the agency's action because the agency was using DFAS as its paying agent).
