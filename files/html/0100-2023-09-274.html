6. Protective Order

Agencies may be concerned more with potential third-party dissemination of arguably privileged or confidential materials than with disclosure of the information to the appellant or to her counsel. Appellants may be concerned about unwarranted circulation of medical or similar documentation generated during the appellate process. Some disputes over the release, rather than the relevance, of documentation may be resolved through protective orders.

The protective order generally is a written agreement, signed by counsel for both parties (and by the appellant or other individuals to be bound by the agreement), and then signed by the AJ and filed in the Board's administrative record. The order states that one party will provide information to the other party based on the agreement that the information will be treated in the manner described by the order. No universal format governs protective orders. A typical clause stipulates that the information provided under the order will not be disclosed to anyone other than the appellant, counsel, counsel's staff, the Board and witnesses appearing at hearings or depositions during Board proceedings. Another provision may require that the information be returned to the provider, along with all copies, at the end of the Board (or judicial review) proceedings. The difficulty with returning material is that it may be necessary to maintain records to explain decisions made during a case following some collateral event, e.g., initiation of malpractice litigation. Protective orders typically call on the Board to segregate (or seal) covered documents to avoid FOIA disclosure of those documents to third parties. Parties may specially mark documents, through a stamp or watermark, disclosed under protective orders, so that copies will appear as duplicates of materials subject to the order. [Refer to Chapter 1, "Precluding Access" and "Gaining Access; Review Procedure," concerning sealing of Board records and access to those records under FOIA.]

The use of a protective order or agreement may precede the initiation of a Board appeal. A protective agreement was suggested in an appeal involving a Justice Department lawyer who wanted to share some materials pertaining to a personnel dispute and whistleblowing disclosure with his counsel, but who was initially prevented from doing so by a manager who intimated disciplinary action if nonpublic information was shared with his counsel without agency authorization. See Jacobs v. Schiffer, 47 F. Supp.2d 16 (D.D.C. 1999) (citing Martin v. Lauer, 686 F.2d 24, 32 (D.C. Cir. 1982)); cf. Heath v. Dept. of Transp., 64 MSPR 638, 650-61 (1994) (sustaining the discharge of an employee who removed agency records to show to his counsel in order to defend against personnel action).

AJs accept into the record protective orders entered by stipulation of the parties--at least that seems to be the practice. Can a party by motion require the Board to issue a protective order? Probably not. Apart from agreements submitted for approval by the Board concerning protection of information, the Board does not have a process for controlling dissemination of material accumulated in an appeal. Miller v. Dept. of Army, CH-4324-09-0891-I-1 (NP 2/23/2011), stated:

In its response to the petition for review, the agency seeks a protective order regarding certain information obtained through the discovery process in this case that the appellant has posted on the internet. The agency's motion fails to meet the requirements and purpose of the Board's regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.55(d) and therefore is denied. See Garst v. Department of the Army, 60 M.S.P.R. 514, 520 (1994) (the purpose of the Board's authority to issue protective orders is to protect a witness or other individual from harassment in order to aid the fact-finding process).

The Board's rule on protective orders, 5 CFR 1201.147 and 5 CFR 1201.55(d) (2022), involve protection of witnesses or other individuals from harassment. [Refer in this chapter to "'John Doe' Appeal: Protecting Appellant's Privacy," and to Chapter 1, "Sealing of Record or Files."]

The question may arise of how a protective order is enforced. The Board does not have the ability to enforce a protective order outside of its own processes. Some agencies use confidentiality agreements that limit disclosure of agency information of a statutorily confidential nature (e.g., taxpayer information for an IRS case). These agreements are frequently used during the reply stage of an adverse action or during labor arbitration.
