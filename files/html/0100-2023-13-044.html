k. Intolerable Working Conditions

"One way to overcome the presumption of voluntariness of a resignation is to show that the employee's working conditions were so difficult that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign." Glenn v. Soldiers' & Airmen's Home, 76 MSPR 572, 580 (1997). In a forced resignation case, the burden is on the appellant to show by a preponderance of evidence that the agency made conditions so unbearable for her that she was coerced into resigning. Jovanovich v. VA, 3 MSPR 438, 440, 3 MSPB 510 (1980); see Glenn v. Soldiers' & Airmen's Home, 76 MSPR 572, 581 (1997) (hearing was warranted on the basis of nonfrivolous allegations that the resignation resulted from harassment, was tolerated by the agency, and of a magnitude sufficient that a reasonable person would feel compelled to resign). A resignation through settlement does not become involuntary if office conditions improve after the settlement and the appellant then changes his mind about working for the agency. See Barker v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 248, 251 (1987).

McKeown v. MSPB, 809 Fed. Appx. 953, 955-56 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP), described what is required to establish intolerable working conditions sufficient to vitiate a resignation:

One way for the petitioner to rebut the presumption of voluntariness is to show that the resignation was the product of coercive acts by the agency, such as intolerable working conditions. Shoaf, 260 F.3d at 1341; Staats v. U.S. Postal Serv., 99 F.3d 1120, 1124 (Fed. Cir. 1996). But "the doctrine of coercive involuntariness is a narrow one." Staats, 99 F.3d at 1124 (describing limited scenarios where a resignation was involuntary, like when it was "induced by a threat to take disciplinary action that the agency knows could not be substantiated" or where the agency attempts to force the employee to quit, without "any legitimate agency purpose"). We objectively consider the totality of the circumstances to determine whether "a reasonable employee confronted with the same circumstance would feel coerced into resigning." Shoaf, 260 F.3d at 1342 (quoting Middleton v. Dep't of Defense, 185 F.3d 1374, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 1999)).

...

Reviewing the record, we have no doubt that Mr. McKeown had a fraught working relationship with his direct supervisor and other FEMA Region IX administrators. However, a stressful working environment does not equate to coercion. Brown v. U.S. Postal Serv., 115 M.S.P.R. 609, 616-17 (M.S.P.B. 2011) (reasoning that a finding of coercion was not supported by an appellant's allegations of, among other things, her supervisors' "increasing demands that she perceived as trying to cause her to fail" and "groundless[ ] critic[ism]" of her work); Miller v. Dep't of Def., 85 M.S.P.R. 310, 322 (M.S.P.B. 2000) ("Dissatisfaction with work assignments, a feeling of being unfairly criticized, or difficult or unpleasant working conditions are generally not so intolerable as to compel a reasonable person to resign.").

Boyd v. Dept. of Labor, AT-0752-12-0513-I-1 (NP 9/17/2013), summarized the elements of an involuntary resignation based on assertions of intolerable working conditions:

Intolerable working conditions may render an action involuntary when the working conditions were made so difficult by the agency that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to leave the workplace, and the appellant put the agency on notice of the objectionable working conditions and requested assistance or remediation from the agency. Peoples v. Department of the Navy, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶¶ 5-10 (1999); Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 577 (1996). The touchstone of the "voluntariness" analysis is whether, considering the totality of the circumstances, factors operated on the employee's decision-making process that deprived her of freedom of choice. Peoples, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 6. Application of this totality of the circumstances test must be gauged by an objective standard rather than by the employee's purely subjective evaluation. Heining v. General Services Administration, 68 M.S.P.R. 513, 519-20 (1995). Furthermore, it is well settled that most resignations are not constructive removals and that the doctrine of coercive involuntariness is a narrow one requiring that the employee satisfy a demanding legal standard. Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc).

An allegation that a resignation was the product of a coercive or intolerable working environment is evaluated under the totality of circumstances, Weng v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-13-0180-V-1 (NP 4/17/2014):

An employee-initiated action, such as a resignation or retirement, is presumed to be voluntary, and thus outside the Board's jurisdiction. Hosozawa v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 110, ¶ 5 (2010). An involuntary resignation, however, is equivalent to a forced removal and therefore is within the Board's jurisdiction. Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322, 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc). To overcome the presumption that a resignation is voluntary, an appellant must present sufficient evidence to establish that the action was obtained through duress or coercion or show that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency. Green v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶ 8 (2009). To establish involuntariness on the basis of coercion, the appellant must establish that the agency imposed the terms of the resignation, the appellant had no realistic alternative but to resign, and the resignation was the result of improper actions by the agency. Hosozawa, 113 M.S.P.R. 110, ¶ 5. If the employee claims that her resignation was coerced by the agency's creating intolerable working conditions, she must show that a reasonable employee in her position would have found the working conditions so oppressive that she would have felt compelled to resign. Id.

The touchstone of the "voluntariness" analysis is whether, considering the totality of circumstances, factors operated on the employee's decision-making process that deprived her of freedom of choice. Green, 112 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶ 8. Application of this totality of circumstances test must be gauged by an objective standard rather than by the employee's purely subjective evaluation. Heining v. General Services Administration, 68 M.S.P.R. 513, 520 (1995). It is well established that the fact that an employee is faced with the unpleasant choice of either resigning or opposing a potential adverse action does not rebut the presumed voluntariness of her ultimate choice of resignation. Green, 112 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶ 8. In a Board appeal, the issue of the Board's jurisdiction in an involuntary resignation case is inextricably intertwined with the merits of the appeal; where the employee establishes the Board's jurisdiction over the appeal by showing that her resignation was involuntary, then she prevails on the merits of her appeal.[2] Quiet v. Department of Transportation, 104 M.S.P.R. 292, ¶ 6 (2006).

A reconsideration decision in Heining v. GSA, 68 MSPR 513, 519-23 (1995), outlined the elements of an appeal alleging that the appellant was forced to resign because of intolerable working conditions involving inequitable treatment:

It is axiomatic that a resignation or retirement is presumed voluntary. See Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d 584, 587, 207 Ct. Cl. 333 (1975). However, the presumption of voluntariness may be rebutted if the employee can establish that the resignation or retirement was the product of duress or coercion brought on by government action, or of misleading or deceptive information, or if the employee was mentally incompetent. See Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574 (Fed. Cir. 1983). The common element in all cases where the Board and the courts find an involuntary resignation or retirement is that factors have operated on the employee's decision-making processes that deprived him or her of freedom of choice. See id.; Perlman v. United States, 490 F.2d 928, 933, 203 Ct. Cl. 397 (1974).

Two scenarios have emerged in cases where the employee has alleged that he or she was coerced by the agency or employer into resigning or retiring. The first scenario arises when the agency or employer has proposed or threatened an adverse action against the employee and the employee resigns or retires in the face of the impending action. See Cruz v. Department of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc); Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d at 584; Barthel v. Department of the Army, 38 MSPR 245 (1988).[1] The second scenario occurs when the agency or employer deliberately takes actions that make working conditions so intolerable for the employee that he or she is driven into an involuntary resignation. See Braun v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 50 F.3d 1005 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Jones v. Department of the Navy, 66 MSPR 421 (1995).[2] In both scenarios, the courts and the Board have determined the voluntariness of the resignation or retirement based on whether the totality of the circumstances supported the conclusion that the employee was effectively deprived of free choice in the matter. See Braun, 50 F.3d at 1007; Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1574; Christie, 518 F.2d at 587; Perlman, 490 F.2d at 933. In both scenarios, the Board and the courts have held that the application of this totality of the circumstances test must be gauged by an objective standard rather than by the employee's purely subjective evaluation. See Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1575; Christie, 518 F.2d at 587; Landgraf, 968 F.2d at 429; Buckley, 758 F.2d at 1530.

With regard to involuntary resignations and retirements brought on by agency coercion or duress, the courts and the Board have followed the principles set forth in Fruhauf v. United States, 111 F. Supp. 945, 951, 126 Ct. Cl. 51 (Ct. Cl. 1953), interpreting "the law of duress." The court stated that as of the date of its opinion, it had found three elements common to all situations where duress had been found to exist. These elements, easily applicable to the line of cases at that time where the employee has tendered his resignation in the face of a threatened or proposed adverse action, are the following: (1) One side involuntarily accepted the terms of another; (2) the circumstances permitted no other alternative; and (3) said circumstances were the result of coercive acts of the opposite party. See Schultz v. Department of the Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Christie, 518 F.2d at 587; Barthel, 38 MSPR at 251 n.11.

However in the case of a constructive discharge due to intolerable working conditions, while the issue of voluntariness is the same, the circumstances are different from the resignation versus adverse action scenario, and the elements set forth in Fruhauf and its progeny must be tailored and/or expanded to fit the situation. Cf. Paolillo v. Dresser Industries, 865 F.2d 37, 40 (2d Cir. 1989) (while the judge's charge to the jury presented the issue as a constructive discharge case because of intolerable working conditions, where he should have stated the issue as whether the retirement was voluntary in the face of time pressures by the employer, the court concluded that the charge was adequate because it adequately posed the issue of voluntariness to the jury). Specifically, in the case of constructive discharge due to intolerable working conditions, the Board has tailored these factors to answer the ultimate question of whether under all the circumstances working conditions were made so difficult by the agency that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign. See Jones, 66 MSPR at 423-24.

OPM argues that the Board departed from the test to establish whether an employee has been constructively discharged because of duress. In this regard, OPM states that the appellant did not establish that the agency imposed the terms of her resignation or that she was faced with the choice between resignation or removal. However, OPM misses the point. As the court indicated in Fruhauf, the law of duress broadens with time "making it virtually impossible to arrive at any clear-cut definition and...must of necessity depend upon the circumstances of each individual case." Fruhauf, 111 F. Supp. at 951. The Board did indeed use the standard employed by the courts to examine the surrounding circumstances to test the ability of the employee to exercise free choice, and found objectively that the reasonable employee in Ms. Heining's position would have had no choice but to resign. The fact that the first factor stated in Fruhauf is inapplicable to most constructive discharge cases where there is no adverse action currently pending does not mean that the appellant was not deprived of free choice by the surrounding circumstances and has not proved coercion.

Indeed, the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit recently held in a very similar case that the employee had made a non-frivolous allegation that, if proven, could establish that his resignation was coerced. Braun, 50 F.3d at 1008. In that case, the employee, who had an impeccable record for twelve years, received eleven disciplinary actions after being assigned to a new supervisor, and filed at least two grievances, a complaint and nine informal complaints alleging discrimination or reprisal for having filed his earlier complaints. The court vacated the Board's administrative judge's finding that the employee failed to make a non-frivolous allegation of involuntariness because at the time of his resignation the agency had not instituted an adverse action or any action that could be viewed as a threatened or attempted removal. The court held that the administrative judge should have reviewed all the circumstances of the case, including the frequency of the supervisor's actions, the employee's prior successful evaluations and his grievances as well as the possible abuse of agency procedures, to conclude that the employee had made a non-frivolous allegation of involuntariness.

In Braun, as in this case, the Fruhauf element of one side involuntarily accepting the terms of another does not apply to the circumstances of the case, and there was no agency adverse action pending in either case at the time of the employees' resignation. As in Braun, Ms. Heining was an exemplary employee before she engaged in protected activity and she then became subject to agency actions such as an unsatisfactory performance rating and the denial of a promotion.[3] Like Mr. Braun, Ms. Heining filed many grievances and a complaint. All these circumstances in Heining were proved after a lengthy hearing and the Board correctly affirmed the administrative judge's conclusion that Ms. Heining's resignation was involuntary.

[3] An agency must have reasonable grounds for threatening to take disciplinary actions, and if it does not, the agency action is "purely coercive." Schultz, 810 F.2d at 1136; Barthel, 38 MSPR at 251. Thus, the agency's retaliatory actions in this case could be properly viewed by the administrative judge as "purely coercive."

OPM argues that the Board erroneously held that unpleasantness in general, rather than choice, is the touchstone for determining voluntariness. However, OPM misconstrues the Board's holding in this case. The Board did focus on Ms. Heining's deprivation of choice as the touchstone of its analysis. In cases where constructive discharge based on intolerable working conditions is alleged both the courts and the Board will find the resignation involuntary only if the employee demonstrates that the employer or agency engaged in a course of action that made working conditions so difficult or unpleasant that a reasonable person in that employee's position would have felt compelled to resign. See Martin, 48 F.3d at 1354; Landgraf, 968 F.2d at 429; Buckley, 758 F.2d at 1531; Clark, 665 F.2d at 1173-76; Jones, 66 MSPR at 424; Taylor, 61 MSPR at 193. By using the "reasonable person" test, the courts and the Board are using an objective standard. Moreover, as in the case where an employee resigns in the face of a proposed removal, the courts and the Board focus on whether the employer's or agency's actions in rendering the workplace so pervasively unpleasant and difficult deprived the employee of any other choice but to quit. Id.

We are not persuaded by OPM's argument that the Board erred in finding that Ms. Heining's working conditions were intolerable to her. The fallacy of OPM's argument is that it segments different facts of the case to find no evidence of intolerable working conditions instead of viewing all the facts together. The totality of the circumstances supports the Board's conclusion that the working conditions were intolerable and the resignation was involuntary. For example, OPM notes that other employees found the working environment unpleasant and did not resign,[4] that the administrative judge and the Board did not specifically connect the denial of the appellant's request for a compressed work schedule to coercion and that the Board did not clearly connect the large amounts of sick leave used by Ms. Heining with her resignation. However, the administrative judge and the Board (by adopting the initial decision) found the totality of the following facts to support the conclusion that a reasonable person in Ms. Heining's shoes would have been compelled to resign: (1) Ms. Heining was rated an exemplary employee before her whistleblowing; (2) she was denied a promotion because of her whistleblowing; (3) she was placed under the supervision of a supervisor about whom she had previously complained; (4) the six-month period that the agency allotted Ms. Heining to demonstrate that she warranted a promotion was a sham and was used to build up a case against her of unsatisfactory performance; (5) she was singled out for inequitable treatment when, unlike others, she was denied a compressed work schedule; (6) after the retaliatory course of action by the agency, and not before, the appellant took a substantial amount of sick leave and stated that her doctor advised her to seek other employment because of the stress of the job; (7) she was rated unsatisfactory in her performance in reprisal for whistleblowing; (8) she was considered a very good worker by a different supervisor before she was to be placed on a PIP, and was to be supervised during her PIP by a person who had been instrumental in the denial of her promotion and in her unsatisfactory rating; and (9) her formal complaint of whistleblowing and her many grievances were either ignored or investigated in an unfair and meaningless manner.

[4] The fact that other employees found the working conditions unpleasant supports Ms. Heining's allegation of unpleasant working conditions. However, this fact has no relevance to Ms. Heining's case of involuntary resignation. There is no evidence that others in her unit were whistleblowers who were subjected to the same retaliatory actions as Ms. Heining. Thus, other employees in her unit cannot represent the "reasonable employee" "in the same shoes" as the appellant.

Finally, OPM contends, citing to Boze v. Branstetter, 912 F.2d 801, 805 (5th Cir. 1990), that the Board's decision is a departure from the principle that a resignation is voluntary if the appellant has not pursued all statutorily prescribed avenues of redress. As previously pointed out by the Board, however, the facts of Boze are distinguishable from this case. See Heining, 61 MSPR at 553. In Boze, the employee resigned after filing a grievance and before his employer rendered even an initial decision. Boze, 912 F.2d at 803-04. He offered no proof of discrimination or of an unpleasant or difficult working environment. Id. at 805. Here, not only did Ms. Heining offer an overwhelming amount of evidence supporting an intolerable working environment, but she did not resign until she pursued many grievances and two complaints, receiving an adverse decision on her grievances in May 1990, just prior to her resignation.

Moreover, the agency's inequitable handling of the investigations of her complaints and grievances constituted aggravating factors contributing to the conclusion that the appellant's working conditions were intolerable. See Clark, 665 F.2d at 1175-76. From that posture, a reasonable person in Ms. Heining's position would have felt locked into a position from which he or she could obtain no relief. Id. A reasonable person in Ms. Heining's position would not "stand pat and fight" any longer, and we reaffirm our finding that her resignation under the circumstances was involuntary. See Christie, 518 F.2d at 587. Cf. Perlman, 490 F.2d at 933 (the doctrine of exhaustion of administrative remedies has no application when the agency involved had defaulted in its obligation toward the employee).

Another example of nonfrivolous allegations of harassment of sufficient intensity to require a hearing on an involuntary resignation appeal is Taylor v. EPA, 61 MSPR 188, 190-93 (1994):

The appellant resigned from her position as Environmental Engineer, GS-9, citing an "unprofessional and intolerable" work environment....

In response to the administrative judge's acknowledgment order, the appellant again requested a hearing, and submitted a 3-page affidavit stating that agency officials intentionally pressured her to resign by, inter alia, assigning her secretarial duties inconsistent with her professional position, criticizing her work and interpersonal skills without foundation, and impugning her veracity and credibility. A resignation is involuntary due to duress or coercion, where: (1) One side involuntarily accepted the terms of another; (2) circumstances permitted no other alternative; and (3) the circumstances were the result of the coercive acts of the opposite party. The Board has found that where an employee's working conditions are rendered so intolerable by alleged discrimination or harassment that she is "forced" into resignation, there has been a constructive discharge, and the employee's resignation is involuntary. The ultimate question to be decided in this regard is whether working conditions were so difficult that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign.

In Collins, 55 MSPR at 188-89, the Board found that the appellant failed to nonfrivolously allege that her resignation was coerced by the agency's discrimination and retaliation, where she did not specify the particular acts of harassment, discrimination, or retaliation directed against her, and did not explain the link between this alleged conduct and the nature and severity of her health concerns.

By contrast, the appellant in the present case alleged specific acts of discrimination and harassment on the part of agency officials, and expressly described the link between this conduct and her health concerns. In addition, the appellant filed a letter from her physician attesting to the severity of her condition. In her letter of resignation, attached with her petition for appeal, the appellant expressly stated that her working conditions were rendered intolerable by the agency's alleged course of conduct, and in her affidavit in response to the administrative judge's acknowledgment order, she described her fear that the stress and pressure caused by these practices would result in a potentially life-threatening epileptic seizure. We find that these allegations, if proven, could support a prima facie case of involuntariness, and that therefore the appellant's claim of constructive discharge cannot be labeled as frivolous.

Allegations warranting a hearing were discussed in Barry v. Dept. of Interior, DC-0752-14-0040-I-1 (NP 9/5/2014):

In his appeal, the appellant alleged that: (1) he is a gay man; (2) he overheard management officials making disparaging remarks about gay people and he heard managers state that the training center was "run by a [expletive]" and an "idiot"; (3) his supervisors falsely told him during a performance review that employees under his supervision had complained that he had created a hostile work environment and that, unless he voluntarily stepped down as Director, an investigation of those complaints would ensue; and (4) when he refused to step down, he was involuntarily reassigned to work under the direct supervision of one of the supervisors who provided him with the false information and was a subject of the appellant's EEO complaint. Circumstances insufficient to require a Board hearing, and insufficient to establish a coerced resignation, were described in Russell v. MSPB, 301 Fed. Appx. 938, 941-42 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP):

Normally, the Board lacks jurisdiction over resignations and retirements, because those acts are presumed voluntary. A petitioner can overcome the presumption of voluntariness and obtain a hearing on jurisdiction, however, by making nonfrivolous allegations that: (1) the resignation or retirement was based on threats of adverse action; (2) the resignation or retirement was based on "alleged misinformation and deception"; or (3) the resignation or retirement was coerced through "working conditions so intolerable" that the employee was forced to leave. Garcia v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., 437 F.3d 1322, 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc).

The test for coercion requires the employee to show: "(1) the agency effectively imposed the terms of the employee's resignation or retirement; (2) the employee had no realistic alternative but to resign or retire; and (3) the employee's resignation or retirement was the result of improper acts by the agency." Shoaf v. Dep't of Agric., 260 F.3d 1336, 1341 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (citations omitted). This three-prong test is objective, and a court must consider the totality of the circumstances to "establish that a reasonable employee confronted with the same circumstances would feel coerced into resigning." Middleton v. Dep't of Def., 185 F.3d 1374, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (citations omitted). The employee must show he or she was effectively deprived of free choice in the matter and that there was no other option but to involuntarily resign. See Braun v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 50 F.3d 1005, 1007 (Fed. Cir. 1995).

On appeal, Ms. Russell argues that she established the agency coerced her into resigning. We agree that Ms. Russell did not make non-frivolous allegations establishing coercion, and therefore was not entitled to a jurisdictional hearing. Ms. Russell focuses mainly on the third prong of the involuntariness test (improper agency acts)--alleging that Ms. McCarty singled her out to create a developmental plan, called her illiterate, and discriminated against her based on race, selecting another individual for charge nurse. Further, Ms. Russell complains that a more senior manager denied her LWOP request pending mediation. Ms. Russell does not attempt to show, however, that the agency imposed the terms of her resignation, or that she had no other realistic alternative but to resign. Indeed, at the time of her resignation, the agency was attempting to mediate the issues between Ms. Russell and Ms. McCarty. In that regard, Ms. Russell could have waited for the resolution of mediation to see if the situation improved. Further, while the situation between her and Ms. McCarty was not ideal, Ms. Russell has not shown she was effectively deprived of free choice in the matter. Finally, it was not unreasonable for the agency to deny Ms. Russell's LWOP request, considering the mediation could have taken weeks or months.

A "feeling of being unfairly criticized," and a stressful work environment normally do not rise to the level of being "intolerable." Miller v. Dep't of Defense, 85 M.S.P.R. 310, 322 (2000). While it is clear that the work situation with Ms. McCarty was stressful, Ms. Russell has not made out a prima facie case of coercion. In addition, she has not shown that she was deprived of free choice and that she had no option but to resign. In cases where intolerable working conditions are alleged, the Board will find an action involuntary only if the employee demonstrates that the employer or agency engaged in a course of action that made working conditions so difficult or unpleasant that a reasonable person in that employee's position would have felt compelled to resign or retire. To establish Board jurisdiction where a claim of involuntary resignation is made, an appellant must overcome the presumption of voluntariness by making a non-frivolous allegation that the resignation was the result of misinformation, deception, or coercion by the agency. Dick v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 290 F.3d 1356, 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2002). Coercion is established by proving: "(1) that one side involuntarily accepted the terms of another; (2) that circumstances permitted no other alternative; and (3) that said circumstances were the result of coercive acts of the opposite party..." Middleton v. Dep't of Def., 185 F.3d 1374, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 1999). The former employee must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that, under the totality of the circumstances, a reasonable person in the appellant's position would have felt compelled to resign. Shoaf v. Dep't of Agric., 260 F.3d 1336, 1341-42 (Fed. Cir. 2001); Middleton, 185 F.3d at 1379.

Under a Middleton analysis, Ms. Davis's resignation was not the result of coercion.
