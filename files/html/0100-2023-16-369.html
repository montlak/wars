broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 05: Remedies, Section B: Failure to Bargain, Subsection 03: Status quo Remedy Not Required, Part e: Undue Interruption of Agency Operations; Impossibility of Restoration of Status Quo
e. Undue Interruption of Agency Operations; Impossibility of Restoration of Status Quo

An SQA remedy was not warranted after a reorganization involving management's failure to abide by its bargaining obligations; the disruption, including reassigning employees who had been relocated as a result of the reorganization, would have been too great. Yet FAA and PASS, 17 FLRA 142, 145 (1985), noted:

The Authority is not unmindful of the serious consequences which flow to unit employees as a result of an agency reorganization, such as involved herein. In such a situation, a goal of collective bargaining should be to mitigate the adverse effects and disruption which are incurred by the affected employees. In the Authority's view, an order which would require the Respondent to give priority consideration to transferred unit employees for existing or future vacancies, for which they are qualified...and which would require the Respondent to bargain with the Charging Party to the extent consonant with law and regulation concerning the impact and implementation of the reorganization would effectuate the purposes and policies of the Statute....

An SQA remedy was rejected in a reorganization case as too disruptive. Dept. of Transp., FAA and PASS, 19 FLRA 472, 477 (1985). The remedy was not warranted in a reorganization affecting more than 50 employees because it would significantly disrupt or impair the effectiveness of agency operations. But, mindful of the effect of the reorganization, FLRA directed a remedy requiring the agency to give priority consideration to transferred unit employees for existing or future vacancies at the unit, coupled with a prospective bargaining order. FAA and PASS, 19 FLRA 436, 437-38 (1985). In another reorganization, an SQA remedy was not warranted for failure of the agency properly to bargain impact of the physical and operational reorganization of an office. New employees were hired after the reorganization. It would not be practical to return to the conditions prior to their hiring. The reorganization was designed to enhance case processing and had that effect. Return to the status quo would adversely affect efficiency of agency operations. DOJ, INS, Los Angeles and AFGE, INS Council, Local 505, 18 FLRA 550, 553 (1985).

An SQA remedy was denied when there was a failure to provide impact bargaining over a decision regarding conditions of detention for illegal aliens. The change occurred because of a funding shortage. A return to the operating procedures preceding the change would have been disruptive for the agency: it would negate the agency's control over its fiscal problems. The impact bargaining order would accomplish its goals only if it were prospective. DOJ, INS, Southern Reg., El Paso and AFGE, INS Council, 11 FLRA 90, 91-92 (1983). An SQA remedy was not required after management failed to bargain over the impact of a decision to change inspection policies at all ports nationally and the remedy would require changes at only some of the ports, causing disruption of agency operations and commercial shipping. USDA, Plant Protection and Quarantine, Animal and Plant Health Inspection Serv. and NAAE, 17 FLRA 281, 282-83 (1985). An SQA remedy would not be ordered for failure to bargain over impact if it would negate safety improvements brought about through implementation of OSHA policies. Dept. of Air Force, Hq., Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB and AFGE Council 214, 22 FLRA 502, 507 (1986).

An SQA remedy was denied after special work groups were established to enhance the efficiency of tax collection procedures. To revert to the original procedures would substantially disrupt agency operations and interfere with effective tax collection. Because after the union became aware of the change there was no bargaining demand, there was no evidence of a willful refusal by the agency to consider the matters of concern to the union. Return to the status quo would not put employees in any better position as to career development or opportunities. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Jacksonville Dist. and NTEU, 15 FLRA 1014, 1016 (1984) (majority opinion joined by dissent by Member Haughton as to need for SQA remedy and proof of disruption of agency operations). Imposition of the status quo was not warranted when the agency failed to bargain over procedures and arrangements for employees adversely affected by implementation of a quality improvement program. The agency met with the union to bargain over impact, the program was implemented nationally, and the program was considered an important management objective that would be significantly impaired by a return to the status quo. SSA and AFGE, 18 FLRA 511, 512 (1985).

An SQA remedy was not required as a result of a failure to bargain over office design; no purpose was served by having the agency dismantle the established office organization before completing negotiations. IRS, Chicago and NTEU Chap. 95, 9 FLRA 648, 651 (1982). An SQA remedy was denied as to a unilateral change in a traffic management and enforcement system. The ULP involved one of 35 tenant activities. A return to the old system for one of those activities would be very disruptive; no great inconvenience or hardship to employees resulted from the unilateral implementation of the system. Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, Dept. of Navy and IFPTE Local 3, 15 FLRA 26, 27 (1984).

No SQA remedy was required for failure to bargain properly over the impact of a decision to abolish a shift and reassign workers to another shift. Although the employees lost a night shift differential in the process, the violation was not willful, and reestablishment of the shift would be disruptive. It could not be said that but for the failure to impact bargain, the employees would have continued receiving the differential. VA Hines Hosp. and SEIU Local 73, 16 FLRA 3, 4-5 (1984). An SQA remedy was not required for failure to bargain impact of increased duties of employees on a shift. The agency had a good faith if incorrect belief that there was no change in preexisting practices and the agency believed that the union bargaining request did not extend to the additional duties to be performed by employees. The modifications in operations resulted from high level changes in the direction of drug-enforcement policies. Return to the status quo would disrupt and impair those significant policy objectives. Customs Serv., Region I and NTEU Chap. 133, 17 FLRA 843, 845 (1985).

Remedies in shift change cases were reviewed in NTEU v. FLRA, 732 F.2d 703, 707 (9th Cir. 1984), aff'g Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., Region VIII and NTEU, 9 FLRA 606 (1982). The court approved:

[T]he two step analytic structure that the Authority has consistently applied. Refusal to bargain about impact and implementation issues, such as starting and quitting times, is an unfair labor practice. Status quo remedies are generally appropriate for shift modifications, but not for violations involving new shifts. Thus, both whether a staffing or impact decision is involved and whether a new or modified shift is involved are "central issues."...

An SQA remedy requiring reinstatement of prior performance standards and critical elements was not warranted because of the consequential disruption and the lack of a demonstration of adverse impact on employees affected by new standards implemented without impact bargaining. Dept. of Transp., FAA and PASS, 19 FLRA 1004, 1007-08 (1985). An SQA remedy regarding the impact of changes in performance standards was not warranted when the agency had a good faith but incorrect belief that the union had surrendered the right to negotiate under the collective bargaining agreement. Rescission of the standards would be disruptive to agency operations. There was no evidence that unit employees were evaluated under the revised standards. VA Cent. Office and AFGE Local 2823, 20 FLRA 199, 200 (1985).

A failure to maintain the status quo during bargaining over implementation of a drug-testing program for employees did not lead to an order directing the restoration of the status quo. A prospective bargaining order was directed instead. The union was partially at fault in the ULP case because it submitted its proposals at the 11th hour, and the SQA remedy would be detrimental to the employer's operations because of the "paramount and incontrovertible need to ensure the integrity of the aviation safety and control system." Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 720 (1991), remanded to ALJ for further factfinding by court order, 46 FLRA 103 (1992), decision following remand to ALJ, 48 FLRA 1211 (1993) (SQA remedy not appropriate because of danger to public resulting from suspension of drug testing of employees whose work was essential to safe operation of national Airspace System).

An SQA remedy was inappropriate when GSA already had the office spaces released by the agency, but the agency was directed to make every effort to reacquire the spaces from GSA and then bargain with the union. Marshals Serv. and AFGE Int'l Council of Marshals Serv. Locals, 12 FLRA 650, 654 (1983). In DHHS, Region IV, Office of Civil Rights, Atlanta and NTEU Chap. 210, 46 FLRA 396, 399 (1992), the agency failed to bargain over the impact of office space reduction. Because office space was relinquished when the ALJ considered the appropriate remedy, FLRA approved a remedial order directing the agency to make some changes that "would return the office configuration as closely as possible in its reduced state to the previous configuration." An SQA remedy was denied after the FAA failed to bargain impact of a new policy of medically disqualifying controllers who have insulin-dependent diabetes. The risks entailed in applying the remedy were outweighed by the need to ensure the integrity of the aviation safety and control system. FAA and NATCA, 42 FLRA 82, 88-89 (1991). An SQA remedy was too disruptive that required the agency to reinstitute direct rather than indirect access of the union to photocopy equipment that was placed into a restricted access area because of internal security concerns. Army Adjutant Gen. Publications Ctr., St. Louis and AFGE Local 2761, 22 FLRA 457, 459 (1986); see Dept. of Air Force, Willow Grove ARS and NAGE Local R3-32, 57 FLRA 852, 857-58 (2002) (SQA was not required when change in responsibilities of several guards resulted from agency vulnerability assessment, security risk; restoration of SQA would disrupt security arrangements), reconsideration granted, result unchanged, 58 FLRA 277 (2003); cf. DHUD and AFGE Local 3701, 58 FLRA 33, 35 (2002) (SQA remedy inappropriate as to rotation system for receptionist duties; neither party wanted to return to prior arrangement under agreement between the parties; no established practice was in evidence concerning rotation of duties).
