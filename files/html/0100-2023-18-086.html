2. Waiver of Reply Right

An agency commits no harmful error by failing to provide an oral reply session for an appellant who waives that right. Gillespie v. DODDS, 5 MSPR 529, 531, 5 MSPB 519 (1981). An agency is not required to schedule an oral response. This responsibility is the appellant's. Vaughn v. United States, 158 F. Supp. 716 (Ct. Cl. 1958) (reply was waived when the plaintiff was given written notice of his right to answer the charges personally and in writing, but he did not request a reply); Campbell v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 92, 107 (1983) ("Neither 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(2), which confers upon appellants the right to an oral reply to the agency charges, nor its implementing regulation, 5 C.F.R. § 752.404 (c)(2), requires the agency to schedule oral responses. Appellants' representative failed to schedule the responses as requested."). Campbell was affirmed: "[W]hile the statutes and regulations clearly provide for a right to make an oral response to agency charges, they do not require the agency to schedule replies sua sponte. Rather, it is incumbent on an employee to take the initiative in scheduling the time to exercise that right." Campbell v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 735 F.2d 497 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The court there showed no sympathy for a "cat and mouse" game played by employees seeking to invite error by indicating their interest in a reply and then making impossible the personal contact needed for scheduling them. See Peden v. United States, 512 F.2d 1099, 1102 (Ct. Cl. 1975) (as to an objection to the adequacy of a reply opportunity: "The 'oral reply' was more of a cat and mouse game than a negotiation, and in the circumstances nothing more could have been expected."); Messersmith v. GSA, 9 MSPR 150, 154 (1981) (likening the alleged confusion in the reply process to a situation described by Peden); cf. White v. VA, 120 MSPR 405, 414-15 ¶¶ 17-18, 2013 MSPB 97 (2013) (appellant's request for time to prepare a reply was not the same as his request to schedule or present a reply: "If the appellant wanted to make an oral response to the proposal to demote him, it was incumbent on him to make an unequivocal request of the deciding official to afford him one. There is no evidence that the appellant did so.").

The request by an employee to personally answer the charges supporting an adverse action must be clear and unequivocal. Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 145, 148 (1982); see Hart v. United States, 284 F.2d 682 (Ct. Cl. 1960) ("[w]here a civil servant is faced with discharge and is notified of his right of personal appearance at the agency level, he must take such action as will constitute a clear assertion of this right or it will be waived."). If an employee seeks to schedule an oral reply session, he must take the initiative and provide notice of his interest. If he submits a written response, the agency will not be faulted for failing to schedule an oral reply session. Atwell v. GSA (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1346); see Wager v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 390, 391-92 (1982) (employee's written reply did not request an oral reply and neither he nor his representative made an explicit request for an oral reply). For an oral reply for an appellant with a representative who had some type of conflict and failed to appear, it was not harmful error for the agency to make a decision on the adverse action proposal if the appellant passed up an opportunity to make a statement. Harris v. FAA, 15 MSPR 600, 602 (1983). There is no error if the appellant gives his representative the right to make the oral reply and later complains that he was not given the right to reply personally. Messersmith v. GSA, 9 MSPR 150, 153 (1981).
