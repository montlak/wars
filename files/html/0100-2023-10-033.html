c. Status of Pending Motion

Although a party may request a continuance of a hearing, the request does not serve as a continuance. A party may not assume that a hearing will be postponed because a continuance request was made; the result may be, as it was in Degani v. Dept. of Air Force, 1 MSPR 671, 673-74, 2 MSPB 81 (1980), that the record will close on the date set for hearing without the opportunity for that party to introduce evidence in support of its case, a result now likely modified Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (describing the process to be followed if the appellant fails to appear at the hearing and the case is then decided on the record). The Federal Circuit noted in Sheehan v. MSPB, 746 F.2d 806, 807 (Fed. Cir. 1984): "It is elementary that a lawyer who relies on the filing of a request for extension of time in lieu of meeting a filing date does so at his/her peril. If such a request is denied after the due date, there is no automatic extension while it is pending. To hold otherwise would allow counsel to manipulate the due date."

If the AJ's hearing order tells the parties not to assume that a hearing is postponed unless they are notified, it is error for counsel to assume a continuance because he sends a letter requesting it. Gordon v. USDA, 25 MSPR 438, 440 (1984); see Markin v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 136, 136 (1984) (AJ's hearing order stated that the parties should not assume a continuance has been granted until the parties are specifically notified). Should counsel submit a motion for a continuance the afternoon before a hearing and the AJ ordered that the motion be submitted a week earlier, there is no prejudicial error if the AJ fails to decide the motion until the day of the hearing and then notifies the attorney by telephone of the ruling. Webster v. District of Columbia Gov't, 7 MSPR 280, 283, 7 MSPB 176 (1981); cf. Jeffcoat v. USPS, 22 MSPR 593, 594-95 (1984) (appellant could not rely on the assumption of counsel that a continuance would be granted and expect a continuance to be granted upon withdrawal of counsel).
