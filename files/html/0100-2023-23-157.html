6. Reinstatement to the Same Position; Status Quo Ante Remedy

In Kerr v. Nat'l Endowment for Arts, 14 MSPR 346, 350 (1983), leading to Kerr v. Nat'l Endowment for Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 732-33 (Fed. Cir. 1984), the appellant disputed whether the agency truly restored him to the job he lost. The Board concluded that it would not become involved in the details of the job regained by Kerr, as long as its title, grade, and pay equaled the job he had lost. The court rejected the Board's approach:

We hold that the MSPB erred as a matter of law in ruling that its authority over the matter of compliance ended when petitioner resumed active duty in his former position at the same grade and pay. See 5 USC 7703(c). Contrary to the Board's characterization...the compliance issue is not an original "personnel action" concerning a reassignment of duties without change of grade or pay, actions which are generally not appealable to the Board. See 5 USC 2302(a)....

...

The purpose of [an order of MSPB cancelling a termination] is to place the employee as nearly as possible in the status quo ante. In our view, appropriate steps to enforce compliance must include more than the formal determination that an individual was reinstated to a position bearing the same title, grade and pay--no matter what the actual content of that position may be at the time of reinstatement. Rather, the Board should, where appropriate, also make a substantive assessment of whether the actual duties and responsibilities to which the employee was returned are either the same as or substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties and responsibilities held prior to the wrongful discharge. Further, the Board should order corrective action in cases of non-compliance.... We cannot distinguish...the MSPB's remedial powers from those of the National Labor Relations Board. As the Supreme Court stated in one NLRB case, the basic purpose of a reinstatement or back pay order is "restoration of the situation, as nearly as possible, to that which would have been obtained but for the illegal discrimination." Phelps Dodge Corp. v. NLRB, 313 U.S. 177 (1941).

Summarized by Foster v. Dept. of Army, 832 Fed. Appx. 697 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP):

As is relevant here, "the purpose of...a cancellation order is to place the employee as nearly as possible in the status quo ante"--that is, the position the employee would have been in, but for the wrongful agency action. Kerr v. Nat'l Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984); see Smith v. Dep't of Army, 458 F.3d 1359, 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2006) ("The [MSPB]'s enforcement power...functions to ensure that employees are returned to the status quo ante upon reversal of the agency's action."). This includes "compensation for any loss of wages or benefits that [the employee] suffered by reason of [the wrongful agency action]." Marshall v. Dep't of Health & Human Servs., 587 F.3d 1310, 1317 (Fed. Cir. 2009). Compliance with a cancellation order is a question of fact, which we review for substantial evidence. See id.

As a result of Kerr, compliance does not end with reinstatement at the equivalent grade and pay--the status quo ante should be restored. Sarver v. Dept. of Treasury, 20 MSPR 499, 502 (1984), on reconsideration, 26 MSPR 685, 688 (1985) (improper to restore police officer to clerk's position). In a successfully appealed RIF case the Board entertained a petition for enforcement asserting that, after the employee won his case, the agency reassigned him out of the position that he had sought and won through the RIF appeal. Because the Board has an interest in avoiding sham compliance, the petition should not have been dismissed by the regional office on the theory that the reassignment problem was separate and unrelated to the RIF appeal. Brown v. GPO, 6 MSPR 194, 195, 6 MSPB 171 (1981). The agency's compliance responsibilities are the same, irrespective of whether the Board orders cancelation of the appealed action or whether the agency cancels the adverse action after an appeal has been filed and before a final decision is issued. Sarver v. Dept. of Treasury, 30 MSPR 226, 227 (1986).

In the context of enforcement of a settlement, Coan v. VA, NY-0752-14-0300-C-1 (NP 7/27/2022), explained:

Placing the appellant in the status quo ante means restoring him, as nearly as possible, to the situation he would have been in had the personnel action that formed the basis of the Board appeal not occurred. Tubesing v. Department of Health & Human Services, 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶ 5 (2009). A return to the status quo ante requires that the agency place the appellant in a position with all the essential privileges of his previous position. Black v. Department of Justice, 85 M.S.P.R. 650, ¶ 6 (2000). In particular, the agency must reinstate the appellant to his former position and duties absent a strong overriding interest or compelling reasons for not doing so. Tubesing v. Department of Health & Human Services, 115 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 5 (2010). When a compelling reason exists for reassigning an appellant to a position other than the one he encumbered at the time of the adverse action at issue, the agency must establish that the duties and responsibilities of the position to which the appellant has been assigned are substantially equivalent in scope and status to those of the position the appellant previously held. Id., ¶ 7. In determining whether the duties and responsibilities of the appellant's pre- and post-reinstatement positions are substantially equivalent, the Board looks beyond the title and grade of the positions involved and conducts an assessment comparing the scope of the duties and responsibilities of the post-reinstatement position with those of the pre-reinstatement position. Id.

If the appellant succeeds in his or her appeal from an adverse action, e.g., a removal, the Board's remedy is ordinarily to return the appellant to the status quo ante, i.e., the position lost through the removal. Brown v. DOD, 94 MSPR 669, 675-76 ¶ 11 (2003). Boyd v. Dept. of Labor, AT-0752-10-0687-C-1 (NP 9/1/2011), explained:

When the Board finds an agency action was unjustified, the aim is to place the appellant, as nearly as possible, in the situation she would have been in had the unjustified action not occurred. White v. Department of the Army, 115 M.S.P.R. 664, ¶ 5 (2011); Gorny v. Department of the Interior, 115 M.S.P.R. 520, ¶ 5 (2011). This is called status quo ante relief. Gorny, 115 M.S.P.R. 520, ¶ 5; Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The agency bears the burden of proving its compliance with a Board order. Tubesing v. Department of Health and Human Services, 115 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 5 (2010).

When the agency is ordered to cancel a disciplinary action, the agency must restore the appellant to the status quo ante, meaning the restoration of the situation, as nearly as possible, to what the appellant would have enjoyed but for the wrongful personnel action. Although the Board does not require perfect consistency as to all aspects of the appellant's pre- and post-removal positions, his return to the status quo ante requires the agency to place the appellant in a position with all the essential privileges of his previous position. Black v. DOJ, 85 MSPR 650, 653 ¶ 6 (2000). "To support a finding of compliance with the reinstatement order, there must be a substantial assessment of the scope of duties and responsibilities compared with the appellant's former position." Hernandez v. USPS, 87 MSPR 418, 424 ¶ 13 (2001) (the status quo requires restoration to the same or comparable duties); see Miller v. Dept. of Navy, AT-3443-11-0083-X-1 (NP 8/15/2014) ("A return to the status quo ante requires that the agency place the appellant in a position with all the essential privileges of his previous position. Black v. Department of Justice, 85 M.S.P.R. 650, 653 (2000). However, the Board does not require perfect consistency with all aspects of an appellant's pre- and post-personnel action positions. Id. Rather, the crucial determination is whether the actual duties and responsibilities of the position to which the appellant returned are either the same or substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties and responsibilities of the position he originally held. Blackmer v. Department of Navy, 47 M.S.P.R. 624, 629 (1991)."); Doe v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 198, 201 ¶ 7 (2003) (same); Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 79 MSPR 342, 347 (1998) ("It is axiomatic that full compliance with a Board order to rescind a removal requires the agency to place the employee as nearly as possible in the status quo ante following an order of the Board to cancel an employee's termination."). But see Walker v. Dept. of Army, 90 MSPR 136, 144 ¶ 17 (2001) (agency was in compliance, although perfect consistency with prior employment could not be achieved when the employee's wife could not accompany him on a foreign tour of duty by reason of security concerns).

The employee may not wish to go back to the job he last held, particularly if the same supervisors and managers are there to recall the misconduct or poor performance they thought justified his removal. Yet the agency insists on reinstating the employee to the old work station. In other cases, the agency seeks to put the employee in another position, but the employee wants his original job back. Board law favors recreating the status quo ante. The crucial determination is whether "the actual duties and responsibilities of the position to which appellant was returned are either the same or substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties and responsibilities of the position the appellant originally held." Blackmer v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 624, 629 (1991); see Schnell v. Dept. of Army, CH-1221-07-0700-X-1 (NP 6/16/2011) ("The agency also has not explained the basis for placing the appellant in the Environmental Engineer GS-12 position. It has not addressed the appellant's argument that this position is not substantially equivalent in its status and duties to his former Supervisory Quality Assurance position, nor has it addressed whether the GS-12 positions to which many of his former duties were reassigned would be substantially equivalent to his former position, or whether the other positions cited by the appellant as appropriate placements would meet this standard. Restoration of the status quo ante requires a substantive assessment of whether the actual duties of the employee's new position are equivalent in scope and status to those the employee would have had absent the wrongful action. See Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984)."); Bruton v. VA, 111 MSPR 489, 494 ¶ 11, 2009 MSPB 110 (2009) (restating that if compelling reasons for reinstating the appellant to other than his former position and duties, the agency must establish that the duties and responsibilities of the current positions are substantially equivalent in scope and status to those of the position held by the employee at the time of removal), following remand, Bruton v. VA, 112 MSPR 313, 317-18 ¶¶ 8-9, 2009 MSPB 149 (2009) ("Because of the appellant's medical limitations, the agency could not assign him to the exact duties he performed prior to his removal, but all of his assignments have been consistent with his housekeeping aid position and his medical limitations. Accordingly, the agency has shown that it had a compelling reason for assigning the appellant different duties after his reinstatement, and that his assignments have been substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties he performed prior to his removal"). There is nothing to prevent the parties from negotiating a reassignment that will suit their mutual interests. But the Board lacks the authority in the ordinary adverse action to direct the agency to transfer an employee to another location. See Cuevas v. USPS, 90 MSPR 391, 394 ¶ 6 (2001).

"Generally, the relief afforded an appellant who has been denied his substantive RIF rights is that the agency effect the status quo ante, i.e., return the employee to the substantially equivalent position and status that he held prior to the denial of his substantive RIF rights." Augustus v. USPS, 69 MSPR 29, 31 (1995). The Board may be somewhat forgiving of delays in restoration of the status quo during adjustments following massive reorganizations and the litigation challenging those reorganizations. See, e.g., Sink v. USPS, 65 MSPR 628, 635 (1994), post.

Normally the Board does not direct what position the appellant will hold when the Board reverses a personnel action in a manner that requires restoration of the status quo. The agency's responsibility was explained, Rubeck v. USPS, 66 MSPR 527, 529-30 (1995):

In general, an administrative judge should not specify the particular position at a specific work location to which the appellant must be restored. See Rodriguez v. Department of the Air Force, 60 MSPR 279, 285 (1994) (the administrative judge technically erred by suggesting in his initial decision that the agency must restore the appellant to a particular position at a specific location); LaBatte v. Department of the Air Force, 58 MSPR 586, 594 (1993) (upon the Board's reversal of a personnel action, restoration to the status quo ante does not necessarily require the employee's reinstatement to his former position). Upon the Board's reversal of an unwarranted personnel action, it is up to the agency to determine, in the first instance, the appropriate position in which it will place the appellant in order to restore him as nearly as possible to the status quo ante, within the limitations of the rules applicable to the agency and the particular circumstances. See generally Sink, 65 MSPR at 632-34. We therefore find that the administrative judge technically erred by specifying that the agency must restore the appellant to his former position, particularly in light of his finding that "[t]he appellant's former position was abolished in the restructuring...."...

As to the appellant's contentions regarding what he deems to be the appropriate relief under the circumstances, he may raise them by filing a petition for enforcement under 5 CFR § 1201.182, if he believes that the agency's actual compliance efforts are insufficient....

The result is that disputes over the adequacy of reinstatement efforts are resolved through enforcement proceedings. Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 34 MSPR 632, 635 (1987), following remand, Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 438, 445 (1988), stated that, upon restoration of an employee, the critical determination is whether:

...the actual duties and responsibilities of the position to which appellant was returned are either the same or substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties and responsibilities of the position appellant originally held, and, if they are not...whether there exist compelling agency interests which support the changes in either the scope or duties of appellant's restored position....

Stone v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 650, 654-56 (1989), declined to permit an agency to restore an appellant without her prior scientific duties on no more than the agency's statement that those duties were no longer available or necessary:

When a removal action is cancelled, an agency is required to return an employee as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The Board is required to determine whether the duties and responsibilities to which the employee is returned are either the same or equivalent to those held by the employee prior to the agency action. id. Any change in the scope or duties of the restored position must be supported by an agency showing of a compelling interest. Eikenberry v. Department of the Interior, 34 MSPR 632 (1987).

Prior to her removal, the appellant's PD stated that she "is a recognized authority in the field of glycosaminoglycans (mucopolysaccharides)" and that her duties included initiating, designing and conducting "investigations into the macromolecular structure of purified mucopolysaccharides and proteoglycans...using spectrophotometry, fluorimetry, spectropolarimetry and computer techniques." The PD also stated that she would investigate, evaluate, apply and/or modify "existing methods (1) for the measurement of circular dichroism, absorption and emission spectroscopy in dye metachromacy and polymer conformation analysis and (2) for the isolation of active oligoglycan fractions, glycosaminoglycans and proteoglycans from neural and other tissue within and outside the scope of the general goal of the Laboratory" of Cell Biology. Supervision was by "collaboration with the Laboratory Chief through discussions concerning the directions of research and of results achieved." The appellant had "full responsibility for the organization and direction of those phases of the overall program assigned to her."

It is clear from the record, and the parties agree, that the agency did not return the appellant to this position.[3] The controlling question, therefore, is whether the agency had a compelling reason for its failure to do so.

[3] The description of the position into which the appellant was placed indicates that the function of the Laboratory of Cell Biology continues to be planning and conducting research on the anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, pharmacology and development of peptidergic neurons in the nervous systems. The appellant, however, is now designated as "a recognized authority in the field of biophysical chemistry." She "initiates, designs and conducts investigations" and "investigates, evaluates, applies and/or modifies existing methods." This quoted language replaced the range of duties enumerated in the preceding paragraph.

There has been no showing by the agency that the appellant has expanded her sphere of expertise to become an authority in the broad field of biophysical chemistry. It takes more than the stroke of a pen to become a recognized authority in so broad a field.

The agency contends that it can assign work to employees as it sees fit. The agency indicated in response to the appellant's request for restoration to her former position that it has ceased research in the area of her expertise.

While the agency has broad discretion to determine the direction its research projects will take, that authority cannot be used to defeat a Board order requiring restoration to the position held before a removal. Absent compelling reasons clearly justifying such a change, an employee is entitled to full resumption of the position held prior to the unwarranted agency action. Yetman v. Department of the Army, 36 MSPR 425 (1988). In this case, the agency has shown no such compelling reasons. The agency asserts that it did not pursue the particular project in which the appellant was engaged at the time of her removal because, in essence, it did not believe the research would be productive. The agency does not assert, however, that there is no possible project within the appellant's expertise which would be of value to the agency. Therefore, the agency has not justified its failure to comply with the Board's order. Accordingly, the agency is ordered to restore the appellant to the position that she held prior to the agency action.

See Duffrin v. Dept. of Transp., 70 MSPR 557, 563 (1996) (if the agency removed the appellant from a developmental position requiring extensive training, the appellant should be restored to her position at an appropriate phase of training); Calarco v. USPS, 58 MSPR 483, 486 (1993) ("[a]n appellant's restoration to the position that he occupied before the improper personnel action, without being allowed to perform the full range of his former duties, does not constitute being returned to the status quo ante."); Rickels v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 596, 603-04 (1989) (agency actions were inconsistent with the contention that the appellant could not be trusted with the resumption of active duty).

The agency's duty is to restore the appellant to her former position. Although the restoration may be uncomfortable for the appellant who fears harassment from supervisors, the appellant is not entitled to be placed in a better position than she would have enjoyed had the adverse action not occurred. See Edwards v. DOJ, 90 MSPR 532, 535 ¶ 8 (2002) (appellant's procedurally improper removal from a term appointment did not expand upon the rights of that term appointment following reinstatement). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Allegation of Reprisal, Bad Faith, Discrimination, or Harassment."]

The job before and after removal should entail no material difference. Cofield v. GPO, 22 MSPR 392, 395 (1985) (improper to reinstate policeman to a guard's job with lesser duties, prestige, and responsibilities [refer to continuation of Cofield later in this chapter, "Lack of Authority to Enforce Compliance"] ), reaff'd on OPM reconsideration petition, 26 MSPR 580, 583 (1985); see Murphy v. USPS, 54 MSPR 202, 205 (1992) (agency did not comply with a settlement agreement calling for the assignment to a specific manager's position when it then created a second position with the same title, responsibilities, and duties; appellant did not agree to share manager's position); Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 438, 447 (1988) (considering unpersuasive the agency's explanations justifying changes in the appellant's position and reassignment following reinstatement). The classification of a position is not controlling, for it is based on guidelines to ensure equal pay for equal work. An agency does not demonstrate compliance if, without a valid reason, it reinstates an employee to a position in a different location or facility. A return "as nearly as possible" to the status quo ante would ordinarily be a return to exactly the same position occupied by the appellant before his removal. The classification standards are not stringent enough for the type of evaluation the Board is required to make under Kerr. Skills, supervisory responsibilities, and duties are determinative. Miller v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 593, 595 (1985).

A good summary of the Board's current position is in Moore v. USPS, 37 MSPR 106, 109 (1988), involving enforcement of a settlement agreement calling for return to the appellant's position:

...A position is comprised of more than mere title and pay. A necessary element is the duties required of the position. The appellant by being assigned to perform less than the full duties of the position has not been returned to the position as agreed.

Position descriptions are useful but not determinative evidence that the appellant was restored to the status quo. The Board looks to all the evidence made available by the parties concerning the details of the restored duties, as well as organizational changes, Gorny v. Dept. of Interior, 115 MSPR 520, 525-29 ¶¶ 15-29, 2011 MSPB 14 (2011):

The agency has complied with its obligation to reassign the appellant to duties and responsibilities that are substantially similar to those of her former position.

In analyzing whether an agency has reassigned an employee to a position that is substantially similar to her former position, the Board looks beyond the title and grade of the positions involved, and compares the scope of actual duties and responsibilities of the new position with those of the former position. Miller, 109 M.S.P.R. 41, ¶ 11, Tubesing, 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶ 7.

In this case, the parties have provided the Board with copies of the new and former position descriptions to compare. Nonetheless, in Tubesing, the Board found it improper to rely on such position descriptions alone, to the exclusion of rebuttal evidence by the appellant that the position description did not reflect the duties he was actually being assigned. Tubesing, 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶¶ 8, 9. Mr. Tubesing submitted a sworn statement that he was performing little or no work commensurate with his grade and position, and the agency offered nothing to rebut his assertion. Id. at ¶ 8. Weighing that evidence, the Board found the agency in noncompliance in Tubesing. Id. at ¶ 9.

As Tubesing makes clear, position descriptions are only one item of evidence and may be rebutted by evidence that an appellant is not actually performing the duties therein. Here, however, the appellant does not contend that the position description is inaccurate. Instead, she contends that the new position description itself reflects that the agency has not assigned her duties and responsibilities that are substantially similar to those of her prior position. The appellant's only disputes over the duties and responsibilities of her new position are: (1) the topical focus of her public affairs duties; and (2) the lack of a provision specifying that she can act as chief in the absence of a superior.

The underlying issue is not whether the appellant's new position is identical to her prior position, but whether it is substantially similar. As the Board has previously reasoned, "Since the appellant's former position has been abolished, [the appellant] 's placement in a different position necessarily involves the performance of different duties." Marion v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 443, 444 (1998). In analyzing this question, we compare the appellant's former position description with her current one. We also consider whether there is any evidence that the agency currently has a position that is more comparable to the position the appellant previously performed. Higashi v. Department of the Army, 26 M.S.P.R. 330, 332 (1985).

1. The topical focus of the appellant's new position is substantially similar to that of her former position.

The appellant maintains that an essential duty of her prior position was public affairs work on oil, gas, and coal issues, and that her prior position description reflected that. The appellant claims that her new position description "does not include these duties," and that "[t]herefore, her duties and responsibilities are not the same as those of her former position." Id.

In brief, the Board does not find by a preponderance of the evidence that the appellant is not performing public affairs duties with oil, gas and coal issues in her new position; in any event, the Board does not find that public affairs duties with oil, gas and coal issues were an essential aspect of the appellant's prior position.

First, as noted earlier in this decision, position descriptions are only one item of evidence that the Board considers in analyzing whether an agency has reassigned an employee to a position that is substantially similar to her former position. Tubesing, 112 M.S.P.R. 393 at ¶¶ 7-9. Thus, the current position description's failure to mention oil, coal, and gas is not necessarily dispositive. Moreover, the appellant fails to squarely assert that her public affairs duties do not still include oil, coal, and gas; instead, she writes that she "does not object" to her position's duties "as long as she is able to take advantage of her reputation and expertise in oil, gas and coal. It is too soon to tell whether this will happen or not. Therefore, Appellant reserves the right to object in the future should this continue as an issue." The Appellant has submitted only speculation that her public affairs duties will not include issues with oil, coal, and gas.

Second, the appellant fails to establish that public affairs duties with oil, coal, and gas were an essential aspect of her prior position. The appellant refers the Board to her prior position description, which she claims "reflected that her duties would be focused in those areas." However, the appellant does not identify where her prior position description contains such a provision. Nor is such a provision apparent, even in the most relevant part of the position description:

The objective of [the Office of Public Affairs] is to involve, inform, and motivate the public throughout the State of Wyoming and adjoining areas in order to facilitate land management decisions in Wyoming that protect the public interest.

* * *

Traditional uses which have dominated the land must often be adjusted to provide for the best multiple use practices. In addition, many of the Bureau's programs in Wyoming which affect specific land uses such as energy development have regional and even national impacts. This situation calls for a continued and concentrated public involvement program with highly qualified public information personnel. The public information specialist assists in the development and continuation of the public affairs activities for these programs[.]

These provisions fail even to assert that public affairs work on the topic of energy development, in general, is an inseparable aspect of the prior position. Instead, these provisions merely mention "energy development" as one topic with which the public affairs specialists might work.

To be sure, the Board looks further than the prior position description and also considers the findings of the administrative judge that mentioned oil, gas, and coal:

Before her removal, she was the main Congressional liaison who handled all of the work related to Congressional hearings; she was the main contact for oil, gas, and coal and land use planning.... Since her reinstatement, she has not responded to any Congressional inquiries.

* * *

She has not been able to use her expertise she gained over the years due to her isolation from the public affairs staff, and no longer has regular communications with the Washington, D.C. office of Public Affairs like she once did.

Although the administrative judge found that the appellant worked with oil, gas and coal issues, the judge did not find that these particular topics were inseparable from the prior position. Instead, the administrative judge's conclusion was that the appellant was not performing public affairs duties at all; the agency was assigning her duties that were "mainly clerical in nature." The administrative judge found these clerical duties did "not reflect even close to the full range of her responsibilities and duties while employed as a Public Affairs Specialist." In reaching that finding, the administrative judge, consistent with Tubesing, properly looked beyond the mere title and grade of the appellant, and found that she was not actually performing the duties of a public affairs specialist. However, the agency has since assigned the appellant to public affairs duties, and the appellant's only dispute here is speculation over the topical focus of those public affairs.

The Board considers also that the appellant has never disputed the agency's claim that the other public affairs specialist has the identical position description. Nor has the appellant alleged that the agency in fact reassigned the appellant's prior work on oil, gas and coal to this other public affairs specialist.

Based on the above evidence, the Board does not find that public affairs duties with oil, gas and coal issues were an essential aspect of the appellant's prior position; if anything, the essential focus of the appellant's prior public affairs duties was on land management in general. This finding is further consistent with the fact that the prior position description--which was classified twenty years ago in 1990--recognized that "[t]raditional uses which have dominated the land must often be adjusted to provide for the best multiple use practices." The prior position description dictated no permanent focus on one land use over another, but instead recognized that the focus of the public affairs work must often adjust. Further, on March 11, 2009, the Secretary of Interior issued an Order establishing renewable energy as a priority for the Department of Interior. That shift further erodes any inference that public affairs duties with a focus on oil, gas and coal issues were critical to the prior position. Therefore, the Board finds that the topical focus of the appellant's new position is substantially similar to that of her former position.

2. The appellant's responsibilities are substantially similar to those of her former position despite the absence of an "acting chief" responsibility.

The appellant's prior position description, created in 1990, specified that she "serve[d] as supervisor of the Office of Public Affairs in the absence of the Chief, OPA." The appellant's new position description contains no such provision, and the appellant contends that this absence makes her new position substantially different from her prior position.

To connect that position description to the present day, we assume, without finding, that at some point after 1990, the Office of Public Affairs was renamed the Office of External Affairs, because the appellant's July 31, 2007 removal notice identifies her as working in the Office of External Affairs. In November 2007, the agency merged the Office of External Affairs with the Visual Communications Team to create the Office of Communications. This re-organization put the two-member Visual Communications Team under the control of the Office of Communications.

The appellant asks the Board to rewrite her current position description to ensure that she retains the "acting chief" responsibility she was described as having in 1990, when she worked in an office that had a different name, organization and set of circumstances than the present Office of Communications.

The appellant puts forth no evidence as to when or how often she in fact acted as chief of the Office of Public Affairs. Nor does the appellant put forth any evidence that another individual in the Office of Communications, such as the other public affairs specialist, is being designated as acting chief.

Under the above facts, the Board cannot conclude that the agency's failure to write an "acting chief" responsibility into the appellant's position description means that her new, actual duties are not substantially similar to her prior duties. Similarly, we do not construe the new position description to preclude the possibility that the agency might designate the appellant as an acting chief.

When the Board speaks of a "position," it refers to the job the appellant held prior to removal, and that job includes the assigned shift. Reassignment to another shift, resulting in loss of night differential pay, does not meet the status quo ante standard. Clemons v. Smithsonian Inst., 50 MSPR 74, 77 (1991). But see Harris v. USDA, 53 MSPR 78, 82 (1992) ("[w]e find that an agency in the exercise of its discretionary authority can assign a former night shift employee to a day shift, when the employee's services are needed on the day shift.... [T]here is no evidence that Mr. Harris was assigned to the day shift for punitive reasons[.]"); cf. Lavelle v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 86, 90-91 (1988) and 40 MSPR 329, 332 (1989) (on second PFR) (discussing back pay overtime calculations affected by shift assignments), overruled in part by Mattern v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 65, 72 ¶ 16 (2001)).

The appellant's duty status has no effect on his status as the incumbent of a position lost by demotion that was later reversed. The appellant's LWOP status while recovering from a compensable injury does not excuse the agency from the obligation to restore the appellant to the status quo at the time the adverse action was taken. Trotter v. DOD, 54 MSPR 563, 564 n.* (1992). Nor does the appellant's intervening separation for disability from a job held through reassignment excuse the agency from reinstating the employee to the job held before a reassignment that was a prohibited personnel practice. If the appellant's medical problems continued after restoration to the position, the agency could propose separation for disability, but restoration of the status quo first required the appellant's return to the old position. See Bonggat v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 402, 413 (1993).

If the agency is directed to reinstate the employee and does so, the employee may challenge, as a compliance issue, his removal for refusal to accept a geographic reassignment following reinstatement. If compliance is improper, removal for refusal to accept the reassignment will be reversed. Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 24 MSPR 550, 553-54 (1984), and 28 MSPR 419, 423-24 (1985). To avoid being caught in the trap of removal proceedings overtaking compliance proceedings in this situation, AJs ought to be given the authority to issue stays of actions that are directly tied into compliance issues. Refer to Gammal v. MSPB, 671 F.2d 654 (1st Cir. 1982) [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Time for Initiating Enforcement," refer to geographical reassignment later in this chapter, "Compelling Reason for Reassignment; Detail; Different Duties"; refer to protective orders in Chapter 13, "Stays of Personnel Actions."]

An AJ reviewing agency action for compliance must determine whether there is evidence that the reassignment of the employee after reinstatement is a sham or a lesser position than that previously held. Presley v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 768, 770 (1984). The Board must make more than a formal determination that the appellant was reinstated to a position bearing the same title, grade, and pay. There must be a substantive assessment of whether the actual duties and responsibilities assigned to the appellant are either the same or substantially equivalent in scope and status to the duties and responsibilities held before termination. Nelson v. VA, 27 MSPR 133, 135 & n.2 (1985) (Board's action on an appeal does not bar the agency from instituting a second removal action based on different charges and placing the appellant into a paid nonduty status pending resolution of the second action); see Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 79 MSPR 342, 347 (1998) ("there must be a substantial assessment of the scope of duties and responsibilities compared with the appellant's former position"); cf. Kreissl v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 106, 108 (1984) (in a pre-Reform Act case, the Board determined that a position was equivalent and that the position descriptions did not reveal qualitative distinctions; the appellant's concern that he would be more vulnerable to a RIF was speculative: "Appellant's reasoning that the agency was obligated under the Board order to reassign the attorney who occupied appellant's position, instead of appellant, simply elevates form over substance.").

If the reinstatement is only a paperwork exercise because the employee elects to receive back pay and then immediately resign, never really resuming his position, the agency need not structure a job for the appellant. Bartel v. FAA, 24 MSPR 560, 564 (1984); see Short v. USPS, 69 MSPR 141, 143 (1995) (although the appellant had resigned before RIF compliance was achieved, the agency was still obligated to follow through with the paperwork to show that the appellant was "restored as nearly as possible" to the status quo); cf. Raphel v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 596, 605 (1992) ("The appellant's claim that the SF-50's are a 'sham' because she was not actually reinstated must be rejected as inconsistent with the agreement. A careful reading of the agreement demonstrates that reinstatement would only be effected on paper, as the purpose of the agreement was to provide the appellant a 'clean slate' and the opportunity to apply for disability retirement."); Okonski v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 489, 493 (1986) ("an agency may not deem an employee's affirmative offer to be a rejection because it is made solely for the purpose of receiving the back pay to which he is entitled").

If the appellant claims that the status quo was not restored after an agency cancels an action, the agency should respond to the appellant's claims, and the AJ is supposed to analyze them, as in Reed v. USPS, 98 MSPR 585, 589-90 ¶¶ 7-10 (2005) (overruled in part, Fernandez v. DOJ, 105 MSPR 443, 446 n.1, 2007 MSPB 99 (2007) ("these decisions and others that frame the test for mootness in this way are OVERRULED because the correct focus is on whether there is any relief that the appellant could receive if the Board ruled in his favor")):

The Board has held that an agency's unilateral modification of its adverse action after an appeal has been filed cannot divest the Board of jurisdiction unless the appellant consents to divesture or the agency completely rescinds the action being appealed. Frank v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 90 M.S.P.R. 458 ¶ 6 (2001). An agency's cancellation of its action constitutes a complete rescission, warranting dismissal, if the appellant is returned to the status quo ante, meaning placement of the appellant as nearly as possible in the same situation he would have been in if the action had never occurred. Rojas v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 400, 404 (1996). However, under Board law, an employee subjected to retaliation for filing a Board appeal has not been returned to the status quo ante. Id. at 406. Hostile actions of agency officials directed at an employee can evidence an agency's refusal to restore an employee in good faith. Williams v. Department of the Navy, 79 M.S.P.R. 364, 367 (1998).

The appellant claims on PFR that she was given the worst tour, the worst days off and one of the "foulest jobs" that does not reflect her wage grade level of 5 in retaliation for filing an appeal with the Board. The appellant had also raised claims of reprisal on appeal, arguing that the agency was not properly "looking after" her pay and did not give her a time badge, that the agency's failure to restore her to a level 5 position demonstrated the level of reprisal against her, and that a manager sought to "disrupt her life" by moving her to another tour. The AJ failed to address the appellant's claims of reprisal in the initial decision. This was error. See Rojas, 70 M.S.P.R. at 406. If the appellant demonstrates that the agency has taken retaliatory actions against her, it would evidence a failure to return the appellant to the status quo ante. See Williams, 79 M.S.P.R. at 367.

The record is insufficiently developed, however, for us to determine whether the agency is taking actions against the appellant in retaliation for filing her Board appeal. The agency did not address the appellant's arguments that it is retaliating against her by not "looking after" her pay, refusing to give her a time badge, or forcing her to perform level 4 work. Moreover, the agency provided very little argument on the reason the appellant's work schedule was changed. The Board has noted that, in complying with a Board order, an agency has the discretion to change an appellant's work schedule, provided there is no loss of pay differential or other evidence of harm; in any case, evidence of improper reasons for a change in an employee's schedule will constitute an abuse of discretion.[1] Rose v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 139, 146 (1997). In response to the appellant's complaints about the change in her work schedule, the agency states only that, as an unassigned regular, the appellant's schedule is temporary and is subject to change. The agency also failed to address the appellant's argument that she was senior to another employee who was allowed to remain on the night tour. In the absence of specific explanations by the agency addressing the appellant's claims, we are unable to determine whether the appellant's change in tour was for a proper reason.

[1] The Board has stated that, to render an appeal moot by canceling the action on appeal, the agency must return the appellant to the status quo ante in a manner identical to that required when the Board orders cancellation of an action. Hill v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 453, 456, aff'd, 104 F.3d 376 (Fed. Cir. 1996).

The appellant should be provided an opportunity on remand to support her allegations of retaliation by the agency. She must introduce proof that, specifically as the result of filing an appeal or the agency's purported rescission, she is being subjected to mistreatment by the agency. See Rojas, 70 M.S.P.R. at 406. If she does so, the agency must rebut this showing to meet its burden of proving that its action was fully rescinded. Id. at 406-07. Particularly, the agency must address the appellant's argument that her tour was changed in retaliation for filing a Board appeal.
