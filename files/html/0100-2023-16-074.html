d. Performance During Detail

Some limits are placed on the opportunity and notice period so that agency action is within the bounds of reasonableness. If an agency gives an employee a temporary promotion to show he can do higher-level work, the agency cannot determine that the employee was unable to do that work during the trial period and, at the same time, call that a warning period justifying removal from the position held prior to the temporary promotion. There would at least have to be specific performance standards covering the situation and notice to the employee of the nature of the trial period. Cortes v. Dept. of Interior, 26 MSPR 88, 90 (1985). The same result applies if an employee is placed on a detail into a job different from the permanent position. If the employee's performance in the detail is unsatisfactory, the agency cannot remove the employee from the permanent position as long as the employee's performance in the permanent position was satisfactory prior to the detail. Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 253, 254-55 (1986). Smith was reaffirmed, Betters v. FEMA, 57 MSPR 405, 408-09 (1993):

In Sandland v. General Services Administration, 23 MSPR 583, 587 (1984), the Board held that the opportunity-to-improve period is a substantive right. Thus, to sustain a removal under 5 USC Chapter 43, the agency must establish by substantial evidence, inter alia, that it gave the employee a reasonable opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance. The administrative judge found that the agency failed in two respects to make this showing. First, he found that the appellant's performance was rated unsatisfactory in a position to which he had been detailed, and he was never informed that his performance in his official position of record was unsatisfactory. Second, he found that when the agency placed the appellant on the PIP, it simultaneously presented him with revised performance standards substantially different from his prior standards, thereby depriving the appellant of a reasonable opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance.

The administrative judge found that the appellant was detailed for a period of 120 days effective April 23, 1990, from the...position of Computer Systems Analyst, Office of Insurance Support Services, to the same position in the agency's Office of Insurance Operations, and that the detail was extended to April 20, 1991.

With regard to the detail, the agency argues that because the position title and the occupational series of the two positions were identical, and only the appellant's supervisor and place of work were changed, it was not actually a detail. The agency reasons that it made every effort to move the appellant into a position with the same position description, in the same grade and series, and with performance standards fashioned to fit the position description. In addition, it notes that the performance standards were developed mutually by the agency and the appellant. It appears that the agency took steps it believed in good faith would ensure that the appellant's job duties remained the same. However, the propriety of a charge of unacceptable performance is judged not based on a position description but rather on the employee's performance plan and the elements and standards derived under it. See Clifford v. Dept. of Agriculture, 50 MSPR 232, 236 (1991) (footnote omitted). An examination of the appellant's performance plan prior to being placed on the detail shows that it differs substantially from the performance plan he received when he was placed on the detail. An employee is deprived of a meaningful opportunity to improve where the agency has not informed him that his performance in his official position of record was unsatisfactory and given him an opportunity to improve under the standards of that position, but instead has rated him unsatisfactory based on his performance in a position to which he has been detailed. See Smith v. Department of the Navy, 30 MSPR 253, 254-55 (1986).[4]

[4] Although Smith is factually distinguishable from the present case (because in Smith the employee had been detailed to a different grade and position), what is similar is that neither appellant was informed by the agency that his performance in his official position of record was unsatisfactory, and both removals were based only on performance in the detail position. This is not to say, however, that poor performance during a detail can never be used (at least in part) as the basis of a Chapter 43 action. Under 5 CFR 430.206(d), when an employee is placed on a detail, ratings on critical elements must be prepared and these ratings must be considered in deriving an employee's rating of record that covers that period.

See Luscri v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 482, 486 n.2 (1989) (appellant's performance while on detail could not be used as proof concerning a charge of unacceptable performance in the appellant's specific permanent position); cf. Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 37 MSPR 78, 82 (1988) (performance of the appellant in a position held by an improper demotion could not support a performance-based removal from that position).

A relatively brief detail to another job within the same agency does not counter evidence of unsatisfactory performance in the appellant's regular job during a PIP. See Clifford v. USDA, 50 MSPR 232, 236 (1991) (AJ should have considered the appellant's contention that his detail resulted in additional duties that prevented him from successfully completing his PIP). A detail to another agency need not be considered concerning performance during an opportunity period with an agency, particularly if there is no evidence in the record of how the detail duties related to the critical elements judged unacceptable in the employee's performance. Coleman v. Dept. of Educ. (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3025).
