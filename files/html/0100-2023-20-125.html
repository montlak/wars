3. Pleading to OSC

Once arguably protected disclosures are made, and reprisal occurs, suitably specific allegations must be presented in an IRA complaint to OSC. Those allegations, generally contained in the Form OSC-14 [formerly OSC-11] or attachments to that form (or in the electronically filed version), should specifically describe the whistleblowing and the resulting reprisal. The actions or events disclosed to OSC must be described to OSC in sufficient detail for OSC to investigate the alleged reprisal. See Ablestein v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-3302) ("These 'disclosures' provide no event or information for the OSC to investigate[.]"); Ashmore v. DOD, SF-1221-21-0549-W-1 (NP 8/8/2022) ("The substantive requirements of exhaustion are met when an appellant has provided OSC with sufficient basis to pursue an investigation."). The OSC-14 [formerly OSC-11] form is submitted to OSC through its Complaints Examining Unit. "Unlike OSC's Complaints Examining Unit, its Disclosure Unit does not review allegations of prohibited personnel practices, and disclosures to it do not satisfy the exhaustion requirement for an individual right of action (IRA) appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3)." Krugman v. VA, DA-1221-13-0288-W-1 (NP 6/12/2014) (citing Mason v. DHS, 116 MSPR 135 ¶ 16, 2011 MSPB 39 (2011)), following remand, Krugman v. VA, DA-1221-13-0288-B-1 (NP 4/2/2015) (finding against the appellant).

At times MSPB cannot discern whether what are described as disclosures have gone to OSC as prohibited personnel practice allegations, to OSC's disclosure unit, or to both. Holmes v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-11-0263-B-3, n.9 (NP 4/15/2022), noted:

If OSC received allegations about the falsification of a complainant's personnel documents, whether as part of an initial complaint or in the course of a PPP investigation, OSC could possibly have made a separate referral to its Disclosure Unit (which receives disclosures under different authority (5 U.S.C. § 1213) than OSC's authority to receive and investigate PPPs (5 U.S.C. § 1212(a)(2), 1214(a)(1)(A))) or simply considered the allegations as part of the PPP investigation. Given the evidence the appellant has provided, it seems more likely that the allegations were simply considered as part of the PPP investigation.

The Board may determine that it lacks jurisdiction over the IRA appeal for failure of exhaust remedies through provision of insufficient information to OSC, Becker v. VA, NY-1221-10-0280-W-1 (NP 3/25/2011):

The Board has jurisdiction over an individual right of action (IRA) appeal if the appellant has exhausted his administrative remedies before the Office of Special Counsel (OSC) and makes nonfrivolous allegations that: (1) he engaged in whistleblowing activity by making a protected disclosure, and (2) the disclosure was a contributing factor in the agency's decision to take or fail to take a personnel action. See Yunus v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 242 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2001); Rusin v. Department of the Treasury, 92 M.S.P.R. 298, ¶ 12 (2002). To meet the exhaustion requirement, the appellant must provide OSC with a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might have led to corrective action. Briley v. National Archives and Records Administration, 236 F.3d 1373, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2001). That is, the appellant must articulate with reasonable clarity and detail the basis for his complaint of whistleblowing reprisal. Id.; Coufal v. Department of Justice, 98 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 4 (2004). In showing that the exhaustion requirement has been met, an appellant is not limited by the issues raised in the initial complaint, but may also show that he amended his complaint through subsequent correspondence with OSC. Pasley v. Department of the Treasury, 109 M.S.P.R. 105, ¶¶ 12-15 (2008). Where, as here, an agency's action is not directly appealable to the Board, and the appellant has sought corrective action from OSC, the appellant must file his IRA appeal: (1) no later than 65 days after the date of issuance of OSC's written notification that it is terminating its investigation into his complaint, or within 60 days of the date he received the notification if he received it more than 5 days after the date of issuance; or (2) at any time after the expiration of 120 days of the date of filing of his complaint if OSC has not notified him that it will seek corrective action on his behalf. See 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3); 5 C.F.R. § 1209.5(a).

We note that 120 days have now passed since the appellant filed his complaint, and it does not appear that OSC has issued a termination letter. Nevertheless, the appellant has not satisfied the exhaustion requirement, because his July 1, 2010 complaint and July 31, 2010 follow-up letter did not provide OSC with a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation that might have led to corrective action. To the extent the submissions are at all legible, they fail to set forth with reasonable clarity and precision even the core of a retaliation claim, which would include at least such rudimentary details as the content of the protected disclosures, the individuals to whom they were made, and, if known, the individuals responsible for the appellant's non-selection. See Swanson v. General Services Administration, 110 M.S.P.R. 278, ¶ 8 (2008) (citing Briley, 236 F.3d at 1378). Without such information, OSC could not have known what to investigate. Because the appellant has not exhausted his remedies with OSC, the Board still lacks jurisdiction over this appeal.

Assuming that the appellant engaged in articulated whistleblowing, Mintzmyer v. Dept. of Interior, 84 F.3d 419, 422 (Fed. Cir. 1996), concluded that if jurisdiction of the MSPB depends on allegations to OSC, those allegations must specify the complainant's whistleblowing and resultant reprisal. Implicit allegations are not favored:

The WPA prohibits government personnel actions taken against an employee in reprisal for whistleblowing. 5 USC § 2302(b)(8) (1994); Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 66 F.3d 279, 282 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Ward v. MSPB, 981 F.2d 521, 523 (Fed. Cir. 1992). Except when there exists an independent right to appeal an adverse personnel action directly to the board, an employee or former employee aggrieved by the action must first seek corrective action from the Office of Special Counsel. 5 USC §§ 1214(a)(3), 1221(b) (1994); Knollenberg v. MSPB, 953 F.2d 623, 625-26 (Fed. Cir. 1992). Only after the Office of Special Counsel has notified the employee or former employee that it has terminated its investigation or has failed to commit to pursuing corrective action within 120 days may that person file an IRA appeal, under 5 USC § 1221, to the board. 5 USC § 1214(a)(3); Ellison v. MSPB, 7 F.3d 1031, 1036 (Fed. Cir. 1993), Knollenberg, 953 F.2d at 625-26.

At the Office of Special Counsel, Mintzmyer argued that the agency had taken the following personnel actions against her in reprisal for whistleblowing: (1) reassigned her; (2) denied her a salary step increase; (3) denied her a bonus; and (4) constructively discharged her through a course of "harassing and retaliatory behavior." Before the board, Mintzmyer alleged that the agency also had taken the following "retaliatory actions": (1) threatened to reprimand her; (2) continuously threatened her with "prosecution"; (3) made several false and slanderous statements "With regard to actions taken against and by" her; and (4) declared that she was a threat to President George Bush just prior to a visit he made to the Mid-Atlantic Region.

Mintzmyer, who has the burden of establishing the board's jurisdiction over her IRA appeal, see Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1244 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (in banc), does not argue that the four claims dismissed as newly raised were directly appealable to the board and thus within its jurisdiction. Rather, she points to the allegation in her complaint filed with the Office of Special Counsel that she was "subjected to constructive discharge by harassing and retaliatory behavior" in arguing that she had raised these claims previously. But she did not raise these four alleged agency actions as independent personnel actions taken against her before the Office of Special Counsel. Mintzmyer was required "to articulate with reasonable clarity and precision the basis for [her] request for corrective action under the WPA" to the Office of Special Counsel. Ellison, 7 F.3d at 1037; see also Ward, 981 F.2d at 526. Her failure to do so deprived the board of jurisdiction over these four claims; thus, its dismissal was proper.

McCarthy v. MSPB, 809 F.3d 1365, 1374-75 (Fed. Cir. 2016), reaffirmed the necessity for presenting specific allegations to OSC before they are presented to the Board in an IRA appeal:

McCarthy's case is an individual right of action ("IRA") appeal, which the Board has jurisdiction to hear under 5 U.S.C. § 1214. However, before an aggrieved employee can file an IRA appeal, he must first seek corrective action from the OSC. 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3) ("Except in a case in which an employee, former employee, or applicant for employment has the right to appeal directly to the Merit Systems Protection Board under any law, rule, or regulation, any such employee, former employee, or applicant shall seek corrective action from the Special Counsel before seeking corrective action from the Board.").

In assessing whether an employee has exhausted his OSC remedies, we look to his OSC complaint, as well as written correspondence concerning his allegations. Serrao, 95 F.3d at 1577; Benton-Flores v. Dep't of Def., No. DC-1221-13-0522-W-1, 2014 WL 3748419 (M.S.P.B. July 31, 2014). "We require that the employee 'articulate with reasonable clarity and precision [before the OSC] the basis for his request for corrective action under the WPA' to allow OSC to effectively pursue an investigation." Miller v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., No. 2015-3054, 2015 WL 4681015, at *6 (Fed. Cir. Aug. 6, 2015) (quoting Serrao, 95 F.3d at 1577); see also Knollenberg v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 953 F.2d 623, 626 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (requiring a "sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might have led to corrective action"). "The Board's jurisdiction over an IRA appeal, assuming the employee does not have an independent right to appeal directly to the Board, is thus limited to those issues that have been previously raised with OSC." Miller, 2015 WL 4681015, at *6.

Applying these principles here, we find that McCarthy has not sufficiently exhausted his OSC remedies with respect to the legal memoranda. McCarthy's OSC complaint only identifies his July 28 report to the OIG and a July 29 disclosure to Congressman Reyes. It makes no mention of his legal memoranda. Given this, the OSC's "basis to pursue an investigation" was only these two disclosures and the circumstances surrounding them, such as whom these disclosures were made to, the Commission's awareness of these disclosures, and how they related to the events that led to McCarthy's termination. This did not provide the OSC with a "sufficient basis to pursue an investigation" with respect to McCarthy's legal memoranda--these were different disclosures, made to different people, at different times.

Moreover, the case that McCarthy presented to the OSC in 2009 substantially differs from the case he presents now. In 2009, McCarthy's legal memoranda were not considered protected disclosures under the WPA; thus, the OSC would have had no basis to investigate the legal memoranda and develop findings related to these disclosures. What McCarthy presents now is in essence a different case, and the OSC has not had an opportunity to "effectively pursue an investigation" with respect to these circumstances. Thus, McCarthy has failed to satisfy § 1214(a)(3)'s exhaustion requirement.

McCarthy nonetheless argues that he has exhausted his OSC remedies because his August 21 statement discussed the substance of his legal memoranda. We disagree. As discussed, this submission was provided and subsequently evaluated against the backdrop of the WPA: the legal memoranda were not protected disclosures and the OSC would have had no basis to "effectively pursue an investigation" with respect to them.

Next, McCarthy argues that "the Board and this court have already considered the import of the legal memoranda." It is not clear to us that McCarthy's selective excerpts show that the Board or this court has done so. Regardless, the consideration that the Board or this court would give to the legal memoranda on appellate review is not necessarily the same consideration that the OSC would give in the first instance, and cannot be substituted for it.

McCarthy also argues that the exhaustion requirement only applies to the content of whistleblowing disclosures, and that existing case law does not require that he correctly affix legal labels to these facts. We agree that the focus of the exhaustion requirement is on substance; however, McCarthy has not satisfied this requirement here. Neither his OSC complaint nor his August 21 statement identifies the legal memoranda with sufficient specificity or context to allow the OSC to effectively investigate his claims. Unlike Briley v. National Archives & Records Administration, 236 F.3d 1373, 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2001), cited by McCarthy, this is not simply a case where McCarthy is presenting a "more detailed account of [his] whistleblowing activities" to the Board than he did to the OSC, but otherwise made submissions to the OSC that "contain[ed] the core of [his] retaliation claim" and "gave the OSC sufficient basis to pursue an investigation." Instead, the core substance of McCarthy's complaint has shifted from a single disclosure to the OIG (which, at best, was at least in part cumulative of a nebulous collection of previous disclosures) to four distinct legal memoranda provided to specific individuals at specific times.

We find, then, that McCarthy has not exhausted his OSC remedies with respect to the legal memoranda. This rendered the Board without jurisdiction to hear his case under 5 U.S.C. § 1214.

The Board did not err in dismissing McCarthy's appeal and we affirm the Board's decision. We note that McCarthy remains free to pursue a complaint with the OSC with respect to his four legal memoranda, as there is no statutory time limit for filing a request for corrective action with the OSC. Augustine v. Dep't of Justice, 50 M.S.P.R. 648, 652 (1991).

Under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3), an employee is required to seek corrective action from OSC before seeking corrective action from the Board. The Board's jurisdiction is limited to issues raised before OSC. See Ellison v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 7 F.3d 1031, 1036 (Fed. Cir. 1993). To meet the exhaustion requirement, the appellant must provide OSC a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might have led to corrective action. Briley v. National Archives & Records Administration, 236 F.3d 1373, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2001). That is, the appellant must articulate with reasonable clarity and precision before OSC the basis of his request for corrective action. Coufal v. Department of Justice, 98 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 14 (2004). Tuten v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP No. 2007-3145) [2007 WL 2914787], emphasized the necessity for explaining to OSC the details of the protected disclosures that led to the reprisals protested to the Board in an IRA appeal:

The Board ruled that two of the allegations contained in Ms. Tuten's letter to the Office of Special Counsel satisfied the exhaustion requirement--the allegation that the agency falsified medical records and the allegation that the agency illegally transferred sick inmates out of the institution to pass program review. The Board concluded, however, that neither allegation was sufficiently specific to constitute a non-frivolous allegation of a protected disclosure. That decision is correct. "Substantive details establishing jurisdiction must be alleged in the complaint." Ellison v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 7 F.3d 1031, 1036 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (citing Livingston v. Derwinski, 959 F.2d 224, 225 (Fed. Cir. 1992)). Ms. Tuten has provided nothing more than bare assertions of wrongdoing by the agency, even after being given an opportunity to provide more detail.

Ms. Tuten argues that the proper standard to evaluate jurisdiction in this case is whether, after discovery and a hearing, she could prove her claim by a preponderance of the evidence. She argues that we should remand the case to the Board with orders that she be allowed to conduct discovery. The flaw in that argument is that, for individual right of action appeals, the jurisdictional predicate is whether the appellant has made allegations that, if proved, would entitle the appellant to relief. Stoyanov v. Dep't of the Navy, 474 F.3d 1377, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (citing Garcia v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., 437 F.3d 1322, 1325 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc), and Spruill v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 978 F.2d 679, 687-89 (Fed. Cir. 1992)). Because Ms. Tuten failed to make the required allegations, the Board has no jurisdiction over this case and therefore has no authority to order discovery on the merits of the claims.

Ms. Tuten also contends that we should reverse the Board's decision because the Board failed to "alert" her to the fact that she was required to make more than summary allegations. Even assuming there is such a requirement, the facts demonstrate that Ms. Tuten was on notice. In response to the Board's jurisdictional orders, she noted that she was required to make non-frivolous allegations that she had engaged in whistleblowing activity. In addition, the Board ordered Ms. Tuten to detail, among other things, the "date, substance, and recipients of the protected disclosure." Given her understanding and the specificity of the Board's order, Ms. Tuten cannot now complain that she lacked sufficient knowledge of what was required of her to establish jurisdiction. We therefore affirm the Board's decision dismissing Ms. Tuten's appeal.

The AJ is responsible for a detailed analysis of the requirement that the appellant provide to OSC the basis for her whistleblowing allegations, Diefenderfer v. Dept. of Transp., 108 MSPR 651, 662 ¶ 28, 2008 MSPB 105 (2008):

To satisfy the exhaustion requirement in an IRA appeal, an appellant must inform OSC of the precise bases for her claim of reprisal, giving OSC a sufficient basis on which to pursue an investigation that might lead to corrective action. Redschlag v. Department of the Army, 89 M.S.P.R. 589, ¶ 94 (2001), review dismissed, 32 Fed. Appx. 543 (Fed. Cir. 2002). She also is responsible for proving by preponderant evidence that she met this exhaustion requirement. See Davis v. Department of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 516, ¶ 8 (2006). OSC's failure to mention the actions to which the agency refers, however, does not preclude a finding that the appellant exhausted her OSC remedy with respect to those actions. See Bloom v. Department of the Army, 101 M.S.P.R. 79, ¶ 10 (2006). Moreover, the administrative judge is responsible for explaining the bases for his findings. See Spithaler v. Office of Personnel Management, 1 M.S.P.R. 587, 589 (1980). Neither the initial decision nor the order in which the administrative judge made his earlier finding that the appellant had exhausted her OSC remedy identifies the basis for that finding.

But under Delgado v. MSPB, 880 F.3d 913, 923-27 (7th Cir. 2018) (following remand, Delgado v. DOJ, 966 F.3d 556 (7th Cir. 2020) (amended on petition for rehearing, Delgado v. DOJ, 977 F.3d 1295 (7th Cir. 2020)), OSC needs to be presented only with sufficient information to conduct its investigation, not every detail relating to the reprisal asserted by the complainant:

Delgado's appeal also failed before the Board for a third reason, which was also based on an arbitrary requirement imposed by the Board's treatment of the exhaustion requirement. The Board relied on a series of Federal Circuit decisions interpreting the Whistleblower Protection Act's command to "seek corrective action from the Special Counsel" to mean that "the employee must inform the Special Counsel of the precise ground of his charge." E.g., Ward v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 981 F.2d 521, 526 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (emphasis added); see also Mintzmyer v. Dep't of the Interior, 84 F.3d 419, 422 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (affirming Board determination that employee failed to exhaust four specific claims of agency retaliation that were not included in charge to OSC); Ellison, 7 F.3d at 1036-37 (agreeing that Board lacked jurisdiction because petitioner failed to inform OSC that claim of reprisal was based on protected disclosure to Inspector General; employee told OSC only of unprotected agency grievance). In Ward itself, the court affirmed the Board's refusal to consider additional episodes of allegedly wasted funds on travel by different person that were not presented in the OSC complaint: "An allegation of a specific alleged travel impropriety--here Dr. Kamely's authorization of Dr. DeFrank's trip to Portugal--was not sufficient notice to the Special Counsel that Dr. Ward also was challenging other travel related activities of Dr. Kamely." 981 F.2d at 526.

We have no disagreement with that general point, at least as stated by our colleagues on the Federal Circuit. But as applied by the Board in Delgado's case and others, exhaustion seems to be determined separately for each fact an employee alleges rather than for each claim of protected disclosure or retaliation. See, e.g., Daniels v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 105 M.S.P.R. 248, 253-54 (MSPB 2007) (concluding that Board lacked jurisdiction to review agency's proposed removal of whistleblower because it occurred three weeks after OSC closed her complaint and thus was not specifically presented, even though removal was consistent with complaint, which alleged campaign of retaliation including threat of "removal of all duties"); Reeves v. Dep't of the Army, 101 M.S.P.R. 337, 341-42 (MSPB 2005) (dismissing for failure to exhaust soldier's claim that disclosure of gross waste of training funds resulted in poor performance evaluation because he neglected to tell OSC "the precise actions he reported" and how he "reasoned that the managers' actions constituted gross waste"). As the Board applies this framework, a whistleblower effectively forfeits reliance on any factual detail not presented first to the OSC, and if the facts the employee presents do not establish a conclusive case, there is no need for the OSC to investigate. As the Board explained in Delgado's case, it agreed with the administrative judge that "nothing [Delgado] had provided indicated that he gave OSC a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation that might lead to corrective action."

The source of this unusually stringent approach is not clear to us. The exhaustion language in the statute is simple and brief. The employee must "seek corrective action from the Special Counsel before seeking corrective action from the Board." See 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3). We do not see why this directive should be read to require a federal employee (who typically is not trained in the law) to present to the OSC a perfectly packaged case ready for litigation. The directive surely cannot mean that an agency whose mission is to investigate must be given upfront all the information necessary to prove a complaint.

Another recent case, Clarke v. Department of Veterans Affairs, illustrates this problem in practice. 121 M.S.P.R. 154, 160-68 (MSPB 2014). Clarke, a physician, filed an OSC complaint containing eight specific disclosures of wrongdoing at the Veterans Administration. The OSC responded with an email seeking "more detailed information" within 10 days. (What exactly the agency wanted is unclear.) Dr. Clarke responded with a detailed narrative, but it covered only two of the eight disclosures due to "the time constraints imposed by OSC." OSC then declined to investigate--at all.

Dr. Clarke appealed, and the Board affirmed dismissal. The Board decided that Dr. Clarke had failed to exhaust the six disclosures not included in his response to the OSC's email--over the dissent of the Vice Chairman, who thought that Dr. Clarke's initial complaint contained plenty of detail, regardless of whether the agency had later requested even more information. Id. at 165-68. Remarkably, however, the majority grounded its finding of failure to exhaust on the OSC's "determination that it might need further information in order to pursue an investigation," a conclusion that the Board thought should be entitled to deference under Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U.S. 837 (1984). Clarke, 121 M.S.P.R. at 160 n.8.

This novel determination prompted the OSC to file its own brief as amicus curiae asking the Federal Circuit to overturn the Board's conclusion that Dr. Clarke had failed to exhaust. The OSC contended that the Board had disregarded the unambiguous statutory language of the exhaustion requirement without understanding "all of the relevant factors that OSC considers in marshaling its limited resources" when deciding whether to investigate a particular complaint among the thousands it receives annually. Brief for U.S. Office of Special Counsel as Amicus Curiae Supporting Petitioner, Clarke v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 623 F. App'x 1016 (Fed. Cir. 2015).

The OSC also argued that the Board's reliance on its decision not to investigate was untenable for two reasons. First, the Whistleblower Protection Act itself forbids the Board from considering such determinations. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 1221(f), 1214(b)(2)(E). Second, Congress specified during passage of the Act "that whistleblowers who seek the OSC's help not be penalized by any OSC decision not to pursue their cases." See S. Rep. No. 100-413, at 10 (1988). Notwithstanding the agency's opposition, the Federal Circuit summarily affirmed the Board's decision that Dr. Clarke had failed to exhaust his remedies before the OSC, but it did so in a non-precedential order. Clarke v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 623 F. App'x 1016 (Fed. Cir. 2015).

As Clarke illustrates, the Board's stringent application of the Whistleblower Protection Act's exhaustion requirement can effectively prevent all but the savviest federal whistleblowers from receiving a hearing on the merits. We think Congress intended the exhaustion requirement simply to give the OSC and the employing agency a chance to resolve issues without litigation.

The correct approach, we conclude, is to interpret the Act's requirement more consistently with other statutory exhaustion schemes. The Federal Tort Claims Act, for example, bars suit against the United States for money damages "unless the claimant shall have first presented the claim to the appropriate Federal Agency and his claim shall have been finally denied by the agency." 28 U.S.C. § 2675(a); see 28 C.F.R. § 14.2(a) (confirming that "claim shall be deemed...presented when a Federal agency receives from a claimant" notice of the incident and a sum certain for damages on Standard Form 95).

To satisfy that important requirement, a claimant is not required to submit evidence or to include every detail that might be necessary to prove at trial. Instead, courts construe pro se administrative complaints generously and deem exhausted any claim fairly implicit in the facts that would be clear to a legally sophisticated reader. Buechel v. United States, 746 F.3d 753, 760 (7th Cir. 2014); accord, e.g., Lopez v. United States, 823 F.3d 970, 975-76 (10th Cir. 2016); Goodman v. United States, 298 F.3d 1048, 1055-57 (9th Cir. 2002) (describing FTCA notice requirement as "minimal" and confirming that a "skeletal" description of the alleged harm will suffice). "All that is required is sufficient notice to enable the agency to investigate," Palay v. United States, 349 F.3d 418, 426 (7th Cir. 2003) (internal quotation marks omitted). A plaintiff is not prohibited from later buttressing the exhausted claim with additional details or evidence. After the claimant provides enough information to put a legally sophisticated reader on notice, it is up to the agency "to fill in the gaps, to the extent possible." Buechel, 746 F.3d at 761.

Perhaps even closer to the whistleblower issue is the standard used to determine whether a particular claim asserted in court under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 U.S.C. § 2000e et seq., has been exhausted before the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. Like the Whistleblower Protection Act's exhaustion scheme, Title VII requires a plaintiff to exhaust a discrimination claim through an agency that is charged with investigating but that will not be a defendant in a later civil suit on the merits of the claim.

The Supreme Court held long ago that although Title VII requires the filing of a timely charge with the EEOC before filing suit, exhaustion is not a jurisdictional requirement and may thus be waived or subject to estoppel and equitable tolling. Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 455 U.S. 385, 392-98 (1982). The Court also has instructed that "technicalities are particularly inappropriate in a statutory scheme in which laymen, unassisted by trained lawyers initiate the process." Lego v. Twomey, 404 U.S. 522, 527 (1972).

With these principles in mind we have held that, because most complaints "are completed by laypersons rather than by lawyers, a Title VII plaintiff need not allege in an EEOC charge each and every fact that combines to form the basis of each claim in her complaint." Cheek v. Western & Southern Life Ins. Co., 31 F.3d 497, 500 (7th Cir. 1994). We regard "a theory raised in court to fall within the scope of an administrative complaint if it is reasonably related to the charges actually set forth in the administrative filing." Dear v. Shinseki, 578 F.3d 605, 609 (7th Cir. 2009); accord, e.g., Sydnor v. Fairfax County, 681 F.3d 591, 595-96 (4th Cir. 2012); Williams v. New York City Housing Auth., 458 F.3d 67, 70-71 (2d Cir. 2006). Even a charge that is not explicit in an employee's complaint will be deemed exhausted if "the current claim reasonably could have developed from the EEOC's investigation of the charges before it,"meaning that "the EEOC charge and the complaint must describe the same conduct and implicate the same individuals." Ezell v. Potter, 400 F.3d 1041, 1046 (7th Cir. 2005). Federal employees alleging discrimination are subject to additional preconditions within the employing agency, see 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16(c), but since the scope of the exhaustion requirement is the same, federal employees' complaints receive the same generous construction. Reynolds v. Tangherlini, 737 F.3d 1093, 1099-1100 (7th Cir. 2013). We see no statutory or other reason it should be substantially more difficult for a federal employee to exhaust a claim of retaliation under the Whistleblower Protection Act than a charge of discrimination under Title VII.

The OSC examiner also faulted Delgado for not providing evidence showing "a causal connection between the disclosures you identified and the ATF's decisions" because "there is no indication that either [disclosure] resulted in any investigation, or that any official suffered adverse impact because of the report." This conclusion seems to have been worded as if Delgado were required to allege that Labno suffered adverse impact. That could not be correct. The issue is retaliation against the alleged whistleblower, not against the target of his disclosures. "Congress passed the whistleblower statute precisely because it did not trust agencies to regulate whistleblowers within their ranks." Dep't of Homeland Security v. Mac-Lean, 135 S. Ct. 913, 920 (2015).

The pertinent inquiry is of course whether Delgado himself suffered adverse action as a result of his disclosures of Labno's possible perjury and reckless firing of his weapon. Delgado's complaint specifies adverse actions he suffered because of his disclosures, including denied promotions, a threat of an unwelcome transfer, and a hostile work environment. Neither OSC nor the Board has identified any particular problem with Delgado's allegations on this score. He provided enough specifics for the OSC to investigate if it had chosen to do so, including identifying specific adverse actions and specific responsible supervisors. Delgado's complaint does not prove that the relevant decisions were motivated by a desire to retaliate against him for his disclosures, but he needed only to provide a sufficient basis for investigation. He did so.

See Dabner v. EPA, CH-1221-17-0112-W-1, n.2 (NP 5/3/2023) ("While this case was pending on review, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit issued Delgado v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 880 F.3d 913 (7th Cir. 2018), rejecting what it viewed as the 'unusually stringent approach' of determining exhaustion 'separately for each fact that an employee alleges rather than for each claim of protected disclosure or retaliation.' Id. at 923 (emphasis in original). Rather, the court concluded, the exhaustion requirement is satisfied if the appellant has provided OSC 'sufficient information to permit a legally sophisticated reader to understand [the] charge of retaliation and to investigate further.' Id. at 927. We find that, to enable a legally sophisticated reader to understand a charge of retaliation for disclosing information to an Inspector General, an employee must at least inform the reader that she made such a disclosure.").

Delgado is on the mark. It is an unsettling phenomenon that without even a murmur of protest from OSC about the level of detail of allegations it receives, and without apparent recognition that OSC is supposed to be an investigatory organization that can develop its own information, the Board feels compelled to set high standards for pleading by employees who file IRA complaints with OSC. OSC is capable of requesting additional information through its Complaints Examining Unit or through its investigative or legal staff. The Board's standards of pleading to OSC may at times amount to a requirement that employees provide OSC not only with allegations but with proof of their allegations for MSPB to later permit them to prove before the Board that the employees suffered reprisal. To be protected under the Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA), disclosures "must be specific and detailed, not vague allegations of wrongdoing regarding broad or imprecise matters." Special Counsel v. Costello, 75 MSPR 562, 580 (1997), rev'd on other grounds, 182 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999); see also Keefer v. Department of Agriculture, 82 MSPR 687 ¶ 10 (1999) (general conclusory allegations lacking in specificity do not constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction in an IRA appeal); Padilla v. Department of the Air Force, 55 MSPR 540, 543 (1992) (characterizing the appellant's assertion of fraud, waste, and abuse in a particular Civil Engineering Squadron as vague allegations of wrongdoing that did not constitute whistleblowing). The Board and its reviewing court have similarly held that vague and conclusory allegations do not meet the exhaustion requirement of 5 USC § 1214(a)(3), because they do not provide OSC with a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might lead to corrective action. See Ellison v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 7 F.3d 1031, 1036 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (an appellant must articulate to OSC "with reasonable clarity and precision" the basis for his request for corrective action under the WPA); Shively v. Department of the Army, 59 MSPR 531, 538-39 (1993) (finding that a document submitted on review in an IRA appeal was not material because the allegations contained therein were vague and imprecise, with the appellant describing his "involvement in a fraud, waste and abuse investigation").

The appellant may have had a reasonable belief that IMSD was being grossly mismanaged. His conclusory allegations in this regard, however, failed to rise to the level of specificity necessary to constitute a whistleblowing disclosure--or to provide a sufficient basis to warrant an investigation by OSC.

Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 325, 332 (1993), determined that "[b]ecause the statute requires that a complaint seeking corrective action from the Special Counsel state both the 'personnel action' and the 'disclosure of information' at issue, the Board may only review those disclosures of information and those personnel actions that an appellant raised before the Special Counsel." See Serrao v. MSPB, 95 F.3d 1569, 1577 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (OSC pleading requirements have not been met when an employee refers in his OSC complaint to an administrative grievance that in turn refers to the employee's initiation of an inspector general investigation); See Redschlag v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 409, 413-15 ¶¶ 8-11 (1998) (to support an IRA appeal, a complaint to OSC, based on EEO allegations, must allege that personnel action was imposed in retaliation for whistleblowing or cite 5 USC 2302(b)(8) as a basis for corrective action; it was insufficient to attach to the OSC complaint memoranda supporting an EEO complaint and grievance that could not reasonably lead OSC to conclude that the memos encompassed disclosures protected under § 2302(b)(8): "Because the appellant did not establish that he alleged reprisal for whistleblowing as a basis for his request for corrective action to OSC and did not otherwise indicate that his disclosures were made for a purpose distinct from his grievance and discrimination complaint, we find that the appellant failed to satisfy the exhaustion requirement because he did not 'give OSC a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might lead to corrective action' regarding the issue of reprisal for disclosures protected by 5 USC § 2302(b)(8)."); Ramos v. Dept. of Treasury, 72 MSPR 235, 240 (1996) (an IRA appellant must have "raised the precise whistleblower reprisal issue before the OSC"); cf.

Walleri v. Fed. Home Loan Bank of Seattle, 83 F.3d 1575, 1580 (9th Cir. 1996) (a strict standard of pleading was not applied in a case initiated in federal district court under federal banking laws prohibiting reprisal against individuals disclosing possible violations of law or regulation); Finston v. HCFA, 83 MSPR 100, 103-04 ¶ 9 (1999) (appellant's IRA allegations to OSC complained of a prohibited personnel practice but not of a whistleblowing reprisal).

In determining its jurisdiction over an IRA case, the Board "may not consider any subsequent recharacterization of those charges put forth by the appellant in her appeal to the Board." Tuten v. DOJ, 104 MSPR 271, 276 ¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 357 (2006) (citing Kalil v. USDA, 96 MSPR 77 ¶ 8 (2004)), aff'd, Tuten v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP No. 2007-3145) [2007 WL 2914787], supra; see Tucker v. DHHS, 73 MSPR 278, 283 (1997) ("To exhaust her remedy with OSC, an appellant must inform OSC of the precise ground of her charge of whistleblowing"; an IRA case cannot be established through recharacterization, during Board appeal, of statement made in OSC complaint); Johnson v. DOJ, 104 MSPR 624, 633 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 42 (2007) (no recharacterization OSC charges when the case goes to the Board). "The test of the sufficiency of an employee's charges of whistleblowing to OSC is the statement that he makes in the complaint requesting corrective action, not his post hoc characterization of those statements." McCollum v. VA, 75 MSPR 449, 459 (1997). See Langer v. Dept. of Treasury, 265 F.3d 1259, 1265 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (test is statement made in OSC complaint, not post hoc characterization). The Board noted in McCollum, id. at 459:

None of the correspondence between the appellant and the OSC contains a specific reference to a law, rule, or regulation that might have been violated or stated that the appellant believed that he disclosed a violation of law, rule, or regulation. Thus, even if the agency action described above could be characterized as a violation of law, rule, or regulation, the appellant has failed to establish Board jurisdiction over his IRA appeal on the basis that he disclosed a violation of law, rule, or regulation because he did not describe his alleged whistleblowing as such in his complaint to the OSC.

Exhaustion of the OSC remedy requires more than a general allegation of reprisal. The appellant should bring to the attention of OSC specific matters, e.g., the level of specificity should extend to a clear statement of the actions forming the basis of the complaint and the claim for relief. The Board will not undertake consideration of the claimed nonselection for promotion or denial of an award if those matters were not specifically raised with OSC. See Kochanoff v. Dept. of Treasury, 54 MSPR 517, 520 (1992); Orr v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 MSPR 117, 124 ¶ 14 (1999) ("We find that this information was precise enough to allow OSC to make a meaningful investigation into his allegations, and that therefore the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation that he exhausted his remedy with OSC.").

If the employee asserts reprisal based on her status as a perceived whistleblower, that assertion must be made to OSC, Mitskog v. MSPB, 680 Fed. Appx. 1005, 1007 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP):

...Mitskog also argues that the Board erred by failing to consider her status as a "perceived whistleblower" and that the nature of her disclosures--protected or not--are irrelevant under this doctrine. See Montgomery v. MSPB, 382 Fed. Appx. 942, 947 (Fed. Cir. 2010) ("The perceived whistleblower doctrine prevents a supervisor from taking retaliatory action against an employee, even if the employee's disclosure is later found unprotected, so long as the retaliation was taken in response to the disclosure."). But this theory was not mentioned in Mitskog's submission to OSC. The record of her OSC submission reflects only her statement to FEC officials that she had "federal whistleblower status." This unilateral assertion is insufficient to demonstrate that agency officials perceived her to be a whistleblower.

If the Board cannot determine from the record what allegations were made to OSC, the appeal will be remanded to the region for development of that information. See Shires v. DOD, 60 MSPR 13, 18 (1993).

Although the OSC complaint should be specific, an IRA case will not be dismissed because allegations in the appeal are more specific. As long as the complaint to OSC is made with sufficient specificity, and although the "appellant may not have presented these allegations in the most lucid and organized manner," an agency will not be heard to complain because the appellant's IRA appeal describes the allegations in more detail. Sood v. The Federal Circuit added in Briley v. NARA, 236 F.3d 1373, 1378 (Fed. We recognize that in her hearing before the administrative judge, Briley gave a more detailed account of her whistleblowing activities than she did in her letters to the OSC. However, Briley's letters to the OSC nevertheless contain the core of Briley's retaliation claim. They gave the OSC sufficient basis to pursue an investigation, and they therefore satisfy Briley's obligation to seek corrective action and exhaust her remedies before the OSC.

See Miller v. FDIC, 122 MSPR 3, 7, 9 ¶¶ 6, 10, 2014 MSPB 83 (2014) (aff'd, Miller v. MSPB, 626 Fed. Appx. 261 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP)) ("Although an appellant may add further detail to his claims before the Board, see Briley v. National Archives & Records Administration, 236 F.3d 1373, 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2001), the appellant must first make a reasonably clear and precise claim with OSC, see, e.g., Mintzmyer v. Department of the Interior, 84 F.3d 419, 422 (Fed. Cir. 1996)." The degree of specificity required by the Board in pleadings submitted by employees to OSC sparked a well-founded dissent by Chairman Erdreich in Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 355, 356-58 (1995):

...Mr. Brown has claimed whistleblower status based on his disclosed allegation that two agency supervisors were claiming overtime pay for themselves and others for work that was not actually being performed. In addition to establishing that a protected disclosure was made, the other elements of a whistleblower's claim to jurisdiction in an Individual Right of Action appeal are the identification of a personnel action allegedly based in reprisal, and evidence that the personnel action at issue was first raised to the Special Counsel for investigation. The administrative judge and the majority have found these two elements lacking in this case. I believe that finding is based on an overly strict interpretation of the evidence presented by Mr. Brown.

In Block 12 of Mr. Brown's appeal form dated April 30, 1994, he identifies the personnel action of which he is complaining as a "Forced Resignation/Constructive Removal." In Block 41.a) when asked if he had sought corrective action from the Special Counsel regarding the personnel action being appealed, Mr. Brown responded "Yes" and attached a copy of his right to appeal letter from the Office of Special Counsel (OSC). In his May 1, 1994 Declaration made in response to the administrative judge's order regarding establishing IRA jurisdiction, the appellant stated that once he received evidence that his "removal" was the result of retaliatory motives, he filed a complaint with OSC.

It is clear from Mr. Brown's choice of language in Block 12 that he sees his alleged coerced resignation to be a form of "removal." In his declaration in support of his claim to jurisdiction, the appellant specifically attests that he complained about his "removal" to OSC. Although it may be possible to interpret the appellant's declaration to mean that the "removal" he was complaining about did not include the constructive removal/coerced resignation that is the subject of the appeal, it is more likely that he is describing a chain of events composed of what to him is a series of improper agency actions including his removal.

The Whistleblower Protection Act is a remedial statute. As such it should be broadly construed "in favor of those whom it was intended to protect."[1] What I stated in my recent dissent in Costin v. HHS, 64 MSPR 517 (1994) holds equally true here:

The level of specificity that the majority here requires in a pro se complaint to OSC flies in the face of this principle. A whistleblower's right to due process should not be defeated by an overly strict application of the Board's jurisdictional requirements. The days of code pleading are gone. Mr. Brown's appeal form and declaration establish that he complained about whistleblower reprisal to OSC before filing with the Board. The agency has not challenged this aspect of his claim. His pleadings should be construed liberally as intended by the Act, and the administrative judge's conclusion that the appellant failed to establish jurisdiction should be reversed.

The Federal Circuit joined forces with the Board to impose on those seeking IRA remedies the burden of particularized pleading to OSC before they can invoke assistance from the Board, Ward v. MSPB, 981 F.2d 521, 526 (Fed. Cir. 1992):

The only issue the administrative judge decided with respect to travel was whether Dr. Kamely's authorization of Dr. DeFrank's trip to address a meeting in Portugal constituted gross waste of funds. The administrative judge refused to consider Dr. Ward's additional charge that "Dr. Kamely herself was taking excessive and unnecessary overseas trips," because under 5 USC 1214(a)(3), Dr. Ward's failure to raise that issue in his complaint to the Special Counsel precluded the Board from considering it and the charge "therefore cannot serve as a basis for Board jurisdiction over appellant's appeal." That section states that, with an exception here inapplicable, an employee "shall seek corrective action from the Special Counsel before seeking corrective action from the Board." 5 USC 1214(a)(3) (Supp. III 1991).

Dr. Ward, relying primarily on his certifications filed with the Board, contends that the administrative judge construed his complaint to the Special Counsel too narrowly, and should have viewed it as also challenging Dr. Kamely's own allegedly unnecessary travel. As we have indicated...however, Dr. Ward's subsequent certifications to the Board concerning what he stated in his complaint to the Special Counsel are not a proper basis for interpreting his complaint.

In any event, the administrative judge justifiably refused to consider an issue that Dr. Ward had not properly raised before the Special Counsel. See Knollenberg v. Department of Navy, 47 MSPR 92, 97 (1991) ("[S]ince the appellant failed to first raise the [whistleblowing] allegation to OSC which he now seeks to appeal to the Board, his appeal must be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction."), aff'd sub nom. Knollenberg v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 953 F.2d 623 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Shillinger v. Department of Labor, 47 MSPR 145, 151 (1991). The purpose of the requirement that an employee exhaust his or her remedies before the Special Counsel before appealing to the Board is to give the Special Counsel the opportunity to take corrective action before involving the Board in the case. The Whistleblower Protection Act provides that, if the Special Counsel determines that there is a substantial likelihood that the information he has received discloses a violation of the Act, he shall transmit the information to the head of the agency involved for investigation and report by the latter. 5 USC 1213(c) (Supp. III 1991). Such inquiries by the Special Counsel and transmittal to the agency for remedial action constitute a major portion of the work of the Special Counsel. A Report to Congress from the U.S. Office of Special Counsel Fiscal Year 1991, Office of Special Counsel, at 16-17.

For the exhaustion remedy to serve its intended purpose, however, the employee must inform the Special Counsel of the precise ground of his charge of whistleblowing. He must "give the Office of Special Counsel sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might have led to corrective action." Knollenberg, 953 F.2d at 626. An allegation of a specific alleged travel impropriety--here Dr. Kamely's authorization of Dr. DeFrank's trip to Portugal--was not sufficient notice to the Special Counsel that Dr. Ward also was challenging other travel related activities of Dr. Kamely.

Lloyd v. Lloyd also follows Ward by requiring appellant to inform OSC "of the precise charge of whistleblowing." To determine the nature of the appellant's allegations to OSC, the Board will consider the text of the appellant's complaint as well as how OSC construed the complaint. See Gaugh v. SSA, 87 MSPR 245, 248 ¶ 8 (2000).

Escandon was employed as a Housekeeping Aide, WG-2, in the Veterans Affairs Medical Center ("VAMC") located in Fresno, California, until he was removed on December 31, 1995 for absence without leave. Prior to his removal, Escandon sent a letter in November 1994 to the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs ("OWCP") in which he related six incidents, in addition to his own workplace injury, which he claimed resulted from the improper management actions of Fred Garcia, the laundry manager at the VAMC. He did not, however, appeal his removal. After his removal, Escandon repeatedly made disclosures regarding the hostile working environment allegedly created by Fred Garcia in the VAMC laundry to the Secretary of Veterans Affairs, the Department of Veterans Affairs Office of Inspector General, a federal grand jury, the Federal Bureau of Investigation and United States Senator Dianne Feinstein.

On December 5, 1997, almost two years after his removal, Escandon sought corrective action from the OSC, alleging his removal was in reprisal for whistleblowing activities. In his letter, he related five incidents, including the incident leading to his own injury, that, he said, typified the hostile work environment at the laundry at the VAMC. Of the five incidents related in his letter to OSC, only four were included in the letter Escandon sent, before his removal, to the OWCP. The incidents alleged in both the letter to the OWCP and the letter to the OSC were: 1) a laundry employee "died from the baiting, goading, taunting and harassment by the foreman"; 2) another employee "nearly had his arm ripped out" in an industrial accident, as he was hurrying to meet unreasonable production quotas set by the manager; 3) another employee was bullied, i.e., "grabbed from the back" by the manager and in her struggle to get free "fell violently and hurt her knees"; and 4) due to the unreasonable production goals, Escandon suffered a knee injury while hurriedly pushing a delivery cart. The OSC did not take corrective action and closed its files on June 23, 1998. Escandon then appealed to the Board.

On October 30, 1998, the CAJ ordered Escandon to show cause--by filing evidence and argument--to establish that his IRA appeal was within the Board's jurisdiction. The order specifically informed Escandon that he must show that he made a disclosure protected under the WPA and that he had exhausted his remedies with the OSC. In response, Escandon reiterated his allegations concerning the conduct of manager Garcia at the VAMC, made a new and non-specific reference to the alleged reuse of soiled, infected laundry bags and submitted the affidavit of Martin Garcia in support of his allegations of wrongdoing by manager Garcia.

The Board has jurisdiction over otherwise unreviewable personnel actions if a petitioner can demonstrate jurisdiction under the WPA by properly alleging that an adverse personnel action was taken against him in retaliation for activities that are protected under the WPA. See Willis v. Department of Agric., 141 F.3d 1139, 1142 (Fed. Cir. 1998). In order for such a personnel action to constitute retaliation for activities protected under the WPA, the personnel action must occur after the employee makes a protected "disclosure" under the WPA.

Before one may pursue an IRA appeal before the Board, the WPA requires that the employee first seek corrective relief from the OSC. See 5 USC § 1214(a)(3) (1994), We explained the rationale for this OSC "exhaustion" requirement in Ward v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 981 F.2d 521 (Fed. Cir. 1992):

The purpose of the requirement that an employee exhaust his or her remedies before the Special Counsel before appealing to the Board is to give the Special Counsel the opportunity to take corrective action before involving the Board in the case.... For the exhaustion remedy to serve its intended purpose, however, the employee must inform the Special Counsel of the precise ground of his charge of whistleblowing. He must give the Office of Special Counsel sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might have led to corrective action.

Id. at 526 (emphasis added and citations omitted).

The Board determined that only four disclosures made by Escandon to the OWCP satisfied this exhaustion requirement because he subsequently raised only them with the OSC. However, the Board held that these four disclosures did not qualify as protected disclosures under the WPA because they did not meet the criteria set forth in the relevant provisions of the WPA, 5 USC § 2302(b)(8) (1994).

To qualify a disclosure as protected, petitioner must show by a preponderance of evidence that the disclosure contains information which he reasonably believes evidences a violation of any law, rule, or regulation, or gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety. See 5 USC § 2302(b)(8) (emphasis added); Ellison v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 7 F.3d 1031, 1034 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Furthermore, an employee must articulate with reasonable clarity and precision to the OSC the basis for his request for corrective action under the WPA. See Ellison, 7 F.3d at 1037.

The Board found that the four 1994 OWCP disclosures did not meet the criteria for protected disclosures in that they were merely conclusory statements of opinion, rather than statements of fact, and that his OSC allegations were similarly lacking in specificity, or what the Ellison court termed, "reasonable clarity and precision." This finding is supported by substantial evidence. Clearly, none of Escandon's OSC assertions allege with reasonable clarity and precision the basis for his request for corrective action. Nor were his OWCP allegations specific enough to constitute protected disclosures. In both letters, he failed to describe the production quotas in question, explain how the quotas or the manager specifically caused injury, provide the names of the injured employees, describe where, when, or how the incidents occurred, or provide any other details that would normally be necessary to investigate such serious accusations. Even assuming the OWCP disclosures were protected, none of the like disclosures to the OSC provided sufficient basis for the OSC to pursue an investigation.

In the alternative, the Board determined that Escandon's allegation of assault, elsewhere described as "bullying" by manager Garcia, that allegedly resulted in injury to an employee's knees did not rise to the level of serious, government wrongdoing of the sort the WPA was intended to root out. Assuming the Board erred, we find the Board's error harmless.

While it is true that the WPA was enacted to encourage employees to report serious wrongdoing, not "arguably minor and inadvertent miscues occurring in the conscientious carrying out of one's assigned duties," Frederick v. Department of Justice, 73 F.3d 349, 353 (Fed. Cir. 1996), an allegation of assault by a government employee clearly can constitute more than a minor or inadvertent miscue and can indeed imply a serious violation of law. It depends on the detailed facts. Insofar as the Board determined that this allegation did not rise to a violation of law, the Board's determination might, depending on the facts which are not clear in the record on appeal, be contrary to law. See generally, Frederick v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 63 MSPR 563, 570 (1994) (allegation that an official permitted time and attendance abuse could possibly evidence a violation of law, i.e., fraud).

Nevertheless, because the Board found Escandon's "assault allegation" was not alleged with sufficient precision and clarity to qualify as a protected disclosure and this finding is supported by substantial evidence, the Board's alternative determination that this disclosure did not concern serious enough misconduct to be protected, if indeed error, is harmless.

To us, Escandon argues that his 1994 disclosures to OWCP, four of which were repeated to OSC in 1997, meet the criteria set forth in section 2302(b)(8) because he submitted an "eyewitness account" of the laundry manager's conduct, presumably through the 1998 affidavit of Martin Garcia, and through his correspondence with JCAHO, which he did not submit to the Board until March 9, 1999. Escandon's reliance on his correspondence with JCAHO and Martin Garcia's affidavit is misplaced. Escandon's 1997 OSC allegations did not provide sufficient basis for the OSC to conduct an investigation and neither the 1998 affidavit nor the 1999 correspondence provided to the Board can retroactively correct the deficiencies inherent in those earlier allegations to the OSC.

Finally, Escandon argues that he was entitled to a Board hearing, given the seriousness of his claim of whistleblowing disclosures and retaliation. However, the Board is not required to conduct a hearing where the IRA appeal does not allege facts which, if proven, would establish that a protected disclosure occurred and thus prove the Board has jurisdiction. See Ward, 981 F.2d at 525.

The Board has jurisdiction over an IRA appeal if the appellant has exhausted her administrative remedies before OSC and makes non-frivolous allegations that: (1) She engaged in whistleblowing activity by making a protected disclosure; and (2) the disclosure was a contributing factor in the agency's decision to take or fail to take a personnel action. Yunus v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 242 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Therefore, before filing with the Board an IRA appeal of an action that is not an "otherwise appealable action," the individual must first seek corrective action from OSC. 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3); 5 C.F.R. § 1209.2(b)(1). To satisfy the exhaustion requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3), an appellant must inform OSC of the precise ground of her charge of whistleblowing, giving OSC a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation which might lead to corrective action. Ward v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 981 F.2d 521, 526 (Fed. Cir. 1992). In the March 6, 2008 Order on Whistleblowing Allegations, the administrative judge notified the appellant of her burden to establish Board jurisdiction over her IRA appeal.

OSC has several powers and functions, including protecting employees, former employees, and applicants for employment from prohibited personnel practices in accordance with the provisions of 5 U.S.C. § 1214; receiving and investigating allegations of prohibited personnel practices, and "receiv[ing], review[ing], and where appropriate, forward[ing] to the Attorney General or an agency head under section 1213, disclosures of violations of any law, rule, or regulation, or gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety." 5 U.S.C. § 1212(a). A "prohibited personnel practice" is defined at 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b), and includes under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) taking or failing to take, or threatening to take or fail to take, a personnel action because of a disclosure of information of wrongdoing as described above. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(1).

Sections 1213 and 1214 of Title 5, United States Code, are respectively entitled, "Provisions relating to disclosures of violations of law, gross mismanagement, and certain other matters," and "Investigation of prohibited personnel practices; corrective action." Under 5 U.S.C. § 1213(b), whenever OSC receives a disclosure of information from an employee which the employee reasonably believes evidences a violation of any law, rule, or regulation, gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety, OSC shall review such information and determine whether there is a substantial likelihood that the information discloses such wrongdoing. If OSC makes a positive determination, it shall transmit the information to the appropriate agency head and generally require that the agency head conduct an investigation and submit a written report. 5 U.S.C. § 1213(c). Any such report shall include a description of any action taken or planned as a result of the investigation, such as restoration of any aggrieved employee. 5 U.S.C. § 1213(d). OSC shall transmit a copy of the report to the complainant, and the complainant may submit comments. 5 U.S.C. § 1213(e)(1). OSC shall then transmit the report, the complainant's comments, and any comments or recommendations by OSC, to the President and the congressional committees with jurisdiction over the agency which the disclosure involves. 5 U.S.C. § 1213(e)(3). If OSC does not transmit the information to the agency head, OSC shall inform the individual of the reasons why the disclosure may not be further acted upon and other offices available for receiving disclosures, should the individual wish to pursue the matter further. 5 U.S.C. § 1213(g)(3).

By contrast, OSC shall receive any allegation of a prohibited personnel practice and investigate the allegation to the extent necessary to determine whether there are reasonable grounds to believe that a prohibited personnel practice has occurred, exists, or is to be taken. 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(1)(A). An employee, like the appellant, may seek corrective action from the Board by filing an IRA appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 1221 if the employee "seeks corrective action for a prohibited personnel practice described in section 2302(b)(8)" from OSC; OSC notifies the employee that an investigation concerning such employee has been terminated; and either no more than 60 days have elapsed since notification was provided to such employee that the investigation was terminated, or 120 days after seeking corrective action from OSC the employee has not been notified by OSC that OSC shall seek corrective action on behalf of the employee. 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3).

Here, the administrative judge erred in dismissing this appeal as untimely filed. In dismissing the appeal as untimely filed, the administrative judge mistakenly relied on the Form OSC-12 that the appellant submitted to OSC to find that the appellant sought corrective action from OSC regarding the agency's alleged improper actions and thus exhausted her administrative remedies. Additionally, the administrative judge mistakenly relied on a January 24, 2005 letter received by the appellant as evidence that OSC terminated its investigation. However, the Form OSC-12, entitled "Disclosure of Information," does not constitute a request for corrective action and the January 24, 2005 letter is not correspondence from OSC terminating an investigation.

Although the Form OSC-12 includes a section for whistleblowers to provide details regarding their disclosures, it does not include a corresponding space for setting forth personnel actions, or otherwise solicit information relating to prohibited personnel practices. The appellant's narrative on the Form OSC-12 simply states that "[I]nformation is sensitive and cannot be disclosed on this page. I would like to request a meeting to discuss the violations." The appellant does not allege any prohibited personnel practices on the Form OSC-12. Additionally, the facsimile cover sheet prepared by the appellant for Form OSC-12 is addressed to "OSC/Disclosure Unit." Moreover, the January 24, 2005 letter described by the AJ as "a closure letter" from OSC was actually sent to the appellant from the U.S. Department of Justice, Civil Rights Division, Special Counsel for Immigration Related Unfair Employment Practices, and notified the appellant that the office did not have jurisdiction over her discrimination complaint against DOD, which was filed on December 28, 2004. The letter, therefore, did not constitute notification from OSC that an investigation had terminated.

We find that the appellant has not exhausted her administrative remedy because she did not allege whistleblower reprisal before OSC with respect to the claims she raises in this appeal, thereby giving OSC a sufficient basis to pursue an investigation that might have led to corrective action. See Langer v. Department of the Treasury, 265 F.3d 1259, 1268 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (the appellant failed to exhaust his OSC remedy with respect to a particular disclosure because he did not specify to OSC that the disclosure was a "WPA action," rather than simply a wrongful personnel action); Clemente v. Department of Homeland Security, 101 M.S.P.R. 519, ¶ 13 (2006); Finston v. Health Care Financing Administration, 83 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶¶ 7-10 (1999) (a complaint to OSC alleging a violation of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(4) did not constitute exhaustion of OSC proceedings regarding whistleblower allegations). OSC's regulations provide that, with an exception not applicable here, "Form OSC-11 ('Complaint of Possible Prohibited Personnel Practice or Other Prohibited Activity') must be used to file all such complaints." 5 C.F.R. § 1800.1(c)(1). Individuals who file disclosures of information, by contrast, are encouraged to use Form OSC-12. 5 C.F.R. § 1800.2(b)(1). There is no indication that the appellant ever filed an OSC Form-11 or otherwise sought corrective action from OSC for a prohibited personnel practice described in section 2302(b)(8). See 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3).

Because OSC did not, therefore, notify the appellant that an investigation concerning a prohibited personnel practice was terminated, the 60-day time limit set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3)(A)(ii) never started, and this appeal should not have been dismissed as untimely filed under that time limit. Similarly, because the appellant has not shown that her June 29, 2005 Form OSC-12 constituted a request for corrective action, 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3)(B), which permits the filing of an IRA appeal with the Board 120 days after seeking corrective action from OSC, does not apply in this case.
