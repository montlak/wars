b. Intentional Changes in Duties

A permissible reason for a RIF includes a reorganization resulting from abolition and reclassification of positions following changes in the duties assigned to those positions. Decker v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 119, 123 (1989) (reorganization initiated as a result of an OPM study criticizing overgraded positions). The Board examines an action to determine if the duties of a position were substantially changed and if, as a result, the agency was required to follow RIF procedures because the employee was released from his competitive level, Wade v. Dept. of Interior, 79 MSPR 686, 688-90 ¶¶ 5-11 (1998):

An appealable RIF demotion occurs when an employee is released from his competitive level due to abolition of his position during a reorganization and is assigned to a position carrying a lower grade or rate of pay. See 5 CFR §§ 210.102(b)(4), 351.901; Robinson v. U.S. Postal Service, 63 MSPR 307, 311-23 (1994). The Board's remand decision stated that the appellant's position could be considered to have been abolished if the oil and gas production, inspection, and enforcement field responsibilities added to the position "fundamentally altered [his] duties." 73 MSPR at 299. As detailed below, we conclude that the appellant has made non-frivolous allegations, supported by record evidence, that the agency abolished his position in a reorganization and assigned him to a lower-graded position. He is therefore entitled to a jurisdictional hearing.

A February 12, 1996 memorandum from the Director of Land Management entitled "Classification of Positions Associated with Oil and Gas Inspection" stated that certain positions had not been classified consistently throughout the agency, and set out "position management and classification guidance...for...immediate action." The memorandum directed that oil and gas inspection positions involving "field work" be classified as GS-10 Petroleum Engineering Technicians, and that positions involved exclusively in production accountability functions ("reports evaluation, reports preparation, [and] record review") be classified as GS-7 Statistical Assistants.

The agency contended on remand that pursuant to this directive it "reclassified" the appellant's GS-11 position to the GS-7 level, and simultaneously promoted him to a GS-10 position with the requisite field work. The agency's contention is not evidence upon which we can rely, however, because it is the statement of the agency's representative in a pleading. See Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 MSPR 163, 168 (1995). Moreover, the agency's contention is not consistent with the official documentation of the action it took. That documentation shows that the agency reassigned the appellant directly from a GS-1801-11 Production Accountability Specialist position to a GS-802-10 Petroleum Engineering Technician position.

The District Manager of the agency's Farmington, New Mexico District Office, in a May 22, 1996 memorandum, described the action that resulted in the reassignment as a "conversion" of the appellant's GS-1801-11 position to a GS-802-10 position. This conversion was accomplished by "amend[ing]" the GS-1801-11 position description, to add the field work requirement. The agency's official documentation of the amendment to the appellant's position description states that the change was brought about by a "[r]ealignment due to FOS [Field Office Staff] reorganization." Further, according to the agency, employees whose positions were converted were entitled to grade retention.

The "conversion" of the appellant's GS-11 position was not a reclassification; rather, as noted in the February 12 memorandum discussed above, the GS-10 position was a different position that had field work as part of its duties. The addition of the field work accounted for the agency's decision to give the Petroleum Engineering Technician position a higher grade than the Statistical Assistant position, which only had office-based production accountability duties associated with it. See 5 CFR § 511.101(e) (defining "position" for purposes of reclassification actions as "the work, consisting of the duties and responsibilities, assigned...for performance by an employee").

An appellant is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing if he makes specific allegations of fact that, if proven, could establish a prima facie showing of Board jurisdiction. See Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994). Here, the appellant has made non-frivolous allegations that the agency conducted a reorganization of the Farmington District Office, that it abolished his GS-1801-11 position, and that it assigned him to a newly-created GS-802-10 position either before the purported "reclassification" of his GS-11 position or after he had been given retained grade as a GS-11. These allegations would support a finding that the agency released the appellant from his competitive level and demoted him by RIF, without invoking RIF procedures, and without affording him his substantive rights under the RIF rules. See McClure v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 32 MSPR 672, 674-76 (1987) (an appellant who had retained grade at the GS-13 level based on a prior reclassification of his position to a GS-12 was effectively released from his GS-13 competitive level and should have had assignment rights based on the GS-13 grade; the agency's action was reversed because it did not afford the appellant his substantive RIF rights when it placed him in a GS-12 position). The appellant has veterans preference, and he claims that he would have had assignment rights to a position at the GS-11 level if the RIF rules had been followed, see 5 CFR § 351.701.

Based on the foregoing, the administrative judge's decision to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, without a hearing, is VACATED. We find no error in the administrative judge's determination that the appellant was not affected by an appealable adverse action, but, as stated above, the appellant has made non-frivolous allegations that he was affected by a RIF demotion appealable to the Board.

Under earlier regulations, if a demotion taken without RIF procedures resulted from a reclassification based upon a change of duties, the demotion would be canceled. Wrenn v. Smithsonian Inst., 21 MSPR 33, 35-36 (1984); see Sheifer v. Dept. of Labor, 39 MSPR 34, 37-38 (1988) (explaining operation of prior regulations relating reclassifications and RIF rights; remanding for the AJ to determine whether changes in the appellant's duties were caused by delayed recognition of the effects of reorganization or the correction of a previous classification error). Under earlier regulations, the Board examined a reclassification resulting from change of duties to ensure that it was not a sham. See Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 278, 279-81, 4 MSPB 350 (1980).
