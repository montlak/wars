a. Not All Charges Sustained

The Board expanded on Lambert in Brunning v. GSA, 63 MSPR 490, 494 (1994). The agency charged the appellant with sexual harassment, improper supervisory conduct, and authorization of the use of a government vehicle for unofficial purposes. The AJ sustained some but not all of the charges. Some of the most serious charges were not sustained. The appellant was demoted to a vacant nonsupervisory position with the least reduction in grade and pay. The AJ then denied counsel fees. Lambert did not control because not all the charges were sustained. The Board disagreed and sent the case back for a fee award. The Board determined that the ultimate question is whether the agency should have known that the penalty selection--part of the "merits" of a decision--would not likely be sustained. The Board explained that Lambert created a presumption of fee entitlement when a penalty is mitigated but all charges are sustained, while Lambert did not create a presumption against fees when a penalty is mitigated but not all charges are sustained:

We reverse the initial decision, however, because the administrative judge interpreted the holding in Lambert too narrowly. As noted above, while that case sets forth a general presumption of entitlement to fees in cases where all the charges are sustained but the penalty is mitigated, it also asserts more broadly that penalty selection is part of the "merits" of a decision under the "knew or should have known" attorney fee category. See 34 MSPR at 507. The holding of Lambert thus is not that fees are warranted when all charges are sustained and the penalty is mitigated, and that they are not warranted when only some of the charges are sustained and the penalty is mitigated. Such a reading of Lambert would produce an anomalous result in which many appellants would be better off if all, rather than some, of the charges against them were sustained. The lesson drawn from Lambert is simply that the "merits" of a decision include the selection of penalty for purposes of the "knew or should have known" attorney fee category. We therefore cannot agree with the administrative judge's more restrictive reading.

One of the most serious charges against the appellant was not sustained, as well as "a substantial number" of the specifications underlying the sustained charges. The administrative judge thus concluded that the penalty was mitigated in large part because the proven misconduct of the appellant was not as severe as that on which the agency relied in arriving at the penalty of removal. He also noted that, in making its penalty selection, the agency had considered allegations of misconduct by the appellant that were not included in the proposal notice. As he correctly observed, the Board has held that consideration of such allegations is improper, see Hernandez v. Department of Education, 42 MSPR 61, 71 (1989), and the agency knew or should have known that it could not consider them. While he also stated that the penalty was not mitigated "solely or primarily" for this reason, the initial decision shows that mitigation of the penalty was based at least in part on this factor.

Brunning was followed, along with other decisions suggesting that a fee is appropriate when a removal is mitigated because the agency did not sustain a serious charge and the agency had the dispositive evidence before it when the action was taken, in Miller v. Dept. of Army, 106 MSPR 547, 550-52 ¶¶ 9-13, 2007 MSPB 211 (2007), although the fee was limited to the time spent on the penalty phase of the case--a difficult calculus, to be sure, for the appellant's counsel, opposing counsel, the AJ, and the Board (until fee parsing as to the penalty phase was overruled in Driscoll v. USPS, 116 MSPR 662, 2011 MSPB 80 (2011), subsequently discussed):

The appellant also argues that the AJ erroneously applied the fifth Allen category to find that attorney fees were not warranted in the interest of justice. Attorney fees are warranted under the fifth Allen category when the Board sustains the charge in an adverse action appeal, but mitigates the penalty based on evidence before, or readily available to, the agency at the time it took the action. Del Prete, 104 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 7; see Brunning v. General Services Administration, 63 M.S.P.R. 490, 494 (1994) (the fifth Allen category also applies when only some of the charges are sustained and the penalty is mitigated). Penalty mitigation alone, however, does not create a presumption in favor of satisfaction of any of the Allen factors. Dunn v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 F.3d 1308, 1313 (Fed. Cir. 1996); Del Prete, 104 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 7.

In the ID, the AJ sustained the agency's charge of conduct unbecoming a federal employee based on the appellant's extramarital affair with a coworker in violation of state law. The AJ found further, however, that the agency did not show that the appellant deliberately misrepresented the facts to the Henry County Police Department when she reported that her coworker had raped her. The AJ thus did not sustain the charge of making false and malicious statements about a coworker. A serious charge against the appellant therefore was not sustained. Taking that fact into consideration, as well as the following mitigating factors, the AJ mitigated the penalty from a removal to a 60-day suspension: The appellant's length of service was substantial; she had a discipline-free record; she was a low-level employee in an entry-level developmental position whose duties were primarily administrative; her coworker initiated the relationship and was primarily responsible for creating the situation; and the agency's table of penalties provides for a range of penalties from a 1-day suspension to removal for a first offense of conduct unbecoming for disgraceful conduct.

All of the evidence relied on by the AJ in mitigating the penalty was before the agency when it took the removal action. [S] ee Del Prete, 104 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 11 (fees are warranted in the interest of justice under Allen category 5 where the AJ's decision to mitigate was based on evidence that was before the agency when it took the removal action); Lambert v. Department of the Air Force, 34 M.S.P.R. 501, 507 (1987) (fees are warranted in the interest of justice under Allen category 5 where the Board's decision to mitigate is based on information or evidence that was presented to the agency at the time it made its decision to remove the appellant). A serious charge against the appellant was not sustained. See Brunning, 63 M.S.P.R. at 494 (finding fees were warranted under the fifth Allen category where the AJ's decision to mitigate was based in large part on the agency's failure to prove one of the most serious charges). No new significant information was introduced at the hearing that was unavailable to the agency before it removed the appellant. See Del Prete, 104 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 11 (fees warranted under the fifth Allen category where no new information was introduced at the hearing that was unavailable to the agency before it removed the appellant). The appellant nonetheless did not allege nor show that the agency improperly relied on inappropriate factors or information when it decided to remove her. Cf. Brunning, 63 M.S.P.R. at 494-95 (attorney fees warranted under Allen category 5 where the agency relied on allegations outside of the proposal notice). Under all of these circumstances, we find that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail in its selection of the penalty. Attorney fees therefore are warranted under the fifth Allen category. See Del Prete, 104 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶¶ 10-11; Brunning, 63 M.S.P.R. at 494; Lambert, 34 M.S.P.R. at 507.

Where, as here, an award of attorney fees is warranted in the "interest of justice" for the change in penalty, the award of fees is limited to legal fees attributed to the change in penalty, and does not include fees and expenses unrelated to the penalty issue. See Del Prete, 104 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 15. The appellant failed to prevail on her challenge to the merits of the agency's charge of conduct unbecoming a federal employee. We therefore find that the appellant's attorney fees award should be limited to the fees and expenses attributed only to the penalty issue and the merits of the charge of making false statements, and that the fees attributed to the appellant's unsuccessful challenge to the merits of the "conduct unbecoming" charge are not compensable. Id. ¶ 16. Based on the existing record, we cannot identify precisely the hours associated with the appellant's unsuccessful challenge to the merits of the "conduct unbecoming" charge. Further, if the reduction must be made on a basis other than by identifying specific hours, the AJ is better able to make such a determination because he is more intimately familiar with the adjudication of this appeal. Id. ¶ 17.
