b. Malicious Initiation of Action

The Board has not dealt extensively with how it should handle adverse actions that may have some foundation but would not have been initiated without the personal animus of a coworker or subordinate of the appellant. In Seavello v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 155, 158, 4 MSPB 239 (1980), charges based on conduct that occurred two years earlier were brought to the agency's attention in an untimely fashion by an employee who knew of the conduct all along, but chose to alert the agency to it for personal reasons. The charges were dismissed because there were serious issues of credibility and because the Board considered the case against the appellant "irrevocably tainted by personal animus." Yet there was no harmful error when an employee improperly reported the misconduct of an appellant, as long as the employer did not order or condone the employee's actions and as long as the employer lacked sufficient knowledge of the employee's action to taint the agency investigation. Bond v. Dept. of Commerce, 22 MSPR 324, 325 n.* (1984) (Board did not clearly identify the improper conduct by the employee who reported the misconduct); see Aliota v. VA, 60 MSPR 491, 497 (1994) (personal animus of the supervisor toward the appellant did not taint the opinions of the proposing or deciding officials); cf. Higgins v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 167, 170 (1982) (charges of insubordination and AWOL supported and warranted removal despite the purported animosity for the appellant by his supervisor). But see Burkett v. United States, 402 F.2d 1002, 1007 (Ct. Cl. 1968) (criticizing an agency for allowing the proposing official to be the supervisor who was allegedly maligned by the employee's false statements against that supervisor described in the letter of charges: "In a case involving the writer's personal interests so directly and strongly, this was an insensitive practice which could better have been avoided.... Perhaps if another official had had the responsibility of deciding whether to proceed formally against plaintiff, and of framing the notice of charges, this matter would have taken a different course.").
