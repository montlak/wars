2. Interaction With Judge

The first in-person meeting with the AJ is usually the hearing, although there will be earlier telephone conferences with the AJ. Those conferences will include, at a minimum, a prehearing conference, and the conferences will likely also include status conferences or conferences concerning motions filed before the prehearing submissions. AJs are customarily addressed by "judge," or "your Honor." Although the hearing officers are not administrative law judges, they were given the title of "judge" by the past acting Chairman of the Board, and the title of administrative judge was codified to statute in 2014 when Congress modified Board appellate procedures for appeals of VA SES members. AJs are civil servants exercising delegated authority as excepted service attorney-examiners. They have the same hopes and fears as other civil servants. They are furloughed when congress fails to pass an appropriations bill. They have a union to protect themselves against their merit-minded employer. They are of diverse temperaments, experience, and education. They have varying feelings about the work they do. Some are friendly, others aloof; some are pompous and officious, others gracious. Some assist appellants in the development of their cases; others act as though they are agency counsel. As excepted service appointees with delegated authority, they must respect the directives of Board members and higher-level officials. They lack the power to depart substantially from Board law and procedure. Their factual findings and legal conclusions can be modified by the Board. Their decisions, not of a precedential nature, are rarely seen by the outside world. The Board does not even do them the honor of mentioning their names (as appellate opinions in federal court do of trial court judges) in decisions on petitions for review. Board AJs have no power to jail people for contempt or to impose monetary fines upon parties. However, they can and do inflict sanctions and issue protective orders, and they can and will bar from hearings attorneys or other representatives who do not respect their roles as advocates and members of the bar.

AJs, who are excepted service attorney-examiners, usually at the GS-14 or GS-15 levels, are under performance standards and those standards emphasize high production, settlements, and prompt, comprehensive, properly formatted, and lucid decisions. Although standards may vary from year to year and place to place, a critical element of "production," an AJ's "fully satisfactory" performance reflected production of 85-100 decisions annually; the "exceeds" level required production of 101 to 120 decisions per year. For the critical element of "timeliness," a "full satisfactory" performance called for 98% of all decisions to be issued within relevant time limits; the "exceeds" level called for performance to reflect all decisions within relevant time limits and an average processing time of 70-75 days. For appeals that are settled, more cases take their place, increasing production rates. Appeals not settled are moved along quickly to keep down average processing time.

Because there are so many (unfounded) allegations of bias in Board cases, AJs may seem isolated and they are not likely to show more than routine courtesy to parties. But advocates are not the adversaries of AJs. There is no reason why the working relationship with an AJ cannot and should not be cordial. During a career, these civil servants hear and decide thousands of cases. Theirs is a production job, sometimes complex, often tedious, with few thanks other than occasional grade advancement and, for some, occasional overseas travel. If the AJ is not viewed as an antagonist, and if counsel treat each other and opposing parties with respect, then the hearing can be an interesting, challenging experience. AJs can improve their public image by projecting some warmth toward the parties and witnesses and by working with representatives to set hearing dates rather than unilaterally imposing them, by taking telephone calls rather than shunting people into voicemail, and by promptly convening telephonic conference calls in an effort to sort out problems that are brought to the fore through motions. Effective use of prehearing conferences, more frequent status conferences, and more intensive and effective settlement efforts, particularly settlement conferences involving personal meetings rather than telephone exchanges, would be helpful.
