C. FAILURE TO COOPERATE IN DISCOVERY

As the appeal moves toward hearing, matters can become complex. Discovery motions and orders proliferate. By intent, ignorance, or neglect, these orders and motions may go without reply or compliance. Motions for sanctions follow.

The discovery process is hampered by the unrealistic time limitations for the initiation and conclusion of the process, the lack of experience of some AJs and representatives (agency, appellant, and union) with discovery procedures followed in federal court and the tendency of some appellants and agencies to use discovery requests wastefully, provoking either no response or an inadequate response. An example of abuse of the discovery process is a set of agency interrogatories asking for the appellant's social security number, address, and job title--all information the agency has in the appellant's Official Personnel File. There are disputes over discovery and attempts to resolve those disputes through imposition of sanctions.

5 CFR 1201.43(a) (2022) outlines the sanctions available to the AJ who punish failure to comply with discovery orders. Under the regulation, the AJ may:

(1) Draw an inference in favor of the requesting party with regard to the information sought;

(2) Prohibit the party failing to comply with the order from introducing evidence concerning the information sought, or from otherwise relying upon testimony related to that information;

(3) Permit the requesting party to introduce secondary evidence concerning the information sought; and

(4) Eliminate from consideration any appropriate part of the pleadings or other submissions of the party that fails to comply with the order.

Sanctions available for obstruction of the discovery process are also explained in the Board's Hearings Procedures Guide. Although the Hearings Procedures Guide has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook, it is still used for training and orientation purposes. The Guide states, at 32:

4. Sanctions

a. Willful Withholding of Evidence

The entire Board process is designed to provide the parties with full disclosure of the available evidence. Thus, a willful withholding of evidence would be contrary to this process. Evidence of such willful withholding should be met with the imposition of sanctions. See Shefield v. OPM, MSPB Docket No. AT0754870543 (Jan. 30, 1989).

b. Unreasonable Denial of Request for Admissions

Requests for admissions are designed to narrow the scope of the appeal and hearing.

A party required to answer such requests should be informed of the necessity for an unequivocal answer and that the failure to provide such an answer might result in an adverse inference being drawn.

The answerer should also be informed that failure to respond may result in the imposition of sanctions.

c. Refusal to Furnish Medical Report

If a party is ordered to produce documents within its control, including medical reports, and the party refuses to provide the documents, sanctions should be imposed.

If the agency is dissatisfied with the extent of the responses to interrogatories, its first remedy is not imposition of sanctions but a motion to compel proper responses. Predicating the imposition of sanctions is not the failure to cooperate in discovery, but the failure to obey a Board order compelling discovery. Ibrahim v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 531, 534 (1986), determined that the AJ had no authority to impose sanctions (barring appellant's testimony in support of affirmative defenses) without an order requiring the appellant to answer the interrogatories. Accord Taylor v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 342, 344 (1987) (no order, no sanctions). If proper discovery or subpoena procedures are not followed, the AJ will not be faulted for imposing sanctions against a party whose witness is desired for testimony. See Head v. OPM, 53 MSPR 421, 422 (1992) (AJ properly declined to impose sanctions based on an allegation that the appellant's requests to OPM for documents were ignored and that the AJ erred in not investigating that allegation; the record did not indicate that the appellant initiated a discovery request or a motion to compel discovery); Alhouse v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 181, 182 (1984) (denial of sanctions was appropriate when the appellant did not request the AJ to order document production until the hearing); Robinson v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 264, 265-66 (1983) (appellants failed to follow the proper procedures for the deposition of a witness or for his subpoena as a witness).

A proper foundation for sanctions includes a motion for an order to compel discovery and, assuming noncompliance with the order, a motion for sanctions. For some reason not readily apparent, the Board seems to desire that the motions be supported by affidavits, although documentary evidence on discovery disputes should suffice. See Van Amber v. USPS, 47 MSPR 320, 327 (1991) (noting that the appellant failed to submit with the motion the agency's partial answer to his interrogatories; denying sanctions for the failure to respond to interrogatories); 5 CFR 1201.73(c)(1)(ii) (2022) (suggesting that the motion be accompanied by the response to the discovery request or, as appropriate, by a sworn statement that no response has been received). Counsel can take care of the problem by narrating the sequence of discovery steps in a letter and certifying that the information in the letter is true in accordance with 28 USC 1746. If a motion for sanctions is submitted through e-filing, the system inquires if information is being provided on the basis of personal knowledge and under penalties of perjury. The information provided includes a statement that a response was not received to an attached discovery request. [Refer in this chapter to "Motion to Compel."]

Assuming a proper foundation for their imposition, sanctions for failure to follow Board discovery orders are a matter for the AJ's sound discretion. Bayne v. Dept. of Energy, 34 MSPR 439, 443 (1987); Carr v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 114, 117 (1983) ("Absent a showing that such discretion has been abused, the presiding official's determination will not constitute reversible error."). Should the ends of justice require it, and without a showing that discretion has been abused, the AJ's determination will not constitute reversible error. In determining whether the imposition of sanctions is warranted, the Board considers a party's diligence, or lack of it, and a party's good faith effort to comply with the AJ's order. Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 72, 77 (1983) (discovery dispute). If sanctions are imposed, the Board will assess the propriety of the sanction, but the Board will also seek to determine whether the sanctioned party suffered real prejudice as a result of the AJ's order. See Gores v. VA, 68 MSPR 100, 110-11 (1995). The Board's regulations do not state when sanctions will be imposed. Eaks v. DOJ, 18 MSPR 328, 335 (1983), found no fault with an AJ's determination to issue sanctions as part of his initial decision.

Miles v. VA, 84 MSPR 418, 421 ¶¶ 9-10 (1999), suggested that the sanction imposed should be tailored to the specific abuse of the discovery process. The AJ improperly precluded the appellant from presenting witnesses or evidence in support of her affirmative defenses when the interrogatories that the appellant refused to answer were unrelated to the affirmative defenses. Miles also suggested that the AJ treat the pro se appellant reasonably during the discovery process. When the appellant objected to producing information that she thought was protected under the Privacy Act, the AJ should have explained that the statute was inapplicable and given the appellant a chance to comply with the discovery requests before imposing sanctions. Additionally, the AJ should not have sanctioned the appellant for delivering discovery submissions to the agency eight minutes after the deadline set by the AJ: "Certainly, such an inconsequential delay should not result in sanctions." Wagner v. DHS, 105 MSPR 67, 70-73 ¶¶ 9-15, 2007 MSPB 58 (2007), post, also emphasized the need to tailor the sanction to the significance of the violation of a discovery order and to avoid dismissing an appeal when any less severe sanction would be appropriate.

Sanctions are available in proceedings where discovery is permitted. Sanctions were imposed against an appellant who was determined not to have complied with agency discovery requests in a compliance proceeding. Yeressian v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-09-0049-C-2 (NP 1/28/2013).
