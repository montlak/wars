5. Format of Motion

At a minimum, 5 CFR 1201.203(a) (2022), requires of counsel:

§ 1201.203 Proceedings for attorney fees.

(a) Form and content of request. A request for attorney fees must be made by motion, must state why the appellant or respondent believes he or she is entitled to an award under the applicable statutory standard, and must be supported by evidence substantiating the amount of the request. Evidence supporting a motion for attorney fees must include at a minimum:

(1) Accurate and current time records;

(2) A copy of the terms of the fee agreement (if any);

(3) A statement of the attorney's customary billing rate for similar work, with evidence that that rate is consistent with the prevailing community rate for similar services in the community in which the attorney ordinarily practices; and

(4) An established attorney-client relationship.

The information required by the Board's regulations is consistent with the premise that the existence of a retainer and the amount of the fee do not come within the privilege of the attorney-client relationship. See Gerlach v. FTC, 20 MSPR 60, 64 n.7 (1984).

The appellant, rather than her counsel, may submit her own fee petition, but it must be complete. See Guerrero v. VA, 2008 MSPB 100 ¶¶ 11-13 (2008) (remanded for development of information relating to fees when the appellant submitted a copy of a check written to counsel, itemized invoices received from counsel, and described the legal service provided and the time spent by counsel on the appeal; the submission lacked information on the reasonableness of the fees, including the fee agreement or evidence of counsel's customary billing rate for similar work, and information concerning counsel's qualifications and relevant experience; AJ should not have inferred that a particular hourly rate was reasonable).

Allen v. USPS, 2 MSPR 420, 428, 2 MSPB 582 (1980), states that the burden rests on the appellant to demonstrate entitlement to counsel fees. Counsel fees should be requested by motion supported by a memorandum demonstrating that the statutory requirements for an award have been met, substantiated by affidavits or other documentary evidence if there is a question of fact to be resolved other than a factual issue already decided on the merits. Affidavits are an appropriate means of establishing reasonableness of fees claimed. As long as the significant information is included, it appears as though an appellant may provide the affidavit concerning counsel fees and the justification for fees; normally, however, counsel will provide that information. See Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., 32 MSPR 497, 501 (1987). The agency should set out in its response all considerations relied on in opposition to an award, including affidavits concerning contested facts pertinent to the motion (other than issues already adjudicated on the merits) or concerning reasonableness of the amount claimed.

Counsel ought to reasonably develop a position on the basis for fee entitlement. The problem of overlapping fee entitlement standards was earlier discussed. It is suggested that counsel decide the Allen criteria that will be relied upon, list them separately, and in each category provide appropriate citation to the leading decision defining that category. Then give a synopsis of the evidence or decisions sufficient to demonstrate that the circumstances of the appeal are covered by the case law controlling the category. The fee motion should provide a specific basis for the fees claimed. The Board need not wade through a lengthy record to determine if the evidence demonstrates any basis for granting fees. Bruning v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3125). The Federal Circuit advised in Sebock v. OPM, 874 F.2d 801 (Fed. Cir. 1989), that the Board need not make the appellant's case for counsel fees. The majority and concurring opinions are quoted here, for they provide a fair indication of what is expected of counsel.

First, from the majority opinion in Sebock, written by Judge Bissell, 874 F.2d at 803:

Although Sebock argues on appeal that because the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail, Sebock did not present evidence or argument concerning the interest of justice issue anywhere in the 51-page petition. The entire petition, instead, was directed to justifying Sebock's attorney's hourly rate. This court will generally not consider arguments made for the first time on appeal....

Sebock failed to raise with specificity the agency actions meeting the criteria of the Allen categories or to direct the Board's attention to any evidence supporting an award of attorney fees. The Board is not required to wade through the entire record to determine if the evidence demonstrates any basis for concluding that fees are warranted in the interest of justice. Accordingly, the Board's denial of attorney fees is affirmed.

Then, from the concurring opinion of Senior Judge Baldwin, 874 F.2d at 803-04:

As the majority notes, and the administrative judge stated, Sebock presented "no evidence or argument concerning the interest of justice issue."...

[The] statutory requirement is not predicated on the prevailing party's presentation of additional evidence or argument. Rather, the reviewing authority is obliged to consider the record before it, and determine whether an award of fees is in the interest of justice. It does not require any great stretch of the imagination to come up with a scenario where the agency's action is so clearly without merit that the prevailing party will be entitled to attorney fees based on a cursory review of the decisions and record available. I see no value in requiring the presentation of additional evidence or argument where a party determines that is not necessary. Similarly, one can easily envision circumstances where a review of the record will reveal that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail.

At the same time, if a party decides to rest on the record in his request for attorney fees, additional arguments, such as those raised by Sebock here, should not be heard at the appellate level.... Clearly, if a party decides to rely solely on the record below, it cannot be heard on new arguments when that reliance proves ill-advised.

Nor do I agree that the language in Allen, quoted by the majority, requires the test enunciated by the majority. The Board's instructions to litigants should not be read, or adopted, as a warning to litigants who fail to present additional argument or evidence that their fee petitions will automatically be denied. Instead, the Board's statement should be recognized for what it was, an instruction to litigants that any arguments or evidence beyond the record already developed which they desire the Board or the administrative judge to consider must be presented with the fee petition. The Board's parenthetical request that petitions not include evidence or affidavits concerning questions of fact already litigated indicates its intent to include in its determination the entire record already developed.

Of course, it is always beneficial for a litigant to provide the deciding body or official with a roadmap of the evidence and arguments upon which it relies for its assertions. However, I cannot agree with the majority's conclusion that without such a roadmap the Board will wade aimlessly through the record, unable to conclude whether or not an award of attorney fees is in the interest of justice.

The administrative judge apparently recognized her responsibility to consider the record presented. She stated clearly that Sebock had presented no evidence or argument on the interest of justice issue, yet she reviewed the record thoroughly and determined that the agency's position was not clearly without merit and that Sebock failed to prove that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail.

By its action today the majority suggest that those portions of the administrative judge's decision are dicta. In reality, those portions constitute the administrative judge's fulfillment of her statutory requirement to make the necessary determinations before denying Sebock's fee request. Although I agree that Sebock's reliance on the record to establish that an award of attorney fees is in the interest of justice was unavailing here, I cannot join the majority's decision to establish a bright-line test to cover cases not presented, and to deprive litigants of their right to receive attorney fees where the record alone amply supports such an award.

In practice, the AJ is given the discretion as to how much specificity to require in a fee petition. In Levenson v. DLA, 49 MSPR 659, 663 (1991), the appellant's fee petition stated:

The payment of attorney's fees is warranted in the interest of justice as shown by the Initial Decision. In summary, the Initial Decision illustrates that the Appellant had a history of difficulty in getting approved his requests for military leave. Less than six months before the Appellant's termination, he had filed a grievance which was upheld by the Agency grievance authority. Nevertheless, the Appellant's supervisor basically ignored the grievance decision and refused the Appellant's request for military leave in direct contravention to the applicable statute. The agency has stipulated that the Appellant acted in good faith.

The Board listed the appellant's points in Levenson, 49 MSPR at 663:

[T]he Board received the agency's response in opposition to the appellant's motion. The appellant replied to the agency's response.... [H]e specifically argued that an award of attorney fees was warranted in the interest of justice because "the agency was not objective," "the Agency had it in for the Appellant," "the Appellant was substantially innocent of the charges brought by the Agency," and "the Agency knew that it would not prevail on the merits."...

The argument was sufficient for an award of fees, 49 MSPR at 664:

...Although the administrative judge "is not required to wade though the entire record" to determine if a fee award is warranted...we find that his discretion to consider the arguments was within his discretion.... Even though the applicable Allen criteria were not specifically identified in the appellant's initial motion for fees, or in the 15-day time period afforded the appellant to amend or supplement his motion, the appellant did "direct the Board's attention to...evidence supporting an award of fees" in his first submission. See Sebock, 874 F.2d at 803. Moreover, his final submission was timely filed before the close of the record.

Stated more directly, the AJ has "substantial discretion with respect to ruling on motions" and the AJ may "look beyond" the parties' pleadings to grant a fee motion on a basis not specified by the appellant, if the basis for granting fees is apparent without extensive review of the evidence. Madera v. OPM, 53 MSPR 310, 315 (1992), followed in Uhlig v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 660, 670 ¶ 29 (2000). Thomas v. USPS, 77 MSPR 502, 506 (1998), added:

We concur with the AJ that the Board is not required to wade through an entire record to determine if the evidence demonstrates any basis for concluding that fees are warranted in the interest of justice, where an appellant has failed to raise with specificity one or more of the interest-of-justice categories cited in Allen, or to direct the Board's attention to any evidence supporting an award of attorney fees. See, e.g., Sebock v. Office of Personnel Management, 874 F.2d 801, 803 (Fed. Cir. 1989); AID at 5. Nonetheless, we find that a basis for an attorney fees award is apparent here without an extensive review of the voluminous record in these appeals. See Pacheco v. Office of Personnel Management, 68 MSPR 129, 133 (1995); Babakitis v. Office of Personnel Management, 60 MSPR 35, 36-37 (1993).
