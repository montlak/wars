2. Timeliness and Jurisdictional Issues

In 1991 the Board decided to avoid jurisdictional analysis by disposing of cases through timeliness determinations when possible, Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193, 196-98 (1991):

In Funk v. Dept. of the Army, 44 MSPR 320, 322 (1990)...[w]e held that an administrative judge must decide issues of Board jurisdiction before dismissing an appeal on procedural or timeliness grounds....

We hold here that, while the existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, a jurisdictional determination would not be in the Board's interest of adjudicatory efficiency and fairness to the parties involved in an untimely appeal in which (a) the Board arguably may have jurisdiction over the appeal and (b) the record developed on the timeliness issue shows no good cause for the untimely filing, or (c) other grounds exist for the dismissal of the appeal. Thus, if the record is sufficiently developed on the issue of timeliness or other grounds to show that an appeal should be dismissed on other than a jurisdictional basis, then an administrative judge may properly determine, by assuming arguendo the appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction (without actually making fact findings and conclusions of law on the jurisdictional issue), that dismissal of the appeal is warranted. In all other circumstances[5], however, the administrative judge must: (1) Make an actual jurisdictional determination; (2) make a determination on the issues of timeliness or other grounds, if necessary; (3) conduct further proceedings on the merits; and (4) make a determination on all other material issues of fact and law. We modify our holding in Funk accordingly.

[5] This would include appeals where the issues of jurisdiction and timeliness are inextricably intertwined so as to require a jurisdictional determination before the timeliness issue can be addressed.

See Epps v. USPS, 70 MSPR 188, 190 (1996) (relying on Popham: "a jurisdictional determination may not be in the interest of efficiency where alternate grounds for dismissal exist, but jurisdiction nonetheless remains 'the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal'"); Kessler v. USPS, 68 MSPR 92, 94 (1995) ("[A] jurisdictional determination would not be in the interest of adjudicatory efficiency where the record is developed on the timeliness issue and shows no good cause for an untimely filing.").

The Board confessed that its approach on timeliness when jurisdiction was at issue was recondite, but sufficient, in Locke v. USPS, 61 MSPR 283, 286 n.1 (1994):

It may appear that, in order to preserve analytical rigor, we must resolve the jurisdictional issue before addressing the timeliness issue; the notion that one could file a timely appeal from an unappealable action is puzzling, if not incoherent. Still, as a prudential matter, it would be pointless to address the appellant's contention that the administrative judge erred with respect to the jurisdictional issue if he failed to show good cause for the late filing of his petition for appeal. Cf. Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193, 197 (1991) (an administrative judge may dismiss as untimely an appeal that is arguably within the Board's jurisdiction by assuming that jurisdiction exists, where the record developed on the timeliness issue shows no good cause for the late filing). Thus, for purposes of resolving the timeliness question, we assume arguendo that the appellant was subjected to an appealable personnel action.

Cf. Garcia v. USPS, 67 MSPR 263, 267 (1995) ("[T]hat the administrative judge adjudicated the appeals of three other similarly situated appellants on jurisdictional grounds, without interposing lack of timeliness as a barrier, is of no moment in our determination of whether the resolution of the instant appeal on timeliness grounds is correct.").

There is an exception to the Popham doctrine. When the lack of jurisdiction is very clear, the case should be dismissed on the basis of jurisdiction rather than timeliness. See Rolon v. VA, 53 MSPR 362, 366 (1992) (appeal to the Board from an arbitrator's decision mitigating a removal was clearly beyond the Board's jurisdiction because there was no allegation of a prohibited personnel practice). But if the jurisdictional issue is a closer issue than that of timeliness, Popham dictates that the appeal be resolved on timeliness. See Glugoski v. Federal Maritime Comm'n, 58 MSPR 514, 517 (1993).

The Popham analytical approach is inappropriate when timeliness is bound up with the jurisdictional issue of whether the appellant was subjected to a constructive adverse action that, in turn, bears on the appellant's right to notice of Board appeal rights (including the time for filing an appeal), Murray v. USPS, 68 MSPR 177, 180 (1995):

In an appropriate case, an administrative judge may assume arguendo that an appellant was subjected to an appealable action and has standing to appeal, and go on to dismiss an appeal as untimely filed, if the record on timeliness is sufficiently developed. See Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193, 197-98 (1991). Such an approach is not appropriate, however, if the jurisdictional and timeliness issues are "inextricably intertwined." Romine v. USPS, 64 MSPR 68, 72 (1994). Specifically, it is inappropriate to assume arguendo that jurisdiction exists and to dismiss an appeal as untimely if the appellant was never advised of his appeal rights and if the question of whether he should have been so advised depends on whether the agency took an appealable action and was in a position to know, based on well-established law, that the action was appealable. See id. at 71-73.

See Hunt v. VA, 88 MSPR 365, 368 ¶ 7 (2001) (it is better to adjudicate jurisdiction rather than timeliness when a timeliness determination could collaterally estop an appellant from litigating a subsequent, timely appeal); Ginsiorsky v. USPS, 68 MSPR 548, 550-51 (1995) ("Dismissal on the basis of untimeliness is appropriate if the record shows no good cause for the delay...and the issue is not inextricably intertwined with that of jurisdiction.").
