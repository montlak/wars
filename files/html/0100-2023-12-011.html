D. REQUIREMENT OF SPECIFIC PROPOSALS; FORMAT

The predicate of the negotiability process is a specific bargaining proposal presented to the employer before it reaches FLRA for a negotiability decision. Even though the agency may make broad statements about the nonnegotiability of a particular area, FLRA wants the union to make, and, if need be, the employer to reject as nonnegotiable, specific proposals. Under AFGE Local 1513 and Naval Air Station, Whidbey Island, 36 FLRA 82, 83-84 (1990):

Part 2424 of the Authority's regulations...contemplates that proposals that are the focus of a dispute must have been subject to a declaration of nonnegotiability by the agency involved prior to the union's filing a negotiability appeal with the Authority.... This practice serves the purpose of avoiding unnecessary litigation in two ways. First, and most obviously, it assures that there actually is a dispute over the negotiability of a proposal. Second, it affords the parties the opportunity to explore alternatives....

[T]he Agency now takes the position before the Authority that "had the specific proposals been provided to agency management, it is possible the issues could have been resolved by the parties developing negotiable language which would address their concerns."...

In view of the Agency's position, we conclude that it would not be a futile act to require the parties to attempt to resolve their dispute bilaterally through the consideration of these proposals and the possibility of alternatives, before they are considered by the Authority in a petition for review.

A proposal need not be elegantly drafted, and handwritten proposals support a negotiability appeal, as in IFPTE Local 3 and Dept. of Navy, Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, 51 FLRA 451, 459-60 (1995) (Proposal 4), but they must be reasonably clear and specific. Assuming that the agency properly declared a proposal nonnegotiable and that the union's petition for review is filed on time, FLRA will not adjudicate the petition unless it can make a definite determination of what is at issue. FLRA must be satisfied that the union proposed specific language for negotiation, Nat'l Ass'n of Agric. Employees and USDA, APHIS, 32 FLRA 1265, 1265-66 (1988), although the occasional proposal may turn on the meaning of an ambiguous phrase, and FLRA will seek clarification from the parties before issuing its negotiability determination. See, e.g., AFGE Local R5-185 and VAMC, Lexington, Ky., 52 FLRA 1024, 1024 (1997). The format of the proposal is less significant than its substance. As long as a proposal is set out specifically, its format is immaterial, permitting, for example, the attachment of the written request for an agency negotiability determination as the context for the proposal. IFPTE Local 4 and Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, 14 FLRA 52, 53 n.2 (1984).

FLRA described the requisite specificity of a proposal in ACT and Ala. NG, 2 FLRA 314, 315-17 (1979), involving what FLRA considered to be a request for a ruling of whether impact and implementation bargaining was required as to an agency program converting National Guard civilian technician positions to positions filled with active duty military personnel. Unless there is compliance with the FLRA regulatory requirement of a statement of specific proposals, a petition for review of a negotiability determination will be denied:

The Federal Service Labor Management Relations Statute establishes that the scope of the duty to bargain in good faith over conditions of employment extends generally to matters not inconsistent with Federal law, Government-wide rule or regulation, or agency rule or regulation for which there is a compelling need. [5 USC 7117] Under the Statute, the Authority must resolve issues relating to whether a matter proposed for negotiation is within the scope of the duty to bargain established by the Statute. [5 USC 7105(a)(2)(E)] The Statute requires that in doing so the Authority issue a written, specifically reasoned decision to the parties in each case. [5 USC 7117(c)(6)]

Thus, an essential element of the statutory scheme for Authority resolution of disputes over the scope of the duty to bargain is that the Authority will decide such cases based on a rational analysis and conclusion as to whether a matter proposed for negotiation is inconsistent with any particular Federal law, Government-wide rule or regulation, or agency rule or regulation for which there is a compelling need. In order to carry out this statutory requirement, the Authority must have sufficient and specific information to enable it to issue a reasoned and conclusive decision whether negotiation of the matters in dispute would violate such laws and regulations. Such a resolution cannot be accomplished in the absence of a bargaining proposal sufficiently specific and delimited in form and content so that the Authority can measure what is proposed for negotiation against specific statutory or regulatory provisions alleged to bar negotiations and thereby determine whether a violation would result if the parties were to reach agreement on the disputed matter.

...

[W]ere a decision on a negotiability issue to be reached in the absence of a specific and delimited proposal, such decision would be of little practical assistance to the parties and to the public. This is so because a broad request to negotiate on a given subject subsumes an infinite number of potential proposals, each particular proposal having specific purposes and consequences which, measured against applicable law and rules or regulations, would be determinative of whether a given proposal is within the scope of the duty to bargain. Thus, a ruling on a general bargaining request could not be specific, but would of necessity be so broad as to be virtually without dispositive significance. Such decisions rendered in the abstract would only delay the bargaining process and promote further litigation between the parties, as well as promote uncertainty in understanding bargaining duties under the Statute.

In addition to specificity, there is a rule of necessity: the proposal must be legible. A proposal may be handwritten, e.g., IFPTE Local 3 and Dept. of Navy, Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, 51 FLRA 451, 459-60 (1995) (Proposal 4), but a proposal submitted to FLRA with scrawled, partially illegible notes will not be reviewed. The parties must ensure that their submissions can at least be read. See MSPB Professional Ass'n and MSPB, 30 FLRA 852, 858-59 (1988) (Proposal 3). If the union fails to provide a legible copy of a proposal, there is no proposal before FLRA for a ruling. AFGE Local 3511 and VA Hosp., San Antonio, 12 FLRA 76, 81 (1983) (Proposal 20).

A proposal must require something--it must call for a specific act (or agreement not to act) by an agency. If the proposal is that a party or the parties "may" take an action, in the sense that the parties are free to act or not as they choose, the proposal may be nonnegotiable if FLRA concludes it is meaningless. Nonnegotiable, because it encompassed no binding commitment by either party, was a proposal that the agency and union may identify who has authority for the parties to bind the parties through MOUs, call caucuses, reach tentative agreements, and perform other responsibilities in the bargaining process, AFGE Nat'l Council of EEOC Locals No. 216 and EEOC, 71 FLRA 603, 606 (2020) (Member DuBester dissenting in part):

The U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit (D.C. Circuit) has held that "a proposal that purports to have no meaning whatsoever [cannot] be within the scope of the statutory duty to bargain."[31] Here, because we have adopted the Union's statement of meaning, even if the parties agreed to the proposal, it would require "nothing" from either party. Consequently, consistent with the court's reasoning, we find that Proposal 2 is outside the duty to bargain because it is effectively meaningless.
