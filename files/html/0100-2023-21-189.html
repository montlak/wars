c. OPM Fault in Creating Overpayment

OPM's fault in creating the overpayment was not determinative of liability for overpayment in Gray v. OPM, 29 MSPR 559, 560-61 (1986), involving what should have been a reduction in retirement benefits when the annuitant or survivor is entitled to old-age or survivors (OASI) benefits under the Social Security Act:

Appellant retired in 1980 at the age of 62 and began receiving a civil service retirement annuity based on approximately 33 years of civilian and military service. Part of the military service included in the computation of appellant's annuity was performed after December 31, 1956. However, active military service performed by an annuitant after December 31, 1956, is statutorily excluded from the computation of an annuity payable to the annuitant or his survivor if the individual or the survivor is entitled, or would on proper application be entitled, to monthly old-age or survivors (OASI) benefits under the Social Security Act based on the individual's wages and self-employment income. See 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j). Appellant became entitled to social security benefits in December of 1980. At that point, OPM should have adjusted appellant's monthly annuity by excluding service credit for his post-1956 military service. OPM did not do so and appellant continued to receive the overpayment until April 30, 1984.

Appellant contended in his petition for appeal that he was entitled to a waiver of collection of the overpayment because: (1) recovery of the overpayment would cause him financial hardship; and (2) recovery would be unconscionable under the circumstances. The presiding official made no specific finding on whether recovery would cause appellant financial hardship. Rather, he reversed OPM's decision, based on his finding that recovery would be "unconscionable" under the circumstances of this case. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.1403(a)(3). Specifically, the presiding official found that if repayment were required, appellant would not receive any credit for his post-1956 military service for the period of time in which the overpayment was made, since he could not retroactively claim benefits based on that service from the Social Security Administration. Further, the presiding official noted that if OPM had timely adjusted the annuity and informed appellant of the change in his annuity status when he reached age 62, it could have ensured that no overpayment would have occurred.

In its petition for review, OPM contends that the presiding official relied on an erroneous interpretation of 5 C.F.R. § 831.1403(a)(3). We agree. Under 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j), OPM is required to exclude from the computation of an annuity military service performed after December 31, 1956, if the individual is entitled, or would on proper application be entitled, to social security benefits. See Wheelahan v. Office of Personnel Management, 28 M.S.P.R. 427 (1985). However, there is no requirement in the statute or regulations that OPM notify an annuitant of eligibility for social security benefits.

In addition, the regulations governing overpayments provide that if OPM is at fault in an annuity overpayment case, that fact alone does not relieve the annuitant of individual liability for the overpayment. See Knowles v. Office of Personnel Management, 24 M.S.P.R. 500, 502 (1986). Under the statute, appellant is not entitled to the benefits he received and OPM's administrative error in not promptly adjusting his annuity does not create an entitlement to the overpayment. Weber v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 M.S.P.R. 562, 564 (1986) (OPM's enforcement of 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j) in such circumstances, absent evidence of financial hardship or detrimental reliance, is not against equity and good conscience).

That OPM contributed to an overpayment problem did not block collection, although monthly deduction was reduced after reevaluation of living and medical expenses, Pierotti v. OPM, 124 MSPR 103, ___¶¶ 5-7, 2016 MSPB 46 (2016):

Recovery of an overpayment from the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund will be waived when the annuitant is without fault and recovery would be against equity and good conscience. 5 U.S.C. § 8346(b); 5 C.F.R. § 831.1401. A recipient of an overpayment is without fault if he has performed no act of commission or omission that resulted in the overpayment. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1402. OPM's Policy Guidelines provide that individuals who know or suspect that they are receiving overpayments are expected to set aside the amount overpaid pending recoupment, and that in the absence of exceptional circumstances, which do not include financial hardship, recovery in these cases is not against equity and good conscience. IAF, Tab 7 at 70 (Policy Guidelines of the Disposition of Overpayments under the Civil Service Retirement System and Federal Employees' Retirement System § I.C.4); see Wright v. Office of Personnel Management, 105 M.S.P.R. 419, ¶ 4 (2007).

Recovery is against equity and good conscience when it would cause financial hardship, the annuitant can show that because of the overpayment he relinquished a valuable right or changed positions for the worse, or recovery could be unconscionable under the circumstances. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1403(a). The appellant bears the burden of establishing his entitlement to a waiver by substantial evidence. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1407(b). Substantial evidence is defined as the degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, might accept as adequate to support a conclusion, even though other reasonable persons might disagree. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(p).

We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant failed to prove that he is without fault. As the administrative judge found, while there is no evidence that the overpayment resulted from any statement that the appellant should have known to be incorrect or from any failure on his part to disclose material facts, he did accept payments that he knew or should have known to be erroneous. OPM's letter of February 16, 2006, stated that it would suspend disability retirement payments to the appellant because he was receiving compensation from OWCP. Based on that letter, the appellant knew or should have known that receipt of continued disability retirement benefits was in error. He certainly was aware that there was no reduction of his benefits despite OPM's statement that payments had been suspended. As the administrative judge noted, although OPM shares some of the blame for the overpayment because it failed to stop paying the appellant even after it learned that he was receiving OWCP benefits, the governing regulations provide that the fact that OPM may have been at fault in initiating the overpayment will not necessarily relieve the recipient from liability. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1402. Under the circumstances presented here, we find the appellant would have been expected to set aside the amount overpaid pending recoupment.
