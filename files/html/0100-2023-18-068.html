C. INADEQUATE REPLY AND NOTICE PERIOD

There are two statutory reply or notification periods in an adverse action case. First, the employee must receive 30 days of advance and specific notice before an action is taken. Second, the employee must have at least seven days to respond orally and in writing to the proposal. 5 USC 7513(b). The two periods run concurrently. The 30-day notice may be omitted only if the agency has reason to believe that the employee has committed a crime that could lead to a sentence of imprisonment. 5 USC 7513(b)(1). [The "crime exception" is discussed later in this chapter, "Shortened Notice Period; Crime Provision."] There is no statutory exception for the seven-day period that must be allowed for the employee to deliver a reply. Subject to OPM regulations, the agency may use administrative leave to keep the employee in pay status but out of the facility and in nonduty status during the notice periods. [Refer to Chapter 9, "Notice Period," for notice periods in a Chapter 43 performance-based case.]

A notice period reduced to less than what statutory and regulatory provisions allow is traditionally treated by the Board under its harmful error rule. The appellant has the burden to prove that he was adversely affected by the denial of procedural protections that Congress and OPM stated he should have. Given the Board's application of the harmful error rule, the allocation of the burden of proof upon the victim of the error, and the formulation of that burden--requiring a demonstration to a near certainty of a different result without the error--the law follows the Board's intended course: it is virtually impossible for the appellant to obtain reversal on the basis of harmful error for problems concerning the timing of an adverse action notice.

In 1991 the Board explained in Stephen v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 672, 689 (1991), that if a tenured employee is denied 30 days of advance notice before being fired, the employee should be paid for that time. The employee is considered to be constructively suspended during the notice period:

Because the agency in this appeal did not afford the appellant the full 30-day advance notice required by 5 USC 4303(b)(1)(A) or 7513(b)(1), we have also considered whether her consequent loss of pay for that time period should be deemed "harmful" within the meaning of 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(A). Our review of the judicial and administrative interpretations of the notice requirement in section 14 of the Veterans' Preference Act of 1944, Pub. L. No. 78-359, 58 Stat. 387, 390 (1944), the predecessor to the Reform Act, provides support for the conclusion that Congress intended in the Reform Act to confer on employees a right to pay during the 30-day advance notice period.

...The requirement of a 30 day advance notice period in the Reform Act suggests that its purpose is more than to ensure fairness through a meaningful opportunity to be heard. It indicates that the intention of the Congress was to confer upon employees in all cases not subject to the crime exception of 5 USC 7513(b)(1), or to a valid regulatory exception, a right to pay for 30 days.

As will be seen from the discussion that follows, the law is changing as to whether an error concerning the reply period is classified and considered under the harmful error rule or whether the action must be set aside as procedurally defective for lack of constitutional due process.
