broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 02: Restoration Rights Upon Recovery From Occupational Injury or Illness, Section C: Meaning of "Recovery", Subsection 02: Fully Recovered After a Year, Part b: Geographic Extent of Consideration
b. Geographic Extent of Consideration

Ordinarily, the employee who fully recovers within a year goes back to the job. For the employee who fully recovers longer than a year after compensation eligibility began, the restorations rights are agencywide and go beyond the locality where the individual was last employed, Pugh v. USPS, 81 MSPR 313, 320 ¶ 12 (1999):

Because the appellant's full recovery from his compensable injury took longer than 1 year from the date his eligibility for compensation began, and he applied for reappointment within 30 days of the cessation of compensation, the appellant was entitled to agencywide priority consideration for restoration to the position he left or an equivalent one. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(b). When an appellant alleges that he was denied restoration rights, it is part of the agency's burden to submit evidence regarding available positions, and any restrictive qualification requirements, in order to show that it made all reasonable efforts to place the employee in his former position or an equivalent one. See Tisdale v. Department of the Treasury, 44 M.S.P.R. 390, 395 (1990); Metsopulos v. U.S. Postal Service, 33 M.S.P.R. 536, 539 (1987). Although the appellant was entitled to agencywide priority consideration, the agency offered no evidence to demonstrate that it considered the appellant for any position other than positions in the Bellmawr facility. Although this would have been appropriate if the agency had properly determined that the appellant had only partially recovered from his compensable injury,see 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d), for fully recovered employees, the regulations provide that:

Candidates...are considered in the commuting area where they last served and, if the agency determines that an appropriate vacancy is unlikely to occur in the area during the candidates' period of reemployment priority, in other locations for which they are available. 5 C.F.R. § 302.303(b).

Whitfield v. USPS, 5 MSPR 239, 241-42 (1981), described the geographical scope of required consideration for an employee who recovered from a compensable injury more than one year after compensation began:

We also find that, as the agency contended in its petition, the presiding official erred in summarily ordering re-employment in Jacksonville, Florida, on the basis that the appellant's residency and family ties were there, even though the appellant had been employed in Miami, Florida, at the time he was separated from the agency's rolls as a result of his compensable injury.

The statutory language establishing the right to priority consideration for re-employment requires that the last employing agency or department "make all reasonable efforts to place, and accord priority to placing, the employee in his former or equivalent position within such department or agency, or within any other department or agency." 5 U.S.C. 815(b)(2) (emphasis added). We construe this language to obligate the agency to attempt to re-employ the qualifying former employee at the facility which last employed him, and if that is not feasible, at any other agency facility having an equivalent position available--unless, of course, the agency and the former employee agree on another mutually acceptable arrangement. Cf. FPM ch. 353, subch. B-3 (October 29, 1975). Consistent with the agency's burden of proof in appeals before the Board, 5 U.S.C. 7701(c)(1), the agency must submit evidence regarding all such available positions, and any restrictive qualification requirements, in order to show that it has made "all reasonable efforts" to re-employ the appellant.

The distinctions in restoration rights for employees who fully recover, and those who partially recover, after a year or more on compensation were highlighted by McDaniel v. USPS, SF-0353-11-0075-I-2 (NP 6/28/2012), noting that consideration is local for the partially-recovered employee and agencywide for the fully-recovered employee:

The Board may have jurisdiction over a Postal Service employee's claim that the agency violated his restoration rights following his full or partial recovery from a compensable injury, regardless of whether the employee would satisfy the definition of "employee" for the purposes of the Board's adverse action jurisdiction. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 353.102-.103; 353.304; Rumph v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 243, ¶ 9 (2006). The extent of an employee's rights to restoration depends on whether he is fully or partially recovered and, if fully recovered, how long such recovery took. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301.

Under 5 C.F.R. § 353.102, "[p]artially recovered means an injured employee, though not ready to resume the full range of his or her regular duties, has recovered sufficiently to return to part-time or light duty or to another position with less demanding physical requirements." Under 5 C.F.R. 353.301(d), "[a]gencies must make every effort to restore in the local commuting area, according to the circumstances in each case, an individual who has partially recovered from a compensable injury and who is able to return to limited duty." A partially recovered employee may appeal to the Board for a determination of whether the agency is acting arbitrarily or capriciously in denying restoration. 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(c). Where there is a bona fide dispute as to any of these elements, the appellant bears the burden of proving them because they are issues that implicate both jurisdiction and the merits. See Welby v. Department of Agriculture, 101 M.S.P.R. 17, ¶ 16 (2006).

Pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(b), "an individual who fully recovers from a compensable injury more than 1 year after compensation begins may appeal to [the Board] as provided for in parts 302 and 330 of this chapter for excepted and competitive service employees, respectively."[3] For an individual to be considered "fully recovered," OWCP payments must have been terminated on the basis that the individual is able to perform all of the duties of the position he left or the duties of an equivalent position. 5 C.F.R. § 353.102; see, e.g., Nixon v. Department of the Treasury, 104 M.S.P.R. 189, ¶ 6 (2006). The requirement that OWCP first find an individual to be fully recovered is a prerequisite to the Board finding that an individual is fully recovered. Steinmetz v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 277, ¶ 8 (2007), aff'd, 283 F. App'x 805 (Fed. Cir. 2008). OWCP's determination that an individual is fully recovered is "final and conclusive for all purposes and with respect to all questions of law and fact." 5 U.S.C. § 8128(b)(1). Accordingly, it is binding on the Board. As'Salaam v. U.S. Postal Service, 85 M.S.P.R. 76, ¶ 15 (2000).

[3] OPM's regulation at 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(b) provides, in relevant part, as follows:

An employee who separated because of a compensable injury and whose full recovery takes longer than 1 year from the date eligibility for compensation began...is entitled to priority consideration, agencywide, for restoration to the position he or she left or an equivalent one provided he or she applies for reappointment within 30 days of the cessation of compensation. Priority consideration is accorded by entering the individual on the agency's reemployment priority list for the competitive service or reemployment list for the excepted service. If the individual cannot be placed in the former commuting area, he or she is entitled to priority consideration for an equivalent position elsewhere in the agency.
