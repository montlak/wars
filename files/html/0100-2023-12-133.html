broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section D: Review of New Evidence, Subsection 02: What Evidence Is New, Part f: Issue of Jurisdiction; Exhaustion of Remedies
f. Issue of Jurisdiction; Exhaustion of Remedies

When the Board started issuing decisions on PFRs in 2022 after a hiatus of five years when it lacked a quorum, a series of nonprecedential decisions, often involving IRA cases, demonstrated a willingness of the Board to receive new evidence and new arguments at the PFR level if the evidence and arguments implicated jurisdiction. Since jurisdiction in IRA cases (and others, e.g., constructive adverse actions, enforcement of last chance agreements, allegations of discrimination) depends on well-pleaded facts, the result was that the Board was providing a de novo review of everything presented in the record, both to the AJ below and to the Board above. The decisions that follow demonstrate the more recent, relaxed approach, to evaluation of new evidence on jurisdictional issues at the PFR level, and the more restrictive approach often applied before 2022.

If the AJ does not provide the appellant with sufficient notice of a required burden of proof on jurisdiction before the issuance of an initial decision, the Board will consider newly-raised evidence on the basis that it was previously unavailable. Boughton v. USDA, 94 MSPR 347, 350 ¶ 6 (2003); see Dodson v. Dept. of Navy, 111 MSPR 504, 507 n.2, 2009 MSPB 113 (2009) (AJ did not adequately explain to the appellant what was required to show that she was an "employee" with appeal rights based on prior service when she appealed her probationary termination; the Board considered evidence of her prior appointment submitted with the PFR); Turner v. USPS, 91 MSPR 669 (2001) (Table) (considering new evidence on a jurisdictional issue of derivative veterans preference eligibility submitted by a pro se appellant who was not adequately informed by the AJ of information needed to establish Board jurisdiction); Koury v. DOD, 84 MSPR 219, 226-27 ¶¶ 11-12 (1999) (rather than remand for the development of a jurisdictional issue, where an appellant represented by counsel at the PFR level demonstrates awareness of and addresses jurisdictional issues in the PFR, the Board considered new evidence and argument submitted with the PFR as to IRA jurisdictional issues that were not specifically brought to the attention of the appellant by either the AJ's show cause order or the agency's motion to dismiss); Orr v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 MSPR 117, 121-22 ¶ 7 (1999) (Board evaluated new arguments on a PFR of dismissal of an IRA claim when the agency's motion to dismiss and the AJ's acknowledgment (or show cause) order did not inform the appellant of jurisdiction elements he had to address, but the AJ's initial decision discussed jurisdictional requirements, providing the appellant an opportunity to meet his jurisdictional burden on the PFR); McCray v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 154, 158 ¶ 7 (1998) (considering evidence and an argument first submitted with a PFR if the appellant was not notified of what was required to establish Board jurisdiction until the issuance of an initial decision); Robinson v. FEC, 33 MSPR 254, 255 (1987) (Board need not consider the appellant's contention that she was a preference eligible after she failed to raise the issue in response to the AJ's jurisdictional order); cf. Fleming v. Dept. of Labor, 97 MSPR 341, 344 ¶ 9 (2004) ("[W]here, as here, the administrative judge did not provide the appellant with sufficient notice that she must address an issue, or of the required burden of proof, the Board will consider such newly-raised evidence and arguments on the basis that they were previously unavailable."); cf. Moncada v. Exec. Office of the President, 2022 MSPB 25, n.2 (2022) (when the agency first challenged jurisdiction over the agency on PFR, evidence could be submitted by appellant in response to the jurisdictional challenge); Campo v. Dept. of Army, 93 MSPR 1, 5-6 ¶ 12 (2002) (information brought out during PFR that pertained to jurisdiction over an IRA appeal was to be considered by the AJ when the case was remanded for other reasons).

An early decision, Hurst v. GSA, 2 MSPR 497, 499, 3 MSPB 21 (1980), not addressing the adequacy of any show cause or similar order from the AJ, stated that the jurisdictional basis for a probationer's appeal cannot be raised for the first time in a PFR as new evidence; it must first be placed before the AJ. But cf. Goodwin v. PBGC, DC-315I-22-0138-I-1 (NP 7/11/2022) (alleged marital status discrimination by terminated probationer: "However, we have considered the appellant's new evidence and argument to the extent it concerns the issue of the Board's jurisdiction because the Board's jurisdiction can be raised at any time including on review. See Pirkkala, 123 M.S.P.R. 288, ¶ 5 (considering evidence submitted for the first time on review because it was relevant to the Board's jurisdiction)."); Ustariz v. Dept. of Treasury, AT-315H-17-0316-I-1, n.3 (NP 6/9/2022) (probationer's appeal: "In any event, under the unique circumstances of this case, wherein the agency's untimely filed submission contains the only copies of the relevant documents in the record below and the administrative judge already relied on it, and because it was only untimely filed by 1 day, we find it appropriate to accept the submission into the record below. See Ackerly v. Department of Transportation, 16 M.S.P.R. 78, 82 (1983) (finding no basis to disturb the presiding official's acceptance of the agency's brief mailed 1 day after the close of the record absent any showing of prejudice to the appellant or unfair advantage to the agency); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.12. Accordingly, we modify the initial decision to accept the agency's untimely filed "Response to Jurisdiction Order and Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Jurisdiction" into the record below."); cf. Benoit v. Dept. of Army, DC-3443-21-0386-I-1 (NP 4/27/2022) (considering evidence from the appellant on the nature of his appointment, offered to rebut agency assertion that he held a NTE appointment); Collins v. USPS, DE-0752-21-0273-I-1 (NP 4/12/2022) (considering PS-50 [equivalent of SF-50] for first time on PFR; the document related to appellant's status as an "employee" with appeal rights, though it did not alter the result reached by the AJ).

As to exhaustion of OSC remedies as a precondition to an IRA appeal, the Board accepted evidence, apparently submitted for the first time with the PFR, based on its jurisdictional significance, Davis v. Dept. of Labor, AT-1221-10-0907-W-1 (NP 5/26/2011):

The appellant exhausted her administrative remedy before OSC with respect to her allegation that the agency improperly lowered her performance evaluations in reprisal for whistleblowing. In that regard, her supplementary letter to OSC can be read as presenting (or elaborating on) her reduced performance rating claim. In addition, her belated presentation of OSC's preliminary determination letter on review makes clear that OSC considered her claim that the agency lowered her performance evaluation in reprisal for whistleblowing. See Mason v. Department of Homeland Security, 116 M.S.P.R. 135, ¶ 8 (2011) (stating that an appellant may demonstrate exhaustion through his initial OSC complaint, evidence that he amended the original complaint, including but not limited to OSC's determination letter and other letters from OSC referencing any amended allegations, and the appellant's written responses to OSC referencing the amended allegations); Ney v. Department of Commerce, 115 M.S.P.R. 204, ¶ 7 (2010) (stating that the issue of the Board's jurisdiction is always before the Board, and it may be raised by either party or sua sponte by the Board at any time). We find that the appellant's submissions satisfy the first IRA jurisdictional requirement.

See DeGrella v. Dept. of Air Force, 2022 MSPB 44, n.5 (2022) ("However, we will consider the appellant's new argument on this jurisdictional issue because jurisdiction is always before the Board and may be raised by any party or sua sponte by the Board at any time during Board proceedings. Lovoy v. Department of Health & Human Services, 94 M.S.P.R. 571, ¶ 30 (2003)."); Buchanan v. VA, DC-1221-21-0390-W-1, n.3 (NP 8/2/2022) (timing/knowledge test: "The Board will consider evidence submitted on review concerning jurisdiction, even if the evidence was previously available, because jurisdiction may be raised at any time during Board proceedings. Pirkkala v. Department of Justice, 123 M.S.P.R. 288, ¶ 5 (2016); Stoglin v. Department of the Air Force, 123 M.S.P.R. 163, ¶ 7 (2015), aff'd, 640 F. App'x 864 (Fed. Cir. 2016). Because the emails could bear on whether the appellant met her jurisdictional burden of proving that she was subjected to a personnel action in connection with the failure to promote her, we have considered them."); Weathers v. VA, AT-1221-15-0633-W-1 (NP 4/5/2016) ("However, the Board accepts evidence of exhaustion presented for a first time on review, even if it was available below, when it implicates the Board's jurisdiction and warrants an outcome different from that in the initial decision. See, e.g., Atkinson v. Department of State, 107 M.S.P.R. 136, ¶ 12 (2007) (accepting evidence, which the appellant submitted for the first time on review although he could have submitted it below, that he exhausted his remedies before OSC). Because the submission of the appellant's OSC complaints go to the heart of the jurisdictional issue, we have considered it on review."); Nicholson v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-1221-14-0765-W-1 (NP 2/27/2015) ("The Board has held that the issue of Board jurisdiction is always before the Board and may be raised by either party or sua sponte by the Board at any time during a Board proceeding. E.g., Covington v. Department of the Army, 85 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 9 (2000); see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(e) (notwithstanding the express criteria for granting a petition for review, the Board reserves the authority to consider any issue in an appeal before it). Moreover, the Board may consider evidence submitted for the first time on review if it implicates the Board's jurisdiction and warrants an outcome different from that in the initial decision. See Schoenig v. Department of Justice, 120 M.S.P.R. 318, ¶ 7 (2013). Thus, we find it appropriate to consider the appellant's additional claims of whistleblower reprisal which she clarified on review because they implicate the Board's jurisdiction and warrant an outcome different from that in the initial decision."); Vazquez v. Dept. of Navy, DC-1221-12-0853-W-1 (NP 9/10/2013) ("[T]he Board accepts evidence of exhaustion presented for the first time on review, even if it was available below, when it implicates the Board's jurisdiction and warrants an outcome different from that in the initial decision. See, e.g., Atkinson v. Department of State, 107 M.S.P.R. 136, ¶ 12 (2007) (accepting evidence, which the appellant submitted for the first time on review although he could have presented it below, that he exhausted his administrative remedies before OSC)."); Atkinson v. Dept. of State, 107 MSPR 136, 141 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 254 (2007) (accepting as new evidence information bearing on the appellant's exhaustion of OSC remedies as a jurisdictional prerequisite to an IRA appeal and citing Trabue v. USPS, 102 MSPR 14 ¶ 6 (2006), accepting as new evidence documentation bearing upon the jurisdictional issue of appellant's preference eligible status). But cf. Davis v. Dept. of Army, DC-1221-12-0605-W-1 (NP 10/29/2013) (although the appellant demonstrated in her PFR that she exhausted her OSC remedy and her appeal was not premature, it was still dismissed, without remand, because her disclosure was not protected under 5 USC 2302(b)(8)). In Molinar v. VA, 80 MSPR 248, 250 ¶ 4 (1998), a seemingly straightforward IRA appeal involving--as IRA appeals almost always do--a "jurisdictional" issue, the Board noted that "[w]e have considered the agency's supplemental response and the appellant's reply because they implicate Board jurisdiction." See Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 73 MSPR 574, 577 n.1 (1997) (IRA appeal: accepting with the PFR a copy of the appellant's communication to OSC--jurisdictional evidence; the appellant was not put on notice at the regional level of the jurisdictional issue); cf. Kirwan v. Dept. of Transp., 88 MSPR 445, 449 n.2 (2001) (IRA appeal: OSC communications bearing on Board jurisdiction considered initially at the PFR stage when materials were "unavailable before the record was closed despite [appellant's] due diligence").

The appellant was permitted to provide evidence with a PFR showing exhaustion of DOL remedies required for pursuit of a VEOA claim. McDonald v. Dept. of State, DC-3330-12-0607-I-1 (NP 5/3/2013) ("Nonetheless, the Board accepts evidence of exhaustion presented for the first time on review, even if it was available below, when it warrants an outcome different from that in the initial decision. See, e.g., Atkinson v. Department of State, 107 M.S.P.R. 136, ¶ 12 (2007); Trabue v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 14, ¶ 6 (2006). Here, the letter from DOL establishes exhaustion, and it warrants an outcome different from that in the initial decision because, as explained below, the appellant has otherwise met the VEOA's jurisdictional requirements. It is also significant that, even though we are not persuaded by the appellant's assertion that he did not receive the acknowledgment order, the order did not sufficiently inform the appellant of his burden of proving that he exhausted his administrative remedy with DOL."); see Ostwald v. VA, DE-4324-14-0615-I-1 (NP 6/8/2015) (evidence of DOL exhaustion apparently presented for first time with PFR after military service discrimination was alluded to in the appeal); Arnold v. Dept. of Army, DC-3330-13-0066-I-1 (NP 11/26/2013) (evidence submitted with PFR of DOL exhaustion led the Board to remand the case for a merits determination).

The Board may consider evidence for the first time with a PFR when the record was closed by the AJ in a manner that precluded submission of evidence bearing on jurisdiction, Johnson v. Admin. Office of U.S. Courts, 96 MSPR 482, 484 ¶ 4 (2004), aff'd, Johnson v. MSPB, 128 Fed. Appx. 139 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP):

The Board normally will not consider evidence or argument filed after the close of the record on review. See, e.g., Brown v. Department of Defense, 94 M.S.P.R. 669 ¶ 4 (2003); Gray v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 161 ¶ 9 (2002). Further, the Board normally does not consider evidence and argument which was not provided to the administrative judge. See, e.g., Turner v. U.S. Postal Service, 90 M.S.P.R. 385 ¶ 5 (2001); Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980); Avansino v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 M.S.P.R. 211, 214 (1980). Here, however, because the appellant withdrew her appeal before the agency was required to submit its file, the record below was wholly undeveloped on the matter of jurisdiction. Both parties have now fully addressed the issue of jurisdiction on review and, under the unique circumstances of this case, we find it appropriate to consider all of the pleadings filed, even though some of them were submitted after the close of the record on review without a showing that they were based on new and material evidence not available before the record closed. Cf. Turner, 90 M.S.P.R. 385 ¶ 5 (the Board considered evidence and argument concerning jurisdiction submitted for the first time on review); Black v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 78 M.S.P.R. 561, 565-66 (1998) (same).

If the Board thinks that it should do so, however, it accepts evidence on a jurisdictional issue that does not qualify as new evidence. That occurred in an appeal by an employee the agency considered to be a probationer. The agency failed to introduce into evidence internal agency directives that countered the personnel and attendance records relied upon by the AJ to find the appellant tenured. Saying only that the Board viewed with "extreme disfavor" the submission of the agency documentation for the first time in a PFR, the Board nonetheless remanded the case for reconsideration in light of the agency evidentiary submissions on probationary status. Conley v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 118, 121, 7 MSPB 603 (1981); see Williams-Hargraves v. HUD, 88 MSPR 176, 180 ¶ 10 (2001) (belatedly accepting evidence from the agency bearing on the nature of an excepted service appointment, a jurisdictional issue); Lowmack v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 491, 498 ¶ 17 (1999) (considering an SF-50 submitted by the agency at the PFR level suggesting a probationary status of the appellant; the case was remanded for, among other reasons, jurisdictional determination of the appellant's employment status).

Silva v. DOJ, AT-0752-15-0028-I-1 (NP 6/5/2015), considered evidence of a constructive demotion--involving Board appellate jurisdiction--for the first time on PFR:

In reasserting that his demotion was involuntary, the appellant has submitted a large amount of evidence that he did not submit below. Generally, the Board will not consider evidence submitted for the first time with the petition for review, absent a showing that it was unavailable before the record was closed despite the party's due diligence. Avansino v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 M.S.P.R. 211, 214 (1980); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115. The Board, however, may consider such evidence if it implicates the Board's jurisdiction and warrants an outcome different from that of the initial decision. Schoenig v. Department of Justice, 120 M.S.P.R. 318, ¶ 7 (2013). After reviewing the appellant's evidence, we find nothing that warrants a different outcome. The evidence fails to establish that his requested demotion was involuntary or even nonfrivolously alleges the same. See, e.g., PFR File, Tab 3, Subtab A (the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's dismissal of the appellant's complaint), Subtab B (correspondence regarding the exchange of pleadings between the appellant, the agency, and the administrative judge), Subtab C (documents related to the appellant's 2011 promotion), Subtab D (organizational charts and position descriptions), Subtab E (salary tables), Subtab F (forms documenting the appellant's demotion), Subtab G (information about Pacific Islanders). Accordingly, we find that dismissal for lack of jurisdiction, without a hearing, remains appropriate.

At times the Board rejects evidence bearing on jurisdiction submitted for the first time with a PFR, despite the Board's frequent incantation that jurisdiction is always at issue. See Moore v. VA, 80 MSPR 268, 272 ¶ 9 (1998) (declining to reopen based on information available to the appellant prior to the close of the record before the AJ, that impacted on whether the appellant was a probationary or tenured employee); Hendrix v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 642, 643 (1985) (declining to accept as "new" evidence submitted with a PFR attempting to demonstrate that prior service should have tacked for the purposes of completion of the probation); Hewitt v. IRS, 20 MSPR 505 (1984) (declining to consider as new evidence materials that the appellant believed established that she had completed her probationary period); Weber v. Dept. of Educ., 9 MSPR 429, 430 (1982) (rejecting "new evidence" challenging the AJ's determination that the appellant was not a probationer: "The record shows, however, that the agency was on notice from appellant's letter of appeal that she was challenging her characterization as a 'probationary employee.' Thus, it was the agency's responsibility to marshal the facts to persuade the presiding official that appellant was indeed a probationer."); cf. Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 106 MSPR 319, 323-24 ¶ 11, 2007 MSPB 182 (2007) (notice of intended modification in rulemaking impacting MSPB jurisdiction over probationers was not new evidence).

Without saying that it was making an exception for jurisdictional evidence from the agency, the Board received and considered as "new" evidence during the PFR stage when that evidence pertained to the nature of the appellant's separation from the service (by adverse action) and a belated appeal. The agency explained that "it had to retrieve the appellant's personnel folder from its archives, and accordingly the information was not available at the time the agency's response to the AJ's order to show cause was due." Townes v. USPS, 99 MSPR 350, 353 ¶ 7 (2005). Harris v. Dept. of Navy, 99 MSPR 355, 360 ¶ 11 (2005), accepted with the agency's cross-PFR documentation submitted for the first time casting light on whether the appellant was terminated for lack of suitability (either at the direction of OPM or through delegated authority to the agency) or whether the appellant was terminated for failure to complete his probationary appointment based on preappointment conditions.

Evidence from a related case pending before a regional office of the Board was accepted through what the Board characterized as official notice during a PFR to recognize the jurisdictionally-determinative status of the appellant as a preference eligible employee, Smith v. USPS, 107 MSPR 410, 413 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 289 (2007):

The AJ correctly found, on the record before her in this case, that the appellant failed to show he is a preference eligible. Nevertheless, the Board may take official notice of matters that can be verified, including documents or actions in other Board appeals. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.64; Thomson v. Department of Transportation, 92 M.S.P.R. 392, ¶ 6 (2002); Woodjones v. Department of the Army, 89 M.S.P.R. 196, ¶ 15 (2001). In this case, the initial decision in MSPB Docket No. CH-0752-07-0527-I-1 finds that the appellant is a preference eligible. We take official notice of that initial decision. Our failure to do so could result in the Board's making inconsistent findings regarding the appellant's preference eligible status.

Sonneborn v. DOD, 80 MSPR 215, 217-18 ¶ 4 (1998), determined as to an agency whose PFR first presented a collective bargaining agreement argued to cover the appellant's RIF and to preclude Board jurisdiction over the appeal:

The Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; rather, it is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule, or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Moreover, the issue of Board jurisdiction may be raised at any time during a proceeding. Morgan v. Department of the Navy, 28 MSPR 477, 478 (1985); see Rysavy v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 27 MSPR 316, 317-18 (1985) (raising Board jurisdiction during an attorney fee proceeding). Thus, the Board has held that even if an agency does not establish that the documents it submits for the first time on review were previously unavailable, the Board will consider those documents if they implicate the Board's jurisdiction. See Blakemore v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 MSPR 390, 393 (1992); see also Flaherty v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 MSPR 36, 38-39 (1995) (considering evidence submitted by the agency for the first time with its motion to reopen a final Board decision when that evidence implicated the Board's jurisdiction). We consider the CBA submitted by the agency because it implicates the Board's jurisdiction.

See Hunt v. VA, 88 MSPR 365, 369 n.2 (2001) (considering portions of a collective bargaining agreement offered with the PFR because the materials should have been filed with AJ; and the documents called the Board's jurisdiction into question).

An appeal need not be readjudicated if an AJ raises a jurisdictional issue in the initial decision but the appellant does not address that issue in a PFR, Masselli v. Dept. of Army, 105 MSPR 79, 82 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 67 (2007):

...Although an appellant must receive explicit information on what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue, Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985), failure to provide such notice will not require further adjudication when the appellant receives the necessary information in an initial decision, but on petition for review does not make nonfrivolous allegations of fact that if proven would establish jurisdiction. See Elliott v. Department of the Air Force, 102 M.S.P.R. 364, ¶ 5 (2006); Milam v. Department of Agriculture, 99 M.S.P.R. 485, ¶ 10 (2005); Fleming v. Department of Labor, 97 M.S.P.R. 341, ¶¶ 8-9 (2004). The appellant received a copy of the initial decision sent to him by the Clerk of the Board. In the initial decision, the administrative judge thoroughly explained the jurisdictional issues and provided specific notice of the requirements for filing a VEOA appeal. The appellant has not indicated that he filed a complaint with DOL. Thus, the failure to serve him below did not prejudice his substantive rights, and the Board lacks jurisdiction over this appeal.

Examples abound of the Board's inconsistent treatment of allegedly new evidence in jurisdictional cases. See McCormack v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 408, 410 n.2 (1984) (rejecting as "new" evidence information from OPM concerning the delegation of suitability determination authority but accepted the same evidence as bearing on its jurisdiction to hear the appeal). The Board accepted as "new" evidence a VA letter verifying the appellant's status as a disabled veteran, a status that provided MSPB jurisdiction over his appeal. Rather than dwell on the point that perhaps the appellant could have sooner obtained that evidence, the Board simply remanded the case for a hearing. Santiago v. USPS, 62 MSPR 41, 43 (1994); see Duvall-Little v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 416, 420 ¶¶ 10-11 (2001) (evidence of mental impairment was available at the time of the regional adjudication but accepted with the PFR; Board jurisdiction (over involuntary resignation) was implicated; strong equities in the appellant's favor; evidence of such weight as to warrant a different outcome); Carey v. USPS, 50 MSPR 359, 361-62 (1991) (evidence of a preference eligible status submitted with the PFR was not new because it could have been earlier obtained; the Board exercised its discretion to reopen the case on its own motion, acknowledge jurisdiction, and remand the case for a merits determination). In Fish v. USPS, 67 MSPR 540, 543 (1995), the Board considered, without criticism of the agency, an assertion by the agency made for the first time in its PFR that the appellant lacked the preference eligible status necessary for Board jurisdiction over his appeal. See Landingham v. USPS, 81 MSPR 77, 81-82 ¶¶ 9-13 (1999) (considering evidence of an EEO complaint submitted with a PFR to demonstrate Board jurisdiction over an appeal from an arbitration award); Olsen v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 60, 63 (1994) (Board considered evidence provided for the first time with a PFR that demonstrated the agency's reason for not allowing the appellant to withdraw her resignation, a jurisdictional issue).

Of belated efforts by the government to submit new evidence on appeal, Judge Plager, writing in an appeal involving a retirement claim for a spousal annuity, said in his opinion concurring and dissenting in part in DuBoise v. OPM, 363 Fed. Appx. 35, 38 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP):

At the conclusion of proceedings before the Board, the Government had failed to satisfy its burden of establishing that adequate notice was sent to Mr. DuBoise, which was the critical issue in the case. The fact that the failure was due to a perhaps understandable degree of confusion on the part of OPM in getting its papers straight is hardly a reason to give the Government a second bite at the adversary apple. Had it been Mrs. DuBoise who failed to provide evidence, I cannot imagine we would send the case back to give her another chance to prove her case.

We should hold the Government to the same degree of care in its litigation behavior as the Government expects from its adversaries. Under the established rules of adversary litigation, Mrs. DuBoise won this particular contest. There are no compelling equities in the Government's favor that demand we make an exception here; indeed, the equities are all on the other side. Because we cannot uphold a decision of the Board that is "unsupported by substantial evidence," 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c), I would award Mrs. DuBoise the survivor benefit to which the rule of law entitles her. I respectfully dissent from the majority's remand of the case to the Board to see if the Government can do better next time.
