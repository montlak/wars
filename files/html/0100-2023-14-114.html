i. Nondisciplinary Sanction

Although an agency seemed to be precluded from considering as prior disciplinary action reprimands or counseling letters not meeting the multipart review standards of Bolling and Howard, supra, lesser actions could be cited in a proposal letter to give the appellant specific reasons for an adverse action, e.g., failure to respond to repeated counseling relative to work performance. Gober v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 354, 357 (1983) (letters of caution outlining work requirements and offering assistance). More recent decisions suggest that consideration of letters of warning is consistent with the factors relevant to assessment of a penalty. Counseling and nondisciplinary instructional material may generally be relied on to support an enhanced penalty. Richard v. Dept. of Air Force, 43 MSPR 303, 308 (1990); see Devall v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 500, 503 (1997) (sexual harassment: "Whether an appellant had received prior counseling about sexual harassment is a factor to consider in assessing the penalty issue, as is the effect of his misconduct upon his supervisors' confidence in his ability to perform his duties."); Thomas v. DOD, 66 MSPR 546, 553 (1995) ("[a]n agency may properly consider non-disciplinary counselings as a basis for an enhanced penalty."); Lovenduski v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 612, 615 (1994) (AJ erred by not considering nondisciplinary counselings that were cited in the proposed removal); Patterson v. Dept. of Air Force, 22 MSPR 6, 8 (1984) ("Consideration of non-disciplinary counselings is consistent with the relevant factors set forth in Douglas and may be relied upon by the agency in imposing an enhanced penalty"); Martinez v. DOD, 21 MSPR 556, 558 n.2 (1984) ("Moreover, counselings and other non-disciplinary, instructional material may generally be relied on in imposing an enhanced penalty."); Coughlan v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 47, 51 (1982) (the law requires that an appellant be given an opportunity to challenge the merits of the past record citation at the hearing before the Board; nothing in the agency's procedures, nor any rule or law, precludes the agency from using counselings as a basis for imposing an enhanced penalty); Jones v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 663, 666 (1982) ("Regardless of whether counseling an employee may constitute discipline, the Board held in Douglas...that the employee's disciplinary record and work record may both be considered in assessing a reasonable penalty.").

Under Tyler v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 85, 88-89 (1988), counseling concerning prior acts of sexual harassment was relevant; those acts could properly have been used as another basis for imposing a penalty. Nothing in any rule or law precludes the agency from using nondisciplinary warnings or counseling as a basis for imposing an enhanced penalty if the reason for the adverse action is inadequate performance, not misconduct. Gober v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 354, 357 (1983), aff'd, Gober v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-1306); see Guthrie v. USPS, 41 MSPR 102, 107 (1989) (warning letter concerning a failure to maintain the assigned work schedule). But see Els v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 27, 34 ¶ 17 (1999) (counseling letter put the appellant on notice of the proper procedures for using government property; however, the appellant was never disciplined for the alleged prior misuse of a government trailer and the agency stated he did not have a disciplinary record).

An agency may not consider nondisciplinary counselings as support for an enhanced penalty unless they are cited in the proposal letters, so that employees can dispute the validity of the counselings. Capito v. VA, 39 MSPR 289, 291 (1988); see Eichner v. USPS, 83 MSPR 202, 211 ¶ 18 (1999) ("Further, because neither the proposal notice nor the decision letter informed the appellant that the agency was relying on the letter of warning for the February 27, 1996 misconduct, the deciding official improperly relied on the letter of warning in his penalty determination. See, e.g., Maddux v. Department of the Air Force, 68 M.S.P.R. 644, 646 (1995), review dismissed, 91 F.3d 170 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). Instead, the deciding official should have considered the appellant's lack of a prior disciplinary record as a mitigating factor in this case. See Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305."); Maddux v. Dept. of Air Force, 68 MSPR 644, 646 (1995) (agency may not rely on nondisciplinary counselings to impose an enhanced penalty unless the agency places the appellant on notice that it intends to rely on the record of those actions; the action was sustained nonetheless based on the severity of the charge). Murdock-Doughty v. Dept. of Air Force, 70 MSPR 119, 123-24 (1996), emphasized the point that formalities must be observed if the agency is to rely upon past informal admonishments or counselings to justify a current penalty:

The proposing official testified that she and the deciding official, in determining the removal penalty, also considered that the appellant had been counseled for, among other things, failing to follow leave-requesting procedures, extended absence without leave status, tardiness, and frequent personal telephone calls. However, neither the proposal nor the decision notice refers to these counselings or the conduct on which they were based and there is no other evidence that the agency placed the appellant on notice that it was going to rely on these matters in making its penalty determination. Accordingly, the agency erred in considering such [counselings] and conduct in imposing and assessing the removal penalty in this appeal. See, e.g., Maddux v. Dept. of the Air Force, 68 MSPR 644, 645-46 (1995). Since the [counselings] and conduct cannot be considered, we find that, in light of the appellant's good work performance shown in the agency's performance appraisals (to which we assign much greater weight than the contrary portrayal set forth in the agency's March 29, 1995 memorandum), the removal penalty exceeds the tolerable limits of reasonableness. See Douglas, 5 MSPR at 306.

The agency need not place the warnings or past counseling letters in the record if they are referenced in the proposal letter for the appealed offense and the references to them are sufficiently clear that the appellant may challenge the accuracy of the references. Mitchell v. USPS, 32 MSPR 362, 365 (1987). The Board may discount the progressive nature of a disciplinary action if a prior warning, relied upon by the agency, concerns conduct that differed in character from the conduct leading to the adverse action. See Sadorf v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 178, 181 (1993).

The Board implied but did not define a difference in weight of a past formal disciplinary letter and a less formal warning, Ibrahim v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 531, 533 n.2 (1986):

The presiding official took into account appellant's two prior suspensions for similar offenses and the agency's apparent consideration of all other relevant factors.... [T]he presiding official incorrectly characterized a letter of warning sent to appellant...as part of the appellant's past disciplinary record. The letter in question was informal and did not provide an opportunity to have it reviewed by a higher level authority. See Bolling v. Dept. of Air Force, 8 MSPB 658, 9 MSPR 335, 339-40 (1981). Moreover, the agency did not cite it as "past record" per se, and did not rely on it as such in their considerations.

If agency regulations or a union contract preclude reliance on informal disciplinary measures, the underlying conduct "is an appropriate matter for consideration in assessing the penalty." Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 236, 239 (1982). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Failure to Include Past Discipline in Proposal: Due Process."]
