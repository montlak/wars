D. PREMATURE APPEALS

The Board regulation on whether the appeal is timely addresses the effective date of a decision or the date of receipt the decision, 5 CFR 1201.22(b)(1) (2022):

Except as provided in paragraph (b)(2) of this section, an appeal must be filed no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of the appellant's receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later....

The 1989 regulation spoke only of the effective date of an action, 5 CFR 1201.22(b): "Time of filing. An appeal must be filed during the period beginning on the day after the effective date of the action being appealed and ending 20 days after the effective date." [54 FR 53508 (Dec. 29, 1989)] The current regulatory language just quoted first appeared in a Final Rule of November 6, 1996 (62 FR 62 FR 59992) [https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/FR-1997-11-06/pdf/97-29311.pdf], providing the explanation:

The Board is authorized by 5 U.S.C. 1204(h) to promulgate regulations to carry out its functions and has used this authority since its inception to prescribe time limits for filing appeals with the Board. Prior to this amendment, the regulation at 5 CFR 1201.22(b), prescribing time limits for filing an appeal, required that an appeal of an agency action be filed no later than 30 days after the effective date of the action or, where the appeal is from a final or reconsideration decision that does not set an effective date, no later than 35 days after the date of issuance of the agency's decision. In establishing the 35-day time limit where the appeal is from a final or reconsideration decision that does not set an effective date, the Board, in effect, was providing the same 30-day time period for filing as in an appeal of an action with an effective date by allowing 5 additional days after the date of issuance of the decision for it to be mailed and received.

Where the 35-day time limit applies and there is a delay by the agency in mailing the decision after it is issued, and/or a delay by the U.S. Postal Service that results in more than 5 days elapsing between issuance of the decision and receipt by the appellant, an appellant could have less than 30 days to file an appeal with MSPB. Should an appellant not receive the agency's decision until after the 35-day time period for filing has expired, any appeal may be dismissed as untimely.

In order to ensure that each appellant, regardless of the nature of the action or decision being appealed, has a full 30 days to file after the event from which the time period begins to run, the Board is amending its regulation at 5 CFR 1201.22(b) to require that an appeal be filed no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later.

The regulation is somewhat unclear. Can an appeal be filed between the date of the decision and the effective date? The regulation suggests that should be the case--assuming that the underlying action is not canceled before the appeal is filed. The decisions that follow address various types of arguably premature appeals. [Refer later in this chapter to "Premature Appeal That Ripens Into Appealable Action During Regional Adjudication or During PFR."]

Under Board decisions law governing appeals other than IRA whistleblower cases involving threats of personnel actions [discussed in Chapter 13, "Threatened Personnel Action"], an action may be appealed only after it is effected. Board regulations do not provide for filing an appeal before the effective date of the action appealed. Betts v. VA, DE-0752-13-0190-I-1 n.1 (NP 1/22/2014) (citing Vaughan v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 106, 108 (1991)). An appeal is properly dismissed if the challenged action has not yet been taken or, if taken, is canceled before the appeal is initiated. See Barrios v. Dept. of Interior, 100 MSPR 300, 301-302 ¶ 5 (2005) (Board regulations do not provide for the filing of an appeal before the effective date of the action being appealed); McGuire v. VA, 76 MSPR 259, 262 (1997) (Board would not accept jurisdiction over an appeal from the denial of law enforcement retirement credit under FERS when the determinative issue was whether that credit should be provided under CSRS regulations; an agency determination had not been received and there was no indication that the agency would not issue that determination); Qatsha v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 561, 563 (1997) (appeal was properly dismissed as premature when the appellant was threatened with separation as a result of a transfer of function, but the separation had not occurred at the time of the appeal); Vaughan v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 106, 108 (1991) (determining to be premature an appeal filed by an appellant on voluntary LWOP status pending action on a disability retirement application; the agency postponed the effective date of a removal until OPM decided the retirement application); Windom v. Dept. of Navy, 12 MSPR 401, 402 (1982) (appeal from a RIF was prematurely filed because the agency notice advised the appellant he could appeal after receipt of the RIF notice rather than, as the Board regulation states, after the effective date of the action); Feltus v. Dept. of Energy, 4 MSPR 232, 234, 4 MSPB 308 (1980) (no jurisdiction over a first appeal when the RIF was canceled; no jurisdiction over the second appeal when the RIF was not yet effective at the time of the appeal). [Refer to Chapter 9, "Within-grade Increase Denials" and "When Jurisdiction Attaches" for within grade increase appeals and the effect of delayed issuances of ALOC and reconsideration decisions on Board jurisdiction; refer earlier in this chapter to "Failure to Seek Timely Reconsideration; Requests to Extend Time to Request Reconsideration," for discussion concerning appeals from OPM retirement decisions.]

As to premature mixed-case appeals, according to 5 CFR 1201.154(c) (2022):

If the appellant files an appeal prematurely under this subpart, the judge will dismiss the appeal without prejudice to its later refiling under § 1201.22 of this part. If holding the appeal for a short time would allow it to become timely, the judge may hold the appeal rather than dismiss it.

The Federal Circuit suggested a liberal view of premature appellate actions, as it remanded a case to an arbitrator to reconsider the timeliness of an allegedly premature invocation of arbitration, Buffkin v. DOD, 957 F.3d 1327, 1333-34 (Fed. Cir. 2020):

Federal courts have long recognized that a premature notice of appeal is effective. "[T]he courts of appeals quite generally have held premature appeals effective." See Advisory Committee's Note on 1979 Amendment to Fed. Rule App. Proc. 4(a)(2). This practice was codified in Rule 4(a)(2) of the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure: "[a] notice of appeal filed after the court announces a decision or order--but before the entry of the judgment or order--is treated as filed on the date of and after the entry." The Supreme Court explained "that Rule 4(a)(2) permits a notice of appeal filed from certain nonfinal decisions to serve as an effective notice from a subsequently entered final judgment." FirsTier Mortg. Co. v. Inv'rs Mortg. Ins. Co., 498 U.S. 269, 274 (1991). "[A] premature notice of appeal does not ripen until judgment is entered. Once judgment is entered, the Rule treats the premature notice of appeal 'as filed after such entry.'" Id. at 275 (quoting Rule 4(a)(2)). The Supreme Court explained that this practice "was intended to protect the unskilled litigant." Id. at 276.

The agency, here, does not argue that the time limit is jurisdictional, and it is clear that it is not. See Gunn, 892 F.2d at 1038, 1039. As we explained in Gunn v. Veterans Admin. Med. Ctr., Birmingham, Ala., 892 F.2d 1036 (Fed. Cir. 1990), where issues of timeliness are not jurisdictional "but mere procedural defects":

Such procedural defects, unless clearly harmful to resolution of the merits, should be resolved against forfeiture of the right to process a grievance. Strict construction of procedural requirements in arbitration, in this case making them even more rigid than in board proceedings, could only chill the selection of arbitration over the selection of administrative review. That result would be inconsistent with the statute, 5 U.S.C. § 7121(e), which is designed to give eligible employees the free choice between appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board and what should be the more informal arbitration procedure.

Id. at 1039-40. Given the informal nature of arbitration and the lack of surprise to the agency, "[l]ittle would be accomplished by prohibiting the [arbitrator] from reaching the merits of" a grievance where a premature request for arbitration has been filed. See FirsTier Mortg., 498 U.S. at 276.

Further, a premature request for arbitration being effective appears to be consistent with the parties' past practices. The agency had not objected in at least three other arbitrations where the union invoked arbitration prematurely, and, as noted earlier, had waited over three years to object to the union doing so here. Nonetheless, since this issue has not been addressed by the arbitrator, we think that the arbitrator should address it in the first instance. On remand, the arbitrator is not, of course, bound to follow FLRA decisions that have held such premature requests for arbitration ineffective. See, e.g., United States Dep't of Def. Domestic Elementary & Secondary Sch. (Agency) & Fed. Educ. Ass'n Stateside Region (Union) (FEA-SR), 71 F.L.R.A. 236 (July 16, 2019).
