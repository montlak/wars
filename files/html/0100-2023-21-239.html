b. Interim Promotion

A restored employee may be entitled to promotion, or consideration for promotion, that he would have received had the compensable injury or illness not occurred, Gavette v. Dept. of Treasury, 44 MSPR 166, 175-77 & 73 n.2 (1990):

The agency also contends that the administrative judge erred in finding that 5 U.S.C. § 8151 required it to consider the appellant for competitive promotions. It argues that the parties' stipulation that the appellant had been restored to his position of Revenue Agent (GS-12, step 10, journeyman level), and had been awarded all within-grade salary increases for which he would have been eligible, satisfied restoration requirements. The agency contends further that the appellant bore the burden of proving that he would have been considered for competitive promotions under OPM regulations in effect at the pertinent time period and that he would have been selected for those promotions. In support of this contention, the agency relies on Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 850 F.2d 682 (Fed. Cir. 1988), and Boese v. Department of the Air Force, 784 F.2d 388, 390 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

[2] 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(1) provides in pertinent part that, consistent with implementing regulations of the Office of Personnel Management:

(1) the department or agency which was the last employer shall immediately and unconditionally accord the employee, if the injury or disability has been overcome within one year after the date of commencement of compensation or from the time compensable disability recurs if the recurrence begins after the injured employee resumes regular full-time employment with the United States, the right to resume his former or an equivalent position, as well as all other attendant rights which the employee would have had, or acquired, in his former position had he not been injured or disabled, including the right to tenure, promotion, and safeguards in reductions-in-force procedures....

(Emphasis supplied.)]

We find Naekel and Boese inapposite inasmuch as they do not concern the restoration rights of employees following a compensable injury under 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(1) and 5 C.F.R. Part 353. Rather, they involve the employees' reinstatement rights following reversal or mitigation of adverse actions effected under 5 U.S.C. Chapter 75. We note that there is no provision in Chapter 75 and its implementing regulations, nor in the Back Pay Act, 5 U.S.C. § 550.801 et seq., that grants employees any right to promotion consideration during the period they are off the agency's rolls. Thus, in Naekel and Boese, the court rejected the employees' contention that they were entitled to promotion rights and benefits, finding that, in the absence of a provision of law mandating a promotion during the period the employees were off the agencies' rolls, or the employees' clear showing that they would have been promoted, they were not entitled to promotion. See Naekel, 850 F.2d at 683-84; Boese, 784 F.2d at 390.

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(1), however, an agency is required to accord a restored employee any promotion rights he would have had but for his compensable injury or disability. See note 2, supra. Additionally, 5 C.F.R. § 353.201(a) (1987) provides in pertinent part that "[e]ach agency shall consider every employee absent because of compensable injury or military duty for all promotions for which he would be considered were he not absent." The regulation does not require actual promotion, only consideration for promotions. There is no requirement in that regulation that the employee prove that he would have been considered or selected for any promotion. As the administrative judge correctly pointed out, it is the agency that bears the burden of proving that it has complied with the restoration requirements of 5 C.F.R. Part 353. See Metsopulos v. United States Postal Service, 33 M.S.P.R. 536, 539 (1987); Whitfield v. United States Postal Service, 5 M.S.P.R. 239, 242 (1981).

We stated in Whitfield that, "[c]onsistent with the agency's burden of proof in appeals before the Board, 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1), the agency must submit evidence regarding all such available positions, and any restrictive qualification requirements" to show that it complied with 5 C.F.R. Part 353. The statutory provision at issue in Metsopulos and Whitfield was 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(2), which concerns the agency's obligations in restoring an employee who takes longer than one year to overcome a compensable injury or disability. However, 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(1), with which we are concerned here, imposes an additional burden on the agency to show that it considered for applicable promotions an employee who recovered within one year from a compensable injury or disability. See note 2, supra. The administrative judge properly found that the agency did not meet its burden of showing that it considered the appellant for promotions.
