a. Inability to Comply

An employee is not insubordinate if an injury known to the agency prevents the employee from performing an assigned task. Harmon v. VA, 10 MSPR 523, 526 (1982); see Allen v. Dept. of Army, 76 MSPR 564, 569 (1997) (appellant could not be required, on pain of insubordination, to return to the work site when his psychologist maintained that his return would be detrimental to his psychological health: "An agency may not charge an employee with insubordination for refusing an order where the record shows that the appellant is unable to carry out the order due to restrictions arising from illness or injury. See Castellanos v. Department of the Army, 62 M.S.P.R. 315, 322 (1994); Harmon v. Veterans Administration, 10 M.S.P.R. 523, 526 (1982)."); Castellanos v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 315, 322 (1994) ("We find that there is no record evidence contradicting the appellant's assertion that he was unable to continue working on May 24, 1990, because of gastrointestinal problems. We therefore find that his leaving work to obtain medical treatment from his own physician, despite Ms. Dove's orders to continue working, cannot form the basis of an adverse action."). But cf. Finney v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 47, 49-50 (1984) (removal for AWOL and a failure to follow instructions: the appellant did not demonstrate through medical evidence his inability to perform his assigned duties). An employee who is too ill to work is not insubordinate in refusing an order to work; however, the employee should state that he is sick rather than refusing the order and later offering his excuse. Mitchum v. TVA, 756 F.2d 82 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Mitchum v. TVA, 20 MSPR 117, 119 (1984).

An employee is not insubordinate if he explains that he is working on another project when asked to do a job. To show insubordination, the employee must refuse to set aside the other work when directed to perform the new assignment. Davies v. USDA, 5 MSPR 251, 254, 5 MSPB 291 (1981). With a charge of failure to complete work, the agency should show whether the assigned task was to be done to the exclusion of others or whether the assignment was only to be done if there was no other work. A charge of insubordination is not proved by evidence showing that an appellant attempted to, but could not, comply with instructions. Hall v. VA, 7 MSPR 161, 163, 7 MSPB 79 (1981); see Phillips v. GSA, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (unsuccessful attempt to accomplish an agency directive concerning the payment of debt was not the same as a refusal to obey the directive). But see Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 614 ¶ 39 (2001) (sustaining a charge of failure to complete assigned duties: "Although the assignment may not have been due at the time the agency detailed the appellant, and the appellant's detail may have relieved her from the responsibility of personally completing the assignment, the agency proved...that the assignment was not completed in a timely manner due to her failure to notify DECAM management that the assignment was still pending completion when she was detailed."); Clark v. GPO, 11 MSPR 363, 366 (1982) (removal for the failure to complete a task, although the instructions were somewhat confused, and AWOL). The closely-aligned charge of deliberate failure to follow instructions cannot be sustained if the appellant's ability to do his work depends on the operation of equipment that malfunctions. See Allen v. USDA, 37 MSPR 234, 238 (1988) (issue of whether the appellant failed to respond to a duty call because his pager was not working through no fault of his own; remanded to resolve issues concerning the fault of the appellant).
