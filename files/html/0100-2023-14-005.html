b. Potential for Future Harm

On occasion the Board considers a penalty (including a penalty based on off-duty conduct) based on what the conduct simplies as to potential (as opposed to actual) harm. Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 6, 9 (1995), involving the removal of an ordnance equipment mechanic at a weapons station following some off-duty use of marijuana at a bachelor party, commented:

[G]iven the nature of his position, the potential for the appellant's misconduct to cause severe harm and damage to life and property, and even failure of a military mission, justifies the penalty of removal. We note that removal was also proposed, but not effected because of early retirement, for two other employees in the appellant's shop who tested positive for a first offense of illegal drug use. In these circumstances, we find that the agency's decision to remove the appellant is in consonance with its zero tolerance policy and is not an abuse of discretion. We therefore defer to the penalty selected by the agency. Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 310, 313, 321.

Moore v. TVA, 23 MSPR 357, 360 (1984), sustained removal of a truck driver at a nuclear plant who reported to duty intoxicated:

In the present case, the record shows that the deciding official considered the appellant's 12-year service record, but concluded that the potential for harm resulting from the offense weighed heavily against such factor. In this respect, the record shows that the agency maintains a safety program with regular weekly safety meetings for employees. The job rule that the appellant was charged with violating is listed under the general heading of "Safety." The appellant acknowledged that he received a copy of the job rules. Paragraph 11c of the rules specifies that the penalty for being under the influence of intoxicants on the project is "discharge." The deciding official testified that he knew of no cases involving intoxication on the project where the removal penalty was not imposed. Since the appellant's job required him to drive a 2-ton truck at a nuclear plant construction project, it is not unreasonable for the agency to have a legitimate concern for the safety of the workforce.

In addition, contrary to the finding of the presiding official, we do not consider it to be of any mitigating value that the appellant was able to perform his duties on the morning of May 16, 1983, without causing injury to himself or others and without damaging any property. As the record supports the presiding official's conclusion that the appellant was under the influence of alcohol during this time, we find that the agency's concern for what he might have done and the deterrent effect that his removal would have on other employees were not unreasonable under the circumstances.

Cf. Macias v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0385-I-2 (NP 6/17/2016) ("Even if we were to consider only the aspects of this charge pertaining to the unsafe operation of the appellant's vehicle, we find that the agency was not obligated to wait for tragedy to occur before disciplining an employee for dangerous behavior.").
