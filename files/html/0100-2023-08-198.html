2. Suspension

5 USC 7512(2) establishes Board appellate jurisdiction over suspensions exceeding 14 days. An appealable suspension is defined by OPM regulations at 5 CFR 752.402 (2022):

Suspension means the placing of an employee, for disciplinary reasons, in a temporary status without duties and pay for more than 14 days.

A suspension for 14 days or less does not make the grade; adverse action appellate jurisdiction does not attach. Pugh v. USPS, 55 MSPR 32, 34 n.2 (1992); Powell v. Dept. of Army, 9 MSPR 237, 238 (1981) (distinguishing practice prior to the Reform Act; erroneous notice of an appeal right to the Board from the agency did not create Board jurisdiction); see Vitray v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3696) (if the appellant is constructively suspended by being placed into a nonduty and nonpay status and his supervisor orders him back to work before the appellant is out for more than 14 days, the Board lacks jurisdiction over the ensuing appeal; the employee may not establish jurisdiction by prolonging his absence after receiving a valid return to work order); Brown v. USPS, 81 MSPR 668, 670-71 ¶¶ 4-5 (1999) (remanding the appeal to the AJ to determine if the period of enforced leave was more than 14 days and within the Board's jurisdiction before the agency placed the appellant on administrative leave); cf. Gonzales v. USPS, 3 MSPR 570, 571, 4 MSPB 88 (1980) (since a 14-day suspension was unreviewable by the Board, also beyond the Board's jurisdiction was the allegation that the suspension was due to discrimination because of physical disability). To challenge short suspensions, other routes exist, including grievance procedures (administrative and contractual), USERRA and EEO complaints, and, for suspensions alleged to be in reprisal for whistleblowing, Individual Right of Action complaints to OSC and to the MSPB [described in Chapter 13] .

An appealable suspension occurred when an agency retroactively canceled the appellant's sick leave and placed him on LWOP, McHenry v. USPS, 121 MSPR 80, 82-83 ¶¶ 7-8, 2014 MSPB 30 (2014):

Regardless of the reason, it is undisputed that the Postmaster directed that the appellant's sick leave be retroactively changed to LWOP effective June 18, 2013. Because the agency completely and retroactively rescinded the appellant's sick leave and changed the nature of his leave status to LWOP before he filed this appeal, we find that he never received the sick leave he requested, and thus, he was never in a sick leave status for purposes of our jurisdictional analysis. See Lefavor v. Department of the Navy, 115 M.S.P.R. 120, ¶ 10 (2010) (the Board's jurisdiction is determined by the nature of an agency's action against an appellant at the time his appeal is filed). Accordingly, the suspension in this matter was not "constructive" and the case law concerning constructive suspensions is inapplicable here. Cf. Alston v. Social Security Administration, 95 M.S.P.R. 252, ¶ 11 (2003) (constructive suspension claims may arise when an agency places an employee on enforced leave pending an inquiry into her ability to perform her job, or when an employee absent from work for medical reasons requests to return to work with altered duties and the agency denies the request), aff'd, 134 Fed. Appx. 440 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

Based on the appellant's arguments below and on review, we find that the Board has jurisdiction over this appeal because the appellant was suspended when the agency retroactively rescinded his sick leave, directed him not to return to work, and placed him in an LWOP status for more than 14 days. The question of whether the appellant was ready, willing, and able to return to work is irrelevant to the issue of whether the agency suspended him. Rather, it is relevant to the issue of back pay. See Rutherford v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 570, ¶ 10 (2009). In addition, we find that the period of "emergency placement" in an LWOP status from August 12 to 20, 2013, was merely a continuation of the suspension, rather than a separate period of LWOP. Because the agency effected the suspension in this matter without affording the appellant minimum due process, we find that the suspension must be reversed. Edwards v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 196, ¶ 13 (2009).

A suspension is initiated by the agency. It is not the same as a constructive suspension, Maibie v. USPS, DE-0752-17-0030-I-1 (12/21/2022):

The record reflects that the appellant's placement on leave was involuntary and we agree with the administrative judge that the appellant established jurisdiction over an actual suspension of longer than 14 days. The agency took affirmative steps to instruct the appellant not to report to work. A constructive suspension, by contrast, arises when an absence that appears voluntary actually is not. Abbott, 121 M.S.P.R. 294, ¶ 7.

A suspension while an employee is on nonpay status may not be an appealable suspension. See Zamora v. DOD, 62 MSPR 338, 340 (1994) (Board lacked jurisdiction over the "suspension" of an individual while that employee was on LWOP status to serve as a union officer); Weaver v. USDA, 55 MSPR 569, 573-75 (1992) (if the agency "suspends" the employee when he is already in a nonduty, nonpay status (as, for example, when the employee is receiving OWCP benefits), he was not "placed" into that status and deprived of his duties and pay through the suspension).
