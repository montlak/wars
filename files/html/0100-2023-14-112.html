g. Past Discipline Time-Barred

An agency may not rely on disciplinary actions that have expired by their terms or due to an agency regulation, Gardner v. USPS, 44 MSPR 565, 570-71 n.6 (1990), law, rule, or negotiated agreement provision prohibiting their consideration after a period of time. Lewis v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 475, 485 (1991); see Kruer v. Dept. of Commerce, CH-0752-13-0595-I-1 (NP 8/28/2014) ("Even if the written reprimand meets the Bolling criteria, it provided that it would remain in the appellant's Official Personnel File for a minimum of 1 year, but no longer than 3 years from January 28, 2005, the date of issuance. Under the circumstances, reliance on the reprimand for purposes of enhancing the penalty was improper. Cantu v. Department of the Treasury, 88 M.S.P.R. 253, ¶ 6 (2001).");Thomas v. DHS, AT-0752-09-0824-I-1 (NP 9/10/2010) ("The Board has long held that an agency may not rely on disciplinary actions that have expired by their terms or by law, rule, or regulation. Lewis v. Department of the Air Force, 51 M.S.P.R. 475, 485 (1991); Gardner v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 M.S.P.R. 565, 570-71 n.6 (1990), aff'd, 983 F.2d 1087 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); Whitmore v. Department of the Navy, 34 M.S.P.R. 137, 140 (1987); Hyatt v. Department of the Army, 30 M.S.P.R. 256, 260, aff'd, 809 F.2d 791 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table). By its terms, the April 10, 2006 Letter of Warning was to remain in the appellant's OPF for a period of no more than 2 years, during which time it could be 'cited as evidence of prior disciplinary action.'"); Whitmore v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 137, 140 (1987) (time limits in an agency regulation barred the consideration of a cited past discipline); McArthur v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 303, 305 (1981) (considering as mitigating factor the agency's reliance on past disciplinary actions that were "outside the normal reckoning period"); cf. Reed v. VA, CH-0752-11-0872-I-1 (NP 2/8/2013) ("The appellant claims that, because the last chance agreement did not specifically provide to the contrary, his unauthorized absences from more than 4 years ago cannot be counted towards progressive discipline now because those absences 'expired' upon his successful completion of the agreement. Id. at 25 (citing Whitmore v. Department of the Navy, 34 M.S.P.R. 137 (1987)). In Whitmore, however, the Board decided that the agency improperly considered the appellant's past disciplinary offenses that were more than 3 years old because it violated an agency regulation prohibiting such consideration. Whitmore, 34 M.S.P.R. at 140. Here, the appellant does not identify an agency regulation or rule that would prevent the deciding official or the administrative judge from considering his previous suspensions in determining the appropriate penalty."). 5 USC 4303(c)(2)(A), limiting to one year the reckoning period for performance actions, does not apply to consideration of past discipline in misconduct cases. See McBriarty v. USDA, 12 MSPR 622, 625 n.1 (1982).

If an agency regulation requires withdrawal from personnel files of disciplinary actions exceeding a certain vintage, those actions cannot be considered in a pending adverse action. See Hyatt v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 256, 260 (1986) (reprimand whose lifespan was limited to three years was not revived by a subsequent disciplinary action citing the past reprimand; the adverse action was sustained, however, because of the seriousness of the charged misconduct); cf. Amell v. GSA, 7 MSPR 531, 534-35 n.1, 7 MSPB 382 (1981) (in assessing a defense of disparity in penalties, the Board could not consider a reprimand to another employee because the reprimand had been withdrawn after three years in accordance with agency regulations). The Board will reevaluate the penalty, after identifying the past discipline improperly relied on by the agency, in accordance with the table of penalties and Douglas factors. Whitmore v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 137, 140 n.* (1987); cf. Reed v. Dept. of Energy, SF-0752-11-0485-I-1 (NP 4/18/2012) ("The appellant argues that the deciding official improperly considered two prior reprimands and a letter of admonishment that had been expunged from agency records at least 6 years earlier, and that were based on charges other than those at issue in this action. The administrative judge correctly found that the consideration of those actions rendered the agency's penalty analysis flawed and therefore not worthy of deference. See, e.g., Von Muller v. Department of Energy, 101 M.S.P.R. 91, ¶ 19, aff'd, 204 F. App'x 17 (Fed. Cir. 2006). The administrative judge conducted his own penalty analysis, properly concluding that a 30-day suspension was the maximum reasonable penalty for the sustained charges.").

Although an agency may have erred in referring to a prior reprimand that should have been time-barred, the error was harmless. The deciding official stated that he tried not to consider the reprimand even though he was aware of it. Marotta v. DHHS, 34 MSPR 252, 261-62 (1987). In another appeal the agency probably should not have considered some past discipline because a settlement agreement called for its expungement. The doctrine of harmless error was invoked to avoid overturning the appellant's removal. The appellant's past disciplinary record was poor, with or without consideration of the settled case. The Board cryptically added that "the appeal of appellant's subsequent removal is not the forum for enforcement of the prior settlement agreement." Radziewicz v. USPS, 42 MSPR 692, 697 (1990); see Nelson v. USPS, 296 Fed. Appx. 927 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (settlement agreement might have precluded consideration of a past disciplinary action had the appellant not violated the agreement); Carrick v. USPS, 67 MSPR 280, 284 (1995) (erred in stating the duration of past suspensions or in characterizing past discipline; error was harmless when other record evidence demonstrated past warnings, counselings, and progressive discipline for the same offense leading to removal); Thornton v. DLA, 21 MSPR 163, 164 n.* (1984) (although the AJ erred concerning the reckoning period for the consideration of a past offense, the error was harmless because the AJ otherwise "properly found that the penalty of removal is within the limits of reasonableness"). Smith v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3066), interpreted a provision in a negotiated agreement that set a reckoning period for consideration of past discipline:

[I]n selecting the penalty of removal, the agency considered disciplinary actions taken against Mr. Smith on December 8, 1993, May 9, 1994, January 5, 1995, and September 12, 1995. Mr. Smith argues that, in removing him, the agency violated the provision of the collective bargaining agreement between his union and the agency which provides that, under certain circumstances, the agency may not consider past offenses more than two years old in fixing a penalty. The provision states:

The records of a disciplinary action against an employee shall not be considered in any subsequent disciplinary action if there has been no disciplinary action initiated against the employee for a period of two years.

The agency answers that, between December 8, 1993, and July 11, 1996, two years have not passed without the agency taking at least one disciplinary action against Mr. Smith. In fact, less than one year passed between each consecutive disciplinary action. Thus, according to the agency, the four disciplinary actions at issue were properly considered in assessing the penalty.

We agree. The provision prevents an agency from relying on old offenses only if the employee's record is clean for a period of two years starting after the most recent offense. In this case, between December 8, 1993, and July 11, 1996, there was never a period of two years during which Mr. Smith was not disciplined. Accordingly, the Board did not err in finding that it was proper for the agency to consider all four prior disciplinary actions....

If a contract bars consideration of past discipline as time barred, under Hammonds v. USPS, AT-0752-10-0399-I-1 (NP 8/31/2011), the prior discipline may still serve as notice of prohibited conduct:

We note that the record also shows that the appellant had a 14-day suspension in November 2002 for unacceptable conduct due to "apparent mishandling and attempted theft of mail," because he was intercepted with some batteries, medical samples and magazines. The agency's collective bargaining agreement precludes consideration of discipline more than 2 years old, and Wilkerson did not treat the 2002 suspension as a factor in deciding to remove the appellant. However, this 2002 suspension, like the one several months before the incident at issue herein, is evidence that the appellant was on clear notice that he was not to remove any unauthorized items from the workplace.

When, as here, an agency errs in considering prior discipline, the Board determines whether the agency's penalty selection may be affirmed without regard to the improperly considered discipline. Prather v. Department of Justice, 117 M.S.P.R. 137, ¶ 35 (2011)....

Moreover, he testified that, although he should not have considered the admonishment as prior discipline, the prior discipline demonstrated that the appellant was on notice about the type of misconduct involved in the present appeal. See Jinks v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 106 M.S.P.R. 627, ¶ 25 (2007) (stating that regardless of whether a prior disciplinary action is considered as an enhancing factor in the penalty determination, the agency can consider the prior action as notice that the appellant had been warned about the type of misconduct involved).

The passage of time alone may render irrelevant comparison of discipline. In Bennett v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 686, 687 (1983), the defense was that air traffic controllers who were fired in 1981 should have been treated in the same fashion as controllers who struck a decade earlier. The Board agreed with the AJ's analysis that the "1970 strike was too remote in time to constitute a substantially similar situation." Kehrier v. DOJ, 27 MSPR 477, 480 n.1 (1985), noted that a 1974 suspension may be too remote to be considered with charges that apparently arose in 1984; suspensions occurring in 1979 and 1980 were not, however, too remote to be considered as past discipline.
