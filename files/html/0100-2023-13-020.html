2. Overview of Constructive Adverse Action Appeals

There are two approaches to constructive adverse actions. They may be examined according to the usual basic cause cited--deceit, harassment, coercion, or mental incapacity of the appellant--without regard to the nature of the action. They may also be examined on the basis of classification of the action--downgrading, resignation, or retirement. The latter course is followed here. Agency action that vitiates a resignation may also destroy the validity of a retirement. Those researching a particular action should review discussion concerning other job actions to locate analogous cases. [Refer to Chapter 16, "Bases for Setting Aside a Settlement," and to Chapter 10, "Resignation," "Retirement," or "Transfer to Another Agency."]

There is at least the suggestion, albeit in a nonprecedential Federal Circuit decision, that an employee has an obligation to seek help from the agency to alleviate intolerable conditions before the employee employs self-help, severs the employment relationship, and then protests unemployment by filing an appeal seeking restoration of employment and backpay, Condit v. VA, AT-0752-10-0256-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010):

The Board assumes jurisdiction over an appeal by an employee who has resigned only if the employee proves by a preponderance of the evidence that his resignation was involuntary and thus tantamount to a forced removal. Staats v. U.S. Postal Service, 99 F.3d 1120, 1123-24 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Where, as here, an appellant alleges that his resignation was involuntary because of agency coercion, he must show that the agency effectively imposed the terms of the resignation or retirement, that he had no realistic option but to resign or retire, and that his resignation or retirement was the result of improper acts by the agency. Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc). Because the agency did not threaten or propose an adverse action against the appellant in this case, the appropriate test for voluntariness is whether, under all of the circumstances, working conditions were made so difficult by the agency that a reasonable person in the appellant's position would have felt compelled to resign or retire. See Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 577 (1996).

The Board has held that, in order to establish an involuntary personnel action based on intolerable working conditions, the employee must allege that he informed the agency of the existence of the objectionable conditions and must request assistance or remediation from the agency. Cf. Peoples v. Department of the Navy, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 8 (1999) (setting out requirements for constructive suspension based on intolerable working conditions). The appellant asserts that beginning in 2001, he raised concerns within the hospital chain of command about allegedly unsafe working conditions. The record further shows that between January 2004 and September 2006, the appellant wrote letters to various government officials concerning potential workplace violence as well as the deterioration of health care of veterans at the hospital. After his resignation, he also testified before Congress on two occasions, in May 2007 and May 2008, concerning the same issues. The appellant contends that by making these disclosures he "informed the agency of the existence of the objectionable conditions and...requested assistance or remediation from the agency." However, the appellant did not inform the agency that he himself suffered intolerable working conditions. The disclosures in question do not specifically concern the appellant's own working environment, but rather reflect his concern for fellow employees and the veterans under the agency's care.

The appellant further asserts that in 2002 he developed post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). However, the appellant has not alleged that he ever informed the agency of his PTSD, and in any event, his condition arose years before his resignation, whereas the focus in an involuntary resignation claim is on the circumstances immediately preceding the action. See Powers v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 43 M.S.P.R. 626, 630, aff'd, 918 F.2d 187 (Fed. Cir. 1990). Furthermore, while the agency's failure to respond to the appellant's complaints was no doubt frustrating, that alone would not have caused a reasonable person to feel compelled to resign.

In sum, the appellant failed to make allegations that, if proven, would establish that his resignation was an involuntary action within the Board's jurisdiction. Consequently, he was not entitled to a jurisdictional hearing. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985). This is the Board's final decision with regard to the appellant's involuntary resignation claim. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.113.

Peoples v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 216, 220 ¶ 9 (1999), cited in Condit, explained:

[T]he agency obviously must be put on notice of the specific nature of the conditions and the employee's inability to cope with them before it can be expected to investigate, attempt remediation of the conditions if necessary, or to consider finding other duties or positions for the employee pending resolution of the complaints. Cf. Thibodeaux, 76 MSPR at 181; Dize, 73 MSPR at 639-40 (employee required to inform agency of medical condition and seek assistance from the agency). The agency's knowledge of the intolerable working conditions, whether actual or constructive, must be shown in order to establish a culpable connection between the objectionable conditions and the agency's duty, if any, to alleviate the conditions. See Heining, 68 MSPR at 522 (employee must show that agency is responsible for conditions compelling the involuntary personnel action).

See Savage v. Dept. of Army, 122 MSPR 612, 626 ¶ 20, 2015 MSPB 51 (2015) ("To establish jurisdiction over a constructive suspension on the basis of intolerable working conditions, an appellant must show that a reasonable person would have felt compelled to absent herself under the conditions and that the agency was culpable for these conditions. Peoples v. Department of the Navy, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 5 (1999). Because no employee is entitled to leave work and remain absent without explanation, the appellant must inform the agency of the existence of the objectionable conditions and request assistance or remediation from the agency. Id., ¶ 8. The agency also must be notified of the specific nature of the conditions and the employee's inability to cope with them before the agency can be expected to investigate, attempt remediation of the conditions if necessary, or to consider finding other duties or positions for the employee pending resolution of the complaint. Id., ¶ 9.").

Often the employee does not seek remediation. The employ quits or retires and later appeals. Many of these appeals occur months, sometimes years, after the resignation, downgrading, or retirement. Some appeals are dismissed on grounds of timeliness, but many proceed because it cannot be said that the appellant knew of his appeal rights at the time of the action later challenged as adverse. How does an agency protect itself and the employee? Two ways spring to mind. First, any time an employee resigns, takes a demotion, or elects to retire, the agency should give the employee a copy of Board regulations and the appeal form for adverse actions. If nothing else, the employee will be alerted to appeal rights and if he should choose, months or years later, to file an appeal from his otherwise voluntary action, the appeal will likely be dismissed as untimely. Second, the agency may request (although it cannot require) the employee who is departing or downgrading to sign a statement to the effect that the action is voluntary, without coercion, and that the employee has had plenty of opportunity to think about and receive advice concerning his decision. If the employee does not want to sign the statement, that reluctance may indicate the employee thinks the action is involuntary and some questions may be in order, and he should be given the written advice and forms concerning appeal rights. Protective measures may result in a few appeals by people who would not otherwise then (or ever) have known of their appeal rights. More likely, however, this approach will reduce the number (or at least the duration) of appeals filed long after an action initiated by the employee. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Timeliness of Appeals" concerning time limits applicable to constructive adverse action appeals.]

As to actions resulting from settlements, e.g., resignations or retirements, and then appealed to the Board, no distinction is made between settlements reached in Board appeals and in cases litigated in other forums, e.g., EEOC. Under Perry v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-0752-12-0486-I-1 n.7 (NP 6/12/2013):

We discern no basis in this matter to distinguish between settlement agreements entered into before the Board and agreements entered into in other forums. Swink v. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 9 (2009), aff'd, 372 F. App'x 90 (Fed. Cir. 2010) ("[T]he Board will review the terms of a settlement agreement and the surrounding circumstances to determine if it retains jurisdiction over an appeal of an action that was settled in another procedural avenue.").
