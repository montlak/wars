A. FALSIFICATION AND MISREPRESENTATION

An agency has the right to expect its workers to be honest, trustworthy, and candid. An employee who knowingly gives false or inaccurate information to his supervisors "strikes at the very heart of the employee-employer relationship." Bradley v. VA, 39 MSPR 598, 604 (1989), rev'd, Bradley v. VA, 900 F.2d 233 (Fed. Cir. 1990); see Childress v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-14-0190-B-1 (NP 3/27/2023) ("The Board has long recognized that dishonest activity by a Federal employee raises serious doubts regarding the employee's reliability, trustworthiness, and continued fitness for employment. Scheffler, 117 M.S.P.R. 499, ¶ 16; Whelan v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 474, ¶ 13 (2006), aff'd, 231 F. App'x 965 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (Table); Kirkpatrick v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 583, 591 (1997)."); Wier v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 348, 354 (1992) ("falsification of an agency document goes to the core of the employer-employee relationship and impeaches the employee's reliability, veracity, trustworthiness, and ethical conduct"; "an employer is entitled to honesty and truthfulness from its employees in every aspect of their employment"); King v. Dept. of Labor, 37 MSPR 110, 114 (1988) (employer has the right to demand that employees be honest and truthful in every facet of their employment (citing NLRB v. Mueller Brass Co., 509 F.2d 704, 713 (5th Cir. 1975))); NLRB v. Wells Fargo Armored Services Corp., 597 F.2d 7, 10 (1st Cir. 1979) (unfair labor practice case: "Any employer has the right to demand that its employees be honest and truthful in every facet of their employment. Absent an antiunion motivation, any employer has the right to discipline an employee for his dishonesty or untruthfulness."); Gustave-Schmidt v. Dept. of Labor, 87 MSPR 667, 678 ¶ 19 (2001) (sustaining an arbitrator's award concluding that the plagiarism of research material justified removal); Brown v. DLA, 65 MSPR 436, 445 (1994) (falsification of work certifications warranted a reduction in grade and pay to a nonsupervisory position; an additional suspension for 30 days was unwarranted due to an otherwise unblemished record and the agency's failure to support a portion of its charges); Montgomery v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 667, 670 (1984) ("Submission of a false document to the agency, regardless of the motivation, is a breach of the employer/employee relationship and constitutes a proper basis for disciplinary action."); Giesler v. Dept. of Transp., 3 MSPR 277, 281, 3 MSPB 367 (1980) ("It can hardly be challenged that an agency has the right to expect its employees to be truthful in all matters of official business.").

Falsification is a serious offense, Hanna v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 233, 240 (1989), warranting severe disciplinary action. Schoeffler v. USDA, 47 MSPR 80, 86 (1991) (Schoeffler was modified by Boo v. DHS, 122 MSPR 100, 2014 MSPB 86 (2014), to require an element of material gain in a falsification case). "[R]emoval for falsification and engaging in dishonest activity promotes the efficiency of the service since such behavior raises serious doubts regarding the employee's reliability, trustworthiness, and continued fitness for employment." Kirkpatrick v. USPS, 74 MSPR 583, 591 (1997); cf. Reed v. OPM, 74 MSPR 616, 622 (1997) (even one intentional false statement in the employment process is sufficient to support a negative suitability determination).

As to nexus, Stanford v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0444-I-1 (NP 9/15/2016):

Falsification is inherently destructive of the agency's faith in an employee's trustworthiness and honesty, essential elements in the relationship of an employer and employee. Connett v. Department of the Navy, 31 M.S.P.R. 322, 328 (1986), aff'd, 824 F.2d 978 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table). The Board has consistently found a nexus between falsification of records and the efficiency of the service. See Washington v. Department of Agriculture, 22 M.S.P.R. 374, 376 (1984); Trybul v. Department of the Army, 22 M.S.P.R. 290, 292 (1984), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1059 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table)....

Charges of misrepresentation, falsification, and lying require the same elements of proof; they include an element of material gain to the appellant's benefit. Boo v. DHS, 122 MSPR 100, 107-109 ¶¶ 11-14, 2014 MSPB 86 (2014), made plain the necessity for proving the element of material gain:

Although the Board previously has stated that it is unaware of any case holding that financial gain is required to prove a falsification charge, see Seas v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 422, 427 (1997);[2] see also Schoeffler v. Department of Agriculture, 47 M.S.P.R. 80, 84, vacated in part, 50 M.S.P.R. 143, enforcement dismissed, 51 M.S.P.R. 20 (1991), in Leatherbury, the Federal Circuit subsequently explained that "the intent element [of falsification]...requires two distinct showings: (a) that the employee intended to deceive or mislead the agency; and (b) that he intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain." Leatherbury, 524 F.3d at 1300 (citing Bradley v. Veterans Administration, 900 F.2d 233, 237 (Fed. Cir. 1990)). Notwithstanding the Board's suggestions in Seas and Schoeffler, the vast majority of the Board's cases addressing falsification--including some of its earliest case law discussing the contours of this charge--either expressly or implicitly considered whether an employee's misrepresentation was made for personal gain. See, e.g., Lacapurcia v. Department of the Army, 27 M.S.P.R. 514, 519 (1985) (finding that the agency failed to demonstrate that the employee intentionally falsified time records for personal gain); Allen v. U.S. Postal Service, 2 M.S.P.R. 420, 426 (1980) (finding that there was not a scintilla of evidence to suggest that the appellant completed his timecard with the intent to obtain 10 or 15 minutes of undeserved pay).

[2] Seas was last cited by the Board for this specific proposition in Gebhardt v. Department of the Air Force, 99 M.S.P.R. 49, ¶ 8 (2005), aff'd, 180 Fed. Appx. 951 (Fed. Cir. 2006). Each of these decisions predates the Federal Circuit's decision in Leatherbury, which was issued in 2008. Although the Board also cited Seas in Prather v. Department of Justice, 117 M.S.P.R. 137, ¶¶ 17-21 (2011), and Crump v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 114 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶¶ 6-9 (2010), in both cases there was no dispute that the appellants had personally benefited from their misrepresentations concerning, inter alia, outside employment activities and educational qualifications.

In light of the Federal Circuit's articulation of the elements of falsification in Leatherbury, and in keeping with the Board's historical approach to the elements of falsification, we MODIFY Seas and Schoeffler to the extent that they suggest that an agency does not have to establish that an employee personally benefited or gained from his misrepresentation when assessing the elements of a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying. See Leatherbury, 524 F.3d at 1300. Accordingly, we find that in order to establish a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying, the agency must establish by a preponderance of the evidence that, inter alia, the employee "intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain."[3] See id.; see also Harrison v. Department of Agriculture, 411 Fed. Appx. 312, 315 (Fed. Cir. 2010) (citing and relying on Leatherbury) ("To prevail on [a charge of falsification], the agency was required to prove that the statement was false, that it was material, and that the employee acted with the intent to deceive the agency for her private gain.").

[3] We do not believe that the Federal Circuit intended to develop a unique set of elements for a charge of falsification based only on an allegation of fraud, and we find that the test set forth in Leatherbury applies to instances of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying involving an employee's attempt to defraud, deceive, or mislead an agency for his own private material gain. See Leatherbury, 524 F.3d at 1300. We note, moreover, that the Board has historically used the terms defraud, deceive, and mislead interchangeably when discussing a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying, and there is nothing in Leatherbury to suggest that the court's articulation of the elements of falsification was limited to instances of fraud. Compare Eilertson v. Department of the Navy, 23 M.S.P.R. 152, 156 (1984) (using deceive or defraud in explaining standard), aff'd, 770 F.2d 179 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table), with Allen, 2 M.S.P.R. at 425 (only reciting defraud as part of standard).

In making this clarification to our cases establishing the elements of a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying, we emphasize that the definition of "own private material gain" is quite broad, and that "the advantage to be secured [by the employee] is by no means limited to monetary gains arising from a falsification." See Bradley, 900 F.2d at 237 n.6 (examples of gain include, inter alia, compensation, overtime payment, per diem reimbursement, travel reimbursement, Office of Workers' Compensation Programs compensation, and offers of employment). Thus, charges of falsification, misrepresentation, and lying have been sustained when there is evidence that the employee engaged in falsification with the intent to influence pending disciplinary proceedings, to secure employment, to use sick leave to avoid being placed on absence without leave (AWOL) or leave without pay (LWOP), and to hide or obscure facts in order to prevent the agency from taking disciplinary or corrective action. See Ahles v. Department of Justice, 768 F.2d 327, 330 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (intent to influence disciplinary proceeding); see also Rigilano v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 614, 618 n.4 (1988) (submission of falsified medical information with intent to avoid placement on LWOP or AWOL); Delessio v. U.S. Postal Service, 33 M.S.P.R. 517, 520-21 (intent to secure employment), aff'd, 837 F.2d 1096 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table); Pitts v. Department of the Air Force, 29 M.S.P.R. 108, 109-10 (1985) (intent to prevent the agency from taking disciplinary action by obfuscating misconduct), aff'd, 795 F.2d 1020 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table).

In making this clarification, we further note that, in cases where there is no evidence that an employee has engaged in falsification, misrepresentation, or lying with the intent to defraud, deceive, or mislead the agency for his own private material gain, agencies may still charge an employee with lack of candor,[4] and they may include among the proposed aggravating factors the intentional nature of an employee's misconduct. See Ludlum, 278 F.3d at 1284 (lack of candor is a broad and flexible concept); see also Chavez v. Small Business Administration, 121 M.S.P.R. 168, ¶ 6 (2014) (lack of candor does not require a showing of intent); Arena v. U.S. Postal Service, 121 M.S.P.R. 125, ¶ 6 (2014) (the Board will consider the nature and seriousness of misconduct when reviewing the reasonableness of an agency-imposed penalty, including whether the offense was intentional or frequently repeated). Alternatively, agencies may charge an employee with the substantive submission of inaccurate information or a more generic charge of conduct unbecoming, neither of which involve an element of intent, and they may include among the proposed aggravating factors the intentional nature of the appellant's conduct in leveling such charges. See Cross v. Department of the Army, 89 M.S.P.R. 62, ¶¶ 8-10 (2001).

[4] Although the colloquial understanding of a lack of candor might not equate to the usage of this term in federal civil service law, we believe the Federal Circuit's holding in Ludlum that lack of candor is a broad and flexible concept is designed to capture all of those instances which do not otherwise meet the definition of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying. See Ludlum, 278 F.3d at 1283-84 (lack of candor and falsification are different, although related, forms of misconduct, and the latter is not a necessary element of the former); see also Hoofman v. Department of the Army, 118 M.S.P.R. 532, ¶¶ 13-14 (2012) (finding the agency charged the employee with lack of candor when it alleged that the employee attempted to deceive his supervisor by covering up both his arrest and the impounding of his government-owned vehicle which did not involve an affirmative misrepresentation on the employee's part), aff'd, 526 Fed. Appx. 982 (Fed. Cir. 2013).

The element of material gain was discussed in Tyner v. USDA, DC-0752-16-0098-I-1 (NP 9/7/2016), involving the charge that the appellant did not provide an accurate description in an agency report of what caused a workplace injury:

The Board has found that charges of misrepresentation and falsification require the same elements of proof. Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 10 (citing Guerrero v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 105 M.S.P.R. 617, ¶ 9 (2007)). To establish a charge of falsification or misrepresentation, the agency must prove by preponderant evidence that the appellant knowingly supplied incorrect information and that he did so with the intention of defrauding the agency. O'Lague v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 123 M.S.P.R. 340, ¶ 6 (2016); Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 10. The intent element requires two distinct showings: (1) that the appellant intended to deceive or mislead the agency; and (2) that he intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain. Leatherbury v. Department of the Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 2008); O'Lague, 123 M.S.P.R. 340, ¶ 6; Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶¶ 11-12.

Here, although the administrative judge analyzed the charge as a charge of misrepresentation or falsification, she failed to address the second component of the intent element, which requires the agency to prove that the appellant intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain. See Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 12 (finding that to establish a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying, an agency must prove that an employee intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain). We find that the agency failed to prove this element of a misrepresentation charge.

While the definition of "private material gain" is quite broad and is not limited to monetary gains, see Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 13, here, the agency did not present evidence regarding any form of private material gain that the appellant hoped to achieve in submitting the inaccurate incident report. Cf. Ahles v. Department of Justice, 768 F.2d 327, 330 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (finding that an agency proved intent to defraud where an appellant testified that he submitted false information to influence a pending disciplinary proceeding against him). The appellant asserted that he provided the incorrect information on the incident report because he knew that J.L. was on parole and he did not want to cause J.L. to be sent back to prison. Although it is not clear why the appellant would want to protect J.L. from returning to prison after J.L. attacked him with a hammer, the agency did not challenge the appellant's testimony to this effect on cross-examination at the hearing, or present evidence regarding any other reason that the appellant misrepresented facts in the incident report. Regardless of whether the appellant's explanation for his actions was ultimately credible, the burden of proof of the charge lies with the agency, and the agency did not present any evidence to prove the element of private material gain. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(b)(1)(ii).

For this reason, we find that the agency failed to prove the charge of misrepresentation. See Parker v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 122 M.S.P.R. 353, ¶ 15 (2015) (finding that an agency failed to prove a specification of filing false charges when it did not demonstrate that an appellant made a false representation with the intent to defraud, deceive, or mislead the agency for her own personal material gain); Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 15 (finding that a charge of falsification could not be sustained when the record lacked any evidence that an appellant intended to defraud, deceive, or mislead the agency for his own private material gain, but instead indicated that he may have acted to assist another individual). Accordingly, we modify the initial decision to find that the agency did not prove a misrepresentation charge.

The Board consistently determines that severe penalties in falsification cases promote service efficiency. See Marsh v. USPS, 66 MSPR 47, 51 (1994) (false mileage reimbursement claim). Although whether an act of falsification warrants removal must be determined in light of the circumstances of each case, Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 186, 202 (1994), removal for falsification of government documents promotes service efficiency because falsification raises serious doubts regarding the employee's honesty, reliability, trustworthiness, and fitness for employment. See Reed v. OPM, 74 MSPR 616, 622 (1997) ("The Board has specifically found that intentional falsification of government documents could reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent effective performance in a federal position or prevent the effective performance by an employing agency of its duties and responsibilities."); Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 242 (1995) ("Removal for falsification of employment documents promotes the efficiency of the service because such falsification raises serious doubts regarding the employee's honesty and fitness for employment."); Tanner v. Dept. of Transp., 65 MSPR 169, 172 (1994) ("The substance of a falsification charge is the issue of the employee's honesty."); Stewart-Maxwell v. USPS, 56 MSPR 265, 275 (1993) ("The Board has held that removal for falsification of government documents promotes the efficiency of the service because such falsification raises serious doubts regarding the employee's honesty and fitness for employment, and her reliability and trustworthiness.").

The falsification itself, not necessarily the nature of the information falsified, raises serious doubts concerning the employee's honesty and fitness for employment. Beardsley v. DOD, 55 MSPR 504, 512 (1992). Submission of a false document is a breach of the employer-employee relationship without regard to motivation and serves as a proper basis for discipline. The magnitude of the discipline depends on the circumstances. Montgomery v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 667, 670 (1984), followed in Fuqua v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 173, 177 (1986) (submission of a false document to the agency (sick leave request), regardless of the motivation, breaches the employer-employee relationship and constitutes a proper basis for discipline). Falsification of records goes to the appellant's reliability, veracity, trustworthiness, and ethical conduct and directly impacts on service efficiency. Removal for falsification of records promotes the efficiency of the service. See Fuqua v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 173, 177-78 (1986); Trybul v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 290, 292 (1984) ("Falsification of records is an action which goes to appellant's reliability, veracity, trustworthiness and ethical conduct and thus directly impacts upon the efficiency of the service."). Akin to falsification are charges that an employee misused government documents to obtain a benefit. Misuse of a prescription form to obtain medications supports removal. King v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-561); see Hawkins v. VA, 13 MSPR 143, 144-45 (1982) (nursing assistant was removed for using a forged VA prescription form to obtain a controlled narcotic substance). Use of forged prescriptions for medication by a nursing assistant warranted removal in Worsley v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-584), and Holcombe v. VA, 12 MSPR 68, 72 (1982) (removal of supervisory clerk who forged the signature of a physician on prescription forms used at a VA pharmacy; it was immaterial that the appellant, as a veteran, was entitled to treatment at the hospital).

Little distinguishes submission of a false statement with the intent to deceive from the offense of intent to defraud. Implicit in the finding that an appellant falsified documents with the intent to deceive or mislead the agency is the finding that he intended to defraud the agency; a showing of either, along with a showing that the appellant knowingly supplied the wrong information, supports a charge of falsification of government documents. Arenz v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 88, 94 (1991). Because the underlying concern in falsification cases centers on the trustworthiness of the employee, it is not necessary for an agency to establish that it relied upon falsified documents before taking action against the employee. Russell v. EEOC, 11 MSPR 90, 92 (1982) (incorrect information on a SF-171 was used to evaluate the appellant for promotion); see Beardsley v. DOD, 55 MSPR 504, 509 (1992) (omission on a SF-86, Questionnaire for Sensitive Positions: "[A]n agency is not required to establish that it detrimentally relied upon an employee's falsification of an employment document for a charge of falsification to be sustained[.] "); Wayne v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 322, 329 (1992) (failure to disclose the use of an assumed identity on a SF-171 and other forms; proof of detrimental reliance by the agency was not required for a falsification charge to be sustained); Beardsley v. DOD, 52 MSPR 457, 463 (1992) (omission concerning the circumstances of a termination of prior employment); Warnock v. DOJ, 38 MSPR 457, 459-60 (1988) (a statement on a SF-171 concerning a bogus college degree; "hindsight by an agency official is not dispositive as to whether the employee would in fact have been hired in the absence of false information on the employment form").

A false statement case is demonstrated if, with the requisite intent, the appellant made a knowingly false statement or made a false statement with a reckless disregard of the truth, Eilertson v. Dept. of Navy, 23 MSPR 152, 157 (1984) (submitting a false W-4 withholding certificate with the agency as part of a plan to challenge tax laws), or with a conscious purpose to avoid learning the truth. Greene v. OPM, 29 MSPR 210, 211 (1985) (falsification of a preemployment medical questionnaire). It may be material to determine whether the appellant knew that what he was submitting was a forgery or false statement. Rigilano v. USPS, 37 MSPR 614, 618-19 (1988) (appellant claimed he did not know that the medical certificate procured on his behalf by his wife was false; the Board did not credit the appellant's explanation).

Fraud need not be designed to obtain money; it may have another purpose, e.g., influencing a disciplinary proceeding involving the appellant. Ahles v. DOJ, INS, 768 F.2d 327, 330 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Mings v. DOJ, 813 F.2d 384, 389 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (implied threat to change testimony in an effort to induce the agency to hire the appellant's son); Seas v. USPS, 73 MSPR 422, 427 (1997) ("We are unaware of any case holding that financial gain is required to prove a falsification charge"; the "absence, presence, or amount of gain, financial or otherwise, may be a factor in assessing the reasonableness of the penalty for an act of falsification."), following remand, 78 MSPR 569, 574 (1998); Schoeffler v. USDA, 47 MSPR 80, 84 (1991) (charge of falsification may be sustained on a determination that the employee intended to deceive or mislead the agency, irrespective of personal gain) (Seas and Schoeffler were modified to require an element of material gain by Boo v. In order to prove falsification of an official document, the agency must prove that the appellant possessed the specific intent to falsify and that the appellant provided the false information with the intent to deceive or mislead the agency....
