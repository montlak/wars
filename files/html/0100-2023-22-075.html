3. Appellant Who Ensnares the Agency

Wise v. MSPB, 780 F.2d 997, 999 (Fed. Cir. 1985), provides additional insight into the Federal Circuit's view of the substantial innocence test, applied to an air traffic controller who did not engage in a strike but who failed to bring information to the attention of his agency that might have quashed the proposed removal before the employee was terminated and forced to invoke the appellate process. The decision is significant because it allows an exception to the result-oriented substantial innocence test if the appellant could have but did not avoid the adverse action at the time agency proceedings commenced. First, reviewing Yorkshire, the court declared:

The whole theory behind category 2's "substantially innocent" concept is that an individual who is substantially innocent of the charges brought against him, and nevertheless has a case brought against him, but is ultimately vindicated by the Board, is entitled to attorney fees in the interest of justice. In Allen, the MSPB conducted a thorough review of the legislative history of the "in the interest of justice" concept as embodied in the Civil Service Reform Act and discerned that the Congress was troubled with the inherent injustice of dragging an innocent individual through expensive and time-consuming legal proceedings.

Wise established guidance on how employee fault could detract from substantial innocence, 780 F.2d at 1000:

It is implicit in the legislative history of the Civil Service Reform Act and the Board's decision in Allen that the "substantially innocent" standard is not satisfied by an individual such as petitioner who (1) knows that he is substantially innocent of the charges brought against him, (2) can prove his substantial innocence, and (3) deliberately does not communicate all the facts to the deciding official which would lead the deciding official to rule against the removal action.[4]

[4] The Board has expressly stated that "[i]n making [a substantially innocent determination], the presiding official should examine the degree of fault on the employee's part and the existence of any reasonable basis for the agency's action." Allen, 2 MSPB at 593, n.35.

Such an individual brings the consequences of the agency's legal proceedings upon himself and is in no way an innocent victim who is entitled to the award of attorney fees in the interest of justice.[5]

[5] Although petitioner cites the decision in Yorkshire to support his request for attorney fees, that decision did not cover a case where the employee intentionally prevented the deciding official from deciding the matter with full knowledge of the relevant facts.

As for petitioner's argument that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits, we do not find arbitrary or capricious the presiding official's conclusion that it was not unreasonable for the deciding official to remove petitioner based on the charges and the information available to him at that time....

...

In sum we hold that the decision of the Board denying petitioner's request for attorney fees is not arbitrary, capricious, contrary to law, or unsupported by substantial evidence. Attorney fees are not warranted in the interest of justice when the administrative process is deliberately and improperly drawn out by the actions of the prevailing party himself.

The Federal Circuit reaffirmed that it is not in the interest of justice to grant counsel fees to a person who withholds exonerating information from agency decisional officials. Walker v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-530). But the evasion must be fairly blatant for the fee award to be denied. That some evidence is withheld until the Board hearing will not defeat the award unless it can be said with conviction that the evidence would have truly determined the outcome of the case. In Ledeaux v. VA, 31 MSPR 585, 588 n.3 (1986), the agency argued that polygraph results should have been presented to the deciding official before they were submitted to the AJ. Given the agency's objection to the use of the evidence as unreliable, however, it could not be said that the outcome with the deciding official would have been different had he received the evidence during the reply stage of the case.
