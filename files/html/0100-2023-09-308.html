a. Material in Control of Third Party

The Board's discovery regulations permit sanctions against parties in control of evidence that should be produced; however, sanctions can also be directed against a party because of refusal of third parties to produce evidence within their control. 5 CFR 1201.43 (2022) provides that sanctions may be imposed as necessary to serve the ends of justice. That the agency does not possess the evidence is not, by itself, a proper basis to avoid sanctions against the employing agency. In Rooney v. USDA, 26 MSPR 142, 146 (1985), sanctions were considered but not imposed when another agency, INS, declined to produce information arguably relevant to the adverse action imposed against the appellant by the Agriculture Department based on a charge that the appellant interfered with an INS surveillance operation:

As the presiding official noted in his initial decision, the portion of the Board's regulations which specifically provides for the imposition of sanctions for failure to produce evidence refers only to "evidence within the party's control." 5 CFR § 1201.43(a). Clearly, the evidence at issue here was within the control of the INS, rather than the agency which is a party to this case. We note, however, that 5 CFR § 1201.43 provides that sanctions may be imposed "as necessary to serve the ends of justice," and that the sanctions are "not limited to the instances set forth in...this section." We agree with the presiding official that Harvey Aluminum (Incorporated) v. National Labor Relations Board, 335 F.2d 749 (9th Cir. 1964), is applicable to this case, and that, under appropriate circumstances, sanctions may be imposed against the agency which is a party to a case before the Board as a consequence of the failure of a nonparty agency to produce evidence necessary to a proper adjudication. The fact that the agency in this case does not possess the evidence at issue therefore does not, by itself, constitute a proper basis on which to overturn the initial decision.

Rooney continued with a discussion and resolution of the law enforcement privilege and materiality issues raised by the agency concerning the information sought by the appellant from the INS.

The question arose of whether sanctions could be imposed against the employing agency if a related nonparty failed to cooperate in discovery after a Bureau of Prisons employee sought to compel the Marshals Service and the FBI to produce documents and witnesses at a hearing. The Marshals Service, the Bureau of Prisons, and the FBI are wholly-owned subsidiaries of the Department of Justice. Although the Board reserved judgment, its opinion, Puhar v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 204, 206 (1986) (also reported at 31 MSPR 71 (1986)), warrants attention:

Section 1201.43(a) of the Board's regulations provides for the imposition of sanctions for failure to produce "evidence within the party's control." The Board is not precluded under appropriate conditions, however, from imposing sanctions against a party to a case before the Board as a consequence of the failure of a nonparty federal agency to produce evidence necessary to a proper adjudication. Rooney v. Department of Agriculture, 26 MSPR 142, 146 (1985); see Harvey Aluminum v. National Labor Relations Board, 335 F.2d 749 (9th Cir. 1964). Section 1201.43 provides that sanctions may be imposed "as necessary to serve the ends of justice," and that the sanctions are "not limited to the instances set forth in...this section." The fact that the Bureau of Prisons is not in control of the evidence or testimony at issue, therefore, does not by itself constitute grounds for not imposing sanctions. The same considerations apply to the question of subpoena enforcement.

The Bureau of Prisons, the Federal Bureau of Investigation and the United States Marshals Service are all organizational components of the Department of Justice. While each component may not be accountable to the other, each is ultimately accountable to the Department of Justice and the Attorney General. The Department of Justice is of course obligated to assure that its organizational components cooperate in the adjudicatory processes of the Board. Accordingly, the Department of Justice is hereby ordered to show cause on or before 20 days from the date of this order why the Board should not remand this case to the presiding official for consideration of the imposition of sanctions or seek enforcement of the outstanding subpoenas against the FBI and the Marshals Service pursuant to 5 USC § 1205(c)....
