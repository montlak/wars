b. Level of Notification

The obligation to provide notice of changes in conditions of employment rests with the agency or activity. Dept. of Army, Soldier Support Ctr., Ft. Benjamin Harrison and AFGE Local 1411, 41 FLRA 926, 940 (1991), modified on other grounds, 48 FLRA 6 (1993). When management plans a change in working conditions, e.g., a reorganization, there may be a question as to what level of the union should receive notice of the change. In Air Force Logistics Command, Aerospace Guidance and Metrology Ctr., Newark and AFGE Local 2221, 4 FLRA 512, 513 (1980) (rev'd, U.S. Air Force v. FLRA, 681 F.2d 466 (6th Cir. 1982), post), an employee, who happened to be a union steward, attended a meeting and learned of the agency's plans for reorganization, but decided not to seek bargaining because he did not think that the reorganization would have an impact. The question before FLRA was whether the union was properly on notice when the steward learned of the reorganization:

The Administrative Law Judge determined that Union steward Price was an appropriate individual to receive notice on behalf of the Union. He further determined that Price's presence at the staff meeting, albeit as an employee, and his testimony that he decided not to seek bargaining because he considered the reorganization at this time to have no impact, constituted a reasonable basis upon which to conclude the Union had sufficient notice of the reorganization. Accordingly, he recommended the complaint be dismissed on the grounds the Union had sufficient notice and failed to request bargaining.

Section 7103(12) of the Statute defines collective bargaining in terms of the "mutual obligation" of the parties to consult and bargain in good faith. The goal of balanced, mutual responsibility on the part of the parties is also stressed in the legislative history of the Statute. Thus, the collective bargaining relationship envisaged by the Statute requires that each party have the ability to function as an equal partner within the relationship. It follows that each party should therefore deal with the other with the directness and dignity appropriate to partners on an equal footing.

There is no evidence in this case that Respondent in any way sought to give appropriate notice of the reorganization to the Union. While the Union might have requested negotiations based upon the chance knowledge of its agent, the failure of the Activity to give appropriate notice to that agent or other union official as a union representative impeded the ability of the Union to perform as an equal partner with Activity management. Thus, Respondent's failure to provide appropriate advance notice to the Union did not comply with the obligation to consult in good faith as required by the Statute, in violation of section 7116(a)(5) and (1) of the Statute.

The result in Local 2221 was reversed and enforcement denied by the Sixth Circuit, U.S. Air Force v. FLRA, 681 F.2d 466, 468 (6th Cir. 1982):

The FLRA's resolution of this case is self-contradictory. On the one hand it finds the union to have received actual notice through Price, but on the other it determines that petitioner violated the statute because that actual notice was not delivered to Price in his role as a union representative. We fail to see how Price, in his role as a human being, could not receive actual notice as an employee and formal notice as a union steward by his singular attendance at the reorganization meeting. Labor statutes such as the one at issue here are designed, in part, to smooth labor-management relations by providing informal mechanisms to guide the operation of the workplace and the resolution of disputes. The Authority's decision appears to interject needless formality into that process....

FLRA may follow the reasoning of the Sixth Circuit, see VAMC, Muskogee and AFGE Local 2250, 19 FLRA 1054, 1056 n.6 (1985), but FLRA may require a more formal, advance notice to the union. See Dept. of Air Force, Sacramento Air Logistics Ctr., McClellan AFB and AFGE Local 1857, 29 FLRA 594, 606-07 (1987); cf. Dept. of Air Force, Willow Grove ARS and NAGE Local R3-32, 57 FLRA 852, 856 (2002) (actual notice was sufficient to trigger union's responsibility to request bargaining or waive right to bargain), reconsideration granted, result unchanged, 58 FLRA 277 (2003); Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs and Indian Educators Fed., 52 FLRA 1442, 1451-52 (1997) (ALJ Decision) (agency committed ULP by dealing directly with employees, despite the presence of shop steward, when group was informed that union would be notified at higher level of proposed changes in working conditions).

If there are levels of exclusive recognition subordinate to the level of the agency or primary national subdivision that issued a standards of conduct regulation, it is incumbent upon agency management at the level of exclusive recognition to notify the exclusive representative of its employees and give the latter an opportunity to request bargaining concerning the regulation before implementation. DLA and NAGE, 12 FLRA 412, 417 (1983). If recognition exists at the national level, without some delegation, negotiations over local changes must also occur at the national level, so that if a local change is generated, the agency is responsible for notification of and bargaining with the union at the national level of recognition. This is particularly so if the local changes result from some change in national policy implemented at a local level. DOD, AAFES and AFGE, 19 FLRA 652, 664-66 (1985) (ALJ Decision). Although it may be difficult for a national office of a union to police or review notices of changes in many local agency installations, if the level of bargaining is established at the national level, and there is no delegation scheme, management does not commit a ULP by notifying the national office of the union of a change affecting a local component of the agency and implementing that change without a request for bargaining from the national office. DHHS, SSA and AFGE Local 3186, 18 FLRA 73, 82-83 (1985) (ALJ Decision). No ULP is committed by an agency that does not elevate local implementation of a regional directive to the national level of recognition if past practice and past dealings establish delegations of bargaining authority to levels below the national level of recognition. DHHS, SSA, Dallas Region and AFGE, Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, 23 FLRA 807, 810 (1986). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Bargaining Delegation; Local Bargaining."]
