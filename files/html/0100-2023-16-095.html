H. DEFENSES

A full range of affirmative defenses is available in a performance action, including EEO, harmful error, whistleblower reprisal, and constitutional due process objections. Van Lancker v. DOJ, 119 MSPR 514, 2013 MSPB 42 (2013) (Vice Chairman Wagner dissenting), determined that an FBI employee could not raise a whistleblower affirmative defense in a Chapter 43 performance action, principally because of the exclusionary effect of the separate whistleblower statutory protections for FBI employees. [Van Lancker is discussed in Chapter 13, "Federal Bureau of Investigation."]

The same rules of adjudication applied to defenses raised in misconduct cases apply to performance actions. If an argument concerning performance standards is to be raised, it must be raised with the AJ. Arguments raised for the first time in a PFR will not be considered by the Board without evidence that they are based on new and material evidence. Lorentz v. Dept. of Labor, 27 MSPR 231, 232 n.* (1985). [Refer to Chapters 2 and 5 for the many decisions concerned with presentation and timing of claims during the appellate process.]

Defenses typically raised in performance cases include the basic building blocks of a performance case, discussed earlier, whether: OPM approved the agency's appraisal system or major changes to it; the appellant's performance standards were not sufficiently objective or were unreasonable, unattainable, or defined in a manner that, although objective, defied measurement or quantitative analysis; the length of the PIP was unreasonably short (or long); the supervisor interfered with the ability of the employee to perform during the PIP; the employee was overloaded with work during the PIP or was not given promised guidance or assistance; and the action was motivated by discriminatory animus or reprisal. One defense that the Board has not entertained is the contention that the appellant should never have been selected for the job, Livingston v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 273, 275 (1985), a Chapter 43 performance case:

Appellant next argues that the presiding official erred in finding the Board had no jurisdiction to address his contentions that the agency committed harmful error in hiring him for a position for which he was unqualified and in failing to fully disclose the duties of the position. First, we note that the errors cited by appellant, even if true, would not be harmful error. Harmful error is defined at 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1)] as error "in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision." The "decision" referenced in the regulations is the decision to take the action being appealed, i.e. appellant's removal. See 5 USC 7701(c). Thus, procedures unrelated to the agency's processing of appellant's removal could not be challenged under the harmful error analysis and the presiding official was correct in declining jurisdiction over this issue.

[Refer earlier in this chapter to "Performance During Detail," and to discussion in Chapter 12, "Inappropriate Job Modification; Increased Job Burden."] A few defenses that the Board has entertained are discussed below.

As to an alleged procedural error, the Board applies its harmful error analysis in a Chapter 43 case, and the appellant's burden of proof on the alleged error is by preponderant evidence, although the employer's burden on the merits of the case is substantial evidence. See Lee v. EPA, 115 MSPR 533, 540 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 240 (2010); cf. Perkins v. Dept. of Interior, CB-7121-12-0020-V-1 (NP 10/24/2013) (harmful error rule applied to an assertion that appellant's performance plan was issued later than agency policy required).

The proposing and deciding officials may be the same in a Chapter 43 action, although a higher level official of the agency must concur in the action. There is no general proscription against the appointment of a proposing or deciding official who is familiar with the facts and who has expressed a predisposition contrary to the appellant's interests. Franco v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 653, 658 (1987), aff'd, Franco v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3567). There also appears to be no minimum time that a supervisor must be responsible for an employee's work before placing that employee on a PIP. Melnick v. DHUD, 42 MSPR 93, 99 (1989).
