4. Filing by Commercial Delivery

5 CFR 1201.4 (2022), providing definitions, states:

(l) Date of filing. ...The date of filing by commercial delivery is the date the document was delivered to the commercial delivery service. The date of filing by e-filing is the date of electronic submission.

In 1993 the Board amended its regulations, 5 CFR 1201.4(l) and 5 CFR 1201.114(c), to include commercial overnight delivery as a means of filing, the filing date to be the date the document was delivered to the commercial (then overnight) delivery service. See McDavid v. Dept. of Labor, 64 MSPR 304, 304 (1994) (if a filing date is disputed, a party should show that the pleading was timely placed in the delivery service's pickup box and that it should have been picked up by the delivery service by the filing deadline in the normal course of business). In 2003, the Board amended its regulations to drop the "overnight" description from the authority to use commercial delivery as a means of filing.

After revisions to Board regulations permitting filings by commercial delivery, the Board extended Raphel v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 614 (1991) (allowing the date of mailing to be established by evidence showing when the PFR was placed into the mailstream, as opposed to when the mail was postmarked), in McDavid v. Dept. of Labor, 64 MSPR 304, 305 (1994):

[A] party may establish timely filing by commercial overnight delivery by showing that, despite a date affixed by a delivery service appearing to indicate that a submission was filed beyond the deadline, the pleading was timely placed in the particular delivery service's pickup box and that it should have been picked up by the delivery service by the filing deadline in the normal course of business.

See Stolarczyk v. DHS, 119 MSPR 343, 347 ¶ 9, 2012 MSPB 112 (2012) (Member Robbins dissenting) (date of filing was the date of the submission of a PFR to a courier; misdirection of the PFR to OSC rather than to MSPB was excused when the delay of about a month was remedied by a filing with the Board upon discovery of the mistake; the courier explained through counsel's submission that he thought OSC and MSPB were the same; Member Robbins dissenting because, he asserted, the courier who misfiled the pleading was not identified as a "commercial" courier for purposes of establishing a timely filing under 5 CFR 1201.4(l)).

Through 1993, mail was not Federal Express or other private courier, meaning that if one used a private express company, the date of filing of the PFR was the date that the Board received the petition at its headquarters offices, not the date the PFR was collected by the express company. The Board went to considerable trouble to explain why use of a private express firm did not equate to deposit of a PFR in the mails in Holland v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 128 (1991), on reconsideration, 56 MSPR 592 (1993), post. See Norman v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 446, 448 (1993) (defaulting the appellant when his representative did not know that his office had recently employed a courier to handle overseas mail by transporting it to the United States from Korea and then postmarking it by meter and placing it into the mails); Esparza v. USPS, 53 MSPR 445, 448 (1992) ("The fact that the Board received the petition more quickly because of the use of express mail also does not establish good cause."), remanded and accepted for adjudication, on remand, 57 MSPR 519, 519 n.1 (1993) (following Holland v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 592, 595 (1993)).

The Board revisited the problem of filings by private courier service in Holland v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 592, 595 (1993), following the appellant's petition to the Federal Circuit and the Board's request for a remand from the court. The Board waived the timeliness objection, noting that its administrative practice in its Clerk's office did not comport with its regulatory requirements:

For the reasons stated in our earlier opinion, we reaffirm our holding that a pleading filed via private express company has been filed by personal delivery under the Board's regulations, with the date of filing being the date of receipt by the Clerk of the Board. Prior to that decision, however, we note that the Clerk of the Board considered such pleadings as filed on the date they were deposited with the private express company. In light of that longstanding administrative practice, we believe it is appropriate to waive the filing deadline set forth at 5 CFR 1201.114(d) in this appeal. See 5 CFR 1201.12 (the Board may waive any of its regulations); Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 531, 534-36 (1988) (suspending the application of a Board regulation, in part because of an inconsistency between the regulation and the Board's previous administrative practice), modified on other grounds, 50 MSPR 174 (1991).
