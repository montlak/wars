broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 02: Procedures for Obtaining Fees, Section E: Fee Motion, Subsection 02: Time and Place of Filing

2. Time and Place of Filing

Fee motions are filed with the regional office that adjudicated the underlying case. Flexibility is to be shown as to ministerial filing requirements. If, for example, someone files a fee petition with the Office of Special Counsel rather than with the Board, the petition will be adjudicated as though it had been originally filed with the proper office. Mitchell v. Dept. of Transp., 25 MSPR 574, 575-76 (1985); see Horneman v. VA, DE-4324-15-0102-X-1 (NP 12/21/2022) (fee petition associated with enforcement proceedings should have been filed with, and was remanded to, regional offices). Under earlier regulations, no error occurred when the fee petition was filed with the Board's Clerk rather than with the AJ. Pope v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 756, 758 (1984). Extensions of time should be requested from the AJ to allow for compilation of records from other attorneys. The Board will not likely recognize submissions from any counsel other than those currently designated as representatives. See Brown v. DHHS, supra. Although fee motions are ordinarily filed with the regional office that adjudicated the underlying appeal, the Board may direct a different path, e.g., a case involving an administrative law judge, when the Board may direct that the fee petition be filed with the Board's Clerk. E.g., VA v. Chiappetta, CB-7521-16-0014-N-1 (NP 12/2/2022) (in notice of right to request attorney fee).

As in other aspects of Board litigation, the appellant is ultimately responsible for the prosecution of the fee petition. The current regulation, 5 CFR 1201.203(d), (e) (2022), mandates:

(d) Time of filing. A motion for attorney fees must be filed as soon as possible after a final decision of the Board but no later than 60 days after the date on which a decision becomes final.

(e) Service. A copy of a motion for attorney fees must be served on the other parties or their representatives at the time of filing. A party may file a pleading responding to the motion within the time limit established by the judge.

Before 1997, regulations allowed 30 days for filing a fee motion, and before 1994, the regulation set the filing time as 20 days from an initial decision and 25 days from a final decision.

Time begins running from the issuance of a favorable final decision. If a decision resolves some charges in a misconduct case in the appellant's favor, but the appellant's removal is still sustained, the appellant may not then file a fee petition since he is not a prevailing party. The appellant must wait until the Board or a court reverses or mitigates the removal. See Shelton v. OPM, 42 MSPR 214, 223 (1989).

The time limit is calculated from the final date of the decision, not from receipt of the final decision. Hopkins v. MSPB, 725 F.2d 1368 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (cautioning that if there is a delay in the mailing of the final decision, an extension for filing the fee petition should be requested). The filing of a PFR suspends the finality of the initial decision until the Board issues a final decision. If an agency requests an extension of time to file a PFR, and then does not file a PFR, the 60-day filing period for a fee petition begins after the extended time period for filing a PFR expires. See Greek v. USPS, 84 MSPR 368, 371-73 ¶¶ 10-16 (1999). A Board decision finding a counsel fee application untimely will not be disturbed only because the Board's final decision did not specifically state that it was a final decision. Bridgeman v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2432). The date the agency complies with a Board order is irrelevant to the timely filing of a fee petition. Tydingco v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 563, 356 n.* (1985). If fees are sought for compliance efforts, the fee petition cannot reach back in time to obtain recovery for an earlier phase of work (a PFR) if the current fee petition would be late as to the earlier work. See Harris v. USDA, 40 MSPR 604, 608 (1989).

The fee motion is timely filed if filed by mail on the sixtieth day. If the filing date is Saturday, the period for filing is extended through Monday. Etherton v. Dept. of Treasury, 26 MSPR 588, 590 (1985) (also deciding that the service of a final decision on the representative was constructive service on the employee); Pope v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 756, 758 (1984); 5 CFR 1201.23 (2022). Although the AJ may set deadlines in an acknowledgment order for the receipt of information concerning a fee petition by the agency and appellant, those time limits may be waived by the AJ. A waiver of a time limit will be reviewed only for abuse of discretion. Williams v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 37, 40 (1994):

[T]he Board favors adjudication on the merits. Our administrative judges have broad authority in adjudicating their cases, and this broad authority includes the authority to sua sponte extend the record closing date to ensure that the record is fully developed and that the case is fairly and justly adjudicated.

In Hand v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 92, 93 (1992), the appellant's removal was mitigated to suspension by the AJ, and counsel concurrently filed a fee petition with the AJ and an appeal to EEOC contending that the Board erred by not finding disability discrimination. While EEOC was reviewing the case, the Board's AJ denied the fee petition as unwarranted in the interest of justice. The same day as the AJ denied fees, EEOC found that the appellant was the victim of disability discrimination and the Commission referred the case back to the Board. Counsel filed a PFR with the Board from the fee denial. He argued that if the Board accepted the EEOC decision, the Board should reverse its AJ and award fees under § 7710(g)(2). The Board accepted the EEOC findings and remanded the case to its AJ for the entry of a fee award. Since the Board did not comment upon the procedure, it apparently approved of the fee petition being filed with its AJ while the appellant simultaneously pursued an appeal with EEOC. See Baker v. USPS, 86 MSPR 349 (2000) (allowing fee petition despite pending EEOC review).

If the Board remands a case to the AJ after the Board considers a PFR, the time limit for filing the fee motion will not begin to run until the decision on remand is final. See Totten v. USPS, 68 MSPR 255, 257 n.2 (1995). If an appellant files a PFR as a vehicle for seeking fees, the Board may deny the PFR but remand the case to the Board for adjudication of the fee request. See Irvine v. OPM, 86 MSPR 484, 487-88 ¶¶ 8-10 (2000). The Board declined to state when the time begins to run for counsel to file a fee petition when his client prevails in the Federal Circuit, but there is no remand to the Board, e.g., the case of an outright reversal. The Board determined that even if there were some rule requiring a fee petition to be filed with the Board on a date initiated by the issuance of the court's decision rather than its mandate, counsel's "delay" in filing a few days after the issued mandate was excused. Metz v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 625, 628-29 (1987); cf. Gallagher v. DOD, 69 MSPR 245, 246 (1995) (dissent by Chairman Erdreich alludes to the question of whether the time for filing a fee petition is stayed pending a PFR to the Federal Circuit). The Board ventured an opinion on the timing of a fee petition when OPM files a petition for reconsideration with the Board, Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 161, 165 (1991):

...Where...the request for reconsideration goes to the core of an appellant's entitlement to an award of fees, judicial economy is not served by adjudicating the entitlement to attorney fees prior to the Board's decision on OPM's request for reconsideration. In these circumstances, the administrative judge should dismiss the motion for attorney fees without prejudice and allow the party requesting attorney fees to refile its motion after the Board decides OPM's request for reconsideration....
