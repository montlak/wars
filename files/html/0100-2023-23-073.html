2. Pay Entitlement: Ready, Willing, and Able to Work

Back pay regulations ensure that employees who are reinstated do not recover more than they would have been entitled to had the unwarranted personnel action not occurred. Swafford v. TVA, 30 MSPR 130, 133 (1986). The appellant is entitled to back pay if he was capable of working during the period his removal was in effect. Deckert v. USPS, 29 MSPR 11, 12 (1985). The rule was explained, Lyle v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 324, 326-27 ¶ 6 (2000):

...Consistent with the Back Pay Act and its implementing regulations, the agency generally may not include any period during which the employee was not "ready, willing, and able" to perform his or her duties because of an incapacitating illness or injury. 5 CFR § 550.805(c)(1). Where the agency produces "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to mere theoretical argument" that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work during all or part of the period during which back pay is claimed, the burden of proof on this issue then shifts to the appellant to show otherwise. See Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 60 MSPR 498, 501-502 (1994), quoting Piccone v. United States, 186 Ct. Cl. 752, 407 F.2d 866, 876 (1969).

A dispute over the ability of an employee to work and to be entitled to back pay is ordinarily resolved through enforcement proceedings, Owens v. Dept. of Army, AT-315H-14-0479-I-1 (NP 9/21/2015):

The agency correctly notes that a back pay award may not include any period during which an employee was not ready, willing, and able to perform his or her duties because of an incapacitating illness or injury. See 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(c)(1). In Hodges, 121 M.S.P.R. 337, ¶ 24, the Board noted that entitlement to back pay is normally a compliance matter, but it decided the issue of Mr. Hodges' entitlement to back pay due to the "unique circumstances" of that case, including that the agency did not learn that Mr. Hodges was able to return to work without restrictions until his doctor testified at the hearing, at which time the agency reinstated him. The Board ultimately concluded that Mr. Hodges was not entitled to back pay because he was not ready, willing, and able to work during the removal period. Id. The agency has not identified any similar "unique circumstances" in this matter, and, in the absence of current medical documentation in the record, we believe it is not appropriate to render a decision on the appellant's entitlement to back pay at the merits stage. Because this order constitutes the Board's final decision in the removal appeal, the parties shall follow the procedures, described below, regarding calculation and payment of back pay. If the appellant believes that the agency has not complied with the Board's order regarding back pay, he should follow the applicable procedures for filing a timely petition for enforcement. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.182.

The merits initial decision, according to Hamilton v. USPS, DA-0754-15-0375-I-1 (NP 5/13/2022), does not address the specifics of back pay entitlement when an action is reversed; disputes over back pay are left to enforcement proceedings:

In reversing the agency's suspension action and finding that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work and thus not entitled to back pay, the administrative judge prematurely addressed a compliance issue in her initial decision. When the Board directs an agency to correct an unwarranted personnel action that resulted in the denial of pay otherwise due the appellant, the agency determines the amount of back pay he would have received had the action had not occurred, and the appellant may petition the Board for enforcement of the final decision if he believes that the agency is not in compliance with the decision. 5 C.F.R. §§ 550.805(a); 1201.182(a). Given the administrative judge's reversal of the agency's suspension action, her findings regarding the appellant's entitlement to back pay were premature prior to the appellant's initiation of a compliance action.[5] Cf. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 512, 517 n.6 (1997) (noting that proving an entitlement to back pay occurs at the compliance stage), rev'd in part on reopening, 79 M.S.P.R. 46 (1998). Accordingly, we vacate the administrative judge's finding that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work and thus was not entitled to back pay, and we order the agency to determine the amount of back pay due the appellant and notify him when compliance is complete. 5 C.F.R. §§ 550.805(a)(2); 1201.181(b). If the appellant believes that the agency has not fully complied with this Final Order, he may petition the Board for enforcement in accordance with 5 C.F.R. § 1201.182.

[5] The administrative judge also did not provide the parties with notice that the issue of entitlement to back pay would be adjudicated; thus, the parties were denied an opportunity to present evidence and argument regarding such an entitlement. See, e.g., Burford v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 460, 464 (1993) (finding the administrative judge did not advise the appellant of his burden of proof and did not afford the parties an opportunity to fully develop the record regarding compliance).

The appellant bears the burden of showing that he was ready, willing, and able to perform the duties of his position to be entitled to back pay. Coffey v. USPS, 60 MSPR 533, 536 (1994); see Hodges v. DOJ, 121 MSPR 337, 346 ¶ 24, 2014 MSPB 54 (2014) ("Although the agency here did not pay the appellant any back pay, we agree that he was not entitled to it because he was not ready, willing, and able to work during the period of his removal. As noted above, in his October 5, 2010 medical report, Dr. Feder stated that the appellant was unable to perform the duties of his position without restrictions."); Senior v. USPS, 85 MSPR 283, 287-88 (2000) (agency did not fail in its compliance responsibilities when the appellant was medically unable to perform job duties following the reinstatement date). Under Senior, supra, an appellant is not ready for work if she cannot assume her regular job because of a medical problem and claims, but is not entitled to, a light duty position controlled by collective bargaining agreement provisions. If the appellant cannot qualify for a security clearance during the back pay period, she may be precluded from recovering back pay. See King v. Dept. of Navy, 100 MSPR 116, 122-23 ¶¶ 14-16 (2005) (inability to hold a clearance appeared to be unrelated to the underlying reemployment issues that formed the basis of the appeal on the merits), aff'd, King v. Dept. of Navy, 167 Fed. Appx. 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP). Back pay awards may be subject to deductions for periods that the appellant could not work. The agency may seek discovery from the appellant to determine periods that the appellant could not work, but the discovery must be reasonable. Swafford v. TVA, supra; cf. Owens v. Dept. of Navy, 335 Fed. Appx. 40, 42 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (arbitrator could properly determine that an employee's leave request should have been honored and that the employee should be reinstated, but without back pay, on presentation to the employer of medical clearance for unrestricted employment). Back pay could be reduced for periods that the appellant did not return to work after receiving notice that he should. See Tatum v. USPS, DA-0752-10-0569-C-1 (NP 9/15/2015) ("We find that the agency's evidence that the appellant failed to report to work constitutes concrete evidence that he was not ready, willing, and able to work as of the date of his first shift after his receipt of the May 6, 2014 letter. See Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361, ¶¶ 13-16.").

The shifting burdens of proof or persuasion relative to the employee's ability to work during a period covered by a back pay claim were described, King v. Dept. of Navy, 100 MSPR 116, 121-22 ¶ 13 (2005), aff'd, King v. Dept. of Navy, 167 Fed. Appx. 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP):

The agency bears the initial burden of proving that it has provided an appellant the appropriate amount of back pay. See, e.g., Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361 ¶ 11. Where, however, the agency produces "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to a mere theoretical argument" that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work during all or part of the period during which back pay is claimed, the burden of proof shifts to the appellant to show her entitlement to back pay. See Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361 ¶ 11; Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 60 M.S.P.R. 498, 501-502 (1994), quoting Piccone v. United States, 186 Ct. Cl. 752, 407 F.2d 866, 876 (1969).

Donovan v. USPS, 101 MSPR 628, 631-33 ¶¶ 6-11, 2006 MSPB 111 (2006), applied the rule expressed in King and Hill to an appeal involving a constructive suspension of an employee as to whom the Board essentially applied a presumption, based on evidence of the employee's attendance record, that the employee would not have worked for much of the period of the constructive suspension:

When an employee has been the victim of an unjustified personnel action, the Board's goal is to place the employee in the circumstances in which he would have been had the personnel action never occurred. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In accordance with that goal, the Office of Personnel Management's (OPM's) regulations and the Board's case law provide that an employee is not entitled to back pay for any period during which he was not "ready, willing, and able" to perform his duties due to an incapacitating illness or injury, or for other reasons unrelated to the unjustified personnel action. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(c); King v. Department of the Navy, 100 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 12 (2005), aff'd, 167 Fed. Appx. 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006); accord Davis v. Department of the Navy, 50 M.S.P.R. 592, 598 (1991) (when the agency presents a "substantial basis" for questioning the appellant's ability to work, it is then incumbent upon the appellant to show that he was ready, willing, and able to work during the relevant period).

The agency bears the initial burden of proving that it has provided the appellant with the appropriate amount of back pay. King, 100 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 13. However, where the agency produces "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to a mere theoretical argument," demonstrating that there is some substance to its affirmative defense that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work during all or part of the period for which he claims entitlement to back pay, the burden shifts to the appellant to show that he is entitled to back pay for that period. Id.; Lyle v. Department of the Treasury, 85 M.S.P.R. 324, ¶ 6 (2000).

The agency contends that the appellant would have only worked for 1 week during the period from September 12, 2003, to January 21, 2004, had he not been suspended. The record evidence supports this assertion. The appellant began working for the agency on July 16, 2001. On July 31, 2001, the appellant applied for Workers' Compensation benefits for an injury he allegedly sustained only 2 days after he began working for the agency. It appears that the appellant worked 50.47 hours during July 2001, but he did not perform any work for the agency from early August 2001, through June 19, 2003. From June 20, 2003, through September 12, 2003, the appellant only worked 13.75 hours. Furthermore, he filed an application for immediate disability retirement on September 9, 2003, 3 days before the constructive suspension period began.

The constructive suspension period ended on January 21, 2004, when the agency ordered the appellant to report for duty or to provide medical evidence indicating the nature of his illness or injury and how his condition impacted his ability to perform his position duties. Yet, the appellant did not return to duty at all during 2004. The record evidence strongly indicates that the appellant would not have been ready, willing, and able to work during a significant portion of his constructive suspension period. Thus, the agency has met its initial burden. See Lyle, 85 M.S.P.R. 324, ¶¶ 7-8 (the agency offered concrete evidence that the appellant was unable to perform his duties during a certain time period based upon letters from his physician so indicating); cf. Joos v. Department of the Treasury, 79 M.S.P.R. 342, 348 (1998) (the agency's "naked assertion of ambiguity and conflicting interpretation" of certain evidence constituted "mere theoretical argument" that was insufficient to shift the burden to the appellant to prove that he was ready, willing, and able to work); Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 60 M.S.P.R. 498, 502 (1994) (where the agency produced no evidence that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work, and did not even raise the issue, the appellant was not required to prove that he was ready, willing, and able to work in order to receive back pay).

In light of this concrete evidence, the burden shifts to the appellant to demonstrate that he was ready, willing, and able to work for more than 1 week from September 12, 2003, to January 21, 2004. However, he never responded to the agency's evidence, despite his opportunity to do so. Nor did he contest the agency's computation of the amount of back pay he received for the 1 week.

On average, the appellant worked approximately 26 hours per year from the time he started working for the agency in July 2001, through September 2003, when the constructive suspension period began. Thus, it appears that the agency was generous in providing the appellant with a full week of back pay for the approximately 4 months that he was on enforced leave. The appellant has failed to submit any evidence, other than his bare assertion establishing that he was ready, willing, and able to work more than the week for which the agency has paid the appellant back pay. The appellant has not met his burden of proof.

The Federal Circuit reviewed the "ready, willing, and able" formulation as to an employee who suffered a procedurally improper period of enforced leave (LWOP) and who prevailed but found the agency unwilling to pay back pay because his medical condition during the period of enforced leave precluded him from working, Eisele v. USPS, 391 Fed. Appx. 862, 864-65 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP):

An employee affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action that resulted in the withdrawal or reduction in pay is entitled to back pay that "the employee normally would have earned or received during the period if the personnel action had not occurred, less any amounts earned by the employee through other employment during that period." 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1) (Back Pay Act). "[t]he basic principle of back pay, adopted by Congress in 1948, holds that an employee is entitled to be made whole whenever an erroneous personnel action which has terminated or reduced his compensation is corrected by appropriate authority." Martin v. Dep't of the Air Force, 184 F.3d 1366, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1999).

The statute itself does not address how an agency must calculate the amount of back pay. Congress authorized the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to prescribe regulations to carry out the Back Pay Act. 5 U.S.C. § 5596(c); Martin, 184 F.3d at 1370. Pursuant to this authority, OPM issued a regulation instructing that when computing the amount of back pay, an agency may not include pay for "[a]ny period during which an employee was not ready, willing, and able to perform his or her duties because of an incapacitating illness or injury." 5 C.F.R. § 550.805. This reflects the desire to make the employee whole and to avoid overcompensation when it is clear that the employee would not have been able to work even in the absence of the wrongful personnel action.

The Board's conclusion that Mr. Eisele was not ready, willing, or able to work due to an incapacitating illness or injury is supported by substantial evidence. Drs. Bradner and Falcon determined that Mr. Eisele was not fit to return to work because of his mental condition, and Dr. Fulmer found that he was unable to work because of his shoulder pain and hemorrhoids. Mr. Eisele himself admitted on an insurance claim that he was "continuously and totally disabled and unable to perform substantially all of his occupational duties from April 17, 2008, to the present." Thus, the Board properly declined to grant Mr. Eisele's petition for enforcement because he was not ready, willing, and able to work during the relevant time frame. Although in her prior opinion the administrative judge ordered the Postal Service to give Eisele back pay, in her second decision (the one here under review) she determined that the amount of back pay actually owed to Mr. Eisele was zero. Thus, effectively, the administrative judge determined that Mr. Eisele was not entitled to back pay. See Eisele II, at 5 ("The undisputed evidence here, bolstered by the appellant's own admission in OWCP claims and a disability insurance form, shows that he was not ready, willing and able to work from June 14, 2008, to present, and thus is not entitled to back pay as ordered in the initial decision issued on November 26, 2008.").

An employee is not entitled to back pay for periods he was unavailable for work if that unavailability was unrelated to the underlying unwarranted personnel action. Bartel v. FAA, 24 MSPR 560, 565 (1984). To collect back pay, the employee must prove that he was "ready, willing, and able" to perform at all times during the period between the date of the wrongful action and his reinstatement. Cisneros v. USPS, 44 MSPR 246, 250 (1990); Detherage v. Dept. of Air Force, 33 MSPR 470, 473 (1987) (applying rule to a claim for back pay following the agency's failure to restore an employee following the cessation of a compensable injury; the appellant's burden was to prove his readiness and ability to work, an issue distinct from his entitlement to the position). Bartel v. FAA, 24 MSPR 560, 564-65 (1984), explained that the purpose of a back pay award is to construct what the circumstances would have been, had the personnel action never taken place. Under Bartel, an employee was not entitled to back pay for periods the employee is unavailable for work as long as the unavailability was not related to the agency's unwarranted personnel action. If the employee is offered reinstatement and declines to return to the job, without a showing that the agency's reinstatement offer was improper, the appellant will not be entitled to back pay for the period he chose not to work. See Laviene v. USPS, 53 MSPR 238, 243 (1992).

It is the appellant's burden to show, by affidavit or other proof, that he was able to work during his absence. If it appears that he was medically unfit for duty for a period of time, he will not receive back pay for that time. Cruz v. USPS, 31 MSPR 458, 460 n.3 (1986) (distinguishing Swafford v. TVA, 30 MSPR 130, 134 (1986)); cf. Lamb v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 79, 85 (1989) (after the EEOC sent an appellant back to the Board with a finding that she was discriminated against because the agency failed to consider her for vacancies that would permit her to work without the aggravation of her allergies, the Board determined that the agency could not have provided a suitable work environment and found the appellant disqualified from the receipt of back pay). The Federal Circuit agrees. If an individual is unavailable for work during a back pay period because of a medical condition, he does not get back pay for that period. Matthews v. DOD (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3048). But see Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 88, 91-92 (1996) ("[w]here, as here, the employee was injured in the course of interim employment which he would not have taken but for the wrongful separation, and where he receives less in state workers' compensation payments than he would have earned in his former position, he has in fact lost something he would otherwise have earned."), modified on OPM reconsideration petition, 79 MSPR 380, 384-85 (1998), rev'd, Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 184 F.3d 1366, 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999) ("Given that the purpose of the Back Pay Act is to place a wrongfully discharged employee back in the position he would have been in had the termination not occurred and to make the employee whole, equity and reason require that if such an employee is unable to work because of an accident or illness closely related or due to interim employment or arises because of the unlawful discharge, the period of disability should be included in a back pay period...with appropriate offsets for the period during which he was receiving workers' compensation benefits"). An employee is entitled to back pay if, during his separation from the agency, his receipt of workers' compensation benefits was caused by injury or illness resulting from the agency's whistleblowing reprisal. The employer may set off from back pay OWCP payments received during the period of the appellant's separation. See Special Counsel v. VA, 81 MSPR 601, 604-06 ¶¶ 8-10 (1999). [Refer below to "Damages; Regulation Governing Recovery" for decisions relating back pay entitlement and receipt of FECA benefits.]

As to an employee who was discharged for AWOL and reinstated on a showing that she was entitled to sick leave, the employee was only entitled to back pay for periods when he was ready, willing, and able to work. See Kmiecz v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 673, 677-78 (1986). If evidence suggests that there is at least some period when the appellant could not work, the agency is justified in failing to provide back pay if the appellant fails to provide concrete information that will allow the agency to ascertain when the employee could or could not work. See Coffey v. USPS, 60 MSPR 533, 536-37 (1994). But if the employee is incapacitated, the Board will, through enforcement proceedings, direct the agency to charge appellant's sick leave, rather than force the appellant to use annual leave during the period of incapacitation following reversal of appellant's separation. Coffey v. USPS, 85 MSPR 625, 629-31 (2000).

Hill v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 498, 501-03 (1994), defined allocations of proof when there is a dispute over whether the appellant was "ready, willing, and able" to work for a period covered by a back pay claim:

In proceedings on a petition for enforcement of a Board order, the agency bears the burden of proving that it has complied with the order. Gay v. US Postal Service, 49 MSPR 219, 230 (1991). When the agency disclaims liability for back pay on the basis that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work, the appellant bears the burden of proving to the contrary. Redding v. US Postal Service, 32 MSPR 187, 191-92 (1987). This burden arises, however, only when the agency makes such a disclaimer; the agency must first come forward with "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to a mere theoretical argument" that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to return to work during the relevant period in order to put the matter in issue. Id. (quoting Piccone v. United States, 407 F.2d 866, 876, 186 Ct. Cl. 752 (1969)); accord Davis v. Department of the Navy, 50 MSPR 592, 598 (1991) (if the agency presents a "substantial basis for questioning the appellant's ability to work, it is incumbent upon the appellant to show that he was ready, willing[,] and able to work during the period" for which back pay is sought).

The agency here did not present any evidence to the administrative judge that the appellant was either stricken with an incapacitating illness or injury or was otherwise unready, unwilling, or unable to work between December 16, 1990, and April 20, 1991. Indeed, the agency did not even raise the issue. Thus, the appellant was not required to prove that he was ready, willing, and able to work between December 16, 1990, and April 20, 1991 in order to receive back pay for that period.

...

...The agency's evidence was sufficient to put in issue the question of whether the appellant was ready, willing, and able to perform his duties after September 27, 1991. The appellant, after being given a chance to respond to the agency's evidence, submitted absolutely no evidence that he was ready, willing, and able to work after September 27, 1991.... Thus, we reject his claim for back pay for the period from September 28, 1991, through November 5, 1991. 5 CFR § 550.805(c).

Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 79 MSPR 342, 348 (1998), clarified the burden of proof:

An appellant's readiness, willingness, and ability to work is not put in issue unless the agency produces "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to mere theoretical argument" that the appellant failed to meet one of these requirements. Only after the production of such sufficient evidence does the burden of proof on the issue shift to the appellant. See Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 60 MSPR 498, 501-502 (1994), quoting Piccone v. United States, 407 F.2d 866, 876 (Ct. Cl. 1969).

Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 450, 455 (1990), added:

For purposes of entitlement to back pay, it is the appellant's burden to prove that he was ready, willing, and able to perform the duties of his job during the period for which he is seeking back pay. See Piccone v. United States, 407 F.2d 866, 876, 186 Ct. Cl. 752 (1969); Detherage v. Department of the Air Force, 33 MSPR 470 (1987); Cruz v. United States Postal Service, 31 MSPR 458, 459-460 (1986) (it is the appellant's burden to prove entitlement to back pay). However, in order to confine this burden within reasonable limits, the courts and the Board have found that the Government should be required to "demonstrate that it has some concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to a mere theoretical argument, that there is some substance to its [affirmative defense] and [it] is not a mere 'fishing expedition' or a method of discouraging employees from seeking back pay on meritorious claims because of the cost of proving readiness, willingness and ability to perform since the adverse action." Piccone, 407 F.2d at 876; see Redding v. United States Postal Service, 32 MSPR 187 (1987).

Choi v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-14-0449-C-1 (NP 4/14/2015), said of the shifting burdens as to the appellant's ability to recover back pay when a substantial question is raised as to his ability to perform work:

When an employee has been the victim of an unjustified personnel action, the Board's goal is to place him in the circumstances in which he would have been had the personnel action never occurred. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In accordance with that goal, OPM's regulations and the Board's case law provide that an employee is not entitled to back pay for any period during which he was not "ready, willing, and able" to perform his duties due to an incapacitating illness or injury, or for other reasons unrelated to the unjustified personnel action. King v. Department of the Navy, 100 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 12 (2005), aff'd, 167 F. App'x 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006); accord Davis v. Department of the Navy, 50 M.S.P.R. 592, 598 (1991) (when the agency presents a "substantial basis" for questioning the appellant's ability to work, it is then incumbent upon the appellant to show that he was ready, willing, and able to work during the relevant period); 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(c).

The agency bears the initial burden of proving that it has provided the appellant with the appropriate amount of back pay. King, 100 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 13. However, where the agency produces "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to a mere theoretical argument," demonstrating that there is some substance to its affirmative defense that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work during all or part of the period for which he claims entitlement to back pay, the burden shifts to the appellant to show otherwise. Id.; Lyle v. Department of the Treasury, 85 M.S.P.R. 324, ¶ 6 (2000).

The determinants of entitlement to back pay deviate from the "ready, willing, and able" to work formulation when the back pay covers a period that the employee was placed out of work by the employer's improper assessment of his ability to work. If removal results from the appellant's lack of fitness for the job, and the removal is then reversed, the appellant should receive pay back to the date of removal, despite the agency's protestations concerning the appellant's inability to work. Dwyer v. USPS, 32 MSPR 181, 185 (1987). [Refer to subsequent discussion in this chapter concerning "Other Post-Reinstatement Action; Medical Certification Requirement."]

The question may arise in a disability discrimination case whether an employee is ready, willing, and able to work to merit back pay. Under Ignacio v. USPS, 35 MSPR 55, 64-65 (1987):

...Here, whether the appellant has proven readiness, willingness and ability to perform cannot be analyzed in a vacuum. The burden must be considered and analyzed within the context of the requirements of the Rehabilitation Act, giving due consideration to the findings made by the Special Panel.

The record is replete with evidence to support appellant's willingness and readiness to perform his job. His efforts in trying to retain his job prior to his discharge and his many efforts to regain his job subsequent to his discharge amply show that he was both ready and willing to perform. The remaining factor in this back pay determination is whether the appellant was able to perform his job within the context of the requirements of the Rehabilitation Act....

...

[T]he wrong committed by the agency was not simply the removal, but the agency's failure to make reasonable accommodation to appellant's handicap or to show that an accommodation would have created an undue hardship upon its operations.... [w]e believe that the Panel's determination that the appellant was a qualified handicapped employee together with its findings that the agency failed to show that it could not have accommodated the appellant within the letter carrier craft leads to the conclusion that the appellant has met his burden to show ability to perform the job within the context of the Rehabilitation Act.

Cason v. NASA, 37 MSPR 261, 270 (1988), ordered back pay for an individual who was placed off the rolls for physical inability to do the work. The appellant demonstrated that the agency precluded him from doing the work by denying him the accommodation necessary to surmount his physical handicap. In Boomer v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 540, 544 (1991), the disabled appellant was insufficiently accommodated by his employer. Although he received OWCP benefits, the Board directed that he receive the salary of the position he should have occupied during the period of his removal, less the OWCP benefits he received during that period. See Nordhoff v. Dept. of Navy, 64 MSPR 478, 486 (1994) (suggesting that if an appellant whose retirement is involuntary is too disabled to work during the period of the challenged retirement, the appellant may receive no back pay, a situation that may bring into question the wisdom of pursuing the appeal if the disabling condition is permanent); cf. Vitanza v. USPS, 94 MSPR 385, 389 n.1 (2003) ("[b]ecause of his disability claim, the appellant would not have been entitled to back pay from the time he became disabled."). [Refer to "Enforced Leave; Constructive Suspension" in Chapter 6; refer to "Rehabilitation Act: Disabling Conditions" in Chapter 12.]

At the behest of the EEOC, the Board adopted a rule that requires back pay to an employee for the period when the employee was discriminatorily placed in a nonduty status covering his rehabilitation from alcoholism. Holley v. DHHS, 50 MSPR 271, 273-74 (1991). The Board's recitation of the EEOC analysis in Holley was nonexistent; the reader is referred to EEOC Decision No. 03910008 (2/15/1991). Briefly summarized, the EEOC Holley ruling is that "an appellant who has demonstrated that he has taken all appropriate steps towards rehabilitation and that he was fit for duty at the time of his removal is entitled to reinstatement and back pay." Hatchett v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 242, 243 (1992) (modifying a conditional reinstatement order by the AJ); see Hulsey v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 473, 479 n.14 (1993) ("An appellant is entitled to reinstatement and back pay if he has taken all appropriate steps toward rehabilitation and was fit for duty, i.e., rehabilitated, during a period of time when he was discriminatorily placed in a nonduty and nonpay status."); Yancy v. GSA, 57 MSPR 192, 199 n.4 (1993) ("[a]n appellant is entitled to reinstatement and back pay if he has taken all appropriate steps toward rehabilitation and was fit for duty at the time of his removal" (citing Holley, supra).

The Board followed Holley, supra, to award back pay despite a relapse (disallowing back pay for the actual period of the rehabilitation and relapse) in the sequel to the 1993 Hulsey decision, Hulsey v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 350, 352-53 (1994):

...For purposes of entitlement to back pay, it is the appellant's burden to prove that he was ready, willing, and able to perform the duties of his job during the period for which he is seeking back pay. See generally Bivens v. Department of the Navy, 43 MSPR 450, 455 (1990), rev'd on other grounds, 923 F.2d 868 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table). See also 5 CFR § 550.805(c)(2).

Based on the foregoing, we adopt the AJ's reasoning and findings with regard to back pay. The question is whether a relapse, as here, vitiates the entire period under consideration for back pay. We find that it does not. An employee who is reinstated after being wrongfully dismissed, however, may not recover more than he would have been entitled to had the unwarranted personnel action never occurred. See Kerr, 726 F.2d at 733; Bartel v. Federal Aviation Administration, 24 MSPR 560, 564-65 (1984).

The AJ found that back pay was warranted except for the period from April 4, 1992, when the appellant suffered a relapse, through August 27, 1992, when he was released from incarceration. In making this finding, she stated that the situation here is analogous to Board cases where the appellant is entitled to back pay for only those periods during which he was ready, willing, and able to work, citing Hulsey, 57 MSPR at 479, n.14.

We agree. In Hulsey, the Board found that an appellant "is entitled to reinstatement and back pay if he has taken all appropriate steps toward rehabilitation and was fit for duty, i.e., rehabilitated, during a period of time when he was discriminatorily placed in a non-duty and non-pay status." Id. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission also has held that an award of back pay contains an implicit finding that the employee is fit for duty and rehabilitated. See Holley v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC No. 03910008, slip op. at 4 (Feb. 15, 1991). While the appellant did not remain completely rehabilitated and totally abstain from alcohol throughout the entire period following his removal, he worked from October 16, 1989 until February 3, 1992, for a private company, indicating that he had abstained from alcohol and enjoyed a state of sobriety and was therefore capable of work for a significant period following his removal. Further, the appellant averred that he received unemployment compensation until April 1993, and his receipt of such compensation was conditioned on his making at least two job attempts per week and remaining ready, able, and willing to work. He further claimed that, since his release from incarceration and as a condition of his probation, he attends AA meetings twice a week and remains sober.

Because the appellant is currently active in a alcohol rehabilitative program, and has abstained and continues to abstain from abuse of alcohol, he has shown that he was fit for duty and rehabilitated, except for the period in 1992 noted above. See 5 CFR § 550.805(c). See also Holley v. Department of Health and Human Services, 50 MSPR 271, 273 (1991) (courts have considered an employee's subsequent rehabilitation an important factor in fashioning remedies for discrimination) (citations omitted); Winslow v. Department of the Navy, 46 MSPR 246, 250 (1990) (because employee was "unavailable" for duty for reasons unrelated to those caused by the agency's removal action, the agency was relieved from its obligation to pay back pay for the period of time during which he was incarcerated).

The agency did not dispute with concrete positive evidence the appellant's assertions that he was fit for duty, and therefore ready, willing, and able to work, except as noted above. Nor are we persuaded that the cases cited by the agency require a finding that the appellant's post-removal behavior, i.e., relapse, unalterably disqualifies him from being made "whole" for the discrimination he suffered as a result of his handicapping condition of alcoholism. Holley, 50 MSPR at 273-74 (1991). The agency therefore failed in its burden to prove its affirmative defense of no liability for back pay and we adopt the Recommended Decision's finding ordering back pay for the relevant period. See Redding v. US Postal Service, 32 MSPR 187, 192 (1987).

Accordingly, we find that the agency has NOT COMPLIED with the Board's decision reversing the appellant's removal.

We agree with the administrative judge that the evidence that the appellant provided was insufficient to prove that he was ready, willing, and able to perform the duties of his position during the period encompassed by the Back Pay Act. Dr. Hamblin consistently based his statements concerning the appellant's ability to return to work during this period on a transfer or significant changes in the appellant's working conditions and the cessation of perceived harassment by the agency.[9] However, an appellant is not entitled to back pay, including restoration of annual leave, for any period during which he was not ready, willing, and able to perform the duties of his position because of an incapacitating illness or injury, or for reasons unrelated to or not caused by the unjustified or unwarranted personnel actions. 5 CFR § 550.805(c)(1), (c)(2); see Hill v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 453, 456 (1996); Winslow v. Department of the Navy, 46 MSPR 246, 250 (1990), aff'd, 935 F.2d 280 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). The appellant here has failed to show that his claims of harassment meet these requirements. Generally, an appellant is entitled to back pay only if he is ready, willing and able to work in the position the employee occupied prior to the wrongful agency action. See Davis, 50 MSPR at 598. The appellant's apparent willingness to return to work during the back-pay period in a position other than the one he occupied prior to his removal is not sufficient to establish his entitlement to back pay in the absence of a finding of handicap discrimination.
