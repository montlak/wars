broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 01: Initial Decision, Section C: Credibility Findings, Subsection 02: Factors Considered; Hillen, Part c: Inconsistencies; Denials; Memory Lapse; Failure to Provide Statement
c. Inconsistencies; Denials; Memory Lapse; Failure to Provide Statement

"The consistency or commonality with other evidence is also one of the factors to consider in assessing the credibility of witnesses." Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 417-18 (1997); see Bryant v. Dept. of Army, 84 MSPR 202, 208 ¶ 12 (1999) ("The inconsistency of the agency's evidence renders its version less credible than that of the appellant."). Inconsistent statements do not necessarily render testimony incredible. See Brown v. DLA, 65 MSPR 436, 443 (1994); Box v. USPS, 51 MSPR 401, 404 (1991) (citing Hillen for the proposition that inconsistent statements do not necessarily render testimony incredible); cf. Thomas v. USPS, 116 MSPR 453, 457 ¶ 5, 2011 MSPB 62 (2011) ("[T]he Board has held that inconsistent statements do not necessarily render a witness's testimony incredible. Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 459 (1987); see also Jefferson v. Defense Logistics Agency, 22 M.S.P.R. 10, 14 (1984) (holding that minor inconsistencies in testimony were not sufficient to justify holding that a witness did not observe an incident)."). The effect of a prior inconsistent statement is not that the present testimony is false, but that the very fact of inconsistency raises doubt as to the truthfulness of both statements. Sternberg v. DODDS, 52 MSPR 547, 555 (1992). If a party offers testimony that is inconsistent, the inconsistencies should be explained or the party bears the risk that its evidence is unpersuasive. See Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 66 MSPR 68, 86 (1994).

An appellate court explained that gaps between deposition and hearing testimony do not necessarily suggest fabrication, Crockett v. Abraham, 284 F.3d 131, 133-34 (D.C. Cir. 2002):

...Self-contradiction by the moving party's witnesses may of course create a genuine issue of material fact precluding summary judgment.... And second-hand gap-filling, if extreme enough, clearly can rise to the level of a contradiction. This would surely be true, for example, if a witness in his second Statement "remembered" salient, memorable facts that (if true) he surely should have remembered and mentioned the first time around, or "remembered" facts which too conveniently explain away troublesome portions of the previous testimony. And this would be especially true if some intervening event--e.g., pressure by a party, prodding of counsel--made the embellishment seem fishy. Cf. Russell v. Acme-Evans Co., 51 F.3d 64, 67 (7th Cir. 1995) (reflecting suspicion of later affidavit used "to patch up" established deficiencies).

Here there is no such contradiction. Sullivan's declaration does not contradict her deposition but rather augments and elaborates upon it. The omissions on which Crockett pounces are easily attributable to understandable lapses of memory or lack of preparation. Cf. id. at 68 (statement in affidavit directly contradicted by earlier deposition will be disregarded "unless it is demonstrable that the statement in the deposition was mistaken, perhaps...because a lapse of memory is in the circumstances a plausible explanation for the discrepancy"). At her deposition Sullivan appeared on some matters obviously ill-prepared. For example, she was rather general in her discussion of how the candidates failed to suit the Department's "skill mix" preferences. She also couldn't recall when Stern had left the Department, when the vacancy was announced, who was on the selection panel, or the exact number of applicants for the job (though she did recall that it was "[f]ewer than we hoped for").... In her later declaration, presumably after reviewing her records and refreshing her memory, Sullivan addressed these gaps and was more specific. She was able to state precisely when Stern had left the Department, when the vacancy was announced, who was on the selection panel, and how many applications were received....

Cf. Delgado v. MSPB, 880 F.3d 913, 922 (7th Cir. 2018) ("After all, there are many possible explanations for discrepancies between the testimony of different witnesses that do not involve criminal intent to lie. Honest differences in perception, memory, and viewpoint often produce differences in testimony, even as between law enforcement officers trained to observe and to testify accurately.").

Commenting on distinctions between deposition and hearing testimony, Berube v. GSA, 37 MSPR 448, 454-55 (1988):

We find that the deposition testimony...is entitled to even greater weight than the Administrator's testimony at the hearing because (1) the deposition testimony is unequivocal and direct in nature, while the hearing testimony is relatively ambiguous and indirect, and (2) the deposition statements were made at a time closer to the appellant's removal than was the Administrator's hearing testimony.

That the appellant presents in his reply to a proposed adverse action the same defense raised at the adverse action hearing bolsters his credibility if the defense is attacked as a post hoc rationalization for misconduct. Johnson v. FAA, 13 MSPR 652, 662 n.28 (1982); cf. Triplett v. OPM, 105 MSPR 575, 2007 MSPB 126 (2007) ("when an appellant avails herself of the opportunity to tell her side of the story and does not bother to raise a clear factual allegation that would have explained her actions, such a clear allegation is less plausible when raised much later," citing Reynolds v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 189, 195 (1994)). Conversely, an appellant's failure to deny charges when responding to the notice of proposed adverse action makes his subsequent denial entitled to less weight. See Noa v. FAA, 15 MSPR 126, 134-35 (1983) (citing Risner v. FAA, 7 MSPR 480, 7 MSPB 342 (1981)). As to an employee who claimed that he was not striking, but was too ill to work, the Board could properly consider the timing of the explanation. If the explanation was omitted during the reply process and in the petition for appeal, it could be found so weak as to be not credible. Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., FAA (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 83-1153). This result is also reached if the appellant allows a representative to present his reply or makes no reply and offers no good reason for failing to raise during the reply a defense subsequently raised in the appeal. Because the agency retains the burden of proof on ultimate issues involved in the charges, the employee's belated defense may still prevail if the agency evidence is unpersuasive. Weiss v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 236, 241-42 (1983). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Admissions."]

Of statements made a different stages of a case, Brookins v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0432-18-0359-I-1 (NP 1/19/2023), offered:

...Although there is no requirement that the appellant raise every argument he intends to raise before the Board in his response to the proposed action, the consistency of prior statements is a relevant consideration in assessing his credibility. Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987). Accordingly, the Board has found an appellant's allegations less credible when he raised them for the first time on appeal. See, e.g., Reynolds v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 189, 195 (1994); Abatecola v. Veterans Administration, 29 M.S.P.R. 601, 607 n.3, aff'd, 802 F.2d 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table). In addition, the Board routinely considers an appellant's response to the proposed action in assessing claims regarding deficient notice or confusion with the charges, e.g., Shibuya v. Department of Agriculture, 119 M.S.P.R. 537, ¶ 11 (2013); Mouser v. Department of Health & Human Services, 30 M.S.P.R. 619, 624 (1986)....

The AJ properly sorts through testimonial inconsistencies to resolve factual disputes, Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 105, 108 (1988):

The appellant next contends that the administrative judge erred in crediting Smart and Shartle's testimonies because their versions of the events in question are inconsistent with each other and with their previous explanations, and because previous incidents involving them and the appellant provided them with a reason to falsify their testimonies.

All of these factors, however, were considered by the administrative judge in assessing their credibility. The administrative judge noted that there were inconsistencies in the testimonies, but found enough consistencies to render their versions believable. The administrative judge also correctly noted that a witness could be credible with regard to one issue and not credible with respect to another. Carrier Corp. v. National Labor Relations Board, 768 F.2d 778, 782 (6th Cir. 1985); National Labor Relations Board v. Universal Camera Corp., 179 F.2d 749, 754 (2d Cir. 1950), vacated and remanded on other grounds, 340 U.S. 474, 71 S. Ct. 456, 95 L. Ed. 456 (1951) (per L. Hand: "It is no reason for refusing to accept everything that a witness says, because you do not believe all of it; nothing is more common in all kinds of judicial decisions than to believe some and not all"); accord: McKoy v. Department of Defense, 33 MSPR 195, 199, 200 (1987). Moreover, disturbing the administrative judge's credibility determinations on Smart and Shartle is especially inappropriate because those findings were also based upon their demeanor while testifying as well as upon the substance of their testimony. Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Further, the administrative judge carefully considered and rejected the appellant's contention that Smart and Shartle had a motive to falsify their testimonies.

See SSA v. Long, 113 MSPR 190, 204 ¶ 32, 2010 MSPB 19 (2010) ("This supplemental information at the hearing, however, is not an inconsistency that undermines the officer's testimony. See Garza v. Department of the Army, 57 M.S.P.R. 214, 222 (a slight discrepancy was insufficient to find that the witness lacked credibility) (citing Awa v. Department of the Navy, 41 M.S.P.R. 318, 321 (1989)), aff'd, 11 F.3d 1073 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).")

The failure to recall an incident is not the same as denying it occurred. Memory lapse does not necessarily impeach credibility concerning a denial--particularly if the denial is buttressed by other evidence of the appellant's character. See Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 293, 302 (1991). Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 599 ¶ 8 (2001), cited 3A Wigmore on Evidence § 1001 (Chadbourne rev. 1970) for the proposition that "[a]n error upon a distant and distinct matter is logically and psychologically much inferior in value to an error upon a closely connected matter, in its bearing upon the trustworthiness of the assertion in question." But see Stein v. USPS, 57 MSPR 434, 439-40 (1993) (AJ could discount the appellant's lack of recall based on the appellant's notice of an ongoing investigation several months after the operative events occurred). The Board may consider whether events are sufficiently memorable that the passage of time would not affect the accuracy of testimony. See Duckenfield v. VA, DC-0752-12-0677-I-2 (NP 7/17/2014) (delay of eight months: "Nor do we think that the passage of several months would cause any of these witnesses to forget observing such egregious harassment as the appellant accused her supervisor of having committed."). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Admissions."]

Credibility is not necessarily impaired by the refusal of a witness to admit to wrongdoing, Garza v. Dept. of Army, 57 MSPR 214, 222 (1993):

In making her credibility determinations, the administrative judge relied upon her perception that Flores failed to recognize that he had provoked the incident as a factor which detracted from his credibility. We disagree. Flores acknowledged the conduct he engaged in. See, e.g., Arenz v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 307, 310-11 (1989) (administrative judge erred in her credibility determination that the appellant contradicted himself when testifying that he had not signed all of the standard forms at issue, but when shown them, admitted they were signed by him, given that the record indicated that there were some forms prepared by the agency which were unsigned). Flores has therefore not been caught in a lie about his actions, as the administrative judge determined. Rather, he has simply disagreed with the perception or characterization of his admitted conduct. His disagreement with that assessment or his unwillingness to recognize the impropriety of his own actions cannot be used to detract from the credibility of his statements about what he did or did not do. Consequently, the administrative judge's conclusion, that Flores was less than forthcoming on this basis, was erroneous.

More importantly, the question of whether an appellant's misconduct was provoked is a factor to assess in determining the reasonableness of the penalty, not a factor to assess in determining the credibility of the alleged provocateur....

"While the failure of a witness to provide an affidavit prior to the hearing could, under certain circumstances, be a factor in determining credibility, it is insufficient by itself to find a witness not credible." Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 54 MSPR 356, 361 (1992).
