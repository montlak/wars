11. Retirement Benefit

If a settlement directly calls for payment of retirement benefits administered by OPM, then it may be that OPM should be a party to the settlement, Labonte v. VA, 53 MSPR 668, 669-670 (1992):

Pursuant to a settlement agreement, the administrative judge dismissed the appellant's original appeal, which alleged that she had been involuntarily retired from her position....

...

In Mas v. Department of the Army, 46 MSPR 46, 47-48 (1990), the Board held that jurisdiction over an employee's appeal from an allegedly involuntary action could not be conferred by the parties' stipulation in a settlement agreement....

Although this appeal presents a similar situation to that in Mas, the Board declines to remand this appeal for a jurisdictional determination.... [T]he issues raised in the appellant's petition for review do not relate to any disagreement between the parties to the settlement agreement; rather, the petition for review relates to actions of OPM, which was not a party to the settlement. Because no controversy between the parties to that agreement has been presented, the issue of Board jurisdiction is rendered moot. Additionally, a decision by the Board on the issue of jurisdiction where there is no matter in controversy would be tantamount to an advisory opinion, which the Board is precluded from issuing....

Labonte did not explain the nature of the problem that linked the settlement agreement to an apparent dispute with OPM concerning overpayment of an annuity. The Board mentioned that the appellant could separately appeal an overpayment dispute with OPM to the Board. The case suggests that care needs to be taken in settlements, when rights are identified, to include the parties as to whom those rights may be enforced when matters go awry. If an appellant seeks to settle with his employer through an agreement that implicates retirement benefits administered by OPM, the appellant and her counsel ought to consider structuring the settlement with the agency so that it is contingent on OPM's later approval of the benefit. Withdrawal of the appeal, one component of the settlement, is then accomplished following favorable action by OPM. The parties may seek a withdrawal of the appeal without prejudice to refile to allow sufficient time for OPM to act on the benefit sought by the appellant. Settlements involving annuity benefits may invite OPM opposition. Settlements affecting retirement entitlements should be reviewed by OPM before they are executed. See Fenlon v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-11-0459-I-4 (NP 3/7/2014) ("Nevertheless, regardless of whether the appellant has filed an application for disability retirement, OPM is not a party to the settlement agreement at issue in this petition for review, and a settlement agreement simply cannot impose obligations on such a third party without that party's assent. E.g., Parker v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 529, ¶ 17 (2003), aff'd, 91 F. App'x 660 (Fed. Cir. 2004)."). [Refer to the OPM leaflet "Guidelines for Settlement of Federal Personnel Actions Involving Civil Service Retirement Benefits," opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/settlement-guidelines.]

If the employing agency has control over a component of the retirement process, e.g., a determination of eligibility for law enforcement officer retirement status, the parties may settle based on the agency's promise to expeditiously issue an LEO determination. Should the agency unreasonably delay the determination, Board compliance procedures may be used to require the agency to issue the determination that was the object of the parties' bargain. See Ford v. Dept. of Navy, 85 MSPR 354, 357-58 ¶¶ 6-8 (2000). If a settlement agreement included provisions concerning the processing of a disability retirement application, e.g., that the agency would provide assistance, and the retirement application was then denied, the employee's remedy was to appeal the OPM retirement decision, not to launch a collateral attack on the settlement agreement if no evidence existed of bad faith or noncompliance by appellant's employing agency. Reyes v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1721) (a result that would likely still occur unless there is a contingency stating that the agreement is void without OPM approval of the retirement); cf. Harris v. VA, 142 F.3d 1463, 1469-72 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (suggesting that the agency may be obliged to pursue a disability retirement application on behalf of an employee who initially entered a settlement agreement based on the pursuit of disability retirement and then, apparently for reasons of mental disability, withdrew the retirement application from OPM). [Refer later in this chapter to "Annuity Issues" and "Unemployment Compensation" under "Specific Settlement Provisions."]

A settlement provision that calls for agency assistance in the retirement application process does not guarantee the result will be an approved retirement, Colleran v. USPS, SE-0752-01-0293-C-1 (NP 8/19/2011):

The appellant essentially argues that the agency materially breached the settlement agreement because it failed to provide the required documentation to the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to expedite his disability retirement application. The relevant provision of the settlement agreement states "[t]he agency agrees to help expedite processing of appellant's disability retirement by writing OPM." Here, the appellant has not shown that the agency materially breached the terms of the settlement agreement. The agency's evidence demonstrates that it was in communication with OPM and the appellant's attorney concerning the status and processing of the appellant's disability retirement application prior to the execution of the settlement agreement. Unbeknownst to the parties, OPM denied the appellant's disability retirement application on the same date the parties executed the settlement agreement. While there is no documentation to demonstrate that the agency sent a letter to OPM relating to the disability retirement application, it certainly appears the agency engaged in good faith efforts to assist in the expeditious processing of the appellant's disability retirement application. Once the parties received notice that OPM had made a decision to deny the appellant's application, there was no longer any need for the agency to take any action to expedite the processing of the appellant's application. Further, the settlement agreement does not guarantee the results of the appellant's disability retirement application, nor does the agency have the authority to grant the appellant's application for disability retirement benefits. Nonetheless, less than 1 month after the settlement agreement was entered into the Board's record for enforcement purposes, OPM approved the appellant's disability retirement application and in March 2002, the appellant elected to receive OWCP benefits rather than a disability retirement annuity from OPM. Thus, the appellant has not explained how the agency's failure to send a letter to OPM constitutes a material breach of the agreement. Moreover, because the appellant's application for benefits was approved, there is no remedy that the Board can now provide for the appellant and absent any harm, there is no corrective action the Board may order to enforce the term. Bables, 86 M.S.P.R. 171, ¶ 20.

Agreements containing provisions affecting, or calling for, retirement need to be carefully structured so that economic and other expectations are met after the agreement is signed and after OPM issues its retirement determination. The settlement may need to be structured so that it is conditioned on the appellant obtaining the benefit of the bargain.
