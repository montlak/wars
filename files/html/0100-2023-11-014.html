1. Unit Status

Under § 7115(b)(1), an allotment for dues deductions terminates when the agreement between the agency and the exclusive representative ceases to be applicable to the employee. The termination of dues is warranted at any time when the employee is not covered by the agreement. If, during the term of the agreement, the employee's classification is properly included within the unit, no termination of dues is justified without a revocation from the employee. IRS, Seattle Dist. and NTEU, 12 FLRA 324, 336-37 (1983) (ALJ Decision). If the agency has the right to terminate the dues allotment, it must bargain with the union concerning both the procedures used in implementing its decision to terminate dues deductions and the impact of that decision on adversely affected employees. When the termination of dues is required by statute, however, a monetary remedy is not appropriate for a failure to bargain. IRS, Fresno Serv. Ctr. and NTEU, 7 FLRA 371, 373 n.4 (1981). Self help by the agency through deduction of past dues erroneously deducted from dues currently owed to the union is not acceptable. See Lowry AFB and AFGE Local 1974, 31 FLRA 793, 797 (1988), post.

When an employee is promoted to a supervisory position, the employee is outside the bargaining unit, and the collective bargaining agreement no longer applies to the employee. The employee's dues deduction must then terminate under 7115. The failure to terminate dues allotments of supervisors outside the unit violates 7116(a)(1) and (8). Dept. of Labor and AFGE Local 2513, 20 FLRA 296, 301 (1985). Transfers of employees out of the bargaining unit justifies the agency discontinuing their dues deduction. DHHS, Region X and NTEU, 19 FLRA 73, 89 (1985) (ALJ Decision).

Initially, FLRA determined that, because § 7115(b) requires dues-withholding authorizations to terminate as a matter of law when an employee is promoted to a supervisory position outside the bargaining unit, the agency could recoup from the union the amount of dues previously forwarded in error to the union. This was so even though, under 5 USC 5584, the union could request a waiver of the claim arising out of the erroneous transmittal of voluntary dues allotments to the union. Dept. of Air Force, 3480th Air Base Group, Goodfellow AFB and AFGE Local 1816, 9 FLRA 394, 397 n.5 (1982). The rule that management may stop deductions and make retroactive refunds of dues payments erroneously deducted from the salaries of employees included individuals who are transferred out of the unit. Nat'l Archives & Records Serv. and AFGE Local 2578, 9 FLRA 413, 415-16 (1982); see DHHS and NTEU, 16 FLRA 586, 586-87 (1984) (reassignment or transfer terminates deduction liability). As to dues deductions recouped from the union because supervisors were not being removed from checkoff quickly enough, the Comptroller General ruled in NFFE Local 1239, Case B-201817 (Jan. 27, 1982), that the union was entitled to request a waiver of the government's claim arising out of the erroneous transmittal of voluntary dues deductions. The payments were made through management's administrative error and were received by the union in good faith and without fraud or misrepresentation. Dugway Proving Grounds and NFFE Local 1239, 9 FLRA 409, 412 (1982); cf. Griffiss AFB and AFGE Local 2612, 12 FLRA 198, 198-200 (1983) (notice of intention to recoup erroneously withheld union dues through a set-off did not, standing alone, interfere with protected rights).

The deduction rule was set aside in the Fifth Circuit in AFGE Local 1816 v. FLRA, 715 F.2d 224, 228 (5th Cir. 1983). The court stated that the issue before it was whether recoupment by the Air Force of dues mistakenly withheld from an employee's wages and paid to the union violated 5 USC 7115, constituting a ULP under 5 USC 7116. An employee joined the union, had dues withheld, became a supervisor, forgot to submit a revocation of dues under the procedures of the contract, learned of her right to recoup the dues money withheld when she was a supervisor, and sought a refund from the Air Force. She was reimbursed for about $172 erroneously withheld. The amount was taken by the Air Force from payroll funds set aside for dues allotments of other employees. The union filed a ULP when the Air Force declined to reimburse the union for the sums withheld. Recognizing that the dues allotment of the employee should have terminated when she left the unit to become a supervisor, the court faulted the means chosen by the Air Force to recover the funds:

Nothing in the plain language of § 7115 or its legislative history justifies Goodfellow's resort to a "self-help" method of recoupment. Section 7115(a) instead imposes an affirmative duty on a federal agency to honor the current assignments of unit employees by remitting regular and periodic dues deducted from their accrued salaries to their exclusive representative. Until the funds thus deducted are delivered to the union, they remain the property of the particular members from whose earned wages they are taken. By applying a portion of the pay of unit employees to extinguish the debt owed another, Goodfellow abrogated its statutory duty to the former....

[W]e are not concerned here with the union's entitlement to Brown's dues. We look only to the method of recoupment. Finally, it is unnecessary for us to address the dual question of the Air Force's responsibility to reimburse Brown for sums mistakenly deducted, and her right to receive these sums despite her union status. Though we recognize the government employer's prerogative "to recover by offset or otherwise sums illegally or erroneously paid,"...the fact remains that the monies against which it effected a set-off belonged to innocent third parties, with the employer's only interest at the time of allotment being the transmittal of such monies to Local 1816. Having thus impinged upon employee protections secured by § 7115(a), Goodfellow's conduct falls within the ambit of § 7116(a)(1) and (8).

Distinguishing the results reached as applying only to the cases then pending before the circuit courts, FLRA declined to be bound by the decisions in AFGE Local 1816 v. FLRA, 715 F.2d 224, 228 (5th Cir. 1983), and AFGE Local 2612 v. FLRA, 739 F.2d 87, 90 (2d Cir. 1984). FLRA stated that it consistently ruled, and continued to hold, that an agency's recoupment of dues, erroneously remitted to the exclusive representative of a bargaining unit on behalf of a former unit member, by deducting the previous overpayment from the subsequent remittance of dues for current members, was consistent with § 7115(a) and did not violate § 7116(a)(1) or (8) of the Statute. Wright-Patterson AFB and AFGE Council 214, 23 FLRA 376, 379-380 (1986). On appeal in the AFGE Local 214 case, FLRA was again reversed. AFGE Local 214 v. FLRA, 835 F.2d 1458 (D.C. Cir. 1987), on remand, Dept. of Air Force, Hq., AFLC, Wright-Patterson AFB and AFGE, Council 214, 31 FLRA 1197 (1988). The appeals court stated, at 835 F.2d at 1462 (over the dissent of Judge Williams):

The employer's error in withholding and remitting dues from supervisory employees does not permit it to compound the error by reducing the allotment it is obliged to make. Even though the union receives the same sum as it was entitled to receive, the employees have suffered the conversion by the employer of wages they still own.

We do not decide whether the employer could recover the amounts improperly withheld from supervisory employees and remitted to the union in a separate action brought against the union.

FLRA called it a day. Lowry AFB and AFGE Local 1974, 31 FLRA 793, 797 (1988), announced that FLRA would follow the courts: agencies violate the Statute if they recoup dues payments for personnel no longer in the unit by deducting funds from later payments to the union.

A proposal is nonnegotiable that requires dues deduction to continue when an employee moves from one bargaining unit to another. Under AFGE Local 2058 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Independence Nat'l Park, 68 FLRA 676, 685-86 (2015) (Member Pizzella dissenting in part) (Provision 3):

The Agency's interpretation of § 7115(b)(1) is consistent with Authority precedent finding that when an employee leaves a bargaining unit with the expectation that the departure is permanent, then the employee "is entitled to the widest possible discretion in resuming obligations [that] were purely discretionary."[134] In this regard, the Authority has found that an agency may not begin automatically deducting union dues once an employee is in a new bargaining unit unless the employee executes a new SF-1187 authorizing the deduction.[135] In contrast, the Authority has found that when an employee leaves a bargaining unit with the expectation that the departure is only temporary (as in a detail), an agency must stop automatically deducting union dues while the employee is out of the bargaining unit, but may resume deducting union dues without the employee executing a new SF-1187 once the employee returns to the unit.[136] Here, there is no claim, or basis for finding, that Provision 3 applies only to temporary transfers.

Consistent with the foregoing, by prohibiting the Agency from observing a transferred employee's discretion to resume or discontinue dues deductions, Provision 3 requires the Agency to violate the plain wording of § 7115(b)(1). And the Union provides no basis for finding that the factors that it cites--the units' shared payroll, human-resources offices, and union officers; the units' geographic proximity; and the unbroken relationship between the Agency and the transferred employee--are relevant here. In this regard, while those types of factors might be relevant in certain non-negotiability contexts, such as when the Authority must determine whether two units should be consolidated,[138] the Union cites no authority for the proposition that those factors have any relevance in a negotiability dispute, or to the application of § 7115(b)(1). Therefore, because NERO and INDE are distinct bargaining units, "the agreement between the [A]gency and the [Union]...ceases to be applicable to" an employee who leaves one unit, and the employee's "allotment...for the deduction of dues...shall terminate."[139] At that point, § 7115(b)(1) requires the Agency to stop deducting Union dues for that employee, even if the employee then joins the other unit.[140] Accordingly, we find that Provision 3 is contrary to § 7115(b)(1) of the Statute.

The Union also argues that Provision 3 concerns a matter negotiable at the election of the Agency under § 7106(b)(1) of the Statute, and is a procedure under § 7106(b)(2) of the Statute. However, a provision that is contrary to law remains so regardless of whether it pertains to a permissive subject of bargaining under § 7106(b)(1),[143] or is a procedure under § 7106(b)(2).[144] Therefore, the Union's arguments provide no basis for finding that Provision 3 is consistent with law.

Unions may be on the receiving end of a ULP when it initiates termination of a dues allotment. If the union holds exclusive recognition, it commits a ULP by requesting that the employer cancel a voluntary dues allotment for an employee who shifts from one craft to another within the unit. The employee has the right to automatic dues deduction as long as he remains in the unit of recognition. The union's internal arrangements with subsidiary locals below the level of recognition cannot defeat the employee's statutory dues deduction right.
