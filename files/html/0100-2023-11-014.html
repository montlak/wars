1. Evidentiary Value of Charge

Charges may serve as proof if they set forth in factual detail the employee's errors and deficiencies and if they are corroborated by other evidence. The evidentiary significance of charges originated with DePauw v. ITC, 782 F.2d 1564 (Fed. Cir. 1986), involving an unacceptable performance case heard by an arbitrator. The agency proposal letter was detailed, but at the arbitration hearing the agency failed to introduce the appellant's work record into the hearing record. The appellant then elected not to testify, believing that the agency's evidence was insufficient to establish a prima facie case. The Federal Circuit suggested that the totality of the evidence, including some testimony of agency witnesses, created substantial evidence. It was implied, but not directly determined, that the proposal letter itself, when uncontradicted, rose to the level of evidence.

The Board mentioned DePauw in Mitchell v. USPS, 32 MSPR 362, 365 (1987):

...The appellant did not challenge the merits of the counselings and letters of warning, even though they were cited in the notice of proposed removal. Although the letters of warning and counselings were not made part of the record, because the merits of the prior record were not challenged, the prior record was properly considered by the agency.... See also DePauw v. U.S. International Trade Comm'n, 782 F.2d 1564, 1567 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (specifications in notice of proposed removal that are sufficiently detailed that they could be disputed by the appellant, if he so desired, may be considered as evidence), cert. denied, [479 U.S. 815], 107 S. Ct. 69 (1986).

Yancey v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 606, 607-08 (1987), credited statements in the agency decision letter in an adverse action with evidentiary weight concerning the nature of the appointment of the appellant, a jurisdictional issue. [Refer to "Admissions" later in this chapter.] Matters evolved with Gill v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 308, 311 (1987), an appeal presented by a pro se appellant discharged for AWOL. The issue was whether the agency proved its case. The adverse action proposal notice carried evidentiary weight:

In refusing to consider as evidence the notice of charges against appellant, the administrative judge relied on the Board's holding in Trachy v. Defense Communications Agency, 18 MSPR 317, 323 (1983), that the notice of proposed removal is tantamount to an indictment and does not constitute evidence of the charge. This has been the Board's position in the past....

Noting the Federal Circuit's DePauw holding, Gill continued, 34 MSPR at 311:

...The court stated that where the letter of charges is not merely conclusory, but sets forth in great factual detail the employee's errors and deficiencies, and where the notice is corroborated by other evidence, the letter of charges may be considered as forming part of the agency's valid proof.... In this case, the notice of proposed removal set forth in detail the dates of appellant's absences and gave a chronology of all written and oral communications between appellant and the agency. Moreover, the notice of charges was supported by memoranda from the agency to appellant, reflecting that she was considered to be AWOL. Thus, the administrative judge erred in giving no probative value to the proposal notice. To the extent that Trachy and its line of cases may be read to operate as an absolute bar against considering a proposal notice of evidence of the charges contained therein, those cases are modified in accordance with DePauw.

The result is that a proposal notice may be evidence if it is sufficiently detailed and corroborated by other evidence. See Render v. VA, 90 MSPR 441, 445 ¶ 12 (2001) (allegation that the appellant failed to respond to a phone call did not constitute preponderant evidence when the agency failed to prove that the appellant was in her office when the call was placed); Perez v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 65 MSPR 287, 289 (1994) (no corroboration; no evidentiary weight to the proposal). Gill was followed in Coyle v. Dept. of Treasury, 62 MSPR 241, 244 (1994), an adverse action appeal:

Notwithstanding the agency's failure to provide documentation in support of the factual circumstances stated in the notice of proposed removal, a letter of charges can itself constitute valid proof of the charges when the notice sets forth in detail the employee's errors and deficiencies and where the notice is corroborated by other evidence. See Gill v. Dept. of the Navy, 34 MSPR 308, 311 (1987). Here, specification two sets forth in great detail the nature of the appellant's unprofessional conduct and the appellant herself corroborated factual circumstances alleged by the notice, including that she disconnected the call simply because she considered that it had become "nonproductive." Under these circumstances, we find that the administrative judge properly found specification two to be supported by preponderant evidence.

Evidentiary corroboration of charges is of significance, Delancy v. USPS, 88 MSPR 129, 133 ¶ 8 (2001):

Both the Bench Decision and the agency's petition for review misstate the circumstances in which the Board has considered allegations in an agency's proposal notice as evidence of misconduct. The Board has never held that an agency's proposal notice can itself constitute preponderant evidence of the charged conduct. It has held that "where the letter of charges is not merely conclusory, but sets forth in great factual detail the employee's errors and deficiencies, and where the notice is corroborated by other evidence, the letter of charge may be considered as forming part of the agency's valid proof." Gill v. Department of the Navy, 34 MSPR 308, 311 (1987) (emphasis added) (citing DePauw v. U.S. Int'l Trade Comm., 782 F.2d 1564 (Fed. Cir. 1986)); see also Perez v. Railroad Retirement Board, 65 MSPR 287, 289 (1994) (rejecting the proposal notice as sufficient evidence where it was not corroborated by other evidence). The agency has pointed to no documentary or testimonial evidence to corroborate the allegations in its charge of failure to follow instructions. And as the AJ observed, the appellant did not admit that this charge was true. Under these circumstances, the AJ correctly found that the charge could not be sustained.

See Beauford v. USPS, CH-0752-14-0104-I-1 (NP 9/24/2014) ("A deciding official's declaration that he lost confidence and trust in an employee may satisfy the nexus requirement. See Ellis v. Department of Defense, 114 M.S.P.R. 407, ¶ 9 (2010) (finding that nexus had been established based upon the declaration of the deciding official). Additionally, an unsworn document such as a proposal notice or decision, as opposed to a declaration, may serve as valid proof where it is "not merely conclusory," sets forth the misconduct in great detail, and is supported by other evidence. See, e.g., Delancy v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 8 (2001)."); Kirkland-Zuck v. DHUD, 90 MSPR 12, 16 ¶ 9 (2001) (Board has not held that charges alone can constitute preponderant evidence of the charged conduct, but where the letter sets forth in great factual detail errors and deficiencies, and where the notice is corroborated by other evidence, the letter of charges may form part of the agency's proof).

In Chapter 43 performance cases, the agency need only prove its charges by substantial evidence. Charges contained in a performance-based proposal or decision letter may take on added, even determinative, evidentiary weight. [Refer to Chapter 9, "Charges, Work Papers."]

Until 1987 an advance notice of adverse action was treated like an indictment--its purpose to inform the employee of the charges. An indictment is not evidence of crime; it is only a charge. See Beamer v. DOJ, 25 MSPR 483, 487 n.* (1984); Queen v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 434, 436 (1984) (agency record of the proceeding, including the notice of proposed removal and the agency's unsupported allegations, were entitled to little or no weight); Stalkfleet v. USPS, 6 MSPR 637, 645 n.3, 6 MSPB 536 (1981) (charges and specifications were not evidence of the charges). An adverse action decision or proposal did not constitute evidence of the charges. See Wildeman v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 313, 320 (1984). The failure of an employee to deny the charges in the advance notice and in the notice of decision was inconsequential; it was not adverse evidence. The appellant had no burden to disprove the charges against him. Rather, the agency was to submit a well-developed record to the AJ to prove its case by a preponderance of the evidence. See Cooper v. United States, 639 F.2d 727, 226 Ct. Cl. 75 (Ct. Cl. 1980) (discussed later in this chapter, "Judicial Interpretations"); Renehan v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 485, 486 (1982) ("[A]llegations contained in ALOC determination decision are not evidence of unacceptable performance[.] "); Walls v. DCAA, 10 MSPR 441, 442-43 (1982) ("The fact that appellant failed to deny the charges contained in the advanced notice is inconsequential. Appellant has no burden to disprove the charges against her."); Barnett v. Dept. of Interior, 6 MSPR 4, 6, 6 MSPB 19 (1981) ("Appellant...did not relieve the agency of its burden of proof by stipulating to the truth of the charges.... The agency was obligated to submit a well developed record to the presiding official in order to prove its case by a preponderance of the evidence"); Hill v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 432, 433 n.1, 3 MSPB 506 (1980) ("Statements contained in proposal and decision notices are merely allegations and do not constitute evidence which may be used by an agency to prove its case."); Powell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 513, 516, 3 MSPB 35 (1980) ("The agency's confusion in distinguishing between that which constituted 'allegations,' as contained in the notice of proposed removal, and that which constituted 'evidence' required to support those allegations resulted in the agency's failure to prove by a preponderance of the evidence, as required by 5 U.S.C. 7701(c)(1)(B) and 5 C.F.R. 1201.56(a)(ii) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1)(ii)], the charges contained in the advance notice."). Charges were not considered evidence, even when they were verified and notarized by the proposing official. The purpose of the charges was to serve as an advance notice, not as an affidavit. Notarization did not alone show that the proposing official had firsthand knowledge of the events described in the charge. Nothing required the proposing official to be personally familiar with the charges. Copeland v. CSA, 6 MSPR 280, 281-82, 6 MSPB 239 (1981). If the appellant generally denied charges, the failure by the appellant to refute each and every one of the charges was not a silent admission, although at times the appellant's silence could be an admission entitled to some probative value as hearsay. Copeland, supra (citing Powell, supra).

There were deviations from the foregoing rule, but they were few. Although a notice of proposed removal was not probative evidence of the charges against an employee, if an employee with personal knowledge of the charges incorporated by reference the proposal notice into his testimony, the testimony could be probative of the charges contained in the notice; barring impeachment, the testimony (incorporating the charges) carried enough evidentiary value to survive a motion to dismiss the agency case. McKenzie v. Dept. of Interior, 16 MSPR 397, 403 (1983), and 18 MSPR 377, 380 (1983). If the employee never denied the charge and the government offered enough evidence to establish a prima facie case (a case sufficient to warrant a finding against the employee if unrebutted), an adverse inference could be drawn against the employee who failed to deny the charges at the hearing. Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 488 (Fed. Cir. 1984). But a prima facie case was presumably based on at least substantial evidence. Adams did not decide that an adverse inference alone, without substantial evidence from the agency, supported an adverse action.
