2. Statutory Definitions Control; Multiple Disclosures, Personnel Actions

Conduct protected by statute may not generally be the subject of discipline. Special Counsel v. Harvey, 28 MSPR 595, 604 n.16 (1984). Reprisal, in a broad sense, includes any action designed to punish an employee for exercising his protected rights or to deter him from exercising those rights. Harvey v. MSPB, 802 F.2d 537 (D.C. Cir. 1986). Considering an IRA appeal, a court summarized the legislative intent in Weber v. Dept. of Army, 9 F.3d 97, 100 (Fed. Cir. 1993):

...The statute makes it a "prohibited personnel practice" for any federal employee to take personnel actions because of disclosure of information by an employee who reasonably believes that government action violates a law, rule or regulation, or is an example of gross mismanagement. Essentially, those in authority may not punish or deter those in their power from making embarrassing information public.

See Scoggins v. Dept. of Army, 123 MSPR 592, 601-02 ¶ 11 & n.6, 2016 MSPB 32 (2016) ("Pre‑WPEA law defined a protected disclosure as a disclosure of information that an appellant reasonably believes evidences a violation of any law, rule, or regulation, gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety.... The WPEA amended the definition at 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)(A)(i) by striking 'a violation' and inserting 'any violation.' WPEA § 101(a)(1).").

The allegation, or proof, of reprisal should conform to the statutory definition. The question of whether conduct constitutes reprisal, particularly under the whistleblowing reprisal section, 5 USC 2302(b)(8), may be jurisdictional, and jurisdiction is a matter of evidence and well-pleaded allegations, not concession by the agency. See Jensen v. USDA, 104 MSPR 379, 384 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 3 (2007) ("Further, regardless of any concessions by the agency, '[t]he question of whether a disclosure is protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) is a matter of mixed fact and law and stipulations relating to such matters are not binding.' Schneider v. Department of Homeland Security, 98 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 17 n.4 (2005) (citing Wojcicki v. Department of the Air Force, 72 M.S.P.R. 628, 634 (1996))").

"In cases involving multiple alleged protected disclosures and personnel actions, an appellant establishes Board jurisdiction over his IRA appeal when he makes a nonfrivolous allegation that at least one alleged personnel action was taken in reprisal for at least one alleged protected disclosure." Mason v. DHS, 116 MSPR 135, 140 ¶ 7, 2011 MSPB 39 (2011). See Tullis v. Dept. of Navy, 117 MSPR 236, 239 ¶ 6, 2012 MSPB 3 (2012) ("In cases involving multiple alleged protected disclosures, the Board has jurisdiction where the appellant has exhausted his administrative remedies before OSC and makes a nonfrivolous allegation that at least one alleged personnel action was taken for at least one alleged protected disclosure.").

If the allegation is whistleblower reprisal, the protected disclosure must actually allege some condition falling within the statutory classification. First, there is supposed to be a disclosure. Garrison v. DOD, 101 MSPR 229, 234 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 38 (2006), noted that "giving a subordinate a high performance rating against the direction of a superior is not a "disclosure of information" protected by the WPA." It is insufficient to call only for an investigation of various events, Berkley v. Dept. of Army, 71 MSPR 341, 348 (1996), although a disclosure is protected even if the information disclosed is already known to management. Geyer v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 13 (1994). A discussion with a supervisor of whether to bring a matter to the attention of higher authority is not protected under the WPA. Garrison v. DOD, 101 MSPR 229, 235 ¶ 11, 2006 MSPB 38 (2006) ("However, this conversation is not protected under the WPA because in speaking with Moser the appellant was not 'bringing the matter to the attention of a higher authority,' but discussing whether or not he should do so. See Huffman v. Office of Personnel Management, 263 F.3d 1341, 1351 (Fed. Cir. 2001)."). But see Meuwissen, post (discussed later in this chapter, "'Gross' Waste or Mismanagement").

The EEO and whistleblower administrative procedures often are used to air generalized grievances concerning employment conditions. The IRA appeal is specific in its requirements and does not function as a catch-all grievance process, Rzucidlo v. Dept. of Army, 101 MSPR 616, 624 ¶ 18, 2006 MSPB 109 (2006):

Taken as a whole, the appellant's alleged disclosures to O'Malley, Valenza, and Kollra are fundamentally his own complaints and grievances about how he was treated by the agency, and, therefore, they do not constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of a protected disclosure. See, e.g., Willis v. Department of Agriculture, 141 F.3d 1139, 1144 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (the WPA "is intended to protect government employees who risk their own personal job security for the advancement of the public good by disclosing abuses by government personnel"); Frederick v. Department of Justice, 73 F.3d 349, 353 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (the purpose of the WPA is to "root out real wrongdoing"); McCorcle, 98 M.S.P.R. 363, ¶ 24 (WPA was not intended to remedy claims regarding the agency's treatment of the appellant's grievances and vague allegations of an agency conspiracy to terminate him). Overall, there is no doubt or ambiguity that the appellant's allegations are not ones that a reasonable person in his position would believe evidenced a violation of any law, rule, or regulation, gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety by either the Gesells or the agency.

See Schutte v. Dept. of Treasury, 203 Fed. Appx. 338, 341 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) ("Many of the allegations, as the Board found, were merely conclusory. Reprisals based on an employee's exercise of her right to complain about the employee's own treatment by the agency do not qualify for whistleblower protection under the WPA. See Spruill v. MSPB, 978 F.2d 679, 690 (Fed. Cir. 1992). To the extent that the alleged disclosures were made to her supervisor concerning the supervisor's own conduct, they were not protected, Huffman, 263 F.3d at 1344, and 'disclosures of trivial violations do not constitute protected disclosures' under the WPA. Langer v. Dep't. of Treasury, 265 F.3d 1259, 1266 (Fed. Cir. 2001).").

Our discussion concerns protections available to federal employees under the CSRA and the WPA. There are other statutes that may provide protections to whistleblowers. Other agencies have responsibility for whistleblower protection under other statutes. See Knox v. Dept. of Labor, 434 F.3d 721 (4th Cir. 2006) (describing federal whistleblower's action under the Clean Air Act); Sasse v. Dept. of Labor, 409 F.3d 773 (6th Cir. 2005) (federal employee's reprisal case brought under Clean Air Act ("CAA"), 42 USC 7622(a); Solid Waste Disposal Act ("SWDA"), 42 USC 6971(a); Federal Water Pollution Control Act ("FWPCA"), 33 USC 1367(a)); Dysert v. Secretary of Labor, 105 F.3d 607 (11th Cir. 1997) (Energy Reorganization Act); Kahn v. Secretary of Labor, 64 F.3d 271 (7th Cir. 1995) (analyzing a private sector employee's suit under the whistleblower provisions of the Energy Reorganization Act); Bartlik v. Dept. of Labor, 62 F.3d 163, 165 (6th Cir. 1995) (noting the existence of the whistleblower provision of the Energy Reorganization Act, 42 USC 5851 (1988)); Pogue v. Dept. of Labor, 940 F.2d 1287 (9th Cir. 1991) (federal employee's case under various environmental whistleblowing statutes: Comprehensive Environmental Response Compensation and Liability Act (42 USC 9610), the Federal Water Pollution Control Act (33 USC 1367), the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (42 USC 6971), and the Toxic Substances Control Act (15 USC 2622).); cf. Saban v. Dept. of Labor, 309 F.3d 376 (7th Cir. 2007) (private sector employee's suit under the Pipeline Safety Improvement Act, 49 USC 60101, et seq.); Meredith v. Federal Mine Safety & Health Review Comm'n, 177 F.3d 1042 (D.C. Cir. 1999) (describing the process for the review of reprisal complaints by miners and their representatives who make complaints to MSHA).
