broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 13: Safety, Section C: Safety Committees, Subsection 02: Non-Certified Committee

2. Non-Certified Committee

A union could require negotiations on proposals requiring establishment of a non-certified occupational safety and health program to comply with Exec. Order 12196 and requiring the employer to establish health and safety committees at each institution, with an equal number of management and nonmanagement representatives, the latter to be designated by the local union. AFGE, Council of Prison Locals and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 11 FLRA 286, 288 (1983). But a proposal for a non-certified safety committee (one not established under Exec. Order 12196) is not negotiable if it establishes duties for its members, creates a rotating chairmanship, and assigns specific functions to the committee, including participation in safety and health inspections and monitoring office safety and health training programs, and establishes the committee as more than an advisor, allowing it to request OSHA inspections. AFGE Local 2786 and DMA, 20 FLRA 193, 195-98 (1985). [Refer in this chapter to "Joint Committees," "Union Rights, Bargaining Rights and Procedures."]

What can a non-certified safety committee safely do? The question was answered in part in Int'l Plate Printers Local 2 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 25 FLRA 113, 144-51 (1987) (majority opinion). FLRA determined to be negotiable a proposal that the parties establish a committee of two management and two union members authorized to: inspect work sites; participate in the scheduling of unit employees to be tested in hearing conservation and other health and safety programs; make recommendations based on reports regarding employees' injuries resulting in time lost from work; monitor reports of compliance with OSHA or agency safety standards; make studies and recommendations regarding safety and health standards, practices and procedures. FLRA noted:

Like the committee in U.S. Department of Justice[, 11 FLRA 286 (1983),] which was authorized to "investigate" unsafe working conditions, the functions of the subcommittee...do not replace or conflict with the exercise of the Agency's rights. The Agency here has not shown that the functions...would bind the Agency in any way or otherwise interfere with its rights.

FLRA subsequently determined that non-certified safety and health committees are forums for the expression of concerns over safety and health matters, and proposals to establish those joint committees are negotiable. If an existing agency safety committee is responsible for specific safety tasks, proposals for union membership are nonnegotiable because the union becomes involved in the deliberative process used by the agency to exercise its rights relating to safety and health matters and because the tasks involved in the committee involve the assignment of work. AFGE Local 85 and VAMC, Leavenworth, 30 FLRA 400, 408-09 (1987).

Another non-certified committee was the subject of commentary, NFFE Local 2052 and Dept. of Interior, BLM, Boise Dist. Office, 30 FLRA 797, 805-06 (1987):

In our view,...uncertified health and safety committees which enable parties to jointly seek out, identify, investigate, report on, discuss, and seek solutions to health and safety concerns which have an impact on employees represented by the Union are within the duty to bargain insofar as they do not interfere with other protected management rights....

However, the following portions of the proposal, which would enable the Union to interject itself into the deliberative process by which management exercises its reserved rights under the Statute, are nonnegotiable for the reasons set out below.

The last sentence of (a) of the proposal provides that not only will the committee notify the District Manager of any harmful environmental or unsafe conditions which its studies reveal but that the committee's notice to the District Manager will specify "the action needed to correct the action." Further, part of section (c) of the proposal would give the health and safety committee the authority to "direct corrective action" concerning unsafe working conditions referred to it. By so doing, these portions of the proposal interfere with management's prerogative to determine who shall determine what is the appropriate corrective action for unsafe or unhealthy working conditions....

Finally, section (g) of the proposal mandates that the members of the committee will receive training in their duties. It is well established...that proposals requiring management to provide formal training, or periodically to assign employees to specific types of training are outside the duty to bargain as they involve assignments of work....
