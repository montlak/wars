d. Offer of Settlement

Generally, an agency's offer of settlement is entitled to no weight in determining the reasonableness of a penalty. Luna v. SSA, 85 MSPR 301, 307 ¶ 15 (2000); see Cheng v. USDA, 84 MSPR 144, 146-47 ¶ 5 (1999) (AJ should have given no weight to a settlement offer, apparently through a last chance agreement, that would have permitted the appellant to remain employed if she sought medical treatment or demonstrated she did not need it); Mills v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 403, 407 (1986) (citing Hayden v. USPS, 15 MSPR 296, 302 (1983)). But see Lampack v. USPS, 27 MSPR 468, 470 (1985) (Board considered a settlement offer of demotion instead of removal as providing some evidence concerning the severity of the removal penalty that was imposed); cf. Eisinger v. MSPB, 236 Fed. Appx. 628, 630 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (Hatch Act case involving penalty determination: "[h]owever, many of the cases cited by Mr. Eisinger are settled cases and are therefore of limited precedential value"); Spahn v. Dept. of Justice, 93 MSPR 195 (2003) (considering settlements relative to a claim of disparate treatment raised in an EEO defense). The offer itself is excluded as evidence because its admission would discourage frank settlement discussions and settlement discussions are encouraged as a matter of public policy. [For further discussion of disparate treatment, refer to Chapter 11, "Past Discipline; Performance Record, Aggravating Factors," concerning harmful error.]

Of settlement offers used to suggest the propriety of mitigation, Trujillo v. DHS, DA-0752-10-0436-I-2 (NP 6/29/2012), noted:

After the agency issued the notice of proposed removal, the parties engaged in settlement negotiations, during which Mr. Vaughan offered to reduce the penalty to a demotion. The Board has long held that settlement offers are not admissible to show the existence or extent of liability. See Frank v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 90 M.S.P.R. 458, ¶ 4 n.2 (2001); Special Counsel v. Costello, 75 M.S.P.R. 562, 609 (1997) (under the Federal Rules of Evidence an unaccepted offer of settlement is ordinarily not admissible to show the existence or extent of liability), rev'd on other grounds, 182 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999). It was appropriate for the agency to make an offer of settlement. See Asberry v. U.S. Postal Service, 692 F.2d 1378, 1381-1382 (Fed. Cir. 1982). However, an agency's offer of settlement is entitled to no weight in considering the propriety of mitigation of the agency-imposed penalty. Williams v. Department of Health & Human Services, 29 M.S.P.R. 525, 528 (1985).

An appellant's suggestion that through settlement the appellant would avoid creating bad publicity for the agency (or avoid blowing the whistle on agency mismanagement) appears to be protected--assuming it has a reasonable relationship to the problem the employee is trying to resolve--and should not be the basis for an adverse action charge. See Berube v. GSA, 30 MSPR 581, 596-600(1986). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Settlement Offer."]
