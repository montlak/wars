8. Insolence, Abusive Language

An agency is entitled to expect its employees to conform to accepted standards of civil behavior and decorum, Redfearn v. Dept. of Labor, 58 MSPR 307, 316 (1993), and to respect the authority of supervisors as well as rules and regulations. See Bassett v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 66, 69 (1987) (stating rule; citing Hubble, post). "Acts of insubordination and lying by an employee to his supervisor concerning the employee's work is a substantial offense regardless of the specific subject matter involved." Pepper v. VA, 8 MSPR 524, 528 (1981). Abusive language and behavior are not acceptable or conducive to a stable working atmosphere. Hubble v. DOJ, 6 MSPR 659, 661, 6 MSPB 553 (1981); see Gill v. DOD, 92 MSPR 23, 29 ¶ 15 (2002) (based on mitigating factors and provocation, a reprimand was justified when the appellant raised her voice to a supervisor); Luna v. SSA, 85 MSPR 301, 308 ¶ 16 (2000) (disrespectful conduct is unacceptable and not conducive to a stable working atmosphere; agencies are entitled to expect employees to conduct themselves in conformance with accepted standards); Special Counsel v. Costello, 75 MSPR 562, 598 (1997) (abusive language to a superior, particularly from a supervisor and law enforcement officer who is held to a higher standard of conduct, furnishes a basis for disciplinary action), rev'd on other grounds, Costello v. MSPB, 182 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Boykin v. USPS, 51 MSPR 56, 59-60 (1991) (removal of a letter carrier for loud and abusive language to a supervisor was sustained; the appellant had a relatively short service record and past discipline); Crawford v. DOJ, 45 MSPR 234, 237 (1990) (correctional officer was removed for inattention to duty and disrespect to supervisors in front of inmates--conduct that "clearly undermines the authority of correctional officers who are required to supervise and maintain order in the facility"); Hockman v. American Battle Monuments Comm'n, 12 MSPR 642, 646-47 (1982) (insubordination was established when, in a loud manner in front of other employees, the appellant called his supervisor a "pathological liar" and suggested that he obtain psychiatric assistance; adverse action was mitigated to a reprimand based on a past excellent work record and length of service); cf. Caesar v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-0752-10-0394-I-1 (NP 3/2/2012) (allegedly rude emails: "that the appellant may have viewed her remarks as merely truthful or the product of frustration does not immunize them from disciplinary action"); Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 13 MSPR 546, 547 (1982) (language used by the appellant to a supervisor was not "Black English," but was graphic and offensive language unacceptable in the workplace). [Refer later in this chapter to "Profanity" and "Criticism, False Statement about Coworker, Subordinate, and Supervisor."]

Insolent conduct includes openly defiant or disrespectful behavior. Beaudoin v. VA, 99 MSPR 489, 495 ¶ 12 (2005). Continuing insolent, insubordinate attitude warrants removal. Insolent disrespect toward supervisors so seriously undermines the capacity of management to maintain employee efficiency and discipline that no agency should be expected to exercise forbearance for that conduct more than once. Carson v. VA, 33 MSPR 666, 669-70 (1987); see Gaines v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 527, 531-32 ¶¶ 11-13 (2003) (removal for disrespectful conduct following prior discipline for similar conduct and discipline for dissimilar misconduct); Brousseau v. USPS, 29 MSPR 623, 624 (1986) ("Removal for use of foul and abusive language, failure to obey orders, insubordination, and creating a workroom disturbance, when considered with appellant's past disciplinary record, promotes the efficiency of the service."); Cormier v. USPS, 22 MSPR 112, 113 (1984) ("[D]isrespect towards supervisors is a serious offense."); Jefferson v. VA, 6 MSPR 348, 352, 6 MSPB 297 (1981) (insolence towards supervisors: "few other types of misconduct go so directly to the heart of maintain the 'efficiency of the service'"); cf. O'Neill v. DHUD, 220 F.3d 1354, 1364-65 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (affirming the removal of a typing clerk for charges that included disrespectful or insolent behavior toward a supervisor)

Hornsby v. Fed. Housing Finance Agency, DC-0752-15-0576-I-2 (NP 4/28/2022), noted of a manager's comments:

...The Board has frequently held that rude, discourteous, and unprofessional behavior in the workplace is outside the accepted standards of conduct reasonably expected by agencies and can be the subject of discipline. See Holland v. Department of Defense, 83 M.S.P.R. 317, ¶¶ 10-12 (1999) (sustaining a removal for rude and discourteous behavior); Wilson v. Department of Justice, 68 M.S.P.R. 303, 309-10 (1995) (sustaining a removal for disrespectful conduct and the use of insulting, abusive language). Although an employee may be allowed more leeway with disrespectful conduct in "certain emotional, confrontational contexts," the conduct at issue in this specification occurred in a normal employment setting where the appellant should have expected normal standards of conduct to apply. See Hamilton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 115 M.S.P.R. 673, ¶¶ 11, 13 (2011). Moreover, the Board has held that, in a conduct unbecoming charge, an agency can hold a supervisor to a higher standard of behavior than other employees. See Ray v. Department of the Army, 97 M.S.P.R. 101, ¶ 58 (2004), aff'd, 176 F. App'x 110 (Fed. Cir. 2006); Special Counsel v. Zimmerman, 36 M.S.P.R. 274, 293 (1988) (stating that, because the appellant was a supervisor, he is held to a higher standard of conduct than subordinate employees and should set an example for other employees to follow)....

Repeated insolent behavior mocks management's authority and supervisory responsibility. See Redfearn, supra. Abusive language and disrespectful behavior constitute just cause for removal. Fonville v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 351, 355 (1986), post; Williams v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-11-0054-I-1 (NP 3/23/2012) ("The Board has explicitly recognized that imposing discipline for an employee's insubordinate, disrespectful, and disruptive conduct promotes the efficiency of the service. See Means v. Department of Labor, 60 M.S.P.R. 108, 113 (1993); Fonville v. Department of Health & Human Services, 30 M.S.P.R. 351, 354-55 (1986)."); Roberson v. VA, 27 MSPR 489, 494 (1985) ("Abusive language and disrespectful behavior are not acceptable conduct and are not conducive to a stable working atmosphere. Therefore, they constitute just cause for removal."). [Refer later in this chapter to "Critical Statements" and "Threat; Inappropriate Conduct."]

An inappropriate comment in an email sent through agency facilities, coupled with failure to exercise sufficient efforts to control or prevent more egregious emails of others in the group that was exchanging the email traffic, warranted termination of an employee in a management ALJ equivalent position in VA v. Chiappetta, CB-7521-16-0014-N-1 (NP 12/2/2022):

On petition for review, the respondent argues that he was not clearly on notice of any obligation to report the inappropriate FOH emails because the agency has no policy on the matter. We agree with the respondent that the agency does not appear to have any written policy covering this specific situation, but we also agree with the ALJ that the respondent knew all along that the FOH email traffic was improper. The respondent not only completed numerous trainings touching on these issues, we find that no specific instruction should have been required for the respondent to understand how to handle this matter appropriately. This is not a situation wherein the respondent ran afoul of an idiosyncratic and nonobvious agency rule. Cf. Gunn v. U.S. Postal Service, 13 M.S.P.R. 576, 578-79 (1982).
