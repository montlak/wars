2. Criteria

The competitive area is the geographic or organizational boundary of the RIF competition, 5 CFR 351.402 (2022):

(a) Each agency shall establish competitive areas in which employees compete for retention under this part.

(b) A competitive area must be defined solely in terms of the agency's organizational unit(s) and geographical location, and except as provided in paragraph (e) of this section, it must include all employees within the competitive area so defined. A competitive area may consist of all or part of an agency. The minimum competitive area is a subdivision of the agency under separate administration within the local commuting area.

(c) When a competitive area will be in effect less than 90 days prior to the effective date of a reduction in force, a description of the competitive area shall be submitted to the OPM for approval in advance of the reduction in force. Descriptions of all competitive areas must be made readily available for review.

(d) Each agency shall establish a separate competitive area for each Inspector General activity established under authority of the Inspector General Act of 1978, Public Law 95-452, as amended, in which only employees of that office shall compete for retention under this part.

(e) When an agency finds that a competitive area defined under paragraph (b) of this section includes pay band positions and positions not covered by a pay band, the agency may, at its discretion, define a separate (and additional) competitive area, otherwise consistent with paragraph (b) of this section, to include only pay band positions. The original competitive area would then include only the remaining positions (i.e., those positions not covered by a pay band.)

The term "competitive area" is associated with the degree and extent of administrative control that the organization head exercises over operations, work functions, and personnel administration for employees in the organization; the term concerns the organizational authority to make decisions to establish or abolish programs, assign duties, and take personnel actions. Williams v. TVA, 24 MSPR 555 (1984) (reversing an action when the competitive area was defined too narrowly); see Bashein v. United States, 279 F.2d 255 (Ct. Cl. 1960) (affirming the validity of early regulations permitting the establishment of separate retention registers for separate components of an agency or activity); Fass v. Gray, 197 F.2d 587 (D.C. Cir. 1952) (same).

An agency's different activities, although located side by side, may be in separate competitive areas if each is (1) under a separate administrative authority; (2) independent of the others in operation, staff, work functions, and personnel administration; and (3) separately organized and clearly distinguished from the others. Under Coleman v. Dept. of Educ., 21 MSPR 574, 577 (1984), organizations can be in separate competitive areas even if they share personnel offices with another activity. The significant factor is the locus of authority to make decisions concerning the establishment or abolition of positions, that is, the authority to take personnel actions--the right to assign duties rather than the authority to issue documents effecting those actions. See Young v. Dept. of Interior, 21 MSPR 568, 570 (1984) (that an organization shares personnel functions with another organization does not preclude it from being a separate administrative area and separate competitive area); cf. Dancy v. USDA, 26 MSPR 321, 322 n.* (1985) (agency properly combined positions in a commuting area under a single administrative authority before a transfer of function); O'Donnell v. Dept. of Army, 13 MSPR 104, 106 (1982) (competitive area must include all or that part of the agency with employees assigned under a single administrative authority; the level of schools employing teachers is not relevant to the construction of the competitive area).

No statute, regulation, or case law requires an agency to expand a competitive area for the sole purpose of providing actual competition for a job. Grier v. DHHS, 750 F.2d 944 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Finch v. United States, 179 Ct. Cl. 1 (1967) (recognizing the difficulties created by a narrow competitive area but, following a review of the legislative history, finding no reason to upset the scheme established by civil service regulations implementing the Veterans Preference Act). A new competitive area does not emerge only because the suborganizations within an established competitive area are changed or reorganized. Blevins v. TVA, 46 MSPR 239, 245 (1990). If two activities have an agreement that, employees of one activity will be in a different competitive area from the employees of the other activity should there be a RIF, the Board may weigh that agreement against an assertion of a single competitive area. Caviness v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 548, 553-54 (1989).
