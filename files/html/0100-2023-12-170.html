9. Justification for Late Filing

5 USC 7701(e)(1) grants the Board the authority to extend the PFR filing period or to excuse a delayed PFR for good cause. The statutory authority is implemented by the Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.114 (2022):

(f) Extension of time to file. The Board will grant a motion for extension of time to file a pleading described in paragraph (a) only if the party submitting the motion shows good cause. Motions for extensions must be filed with the Clerk of the Board on or before the date on which the petition or other pleading is due. The Board, in its discretion, may grant or deny those motions without providing the other parties the opportunity to comment on them. A motion for an extension must be accompanied by an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746. (See Appendix IV.) The affidavit or sworn statement must include a specific and detailed description of the circumstances alleged to constitute good cause, and it should be accompanied by any available documentation or other evidence supporting the matters asserted.

(g) Late filings. Any pleading described in paragraph (a) of this section that is filed late must be accompanied by a motion that shows good cause for the untimely filing, unless the Board has specifically granted an extension of time under paragraph (f) of this section, or unless a motion for extension is pending before the Board. The motion must be accompanied by an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746. (See Appendix IV.) The affidavit or sworn statement must include: The reasons for failing to request an extension before the deadline for the submission, and a specific and detailed description of the circumstances causing the late filing, accompanied by supporting documentation or other evidence. Any response to the motion may be included in the response to the petition for review, the cross petition for review, or the response to the cross petition for review. The response will not extend the time provided by paragraph (e) of this section to file a cross petition for review or to respond to the petition or cross petition. In the absence of a motion, the Board may, in its discretion, determine on the basis of the existing record whether there was good cause for the untimely filing, or it may provide the party that submitted the document with an opportunity to show why it should not be dismissed or excluded as untimely.

The statute and regulation are interpreted through hundreds of decisions on the timeliness of PFRs.

"The Board may grant or deny a request for waiver of the time for filing a petition, in the interest of justice, after considering all of the facts and circumstances of a particular case." Wrighten v. Dept. of Army, 92 MSPR 71, 72 ¶ 3 (2002); Kinan v. DOD, 89 MSPR 407, 409 ¶ 3-4 (2001) (party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case). [Refer to treatment of belated appeals in Chapter 2, "Justification For Late Filing."]

Fassler v. USPS, 69 MSPR 672, 675 (1996), explained that:

A petition for review must be filed within thirty-five days of the issuance of the initial decision. 5 CFR § 1201.114(d). A late-filed petition for review must be accompanied by an affidavit or a sworn statement showing good cause for the untimely filing. Id. § 1201.114(f). To establish good cause for the untimely filing of a petition, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Dept. of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980).

Powell v. VA, CH-0714-18-0579-C-1 (NP 9/30/2022), emphasized:

...An appellant who files a petition for review late but does not file the required motion concerning the timeliness of his petition for review has failed to show the due diligence and ordinary prudence necessary for the Board to find good cause for his delay in filing. Galleno v. Office of Personnel Management, 63 M.S.P.R. 440, 442 (1994) (finding that an appellant who filed her petition for review 4 months late, and failed to file the required motion on timeliness, failed to show good cause for waiver of the filing deadline), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1236 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); Goldberg v. Department of Defense, 39 M.S.P.R. 515, 518 (1989) (stating that in the absence of good cause shown, the Board will not waive its timeliness requirements even if the delay is minimal); Mejia v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 M.S.P.R. 472, 473 (1988) (finding that, where the appellant failed to respond to the Clerk 's notice concerning the timeliness of his petition for review and his untimely filing did not include a motion for waiver of the time limit or an affidavit or statement to establish good cause, the appellant had not demonstrated good cause for the untimely filing)....

The burden is on the party seeking the waiver to establish good cause, as Batdorf v. MSPB, 582 Fed. Appx. 869, 871-72 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), noted, also explaining the deference shown by the court to the Board's waiver decision:

The petitioner carries the burden to show, by preponderant evidence, that good cause existed for the delay. Williams v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 71 M.S.P.R. 597, 601 (1996). To establish good cause, the petitioner "need not show that it was impossible to file timely, only that the delay was excusable under the circumstances where diligence or ordinary prudence had been exercised." Phillips v. U.S. Postal Serv., 695 F.2d 1389, 1391 (Fed. Cir. 1982).

We must affirm the Board's decision to deny a good cause waiver of a filing deadline unless the decision is "(1) arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law; (2) obtained without procedures required by law, rule, or regulation having been followed; or (3) unsupported by substantial evidence." 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c). We will not substitute our own judgment for that of the Board in determining whether the regulatory time limit for an appeal should be waived based upon a showing of good cause. Mendoza v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 966 F.2d 650, 653 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (en banc).

Kimble v. USPS, 74 MSPR 539, 542-43 (1997), added:

...To determine whether an appellant has shown good cause, the Board will consider the length of the delay, the reasonableness of his excuse and his showing of due diligence, whether he is proceeding pro se, and whether he has presented evidence of the existence of circumstances beyond his control that affected his ability to comply with the time limits or of unavoidable casualty or misfortune which similarly shows a causal relationship to his inability to timely file his petition. Moorman v. Dept. of the Army, 68 MSPR 60, 62-63 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

See Pettus v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0353-13-0409-B-1 (NP 4/4/2022) ("Importantly, the fact that the appellant's petition was less than 5 minutes late indicates that she was most likely diligently trying to timely file at the time of the deadline."); Adams v. OPM, 98 MSPR 541, 545 ¶ 12 (2005) (excusing a "minimal" delay of one day in filing a PFR; the appellant was pro se and there was some evidence that she suffered depression and anxiety, and had particular feelings of being overwhelmed and unable to handle her affairs during the few weeks immediately preceding the due date for the PFR); Rosati v. USPS, 91 MSPR 122, 124 ¶ 4 (2002) (considering length of delay; reasonableness of excuse and showing of due diligence; whether proceeding pro se; whether evidence presented of circumstances beyond appellant's control that affected ability to comply with time limits; unavoidable casualty or misfortune showing causal relationship to inability to timely file PFR); Summers v. USPS, 87 MSPR 403, 405 ¶ 6 (2000) ("The Board must consider the length of the delay in every good cause determination.").

At times the Board couples the necessity for showing good cause for belated filing with good cause for failing to seek an extension to file the PFR. See Tyler v. USPS, 87 MSPR 460, 462-63 ¶ 4 (2001) ("To establish good cause for an untimely filing, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case.... In addition, the appellant must show good cause for not seeking an extension in advance of the filing date.").

Pro se status is but one factor in the Board's waiver equation. See, e.g., Marley v. OPM, 84 MSPR 215, 218 ¶ 8 (1999). The Board expects a degree of prudence in complying with important, clearly stated deadlines. Wilsey v. OPM, 52 MSPR 42, 43 (1991). An unexplained delay of but one day by a pro se resulted in rejection of her PFR as untimely. Pangelinan v. DHS, 104 MSPR 108, 111 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 338 (2006); see Henderson v. OPM, AT-0831-15-0578-I-1 (NP 1/25/2023) ("While the appellant is apparently acting pro se, that fact alone cannot overcome his filing delay and the absence of evidence showing due diligence. De La Cruz Espan v. Office of Personnel Management, 95 M.S.P.R. 403, ¶¶ 6-7 (2004).").

Whatever the delay's duration, the Board expects the appellant to provide an explanation if the delay is to be excused. If the appellant offers no evidence of the existence of "circumstances beyond his control," he has not established good cause for untimely filing. Pena v. OPM, 58 MSPR 404, 407 (1993). Many reasons are advanced, and some are accepted, for acceptance of untimely submissions. Certainly "[t]he length of the delay is a factor in any good cause determination." Smith v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 607, 610 (1995); see Lewin v. DOJ, 74 MSPR 294, 298 (1997) (excusing a possible one-day delay by the agency in filing the PFR by Federal Express; the agency followed the procedure it had successfully used for the past eight years). If the appellant is confused over a filing deadline, as a reasonably prudent person he is expected to contact the regional office or the Clerk of the Board for guidance. Williams v. OPM, 46 MSPR 560, 563 (1991). Even when there may be cause shown for some delay, the Board will scrutinize additional delay in the filing of the PFR to ascertain whether that delay is reasonable under the circumstances. See, e.g., Price v. Dept. of Navy, 54 MSPR 215, 217 (1992) (delay was unjustified beyond the time allowed for belated receipt of a compliance initial decision).

The decisions that follow explain some circumstances leading the Board to accept or reject a late PFR. Timeliness of a petition for review is a matter committed to the Board's discretion. The Board's decisions will not be disturbed unless there is a showing of abuse. Amie v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1191) (citing Hopkins v. MSPB, 725 F.2d 1368, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1984)). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Timeliness of Appeals," for analogous decisions involving late appeals.]

The Board is not particularly forgiving of short (or long) delayed PFRs. The Federal Circuit suggested a more realistic approach, Ladrido v. MSPB, 248 Fed. Appx. 184, 186-87 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Other factors in this case also counsel in favor of waiving the appeal deadline when combined with the incomplete notice and Ladrido's actions following removal, namely, Ladrido's age, his declaration of emotional strain, his pro se status at the time he received the Removal Letter, and his first language being other than English. Sadian v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 45 M.S.P.R. 100, 103 (M.S.P.B. 1990) (taking age and English language capability into account in waiving appeal deadline). The AJ correctly found that none of these circumstances alone was sufficient to justify waiving the appeal deadline. Similarly, the AJ held that the deficiency in the notice was not material enough, by itself, to warrant waiver of the filing deadline. Given all these circumstances in toto plus the incomplete notice, plus Ladrido's relative diligence, however, the AJ abused his discretion by failing to waive the appeal deadline, particularly where no prejudice to the government from the delay was shown or even alleged.

Vice Chairman Wagner endorsed the Ladrido approach in her dissent to Chandler v. VA, SF-0752-13-0344-I-1 (NP 1/9/2015), where the majority resolved as untimely a PFR filed by an appellant suffering multiple disadvantages and who sought extension of time to file the PFR, clearly demonstrating his intent to pursue the PFR process.
