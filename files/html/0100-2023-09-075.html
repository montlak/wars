b. Miscalculation of Time for Appeal

Ambiguities in agency decision notices may confuse appellants attempting to determine a filing deadline based on how the "days" allowed for an appeal should be counted. Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1994) [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Current Law on Waiver; Multifactor Analysis"], required agencies to clearly explain to appellants the deadline for filing an appeal and the calculus of that deadline. See Knox v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 669, 671 (1995) (following Walls on a point of confusion about the calculation of "calendar" days allowed for refiling an appeal by the employee whose appeal was dismissed without prejudice during active military service).

Neal v. Dept. of Air Force, 68 MSPR 26, 29-30 (1995), reconfirmed application of Walls:

We find that three factors combine to compel a finding that waiver of the filing deadline is appropriate here. First, the untimeliness was de minimis: only one day.[4] Second, and most important, the appellant's excuse was reasonable because it was based on an ambiguity in the filing instructions given him. The notice did not specify a date by which the appeal had to be filed. The appellant proceeded with his own calculations, and arrived at the wrong date. Although, clearly, it would be the better practice for agencies to issue notices that specify a date, rather than a process for calculating it, we do not suggest that a notice which does not do so will always be held to be ambiguous. However, we find in this instance that the calculation process specified here--"an appeal must be filed no later than 20 days following the effective date of your removal"--is ambiguous in this context, as it was in Walls, see 29 F.3d at 1583, and because, again as in Walls, it did not specify whether the days were calendar days, or working days. Id. Finally, we note in this connection that the appellant was proceeding pro se. As Walls instructs, "we do not expect a pro se litigant to be made to jump through a confusing array of procedural hoops." Id., citing Mendoza v. MSPB, 966 F.2d 650, 653 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (noting, however, that where an order or instruction is clear and unambiguous, the appellant's pro se status does not excuse noncompliance). The minimal untimeliness, and the fact that "the agency's decision letter was sufficiently ambiguous under the circumstances to not properly discharge the Agency's notice obligation," see Walls, 29 F.3d at 1583, show both an absence of negligence and the presence of due diligence on the appellant's part, since he filed during what he believed was the filing period. Id. at 1582.

[4] We reiterate our finding in Simon v. Dept. of Veterans Affairs, 65 MSPR 176, 181 (1994), that minimal delay and an appellant's pro se status do not, without other factors, demonstrate good cause for untimely filing.

We therefore find that the appellant has established good cause for his untimely filing, and we waive the filing deadline in this matter in the absence of any showing by the agency of prejudice.

Munir v. OPM, 67 MSPR 328, 330 (1995), confirmed that the Board would allow filings based on pro se appellants' miscalculations of "working" and "calendar" days unless there was clear notice of the proper basis for determining the filing period:

A Board appeal must be filed within 25 calendar days of the issuance of an OPM reconsideration decision. 5 CFR §§ 1201.4(h); 1201.22(b).[1] In the present case, the filing deadline was September 7, 1993. Accordingly, the appellant's appeal was filed seven days late.

[1] The longer, 35-day filing period provided for in the Board's new June 17, 1994 regulations does not apply to this appeal. 59 Fed. Reg. 31,109 (1994).

To establish good cause for an untimely filing, a party must show that he exercised diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Dept. of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980). In Shiflett v. USPS, 839 F.2d 669, 674 (Fed. Cir. 1988), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that the agency's failure to provide the employee with notice of her appeal rights, without more, constituted good cause for her untimely filing. Furthermore, in Walls, 29 F.3d at 1583, the court held that an agency's notice, stating only that an appeal must be filed "within 20 days," without further qualification as to whether this referred to calendar days or working days, was subject to reasonable misinterpretation, and therefore did not properly discharge the agency's notice obligation under 5 CFR § 1201.21(a). The court further noted that the Board itself, ostensibly in an effort to provide unambiguous notice of the applicable filing period for appeals of its initial decisions, requires provision of the date upon which such decisions will become final, rather than a mere reference to a filing period. Id. In addition to the agency's inadequate notice, the court cited the minimal nature of the appellant's filing delay, his averments of emotional stress, and his pro se status, as grounds for finding good cause for the appellant's untimely filing. Id.

As in Walls, OPM's reconsideration decision here merely advised the appellant that she had "25 days from the date of this letter to file your appeal," without indicating whether the filing period referred to calendar days or working days; nor did it specify a particular deadline date. Accordingly, we find that the agency did not adequately discharge its notice obligation under 5 CFR § 1201.21(a). Further, as in Walls, the appellant here proceeded without benefit of legal counsel. Moreover, the appellant filed her appeal within the regulatory time period as construed by her (i.e., 25 working days), thus demonstrating reasonable prudence and diligence in pursuing her appeal. The resulting delay was brief.

In light of these circumstances, we conclude that good cause exists for the appellant's untimely filing, and that waiver of the filing deadline is therefore appropriate. Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 184; 5 CFR § 1201.12.
