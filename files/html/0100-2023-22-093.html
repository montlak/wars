i. Application to MSPB Cases

EAJA is seldom encountered in Board litigation. Phillips v. GSA, 924 F.2d 1577, 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1991), precludes the Board from awarding fees for time spent by counsel in court challenging an MSPB decision. [Phillips is discussed in this chapter, "Time in Court"] If counsel prevails in court on an appeal from the Board, two fee petitions may be necessary. A fee petition will be needed to cover time in court under the Back Pay Act and EAJA. [Refer to Fed. Cir. Rule 47.7 for the time limits for filing a fee application in court.] The fee petition filed in court may seek CSRA fees for the time before the Board. Under Gavette v. OPM, 785 F.2d 1568 (Fed. Cir. 1986), supra, the court may remand to the Board for a determination of the CSRA portion of the fee petition pertaining to time before the Board. Or, if the case is being remanded as part of the relief on the merits, a separate petition under CSRA and the Back Pay Act may be filed with the Board for time before the Board. The two petitions will then be simultaneously adjudicated. The statute, applied in other contexts, spawned a generation of law defining what is and is not a "substantially justified" government position. The Act includes a $125 [formerly $75] per hour limitation, not a limit included in or observed under Reform Act awards.

Assuming that the civil service case decided by the court is remanded to the Board, there remains the question of whether counsel should petition the court under the Back Pay Act to award fees for time spent before the Board. The Federal Circuit's track record for fee awards is not particularly good. Some fee petitions for time in court are summarily denied. If the court is presented with a fee petition that also includes fees for Board litigation and summarily denies the petition, counsel has no realistic second chance (other than petitioning for judicial rehearing) to obtain those fees. With little explanation concerning the nature of the fee petition or the standards that governed its adjudication, in an appeal involving a fee motion apparently first denied by the Federal Circuit (after a successful appeal to that court), Dedonato v. USPS, 32 MSPR 51, 52 (1986), declared:

Under the doctrine of res judicata, a judgment "on the merits" in a prior suit involving the same parties or their privies bars a second suit based on the same cause of action.... Based on our review of the record, the Board concurs in the administrative judge's finding that the determination of the Federal Circuit on the merits of appellant's motion for attorney fees is res judicata and bars his request for the same attorney fees before the Board.

It is probably a better approach, assuming that the case will be remanded to the Board, to petition the court for fees associated with the court appeal and to petition the Board for the time spent before the Board. If the court is presented with and denies a petition for fees incurred before the Board, this result will collaterally estop the Board from adjudicating the same claim. If the Board denies the fees, there will at least be an articulated decision that can be appealed from the AJ to the Board and then to the court.
