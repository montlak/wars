2. Preliminary Steps

5 CFR 1201.73(d) (2022) sets a time limit for the initiation of discovery and explains the procedure for filing discovery motions:

(d) Time limits.

(1) Unless otherwise directed by the judge, parties must serve their initial discovery requests within 30 days after the date on which the judge issues an order to the respondent agency to produce the agency file and response.

(2) A party or nonparty must serve a response to a discovery request promptly, but not later than 20 days after the date of service of the request or order of the judge. Any discovery requests following the initial request must be served within 10 days of the date of service of the prior response, unless the parties are otherwise directed by the judge. Deposition witnesses must give their testimony at the time and place stated in the request for deposition or in the subpoena, unless the parties agree on another time or place.

(3) Any motion for an order to compel or issue a subpoena must be filed with the judge within 10 days of the date of service of objections or, if no response is received, within 10 days after the time limit for response has expired. Any pleading in opposition to a motion to compel or subpoena discovery must be filed with the judge within 10 days of the date of service of the motion.

(4) Discovery must be completed within the time period designated by the judge or, if no such period is designated, no later than the prehearing or close of record conference.

Discovery motions and sanctioning authority are addressed by 5 CFR 1201.74 (2022):

(a) Motion for an order compelling discovery. Motions for orders compelling discovery and motions for the appearance of nonparties must be filed with the judge in accordance with § 1201.73(c)(1) and (d)(3). An administrative judge may deny a motion to compel discovery if a party fails to comply with the requirements of 5 CFR 1201.73(c)(1) and (d)(3).

(b) Content of order. Any order issued will include, where appropriate:

(1) A provision that the person to be deposed must be notified of the time and place of the deposition;

(2) Any conditions or limits concerning the conduct or scope of the proceedings or the subject matter that may be necessary to prevent undue delay or to protect a party or other individual or entity from undue expense, embarrassment, or oppression;

(3) Limits on the time for conducting depositions, answering written interrogatories, or producing documentary evidence; and

(4) Other restrictions upon the discovery process that the judge sets.

(c) Noncompliance. The judge may impose sanctions under § 1201.43 of this part for failure to comply with an order compelling discovery.

Discovery requests go to those from whom discovery is sought. The Board does not forward discovery requests from one party to another. AJs may return to appellants discovery requests they incorrectly send to the Board. The letters returning the discovery requests may not be particularly informative, but no error occurs if the appellant--through the Board's acknowledgment order or other means--is fairly informed of discovery procedures. See Radziewicz v. USPS, 42 MSPR 692, 695 (1990).

Initial requests for discovery must be served by both parties within 30 [previously 25] days from the date the Board acknowledgment order is issued to the agency directing it to respond to the petition for appeal. 5 CFR 1201.73(d)(1) (2022). See Key v. GSA, 60 MSPR 66, 68 (1993) (refusing to waive the time limit for a pro se appellant who later obtained counsel who wanted to pursue discovery: "It is well settled that a delay caused by an appellant's efforts to obtain legal counsel is not good cause for an untimely filing."). The time limit also applies to motions to depose nonparties (along with associated requests for subpoenas). Masood v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 399, 403 (1991) (delay in initiating discovery is not automatically excused by the inexperience of a representative). The time limit is short; watch it closely. The time for initiating discovery was lengthened from 25 to 30 days to permit an appellant to start discovery after reviewing the agency appeal response that is supposed to be filed within 25 days from the date of the acknowledgment order. The idea was that requiring earlier discovery could lead to requests for material included in the agency's response. The response of a party or nonparty federal agency to a discovery request is due, within the time provided by 1201.73(d)(2) (2012). That regulation requires a response to be filed not later than 20 days after the date of service of the request or Board order compelling discovery. As with other regulatory provisions, those applied to discovery can be adjusted by motion. 5 CFR 1201.12 (2022). The time of day for service of discovery seems immaterial. Cf. Gresham v. USPS, AT-0752-15-0311-I-1 (NP 4/15/2016) ("The agency refused to respond to the appellant's discovery request, asserting that it was untimely filed after the close of business, and the appellant filed a motion to compel. The administrative judge found that the agency's close of business argument was without authority or merit and granted the appellant's motion to compel discovery.").

Any discovery requests following the initial request shall be served within ten days of the date of service of the prior response, unless the Board otherwise directs (on motion). 5 CFR 1201.73(d)(2) (2022). The ten-day rule (formerly a seven-day rule and before that a ten-day rule) has no known ancestry in the Federal Rules, and there seems to be no good reason for its generation or perpetuation. Yet it is mentioned in reported decisions that enforce the rule, Richard v. DOD, 66 MSPR 146, 156 (1995):

...In a discovery request dated May 23, 1994, the appellant sought, among other things, transcripts of grades for students who were involved in parent-teacher conferences with the appellant, performance appraisal forms for each teacher that was evaluated by the school's principal, and copies of each letter written by a fellow teacher regarding each conference that he attended in his role as a Teacher. In order for this May 23, 1994 discovery request to be timely filed, it must have been served within ten days of the date of service of the prior response. See 5 CFR § 1201.73(d)(2). Our review of the record indicates that the prior response to the appellant's initial discovery request was dated May 10, 1994. The appellant has not shown that she served her May 23, 1994 discovery request within ten days of the date of service of the agency's May 10, 1994 response. Thus, we find no error in the administrative judge's determination that the appellant's May 23, 1994 discovery request was untimely filed. We also discern no error in the administrative judge's finding that the information requested was not relevant or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. See 5 CFR § 1201.72(a)-(b).

The discovery process must be wrapped up quickly. 5 CFR 1201.73(d)(4) (2022) says that discovery must be completed within the time designated by the AJ. On motion, the AJ may vary the time limit after considering the particular situation, including the dates set for the hearing and the closing of the case record. The Board's regulations also provide that the AJ may request the production of evidence concerning an issue and order its submission at any stage of a proceeding. 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(10) (2022). Jones v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 310, 312 (1989) (remanding because the AJ failed to direct the production of agency regulations pertaining to Board jurisdiction).

Discovery in MSPB appeals moves quickly in routine cases since the Board's goal is to complete a case at the regional office within 120 days. Litigation in federal courts may extend for a year or two or more, and, as a result, there is more time available for discovery in court cases. To understand what can happen in practice, assume that the appellant is quick off the mark and files a set of interrogatories and requests for documents on the day the Board sends out its order requiring the agency to respond to the appeal. The agency has 20 days to respond. Assume that it objects. Ten days later the appellant files a motion to compel discovery. The agency responds to the motion in 10 days. The AJ decides the motion in record time, 5 days later. The agency has another 15 days to produce the information ordered by the AJ. Counting the days, 60 days elapsed from the day the Board ordered the agency to respond to the appeal. The hearing is drawing near and closer still is the date for prehearing submissions. The party who initiated discovery may need to obtain more information through a follow-up set of interrogatories or document requests. Even if the system runs like clockwork--and it does not--the discovery deadlines in the regulations are too short. The parties must act quickly; they must request extensions from the AJ as necessary.

The Board's "suspension" procedure, 5 CFR 1201.28 (2022) permits the parties an additional 30 days to conduct discovery; the suspension may be once renewed. All that is required is the request by one or both of the parties early in the appeal. If the suspension request is jointly made, the parties need not provide evidence and argument to support the request, as long as the request is made early on. The suspension process is described in the acknowledgment order issued after the appeal is filed. A dismissal without prejudice to refile the appeal may also be used to lengthen the period to complete discovery. The AJ may lack the authority, however, to issue formal discovery orders resolving disputes while the appeal is under suspension or dismissed without prejudice to refile. The AJ may DWOP a case while the parties pursue their discovery, but as with the appeal suspension an order resolving a dispute cannot be issued during the DWOP. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')"; refer to Chapter 3, "Case Suspension" and "Postponement of Hearing" for the standard notice explaining the use of the suspension procedure and the time limits for invoking the procedure.]

On regulatory discovery deadlines, for example, response times and the time allowed to file motions to compel, counsel should attempt to work out their issues, but if their arrangements include varying the regulatory scheme, the best practice is to file a consented motion with the AJ to waive or extend a deadline. There are a few AJs who will not honor the informal efforts of parties to adjust discovery deadlines (and this can occur when a party finally has to file a motion to compel) if the AJ's consent was not earlier obtained for the variance. These AJs may be considered too controlling by some counsel who can work out discovery matters amongst themselves, but when at the Board you play by their rules. Since the Board gives considerable discretion to AJs' discovery rulings, good practice suggests that parties and representatives not assume permission to informally vary regulatory requirements. When in doubt, set up a conference call with the AJ and opposing counsel to discuss informal variations in the discovery processes set by regulation.
