6. Unit Status of Employee Representative

Official time entitlement under § 7131(a) accrues only to an employee, serving as a representative of an exclusive representative, who is a member of the bargaining unit to be covered by the negotiated agreement under negotiation. 2750th Air Base Wing Hq., AFLC and Wright-Patterson AFB Fire Fighters Local F-88, IAFF, 7 FLRA 738, 741-42 (1982). The negotiability of a proposal requiring an employer to grant official time to its employee to engage in collective bargaining involving another unit was the subject of a reconsideration decision, Naval Space Surveillance Sys. and Naval Surface Weapons Ctr., Dahlgren, and AFGE Local 2096, 12 FLRA 731, 731-32 (1983). FLRA ruled there was no requirement to negotiate. The right to bargain involves, under § 7101(a)(1), bargaining by employees and their unions about decisions affecting them in order to reach settlements of disputes between employees and their employers. Requiring an employer to bargain as to official time for its employees to engage in bargaining with a separate and independent agency or activity regarding conditions of employment for employees of the other agency or activity does not fall within the congressional intent. On review, FLRA was affirmed, AFGE Local 2096 v. FLRA, 738 F.2d 633, 635 (4th Cir. 1984):

We are convinced that FLRA's ultimate decision in its ruling...was not contrary to law, but rather was wholly correct....

Relevant portions of the Act provide that "collective bargaining" is "the performance of the mutual obligation of the representative of an agency and the exclusive representative of employees in an appropriate unit in the agency to meet...and to consult and bargain.... The exclusive representative is to be selected "by a majority of the employees in an appropriate unit,"...with such representation designed to enhance "the right of employees to organize, bargain collectively, and participate through labor organizations of their own choosing in decisions which affect them [quoting from Statute]. It is apparent that the plain language and overarching statutory intent of the Act are designed to emphasize the rights of employees within a given bargaining unit. Thus, the entitlement to official time under section 7131(a) properly applies only to employee-negotiators who are themselves members of the unit, and who are working to negotiate reasonable solutions to conditions of employment that directly affect the unit.

...

[T]he Act was not intended to "confer on the FLRA an unconstrained authority to equalize the economic positions of union and management."... While Congress could have drafted an explicit provision that non-unit negotiators be granted official time, it did not choose to do so. Thus, while management and labor are both free to establish and employ their own cadres of professional negotiators at their own expense, the Union in this case may not use the official time provisions of 5 U.S.C. § 7131(a) or (d) to compel employer subsidization of non-unit negotiators.[8]

[8] Indeed, one can imagine a scenario in which there would develop a group of federal employees who did nothing but negotiate contracts, moving from unit negotiation to unit negotiation while being reimbursed for official time all along. While this result may be desirable from a policy standpoint, there is no indication that Congress ever intended such a result.

The same result was reached in AFGE Local 1592 v. FLRA, 744 F.2d 73 (10th Cir. 1984), aff'g Dept. of Air Force, Air Force Logistics Command, Ogden Logistics Ctr., Hill AFB and AFGE Local 1592, 10 FLRA 245 (1982). At Hill AFB there were two units, one involving appropriated fund employees of the Air Force Logistics Command, the other unit including NAF employees at a separate organization. During negotiations, the union requested management to provide official time for two appropriated fund employees to serve on a bargaining team representing the NAF employees. Following the approach of the Fourth Circuit in Local 2096, the Tenth Circuit stated, 744 F.2d at 76:

[E]mployees are entitled to have a voice in their own working conditions at government expense, not the working conditions of every federal employee. If we follow the union's interpretation of section 7131(a), there is nothing to stop federal employees from becoming full-time negotiators for various unrelated bargaining units at their regular pay but without performing their regular jobs.

[W]hile union negotiators need not be barred from participating in collective bargaining on behalf of units other than their own, we do not believe that the Air Force must absorb the cost.

FLRA reaffirmed its position that official time need not be extended to employees for bargaining outside their units, even if the unit they seek to represent is in the same activity as their own. DOD, Dept. of Air Force, San Antonio Air Logistics Ctr., Kelly AFB and Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters, Local F-89, 15 FLRA 998, 1000 (1984). A proposal to permit official time for an individual in a nonappropriated fund unit to represent an individual in an appropriated fund unit was nonnegotiable. NAGE Local R7-23 and Scott AFB, 16 FLRA 650, 651-52 (1984) (citing Naval Space Surveillance Sys. and Naval Surface Weapons Ctr., Dahlgren, and AFGE Local 2096, 12 FLRA 731, 734-35 (1983)), aff'd, AFGE Local 2096 v. FLRA, 738 F.2d 633, 635 (4th Cir. 1984). A proposal for employees in one unit to represent employees in other units represented by the same union was not negotiable to the extent it required official time to be granted when representatives crossed unit lines. NFFE Local 405 and Army Aviation Sys. Command, 20 FLRA 354, 356 (1985). Reviewing an arbitration award, Dept. of Air Force, Tinker AFB and AFGE Local 916, 55 FLRA 805 (1999), FLRA seemed to find nothing objectionable about a union representative on 100% official time spending nonduty time representing employees of another unit. The case was remanded for a determination by the arbitrator on whether the representative's nonduty time was in accordance with the scheduling provisions of the parties' negotiated agreement.

The preclusion of official time for employees of one unit representing employees of another unit is not to be confused with the appropriateness of official time grants for a unit employee in one location or activity representing a unit employee in another location, Dept. of Navy, Naval Weapons Station and NAGE Local R4-1, 55 FLRA 1112, 1115 (1999):

[A] union representative seeking to negotiate on behalf of employees in his or her bargaining unit may not be denied official time because that representative is employed in a different location or by a different entity within the agency from the employees he or she wishes to represent. Under the Statute and Authority precedent, an employee is entitled to official time under section 7131(a) when negotiating on behalf of employees in the same bargaining unit.

Official time requests aside, FLRA also resolved as nonnegotiable proposals that would place nonunit "observers" in a nonduty status. The Authority characterized as speculative the union's argument that placing observers in nonduty status would expedite negotiations or substantially affect working conditions of unit employees. FEMTC, Charleston and Charleston Naval Shipyard, 36 FLRA 148, 152 (1990).
