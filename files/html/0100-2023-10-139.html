a. Official Time

The regulation provides, 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022):

Federal Witnesses.

(a) Every Federal agency or corporation, including nonparties, must make its employees or personnel available to furnish sworn statements or to appear at a deposition or hearing when ordered by the judge to do so. When providing those statements or appearing at a deposition or at the hearing, Federal employee witnesses will be in official duty status (i.e., entitled to pay and benefits including travel and per diem, where appropriate). When a desired witness is employed by an agency who is not a party to the Board proceeding, the requesting party may avail itself of the provisions of sections 1201.81 to 1201.85 of this part regarding subpoenas to ensure the attendance of the witness. In addition, the Board and the parties will implement this provision, to the maximum extent possible, to avoid conflict with other regulations governing the production of Federal employees in matters in litigation.

(b) A Federal employee who is denied the official time required by paragraph (a) of this section may file a written request that the judge order the employing agency to provide such official time. The judge will act on such a request promptly and, where warranted, will order the agency to comply with the requirements of paragraph (a) of this section.

(c) An order obtained under paragraph (b) of this section may be enforced as provided under subpart F of this part.

A comment in the Federal Register notice accompanying the revised 2012 regulation noted, 77 Fed. Reg. 62349, 62354 (Oct. 12, 2012):

...However, we have revised the regulation to indicate that the Board and the parties will implement this provision, to the maximum extent possible, to avoid conflict with other regulations such as those issued pursuant to United States, ex rel. v. Touhy, 340 U.S. 462, 467 (1951) regarding the production of evidence from Federal employees in matters in litigation.

5 CFR 1201.182(c)(2) (2022), part of the Board's enforcement regulations, states that "[u]nder § 1201.33(c) of this part, a nonparty witness who has obtained an order from a judge that his or her employing agency provide the witness with official time may petition the Board for enforcement of the order." For procedures in effect prior to the regulation for federal employees to secure Board assistance in obtaining payment of salaries while attending hearings, refer to Sapp v. USPS, 73 MSPR 189, 196-97 (1997). See Sapp v. USPS, 82 MSPR 411, 418-19 ¶ 17 (1999), post.

Agencies are required to make their employees available as witnesses or to furnish sworn statements if requested by the AJ. 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022). Some AJs expect that witnesses who are federal employees not employed by the respondent-agency will be obtained through a subpoena. This practice probably developed from a determination that if the witness is an employee of an agency not a party to the appeal, the better practice is to subpoena that individual. Porter v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 301, 303 n.1, 6 MSPB 257 (1981). The Board observed that the regulations do not require a subpoena for this purpose. The AJ has authority to compel without subpoena the attendance of any federal employee as a witness. Kennedy v. Military Sealift Command, 24 MSPR 96, 98 (1984). [Refer in this chapter to "Others; Former Employee; Subpoena."]

Some regional offices issue hearing notices or acknowledgment orders stating that the parties normally make their own arrangements for the appearance of witnesses on their witness lists. This is fine for the agency producing its own personnel, but the appellant has no control over agency witnesses. In Mingoia v. USPS, 33 MSPR 169, 171-72 (1987), it seems that the appellant notified the agency that he wanted a particular witness at the hearing, and the agency informed the witness that he was requested to testify. The witness did not appear at the hearing, however, and the AJ did not compel his attendance. The Board determined there no error when there was no offer of proof concerning the testimony of the witness, but the Board did not state whether the regional practice conformed to the intent of Board rules. Appellants must either examine the AJ's hearing notice for guidance or ask the AJ before or during the prehearing conference for his preferred practice concerning the designation of witnesses and the issuance of orders directing their attendance. If no guidance is available, appellants should file a motion for an order to be issued by the AJ directing the agency to produce at hearing those approved witnesses who are agency employees. When ordered by the Board to do so, the agency will then direct the appearance of those witnesses, rather than informing them that their appearance has been "requested." If the desired witness does not appear at the hearing, the AJ should be requested to inquire of the agency whether the witness was ordered to appear and why the witness has not appeared. With that information, it may be necessary to request a continuance, seek other means outside of the hearing to obtain the testimony, stipulate as to the facts the witness would prove through his or her testimony, or seek sanctions. If the AJ seems disinclined to direct the appearance of the witness or to take steps to preserve the testimony from that witness, a written offer of proof should be made stating what it is understood that the witness would say if he testified.

The Board's regulations call for official time for federal employees who appear as witnesses. The official time grant does not extend to federal employees who provide representation to appellants, Sapp v. USPS, 82 MSPR 411, 418-19 ¶ 17 (1999):

On petition for review, Mr. Wright contends that he is entitled to "official time for 2 hours of preparing the PFR as the Appellant's then representative (which also was not done on either the Union payroll or on [agency] time." Mr. Wright, in effect, is seeking reimbursement for services rendered during non-duty hours. Federal employee witnesses are in official duty status when appealing at a hearing and are entitled to pay and benefits including travel and per diem, Sapp, 73 MSPR at 196.[4] There is no statutory or regulatory authority for the Board to provide reimbursement for any time Mr. Wright spent on preparing the appellant's petition for review.[5] A non-attorney representative such as Mr. Wright is not entitled to be paid for the time he spent representing the appellant because there was no attorney-client relationship. 5 USC § 7701(g); 5 CFR § 1201.202; see Metsopulos v. U.S. Postal Service, 35 MSPR 496, 498 (1987) (fees cannot be awarded to a representative who is not an attorney); cf. Shimotsukasa v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 MSPR 679, 682 (1998) (where an attorney-client relationship exists, fees for services rendered by certain non-attorneys under the attorney's direction may be recoverable). We therefore find that the AJ did not err in denying the appellant official time for the time he spent representing the appellant.

[4] 5 CFR § 1201.33 provides that employees who furnish sworn statements or appear as witnesses in Board hearings are entitled to be in official duty status for those times, and to receive pay and benefits including travel and per diem, where appropriate. No comparable regulation exists with regard to an employee's use of official time for otherwise pursuing an appeal. See White v. Social Security Administration, 76 MSPR 447, 467, n.12 (1997).

[5] We note that the grant of official time for an employee to represent another employee in an MSPB proceeding is negotiable. American Federation of Government Employees National Immigration and Naturalization Council v. Department of Justice Immigration & Naturalization Service Washington, 45 FLRA 391, 399-401 (1992). Mr. Wright does not contend that any agreement or practice related to this issue exists.

Rules of the EEOC governing the federal sector complaint process provide for official time for representatives who are federal employees. While the Board develops records in mixed cases for eventual EEOC review, the Board does not incorporate the official time provisions of the Commission's regulations and policies into its own hearing process.
