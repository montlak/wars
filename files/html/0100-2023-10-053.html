1. Change, Resignation, or Death of Judge

AJs are assigned to appeals by regional directors (AKA chief administrative judges). Assignments are supposed to be made on the basis of workload and expertise of the AJs; there is no strict rotation of case assignments among AJs. When the region acknowledges the appeal, the AJ assigned to the case is usually identified in the acknowledgment order. There is no choice among AJs. The last-minute substitution of one AJ for another is not, by itself, prejudicial. It will not be assumed that the new AJ is unable to acquaint himself with the case sufficiently before the hearing. See O'Reilly v. CSA, 16 MSPR 44, 47 (1983).

If there is conflicting testimony involving credibility disputes on material issues, and if the AJ is replaced before making a decision, then ordinarily the testimony should be heard again. When the initial decision contains credibility determinations material to the outcome of the case, the AJ's findings should be made, unless there are unusual circumstances, from personal observation of the witnesses' demeanor while testifying at the hearing. The Board may assign a new AJ to achieve that result. Roach v. VA, 48 MSPR 373, 378 (1991); see see Lin v. Dept. of Air Force, 2023 MSPB 2, ¶ 24 (2023) ("When there is conflicting testimony on a material issue, and a new administrative judge will decide the case, the testimony should be heard again to permit her to make credibility determinations based on witness demeanor. Stinson v. Department of Justice, 4 M.S.P.R. 521, 523 (1981). A factual dispute is "material" if, in light of the governing law, its resolution could affect the outcome. Oram v. Department of the Navy, 2022 MSPB 30, ¶ 9 n.5."); Atamantyk v. DOD, 35 MSPR 233, 235-36 (1987) (adverse action appeal: "where there is conflicting testimony on material issues, and the hearing officer is replaced before making a decision, the testimony should be heard again"); Coughlan v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 230, 233 (1987) (remanding appeal for factual findings on an adverse action charge and requiring that a new hearing be held on remand after the departure of the AJ who heard the case); Gillis v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 426, 428 (1986) (requiring another hearing when there was a change in AJs in a Chapter 43 appeal; testimony and evidence was conflicting as to the adequacy of opportunity to improve); Davis v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 28 MSPR 130, 132 (1985) and 39 MSPR 470 (1989) (new AJ was assigned following the retirement of the first AJ; the successor AJ readjudicated the case on the existing record when neither party asked for the hearing to be reconvened and the appellant urged the Board to adjudicate on the existing record); Morgan v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 273, 276 (1984) (RIF appeal: as to credibility determinations material to the outcome of the case, "it was error when the presiding official who wrote the initial decision was not the presiding official who conducted the hearing");Wright v. DHHS, 5 MSPR 178, 180 n.1, 5 MSPB 232 (1981) (remanding for another hearing on a credibility issue that was not resolved when the original AJ died before the appeal could be heard on remand); Stinson v. DOJ, 4 MSPR 521, 5 MSPB 49 (1981) (both parties objected to a decision without a rehearing when the AJ resigned before the case was reconsidered on remand). But see Hawkins v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 655, 658, 6 MSPB 550 (1981) (on a counsel fee issue, involving no credibility issues, the availability of the transcript of the hearing on merits sufficed for a review of the record by the successor to the AJ who died).

It is the responsibility of the second AJ to inform the parties that he replaced the AJ who heard the appeal. The parties will then be on notice of the replacement and they will have the opportunity to request a rehearing. See Gormley v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 330, 335 (1990); Darby v. USPS, 25 MSPR 498, 499-500 (1984) (credibility issues were material, requiring a remand for rehearing; there was no indication of a notice of substitution to the parties and no opportunity for the parties to request a rehearing).

If credibility issues are not material, there is no need to rehear the appeal if the AJ should change. Guy v. Farm Credit Admin., 25 MSPR 500, 503 (1984) ("Resolution of the conflicting testimony did not require a determination of the credibility of the witnesses. We therefore find that appellant has failed to show that he was entitled to a de novo hearing on the basis of the credibility issues raised."); see Hall v. OPM, 51 MSPR 560, 565 (1991) (no error in a switch of AJs when the appeal was decided on the basis of affidavits rather than live testimony). But the difficulty is in determining whether credibility issues are significant. In Guy, the Board made the dubious judgment that no credibility problems arose because the appellant's testimony was contradicted by documentary evidence, implying that the hearing was no more than a review of paperwork. The Board cautioned in Guy that the appellant's failure to object to the substitution of an AJ could waive the right to raise the issue in a PFR. Guy was extended by Ladd v. USPS, 25 MSPR 506 (1984). Reviewing all the evidence on matters of credibility in an effort to determine whether the appeal should be reheard following the substitution of a AJ, Ladd determined that, because the evidence of record weighed against the appellant, there was no need for a new hearing. If, on PFR, just one of several charges is remanded, and the AJ changed, the new AJ need convene a hearing only as to the charge on remand, leaving intact the remaining factual or credibility findings. See Roach v. VA, 43 MSPR 443, 445 (1990) (remanding for receipt of the posthearing deposition that the first AJ should have considered).
