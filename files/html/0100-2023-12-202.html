H. FORMAT AND SUBSTANCE OF PFR; REPLY; RESPONSE TO REPLY

"A petition for review is a pleading in which a party contends that the initial decision was incorrectly decided in whole or in part and includes all of the party's legal and factual arguments as to such error." Washington v. Dept. of Air Force, SF-0752-12-0106-I-1 (NP 3/18/2013). "The Board will, in the interest of justice, give wide latitude to appellants' petitions and will not technically construe the Board's regulatory procedural requirements." Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 382, 383 (1984). Some formalities must be observed. The PFR must state "objections to the initial decision, including all of the party's legal and factual arguments, and must be supported by references to applicable laws or regulations and by specific references to the record." 5 CFR 1201.114(b) (2022). "Although the language of a petition for review may be simply drawn, it must contain at least enough specifics to enable the Board to ascertain whether there is a serious evidentiary question justifying a complete review of the record." Lawrence v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 556, 557 (1983); see Archer v. OPM, 90 MSPR 228, 231 ¶ 5 (2001) (it was insufficient to state only that "I wish to petition the review board on my case for medical retirement"; the Board reopens on its own motion).

State the issues, then the facts as determined by the initial decision, then the governing law and the errors of law, rule, or regulation reflected by the initial decision. If the issue is one of legal error, be specific: cite the statute or regulation or collective bargaining agreement provision violated by the analysis of the initial decision. If the issue is one of credibility or a factual resolution, be specific: state what the AJ ignored or how the initial decision incorrectly resolved the factual issues. Be succinct: delete from the draft the invective directed against opposing counsel or the AJ. Let someone unfamiliar with the case read the PFR. If it does not make sense to that individual on the first reading, it is time for editing.

Of one set of exceptions (filed as to a complaint against an ALJ, but similar to a PFR), the Board lamented in SSA v. Burris, 39 MSPR 51, 53-54 (1988):

...Generally speaking, the respondent's exceptions to the recommended decision have not assisted the Board in conducting its analysis of the recommended decision. Those exceptions were contained in a rambling, 840-page filing which rarely addressed specific findings or charges, and which contained a massive amount of hyperbole, but little persuasively reasoned argumentation....

Dec v. OPM, 47 MSPR 72, 77 (1991), said of an agency's inadequately referenced PFR:

OPM asserts that the appellant exhibited no hearing problems at the Board proceeding and thus her testimony should be considered incredible. OPM, however, has provided no specific evidence to support its contention, for example, an affidavit from its representative, who was present at the hearing. Rather it simply instructs the Board to "Refer to hearing tape." Thus, it has not presented a basis for the Board to reject the credibility determinations of the administrative judge, who was also present to observe the appellant's demeanor at the hearing. Schafer v. Dept. of Defense, 6 MSPR 307, 309, 6 MSPB 262 (1981); Weaver, 2 MSPR at 133-34.

The PFR is usually filed by a party or the party's representative. However, the Board will consider filings by both an appellant and the appellant's representative, treating them as a unified PFR. See Parker v. USPS, 59 MSPR 603, 607 n.2 (1993). The same is true if the appellant has multiple representatives who file PFRs. See Shires v. DOD, 60 MSPR 13, 16 n.1 (1993) (considering submissions from representatives).

On occasion parties seek to submit evidence with a PFR in the form of narrations of events or facts. Earlier discussed in this chapter was Board law governing or limiting the submission of new evidence with a PFR. But if evidence is to be submitted, the Board gives greater credit to evidence, when it is otherwise admissible, that is submitted with a PFR if the evidence is in the format of a sworn statement or affidavit, Blue v. USPS, 65 MSPR 370, 375-76 (1994):

The appellant's contentions on petition for review that he timely filed the appeal or, in the alternative, that there was good cause for his filing delay, are not sworn or in affidavit form. An appellant's explanations regarding the timeliness issue before an administrative judge need not be sworn, although the administrative judge may require sworn explanations if he or she deems it necessary under the circumstances. See Cohen v. Dept. of Commerce, 56 MSPR 578, 582 n.1 (1993). However, where, as here, the explanations are first proffered on petition for review by an appellant who is represented by an attorney, the fact that they are not sworn does detract from any evidentiary weight they might have. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575. In fact, as noted above, the general rule regarding unsworn statements in a party's pleading is that they do not constitute evidence. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575 n.3; Vincent, 32 MSPR at 268-69. Nor has the appellant provided any documentary evidence to support his assertions. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575; Vincent, 32 MSPR at 269.

See Slaughter v. USPS, 56 MSPR 307, 310 (1993) ("If evidence is to be offered with a PFR, submissions should be in affidavit form or signed under penalty of perjury."). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Review of New Evidence."]
