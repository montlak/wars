B. SETTING ASIDE A SETTLEMENT

Challenges to settlements generally arise when the appellant decides that the settlement is undesirable after the appeal was dismissed based on the settlement. Ordinarily, a challenge to the validity of a settlement must then be raised through a timely PFR from the initial decision that dismissed the appeal as settled. "It is well-settled that an attack on the validity of a settlement agreement must be made in the form of a petition for review of the initial decision, and not in a petition for enforcement." Hatcher v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 97, 99 (1997); see Vogel v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 451, 454 ¶ 6, 2007 MSPB 200 (2007) (enforcing waiver of right to file an IRA whistleblowing appeal following settlement of an adverse action appeal: "to the extent that the appellant argues that the 2004 settlement agreement was fraudulently obtained because the agency never had any intention of placing him in a GS-7 Accountant position, that argument is properly raised in a petition for review challenging the initial decision dismissing the appeal as settled."); Brasel v. MSPB, 97 Fed. Appx. 289, 292 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) ("a challenge to an initial decision upholding the voluntariness of a settlement agreement must be brought in a petition for review of that initial decision and cannot be raised in collateral proceedings"); Gareis v. Dept. of Interior, 90 MSPR 107, 109-110 ¶ 5 (2001) (AJ should not have treated the validity of a settlement in a compliance proceeding; the challenge should have been forwarded to the Board as a PFR); cf. Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 101 MSPR 258, 265 ¶ 11, 2006 MSPB 43 (2006) ("[s]uch attacks on the validity of a settlement agreement must be made in the form of a petition for review of the initial decision dismissing the case as settled"; remanding to the region for evidentiary findings on whether the appellant's attorney was authorized to enter into the settlement agreement).

The PFR to set aside a settlement must be timely, or the Board must waive the time limit, if the PFR is to be considered. See, e.g., Ferguson v. Dept. of Air Force, 105 MSPR 301, ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 88 (2007) (PFR seeking to set aside a settlement was untimely: "appellant fails to identify the specific time period during which he suffered a mental impairment from taking prescribed medications, the extent of the mental impairment, and how it precluded him from filing the petition during the filing time limit"). [Refer to Chapter 5 for procedures governing PFRs and timeliness issues.] Once the Board has determined the validity of a settlement, its legitimacy may not be repeatedly challenged. See Langston v. Dept. of Army, 84 MSPR 597, 599 ¶ 6 (1999).

Alternatively, the appellant may be satisfied with the settlement, but the appellant may assert that the agency breached the agreement and the appellant may then decide to seek rescission of the agreement and restoration of the underlying appeal rather than enforcement of the agreement. The issues of breach and rescission are addressed by the Board through enforcement proceedings at the regional level. The Board summarized the distinctions between use of a PFR and a regional enforcement petition, Perkins v. VA, 101 MSPR 642, 644 ¶ 4, 2006 MSPB 114 (2006):

When an administrative judge dismisses an appeal pursuant to a settlement agreement and the appellant believes that the agreement is invalid because it was obtained by fraud or coercion, or because it was based on mutual mistake, he may file a petition for review of the initial decision and seek to have the settlement set aside. See Harris v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 99 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶ 4 (2005); McKinney v. Department of Agriculture, 70 M.S.P.R. 165, 166-67 (1996). If, however, the appellant is not challenging the validity of the settlement agreement, but instead believes that the agency has failed to comply with a term of the agreement, he may file a petition for enforcement with the regional or field office that issued the initial decision. McKinney, 70 M.S.P.R. at 166-67. If the appellant substantiates his allegation that the agency has breached the agreement, he is entitled to either enforcement of the agreement or rescission of the agreement and reinstatement of his claim. See Murphy v. U.S. Postal Service, 54 M.S.P.R. 202, 206 (1992).

[Refer later in this chapter to "Agreement Challenged as Invalid; Use of PFR."]

If the settlement challenge is initiated at the regional office, the AJ should inform the appellant what she must show for the agreement to be set aside (as in, for example, a claimed material breach). But if the AJ's notice is insufficient, a remand is not necessary if the agency's pleadings pointed out the need for the appellant to meet the required burden of proof, Brown v. USPS, SF-0752-17-0611-I-1, n.3 (NP 1/23/2023):

Although the administrative judge did not provide the appellant with jurisdictional burdens of proof concerning the settlement agreement, we find it unnecessary to remand this appeal for a proper jurisdictional notice. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (finding that an appellant must receive explicit information on what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue). The Board has found that an administrative judge's defective notice can be cured if the agency's pleadings contain the notice that was lacking in the jurisdictional order. Scott v. Department of Justice, 105 M.S.P.R. 482, ¶ 6 (2007). Here, the agency adequately informed the appellant in its narrative response that the appellant has provided no showing that the settlement agreement was obtained under coercion or misrepresentation....

If the settlement agreement is set aside for breach, the status quo is restored, the appeal is reinstated, and consideration received under the settlement may need to be restored, as was explained in Williams v. DHHS, 114 MSPR 11, 18 ¶ 14, 2010 MSPB 72 (2010), involving an agency's enforcement initiative based on the breach by the appellant of a provision of a settlement precluding further appeals arising out of the settled adverse action:

[W]here one party commits a material breach of a settlement agreement, the other party is entitled to either enforce or rescind the agreement. Eagleheart, 110 M.S.P.R. 642, ¶ 15; Fuller, 45 M.S.P.R. at 614. Rescission of a settlement agreement involves the parties returning the benefits obtained under the agreement and restoring the parties to the positions they were in prior to the execution of the agreement. See Hernandez v. Department of Defense, 112 M.S.P.R. 262, ¶ 9 (2009) (if a settlement agreement is rescinded it becomes inoperative); Stipp v. Department of the Army, 64 M.S.P.R. 124, 127-28 (1994) (stating that "inherent in the Board's authority to set aside a settlement agreement is the authority to order a party to take action necessary for the restoration of the status quo ante; otherwise, the setting aside of the agreement would be without effect"); overruled in part on other grounds, Wisdom v. Department of Defense, 78 M.S.P.R. 652 (1998). Thus, if a party breaches a settlement agreement, and the nonbreaching party wishes to recover the contractual consideration provided to the breaching party, the nonbreaching party must seek rescission of the settlement agreement.

Less frequently, an appellant may appeal an action that the agency claims was settled before the appeal and then is beyond the Board's jurisdiction. Assuming that the action that was settled would have been within the Board's jurisdiction, the AJ should inform the appellant that to set the agreement aside, the appellant must show that the agreement was breached by the agency or that it is tainted with invalidity through fraud or mutual mistake. See Marshall v. Dept. of Navy, 81 MSPR 305, 311 ¶¶ 10-11 (1999) ("Unless and until it is determined in [compliance] proceeding[s] that the agency breached the settlement agreement, the waiver of appeal rights contained in the settlement agreement is valid and enforceable.... If it is subsequently determined in the compliance proceeding that the agency materially breached the settlement agreement, the Board may vacate this jurisdictional dismissal and reopen these matters for further consideration."), aff'd as modified on other grounds, 84 MSPR 676, 679 ¶ 10 (2000) (order unconditionally dismissing Marshall's enforcement petition to permit judicial review); cf. Kince v. USPS, 56 MSPR 64, 67 (1992) (on remand because the AJ did not advise the appellant that a party seeking to set aside a settlement must show that it is invalid by reason of fraud or mutual mistake). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Waiver of Appeal Through Last Chance or Other Preappeal Agreement."]

The Board separates its review authority into two untidy divisions: challenges to the underlying validity of the agreement, to be raised by a timely PFR from the initial decision dismissing the appeal as settled; challenges to compliance with the agreement, to be initiated through an enforcement petition sent to the Board office that adjudicated the appeal leading to the settlement. If an appellant selects the wrong route, the Board could default him for the error of his choice. Issues are not so readily parsed in the real world populated by litigants. Many litigants file PFRs challenging settlements for noncompliance, and the Board ends up sending the cases back to the regions for adjudication. Then there are cases involving enforcement petitions that may ultimately lead to allegations that the agreement was the result of mutual mistake or that a material provision is in violation of public policy. The AJ, if he is following what goes on, sends the case up the Board as a PFR. Any of these cases may involve factual issues, and factual issues are best resolved at the regional level by an AJ. Encouraging settlement and boasting of an appellate settlement rate of 60%, the Board should simplify the process of addressing problems arising out of settlements, and require that all challenges to them or proceedings to enforce them be initiated at the regional office that adjudicated the underlying appeal.
