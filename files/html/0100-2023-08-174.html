4. District of Columbia

In Drew v. District of Columbia Gov't, 2 MSPR 376, 2 MSPB 539 (1980), appellants were veterans preference eligibles in excepted service positions. Construing the legislative history of the Civil Service Reform Act and the District of Columbia Self-Government and Government Reorganization Act, the Board concluded that D.C. Government employees do not have appeal rights to the Board; their appeal lies within the D.C. Government. See Scott v. Dept. of Treasury, 71 MSPR 42, 46 n.2 (1996) (mentioning in passing that since the 1978 DCCMPA, the Board has not had jurisdiction over retirement appeals of employees under the District of Columbia retirement system); Cyrus v. District of Columbia Gov't, 25 MSPR 396, 397 n.* (1984); Pair v. District of Columbia Gov't, 7 MSPR 175, 176, 7 MSPB 91 (1981) (Board lacked jurisdiction over appeals from excepted service employees of the District of Columbia Government); cf. Griffin v. OPM, 98 Fed. Appx. 845, 847 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (D.C. Department of Corrections is an agency of the District of Columbia government, not the federal government). In an unusual case, the Board had jurisdiction over an appeal by an individual employed at St. Elizabeth's Hospital in the District. The action arose before the transfer of the functions of the Hospital to the District. Robinson v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 110, 113 (1988).

Milligan v. Government of the District of Columbia, DC-0351-13-1299-I-1 (NP 8/11/2014), explained the Board's lack of jurisdiction over District of Columbia Government employees' reduction in force appeals:

The Board's jurisdiction is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule, or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). For the purposes of Title 5 and for the Board's jurisdiction, unless specifically modified, an "employee" means an individual appointed in the civil service. 5 U.S.C. § 2105(a)(1). Additionally, OEA was established beginning in December 1980 to adjudicate District of Columbia employee appeals locally, as opposed to on a federal level. See District of Columbia v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 762 F.2d 129, 130-31 (D.C. Cir. 1985). The appellant pursued his appeal rights as a District of Columbia employee through OEA and the Superior Court of the District of Columbia. Although the appellant claims jurisdiction because he was a District of Columbia employee, he does not contend that he was employed in the federal civil service. See id. at 3. Employees of the Government of the District of Columbia do not come within the statutory definitions set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 2101 ("civil service"), 5 U.S.C. § 2105 ("employee"), and 5 U.S.C. § 2103 ("excepted service"); nor do they have appeal rights to the Board. Cyrus v. Government of the District of Columbia, 25 M.S.P.R. 396, 397 n.* (1984). The Board therefore does not have jurisdiction over this case.[2] See 5 U.S.C. §§ 1221(a), 2105(a)(1), 7511(a)(1).

[2] To the extent that the appellant is attempting to bring an individual right of action (IRA) appeal alleging whistleblower reprisal, the right to file an IRA appeal is limited to employees, former employees, or applicants for employment, and the appellant does not qualify for the reasons explained above. See 5 U.S.C. § 1221(a). Moreover, the appellant argues that the Office of Special Counsel (OSC) has jurisdiction over this case but does not dispute that he failed to exhaust his administrative remedies before OSC as is required for Board jurisdiction over an IRA appeal. See Yunus v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 242 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Similarly, to the extent that the appellant is attempting to bring a VEOA claim, and without reaching the issue of whether the appellant is a preference eligible, the Board lacks jurisdiction over such a claim because the appellant admits that he has not exhausted his remedy with DOL as is mandatory prior to exercise of Board jurisdiction. Hillman v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 95 M.S.P.R. 162, ¶ 9 (2003). We also note that the appellant's claims of prohibited personnel practices would not provide a basis for Board jurisdiction, even if the appellant met the statutory definition of "employee." See Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982).

See Milligan v. Government of the District of Columbia, DC-4324-16-0805-I-1 (NP 4/5/2023) (D.C. Government is not a Federal executive agency, 5 USC 105, and MSPB lacks jurisdiction over an action under VEOA, USERRA, or an appealable RIF).

A few cases involving D.C. employees are noted in the Guide. These were decided through an agreement with the D.C. Government in effect through November of 1980, when the District was setting up its appeals program. See Diefenbach v. District of Columbia Gov't, 12 MSPR 528, 530 (1982). The agreement stipulated that the Board would provide appellate review over actions "pending" upon expiration of the agreement. Subsequently, the Board and the District determined that "pending" meant that the Board would retain jurisdiction over any appeal filed under the agreement if the employee received notice of the proposed action before November 1, 1980. See Leonard v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3375) (citing Johnson v. District of Columbia, 7 MSPR 563, 564-65 (1981), and Diefenbach, supra); Meadows v. Palmer, 775 F.2d 1193 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (describing the D.C. government personnel system as it evolved from the 1973 District of Columbia Self-Government and Governmental Reorganization (Home Rule Act). In District of Columbia cases adjudicated by the Board under contract during the period following 1978, when the D.C. Office of Employee Appeals was being established in accordance with the District's Comprehensive Merit Personnel Act of 1978, the Board distinguished the effect of orders entered under its agreement (although it is difficult to determine the source of the Board's authority to provide services under a contract) from its statutory authority to issue decisions in statutory appeals. The Board determined that it lacked authority to undertake or enforce compliance in the D.C. contract cases. Burrows v. District of Columbia Gov't, 27 MSPR 149, 151-52 (1985). D.C. employees first employed before October 1, 1987, may be covered under the federal retirement systems. See Watkins v. OPM, DC-0831-16-0353-I-1 (NP 7/19/2022) (CSRS) (considering amount of creditable service, involving court award of front pay).
