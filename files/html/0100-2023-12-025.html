d. Contemporaneous Notation, Statement; Post Hoc Explanation

If there is a conflict in testimony concerning statements made at a meeting, weight should be given to contemporaneous notes made immediately following the meeting. Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 24 MSPR 550, 553 (1984); see Thompson v. Dept. of Army, 122 MSPR 372, 384 ¶ 26, 2015 MSPB 31 (2015) (evidence of counseling during a Chapter 43 PIP: "We agree with the administrative judge that, if the meetings took place as the agency asserted, there should have been some corroborating evidence such as meeting notes or memoranda for the agency to submit for the record. The agency's failure to submit any such evidence casts serious doubt on its version of events."). A memorandum made immediately after a conversation carries substantial weight and probative value, especially if statements contained in the memo can be corroborated by independent sources. Rodriguez v. USDA, 27 MSPR 79, 86 (1985). That a witness needs to refer to notes during testimony does not make the testimony unworthy of belief. Scroggins v. USPS, 48 MSPR 558, 562 n.2 (1991). When considering contemporaneous writings as a factor in a credibility determination, it may be necessary to determine when and under what circumstances those writings were prepared. See Garza v. Dept. of Army, 57 MSPR 214, 219, 223 (1993). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Notes of Witnesses; Memoranda of Conversations," for decisions discussing weight of notes of supervisors and witnesses.]

Contemporaneous statements may bolster credibility of events reflected by those statements. See Aldridge v. USDA, 111 MSPR 670, 677 ¶ 14, 2009 MSPB 146 (2009) (appellant's testimony of what she was told by agency officials was bolstered by her contemporaneous statements to a colleague of what she was told). Post hoc (after the fact) explanations of events may be discounted. Graves v. VA, 114 MSPR 245, 251-52 ¶¶ 16, 2010 MSPB 123 (2010), involving a challenge through VEOA to a nonselection, observed:

The letter to DOL and the cited hearing testimony conflict with the record documents that were contemporaneous with Glover's selection. For example, there was no explanation regarding why Ottem requested Duff's approval to hire Glover on February 20, 2009, if Glover had been selected in January 2009 or before. Thus, the testimony and letter to DOL appear to reflect the agency's post hoc characterization of the events, and as such, they are not as credible as the contemporaneous documents. See Estate of Kravitz v. Department of the Navy, 110 M.S.P.R. 97, ¶¶ 11-15 (2008) (the Board rejected the agency's post hoc characterization of the events regarding the appellant's application for certain positions); Grubb v. Department of the Interior, 96 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 43 (2004) (the Board found the appellant's post hoc explanation for her absence "not very credible" where it was markedly different from the reason she initially presented and was not supported by corroborating medical evidence). The administrative judge credited the testimony of Jones and Ottem without making actual credibility determinations regarding the testimony and without truly considering the contrary documentary evidence. ID at 6-9, 12-13; see Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1302 (Fed. Cir. 2002).
