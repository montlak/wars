c. Initiation of New Action Based on Events Leading to Canceled Action

An agency may take an action and cancel it following an appeal, restore the status quo, but take a new action based on the events leading to the canceled action. That occurred in Rivera v. SSA, 109 MSPR 21, 25-26 ¶¶ 5-7, 2008 MSPB 110 (2008), when the agency terminated the appellant, believing him to be a probationer and, determining that he had MSPB appeal rights based on prior service, canceled the termination but then suspended the appellant for the events leading to the probationary termination:

The administrative judge's conclusion that the Board lacked jurisdiction over this appeal was based on her finding that the agency had completely rescinded its action terminating the appellant's employment. The Board has held, however, that an agency's rescission of the action underlying an appeal does not divest the Board of jurisdiction over the appeal unless the employee received all the relief he could have received if the appeal had been adjudicated and he had prevailed. Fernandez v. Department of Justice, 105 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 5 (2007).

The appellant has submitted a copy of the notice by which the agency has proposed to suspend him. He notes that the incidents that formed the basis for his involuntary separation are cited as bases for the proposed suspension; he asserts that the agency's reliance on those incidents shows that it did not remove all references to them from his personnel file, as the administrative judge believed had been done; and he argues that his appeal therefore should not have been dismissed.

If the appellant's appeal of his separation had been adjudicated, and if that separation had been found to have been improper, the administrative judge would have ordered the agency to cancel the action, restore the appellant to his position retroactively, and pay the appellant back pay and other benefits. See, e.g., Steinhoff v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 101 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶¶ 6-9 (2006). There is no general requirement, however, that the agency destroy all records relating to the alleged misconduct or other matter underlying the action. In fact, after canceling the action, the agency would have been permitted to take a new disciplinary action against the appellant, and would have been permitted to base the new action on the same incidents underlying the original action. See Jolivette v. Department of the Navy, 100 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 12 (2005) (the agency was permitted to bring another action on the same grounds because the Board was overturning the removal at issue on procedural grounds, without reaching the merits). The agency's issuance of a suspension proposal based on incidents underlying the appellant's separation therefore is not inconsistent with a finding that the separation was completely rescinded, or with a finding that the appellant received all the relief he would have received if his appeal had been adjudicated and he had prevailed.

It was suggested in Tawadrous v. Dept. of Treasury, DA-0752-10-0493-I-1 (NP 8/8/2011), that if the agency reinitiates an action, cancelation of the prior action need not lead to restoration of ordinary duties to the appellant in the interim between the cancelation of the prior action and initiation of the successor action:

The appellant also reiterates his claim from below that he was not given meaningful work when he was reinstated on August 2, 2010. On November 17, 2010, the agency issued a decision letter removing the appellant for a third time. The appellant has filed a Board appeal regarding this removal, which is currently pending before the Board on petition for review. See Tawadrous v. Department of the Treasury, MSPB Docket No. DA-0752-11-0106-I-1. Therefore, even if the appellant's job assignments upon reinstatement did not amount to a return to the status quo ante, he has received back pay to the date of his reinstatement and there is no further remedy available for the agency's actions. Cf. Strope v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 539, 542 n.2 (1997) (there was no compliance remedy available for placement of the appellant on administrative leave pending the outcome of a second removal proceeding).

See Rosado-Torres v. DHS, DA-0752-12-0596-I-1 (NP 2/4/2014) ("[T]he agency did not err when it issued the proposal notice in the instant case before the administrative judge had issued the initial decision in the first removal because the agency had unilaterally restored the appellant to a pay status on May 2, 2012, 2 days before issuance of the proposal notice.").
