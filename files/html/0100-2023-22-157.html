broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 02: Procedures for Obtaining Fees, Section C: Reasonableness of Fees, Subsection 05: Reasonableness of Time Invested, Part c: Notice to Counsel of Defect in Fee Motion
c. Notice to Counsel of Defect in Fee Motion

If an agency does not oppose a fee request as involving unreasonable rates or amounts of time, the AJ is placed in the peculiar position of becoming the antagonist of counsel and client in evaluating the fee petition. The AJ assumes this position without a record that allows reasoned findings. Theoretically, in Board practice each case stands on its own record. If an agency does not oppose the reasonableness of a fee petition, the record of the case contains only the submissions of appellant's counsel demonstrating that the time and rates are reasonable. How is the AJ supposed to refute that evidence or those contentions? The Board does not supply an acceptable answer, but only suggests that the AJ use his or her experience in similar cases. Yet cases handled by different lawyers in different communities involving different issues are not similar, and that there seems to be no requirement that the AJ articulate what experience she is drawing from, meaning that on appeal the record lacks anything other than an expression of subjective estimate by the AJ of what the fee or time investment should be. All this is done by the AJ without any advance notice to counsel of any perceived deficiencies in the fee petition. If the agency contests the fee petition as unreasonable, there is the opportunity for reply or, perhaps, a hearing on the disputed issues. When the AJ unilaterally challenges the same issues, the appellant receives no notice and no opportunity to reply.

The proper practice is for the AJ to notify appellant's counsel of any concerns the AJ may have about the fee petition in advance of the issuance of the initial decision, allowing the attorney the opportunity to supply the Board with additional information, through affidavits, billing records, or testimony at a hearing. In Wilson v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 158, 162 (1987), the AJ, without any agency objection to the reasonableness of the fees demanded, disallowed some time as excessive and insufficiently documented. On PFR, the Board sustained the AJ: "counsel has failed to submit any additional information with his petition for review sufficient to support the hours claimed for work performed...." In the Federal Circuit, counsel argued that the AJ did not properly examine the fee petition and that the AJ did not give counsel an opportunity to address the deficiencies she found. The court made some cogent observations about the due process AJs should provide to counsel fee petitions, Wilson v. DHHS, 834 F.2d 1011, 1012 (Fed. Cir. 1987):

Petitioner's counsel complains that when the presiding official--not having held a hearing, and the agency not having contested the amount--decided on her own to reject certain of the attorney's submissions, she should have first communicated her problems to the attorney so that he could have the chance to supply reasons or new material and arguments.[2]

[2] In papers submitted with the fee motion, counsel stated that "[i]f...there is any question relating to the reasonableness of the fees requested or the accuracy of the information presented in the presentations of counsel, then a hearing is requested in order to resolve those factual disputes." As we say, infra, we disagree as to the need for a hearing, but we certainly concur as to counsel's being given the opportunity to meet the presiding official's objections.

We agree in substance. In the circumstances it was incumbent on the presiding official (now administrative judge) to give the attorney an opportunity to deal with the asserted deficiencies first raised by the presiding official. This could and should have been done informally and would not require an elaborate process or a formal hearing. Of course, an oral argument could be held.

We do not believe, however, that a full-scale hearing, with the formal presentation of witnesses and evidence, is required every time one or a few factual disputes surface. A hearing may well be called for when there are many disputes to be resolved, or a large amount is involved, or the problems are complex.... But we decline to rule that a hearing is mandatory simply because there is some dispute over amounts. Neither the statute nor the regulation calls for an automatic hearing...and the Supreme Court has cautioned that "[a] request for attorney's fees should not result in a second major litigation." Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424, 427 (1983). In the current case, the problem could have been handled if the presiding official had simply communicated with the attorney, expressing her doubts and giving the attorney the opportunity to answer. That should have been done.

The Board accepted the Wilson result. An AJ should have notified counsel of his difficulty in determining how much time was spent on each of the multiple activities listed for each of the dates in question in a fee petition, and he should have allowed counsel to supplement his fee request. Russell v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 157, 162-64 (1989), modified in part on other grounds, Garstkiewicz v. USPS, 50 MSPR 476, 478-79 (1991). If an AJ fails to notify counsel of a problem that the AJ addresses sua sponte in a fee petition, a remand is not necessarily required. The Board may address the issue through consideration of submissions on PFR. See Driscoll v. USPS, 116 MSPR 662, 669 ¶ 12, 2011 MSPB 80 (2011) ("Because the appellant's attorney has proffered explanations for some of these charges in her petition for review, we are able to make a determination regarding the reasonableness of the disputed fees without remanding the matter to the administrative judge."); Hart v. Dept. of Transp., 115 MSPR 10, 18 ¶ 20, 2010 MSPB 183 (2010) (when, after the AJ failed to address his concerns about deficiencies in a fee petition by providing the party an opportunity to address those matters, the Board decided the dispute on PFR when supplemental information was provided with the PFR); Ruble v. OPM, 96 MSPR 44, 48 ¶ 8 (2004) ("While the AJ should have provided the appellant with notice that her motion for attorney fees was deficient, and provided her with an opportunity to correct the deficiency, see, e.g., Martinez v. U.S. Postal Service, 89 M.S.P.R. 152, ¶ 16 (2001), we find that this has not prejudiced the appellant's substantive rights, see Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984) (an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision)."); Blackman v. USPS, 67 MSPR 382, 385 (1995) (although the AJ should have notified the appellant of problems with a fee petition that were identified by the AJ, no harm was done because the issues were addressed in the appellant's reply to the agency opposition to the fee motion); Stein v. USPS, 63 MSPR 537, 541-42 (1994) (Board addresses through the PFR the matters not brought to the appellant's attention by the AJ: facsimile expenses and allegedly inflated fees and costs); Harris v. USDA, 48 MSPR 189, 194 (1991) (AJ did not provide counsel with a reasonable opportunity to explain the AJ's perceived deficiencies; reviewing information submitted by counsel with the PFR).
