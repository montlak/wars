b. Restoration of Status Quo Ante

For an action to be well and truly canceled, the employee must be restored to the status quo ante. He must be placed as nearly as possible in the same situation that he would have been in had the action rescinded never occurred. See Daujotas v. DOD, CH-0752-19-0455-I-1 (NP 2/23/2023) ("To cancel an appealable action either when ordered by the Board or to render an appeal moot, an agency must remove all references to that action from the employee's personnel record. Price v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 222, ¶ 13 (2012). The agency has the burden of proving mootness. See Friends of the Earth, Inc. v. Landlaw Environmental Services (TOC), Inc., 528 U.S. 167, 189 (2000) (reflecting that the burden of proving mootness is on the moving party)..."): Harris v. Dept. of Air Force, 96 MSPR 193, 196 ¶ 6 (2004) (restoration of the status quo will generally entail return of the employee to his former position or in a position substantially equivalent in scope and status to his former position; the same grade, pay, and tenure; back pay, removal of all references to the adverse action from the appellant's personnel file; proper documentation of the restoration from the agency and an opportunity for the appellant to respond to the agency's submissions); Thomas v. USPS, 73 MSPR 120, 125 (1997) (an individual "cannot be left in a worse position as a result of a rescission of an action under the concept of status quo ante"); Thomas v. USPS, 65 MSPR 268, 272 (1994) ("A return to the status quo ante requires that the employee be placed back in his former position or in a position substantially equivalent in scope and status to the former position."); Tyrrell v. VA, 60 MSPR 276, 279 (1994) (remanding for consideration of the appellant's allegations that the agency failed to provide full back pay and benefits, failed to expunge adverse references in personnel records, and harassed him upon his return to work); Bennington v. MSPB, 482 Fed. Appx. 550, 553 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP) ("Where there is a dispute about the equivalence of the positions, the employee's appeal is not moot. See Kerr v. Nat'l Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 & n.3 (Fed. Cir. 1984) ('The injured party is to be placed, as near as may be, in the situation he would have occupied if the wrong had not been committed.' (quoting Wicker v. Hoppock, 73 U.S. (6 Wall.) 94, 99 (1867)))."). Consideration of the state of the status quo extends to various appeals within Board jurisdiction. See Domingo v. USPS, 33 MSPR 468, 470 (1987) (jurisdiction was not lost when the agency rescinded a demotion and substituted a letter of warning); Howard v. USPS, 25 MSPR 132, 133 (1984) (unilateral reduction of a suspension to one day did not divest the Board of jurisdiction without the appellant's consent and unless action was completely rescinded). But see Rojas v. USPS, 70 MSPR 400, 404 (1996) ("The Board's jurisdiction is determined by the nature of the action against the appellant at the time that he files his appeal. If an agency completely rescinds the action appealed, the Board has no jurisdiction over the rescinded action.") (Rojas overruled in part, Fernandez v. DOJ, 105 MSPR 443, 446 n.1, 2007 MSPB 99 (2007) ("these decisions and others that frame the test for mootness in this way are OVERRULED because the correct focus is on whether there is any relief that the appellant could receive if the Board ruled in his favor")); Mungaray v. USPS, 19 MSPR 485, 487 (1984) (no Board jurisdiction when a removal and a subsequent suspension were rescinded, but the appellant was concerned about not getting paid for a period of two days that were not included in the suspension); cf. Toby v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2023 NP) ("For an appeal to be moot, an employee must have received all of the relief that he could have received if the matter had been adjudicated and he had prevailed....See also Holleman v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 629 F. App'x 942, 948 (Fed. Cir. 2015). Dismissal of an appeal as moot equates to a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction because, in such cases, there is no final decision from which to appeal nor grounds for the Board to retain jurisdiction. See Wells v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 730 F. App'x 909, 911 (Fed. Cir. 2018)."); Favreau v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-12-0547-I-6 (NP 3/9/2023) (appeal moot despite removal of collateral duties following cancelation of action and restoration of appellant to regular job). [F]or an appeal to be deemed moot, the appellant must have received all of the relief that she could have received if the matter had been adjudicated and she had prevailed, and the agency must return her "as nearly as possible" to the status quo ante. When, as here, the agency has not reinstated the appellant to her former position and duties, the Board will examine whether the agency had compelling reasons for not doing so. See Currier v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 191, 199 (1996). Berteletti v. USPS, 45 MSPR 1, 6-8 (1990), describes the agency's responsibilities upon cancelation of an appealed adverse action:

The Board's jurisdiction is determined by the nature of the action at the time the appellant filed his appeal. See Mavronikolas v. United States Postal Service, 39 MSPR 442, 444 (1989). An agency's cancellation of an appealed adverse action may divest the Board of its jurisdiction, but only where it constitutes a complete rescission of the action. See id. See also Guy v. Department of Energy, 37 MSPR 230, 232-33 (1988) (the agency's failure to completely rescind an appealed action results in a retention of jurisdiction by the Board). The cancellation constitutes a complete rescission only if the appellant is returned as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. See Maki v. United States Postal Service, 41 MSPR 449, 456-57 (1989); Mavronikolas, 39 MSPR at 444.

In Sarver v. Department of the Treasury, 20 MSPR 499, 502 (1984), recon. denied in part, granted in part on other grounds, 26 MSPR 685 (1985), the Board noted that the matter of returning an employee to the status quo ante upon the agency's rescission of an appealed action does not end with the employee's formal reinstatement at an equivalent grade and pay. Subsequently, in Mann v. Veterans Administration, 29 MSPR 271, 274-75 (1985), the Board set out a four-part progressive test to determine whether the employee had been returned as nearly as possible to the status quo ante: (1) Was the employee reinstated to his former position; (2) does the former position still exist at the same grade and classification level; (3) does the agency have an overriding interest in not returning the employee to his former position; and (4) is the position to which the employee was reassigned in fact substantially similar to the former position.[17] See also Saal v. Department of the Navy, 38 MSPR 670, 674 n.6 (1988). In Mann, the Board held that the employee's reinstatement followed immediately by a detail or reassignment did not satisfy the test of whether the employee was reinstated to her former position because the agency did not show a compelling reason for not reinstating her to her former position, and the position to which she was reassigned was not substantially similar to her former position. See Mann, 29 MSPR at 275.

[17] While the agency is generally required to reinstate the employee to his former position, where the agency claims that it has a strong overriding interest for not placing the employee in the identical position, the Board will analyze the legitimacy of the agency's proffered rationale in order to determine if it is sufficient to justify not returning the employee to his former position. See Saal v. Department of the Navy, 38 MSPR 670, 673-74 (1988); Mann, 29 MSPR at 274-75. See also Yetman v. Department of the Army, 36 MSPR 425, 429 (1988) (absent compelling reasons clearly justifying a change, an appellant is entitled under law to a full resumption of the position held prior to an unwarranted personnel action). In such cases, if the Board finds that the interests of the agency are so compelling, then those reasons, in appropriate cases, may be sufficient to override the appellant's entitlement to return to his former position. Mann, 29 MSPR at 274-75. The Board must address in such cases the question of whether the position to which the appellant has been reassigned is in fact substantially similar to the former position. Id. at 275.

In the present case, although the appellant fervently argues that he is not challenging the agency's rescission of the demotion action, he is asserting that, because he has been detailed, he has not been returned to the status quo ante. Indeed, it appears that the appellant has not been returned to the status quo ante because he has remained on detail since the agency rescinded the adverse action. See Mann, 29 MSPR at 274. The agency has not shown that it has a continuing compelling interest for not placing the appellant in his original position. Nor has it shown that the position to which he has been detailed is substantially similar to his original position. See Saal, 38 MSPR at 673-74; Mann, 29 MSPR at 274-75.

Although the Board does not have jurisdiction to review a detail of an employee[18], it does have authority to review whether the appellant has been returned as nearly as possible to the status quo ante in the context of determining jurisdiction over an allegedly cancelled appealable action. See, e.g., Mann, 29 MSPR at 274-75. Therefore, it is necessary to remand this case to the regional office for further adjudication to determine whether the appellant has been returned to the status quo ante, and if not, whether the agency has shown a compelling interest for temporarily not placing the appellant in his former position or a substantially similar position. Id.

[18] See Snow v. Department of the Air Force, 39 MSPR 582, 584 (1989); Loveland v. United States Air Force, 34 MSPR 484, 492 (1987); Chance v. Federal Aviation Administration, 5 MSPB 311, 5 MSPR 277, 279 (1981) (a detail of an employee, even to a lower-graded position in a different competitive level, does not constitute an adverse action appealable to the Board). See also Cook v. United States Postal Service, 30 MSPR 671, 674 (1986) (although the agency is required to return the employee to the status quo ante, the agency is not precluded from taking legitimate personnel actions after the cancellation of the action as long as a valid reason exists for the later action).

There is an exception to the rule that the status quo is not restored if the agency cancels the appealed action and reassigns the employee to a different position. If the agency has a strong overriding interest or compelling reason for not placing the employee in his former position, it may be sufficient to negate the appellant's entitlement to his former position. One reason for the reassignment might be that the employee is legally disqualified for his prior position. If there is a dispute over the employee's qualifications, however, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on whether the Board retains jurisdiction of the original, "canceled" adverse action. See Mobery v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 110, 113-14 (1994) (relying on Mann v. VA, 29 MSPR 271, 274 (1985)). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Compelling Reason for Reassignment; Detail; Different Duties."]

In a USERRA military leave case, the Board determine that an enforcement petition may not be brought when an appeal is improperly dismissed as moot, Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616, 624-25 ¶¶ 15-18 (2007), 2007 MSPB 234 (2007):

...If an appeal is not truly moot despite cancellation of the action under appeal, the proper remedy is for the Board to retain jurisdiction and to adjudicate the appeal on the merits. Fernandez, 105 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 5; Hagan, 99 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 6.

The administrative judge found that the agency's representation that it intended to provide status quo ante relief as to 11 dates on which it incorrectly charged the appellant military leave was sufficient to render the appeal moot as to those dates. The administrative judge further found that any claim that the agency did not actually provide status quo ante relief was properly raised in a future petition for enforcement. This analysis is incorrect for three reasons.

First, the dismissal of an appeal as moot amounts to a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction. See Rojas v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 400, 404 (1996) (when an agency completely rescinds the action appealed, the Board has no jurisdiction over the rescinded action), overruled on other grounds, Fernandez, 105 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 5 n.1; Mobery v. Department of the Navy, 65 M.S.P.R. 110, 113 (1994) (when an agency completely rescinds an adverse action after an appeal is filed, the appeal is rendered moot and the Board is divested of jurisdiction). The Board lacks the authority to order enforcement if it lacks jurisdiction over the underlying appeal. Cf. Hunter v. Department of the Air Force, 83 M.S.P.R. 7, ¶ 11 (1999) (absent a Board order with which the agency was directed to comply, the Board lacks the authority to order enforcement); Sellman v. U.S. Postal Service, 63 M.S.P.R. 145, 151 (1994) (absent a settlement agreement that has been entered into the record for enforcement purposes, the Board may not order enforcement in a matter that has not been adjudicated on the merits); Bell v. Department of the Army, 48 M.S.P.R. 86, 91 (1991) (because the Board lacked jurisdiction over the underlying appeal, it could not enter the parties' settlement agreement into the record for enforcement purposes). Therefore, where an appeal is dismissed as moot, the Board lacks the authority to adjudicate a petition for enforcement alleging that the appellant has not received the relief to which she is entitled. See Parkin v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶¶ 7-8 (2006) (because the underlying appeal was properly dismissed as moot, the Board lacked jurisdiction to order compliance); McCulley v. U.S. Postal Service, 54 M.S.P.R. 207, 210 (1992) (where, after the appeal was dismissed as moot, a dispute arose as to whether the agency had afforded all appropriate relief, the Board was without authority to order the agency to provide relief because the Board cannot order compliance in a matter that has not been heard on the merits or resolved through a settlement agreement entered into the record); Sarver v. Department of the Treasury, 26 M.S.P.R. 685, 689 (1985) (where the underlying appeal was dismissed as moot upon the agency's incomplete cancellation of the appealed action, the Board lacked authority to order enforcement; rather, the proper remedy was to retain jurisdiction over the appeal and adjudicate it on the merits).

In finding that the appellant could bring a petition for enforcement if she had any claims about the relief provided by the agency after the appeal was dismissed as moot, the administrative judge relied on Hill v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 453, 456, aff'd, 104 F.3d 376 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table), and Hatler v. Department of the Air Force, 3 M.S.P.R. 322, 325 (1980). [S]ee also Cupp v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 4, 6 (1994). Although those cases support the administrative judge's conclusion, we find that the analysis underlying those cases is legally unsound for the reason noted above. Therefore, we OVERRULE those decisions and others that conclude that a petition for enforcement can be brought after an appeal is dismissed as moot.

When an AJ receives an agency motion to dismiss an appeal as moot, the AJ will order the appellant to respond with evidence and argument as why the appeal is not moot. A substantive response from the appellant will likely lead the AJ to require evidence from the agency supporting its position, and the AJ will not likely dismiss the appeal as moot until the agency has met the appellant's objections. There may be several rounds of status conferences or orders until the mootness issue is resolved. If the appeal is dismissed as moot, the appellant properly challenges the improper dismissal for mootness through a petition for review, Stempihar v. USPS, 106 MSPR 115, 119-20 ¶¶ 9-10, 2007 MSPB 151 (2007):

The initial decision was also incomplete on its own terms, insofar as it informed the appellant that he could file a petition for enforcement at some future time, but failed to include the Board's customary notice of the procedures and deadline for obtaining compliance. Moreover, the initial decision was misleading; a decision dismissing an appeal as moot is not enforceable by the appellant because it is not a decision on the merits in the appellant's favor. See Parkin v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶ 7 (2006).

In sum, when the appellant objected to having his appeal dismissed as moot, the administrative judge informed him, in essence, that he could return to the Board for additional proceedings if he was dissatisfied with the agency's actions. The administrative judge did not explain when such a future proceeding should be instituted, the appellant never took any actions indicating an intent to abandon his claim for lost overtime, and the appellant acted promptly in filing what he thought was a petition for enforcement but which must be deemed a petition for review in light of Parkin. Under the unusual circumstances of this case, we find that the appellant has shown good cause for the untimeliness of his petition for review.

A new appeal is not the proper process for responding to an initial decision dismissing an appeal as moot, Joyner v. USPS, 76 MSPR 473, 476 (1997), post.

Haskins, supra, rejected the idea that a promise to pay, rather than delivery of payment, is sufficient to moot an appeal, 106 MSPR 616, 625-26 ¶¶ 20-22, (2007):

[T]he administrative judge relied on Dellera v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 65 M.S.P.R. 636, 642 (1994), aff'd, 82 F.3d 434 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table), in finding that the agency's present intent to restore the appellant to the status quo ante was sufficient to render the appeal moot even though it is apparently not disputed that the agency had not yet completed its efforts to afford relief at the time the appeal was dismissed. Cf. Williams v. Department of the Army, 97 M.S.P.R. 246, ¶ 10 (2004) (the agency's documentary evidence showing that it had properly calculated and paid the appellant's back pay, and its sworn affidavit stating that the agency was working with its payroll agent to ensure that the appellant properly received the interest on his back pay constituted satisfactory evidence that the agency provided the appellant with all of his back pay), overruled on other grounds, Durr v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 99 M.S.P.R. 283, ¶ 11 (2005). However, the Board has held also that an appeal is not moot where there is a genuine factual dispute as to whether the appellant has received all of the relief to which she is entitled. See Pupis v. U.S. Postal Service, 105 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 6 (2007); Harris, 96 M.S.P.R. 193, ¶ 9; Gillespie, 90 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 10.

Upon further consideration, we are persuaded that the latter rule is the better practice. The statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence. Pupis, 105 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 5; Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995). Although we have no reason to doubt the agency's good faith in this appeal, the fact remains that the agency's good faith assurance that it will provide relief is not evidence that it has provided relief, and an appeal simply is not truly moot until all appropriate relief has been provided.

We hold, therefore, that an appeal may not be dismissed as moot until the agency provides acceptable evidence showing that it has actually afforded the appellant all of the relief that she could have received if the matter had been adjudicated and she had prevailed. See Fernandez, 105 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 5. To the extent that Dellera and any similar cases suggest that an appeal may be dismissed as moot prior to the submission of evidence showing that all appropriate relief has actually been afforded, they are OVERRULED.

The Board expects the agency to respond with evidence if it challenges that appellant's sworn assertion that the status quo, e.g., back pay, was not provided. Promises of compliance and representatives' statements do not provide the requisite proof, Pupis v. USPS, 105 MSPR 1, 3-4 ¶¶ 5-6, 2007 MSPB 47 (2007):

In his petition for review, the appellant states under penalty of perjury that he has not yet received any back pay from the agency. In response, the agency has submitted the unsworn statement of its representative contending that it has submitted all appropriate documentation to secure payment of the appellant's back pay and that he "is due to receive the payment for all due and owing back pay, shortly." The statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence. Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995). The agency has not submitted any evidence to support its claim that it has actually submitted the appropriate documentation to effect the appellant's back pay payments. See Harris, 96 M.S.P.R. 193, ¶¶ 8-9 (the agency did not show that it restored the appellant to the status quo ante where it did not submit any evidence, such as letters to the appellant or declarations by agency officials, showing that it had canceled the appellant's removal and he had received all back pay to which he was entitled); Gillespie, 90 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶¶ 9-10 (the agency did not prove that it restored the appellant to the status quo ante where it submitted no evidence, such as an SF-50, letters to the appellant, or declarations from agency officials, establishing that it had canceled the appellant's removal and he had received all back pay to which he was entitled); cf. Williams v. Department of the Army, 97 M.S.P.R. 246, ¶ 10 (2004) (the agency's documentary evidence showed that it had properly calculated and paid the appellant's back pay, and its sworn affidavit stating that the agency was working with its payroll agent to ensure that the appellant properly received the interest on his back pay constituted satisfactory evidence that the agency provided the appellant with all of his back pay), overruled on other grounds, Durr v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 99 M.S.P.R. 283, ¶ 11 (2005).

An appellant is not restored to the status quo ante where he does not receive all of the back pay to which he is entitled. Harris, 96 M.S.P.R. 193, ¶ 9; Gillespie, 90 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 10. The appellant's sworn statement that the agency has not paid him all appropriate back pay constitutes a nonfrivolous allegation that he has not been restored to the status quo ante. See Jasper v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 27, ¶ 9 (2001). This matter must be addressed and resolved before the appeal can properly be dismissed as moot. See Harris, 96 M.S.P.R. 193, ¶ 9; Gillespie, 90 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 10. Therefore, it is appropriate to remand this appeal to the regional office for resolution of this issue. See Harris, 96 M.S.P.R. 193, ¶ 9; Gillespie, 90 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 10.

...When the Board orders cancellation of an action, it requires that the agency remove all references to that action from the employee's personnel record. U.S. Postal Service, 44 M.S.P.R. 517, 519-20 (1990) (citing Dancer v. U.S. Postal Service, 36 M.S.P.R. 235, 238, rev'd in part on other grounds, 38 M.S.P.R. 224 (1988)); Kellus v. See Harris, 96 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 8. Remand is therefore appropriate. See Guy v. Department of Energy, 37 M.S.P.R. 230, 233 (1988); Kellus, 35 M.S.P.R. at 339-40.

The cancelation may be demonstrated through a SF-50 or some other documentation, e.g., a letter, that the Board deems adequate proof of the reversal of the earlier personnel action. Taylor v. USPS, 31 MSPR 12, 14 (1986). Assuming other proof of cancelation of an appealed action, the absence of a SF-50 is not fatal to a claim of mootness. See Bolton v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 333, 339 (1998). If a SF-50 is used, however, and if the SF-50 makes reference to the MSPB appeal as the reason for the cancelation, the agency has not removed all references to the canceled actions from the appellant's personnel record; the status quo has not been restored. Veal v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 66, 68 (1991). Other documentation must be corrected. See Marren v. DOJ, 55 MSPR 1, 3 (1992) (in an IRA appeal of AWOL charge, the agency would be required to remove reference to that charge from all agency records if it sought to moot the appeal by canceling the AWOL charge); Gonzales v. If on PFR the record is unclear or disputed as to whether the status quo has been restored, the Board may remand the appeal for further development, Shu v. USPS, SF-0353-11-0065-I-1 (NP 8/5/2011):

...The initial decision found that the appeal was moot based on the appellant's representation in a status conference call that he had been returned to duty and received all of the relief he sought in the appeal.

For an appeal to be rendered moot, an appellant must receive all of the relief that he could have received if the matter had been adjudicated and he prevailed. Green v. Department of the Air Force, 114 M.S.P.R. 340, ¶ 7 (2010); Harris v. Department of Transportation, 96 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 8 (2004). Further, an appeal may not be dismissed as moot until the agency has submitted acceptable evidence that it has actually afforded the appellant all the relief to which he would be entitled if he prevailed. Vidal v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 254, ¶ 4 (2010); Haskins v. Department of the Navy, 106 M.S.P.R. 616, ¶¶ 19-22 (2007), reviewed dismissed, 267 F. App'x 934 (Fed. Cir. 2008).

Here, the appellant has asserted on petition for review that he did not receive all the relief to which he was entitled, nor did he make that representation. Further, the record does not establish what relief the appellant would have been entitled to had he prevailed in his restoration appeal, and the agency has not produced proof that it provided everything that the appellant would have been entitled to had he prevailed. We therefore GRANT the appellant's petition, VACATE the initial decision and REMAND the appeal for further proceedings.

Remanding an appeal for lack of adequate agency evidence of restoration of the status quo, Maino v. Dept. of Treasury, NY-0752-14-0302-I-1 (NP 7/7/2015), observed:

...In support of its claim that it had rescinded the appellant's suspension, the agency provided an unsworn letter from the chief of its Austin Payroll Center and a series of computer "[s]creenshots detailing the contributions" and leave balances at issue. Although the screenshots may evidence the agency's payment of back pay and interest, and the restoration of his leave balances, the description of the evidence is terse and does not appear complete. Moreover, the statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence. E.g., Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995).

Additionally, the agency's unsworn assertion that any references to the appellant's suspension have been removed from his OPF is insufficient to demonstrate that the agency has actually done so. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 450, 452 (1992) (appeal from denial of a within-grade increase was mooted by cancelation of the action, retroactive pay for the period of the denial, removal of all references from the personnel record, and amendment of the acceptable level of competence determination and the underlying performance evaluation); Jazdzewski v. USDA, 35 MSPR 541, 543 (1987) (WIGI appeal was mooted by retroactive restoration of the step increase; an objectionable reassignment did not sustain Board jurisdiction). But restoration of the status quo does not mean an improvement on it. If the agency takes action under a last chance agreement, the employee appeals, and the agency rescinds the action, the appeal will be dismissed as moot. The Board will not release the employee from the remaining term of the last chance agreement. See McCourt v. VA, AT-0752-12-0251-I-1 (NP 11/21/2012); Rojas v. USPS, 70 MSPR 400, 404 (1996) ("When an agency itself elects to cancel an adverse action, a return to the status quo ante requires that all references to such action be removed from the appellant's personnel record...however, nothing requires an agency to expunge records of prior disciplinary actions upon which the agency relied in effecting the action appealed, because those actions are not properly before the Board."). Once the action is canceled and the status quo ante is restored, the action is not ordinarily resurrected because the agency reassigns the appellant or because an AWOL charge is pending against the appellant that could lead to another removal. Maki v. USPS, 41 MSPR 449, 457-60 (1989) (cancelation of an appealed action involving, among others, an AWOL charge did not require reversal of the AWOL charge as part of the restoration of the status quo ante; reversal of the AWOL would have placed the appellant in a position better than she would have had without an adverse action); Bruning v. VA, 32 MSPR 213, 216 (1987) (status quo was restored), rev'd, Bruning v. VA, 834 F.2d 1019 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (status quo was not restored); O'Rourke v. USPS, 29 MSPR 130, 132 (1985) (implying that the Board lacked jurisdiction of the appellant's detail following his restoration to his position).

The appellant need not be restored to a better position than he held before the initiation of the adverse action. In Hagan v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 313, 317-18 ¶¶ 8-9 (2005), the employee was on AWOL status after his OWCP benefits were terminated and up to the time he was removed for AWOL when the employee did not return to work following the employer's request that he do so. OWCP benefits were reinstated, the agency rescinded the removal action, and it then placed the appellant on extended LWOP status. Restoration to LWOP status when the action was rescinded, rather than placement of the employee in pay status, was appropriate:

A status quo ante remedy does not require that the appellant be placed in a better position than he was in at the time of the agency's action. See Covert v. Department of the Navy, 31 M.S.P.R. 376, 382-83 (1986). Because the appellant was in an unauthorized absence status and OWCP subsequently determined that the appellant had a compensable injury for the period of his absence, he should have been in an approved leave status. See Burton v. Department of the Army, 60 M.S.P.R. 392 (1994). Accordingly, the agency was required only to place the appellant in the position that he was in prior to his removal, which in this case, was an approved leave status while receiving OWCP benefits. See Sellman, 63 M.S.P.R. at 154 n.3.

Thus, the AJ properly found that placement of the appellant on LWOP constitutes a return to the status quo ante. Because the appellant was returned to the status quo ante, the removal appeal was properly dismissed as moot.

If AWOL was the status of the employee immediately prior to her removal, when the agency cancels the removal it restores the status quo by returning the employee to AWOL, assuming no claim is made as to a constructive suspension, Hess v. USPS, 123 MSPR 183, 187 ¶ 7, 2016 MSPB 5 (2016):

...The appellant concedes that she was in an AWOL status during the period of time preceding her removal. The Board has found that it generally is inappropriate to direct an agency to change an employee's AWOL status or leave status prior to an appealable action because this status is not, in itself, appealable to the Board. See Maki v. U.S. Postal Service, 41 M.S.P.R. 449, 457 (1989). The Board does not have before it the circumstances of the appellant's AWOL prior to the effective date of her removal.[2] To require the cancellation of the appellant's preremoval AWOL status thus would violate the status quo ante principle because it would place the appellant in a more advantageous position than she was in prior to her removal. Id. We therefore find that the agency properly returned the appellant to the status quo ante by rescinding all references to her removal and placing her in a nonpay status.

Under Guy, supra, the agency may not undercut the cancelation of the action appealed. It is improper, for example, to include gratuitous notations on a SF-50 suggesting that an employee's WIGI was restored for reasons other than the employee's performance. See Storm v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 14, 20 (1994) (suspension was not moot when the SF-50 suggested that cancelation would not be in the agency's best interests to avoid conflict with the subsequent action it took against the appellant). Making the point that adverse notations in personnel files may deflect claimed mootness, Murray v. DOD, 92 MSPR 361, 368-69 ¶¶ 16-18 (2002), explained:

...Mootness can arise at any stage of litigation, and an appeal will be dismissed as moot when, by virtue of an intervening event, the Board cannot grant any effectual relief in favor of the appellant, viz., when the appellant, by whatever means, obtained all of the relief he could have obtained had he prevailed before the Board, and thereby lost any legally cognizable interest in the outcome of the appeal.... The agency's unilateral modification of its personnel action after an appeal has been filed cannot divest the Board of jurisdiction, unless the appellant consents to such divestiture or the agency completely rescinds the action being appealed. Gillespie v. Department of Defense, 90 M.S.P.R. 327 ¶ 7 (2001). For an appeal to be deemed moot the agency's rescission must be complete, i.e., the appellant must be returned to the status quo ante and not left in a worse position because of the cancellation than he would have been in if the matter had been adjudicated and he had prevailed. Id.

The Board has recognized a "capable of repetition, yet evading review" exception to the mootness concept. See Department of Agriculture v. Palmer, 68 M.S.P.R. 586, 589 (1995). The Supreme Court has permitted cases that would otherwise be deemed moot to continue under this exception--

only in the exceptional situations where the following two circumstances are simultaneously present: (1) The challenged action [is] in its duration too short to be fully litigated prior to its cessation or expiration, and (2) there is a reasonable expectation that the same complaining party would be subjected to the same action again.

Lewis v. Continental Bank Corporation, 494 U.S. 472, 481-82, 110 S. Ct. 1249, 108 L. Ed.2d 400 (1990). This exception requires that there be a reasonable expectation or demonstrated probability, rather than a mere physical or theoretical possibility, that the same controversy will recur involving the same complaining party. Murphy v. Hunt, 455 U.S. 478, 482, 102 S. Ct. 1181, 71 L. Ed.2d 353 (1982). Here, even assuming that the appellant would not have retired, we find that the record contains no evidence to suggest that the agency was likely to reinstate the suspension action, after having rescinded it. Thus, the appeal is not affected by the "capable of repetition, yet evading review" exception to the mootness doctrine.

However, we find, in any event, that the agency has not shown that the matter that is the subject of this appeal, the 30-day suspension, is moot. In Cooper v. Department of the Navy, 108 F.3d 324, 326 (Fed. Cir. 1997), the court held that the Navy's cancellation of the removal action and removal of all references to that action from the appellant's official personnel file (OPF) eliminated all the consequences of that action and thus rendered the appeal moot. Here, although it is undisputed that the agency did not effect the 30-day suspension, it has not submitted documentation proving that it cancelled the suspension and removed all references to it from the appellant's OPF. See Gillespie, 90 M.S.P.R. 327 ¶ 9 (the Board found that the appeal was not moot when the agency failed to submit a declaration attesting to the purging from the appellant's personnel record of all references to the removal); compare Nguyen v. Department of the Navy, 84 M.S.P.R. 603 ¶ 5 (1999) (the Board dismissed the appeal as moot after the agency, in response to the appellant's allegations that all references to the removal had not been removed from her OPF, submitted an affidavit declaring that they had been removed). Thus, there remains some relief that the Board can order the agency to afford, i.e., assuring that the appellant's OPF has been purged of all references to the suspension. Accordingly, we find it necessary to remand the appeal of the suspension.

Overtime earnings may present issues concerning restoration of the status quo. See Harris v. But collateral consequences have their limits. Tyrrell v. VA, 60 MSPR 276, 278 (1994). An unfavorable performance appraisal for a period postdating the canceled removal action did not preserve MSPB jurisdiction. See Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 32 MSPR 221, 224 (1987). When the appellant was furloughed, the agency then returned the appellant to work with pay for the period of the furlough, the appeal was moot despite the appellant's argument that the agency's original error placing the appellant's position was in the group permissibly to be furloughed might again occur, Blackwell v. DHS, DA-0752-14-0030-I-1 (NP 7/30/2014):

Although the appellant alleged in her initial appeal that DHS erred in furloughing her, she did not identify a requested remedy. When asked by the agency what she was seeking, the appellant indicated that she was not seeking money but was seeking a change in the agency's policies of determining who was "essential" in order to prevent any potential harm in the future, such as another furlough or a denial of promotional opportunities. She also requested a change in classification from a 340 series manager to a 1895 series manager because the former was excepted during the shutdown and the latter was not. The appellant's response to the administrative judge's show cause order similarly pointed to fear of future harm. She argued that the back pay she received was not a remedy because she remained in a position where DHS could apply the same procedures during subsequent furloughs, shutdowns, or sequestrations. Id. The appellant's petition for review explained that, "The request for relief is a finding on whether the agency's decision to furlough the appellant showed harmful error."

The Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). As detailed above, the appellant has consistently indicated that she is not appealing her back pay. Instead, she seeks either a change in classification or a declaration that the agency's shutdown procedures were applied inappropriately, so that she does not suffer future harm. However, the Board lacks jurisdiction over cases concerning the proper classification of a position. Saunders v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 757 F.2d 1288, 1290 (Fed. Cir. 1985). In addition, speculation that an adverse action may occur in the future does not bring an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction. Larkin v. Veterans Administration, 6 M.S.P.R. 245, 247 (1981). To the extent that the appellant seeks to ensure that the agency institutes future furloughs in a particular manner, the Board is specifically prohibited from issuing advisory opinions. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(h).

While the appellant seeks either a change in classification or a declaration that the agency erred in order to prevent future harm, neither is a remedy the Board can provide. Therefore, the dismissal of her appeal as moot is affirmed.
