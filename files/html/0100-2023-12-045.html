b. Revisions to Proposal

The review process is not triggered if the union presents to FLRA a request for review of a proposal that differs substantially from the proposal submitted to the agency. The union's request for review will be dismissed, with leave to the union to resubmit the request for review to FLRA once it obtains a declaration of nonnegotiability of the same proposal it submits to FLRA. See AFGE, Dept. of Educ. Council of Locals and Dept. of Educ., 36 FLRA 130, 136-37 (1990) (Proposal 3). If the union rewords a proposal in its response to an agency statement of position, the proposal will not be considered by FLRA, because the union has not obtained an agency allegation of nonnegotiability concerning the reworded proposal. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, BATF, 38 FLRA 263, 269 (1990); see Tidewater Va. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Navy Pub. Works Ctr., Norfolk, 46 FLRA 1026, 1026 (1992) (petition for review dismissed because agency's allegation of nonnegotiability "concerns a proposal which is different from the one in the Union's petition for review"). But see AFGE Local 2902 and Dept. of Army, Army Garrison, Ft. A.P. Hill, 44 FLRA 3, 7 (1992) (noting, as to the Union's Response to the Agency's Statement of Position, "[a]s so explained, the Union has modified the intent of the provision. For purposes of our decision, we adopt the Union's explanation contained in its response.").

It may occur that, once an agency rejects a proposal as nonnegotiable, the union decides to revise the proposal. The revision must be submitted to the agency for another negotiability determination. It is impermissible to first suggest changes to a proposal (or to suggest that omission of certain language was an oversight) in a brief to FLRA during the course of a negotiability appeal. AFGE Local 2578 and GSA, Nat'l Archives and Records Serv., 3 FLRA 109, 110 (1980); see Int'l Plate Printers Local 2 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 25 FLRA 113, 144 (1987) (Provisions 33 and 34: "the Union's petition in this case did not include any written allegation of nonnegotiability as to the revised versions of the provisions and there is no indication in the record that such a written allegation was requested.").

An exception to FLRA's decisions pertaining to revisions of proposals during the negotiability process was found in FLRA's negotiability regulation that permits the parties to "discuss, clarify and resolve...[t]he meaning of the proposal. 5 CFR 2424.36(b) (2000). FLRA interpreted that regulation to mean that, where appropriate, modification of the wording of a proposal or provision to conform to the intended or agreed-upon meaning of the proposal or provision will be encouraged, Hawaii FEMTC and DOD, Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard, 57 FLRA 450, 451 (2001) (citing Negotiability Proceedings, 63 Fed. Reg. 66,405, 66,408 (Dec. 2, 1998)), and that can occur in the post-petition conference, earlier discussed. The current regulation establishes a "post-petition conference," 5 CFR 2424.23(b)(1) (2022) to discuss and clarify the meaning of the disputed proposal or provision. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 72 FLRA 556, 556-57 (2021) (Member Abbott concurring; Chairman DuBester dissenting), spoke to amendments to proposal as the parties reach the post-petition conference:

Prior to the PPC, the Union amended the wording of both proposals in its response to the Agency's statement. The Union revised the proposals to address the Agency's claim that the proposals went beyond the scope of the proposed change. The Union also argued at the PPC that the amended wording of the proposals--as they appear in the response--should supersede the wording that the Union provided in its petition. Subsequently, the Agency objected to discussing the revised wording of the proposals at the PPC. The Agency argued that the Authority should not consider the revised proposals because it never declared the amended proposals to be nonnegotiable and § 2424.22 of the Authority's Regulations requires the Union to set forth the "exact wording" of the proposals in its petition.

The Agency asserts that there is no precedent to permit a union to revise the wording of its proposals after filing a petition. That argument is not correct. The Authority has held that a union may amend its petition, including the wording of disputed proposals, before and during a PPC,[9] especially where, as here, there is no evidence that a party was prejudiced by the revised wording.[10] Here, the Agency was aware that the Union amended the wording of the proposals prior to the PPC; the Agency had the opportunity to consider the revised proposals both at the PPC and in its reply to the Union's response; and the Union submitted the revised wording in direct response to the Agency's initial objections in its statement. Therefore, we will only consider the revised proposals because they advance the resolution of the dispute.
