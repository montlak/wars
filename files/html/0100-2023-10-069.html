a. Exclusion From Hearing Room of an Individual Whose Presence May Intimidate a Witness

The AJ may exclude from the hearing any person who is disruptive; other persons may be excluded for good cause. See 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(6), (7) (2022). In practice, witnesses are sequestered so that their testimony is not affected by the testimony of other witnesses. But a witness may return to the hearing room after testifying. A person other than a witness may lurk in the hearing room. Hearings are open to the public. A party may seek to exclude persons whose presence might have a chilling effect on a testifying witness. A motion to exclude a manager might be appropriate if all of his subordinates will be called to buttress a case of his reprisal against an employee. Likewise, a group of heavies partial to the appellant could reasonably be asked to leave if their presence might intimidate a meek supervisor. If an AJ's assessment of a request to exclude an individual from the hearing is to be overturned, the objecting party must present a strong case that the AJ erred.

If the presence of an agency official in the hearing room, e.g., the technical advisor, is considered intimidating by the appellant, the appellant must object the continuing presence of the agency official. Without an objection at the time of the hearing, the Board will not consider an objection in the PFR. See Flanagan v. Dept. of Army, 48 MSPR 430, 433 (1991). Stankis v. EPA, 12 MSPR 127, 130 (1982), concluded there was no prejudice when the agency used a supervisor as a technical advisor:

Appellant's supervisor who also had supervisory authority over many of the witnesses in the hearing, was allowed to remain in the hearing room as the agency's technical advisor. Appellant argued that his supervisor's presence would have a chilling effect on the testifying witnesses. In his petition for review, appellant argues that the presiding official was in error in her ruling to allow the supervisor to remain. Yet, appellant did not cite examples of any such alleged affected testimony. The presiding official questioned each witness out of the presence of the supervisor as to whether each felt that the supervisor's presence would inhibit the testimony. We find that this procedure was adequate and that there was no harm to appellant.

An AJ was not faulted for permitting supervisors to remain in the hearing room while subordinates testified. Appellant's counsel objected to the visitors' presence because it was feared that intimidation would result, but the Board decided there was no harm when they were not directed to leave, Gregory v. FCC, 84 MSPR 22, 30-31 ¶¶ 15-17 (1999):

The appellant asserts that the AJ improperly allowed her witnesses to be intimidated by the presence of agency personnel during the supplemental jurisdictional hearing. The agency acknowledges that the Deputy Associate General Counsel, a Labor Relations Specialist, and the Assistant Division Chief were seated at the back of the hearing room.... At the outset of the supplemental hearing, the appellant's attorney asked that "the visitors" in the room be excluded during the hearing "because they are, in essence, presenting a chilling effect on my witnesses." The AJ denied the request on the basis that Board hearings are open to the public, but she stated that if it became a 'problem,'" she would ask the spectators to leave. The appellant's attorney noted her exception to the AJ's ruling.

The appellant argues that Milne stated during her testimony that she felt intimidated by Dziedzic's presence, and asserts that Forster, "who was extremely nervous, was sweating profusely, and appeared on the point of fainting," indicated that he was uncomfortable testifying. The record reflects that Milne did state that she felt intimidated, because "he [Dziedzic] is going to give references for me in the future," but that when Forster was questioned as to whether he felt intimidated by being asked to give testimony, he replied that "[i]ntimidation may be a little strong of word."

AJs have wide discretion in controlling the proceedings, and to support a claim of witness intimidation, the appellant must show that the AJ's conducting of the proceedings denigrated her substantive rights. See Butler v. Department of the Air Force, 73 MSPR 313, 317-18 (1997). Further, in order for the Board to find that an agency official intimidated a witness, the appellant must present evidence showing that the official threatened the witness with adverse consequences, such as disciplinary action, or suggested that the witness either not testify or not testify truthfully. See Bernstein v. Department of the Army, 82 MSPR 375, 380-81 ¶¶ 10-13 (1999). The appellant here has made no such showing. Moreover, the agency official was not a witness in the case. Finally, we note that, after the appellant concluded her redirect of Milne, Dziedzic left the hearing room, apparently of his own volition. The agency then conducted a brief recross of Milne, at which point the AJ indicated that she would be excused. Although the appellant might have, she did not ask to be allowed to pose further questions of Milne, once Dziedzic was out of the hearing room. The appellant has not explained how Milne or any of the other witnesses would have testified differently in Dziedzic's absence or how that testimony would have affected the result reached in this case. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984). Thus, while under the particular circumstances of this case, it would have been preferable for the AJ to exclude Dziedzic from the hearing room, the appellant has not shown that she committed a prejudicial abuse of discretion in not doing so. See Butler, 73 MSPR at 317-18.

For there to be intimidation, there should be established adverse impact, Covington v. Dept. of Interior, 2023 MSPB 5, ¶ 48 (2023):

The appellant contends that she felt intimidated by the presence of a human resources employee at the hearing. Although the appellant testified that she felt intimidated at the hearing, she has not alleged or shown that other witnesses felt intimidated. In any event, for the Board to find that an agency official intimidated a witness, an appellant must present evidence showing that the official threatened the witness with adverse consequences, such as disciplinary action, or suggested that the witness not testify or not testify truthfully. Gregory v. Federal Communications Commission, 84 M.S.P.R. 22, ¶ 17 (1999), aff'd per curiam, 232 F.3d 912 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table). The appellant has made no such showing.
