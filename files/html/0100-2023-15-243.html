f. Pay and Allowances Claim

Removal was proper for an employee who falsely stated interim earnings to unjustly increase a back pay award he received. Harp v. Dept. of Army, 791 F.2d 161 (Fed. Cir. 1986). False or improper claims for overtime and per diem supported removal. Quinton v. Dept. of Transp., 808 F.2d 826 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Termination was justified for an employee who repeatedly submitted claims for emergency salary payments after he received and cashed his regular paychecks. Seibert v. Dept. of Treasury, 41 MSPR 133, 138 (1989); see Starks v. Dept. of Army, 94 MSPR 95, 99-100 ¶ 11 (2003) (appellant claimed pay under two different pay systems for the same hours of work); Childs v. USPS, 67 MSPR 348, 356 (1995) (removal of a Postal Service police officer for receiving military (leave) pay from the Postal Service for periods he was not on military duty); Goode v. DLA, 31 MSPR 446, 450-51 (1986) (charge of embezzlement arising out of the appellant's securing and failing to return duplicate paychecks; removal mitigated to suspension under the circumstances).

Included in the category of improper claims for pay are improper claims for allowances, e.g., living quarters allowances for overseas employees. See Lloyd v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 342 (2005) (removal of supervisor despite good past record, for receipt or retention of excessive living quarters allowance), aff'd, Lloyd v. Dept. of Army, 180 Fed. Appx. 911 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP); Shane v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 269, 272-73 (1994) (suspension of 30 days for filing false claims for temporary quarters subsistence expenses); Arenz v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 88, 94 (1991) (allowances: "[i]mplicit in the finding that an appellant falsified documents with the intent to deceive or mislead the agency is the finding that he intended to defraud the agency since these findings are interchangeable; and a showing of either, along with the showing that the appellant knowingly supplied the wrong information, will support a charge of falsification of government documents"); cf. Stanford v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0444-I-1 (NP 9/15/2016) (removal for charges that included an improper claim for hazardous duty pay); Randall v. DOD, CH-0752-13-1797-I-1 (NP 9/2/2015) (adverse action sustained when the appellant improperly obtained payment for Internet access costs incurred by a homeowner who was not reimbursed by appellant), aff'd, Randall v. DOD, 652 Fed. Appx. 929, 930-31 (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP) ("an employee who failed to apply a reimbursement to a charged expense could be found to misuse agency funds. See, e.g., Allen v. United States Postal Serv., 466 F.3d 1065, 1069-70 (Fed. Cir. 2006)"; "the Board premised its analysis on Mr. Randall's receipt of the Service's money for costs that his father paid for and that were never passed on to Mr. Randall").

Of a dispute over rent due to the government, Barrett v. Dept. of Air Force, 5 MSPR 43, 46, 5 MSPB 121 (1981), determined that the element of knowledge in a fraud case is established if a person ought to know, has a duty to know, or has the means to know the truth. But to find that an employee should have known, the duty must be clear; if the duty is unclear, then the employee cannot be removed on the basis that he fraudulently or negligently breached that duty. Removal was sustained when an employee submitted an incentive award for himself for $1,585 (5% of his salary) over the photocopied signature of a former supervisor who neither saw the award nor initiated the recommendation for the award. See Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 616, 621 (1994) (coupled with a charge of unbecoming conduct resulting from a dispute between the appellant and a labor relations specialist concerning the award documentation). Receipt and retention of an excessive travel advance was a basis for removal of a supervisor with considerable agency service. The amount received was approximately five times the amount that the appellant had requested. Garner v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 362, 364-65 ¶¶ 5-8 (2004), aff'd, Garner v. Dept. of Treasury, 127 Fed. Appx. 477 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP).

An agency could not discipline an employee who received travel and per diem during government travel ordered by the agency when, after the appellant arrived at his destinations, the agency canceled the business intended for the trips and the appellant used his time for personal purposes. Any other result would require employees to take leave any time they were on travel and scheduling changes occurred not within their control. Kopec v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 576, 582 (1994). A related case, Cruppi v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 617, 622-24 (1994), confirmed that an employee cannot be charged with false reports of time worked when a supervisor orders an employee not to work during the duty day. There was culpability, however, when the employee was improperly excused by a supervisor from duty for a portion of a paid workday and it should have been clear to the employee that the time off should not have been excused:

Both the charges and factual circumstances regarding Lambert's trip to Chicago, and Cruppi's activities on July 14, 1993, are very similar to those in Kopec v. Department of the Army, 63 MSPR 576, in which we found that the charges of knowingly claiming salary for hours not worked, and knowingly claiming reimbursement for travel that was improper, could not be sustained. Kopec, like the appellants, was employed in the Real Estate Division of the New Orleans District of the Army Corps of Engineers, and his first-level supervisor was T.J. Brown. Pursuant to arrangements made by Brown, Kopec travelled from New Orleans to Washington, D.C. on June 30, 1993, to perform official business and, the following day, attend a retirement luncheon. After Kopec's arrival, Brown postponed the meeting until the following day. Having no duties to perform for the remainder of his regular workday, Kopec departed for Baltimore where he attended a baseball game in the evening. The following day he attended the retirement luncheon and returned to New Orleans.

We found that Kopec would have worked an eight-hour workday on June 30 if he could have done so, but that he was prevented from doing so by Brown. In the absence of fault or blame on Kopec's part, we found that any failure to perform work during the two-and-one-half hour period in question was unavoidable. Under the circumstances, we concluded that a charge of knowingly claiming salary to which one was not entitled was tantamount to a charge that Kopec was absent without leave for the period in which his supervisor prevented him from carrying out his official duties. Further, to the extent that the agency's charge could be construed as an allegation that Kopec falsified his time sheet, we found that the agency failed to prove that Kopec intended to defraud the government.

In the instant case, the evidence establishes that Lambert would have performed official duties during the three days she was in Chicago, but was prevented from doing so by her supervisor, T.J. Brown. The record similarly establishes that Cruppi would have performed official duties for a full eight hours on July 14, 1993, but was prevented by Brown from doing so. The agency's charges and specifications of misconduct relating to these matters can therefore not be sustained.

We disagree with the administrative judge's conclusion that Brown could properly excuse the appellants' absence from work on August 6, 1993. The agency manual does provide, as cited by the administrative judge, that managers are responsible for determining the situations in which they will excuse employees from duty with no charge to leave or loss of pay, and that such absences should be excused if the reasons are justifiable to the approving authority. The manual further provides, however, that "[s]uch excused absences will be administered on an impartial basis and will be uniformly applicable to all employees," and lists the following as "common situations in which absences should be excused: to vote or register in an election; to participate in certain capacities in a funeral ceremony for a member of the Armed Forces; and to make blood donations for which the employee is not paid."

As to the impartiality and uniformity criteria, the record does not indicate that Brown offered excused leave to other employees in the Real Estate Division to attend the wedding on August 6. More importantly, being absent from work to attend the wedding of a former co-worker is not even remotely similar to any of the situations in which approval of excused leave is indicated. Attending a wedding is of such an obviously personal nature, devoid of the public service or civic duty implications evident in the approved examples, that it should have been apparent both to Brown and to the appellants that it would be improper for Brown to excuse the appellants' absence for this purpose, and that they should have instead taken annual leave. We therefore find that the agency established that the appellants knowingly claimed and accepted salary for hours they did not work on August 6, 1993.
