b. Penalty Determination

SSA v. Glover, 23 MSPR 57, 78-79 (1984), explained the Board's approach to penalties in complaints against ALJs:

The Board's authority over the penalty in an original jurisdiction disciplinary action is distinct from that in an ordinary appeal case. When a matter is before the Board on appeal, the agency has already imposed the penalty. Before imposing the penalty, the agency is statutorily required to provide the employee an opportunity to respond and the agency must consider the employee's response to the agency's notice of the charges. 5 USC § 7513. If the agency has considered factors that would affect the choice of penalty, the Board will not substitute its judgment on the penalty provided the agency's penalty is within tolerable limits of reasonableness. Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313 (1981).

In contrast, in a disciplinary action against an administrative law judge, no penalty may be imposed except with Board authority and only after an opportunity for a full hearing on the record. 5 USC § 7521; 5 CFR § 1201.132. Unlike an appeal case, and contrary to the agency's argument on exceptions, the agency's "selection" of removal as the penalty is not due a high degree of deference. We reject the agency's contention that its choice of penalty should not be disturbed except for compelling reasons....

The Board is the first entity to entertain the question of penalty after the parties have had an opportunity to raise any matter they consider pertinent to the choice of penalty. Using Douglas v. Veterans Administration, supra, as a guide, the Board will consider whatever evidence of record affects the choice of penalty."...

See VA v. Chiappetta, CB-7521-16-0014-N-1 (NP 12/2/2022) (various Douglas factors reviewed in decision affirming removal of employee in ALJ-equivalent position).

SSA v. Steverson, 111 MSPR 649, 658 ¶ 18, 2009 MSPB 143 (2009) (aff'd, Steverson v. SSA, 383 Fed. Appx. 939 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)), addressed application of Douglas factors to ALJ discipline:

In an original jurisdiction case, where the Board is the first entity to consider the evidence of the charged conduct as well as any mitigating factors, the choice of the penalty is for the Board. The Board has adopted for guidance in assessing a penalty in an original jurisdiction case the same standards required of an agency in an appellate case. Thus, the Board will examine the record in this case with a view to balancing the relevant factors suggested in Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305-06. Social Security Administration v. Brennan, 27 M.S.P.R. 242, 251 (1985), aff'd, 787 F.2d 1559 (Fed. Cir. 1986); see also Social Security Administration v. Davis, 19 M.S.P.R. 279, 282 n.5 (stating that, although Douglas deals with penalty selection and mitigation in traditional adverse action cases that are governed by the efficiency of the service standard, the considerations it details for selecting a penalty are equally appropriate to cases under 5 U.S.C. § 7521 where it is the Board, rather than the employing agency, that selects the appropriate penalty), aff'd, 758 F.2d 661 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (Table). The Board will consider whatever evidence of record affects the choice of penalty. Social Security Administration v. Glover, 23 M.S.P.R. 57, 79 (1984). In evaluating whether a penalty is reasonable, however, the Board will consider, first and foremost, the nature and seriousness of the misconduct and its relation to the employee's duties, position, and responsibilities. E.g., Jackson v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 6 (2005).

SSA v. White, CB-7521-11-0004-T-1 (NP 4/22/2013), provided Board analysis in an ALJ disciplinary case:

In an original jurisdiction case, where the Board is the first entity to consider the evidence of the charged conduct as well as any mitigating factors, the choice of the penalty is for the Board. Social Security Administration v. Steverson, 111 M.S.P.R. 649, ¶ 18 (2009), aff'd, 383 F. App'x 939 (Fed. Cir. 2010). The Board does not give deference to the agency's selection of a penalty, as it does in reviewing actions taken under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(a). Long, 113 M.S.P.R. 190, ¶ 47. However, assessing a penalty in an original jurisdiction case, the Board has consulted the same factors applicable in an appellate case, as articulated in Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305-06 (1981). See Long, 113 M.S.P.R. 190, ¶ 47; Social Security Administration v. Davis, 19 M.S.P.R. 279, 282 n.5, aff'd, 758 F.2d 661 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (Table).

In applying the Douglas factors, the Board considers first and foremost the seriousness of the misconduct and its relationship to the employee's position and duties. Steverson, 111 M.S.P.R. 649, ¶ 18. We agree with the ALJ that the respondent engaged in serious misconduct by opening and examining a private file, even if, as the respondent maintains, she did so absentmindedly. The respondent also engaged in serious misconduct by yelling and being argumentative in the office of Senator Landrieu, who, as Lillios testified, "had the means and mechanisms by which to cause the agency very serious issues...had she chosen to do so." The respondent's failure to follow Flexiplace procedures was likewise serious misconduct. See Abrams v. Social Security Administration, 703 F.3d 538, 543 (Fed. Cir. 2012) (upholding Board's determination that failure to follow instructions is sufficient "good cause" for removal under 5 U.S.C. § 7521).

Also to be considered under Douglas is the respondent's job level and type of employment, contacts with the public, and the prominence of her position. Long, 113 M.S.P.R. 190, ¶ 50; Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305. As the Board has consistently recognized, ALJs "occup[y] a high and prominent federal office." Social Security Administration v. Manion, 19 M.S.P.R. 298, 319 (1984). Accordingly, an ALJ is expected to behave in a fitting manner, in accordance with the ABA Model Code of Judicial Conduct, which provides that a judge should act at all times so as to promote public confidence in the judiciary. Long, 113 M.S.P.R. 190, ¶ 50. In this regard, it is especially significant that the respondent identified herself as an ALJ in Senator Landrieu's office, thus potentially bringing the agency's ALJs into disrepute.

The ALJ also considered pertinent mitigating factors in this case, including the respondent's 21 years of service, her clean disciplinary record, and the lack of any complaints concerning her performance. However, we discern no error in the ALJ's finding that these factors do not warrant a lesser penalty. The ALJ also considered the respondent's argument that, under the applicable collective bargaining agreement (CBA), loss of Flexiplace privileges is the only sanction available for violation of Flexiplace procedures. Article 15, Section 6 of the CBA in fact provides that an ALJ "will not be removed from the Flexiplace program as an act of discipline, adverse action, or reprisal for protected activities." Thus, the ALJ correctly found that a 45-day suspension is consistent with the terms of the CBA.

The respondent cites a number of cases in which, she contends, the Board imposed lesser penalties for more egregious conduct. Each of these cases is readily distinguishable. In Raco v. Social Security Administration, 117 M.S.P.R. 1 (2011), the Board found a 14-day suspension an appropriate penalty for a charge of conduct unbecoming based on 22 discrepancies between the appellant's recorded departure time and actual departure time. In mitigating the penalty, however, the Board relied on the fact that 19 of the 23 discrepancies were de minimis, involving variances of less than 5 minutes. Id., ¶¶ 7, 14. The appellant in Raco was also not charged with any misconduct resembling the conduct described in Charge I and also did not hold an ALJ position--which, as discussed above, is a significant aggravating factor in this case. The respondent also cites Social Security Administration v. McQuary, MSPB Docket No. CB-7521-11-0003-T-1, Nonprecedential Order (July 17, 2012), a nonprecedential case in which the Board imposed a 5-day suspension for a charge of conduct unbecoming an ALJ, based on an incident in which the respondent interrupted a hearing and yelled at a colleague. However, the respondent in that case suffered from post-traumatic stress disorder, a significant mitigating factor not present here, and was not charged with failure to follow instructions. The respondent also cites Social Security Administration v. Carter, 35 M.S.P.R. 485, 495 (1987), in which the Board found that a 10-day suspension was warranted for an incident of unwelcome and unsolicited touching of an employee of the opposite sex, which was found to constitute conduct unbecoming an ALJ, but not sexual harassment.[7] Again, as with McQuary, the respondent in Carter was not charged with failure to follow instructions. Accordingly, none of the cited cases provides a basis for disturbing the ALJ's thorough and well-reasoned Douglas factors analysis.
