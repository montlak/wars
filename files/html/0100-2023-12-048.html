a. Evidentiary Disputes

Under 5 CFR 1201.115 (2022), evidentiary issues provide a basis for a PFR:

The Board normally will consider only issues raised in a timely filed petition or cross petition for review. Situations in which the Board may grant a petition or cross petition for review include, but are not limited to, a showing that:

(a) The initial decision contains erroneous findings of material fact.

(1) Any alleged factual error must be material, meaning of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from that of the initial decision.

(2) A petitioner who alleges that the judge made erroneous findings of material fact must explain why the challenged factual determination is incorrect and identify specific evidence in the record that demonstrates the error. In reviewing a claim of an erroneous finding of fact, the Board will give deference to an administrative judge's credibility determinations when they are based, explicitly or implicitly, on the observation of the demeanor of witnesses testifying at a hearing.

Before the 2012 regulatory revisions, it was not explicitly stated that a PFR could be filed for factually inadequate analysis by the AJ. Taken literally, the PFR process was limited to appeals involving new evidence (and there are but few of those) and appeals involving errors of law. That approach suggested the anomaly of the Federal Circuit reviewing appeals for substantial evidence and the Board ignoring petitions challenging decisions for lack of supporting evidence. Most appeals decided by AJs involve no striking legal issues. They involve either factual disputes or the application of law to facts. Despite the restrictiveness of the prior PFR regulation, the Board can and did review through PFRs factual disputes and the application of law to facts. If that species of PFRs were eliminated, there would have been little grist for the mill of the Office of Appeals Counsel--the lawyers at MSPB headquarters who review PFRs and write draft decisions.

Billingsley v. USPS, 4 MSPR 368, 370, 4 MSPB 428 (1980), explained the justification for granting a PFR advanced on evidentiary issues under the then-controlling regulation:

Since a presiding official acts as the Board's agent and has no independent statutory jurisdiction, 5 USC 7701, "this Board is free to substitute its own determinations of fact for those of the presiding official, giving the (initial) findings (such) weight as may be warranted by the record and...reasoning" of the presiding official. Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 MSPB 297, 298, 2 MSPR 129, 132 (1980). The Board, however, respects the opportunity given the presiding official to observe the demeanor of those who testify. Weaver, Id.

In this case, the petition for review specifically identifies evidence not considered by the presiding official, as well as misinterpretation of evidence presented. The evidentiary question is sufficient to justify review of the initial decision to ascertain if the initial decision is based on an erroneous interpretation of the statutory requirements governing the weight of the evidence....

See Gores v. VA, 68 MSPR 100, 112 (1995) (error when the AJ did not consider all of the evidence of record supporting the agency's adverse action decision: "Thus, the initial decision deviates from the statutory requirement that decisions of an agency based on chapter 75 of title 5, such as the agency's decision here, shall be sustained if the decision is supported by a preponderance of the evidence. See 5 USC § 7701(c)(1)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.56(a)(1)(ii) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1)(ii)]; Young v. Dept. of Navy, 53 MSPR 384, 386-87 (1992).").

Although the prior PFR regulation might not allow the Board discretion to set aside an initial decision based on erroneous application of law to facts, the inherent administrative responsibility of the Board under 5 CFR 1201.117 (2022) provides that authority. See Dunning v. NASA, 718 F.2d 1170 (D.C. Cir. 1983). Connolly v. DOJ, 766 F.2d 507, 510-11 (Fed. Cir. 1985), harmonized the Board's review authority under its regulations:

...The presiding official's initial decision becomes the decision of the board if review is denied, or if the board does not open the case sua sponte. The regulation simply spells out two circumstances under which review may be granted. But, the board's authority is plenary as to those actions made appealable to it by law. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The presiding official's initial decision is a part of the record the board may review, but it may make its own record. The regulations contain other language which provides for reopening of the case by the board, oral arguments, and the filing of briefs. The board may "[t]ake any other action necessary for the final disposition of the case," 5 CFR 1201.116(a)(4), or it "may affirm, reverse, remand, modify or vacate the decision of the presiding official, in whole or in part. Where appropriate, the Board shall issue a final decision and order compliance."...It is further provided in section 1201.117:

The Board may reopen and reconsider a decision of a presiding official on its own motion at any time, notwithstanding any other provisions of this part. Cf. 5 USC 7701(e)(1)(B).

...

...It would be anomalous to assume that pursuant to 5 USC 7701(j) the board would prescribe one regulation limiting its authority to grant a petition for review, only to prescribe another regulation providing itself with absolute review authority. A regulation which by its own definition is permissive, not precatory, cannot be a mandatory restriction. See generally Dawson v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 712 F.2d 264, 266-67 (7th Cir. 1983) (discussing the board's authority to reopen a case). Accordingly, we hold that the board may grant a petition for review on grounds not falling within the ambit of section 1201.115(a) or (b). Such a holding is consonant with the broad policy considerations discussed in Bookman v. United States, 453 F.2d 1263, 1265 (Ct. Cl. 1972).

Many PFRs challenge AJs' initial decisions based on errors of interpretation of statute or decisional precedent. The results of those challenges appear through this Guide as the Board's precedential and nonprecedential final decisions. Other challenges are to findings of fact and credibility findings of AJs, and those are discussed later in this chapter, along with PFRs bringing to the attention of the Board new evidence or new law, i.e., legal developments after the issuance of the initial decision. [Refer in this chapter to "Review of Factual and Credibility Findings," "Review of New Evidence," and "Review of New Law."]
