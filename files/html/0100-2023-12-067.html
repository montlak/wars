b. Due Diligence

There are practical limitations on the reopening process. A factor considered in an appellant's reopening request is his attention to his appeal: the Board will reopen an appeal only if the appellant exercised due diligence in seeking reopening. Langford v. Dept. of Treasury, 73 MSPR 129, 136-37 (1997); see Riley v. Dept. of Army, 91 MSPR 336, 340 ¶ 9 (2002) (appellant must exercise due diligence in seeking reopening). "[T]he same factors are generally relevant to the determination on reopening as are considered in the determination of whether good cause exists." Brakas v. USPS, 90 MSPR 332, 336 ¶ 12 (2001). In Anthony v. OPM, 70 MSPR 214, 218-19 (1996), the Board considered both the delay and probable mootness of the appellant's reopening request to be sufficient reasons to decline to reopen the case.

Several years was too long a time to elapse for the Board to reopen the AJ's factual findings. See Lynch v. Bennett, 665 F. Supp. 62 (D.D.C. 1987) [discussed in Chapter 12, "Prima Facie Case; Accommodation Requirements"]. Gratehouse v. United States, 512 F.2d 1104, 1109 (Ct. Cl. 1975), added:

...We have many times held that an agency has the inherent power to reconsider and change a decision if it does so within a reasonable period of time....

What is a short and reasonable time period will vary with each case, but absent unusual circumstances, the time period would be measured in weeks, not years. A correction of an error within a reasonable time period will have retroactive effect, or, in other words, will preclude any cause of action based on the original error.

Where reasonable time for reconsideration has expired, there is no longer an opportunity to correct the procedural error retroactively....

A delay of two years, among other factors, led the Board to decline reopening, Jennings v. SSA, 123 MSPR 577, 584-85 ¶¶ 17-19, 2016 MSPB 31 (2016):

In deciding whether to reopen a closed appeal, the Board will balance the desirability of finality against the public interest in reaching the correct result. Carson v. Department of Energy, 109 M.S.P.R. 213, ¶ 37 (2008), aff'd per curiam, 357 Fed. Appx. 293 (Fed. Cir. 2009). Thus, the Board will exercise its discretion to reopen an appeal only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances, such as an intervening event that directly bears on the result or the discovery of misrepresentation or fraud after the issuance of the initial decision, and generally within a short period of time after the decision becomes final. Id.; 5 C.F.R. § 1201.118. Such a short period of time is usually measured in weeks, not years. Murray v. National Aeronautics & Space Administration, 112 M.S.P.R. 680, ¶ 5 n.1 (2009), aff'd per curiam, 387 Fed. Appx. 955 (Fed. Cir. 2010).

Here, the Board's decision in Jennings I became final on January 6, 2009. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.113(b) (providing that an initial decision becomes final when the Board issues its last decision denying a petition for review). As set forth above, the appellant filed this USERRA appeal on February 3, 2011. We find that the appellant's request to reopen Jennings I is far beyond the short period of time during which the Board will consider reopening. In addition, the administrative law judge's decision in this case does not constitute unusual or extraordinary circumstances that warrant reopening Jennings I. There has been no discovery of misrepresentation or fraud after the issuance of that initial decision. Moreover, the administrative law judge's findings regarding the appellant's retroactive placement on LWOP and the calculation of the appellant's debt to the agency, as well as any evidence supporting those determinations, have no bearing on the decision in Jennings I that the agency proved its charges by preponderant evidence and that there was good cause to remove the appellant from his position.

The Board also has held that reopening may be appropriate when there is a clear and material legal error generally confined to a conflict between the holding of the decision and a controlling precedent or statute, either because of an oversight or a change in the controlling law between the date of the original decision and any reopening request. Hayes v. Department of the Army, 106 M.S.P.R. 132, ¶ 6 (2007); Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 7 M.S.P.R. 357, 360 (1981). As set forth below, we find that the appellant has not shown a clear and material legal error in Jennings I that warrants reopening.

See Tarr v. VA, 115 MSPR 216, 222 n.2, 2010 MSPB 223 (2010) (reopening request was timely when it was made less than two weeks after the appellant learned from an AJ that he needed to address his concerns to the Board about an earlier initial decision following the appellant's withdrawal of an appeal when he was incorrectly informed about his right to a hearing concerning the basis for his probationary termination); Dean v. USPS, 101 MSPR 356, 361 ¶ 13, 2006 MSPB 55 (2006) ("The Board's authority to reopen a case is limited by the requirement that such authority be exercised within a reasonably short period of time. See Mitchell v. Department of Commerce, 100 M.S.P.R. 415, ¶ 9 (2005); Bagunas v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 328, ¶ 5 (2005). That period of time is usually measured in weeks not years."); cf. Crumpler v. Dept. of Defense, 113 MSPR 94, 98 n.*, 2009 MSPB 233 (2009) (considering that the petition to reopen was filed within the period allowed for initiating a judicial petition for review with the Federal Circuit, i.e., 60 days from the final date of the Board decision, "supports our finding that the desirability of finality is minimal in this case compared with the public interest in achieving the right result). But see Doe v. PBGC, DC-0752-09-0881-C-1 (NP 11/19/2013) (complex back pay issues and possible inconsistent application of past Board decisions: "Although more than a year has elapsed since the date of the Board's final decision in Doe, we nevertheless find that reopening and reconsidering Doe is warranted under the circumstances. Those circumstances include the fact that the appellant acted promptly in requesting back pay in her April 26, 2012 petition for enforcement of the Board's March 27, 2012 decision in Doe, and the fact that Board precedent supports granting back pay under the circumstances present in this case.").

Several months was too great a delay to permit reopening an appeal based on alleged fraud in the procurement of a settlement agreement, Myers v. VA, DA-0752-10-0340-I-1 (NP 6/22/2011):

[T]he Board will exercise its discretion to reopen an appeal only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances, such as an intervening event or the discovery of a misrepresentation or fraud. Mitchell v. Department of Commerce, 100 M.S.P.R. 415, ¶ 9 (2005), review dismissed, 175 F. App'x 340 (Fed. Cir. 2006). The Board also will only reopen a case if the appellant has exercised due diligence in seeking reopening. See McCoy, 112 M.S.P.R. 256, ¶ 11; see also Keys v. Office of Personnel Management, 113 M.S.P.R. 173, ¶ 8 (2010) (a request to reopen must be presented in a timely manner, generally measured in weeks).

The appellant argues that her delay must be excused or her appeal reopened because the settlement agreement was procured by fraud. The appellant further asserts that she did not learn of the agency's alleged fraud until the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) dismissed her appeal based upon the settlement agreement that terminated her Board appeal.

The appellant is correct that the discovery of fraud that renders a settlement invalid can provide both good cause for the untimely filing of a petition for review and a basis for reopening an appeal. See Linares-Rosado v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 599, ¶¶ 7, 11 (2009). But even if we accepted the appellant's explanation in its entirety, she still would have failed to show due diligence, a requirement for both excusing an untimely petition for review and reopening an appeal.

The appellant acknowledges that the EEOC issued its dismissal order on August 19, 2010, and that she received it on August 24, 2010. The appellant also states that she hired counsel to assist her with challenging the settlement on September 14, 2010. Yet she did not file her request to reopen (which the Board also construed as a petition for review) with the Board until December 1, 2010,--more than 3 months after she learned of the potential fraud and more than 2 1/2 months after she engaged counsel. Further, the appellant and her counsel fail to explain this delay, with her counsel merely opining that the delay did not prejudice the agency. See also Mauldin v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 513, ¶ 13 (2011) (the appellant is responsible for her representative's errors and such errors do not provide good cause to justify a delay).

The appellant's delay of over 3 months after learning of the potential fraud forecloses a finding that she exercised due diligence. See Walker v. Department of the Air Force, 109 M.S.P.R. 261, ¶ 8 (2008) (the appellant failed to show due diligence by delaying 30 days in contacting the Board after he learned the initial decision had been issued and the time to file a petition for review had passed). This lack of due diligence also forecloses the reopening of her appeal. See McCoy, 112 M.S.P.R. 256, ¶ 11 (the appellant's 6-month delay demonstrates that he was not duly diligent and his request to reopen must be denied); cf. Hazelton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 357, ¶ 5 (2009) (the appellant demonstrated due diligence by filing his petition within approximately a week of learning of the potential fraud). The Board will not reopen a case simply to cure the untimeliness of a petition for review. See Keys, 113 M.S.P.R. 173, ¶ 8.

If a party is to seek reopening for a legitimate reason, for example, because it failed to receive a copy of a Board order directing that it provide a submission and first learned of the Board's action when it received the Board's decision, the motion to reopen must be prompt, within a short and reasonable time period. Jordan v. USPS, 49 MSPR 544, 547 (1991), determined that four months--the period between the agency's receipt of the Board's decision and the agency's request to reopen--too long. See McFalls v. OPM, 72 MSPR 252, 259 (1996) (one year too long); Sobol v. USPS, 68 MSPR 611, 614 (1995) (11 months too long); Miller v. USPS, 82 MSPR 660, 664-65 ¶¶ 10, 12 (1999) (petition to reopen an appeal was timely when filed within a few weeks of an initial decision dismissing the appeal as withdrawn; the appellant contended that his representative lacked authority to withdraw the appeal). But a lapse of nearly two years between the issuance of a final decision and a request for reopening was excused based on a "rather unique set of circumstances" involving legislation that retroactively granted FAA employees appeal rights to the Board after earlier legislation removed IRA appeals from the Board's jurisdiction. Tarrant v. Dept. of Transp., 87 MSPR 308, 310-11 ¶ 5 (2000); Collins v. Dept. of Transp., 89 MSPR 582, 586-87 ¶ 9 (2001) (analysis similar to Tarrant); Perez v. Dept. Transp., 87 MSPR 503, 505-06 ¶¶ 4-5 (2001) (noting pendency of litigation concerning Board jurisdiction over FAA appeals and excusing delayed PFR).

In Marshall v. GPO, 43 MSPR 346, 349-51 (1990), the Board stressed the limits on its authority to reopen a case. The litigation involved a 1981 removal, a Board final decision in 1986 on the merits, and an early 1989 compliance decision:

It is axiomatic that the Board may reopen or reconsider a case on its own motion to correct its own errors or to modify its judgment, decree, or order.[3] However, because the policy of achieving the right result competes with the policy favoring finality of decisions, the Board and the courts have consistently held that any reopening and reconsideration must be obtained within a short and reasonable time period....

[3] We note that, in some circumstances, the Director of the Office of Personnel Management may petition the Board for reconsideration. 5 USC § 7703(c). Although a party has no right to seek reopening or reconsideration of a final decision, one can bring to the Board's attention, within a reasonable time, an argument or evidence that might cause the Board to reopen and reconsider a case....

Although the definition of a "short and reasonable time" necessarily varies with the circumstances of each case, "absent unusual circumstances, the time period would be measured in weeks, not years."...

The parties must be especially cognizant of the "reasonable time" rule, because once this period has passed, the parties lose their opportunity to correct previous errors and the decision stands as final....

...In all the many proceedings before the Board during the long history of this case, the agency has never, until now, seriously challenged the validity of the Board's 1986 final order.... We find that the agency's most recent argument regarding the Rehabilitation Act's applicability to GPO's employees could have been argued during the pendency of Marshall's appeal on the merits. The agency's attempt to reopen this case on the merits based on Judd v. Billington, [863 F.2d 103 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (finding the Rehabilitation Act inapplicable to Library of Congress employees)], does not present unusual circumstances that would circumvent the rule that to reopen a case, it must be done within a short and reasonable time....

See Brewer v. OPM, 69 MSPR 609, 613 (1996), reconsideration denied, 75 MSPR 163, 166 (1997) (although OPM arguably presented new evidence suggesting that an earlier Board compliance decision was incorrectly decided, the Board declined to reopen the appeal because "more than a year has passed since the Board issued this decision, and seven months had passed before OPM submitted the documents in question to the Board," and because OPM did not establish that unusual circumstances prevented it from bringing the documents to the Board's attention at the time of the earlier compliance decision).

An appeal from the denial of restoration rights to a member of the National Guard was decided adversely to the appellant in 1990. The decision was that the appellant was not within the Board's jurisdiction because he was not an employee of an agency of the executive branch. In 1991, in another National Guardsman's appeal, the Board reversed its position on the jurisdictional issue. In 1992, the appellant from the 1990 case sought reopening by the Board based on its 1991 change in position. Moriarty v. Rhode Island Air Nat'l Guard, 56 MSPR 144, 147-50 (1992), denied the petition to reopen, concluding that the balance of public interest in correct adjudication and the interest in final adjudication weighed against the appellant:

The Board has statutory and regulatory authority to reopen, on its own motion, appeals in which it has rendered a final decision, 5 USC § 7701(e)(1)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.117, and it has exercised this authority when it has found it proper to do so. See Stephens, 30 MSPR 680; Woodall v. FERC, 28 MSPR 192, 194-95 (1985). Nonetheless, even where jurisdiction to reopen is present, "its exercise has uniformly been held to be discretionary, and required only in extraordinary circumstances." Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 7 MSPR 357, 359 (1981). A determination of whether to reopen an appeal involves "balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result." Woodall, 28 MSPR at 195.

"Clear and material legal error" based on a change in the controlling law is accepted as a standard for granting reopening, see Sullivan, 7 MSPR at 360, but it is not the sole consideration to be taken into account in determining whether to reconsider a closed case. A significant limitation on an agency's power to reconsider is that such authority must be exercised within a reasonable period of time. See, e.g., Dawson v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 712 F.2d 264, 267 (7th Cir. 1983); Anderson v. Department of Transportation, 46 MSPR 341, 348 (1990), aff'd, 949 F.2d 404 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table); Woodall, 28 MSPR at 194. Indeed, in the first decision to hold that the Board's predecessor agency, the Civil Service Commission, had the authority to reconsider its decisions, the Court of Claims ruled that the administrative action reopening a case must be conducted within "a short and reasonable time." Bookman v. United States, 453 F.2d 1263, 1265 (Ct. Cl. 1972). The determination of a reasonable period of time varies with the circumstances of the case, but the Board has held that absent unusual circumstances, the time is generally measured in weeks rather than years. See Anderson, 46 MSPR at 349.

In this case, the Board's final decision was issued on September 7, 1990 and the motion for reopening was not filed until March 9, 1992, after the passage of 18 months. Although the change in controlling precedent might otherwise provide a basis for reopening the appeal, we hold that the passage of that amount of time requires a finding that the interest in finality outweighs the interest in correcting the error in our earlier decision.

As stated above, the Board has exercised its authority to reopen certain cases. Those cases are distinguishable, however. Unlike Stephens, 30 MSPR 680, which also involved a change in the controlling law, the Board reopened its decision on attorney fees, which did not require reopening the appeal on the merits. See Marshall v. Government Printing Office, 43 MSPR 346, 351 (1990). And, unlike Woodall, 28 MSPR 192, where the change in the law had occurred while the appeal was pending, the request to reopen the appeal was filed within a month of the issuance of the final decision that failed to take notice of it. 28 MSPR at 194 & n.4.

Rather, we find the instant case to be more like the situation in Marshall, 43 MSPR 346. There, subsequent to the issuance of a final decision reversing the agency's action for failure to comply with the requirements of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, the circuit court sitting in the jurisdiction in which the appellant lived and worked, held that the agency was not subject to the Act. When it sought reconsideration from the Board of its decision, it was denied. The Board found that "considerations of finality dictate that this case not be reopened 3 years after the decision on the merits." 43 MSPR at 351. While 3 years is a longer period than is at issue here, in so holding, the Board relied, inter alia, on Gubisch v. Brady, Civil Action No. 88-2031, slip op. at 23, 49 Fair Empl. Prac. Cas. (BNA) 1063, 1989 WL 44083 (D.D.C. April 20, 1989). There, it noted, the court found that the decision of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to reopen an appeal 11 months after the agency's motion to reopen and 16 months after the decision had become final could not be sustained because the period at issue was unreasonable.

...

In its decision in Bookman, 453 F.2d at 1265, the court held that:

[t]he importance of the right of reconsideration is dependent upon the importance of the challenged decision. That is to say, the public's interest in a "right result" is consonant with the expanding application of the decision either in terms of the number of individuals directly or presently affected, or in future precedent value.

Here, we find the public interest factor does not weigh heavily. First, we note that the Federal Circuit has characterized "the public interest in the effectiveness of the Civil Service Reform Act [of 1978]" as "comparatively remote." See McCall v. US Postal Service, 839 F.2d 664, 667 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Second, because the decision finding jurisdiction has been applied consistently since the issuance of the reconsidered Kostan decision, the interest in a "right result" has been vindicated since that time. In this regard, we note that the appellant's request, although based on an assertion that he is a federal employee, is grounded in his reading of a state unemployment compensation decision and proceedings, which are not binding on the Board, rather than on Kostan, which is not mentioned in his request. We note further that the appellant's main concern in pursuing this matter, being restored to his position, would not be accomplished directly by a decision to reopen his appeal, inasmuch as the new law addresses only a preliminary issue. The Board has never addressed the merits of his claim to restoration and expresses no opinion on it now. Finally, the Kostan decision was issued less than two years after the interpretation in the Gordon decision that it overturned, so that there is only a relatively limited universe of cases which it could have affected.

For these reasons, we conclude that the relatively limited interest in reopening to reconsider the jurisdictional issue is outweighed by the interest in finality. Accordingly, the request to reopen is denied and the decision of the Board at 46 MSPR 38 remains final....

See Fischer v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 546, 549 ¶ 5 (2004) (reopening request was properly made within month a after the Board's most recent decision); Ramos v. OPM, 71 MSPR 39, 41 (1996) (Board authority to reopen is limited by the requirement that the authority be exercised within a short and reasonable time period, measured in weeks rather than years; for substantial delays (in appellant's case, six years), "the limited public interest" in reopening the appeal is outweighed by the interest in finality); Lauer v. Dept. of Transp., 65 MSPR 224, 226 (1994) (same result).

Burciaga v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 460, 462-63 ¶¶ 4-9 (1999), involved a request for reopening by the appellant in a RIF appeal. After he lost his appeal, the appellant received a service-connected disability rating from the VA that could have altered his RIF retention standing had he received the rating prior to the RIF. The Board declined to reopen the case, then approximately two years old, because of the need for finality in the RIF process:

The Board may reopen or reconsider a case on its own motion to correct its own errors or to modify its judgment, decree, or order. See Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 503, 506 (1996). The Board's exercise of its authority to reopen an appeal has uniformly been held to be discretionary, and required only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances. See Anthony v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 MSPR 214, 219 (1996).

The appellant contends that the DVA's award of a 50% service-connected disability rating changes his standing on the agency's retention register, i.e., his subgroup would change from A to AD (preference eligible employees who have a compensable service-connected disability of 30% or more) and, therefore, his tenure group would become I-AD. See 5 CFR § 351.501. He argues that this change in his tenure group would have resulted in his retention by the agency at the time that the 1995 RIF was conducted.

A determination of whether to reopen involves balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result. See Payne, 69 MSPR at 506. Balancing the various interests at issue here, we find that reopening is not appropriate. First, as found by the Board, the agency properly separated the appellant at the time of the RIF. Secondly, the appellant filed his disability rating application with the DVA in May 1995, approximately five months before the RIF became effective. However, the disability claim was based upon the appellant's military service that occurred in 1969-1970. There is no indication in the evidence submitted as to why the appellant did not pursue the claim at some earlier point during the intervening 25 years. Thirdly, it would be exceedingly onerous to the agency to require it to reconstruct its 1995 RIF based upon the retroactive effective date of the appellant's disability rating that was imposed by the DVA. Cf. Stephens v. Office of Personnel Management, 33 MSPR 292, 296-99 (1987) (the Board decided to reopen several appeals and apply a new rule of law retroactively, in part, because the burden on the affected agency would not be great). Finally, if it resulted in the appellant's reemployment, the repercussions of such a reconstruction on other employees could be significant.

Section 351.506, 5 CFR, which sets forth the criteria for the effective date of retention standings, does not require a contrary result. Section 351.506(c), 5 CFR, states, "When an agency discovers an error in the determination of an employee's retention standing, it shall correct the error and adjust any erroneous reduction-in-force action to accord with the employee's proper retention standing as of the effective date established by this section." However, nothing in our review of the regulatory history regarding section 351.506 convinces us that the appellant's RIF rights should be enhanced by events that occur subsequent to the RIF. Our interpretation of this provision is that the agency is required to correct any error it makes regarding an employee's retention standing at the time that such a determination is made or soon after. The agency did not make an error in the present case. It not only acted on the basis of the information it had, but such information was correct at the time. That the appellant's status was subsequently changed by another entity does not make the agency's action erroneous. See generally Menoken v. Department of Health & Human Services, 784 F.2d 365, 368-69 (Fed. Cir.) (in determining retention rights of former employees, the Board necessarily has to look at situation as it actually existed in agency when RIF took place, not to situation that might or should have existed on such date; RIFs deal with actual and not theoretical or possible situations), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 883, 107 S. Ct. 273, 93 L. Ed.2d 249 (1986).

Further, this case is not similar to those cases in which we have reopened an appeal based upon the reversal of an appellant's criminal conviction. See generally Czubinski v. Department of the Treasury, 76 MSPR 552, 556 (1997); Payne, 69 MSPR at 508. In those cases, the dispositive evidence, i.e., the reversal of the appellant's conviction, did not exist at an earlier date. Unlike the appellants' situations in Payne and Czubinski, the disabling condition of the appellant in the present case did not change after he was separated; rather, because of the delay in applying, the disabling condition was not acknowledged by the DVA until after the RIF.

Thus, because of the appellant's failure to pursue his claim earlier and the onerous effect this very late reopening would place on the agency and other employees, we decline to reopen this appeal. While we acknowledge that a retroactive change in tenure could have significant consequences for the appellant, we find that the totality of the circumstances weighs on the side of finality.

The Board permitted reopening after four months passed from the decision that the appellant sought to reopen in Golden v. USPS, 60 MSPR 268, 273 (1994) ("Under the unique circumstances of this case, then, we find that the public interest in reaching the right result outweighs the desirability of finality."). The Board implied in Rogers v. OPM, 67 MSPR 251, 256-57 (1995), that five or six months might not be too long to bar reopening, as it considered the authority of OPM to reopen one of its decisions in a retirement matter:

Further, we find that OPM has the authority to timely reopen or reconsider a reconsideration decision in order to prevent an award of benefits not permitted by law. See, e.g., OPM v. Richmond, 496 U.S. 414, 416, 434, 110 S. Ct. 2465, 2467, 2476, 110 L. Ed.2d 387 (1990); Danganan v. OPM, 55 MSPR 265, 269 (1992), aff'd, No. 93-3057, 1994 WL 47810 (Fed. Cir. Feb. 15, 1994) (Table); see also Brown v. OPM, 51 MSPR 261, 263 (1991); Chavez v. OPM, 6 MSPB 343, 6 MSPR 404, 413 n.13 (1981).

Additionally, contrary to the appellant's contention, we find that OPM's reopening of its prior reconsideration decision was not untimely. The general rule is that administrative agencies have the power to reconsider decisions on their own initiative, as long as proper notice is given and the right is exercised within a reasonably short period of time. Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 46 MSPR 341, 348 (1990), aff'd, 949 F.2d 404 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). The Board has found that, absent unusual circumstances, the time period concerned should be measured in weeks rather than in years. Id. at 349; see also Marshall v. Government Printing Office, 43 MSPR 346, 349 (1990). Also, the Board has held "that a determination of whether to reopen and reconsider a decision involves 'balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result.'" Marshall, 43 MSPR at 348-49, quoting Woodall v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, 28 MSPR 192, 195 (1985).

As we found above, however, there was no final adjudicatory decision on the merits in this appeal, and OPM issued its new reconsideration decision on July 21, 1992, approximately 5 1/2 months after the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit issued its February 6, 1992 decision, affirming the Board's final decision that dismissed the intervenor's appeal of OPM's original reconsideration decision as untimely. Rogers v. OPM, 956 F.2d 1172 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). In Woodall, 28 MSPR at 194-97, the Board reopened and vacated its final decision in that appeal, issued approximately 3 1/2 months earlier, finding that the decision conflicted with Federal Circuit precedent and that the need for finality was outweighed by the public interest in reaching what ultimately appeared to be the right result. We find similarly here that the 5 1/2 month delay was reasonable under the circumstances.

A six-month delay in reopening was excused in Robinson v. VA, 72 MSPR 444, 447-50 (1996), a decision that downplayed the significance of the amount of time between an erroneous decision and a Board determination to reopen the case:

Although the appellant's petition for review is untimely filed, we find that it is unnecessary to determine whether the appellant has shown good cause for the 6-month delay in filing his petition for review. Instead, we reopen this appeal sua sponte under 5 USC § 7701(e)(1)(B)[1] and 5 CFR § 1201.118 to address the appellant's argument that the initial decision is in error because the administrative judge failed to apply controlling precedent, namely the decision of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit in Mays v. Department of Transportation, 27 F.3d 1577 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

[1] Section 7701(e)(1)(B) states, in pertinent part, as follows: "[A]ny decision under subsection (b) of this section shall be final unless...the Board reopens and reconsiders a case on its own motion."

In Mays, the agency informed the employee on December 1, 1992 that it would effect her removal on December 4, 1992. The employee retired effective December 4, 1992. Mays, 27 F.3d at 1578. When the employee attempted to appeal her removal, the Board dismissed her appeal for lack of jurisdiction, reasoning that, because she retired on the effective date of her removal, the removal was merely proposed and not effectuated. The Federal Circuit reversed. It found that the employee's removal was not merely "proposed" because the agency made a final decision on December 1, 1992 to remove her on December 4, 1992. Mays, 27 F.3d at 1580. The court also found that 5 USC § 7701(j) states that retirement status may not be taken into account in determining the appealability of a removal action; the statute makes no distinction as to when retirement status becomes effective. Mays, 27 F.3d at 1580. The court thus determined that the Board had jurisdiction over the employee's appeal.

Mays issued approximately 6 weeks before the administrative judge issued his initial decision and (assuming that the appellant retired rather than resigned) it is clearly applicable in this appeal. Decisions of the Federal Circuit are controlling authority on the Board. See, e.g., Hoover v. Dept. of the Navy, 57 MSPR 545, 552 (1993). Therefore, the administrative judge committed clear and material legal error by failing to apply the controlling precedent of our reviewing court in this appeal.

Congress explicitly granted the Board the full authority to reopen any decision on our own motion. See 5 USC § 7701(e)(1)(B). This authority was implemented in 5 CFR § 1201.118, which provides that: "The Board may reopen an appeal and reconsider a decision of a judge on its own motion at any time, regardless of any other provisions of this part." See also Stevenson v. Dept. of Defense, 55 MSPR 625, 629 (1992); Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 46 MSPR 341, 348 (1990), aff'd, 949 F.2d 404 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). Our reviewing court has stated that the Board has "broad discretion" in deciding which initial decisions to review sua sponte. Azarkhish v. OPM, 915 F.2d 675, 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990). It has further stated that the policy goal behind 5 CFR § 1201.118 presumably is aimed at assuring consistency between and among initial decisions." Azarkhish, 915 F.2d at 679. The Federal Circuit has also taken cognizance of the policy considerations defined by the United States Supreme Court:

Whenever a question concerning administrative...reconsideration arises, two opposing policies immediately demand recognition: [T]he desirability of finality, on the one hand, and the public interest in reaching what, ultimately, appears to be the right result on the other.

Azarkhish, 915 F.2d at 679 (quoting Civil Aeronautics Board v. Delta Air Lines, Inc., 367 U.S. 316, 321, 81 S. Ct. 1611, 1616, 6 L. Ed.2d 869 (1961)).

The Board has recognized and applied these principles. See, e.g., Stevenson, 55 MSPR at 629; Anderson, 46 MSPR at 349, citing Woodall v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, 28 MSPR 192, 195 (1985). In Woodall, the Board stated that:

[R]eopening and reconsideration may be appropriate where there is clear and material legal error, and a conflict between the holding of the decision and a controlling precedent or statute, either because of oversight or a change in the controlling law between the date of the original decision and the reopening request.

The Board then found reopening warranted after determining that its decision conflicted with a Federal Circuit decision issued a few weeks earlier. The Board concluded that the need for finality was outweighed by the public interest in reaching what appeared to be the right result. As in Woodall, we find that reopening this appeal to address a clear legal error in the initial decision reaches the proper balance between the competing public interests of preserving the finality of Board decisions and achieving what appears to be the correct result in this appeal.

We recognize that in Woodall, the motion to reopen was filed only a few weeks after the Board's final decision had issued as compared to the six month period at issue here. The Board in Woodall, however, did not expressly rely on the short time period. It focused instead upon the clarity of the legal error in view of the fact that controlling precedent changed before the Board's final decision issued. See Woodall, 28 MSPR at 195-96. The difference between a few weeks and 6 months is not of such a magnitude as to warrant a different result here. In both cases, the controlling precedent changed before the issuance of a final decision by the Board.[2] The difference of a few months in receiving notice of the error that occurred as a result of the change in precedent does not, under these circumstances, justify turning a blind eye to a clear and material legal error.[3] Moreover, the six month period at issue here fits within the time period considered reasonable in other cases involving sua sponte reopening of appeals. See, e.g., Payne v. USPS, 69 MSPR 503, 506-07 (1996) (13 months); Welber v. USPS, 62 MSPR 98, 100-01 (1994) (18 months).

[2] Cf. Tipsword v. Dept. of the Army, 66 MSPR 53, 56-57 (1994) (where Mays issued more than 8 months after the initial decision, the Board found that any interest in correcting an error was outweighed by the interest in finality).

[3] In other contexts, both administrative judges and the Board have, sua sponte, refused to turn a blind eye to clear error that affected an appellant's rights. The Board has a line of cases dating from our very earliest decisions that stands for the proposition that an administrative judge may sua sponte address an error that implicates an employee's basic procedural rights when cognizance of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice. See Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPB 489, 1 MSPR 505, 517 n.9 (1980); Chance v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 583, 588 (1983). While these cases typically concern an administrative judge's authority to raise an affirmative defense sua sponte, the pertinent legal principle is applicable here, where the issue is whether the Board should sua sponte address the administrative judge's clear error of law that prevented the appellant from showing that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal.

More importantly, in a recent Board decision, a unanimous Board reopened an appeal to address an issue of law that was not raised by the appellant in a timely petition for review and, indeed, was not raised at all by either party. In Henry v. USPS, 69 MSPR 555 (1996), the administrative judge dismissed the appellant's appeal as untimely refiled without a showing of good cause and the Board denied the appellant's petition for review. However, although neither the administrative judge nor either party to the proceeding had considered the effect of the tolling provisions of the Soldiers' and Sailors' Civil Relief Act, the Board reopened the appeal to do so. Henry, 69 MSPR at 558-59. The Board found that "orderly rules of procedure do not require sacrifice of the rules of fundamental justice." Henry, 69 MSPR at 558 (citing Ricard v. Birch, 529 F.2d 214, 216 (4th Cir. 1975)), and found the appeal was timely refiled.

Accordingly, reopening and reconsideration of the instant appeal is warranted under the principles governing the Board's authority to reopen decisions on its own motion. Furthermore, it is axiomatic that the power granted by Congress to the Board to reopen its final decisions entails the obligation to exercise that power to correct clear legal error when, as here, all the conditions for the proper exercise of power have been met.

See Jackson v. USPS, 79 MSPR 46, 50 (1998) (in considering whether to reopen, the Board "will not turn a blind eye to a clear and material legal error"; six weeks passed between the issuance of the Board decision and the filing that brought the apparently erroneous holding to the Board's attention). Unless there are unusual circumstances, a year's delay would be too long to seek reopening. Romero v. OPM, 66 MSPR 109, 111 (1995); see Felders v. USPS, 89 MSPR 216, 218 ¶ 10 (2001) (without unusual circumstances, four years is too long to reopen). But see Humme v. Dept. of Interior, 109 MSPR 678, 683 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 205 (2008) ("We recognize that the initial decision became final approximately 3 years before the appellant requested reopening. However, we note that the appellant appears to have acted promptly in requesting reopening upon learning of the retroactive termination of the demonstration project."); Rivera v. Dept. of Transp., 94 MSPR 216, 222 ¶ 14 (2003) (similar to Tarrant: a delay of about two years was permitted in a reopening proceeding involving intervening statutory revisions in the Board's jurisdiction over appeals from employees of the FAA).

Despite the timeliness issues and results involved in reopening petitions just discussed, the Board determined that extraordinary circumstances warranted a rather protracted reopening process, in Welber v. USPS, 62 MSPR 98, 101-03 (1994), involving a two- or three-year period between a reopening petition and the several related decisions subject to the petition:

Pursuant to 5 USC §§ 7701(e) and 1204(a), the decisions of the Board in this appeal are final. Nonetheless, the Board may reopen even final decisions under certain circumstances, pursuant to 5 USC § 7701(e) and 5 CFR § 1201.117. A determination on whether to reopen a case is discretionary, Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 7 MSPB 239, 7 MSPR 357, 359 (1981), and involves "balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result," Woodall v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, 28 MSPR 192, 195 (1985). See also Anderson v. Department of Transportation, 46 MSPR 341, 348-49 (1990). A case may be reopened "in the interests of justice," Parkinson v. US Postal Service, 58 MSPR 393, 397 (1993), particularly where the evidence is of such weight as to warrant a different outcome, see Carey v. USPS, 50 MSPR 359, 361 (1991); Sansom v. Office of Personnel Management, 7 MSPB 661, 8 MSPR 185, 188 (1981).

There is a substantial limitation on an agency's authority to reopen an appeal, however. That is, such authority must be exercised within a reasonable period of time. See, e.g., Dawson v. MSPB, 712 F.2d 264, 267 (7th Cir. 1983). Because the time period is generally "measured in weeks, not years," Anderson, 46 MSPR at 349, the Board has rejected requests for reopening that have been filed beyond that time frame, even where there has been an intervening change in the controlling law. See, e.g., Moriarty v. Rhode Island Air National Guard, 56 MSPR 144 (1992).

The instant case clearly involves a delay of more than just weeks. We find it to be distinguishable, though, from other cases in which reopening has been denied or found to be erroneous on that basis. Unlike the situation in Moriarty, where the Board had addressed only preliminary issues and not the appellant's entitlement to the restoration he requested, here the appellant has been found entitled to priority consideration for restoration, and the agency's petition for review challenging that finding was filed well beyond the regulatory deadline, without a showing of good cause for the delay. This case is also unlike the situation in Lynch v. Bennett, 665 F. Supp. 62 (D.D.C. 1987), where the court found that the Board's decision to reopen was not only made after the expiration of a reasonable period but was also beyond its limited scope of authority under 5 USC § 7702 to act on a case already reviewed by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. And, unlike Gubisch v. Brady, Civil Action No. 88-2031, slip op. at 23, 49 Fair Empl. Prac. Cas. (BNA) 1063, 1989 WL 44083 (D.D.C. April 20, 1989), in which the EEOC was taken to task for its decision to reopen merely to reconsider the same facts and law, here there has been a material change in the controlling facts of the case.

Indeed, in this case, the Board previously reopened the appeal on its own motion because of an OWCP decision that materially affected the appellant's right to restoration, the same change that has recently occurred again. See Welber, 50 MSPR at 561. There is no question that the change in OWCP's position is the dispositive evidence of the appellant's entitlement to compensation. See Hicks v. USPS, 19 MSPR 18 (1984) (OWCP decision vacating earlier rejection of the appellant's claim for compensation constituted new and material evidence warranting reversal of initial decision); Elias v. VA, 7 MSPB 1, 7 MSPR 66 (1981) (inadvertent omission from record considered by Board of OWCP decision accepting the appellant's claim for compensation constitutes basis for reopening and reversing earlier decision).

In addition to the centrality of the evidence presented by the appellant to the issue of his appeal, we also find it significant that the evidence was not in existence, and therefore not available at an earlier date, through no fault of his. Not only had the appellant diligently pursued a change in the OWCP position, but he submitted it to the Board within eight days of its issuance. See Lopez v. Dept. of Justice, 55 MSPR 644, 646 (1992) (petition for review filed more than five years late showed good cause for its untimeliness where it was based on evidence not available to the appellant until days before he submitted it to Board; that evidence, reversal of an element of the appellant's past record on which the agency relied in imposing adverse action, required mitigation of the penalty). Nor was the matter ever completely closed, because although the Board had issued final decisions, both parties were aware of the continued pendency of the appellant's claim with OWCP for reversal of the order denying him benefits. One purpose of that claim, of course, was to provide the appellant with a basis for being restored to duty. Finally in this regard, we note that during the compliance proceedings before the Board, the agency asserted that it had complied with the June 8, 1990 initial decision, and submitted documentation in support of its position. Thus, there is no apparent prejudice to the agency and renewed compliance proceedings should not be unduly burdensome to it. See Stephens v. OPM, 33 MSPR 292, 296-99 (1987) (Board decided to reopen appeals and apply a new rule of law retroactively, in part because burden on affected agency would not be great).

But see Holloway v. VA, 65 MSPR 363, 366 (1994) (although a new OWCP decision affected the appellant's restoration rights, he did not request the reopening in the short period following the issuance of the OWCP decision); King v. Dept. of Navy, 90 MSPR 341, 346 ¶ 8 (2001) (the Board did not reopen a prior case that was settled when, years after the issuance of the decision, the OWCP issued a decision granting benefits for at least part of period charged as AWOL; there was no allegation of fraud as to the settlement; remanding the case for evaluation of claimed restoration rights).

Recognizing the usual requirement that a reopening petition be filed quickly, there are a few decisions, discussed earlier and in the sections that follow, that permit reopening, despite a considerable passage of time, under extraordinary circumstances.
