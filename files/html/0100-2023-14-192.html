4. Advance Notice for Adverse Action

In NFFE Local 1380 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Coastal Sys. Ctr., Panama City, 36 FLRA 725, 732, 734 (1990), Proposal 2 was approved for negotiation, requesting "a reasonable time, but not less than 10 calendar days to answer orally and in writing and to furnish affidavits and other documentary evidence in support of the answer." 5 USC 7513(b)(2) mandated "a reasonable time, but not less than 7 days," to respond. The proposal was no more than a negotiable procedure, consistent with the statute. Risks to the agency involving retention of dangerous employees could be met through the use of administrative leave under 5 CFR 752.404(d) (2022). POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 41 FLRA 795, 832 (1991), resolved as nonnegotiable Provision 10, requiring the agency to provide the employee with 30 days of advance written notice of a proposed adverse action. The proposal negated the statutory right of the agency to shorten the advance notice provision when reasonable cause existed to conclude that the employee committed a crime punishable by imprisonment. See NTEU and NRC, 43 FLRA 1279, 1295-96 (1992) (Proposal 3) (discussed in subsequent section on stay of disciplinary actions, comparing stay and advance notice proposals); NFFE and Dept. of Interior, Geological Survey, E. Mapping Agency, 21 FLRA 1105, 1105 (1986) (nonnegotiable: "[a]ny disciplinary action resulting in removal shall not be effected against the employee for at least ten (10) working days from the receipt of the letter from the deciding official"), overruled, AFGE Local 3732 and Dept. of Transp., Merchant Marine Acad., Kings Point, N.Y., 39 FLRA 187, 199-200 (1991), post. Refer to POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 41 FLRA 795, 821-22 (1991), post, concerning a proposal seeking amendment of proposal notices when additional evidence is relied on that could lead to new charges.

A proposal was not negotiable that prohibited any removal from being effected before at least 10 working days from receipt of the deciding official's letter. The proposal did not include a statutory exception for a shortened notice period for employees involved in actions presenting reasonable cause to believe that the employee committed a crime with potential for imprisonment. The failure of the proposal to acknowledge managerial authority in that situation substantively interfered with the right to take disciplinary action. The proposal made it impossible for management to hold employees accountable for their failure or refusal to carry out assigned work during the 10 workdays following the effective date of their removal. The proposal was not an appropriate arrangement because, although it would ameliorate the adverse effect of a removal to a limited degree, the deleterious effect on management's rights to impose discipline, direct employees, and assign work outweighed the limited benefit to the employees, and excessively interfered with management rights. NFFE and Dept. of Interior, Geological Survey, E. Mapping Agency, 21 FLRA 1105, 1105-08 (1986). A proposal was negotiable, however, that stated the effective date of a suspension would not be less than 10 days from the date of the decision letter. The "crime provision" in 5 USC 7513(b)(1) allowing for shortened notice periods, is inapplicable to suspensions of 14 days or less under 5 USC 7503. Nothing prevented the agency from continuing to hold employees accountable for meeting the standards established for their work performance. NFFE and Dept. of Interior, BLM, 29 FLRA 1491, 1522 (1987), aff'd, Dept. of Interior, BLM v. FLRA, 873 F.2d 1505, 1511-12 (D.C. Cir. 1989).

AFGE Local 3732 and Dept. of Transp., Merchant Marine Acad., Kings Point, N.Y., 39 FLRA 187, 204-06 (1991), declared negotiable a requirement of three days' advance notice of a final adverse action decision:

Provision 4 also requires that the Agency's decision on the adverse action be furnished to the affected employee no later than 3 days prior to the action's effective date. That is, Provision 4 requires that affected employees receive 3 days' notice of the effective date of the action. For the following reasons, we reject the Agency's argument that this portion of the provision is nonnegotiable....

...

...Provision 4 here preserves the Agency's right under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b) to suspend the usual notice period when there is reasonable cause to conclude that the affected employee has committed a crime punishable by imprisonment....

...

Similarly, Provision 4 does not expressly apply to situations where the Agency's decision to remove an employee has been sustained on appeal and, consequently, no possibility exists for the employee's continued employment by the Agency....

Nevertheless, in limited circumstances, Provision 4 would have the same practical consequences as the disputed provision in Eastern Mapping Agency [NFFE and U.S. Dept. of Interior, U.S. Geological Survey, Eastern Mapping Agency, 21 FLRA 1105 (1986)]. Provision 4 would, in this regard, apply in situations where the Agency had decided to remove an employee and that employee had not appealed, and did not intend to appeal, the action. In this situation, the proposal is analogous to Eastern Mapping.

Recognizing the existence of these situations, we have reexamined, and now reject, the Authority's conclusion in Eastern Mapping Agency that a proposal providing affected employees with notice of an agency's decision to take adverse action against them directly interferes with management's rights to assign work and direct employees because management would have no effective method of holding those employees accountable for their work during the notice period. We note the following.

First, the effect of the proposal is only that the Agency is required to provide affected employees with notice of the decision to take an adverse action. The proposal is not aimed at, or focused on, the assignment of work or the direction of employees....

It is clear, in this regard, that unless the Agency placed affected employees in a non-work, pay status, the proposal would require the Agency to provide some work to employees during the 3-day period. The requirement to assign some work, however, flows solely from the requirement that the Agency provide notice of its decision to take adverse action. That is, the obvious and necessary consequence of a removal of an employee is the cessation of the assignment of work to that employee. The fact that the assignment of work ceases when an employee is removed, however, does not support a conclusion that all proposals affecting the timing of a removal action interfere with the rights to assign work and direct employees.

Moreover, it is not clear that employees would, in the circumstances of the proposal, refuse to perform work. Instead, it is reasonable to conclude that employees' individual responses to removal actions would vary depending on such factors as the events surrounding the removal action and the employees' desires to gain employment in the Agency or elsewhere.

Put simply, it is speculative to assume that, as employees could take certain actions, they would do so. Further, even assuming that, in the limited circumstances where an employee who is being removed has decided not to appeal that removal action, the employee would refuse to perform work, nothing in the proposal affects, or limits in any way, the Agency's rights to respond appropriately to such refusal. It may be more difficult for Agency managers effectively to assign and direct the work of such employees, but the existence and extent of such difficulties depend on the particular facts and circumstances of each case--not on the proposal.

On reexamination, therefore, we hold that the proposed 3-day notice of decisions involving adverse actions does not directly interfere with the Agency's rights to direct employees and assign work. To the extent that the Authority's decision in Eastern Mapping Agency holds to the contrary, we will no longer adhere to it.
