1. Back Pay Act

The Board may award fees under either 5 USC 7701(g) or under the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596. Burrows v. District of Columbia Gov't, 10 MSPR 478, 480 (1982). Despite some ambiguities in the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596(b)(1)(A), counsel fees claimed under this statute as to adverse action cases are awarded on the same basis as fees claimed under 5 USC 7701(g). Sims v. Dept. of Navy, 711 F.2d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (harmonizing the fee provisions of the Back Pay Act and the Reform Act); Brown v. Coast Guard, 28 MSPR 539, 546-47 (1985) ("The attorney fee provision of § 5596 expressly refers to the attorney fee provision of § 7701(g) and both the Board and the courts have found that since Congress considered and enacted both provisions at the same time, Congress intended to require that § 5596 fee awards be made in accordance with the standard set forth in § 7701(g). In Crowley v. Schultz, 704 F.2d 1269, 1274 (D.C. Cir. 1983), the court concluded that '[t]here is no reason to suppose...that judicial relief [for attorney fees] under the Back Pay Act should be treated any differently' from relief under § 7701(g)."); Sims v. Dept. of Navy, 13 MSPR 393, 395 (1982) ("The attorney fees provision contained in the Back Pay Act itself provides that an employee is entitled to receive 'reasonable attorney fees related to the personnel action which...shall be awarded in accordance with standards established under § 7701(g) of this title.' 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(A)(ii)."); Shock v. Gov't of District of Columbia, 11 MSPR 1, 2 n.1 (1982) ("Since 5 USC 5596 incorporates by reference the standards of 5 USC 7701, the analysis of claims asserted under each section is identical."). The Back Pay Act provided the Board with authority to award counsel fees against the District of Columbia Government. McKenzie v. District of Columbia Gov't, 20 MSPR 528, 529-30 (1984).

Labor arbitrators rely on the Back Pay Act for their authority to award fees in civil service cases generating back pay awards. [Refer to Broida's A Guide to Federal Labor Relations Authority Law and Practice (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2023).] As a matter of practice, if a case is heard under § 7701, the fee claim will ordinarily be under § 7701(g). It does an appellant no harm to cite the Back Pay Act as well, but 7701(g) should not be omitted unless it is clearly inapplicable to the case.

The Back Pay Act is available to reviewing courts to award fees for both time in court and time before the MSPB. Under the "interest of justice" standard, the permissive "may" in the statute establishes that the court has significant discretion in deciding when an award is proper. This discretion is exercised by looking to the five Allen categories. Massa v. DOD, 833 F.2d 991 (Fed. Cir. 1987). Massa suggested that the petitioner may seek counsel fees in court under both EAJA and the Back Pay Act. Counsel may seek fees under both statutes, but need not do so--one statute may be relied upon. Assuming that both statutes are invoked, the Back Pay Act entitlement is considered first, since the EAJA supplements the Back Pay Act. If a fee award is appropriate under the Back Pay Act, fees and expenses will be denied under EAJA, because the latter statute does not apply to situations covered by the Back Pay Act. Massa v. DOD, 833 F.2d 991 (Fed. Cir. 1987). An award under the Back Pay Act is usually more generous than one under the EAJA. Under EAJA, the hourly rate is limited to $125 [formerly $75], subject to a cost of living enhancement. One must satisfy a higher standard to win an award under the Back Pay Act than to win an award under the EAJA. If a party chooses just one of the statutes, however, the court will consider the application for fees under the invoked statute without requiring the party to prove the inapplicability of the other.
