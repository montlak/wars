b. Affirmative Defense

In addition to providing an affirmative grant of jurisdiction to matters not ordinarily within the Board's appellate purview, USERRA may be asserted as an affirmative defense to an action brought against an employee and within the Board's ordinary appellate authority, Morgan v. USPS, 82 MSPR 1, 4-6 ¶ 7-13 (1999), aff'd, Morgan v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3435):

Congress enacted USERRA as remedial legislation which was to be interpreted liberally to protect those persons who have served in our armed forces. See H.R. Rep. No. 103-65, at 19 (1993), reprinted in 1994 USCCAN 2449, 2452 ("the Act is to be 'liberally construed.'"). The House of Representatives' Committee on Veterans' Affairs explicitly intended that USERRA's anti-discrimination provisions "be broadly construed and strictly enforced." H.R. Rep. No. 103-65, at 23 (1993), reprinted in 1994 USCCAN 2449, 2456. In this spirit, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Board have recognized that an appellant can implicitly plead a USERRA claim. See Yates v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 145 F.3d 1480 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (court found that the appellant had implicitly raised USERRA as a claim); Roberson v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 MSPR 569 (1998) (Board recognized the appellant's implicitly pled USERRA claim); Jasper v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 MSPR 367 (1998) (same).

Section 7701(c)(2), 5 USC, title 5, lists the three categories of affirmative defenses which an appellant may assert on appeal to the Board. A USERRA affirmative defense does not involve harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures, 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(A), and is not one of the enumerated prohibited personnel practices under 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(B). We find that it does, however, come under the third category of affirmative defenses, namely, that "the [agency's] decision was not in accordance with law." 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(C).

One of the purposes of USERRA is "to prohibit discrimination against persons because of their service in the uniformed services." 38 USC § 4301(a)(3). Section 4311 specifically prohibits an employer from denying an employee retention in employment or any other benefit of employment because of the employee's past, present, or future military service. 38 USC § 4311(a). Thus, an employer who takes an action against an employee because of the employee's military service is taking an action which is not in accordance with law. An allegation of such discrimination may, therefore, be asserted as an affirmative defense under 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(C), and proof of such defense requires that the agency's decision not be sustained. Id. Indeed, the language of the USERRA statute contemplates that a claim involving this type of discrimination will be raised in response to an adverse action taken by the agency. See 38 USC § 4311(c)(1).

[G]iven the liberal construction afforded to USERRA, we find that in cases in which the appellant either explicitly or implicitly alleges USERRA as an affirmative defense, the administrative judge must inform the appellant of his burden of proof and of his burden of going forward with the evidence as well as the type of evidence necessary to prove the defense.

In the present case, the appellant implicitly pled USERRA as an affirmative defense to the agency's action. He claims that the agency terminated his employment because of his disabled veteran status. Therefore, he is entitled to present testimony and evidence at the hearing on the merits of his allegation that the agency discriminated against him because of his prior military service. Before a hearing on the merits of the appellant's USERRA claim can be held, however, the administrative judge must first determine whether it is currently ripe for adjudication. If the appellant has sought the assistance of the Secretary of Labor by filing a complaint within the meaning of 38 USC § 4322 and proceedings before the Secretary have not been exhausted, the Board does not have jurisdiction over this claim. See 38 USC § 4324(b); Milner, 77 MSPR at 46-47.

In Duncan v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 MSPR 86, 93-94 (1997), the Board held that if an appellant supports his claim of a violation of USERRA through the use of direct evidence of discriminatory intent, the administrative judge shall analyze his claim in accordance with the USERRA standard of review. See 38 USC § 4311(b); Jasper, 73 MSPR at 370-71; Petersen v. Department of the Interior, 71 MSPR 227, 239-40 (1996). On the other hand, if an appellant supports his claim more indirectly by showing that discriminatory intent could be inferred from relevant circumstantial evidence, the administrative judge should apply analogous burdens of proof and production under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 for analyzing discrimination claims based on circumstantial evidence. Duncan, 73 MSPR at 93-94; see Coleman v. Department of the Air Force, 66 MSPR 498, 508 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1165 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table); see also Decker v. Department of Health & Human Services, 40 MSPR 119, 132 (1989) (application of Title VII burdens of proof and production to an age discrimination case); Stalkfleet v. U.S. Postal Service, 6 MSPR 637, 646-48 (1981) (use of Title VII burdens of proof and production in a disability discrimination case).

See Greene v. Dept. of Air Force, DE-0432-09-0448-I-1 (NP 1/7/2011) ("In a removal appeal, the Board can consider an appellant's claims pursuant to the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA) and the Veterans Employment Opportunities Act (VEOA) as affirmative defenses under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(C). Aguilar v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 102, ¶ 8 (2006), aff'd, No. 2006-3327 (Fed. Cir. Mar. 9, 2007) (NP).").

In a discharge case involving a USERRA affirmative defense, the Board may determine that the evidence of misconduct is sufficiently great that the Board will assume unlawful motivation but then decide that the action would have been taken without regard to past military service. See Fahrenbacher v. VA, 89 MSPR 260, 268 ¶ 14 (2001) (disregard of supervisory directives).
