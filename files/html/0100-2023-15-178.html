5. Defenses

The Board does not seem inclined to accept evidence of the social life of the complaining witness in defense to a sexual harassment charge. See Cain v. Dept. of Army, 13 MSPR 252, 254 (1982) (also rejecting the argument that a victim of discrimination should pursue EEO remedies rather than trigger an adverse action by complaining to the personnel office). Oliveros-Ballon v. USPS, SF-0752-15-0615-I-1 (NP 4/15/2016), observed:

The appellant also argues that the administrative judge failed to consider evidence of the work environment, where "sexually vulgar talk" was pervasive. However, such evidence does not mitigate the impropriety of the appellant's conduct because supervisors are responsible for maintaining a work environment free of sexual harassment. Cisneros v. Department of Defense, 83 M.S.P.R. 390, ¶ 19 (1999), aff'd per curiam, 243 F.3d 562 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table); see Lowe v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 73, 75, 77 (1994) (finding that testimony regarding the office atmosphere of sexual joking and innuendo was irrelevant to the appellant's defense against a charge of sexual harassment because it would not have mitigated the offensiveness of his behavior of kissing, propositioning, and putting his arms around a fellow employee); cf. Herrera-Martinez v. Social Security Administration, 84 M.S.P.R. 426, ¶ 16 (1999) (finding that, although condonation of behavior in the workplace may be a factor mitigating the penalty, it does not always warrant mitigation).

A female employee's voluntary participation in sexually related conversations with her peers is irrelevant to charges against an appellant of unsolicited, unwelcome sexual advances made a condition of employment, Ballentine v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 652, 658-59 (1986), although lack of complaints by an employee who engages other employees in frank discussions may be a factor in evaluating harassment:

...Deputy Kish's voluntary participation in sexually-related conversations with her peers is irrelevant to the type of conduct with which appellant is charged here--unsolicited, unwelcome sexual advances which are made a condition of employment and the rejection or acceptance of which is calculated to affect the employee's employment or working environment. See Hosemann v. Technical Materials, Inc., 554 F. Supp. 659, 666 (D.R.I. 1982); Guyette v. Stauffer Chemical Co., 518 F. Supp. 521, 526 (D.N.J. 1981) (non-supervisory employee not subject to liability under Title VII). See also Downes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 775 F.2d 288, 290-93 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (explanation of sexual discrimination and distinction between "quid pro quo" sexual harassment and sexual harassment which creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment); Barnes v. Costle, 561 F.2d 983, 994 & n.81 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (discussion of the impact of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 on prohibited superior-subordinate sexual relationships and voluntary personal relationships); Hillen v. Department of the Army, 29 MSPR 690, 694 (1986) (prima facie case of sexual harassment established where evidence showed that appellant's touching of female employee was without her consent and unwelcome, that appellant's conduct was pervasive, and that it affected the employee's work performance).

Nevertheless, we find that the presiding official properly considered evidence that the witness had not previously complained of sexual harassment to a close on-the-job friend with whom she had exchanged sexual comments and that witnesses who were allegedly present at the incident concerned did not mention the incidents in their statements to agency investigators. Thus, we find no error by the presiding official in considering these circumstances in finding Deputy Kish's testimony not credible.

The Board considered evidence of psychological problems contributing to sexually inappropriate behavior in Walker v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 309, 324 (1993). Although mental impairment was a mitigating factor entitled to considerable weight, mitigation was inappropriate because of the severity of the conduct and concern that the appellant would pose a problem in the future:

The Board will review an agency-imposed penalty only to determine if the agency considered all the relevant factors and exercised management discretion within tolerable limits of reasonableness. Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPR 280, 306 (1981). Most of the relevant factors in this case support a severe penalty. Sexual harassment is inherently a serious offense, particularly when it involves physical contact. See Vakili v. Department of Agriculture, 35 MSPR 534, 539 (1987). The appellant's harassing and disgraceful behavior was repeated, and involved more than one employee. Moreover, the appellant had been counseled and warned about sexual harassment after Bryan complained to management in late June, and had been interviewed by the Naval Investigative Service about those allegations on July 9, less than two months before his offense against Donna Brooks on September 4, 1991.

The appellant's offenses had a serious effect on his supervisor's confidence in his ability to do his job and on the morale of other employees. Dorothy Dreger, his first-line supervisor, testified that she has been approached by a number of female employees who do not want to work with the appellant, and she said that she does not want to supervise the appellant if she cannot guarantee the safety of her female employees. Ms. Dreger also stated that the appellant has told her that he has a real problem dealing with a woman in a position of authority. She added that, if the appellant were returned to duty, she would consider it a slap in the face to her as a supervisor and to the women on base, and that she would seek another position if this were to occur.

The only substantial factor supporting a penalty less than removal was the medical evidence, which tended to show that the appellant's medical and psychological condition contributed to his misconduct, and his therapists' opinion that the appellant could return to the work place without endangering women employees. Evidence that an employee's medical condition or mental impairment played a part in the charged conduct is ordinarily entitled to considerable weight as a mitigating factor. See Wellman v. Department of the Navy, 49 MSPR 149, 152 (1991); Ajanaku v. Department of Defense, 44 MSPR 350, 356 (1990). The Board has found that removal was nevertheless appropriate when the misconduct was extremely serious, or in the absence of any indication in the record that the mental condition, and thus the conduct, would be corrected in the foreseeable future. See Anderson v. Department of the Navy, 42 MSPR 16, 19 (1989); Murray v. Department of the Army, 40 MSPR 250, 258 (1989).

As discussed above, the appellant's misconduct in the instant case was extremely serious. Although the appellant's therapists opined that the appellant could return to the work place without endangering women employees, it is questionable whether this opinion was based on all the relevant information. Dr. Dixon testified that the appellant related only one incident, in which he asked a woman employee to touch his penis and he touched her breasts. Dr. Dixon was unaware of any sexual indiscretions on the appellant's part since he started treating the appellant. The appellant obviously did not tell Dr. Dixon about the incident with Donna Brooks on September 4, 1991, which occurred about a week after his first therapy session on August 29.

Under all the circumstances of this case, we find that the removal penalty is within the bounds of reasonableness.

A penalty of removal is within the tolerable bounds of reasonableness for a sustained charge of criminal sexual misconduct. See, e.g., Graybill v. US Postal Service, 782 F.2d 1567 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 963, 107 S. Ct. 462, 93 L. Ed.2d 407 (1986); Williams v. General Services Administration, 22 MSPR 476, 478-79 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 182 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table); Hayes v. Department of the Navy, 15 MSPR 378 (1983), aff'd, 727 F.2d 1535 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The agency's table of penalties indicates that removal is within the range of penalties for a first offense of "indecent or immoral conduct." See Peterson v. Department of Transportation, 54 MSPR 178, 184 (1992) (consistency of removal penalty with agency's table of penalties is a relevant factor in determining the reasonableness of a penalty). The agency deciding official considered the relevant factors and, in our view, exercised management discretion within tolerable limits of reasonableness. Accordingly, we conclude that the agency established that the penalty of removal is within the bounds of reasonableness and will, under the circumstances, promote the efficiency of the service....
