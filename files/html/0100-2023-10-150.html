b. Leading Questions

Leading questions suggest the desired answer. ("Did you tell the appellant on the afternoon of Friday the 13th that he would be suspended if the infraction was repeated?") They are objectionable during direct examination and permitted (encouraged) on cross-examination or direct examination of an adverse witness. The theory is that a witness being questioned by a friendly party (direct examination) should testify rather than affirm what his counsel has to say. But in cross-examination the idea is to force the hostile witness to admit matters by demanding a concession to a stated proposition. Leading questions are permitted during direct examination on preliminary matters ("You state that you are the appellant, John Jones?"), and they are permitted to refresh the recollection of a witness during direct examination (assuming an exhausted memory). They are also permitted if the witness is very young or for some other reason is not readily able to answer open questions. Nonleading or open questions often begin with "when, how, why, where, who."

Rule 611 of the Federal Rules of Evidence states, in part:

Mode and Order of Interrogation and Presentation

...

(c) Leading questions.

Leading questions should not be used on the direct examination of a witness except as may be necessary to develop the witness' testimony. Ordinarily leading questions should be permitted on cross-examination. When a party calls a hostile witness, an adverse party, or a witness identified with an adverse party, interrogation may be by leading questions.

Leading questions will not lead to automatic reversal or remand, Sletten v. Dept. of Air Force, 2 MSPR 35, 39-40, 2 MSPB 209 (1980):

[A]ppellant argues that the presiding official should not have allowed the agency representative to ask leading questions on direct examination or pose questions on redirect examination that should have been asked on direct. Appellant also asserts unfairness because the agency's representative, a colonel, was asking leading questions of enlisted men, and this would "inevitably" bring forth "affirmative" answers. Appellant does not give any reasons for believing that the substance of the answers would have been any different had they been elicited through nonleading questions. Nor does appellant claim that his representative was denied full opportunity to question the agency's witnesses on the matters raised in the agency's examination.

If an appellant has a representative (who would be expected to object), the Board will not lightly overturn an initial decision because the AJ allowed leading questions by agency counsel to friendly witnesses if objections are not registered. There may be unusual circumstances that require an AJ to control questioning to protect a party or a witness from manifest injustice. Sletten v. Dept. of Air Force, supra, 2 MSPR 35, 40, 2 MSPB 209, 212 (1980). The Board may note (and perhaps discount) testimony that is the product of leading questions. See Sherlock v. GSA, 103 MSPR 352, 360 ¶ 18, 2006 MSPB 277 (2006); Special Counsel v. Nichols, 36 MSPR 445, 450-51 (1988) ("The absence of a motive for falsely inculpating Nichols is a factor for giving weight to Scherr's statements, along with the fact that they were made under oath. However, the leading nature of the examination which produced them is clear from the transcript."). But in the end, leading questions are often permitted, even if the appellant proceeds pro se. The Federal Circuit said in Yanopoulos v. Dept. of Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986):

Petitioner has not persuaded us that he was prejudiced by the use of leading questions by the agency's counsel. Although the Federal Rules of Evidence do not apply to Board hearings,...they are a helpful guide to proper hearing practices. Even the Federal Rules of Evidence, which apply in stricter and more formal trial proceedings, allow for the use of leading questions to develop testimony. Fed. R. Evid. 611....

Condensed, the Federal Rules of Evidence generally preclude leading questions during significant, material areas of direct examination; the Board occasionally permits them; and the Federal Circuit lets the Board adopt its own procedures concerning admission of evidence. Nevertheless, leading questions on critical issues are inappropriate on direct examination. They effectively make the attorney the witness. Counsel should object to leading questions. It is not enough for the AJ to state that the leading nature of the question goes to the weight of the answer. More to the point, the nature of the question goes to the nature of the question and the acumen of the person asking the question. Some AJs are more lenient than others about leading questions. Some representatives (lawyers and nonlawyers alike) do not know what a leading question is and cannot figure out how to rephrase the question to make it unobjectionable. This can be amusing at a hearing if you do not happen to be aligned with the luminary who is experiencing difficulties. Frequently, the result is that the objectionable question is dropped and the inquiry proceeds to another--less important--topic. To avoid this problem, be sure to understand what constitutes a proper question before the hearing commences. The hearing is not the best time to learn trial procedure. If all else fails, however, ask the AJ how to correct the question rather than abandoning the inquiry.

The Board offered guidance to AJs concerning the use of leading questions in the Hearings Procedures Guide. Although the Hearings Procedures Guide has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook, it remains an excellent source for guidance and is still used for training and orientation. The Guide provides, at 18-19:

f. Leading Questions

(1) When Permitted

Leading questions are permitted:

(a) On cross-examination;

(b) when a party calls a hostile witness;

(c) when a party calls an adverse party or witness identified with an adverse party; Fed. R. Evid. 611(c).

(d) where the witness is immature, timid, or frightened. Rotolo v. United States, 404 F.2d 316 (5th Cir. 1968);

(e) when the witness is a child or an adult with communication problems; and

(f) with a witness whose recollection is exhausted.

(2) When Not Permitted

Leading questions should not be allowed:

(a) On direct examination;

(b) when the questions call for speculative answers;

(c) with regard to written statements;

(d) with inconsistent statements; and

(e) when refreshing memory.

It frequently occurs in Board litigation that one party calls as his or her own witness an individual closely identified with the other side's case. For example, the appellant calls the deciding official, or the agency calls the appellant, as witness. Ordinarily one can use leading questions to examine witnesses on direct examination who are considered "hostile" through their position in the case. But then the examination concludes, and counsel for the side with whom the witness is aligned begins his or her examination of the same witness. Is that cross-examination? May counsel lead his or her own witness? The advisory committee notes for Federal Rule of Evidence 611 suggest that leading should not be allowed under the guise of "cross" examination:

Subdivision (c).

The rule also conforms to tradition in making the use of leading questions on cross-examination a matter of right. The purpose of the qualification "ordinarily" is to furnish a basis for denying the use of leading questions when the cross-examination is cross-examination in form only and not in fact, as for example the "cross-examination" of a party by his own counsel after being called by the opponent (savoring more of re-direct) or of an insured defendant who proves to be friendly to the plaintiff.

On occasion the Board will consider whether questions were improperly leading as it evaluates the weight of testimonial evidence. See, e.g., Baldwin v. VA, 111 MSPR 586, 598 ¶ 37, 2009 MSPB 126 (2009) (earlier decision, Baldwin v. VA, 109 MSPR 392, 2008 MSPB 169 (2008), on further review following counsel fee petition, Baldwin v. VA, 115 MSPR 413, 2010 MSPB 221 (2010)); Special Counsel v. Nichols, 36 MSPR 445, 450-51 (1988), supra.
