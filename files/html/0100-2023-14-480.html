broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 15: Pay, Premiums, and Allowances, Section O: Incentive Pay; Awards, Subsection 03: Standards Governing Incentive Awards, Part a: Pre-1995 Regulations and Cases
a. Pre-1995 Regulations and Cases

Earlier OPM regulations required higher-level approval for incentive awards, and that requirement served to bar many proposals designed to provide awards on the basis of defined criteria. In 1988 FLRA determined in NTEU and IRS, Indianapolis Dist., 30 FLRA 1170, 1172 (1988), that, based on court decisions concerning the composition of incentive awards committees [discussed later in this chapter, "Joint Committees"], a union could propose that employees would automatically be entitled to a previously established award if they received an overall rating at the outstanding level. The agency remained free to continue or discontinue the program; at issue was only the level of performance that justified the award. Subsequently FLRA determined that, based on OPM regulations, the award structure was to conform with OPM requirements to the extent that procedures for making awards determinations must include a requirement for review and approval of each determination by an official at a higher level than the official who made the initial decision. Tidewater Va. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Shipyard, 37 FLRA 938, 943 (1990) (Proposal 2); see IFPTE Local 89 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, Pac. Northwest Region, 45 FLRA 597, 601-02 (1992) (restating FLRA's restrictive views concerning proposals requesting specified amounts or percentages of salaries for particular ratings); NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, BATF, 43 FLRA 1442, 1460-61, 1465 (1992) (Proposal 16: also barring the negotiability of proposals setting level of awards for given ratings); NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 43 FLRA 47, 52-53 (1991) (reaffirming bar to negotiability presented by the OPM regulatory revisions of § 430.504(d) in 1991); ACT and DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau, Rhode Island NG, 38 FLRA 1005, 1007-09 (1990) (Proposal 1, requesting awards of 15% for "outstanding," 10% for "excellent," and 5% for "fully acceptable" ratings, was inconsistent with 5 CFR 430.503(c)(1)); AFGE Local 1770 and Dept. of Army, Hq., XVIII Airborne Corps, 38 FLRA 626, 630 (1990) (as to awards required on basis of overall rating levels: "5 CFR 430.503(c), which requires that an agency official who is at a higher level than the recommending official review and approve each award.... The authority...to approve performance awards includes the authority to disapprove those awards and mandatory performance awards are inconsistent with that express authority."). But see NTEU v. FLRA, 30 F.3d 1510, 1514-15 (D.C. Cir. 1994) (awards tied to ratings were negotiable because OPM had not yet provided authoritative interpretation of its own regulations).

FLRA decisions under prior OPM regulations were consistent. A proposal that established award entitlement through assessment of performance against established schedules of ratings was nonnegotiable, IFPTE, Local No. 1 and Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Shipyard, 38 FLRA 1589, 1596-99 (1991), because the proposal stripped management of the discretion to disapprove awards. NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 38 FLRA 456, 490, 492 (1990) (Proposals 11 and 12), was distinguished because the proposals in that case specified "the type of award that may be given for a particular performance rating" and establish "a [payment] range based on percentage of salary," but the proposals "do not mandate that awards must be given." Because of the requirement of higher-level approval for awards, even novel approaches to awards were nonnegotiable. NFFE, Forest Serv. Council and USDA, Forest Serv., Region 6, Portland, 46 FLRA 145, 155-56 (1992), disapproved Proposal 3, requiring payments to employees to encourage them to vacate surplus positions during times of workforce realignments and RIFs.

Proposals indirectly requiring a given level of awards were nonnegotiable when they interfered with the OPM regulatory scheme. See NFFE Local 858 and USDA, Fed. Crop Ins. Corp., 48 FLRA 552, 558, 560 (1993) (disapproving section of Proposal 2 seeking agreement that "employees at all grade levels will be reasonably represented in the awards given"). But a linkage between performance standards and awards did not preclude agencies from establishing standards of an absolute nature (permitting no errors). UPTO and Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs, Walla Walla, 44 FLRA 1145, 1159-60 (1992) (Proposal 3).

Even while restrictively interpreting OPM regulations that required higher-level approval of awards, FLRA allowed some negotiated limits on managerial discretion concerning decisions on performance awards in AFGE Local 1409 and Dept. of Army, Aberdeen Proving Ground Support Activity, 38 FLRA 747, 748, 751 (1990). The proposal was that employees receiving ratings higher than fully satisfactory be nominated for performance awards set within a range of percentages of employees' salaries and that their nominations be forwarded to the commanding officer and would "normally be approved by the Commander or his designee." Further, "[p]rior to disapproval for cause the Commander (or his designee) will notify the Union of the disapproval and discuss the matter." The proposal was generally negotiable, with one exception--disapproving the specific identification of the agency official responsible for making that determination.
