g. Selective Prosecution

Related to the defense of disparate penalties is the assertion of selective prosecution, a defense the Board has not yet accepted. There is no harmful error if an otherwise proper action against an employee is the first to reach the stage of adverse action despite the involvement of other employees. Scarberry v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 246, 248 (1984); see Thompson v. USPS, 596 F. Supp. 628, 634 (W.D. Va. 1984) (sustaining a discharge for removal of items from the mail for personal use: "The fact that other employees were not caught is not a legitimate defense, and it cannot lead to the conclusion that his punishment was pretextual."); Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 37 MSPR 659, 664 (1988) (agency made a reasonable effort by conducting an investigation into thefts; the agency should not be precluded from taking action because the discipline of others may be warranted for reasons yet unknown to the agency (citing Ferguson, post)); Kuhlmann v. DHHS, 10 MSPR 356, 358 (1982) (declining to accept a mitigation argument in a Chapter 75 performance case: "The only disparity in the appellant's treatment is that her case was the first to reach the stage of an adverse action.").

There is no requirement that an agency exert all possible efforts to identify each and every employee for potential disciplinary action. It is sufficient that the agency make a reasonable effort. If the appellant's removal is warranted on the basis of the sustained charge and past record of disciplinary action, the agency should not be precluded from taking action only because the discipline of other employees may be warranted for reasons yet unknown to the agency. Ferguson v. USPS, 19 MSPR 52, 55 (1984) (record did not indicate that the agency investigation or identification process was based on negligence, bad faith, or discriminatory motive); see Gomez v. DOJ, 36 MSPR 56, 62 n.6 (1988) ("There is no requirement that an agency exert all possible efforts to identify each employee for whom potential disciplinary action may be proposed. The agency should not be precluded from taking action simply because the discipline of other employees may also be warranted for reasons not yet known to the agency."); cf. Golden v. USPS, 60 MSPR 268, 273-74 (1994) (rejecting a discrimination defense based on the alleged conduct of other employees when the appellant did not prove that management officials were aware of that conduct and then chose not to pursue disciplinary action); Newberry v. USPS, 49 MSPR 348, 354 (1991) (arbitration review: there was no evidence that an agency deciding official knew of other employees' allegedly similar misconduct); Bernal v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 283, 285 (1984) ("[A]ppellant failed to establish that prior to preparation for the hearing on appellant's appeal, Airman Edgar's use of marijuana was known to agency officials.").

If there is a showing of some selectivity by the agency, the concern is then whether the actions of others are of the same serious nature as those charged against the appellant or, alternatively, whether the agency can be said to have condoned the appellant's actions. Bixler v. USDA, 19 MSPR 631 (1984) (Table); see Richardson v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 30, 32 (1982) ("While appellant may have been the only individual who had all of her work reviewed...the record contains ample evidence to establish that the agency had a proper basis for proceeding in this manner.... [T]he record fails to indicate that any other employee had difficulty meeting the 80 percent quality standard.").
