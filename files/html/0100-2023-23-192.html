4. Election Between Enforcement and Rescission; Pleading in the Alternative

Strange v. USPS, 48 MSPR 674 (1991), offered guidance to appellants and AJs who are confused as to how to treat efforts by appellants to either enforce or rescind settlements. Strange suggested that if the appellant seeks to set aside an agreement because of fraud or mutual mistake, the proper route is the PFR. If the issue is one of enforcement of the agreement rather than the validity of the settlement, an enforcement petition filed at the regional level is appropriate. If the appellant complains that the failure of the agency to abide by the agreement evidences fraud in the formation of the agreement, the Board may treat the matter as one of enforcement rather than validity of the agreement. See Dial v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 219, 221 (1992).

Under Strange, if it is unclear what the appellant seeks, a PFR may be remanded to the AJ to determine whether the appellant is seeking reinstatement of the appeal by setting aside the agreement or whether he is seeking compliance, 48 MSPR at 676-78:

When an administrative judge has dismissed an appellant's appeal pursuant to a settlement agreement, and the appellant believes that the agreement is invalid because it was obtained by fraud or because it was based on mutual mistake, he may file a petition for review of the initial decision dismissing his appeal.... If the appellant substantiates his allegation that the agreement is invalid, the appropriate remedy is reinstatement of the appeal.... If the employee is not challenging the validity of the agreement, however, but instead believes that the agency has failed to comply with the provisions of the agreement, he may file a petition for enforcement with the regional office that issued the initial decision on his appeal.... If the appellant substantiates his allegation that the agency has not complied with the agreement's provisions, the appropriate remedy is to order compliance with those provisions. See Soffer v. Department of the Army, 44 MSPR 402, 406-07 (1990).

...

...The administrative judge should determine whether the appellant is seeking reinstatement of his appeal from his...removal on the ground that the settlement agreement is invalid. He also should determine whether the appellant wishes to seek both remedies in the alternative and, if so, which remedy he prefers. If he finds that the appellant is seeking to enforce the existing agreement, he should review the appellant's allegations concerning that matter and issue a compliance initial decision.... If he finds that the appellant is seeking reinstatement of his appeal, he should issue a supplemental initial decision addressing the issue of the validity of the agreement. If he finds that the appellant is seeking each remedy in the alternative, he should consider the secondary request only if the appellant does not prevail on his primary request.[4]

[4] We note that the appellant has requested a hearing.... The administrative judge should grant his request if he finds that there is a dispute concerning any fact material to the matters at issue....

Sixeas v. VA, DC-4324-15-0385-I-1 (NP 9/9/2016), determined there was nothing amiss about the alternate pleading scheme:

In his petition for review of the compliance initial decision, the appellant does not contest the administrative judge's finding that she lacked authority to enforce the settlement agreement because the QSI provision of the agreement was not authorized by law, and we discern no basis to disturb that finding. See Day v. Department of the Air Force, 78 M.S.P.R. 364, 367 (1998) (determining that the Board could not enforce a provision of a settlement agreement requiring an agency to provide an appellant with a combination of benefits that was not authorized by law); Stipp, 61 M.S.P.R. at 420 (finding that the Board lacked authority to enforce a provision of a settlement agreement that was not authorized by law).

Instead, the appellant argues that the administrative judge erred in requiring him to file a petition for review of the initial decision in the merits appeal to set aside the settlement agreement and reinstate the merits appeal. He contends that this process was "roundabout and time-consuming" and that the administrative judge should have invalidated the settlement agreement and reinstated his appeal during compliance proceedings. Id. We disagree.

Generally, an attack on the validity of a settlement agreement must be made in the form of a petition for review of the initial decision dismissing the case as settled. Weldon v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 478, ¶ 5 (2013); Hazelton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 357, ¶ 8 (2009). However, when a petition for enforcement "unmistakably" challenges the validity of a settlement agreement, the Board will treat it as a petition for review of the initial decision dismissing the appeal as settled. Miller v. Department of the Army, 112 M.S.P.R. 689, ¶ 12 (2009). Here, the appellant's petition for enforcement did not unmistakably challenge the validity of the settlement agreement, but instead, requested that the Board enforce the settlement agreement. Consequently, we discern no error in the administrative judge requiring the appellant to file a petition for review of the initial decision in the merits appeal if he wanted to rescind the settlement agreement and reinstate his appeal.

When the appellant's enforcement petition is unclear as to the remedy requested, i.e., enforcement or rescission, it is appropriate for the AJ to conduct a conference with the parties "to fully examine the legal issues as well as the practical considerations involved" in the choice to permit the appellant to make an informed decision about her preferred remedy for the agency's breach of the settlement agreement. Womack v. USPS, 63 MSPR 213, 218 (1994).

It is now recognized that rescission, sought through enforcement proceedings, is justified by a material breach of a settlement, Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 77 MSPR 453, 458 (1998):

The Board has held that a material breach is a breach which relates to a matter of vital importance, or goes to the essence of the contract. Littlejohn v. Department of the Air Force, 69 MSPR 59, 62 (1995) (citing 5 Arthur L. Corbin, Corbin on Contracts § 1104 (1964))....

Where there has been a material breach of the settlement agreement, the non-breaching party has the option of enforcing the terms of the agreement or rescinding it and going forward with the original claim. Baig v. Department of the Navy, 66 MSPR 269, 277 (1995), aff'd, 64 F.3d 677 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). We therefore find the agency has breached the settlement agreement, and the appellant is entitled to rescind the agreement and reinstate his appeal.

See Burks v. Dept. of Interior, 87 MSPR 641, 644-45 ¶ 7 (2001) (determining that several delays and problems with the implementation of a settlement did not constitute material breaches of the agreement).

The parties may set their own terms concerning rescission of an agreement, Graff v. Dept. of Air Force, 39 MSPR 639, 644 (1989):

Paragraph 3d of the settlement agreement states:

The parties agree that if the Agency does not carry out or rescinds any action specified by this agreement for any reason not attributable to the acts or conduct of Appellant, Mr. Ronald L. Graff, the Appellant will have the right to reinstate his appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board for further proceedings.

Because the settlement agreement provides for reinstatement of the appeal as a remedy if the agency does not comply with the agreement, we find that the appellant may reinstate his appeal if the agency fails to comply with the agreement as required by this Opinion and Order.

The parties may structure their agreement to provide that the only remedy for the appellant is specific enforcement, precluding rescission of the agreement by the appellant as a result of an agency breach. The Board will enforce that provision. See Young v. USPS, 113 MSPR 609, 628-29 ¶ 45, 2010 MSPB 92 (2010).

It may occur that there is no effective remedy for the breach of a settlement agreement. In Markey v. Dept. of Transp., 110 MSPR 196, 202-03 ¶ 13, 2008 MSPB 238 (2008) (overruled in part, Shirley v. Dept. of Interior, 120 MSPR 195, 206 n.6, 2013 MSPB 76 (2013)), the agency breached a nondisclosure clause. The appellant did not desire the agreement to be rescinded. There was no remedy:

Despite the agency's material breach of the settlement agreement, we find that there is no available enforcement remedy that would cure the agency's breach. In most cases, "[w]hen a party to a settlement agreement materially breaches the agreement, the non-breaching party may elect either to enforce the agreement or to rescind the agreement and reinstate the appeal." Lutz v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 695, ¶ 11 (2008). Moreover, "[t]he choice of seeking enforcement or rescission of a settlement agreement belongs to the non-breaching party, and the breaching party does not have the right to select the remedy." Id., ¶ 11. However, "the Board has recognized that in some instances, the only available option to the nonbreaching party is rescission." Powell, 98 M.S.P.R. 398, ¶ 14; see Diehl, 82 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 13 ("The breach of [a] nondisclosure provision is not the type of breach that may be remedied by a Board order of enforcement...."). In her response to the administrative judge's recommendation, the appellant indicated that she does not want the settlement agreement to be rescinded. Given the appellant's request that the settlement agreement not be rescinded, we identify no viable enforcement remedy to impose in this case.[*] In the absence of a viable enforcement remedy, we DISMISS the appellant's petition for enforcement.

[*] The appellant also requests other forms of relief, including the imposition of sanctions against the agency and the agency's counsel, and permitting the settlement agreement to remain in force while simultaneously allowing the appellant to pursue her IRA appeal. It is well settled that the imposition of sanctions "is a means to enforce compliance," and in light of our findings in this case, imposing sanctions would be inappropriate. Mavronikolas v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 113, 116 (1992), aff'd, 979 F.2d 216 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). Further, we find no legal basis, and the appellant has identified none, for allowing the appellant to pursue her IRA appeal without rescinding the settlement agreement.

If during enforcement proceedings, the agency cures the breach, the Board may decline to honor a request to rescind the agreement, Wine v. Dept. of Interior, DA-0752-18-0116-X-1 (NP 2/10/2023):

Although the appellant seeks to rescind the settlement agreement and reinstate his removal appeal due to the original breach of the agreement, we hold that such relief is inappropriate where, as here, the agency has cured the breach and the appellant has received the full benefit of his bargain. See Tretchick v. Department of Transportation, 109 F.3d 749, 752 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (rejecting suggestion that there is an "absolute right" to rescission in response to a breach and rejecting rescission where any purported breach had been cured); King v. Department of the Navy, No. 98-3342, 1999 WL 37406, at *2 (Fed. Cir. Jan. 12, 1999) (unpublished) (affirming denial of rescission where the agency had cured its breach of the settlement agreement 6 years later, and the appellant provided no evidence that she was harmed by the breach or the delay in curing it); cf. Lutz v. U.S. Postal Service, 485 F.3d 1377, 1381-82 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (finding material breach potentially justifying rescission where "negative statements contained in" the agency forms "prejudiced the disability proceedings" and resulted in denial of benefits). Thus, assuming arguendo that the agency's breach was material--which the administrative judge did not address, and we need not now decide--we find that, as in Tretchick, rescission is inappropriate because the agency cured its breach of the agreement and the appellant received all the benefits to which the agreement entitled him.
