broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 21: Union Rights, Bargaining Rights and Procedures, Section F: Joint Committees, Subsection 05: Job Content; Work Planning

5. Job Content; Work Planning

A union could not require negotiation of a proposal requiring that curriculum and selection of textbooks be determined by curriculum review committees with required participation of teachers. The committees were established to facilitate decisionmaking about how work would be performed, i.e., the conduct of teaching. Negotiation over composition of the committee was equivalent to bargaining over the work itself. Panama Canal Fed'n of Teachers, Local 29 and DODDS, 19 FLRA 814, 815-16 (1985) (Proposals 1 and 8). Proposals calling on principals and regional directors to establish committees, and requiring the attendance of certain officials at local level teachers conferences, were beyond the scope of bargaining. They infringed on the right to assign work. The same was true as to a proposal for union representation through employees on statutorily established School Advisory Councils convened to make recommendations and take action on budget and curriculum. Panama Canal Fed'n of Teachers, Local 29 and DODDS, 19 FLRA 814, 818-19 (1985) (Proposals 9 and 11). Proposals requiring that certain unit and nonunit employees and community leaders be involved in planning specific agency programs either infringed the right to assign work or did not concern conditions of employment of unit employees. Panama Canal Fed'n of Teachers, Local 29 and DODDS, 19 FLRA 814, 819 (1985) (Proposal 6).

A negotiable proposal established a joint committee to review and evaluate the impact and implementation of an agency manual that appeared to establish some job responsibilities by defining the content of an educational program for students taught by unit employees. The committee's review could lead to bargaining proposals by the union that would subsequently be subject to negotiability review under 5 USC 7117, Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 28 FLRA 700, 706 (1987) (Proposal 3):

In significant part this is a proposal to bargain in the future, to the extent consistent with applicable laws and regulations, over whatever matters related to the Manual the parties may decide, based on the advice and recommendations of the joint committees. The proposal does not itself either mandate changes in the Manual or its impact and implementation, or require negotiation over any particular changes. It only would establish a procedure which might, depending on the results of the committees' reviews and the parties' reactions to them, culminate in proposals for negotiations. Based on the foregoing, we find that the record does not establish that the proposal interferes with any Agency rights under section 7106. If agreed upon, this proposal in the future could give rise to bargaining proposals that are nonnegotiable because, for example, they would impermissibly interject the Union into management's deliberative process leading to the exercise of its statutory rights. This proposal, however, does not purport to obligate the Agency to bargain regarding such new proposals....
