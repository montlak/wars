2. Proof, Rebuttal of Past Discipline

Fairness requires the Board not to rely on unproved charges. If the Board's standards governing past discipline are met, the agency need not prove the merits of the prior disciplinary actions. Stephens v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3020); see Moore v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-15-0289-I-1 (NP 8/25/2016) ("When the employee was informed of the action in writing, the action is a matter of record, and the employee was permitted to dispute the charges before a higher level of authority than the one that imposed the discipline, the Board's review is limited to determining whether the prior action was clearly erroneous. Bolling v. Department of the Air Force, 9 M.S.P.R. 335, 339-40 (1981). The mere fact that an employee has challenged a prior disciplinary action does not alter this mode of analysis. Guzman-Muelling v. Social Security Administration, 91 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 15 (2002)."). If due process protections were not provided for past discipline, the burden of proof is on the agency to support its previous action by a preponderance of the evidence, just as it must do for any other element of the charges. McConnell v. Dept. of Navy, 9 MSPR 22, 24 (1981). If the agency relies on uncharged incidents of past misconduct to determine a penalty, the appellant may deny that misconduct, and the Board must then analyze evidence to determine whether it occurred. See Thomas v. USPS, 96 MSPR 179, 184-85 ¶¶ 9-12 (2004) (uncharged past misconduct was referenced in the proposal, so that the appellant had the opportunity to respond to the allegation).

If an employee was given due process in an earlier proceeding, the merits of the charges underlying the past disciplinary actions need not be relitigated. The elements of administrative due process necessary to preclude relitigation of previously imposed discipline are (1) the employee must have been informed of the action in writing; (2) the action must be a matter of record; and (3) the employee must have been allowed to challenge the charges at a level higher than the official who imposed the discipline. If the employee has not had a prior opportunity to dispute the charges and the agency offers no proof concerning the prior disciplinary action, the action may not properly be considered in reviewing a penalty. Howard v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 205, 206, 6 MSPB 180 (1981). If the past discipline does not meet the requirements established by the Board, the agency must prove each element of its case on each disciplinary action charged. Hanson v. TVA, 18 MSPR 705, 707 (1984) (letter of warning: there was no evidence that the appellant was given an opportunity to appeal or grieve the prior charge to a higher level than the authority that imposed the discipline). The record must contain documentary evidence showing that the appellant was informed of the prior actions in writing, that the actions were a matter of record, and that the appellant was permitted to dispute the charges before an authority different from the authority that took the actions against him. See Schoeffler v. USDA, 47 MSPR 80, 89 (1991). The information constituting the record of the past disciplinary action appears, first, as part of the evidence file given to the employee that supports the adverse action proposal and, second, in the agency response to the appeal.

If the agency relies on past discipline, Board review of that action is restricted to the record, assuming the opportunity for challenge, and the Board discounts the prior action only if it is convinced that a mistake was committed. Ott v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 642, 646 (1983). Guzman-Muelling v. SSA, 91 MSPR 601, 606-07 ¶ 15 (2002), reiterated that if the prior discipline could have been challenged, Board review is limited to review of the record created by that challenge:

...The Board has limited its review of a prior disciplinary action to determining whether the action is clearly erroneous, provided that the employee was informed of the action in writing, the action is a matter of record, and the employee was permitted to dispute the charges before a higher level of authority than the one that imposed the discipline.... Inasmuch as those three criteria were met in both prior suspensions in this case, review of the actions is limited to determining whether they were clearly erroneous. In making that determination, the Board's review is limited to the record, and no new evidence or argument, other than the appellant's reasons for the challenge, is admissible. On review, the prior challenged actions here are not clearly erroneous in that they do not leave the Board with the firm conviction that a mistake has been made. See Bolling, 8 MSPB 658, 9 M.S.P.R. at 340. Accordingly, both prior suspensions may be properly considered as penalty enhancing.[3] See Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 306 (1981).

[3] The appellant challenges Bolling's "clearly erroneous" standard as being inconsistent with the requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B) that the Board sustain an agency's decision only if it is supported by a preponderance of the evidence. This is the same argument advanced by Justice Ginsburg in her concurring opinion in Gregory. A majority of the Court found that the adequacy of "Bolling review" was "not before us."

Because an appellant may not relitigate prior disciplinary actions in an appeal if due process protections were available, the AJ properly prohibits the questioning of agency witnesses by appellant's counsel concerning the merits of the prior actions. Wallace v. USDA, 15 MSPR 344, 377 (1983). If the AJ does not properly analyze the evidence concerning the Bolling criteria, the Board does so on PFR. See Mahnken v. USPS, 34 MSPR 1, 7-8 (1987). The Board may acknowledge past discipline and diminish its importance if the current offense is mitigated and the past discipline is based on offenses similar to the current offense and could be mitigated on the same basis as the current offense. See Bond v. Dept. of Energy, 82 MSPR 534, 545 ¶ 30 (1999) (current offense of sleeping on duty was mitigated on the basis of the side effects of medication; past discipline was based on similar misconduct).

If past discipline, relied on to support current discipline, is challenged as retaliatory, the challenge is considered as part of the assertion that the current discipline is retaliatory under the "convincing mosaic" analysis applied to a Title VII affirmative defense. See Rhee v. Dept. of Treasury, 117 MSPR 640, 650-59 ¶¶ 19-37, 2012 MSPB 26 (2012). But cf. Walton v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-15-0550-I-1 (NP 4/27/2023) ("'convincing mosaic' merely describes one type of evidence that, either alone or in combination with other evidence, an appellant may introduce in support of his claim. See Pridgen, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶ 24."). [Refer later in this chapter to "Reprisal," and to Chapter 12, "Convincing Mosaic; Evidence Considered as a Whole; Comparators."]
