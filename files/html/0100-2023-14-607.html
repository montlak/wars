broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 19: Critical Elements and Performance Standards, Section B: General Definitions, Subsection 02: Number of Performance Levels

2. Number of Performance Levels

The number of performance levels for both individual job levels and overall performance is nonnegotiable. The number of performance levels directly affects how precisely management establishes and communicates job requirements, the range of judgments management can make regarding performance in the context of appraisals, and the range of rewards and sanctions management can apply to that performance. AFSCME Council 26 and DOJ, 13 FLRA 578, 580 (1984). Proposals were not properly the subject of negotiations that set the number of rating levels. AFGE Local 3028 and DHHS, Pub. Health Serv., Alaska Area Native Health Serv., 13 FLRA 697, 700-01 (1984); see NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 38 FLRA 456, 472-73 (1990) (Proposal 5) (nonnegotiable, in absence of an arrangements argument, was Proposal 5, requiring ratings to be issued on five-level scale). It was immaterial that the proposal, requiring five levels, did no more than track OPM regulations. Incorporation into the contract of regulatory limits required management to comply with those terms irrespective of later regulatory revisions. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA 422, 426-27 (1987) (distinguishing proposal that incorporated regulatory provisions by reference). A proposal establishing the particular levels of performance and individual job elements required to achieve a particular summary rating for overall performance was not within the duty to bargain. It was inconsistent with management's rights to assign work and to direct employees. AFSCME Council 26 and DOJ, 13 FLRA 578, 578-79 (1984); see SEIU Local 150 and VAMC, Milwaukee, 35 FLRA 521, 531-33 (1990) (Provision 5 nonnegotiable: setting number of rating levels and criteria for evaluations); NFFE Local 1461 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Observatory, 26 FLRA 808, 810-12 (1987) (Proposal 2, identifying marginal and outstanding levels, and defining use of noncritical elements, was nonnegotiable; proposal was not appropriate arrangement); Tidewater Va. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Navy Pub. Works Ctr., Norfolk, 25 FLRA 3, 10, 12-13 (1987) (nonnegotiable: proposal requiring two or more examples of performance exceeding highly satisfactory for each critical element rated outstanding).

Nonnegotiable were proposals that set levels of performance, e.g., marginal, phrased in terms of whether the employee has barely met established standards, and outstanding, phrased to embrace the employee who has significantly exceeded the established standard. Dept. of Air Force, Lowry AFB and AFGE Local 1974, 22 FLRA 464, 466-67 (1986). By establishing the level of performance required to achieve a particular summary rating, a proposal violated management's rights to assign work and direct employees. Illinois Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, N. Chicago, 27 FLRA 714, 735 (1987) (citing POPA and PTO, 25 FLRA 384 (1987)). It was immaterial that the levels sought to be negotiated were about the same as those defined by agency regulations. The proposal interfered with the right to make changes in the levels during the life of the agreement. AFGE Local 1858 and Army Ordnance Missile and Munitions Ctr. and Sch., Redstone Arsenal, 26 FLRA 102, 104-05 (1987); see Philadelphia Metal Trades Council and Dept. of Navy, Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, 38 FLRA 59, 62-64 (1990) (proposals were not negotiable because they mirrored agency instructions; proposals tending to ameliorate the effects of tougher, revised standards will be subject to analysis under appropriate arrangements test; if proposal does no more than prevent the revisions, it will not likely be seen by FLRA as conferring benefit on employees outweighing negative impact on management).

AFGE Council of GSA Locals and GSA, 55 FLRA 449, 452-53 (1999), rejected the argument that a proposal setting the number of performance levels constituted a method or means of performing agency work within the agency's permissive bargaining authority:

[A]side from emphasizing that the proposal constitutes a "measure" used for the accomplishment of the Agency's work, the Union does not explain how the proposal is encompassed within the Authority's definition of the terms "methods" and "means." Proposals concerning the number and designation of rating levels do not concern how an agency performs its work or what an agency uses to accomplish its work. Rather, such proposals concern how an agency evaluates the manner in which its employees perform the work to which they have been assigned. The Authority has consistently held that an agency's determinations as to performance standards and rating levels concern the work objectives for employees.... An agency's determination of the methods and means of performing work, on the other hand, concerns how employees will do their work, and what they will use, to accomplish those objectives. See American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1164 and Social Security Administration, District Office, New Bedford, Massachusetts, 54 FLRA 1327, 1350-52 (1998) (plan for rotational assignment of claims processing a methods and means of performing work within the meaning of section 7106(b)(1)).

The foregoing case law was absent from discussion in AFGE Local 3172 and DHHS, SSA, Vallejo Dist. Office, 35 FLRA 1276, 1284, 1285-86 (1990), requiring bargaining over Proposal 6: "Numeric performance standards will be developed for level 4 for all GJT's [Generic Job Tasks] in all positions where numerics are defined for levels 2 and 3":

[T]he proposal does not compel the Agency to use numeric, or any other specific types of standards, and does not dictate the number of levels to be used in evaluating employee performance. That is, if the Agency employed an evaluation system incorporating a number other than five levels or described those levels in other than a numeric form, Proposal 6 would not apply....

...

...5 C.F.R. § 430.204(e) neither bars nor requires written performance standards for more than one level of performance. Therefore, Proposal 6, calling for a numeric definition of level 4 where numeric standards are stated for levels 2 and 3, is not inconsistent with applicable regulations.

AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 60 FLRA 533, 535 (2004), declared nonnegotiable Proposal 2 and Proposal 3, setting rating levels for critical elements and levels of overall performance:

The Authority has consistently held that proposals establishing the rating levels or criteria for performance evaluations affect management's rights to direct employees and assign work. See Phila. Metal Trades Council, 38 FLRA 59, 61 (1990); Serv. & Hosp. Employees Int'l Union, Local 150, 35 FLRA 521, 531-33 (1990) (Hosp. Employees Int'l); and Am. Fed'n of State, County & Mun. Employees, AFL-CIO, Council 26, 13 FLRA 578 (1984). Applying this precedent, the Authority has found that proposals establishing particular levels of performance in individual job elements required to achieve a particular summary performance rating are outside the duty to bargain. See National Education Association, Overseas Education Association, Fort Bragg Ass'n of Educators, 53 FLRA 898 (1997).

There is no dispute that Proposal 2 would require supervisors to rate employees using one of the four rating levels set forth in the proposal. As such, the proposal affects the Agency's rights to direct employees and assign work. See Hosp. Employees Int'l, 35 FLRA at 533; and AFGE, AFL-CIO, Local 1940, 16 FLRA 816, 821-22 (1984). There also is no dispute that Proposal 3 establishes the particular levels of performance required to achieve a particular summary performance rating. Consequently, this proposal also affects the Agency's rights to direct employees and assign work. See AFGE, Local 225, 56 FLRA 686, 687-88 (2000); and NAGE, Local R1-144, Fed. Union of Scientists & Eng'rs, 38 FLRA 456, 473 (1990).

The Union does not assert that Proposals 2 and 3 are negotiable procedures or appropriate arrangements under § 7106(b)(2) or (3) of the Statute. Rather, the Union argues only that the proposals are negotiable because similar wording was included in the parties' 1992 agreement. The Authority has long held that "[t]he inclusion of an identical provision in a previous contract is not dispositive of the issue of whether th[at] provision [or any identical proposal] is inconsistent with applicable law and regulations." See Portsmouth Fed'l Employees Metal Trades Council, 34 FLRA 1150, 1157 (1990) (Metal Trades Council). Consequently, we conclude that Proposals 2 and 3 are outside the Agency's duty to bargain.

If the agency decides to use a given number of rating levels, nothing bars a proposal ascribing to those levels descriptions that follow the OPM regulations. Specifically, POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 77-79 (1993), approved as negotiable Provisions 40 and 41 requiring the employer to use for a five-level rating system the terms "outstanding, commendable, fully successful, acceptable, and unacceptable (or unsatisfactory)," and for a three-level system, "outstanding, fully successful, and unacceptable (or unsatisfactory)."
