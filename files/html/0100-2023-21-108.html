c. Delay in Notice to OPM of Annuity Election

The time limits for making a survivor annuity election following retirement and upon marriage were summarized, Larson v. OPM, 93 MSPR 433, 436 ¶ 7 (2003):

The applicable statute and regulations provide that, where a Federal annuitant is unmarried at the time of his retirement under FERS, but marries after retirement, he may elect to provide a survivor annuity for his spouse within two years after his marriage. 5 U.S.C. § 8416(c)(1); 5 C.F.R. § 842.612. The two-year deadline for making a post-retirement election for a survivor annuity under FERS is basically indistinguishable in substance from the two-year deadline for making such an election under CSRS. Compare 5 U.S.C. § 8339(k)(2)(A) and 5 C.F.R. § 831.631 with 5 U.S.C. § 8416(c)(1) and 5 C.F.R. § 842.612. Here, it is undisputed that the appellant did not elect a survivor annuity for his spouse before the end of the two-year deadline under 5 U.S.C. § 8416(c)(1). t is further undisputed that the appellant received annual notices sent to all annuitants by OPM informing him of his right to make an election for a survivor annuity within the statutory two-year deadline under FERS. See Brush v. Office of Personnel Management, 982 F.2d 1554, 1559-60 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Balkovec v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 621, ¶¶ 2, 7 (1999).

Assuming notice from OPM, the requirement to notify OPM of a survivor annuity election within two years of marriage cannot be waived under CSRS, even for an annuitant who may be mentally incompetent, Lee v. OPM, 118 MSPR 604, 605-07 ¶ 4-7, 2012 MSPB 114 (2012):

An individual seeking retirement benefits bears the burden of proving entitlement to those benefits by preponderant evidence. Cheeseman v. Office of Personnel Management, 791 F.2d 138, 140-41 (Fed. Cir. 1986); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)] . To meet this burden in this case, the appellant must show that he elected to provide a survivor annuity for his spouse "in a signed writing received" by OPM within 2 years after his marriage. See 5 U.S.C. § 8339(k)(2)(A); Vincent v. Office of Personnel Management, 78 M.S.P.R. 307, 310 (1998). In addition, the Board has recognized three possible bases for waiving a filing deadline prescribed by statute or regulation: (1) the statute or regulation may provide for a waiver under specified circumstances; (2) an agency's affirmative misconduct may preclude enforcement of the deadline under the doctrine of equitable estoppel; and (3) an agency's failure to provide a notice of rights and the applicable filing deadline, where such notice is required by statute or regulation, may warrant a waiver of the deadline. See Perez Peraza v. Office of Personnel Management, 114 M.S.P.R. 457, ¶ 7 (2010).

In this appeal, the appellant has not alleged that an agency's affirmative misconduct precluded him from filing a timely request for a survivor annuity, nor has he asserted that OPM failed to provide the required notice of rights and the applicable filing deadline. The dispositive issue, therefore, is whether a statute or regulation provides a basis for waiving the filing deadline. As noted above, the appellant has primarily argued throughout this appeal that he is entitled to a waiver of the 2-year filing deadline on the basis of mental incompetence.

However, our reviewing court has unequivocally held that 5 U.S.C. § 8339(k)(2)(A)[2] does not permit waiver of the filing deadline based on mental incapacity. See Schoemakers v. Office of Personnel Management, 180 F.3d 1377, 1381-82 (Fed. Cir. 1999); see also Dorsey v. Office of Personnel Management, 587 F.3d 1111, 1115 (Fed. Cir. 2009). It has found that statutory language unambiguous in its requirement that, to obtain a survivor's annuity upon post-retirement marriage, an annuitant must file a signed writing within 2 years after the marriage and that nothing in the language or the legislative history suggests that Congress intended to permit a waiver of this deadline because of an annuitant's mental condition or incapacity. Schoemakers, 180 F.3d at 1382. As it noted in Schoemakers, "[w]here Congress intended to permit the waiver of filing deadlines for government annuitants because of their mental problems, it explicitly so provided." Id. Observing that another CSRS provision, 5 U.S.C. § 8337(b), specifically provides for such a waiver of the filing deadline for disability retirement applications, the court stated that the unequivocal requirement of filing within 2 years of marriage in section 8339(k)(2)(A) "strongly indicates that Congress did not intend to permit waiver of that requirement because of the annuitant's mental condition." Schoemakers, 180 F.3d at 1382. Based upon this precedent, we must conclude that the appellant has failed to show that he is entitled to waiver of the deadline under statute or regulation.

Cartsounis v. OPM, 91 MSPR 502, 505 ¶ 6-7 (2002), declined to waive the two year filing period for electing a former spouse annuity based on an allegation of lack of notice from OPM or the mental state of the employee who needed to file the notice:

In attempting to meet its burden of proving that it sent the appellant the required annual notice, OPM submitted a February 8, 1989 affidavit by James Bryson, Chief of its Administrative Management Branch. Mr. Bryson's affidavit states that he managed the printing and distributing of OPM's notices and forms for the Retirement and Insurance Group, and that general notices regarding survivor elections required by Public Law No. 95-317 were sent to all annuitants in December of 1986, 1987, and 1988. He further explained that OPM's computer master annuity roll contains separate addresses for purposes of sending payments and for sending correspondence, and that beginning with the December 1986 mailing, OPM sent notices to all annuitants in the same manner by computer generating a tape listing of all annuitants and their correspondence addresses, sending the list to a private contracting firm specializing in mass mailing, and using that tape to print the name and address directly on franked, printed notices that were then mailed by the contractor. Mr. Bryson further stated that all mailing lists were generated entirely by machine from the annuity rolls, and that a notice was sent to "each and every" annuitant on the rolls at the time of each mailing. OPM also provided a copy of the December 1986 notice.

In Schoemakers v. Office of Personnel Management, 180 F.3d 1377, 1380-81 (Fed. Cir. 1999), our reviewing court held that a virtually identical affidavit satisfied OPM's burden of proving both that it sent the required annual notice and the contents of that notice. We, therefore, find that OPM's similar affidavit and notice here satisfies its burden of proof on these two issues, and the burden shifts to the appellant to show that he did not receive the annual notice. See id. at 1381; Brush, 982 F.2d at 1561. The appellant claims that he suffers from depression, that he "accidentally" discovered the notice requirement when he called OPM to provide OPM with a change of address, and that OPM should have made the annual notice of the election requirement "more obvious." Appeal File, Tab 1. The appellant's mental state, however, does not show that he did not receive the notice or provide a basis for waiving the statutory election deadline. See Schoemakers, 180 F.3d at 1381-82. Further, the appellant's claim that the notice should have been "more obvious" also does not support the conclusion that he did not receive the notice. Accordingly, we find that OPM met its burden of proving that it sent the required annual notice and properly denied the appellant's untimely attempt to elect a survivor annuity for his former spouse.
