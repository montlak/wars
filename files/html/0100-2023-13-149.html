a. Assistance in Securing Counsel

Jones v. DHUD, 87 MSPR 269 (2000), determined that when, in the course of an adverse action appeal, it becomes apparent to the AJ that the pro se appellant may be mentally disabled, the AJ may require the agency to consider initiating disability retirement if the conditions of 5 CFR 844.202 (2022) are met and the AJ may be obliged to make efforts to secure the assistance of a pro bono attorney under French v. OPM, 37 MSPR 496 (1988). Under Jones, 87 MSPR at 273-75 ¶¶ 9-13:

Section 8451(a)(1)(A) of title 5 of the U.S. Code is the statutory authority for agencies to file disability retirement applications for their employees under the Federal Employees' Retirement System, and the regulation at 5 CFR § 844.202 sets the conditions under which the obligation arises. In Harris v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 142 F.3d 1463 (Fed. Cir. 1998), the Federal Circuit addressed their requirements. That appeal arose from the settlement of an adverse action appeal, and the court concluded in it that the language of the regulation, the intent of OPM, and the comparable regulation OPM had promulgated under the Civil Service Retirement System "all mandate that section 844.202 be construed to impose a duty on the agency for the benefit of its employees when the conditions described in the regulation are met." 142 F.3d at 1470. Based on its own review of the record evidence, it concluded that the regulatory conditions may be satisfied in that case because the record suggested that the employing agency had "reason to believe" that the appellant was incapable of making a decision to file an application for disability retirement. It stated, too, that its decision to vacate the Board's decision requires the Board to follow the procedures it set in French [v. OPM, 37 MSPR 496 (1988)] to determine whether the agency was in breach of its obligation. In that regard, it noted that "[w]hen the Board is on notice of a person's likely incapacity in connection with a disability retirement application, it must enforce the rules it established in French for such cases." Id. at 1471.

Thus, while French arose in the context of a disability retirement appeal and its requirements apply directly only to such appeals, the Board must also consider those requirements when the record before the employing agency or before it indicates that an appellant may be incapable of filing for her own disability retirement.

As the administrative judge ruled, this record contains evidence that the regulatory conditions may be met. The appellant's submissions, several of which are repeated copies of earlier filings, contain citations to apparently nonexistent requirements, make unrealistic demands, and otherwise indicate that she may be suffering from a mental inability to act in her own best interests in connection with the question of disability retirement. The record before the agency, too, while devoid of any such writings from the appellant, contained indications of aberrant behavior. The appellant had apparently been a well-respected, long-term employee of the agency, with no prior history of discipline, and was a Program Manager at the GS-12 level. Yet, she remained absent from the agency for more than a year and out of contact with it for long periods of that time, including after her removal had been proposed, when she was offered the opportunity to make written and oral replies. The agency's largely unsuccessful efforts to contact her indicate that it was aware, apparently through the appellant's mother, that the appellant was experiencing problems, and that it both offered to assist her in filing for disability retirement and considered filing on her behalf, even after her removal. In addition, the appellant's mother told the agency that she was attempting to have a guardian appointed for the appellant in connection with a possible benefits application. In the end, however, no application was filed.

Under the circumstances, the record supports the administrative judge's conclusion that it is appropriate in this case to inquire into whether the agency met any obligation it may have had to the appellant under 5 USC § 8451 and 5 CFR § 844.202. Should he find that the obligation arose in this case and that the agency is in breach of it, the agency should now file the application and OPM should process it "expediently and in good faith," Harris, 142 F.3d at 1471, with the goal of 'implementing section 844.202 in a manner which draws the Board, the [agency], and OPM together in a cooperative undertaking to assure that persons of likely mental incapacity will not suffer an impairment of their rights on account of their incapacity." Id., at 1471-72.

Because of this holding and the court's ruling in Harris, 142 F.3d at 1471, if the agency does not, on its own accord, file an application for the appellant, then the use of French procedures would be appropriate. We do not thereby decide whether Marbrey and its progeny should be overruled or whether the appointment of counsel should generally be required in adverse action appeals, since the ensuing procedure, although having its genesis in an adverse action appeal, will be under the retirement law and regulations. Unlike adverse action appeals, the nature of the proceeding directed here and in Harris, and as discussed in French, is nonadversarial, one in which all of the parties act in the appellant's best interest.
