5. Industry Practices

A union could not compel negotiation of a regulation that required management to establish and use certain positions traditionally defined and recognized throughout the maritime industry. The proposal was negotiable only at the election of the agency under § 7106(b)(1). Dist. No. 1, Pac. Coast Dist., Marine Eng'rs Beneficial Ass'n and Panama Canal Comm'n, 26 FLRA 390, 391 (1987); see Dist. No. 1, MEBA, Panama Canal Area and Panama Canal Comm'n, 49 FLRA 461, 467 (1994) (rejecting negotiability of Proposal 2 and 3, preserving for oilers work traditionally assigned to them). As to prevailing rate employees whose work practices were negotiated prior to August 1972, however, proposals that preserved their jurisdiction over defined tasks for employees within units were negotiable under § 704(a) of the Reform Act. The whole subject of work preservation for prevailing rate employees falling within the coverage of § 704 was exhaustively discussed in NFFE Local 1418 and USIA, Voice of Am., 37 FLRA 1385, 1386 (1990), concerning a proposal describing work to be performed exclusively by VOA radio broadcast technicians. That decision, however, was remanded to for reconsideration by USIA v. FLRA, 960 F.2d 165, 170 (D.C. Cir. 1992), directing FLRA to reconsider its decision in light of interpretations of § 704 offered by Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs v. FLRA, 887 F.2d 172, 175-76 (9th Cir. 1989), and Dept. of Interior v. FLRA, 908 F.2d 570, 577-78 (10th Cir. 1990). In its remand decision, NFFE Local 1418 and USIA, Voice of Am., 49 FLRA 1262, 1286-89 (1994), FLRA directed and accepted factfinding by one of its ALJs to resolve disputed factual issues necessary for its adjudication of the issues remanded by the court. FLRA decided that it was the congressional intent to authorize and require agencies that had, in accordance with prevailing practice, negotiated terms and conditions of employment benefits prior to August 19, 1972, to continue to negotiate with the exclusive representatives of their employees on those matters. In particular, the Act gave agencies and unions specific authorization to renew, extend, modify, or improve upon previously agreed-upon matters when negotiating a new agreement, without regard to any restrictions contained in the Reform Act. The authority to negotiate extended only to the specific term and condition of employment or other employment benefit subject to negotiations prior to August 1972.

If, based on consideration of the pertinent factors (text of relevant proposal or provision; evidence, including bargaining history, relating to the intent of the relevant proposal or provision; evidence as to the current and pre-1972 work situations of the unit; and the parties' contentions as to congressional intent as to the scope of a subject matter), FLRA determines that a current proposal concerns the same specific matter that was subject to negotiations prior to August of 1972, the subject-matter requirement of § 704(a) is met, and the parties may bargain over a current proposal that renews, extends, modifies, or improves upon that subject matter. FLRA concluded in its 1994 USIA decision that its approach on work preservation issues was consistent with that adopted by the Supreme Court in NLRB v. Int'l Longshoreman's Ass'n, 447 U.S. 490, 507 (1980), emphasizing an analysis of "all the surrounding circumstances," including "the nature of the work both before and after the innovation." Having defined its method of analysis, FLRA proceeded in the USIA litigation to review each proposal to determine whether the circumstances of the work prior to 1972 suggested that the work included in the proposal was a sufficient match to permit bargaining of the proposal. To the extent a match was not established, FLRA proceeded to determine whether the proposal interfered with management's right to assign work. [For more on the intricacies of § 704, refer later in this chapter to "Prevailing Rate Employees; Pay and Pay Practices."]
