3. Prehearing Submission

If the case is going to a hearing, the AJ requires the parties to prepare prehearing submissions. The requirements for preparation of the prehearing submissions will be explained in the AJ's hearing order. That order states the place, date, and time for the hearing; the order requires each party to prepare and submit a multipart prehearing submission; and the order sets and time and date for a telephonic prehearing conference. A typical hearing order will require each party to state the material facts and issues (including affirmative defenses), list any stipulations, identify witnesses, and enumerate exhibits. A hearing order should ensure that the parties have a fair opportunity to accomplish its directives. If an AJ directs an appellant to state how evidence and issues in that party's appeal differ from evidence and issues in another appeal raising similar issues but involving different parties, the AJ should ensure that the appellant is provided a copy of both the earlier decision of the Board and the evidence that supports that decision. Strauss v. OPM, 39 MSPR 132, 136-37 (1988).

The prehearing submission is important. It is inclusive: it identifies the issues and facts, witnesses and exhibits; for the hearing. It is preclusive: if significant areas of disputed facts, issues, witnesses, and exhibits are not identified in the prehearing submission, they may be excluded from further consideration in the appeal and cannot then be resurrected on PFR in the absence of extenuating circumstances, e.g., the AJ failed to provide proper notice of burdens of proof or jurisdictional requirements.

Care should be taken in writing the prehearing submission. Berman v. Dept. of Interior, 447 Fed. Appx. 186, 191 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP):

The Agency's "Prehearing Submission" to the Board characterized the issue before the Board as "[w]hether the Board should affirm the Agency's decision to remove [Berman] for being found by a court to have violated 18 U.S.C. § 209(a)." ...[W]e interpret the Agency's "Prehearing Submission" as judicially estopping the Agency from arguing that violation of Section 209(a) was not an element of the charged misconduct. See Trustees in Bankruptcy of N. Am. Rubber Thread Co. v. United States, 593 F.3d 1346, 1353-57 (Fed. Cir. 2010); Key Pharms. v. Hercon Labs Corp., 161 F.3d 709, 714-15 (Fed. Cir. 1998).

The prehearing submissions are reviewed and addressed by the AJ and the parties during a prehearing conference. The AJ usually issues a prehearing memorandum following the conference. That memorandum generally summarizes the issues, lists the witnesses approved to testify, and states whether or how exhibits are or will be admitted into the record. As a matter of practice nothing precludes a party from submitting to the AJ, in advance of the prehearing conference, an objection to some component of the opposing party's prehearing submission. The objection puts the AJ on notice that the issue needs to be explored during the prehearing conference. If the prehearing conference summary is incorrect, omits issues, fails properly to identify approved witnesses, misstates the treatment of exhibits, counsel should immediately file an objection and request reconsideration. Only by doing so are the errors preserved for PFR with the Board if they are not first corrected by the AJ. If the prehearing conference summary is issued too close in time to the hearing to permit a filing by mail, fax, or e-filing, counsel should make their objections known at the start of the hearing.

The point that a party must state all of its issues in the prehearing submission was dramatically made in an appeal resulting in the agency's inability to pursue impeachment evidence at the hearing because it failed to identify its line of inquiry in the prehearing proceedings, Morgan v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 380, 381-82 (1994):

At the hearing, the agency sought to challenge the truthfulness of certain assertions the appellant had made in his appeal regarding his work history. The agency raised this challenge by asking the appellant if various statements on the appeal form were true and then seeking to introduce evidence to impeach his affirmative replies. The administrative judge properly did not allow the agency to pursue these topics. As the administrative judge found, this impeachment was part of the agency's planned presentation and it had failed to give the appellant advance notice that these matters would be addressed at the hearing. The importance of giving the opposing party fair notice of evidence to be presented at the hearing had been fully addressed in the prehearing conference.

The AJ may exclude evidence not submitted within the time limit set by the prehearing conference memorandum. Privette v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 150, 152 (1993); see Douglass v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 1, 3 (1993) (AJ can set a date for receipt (as opposed to mailing) of submissions). The AJ may exclude witnesses who could and should have been, but were not, listed in a prehearing submission. See Wilson v. DHS, 208 Fed. Appx. 876, 878 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) ("Two of Mr. Wilson's witnesses were excluded because they were not designated in a timely manner and that a third witness was excluded because the administrative judge did not find the witness's testimony to be relevant or material. However, affidavits from all three witnesses were accepted into the record. Mr. Wilson does not provide any reason why this court should find that these procedural decisions constituted an abuse of discretion or that they resulted in harm to his case. The board is certainly permitted to set and enforce deadlines throughout the appeal process. Further, in this case, the affidavits of all three witnesses were admitted into the record."); Stewart-Maxwell v. USPS, 56 MSPR 265, 270-71 (1993) (ruling was applied to a rebuttal witness).

To preserve the point for a PFR, the appellant should object to materials not identified in the prehearing submissions that the agency offers at the hearing. Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 621, 625 (1993). If an agency is misled by the AJ as to the exact issues involved in an appeal during discussions at a preliminary conference, there is no reversible error if the issues are fully litigated at the hearing with no claim of surprise. Green v. District of Columbia Gov't, 6 MSPR 102, 106, 6 MSPB 98 (1981).

If an issue not addressed in the prehearing submissions initially surfaces at the hearing, the Board signaled its inclination to consider the issue on PFR, Paula v. SSA, PH-0752-10-0251-I-1 (NP 4/18/2012):

In his closing brief at the conclusion of the hearing, the appellant raised a new due process argument concerning ex parte communications, which was based on Mr. Rohde's testimony during the hearing. The agency moved to strike that portion of the appellant's brief, alleging that it was untimely raised for the first time, and the administrative judge granted the motion and did not make findings on the issue. It appears, however, that the appellant learned of Mr. Rohde's reliance on his conduct during the QAR for the first time at the hearing. Although the agency argues otherwise, it has not pointed to anything in the record demonstrating that the appellant was previously aware that Mr. Rohde had considered his conduct during the QAR to reach his decision. The entire report of investigation, which contained among its 145 pages a memorandum describing the appellant's conduct during the QAR, was attached to the notice of the proposed removal; however, the notice explicitly identified 17 specifications as the bases for the charge and included a thorough analysis of the penalty, and none of the specifications or penalty factors related to the appellant's conduct during the QAR. The appellant responded in detail to the allegations in the notice of proposed removal, but he was not on notice of any need to respond to allegations regarding his conduct during the QAR. Although it is not ideal for a party to raise such a late claim based on facts learned for the first time at the hearing, because we believe that the appellant did not know of the deciding official's reliance on the QAR incident until the hearing and he properly objected to the agency's pursuit of such evidence, we find that the administrative judge erred by failing to consider this issue below. See Simon v. Department of the Air Force, 60 M.S.P.R. 482, 485-86 (1994).

See Bailey v. USPS, DC-0752-16-0463-I-1 (NP 5/25/2022) ("The appellant's assertions made below in her closing argument clearly indicated her intent to raise due process violation and harmful error claims. Therefore, the appellant was entitled to be apprised of the applicable burdens of proving her affirmative defenses and harmful error and due process claims, as well as the kind of evidence required to meet those burdens, and to have those defenses addressed in a close-of-record order or prehearing conference summary and order. Hulett v. Department of the Navy, 120 M.S.P.R. 54, ¶ 10 (2013).").

A broad description of a due process issue prior to the prehearing conference was sufficient to encompass a specific due process argument that was first raised during closing argument, Nunnery v. USDA, DA-0752-15-0378-I-1 (NP 6/9/2016):

Below, the appellant raised this argument in the first instance during closing argument at the conclusion of the hearing. However, the appellant raised an affirmative defense of violation of due process prior to the prehearing conference. Construing the appellant's pleadings liberally, we find that the appellant did not waive her argument that the choice of deciding official violated her due process rights by failing to raise it until the conclusion of the hearing. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.24(b) (requiring an appellant to show good cause for raising claims or defenses for the first time after the conference defining the issues in the case).
