a. Settlement Resources; Mediation Appeals Program

Several Board settlement processes are routinely offered to the parties. These procedures are described in the acknowledgment order sent out after an appeal is filed. Regional and field offices have a program providing for a "settlement judge" on request of a party who wants to engage in settlement discussion but who does not wish to involve in those discussions the same AJ who will decide the appeal on the merits. Information about the use of a settlement judge is usually provided with the notice from the Board acknowledging the appeal. A call to the AJ assigned to the case or to the regional director will also provide an opportunity to explore the possibility of assignment of a settlement judge. The Board has a "mediation appeals program" (MAP), described in the website link "Mediation Appeals Program," mspb.gov/appeals/mediationappeals.htm#about, and referenced in acknowledgment orders issued after appeals are filed. MAP uses Board employees (not all of whom are AJs) who receive mediation training and who conduct in-person mediations to facilitate settlements. MAP mediators usually come from offices of the Board other than the regional or field office adjudicating the appeal. The parties will usually be offered settlement assistance from the AJ assigned to adjudicate the appeal. Some AJs call the parties soon after the appeal is underway to discuss settlement. Some AJs wait until the appeal is at the prehearing stage. Some AJs are reluctant to engage in settlement discussions because of concerns that their remarks could lead to a challenge for bias. Those AJs will urge the parties to use a settlement judge or MAP. Settlement of PFRs is encouraged by the Board, and MAP or assistance of a settlement attorney is available for that purpose. [Refer in Chapter 5 to "PFR Settlement Program."]

The Mediation Appeals Program provides an in-person process, allowing the parties to meet with the mediator. The process can be undertaken by videoconference or teleconference should travel or travel costs be a problem. Requests for MAP are joint. Those requests are routinely approved. If MAP is not used but a settlement judge (another AJ in the office) is appointed from the region handling the appeal, assistance by a settlement judge is usually by telephone. Settlement assistance by the adjudication AJ is usually initially by telephone and, often, at the onset of or during the hearing. Should an appeal not settle through the efforts of MAP or a settlement judge, the adjudication judge is sure to make some effort to resolve the case. Review in 2014 of a couple hundred evaluation sheets submitted during the past few years by parties engaged in MAP shows that most respondents, agency and appellant, are satisfied with the program--whether or not their case settles. As to appeals that do not settle, comments show that people found MAP valuable for allowing informal and informed exchanges of opinions and information about cases; and of the cases that did not settle through MAP, progress toward settlement was made in some cases, with the possibility of a later settlement built on productive discussions during the mediation. There were comments critical of the program, the most common one being--and this as to only a handful of MAP evaluations--that the agency did not come to the table with a manager with full authority to settle, meaning as a practical matter that time is wasted while agency representatives at MAP engage in phone calls to an empowered executive to explain what is going on at MAP and to obtain authority to make an offer, a process that is repeated throughout the mediation until the appeal settles or mediation reaches impasse. The Board's mediation program, and others like it, would be improved by setting, as a precondition to mediation, the requirement that each side have a manager or executive at the table with full authority to settle as opposed to full authority to engage in phone calls.

Before the MAP mediation, the appointed mediator will call or send an email to the parties and counsel by way of introduction and to arrange for a mediation date, time, and place or method. Days before the mediation session, the mediator, who has access to the Board case file, may speak separately with the parties and counsel to get some case background and general information as to the options possible for settlement. The mediator will ask the parties and counsel to sign and return to the mediator an agreement that is specific concerning confidentiality requirements and that will look something like:

MERIT SYSTEMS PROTECTION BOARD AGREEMENT TO MEDIATE

Re: [MSPB Case Name]

MSPB Docket Number:

1. AGREEMENT TO MEDIATION: The undersigned agree to engage in a mediation in an effort to resolve issues raised before the Merit Systems Protection Board.

2. STATEMENT AS TO VOLUNTARINESS: The parties understand that mediation is voluntary and may be terminated at any time.

3. NEUTRALITY OF MSPB MEDIATOR: The parties understand that the mediator has no authority to decide the case and is not acting as an advocate or attorney for any party.

4. ROLE OF MEDIATOR AS FACILITATOR; The parties understand that the mediator acts as a facilitator to help the parties reach their own settlement on mutually acceptable terms.

5. RIGHT TO REPRESENTATION: The parties understand that they have a right to have a representative assist them during the mediation process.

6. AGREEMENT NOT TO SUBPOENA AND NON-DISCLOSURE; The parties agree not to subpoena the mediator, any observer, or any documents prepared by or submitted to the mediator. The mediator will not voluntarily testify on behalf of any party, disclose communications of the parties, or submit any type of report in connection with this mediation. The mediator's non-disclosure will not extend to violations of the law for which confidentiality cannot be assured.

7. AGREEMENT AS TO CONFIDENTIALITY DURING MEDIATION SESSION: The parties agree to keep confidential statements made by the parties during the mediation session. The parties will not voluntarily testify or disclose communications made by the parties during the joint sessions of the mediation. The parties' non-disclosure will not extend to violations of the law for which confidentiality cannot be assured.

8. EXTENT OF LEGAL OBLIGATIONS OF PARTIES: No party shall be bound by anything said or done at the mediation unless a written settlement is reached and executed by all necessary parties. If a settlement is reached, the agreement shall be reduced to writing and, when signed and approved by the appropriate authorities for all parties, shall be binding upon all parties to the agreement.

9. AGREEMENT AS TO SETTLEMENT AGREEMENT AND ENFORCEMENT OF TERMS: The parties fully understand, acknowledge, and agree that in the event that a fully executed settlement agreement is reached as a result of efforts set forth during this comediation, the parties may request that the Board enter the settlement agreement into the record for purposes of future enforcement.

10. AGREEMENT AS TO NON-RECORDATION OF MEDIATION SESSION: The parties agree that this mediation session will not be video taped or recorded and no transcript will be produced of the mediation session.

11. ACKNOWLEDGMENT. UNDERSTANDING. AND AGREEMENT: By agreement below, we acknowledge that we have read, understand, and agree to this Mediation Agreement.

The parties may seek their own assistance: a commercial mediation service; a neutral arbitrator who agrees to mediate; the Shared Neutrals Program (now operated by FMCS). Information about it may be obtained by calling (202) 796-1533, and by checking an FMCS website: https://www.fmcs.gov/sharedneutrals/. OPM devotes a website to shared neutrals programs outside of Washington: http://archive.opm.gov/er/adrguide/Section3-shared-neutrals.asp. Also available is the Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service, www.fmcs.gov. Most agencies have ADR programs in place. ADR programs differ. Some are staffed by amateurs and some by well-trained, experienced mediators. In mediation, quality counts. No particular credentials are required for a mediator. Anyone willing to help who is accepted by both parties may serve as a mediator or facilitator. The parties can hire their own mediator from the private sector. If assistance is to be used outside the several ordinary MSPB processes, the parties will likely need to notify the AJ of their interest in mediation and jointly request a suspension of the proceedings or a dismissal of the appeal without prejudice to its refiling to allow enough time for the process to be commenced and concluded. [Refer to the "Case Suspension," described in Chapter 3, and the "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')," described in Chapter 2.] A good overview of ADR is provided by the OPM publication Alternative Dispute Resolution A Resource Guide, http://www.opm.gov/er/adrguide/toc.asp.
