j. Correction of Administrative Error

The discussion of pay rate corrections in Chapter 1, "Correction of Pay, Grade," points out that the Board generally finds the correction of administrative errors in pay rates to be beyond its appellate jurisdiction. Correction of the error may involve reduction of the employee's grade or it may involve reduction of the level of pay within a grade or pay-band level. We include discussion of both situations here. There are qualifications concerning jurisdiction, however, and decisions in Chapter 1 should be reviewed. If there are disputes concerning the justification for a demotion based on administrative error, i.e., appropriateness of an agency claim of administrative error, the appellant is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing, as in Riley v. NASA, 79 MSPR 505, 506-10 ¶¶ 2-11 (1998), involving an employee who was promoted to GS-14 at one station shortly after he accepted a GS-13 at another station. He was paid at a GS-14 rate at the second station. The agency then sought to correct his pay rate by reducing him to a GS-13. Given the appellant's averment that he was entitled to a GS-14, and the agency's contrary protestations, the Board remanded the case for a jurisdictional hearing:

On January 13, 1997, the appellant, who at the time held a GS-13 Technical Management Position at the agency's Kennedy Space Center (KSC) in Florida, accepted a GS-13 Technical Management position at the agency's Stennis Space Center (SSC) in Mississippi. In February 1997, prior to her transfer to SSC, the appellant was promoted to a GS-14 Technical Management position. On May 11, 1997, the appellant was reassigned to SSC; on May 22, 1997, due to an alleged administrative error by the personnel office at SSC, a Standard Form (SF)-50 documenting that the appellant was reassigned from KSC to a GS-14 position at SSC, effective May 11, 1997, was signed and issued by Dorsie Jones, a Personnel Management Specialist, as the approving official. In mid-June, 1997, the personnel office issued another SF-50 correcting the initial SF-50 to document that the appellant had been downgraded to a GS-13 grade level, effective May 11, 1997, at her request....

...

Based on the written record, the administrative judge found that the appellant's reassignment to a GS-14 position at SSC was an administrative error because the appellant was offered and accepted a GS-13 position at SSC, the appellant failed to make a nonfrivolous allegation that she was involuntarily demoted, when SSC officials realized that a mistake had been made on her SF-50, they corrected it, there was no deception, coercion, duress, or misrepresentation on the part of SSC officials, and the appellant, at most, made a unilateral mistake in believing that she would be transferred to a GS-14 position at SSC. Relying upon Henry v. Department of Defense, 33 MSPR 562, 564 (1987), the administrative judge found that the agency's subsequent correction of this administrative error by downgrading the appellant's position is beyond the Board's jurisdiction. Having found that the Board lacks jurisdiction over this appeal, the administrative judge did not address the timeliness issue. She, thus, dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

...

First, we find that the administrative judge erroneously relied upon Henry in dismissing this appeal. In Henry, the Board held that it lacks jurisdiction over an appeal of a reduction in pay that results from an agency's action in correcting an administrative error in setting a within-grade step. Henry, 33 MSPR at 564. The Board has subsequently held that this holding in Henry does not apply to cases in which the employee appeals an administrative error resulting in a demotion, i.e., a change to a lower grade or a lower rate of pay. See Barnes v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 MSPR 337, 340 (1996). Thus, Henry is inapposite here where the appellant appealed her demotion, i.e., the agency's action in downgrading her position.

Second, we find that the administrative judge failed to identify and consider the applicable legal precedent. See Spithaler v. Office of Personnel Management, 2 MSPB 2, 1 MSPR 587, 589 (1980). In Clark v. Department of the Interior, 68 MSPR 453, 457 (1995), the Board set forth the test for jurisdiction in a case involving an alleged demotion after cancellation of a promotion or an appointment. In order to establish Board jurisdiction in an appeal from the cancellation of a promotion or an appointment, the appellant must raise a nonfrivolous allegation that the promotion or appointment actually occurred; that is, that the promotion or appointment was approved by an authorized appointing official aware that he was making the promotion or appointment, and that some action denoting acceptance of the promotion or appointment was taken. The promotion or appointment must not have been revoked prior to the appellant's performance in the higher grade. Id.; see also Watts v. Office of Personnel Management, 814 F.2d 1576, 1580 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 484 U.S. 913, 108 S. Ct. 258, 98 L. Ed.2d 216 (1987). Because the administrative judge did not apprise the appellant of this jurisdictional test, remand is warranted. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (an appellant must receive explicit information on what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue).

Moreover, evidence submitted by the appellant shows that she was, in fact, assigned to a GS-14 position upon her transfer to SSC on May 11, 1997, and that she was, in fact, reduced-in-grade to a GS-13 position. The SF-50 hearing the approval date of May 9, 1997, and documenting that the appellant was reassigned to a GS-14 position at SSC, effective May 11, 1997, appears to be an official and properly executed and signed form. This SF-50 combined with the SF-50 bearing the approval date of May 11, 1997, and documenting that the appellant was downgraded to a GS-13 position, effective May 11, 1997, are sufficient to establish a nonfrivolous allegation of Board jurisdiction because they document that there was an assignment to a GS-14 position, that there was an authorizing official, that the appellant accepted the assignment, and that the appellant was demoted. See Barnes, 71 MSPR at 340-41; see also Watts, 814 F.2d at 1580.

As noted above, the record includes argument and evidence, submitted by the agency, that the appellant's assignment to a GS-14 position at SSC may have resulted from administrative error. The record also shows, however, that the appellant consistently argued below that there was no clerical error, and that she was, in fact, assigned to a GS-14 position at SSC, effective May 11, 1997, because KSC and SSC officials discussed her promotion at the end of January 1997, KSC's personnel office transmitted to SSC's personnel office the SF-75 preliminary employee information documenting that she had been promoted to a GS-14 grade level by KSC in February 1997, and the SF-50 approved on May 9, 1997, documented that she had been reassigned from KSC to a GS-14 position at SSC, effective May 11, 1997. Further, the appellant alleged below that her supervisor, Kirk Sharp, began assigning her GS-14 duties in the last week of May when he received the SF-50 showing the appellant's assignment to a GS-14 position. This allegation is supported by Sharp's written chronology of events submitted by the agency.

In light of the contradictory record evidence, the question of the validity of the appellant's assignment to a GS-14 position at SSC, that is, whether it was approved by an official having authority to approve it, and who was aware that he or she was granting such an assignment, is a question of fact, warranting a jurisdictional hearing. See Barnes, 71 MSPR at 341. To the extent that the administrative judge considered the evidence submitted by the agency to resolve this question of fact without affording the appellant her requested jurisdictional hearing, she erred. See Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994) (in determining whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction entitling him to a hearing, the administrative judge may consider the agency's documentary submissions; however, to the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions of the parties and the agency's evidence may not be dispositive).
