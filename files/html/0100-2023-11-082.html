2. Negative Inferences; Omitted Witnesses, Documents

The use of a negative (or adverse) inference against an agency for failing to have two witnesses testify at the adverse action hearing was considered in Shustyk v. USPS, 32 MSPR 611, 614-15 (1987):

The use of negative (or adverse) inferences has traditionally been allowed in certain cases "[W]hen it would be natural under the circumstances for a party to call a particular witness, or take the stand himself as a witness...and he fails to do so..." McCormick, Evidence, sec. 272 at 804 (1984 ed.). The cases may be separated into two groups: (1) those where an adverse inference may be drawn against a party for failure to produce a witness reasonably assumed to be favorably disposed to the party; and (2) those where an adverse inference may be drawn against a party who has exclusive control over a material witness but fails to produce him, without regard to any possible favorable disposition of the witness toward the party. Id. at 805. In either group, if the testimony of the witnesses would be merely cumulative, the inference is unavailable. Id.

The use of the adverse inference has been held to be proper in Board cases when a party is silent in the face of probative adverse evidence. See Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 488, 492 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (appellants' silence before the Board after the agency had established a prima facie case warranted the Board's drawing of an adverse inference). See also Book v. USPS, 6 MSPB 322, 6 MSPR 378, 380 (1981), aff'd, 675 F.2d 158 (8th Cir. 1982); Askew v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 703, 707-08 (1983). The Board has not permitted a negative inference to be made, however, against a party who simply fails to call its own non-hostile witnesses at the hearing. See Logan v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 573, 577-78 (1986) (the administrative judge's imposition of sanctions by drawing negative inferences against the agency for its failure to produce two witnesses at the hearing was found to be erroneous).

"The inference cannot fairly be drawn except from the non-production of witnesses whose testimony would be superior in respect to the fact to be proved." 2 Wigmore, Evidence, sec. 287 at 203 (1979 ed.). In this case, the witnesses' testimony would not have been "superior," as it would have contributed only marginally to the agency's satisfaction of its burden of proof concerning the charge against appellant. The witnesses who were not produced in the instant case were the agency's deciding official and appellant's second-level supervisor. Because the agency both produced adequate documentary evidence and had appellant's immediate supervisor testify at length about the matter in dispute, we find that the agency's production of the other two witnesses in question was in no way critical to the administrative judge's determination of whether it had satisfied its burden of proving by preponderant evidence the charges against appellant. Further, any information which the two witnesses may have been able to provide at the hearing could have been produced before the hearing--by either party--through the use of available discovery procedures....

Finally, we note that the agency did not violate an order or directive of the Board in deciding not to call the witnesses in question. Rather, the witnesses' failure to testify was apparently the result of the agency's determination that their testimony was not necessary to supplement the evidence already presented to support the agency's position at the hearing.

See Meza v. DHS, 275 Fed. Appx. 987, 992 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (quoting from Shustyk); Davis v. DHHS, DE-0752-10-0062-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010) ("To the extent that the appellant suggests that the administrative judge should have drawn a negative inference in this case, we note that the appellant also did not call John Doe I as a witness. See Bufco Corp. v. Nat'l Labor Relations Board, 147 F.3d 964, 971 (D.C. Cir. 1998) (a negative inference is unwarranted where the party requesting the inference could have called the witness itself)."); King v. USDA, AT-0752-18-0079-I-1, n.4 (NP 10/20/2022) ("The agency argues for the Board to draw an adverse inference because the appellant did not call one of the witnesses whom he alleged witnessed the exchange underpinning charge (1). The Board has held that an adverse inference of misconduct can be inferred from the failure of the appellant to testify or to call a witness only in limited circumstances. Shustyk v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 M.S.P.R. 611, 613-15, aff'd, 831 F.2d 305 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table). The Board has not permitted a negative inference to be made against a party who, as here, simply fails to call his own nonhostile witnesses at the hearing. See Logan v. Department of the Navy, 29 M.S.P.R. 573, 577-78 (finding that the administrative judge erred when he imposed sanctions by drawing negative inferences against the agency for its failure to produce two witnesses at the hearing), aff'd, 809 F.2d 789 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table)."); Perrin v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0752-15-0282-I-1 (NP 8/12/2016) ("the Board has not permitted a negative inference to be made against a party who simply fails to call its own nonhostile witnesses at the hearing. See, e.g., Shustyk v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 M.S.P.R. 611, 614-15, aff'd, 831 F.2d 305 (Fed. Cir. 1987).").

If an agency is directed to produce a witness called by the appellant and that agency fails to do so without good cause, the Board may draw an adverse inference against the agency--assuming the materiality of the testimony from the absent witness--when the Board assesses credibility issues inherent in the agency's case. Taylor v. USPS, 75 MSPR 322, 326 (1997), discounted hearsay offered by an agency when the agency failed to produce a witness with direct knowledge of the appellant's statements and offered no bona fide reason for not ensuring appearance of the witness at hearing:

[T]he appellant is entitled to an inference in her favor due to the agency's failure to show good cause for Brown's unavailability. Under 5 CFR § 1201.43(a)(1), when a party does not comply with an administrative judge's order to provide information to the opposing party, the administrative judge may impose the sanction of drawing an inference in favor of the party requesting the information.

See SSA v. Carr, 78 MSPR 313, 332-33 (1998) (declining to draw an adverse inference when the appellant "had the opportunity to request [the] witnesses herself but chose not to do so"); Johnson v. DOJ, 61 MSPR 110, 115 (1994) (adverse inference is not warranted if testimony of a withdrawn witness would have been cumulative); cf. Rint v. OPM, 48 MSPR 69, 72 (1991) (belated claim of the failure to receive a retirement refund check was held by the Board to weigh against the validity of the assertion); Martinez v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 567, 568, 568 n.3 (1984) (adverse inference should not have been drawn when a pro se appellant did all he could to obtain the presence of the witness whose failure to testify led to the adverse inference).

An adverse inference should not be drawn when "[T]he variety of inconsistent inferences which may be drawn with equal logic and probability...deprives any single such inference of greater probative weight than a contrary inference." Remy v. VA, 8 MSPR 141, 147, 7 MSPB 624 (1981). An adverse inference, rather than sanctions, may be drawn against an agency that fails to produce material documentation during the course of discovery. See McIntire v. FEMA, 55 MSPR 578, 585 n.6 (1992).

Although there may be circumstances justifying a negative inference to be drawn against an appellant who fails to produce evidence during a hearing, the burden of proving the charges against the appellant still does not shift from the agency. See Brown v. DLA, 65 MSPR 436, 442 (1994). An adverse inference should not be drawn against an appellant who does not call a witness with information that would support the agency's case, Bradley v. VA, 78 MSPR 296, 299 (1998): "Where, however, as here, the absence of a witness affects the probative weight of the agency's evidence, the appellant's failure to call the witness is irrelevant. See Borninkhof v. Department of Justice, 5 MSPB 150, 5 MSPR 77, 82-87 (1981)."

Admissions or negative inferences may involve matters other than attempts to demonstrate wrongdoing in the course of an adverse action or performance-based appeal. An agency's silence, after its response was requested and it was warned of the consequences of failing to provide a response, constituted the admission of detailed facts set out in an employee's compliance petition. See Zinno v. USPS, 77 MSPR 290, 294-95 (1998).

An agency need not document actions to avoid an adverse inference. See Coulter v. Dept. of Navy, SF-1221-12-0271-B-2 (NP 6/15/2015) ("He contends that this evidence was self-serving and not supported by any documentation, which he believes the agency should have created as a matter of routine to document conversations, meetings, and incidents as they occur throughout every work day. The appellant has identified no law, rule, or regulation to support his belief that such documentation is required."). But as to existing documents, an agency found itself on the receiving end of an implied adverse inference after losing material original evidence, Natividad v. USDA, 5 MSPR 415, 418, 5 MSPB 426 (1981):

The agency did not submit into evidence the book in question, nor did it submit the original page, torn from the book by Dr. Bikos, which contained the alleged falsified data. Instead, the agency submitted an unclear copy of what it purports to be the page. The agency alleged that both the book and original page were misplaced. The Board finds that an examination of the book in question could indeed have been dispositive of the factual issues in this case. We find that the non-production of the notebook, which was taken from petitioner and was in the agency's control, is prejudicial to petitioner's ability to support his defense. Thus, in the interest of fairness and justice, petitioner should not bear the burden of the agency's negligence in losing critical evidence and we find, instead, that the non-production seriously impairs the strength of the record presented by the agency to support its charges. See United States V. Rose, 590 F.2d 232, at 237 (7th Cir. 1978); United States v. Miranda, 526 F.2d 1319, at 1325-1328 (2d Cir. 1975).

See Butler v. USPS, 8 MSPR 528, 531 (1981) (when the agency produced no witnesses or exhibits to support a charge that the appellant possessed alcoholic beverages on agency premises, the Board found that the agency's failure to produce the bottles of liquor allegedly possessed by the appellant "seriously impairs the strength of the evidence in support of this charge.").

Care should be taken to avoid a negative inference, or negative finding, when the lack of evidence is the result of a sanction barring the introduction of evidence for failure to comply with a discovery order, Dunbar v. Dept. of Transp., DC-0752-14-1003-I-1 (NP 11/15/2016):

The administrative judge's ruling precluded the appellant from offering evidence or factual testimony regarding her affirmative defenses. Then, based on a lack of proof, the administrative judge found against her on those defenses. They included retaliation for protected EEO activity, specifically, the appellant's having filed two EEO complaints and participated in the complaint of a coworker which, like hers, named the appellant's supervisor, retaliation for protected disclosures the appellant made regarding prohibited personnel practices at the agency and allegedly illegal information technology, cyber-security, and Privacy Act violations, and harmful procedural error regarding restrictions on her right to make an oral reply to the charges. Our reviewing court has ruled that it is an abuse of discretion to exclude evidence during a Board proceeding that would tend to support an employee's claim of retaliation for whistleblowing, and then use that lack of evidence to find against the employee on that claim. Whitmore v. Department of Labor, 680 F.3d 1353, 1369-70 (Fed. Cir. 2012). The administrative judge's discovery rulings in this case and her findings on the appellant's affirmative defenses are the kinds of actions that the court in Whitmore cautioned against. See id. at 1369.
