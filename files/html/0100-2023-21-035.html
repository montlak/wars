6. Partial Disability Does Not Qualify

To obtain disability annuity benefits under the statutory definition, the disability cannot be partial, Boyd v. OPM, 5 MSPR 155, 157 (1981):

The question to be resolved by the presiding official was whether the appellant is totally disabled so that he meets the requirements for disability retirement under 5 U.S.C. 8337. The appellant must meet the cited definition of disability under 5 U.S.C. 8331(6), i.e., total disability for useful and efficient service in the grade or class of position last occupied.

The record establishes that appellant suffers a back condition which is uncomfortable and, at times, painful. However, the Civil Service retirement system does not provide for an annuity based on partial disability. Therefore, an employee may be found ineligible for a disability retirement annuity, and yet be so physically unfit as to be unable to continue to perform satisfactorily in his position. See Polos v. United States, 621 F.2d 385, 393 (Ct. Cl. 1980); Piccone v. United States, 407F.2d 866, 872 (Ct. Cl. 1969); Page v. Office of Personnel Management, 4 MSPB 436 (1980).

Commuting difficulties for an individual with back problems did not sway the Board's determination to deny a disability annuity in Jolliffe v. OPM, 23 MSPR 188, 191 (1984) ("While she does suffer pain she is neither totally incapacitated nor completely limited in her activities. Moreover, while it may be difficult or well nigh impossible for appellant to commute from Leesburg, this is not a relevant consideration in a disability retirement determination. The relevant consideration is whether appellant can perform the duties of her position and the record does not establish that there are any duties that appellant is unable to perform.").

The ability to perform fewer than all job assignments was insufficient to qualify for disability retirement, Chapman v. OPM, 11 MSPR 107, 109-10 (1982):

Taking all these factors into consideration, however, we must also note the March 3, 1980 statement of appellant's superior officer indicating in pertinent part that appellant was only able to perform, at maximum, two thirds of her job assignments. Thus, what the record shows is that appellant has numerous ailments all of which are mild as opposed to severe. It also shows that she is bothered by these ailments and experiences some difficulty in working. Finally, the record shows that her efficiency was reduced by these ailments. Therefore, in the record as it exists in this case, there is no showing of total disability.
