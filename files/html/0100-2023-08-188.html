b. Actions Against Healthcare Professionals Not Involving Professional Conduct or Competence

The VA medical professional's appeal to a Disciplinary Appeals Board is from a "major adverse action" involving a "question of professional conduct or competence." The distinctions between major and other adverse actions, and the associated appeal or grievance rights, are found at 38 USC 7461-64. If the action taken against the professional does not arise from conduct or competence, review is through the VA grievance system; the Individual Right of Action (discussed in Chapter 13) is available in whistleblower reprisal cases. in an IRA appeal, Cochran v. VA, 67 MSPR 167, 171-74 (1995), explained the challenge available to a letter of admonishment and removal that were not based on professional conduct or competence:

Under the 1991 Act, when the agency's Chief Medical Officer takes a "major adverse action" against an employee appointed under section 7401(1), and the adverse action involves a "question of professional conduct or competence," the employee has a right of appeal to a Disciplinary Appeals Board under section 7462.[*] 38 USC 7461(a) and (b)(1). Upon hearing such an appeal, the Disciplinary Appeals Board renders a decision, which the Secretary of the DVA shall execute, reverse, vacate and remand, or mitigate as to the discipline. See id. § 7462. In cases involving questions of professional conduct or competence, the 1991 Act provides that "[t]he Secretary's execution of a board's decision shall be the final administrative action in the case." Id. § 7462(d)(4). Moreover, in such cases, the Act provides that the avenue for appeal of the Secretary's decision is judicial review. See id. § 7462(f).

[*] A "major adverse action" is defined as an adverse action involving a suspension, transfer, reduction in grade or basic pay, and discharge. 38 USC 7461(c)(2). A "question of professional conduct or competence" is one involving "direct patient care" or "clinical competence." Id. § 7461(c)(3).

In contrast, the 1991 Act, in 38 USC 7463, establishes a separate procedure for cases either not involving a "major adverse action" or not arising out of a "question of professional conduct or competence." See id. § 7463(a). It provides that the Disciplinary Appeals Board lacks jurisdiction over such cases, and that in them, the employee is entitled to a grievance procedure in which findings are made by an impartial examiner whose findings and recommendations are reviewed by "an official of a higher level than the official who decided upon the action." Id. § 7463(d). Section 7463 of title 38 does not state that the reviewing official's decision shall be final.

The record in the instant appeal shows that the agency's removal action did not arise out of a question of professional conduct or competence, but rather out of a question of "preemployment suitability." The hearing the appellant received was not before a Disciplinary Appeals Board, but rather before a Professional Standards Board, which the record evidence shows is a different board. Moreover, the removal action proceeded under the agency's grievance procedures set forth at MP-5, Part II, Chapter 9; the action did not proceed under MP-5, Part II, Chapter 8, section A, which involves procedures to be followed when an action is a major adverse action arising out of a question of professional conduct or performance. Based on the documentary evidence, we accept the agency's characterization of the removal action as one falling within the provisions of 38 U.S.C. § 7463. See H.R. Rep. No. 466, 101st Cong., 2d Sess., at 30 (1990) ("the issue of whether a matter or question involves professional conduct or competence is not subject to review by any other agency").

The August 20, 1993, admonishment letter states that it was not being issued for reasons involving a question of professional conduct or competence. We therefore find that it, too, was an action taken under 38 U.S.C. § 7463.

The following aspects of the statutory scheme involving a section 7401(1) employee who is subject to an adverse action under section 7463 action show that it would be consistent with that scheme for the Board to exercise jurisdiction over an IRA appeal by such an employee: (1) section 7463 does not state that the reviewing official's decision shall be final, as did the statutory provisions at issue in Chan and Alvarez; (2) section 7463, unlike section 7462, does not limit further review of the agency's action in any way; (3) Congress did not include the relevant provisions of the WPA in the list of title 5 provisions which expressly do not apply to section 7401(1) employees; and (4) section 7401(1) does not state that civil service laws are inapplicable to a physician employed under that provision, as did the statutory provision discussed in Chan. There are yet additional considerations that support this conclusion.

The legislative history of the 1991 Act emphasizes that the Secretary's decisions as to actions taken under section 7462 are final and not reviewable by any other agency, while references to what became section 7463 make no such mention of the finality of the agency's decisions as to actions taken under that section. See H.R. Rep. No. 466, at 29-32. Moreover, Congress' intent in enacting the 1991 Act was, in part, to afford title 38 employees an opportunity to have "disciplinary actions and grievances related to their employment resolved in a timely fashion with an opportunity for outside review." 137 Cong. Rec. S4543 (Apr. 17, 1991) (statement of Sen. Cranston). As stated above, section 7463 does not limit the scope of outside review of agency actions taken under that section (as does section 7462), leaving open the opportunity for review of IRA appeals by the Board in cases involving section 7463 discipline. Further, Congress can be presumed to have known of the provisions of the WPA when it enacted the 1991 Act and to have contemplated that "outside review" of section 7463 actions could, in the absence of any limiting language, be exercised by the Board under the WPA. See D'Elia v. Department of the Treasury, 60 M.S.P.R. 226, 232 (1993).

In addition, a disciplinary action taken under 38 U.S.C. § 7462 requires review and execution by the Secretary of the Department of Veterans Affairs, the highest-ranking administrative officer in the agency. See 38 U.S.C. § 7462(d). Under such circumstances and in view of the statutory language that the Secretary's action shall be the "final administrative action in the case," the Secretary's action would be entitled to due deference and not subordinated to review by the Board absent an express statutory grant of review authority, such as was provided in the 1994 amendments to the WPA discussed above. See Colorado Nurses Association v. Federal Labor Relations Authority, 851 F.2d 1486, 1488-92 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (where the relevant statute indicated Congress' intent to give the Administrator of the Veterans Administration final and exclusive authority to prescribe regulations concerning conditions of employment, the Administrator's exercise of authority in that regard could not be subordinated to review by the Federal Labor Relations Authority).

On the other hand, disciplinary actions taken under 38 U.S.C. § 7463 are subject only to a grievance procedure wherein the employee has a right to "formal review by an impartial examiner within the Department of Veterans Affairs" and a review of the examiner's findings and recommendations "by an official of a higher level than the official who decided upon the action." 38 U.S.C. § 7463(d). There is no statutory requirement that a section 7463 disciplinary action be reviewed or executed by the Secretary, let alone that the action constitute the "final" administrative action in the case. Thus, we find that a section 7463 disciplinary action need not be accorded the deference given to a section 7462 action and may be reviewed by the Board "consistent with" 5 U.S.C. § 1221. Cf. Colorado Nurses Association, 851 F.2d at 1488-92.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant has shown that the Board can exercise jurisdiction over his IRA appeal consistent with the disciplinary scheme established by Congress for section 7401(1) employees who were subject to a disciplinary action that was grievable under 38 U.S.C. § 7463. However, to establish the Board's jurisdiction over his IRA appeal, the appellant also must establish, by preponderant evidence, that: (1) He engaged in whistleblower activity by making a disclosure protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), i.e., he disclosed information he reasonably believed evidenced a violation of law, rule, or regulation, gross mismanagement, gross waste of funds, abuse of authority, or substantial and specific danger to public health or safety; (2) the agency took or failed to take, or threatened to take or fail to take, a "personnel action" as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2) after the July 9, 1989, effective date of the WPA; and (3) he raised the whistleblower issue before OSC, and proceedings before OSC have been exhausted. See Geyer v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 13, 16-17 (1994).

Here, the agency's removal action and its issuance of the letter of admonishment each constitute a "personnel action" under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2), and both actions were effective after the effective date of the WPA. See 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(iii); Geyer, 63 M.S.P.R. at 17. Thus, on remand, the administrative judge shall provide the appellant an opportunity to submit argument and evidence showing that he made a disclosure or disclosures protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), that he raised the whistleblower issue before OSC, and that proceedings before OSC have been exhausted. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)] . If the administrative judge finds that the appellant has met all of the jurisdictional elements of his claim, he shall issue a new initial decision adjudicating the appeal on the merits. See Geyer, 63 M.S.P.R. at 19.
