1. General Policy

FLRA does not desire to relitigate the merits of grievances decided by arbitrators. See VA Hosp., Perry Point and Local 331, AFGE, 3 FLRA 236, 237 (1980). To avoid this problem, many of the exceptions to arbitration awards are denied as "mere disagreement" with the arbitrator, i.e., reargument of the merits of the case. FLRA will not review an award if the party objects to the reasoning applied by the arbitrator to the merits of the dispute. This rule is frequently invoked, finding its first application under the Reform Act in a case involving a suspension for alleged unauthorized receipt and disposal of government property. The union contended that the arbitrator failed to resolve an issue involving the legal ownership of the property in question. The arbitrator apparently resolved the case against the grievant on the basis of who had possession of the property rather than on the basis of true legal ownership. FLRA refused to overturn the award. It viewed the petition for review of the award as presenting only disagreement with the arbitrator's reasoning on the merits of the dispute. Fed. Aviation Science and Tech. Ass'n and FAA, Albuquerque Airway Facilities Sector, 2 FLRA 680, 681-82 (1980). Stated more simply, or more directly: "It is a well established principle that the arbitrator's construction of the contract is not subject to review." NAGE Local R4-6 and Hq., Army Transp. Ctr., 3 FLRA 223, 225 (1980) (a promotion dispute).

Generally stated (and with exceptions), an arbitrator need not specify the particular items of evidence supporting his award. Int'l Trade Comm'n and AFGE Local 2211, 13 FLRA 440, 441 (1983); see Dept. of Energy and OPEIU Local 2001, 63 FLRA 219, 221 (2009) ("with certain exceptions not relevant here...arbitrators are not required to set forth specific findings in their awards. See United States Dep't of Health & Human Servs., Soc. Sec. Admin., Boston Region, 48 FLRA 943, 950 (1993) (citing AFGE, Local 3529, 35 FLRA 1108, 1113 (1990))."); Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs and NFFE Local 341, 55 FLRA 152, 156 (1999) ("[A]rbitrators are not obligated to provide a rationale for their findings unless required to do so by contract, submission of the parties, or law."); AFGE Local 916 and Defense Distrib. Depot, Oklahoma City, 50 FLRA 244, 248 (1995) ("Although the Arbitrator did not identify which memoranda of understanding were violated, he was not obligated to set forth findings and a rationale more specific than he provided in this case.").

FLRA carved out one exception to its general rule that is designed to avoid decisions by arbitrators that constitute unarticulated interpretations of contractual reservations of management rights, DOD, DMA, Aerospace Ctr., St. Louis and NFFE Local 1827, 43 FLRA 147, 153-54 (1991):

As a general proposition, we will not disturb an award that is based solely on a contract interpretation. However, where, as here, that contract provision is a reiteration of the management rights provision of the Statute, we must exercise care to ensure that the interpretation is consistent with the Statute, as well as the parties' agreement. If parties intend that a contractual management rights provision which is identical to the language set forth in section 7106 of the Statute be interpreted in a manner that differs from, but is not inconsistent with, the Statute, that should be made known to the arbitrator, who can then clearly specify the basis for an award. The Authority would uphold the award insofar as it is not otherwise inconsistent with law, rule, or regulation. In this case, we find that the Arbitrator did not interpret the parties' agreement so as to restrict the exercise of management's rights in a manner that is inconsistent with the Statute. Consequently, the Arbitrator's award, to this extent, is not inconsistent with the Statute. However, such a finding does not end our inquiry.

...

[W]e are unable to ascertain from the award whether the Arbitrator applied only the provisions of the agreement, or the provisions of the Statute, as well.

...In the absence of a clear understanding as to the basis of the Arbitrator's award, we are unable to assess whether the award is contrary to law, rule and regulation.

Therefore, we will remand this case to the parties for resubmission to the Arbitrator to clarify the basis of his award....

See Dept. of Transp., NHTSA and AFGE Local 3313, 58 FLRA 333, 336 (2003) (although FLRA generally defers to an arbitrator's interpretation of collective bargaining agreement, when arbitrator finds violation of contract provision that restates statutory provision, FLRA exercises care in reviewing that finding to ensure arbitrator's interpretation is consistent with FLRA precedent interpreting the statute); cf. Dept. of State Passport Services and NFFE Local 1998, 71 FLRA 12 (2019) (rejecting challenge to reasoning of award upholding parties' settlement agreement concerning counting of work during periods of computer systems disruptions or outages).

As will be seen from the discussion that follows, FLRA also expects arbitrators to provide articulated awards when awards provide for back pay, counsel fees, and when other remedies depend upon findings of statutory or regulatory violations.
