VII. REVIEW AND ENFORCEMENT OF DECISIONS

Negotiability issues aside, the Reform Act does not provide for direct appeals to FLRA from final Panel decisions. Review is obtained when an aggrieved party files an unfair labor practice charge alleging noncompliance with the decision of the Panel. New York, Div. of Military and Naval Affairs and New York Council, ACT, 2 FLRA 186, 188 (1979). Under SSA and AALJs, IFPTE, 71 FLRA 652, 652-53 (2020) (stay granted following union's complaint in district court, 71 FLRA 763 (2020) (Member DuBester dissenting)):

Section 7119(c)(1) of the Statute establishes the Panel "as an independent entity within the FLRA and commits the Panel the broad authority to make decisions to resolve negotiation impasses."[9] Panel decisions are "not directly reviewable by the Authority or the courts."[10] Rather, parties may challenge a Panel order through other statutory procedures. Specifically, it is an unfair labor practice for an agency or a labor organization "to fail or refuse to cooperate in impasse procedures and impasse decisions."[11] If a party fails or refuses to comply with a Panel order, and is consequently charged with an unfair labor practice, it may challenge the Panel's jurisdiction as part of the ensuing proceedings.[12]

Section 7119(c)(1) of the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute (the Statute) establishes the Panel as "an entity within the Authority"[3] and "authorizes [the Panel] to investigate 'promptly' any negotiation impasse and to 'take whatever action is necessary and not inconsistent with this chapter to resolve the impasse.'"[4] Panel orders are not directly reviewable by the Authority or the courts.[5] Instead, the Statute provides an avenue for parties to challenge a Panel order. Specifically, it is an unfair labor practice (ULP) for an agency or a labor organization "to fail or refuse to cooperate in impasse procedures and impasse decisions."[6] A party that fails or refuses to comply with a Panel order, and is consequently charged with a ULP, may then challenge the Panel's order.[7]

It was implied that Panel decisions could be collaterally reviewed through ULP proceedings, and issues must be raised before the Panel if they are to be reviewed by FLRA. Nevada NG and NAGE Local R12-130, 7 FLRA 245, 252-53 (1981). More recently, and more directly, if, during FSIP proceedings, negotiability issues are raised as to articles imposed by the Panel, a ULP brought to cure noncompliance with the Panel decision must specifically allege negotiability of those articles. When FLRA undertakes that review in the ULP case, it may make a negotiability determination, returning the parties to bargaining if they request that approach should FLRA find a disputed article to be nonnegotiable. Member Frazier explained, in a concurrence to a decision not to issue a general statement of policy, that an FSIP decision may only be reviewed through a ULP proceeding. In that proceeding, the party charged with noncompliance may defend its action on the grounds that the Panel's decision was outside the scope of its jurisdiction or was otherwise contrary to the Statute. Decision on Request for Gen. Statement of Policy or Guidance, 12 FLRA 346, 351 (1983). More explicitly, FLRA reviews issues of FSIP jurisdiction and practice in ULP cases, e.g., whether an issue was appropriately raised by a union request for Panel assistance. NASA Hq. and Am. Fed'n of Technical Eng'rs, Local 9, 12 FLRA 480, 496 (1983) (ALJ Decision). But it is also stated that a final action of FSIP is binding on the parties and is not subject to collateral attack in a ULP proceeding if the only issue is compliance with the FSIP decision. Florida NG and NAGE Local R5-91, 9 FLRA 347, 357 (1982) (ALJ Decision). The prevailing view, however, is that FLRA will also review the language imposed by the Panel to determine if it is contrary to law, e.g., the scope or complexity of a grievance procedure. NASA Hq. and Am. Fed'n of Technical Eng'rs, Local 9, 12 FLRA 480, 500-01 (1983) (ALJ Decision). To obtain court review of a FSIP order, a party must ignore or refuse to follow it, await the filing of a ULP complaint, and then obtain a final, judicially reviewable FLRA decision. Florida NG and NAGE Local R5-91, 9 FLRA 347, 353 (1982) (ALJ Decision). If the FSIP order favors the agency, the union may find it difficult to obtain any review. The agency would not likely charge the union with a ULP for disobeying a FSIP order that favors the agency as to apportionment of the benefits of collective bargaining. See Council of Prison Locals v. Brewer, 735 F.2d 1497, 1499 (D.C. Cir. 1984). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Negotiability Problems During FSIP Proceedings" and in Chapter 10, "Arbitration Award."]

Through ULP proceedings, FLRA has considerable discretion in reviewing FSIP actions and decisions, Antilles Consol. Educ. Assn. v. FLRA, 977 F.3d 10, 18 (D.C. Cir. 2020):

The FLRA further held that the Impasses Panel exceeded its authority by ordering the parties to adopt an entire collective-bargaining agreement, including scores of provisions that the parties had tentatively agreed to before the union sought help from the Panel. The union contends that the agency failed to preserve this issue before the Impasses Panel and that the FLRA decided it incorrectly on the merits.

On the forfeiture point, we hold that the FLRA permissibly considered the agency's objection. No statute or regulation prohibits the FLRA from considering issues that were not raised before the Impasses Panel. Moreover, the dispute here implicates the power of the Impasses Panel, which Congress authorized to act only in the event of a "negotiation impasse." 5 U.S.C. § 7119(b). And the Statute constitutes the Impasses Panel as a subordinate entity within the FLRA, id. § 7119(c), which has "leadership in establishing policies and guidance relating to matters" involving federal- sector collective bargaining, id. § 7105(a)(1). The union notes that parties generally must preserve arguments before agencies in order to raise them in court. See, e.g., United States v. L.A. Tucker Truck Lines, Inc., 344 U.S. 33, 36-38, 73 S. Ct. 67, 97 L. Ed. 54 (1952). True enough, but that rule helps channel issues to the agencies entrusted with primary responsibility for the subject at issue. It does not restrict agencies in deciding how to manage their own components.

An FLRA ULP decision setting aside as nonnegotiable a Panel-directed article excuses the agency from noncompliance with that article. ...In the context of agency-head review of an agreement under § 7114(c) of the Statute, the Authority has recognized a default rule that, if any of the wording in an agreement is timely disapproved, then that disapproval generally prevents the whole agreement from taking effect and binding the parties.[85] However, here, the Agency chose not to submit for agency-head review the complete agreement that the Panel ordered the parties to adopt. And for the reasons explained below, the effects of the legal deficiencies in certain provisions of the successor agreement are not the same here as they would have been if the Agency had conducted agency-head review under § 7114(c).

[85] POPA, 41 FLRA 795, 802 (1991).

In the ULP context, when an agency refuses to comply with Panel-imposed contract provisions, the Authority evaluates the legality of each provision separately to determine the agency's compliance obligations.[86] And in that context, a finding that a Panel-imposed provision is unlawful relieves the agency of its compliance obligation with respect to only that provision; the agency must still comply with the remainder of a Panel decision concerning other, lawful provisions.[87] Further, we note that, in this case, the Panel ordered the parties to adopt--and the Agency has not challenged--a contract provision that reinforces the Agency's obligation to comply with lawful, Panel-imposed contract provisions even when other provisions in the agreement are found unlawful.[88] Thus, we reject the Agency's argument that the legal deficiencies in some provisions of the Panel-imposed successor agreement completely absolved the Agency of its obligation to obey the lawful portions of the Panel's decision.

Although a party may challenge an order of the Panel in a ULP proceeding, it runs afoul of the Statute if the order is deemed proper. Refusal to sign a prenegotiation agreement directed by the Panel constitutes a union violation of § 7116(b)(5). AFGE Local 3732 and Dept. of Transp., Maritime Admin., 16 FLRA 318, 318-19 (1984). Sections 7116(a)(1) and (6) are violated when an agency fails to comply with an order of FSIP directing the inclusion of provisions of a negotiated agreement. California NG and NAGE Local R12-125, 8 FLRA 54, 55 (1982). The failure to implement the FSIP decision violates §§ 7116(a)(6) and (8), and the failure to consult and negotiate with the union regarding the implementation of a Panel decision violates 5 USC 7116(a)(5). Florida NG and NAGE Local R5-91, 9 FLRA 347, 358 (1982) (ALJ Decision); Nevada NG and NAGE Local R12-130, 7 FLRA 245, 264 (1981) (agency violated § 7106(a)(1), (6) when it failed to implement Panel Decision and Order). If a Panel decision resolves disputed articles while the parties are negotiating a broader agreement, the agency violates the Statute by immediately implementing the Panel order when that order requires the parties to adopt the articles into their final agreement. See NTEU and DHHS, 73 FLRA 315, 318 (2022).

Neither an interest arbitration award nor a FSIP order constitutes a final order of FLRA for purposes of judicial enforcement. The enforcement procedure is through a ULP charge rather than through a direct appeal to FLRA for enforcement of either directive. Dept. of Air Force, Air Force Logistics Command and AFGE, 4 FLRA 717, 720 (1980). On reconsideration, FLRA reaffirmed that a violation of an FSIP interest award is to be remedied through the ULP process. That it takes considerable time to do so was not a basis for making the award a final order of FLRA capable of judicial enforcement. Air Force Logistics Command and AFGE, 7 FLRA 839, 839 (1982); see Dept. of Navy, Naval Air Station, Patuxent River and AFGE Local 1603, 8 FLRA 148, 150 (1982) ("The statute does not sanction direct authority review of panel decisions and orders, neither does it sanction petitions to U.S. Courts of appeals for enforcement of such decisions and orders...even assuming that the award of the interest arbitration panel could be regarded as a FSIP decision, resolution of any compliance issues would commence with the unfair labor practice procedures.").
