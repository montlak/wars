5. Proof of AWOL; Telework Status; Leave Request

To prove AWOL, an agency must show by preponderant evidence that an employee was absent and that the absences were not authorized or that a request for leave was properly denied. Little v. Dept. of Transp., 112 MSPR 224, 228-29 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 175 (2009); see Wilson v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-13-0551-I-1 (NP 12/4/2014) ("[W]here absence during work hours is shown, the burden is on the appellant to show that it was authorized. See Avant v. Department of the Air Force, 71 M.S.P.R. 192, 197 (1996)."); Robb v. DOD, 77 MSPR 130, 132-33 (1997) ("To prove an AWOL charge, an agency must show that the employee was absent from duty, and either that his absence was not authorized or that his request for leave was properly denied. See Boscoe v. Department of Agriculture, 54 M.S.P.R. 315, 325 (1992)."); Boscoe v. USDA, 54 MSPR 315, 325 (1992) (stating rule); Staten v. USPS, 26 MSPR 206, 208 (1985) (stating rule); Carr v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 498, 500 (1982) (stating rule); cf. Thompson v. USPS, 62 MSPR 60, 63-64 (1994) (describing in detail the intricacies of postal regulation provisions governing personal absence privileges and attendance requirements for FLSA-exempt postmasters). Patterson v. Dept. of Air Force, 74 MSPR 648, 652 (1997), summarized the cases:

To support a charge of AWOL, an agency must show that the appellant was absent and that either the absence was unauthorized or that a request for leave was properly denied. See Cooke v. US Postal Service, 67 MSPR 401, 404, aff'd, 73 F.3d 380 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). If an appellant has sufficient sick leave to cover the period in question, the agency must grant the leave request where the appellant has provided administratively acceptable evidence of incapacitation due to illness or injury, regardless of whether the employee has complied with applicable leave procedures. See Riley v. Department of the Army, 53 MSPR 683, 689, 689 n.4 (1992). See also Wade v. Department of the Navy, 829 F.2d 1106, 1109 (Fed. Cir. 1987). Where an employee has exhausted all leave, an agency may deny leave without pay and place the employee on AWOL, where there is no foreseeable end to the employee's absence, and the absence is a burden to the agency. Under these circumstances, the Board will examine the record as a whole to determine if the denial was reasonable. See Joyner v. Department of the Navy, 57 MSPR 154, 159 (1993).

See Flood v. Dept. of Army, PH-0752-11-0085-I-2 (NP 4/9/2012) ("We note that an AWOL charge cannot be sustained if an appellant presents administratively acceptable evidence showing that she was incapacitated for duty during the relevant time period if the employee has sufficient sick leave to cover the period of absence. Thom v. Department of the Army, 114 M.S.P.R. 169, ¶ 5 (2010); Valenzuela v. Department of the Army, 107 M.S.P.R. 549, ¶ 9 (2007)."); Thom v. Dept. of Army, 114 MSPR 169, 172 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 110 (2010) ("[T]he agency properly considered him to be AWOL because he failed to provide the agency with administratively acceptable evidence of his incapacitation during the relevant period, and nothing in the record indicates that his medical conditions precluded him from providing such information or caused him to miss medical appointments."); Foreman v. USPS, 89 MSPR 328, 331 ¶ 8 (2001) (agency must grant a leave request when the employee provides administratively acceptable evidence of incapacitation because of illness or injury and the agency may not charge AWOL for that period, regardless of whether the employee complied with leave procedures); Young v. USPS, 79 MSPR 25, 32 (1998) (relying on Patterson); cf. Wesley v. USPS, 94 MSPR 277, 286 ¶ 18 (2003) ("A charge of AWOL will not be sustained if the appellant presents evidence to the Board that was not previously presented to the agency showing that he was incapacitated for duty during the relevant time period."); cf. Bal v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0442-I-1 (NP 9/12/2016) ("Although the reports state that the appellant suffers from major depression as a result of his marital problems and related feelings of worthlessness, they do not establish that he was incapacitated from performing his duties or from requesting leave during the period at issue.") (remanded, Bal v. Dept. of Navy, 729 Fed. Appx. 923 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) (Judge Mayer dissenting), for reassessment of mental impairment when MSPB improper equated impairment with incapacitation).

Patterson distinguished an AWOL charge from the related but distinct charge of inability to perform the duties of a position [a charge discussed in Chapter 6, under "Evaluation of Physical Impairment"], 74 MSPR at 658 n.2:

We further note that the AWOL charge in the present case distinguishes it from cases concerning actions based upon an appellant's inability to perform the duties of a position, and the different analytical approach that accompanies those cases. See e.g., Jackson v. US Postal Service, 73 MSPR 512 (1997); Spencer v. Department of the Navy, 73 MSPR 15 (1997); Merzweiler v. US Postal Service, 69 MSPR 274, 279, aff'd, 98 F.3d 1359 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

"The Board may sustain a charge of AWOL even when the agency fails to prove that the appellant was AWOL for the entire period." Senior v. USPS, 85 MSPR 283, 289 (2000); see Winter v. DOD, SF-0752-15-0467-I-1 (NP 2/18/2016) ("An AWOL charge may be sustained even when the agency fails to prove that the employee was AWOL for the entire period charged. Senior v. U.S. Postal Service, 85 M.S.P.R. 283, 289 (2000)."). Introduction of time and attendance cards into evidence is not an absolute prerequisite to the proof of AWOL. See Cusick v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 161, 163 (1982) (noting that the "appellant herself never questioned the nature of the charge against her"); cf. Arufe v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-16-0217-I-1 (NP 10/6/2022) ("We find that the agency properly considered the appellant AWOL because his medical documentation does not constitute persuasive evidence of incapacitation due to the lack of detail as to his medical condition, diagnosis, and prognosis."). AWOL can be established if the appellant admits he had not notified his supervisor that he would be absent from work and that he had not requested any form of leave. Jackson v. DLA, 10 MSPR 123, 124 (1982) (agency regulation defined AWOL as an "absence from duty which was not authorized or approved or for which a leave request has been denied"). Denial of leave may be demonstrated by proving that the employee ignored a request to return to work. An agency reasonably initiates AWOL charges if it notifies an appellant by certified mail of its desire to have him report to work or provide justification for his absence. It is sufficient, against a claim of nondelivery of the letter, if the appellant's wife signs for the letter. Baldwin v. USPS, 26 MSPR 383, 385 n.1 (1985).

The employee may not shift to the agency the burden of proving that time charged to unauthorized absence was, in fact, not spent on agency business, Office of Hearings & Appeals v. Whittlesey, 59 MSPR 684, 694 (1993):

[Respondent] contends that the agency did not meet its burden of proof because its evidence failed to show that he was not conducting agency business during his absences from the office. Whittlesey suggests that there are many possible legitimate reasons for absence from the office, such as library research, reviewing files in other OHA offices, and discussions off-premises with SSA claimants' attorneys. However, respondent has not contended that he requested or received approval to conduct business away from his official duty station. Such approval is implicitly required by the agency's rule requiring him to work an eight-hour day and to record his comings and goings on a register. The employer is entitled to have employees in attendance when they are expected to be, absent a valid excuse. Davis v. Veterans Administration, 792 F.2d 1111, 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Where absence during work hours is shown, the employee has the burden to show that he had authorization to be out of the office. Respondent presented no evidence that he was authorized to conduct business out of the office during his work hours or that he was engaged in agency business during his absences....

Whittlesey was followed, Avant v. Dept. of Air Force, 71 MSPR 192, 197-98 (1996):

The agency asserted that, from November 1 to December 3, 1993, each appellant left work prior to the end of his duty day without charges to leave, with 16 specifications as to Kratz, and 18 as to Avant.... Instead, the appellants claimed that they were on compensatory time; that they were, in addition, keeping a "mental running total" of compensatory time they had earned within the last six months or so; and that this type of recordkeeping was condoned by their first-line supervisor, Miller.

We find no error in the administrative judge's sustaining of this charge, based on the notes of the month-long surveillance conducted by the two AFOSI agents and on the appellants' admissions that they left work prior to the end of their shifts.

As to the appellants' claim that they were on unofficial undocumented compensatory time, we note that it is well settled that where absence during work hours is shown, the burden is on the appellant to show that it was authorized. See Office of Hearings and Appeals, Social Security Administration v. Whittlesey, 59 MSPR 684, 694 (1993), aff'd, 39 F.3d 1197 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table), cert. denied, __U.S.__, 115 S. Ct. 1690, 131 L. Ed.2d 555 (1995). The administrative judge correctly found no record evidence that either appellant was on any official or approved flexible work schedule that would permit a "pattern of unsubstantiated and undocumented absenteeism." We also find that despite the apparent condonation by Miller, the appellants knew or should have known that their practice of leaving early was not permissible. Accordingly, we find no error in the administrative judge's ruling that Miller's failure of supervision did not extenuate the appellants' "wholesale leave abuse."

If the employee claims that she worked telework and the agency asserts the employee performed no work, the Board may consider whether the employee can provide evidence of work that was performed. See Martin v. GSA, SF-0752-13-0050-B-2 (NP 5/3/2016) ("The appellant has not shown error in the administrative judge's findings that she was not approved for full-time telework, failed to show that she performed any meaningful work during the period in question, and did not request leave to cover the vast majority of her absences. Under the circumstances, we find that the administrative judge properly sustained the AWOL charge. Wesley v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 277, ¶ 15 (2003); see Little, 112 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 6.").

If the agency establishes that an employee should have teleworked under agency continuity of operations regulations during the pandemic and did not, the resultant AWOL-based removal can be sustained, Carter v. DOD, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP):

Before her removal, Ms. Carter was an Acquisition and Financial Support Specialist with Defense's Office of Net Assessment (ONA). In March 2020 and in response to the novel coronavirus pandemic, Ms. Carter was authorized for "Weather and Safety Leave." Keith Walters, chief of staff for ONA, subsequently informed her that she could not remain on leave throughout the pandemic and arranged for her to telework--i.e., provided a laptop, network-access, a detailed outline of her duties and responsibilities, and necessary teleworking resources and training. Ms. Carter, however, did not respond to Mr. Walters nor otherwise attempt to telework.

On December 8, 2020, Mr. Walters notified her that she needed to complete required telework training no later than December 15, 2020, explained that she was required to begin teleworking on January 4, 2021, and stated that she was permitted to take some of her "197 hours of use/lose leave" but was not authorized to take administrative leave. Ms. Carter responded to Mr. Walters the same day, stating:

Please stop asking about telework. Talk with Col Regan (prior chief of staff), my supervisor and you, I am no longer interest in telework agreement. Do not schedule annual leave. I am already on weather and safety leave.

The next day, Mr. Walters issued a written memorandum notifying her that, effective January 4, 2021, she would not be authorized for Weather and Safety Leave, and would be required to report for duty via telework on that date pursuant to Defense's continuity of operations plan. The memorandum again instructed her to complete the required telework training and informed her that failure to follow the procedures outlined in the notice would result in disciplinary action up to and including removal.

Ms. Carter did not complete the required training or begin telework in accordance with Mr. Walters's memorandum email instructions. Consequently, Mr. Walters informed her on January 12, 2021, that she was absent without leave (AWOL), retroactive as of January 4, 2021, the date upon which she was to begin teleworking. Since Ms. Carter did not report for duty until February 4, 2021, her records reflected her AWOL status from January 4 through February 3, 2021.

On April 7, 2021, Defense issued a "Notice of Proposed Removal" based on: (1) AWOL charges, with 21 supporting specifications corresponding to each day she did not report for telework duty, and (2) failure to follow instruction charges, with 23 supporting specifications corresponding to instructions to request approval for leave and to complete required telework training. Ms. Carter provided a written response on April 15, 2021, and Defense issued a final decision on May 21, 2021,5 ordering her removal effective June 4, 2021. Ms. Carter retired instead.

On June 21, 2021, Ms. Carter appealed her removal to the Board. On October 28, 2021, an administrative judge issued an initial decision on her appeal, which became the Board's final decision when she did not petition for Board review within 35 days. The Board found that Defense proved all of the charged misconduct by a preponderance of the evidence. The Board rejected Ms. Carter's argument that Defense had no authority to compel her to telework, noting that Defense's written policies provided the necessary authorization. The Board also rejected Ms. Carter's assertion that she did not receive notice to telework until February 3, 2021, as "incredible." The Board found that it was "highly improbable" that Ms. Carter did not receive any of Mr. Walters many communications about the telework requirement via memorandum, phone calls, and email "as she had no problem communicating with him using these methods prior to December 8, 2020." Instead, the Board found it was "probable that [Ms. Carter] simply refused to engage in any further conversations or communications with [Mr. Walters] regarding telework."

The Board then concluded that Defense established the requisite nexus between Ms. Carter's actions and efficiency of service. The Board reasoned that her AWOL status, which "by its very nature, disrupts the efficiency of the service," and "failure to follow instructions affect[ed] the agency's ability to carry out its mission." Lastly, the Board reviewed Defense's consideration of the Douglas factors, and concluded that Defense did not abuse its discretion in removing her from federal service as the penalty for her conduct. The Board, therefore, affirmed Ms. Carter's removal.

An agency must establish three things when it takes an adverse action against an employee: (1) that, by a preponderance of the evidence, the charged conduct occurred; (2) that there is a nexus between the conduct and efficiency of the service; and (3) that the penalty imposed was reasonable. Bryant v. Nat'l Sci. Found., 105 F.3d 1414, 1416 (Fed. Cir. 1997).

Ms. Carter contends that Defense lacked legal authority to remove her for being AWOL from telework duty or for failing to follow instructions to telework because, under the Telework Enhancement Act, 5 U.S.C. §§ 6501 et seq. (TEA), she was not eligible for and could not be compelled to telework. We disagree.

The TEA requires government agencies to establish telework policies, determine telework eligibility, notify agency employees of their eligibility, provide teleworking training to eligible employees, and treat teleworkers no differently than non-teleworkers. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 6502(a), 6503(a). Before eligible employees participate in telework, the agency must enter into a written telework agreement with the eligible employee that "outlines the specific work arrangement that is agreed to." Id. § 6502(b). Such teleworking policies and agreements notwithstanding, the TEA also requires agencies to "incorporate telework into the continuity of operations plan of that agency," id. § 6504(d)(1), "supersed[ing] any telework policy" "during any period that an executive agency is operating under a continuity of operations plan," id. § 6504(d)(2).

The Board correctly noted that, under established Defense policy, Defense employees typically cannot be ordered to telework unless their duties are designated mission-critical. Dep't of Defense, Instruction 1035.01, Telework Policy, Enclosure 3 § 2(f) (Apr. 7, 2020) (Telework Policy) ("Although use of telework is encouraged, employees cannot be ordered to telework, unless the employee's duties are designated as mission-critical and the employee is required to report to an alternative worksite or the employee's telework agreement addresses this requirement."); U.S. Off. of Pers. Mgmt., 2021 Guide to Telework and Remote Work in the Federal Government, at 14 (Nov. 2021) ("[T]he Act does not obligate an employee to participate in an agency telework program. Accordingly, employee participation in a telework program is voluntary.").

Although Ms. Carter's duties were not designated mission-critical, the Board correctly found that, in light of the novel coronavirus pandemic, Defense had authority to require her to telework pursuant to its continuity of operation (COOP) policies. Defense's COOP policies "supersede the telework policy" and provide that employees not normally eligible for telework may nonetheless be required to telework during a pandemic:

Employees who are telework-ready (i.e., approved and equipped for routine or situational telework) who are not able to report to their assigned office location due to a government closure from a natural or manmade emergency event (e.g., snow emergency, flood, hurricane, earthquake, wild fire, act of terrorism, pandemic) will telework each regularly scheduled work day during the emergency situation. Contingent upon supervisory approval, telework-ready employees may telework when government offices are open with the option for unscheduled telework when weather conditions make commuting hazardous, or similar circumstances compromise employee safety. During any period that a WHS-serviced Component is operating under the COOP plan, that plan will supersede the telework policy and the provisions of the telework agreement.

* * *

In the event of a pandemic health crisis, employees with COOP responsibilities, Service members, and employees who do not have COOP responsibilities but are trained and equipped to telework may be asked to telework to prevent the spread of germs. These employees or Service members should telework on a regular basis to ensure their proficiency and telework's effectiveness in continuing operations. Employees or Service members in positions not typically eligible for telework should telework on a situational basis when feasible.

Dep't of Defense, Dir. of Admin. & Mgmt., Admin. Instruction 117, Telework Program, Enclosure 3 §§ 8(f), (g)(3) (Mar. 31, 2015) (emphasis added); see also Appx. 157-58, Telework Policy, Enclosure 3 § 3(i)(1)-(2) ("During any period that a Component is operating under the COOP plan, that plan shall supersede the telework policy and the provisions of the telework agreement....In the event of a pandemic health crisis,...Employees or Service members in positions not typically eligible for telework should telework on a situational basis when feasible."). Ms. Carter admits that the novel coronavirus pandemic affected Defense's operations, and she does not challenge the validity of Defense's COOP policies regarding emergency telework. Moreover, Defense's COOP policies are consistent with § 6504(d)(1) and (2) of the TEA.

The Board further observed that, consistent with agency policy, Defense initiated a continuity of operations plan in response to the novel coronavirus pandemic and provided Ms. Carter with the necessary equipment (i.e., laptop) and opportunities for training to work from her home (i.e., an approved location for Ms. Carter to perform work) so that she could telework on a situational basis. After many months passed in which Ms. Carter declined requests to consider teleworking, she was instructed to commence telework on January 4, 2021, and there is no dispute that she refused to and did not do so until February 4, 2021. In view of this evidence, the Board's finding that Defense demonstrated by preponderance of evidence that Ms. Carter was AWOL from January 4 through February 3, 2021, and failed to follow instructions to report for telework duty was not arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law.

Regarding nexus, our review is limited by statute to whether the Board's affirmance meets the statutory criteria. 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c). We hold in this case that it does.

An employee's AWOL status, "by its very nature, disrupts the efficiency of the service," and is therefore a proper basis for removal. See Davis v. Veterans Admin., 792 F.2d 1111, 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (holding that "any sustained charge of AWOL is inherently connected to the efficiency of the service"); see also Bryant, 105 F.3d at 1417 (noting that, since Davis, "both this court and the Board have routinely held that the nexus between the charged offense and the efficiency of the service is automatic when the charged offense is AWOL"). Additionally, there is a direct relationship between service efficiency and an agency's rules and regulations regarding attendance and authorized absences because an employee's failure to follow such instructions inherently affects the agency's ability to carry out its mission. Accordingly, the Board did not err in concluding that Defense established the requisite nexus between Ms. Carter's AWOL and failure to follow actions and the efficiency of the service.

Lastly, we are satisfied that the removal penalty selected by Defense, as affirmed by the Board, was supported by substantial evidence and was not an abuse of discretion or violation of law. The record in this case establishes that both Defense and the Board properly considered the relevant aggravating factors based on Ms. Carter's failure to report for telework duty and mitigating factors based on her prior, satisfactory performance record. Moreover, Defense specifically "considered the consistency of the penalty with that imposed upon other employees for the same offense and the adequacy of alternative sanctions to deter future misconduct," noting that "[o]ther employees have been removed for AWOL and Failure or Delay in Carrying Out Written Regulations, Orders, Rules, Procedures, or Instructions." In view of this evidence, we cannot say that the Ms. Carter's removal is so "outrageously disproportionate" to the offense as to constitute an abuse of discretion or violation of law. See Yeschick v. Dep't of Transp., 801 F.2d 383, 384-85 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

If the employee claims that she is not fit for duty because of illness, the employee is obligated to prove the leave entitlement. The agency is not required to pursue a fitness examination unless there is reason to believe that the problem is due to mental illness and the employee has the service credit for retirement. See Staten v. USPS, 26 MSPR 206, 208 & n.2 (1985) (interpreting both Postal Service and OPM regulations; an approach that has somewhat changed by regulations at 5 CFR 831.1203-1204 (2022)); cf. Thomas v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 230, 232-33 (1987) (employee removed for excessive unauthorized absence did not prevail on the argument that the agency should have sent him for a fitness exam prior to removal; OPM regulations then controlling medical evaluations, 5 CFR Part 339, did not cover the appellant's situation). The employee is responsible for providing information requested from her treating physician. The agency need not request the information directly from the doctor. See Goens v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 456, 461 (1989). The employee's proof should demonstrate the nature and extent of the appellant's illness at the time he was charged with AWOL and how the illness affected his ability to comply with agency instructions. Kulinski v. USPS, 37 MSPR 574, 577-78 (1988), supra. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Agency-Initiated Retirement."]

When the employee was terminated because he violated leave-related provisions of a last chance agreement, and the employee's defense is that the agreement was improperly invoked by the agency, it becomes the employee's burden to prove that he was entitled to leave that was improperly denied, Bahrke v. USPS, 98 MSPR 513, 519-20 ¶ 14 (2005):

Although the appellant had not raised the argument, the administrative judge found that because the agency failed to prove that it properly denied the appellant FMLA leave for the absences that were the basis of the August 15, 2003 proposed removal, the LCA based on those absences was invalid. The administrative judge incorrectly placed the burden on the agency to prove that it properly denied the appellant leave under the FMLA. [C] f. Burge v. Department of the Air Force, 82 M.S.P.R. 75 ¶ 13 (1999) (the agency bears the burden of proving that, in a leave-related disciplinary action, it properly denied an eligible employee leave under the FMLA). The action at issue here is the appellant's removal for violating the LCA, not the earlier proposed removal for unacceptable attendance. Thus, the burden was the appellant's to show that the August 15, 2003 proposed removal could not be substantiated, which, if established, would render the LCA involuntary. See Fassett, 85 M.S.P.R. 677 ¶¶ 6-8; Sullivan, 79 M.S.P.R. at 85; Bowie, 72 M.S.P.R. at 44-45.

The appellant's request for leave should be made with reasonable clarity, or the appellant should clarify any ambiguity or confusion as soon as possible to avoid an erroneous AWOL charge. See Young v. USPS, 79 MSPR 25, 32 (1998) (agency may learn of a need for sick leave indirectly, e.g., through communications from relatives and from workers' compensation documents); Meade v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 516, 518 (1982) ("[T]he appellant said no more than the agency could have offered him annual leave for the time he was in jail, and we find no reasonable basis for construing it to be more."). The agency's response to a leave request should be clear. In one situation, when an employee called in and asked for leave, the supervisor's response was to "be in here tomorrow." The Board decided that the leave was impliedly granted and refused to sustain an AWOL charge. Greene v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 502, 504, 5 MSPB 499 (1981).
