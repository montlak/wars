G. AGENCY DECISIONAL PROCEDURES; EX PARTE COMMUNICATIONS

Sometimes the important aspect of an adverse action defense is not what is said, but the source of what is said. People vary in their sensitivity, sensibility, intelligence, wit, and organizational independence or subservience. The decision on an adverse action at the agency level may involve a number of individuals. Some actors are readily identified; some are not. How much behind-the-scenes lobbying is permissible in an adverse action is of concern to appellants who do not feel that their agencies are disclosing the real players or the true motivations in an adverse action. Some problems discussed in this section involve the official who becomes involved in something less than a dispassionate search for truth and equity in the course of an adverse action, the proposing and deciding officials who discuss the case, or the ubiquitous personnel official or agency counsel who gives some seemingly clandestine advice, recommendations, or thinly (or not so thinly) disguised direction. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Due Process Considerations; Notice" and "Waiver of Appeal Through Last Chance or Other Preappeal Agreement."]

Information is considered to be ex parte if the appellant was not informed that it would be taken into account by the agency decisionmaker. Walls v. VA, DE-0752-13-0278-I-1 (NP 9/7/2016). Reliance on ex parte information creates an issue of constitutional dimensions: the tenured federal employee has a property interest in employment to the extent that he is entitled to fair notice of charges and a fair opportunity to respond to charges. Under Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532, 542-46 (1985):

An essential principle of due process is that a deprivation of life, liberty, or property "be preceded by notice and opportunity for hearing appropriate to the nature of the case."... This principle requires "some kind of hearing" prior to the discharge of an employee who has a constitutionally protected property interest in his employment....

...

The essential requirements of due process...are notice and an opportunity to respond. The opportunity to present reasons, either in person or in writing, why proposed action should not be taken is a fundamental due process requirement.... The tenured public employee is entitled to oral or written notice of the charges against him, an explanation of the employer's evidence, and an opportunity to present his side of the story.

The dividing lines between proper and improper communications are indistinct. The bottom line is not. Somehow or another, an adjudicator must be convinced that the minimal due process guaranteed through the Fifth Amendment right to due process was sufficiently compromised that the action against the employee is set aside for lack of fundamental fairness.
