D. CIRCUMSTANTIAL AND SECONDARY EVIDENCE

Circumstantial evidence is indirect proof. It is evidence of a collateral fact permitting the inference of the existence or absence of a material fact. Smoke wafting from a gun is circumstantial evidence the gun was fired; direct proof is testimony of the shooter. Circumstantial evidence can constitute proof by a preponderance of the evidence if there is no significant contrary proof. Burkett v. GSA, 27 MSPR 119, 121 (1985); see Special Counsel v. Lee, 58 MSPR 81, 87 (1993) (Hatch Act case); Special Counsel v. Hathaway, 49 MSPR 595, 603 (1991) ("[I]nferences from circumstantial evidence may in certain instances be strong enough to overcome the effect of direct testimony."); see Anson v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-13-0362-I-1 (NP 3/10/2014) (analyzing data concerning the source of an email: "Circumstantial evidence is based on inferences, rather than the direct observation of eyewitnesses or participants. See Black's Law Dictionary 576 (7th ed. 1999). Nevertheless, a body of circumstantial evidence may be so compelling that there is little doubt as to the truth of the inferred conclusion.").

Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 562 ¶ 39 (1999), involving charges that included conspiracy to counterfeit government checks, noted of circumstantial evidence:

When direct evidence to support a given charge is absent, circumstantial proof may be offered to establish the charge if such evidence makes any fact of consequence more or less probable than it would be without the proffered circumstantial proof. Wigmore On Evidence, Section 38, V. 1-A (1983). While circumstantial evidence may be used to meet the agency's burden of proof, care must be taken to insure that the fact to be proved is supported by a preponderance of the evidence, and that the inferences drawn from the circumstantial evidence depends on the strength of that evidence. Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 41 MSPR 70, 76 (1989), aff'd, 918 F.2d 185, 1990 WL 143796 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table), citing Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 782 F.2d 975, 978 (Fed. Cir. 1986), and Delessio v. U.S. Postal Service, 33 MSPR 517, 519 (1987), aff'd, 837 F.2d 1096, 1987 WL 24174 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table). When there is no significant contrary proof, circumstantial evidence can constitute proof by preponderant evidence. Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 420, 425 (1996).

Gabrielli v. Dept. of Treasury, 322 Fed. Appx. 980, 983 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), also considered the weight of circumstantial evidence:

It is not unreasonable to infer from that circumstantial evidence that Ms. Gabrielli altered the forms. Direct evidence of her conduct is not necessary. "Circumstantial evidence is not only sufficient, but may also be more certain, satisfying and persuasive than direct evidence." Fuji Photo Film Co. v. Jazz Photo Corp., 394 F.3d 1368, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (citation omitted); Beverly v. U.S. Postal Service, 907 F.2d 136, 138 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (affirming the Board's decision to affirm an employee's removal based on credible testimony and circumstantial evidence). Here, the administrative judge credited the testimony of the other witnesses over Ms. Gabrielli's testimony. Such credibility determinations by an administrative judge are virtually unreviewable. See Gibson v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 160 F.3d 722, 725 (Fed. Cir. 1998).

See Parbs v. USPS, 107 MSPR 559, 569 ¶ 20, 2007 MSPB 302 (2007) (aff'd, Parbs v. USPS, 301 Fed. Appx. 923 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)) ("When there is no significant contrary proof, circumstantial evidence can constitute proof by preponderant evidence. E.g., Fouquet v. Department of Agriculture, 82 M.S.P.R. 548, ¶ 39 (1999)."); cf. Delapenia v. MSPB, 409 Fed. Appx. 332, 334 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) ("'In establishing an employee's intention to deceive or mislead the agency, circumstantial evidence may be considered.' Kumferman v. Dep't of the Navy, 785 F.2d 286, 290 (Fed. Cir. 1986).").

An example of circumstantial evidence overcoming direct testimony (denying misconduct) by the appellant is Suarez v. DHUD, 96 MSPR 213, 221 ¶ 25 (2004):

...To the extent that the AJ discounted the agency's evidence because it was circumstantial, rather than direct, this was error. The agency was only required to prove its case by preponderant evidence, that is, by the degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, would accept as sufficient to find that a contested fact is more likely true than not true. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(c)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(q)]. In a case such as this, were there are only two people who were in a position to provide direct evidence in this case, one of whom is deceased, and the other of whom is the appellant, the agency would have to rely on circumstantial evidence to prove its case. The mere fact that there are no eyewitnesses to some of the crucial events does not, in and of itself, mean that the Board cannot draw whatever inferences that the evidence suggests. If the agency provided enough circumstantial evidence to lead the Board to conclude that it is more likely than not that the appellant committed the charged misconduct, this is sufficient to meet the agency's burden of proof, regardless of the absence of any direct evidence. See Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 420, 425 (1996) (circumstantial evidence can constitute proof by preponderant evidence).

Circumstantial evidence is generally used to establish intent if intent is implicit in the charge. Circumstantial evidence is sufficient to prove an intent to defraud, a state of mind that can seldom be directly proved. Melillo v. USPS, 4 MSPR 483, 484-85, 5 MSPB 20 (1981); see Caronia v. DOJ, 78 MSPR 201, 213 (1998) (overruled on other grounds, Southerland v. DOD, 119 MSPR 566, 2013 MSPB 46 (2013)) (intent is a state of mind proven by circumstantial evidence after all evidence regarding intent, including a plausible explanation, is considered); Pappas v. OPM, 76 MSPR 152, 156 (1997) (in a suitability appeal, the specific intent to make a false statement concerning the background described in an employment application may be inferred when the alleged misrepresentation was made with reckless disregard for the truth).

Although circumstantial evidence may be used to satisfy the agency's burden of proof, inferences allowable from that evidence depend on the strength of the evidence. No per se evidentiary rules apply. All the evidence must be considered, particularly in cases involving intent. See Smith v. USPS, 41 MSPR 70, 76 (1989); Delessio v. USPS, 33 MSPR 517, 519 (1987) ("[I]n cases such as this, in which proof of intent must be derived inferentially from circumstantial evidence, no per se evidentiary rules apply and all of the evidence must be considered."); Robinson v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 110, 115 (1988) (finding of patient abuse was reversed when the appellant provided credible evidence and the agency's secondary or hearsay evidence was discounted as a result of the agency's failure to present the testimony of witnesses with firsthand knowledge of the events); Jablonski v. VA, 13 MSPR 637, 639 (1982) (evidence of patient abuse was credible, despite the retraction of a patient's statement, based on the affidavits and statements of nurses who interviewed and examined the patient within minutes of the alleged abuse). "The variety of inconsistent inferences which may be drawn with equal logic and probability from circumstances deprives any single such inference of greater probative weight than a contrary inference." Maxfield v. Dept. of Transp., 12 MSPR 42, 46 n.3 (1982) (citing Remy v. VA, 8 MSPR 141, 146, 7 MSPB 624 (1981) (weighing conflicting evidence and inferences pertaining to a charge of intentionally disabling agency air traffic control equipment)).

Related to circumstantial evidence is secondary evidence. Evidence of employment status, for example, is frequently presented through copies of appointment papers, e.g., copies of forms SF-50 or SF-52. If those documents become unavailable or were destroyed, the Board accepts secondary evidence through testimony or other documents, e.g., a collective bargaining contract, that sheds some light on the disputed issue. See, e.g., Fredeluces v. OPM, 57 MSPR 598, 602 n.4 (1993).

The Board may infer the existence of evidence from documents referring to that evidence. Although the best evidence rule might preclude other adjudicators from establishing the existence and contents of documents not in the record, the Board is not deterred. In Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 450, 456 n.5 (1990), the Board commented on a medical report:

We find that we may consider the September 9, 1988, letter from Dr. Juguilon in this case, despite the fact that it is not contained in the record, because the following evidence in the record establishes both its existence and its substance: (1) a letter dated September 20, 1988, from the agency's representative to the appellant's representative acknowledging receipt of a letter of September 9, 1988, containing "a statement from Dr. Juguilon regarding [appellant's] fitness for duty from July 1980 to February 6, 1983,"...and (2) the affidavit of Dr. Juguilon, dated June 5, 1989, stating that he signed appellant's disability retirement application "only to acknowledge that [appellant] had a medical condition" and that "he steadfastly maintained that [appellant] could work."...

See Garcia v. DHS, DA-0752-15-0297-I-1 (NP 8/24/2016) ("[H]e contends that the agency should have called as a witness the investigator who conducted his background investigation interview to authenticate the affidavit she generated for the appellant's signature at the completion of the interview....[T]he appellant is essentially invoking the best evidence rule, which does not apply in Board proceedings. See Howard v. Office of Personnel Management, 31 M.S.P.R. 617, 620 (1986). The record already contains evidence that is sufficient to sustain the charge, in particular, the appellant's stipulations.").

A charge of drug use was proved by preponderant evidence based on testimony that the appellant was driving for several hours when he was stopped and that the investigating officer smelled marijuana smoke in and confiscated a partially smoked marijuana cigarette from the vehicle. Williams v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 173, 178 (1989). Another example of the probative value of circumstantial evidence is Boylan v. USPS, 12 MSPR 524, 527 (1982), sustaining removal of a letter carrier found to have discarded mail based on the discovery of mail dumped along his route. The employee could not explain how the mail came to be there; there was no forced entry into his vehicle and no indication that the mail in his truck was disturbed. The mail was third class and recently posted.

Despite its acceptance of circumstantial evidence, the Board does not embrace all the evidentiary variants employed in tort law. Creer v. USPS, 62 MSPR 656, 659-60 (1994), rejected the use of an evidentiary presumption in tort law, known as res ipsa loquitur, i.e., the "fact speaks for itself." Nevertheless, the Board sustained the charge, failing to report a vehicular accident, on the basis of preponderant evidence:

In concluding that the appellant had an accident that resulted in damage to the vehicle, the administrative judge applied the doctrine of res ipsa loquitur. He found that, absent an explanation, a showing that the appellant received the vehicle in good condition, that he had exclusive possession of it, and that it had damage to its right front wheel well and door frame upon his return was reasonable evidence that the appellant had an accident with the vehicle that he failed to report.

We do not agree that this well-settled principle of tort law has any evidentiary relevance to this case. The doctrine of "[r] es ipsa loquitur allows a plaintiff to prove the defendant's negligence by circumstantial evidence 'when the direct evidence concerning the cause of the injury is primarily within the knowledge and control of the defendant.'" Neace v. Laimans, 951 F.2d 139, 141 (7th Cir. 1991), quoting Senase v. Johns, 96 III. App.3d 164, 51 III. Dec. 546, 549, 420 N.E.2d 1104, 1107 (1981). "Res ipsa loquitur acts as a form of circumstantial evidence that permits an inference of negligence to be drawn from a set of proven facts." Reber v. United States, 951 F.2d 961, 964 (9th Cir. 1991), cert. denied, _ U.S. _, 112 S. Ct. 1675, 118 L. Ed.2d 393 (1992); see also O'Donnell v. Elgin J. & E. Ry. Co., 338 U.S. 384, 390, 70 S. Ct. 200, 204, 94 L. Ed. 187 (1949). Unlike these cases, there was no showing here that the direct evidence concerning the cause of the vehicular damage was primarily within the knowledge and control of the appellant. Nor was the appellant charged with negligence. Therefore, we find that the administrative judge erred by applying the doctrine of res ipsa loquitur to sustain the appellant's removal.

We find further, however, that the administrative judge's error did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights. See Panter v. Dept. of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984). While there is no direct evidence that the appellant had an accident with the vehicle while it was within his control, the circumstantial evidence presented is sufficient to show that it is more likely than not true that he had.

Circumstantial evidence may be used to satisfy an agency's burden of proof, but the inferences allowable from such evidence depend upon the strength of that evidence. Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 41 MSPR 70, 76 (1989), aff'd, 918 F.2d 185 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table); Delessio v. U.S. Postal Service, 33 MSPR 517, 519, aff'd, 837 F.2d 1096 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table). The record here shows that, when the vehicle was assigned to the appellant on October 7, 1992, it was undamaged, that the vehicle remained in the appellant's control during his tour of duty on that date, and that no other employee was assigned the vehicle until October 8, 1992, when the damage was discovered. Further, we concur in the administrative judge's finding that the appellant's testimony that he did not know how the damage happened was improbable because the damage to the truck's right-front quarter was the type of damage that results from driving a truck over a curb or against a road hazard, and in the administrative judge's consideration of the uncontroverted fact that the appellant told his supervisor that the damage probably occurred on Bessemer Avenue, one of the streets along his postal route. Thus, we find it more likely than not that the appellant had a vehicular accident on October 7, 1992, and that he failed to report it. We therefore conclude that the agency has supported its charge by preponderant evidence.

See Jefferson v. DLA, 22 MSPR 10, 13 (1984) (inferences allowed from circumstantial evidence depend upon its strength).
