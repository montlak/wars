1. Attorney-Client Relationship; Fee Payment

In O'Donnell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 445, 453, 2 MSPB 604 (1980), OPM argued that the word "incurred" in 7701(g)(1) permits the Board only to direct an agency to reimburse an employee for attorney fees actually incurred by the employee. If counsel is retained by a union, OPM argued, the employee incurs no fees and no fees may be awarded. The Board rejected that argument, noting that in court cases the presence of an attorney-client relationship, rather than evidence of payment of fees, entitles prevailing litigants to receive attorney fee awards. Case law construing other fee statutes permits recovery of reasonable attorney fees by public interest or union-paid counsel if legal services were rendered to an otherwise nonpaying litigant in the course of an attorney-client relationship:

In construing the word "incurred" in 7701(g)(1), the broad remedial purposes of the Reform Act must be considered.... That provision was clearly intended to remove the cost of legal representation as a burden or barrier to relief from unfounded agency actions in appropriate cases. Moreover, in a broader sense, the award of attorney fees under 7701(g)(1), by assuring payment for the cost of vindicating employee rights when the interest of justice so warrants, furthers the statutory mandate that federal agencies adhere to the merit system principles set forth in the Reform Act.

Counsel fees are incurred even though the appellant has no legal obligation to pay them, as long as there is an attorney-client relationship and counsel provided legal services for the appellant in a Board appeal. Farrar v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 26, 27, 5 MSPB 106 (1981) (citing O'Donnell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 445, 452 (1980)); see AFGE v. FLRA, 944 F.2d 922, 933 (D.C. Cir. 1991) ("MSPB has held under Section 7701(g) that the fact that an employee had no contractual obligation to pay for legal services does not render inappropriate an attorneys' fee award to union-paid counsel"); Anderson v. GPO, 55 MSPR 548, 550 (1992) (requisite relationship existed between the appellant and lawyers employed by a law school and acting through a law school clinic; a cardinal point in establishing an attorney-client relationship included the client's belief that he was consulting a lawyer and "his manifested intention to seek professional legal advice"). The attorney-client relationship exists if the appellant consults with an attorney who did not become involved in the Board proceedings. Dungan v. USPS, 25 MSPR 349, 350-51 n.2 (1984); see Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 78 MSPR 198, 199-200 (1998) (awarding fees for consultations with counsel prior to entry into a retainer agreement). The relationship exists if counsel first enters the case during proceedings in an appellate court and the case is then remanded to the Board; formal notice of appearance in the Board case is not required to establish the existence of the attorney-client relationship. Gearan v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 313, 316-17 (1989). But to obtain reimbursement for fees, one must be a proper representative. If an attorney is disqualified because of a conflict, compensation awarded that attorney may not include services following disqualification. Price v. Dept. of Treasury, 26 MSPR 513, 514-15 (1985) (immaterial that the Board later determined a disqualification unlawful; an award of fees to the first attorney would duplicate payment for representation); cf. Phillips v. GSA, 924 F.2d 1577 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (considering proper applicant for EAJA fees) [Refer in this chapter to "Hourly Rate; Pro Bono or Contingent Representation; COLA; Expenses"; refer in this chapter to "Compensable Services."]

What does this mean in practice? Although it is routine for lawyers to charge an hourly rate on the basis of a written fee agreement that may or may not include a retainer, other arrangements between client and counsel support fees. An appellant may be represented by a friend, family member, law professor, or retired practitioner, and those attorneys may claim fees without retainer agreements. They may need to produce more paperwork to demonstrate the value of their time than the law firm member who uses a standard fee agreement, but they can obtain compensation. All that is necessary is that the fee be claimed for an attorney who performs services traditionally provided by counsel. Under O'Donnell, the client need not even be presented with a bill.
