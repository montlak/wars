5. Order and Burden of Proof

"Discrimination on the basis of a handicapping condition is an affirmative defense that must be pleaded and proven by the employee who asserts it." Burnette v. USPS, 12 MSPR 230, 232 (1982). Pugh v. USPS, 81 MSPR 313, 321 ¶ 14 (1999), explained the general elements of a prima facie disability discrimination case:

...Although the necessary elements of a prima facie case of disability discrimination will vary according to the particular facts and circumstances at issue, they generally include: a showing that the person is a disabled person and the action appealed was based on his disability; and, to the extent possible, an articulation of a reasonable accommodation under which the appellant believes he could perform the essential duties of his position or of a vacant position to which he could be reassigned. Savage v. Department of the Navy, 36 MSPR 148, 151-52 (1988). A disabled person is one who "(i) [h]as a physical or mental impairment which substantially limits one or more of such person's major life activities; (ii) [h]as a record of such an impairment; or (iii) [i]s regarded as having such an impairment." 29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(1); Yates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 172, 178 (1996)....

The appellant may introduce evidence of his disability for the first time at the Board hearing, for that hearing is de novo. Konieczko v. USPS, 47 MSPR 509, 514 (1991).

ANALYSIS

The Board has jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's decision under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) when the subject matter of the grievance is one over which the Board has jurisdiction, the appellant has alleged discrimination under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1) in connection with the underlying action, and a final decision has been issued. Kirkland v. Department of Homeland Security, 119 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 10 (2013). Each of these conditions has been satisfied in this case. First, the appellant's grievance concerns her removal for unacceptable performance, a subject matter over which the Board has jurisdiction. See 5 U.S.C. § 4303(e); Galloway v. Social Security Administration, 111 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 11 (2009). Second, the appellant alleged in her grievance and alleges in this request for review that the agency's action was based on disability discrimination. Finally, the arbitrator has issued a final decision.

The Board's standard of review of an arbitration decision is deferential. Kirkland, 119 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 12. The Board will modify or set aside an arbitration decision only when the arbitrator has erred in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation. Id. The appellant does not object to the arbitrator's findings concerning her failure to demonstrate a minimally acceptable level of performance during her PIP period, and we discern no reason to disturb those findings. The appellant's arguments concern her reasonable accommodation claim and her assertion that the agency's action did not promote the efficiency of the service. We address each in turn.

The appellant did not prove her affirmative defense of disability discrimination.

The appellant alleges that the agency violated the Rehabilitation Act by failing to process her request for a reasonable accommodation. An employer commits unlawful disability discrimination by "not making reasonable accommodations to the known physical or mental limitations of an otherwise qualified individual with a disability who is an applicant or employee" unless the agency can demonstrate that the accommodation would impose an undue hardship. 42 U.S.C. § 12112(b)(5)(A). An appellant may establish a disability discrimination claim based on failure to accommodate by showing that: (1) She is a disabled person; (2) the action appealed was based on her disability; and (3) to the extent possible, there was a reasonable accommodation under which the appellant believes she could perform the essential duties of her position or of a vacant position to which she could be reassigned.[5] Kirkland, 119 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 18. In addition, once an employee informs the agency that she requires an accommodation, the agency must engage in an interactive process to determine an appropriate accommodation. Id. The failure to engage in the interactive process alone does not violate the Rehabilitation Act; rather the appellant must show that this omission resulted in failure to provide reasonable accommodation. Sanchez v. Department of Energy, 117 M.S.P.R. 155, ¶ 18 (2011).

[5] As a federal employee, the appellant's claim arises under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, but the regulatory standards for the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) have been incorporated by reference into the Rehabilitation Act and are applied to determine whether there has been a Rehabilitation Act violation. Kirkland, 119 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 18 n.5. The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA) became effective on January 1, 2009, and the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission subsequently issued amended regulations and guidance. Id. The ADAAA did not change the statutory provisions regarding reasonable accommodation. Id.

The ADAAA prohibits discrimination "against a qualified individual on the basis of disability in regard to job application procedures, the hiring, advancement, or discharge of employees, employee compensation, job training, and other terms, conditions, and privileges of employment." See 42 U.S.C. § 12112(a). The ADAAA defines "qualified individual," in part, to mean "an individual who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the employment position that such individual holds or desires." See 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8). The ADAAA defines "disability" to mean: "a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities of such individual; a record of such an impairment; or being regarded as having such an impairment (as described in paragraph (3))." See 42 U.S.C. § 12102(1)(A)-(C). "Major Life Activities" include, but are not limited to, "caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, seeing, hearing, eating, sleeping, walking, standing, lifting, bending, speaking, breathing, learning, reading, concentrating, thinking, communicating, and working." See 42 U.S.C. § 12102(2)(A).

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's (EEOC's) ADAAA regulations provide that the term "'substantially limits' shall be construed broadly in favor of expansive coverage to the maximum extent permitted by the terms of the ADAAA. 'Substantially limits' is also not meant to be a demanding standard." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(i). "An impairment is a disability within the meaning of this section if it substantially limits the ability of an individual to perform a major life activity as compared to most people in the general population. An impairment need not prevent, or significantly or severely restrict, the individual from performing a major life activity in order to be considered substantially limiting. Nonetheless, not every impairment will constitute a disability within the meaning of this section." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(ii). "The primary object of attention in cases brought under the ADA should be whether covered entities have complied with their obligations and whether discrimination has occurred, not whether an individual's impairment substantially limits a major life activity. Accordingly, the threshold issue of whether an impairment 'substantially limits' a major life activity should not demand extensive analysis." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(ii). "The comparison of an individual's performance of a major life activity to the performance of the same major life activity by most people in the general population usually will not require scientific, medical, or statistical analysis." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(v). The term "reasonable accommodation" includes job restructuring; part-time or modified work schedules; reassignment to a vacant position; acquisition or modification of equipment or devices; appropriate adjustment or modifications of examinations, training materials, or policies; the provision of qualified readers or interpreters; and other similar accommodations for individuals with disabilities. 42 U.S.C. § 12111(9)(B); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(2)(ii).

Despite a full and fair opportunity to develop the record concerning her disability discrimination claim based on a failure to accommodate, the appellant failed to demonstrate that the action appealed was based on her disability or that there was a reasonable accommodation under which she believed she could perform the essential duties of her position or of a vacant position to which she could be reassigned. See 42 U.S.C. § 12112(b)(5)(A). In support of her claim, the appellant identifies two letters from her doctor, Felton Anderson, dated August 20, 2009, and August 24, 2009. The August 20, 2009 letter states as follows:

I have been [the appellant's] attending physician since June 2008. She is currently being treated for anxiety and depression. Due to these conditions it is imperative that she receive special accommodations and restoration of her leave immediately.

The August 24, 2009 letter states:

I have been [the appellant's] attending physician since June 2008. Due to the overwhelming stress that my patient is experiencing, at this time I am requesting that [the appellant] be allowed to work no more than three days a week for the next 30 days. After that time she will be reevaluated. This is to be effective immediately. Her limited work schedule will allow us to properly gage the benefits of her new therapy.

It has come to my attention that [the appellant's] sick coverage is depleted. Please provide her coverage from the company sick leave bank.

These documents, and the appellant's brief testimony that she was depressed, stressed, and "dealing with anxiety on medication" are the only significant pieces of evidence in the record concerning the appellant's medical condition. Although the appellant did not include any documents concerning diagnoses, treatment, duration of her condition, severity of her condition, or how it affects a major life activity, we still find that she demonstrated that she was "disabled" under the ADAAA's expansive definition of the term.

The appellant testified that she submitted both letters from Dr. Anderson to her supervisor, Jane Tamai, in August 2009. She also testified that Ms. Tamai then requested more information. According to the record, Ms. Tamai responded by email on August 27, 2009, to inquire whether the appellant intended to take unpaid leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA) and to indicate that the agency required further information from the doctor regarding medical certification under the FMLA. The record also reflects that the appellant responded by stating that she was requesting advanced leave, that she would forward Ms. Tamai's request to her physician and attorney, and that she would respond to Ms. Tamai once she received an update. Instead, on September 18, 2009, the union submitted a demand to bargain, stating that the agency violated the ADA when Ms. Tamai asked the appellant to submit updated medical history and information concerning her request for reasonable accommodation.[7] The appellant did not submit further documentation concerning her medical condition. Further, the record does not contain any evidence about how her medical condition affected her ability to perform her job or how an accommodation would have allowed her to perform the essential duties of her job or of a vacant position to which she could have been reassigned. See 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8); Sanders v. Social Security Administration, 114 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 18 (2010). Given the appellant's failure to cooperate with Ms. Tamai when she reasonably requested additional information on the appellant's medical condition, we find that the appellant has failed to establish a disability discrimination claim based on a failure to accommodate. See Simpson v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 346, ¶¶ 17-18 (2010) (finding that the appellant did not prove that he was denied a reasonable accommodation in part because he was not responsive to the agency's requests for medical information); see also EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, http://www.eeoc.gov/policy/docs/accommodation.html#requesting [now at https://www.eeoc.gov/laws/guidance/enforcement-guidance-reasonable-accommodation-and-undue-hardship-under-ada#requesting] (question 6) (the employer may ask for reasonable documentation about the disability and functional limitations because the employer is entitled to know that the individual has a covered disability for which she needs a reasonable accommodation).

[7] The record also contains an Accommodation Request Form indicating that the appellant had a temporary psychiatric disability and needed an altered work schedule. The testimony of Sheila Wilson, the union representative, regarding the timing of these forms is not clear.

With respect to the appellant's argument that the agency failed to engage in the interactive process, the appellant asserts that Ms. Tamai acknowledged that she was not familiar with laws governing reasonable accommodation, and that the union's demand to bargain over the appellant's request for a reasonable accommodation went unanswered).[8] An agency's failure to engage in the interactive process alone does not violate the Rehabilitation Act; rather, the appellant must show that this omission resulted in failure to provide reasonable accommodation. Sanchez, 117 M.S.P.R. 155, ¶ 18. In any event, although Ms. Tamai perceived the appellant's request for unpaid leave as an action under the FMLA and not a formal request for reasonable accommodation, her actions still reasonably fulfilled the agency's initial obligation to engage in the interactive process. Furthermore, it was the appellant's refusal to provide additional medical documentation that cut off the process; therefore, the appellant has not shown that the agency failed to engage in the interactive process. In addition, the appellant did not submit argument or evidence concerning how a reduced schedule or any other accommodation would have allowed her to perform in her position or in any other vacant position to which she could have been reassigned. See Gonzalez-Acosta v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 277, ¶¶ 16-17 (2010).

After filing a grievance over the appellant's removal on November 1, 2010, the union amended the grievance to include the disability discrimination claim.

Finally, contrary to the appellant's bare assertion concerning violations of the agency's policy, it appears that Ms. Tamai did have the authority, per agency policy, to both process the appellant's request and gather sufficient information or documentation to determine that the appellant had a disability. Thus, we find that the appellant has failed to establish a disability discrimination claim based on a failure to accommodate.

The Board finds that section 501 of the Act, 29 USC § 791, is also intended to be part of the general corpus of discrimination law.[6] The analysis utilized in establishing a prima facie case of discrimination set forth in McDonnell Douglas is, therefore, applicable to discrimination based on physical or mental handicap under section 501 of the Act. The Board recognizes, as did the Supreme Court in McDonnell Douglas, supra, 411 U.S. 802 n.13, 93 S. Ct. at 1824 n.13, that the criteria to establish a prima facie case will vary according to the factual situation and the nature of the agency action. Cf. Mahurin v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 372, 373, 4 MSPR 302, 304 (1980). However, for purposes of this removal action, if appellant establishes that he is a "qualified handicapped person," 29 CFR § 1613.702(f), for purposes of employment, and that the agency has failed to accommodate his handicap condition, a prima facie case raising an inference of handicap discrimination by the agency will be established. Appellant may establish that he is a "qualified handicapped person" with respect to employment if he shows that he is a "handicapped person" within the definition of 29 CFR § 1613.702(a), and if, to the extent possible, he articulates a reasonable accommodation under which he can perform the essential functions of his position without endangering the health and safety of himself and others.

Once a prima facie case is established, the burden of going forward with the evidence, although not the ultimate burden of persuasion on the issue of discrimination, shifts to the agency to demonstrate that its action was based on some legitimate nondiscriminatory reason. McDonnell Douglas, supra, 411 U.S. at 802, 93 S. Ct. at 1824. Such legitimate nondiscriminatory reason, in this case, includes demonstration by the agency that reasonable accommodation of appellant's physical handicap "would impose an undue hardship on the operation of its program." 29 CFR § 1613.704(a). If the agency's reason rebuts the prima facie case of discrimination, appellant must "be afforded a fair opportunity to show" that the agency's stated reason for failing to accommodate his handicap is "in fact pretext" for prohibited handicap discrimination. McDonnell Douglas, supra, at 804, 93 S. Ct. at 1825. If the presiding official finds that appellant has carried his burden of proof that the removal action was based on a prohibited personnel practice of handicap discrimination in violation of 5 USC § 2302(b)(1)(D), the action may not be sustained. 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.56(b)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(2)].

To state a disability discrimination claim based upon a failure to accommodate, the appellant must prove that: (1) he is a disabled person; (2) the action appealed was based on his disability; and (3) to the extent possible, he must articulate a reasonable accommodation under which he believes he could perform the essential duties of his position or of a vacant funded position to which he could be reassigned. To maintain either a failure to accommodate claim or a disability-based disparate treatment claim, an appellant must show that he was a qualified individual with a disability at the time of the alleged unlawful discriminatory conduct. See Wilson v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01A14669, 2003 WL 660615, at *3 (E.E.O.C. Feb. 21, 2003); see also Combs v. Social Security Administration, 91 M.S.P.R. 148, ¶ 23 (2002). A qualified individual with a disability is an individual with a disability "who satisfies the requisite skill, experience, education and other job-related requirements of the employment position such individual holds or desires, and who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of such position." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(m); see Henson, 110 M.S.P.R. 624, ¶ 7.

As stated, it is undisputed that a valid CDL was required for the performance of the appellant's job duties as a Motor Vehicle Operator. Thus, by losing his CDL, the appellant failed to maintain a requirement of his position that was necessary to perform his essential duties. Because he was no longer technically qualified for his position, we find that, at the time of the agency's removal action, the appellant was not a qualified individual with a disability. See, e.g., Malbouf v. Accordingly, we modify the initial decision to find that the appellant was not a qualified individual with a disability who could assert either a denial of reasonable accommodation claim or a disability-based disparate treatment claim.

Widnall, EEOC Petition No. 03960050 (Jul. 10, 1996); Guillory v. Dalton, EEOC Petition No. 01945298 (Jan. 24, 1996); Hutchinson v. West, EEOC Petition No. 03940051 (Mar. 31, 1995).

Where an appellant must rely on indirect evidence to establish his disability discrimination claim, i.e., where the agency alleges that it was unaware that he was disabled, or that the action was taken for a reason unrelated to the alleged disability, the legal analysis will follow the burden-shifting framework set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-04, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 1824-25, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973), and Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 255-57, 101 S. Ct. 1089, 1094-96, 67 L. Ed.2d 207 (1981). See Monette v. Electronic Data Systems Corp., 90 F.3d 1173, 1184-86 (6th Cir. 1996). Once the appellant has established a prima facie case, the burden of production shifts to the employer to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action. Id. If the employer satisfies this burden of production, then the appellant must introduce evidence to show that the proffered explanation is pretextual. Id. at 1176 (citing St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, 509 U.S. 502, 515-16, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 2751-52, 125 L. Ed.2d 407 (1993)).

Disability discrimination claims, however, generally involve direct, rather than indirect, evidence that the agency took the appellant's mental or physical condition into account in making its decision. We note that the Supreme Court developed the McDonnell Douglas/Burdine burden-shifting analysis as a method of proof on the element of the employer's subjective intent, where no direct evidence of such intent exists.... Therefore, it is inapplicable where there is direct evidence of discrimination, i.e., where, as here, the agency acknowledges that the appellant was disabled, and that the disability was the basis for his removal. Id. at 841-42; Barth v. Gelb, 2 F.3d 1180, 1185 (D.C. Cir. 1993), cert. denied, 511 U.S. 1030, 114 S. Ct. 1538, 128 L. Ed.2d 190 (1994). Similarly, it is inapplicable in cases where an appellant alleges that an agency's facially neutral policy or practice affects disabled individuals in a significantly discriminatory manner, or that a job function deemed "essential" by the agency is not essential. See Monette, 90 F.3d at 1178 n.5, 1184. In such cases, a different inquiry is required.

In setting forth the appropriate analytical framework for cases involving direct evidence of disability discrimination, it is necessary to distinguish between those cases in which the appellant alleges that he can perform the essential functions of the position without reasonable accommodation, and those in which the appellant concedes that he cannot perform the essential functions of the position without reasonable accommodation. See Monette, 90 F.3d at 1182-83. In the first type of case, objective evidence will usually be sufficient to establish whether the appellant's disability renders him unqualified to perform the essential functions of the job. Id. Alternatively, where the appellant seeks some form of accommodation, he must show that he is a disabled person, and that the action appealed was based on his disability, and, to the extent possible, he must articulate a reasonable accommodation under which the appellant believes he could perform the essential duties of his position or of a vacant position to which he could be reassigned. See Sheehan v. Dept. of the Navy, 66 MSPR 490, 493 (1995); Savage v. Dept. of the Navy, 36 MSPR 148, 151-52 (1988).

If the appellant establishes a prima facie case, the agency must produce evidence to rebut the appellant's claim. If the employer claims that the disabled individual is unqualified to perform the job, even with the proposed accommodation, the disabled individual must prove that he would, in fact, be qualified to perform the essential elements of the job if the employer were to adopt the proposed accommodation, and that the proposed accommodation is objectively reasonable. The burden of proof remains with the appellant in such a case. See Monette, 90 F.3d at 1183. If the appellant does prove that he can perform his job, however, or a position to which he can be reassigned, with reasonable accommodation, and/or the agency concedes this point, the burden of production shifts to the agency to show that the reasonable accommodation at issue would create an undue hardship. In determining whether a proposed accommodation would create an undue hardship for the agency, the Board will consider: (1) The overall size of the agency's program with respect to the number of employees, number and type of facilities and size of budget; (2) the type of agency operation, including the nature and composition of the agency's work force; and (3) the nature and cost of the accommodation. See 29 CFR 1613.704(c); Barth, 2 F.3d at 1186-87. As the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit noted in Monette:

Put simply, if the employer claims that a proposed accommodation will impose an undue hardship, the employer must prove that fact. If the employer claims instead that the disabled individual would be unqualified to perform the essential functions of the job even with the proposed accommodation, the disabled individual must prove that he or she would in fact be qualified for the job if the employer were to adopt the proposed accommodation.

Id. at 1184.

Ultimately, the appellant must prove that he is a qualified person with disabilities, i.e., a disabled individual who can perform the essential functions of his position, with or without reasonable accommodation, without endangering the health and/or safety of himself and/or others. See 29 CFR 1614.203(a); Sargent v. Dept. of the Air Force, 55 MSPR 387, 395 (1992). However, to meet his burden of proof with respect to establishing a prima facie case where, as here, it is undisputed that the appellant could not perform his job without some form of reasonable accommodation, the appellant need merely articulate a reasonable accommodation under which he believes he could perform the necessary duties of his position or of a vacant position to which he could be assigned. See Gray v. USPS, 59 MSPR 142, 147-48 (1993); see also Lynch v. Dept. of Educ., 52 MSPR 541, 545 (1992), dismissed, 21 F.3d 1084 (Fed. Cir. 1994); Nordquist v. USPS, 37 MSPR 244, 246 (1988). In this case, as noted above, it is undisputed that the appellant suffered from a physical impairment, i.e., degenerative disc disease, resulting in chronic lower back pain. It is further undisputed that, as a consequence of his impairment, the appellant is substantially limited in the major life activities of lifting, sitting, standing, and bending. See 29 CFR 1614.203(a)(1)-(3). Further, the agency regarded him as having such an impairment. Id. There is also no question that the appellant's disability caused his removal.

In reaching its determination with respect to the appellant's ability to perform the essential functions of his position with accommodation, the agency is obligated to fully assess the appellant's medical condition, his job skills, the work environment, and the agency's resources. See McKinney v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 01940021 (Mar. 16, 1995); see also Hall v. USPS, 857 F.2d 1073, 1080 (6th Cir. 1988) (the burden is on the employer to present credible evidence that a reasonable accommodation is not possible in a particular situation; determining whether the employer has demonstrated that the proffered accommodation imposes an undue hardship on its operations requires an individualized inquiry to ensure that the employer's justifications reflect a well-informed judgment grounded in a careful and open-minded weighing of the risks and alternatives); Mantolete v. Bolger, 767 F.2d 1416, 1422-24 (9th Cir. 1985) (before excluding an individual from the workplace on the basis of his disabling condition, the employer must independently assess both the probability and severity of potential injury to the individual which might be expected to result from his employment in a particular position; absent such an independent assessment, a good faith or rational belief on the part of the employer that the disabled individual cannot safely perform in a particular position will not be a sufficient defense to an allegation of discrimination).

In establishing a prima facie case of disability discrimination, an appellant is not required to prove conclusively that specific job modifications are reasonable. Rather, he is only required to make a facial showing that his disability can be reasonably accommodated. See Savage, 36 MSPR at 152 n.4 (citing Treadwell v. Alexander, 707 F.2d 473, 478 (11th Cir. 1983)). The agency is generally expected to know more about the job duties and its ability to make modifications. Id. (citing Prewitt v. USPS, 662 F.2d 292, 308 (5th Cir. 1981)). Thus, the appellant need only present evidence as to his or her individual capabilities and suggestions for job modifications. See Gilbert v. Frank, 949 F.2d 637, 642 (2d Cir. 1991). He is obligated, however, to make a good faith effort to work with his employer to determine what constitutes a reasonable accommodation. See Beck v. University of Wisconsin Board of Regents, 75 F.3d 1130, 1135-36 (7th Cir. 1996).

Here, the record supports a finding that the appellant has sufficiently articulated a number of reasonable accommodations under which he can perform his current job or a position to which he could be reassigned. He suggested a number of specific modifications, such as top loading the mail utility carts, raising the OCR equipment which he operated, and periodic sitting. The appellant further suggested using taller mail containers, rotating to manual distribution at a letter case, and reducing the weight of letter trays so as not to exceed twenty pounds. Moreover, the appellant suggested modifying his position to allow him to work at a slower pace when performing the sweeper functions, modifying the post-con with a lever to eliminate the need for bending, and receiving assistance from the feeders if the stacks became full.

Mr. Warrick testified that top loading the mail utility carts, as suggested by the appellant, would be a safety hazard, because it would make them top heavy, and cause them to tip over easily. Similarly, he stated that using modified post-cons would require transferring mail from nonmodified to modified equipment. Moreover, he stated that raising the OCR, another suggested accommodation, would be impractical for the other employees, particularly the shorter employees, who would have to reach and stretch farther, and that it would make it more difficult to clear the machine when it jammed. In addition, he testified that there were no manual distribution operations to which the appellant could be rotated.

We note, however, that the letter of proposed removal contained only general responses to the appellant's suggested modifications, and did not cite to any ergonomic, medical or cost-related evidence that would support the conclusion that the suggested accommodations would impose an undue hardship. Mr. Warrick and Mr. Anthony Siary, Manager of Distribution Operations, and the agency's deciding official, both testified that they did not identify any possible job modifications on their own and merely responded to the appellant's suggestions, despite their superior understanding of the relevant job duties and parameters for modification. See Prewitt, 662 F.2d at 303. Mr. Warrick admitted that he had never considered suggestions similar to the appellant's in the past, had never asked mailhandlers to top load mail utility carts (as the appellant suggested), and had never considered trying the appellant out at a slower pace. Moreover, there is no evidence that the agency ever conducted independent studies before initiating the removal.

In addition to the modifications noted above, the appellant also suggested "work hardening," i.e., muscle strengthening and improved techniques for performing job duties, as a possible accommodation. In a medical report issued in November 1993, the examining physician stated that the appellant "will attend Work Hardening program and re-evaluation with surgeon next month. Prognosis good." Dr. Rogers, who conducted the fitness-for-duty examination, concluded that the appellant should not continue to work as a Mail Processor, but that "[I]t is possible with a work hardening course that he may improve his functional capacity to some degree." Mr. Warrick's affidavit regarding his April 7, 1994, meeting with the appellant also suggests that work hardening was discussed. Mr. Warrick stated that the appellant "acknowledges that repetitive twisting is a factor but that, using proper lifting/turning techniques, he could do so without harm." Dr. Johanning testified that work hardening, in combination with other modifications, would be beneficial. Nevertheless, the appellant's testimony that the agency never sent him to a work-hardening course is undisputed.

Moreover, in his response to the notice of proposed removal, the appellant referred to a report issued by the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH). He also submitted this report in connection with his subsequent equal employment opportunity (EEO) complaint. The NIOSH report discussed ergonomic hazards, including back disorders, which were associated with the operation of the OCR and similar automated mail processing equipment. Neither Mr. Warrick nor Mr. Siary reviewed the NIOSH report, however. Mr. Siary stated that he was "sure" that the Safety Department had reviewed the NIOSH report, but the record contains no evidence that the report was referred to or analyzed by that department.

Thus, on the basis of the evidence in the record, we find that the agency failed to meet its burden of persuasion with respect to its claim that the requested accommodations would be unduly burdensome. Alternatively, to the extent that the agency argued that the appellant could not safely perform the essential functions of his job with the requested accommodations, or that these accommodations were not "reasonable," the appellant has rebutted the agency's evidence and argument. See Vande Zande v. State of Wisconsin Dept. of Admin., 44 F.3d 538, 543 (7th Cir. 1995) (employee must show that "the accommodation is reasonable in the sense both of efficacy and proportionality to cost). In doing so, however, he has not necessarily shown that the agency's position was 'pretextual.'" Such a showing is not necessary under the analytical framework for establishing disability discrimination applicable to this case. See Burns, 91 F.3d at 843; Barth, 2 F.3d at 1185.

...

In summary, the appellant established that he is disabled, i.e., that he suffered from a physical condition that significantly limited him in the major life activities of lifting, sitting, standing, and bending. The agency acknowledged that it was aware of the appellant's disability, and that it was the basis for its removal action. Further, the appellant articulated a number of possible reasonable accommodations. The agency, however, failed to show that the accommodations proposed by the appellant would create an undue hardship, and it made no efforts to determine, on its own, what other forms of accommodation might be possible. Accordingly, we find that the appellant has established a claim of disability discrimination, and is entitled to reinstatement to the Mail Processor position with reasonable accommodation.

Foland v. Dept. of Air Force, 75 MSPR 92, 96-99 (1997), summarized Clark and further explained approaches to proof of disability discrimination:

It is unclear whether the appellant is alleging that he has direct proof of discrimination. To the extent that the appellant is arguing that he was not considered for reassignment because his former supervisor did not believe that he could perform the functions of the vacant positions for which he was otherwise qualified, due to his disability, then, to establish a prima facie case of discrimination, the appellant must show that: (1) He is a disabled person; (2) the action being appealed to the Board was based on his disability; and (3) he can perform the essential functions of the position without accommodation from the agency, or he can perform the actual essential functions of the position with a proposed reasonable accommodation, which he must articulate to the extent possible. The appellant can furthermore challenge whether a function deemed 'essential" by the agency is actually "essential" as defined by the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. See Clark, 74 MSPR at 558-559.

If, on the other hand, the appellant does not have direct proof of discrimination, he may establish his prima facie case indirectly, by the McDonnell Douglas/Burdine proof scheme. He must show that: (1) He is disabled or perceived as disabled; (2) he is otherwise qualified, with or without reasonable accommodation, for the position in question; (3) he suffered an adverse employment decision (which is within the Board's jurisdiction); (4) the agency knew or had reason to know of the appellant's disability, or perceived him as disabled;, and (5) the position remained open while the agency sought other applicants or the appellant was replaced. See Monette v. Electronic Data Systems Corp., 90 F.3d 1173, 1185-86 (6th Cir. 1996); see also Clark, 74 MSPR at 559, 561.

Rowe v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-10-0744-I-1 (NP 7/20/2011), briefly described several types of proof indicative of a prima facie case of disability discrimination:

...She has also described her affirmative defense by alleging the following: she is a preference eligible disabled veteran; the agency knew of her disability; she had several medical issues that had required the use of sick leave while she was employed by the agency; she had requested leave for a scheduled surgery at the time the agency was considering the removal action; the agency's allegations of misconduct against her, and the removal action, were a discriminatory action to prevent her from receiving priority placement and mandatory placement into the position; agency officials repeatedly referred to the appellant's illnesses; and an agency official, Gretchen Leigh, in informing the appellant of the removal action stated that "she had 'good news.' Paris v. Dept. of Treasury, 104 MSPR 331, 336-44 ¶¶ 11-28, 2006 MSPB 362 (2006), discussed the prima facie case that may be established through articulation of an accommodation for a disabled employee, the relation of the interactive process that is supposed to more narrowly focus or tailor a possible accommodation, and the appellant's ultimate burden of proving that the suggested accommodation will, in fact, permit him to perform the essential elements of the job:

An employer commits unlawful disability discrimination by "not making reasonable accommodations to the known physical or mental limitations of an otherwise qualified individual with a disability who is an applicant or employee." 42 U.S.C. § 12112(b)(5)(A).[1] The term "qualified individual with a disability" means "an individual with a disability who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the employment position that such individual holds or desires." 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8). The agency argues on review that the administrative judge erred in finding that the appellant is "disabled," that the appellant did not request reasonable accommodation at the relevant time, and that the appellant did not meet his burden of proving that there was a reasonable accommodation available that would allow him to perform the essential functions of his position. We address each contention in turn.

[1] The cited statutory provision is from the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), 42 U.S.C. §§ 12101 et seq., but the agency is covered by the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. §§ 791-794. However, in a 1992 amendment to the Rehabilitation Act, the employment-related portions of the ADA were incorporated by reference into the Rehabilitation Act. See 29 U.S.C. § 791(g); Fraser v. Department of Agriculture, 95 M.S.P.R. 72, 76 n.* (2003); see also 67 Fed. Reg. 35,732, 35,735 (2002) (explaining that the standards used to resolve employment-related discrimination claims under the ADA, set forth in 29 C.F.R. Part 1630, shall also be used in resolving employment-related discrimination claims under the Rehabilitation Act). Accordingly, throughout this decision, in addition to principles developed under the Rehabilitation Act, we cite the ADA, caselaw developed under the ADA, and regulations issued under the ADA.

The appellant has shown that he is "disabled."

As relevant here, the term "disability" means, "with respect to an individual[,] ...a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of the major life activities of such individual." 42 U.S.C. § 12102(A); see Carter v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 641, ¶ 13 (2001). "Major life activities" refers to "functions such as caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, walking, seeing, hearing, speaking, learning, and working." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(i); Carter, 88 M.S.P.R. 641, ¶ 13.

The agency argues that the administrative judge erroneously made a conclusory finding that the appellant is "disabled." Relying largely upon Toyota Manufacturing, Kentucky, Inc. v. Williams, 534 U.S. 184 (2002), the agency argues that the appellant failed to prove that his medical restrictions substantially limit his ability to perform any major life activity. Instead, the agency contends, the record evidences that the appellant is only incapable of performing prolonged typing, which in and of itself is not a major life activity.

In Toyota, the Supreme Court indicated that, when addressing the major life activity of performing manual tasks, which is the issue in this appeal, the central inquiry must be whether the employee is unable to perform the variety of tasks central to most people's daily lives, not whether the employee is unable to perform the tasks associated with a particular job. 534 U.S. at 197. The Court further stated as follows:

An individualized assessment of the effect of an impairment is particularly necessary when the impairment is one whose symptoms vary widely from person to person. Carpal tunnel syndrome, one of respondent's impairments, is just such a condition. While cases of severe carpal tunnel syndrome are characterized by muscle atrophy and extreme sensory deficits, mild cases generally do not have either of these effects and create only intermittent symptoms of numbness and tingling. Carniero, Carpal Tunnel Syndrome: The Cause Dictates the Treatment, 66 Cleveland Clinic J. Medicine 159, 161-162 (1999).... Given these large potential differences in the severity and duration of the effects of carpal tunnel syndrome, an individual's carpal tunnel syndrome diagnosis, on its own, does not indicate whether the individual has a disability within the meaning of the ADA.

Id. at 199.

In his analysis of the evidence, the administrative judge properly performed an individualized assessment of the effect of the appellant's impairment. In particular, the administrative judge noted that the medical assessments of the appellant's physical restrictions determined that he can lift up to 5 pounds on only an occasional basis; he is unable to lift 5 pounds above shoulder level; he can only perform simple grasping for a total of 1 hour per day; and he can perform fine manipulation for up to 15 minutes continuously or 45 minutes intermittently. The administrative judge further found that the evidence indicated that the appellant's condition is permanent, and has caused markedly diminished hand strength, decreased range of motion in the hands and wrists, and constant shooting and stabbing pains in the hands, wrists, and elbows. The administrative judge also noted that the appellant reported that only a few minutes of keyboarding resulted in a recurrence of hand pain and numbness, which caused him to make erroneous double entries on the computer. In addition, the extensive medical documentation in the record establishes that the appellant's carpal tunnel symptoms are severe and substantially limit tasks central to daily life. For example, the functional capacity examination found that the appellant has difficulty climbing stairs because he is not able to tolerate the pressure on his hands required to use the rail. The record also shows that the appellant reported that he has difficulty in dressing himself because of numbness in his hands and that he will drop things, such as a plate, a glass, or a pen, if he looks away or does not concentrate on the item. Thus, the administrative judge was therefore correct to find that the appellant is a disabled person under the standard set forth in Toyota. See Kirkland v. Harvey, EEOC Appeal No. 01A42874, 2005 WL 2492830, at * 3 (2005) (complainant presented sufficient evidence to establish that her disability, carpal tunnel syndrome, substantially limits the major life activities of performing manual tasks and lifting).

The appellant's claim that the agency should have accommodated his disability by providing him with voice-recognition software is properly before the Board.

The agency argues that the appellant never requested accommodation, and further argues that even if he did, he never asked for the specific accommodation of voice-recognition software.

A disability discrimination claim will fail under the ADA or the Rehabilitation Act if the employee never requested accommodation while employed. Ballard v. Rubin, 284 F.3d 957, 964 (8th Cir. 2002); Robin v. Espo Engineering Corp., 200 F.3d 1081, 1092 (7th Cir. 2000); Crandall v. Paralyzed Veterans of America, 146 F.3d 894 (D.C. Cir. 1998); Gantt v. Wilson Sporting Goods Co., 143 F.3d 1042, 1046-47 (6th Cir. 1998); Burch v. Coca Cola Co., 119 F.3d 305, 318 (5th Cir. 1997). Nevertheless, an employee only has a general responsibility to inform his employer that he needs accommodation for a medical condition. 29 C.F.R. Pt. 1630 App. § 1630.9. Once the employee has requested accommodation, the employer must engage in the interactive process in an effort to determine an appropriate accommodation. Id.; 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(3); Gantt, 143 F.3d at 1046; see also Fleetwood v. Harford Systems Inc., 380 F. Supp.2d 688, 702 (D. Md. 2005) (an employer cannot escape liability for disability discrimination simply because the employee failed to suggest a particular accommodation). As the Board has repeatedly stated, an employing agency is in a better position than a disabled employee to know about its ability to modify duties or working conditions to meet the needs of the employee. Baker v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 680, 693 (1996); Lynch v. Department of Education, 52 M.S.P.R. 541, 544 (1992); Savage v. Department of the Navy, 36 M.S.P.R. 148, 152 n.2 (1988).

With these principles in mind, we find that the appellant met his responsibilities. The appellant informed the agency on March 5, 2004, that the recurrence of pain and numbness in his hands prevented him from completing his work, and in response, the agency informed him that he would need to provide additional medical documentation from Dr. Scott. In addition, Debbi Creed, a Certified Industrial Hygienist with the agency, met with the appellant on March 10, 2004, to review his position. She prepared an Ergonomic Evaluation Report, dated March 12, 2004, which recommended that the appellant receive a wave-style (split) keyboard with a built-in touch-sensitive mouse and additional training to improve his keyboarding skills. On appeal, in a statement submitted under penalty of perjury, the appellant stated that Creed also suggested during their meeting that a voice-recognition program may be a reasonable accommodation for his condition. The agency did not submit a statement from Creed or any other evidence to refute the appellant's sworn statement on this matter.

On March 15, 2004, Dr. Scott prepared a detailed statement of the appellant's restrictions. He reiterated previously recommended limitations of no more than 5 pounds lifting and avoidance of repetitive hand and arm movements. Dr. Scott also recommended that the appellant perform no more than 5 minutes of keyboarding at a time, followed by at least 15 minutes to rest his hands.

On March 22, 2004, the agency decided that the appellant's keyboarding restrictions could not be reasonably accommodated in his modified Currency Controller position. Without consulting with the appellant or Dr. Scott, the agency determined that the appellant could not perform the essential functions of his position or any other vacant position, with or without an accommodation. After determining that no other vacant positions existed to which the appellant could be assigned, the agency proposed the appellant's removal on April 6, 2004, for inability to perform the duties of his current Currency Controller position and his prior Currency Exchanger position. It later removed him, leading to this appeal.

Contrary to the agency's argument, the record establishes that the appellant fully cooperated in the interactive process envisioned in 29 C.F.R. Pt. 1630 App. § 1630.9 by promptly providing additional medical information on the nature of his disability, by indicating his interest in finding a reasonable accommodation, and by meeting with Creed, the agency's Industrial Hygienist. All indications are that the agency was on notice before it removed the appellant that he had difficulty performing keyboarding tasks and that he was interested in finding an accommodation. Further, the agency either expressly considered (through Industrial Hygienist Creed) or should have considered the obvious possibility that voice-recognition software might alleviate the appellant's difficulties. The agency, in essence, argues that the appellant sat silently by while it removed him and is now ambushing it with a claim of disability discrimination seeking the accommodation of voice-recognition software. The evidence does not support the agency's argument, however.

The appeal must be remanded for further consideration of the appellant's disability discrimination claim.

The administrative judge found in his initial decision that the appellant had established a "prima facie case" of discrimination because he "articulated" a reasonable accommodation by asserting that voice-recognition software would reduce the need to use his hands for data entry. The administrative judge emphasized that the appellant was "not required to prove" that installation of voice-recognition software was a reasonable accommodation in order to make out a "prima facie case." The administrative judge then found that the agency had failed "to show" that installation of voice-recognition software would pose an undue hardship. The administrative judge thus held that the agency committed disability discrimination.

The administrative judge's approach was not appropriate. The "prima facie case" inquiry is intended to guide an adjudicator who is assessing the sufficiency of a complaint and/or considering a motion for summary judgment. As far as the administrative judge was concerned, however, the record was complete and both parties had had an opportunity to submit evidence on the discrimination issue. At that stage in the proceedings, whether the appellant made out a prima facie case was immaterial, and the proper inquiry was whether the appellant proved, by preponderant evidence, that the agency committed discrimination. See U.S. Postal Service Board of Governors v. Aikens, 460 U.S. 711, 714-15 (1983); Simien v. U.S. Postal Service, 99 M.S.P.R. 237, ¶¶ 26-28 (2005); Schrodt v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 609, 616 (1998); Beam v. Office of Personnel Management, 77 M.S.P.R. 49, 55 (1997); accord Filipovich v. K & R Express Systems, Inc., 391 F.3d 859, 863 (7th Cir. 2004); Kanida v. Gulf Coast Medical Personnel LP, 363 F.3d 568, 575 (5th Cir. 2004).

In a disability discrimination case concerning denial of a reasonable accommodation, the appellant ultimately must prove that he is a qualified person with a disability, i.e., a disabled individual who can perform the essential functions of his position, with or without reasonable accommodation. The appellant cannot prevail merely by "articulating" a reasonable accommodation. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(B); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(m); Conaway v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 30 (2002); Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 46, 54 (1998).[3] To rule in favor of an employee on a disability discrimination claim merely because he has "articulated" a reasonable accommodation not only relieves the employee of his burden of proof, but it can lead to an unenforceable decision. See Jackson, 79 M.S.P.R. at 52-54 (decision finding disability discrimination was not enforceable because in fact, after the evidence was weighed, it was clear that the accommodation "articulated" by the appellant was not feasible); see also Henry v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 100 M.S.P.R. 124, ¶¶ 8-14 (2005) (same).

[3] To the extent that Clark v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 552, 561 (1997), suggests that an appellant can ultimately prevail on a disability discrimination claim by "articulating" an accommodation without proving by preponderant evidence that the requested accommodation is reasonable, we overrule it. As we noted above, when the record in an appeal is fully developed, a "prima facie case," burden-shifting analysis is inappropriate, and instead the inquiry is whether the appellant met his ultimate burden of proving discrimination by preponderant evidence. Jackson, 79 M.S.P.R. at 52-53.

Despite the foregoing, we decline to resolve the appellant's disability discrimination claim at this stage. On this record, we are not left with any clear sense of whether voice-recognition software would or would not allow the appellant to perform the essential functions of his position. If, as might be the case, such software running on a dummy personal computer would act as the functional equivalent of a keyboard, it would not be at all surprising to learn that the software allowed the appellant to perform all of his duties. On the other hand, if introducing voice-recognition software into the agency's system required the agency to rewrite the code for what it claims is a proprietary system, or if, as the agency claims on review, voice-recognition software compromised the agency's security, then it might not be a reasonable accommodation. We simply do not know, because evidence is lacking from both parties.

Whether voice-recognition software would be a reasonable accommodation for the appellant's condition is a fact-intensive inquiry. Resolving this issue requires clear findings on the following points, among others: Whether there is a voice-recognition program that is compatible with the appellant's speech patterns, see Miller v. Principi, 2006 WL 2222682, *5 (W.D. Tenn. 2006) (claim of discrimination under the Rehabilitation Act brought by a nurse with carpal tunnel syndrome failed because, among other things, the voice-recognition software that might otherwise have allowed her to enter data into a hospital computer system was incompatible with her speech patterns); whether such software is compatible with the agency's computer system, see Alek v. University of Chicago Hospitals, 2002 WL 1332000, *7 (N.D. Ill. 2002) (proposed accommodation of voice recognition software for an individual's carpal tunnel syndrome was not compatible with the employer's computer system), aff'd, 54 Fed. Appx. 224 (7th Cir. 2002); whether the expected rate at which voice-recognition software would misinterpret the appellant's speech and introduce errors into the agency's currency tracking system is acceptable, as compared to the tolerated error rate from typing; and whether the appellant could perform his job with voice-recognition software as quickly as the agency expects someone in his position to perform.

The administrative judge's finding that the agency committed disability discrimination when it failed to reasonably accommodate the appellant with voice-recognition software is not based on any probative evidence. Cf. Bass v. County of Butte, 2004 WL 1925468, *4 (E.D. Cal. 2004) (the general statements of a vocational counselor concerning the use of voice recognition software were insufficient to generate a triable issue of fact on a disability discrimination claim brought by a police dispatcher whose carpal tunnel syndrome limited her ability to type; the employer was entitled to summary judgment). The initial decision, if allowed to stand, could lead to an enforcement proceeding in which the evidence showed that voice-recognition software is not a reasonable accommodation. We are loathe to create the possibility of a repeat of Jackson and Henry, where the appellants obtained final Board decisions finding disability discrimination, and later had their victories taken away from them on reopening when, after further evidence was presented, it became clear that the accommodations identified in the final Board decisions were not reasonable.

Before the record closed below, the administrative judge merely informed the appellant that he "must show" that there was an accommodation that would allow him to perform the essential functions of his position or a funded vacant one to which he could be reassigned. The administrative judge's apparent belief that the appellant could prevail merely by "articulating" an accommodation may have led him to issue this terse order. However, the administrative judge should have gone into much more detail, and should have explained to both parties how complex and fact-specific the inquiry in this particular case is. This is not a simple case involving, say, a lifting restriction--where an administrative judge might examine a position description and then use common knowledge to determine whether an asserted accommodation is reasonable--and it should not be treated as if it were a simple case. See Narcum v. Department of the Interior, 98 M.S.P.R. 645 (2005) (appeal remanded for presentation of additional argument and evidence where the appellant attempted to claim disability discrimination and he was not put on specific notice of the elements of a failure-to-accommodate claim and his burden of proof).

OPM's contention that the Board's decision in this case is contrary to law centers upon the Board's finding that the appellant had identified accommodations that "quite possibly would ameliorate the adverse effects" of his handicapping condition. 37 MSPR at 15. OPM contends that the effect of this finding is to require the agency to disprove that the accommodation might possibly allow the appellant to perform the duties of his position, thus placing upon the agency the ultimate burden of proving that the appellant was not a qualified handicapped employee, a burden that does not fall upon the agency by law and regulation. Department of the Navy, 36 MSPR 148 (1988). In this regard, OPM contends that the Board correctly stated the standard in Stalkfleet, but incorrectly stated it in this case and in Savage.

A review of these decisions demonstrates that there is no inconsistency in the test which the Board applied in Lynch and in other handicap discrimination cases. The Board has consistently required appellants to make a facial showing that their handicaps can be accommodated. The numerous Board decisions which have articulated and applied this standard span the past decade. See, e.g., Stalkfleet, 6 MSPR at 647; Savage, 36 MSPR at 152; Cason v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 37 MSPR 261, 267 (1988); Givens v. United States Postal Service, 49 MSPR 374, 380 (1991). The Board has also consistently held that this facial showing need not include a complete list of all possible effective accommodations or proof that the suggested accommodations would without doubt allow the employee to perform his job. E.g., Savage, 36 MSPR at 152 n.2; Ignacio v. United States Postal Service, 35 MSPR 55, 63 (1987). The Board's holdings regarding the nature of the appellant's facial showing are consistent with EEOC and court decisions. E.g., Alvarado v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Pet. No. 03830052 (October 13, 1983), and Prewitt v. United States Postal Service, 662 F.2d 292, 308-10 (5th Cir. 1981).

The Board in Savage set forth a detailed explanation of the appellant's burden of articulating a reasonable accommodation:

The extent to which an appellant is able to articulate a reasonable accommodation will vary depending on the issues and circumstances of the case. In general, however, the agency can be expected to know more than the appellant about the essential duties of the position or positions at issue, about its own ability to modify duties or working conditions, and about the availability of positions that could be filled by employees with certain limitations.... On the other hand, the appellant can be expected to know more than the agency about his qualifications and capabilities, and, as we have noted above, he bears the ultimate burden of persuasion in establishing his affirmative defense of handicap discrimination. Accordingly, although the agency has the ultimate burden of showing an inability to accommodate the appellant's handicap, the appellant "initially has the burden of coming forward with evidence to make at least a facial showing that his handicap can be accommodated." Treadwell v. Alexander, 707 F.2d 473, 478 (11th Cir. 1983).

Savage, 36 MSPR at 152 n.2 (emphasis supplied.)

OPM's contention that the Board articulated an erroneous subjective standard relies on a single phrase in Savage which referred to the appellant's belief that his handicap could be accommodated. Id. at 152. This argument, however, ignores the accompanying explanation, quoted above, which clearly dispels any suggestion that the Board was applying a subjective standard. A review of the Board's decisions since Savage which have addressed claims of handicap discrimination supports this view. There is no indication in any of these decisions that the Board relied on an appellant's mere subjective belief that his or her handicap could be accommodated. Rather, consistent with the Treadwell decision on which the Board relied in Savage, the Board has required appellants to present evidence which makes at least a "facial showing" that their handicaps can be accommodated. E.g., Cason, 37 MSPR at 267; Widger v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR 368, 371 (1988); Clopton v. Department of the Navy, 36 MSPR 373, 378 (1988).

Ellshoff v. Dept. of Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 78-79 (1997), emphasized that a burden-shifting analysis is not used when there is direct evidence of disability discrimination in the form of a removal based on appellant's inability to perform her job as a result of depression:

An appellant who raises a claim of disability discrimination may establish that she is disabled by showing that she is substantially limited in a major life activity, that she has a record of such a limitation, or that she is regarded as having such a limitation. functions such as caring for one's self, performing manual tasks, walking, seeing, hearing, speaking, breathing, learning or working.

29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(3); Clark, slip op. at 4-5. Here, the evidence showed that the appellant was not actually disabled as of the time of her removal but that she was erroneously "regarded as" disabled by the agency (since the agency removed her based on its unproven determination that she was disabled).[4]

Washington v. Brown, EEOC Appeal No. 01953462, slip op. at 3-4 (Nov. 30, 1995); see also Keown v. Department of the Navy, 67 MSPR 629, 639 (1995) (the "regarded as" category applies to individuals who "do not in fact have the condition which they are perceived as having").

In Clark, issued after the AJ issued the initial decision here, the Board held that the burden-shifting analytical framework under McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-04 (1973), and Texas Department of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 255-57 (1981), was inappropriate in situations where there was direct evidence of discrimination, e.g., where the action taken was explicitly based on the alleged disability. Clark, slip op. at 5-6 (citing Monette v. Electronic Data Systems Corp., 90 F.3d 1173, 1184-86 (6th Cir. 1996)).

Here, the agency's charge of inability to work due to depression is directly related to the appellant's perceived disability. In addition, the agency's charge of AWOL is substantially related to the appellant's disability due to depression, and does not involve any independent acts of misconduct unrelated to those issues. Because the agency explicitly based its action on the appellant's alleged disability, thus presenting direct evidence of discrimination, and did not assert any independent, nondiscriminatory reason for the action, we find that the burden-shifting analytical framework is inappropriate. See Clark, slip op. at 6; see also Monette, 90 F.3d at 1180, 1182; Barth v. Gelb, 2 F.3d 1180, 1186-87 (D.C. Cir. 1993), cert. denied, 11 U.S. 1030 (1994). The only question left for resolution is whether the agency's admittedly discriminatory action was permissible or prohibited, i.e., whether the appellant is a qualified disabled person against whom the agency may not discriminate. See Clark, slip op. at 9; Monette, 90 F.3d at 1180, 1182, 1183 & n.9.

A "qualified" disabled person is a disabled person who can perform the essential functions of her position, with or without reasonable accommodation, without endangering the health and/or safety of herself and/or others. 29 CFR § 1614.203(a); Clark, slip op. at 9. The appellant showed that she was a qualified disabled person protected by the discrimination laws because, as discussed above with respect to the charges, the evidence showed that she was capable of performing her job duties without accommodation. Because the agency removed this qualified disabled appellant based on her perceived disability, the agency committed prohibited disability discrimination.

A necessary component of a disability case is definition of the essential elements of a job. Shestak v. SSA, 84 MSPR 307, 310-12 ¶¶ 8-10 (1999), suggested, but did not directly hold, that it is the appellant's responsibility to identify essential job functions:

The appellant also claims that the arbitrator did not require the agency, rather than the appellant, to prove that face-to-face interviews were an essential function of the CR position, as set forth in Monette v. Electronic Data Systems Corp., 90 F.3d 1173, 1184 (6th Cir. 1996) (if a disabled individual is challenging a particular job requirement as unessential, the employer will bear the burden of proving that the challenged criterion is necessary). See also Hamlin v. Charter Township of Flint, 165 F.3d 426, 430 (6th Cir. 1999) (applying Monette). The court in Monette noted that 42 USC § 12112(b)(6) provides that an employer discriminates within the meaning of the statute by "using qualification standards, employment tests, or other selection criteria that screen out or tend to screen out an individual with a disability...unless the standard, test, or other selection criteria, as used by the covered entity, is shown to be job-related for the position in question and is consistent with business necessity." Monette, 90 F.3d at 1184. The court reasoned that "[A]lthough this section of the statute expressly concerns only qualification standards, testing procedures, and selection criteria that tend to screen out disabled individuals, we believe its clear import dictates that employers bear the burden of proving that a challenged job requirement is job-related." Id.

Ultimately, the employee claiming disability discrimination must prove that he is a "qualified" disabled person, i.e., a disabled individual who can perform the essential functions of his position, with or without reasonable accommodation, without endangering the health and safety of himself or others. Fox v. U.S. Postal Service, 81 MSPR 522 ¶ 8 (1999). Contrary to Monette, other courts have held that it remains the plaintiff's duty, in establishing her prima facie case, to show that she was "otherwise qualified," which includes establishing the essential functions" of her job. See Laurin v. Providence Hosp., 150 F.3d 52, 58-59 (1st Cir. 1998) (since an ADA plaintiff ultimately must shoulder the burden of establishing that she was able to perform all "essential functions" of her position, she bore the burden of adducing competent evidence from which a rational factfinder could have found in her favor on the "essential function" inquiry); Hernandez v. City of Hartford, 959 F. Supp. 125, 131 n.6 (D. Conn. 1997) (citing Borkowski v. Valley Cent. School Dist., 63 F.3d 131, 137 (2d Cir. 1995)); cf. Benson v. Northwest Airlines, Inc., 62 F.3d 1108, 1113 (8th Cir. 1995) ("Because Northwest disputes Benson's evidence that he can perform the essential functions of the mechanic's job, it must put on some evidence of those essential functions."). Although bound to follow it, a district court criticized Monette on this issue. See Hamlin v. Charter Township of Flint, 942 F. Supp. 1129, 1137-38 (E.D. Mich. 1996) (Monette "equates a statutory prohibition (i.e., no unlawful or pretextual screening mechanisms) with the creation of a legal burden," and "effectively turns on its head the burden of proof in an ADA case because rather than the plaintiff being required to show that he or she is capable of performing all of the essential functions of a job, all he or she need do is challenge the essentiality of one of the job functions, and, thereby, shift the burden of proof on one of the critical factors for showing that he or she is qualified for the job. Such a shift is clearly unwarranted because it allows a plaintiff to avoid making his or her prima facie case.") (footnote omitted).

We need, not resolve this issue because, as previously found, the appellant has demonstrated no legal error in the arbitrator's finding that she did not show that her impairment substantially limited a major life activity. Therefore, she has not shown that she was an individual with a disability, let alone a qualified individual with a disability. In any event, because there appears to be a split in the courts on the issue of who has the burden of proving the essential functions of a position, and the appellant has identified (and we have found) no Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) decision directly addressing this issue, the appellant has not demonstrated legal error by the arbitrator.

If the agency fails to meet the employee's prima facie case of disability discrimination or, more particularly, if the agency fails to establish that it could not make reasonable accommodations to the appellant without imposing an undue hardship on its operations, the appealed action will be reversed. See McGraw v. Assuming the agency meets its burden, however, the appellant then has the opportunity to show that the agency's stated reason for failing to accommodate his disability is a pretext for the prohibited disability discrimination.
