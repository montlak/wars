broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part j: Incorrect Information From MSPB, Others
j. Incorrect Information From MSPB, Others

Incorrect information in the initial decision concerning the date that a PFR is due justifies delay until the stated due date. Reynolds v. VA, 75 MSPR 509, 512 (1997); see Pendarvis v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 492, 495 (1996) (delayed PFR was excused by conflicting statements in the AJs' orders concerning the time limit for an appeal following the filing of an EEO complaint and concerning the time for filing a PFR following the issuance of an initial decision dismissing the appeal); DeVitto v. Dept. of Transp., 64 MSPR 354, 356 (1994) (delay was excused because of the AJ's incorrect advice to the appellant concerning the process for appealing the decision concerning his retirement status); Greco v. Dept. of Interior, 52 MSPR 357, 360 (1992) ("because the administrative judge incorrectly informed the parties that the initial decision would become final on August 4, 1991, and the appellant filed his petition for review on August 3, 1991, within the time limit provided in the initial decision, he has shown that he acted with due diligence under the circumstances, and has established good cause for waiver"); Anderson v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 691, 694-95 (1991) (time for filing the PFR was waived when the appellant filed a PFR within the time set by the AJ, even though the date set by the AJ was incorrect); Adamov v. Marine Corps, 41 MSPR 275, 276 n.1 (1989) (same result as Anderson); Ensinger v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 430, 433 (1988), followed, Beatty v. OPM, 46 MSPR 623, 627 (1991) (AJ incorrectly advised the parties that the initial decision would become final on Saturday; the PFR was timely when filed on the next workday); cf. Natividad v. DHHS, 63 MSPR 114, 117 (1994) (delay was excused by a confusing series of events involving the processing of a mixed case final agency decision, an appeal to MSPB, cancelation of the final agency decision, and direction from EEOC to the agency to reissue the notice of appeal rights).

If the confusion is legitimately caused by the impact of a Board errata notice correcting the docket number of an initial decision, a short delay will be excused. See Carroll v. OPM, 114 MSPR 310, 314 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 131 (2010) (erratum notice, containing a material change in the result of an annuity overpayment recovery appeal, justified a PFR that was timely if measured from the notice and untimely if measured from the date of the initial decision); Lott v. USPS, 27 MSPR 463, 464-65 (1985); cf. Strickland v. MSPB, 748 F.2d 681 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (appellant should not have been defaulted when the Board caused confusion by assigning two docket numbers to the same case and then failing to respond to the appellant's inquiries concerning the status of his case). But see Murphy v. USPS, 100 MSPR 490, 493 ¶ 7 (2005) (possible confusion concerning MSPB docket numbers did not justify a considerable delay in filing a PFR).

If there is a relation between erroneous information from the Board and delay in filing, the delay must be reasonable. A delay of years in filing a PFR after some initial confusion concerning the due date was not excused. A reasonably prudent person would have checked with the Board to ascertain with greater certainty the proper filing date. Mason v. Dept. of Transp., 32 MSPR 138, 141 (1987). An AJ's failure to provide mixed case appeal rights did not justify submission of a late PFR when the initial decision stated the deadline for the PFR. See Wright v. VA, 85 MSPR 358, 361-62 ¶¶ 5-7 (2000). However, incorrect advice from an agency may affect the date the appellant files a PFR. The Board may take the agency's error into account and permit a belated filing. See Terrell v. USPS, 70 MSPR 296, 300 (1996). But see Andre v. Dept. of Army, 91 MSPR 342, 346 ¶ 9 (2002) (Board declined to find the reasonable belief of the appellant's attorney that agency counsel, rather than the Board, could waive the filing deadline set in the initial decision). If incorrect advice from the Board concerning a filing date or procedure was received after the PFR was already late, the Board's error does not prejudice the appellant. See Thomas v. DOJ, 44 MSPR 460, 462-63 (1990) (Board incorrectly advised the appellant, after the due date for a PFR, that the appellant's former counsel filed a PFR).

If the appellant claims that he was misled by bad advice concerning the filing deadline, he must be specific; delay was not excused based on the appellant's "simple assertion that he received some unspecified wrong advice from an unknown and unnamed source...." Boyer v. USPS, 35 MSPR 217, 219 (1987). An unspecific, unsworn, and belated allegation that the appellant "did not receive the proper papers from the MSPB" did not excuse a late PFR in Rouse v. USPS, 66 MSPR 358, 360 (1995). If there is a dispute over what advice concerning filing a PFR was given to the appellant by a Board regional office, the Board will accept and give weight to contemporaneous memos made by Board employees. See High v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 441, 443 (1986) (unsworn statement by appellant that she was allowed three years to file PFR was discounted as a result of memos by Board employees concerning their conversations with appellant); Aragon v. DOJ, 25 MSPR 637, 639 (1985) (dispute concerning when the Board received a PFR was resolved by a statement from a Board employee); cf. Krzewinski v. USPS, 72 MSPR 353, 358 (1996) (discounting statements attributed to an MSPB employee because "[t]he appellant does not identify the person to whom he allegedly spoke"); Pope v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 352, 356 (1990) (misinterpretation attributed to the statement of a Board employee concerning the calculation of deadline was discounted by MSPB with the observation that "the appellant's unsupported assertion...[is] unlikely"); cf. Lilly v. USPS, CH-0353-16-0244-I-1 (NP 8/25/2022) ("We acknowledge the appellant's allegations that she called the Clerk's Office several times but was not provided with the assistance she sought. However, she alleges that the telephone calls occurred...approximately 6 weeks after the filing deadline. The alleged phone calls, therefore, do not provide an explanation as to why the appellant missed the deadline.").

There are exceptions concerning the interpretation of Board regulations or instructions: "Erroneous information given to an appellant by an agency EEO official may constitute good cause to excuse an untimely filing." Fassler v. USPS, 69 MSPR 672, 676 (1996) (citing Williams v. DHUD, 40 MSPR 506, 508 (1989), but still not excusing the belated PFR); see Baker v. OPM, 7 MSPR 379, 380 (1981) (excusing a delayed PFR when the appellant thought that the OPM representative, believed by the appellant to work for the Board, granted a 15-day extension to file). But see Smith, supra, 68 MSPR 607 (1995) (not excusing delay based on information from agency EEO office concerning PFR filing time). The Board excuses a late PFR if the appellant essentially files a PFR but styles the pleading as an enforcement petition when an AJ issued a decision advising the appellant how but not when to seek enforcement if there were problems with compliance involving the decision issued by the AJ. Stempihar v. USPS, 106 MSPR 115, 118-20 ¶¶ 3-10, 2007 MSPB 151 (2007).
