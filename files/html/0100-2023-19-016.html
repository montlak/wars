4. Duty of Judge to Resolve Issues and Inform Appellant of Elements of Proof

The AJ must analyze any affirmative defense of discrimination raised and not abandoned during an adverse action appeal. Ivory v. GSA, 45 MSPR 371, 374 (1990); see Viana v. Dept. of Treasury, 114 MSPR 659, 662 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 196 (2010) (remanding because the AJ did not address a discrimination defense and the record did not demonstrate abandonment of the defense); Erkins v. USPS, 108 MSPR 367, 370 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 63 (2008) (AJ must apprise the appellant of the applicable burdens of going forward with the evidence and of proving a particular affirmative defense, as well as the kind of evidence the appellant is required to produce to meet his burden; when the AJ fails to inform the parties of their burden and methods of proof, the Board typically remands the appeal so the AJ can provide that notice and an opportunity to submit evidence and argument under the proper standard); Nordell v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 37, 41 (1983) ("Appellant alleged that the removal was based on sex discrimination and reprisal for EEO activities. The record discloses no basis for a finding that appellant abandoned these affirmative defenses, and we therefore hold that the presiding official erred when he did not consider them"); cf. Kokkinis v. VA, 81 MSPR 26, 30-31 ¶ 13 (1998) (AJ should take care to ensure that a prehearing order places the appellant on notice of any limitations of his EEO reprisal claims); Moran v. Dept. of Treasury, 77 MSPR 532, 535-36 (1998) (implying that Board jurisdiction attaches over an otherwise timely appeal even if an EEOC hearing has been requested prior to the Board appeal). But see Kye v. DLA, 64 MSPR 570, 573 (1994) (although the AJ did not analyze evidence concerning an appellant's disability discrimination claim, the adjudicatory error was harmless; appellant failed to demonstrate how claimed medical conditions caused misconduct).

Wynn v. USPS, 115 MSPR 146, 149-50 ¶¶ 9-10, 2010 MSPB 214 (2010), overruled in part, Thurman v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 21 (2022) (establishing multifactor analysis governing waiver of affirmative defenses) was emphatic about the AJ's responsibility to resolve EEO issues, along with other affirmative defenses, even if they are only checked on boxes on the appeal form, unless there is an affirmative waiver of those issues (as opposed to an implied waiver by the appellant's failure to list the issues in a prehearing submission):

Generally, the Board has held that an appellant is deemed to have abandoned a discrimination claim if it is not included in the list of issues in a prehearing conference summary, and the party was afforded an opportunity to object to the conference summary. See Henson v. U.S. Postal Service, 110 M.S.P.R. 624, ¶ 10 (2009); Yovan v. Department of the Treasury, 86 M.S.P.R. 264, ¶ 7 (2000); cf. Mata v. Department of the Army, 114 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 10 (2010) (the appellant preserved his claim by raising it in a supplemental submission when the administrative judge failed to include it in the prehearing summary). The present case, however, is similar to Erkins v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 367, ¶ 7 (2008), in that the record below does not establish that the appellant withdrew or abandoned his affirmative defenses.

We now make clear that when an appellant raises an affirmative defense in an appeal either by checking the appropriate box in an appeal form, identifying an affirmative defense by name such as "race discrimination," "harmful procedural error," etc., or by alleging facts that reasonably raise such an affirmative defense, the administrative judge must address the affirmative defense(s) in any close of record order or prehearing conference summary and order. If an appellant expresses the intention to withdraw such an affirmative defense, in the close of record order or prehearing conference order the administrative judge must, at a minimum, identify the affirmative defense, explain that the Board will no longer consider it when deciding the appeal, and give an appellant an opportunity to object to withdrawal of the affirmative defense. The record in this appeal simply does not establish that the appellant abandoned or withdrew the affirmative defenses he had raised in his appeal. As explained below, in the absence of evidence establishing the appellant had withdrawn or abandoned his affirmative defenses, it was incumbent on the administrative judge to advise the appellant of applicable burdens of proving a particular affirmative defense, as well as the kind of evidence the appellant is required to produce to meet his burden.

See Simpson v. VA, DA-0752-11-0108-I-4 (NP 12/16/2013) (citing Wynn: "when an appellant raises an affirmative defense in an appeal either by checking the appropriate box in an appeal form, identifying an affirmative defense by name such as 'race discrimination,' 'harmful procedural error,' etc., or by alleging facts that reasonably raise such an affirmative defense, the administrative judge must address the affirmative defense(s) in any close of record order or prehearing conference summary and order. Id., ¶ 10."..."During the July 2, 2012 prehearing conference call, the appellant requested permission to submit a document containing his objections to the FAD. Because the FAD was already in the appeal file and the appellant requested to add an exhibit to the appeal file that specifically addressed the merits of the FAD, we find that the appellant raised an affirmative defense of disability discrimination and the administrative judge should have specifically addressed that affirmative defense in his prehearing conference summary."). [Refer to "Treatment of EEO and Other Affirmative Defenses; Summary Judgment" for discussion of Thurman, supra.]

Even if the adverse action is reversed for procedural reasons, substantive EEO allegations should be decided. Lambert v. Dept. of Navy, 85 MSPR 130, 134 ¶ 10 (2000). The result on the EEO case may, for example, have a bearing on a counsel fee award. See White v. Dept. of Treasury, 45 MSPR 475, 479 (1990). The AJ should not determine a discrimination defense not affirmatively raised. Logan v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 573, 581 (1986) (alcoholism). The Board's ruling in Logan was without analysis: generally issues that are tried without objection during a hearing are treated as amendments of pleadings if they were not originally pleaded. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Required Analysis" and "EEO, Prohibited Personnel Practice Issue" for analysis of decisions defining AJs' analytical responsibilities.]

As part of the AJ's duty to resolve issues, the AJ should inform the appellant of what is expected of him to establish his EEO affirmative defense, Milner v. DOJ, 77 MSPR 37, 46 (1997):

[T]here is no indication that the administrative judge apprised the appellant of the applicable burdens of proof and of going forward with the evidence with respect to the affirmative defense he was raising or of the kind of evidence he was required to produce to meet his burden. See, e.g., Phillips, 74 MSPR at 58; Marr v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 196, 200-01 (1991). The only reference in the administrative judge's orders to the discrimination claim is a bare statement in a prehearing order that one of the affirmative defenses that the appellant was raising was "Prohibited discrimination based on race." Thus, the appellant did not receive a "fair and just adjudication" of his affirmative defense of race discrimination. See Phillips, 74 MSPR at 58.

Milner was followed in Butler v. DOJ, 83 MSPR 64, 66 ¶ 5 (1999). Thompson v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 245, 247 ¶¶ 6-7 (1998), emphasized the responsibility of an AJ to inform the (pro se) appellant of the elements of an affirmative defense of discrimination (RIF appeal):

We REOPEN this case on our own motion, however, because the administrative judge erred by failing to apprise the appellant of his burden and elements of proof on his discrimination claims. See Clarke v. Office of Personnel Management, 73 MSPR 435, 442 (1997); Marr v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 196, 200-01 (1991). The administrative judge was required to determine whether the appellant was claiming discrimination based on direct evidence or circumstantial evidence and apprise him of his burden and elements of proof accordingly. See Jones v. Department of the Army, 68 MSPR 398, 402-06 (1995). Accordingly, we must remand this case for further adjudication.

ORDER

On remand, the administrative judge shall apprise the appellant of his burden and elements of proof of discrimination based on sex, race, and age. Further, the administrative judge shall afford the appellant an opportunity for additional discovery on these affirmative defenses, and a supplemental hearing on them if the appellant requests one, and shall issue a supplemental initial decision addressing these defenses.

See Warner v. Dept. of Interior, 115 MSPR 281, 283-84 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 233 (2010) (remanding when the AJ failed to explain the burden of proof in an ADEA disparate impact claim); Turner v. VA, 94 MSPR 381, 384 ¶ 8 (2003) (remanding for judge to inform the appellant of the burden of proof concerning his affirmative defense of disability discrimination); Alhilawi v. Dept. of Interior, 88 MSPR 360, 362 ¶ 7 (2001) ("Where an appellant alleges discrimination, he is entitled to be informed of the burden of proof on the discrimination claim and the kind of evidence he is required to produce to meet his burden."); Qatsha v. DOD, 86 MSPR 121, 124-25 ¶ 8 (2000) (remand for further hearing and discovery when the AJ did not advise the appellant of either burdens of proof respecting his reprisal claim or the type of evidence required to meet those burdens); Coleman v. Dept. of Energy, 82 MSPR 251, 253 ¶ 6 (1999) (remanding for adjudication of retaliation and discrimination claims: "With respect to the appellant's discrimination claims, the administrative judge was required to determine whether the [pro se] appellant was claiming discrimination based on direct evidence or circumstantial evidence and apprise her of her burdens and elements of proof accordingly."); Kokkinis v. VA, 81 MSPR 26, 31 ¶ 14 (1998) ("[A]t no time did the administrative judge apprise the appellant of the burdens and elements of proof on his EEO retaliation and whistleblowing reprisal claims, as he was required to do.").
