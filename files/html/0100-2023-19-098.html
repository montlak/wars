4. Agency Regulation

A removal will be reversed if the employee shows that he probably would not have been removed if the agency adhered to a rehabilitative program established under its own regulations. Berry v. VA, 25 MSPR 50, 53 (1984) (also addressing harmful procedural error). An agency is required to conform to the procedures it adopts. Cunningham v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 599, 601 (1985) (noting the existence of a program placing medically disqualified employees into a disability program providing 60 days for reassignment attempts; the agency was required to attempt, but not necessarily achieve success, in reassignment efforts). If an agency regulation requires consideration of reassignment or other accommodation for a disabled individual, the Board will require the agency to follow its own regulation. Billings v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 20, 23 (1984) (agency complied with regulation by considering the restructuring of the appellant's position, training her for another position, and undertaking "positive efforts to effect appellant's reassignment").

By reversing a removal based on tardiness and AWOL, Gilliam v. Dept. of Commerce, 6 MSPR 57, 60-62, 6 MSPB 59 (1981), emphasized that an agency must follow its regulations designed to assist individuals with personal or health problems:

Under 5 U.S.C. 7901, agency heads are authorized to establish health service programs for the purpose of promoting and maintaining the physical and mental fitness of employees. Pursuant to that authority and Department of Commerce Administrative Order 202-792, NOAA Circular 76-54 sets forth the agency's policy governing administration of its employee assistance program to employees who have personal problems which "reduce their dependability.... These personal problems may be caused by the abuse of alcohol or other drugs or be caused by emotional distress." Under Section 4 of that circular, the agency "has a responsibility to offer assistance to employees troubled by personal problems..." (emphasis supplied). Supervisors are specified as being "in key positions to recognize troubled employees" and have the responsibility for conducting an interview and informing the employee of available counseling services in the event the employee's poor performance is caused by any personal or health problem. Further, it is the "policy of NOAA to try to assist the individual to become a useful employee again, through nondisciplinary procedures if possible."

We find that the agency was obligated to conform to these procedures. See Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535 (1959); accord, Service v. Dulles, 354 U.S. 363 (1957); United States ex rel. Accordi v. Shaughnessy, 347 U.S. 260 (1954). In Watson v. United States, 162 F. Supp. 755 (1958), the Court of Claims held that procedural regulations of an agency issued under statutory authority have the force and effect of law and must be complied with by the agency that issued them. See also Gulf States Mfrs., Inc. v. N.L.R.B., 579 F.2d 1298 (5th Cir. 1978); Whelan v. United States, 529 F.2d 1000 (Ct. Cl. 1976). Thus, the Employee Assistance Program, in effect implementing 5 U.S.C. 7901, has the force and effect of an agency regulation, and is part of agency procedures. In this case the provisions of NOAA Circular 76-54 represent guiding principles and established nondiscretionary policy under which NOAA operates and which have the effect of regulatory requirements.

Appellant contended that the agency was aware of his problem (nervous condition) prior to the effective date of removal. He asserted in his petition for review that his supervisor was in violation of section 5 of NOAA Circular 76-54, which required, in pertinent part, that the supervisor be alert to changes in the work and/or behavior of an employee, and inform him when his pattern of performance appears to be deteriorating. Appellant also asserted that the agency failed to conduct an interview and inform him of available counseling service.

The medical evidence before the presiding official revealed that appellant was hospitalized at a VA Hospital for a "nervous condition" from July 27 to August 3, 1979. The meteorologist in charge of the forecast office testified that he was aware of appellant's hospitalization and he knew that appellant had a nervous condition.

Appellant presented medical evidence at his oral reply which, in our view, reasonably placed the agency on notice that a medical condition, i.e., nervous condition requiring hospitalization, existed which made appellant a "troubled employee" as defined in section 3 of the NOAA Employee Assistance Program. Moreover, appellant presented undisputed evidence that one supervisor had suggested to him a year earlier that he needed medical assistance, but that when he attempted to discuss his problem with the meteorologist in charge that official had abruptly broken off the conversation as "none of my business."

Under 5 U.S.C. 7701(c)(2), an agency's action may not be sustained "if the employee...shows harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision...." The NOAA employee assistance program required, at a minimum, that the agency give counseling to employees who have "personal problems" that reduce their dependability. Moreover, under that program it was "the policy of NOAA to try to assist the individual to become a useful employee again, through nondisciplinary procedures if possible" (Sec. 6).

Under these circumstances, the appellant has made a sufficient showing of likelihood that the error of the agency in not offering rehabilitative assistance in lieu of or as part of the initial disciplinary process affected the result. It was within the range of appreciable probability on this record that appellant would not have been removed had the agency adhered to its policy of assisting the individual to become a useful employee again through nondisciplinary procedures if possible. Thus, appellant was denied the substantive benefits of the agency's policy of offering assistance to an employee who had personal problems which temporarily reduced his dependability. The issue was central to appellant's accountability for his erratic behavior, and the agency took no steps to mitigate the effect of its error even after it was clearly put on notice of appellant's problem prior to the effective date of his removal. See Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 MSPB 489, 493 (1980).

The employee is also expected to follow agency regulations. Under an agency regulation requiring consideration of placement in a vacancy suitable for a person with a disability, the employee must prove that he has the qualifications for the job and the employee must also provide a medical certificate if one is needed for the job he desires through reassignment. There was no error in failing to reassign an employee because he failed to demonstrate that the job he desired had no requirement of a medical certificate. Baker v. FAA, 19 MSPR 370, 374 (1984). Member Devaney criticized this result, suggesting that having recognized the limited regulatory right to a reassignment, the Board eviscerated the right through its imposition of a heavy burden of proof on the employee. To secure a position under an agency regulation calling for an attempt at reassignment before removing an appellant for disability, the employee must also show an interest in the position offered. If the job offered is refused, the agency need not go further under its regulation. Coley v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 101, 105-06 (1985).

A regulation requiring an agency to make reasonable efforts to offer a disabled employee a vacant position within her qualifications does not guarantee that the effort will result in reassignment. See Herghelegiu v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 38, 40 (1984) (appellant referred to, but not medically qualified for, two available positions). If agency regulations require that the agency make reasonable efforts to reassign an employee based on his qualifications and physical limitations, the obligation is met if the agency reviews available positions and finds no suitable vacancies. The Board implied that the agency is not required to extend a detail. Almazan v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 318, 320 & n.3 (1985) (interpreting agency regulations).

Although an agency regulation may, as a matter of policy, prohibit a certain type of accommodation, e.g., use of crutches (because the policy states that the accommodation is unsafe), it is unlikely that the agency may rely on the policy alone. A better case is made if proof is presented to support the denial of accommodation; if the proof is not forthcoming, the agency may fail in its accommodative responsibilities. Vaughn v. USPS, 33 MSPR 496, 502 (1987) (agency did not show that there was a realistic chance that the appellant or another person could be injured as a result of his use of crutches).
