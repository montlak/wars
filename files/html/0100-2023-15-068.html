e. Law Enforcement

Although holding unto itself the authority to review an agency's decision to deny credentials to an individual who is then removed for lack of the credentials, the Board does not impose a due process requirement on the credentialing process. Under Steele v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-12-0665-I-1 (NP 12/9/2013), involving a park ranger who did not secure a required law enforcement commission:

In her reply to the agency's response, she argues that the situation is no different, for purposes of due process, than a federal employee's loss of her security clearance, and she posits that such an employee is entitled to due process before her security clearance is revoked. While we do not accept the appellant's suggestion that the two situations are analogous, the cases she has cited do not support her position. In Rahgozar v. Department of the Air Force, 118 M.S.P.R. 37, ¶ 19 (2012), the Board did not address the issue of due process, but considered only whether the agency followed its own regulations governing the denial of a request to reinstate a security clearance and remanded the case to the administrative judge for her to consider if any procedural deficiencies resulted in harmful error. And, to the extent that Gaitan v. Department of Homeland Security, 118 M.S.P.R. 180, ¶ 23 (2012), may be read as supporting her position, its due process analysis is doubtful in light of Gargiulo v. Department of Homeland Security, 727 F.3d 1181, 1186 (Fed. Cir. 2013), wherein our reviewing court recently held that the Board erred by finding that due process provides an employee with procedural rights in connection with a security clearance determination and justifies an inquiry into whether the agency had reasonable grounds for suspending or revoking the employee's security clearance.

Moreover, we are aware of no case in which, in a situation where an appellant is removed for failure to meet a condition of employment, the Board has subjected the underlying process to a due process analysis. See, e.g., Thompson v. Department of the Air Force, 104 M.S.P.R. 529 (2007) (Air Traffic Control Specialist's failure to maintain an Air Traffic Control Specialist certificate); Laycock v. Department of the Army, 97 M.S.P.R. 597 (2004) (withdrawal of agency approval of employee's qualifications as an attorney), aff'd, 139 F. App'x 270 (Fed. Cir. 2005); Boulineau v. Department of the Army, 57 M.S.P.R. 244 (1993) (helicopter flight instructor's disqualification under medical fitness standards); Graham v. Department of the Air Force, 46 M.S.P.R. 227 (1990) (medical officer's failure to maintain agency medical credentials). We find, therefore, that the appellant has failed to show that the administrative judge erred in finding that the appellant was not entitled to due process in connection with her removal from training and the removal of her LE commission.
