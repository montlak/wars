a. No Decision Letter

The purpose of the decision letter is, naturally, to state the agency's decision and to provide notice of rights of appeal, if there are any. The Board does not find harmful error if the employee learns of an appealable action, appeals, but never receives a formal agency decision, Torres v. USPS, 35 MSPR 655, 660 (1987):

In considering the effects of agency procedural errors under 5 USC § 7513, the Board focuses on whether the employee's ability to defend against the adverse action was impaired by the error, or whether the error otherwise had a likely effect on the agency's action so as to constitute harmful error under 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(A). See Baracco, 15 MSPR at 121. In this case, the principal potentially harmful effects on the appellant caused by the agency's failure to provide him with a final written decision to indefinitely suspend him were the possibility that any oral or written reply that he made to the charges might not have been considered by the agency in rendering its final decision to suspend him, and his ignorance of his right to appeal the adverse action to the Board.

The appellant here, however, had an opportunity to reply to the charges contained in the notice of proposed suspension, but apparently made no effort to avail himself of it within the seven days afforded him to do so. Moreover, the Board has held that even if an agency erroneously prevents an employee from making such a reply, that failure is not per se harmful, but rather must be shown by the appellant likely to have had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the proposed adverse action before the agency. See, e.g., Ware v. Department of Education, 11 MSPB 265, 12 M.S.P.R. 666 (1982); Hockman v. American Battle Monuments, 11 MSPB 246, 12 MSPR 642, 646 (1982); and Messersmith v. General Services Administration, 8 MSPB 496, 89 MSPR 150, 154 (1981). The appellant has made no such showing in this case.

Further, having found good cause to waive the regulatory deadlines for filing a Board appeal of the indefinite suspension, we have alleviated any harm that may arguably have been caused by the agency's failure to inform the appellant of his right to file such an appeal.
