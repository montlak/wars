11. Bargaining Delegation; Local Bargaining

Negotiations may occur at different levels of an agency, FDA and AFGE Council 242, 54 FLRA 630, 640-41 (1998):

[T]he term "agency" does not define the scope of the Respondent's bargaining obligations. The Statute explicitly requires the Authority to determine appropriate bargaining units "on an agency, plant, installation, functional, or other basis[.]" 5 USC 7112(a). Thus, as we noted in FDA, 53 FLRA at 1276, the Statute contemplates that executive agencies will be subdivided for bargaining purposes. The collective bargaining obligation set out in the Statute requires that an agency provide "appropriate representatives" to bargain with "any exclusive representative in any appropriate unit in the agency[.]" 5 USC 7114(a)(4). These provisions demonstrate that the Statute requires an agency to bargain at the "unit" level, and that the broad definition of the term "agency" does not affect that obligation. See 5 USC 7103(a)(12) ("collective bargaining" is "an obligation of the representative of an agency and the exclusive representative of employees in an appropriate unit in the agency[.]")

Without a knowing waiver by the union, management has the obligation to send representatives who are fully authorized to commit management to an agreement to the bargaining table. IRS and NTEU Chap. 53, 23 FLRA 63, 84 (1986) (ALJ Decision). If a union holds exclusive recognition in a component of an agency, that component is obligated to bargain over conditions of employment, although control over a particular condition of employment rests with a different organizational component in the same overall agency. The only limits on an agency's obligation to bargain over conditions of employment in those circumstances are limits placed on its discretion by provisions of law, government-wide rule or regulation, or agency regulations supported by a compelling need. Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 22 FLRA 351, 361 (1986) (Proposal 5). That is because, to the extent an agency has discretion regarding a matter affecting conditions of employment, it is obligated to exercise that discretion through negotiations, unless precluded by regulatory or statutory provisions. DMA, Aerospace Ctr., St. Louis and NFFE Local 1827, 40 FLRA 244, 257-58 (1991) (ALJ Decision). Even without delegated authority, an agency cannot claim that a proposal is not negotiable because a higher level of the agency has not delegated to it the authority to negotiate that proposal. The duty to bargain requires the representatives of the parties to be prepared to discuss and negotiate any condition of employment. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 13 FLRA 554, 556 (1983).

An agency may not foreclose bargaining on an otherwise negotiable matter by delegating authority as to that matter only to an organizational level within the agency different from the organizational level of recognition. The statutory obligation to provide authorized representatives for negotiations cannot be obviated by an agency's internal regulations. However, the matters that authorized representatives can negotiate, i.e., the scope of bargaining at the level of negotiations, may be limited by internal regulations supported by a compelling need under § 7117 of the Statute. Dept. of Air Force, Eielson AFB and AFGE Local 1836, 23 FLRA 605, 610 (1986); see Dept. of Army, Ft. Greely and AFGE Local 1949, 23 FLRA 858, 865-66 (1986) (compelling need argument is inapposite when agency's arguments relate to delegation of authority). Without that compelling need, delegation may not excuse bargaining, i.e., an agency may not prevent bargaining on an otherwise negotiable matter by delegating authority over that matter to an organizational level within the agency other than at the level of recognition. Dist. No. 1, Pac. Coast Dist., Marine Eng'rs Beneficial Ass'n and Panama Canal Comm'n, 26 FLRA 390, 395 (1987). If no compelling need is demonstrated to bar negotiations over a regulatory change, if the matter is not otherwise removed from the required scope of bargaining, and if there are levels of exclusive recognition subordinate to the level of the agency or primary national subdivision that issued the regulation, then agency management at the level of exclusive recognition must, once the regulation is promulgated, notify the exclusive representative of its employees and provide the union with an opportunity to request bargaining concerning the regulation, as to both substance and impact, as appropriate, prior to implementation. See DHHS, Office of Sec'y and AFGE Local 41, 20 FLRA 175, 178-80 (1985); DLA and NAGE, 12 FLRA 412, 417 (1983) ("Where, as here, there are levels of exclusive recognition subordinate to the level of the agency or primary national subdivisions which issued the regulation, it is incumbent upon agency management at the level of exclusive recognition to notify the exclusive representative of its employees and afford the latter an opportunity to request bargaining concerning the regulation prior to implementation."), aff'd, DLA v. FLRA, 754 F.2d 1003, 1111-14 (D.C. Cir. 1985).

An agency with national recognition may bargain both nationally and for subordinate organizations within the unit, or the parties may provide for dual-level bargaining. That was made clear in a case involving a proposal for national and local EEO action plans, AFGE and Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB, 2 FLRA 604, 619 (1980) (Proposal 9):

[A]n agency is obligated to negotiate with an exclusive representative...unless otherwise prohibited by law and regulation, at the level of the exclusive recognition. [Citing section 7114(a)(1)] Moreover, in circumstances which involve a comprehensive unit...the agency is also under an obligation to negotiate at the level of the exclusive recognition with respect to the particular conditions of employment which will apply at subordinate activities within the unit. That is, the parties may provide in a master agreement at the level of recognition for the negotiation of supplemental agreements at the local level regarding matters pertaining to the conditions of employment of unit employees at the local level. For example, if the particular agency actions at issue in a proposal could be negotiated...at the command level, to be performed by the agency at the command level, the Statute does not preclude the parties from providing in a master agreement at the command level for these actions to be performed at the level of subordinate activities within the unit.

Consolidation may affect the level of bargaining. Following national consolidation, negotiation authority exists at the national level. Intermediate and local levels of recognition essentially disappear. Local unions lose the authority to negotiate working conditions unless there is some delegation from the national level of recognition. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 16 FLRA 674, 682 (1984) (ALJ Decision).

If the level of recognition is at the national level, the Statute does not require negotiations other than on the national level. DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 12 FLRA 52, 53 (1983); see NTEU Chap. 137 and DHS, C&BP, 60 FLRA 483, 486 (2004) (reconsideration denied, 61 FLRA 60 (2005)) (level of exclusive recognition was at national level, that is, between national union and the agency: "[I]t is well established that there is no statutory obligation to bargain below the level of recognition."). Bargaining occurs at the level of recognition. If there is a national or regional unit of recognition, bargaining at the local level occurs only if that lower-level bargaining is authorized at the level of recognition, DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCC Lompac and AFGE Local 3048, 66 FLRA 978, 980-81 (2012):

Under Authority precedent, it is well established that there is no statutory obligation to bargain below the level of recognition. See, e.g., U.S. FDA Admin., Ne. & Mid‑Atl. Regions, 53 FLRA 1269, 1274 (1998) (FDA); U.S. Dep't of the Air Force, Ogden Air Logistics Ctr., Hill Air Force Base, Utah, 39 FLRA 1409, 1417-18 (1991) (Hill AFB). But the Authority also has held that parties at the national level may "authorize local components to bargain supplemental and other agreements over particular subjects or in particular circumstances." FDA, 53 FLRA at 1274; see also Hill AFB, 39 FLRA at 1417.

If an agency notifies a union of a nationwide change in work practices, and provides an opportunity for impact bargaining, the union may designate a local to represent it in national negotiations, or it may bargain using national representation. Once that choice is made, and the union bargains using representatives of its national office, the union may not, contrary to the agreed-upon bargaining arrangements, then demand bargaining at the local level after national negotiations have concluded. IRS, Ogden Serv. Ctr. and NTEU, 16 FLRA 777, 786-87 (1984) (ALJ Decision). If the union holding national exclusive recognition delegates to its locals the authority to act on behalf of the exclusive representative as to midterm bargaining on matters at the local level, the agency is obliged to bargain with the local unless the union waives its bargaining rights. See DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Nat'l Council of SSA Field Office Locals, Council 220, 47 FLRA 1004, 1014 (1993). The agency may not inject into a refusal-to-bargain case the inappropriate delegation of a union's right to request negotiations if, in response to a bargaining demand, the agency declined to bargain solely for another reason, e.g., its belief that the union was not a validly certified bargaining representative. FDIC and NTEU, 48 FLRA 313, 328 (1993). If there is a question about whether a bargaining demand is made by a duly authorized representative of a union holding recognition, FLRA examines the circumstances of the bargaining demand and the union internal procedures, including the union constitutional provisions regulating delegation of bargaining or representational authority. See FEMA and AFGE Local 4060, 49 FLRA 1189, 1201-02 (1994) (ALJ Decision).

As to subjects of permissive bargaining, although management may elect to bargain on a proposal, that election is not made if the bargaining procedure requires local agreements to be made subject to higher-level approval. If the local agreement includes agreement on some permissive subject of bargaining, but that proposal is disapproved at a higher level, then the agency has not elected to bargain on that matter. NFFE Local 1979 and Forest Serv., San Dimas Equip. Dev. Ctr., 16 FLRA 369, 373-74 (1984) (Provision 2).

There are occasional disputes concerning who within a union has the authority to invoke bargaining on behalf of a unit. The union may designate whomever it wishes to request bargaining, and management is not free to disregard a request for negotiations made by union representatives known to management, unless there is a clear designation vesting the union's authority in some other individual, Air Force Materiel Command and AFGE Local 987, 54 FLRA 1529, 1534-36 (1998):

This case concerns the Agency's refusal to bargain based on its assertion that the request to bargain submitted by the LUJ steward was not an authorized request to bargain. The Respondent refused to recognize the request because bargaining at the directorate level was usually conducted by the LU steward, not the LUJ steward. However, the bargaining request asked that the Respondent bargain with the Union, not with the LUJ steward personally.

The Authority has held that an exclusive representative has the right to designate its representatives when fulfilling its responsibilities under the Statute, and an agency violates section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute when it refuses to honor the union's designation of a representative. See, e.g., Food and Drug Administration, Newark District Office, West Orange, New Jersey, 47 FLRA 535, 566 (1993).[4] The Authority has also held that because an agency may be charged with an unfair labor practice for failing or refusing to notify and bargain with a duly authorized representative of the exclusive representative, it follows as a matter of law and fundamental fairness that an agency is entitled to a clear notification of any delegation of authority an exclusive representative may make. See FEMA, 49 FLRA at 1201-02. In other words, the burden is on the exclusive representative to clearly inform an agency of any relevant delegation, i.e., what organization or individuals are authorized to act as its agent or representative and the scope of their authority. See id. at 1202.

[4] However, where the level of recognition is at the national level, an agency does not commit an unfair labor practice by refusing to negotiate with the president of a union local where it has not been shown that any authority has been delegated to the local. See Federal Emergency Management Agency, Headquarters, Washington, D.C., 49 FLRA 1189, 1201 (1994) (FEMA), citing Department of the Air Force, Ogden Air Logistics Center, Hill Air Force Base, Utah and Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson Air Force Base, Ohio, 39 FLRA 1409, 1417-18 (1991). There is no contention that national level recognition is at issue in this case.

In this case, we conclude that the Union met its burden of notifying the Agency regarding its designation of representatives. In fact, it is undisputed that the LU and LUJ stewards were duly-designated stewards, known to the Respondent. The record also shows that the LU steward usually conducted Union affairs with the Colonel in charge of the LU directorate. However, there is nothing that would limit another steward or Union officer from contacting the Colonel on Union business. There is no contention that the parties negotiated any system limiting the designation of stewards or the tasks that a particular steward could undertake, and there is no allegation that the Union waived its right to designate its representatives and stewards. Accordingly, we also conclude that the LUJ steward was authorized to act on behalf of the Union.

The Authority has held that an agency commits a violation of section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute when it designates the representatives of a labor organization with whom it will deal, rather than allowing the labor organization the right to designate its own representatives. See Department of the Air Force, 915th Tactical Fighter Group, Homestead Air Force Base, Florida, 13 FLRA 135, 136 (1983). In this regard, the Respondent's refusal to respond to the bargaining request submitted by the LUJ steward, and its contention that the LUJ steward was not delegated authority to request bargaining, reflect the Respondent's insistence that it would deal only with the LU directorate steward, rather than permitting the Union to designate its representative.

If the Respondent had any question as to the Union's designation of authority to bargain, then it should have inquired. The Respondent neither inquired nor gave any indication that it questioned the LUJ steward's authorization. Absent a specific designation from the Union to that effect, the Respondent could not insist that only the LU steward was authorized to submit the bargaining request. See id., 13 FLRA at 136. In these circumstances, the Union was not limited to communicating with the Respondent only through the LU steward, and the Respondent could not insist that the LU steward would be the only Union representative with whom it would deal. Accordingly, the Respondent impermissibly refused to recognize the Union's request to bargain submitted by the LUJ steward. For these reasons, we find that the Respondent violated section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute.
