broida mspb Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices, Subchapter 04: Cases Brought by OSC, Section D: OSC Disciplinary Action, Subsection 01: Limits on Prosecutorial Authority, Part a: Employee Who Takes or Recommends Personnel Action
a. Employee Who Takes or Recommends Personnel Action

The concern in the disciplinary case is the employee, à la 5 USC 2302(b), "who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action." Special Counsel v. Eidmann, 49 MSPR 614, 623-24 (1991), expressed that the actual exercise of duties, rather than the official description of those duties on an organizational chart or position description, governs an individual's liability for prohibited personnel practices:

...As the CALJ correctly found, Board precedent makes it plain that the concept of supervisory or personnel authority under section 2302(b) is to be broadly construed. Acting Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 6 MSPB 442, 6 MSPR 526, 545 (1981). A specific delegation of authority to recommend or take a personnel action is not a prerequisite for liability under section 2302(b). Special Counsel v. Harvey, 28 MSPR 595, 608-09 n.25, rev'd on other grounds sub nom., Harvey v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 802 F.2d 537, 544 (D.C. Cir. 1986).

In his exceptions, the respondent contends that the CALJ erred in relying on Sullivan and Harvey because, unlike those employees, the respondent did not "occupy a prominent position" in the agency which would give him "the appearance or color of authority."...The record in this case clearly refutes the respondent's contention that he did not act under color of authority in advising Mr. Gouryeb to terminate Mr. Levin. The respondent's position description as administrative officer required him to serve as Mr. Gouryeb's [the deciding official] chief advisor on all personnel matters, including employee-management relations. He was also responsible for "investigat[ing] grievances...and counsel[ing] employees on individual personnel matters." In light of this evidence, we find no error in the CALJ's finding that the respondent had the requisite authority to advise Mr. Gouryeb to terminate Mr. Levin and actually did so.

Further, the record shows that the respondent was so thoroughly immersed in Mr. Levin's removal that he can truly be viewed as the moving force behind the termination. He served as Mr. Gouryeb's primary source of information regarding Mr. Levin; offered his opinions to Mr. Gouryeb on the information he had gathered; obtained advice from a personnel management specialist, whom he supervised, that Mr. Levin could be terminated based on his "character"; provided this advice to Mr. Gouryeb; and wrote the termination letter on a "cooperative basis" with Mr. Gouryeb.

Finally, when Mr. Levin's supervisors resisted the termination action on the ground that there was no basis to remove him, the respondent pressed Levin for information he knew would guarantee his removal, i.e., that Levin still did not accept management's interpretation of the smoking regulations, had sought assistance concerning this issue from officials outside the New Jersey office, and intended to continue attempting to resolve the matter, if necessary. The respondent immediately relayed Mr. Levin's disclosures to Mr. Gouryeb and urged him to remove Levin in the strongest terms: "No if's, and's or but's. The man should be terminated." After Mr. Gouryeb agreed to go forward again with the termination, the respondent personally handed the termination notice to Mr. Levin. Under these circumstances, we find no error in the CALJ's finding that the respondent recommended Mr. Levin's termination and therefore exercised the requisite authority to be covered by 5 USCA 2302(b) (West Supp. 1990).

Under 5 USC 2302(b), "any employee who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action, shall not, with respect to" that authority engage in any of the enumerated prohibited personnel practices other than those described at § 2302(b)(8) (whistleblowing reprisal) and § 2302(b)(9) (other forms of reprisal relating to protected disclosures, appeal rights, and refusals to violate laws). As to reprisals covered under §§ 2302(b)(8) & (9), liability is incurred by those who "take or fail to take, or threaten to take or fail to take, a personnel action...." What may be imprecise legislative drafting led to problems in determining the liability of those responding to OSC disciplinary actions.

The Federal Circuit determined that liability for reprisals against whistleblowers attaches only to those who take or fail to take a personnel action, Frederick v. DOJ, 73 F.3d 349, 354 (Fed. Cir. 1996):

The WPA specifically distinguishes between those who recommend personnel actions and those who take or fail to take personnel actions. Id. § 2302(b)-(b)(8). In terms of being within the scope of the WPA, the act applies to those who have authority to recommend a personnel action. Id. § 2302(b). However, the WPA under section 2302(b)(8) only attaches liability to those who take or fail to take a personnel action. See Eidmann, 976 F.2d at 1407. Supervisors such as Frederick are fully encouraged to make honest recommendations concerning employees, but they must be more careful of actions they take (or fail to take) concerning employees.

Frederick did not take or fail to take a personnel action against Womack. On the contrary, his C & E evaluation was merely a recommendation to the agency. It is only when one takes or fails to take a personnel action against an employee because of a prohibited disclosure that liability attaches under the relevant section of the WPA, and no such action occurred here. The board's holding that Frederick took a personnel action and that the third element of the Eidmann test was met, see id. at 1407, was thus not in accordance with law because it resulted from an erroneous interpretation of the WPA.

See Ferro v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 99-3258) ("[T]hough the WPA applies to those who have authority to recommend a personnel action, liability attaches only to those who take or fail to take a personnel action[.]").

The Board earlier decided that an individual who recommends but does not take an improper personnel action may be guilty of reprisal. See Special Counsel v. Starrett, 28 MSPR 60 (1985); cf. Caster v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 436 (1994) (manager demoted for taking reprisal against a whistleblower by recommending disciplinary action). The Board made the point again in Frederick v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 517 (1994), involving whistleblower reprisal, but its analysis was reversed in Frederick v. DOJ, 73 F.3d 349 (Fed. Cir. 1996), supra. In Frederick the Board concluded that no distinction existed between the liability of those who commit prohibited personnel practices generally and whistleblower reprisals in particular, 65 MSPR at 528:

The appellant nonetheless argues that his recommendation does not fall within the WPA's jurisdiction because he did not actually make the decision that Mr. Womack would not be retained and would not be appointed to a career Border Patrol Agent position. As the administrative judge noted, the Board has construed the exercise of supervisory or personnel authority under 5 USC § 2302(b) quite broadly. See Special Counsel v. Harvey, 28 MSPR 595, 608-09 n.25 (1984), rev'd on other grounds, Harvey v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 802 F.2d 537, 544 (D.C. Cir. 1986). When Congress adopted the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989, amending 5 USC § 2302(b), it was presumed to have knowledge of this broad construction. See Benedetto v. Office of Personnel Management, 32 MSPR 530, 534 (1987), aff'd, Horner v. Benedetto, 847 F.2d 814 (Fed. Cir. 1988). And, neither the amended statute nor the legislative history shows that Congress wished to mandate a more restrictive interpretation. See 5 USC § 2302(b)(8); H.R. Rep. No. 274, 100th Cong., 1st Sess. (1987); S. Rep. No. 413, 100th Cong., 2d Sess. (1988). In addition, both the Board and the court found in Eidmann that the appellant recommended an employee's termination and therefore exercised the requisite authority to be covered by 5 USC § 2302(b). See Special Counsel v. Eidmann, 49 MSPR 614, 624 (1991), aff'd, Eidmann v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 976 F.2d 1400, 1407 (Fed. Cir. 1992). The administrative judge thus correctly found that the appellant had exercised the requisite authority to be covered by 5 USC § 2302(b) when he recommended that Mr. Womack not be retained. See id.; Harvey, 28 MSPR at 608-09 n.25.
