1. Allen Criteria

The Board's premier decision on entitlement to counsel fees under 5 USC 7701(g) is Allen v. USPS, 2 MSPR 420, 436, 2 MSPB 582 (1980). It is one of MSPB's top 12 all-time popular releases.

Allen reversed a decision of an AJ who awarded fees to an appellant because he prevailed in his appeal. The decision set standards governing the adjudication of counsel fee awards. To understand Allen, one must first review the statute, 5 USC 7701(g):

(1) Except as provided in paragraph (2) of this subsection, the Board, or an administrative law judge or other employee of the Board designated to hear a case, may require payment by the agency involved of reasonable attorney fees incurred by an employee or applicant for employment if the employee or applicant is the prevailing party and the Board, administrative law judge, or other employee (as the case may be) determines that payment by the agency is warranted in the interest of justice, including any case in which a prohibited personnel practice was engaged in by the agency or any case in which the agency's action was clearly without merit.

[Refer to attorney fee provisions of the Whistleblower Protection Act and other statutes under "Statutory and Regulatory Basis for Fees" earlier in this chapter.]

The essential three factors in an entitlement analysis were determined in Allen to be: (1) the appellant must be the prevailing party, a factor already discussed; (2) the award of attorney fees must be warranted in the interest of justice; and (3) the fees awarded must be reasonable. Attention is now directed to the interest of justice standard; if this is not met, concerns about the reasonableness of the fee requested are immaterial. [Refer later in this chapter to "Reasonableness of Fees."]

To determine whether an award is in the interest of justice, after reviewing the legislative history of the counsel fee provision of the Reform Act, the Board outline several determinative factors in Allen, 2 MSPR at 434-35:

1. Where the agency engaged in a "prohibited personnel practice" (§ 7701(g)(1));

2. Where the agency's action was "clearly without merit" (§ 7701(g)(1)), or was "wholly unfounded," or the employee is "substantially innocent" of the charges brought by the agency (any such determination should be based on the record established by the Board on the merits of the agency's action, without a separate hearing on the question. In making such determination, the presiding official should examine the degree of fault on the employee's part and the existence of any reasonable basis for the agency's action);

3. Where the agency initiated the action against the employee in "bad faith," including:

a. Where the agency's action was brought to "harass" the employee;

b. Where the agency's action was brought to "exert improper pressure on the employee to act in certain ways";

4. Where the agency committed a "gross procedural error" which "prolonged the proceeding" or "severely prejudiced" the employee (a criterion which goes beyond harmful procedural error and requires weighing the nature of and any excuse for the error against the prejudice and burden resulting for the employee);

5. Where the agency "knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits" when it brought the proceeding (this may include circumstances in which the agency prepared or presented its case so negligently as to make it a foregone conclusion that the action could not be sustained on the record established before the Board).

The Board did not limit its discretion in Allen, 2 MSPR at 434:

The principal constraint upon the Board's section 7701(g)(1) discretion to determine when an award is warranted arises from the Board's duty to exercise that discretion reasonably, which necessarily includes the duty to articulate a rational explanation for each award.... Particular applications can best be determined by the Board on a case by case basis, with the benefit of a full record and the insights and reasoning of the presiding official who heard the evidence in each case. Such self-restraint is both appropriate to the Board's quasi-judicial functions and in accord with the expectation of Congress that the Board will allow more particular criteria for fee awards to "develop in light of its experience." [2 MSPB at 592].

The Federal Circuit "has frequently cited the Allen factors with approval." Hilliard v. USPS, 403 Fed. Appx. 504, 506 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP).

Through the commission of a prohibited personnel practice, the statutory standard is met without more analysis. See Freeman v. VA, 66 MSPR 125, 130 (1995) ("The administrative judge's finding of a prohibited personnel practice also makes an award of attorney fees warranted in the interest of justice under 5 USC 7701(g)(1).... Whether, as the agency argues, the administrative judge erred in finding that an award of attorney fees is also warranted in the interest of justice because the agency knew that it would not prevail on the merits, is thus immaterial."); Smit v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 612, 616 n.2 (1994) ("[t]he requirement under 5 USC 7701(g) that the award be in the interest of justice does not apply to requests for attorney fees under 5 USC 1221(g)(1)[.] "); Allen v. USPS, 2 MSPR 420, 434-35 (1980) (award of fees was warranted in the interest of justice under 5 USC 7701(g) upon finding that the agency engaged in a prohibited personnel practice).

In cases of discrimination, 5 USC 7701(g)(2) requires payment of fees in accordance with 42 USC 2000e-5(k), a statute requiring only prevailing party status. A separate body of law has developed under Title VII of the 1964 Civil Rights Act concerning counsel fees. Those decisions are beyond the scope of this discussion. Once entitlement is established, however, the Board does not appear to treat reasonableness of fees any differently in discrimination cases than it does in cases involving construction of § 7701(g)(1). [Refer to Davis and Hadley's A Guide to Federal Sector Equal Employment Law and Practice (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2023) for fee awards in federal sector EEO cases.]

Findings of violations of civil rights, whistleblower, and veterans protection statutes aside, in one form or another, the Allen criteria govern fee awards under the CSRA and the parallel fee provisions of the Back Pay Act (applicable to arbitration cases). The Federal Circuit has invested considerable effort interpreting and applying these criteria. Although the court occasionally revises the Board's interpretations of Allen, the Federal Circuit has not significantly departed from the Allen criteria. The court did, however, do the unthinkable: it awarded fees, reversing a Board decision denying fees, not because of wrongdoing by the employing agency, but because of delays in adjudication caused by the Board in the nonprecedential case of Lokos v. EEOC, 928 F.2d 412 (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP), reversing Lokos v. EEOC, 44 MSPR 115, 124 (1990):

Lokos appeals from a decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board...reversing the initial decision of the administrative judge who had ordered the EEOC to pay Lokos attorneys fees in the amount of $12,949 in accordance with the provisions of 5 USC 7701(g)(1) (1988).

Because we find that the Board's unreasonable delay in handing down its decision constituted a violation of Lokos' rights under 5 USC 7701, we vacate the decision of the Board and we order the EEOC to pay Lokos' counsel in the administrative proceedings fees in the sum of $12,949.

...

[T]he Board's decision involved in this appeal was not issued until three years and five months after the case was ready for decision. We find nothing in the administrative record filed with the court nor in the Government's brief which excuses or justifies the Board's long delay.

After review of the entire record, we conclude, as Lokos contends, that the Board's delay was unreasonable and constitutes a violation of his rights under 5 USC 7701.

In order to compensate claimants who have been deprived of benefits because of unreasonable delays in administrative proceedings which have violated claimants' statutory rights, the courts have fashioned equitable remedies, which may include payment of money damages. In so doing, the courts have undertaken to provide remedies which will balance efforts to afford claimants their rights with an appreciation of an agency's administrative problems. See White v. Mathews, 559 F.2d 852, 860 (2d Cir. 1977), cert. denied, 435 U.S. 908 (1978); Sharpe v. Harris, 621 F.2d 530, 531 (2d Cir. 1980); Lewis v. Heckler, 605 F. Supp. 9, 10-12 (E.D. Ky. 1984).

Under the circumstances of this case, we have determined that the appropriate equitable remedy to be provided in this case is to adopt the decision of the administrative judge as the basis for the court's decision and judgment.

The Board states that "once it finds that fees are warranted in the interest of justice under one Allen category, it does not address the appellant's argument that fees are warranted under other Allen categories." Gensburg v. VA, 80 MSPR 187, 195 ¶ 17 (1998), following remand, Gensburg v. VA, 85 MSPR 198, 203 ¶ 3 (2000). The Board did not explain in Gensburg whether its "rule" applied only to the Board or to its AJs as well.
