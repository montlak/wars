2. Preference Eligible Status

Since veterans preference status may be the basis of an appeal right to the Board under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B), reference is made to the statutory basis for veterans preference, 5 USC 2108:

For the purpose of this title--

(1) "veteran" means an individual who--

(A) served on active duty in the armed forces during a war, in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized, or during the period beginning April 28, 1952, and ending July 1, 1955;

(B) served on active duty as defined by section 101(21) of title 38 at any time in the armed forces for a period of more than 180 consecutive days any part of which occurred after January 31, 1955, and before October 15, 1976, not including service under section 12103(d) of title 10 pursuant to an enlistment in the Army National Guard or the Air National Guard or as a Reserve for service in the Army Reserve, Naval Reserve, Air Force Reserve, Marine Corps Reserve, or Coast Guard Reserve;

(C) served on active duty as defined by section 101(21) of title 38 in the armed forces during the period beginning on August 2, 1990, and ending on January 2, 1992; or

(D) served on active duty as defined by section 101(21) of title 38 at any time in the armed forces for a period of more than 180 consecutive days any part of which occurred during the period beginning on September 11, 2001, and ending on the date prescribed by Presidential proclamation or by law as the last date of Operation Iraqi Freedom;

and, except as provided under section 2108a, who has been discharged or released from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions;

(2) "disabled veteran" means an individual who has served on active duty in the armed forces, (except as provided under section 2108a) has been separated therefrom under honorable conditions, and has established the present existence of a service-connected disability or is receiving compensation, disability retirement benefits, or pension because of a public statute administered by the Department of Veterans Affairs or a military department;

(3) "preference eligible" means, except as provided in paragraph (4) of this section or section 2108a(c)--

(A) a veteran as defined by paragraph (1)(A) of this section;

(B) a veteran as defined by paragraph (1)(B), (C), or (D) of this section;

(C) a disabled veteran;

(D) the unmarried widow or widower of a veteran as defined by paragraph (1)(A) of this section;

(E) the wife or husband of a service-connected disabled veteran if the veteran has been unable to qualify for any appointment in the civil service or in the government of the District of Columbia;

(F) the parent of an individual who lost his or her life under honorable conditions while serving in the armed forces during a period named by paragraph (1)(A) of this section, if--

(i) the spouse of that parent is totally and permanently disabled; or

(ii) that parent, when preference is claimed, is unmarried or, if married, legally separated from his or her spouse;

(G) the parent of a service-connected permanently and totally disabled veteran, if--

(i) the spouse of that parent is totally and permanently disabled; or

(ii) that parent, when preference is claimed, is unmarried or, if married, legally separated from his or her spouse; and

(H) a veteran who was discharged or released from a period of active duty by reason of a sole survivorship discharge (as that term is defined in section 1174(i) of title 10);

but does not include applicants for, or members of, the Senior Executive Service, the Defense Intelligence Senior Executive Service, the Senior Cryptologic Executive Service, or the Federal Bureau of Investigation and Drug Enforcement Administration Senior Executive Service;

(4) except for the purposes of chapters 43 and 75 of this title, "preference eligible" does not include a retired member of the armed forces unless--

(A) the individual is a disabled veteran; or

(B) the individual retired below the rank of major or its equivalent; and

(5) "retired member of the armed forces" means a member or former member of the armed forces who is entitled, under statute, to retired, retirement, or retainer pay on account of service as a member.

5 USC 2108a adds:

(a) VETERAN.--

(1) IN GENERAL.--Except as provided under paragraph (3), an individual shall be treated as a veteran defined under section 2108(1) for purposes of making an appointment in the competitive service, if the individual--

(A) meets the definition of a veteran under section 2108(1), except for the requirement that the individual has been discharged or released from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions; and

(B) submits a certification described under paragraph (2) to the Federal officer making the appointment.

(2) CERTIFICATION.--A certification referred to under paragraph (1) is a certification that the individual is expected to be discharged or released from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions not later than 120 days after the date of the submission of the certification.

(b) DISABLED VETERAN.--

(1) IN GENERAL.--Except as provided under paragraph (3), an individual shall be treated as a disabled veteran defined under section 2108(2) for purposes of making an appointment in the competitive service, if the individual--

(A) meets the definition of a disabled veteran under section 2108(2), except for the requirement that the individual has been separated from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions; and

(B) submits a certification described under paragraph (2) to the Federal officer making the appointment.

(2) CERTIFICATION.--A certification referred to under paragraph (1) is a certification that the individual is expected to be separated from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions not later than 120 days after the date of the submission of the certification.

(c) PREFERENCE ELIGIBLE.--Subsections (a) and (b) shall apply with respect to determining whether an individual is a preference eligible under section 2108(3) for purposes of making an appointment in the competitive service.

See Semper v. United States, 694 F.3d 90, 92 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2012) ("Preference eligible personnel include certain veterans and some close relatives. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3)."); cf. Badana v. Dept. of Air Force, 104 MSPR 182, 185 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 345 (2006) ("[p]reference eligibility does not attach to a member of the armed forces who retires at the rank of major or above unless the individual in question is a disabled veteran. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(4).").

Veterans preference eligibility may provide advantage to the veteran who applies for a job with the federal government; it may establish a Board appeal right for an individual in the excepted service; and it may provide competitive advantage among employees vying for a limited number of positions during a reduction in force. As to appeal rights, 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B) defines employees who may bring appealable actions to the Board to include a "preference eligible in the excepted service who has completed 1 year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions: (i) in an Executive agency; or (ii) in the United States Postal Service or Postal Rate Commission." See Vercelli v. USPS, 70 MSPR 322, 329 (1996) ("[T]he appellant is not an 'employee' under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B) and entitled to appeal an adverse action because, although he is a preference eligible in the excepted service, he has not completed 1 year of current, continuous service in the same or similar positions."); White v. USPS, 34 MSPR 687, 689 (1987) (("A preference eligible in the United States Postal Service who has completed one year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions is an 'employee' who is entitled to appeal certain adverse actions (including a demotion) to the Board."). As to other benefits of preference eligibility, Smith v. USPS, 81 MSPR 92, 99 n.6 (1999), defines the role of veterans preference in the selection and initial appointments into the civil service, retention standing during a RIF, and priority consideration for reemployment. [Refer to Chapter 10, "Veteran, Other Preferences," concerning preference eligibility in appeals from reductions in force; refer later in this chapter to "Veterans Employment Opportunities Act."]

A preference eligible in the excepted service does not serve a period of probation or a trial period to secure appeal rights. Rather, under Silas v. Dept. of Treasury, DA-3443-16-0074-I-1 (NP 8/17/2016):

...The definition of "employee" includes "a preference eligible in the excepted service who has completed 1 year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions--(i) in an Executive agency; or (ii) in the United States Postal Service or Postal Regulatory Commission." 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B). Notably, for a preference eligible in the excepted service, the absence or completion of a probationary or trial period is not determinative of "employee" status. Maibaum v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 116 M.S.P.R. 234, ¶ 9 (2011). Rather, the dispositive issue is whether the appellant satisfied the 1-year "current continuous service" requirement when she was separated. Id. The term "current continuous service" means a period of employment or service either in the competitive or excepted service that immediately precedes an adverse action without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday. Id., ¶ 14; 5 C.F.R. § 752.402.

Through 1990 and the Civil Service Due Process Amendments, even though an individual completed one year of service, he did not have an appeal right in an action brought under 5 USC 7511-13 if he was in the excepted service without veterans preference. Richardson v. Dept. of Treasury, 5 MSPR 350, 352, 5 MSPB 373 (1981); Henderson v. NSA, 2 MSPR 15, 16, 2 MSPB 189 (1980) ("On the issue whether the appellant had a right to appeal to the Board from his allegedly involuntary resignation, the record shows, and it is undisputed, that he was appointed under the provisions of Section 2, Pub. L. No. 86-36, as amended (50 U.S.C. 402 note), and therefore was an employee in the excepted service. Also, he was not a preference eligible. Under 5 U.S.C. 7511, as an employee in the excepted service who was not a preference eligible, he was not an 'employee' for the purpose of Subchapter II (Removal,...). As such, he was not entitled to the procedural or appellate rights provided to certain employees in removal cases under Title 5, and had no entitlement to appeal his allegedly involuntary resignation...."). Following the Civil Service Due Process Amendments, as of August 17, 1990, a preference eligible in the excepted service continued to enjoy appeal rights after a waiting period of one year; without preference eligibility, other excepted service employees acquired MSPB appeal rights upon completion of a two-year waiting period.

If the right to appeal depends on veterans preference, that status is of jurisdictional significance, Olson v. VA, 92 MSPR 169, 173 ¶ 8 (2002):

Preference eligibility is a matter of considerable jurisdictional significance for the MSPB. In one case, an agency's personnel paperwork stated that the appellant was a preference eligible but the agency then discovered at the PFR stage that the appellant was not entitled to preference eligible status. On the theory that jurisdiction may be raised at any time, the Board allowed the agency to challenge the appellant's status at the PFR stage and remanded the case to the judge for receipt and evaluation of evidence concerning that status. See Fish v. USPS, 67 MSPR 540, 543 (1995); cf. Wise v. TVA, 7 MSPR 92, 94 (1981) (where appellant makes an error on his appeal form and checks "no" in response to the question "Are you a veteran?," the judge should adjudicate the appeal if the evidence submitted by the agency clearly indicated that the appellant was a veteran).

Veterans preference status, and distinctions between that status and military service demonstrating that an individual is a veteran without preference eligibility, were summarized, Geiren v. USPS, CH-0752-10-0958-I-1 (NP 4/27/2011)) (aff'd, Geiren v. MSPB, 476 Fed. Appx. 256 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP, post):

...While the appellant asserts that she is a veteran, not all veterans are preference eligible. To qualify as a preference eligible, a veteran, such as the appellant, must, among other things, have served on active duty in the armed forces of the United States, including in the reserve components. 5 U.S.C. § 2108; 38 U.S.C. 101(10); Strachan v. U.S. Postal Service, 64 M.S.P.R. 333, 336-37 (1994). "Active duty" is defined in relevant part as "full-time duty in the Armed Forces, other than active duty for training." 38 U.S.C. § 101(21)(A); see Strachan, 64 M.S.P.R. at 337. "Active duty for training" is defined, in pertinent part, as "full-time duty in the Armed Forces performed by Reserves for training purposes." 38 U.S.C. § 101(22)(A); Strachan, 64 M.S.P.R. at 337.

We agree with the administrative judge findings that, while the appellant is a veteran, she is not preference eligible. Specifically, on an initial employment form, the appellant clearly indicated that she is not preference eligible. Further, the appellant's initial employment form and DD-214 form indicate that the appellant served in the Army Reserves for approximately 4 months, that she had no prior active service, and that the appellant was separated from "initial active duty training." Because the appellant was serving in the Army Reserves for training purposes, she does not meet the definition of "active duty" under 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3), and therefore has not shown that she is preference eligible. See 38 U.S.C. § 101(21)(A) and 38 U.S.C. § 101(22)(A). Accordingly, the administrative judge properly found that the appellant has no appeal rights to the Board.

Reserve training status relative to preference eligibility was discussed in Geiren v. MSPB, 476 Fed. Appx. 256, 258-59 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP):

The Board correctly found that Ms. Geiren does not satisfy those requirements. While there is no dispute that Ms. Geiren completed at least one year of continuous service with the Postal Service, the evidence in the record indicates that Ms. Geiren is not a preference-eligible veteran. The statute defining veterans' preference provides that veterans who served on "active duty" are eligible for preference. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(1). The term "active duty" is defined as "full-time duty in the Armed Forces, other than active duty for training." 38 U.S.C. § 101(21)(A) (emphasis added). The term "active duty for training," in turn, is defined as "full-time duty in the Armed Services performed by Reserves for training purposes." 21 U.S.C. § 101(22)(A), (26), (27)(A). Thus, the statute expressly excludes reserve training from the type of service that qualifies a veteran for preference. Cf. Burrow v. Nicholson, 245 Fed. Appx. 972, 973 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (noting that Department of Veterans Affairs regulations distinguish active duty from Army Reserve service). Ms. Geiren's Form DD214 indicates that she participated only in initial active duty training with the Army Reserve for about four months. There is no evidence that she served in any capacity other than reserve training. Ms. Geiren, therefore, cannot claim that she is a preference-eligible veteran.

In her informal brief, Ms. Geiren argues that "the law states a veteran qualifies under the requirements of MSPB." Ms. Geiren fails to take into account the preference-eligibility requirement. As we have explained above, not all veterans have the right to appeal; only preference-eligible veterans do. Ms. Geiren's four months of reserve service is insufficient to make her eligible for veterans' preference.

Although veterans preference is among the most direct and calculable of a government's benefits to ex-servicemen, Monaco v. United States, 523 F.2d 935, 939 (9th Cir. 1975), the preference is no broader than the statutory grant, Crowley v. United States, 527 F.2d 1176, 1182-83 (Ct. Cl. 1975):

There can be no dispute that the granting of preference to veterans in certain aspects of public employment is an established principle in both State and Federal practice....

That the Veterans' Preference Act reflects Congress' will, as stated in Johnson v. Robison, 415 U.S. 361, 382, 94 S. Ct. 1160, 39 L. Ed.2d 389 (1974), to encourage and reward military service to the Nation, does not, however, render limitless the rights and benefits which the Act furnishes. Nor does the Act, as plaintiffs would apparently have us believe, cloak veterans with any "penumbral rights"; its provisions are necessarily specific, and for plaintiffs to benefit therefrom they must show themselves to be clearly within the intended ambit of those provisions. We noted as much in Powers v. United States, 169 Ct. Cl. 626, 630 (1965), where, in upholding the Government's removal without hearing of a veteran career-conditional employee serving a one-year probationary period, we stated:

This court is unable to accept the assertion of plaintiff that, on the basis of a general policy of the Government (and without regard to specific statutes and regulations), a veteran is entitled to special treatment, including a hearing. Since plaintiff has not shown any failure of the Government to comply with any applicable statute or regulation, we must conclude that the removal of plaintiff involved no violation of his procedural rights.

More elaborate explanation of the sources of veterans preference status is provided by Hay v. USPS, 103 MSPR 167, 173-74, 2006 MSPB 251 (2006) (following remand to the AJ, 106 MSPR 151, 2007 MSPB 156 (2007)):

It is undisputed that the appellant's DD Form 214 shows he served on active military duty from 1989 until 1997, when he received an "Honorable" discharge. The agency, relying upon 5 U.S.C. § 2108(1)-(3), asserted below that an employee is a preference eligible if he served on active duty in the armed forces during a war between 1952-1955, 1955-1976, or 1990-1992, or served in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized; is a disabled veteran; or shares certain familial relationships with a preference eligible or disabled veteran. The agency argued below that the appellant's "mere service on active duty" did not make him a preference eligible and that there was no evidence to show he had served in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge was authorized. The administrative judge relied upon this reasoning in his initial decision dismissing the appellant's prior appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

Congress, however, amended 5 U.S.C. § 2108 in 1998[2] to expand the definition of a preference eligible to include an individual who "served on active duty as defined by section 101(21) of title 38 in the armed forces during the period beginning on August 2, 1990, and ending on January 2, 1992...and who has been discharged or released from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions." 5 U.S.C. § 2108(1)(C), (3)(B).[3] Whereas under prior law an individual had to have actually served in the southwest Asia theater of operations to receive preference-eligible status based upon active duty during the first Persian Gulf conflict, that requirement no longer holds, and "mere service on active duty" between August 2, 1990, and January 2, 1992, is sufficient to confer preference-eligible status. The agency correctly amended the appellant's PS Form 50 to show that he was a preference eligible based upon his service on active military duty from 1989 until 1997, followed by his "Honorable" discharge. Thus, the appellant, as a preference eligible who has completed one year of current continuous service, has Board appeal rights. See Bolton, 154 F.3d at 1316 & n.2.

Determination of preference eligibility can become complex, depending on the timing and nature of the military service and the terms of discharge from the service. The term "preference eligible" most commonly reflects a special privilege as to appointment, retention, or appeal rights conferred upon those who have served in our Armed Forces. The term is defined at 5 USC 2108 to include disabled veterans and certain veterans who have served in time of conflicts or from 1952 through 1976, with exceptions for short-term service and some service in the National Guard and reserves. As further defined by 5 USC 2108(1), a preference eligible is an individual who served on active duty and who was separated from the Armed Forces under honorable conditions. For an explanation of the conditions of separation from the armed services that permit preference eligibility, refer to Dooley v. TVA, 43 MSPR 462, 464-65, 467 (1990) ("[A]ny period of qualifying military service followed by an honorable discharge falls within the scope of 5 USC 2108(1)."), and to Clark v. USPS, 118 MSPR 527, 531 ¶ 9, 2012 MSPB 106 (2012) (the appellant is a preference eligible if a qualifying period of military service ends with an honorable discharge even if a later period of military service, prior to termination of civil service employment, was not concluded with an honorable discharge; remanding to determine if Clark's service was qualifying under Dooley v. TVA, 43 MSPR 462 (1990), distinguishing "conditional" discharges (discharges that do not complete the individual's obligated service), and unconditional discharges (discharges that occur at a time when the person has completed the service he was obligated to perform), and to Valcin v. USPS, AT-3330-16-0681-I-1 (NP 2/27/2023) ("We previously have held that if an individual is separated from a qualifying period of military service under honorable conditions, a subsequent discharge under other than honorable conditions does not necessarily disqualify him from preference-eligible status under 5 U.S.C. § 2108. Here, however, the appellant was not separated from a qualifying period of military service under honorable conditions. Strachan v. USPS, 64 MSPR 333, 336-39 (1994), discussed preference eligibility relative to the nature of service on active duty and in the reserves:

The appellant has claimed preference eligibility under 5 USC 2108(3)(B), which defines a "preference eligible" to include a "veteran" as defined by 5 USC 2108(1)(B). Section 2108(1)(B), 5 USC, defines "veteran" as an individual who has:

served on active duty as defined by section 101(21) of title 38 at any time in the armed forces for a period of more than 180 consecutive days any part of which occurred after January 31, 1955, and before the date of the enactment of the Veterans' Education and Employment Assistance Act of 1976, not including service under section 511(d) of title 10 pursuant to an enlistment in the Army National Guard or the Air National Guard or as a Reserve for Service in the Army Reserve, Naval Reserve, Air Force Reserve, Marine Corps Reserve, or Coast Guard Reserve; and who has been separated from the armed forces under honorable conditions[.] [Emphases added.]

We need not determine whether the appellant has been "separated from the armed forces under honorable conditions," because we find that the appellant has failed to satisfy the "active duty" requirement for preference eligibility under 5 USC 2108(1)(B), (3)(B).

The record shows that the appellant performed some type of active duty service for slightly more than 180 days and that this service occurred in 1961, a period covered by 5 USC 2108(1)(B). The issue is whether this type of active duty constituted "active duty as defined by section 101(21) of title 38" and not "service under section 511(d) of title 10 pursuant to an enlistment...in the Army Reserve...."

"Active duty" is defined by section 101(21) of title 38, in pertinent part, as "full-time duty in the Armed Forces, other than active duty for training." 38 USC 101(21)(A). For purposes of this definition, "Armed Forces" is defined as "the United States Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, and Coast Guard, including the reserve components thereof." 38 USC 101(10); cf. 5 USC 2101(2) (defining "armed forces" as "the Army, Navy, Air Force, Marine Corps, and Coast Guard" without specifically including the reserve components). "Active duty for training" is defined, in pertinent part, as "full-time duty in the Armed Forces performed by Reserves for training purposes." 38 USC 101(22)(A).

Under section 511(d) of title 10, a "non-prior-service person" who enlists in the Reserves must "perform an initial period of active duty for training of not less than twelve weeks to commence insofar as practicable within 270 days after the date of that enlistment."

The administrative judge determined that, since individuals enlisting in the Reserves were required to "perform an initial period of active duty for training of not less than twelve weeks," under 10 USC 511(d), the appellant's 6 months of active duty must have included a minimum of 12 weeks, or approximately 3 months, of active duty for training. She further determined that, when the 3 months of active duty for training were subtracted from the 6 months of total active duty service, the remaining active duty service period did not satisfy the minimum 180 consecutive days of active duty, not for training, required for preference eligibility.

The appellant argues on petition for review that the administrative judge erred in relying on 10 USC 511(d) to deny his claim of preference eligibility, because that provision was not enacted until 1963, several years after his period of active duty service. We need not address this argument because the appellant did not raise it below and has not shown that it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite his due diligence.... Moreover, even considering the substance of the argument, we find that it is without merit.

Indeed, subsection (d) of 10 USC 511 was not enacted until 1963. Universal Military Training Act, Pub. L. 88-110, § 3, 77 Stat. 134-35 (1963). Our review of the legislative history behind Pub. L. 88-110 reveals, however, that, even before this provision was enacted, those enlisting in the Reserves were required to undergo 3-6 months of "active duty for training," depending on the particular enlistment program, and that the purpose of the provision was to provide "flexibility, uniformity, and equity within the Reserve enlistment programs" by, inter alia, requiring "[a] minimum period of active duty for training of not less than 4 months and leaving the maximum to be determined by the training period necessary to qualify the individual in the military specialty for which he enlisted." S. Rep. No. 388, 88th Cong., 1st Sess. (1963), reprinted in 1963 USCCAN 849-50.

Thus, it is apparent that, at the time the appellant performed his active duty service in the Reserves, in 1961, Reserve enlistment programs required a minimum of 3 to 6 months of active duty for training. The appellant admitted that he enlisted under a program requiring only 6 months of active duty service and has, in fact, claimed only 6 months of active duty service. Because this 6-month period must have included, at a minimum, 3 months of active duty for training, the appellant has failed to establish that he served 180 consecutive days of active duty, other than active duty for training. We therefore agree with the administrative judge that the appellant failed to establish his preference eligibility.

There is, however, another issue that we must consider in order to determine whether the appellant is preference eligible. An individual who entered on active duty after October 13, 1982, and who was discharged or released from a period of active duty before completing the shorter of either 24 months of continuous active duty, or the full period for which she was called or ordered to active duty, is generally not eligible by reason of such period of active duty for any benefit based on meeting the definition of preference eligible in 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3). 38 U.S.C. § 5303A(d); Kelley v. Evidence submitted below by the appellant shows that she served on active duty from February 9, 1989, until April 28, 1989, and from November 21, 1990, until January 15, 1991. The agency has asserted that the appellant received a "hardship honorable discharge." However, if the appellant did receive a hardship discharge, she would be exempt from the minimum active service requirement in 38 U.S.C. § 5303A(d). 38 U.S.C. § 5303A(b)(3) (exempting from the minimum active service requirement "a person who is discharged or released from active duty under section 1171 or 1173 of title 10"); 10 U.S.C. § 1173 (authorizing hardship discharges).

Even assuming that the appellant is not exempt from the minimum active service requirement, whether she satisfied that requirement remains an open question. The appellant's unit was called to active duty "for a period of [180] days ([6] months) unless sooner released or later extended." The appellant was released from active duty just 55 days after she was called up. It is not clear from the record before us why the appellant served less than the full 180 days. It is possible that the Army released the appellant's entire unit from active duty earlier than initially planned. If so, the appellant would have served the entire period for which she was called to active duty, and she would therefore be entitled to veterans' preference.

Given the absence of evidence in the record concerning the circumstances under which the appellant was released from active duty and discharged from the Army, we are unable to determine whether the Board has jurisdiction over this appeal. Perez v. MSPB, 85 F.3d 591, 593-94 (Fed. Cir. 1996), elaborated on the connection between preference eligibility and eligibility for a campaign badge:

Section 2108(1)(A) addresses three types of active duty service that will grant an individual veteran status for purposes of title 5. Those three are service "during a war, in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized, or during the period beginning April 28, 1952, and ending July 1, 1955." 5 USC 2108(1)(A) (1994). We note that, in establishing these three types of active duty service, the statute utilizes two different prepositions, "during" and "in," to define how broadly the preference is to be granted.

Thus, we must determine the distinction, if any, drawn in section 2108(1)(A) by the use of the different prepositions "during" and "in." It is true that "in" can mean "during." See Webster's Third New International Dictionary 1139 (1986) (defining "in" as, "inter alia," "during the course of" or "during"). However, to construe "in" to mean "during" here would render superfluous the distinctions created by the terms of the statute ("active duty in the armed forces during a war [or] in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized") (emphasis added). As this court must construe a statute so as to give effect, if possible, to every clause and word...we decline to hold that in section 2108(1)(A) Congress used "in" to mean "during."

In section 2108(1)(A), "during" modifies two periods of time--"during a war" and "during the period beginning April 28, 1952, and ending July 1, 1955." As used in section 2108(1)(A), however, "in" modifies "a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized." The statute thus requires service "in" the campaign, rather than "during" the campaign or "at the time of" the campaign. The limitation imposed by "in" is not just one of time, but also one of involvement or participation.

Therefore, in order to be accorded veteran status under section 2108(1)(A), only one condition is required when the country is at war--service during, or at the time of, the war. When the country is not at war, but the armed forces have conducted a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized, the statute requires more. It is not enough to be on active duty in the armed forces during, or at the time of, the campaign; the individual must have served in the campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized.

Perez added, 85 F.3d at 594 n.3:

Perez contends that he is entitled to preference eligible status because he received the Republic of Vietnam Gallantry Cross. Receipt of a campaign badge from the Republic of Vietnam does not qualify one as a veteran under section 2108(1)(A). The statute requires service in a campaign or expedition. It does not bestow veteran status upon those awarded a badge by a foreign country.
