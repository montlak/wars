6. Government Appellate Review Request; Cross-Appeal

Bennett v. Dept. of Navy, 699 F.2d 1140, 1146 (Fed. Cir. 1983), stated that when an employee appeals from an MSPB decision, and the government seeks through appellate proceedings to lessen the remedy (increase the penalty) she has already received, the proper route is for the government to file a cross-appeal. Without this, the court will not consider the government's arguments in derogation of the Board's final decision. A patent case, Lazare Kaplan Int'l. v. Photoscribe Technologies, Inc., 714 F.3d 1289, 1293 (Fed. Cir. 2013) (Judge Dyk dissenting), stated:

It is well-settled that a party must file a cross-appeal if, although successful in the overall outcome in the district court, the party seeks, on appeal, to lessen the rights of its adversary or to enlarge its own rights. El Paso Natural Gas Co. v. Neztsosie, 526 U.S. 473, 479 (1999) ("Absent a cross-appeal, an appellee... may not 'attack the decree with a view either to enlarging his own rights thereunder or of lessening the rights of his adversary.'") (quoting United States v. Am. Ry. Express Co., 265 U.S. 425, 435 (1924)); see also Bailey v. Dart Container Corp. of Mich., 292 F.3d 1360, 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2002) ("It is only necessary and appropriate to file a cross-appeal when a party seeks to enlarge its own rights under the judgment."). The Supreme Court has long recognized this cross-appeal rule as "inveterate and certain," Morley Constr. Co. v. Md. Cas. Co., 300 U.S. 185, 191 (1937), and the rule's application has been noted since the Court's earliest years, McDonough v. Dannery, 3 U.S. 188, 198 (1796). See Greenlaw v. United States, 554 U.S. 237, 244-45 (2008); El Paso, 526 U.S. at 479. Application of the rule promotes orderly functioning of the appellate courts by providing "notice of issues to be litigated and encouraging repose of those that are not." El Paso, 526 U.S. at 481-82.

Apparently a cross-appeal is not necessary if the government seeks affirmation of a Board decision on a theory other than the one explicitly relied upon by the Board, Thomsen v. Dept. of Treasury, 169 F.3d 1378, 1381 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 1999):

[2] Mr. Thomsen asserts that this finding cannot be challenged by the Agency because it did not file a cross-appeal. This is not correct. The Board's dismissal for untimeliness explicitly relied on its holding that membership in the Ready Reserve was a benefit of employment under USERRA; as such, the Agency is entitled to raise this argument in support of the appealed judgment. Cf. Securities & Exchange Comm'n v. Chenery, 332 U.S. 194, 196 (1947). Accepting such an argument would not change the rights accorded by the judgment below, making a cross-appeal unnecessary. See, e.g., United States v. New York Telephone Co., 434 U.S. 159, 166 n.8 (1987).

The government may not ignore dispositive issues during its appellate briefing. If the government addresses only a jurisdictional issue in an appeal before the Federal Circuit and knowingly declines to brief the merits of the case, the court may conclude that it has no alternative but to treat the merits of the case as conceded, and reverse the result below if the government loses the jurisdictional issue. Howell v. MSPB, 785 F.2d 282, 285 (Fed. Cir. 1986). [Refer in this chapter to waiver of positions based on limited briefing, "Proper Respondent in Court."]
