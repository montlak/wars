4. Shift Swap

A negotiable proposal set criteria for determining whether to grant employees' requests to switch from one shift to another. It only involved the performance by employees of the duties of their positions during a different shift of work. There was no infringement on the right to assign work. The work was already been assigned and the employees were, in effect, interchangeable. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 14 FLRA 243, 252-53 (1984). A negotiable proposal required day-shift vacancies be filled by transfer from the night shift if requested by the employee, provided that the employee was qualified for the position. If more than one employee desired a shift change, seniority would control. The provision did no more than determine the shift for the employee to perform a previously designated assignment. AFGE Local 1409 and Army Adjutant Gen. Publications Ctr., 16 FLRA 352, 354 (1984). Another negotiable procedure permitted the trading of days scheduled off and schedule swaps, providing qualified employees had supervisors' approval. The proposal stipulated that the requests would not be unreasonably denied. The proposals did not establish particular substantive standards for arbitrators to apply to management actions; instead, the proposals required the agency to follow a rational process in applying whatever standards it decided to use. Nat'l Union of Hosp. and Health Care Employees, Dist. 1199 and VAMC, Dayton, 28 FLRA 435, 484-85 (1987).

A negotiable clause stated that an employee may request a permanent shift or shift relief and that the request would be granted if staffing permitted and if the employee had demonstrated competence and reliability in performing duties with minimal supervision. It was understood, through the reference to staffing, that the employer would not be required to change a shift unless there were sufficient numbers, types, and grades of employees with requisite skills available on all shifts. NFFE Local 1798 and VAMC, Martinsburg, W.Va., 27 FLRA 239, 261-62 (1987) (Proposal 9); see New York State Nurses Ass'n and VA Bronx Med. Ctr., 30 FLRA 706, 739 (1987) (Proposal 13) (Section 8.05 (permanent shift assignments): a negotiable procedure because "[t]his section expressly provides management with the flexibility to assign the employee with the particular skill needed on a particular shift"; '[m]anagement is not required to grant the request unless staffing permits and the employee has demonstrated competence and reliability in performing duties with minimal supervision"). But a requirement of shift rotation was nonnegotiable in NFFE Local 1798 and VAMC, Martinsburg, supra (Proposals 5, 6 and 7), because it precluded management from taking into account its patient care needs. The key to the negotiability of the proposals was whether the employees' duties are fungible or specialized, with respect to particular jobs on various shifts. See NTEU, Atlanta and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 32 FLRA 879, 883-84 (1988). Illinois Nurses' Ass'n and VAMC, Hines, 28 FLRA 212, 232-33 (1987) (Proposal 5, § 9), determined to be nonnegotiable a proposal that allowed nurses to request and be assigned to evening or night tours indefinitely, to be returned to day tours for the purpose of closer supervision, training, or development. The proposal interfered with the right to assign work. The Hines proposal added limitations to management's ability to change an assignment once the request was made. The Martinsburg proposal allowed the agency the flexibility to deny the request and assign an employee to particular shifts consistent with patient needs.
