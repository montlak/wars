a. In Camera Inspection

To resolve claims of privilege, a procedure referred to as "in camera" examination can be used. Assume the agency is requested to provide documents that include those compiled by agency counsel. The agency claims privilege--attorney work product. The appellant disputes the privilege claim. The AJ may request that the agency provide the disputed materials directly to the AJ, so that the AJ may make an informed ruling on the privilege claim. The AJ may then direct that the information be produced, that it be produced with redaction, or that it not be produced at all. The appellant does not view the materials submitted in camera, although the AJ's ruling following inspection provides a general description of the type of documents that he reviewed and his rulings on the claims of privilege. What the AJ sees but does not direct disclosure of, the AJ officially forgets. The materials reviewed in camera may not be considered in adjudication of the case. Only if materials are submitted in the ordinary course of the appeal, following the in camera inspection, can they be considered during merits adjudication.

Beamon v. Dept. of Labor, 35 MSPR 15, 19-20 (1987), made passing reference to the in camera inspection process:

[A]n in camera inspection "is a highly appropriate and useful means of dealing with claims of governmental privilege." Kerr v. United States District Court, 426 U.S. 394, 406 (1976).... Where, as in the instant case, law enforcement evidentiary privilege relates to evidence given by informers, evidence which would tend to reveal law enforcement investigative techniques or sources, and intragovernmental memoranda containing policy-making opinions or recommendations, the privilege claimed is qualified rather than absolute.... Such a qualified privilege requires the balancing of conflicting interests and the examination of the files in camera to determine the validity of the asserted privilege.... Additionally, an in camera inspection may afford an acceptable accommodation of the moving party's interest by making it possible for the trier of fact to make unprivileged portions of documents available by excising privileged information....

Durr v. VA, 119 MSPR 195, 205-06 ¶¶ 21-22, 2013 MSPB 12 (2013), said of in camera review of VA records concerning a physician's whistleblowing challenge to delay in his appointment to a permanent position:

The appellant asserts that the administrative judge's in camera review of the PSB minutes was a prohibited ex parte communication because it concerned the merits of the appeal and the appellant did not have an opportunity to participate in the in camera review. The Board's regulations generally prohibit oral or written communications between the adjudicating administrative judge and an interested party to a case before him, when those communications concern the merits of a matter before the Board for adjudication, and when they are made without providing the other party to the appeal with an opportunity to participate. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.101, 1201.102. In camera review of sensitive material sought in discovery may be appropriate before the administrative judge determines the extent to which release of the material to the party requesting it is appropriate. Fickie v. Department of the Army, 86 M.S.P.R. 525, ¶ 14 (2000). An agency's request for such a review, however, and any request for a protective order, concern the merits of the appeal, and they therefore should not be made on an ex parte basis without notice to the appellant. See id. When a communication is made to an administrative judge in violation of the general prohibition on ex parte communications, the communication--or a memorandum stating the substance of it, if the communication was made orally--is to be made part of the record, the administrative judge is to notify the parties of the violation in writing, and she is to give the parties 10 days to file a response concerning the matter. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.103(a), (b). It appears that the administrative judge may not have followed these regulations in this case. Thus, to the extent the administrative judge has not already done so, she shall on remand retroactively apply the Board's regulations relating to ex parte communications.

In camera inspection is available, but its use is discretionary as to claims of privilege, Rooney v. USDA, 26 MSPR 142, 148 (1985):

We note further that, although an in camera review of the documents at issue here could be helpful in balancing these interests, such reviews are not always essential or appropriate. In Kinoy, 67 F.R.D. at 8-9, the court held that, in deciding privilege questions, it had "a range of options, from accepting simply the formal claim where the showing of need is minimal, to in camera inspection of the documents, as a last resort, where the litigant's need is great and the Government's claim otherwise unsubstantiated." This holding is supported by numerous other decisions in which the courts stated or implied that an in camera review of allegedly privileged material was not always necessary. See, e.g., Armstrong Bros. Tool Co. v. United States, 463 F. Supp. 1316, 1320 (Cust.Ct. 1979) (in camera inspection is not to be utilized automatically in every case); SCM Corp. v. United States, 473 F. Supp. 791, 799 (Cust.Ct. 1979) (in camera inspection found unnecessary, and claim of privilege upheld); Branch v. Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 638 F.2d 873, 883 (5th Cir. 1981) (in camera inspection by lower court, to which circuit court remanded case, might not be required in connection with lower court's review of privilege allegations). We find that an in camera review is not essential to our review of this petition.

If an individual or entity responding to a document request (or subpoena) submits material to the AJ in camera rather than to the party requesting the material, notice should be provided to the party requesting the documents of the in camera submission at the time that submission is made. See Fickie v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 525, 532 ¶ 15 (2000) (IG's failure to notify appellant of an in camera submission was harmless because the appellant was eventually provided the materials by the AJ). If the AJ directs material to be produced in camera, the agency may state its objections with the submissions, but "[n]either the Board's regulations, nor the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure...require that the AJ take any further action prior to dissemination," Montgomery v. Dept. of Army, 80 MSPR 435, 439-40 ¶¶ 11-12 (1998):

In any event since the AJ ordered the submissions in camera, the agency could have furnished the documents and made its privilege arguments with specificity at the time of submission. Indeed, with regard to the attorney-client privilege, the appellant has conceded that although the AJ had previously overruled the agency's objection, there is a possibility that some of the notes might be privileged, but that determination should have been made by the AJ. As noted by the appellant, it would have been easy for the agency's counsel to distinguish the asserted privileged statements from notes on what the witnesses said to the deciding official, and to submit such marked notes to the AJ. The agency, however, chose not to comply with the AJ's Orders and now complains of the consequences of such noncompliance.

The agency did make a late partial submission of one item ordered by the AJ. It provided a portion of the IG report to the AJ in camera and now complains that the AJ improperly furnished such material to the appellant. Even its submission to the AJ of this material reflects that the agency seriously misconstrued its obligations to comply with the Board's order. In its cover letter to the submission, the agency stated that "The records will not be reproduced or disseminated without additional written consent of The Inspector General" but then went on to advise that if any of the records were filed before the Board, they should be filed under seal, "with access restricted to the Board personnel and the parties and their legal representatives." The letter further stated that any materials not made a part of the Board record should be returned to the agency. This material, however, was not furnished to the AJ in response to a request. Instead, it was required to be produced pursuant to a Board order. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2). It was not up to the agency to place conditions on its compliance with a Board order. Likewise, the Board and its AJs are not subject to response to his order, the AJ properly examined it in camera and then furnished it to the appellant upon his determination that it was relevant and not privileged. Neither the Board's regulations nor the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, to which we look for guidance, require that the AJ take any further action prior to dissemination. Moreover, even if the AJ had somehow acted improperly in furnishing the submitted material to the appellant, it would not provide an excuse for failure to submit the remainder of the required documents. the agency's internal regulations. In camera inspection differs from an attempt to submit documents to an AJ ex parte. If the appellant declines to share offered evidence with the agency, the AJ properly refuses to consider the material. See Martin v. VA, SF-0752-10-0064-I-2 (NP 4/26/2011) ("We find that the record supports the administrative judge's rulings that barred the appellant from submitting certain Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP) reports because the appellant refused to obey orders to share the reports with the agency. Under these circumstances, the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge abused his broad discretion in regulating discovery or evidence. See Guzman v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 114 M.S.P.R. 566, ¶ 12 (2010); Brasch v. Department of Transportation, 101 M.S.P.R. 145, ¶ 17 (2006).").

If a discovery dispute before the AJ is decided on the basis of in camera inspection of documents claimed to be privileged, and if those documents were denied to the appellant and returned to the agency without being made part of the record, and if the PFR raises the propriety of the discovery order, then the Board will request in camera inspection of the documents to determine the propriety of the procedural ruling by the AJ. Vogel v. DOJ, 8 MSPR 114, 114, 7 MSPB 599 (1981).
