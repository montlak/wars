(i) Historical Application of Cook and Related Decisions

The ability of an agency to take adverse action based on approved leave, including LWOP, is tangled--too much so to straighten out by an evolutionary parsing of decades of decisions directly or indirectly addressing the topic. In this section we cautiously trace the decisions predating the Board's attempt in McCauley to provide a grand unifying theory. To the extent the McCauley analysis is not quite as unifying as the Board might hope, these cases may be of assistance, depending on specific factual contexts. The historical discussion is included because fact patterns may arise that make some of the material relevant. Additionally, the Board swings back and forth in its development of the law. What was once the law, and now overruled, may again creep back into the law as Board membership changes or, as occasionally occurs, OPM takes sufficient interest in an issue to pursue it, first through the reconsideration process with the Board, and then into the Federal Circuit, or failing to then achieve success through adjudication, OPM changes government-wide regulations.

The exception to the rule that adverse action should not be taken on the basis of approved leave was addressed in the somewhat opaque analysis in Ryan v. Dept. of Air Force, 107 MSPR 71, 73-75 ¶¶ 2, 5-7, 2007 MSPB 240 (2007) (modified or overruled by McCauley v. Dept. of Interior, 116 MSPR 484, 489-90 ¶ 10, 2011 MSPB 59 (2011), supra), applicable only to employees on long-term unscheduled leave without pay when (1) An employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond his control so that agency approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial because the employee could not be on the job; (2) the absences continued beyond a reasonable time, and the agency warned the employee that an adverse action could be taken unless the employee became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and (3) the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis and as noted in Curtis v. USPS, supra, when the employee cannot return to duty because of the continuing effects of illness or injury. Under Ryan:

Effective March 21, 2006, the agency removed the appellant, a GS-12 Aerospace Engineer, based on a charge of excessive absence. In the agency's January 20, 2006 notice of proposed removal, it alleged that the appellant performed the essential duties of his position less than 50% of the time from July 24, 2005, through November 18, 2005, and failed to report to work after November 18, 2005, without providing sufficient medical documentation. The agency further noted that, while the appellant's absences may have been a result of circumstances beyond his control, the lack of a foreseeable end to his absence was causing undue hardship on the agency.

...

As a general rule, an agency's approval of leave for unscheduled absences precludes the agency from taking an adverse action on the basis of those absences. Holderness v. Defense Commissary Agency, 75 M.S.P.R. 401, 404 (1997). An exception to this general rule exists where an employee makes excessive use of unscheduled leave without pay (LWOP). Id.; see Cook, 18 M.S.P.R. at 611-12. To sustain an action based on excessive approved absences, an agency must establish that: (1) An employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond his control so that agency approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial because the employee could not be on the job; (2) the absences continued beyond a reasonable time, and the agency warned the employee that an adverse action could be taken unless the employee became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and (3) the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. Cook, 18 M.S.P.R. at 611-12. This exception, however, only applies in a situation where the employee's absence was due to excessive use of unscheduled LWOP. Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404.

Here, the administrative judge found that the agency met the Cook criteria and sustained the charge of excessive absence. The agency, however, presented no evidence that the appellant made excessive use of unscheduled LWOP. In fact, it presented no evidence that the appellant was carried on LWOP at all. Rather, the appellant used sick leave to work a reduced schedule from July 24, 2005, through November 18, 2005, as an accommodation for his back injury, and used sick, annual, and holiday leave, among other leave, to cover the period from November 18, 2005, to January 2, 2006. Thus the exception in Cook does not apply. See Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404. Accordingly, since the agency has not shown that the appellant was on LWOP during the period of charged absences, and Cook only applies in situations where the employee's absences were due to excessive use of LWOP, the administrative judge erred by applying the Cook exception in this case. See Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404.

While there is some conflicting evidence regarding whether the appellant was AWOL from January 3, 2006, through the effective date of his removal, resolution of this issue is unnecessary since the agency did not charge the appellant with AWOL, and an adverse action cannot be upheld on the basis of a charge that the agency could have brought, but did not. See Nazelrod v. Department of Justice, 54 M.S.P.R. 461, 466 (1992), aff'd, 43 F.3d 663 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

Ryan was overruled by McCauley v. Dept. of Interior, 116 MSPR 484, 489-90 ¶ 10, 2011 MSPB 59 (2011) (excessive use of leave charge: "[b]ecause the efficiency of the service may suffer in the absence of an employee's services, regardless of the type of leave used, we hold that whether the leave is sick leave, annual leave, LWOP, or AWOL will not be dispositive to a charge of excessive absences").

The now overruled Ryan and other decisions were noted in Curtis v. USPS, 111 MSPR 626, 631-32 ¶¶ 9-13, 2009 MSPB 134 (2009) (also modified or overruled by McCauley v. Dept. of Interior, 116 MSPR 484, 489-90 ¶ 10, 2011 MSPB 59 (2011), supra):

The agency's charge of unacceptable attendance was based on absences totaling 81 days, of which 1 day was approved sick leave, 3 days were AWOL, and 77 days were approved LWOP. With regard to the single day of sick leave, we note that an agency cannot discipline an individual for his use of approved sick leave. Ryan v. Department of the Air Force, 107 M.S.P.R. 71, ¶ 6 (2007) (citing Holderness v. Defense Commissary Agency, 75 M.S.P.R. 401, 404 (1997)); Gartner v. Department of the Army, 104 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶ 7 (2007). Therefore, we do not take into consideration the appellant's use of 1 day of approved sick leave in deciding this appeal. We next examine whether the appellant's use of 77 days of unscheduled but approved LWOP and 3 days of AWOL constitutes unacceptable attendance/failure to meet the attendance requirements of his position. See Gartner, 104 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶ 10 (combining consideration of AWOL and LWOP absences to determine if charge of excessive absences could be sustained).

An agency's approval of unscheduled leave generally precludes the agency from taking adverse action on the basis of those absences. Combs v. Social Security Administration, 91 M.S.P.R. 148, ¶ 14 (2002) (citing Rabago v. Department of the Army, 25 M.S.P.R. 530 (1985)); Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404. An exception to this general rule exists, however, where an employee makes excessive use of unscheduled LWOP. Ryan, 107 M.S.P.R. 71, ¶ 5; Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404; Cook v. Department of the Army, 18 M.S.P.R. 610, 611-12 (1984). The exception permitting an adverse action for excessive use of unscheduled LWOP is applicable to the appellant's 77 days of LWOP. See Combs, 91 M.S.P.R. 148, ¶ 14 (applying this exception to a charge of inability to maintain regular attendance).[4]

[4] There is also an exception permitting an agency to discipline an employee for failing to follow leave-requesting procedures, even though the agency approves the leave, where the employee was on notice of the agency's requirements. Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404; Fleming v. U.S. Postal Service, 30 M.S.P.R. 302, 308 (1986). In this case, however, the exception is not applicable, because the appellant was not charged with failing to properly request leave.

In order to take an adverse action against an employee based on excessive use of unscheduled LWOP, an agency must show that: (1) The employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond his control so that the agency's approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial, because the employee could not be on the job; (2) the absences continued beyond a reasonable time, and the agency warned the employee that an adverse action could be taken unless the employee became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and (3) the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. Ryan, 107 M.S.P.R. 71, ¶ 5; Holderness, 75 M.S.P.R. at 404; Cook, 18 M.S.P.R. at 611-12. In addition, an adverse action should be taken only under unusual circumstances, such as when the employee cannot return to duty because of the continuing effects of illness or injury. Gartner, 104 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶ 9; Combs, 91 M.S.P.R. 148, ¶ 13.

In this case, the appellant has at all times asserted that he was incapacitated for duty. HCD. He also has produced forms signed by his physician stating that he was unable to work during the entire period for which he had unscheduled absences. Furthermore, although the agency found the appellant's medical documentation inadequate under the ELM, it does not dispute that he had incapacitating medical conditions. We find that the agency may rely upon the appellant's representations that he was unable to work, for which it approved LWOP in lieu of sick leave. Therefore, we find that the record shows that the agency's approval or disapproval of the appellant's leave was immaterial, because he could not work.

The appellant's absences on AWOL and LWOP, together totaling 80 days, clearly continued beyond a reasonable time. The agency warned the appellant in its letter of July 18, 2007, that disciplinary action could result if he failed to return to work. The appellant also had prior discipline for unscheduled absences, i.e., a February 2006 suspension and an April 2007 Letter of Warning. Furthermore, Stark and Kim testified that the appellant's extended absence resulted in untimely mail deliveries and caused changes in other employees' schedules and workloads. HCD. We therefore find that all three of the above criteria for taking an adverse action based on excessive use of unscheduled LWOP have been met. It also appears, based on his testimony and record of continuing absences, that the appellant was unable to return to work because of the effects of his medical conditions. Accordingly, we find that the agency's removal of the appellant for unacceptable attendance, i.e., his excessive use of unscheduled LWOP in conjunction with the AWOL absences, was properly sustained. See Gartner, 104 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶¶ 10-11.

The history of Ryan is traced below.

An initial decision reprinted in McKenzie v. USPS, 1 MSPR 498, 499-50 (1979) (initial decision), first stated the exception to the rule against taking action against employees who use LWOP--an agency may remove an employee who is known to be unable to return to duty because of illness or injury:

The notice of January 19, 1979 proposed the appellant's removal for the following reason:

"There is reason to believe that you are physically unable to perform your assigned duties. Your absences have continued beyond a reasonable period of time as you have been in a leave without pay status in excess of two (2) years due to illness or injury."

When an employee is absent from work in a leave without pay status, his absence is approved. When the Postal Service exercised its authority to carry the appellant in an approved leave status, the employee was released from his obligation to report for duty and his absence did not constitute a breach of the employer-employee relationship. Accordingly, an adverse action based upon absence on approved leave in any amount fails to state a cause of action and the disciplinary action based thereon is not for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service.

There is, however, one exception to the above policy which provides that an agency may rely upon approved leave without pay as a basis for removal when:

(1) The employee was prevented from reporting for duty by compelling reasons beyond his control,

(2) The absences continued beyond a reasonable period of time, and,

(3) The employee was warned that an adverse action might be initiated unless he became available for duty on a regular, full-time basis.

This exception is designed to allow an agency to remove an employee who is known to be unable to return to duty because of illness or injury, as is the case in the instant appeal, and is not expected to be used as a remedy for common attendance problems. My examination of the record in the instant case discloses that the removal action on appeal herein falls within the exception. The record reflects that the appellant was unable to report for duty due to circumstances relating to his illness or injury, that the absences continued beyond a reasonable period of time, more than two years, and that the appellant was warned by letter dated December 12, 1978, that adverse action would be initiated against him unless he became available for duty on a full-time basis. Accordingly, I find that the appellant's absences in a leave without pay status falls within the exception above-cited and that the Postal Service has set forth a proper cause of action for the appellant's removal in the instant case.

With respect to the merits of the agency's reason, the record is replete with evidence supportive of the agency's reasons that the appellant was in fact continually absent for a period exceeding two years prior to the effective date of his removal. The appellant has acknowledged his absence citing his medical problems as the basis therefor. Accordingly, I find that the agency's reason for the appellant's removal is supported by a preponderance of the evidence and that reason is, therefore, sustained.

Although McKenzie did not cite the basis for its rationale, the Board adopted OPM guidance in the (now defunct) FPM Ch. 752, Subch. 3, § 3-2b(4)(c) providing the exception to the general rule that adverse action could not be based on an employee's use of approved leave. The FPM section stated:

(c) The one exception to the general rule which most appellate decisions have upheld, is that adverse action may be taken based on a record of excessive unscheduled leave without pay (LWOP) when three criteria are met:

1. The record shows the employee absent for compelling reasons beyond his control so that the agency approval was immaterial inasmuch as the employee could not be on the job;

2. The absences continued beyond a reasonable time and the employee was warned that adverse action might be started unless the employee became available on a regular full-time or part-time basis; and

3. The agency shows that it was important for the position to be filled by an employee available for regular duty.

This exception would be applicable only under certain unusual circumstances, e.g., the inability of an employee to return to duty or to work on a regular basis because of the continuing effects of illness or injury (on or off-the-job). Other circumstances in individual cases may meet these criteria, but OPM believes they are uncommon. For example, repeated absences because of failure to obtain adequate transportation to work or to tend to personal affairs would presumably be situations under the control of the employee, and thus reasons for denial of leave and charges of AWOL. (Agencies with employees who are on leave while receiving employee's compensation will wish to be aware of the positions of chapter 353.)

With regard to the matter of basing an adverse action on approved leave, subsequent to the issuance of the initial decision, the Board addressed this issue in Webb v. United States Postal Service, 9 MSPB 749 (1982), where we held:

The Board concludes that it would be inequitable to allow an agency to take an adverse action against an employee based on his or her utilization of approved leave. Such would also be precluded by the laws (5 U.S.C. Ch. 63) and regulations (5 C.F.R. Part 630) that entitle an employee to use annual and sick leave within prescribed circumstances and limitations. Adverse actions based on periods of approved leave would have such a negative influence on the exercise of these rights so as to deny them. Additionally, the employer-employee relationship created at the time one commences his or her employment in the federal service allows for the adverse effects of approved leave usage to the extent that is properly exercised and is within the limits of the annual and sick leave periods specified. Therefore, an adverse action taken by an agency against an employee based on periods of approved leave is not "for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service." 5 U.S.C. § 7513(a).

Webb, supra, at 753.

Accordingly, the Board finds that the presiding official improperly determined that approved leave could be used as a specification in support of the removal action. Thus, the charge against appellant is sustained only to the extent of the four specifications of AWOL, involving approximately 13 1/2 hours.[2]

Although periods of approved leave may not form the basis for an adverse action, nor may a prior disciplinary record based on approved leave be considered as warranting an enhanced penalty, an agency, in an otherwise appropriate case, may use an employee's record of approved leave in order to show that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his or her unreliable attendance. Cade v. U.S. Postal Service, 8 MSPB 362, 364, 8 M.S.P.R. 717, 721 (1981). An agency may properly consider an "employee's past work record, including length of service, performance on the job, ability to get along with fellow workers, and dependability, as a factor in determining the appropriateness of a penalty." Douglas, supra.

Dunnock referenced Cade, involving the removal of a Postal Service distribution clerk. Under Cade v. USPS, 8 MSPR 717, 720 (1981), prior disciplinary actions for unsatisfactory attendance cannot be considered as aggravating factors under a Douglas penalty analysis when those prior actions involved approved leave:

Although we have made it clear that a prior disciplinary record based on approved leave may not be treated as a disciplinary record warranting an enhanced penalty, we also point out that this principle need not preclude an agency, in an otherwise appropriate case, from showing that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his unreliable attendance. This evidence would be pertinent to an "employee's past work record, including length of service, performance on the job, ability to get along with fellow workers, and dependability...." which is a factor relevant to the appropriateness of a penalty. Id. at 332. The underlying facts relating to such a factor, of course, must be proven by the agency. Id. at 324-25. An agency's reliance upon the employee's "past work record" is thus very different from its reliance on an employee's "past disciplinary record" as a factor relevant to the penalty, because the latter normally requires proof only of the disciplinary action and not of the underlying facts. In this case the agency did not present evidence, other than the disciplinary records themselves, tending to show that appellant was an unreliable employee due to excessive absenteeism.

U.S. Postal Service, 1 MSPB 480, 481 (1980), the Board noted with regard to an employee who had been in a leave without pay status in excess of two years: "Employee absence for which no foreseeable end is in sight is a burden which no employer can efficiently endure."

The testimony shows that during appellant's approximate two-year absence from her job, her supervisor had to recall and furlough seasonal employees and see that they were trained. In Webb v. United States Postal Service, 9 MSPB 749, 753 (1982), we concluded that it would be unsuitable for an agency to base an adverse action on an employee's utilization of approved leave. We noted that the statutes and regulations controlling an employee's use of leave prescribe limitations on leave approval and use. Once an employee has obtained approval for leave, it would generally be inequitable to allow an agency to penalize an employee for absence from the work place, and it would not promote the efficiency of the service to do so. See also Dunnock v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 MSPB 66, at 67-68, 14 M.S.P.R. 382 at 384-85 (1983).

However, we have also noted that Federal Personnel Manual (PPM) Chapter 752, Subchapter 2, paragraph 3-2b (4) (c), provides an exception to the general rule that an adverse action cannot be based on an employee's use of approved leave. See Cook v. Department of the Army, 18 M.S.P.R. 610 (1984. The exception applies in situations where an employee makes excessive use of unscheduled leave without pay, and where the following three criteria are also met:

(1) The record showed that the employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond his or her control so that agency approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial because the employee could not be on the job;

(2) The absence or absences continued beyond a reasonable time and the employee was warned that adverse action might be initiated unless the employee became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and

(3) The agency showed that the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis.

Id. at 611-612. We also noted that the FPM provision should only be applied in unusual cases. Id. at 612.

In the instant case appellant had been frequently absent from the workplace over a two year period. However, the record indicates that the agency granted appellant approved leave for all of the absences. The agency has not shown that appellant was granted excessive unscheduled leave without pay to cover her absence. The record establishes that the agency predicated its adverse action on the basis of approved leave, contrary to the directive outlined in Webb, and did not have a basis to claim the benefit of the FPM exception. See Cook, supra.

The presiding official, while noting the existence of the Webb precedent, relied on McKenzie v. U.S. Postal Service, 1 MSPB 480 (1980), in concluding that prolonged absence from the workplace can be a valid cause for removal. In McKenzie, an employee was removed from his position after being in leave without pay status in excess of two years. The agency contacted the employee, and ordered him to report for duty within 14 days, or to contact the agency in writing if he was unable to report. The employee failed to report or otherwise reply to the agency directive. The agency sent a second request to the employee, informing the employee that if he failed to reply or report within 14 days, he would be removed. When the employee failed to respond to this second letter, the agency removed him. McKenzie, supra, at 480-81.

In the instant case, appellant was warned that her attendance had to improve or she would face adverse action. However, unlike the situation in McKenzie, appellant did not disregard a specific order to report for duty. The employee in McKenzie, by failing to report for work or to obtain agency authorization excusing his absence, was effectively absent without leave (AWOL). Although the agency also provided a warning to appellant in the instant case, the agency has not shown that it directed appellant to report for duty and that appellant failed to report without obtaining leave to excuse her absence. Thus, the instant case can be distinguished from McKenzie.

In its reply to the appellant's petition for review the agency notes that it never carried appellant as being absent without leave. Having admitted such, the agency is precluded from basing as adverse action on appellant's use of approved leave.[4] Komora v. Veterans Administration, 10 MSPB 719, 720 (1982).

Gaskins v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 331, 334-35 (1988), sustained removal resulting from lengthy LWOP and the medical inability of the employee to return to work (no AWOL was involved):

In Cook v. Department of the Army, 18 M.S.P.R. 610 (1984), the Board recognized that FPM Chapter 752, Subchapter 3, paragraph 3-2b(4)(c) provides an exception to the general rule that an adverse action cannot be based upon an employee's use of approved leave. To invoke this exception, an agency must establish that three criteria are met: (1) The employee must be absent for compelling reasons beyond his or her control so that agency approval or disapproval is immaterial because the employee can not be on the job; (2) the absences must continue beyond a reasonable time and the employee must be warned that an adverse action may be taken unless the employee becomes available for duty on a regular, full-time, or part-time basis; and (3) the agency must establish that the position needs to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. The Board also stated, however, that the FPM exception only applies to unusual circumstances, such as when the employee is unable to return to duty because of the continuing effects of illness or injury. Cook at 611-12.

We agree with the administrative judge's conclusion that the criteria for taking an action on the basis of approved leave are satisfied in the present case. The record establishes that appellant's medical condition disabled her from performing her duties during the approximately eight months at issue. Thus, appellant was absent for compelling reasons beyond her control and agency approval or disapproval of her leave request was immaterial because appellant could not be on the job. See Taylor v. Department of Health and Human Services, 32 M.S.P.R. 342, 344 (1987).

We also find that appellant's absence continued beyond a reasonable time and appellant was warned that an adverse action could be taken unless she became available for duty. Appellant was continuously absent for a period of approximately eight months. The agency specifically directed appellant to return to work, and warned her that her failure to report could result in her removal. Thus, the agency satisfied the second criteria for invoking the FPM exception. See Rabago v. Department of the Army. 25 M.S.P.R. 530, 533-34 (1985).

Finally, as found by the administrative judge on the basis of the hearing testimony, the agency established that it needed to fill appellant's position on a regular basis.

Holderness v. DCA, 75 MSPR 401, 404-05 (1997) (modified or overruled by McCauley v. Dept. of Interior, 116 MSPR 484, 489-90 ¶ 10, 2011 MSPB 59 (2011), supra), seemed to return the analysis to situations only involving grants of LWOP:

...As a general rule, an agency's approval of leave for unscheduled absences precludes the agency from taking adverse action on the basis of those absences. Williams v. Department of the Army, 24 M.S.P.R. 537, 541 (1984). There are two recognized exceptions to this rule, but neither applies here.

The first exception is that an employee may be disciplined for failing to follow leave-requesting procedures, even though the agency eventually approves the leave request, so long as the employee is on notice of the agency's requirements. See Wilkinson v. Department of the Air Force, 68 M.S.P.R. 4, 7 (1995). That exception is not applicable in the instant case because the agency did not charge the appellant with failing to properly request leave. See Gottlieb v. Veterans Administration, 39 M.S.P.R. 606, 609 (1989) (the Board is required to review the agency's decision on an adverse action solely on the grounds invoked by the agency; the Board may not substitute what it considers to be a more adequate or proper basis).

The second exception is where an employee makes excessive use of unscheduled leave without pay (LWOP); an adverse action based on that excessive use may be sustained if certain criteria are met. Cook v. Department of the Army, 18 M.S.P.R. 610, 611-12 (1984). The administrative judge found that the agency met the criteria and sustained the charge of excessive absences. The record evidence, however, does not show that the appellant made excessive use of unscheduled LWOP.

In its letter of proposed removal, the agency stated that the appellant used over 300 hours of sick leave and LWOP between June 21, 1995, and October 3, 1995. During the hearing, however, the proposing official testified that from June 21, 1995, to October 3, 1995, the appellant was on sick leave. Moreover, the "Individual Daily Time and Attendance Records" show that the appellant was on sick leave from June 21, 1995, through October 3, 1995. Accordingly, since the agency has not shown that the appellant was on LWOP during the period of charged absences, and the Cook exception only applies in situations where the employee's absence was due to excessive use of unscheduled LWOP, the administrative judge erred by finding that the Cook exception applied in this matter. See Rabago v. Department of the Army, 25 M.S.P.R. 530, 533 (1985). Under the circumstances of this case, the agency's charge of excessive absences cannot be sustained. To the extent that the agency implies in its petition for review that the appellant's removal should be upheld on the ground that she was unable to perform her duties on the date that the agency took this adverse action, we disagree. The agency did not charge the appellant with inability to perform, and an adverse action cannot be upheld on the basis of a charge that the agency could have brought, but did not. See Nazelrod v. Department of Justice, 54 M.S.P.R. 461, 466 (1992), aff'd, 43 F.3d 663 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

Allen v. Dept. of Army, 76 MSPR 564, 570-71 (1997), similar to Gaskins, permitted adverse action against the employee for prolonged sick leave (that the Board implied should have been granted) rather than for LWOP or AWOL status:

The administrative judge decided not to sustain the charge of excessive absenteeism because "the agency improperly denied the appellant's request for sick leave. The agency argues that the administrative judge erred in not sustaining this charge, and we agree. As noted above, an agency can bring an action against an employee for excessive absence even if the absence is excused on grounds of poor health. See Schultz, 810 F.2d at 1137 n.*; Wilkinson, 68 M.S.P.R. at 6. Prolonged absence with no foreseeable end can provide just cause for removal because it constitutes a burden which no employer can efficiently endure. See Rhodes v. Department of the Interior, 21 M.S.P.R. 193, 195-97 (1984); aff'd in part, vacated in part, 770 F.2d 182 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table), cert. denied, 474 U.S. 1103 (1986); McKenzie v. U.S. Postal Service, 1 M.S.P.R. 496, 497 (1980). To take such an action, however, an agency must establish that: (1) The employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond his or her control so that agency approval or disapproval was immaterial because the employee could not be on the job; (2) the absences continued beyond a reasonable time, and the employee was warned that an adverse action could be taken unless the employee became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and (3) the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. Such an action should also be taken only under unusual circumstances, i.e., such as when the employee is unable to return to duty because of the continuing effects of illness or injury. See Gaskins v. Department of the Air Force, 36 M.S.P.R. 331, 334 (1988); Cook v. Department of the Army, 18 M.S.P.R. 610, 611-12 (1984).

In this case, the parties stipulated that the appellant was absent for reasons beyond his control, and the discussion above demonstrates that his absence was compelled because of psychological illness that incapacitated him for duty. As noted above, moreover, the appellant had been continuously absent for almost seven months when the agency proposed his removal, and it had warned him that his prolonged absence could lead to disciplinary action unless he returned to duty as soon as possible. The agency has also presented documentary evidence showing that the appellant's position needed to be filled and that his absence created a significant staffing problem for the Oakland Arts and Crafts Center. The parties stipulated that the appellant was absent from January to August of 1994 based on Dr. Volk's advice that he could not return to work unless he was "insulated" from contact with Mr. Ortega; they also stipulated that the appellant "would have been unable to work after August 17, 1994, because Dr. Volk advised him that he should not work unless he was insulated from contacts with Victor Ortega."

The criteria for taking an action against the appellant based on excessive absence thus appear to have been met here because: (1) He was absent for compelling reasons beyond his control and could not be on the job so that agency approval or disapproval of his leave requests was immaterial; (2) he was absent for almost seven months, and the agency warned him that failure to report for duty could result in disciplinary action; and (3) the agency needed an employee to fill the appellant's position. Based on the parties' own stipulation, moreover, there did not appear to be any foreseeable end to his absence. Under those circumstances, the agency properly took action against the appellant based on his excessive absence. See Gaskins, 36 M.S.P.R. at 334-35; Rhodes, 21 M.S.P.R. at 195-97 (the agency properly removed an employee who could not return to her position at a foreseeable time because the work environment exacerbated her emotional problems, and where the agency needed the employee to serve in her position on a regular basis). The administrative judge thus erred by not sustaining this charge.

Cole v. VA, 77 MSPR 434, 440-41 (1998), sustained removal involving a medical condition leading to long-term placement in nonpay status. AWOL was disavowed as a necessary condition to the adverse action:

The undisputed record evidence shows that the appellant stopped working on October 4, 1993, when she took several weeks of emergency annual leave to care for her son. She subsequently requested continued leave, citing her rheumatoid arthritis and depression. By April 12, 1994, she exhausted her sick and annual leave balances, as well as donated leave made available through the Voluntary Leave Donor Program, and was placed on non-paid status until she was removed.

An agency may take an adverse action based on excessive use of leave without pay (LWOP), where (1) the record shows that the employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond her control so that the agency's approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial because the employee could not be on the job; (2) the absences continued beyond a reasonable time, and the employee was warned that adverse action might be initiated unless the employee became 6 available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and (3) the agency shows that the position must be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. Holderness v. Defense Commissary Agency, 75 M.S.P.R. 401, 404 (1997).

First, the appellant's absences here were due to her severe, incapacitating rheumatoid arthritis, a compelling reason beyond her control, so that the agency's approval or disapproval of leave was immaterial. Second, her total continuous absences lasted more than two years, of which her continuous unpaid absences lasted more than nineteen months and, thus, continued beyond a reasonable period of time. In addition, the agency specifically warned the appellant several times that an adverse action up to and including removal might be initiated unless she became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis. And third, the record shows that the agency considered the appellant's Medical Technician position on the evening shift to be a "critical" position, solicited volunteers for the position during her absence, and assigned other staff members to perform her duties during her absence. The agency deciding official stated that the appellant's absences resulted in "thousands of dollars in overtime and unscheduled hours." The record thus shows that the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, fulltime or part-time basis. We therefore find that the criteria for sustaining a charge of excessive unpaid absences are satisfied here.[4]

It is undisputed that all of the appellant's absences during the period in question were approved by the agency. As a general rule, an agency's approval of leave precludes the agency from taking an adverse action based on those absences. Holderness v. Defense Commissary Agency, 75 MSPR 401, 404 (1997). Notwithstanding this general rule, an agency may take an adverse action based on approved absences if the following criteria set forth in Cook, 18 MSPR at 611-12, are satisfied:

(1) The record showed that the employee was absent for compelling reasons beyond his or her control, so that agency approval or disapproval [of leave] was immaterial because the employee could not be on the job;

(2) The absence or absences continued beyond a reasonable time and the employee was warned that adverse action might be initiated unless the employee became available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis; and

(3) The agency showed that the position needed to be filled by an employee available for duty on a regular, full-time or part-time basis.

Id. All three criteria must be satisfied before an agency may take an adverse action based on approved leave. See id.; see also Taylor v. Department of Health & Human Services, 32 MSPR 342, 344 (1987). The AJ found that the agency established neither the first nor the second criterion.

Regarding the first criterion--whether the appellant was absent for compelling reasons beyond her control--the agency must establish that the appellant was unable to report for duty because of "unusual circumstances, i.e.,...because of the continuing effects of illness or injury" and not because of "personal affairs, as these situations would presumably be under the control of the employee and thus the agency could have legitimate reasons for denial of leave." Cook, 18 MSPR at 612. The AJ found that the appellant's absences were not for compelling reasons since the agency acknowledged in its prehearing submission that the absences were due to the appellant's "personal problems."

In challenging this finding on review, the agency contends that, had it been given the opportunity, it would have presented evidence that the appellant requested leave for medical reasons, based on medical documentation, and also would have solicited hearing testimony from the appellant showing that she believed she was incapable of working on the days she was absent.

Regarding the agency's contention that it would have solicited hearing testimony from the appellant, an agency has no statutory right to a hearing. Hulett v. Department of the Navy, 27 MSPR 510, 512-13 (1985). Moreover, it is the agency's burden to establish the charge, and the agency may not require the appellant to submit evidence to prove its case. See Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 71 (1997) (the appellant was not required to submit medical evidence to assist the agency in proving its charge against her). The agency has not alleged or shown why it could not have submitted pertinent documentary evidence to establish its charge.

The agency was not prevented below from submitting evidence showing that the appellant's absences were for compelling medical reasons. Nor does the agency submit any such evidence on review. The agency has not submitted any documentation of the appellant's requests for leave, and in fact admitted during discovery that it had no written documentation of her leave requests. Although the agency contends on review that the appellant stated in a deposition that her absences were due to "severe depression," the deposition has not been submitted for the record. In a "Memorandum for Record" submitted below, the appellant's supervisor, George Story, stated that the appellant's "absences were due to illness and personal emergencies." However, the record contains no specifics regarding the particular reasons for the appellant's intermittent absences. The agency contended in its prehearing submission that Story would testify "about the Appellant's relationship with her abusive boyfriend (Mr. Keith Shields) and how this prevented her from coming to work" and "that the Appellant never told him that she was unable to report for work because of depression...." Under these circumstances, we find that the agency failed to establish error in the AJ's decision not to hold a hearing, or in the AJ's finding that the appellant's absences were not shown to have been for compelling medical reasons.

The agency disputes the AJ's statement that the agency could have properly denied many of the appellant's requests for leave. The agency contends that it could not have denied leave that was requested for medical reasons if there was a foreseeable end to the absences, citing Bologna v. Department of Defense, 73 MSPR 110, 114, aff'd, 135 F.3d 774 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table), and Joyner v. Department of the Navy, 57 MSPR 154, 159 (1993) (where an employee who is incapacitated for duty has exhausted all of her leave, an agency may properly deny her request for leave without pay where there is no foreseeable end in sight to her absence and where that absence is a burden on the agency). As noted above, however, the agency submitted scant evidence on the circumstances surrounding the appellant's absences, and thus failed to show that it could not have denied leave under the circumstances.

Cook expressly presumes that absences due to personal affairs are within the control of the employee, so that the agency would have legitimate reasons for denying leave for such absences if excessive. 18 MSPR at 612. Thus, there is a presumption that absences due to personal affairs are not covered by the Cook exception. The record here simply does not warrant any reexamination or alteration of Cook's presumption concerning the appellant's control over her personal affairs because the agency utterly failed to document the nature of the appellant's absences. Because the agency has not shown that the appellant's absences were for compelling reasons beyond her control so as to make the agency's grant of LWOP superfluous, we concur in the AJ's finding that the agency failed to establish the first criterion under Cook.

Regarding the second criterion--whether the appellant's absences continued beyond a reasonable time--the AJ found that the absences did not continue beyond a reasonable time because they were intermittent in nature. Although the agency challenges on review the AJ's finding, we find it unnecessary to resolve this issue, in view of our dispositive finding that the agency failed to establish the first Cook criterion.

Here, the agency's charge was "excessive absences."
