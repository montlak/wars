1. Test Defined; Scope of Review

The Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.4(p) (2022), defines substantial evidence as "the degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, might accept as adequate to support a conclusion, even though other reasonable persons might disagree. This is a lower standard of proof than preponderance of the evidence." [formerly 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1) (2014)] The definition of what a reasonable person might do, with other reasonable folk disagreeing, is impossible to further define, given the rather broad range of thinking and actions engaged in by ostensibly reasonable people. The Board attempted to explain the test in a few of its decisions. If its efforts seem to confuse more than edify, take comfort: appellate courts have as much trouble clearly defining and applying the substantial evidence test. The constitutionality of the substantial evidence test was questioned but not answered in Bolton v. SSA, DC-0432-12-0121-I-1 (NP 1/24/2014) ("While the Board has the authority to adjudicate a constitutional challenge to an agency's application of a statute, it is without authority to determine the constitutionality of federal statutes.").

Wells v. Harris, 1 MSPR 208, 248, 1 MSPB 199 (1979), stated that Congress created the substantial evidence test for unacceptable performance actions to surmount the difficulty faced by agencies in proving unacceptable performance. The substantial evidence standard requires evidence only of such quality and weight that reasonable and fair-minded persons exercising impartial judgment might reach different conclusions, while the preponderance standard requires evidence that a reasonable person would accept as sufficient to find a contested fact more probably true than untrue. Borninkhof v. DOJ, 5 MSPR 77, 87, 5 MSPB 150 (1981). A better definition was offered in Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPR 505, 1 MSPB 489 (1980), involving the denial of a within-grade increase. The Board concluded that a substantial evidence, rather than a preponderance of evidence, standard was applicable. Recognizing that the substantial evidence test was one normally applied as a means of judicial review rather than initial adjudication following a hearing, the Board felt compelled to give effect to the statutory requirement that performance and WIGI appeals be decided on the basis of substantial evidence. The Board explained in Parker, 1 MSPR at 529, that its regulation, 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1) (1980), incorporated the substantial evidence test defined in Universal Camera Corp. v. NLRB, 340 U.S. 474, 477 (1951):

...more than a mere scintilla. It means such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion.'... Accordingly, it...'must be enough to justify, if the trial were to a jury, a refusal to direct a verdict when the conclusion sought to be drawn from it is one of fact for the jury.

Wells, supra, considered that the conferees to the Reform Act expressed some reservations about the use of the test defined by Universal Camera. Senator Chiles stated during conference discussion of the substantial evidence test that it would involve "[a]ny evidence based on the reasonable man test, not any evidence at all, not a scintilla of evidence." Parker, 1 MSPR at 528 n.41, 1 MSPB at 507. The Board noted with approval the principle in Universal Camera that requires a weighing of the record as a whole when substantial evidence is measured, meaning that the substantiality of evidence must take into account whatever in the record fairly detracts from its weight. The Board summed up its views on substantial evidence in Parker, 1 MSPR at 530-31, 1 MSPB at 509:

Unlike the preponderance standard, which requires evidence that a reasonable person would accept as sufficient to find a contested fact more probably true than untrue, the substantial evidence standard requires only evidence of such quality and weight that reasonable and fair-minded persons in exercising impartial judgment might reach different conclusions. This standard precludes the Board's presiding official from substituting his or her own judgment for that of the agency. It obliges the presiding official to determine only whether, in light of all the relevant and credible evidence before the Board, a reasonable person could agree with the agency's decision (even though other reasonable persons including the presiding official might disagree with that decision).

A court rejected the "scintilla" approach toward substantial evidence: an AJ errs in a performance case by relying on a few examples among many specific defects noticed to support removal. All the evidence must be considered. A case involving 36 examples of unacceptable performance was remanded for reconsideration because the AJ accepted evidence on six examples as sufficient to determine whether the record contained substantial evidence supporting the agency's charges. Saposnekoo v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3234). Vera v. Dept. of Army, DA-0432-16-0517-I-1, n.3 (NP 10/13/2022), offered a synopsis of the substantial evidence standard:

Substantial evidence is the "degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, might accept as adequate to support a conclusion, even though other reasonable persons might disagree." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(p). Our reviewing court has described substantial evidence as "more than a mere scintilla. It means such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion." Leatherbury v. Department of the Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (quoting Bradley v. Veterans Administration, 900 F.2d 233, 234 (Fed. Cir. 1990)); see Adamsen v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 331, ¶ 7 (2011). Additionally, it "must do more than create a suspicion of the fact to be established...." Bradley, 900 F.2d at 234 (quoting National Labor Relations Board v. Columbian Enameling & Stamping Co., 306 U.S. 292, 300 (1939)); see Adamsen, 116 M.S.P.R. 331, ¶ 7.

The substantial evidence test provides considerable deference to agency decisions. If an adjudicator decides that the agency decision could be reasonable, the agency wins. If the agency's testimony concerning disputed facts and the appellant's testimony on the same facts are equally plausible, under the substantial evidence test the agency carries the day; the agency is not required to present evidence more persuasive than that presented by the appellant. Shuman v. Dept. of Treasury, 23 MSPR 620, 624 (1984); see Punch v. NASA, DA-0432-13-4323-I-2 (NP 2/9/2016) ("The agency need not present more persuasive evidence than that presented by the appellant in order to meet its burden. Shuman v. Department of the Treasury, 23 M.S.P.R. 620, 624 (1984)."). Once the evidentiary deference is understood, the significance of the unacceptable performance action becomes clear. If there is a conflict between agency testimony and that offered by the appellant, the agency testimony will likely be accepted if it is plausible. If an agency offers its evidence, an appellant provides his or her evidence, and the AJ weighs both sides, no harm is done to the appellant. The substantial evidence test, being a standard of proof lower than a preponderance of the evidence, is necessarily met by preponderant evidence. Ooms v. Dept. of Treasury, 15 MSPR 389, 391 n.2 (1983); see Alley v. VA, 690 F.2d 153, 154 (8th Cir. 1982) ("Alley argues that the correct standard is the substantial evidence test. This argument is without merit. Even if the Board misapplied the burden of proof by requiring the VA Medical Center to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that Alley was guilty of unacceptable job performance, this mistake was harmless and inured to the benefit of Alley, because the standard applied was more burdensome than the standard Alley suggests."); cf. France v. Dept. of Energy, SF-0432-12-0299-I-1 (NP 7/18/2013) (discounting evidence from coworkers concerning the appellant's performance "[u]nder the substantial evidence standard, which we noted above, the agency is not required to provide evidence regarding the appellant's performance that is more persuasive than that presented by the appellant. Fernand v. Department of the Treasury, 100 M.S.P.R. 259, ¶ 8 (2005)."). As to a component of a standard measuring quality of performance, the AJ should not have substituted her judgment as to the adequacy of performance in place of the agency's evaluation under the substantial evidence test. Rocheleau v. SEC, 29 MSPR 193, 197 (1985).

In a Chapter 43 case an AJ is not excused from preparing a decision that meets Board standards of adjudication. Detailed findings of fact must be prepared. As to credibility issues, the AJ must provide an adequate explanation for rejecting the testimony of one witness, the appellant, and accepting the testimony of another, the supervisor. Cordioli v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 374, 377 n.4 (1988) (determining to be inadequate the conclusion that the agency witnesses "gave consistent and responsive testimony, which stood up well under cross examination"). The AJ's decision will be considered adequate if, on the merits, it reviews the factual basis underlying each charge, and discusses how the appellant's performance failed to meet the required standards. Darcy v. DOJ, 24 MSPR 449, 451 (1984). See Jensen v. USDA, 61 MSPR 469, 472 (1994) (AJ's conclusions concerning incidents of substandard performance were not supported by testimony recorded in hearing tapes); Cranwill v. VA, 52 MSPR 610, 617 (1992) (remanding because the initial decision did not recount the particulars of the supervisor's testimony, or of the appellant's rebuttal evidence, as to eight of the 11 loan files counted as errors); Migliori v. Dept. of Army, 47 MSPR 448, 452 (1991) (remanding for credibility the findings required by Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453, 458 (1987), because of conflicting testimony of witnesses concerning the adequacy of appellant's performance in a WIGI denial appeal); Walls v. DCAA, 10 MSPR 441, 442 (1982) (when the agency produced neither a draft work product nor testimony identifying the appellant's alleged errors, the AJ properly determined that the agency failed to sustain the charge).

As to the possibility of overlap of performance assessed under more than one standard, Towne v. Dept. of Air Force, 120 MSPR 239, 254 n.12, 2013 MSPB 81 (2013), stated:

While not raised by the appellant, we recognize that there appears to be some overlap between the appellant's performance that formed the basis for finding her unacceptable under this critical element and the performance that formed the basis for finding her unacceptable under other critical elements. We need not decide whether the same instances of poor performance can be used to find an employee unacceptable under more than one critical element because the finding of unacceptable performance under each critical element is fully and independently supported by the record in this case. Furthermore, failure to demonstrate acceptable performance under a single critical element will support a removal under chapter 43. Stein-Verbit v. Department of Commerce, 72 M.S.P.R. 332, 339-40 (1996).
