3. Jurisdiction Through Agency Regulation

5 USC 1204(a) gives the Board the authority to hear and adjudicate matters within the Board's jurisdiction under Title 5 (regular civil service), Chapter 43 of Title 38 USC (certain employees of the Department of Veterans Affairs), "or any other law, rule, or regulation." Board jurisdiction through regulation appears to be limited to regulations of the Office of Personnel Management, one example being the right of probationers to appeal their terminations on limited grounds. Another example is Board jurisdiction to consider employment practice challenges under 5 CFR 300.104 (2020). [Refer later in this chapter to "Employment Practice Challenges."] Although the Reform Act gives the Board authority to prescribe regulations for the performance of its functions, 5 USC 1204(h), that authority cannot confer upon the Board the power to extend appellate coverage to employees who have not been provided a right of appeal by law, rule, or regulations of OPM implementing a law. Acosta v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 96, 97 (1980). The Board appears to be disinclined to permit agencies other than OPM to confer jurisdiction on the Board through their regulations, and guidance (as opposed to regulations) of OPM will not establish Board jurisdiction, Monser v. Dept. of Army, 67 MSPR 477, 481 (1995):

[A]gency regulations alone are not a source of Board jurisdiction. See Siegert v. Dept. of the Army, 38 MSPR 684, 691 (1988) (an agency cannot by its own action confer or take away Board jurisdiction). Although the Board has held that the Office of Personnel Management may create a right of appeal to the Board by regulation, pursuant to its statutory authority to execute, administer, and enforce civil service rules and regulations, see Gaxiola v. Dept. of the Air Force, 6 MSPB 432, 6 MSPR 515, 518-19 (1981), we agree with the administrative judge that 10 USC 1590 [repealed in 1996 by P.L. 104-201, but previously allowing the Secretary of Defense to establish positions for civilian intelligence personnel of the military departments without regard to the provisions of other laws] does not grant the Secretary of Defense analogous authority.

Further, the Board is not bound by the guidance in the Federal Personnel Manual. See Silvestri v. Dept. of the Army, 60 MSPR 594, 601 n.11 (1993). Thus, the provisions of the Federal Personnel Manual that were in effect when this appeal arose are not a source of Board jurisdiction over this appeal.

Cf. Sage v. Dept. of Army, 108 MSPR 398, 403 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 68 (2008) ("An agency cannot through its own action confer or take away Board jurisdiction. Siegert v. Department of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 684, 691 (1988).").

If the Board's jurisdiction is conferred by an OPM regulation, the Board defers to OPM's interpretation of the intended scope of the regulatory grant. Hellman v. OPM, 10 MSPR 639, 643 (1982). OPM regulatory appeal rights may not conflict with Reform Act provisions. See Shane v. Dept. of Navy, 1 MSPR 720, 721, 2 MSPB 128 (1980).

To confer Board jurisdiction, the regulatory grant must be specific, Wheaton v. MSPB, 267 Fed. Appx. 927, 930 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP):

Mr. Wheaton acknowledges that the Board's jurisdiction is governed by 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a). Nevertheless, he argues that the regulation, 5 C.F.R. 1201.3(a), lists twenty possible types of appeals, but uses the language "includes." That language, he avers, leaves the Board's jurisdiction open to other actions as well. The regulation, however, tracks the language of the statute, stipulating that "[t]he Board has jurisdiction over appeals from agency actions when the appeals are authorized by law, rule, or regulation." 5 C.F.R. 1201.3(a) (emphasis added). Notably, following each of the listed appeals, the regulation or statute that authorizes jurisdiction is referenced. Mr. Wheaton cites to no law, rule, or regulation that authorizes jurisdiction over his claim.

See Carell v. MSPB, 131 Fed. Appx. 296, 299 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP) (citing Meyers v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 417, 420 (1987)) (5 CFR 1201.3 (2022), describing Board jurisdiction through references to other statutes and regulations, is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction).
