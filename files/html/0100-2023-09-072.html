6. Need to Obtain Counsel

The Board generally determines that an employee's untimely filing cannot be excused by failure of a lawyer to act or by inability to secure a lawyer. Lake v. Dept. of Transp., 33 MSPR 203, 207 (1987) ("The appellant's untimely filing can also not be excused by the fact that the first two lawyers he contacted did nothing[.] "; "Moreover, the appellant had previously appealed to the Board from an earlier removal for striking and thus can be charged with some knowledge of his appeal rights."); see Goldtooth v. Dept. of Interior, 69 MSPR 662, 664 (1996) (appellant's efforts to obtain counsel after his appeal was due did not excuse an untimely filing: "The appellant needed only to place postage on the appeal form that he completed, with the help of his relative...address it to the Board's regional office, and place the envelope in a mail box.... However, he did not perform this simple act, and his subsequent attempts at obtaining legal representation do not relieve him of the responsibility of filing an appeal on time."), aff'd, Goldtooth v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1997 NP No. 96-3215); Richards v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 6, 9 (1990) (appellant was sufficiently involved in his case that the Board discounted his explanation that he was unfamiliar with legal proceedings; he alluded to a representative but did not designate one); cf. Baker v. USPS, 80 MSPR 333, 335 ¶ 5 (1998) ("The fact that she did not think she would be able to represent herself properly also does not establish good cause."); cf. Depierro v. USPS, 54 MSPR 251, 254 (1992) (delayed PFR was not excused because of the appellant's belief that an attorney would file the petition; the appellant was personally responsible for the prosecution of his appeal). A three-month delay in filing an appeal after counsel was finally located was deemed inexcusable in Sainz v. DOJ, 32 MSPR 678, 681 (1987).

At times the Board demonstrates more flexibility concerning filing deadlines, particularly with short filing delays of pro se appellants who become confused or overwhelmed by the appellate process or the process of finding representation. A two-day delay in filing was excused in Lytle v. VA, 76 MSPR 388, 391 (1997), when the appellant could not obtain timely assistance from his representative:

[T]he appellant was pro se at the time he filed his appeal. The agency's final decision informed him that he had 30 calendar days to file his appeal. The length of the delay in filing, however, was minimal, 2 days. Further, there is no indication in the record that the appellant was negligent in filing his appeal. The appellant asserted below, without contradiction, that the individual who was apparently to be his union representative was unavailable to assist him in filing his appeal and that, when he finally located this individual, he learned that this individual was absent on family sick leave. The appellant also stated that he attempted to call his union office but was unable to reach anyone. He stated that, since he was unable to obtain help, he filed the appeal himself. He also stated that, if he had known where or how to obtain assistance, he would have done so. Thus, the appellant established that he acted diligently under the circumstances and did not unduly delay in filing his appeal, as indicated by the minimal delay. See Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1994); Anderson v. Dept. of Justice, 999 F.2d 532, 534 (Fed. Cir. 1993); Sanders v. OPM, 67 MSPR 21, 24 (1995); Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 184. Further, we find no indication that the agency would be prejudiced by a waiver of the filing time limit.

Therefore, in view of the appellant's pro se status, the unavailability of the representative, the appellant's efforts to obtain assistance, and the minimal delay involved in the filing of the appeal, we find that good cause exists for a waiver of the time limit for the filing of the appellant's appeal. See Walls, 29 F.3d at 1582-84; Anderson, 999 F.2d at 534-35; Shiflett v. USPS, 839 F.2d 669, 670-74 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Sanders, 67 MSPR at 24-25.

The Board excused a three-day delay in filing an appeal by an employee involved in continuing legal struggles with the agency that caused the appellant mental and financial distress. There was no prejudice to the agency. Affidavits were submitted from three attorneys showing that the appellant sought and was having problems getting counsel. Nagel v. DHHS, 5 MSPR 266, 268, 5 MSPB 303 (1981). An appeal involving a short delay managed to avoid dismissal for untimeliness in Murroughs v. OPM, 67 MSPR 115, 118-19 (1995), when the appellant could not immediately meet with counsel:

In this case, the appellant and her attorney submitted statements, signed under penalty of perjury, in response to the administrative judge's show-cause order on timeliness. The appellant asserted in her statement that: she contacted her attorney on the same day that she received OPM's reconsideration decision, November 17, 1993; she was told that he was in trial out of state; and her attorney's office scheduled an appointment for them to meet on December 6, the day he returned. Both the appellant and her attorney asserted in their statements that: on December 6, the attorney was ill and did not return to his office until December 14; the appellant met with him that day; and her appeal was filed on that day. Since the appellant's and her attorney's statements under penalty of perjury are unrebutted and uncontroverted, they are sufficient to meet her evidentiary burden. See Jordan v. Dept. of Justice, 54 MSPR 609, 611 (1992) (sworn statements that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters asserted therein).

We find that, under the particular facts and attending circumstances of this case, the appellant has demonstrated that she exercised diligence or ordinary prudence in filing her appeal. See Hennelly v. Dept. of Transp., 26 MSPR 595, 596-97 (1985) (illness of agency's attorney of record established good cause for granting of extension of time for filing response to petition for review and for waiver of time limit for filing response). The appellant's attorney's severe illness sufficiently explains, too, why he was incapable of requesting an extension of time from the Board before the deadline for filing. See id.

Finally, despite these circumstances, the appellant filed her appeal only two days after the filing deadline, and the agency has not shown that it was prejudiced by the short delay. See Mata v. OPM, 53 MSPR 552, 555 (lack of prejudice to the agency is not considered until after the appellant establishes good cause for the untimely filing), aff'd, 983 F.2d 1088 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

Cf. Pawn v. USDA, 88 MSPR 388, 389 (2001) (refiling deadline was waived based on the appellant's pro se status, the difficulty in obtaining legal representation in a remote location, and a lack of sophistication in legal matters).
