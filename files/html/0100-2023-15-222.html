b. Managerial Disagreement

An intemperate memo, criticizing reorganization of an agency program, circulated among staff by a supervisor with responsibility for implementing the program, may contain some elements of insubordination. However, when the delay resulting from the objections to the program was short--a few days--and if there was no personal gain and no adverse impact on employee morale, and if the memo was written in the good faith belief that the planned reorganization would lessen the quality of patient care, the Board, weighing the interests, declined to sustain a demotion, Schofield v. VA, 7 MSPR 94, 98-99, 7 MSPB 23 (1981):

This is not to say that appellant's actions were necessarily proper. If his attempts to defeat implementation of the plan crossed over into the area of insubordination, then discipline would be proper. Appellant's actions and speech certainly contained some elements of insubordination. Balanced in his favor, however, are the facts that he was supervisor in charge of the affected section and was responsible for the daily operations. As such, his involvement was part of his duties and his resistance to Plan 2 was born of his good faith belief that the reorganization would overburden the staff and lessen the quality of patient care. Thus, while his acts and words were intemperate to some degree it is a close question as to whether they strayed to the area of insubordination when considered in light of appellant's duties and responsibilities.

Given the close question on the facts as to whether the statutory standard of promoting the efficiency of the service has been satisfied, we must be mindful of the difficult constitutional question that would be raised if the statute were interpreted to sustain this demotion.[3] In light of that serious constitutional question, we find that evidence presented by the agency is insufficient for us to conclude by a preponderance of the evidence that appellant's demotion was warranted by the efficiency of the service. In reaching this conclusion, we attach weight to the fact that there is no evidence of actual adverse impact on employee discipline or morale, no evidence that appellant was seeking to promote any personal interest or gain for himself, and no evidence that he acted unreasonably or in violation of any regulation by sharing his April 20 memo with his immediate staff and trainees.

[3] The entitlement of government employees to exercise First Amendment rights has been a subject which courts have struggled to delineate. The difficulty lies with the competing interests involved in such cases--the interest of the employee-citizen to comment upon matters of public concern, as well as the public benefits such comments may engender, as balanced against the interest of the government-employer to promote the efficiency of its public services. Pickering v. Board of Education, 391 U.S. 563, 568, 88 S. Ct. 1731, 1734, 20 L. Ed.2d 811 (1968). Deciding whether the speech is protected under the First Amendment is largely a factual question as to whether the nature of the speech is in one sphere of interest rather than the other. Compare Porter v. Califano, 592 F.2d 770 (5th Cir. 1979), with Foster v. Ripley, 645 F.2d 142 (D.C. Cir. 1981). The problem is, of course, that often the speech does not squarely fall within either sphere. See Smith v. United States, 502 F.2d 512 (5th Cir. 1974).

The appellant's activities were directed at attempting to prevent the hospital from implementing a plan which he felt would decrease the quality of patient care. In that sense, his speech was of concern to the public, the hospital employees, and the patients. See Porter v. Califano, supra, at 779. On the other hand, he was in a position which required that he be able to work for Baginsky, the person he directly criticized, Foster v. Ripley, supra at 1148, and the statements were unnecessarily circulated to superiors, co-workers, and staff, see Prescott v. HHS, 6 MSPB 216, 6 MSPR 252 (1981); cf. Pickering, supra, 391 U.S. at 569-70, 88 S. Ct. at 1735.
