7. Who May Obtain Review

The regulation tells us, 5 CFR 1201.114 (2022):

(c) Who may file. Any party to the proceeding, the Director of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), or the Special Counsel (under 5 U.S.C. 1212(c)) may file a petition or cross petition for review. The Director of OPM may request review only if he or she believes that the decision is erroneous and will have a substantial impact on any civil service law, rule, or regulation under OPM's jurisdiction. 5 U.S.C. 7701(e)(2). All submissions to the Board must contain the signature of the party or of the party's designated representative.

Review is sought by one or both of the parties to the case. A nonparty witness lacks standing to obtain review of an initial decision through a PFR. Womack v. USPS, 18 MSPR 434, 435 (1983). The Board will not consider a PFR filed by a nonparty who is not a designated representative. See Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 382, 383 (1984) (declining to consider a submission from a coworker who was not a designated representative or intervenor). Nor will a PFR be considered if it is filed by an attorney not designated by the appellant. Smalle v. DOD, 58 MSPR 449 (1993) (Table) (not disturbing the Board's practice of permitting a PFR to be filed by an attorney designated in regional proceedings); see Marshall v. DHHS, 111 MSPR 1, 1 n.1, 2008 MSPB 245 (2008) (when the PFR was filed by an undesignated representative, the Board adjudicated the PFR, but the Board referred to the appellant as proceeding pro se); Staines v. USPS, 65 MSPR 84, 85 (1994) (applying the rule to an undesignated union representative).

Nothing bars the appellant from filing his own PFR even though he has, or had, a designated representative. See Love v. USPS, 58 MSPR 490, 492 n.1 (1993). But the Board may dismiss a PFR that is not signed by the appellant and that is not accompanied by a pleading or other submission designating a representative. See White-Jackson v. OPM, 114 MSPR 33, 36 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 79 (2010). In complex cases, the Board may grant, on motion, leave to file consolidated PFRs addressing common issues presented by related appeals, see Former Air Traffic Controllers v. Dept. of Transp., 14 MSPR 470, 471-73 (1983) (presenting issues arising from the termination of striking air traffic controllers), or the Board may consolidate PFRs on its own motion, as in Long v. VA, 91 MSPR 685, 686 (2002).

PFRs by pro se appellants are liberally construed. See Cohn v. DOD, DC-0752-14-0966-I-1 (NP 6/25/2015) ("This pro se appellant's arguments are somewhat unclear. In view of the appellant's pro se status, however, we have interpreted his arguments in the light most favorable to him. See Patterson v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 332, 335 (1996), aff'd, 106 F.3d 425 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table).").
