13. Refusal or Failure to Conclude Agreement

Whether good faith bargaining occurred during negotiation sessions is based on the total context of what occurred between parties during the course of proposals and counterproposals. Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 15 FLRA 829, 845 (1984) (ALJ Decision). Proper bargaining is evidenced by a give and take on various proposals, although agreement may not be reached. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 16 FLRA 217, 228 (1984) (ALJ Decision) (impact bargaining). Whether there was a failure of good faith bargaining depends on the totality of the evidence. Considered is whether the parties approached the negotiations with a sincere resolve to reach an agreement, were represented by authorized representatives prepared to discuss and negotiate on any condition of employment, met as frequently as was necessary, and avoided unnecessary delays. Exploration and discussion of each party's position embodies the essence of negotiations. Requirements for stylized forms of communication are to be eschewed. An employer's failure to submit counterproposals is not generally regarded as a per se violation of the duty to bargain in good faith. But delays by management for over a year in the negotiation process, with insufficient responses to union proposals, followed by unilateral changes in work procedures, can constitute bad faith bargaining. VA and AFGE, 24 FLRA 9, 29-30 (1986) (ALJ Decision); see Dept. of Air Force, Hq., Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB and AFGE Council 214, 36 FLRA 524 (1990) (bad faith bargaining as result of bargaining delays by management representative).

A lack of communication or coordination between union and agency does not constitute an unlawful refusal to bargain. As long as the agency's response to an acknowledged duty to bargain does not constitute a direct refusal or dilatory tactics of such a nature as to be tantamount to a refusal to bargain, no ULP occurs. Dept. of Treasury, BATF and NTEU, 7 FLRA 431, 433 (1981). The totality of the evidence must be considered to determine whether a party has attempted to evade or frustrate its bargaining responsibility. Although an employer's withdrawal of tentative agreements or previous proposals is evidence of bad-faith bargaining, those actions do not establish the absence of good faith per se. The actions of the employer must be viewed in the context of the negotiations. Div. of Military and Naval Affairs, New York and New York State Council of ACT, 7 FLRA 321, 338 (1981) (ALJ Decision); see Navy, Charleston Naval Shipyard and Planners and Estimators Local 8, 34 FLRA 554 (1990) (ALJ Decision).
