13. Specificity of Decision Letter

The decision letter need not be particularly specific if the proposal letter is sufficiently specific, Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 145, 151-52 (1982):

The Board agrees with the presiding official that while the removal decision does not set forth findings with respect to each specification in support of the charge, the courts have held in cases arising prior to enactment of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 that where the charge and specifications are stated with sufficient specificity in the proposal notice, it is not necessary to repeat them in the decision notice in order to satisfy the requirement that the "reasons" for the decision be stated, and that a direct reference to the charges is sufficient. Dew v. Halaby, 317 F.2d 582, 588 (D.C. Cir. 1963), cert. dismissed, 379 U.S. 951, 85 S. Ct. 452, 13 L. Ed.2d 550 (1964); Green v. Baughman, 243 F.2d 610, 612 (D.C. Cir.), cert. denied, 355 U.S. 819, 78 S. Ct. 25, 2 L. Ed.2d 35 (1957); Kenny v. United States, 145 F. Supp. 898, 899, 134 Ct. Cl. 442, cert. denied, 352 U.S. 893, 77 S. Ct. 131, 1 L. Ed.2d 86 (1956). See also Erenreich v. United States, 164 Ct. Cl. 214, 220 (Ct. Cl.), cert. denied, 379 U.S. 857, 85 S. Ct. 111, 13 L. Ed.2d 60 (1964), and Graham, supra, 2 MSPB at 381, 2 MSPR at 218 (where an agency's notice of decision states that it is based on a full consideration of the evidence presented, no specific finding need be included by the agency for each affirmative defense raised by the employee).

The Board finds that the removal decision in the instant case is sufficiently specific to satisfy the requirement of § 7513(b)(4) that the "specific reasons" therefor be set forth....

Rodriguez v. VA, 8 F.4th 1290, 1304 (Fed. Cir. 2021), did not fault brevity in a decision that was otherwise clear in its analysis through adoption of portions of the proposal notice:

Mr. Rodriguez also argues that Ms. Klinker's written decision was not sufficiently detailed to satisfy due process requirements. While the substantive portion of Ms. Klinker's removal decision was short, it referenced and adopted the pertinent provisions of the notice of proposed removal, which contained substantial detail regarding the charges against Mr. Rodriguez and the justifications for removing him. The materials generated by the DVA and provided to Mr. Rodriguez were sufficient both to give him notice of the charges against him and to explain the decision to remove him. Accordingly, we reject Mr. Rodriguez's due process claims predicated on alleged flaws in the proceedings before the DVA.

See McDade v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0752-13-0981-I-1 (NP 6/23/2015) ("Further, the fact that the decision notice may not have addressed all of the appellant's specific arguments in her reply does not mean that they were not considered or that the agency committed harmful procedural error. See, e.g., Salo v. Department of Defense, 122 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 9 (2015) (noting that the appellant did not identify any regulatory, statutory, or judicially imposed requirement that the agency must specifically address all arguments raised in a response to a proposal notice, and concluding that the appellant did not show that any alleged error likely caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error)."); Morton v. SSA, AT-0752-14-0806-I-1 (NP 5/27/2015) ("The appellant argues that the deciding official failed to comply with 5 C.F.R. § 752.404(g)(1) because he "did not duly consider appellant's answer" to the proposal notice. The plain language of the regulation, however, does not require the agency to address every contention that the appellant raised in his response. Instead, it requires the agency simply to 'consider' the response. An agency is presumed to have considered a response if, as here, the response is referenced in the decision letter and submitted with the agency file. Franks v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 502, 505 (1984)."); Coppola v. USPS, 47 MSPR 307, 313 (1991) ("Where the agency's charges and specifications of misconduct are stated with sufficient specificity in the proposal notice, it is not necessary to repeat them in the decision notice to satisfy the requirement that the specific instances of misconduct be stated.").
