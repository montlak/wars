4. Gambling

Gambling on government property erodes efficiency and morale; disciplinary action is warranted. Landreth v. TVA, 20 MSPR 359, 360 (1984). Gambling supports removal when conducted on the job site during the workday and when it affects the work of others. Howard v. USPS, 26 MSPR 393, 398 (1985) (noting an agency regulation prohibiting gambling on the premises, 45 CFR 73.735-305(a)). The penalty of discharge for violation of an antigambling regulation, even for a first-time offense, is appropriate. Hunt v. DHHS, 758 F.2d 608 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Ricci v. United States, 507 F.2d 1390 (Ct. Cl. 1974) (comparing the offenses of gambling and promotion of gambling); cf. Luna v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 696, 699 (1988) (removal for gambling was reduced to a 90-day suspension; the appellant did not profit from the gambling operation, accepted responsibility, cooperated in the investigation, and showed remorse); Lampack v. USPS, 27 MSPR 468, 470 (1985) (mitigating removal to a demotion of an appellant whose removal of funds was caused by a gambling compulsion); Chow v. SSA, 4 MSPR 224, 225-26, 4 MSPB 300 (1980) (conversion of money orders was not excused because of an alleged gambling addiction; gambling addiction was not covered by the Rehabilitation Act). [Refer to Chapter 12, "Gambling Compulsion" concerning addiction in relation to disability discrimination and reasonable accommodation.]

The Board again decried gambling on the clock or on the employer's premises, Jones v. Dept. of Interior, 97 MSPR 282, 289-90 ¶¶ 16-18 (2004):

[T]he Board and the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit have held that removal is appropriate for on-duty gambling offenses, such as those committed here. See, e.g., Hunt v. Department of Health & Human Services, 758 F.2d 608, 611 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Howard, 26 M.S.P.R. at 398-400; Landreth v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 20 M.S.P.R. 359, 360-61 (1984); Suliman v. Department of the Navy, 14 M.S.P.R. 415, 416, 418 (1983).

In mitigating the removal, the administrative judge relied in part on Luna v. Department of the Army, 38 M.S.P.R. 696, 699 (1988). In Luna, the appellant promoted gambling while on government premises and on duty. Id. at 697. The Board mitigated his removal to a 90-day suspension because it was his first offense, he did not profit from the gambling operation, and he had rehabilitative potential because he was remorseful, accepted full responsibility for his conduct, and alerted the agency to continuing gambling activities. Id. at 699.

It appears that Luna is inconsistent with the above-cited cases, which hold that removal is an appropriate penalty for on-duty gambling offenses. Moreover, this case is distinguishable from Luna in several respects. Here, the appellant was convicted of a felony in connection with promoting gambling activity on agency premises while on duty. In Luna, although the appellant was arrested for conspiracy and illegal gambling, it does not appear that he was convicted of these crimes. Luna, 38 M.S.P.R. at 697. Additionally, there is no evidence that the appellant in this matter informed agency management of continued gambling activity, or otherwise assisted in the agency's investigation. Accordingly, Luna does not mandate mitigation in this case. The removal action is SUSTAINED.
