9. Opposition to Motion

Under Allen v. USPS, 2 MSPR 420, 436, 2 MSPB 582 (1980), the agency should set out in its response to the request for attorney fees all considerations relied upon in opposition to an award, including affidavits relating to contested facts pertinent to the motion or to the reasonableness of the amount claimed. The affidavits should also, if necessary, oppose a claim that the result achieved, e.g., settlement, is related to the Board appeal. Factual statements in briefs unsupported by affidavits are not evidence on contested evidentiary issues. See Ingram v. VA, 29 MSPR 641, 645 (1986). The Board does not take kindly to line item objections to multitudes of time entries. See Freeman v. VA, 66 MSPR 125, 131 (1995) ("For the most part, the agency's attack on virtually every line-item in the appellant's counsel's statement 'verge[s] on nit-picking,'...and does not provide a basis for setting aside the administrative judge's finding that the claimed fees are reasonable.") (citing Wilson v. DHHS, 834 F.2d 1011, 1013 (Fed. Cir. 1987)). But see Gubino v. Dept. of Transp., 85 MSPR 518, 521-30 ¶¶ 6-27 (2000) (line-by-line examination of fee petition).

One source of evidence for an opposition to a fee petition is the agency attorney's own time records, assuming they are kept. If appellant's counsel claims three times as much time on an appeal as the agency legal defense team, an argument can be made that appellant's counsel should not be rewarded for inefficiency. The significance of agency time records was a point made in decision considering a fee petition against the MSPB under EAJA. MSPB argued that counsel's fees were excessive. But the Board had to use more than moral suasion to convince others of the unreasonableness of the investment of time by counsel. Keely v. MSPB, 793 F.2d 1273, 1276 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Writing for the court, Judge Bissell commented:

The Board also asserts that the requested amount is excessive. In his fee application, Keely claims 106.3 hours in fees and $146.77 in expenses. It appears that the Board only disputes the approximately 76 hours of work on the brief for the appeal before this court. The Board argues that this is excessive because the content of the brief is essentially repetitive of the content of briefs filed in earlier stages of this litigation.

We are not persuaded. The 76 hours claimed are not per se unreasonable, see Devine v. Sutermeister, 733 F.2d at 900 n.9 (rejecting unsupported contention that 99.5 hours is per se excessive for work relating to principal and supplemental briefs and preparation for oral argument), and the Board has failed to tell us how much time it spent working on the appeal. We have reviewed the affidavit in Keely's application and we regard the amount requested adequately documented and reasonably expended.

Keely mentioned that "the Board has failed to tell us how much time it spent working on the appeal." The words have significance. The court recognized that although anyone can attack another's fee claim by generalized claims that the time spent is excessive, more than verbiage will be necessary if the argument is to be accepted. Because neither the Board nor agency counsel has any reasonable and objective outside measure of what in the circumstances of any particular appeal is a reasonable investment of time, examination of that issue should turn inward: how much time was spent by the side complaining of the other's greed or inefficiency? Agency counsel should keep time records. The Board endorsed Keely in Freeman v. VA, 66 MSPR 125, 131-32 (1995), stating that "it should be noted that the agency's representative does not say how many hours he spent on the appeal; thus, the agency's claim that the appellant's counsel spent unreasonable amounts of time on the appeal cannot be viewed in context." If agency counsel does not access to electronic timekeeping, keep a contemporaneous paper record, as in days of yore.
