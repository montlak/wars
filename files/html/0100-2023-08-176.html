6. Federal Bureau of Investigation

5 USC 7511(b)(8) excludes from adverse action appeal rights all but preference eligible FBI employees. Patterson v. DOJ, 52 MSPR 651, 653-54 (1992) (noting a special whistleblowing protection statute for FBI employees, 5 USC 2303). See Jones v. DOJ, DC-315I-12-0847-I-1 (NP 10/28/2013) (appellant did not qualify for veteran preference and so did not have an appeal right upon termination). FBI employees apparently are not excluded from appealing RIFs. See Mead v. DOJ, 9 MSPR 283, 286 (1981) (Board entertained a RIF appeal by an excepted service employee who was not a preference eligible; the decision also mentioned at n.3 that "[u]nder 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(C)(ii), 2303, and 7701(c)(2)(B), the Board lacks jurisdiction to consider allegations of prohibited personnel practices by the FBI"), aff'd, Mead v. MSPB, 687 F.2d 285 (9th Cir. 1982) (describing at n.2 an alternate means of resolving personnel disputes and related constitutional and whistleblowing issues involving FBI employees). The Board lacked jurisdiction over a Chapter 43 action against an FBI employee. Todd v. DOJ, DC-0432-11-0640-I-1 (NP 3/16/2012) ("The right to appeal an action based on unacceptable performance to the Board derives from statute, specifically 5 U.S.C. § 4303(e). Only preference eligible individuals, individuals in the competitive service, and individuals in the excepted service who are covered by subchapter II of chapter 75 may appeal a chapter 43 action to the Board. Pennington v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 57 M.S.P.R. 8, 10 (1993). The appellant is not in the competitive service and he is not preference eligible. Employees of the FBI are explicitly excluded from coverage under subchapter II of chapter 75. 5 U.S.C. § 7511(b)(8); Patterson v. Department of Justice, 52 M.S.P.R. 651, 653 (1992). Therefore, the appellant is not among the categories of employees with appeal rights under chapter 43, and the Board lacks jurisdiction over his appeal.").

The James M. Inhofe National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2023, Pub. L. 117-263 (12/23/2022), included Section 5304, extending MSPB jurisdiction to FBI employees pursuing whistleblower reprisal allegations under 5 USC 2303, adding Board appeal rights at 5 USC 2303(d), incorporating the processes at 5 USC 1221.

Bullard v. Webster, 623 F.2d 1042, 1045-46 (5th Cir. 1980), presented early discussion of the appeal rights of FBI agents in a suit in district court to enjoin an agent's transfer:

[S]pecial agents of the FBI are not afforded many of the procedural safeguards available to other government employees. First, persons in the FBI are excepted from the competitive civil service. 28 USC 536. Therefore, FBI employees do not have the procedural rights guaranteed members of the competitive civil service. 5 USC 7501(b). Also, Bullard is not a "preference eligible veteran," a status that would afford him additional protections. 5 USC 7512. Therefore, neither the protections of the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 USC 7501, et seq., nor the Veterans Preference Act, 5 USC 7511, et seq, are available to Bullard.

But cf. Carter v. United States, 407 F.2d 1238, 1242 (D.C. Cir. 1968) (although an FBI clerk had no statutory appeal rights because he was reinstated after a period of military service, he could not be discharged, other than for "cause," in the first year following the resumption of civilian life under § 9(c) of Universal Military Training and Service Act, 50 USC App. 459(c) (1964)).
