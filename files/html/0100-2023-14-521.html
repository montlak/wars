broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 16: Facilities, Benefits for Employees, Section G: Building and Office Design, Subsection 04: Smoking Rules; Related Office Arrangements

4. Smoking Rules; Related Office Arrangements

An agency's designation of smoking areas is generally negotiable. 814th Combat Support Group, Beale AFB and AFGE Local 2025, 43 FLRA 1173, 1179 n.2 (1992). NAGE Local R14-32 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Leonard Wood, 26 FLRA 593, 597-99 (1987), considered negotiability of proposals modifying agency rules banning smoking in various areas. The proposals had a limited effect on nonunit employees. The no-smoking rules affected a condition of employment. Their violation could result in adverse action and they affected the ability of employees to smoke in working areas. The proposals were not barred by an agency claim of a contrary regulation supported by a compelling need:

...In essence, the Agency maintains that the portions of the Policy...and the DOD Directive...are essential for the functioning of the Departments of Defense and Army in an efficient and effective manner for reasons of cost and employee health. The Agency cites various authorities to support its claim that the reduction or elimination of smoking will increase productivity and decrease illness, absenteeism, and premature death among its employees. The Agency contends that it is essential that the smoking policy be applied consistently throughout the Army to all employees, military and civilian, in and out of bargaining units, because of the potential detrimental effects of smoking on nonsmokers.

We find that the Agency has failed to meet its burden of demonstrating that a compelling need exists for the Army Policy...or the DOD Directive to bar negotiations under section 7117(a)(2) of the Statute. In order for the Authority to find that a compelling need exists for an agency regulation to bar negotiations on a proposal, the agency must (1) identify a specific agency-wide regulation; (2) show that there is a conflict between its regulation and the proposal; and (3) demonstrate that its regulation is supported by a compelling need under the standards set forth in section 2424.11 of the Authority's Rules and Regulations.... In this case, in order to support its allegation of compelling need, the Agency must show that the cited regulations are essential, as distinguished from helpful or desirable, to the accomplishment of its mission or the execution of its functions in a manner which is consistent with the requirements of an effective and efficient Government.

...

[T]he Agency has demonstrated generally that smoking can have deleterious effects on employee health, and that the costs associated with those effects in terms of workforce effectiveness and productivity can be significant. However, the Agency has not shown that without the particular regulatory restrictions as to the areas in which smoking will be permitted it will be unable to achieve its objectives of greater employee health, enhanced productivity, and reduced operational costs. That is, even assuming that the objectives of the Agency's regulation restricting smoking are related to the accomplishment of its mission in an effective and efficient manner, the Agency has not shown that the particular restrictions set forth in its regulations are essential to those purposes within the meaning of the Authority's criterion.

As the Union points out, the Agency's regulations permit smoking in some areas. The Agency has apparently determined therefore that smoking by employees in the workplace is not wholly incompatible with the achievement of its objectives. But if smoking in some areas of the workplace can be permitted consistent with the Agency's objectives, then it is not essential to absolutely preclude smoking in order to achieve those objectives. If it is not essential to preclude all smoking, it cannot be essential to limit smoking to one area rather than another. The most that can be claimed for particular limitations in the Agency's regulations as to areas in which smoking is permitted therefore is that they are helpful in achieving those objectives. They have not been shown to be essential. Consequently, we find no merit in the Agency's contention that a compelling need exists for its policy....

Compelling need urged on the basis of the statutory responsibility of an agency to promote the avoidance of smoking was rejected as a bar to the negotiability of proposals seeking arrangements for smokers. NTEU Chap. 250 and DHHS, Family Support Admin., 33 FLRA 61, 66-71 (1988) (not crediting arguments that proposals concerned agency's means of performing its work, i.e., implementation of national policy dissuading smoking; rejecting as well that proposals interfered with management's right to discipline employees who refused to comply with agency work rules), enf'd, DHHS v. FLRA, 920 F.2d 45, 47-48 (D.C. Cir. 1990) [the circuit court opinion is discussed in Chapter 5, "Compelling Need Determinations"], followed in NFFE Local 414 and DHHS, Indian Health Serv., Oklahoma City, 38 FLRA 861 (1990); see DHHS, Pub. Health Serv. and Ctrs. for Disease Control, NIOSH and AFGE Local 3430, 39 FLRA 1306 (1991), reconsideration denied, 43 FLRA 1394 (1992) (new studies concerning effects of tobacco smoke were insufficient basis to reconsider earlier decision). Ft. Leonard Wood was followed and a proposal that smoking be permitted in military vehicles was negotiable. AFGE Local 2324 and Dept. of Army, Hq., 1st Infantry Div., Ft. Riley, 27 FLRA 33, 34 (1987).

Another decision concluded that smoking policies were negotiable despite an agency claim that negotiations interfered with the right of management to determine the technology, methods, and means used in performing work. The employer did not show that without a total ban on smoking it was unable to achieve its goal of achieving the health of the people it was tasked to serve. DHHS, Pub. Health Serv., Health Resources and Serv. Admin., Oklahoma City Area, Indian Health Serv. and Oklahoma Area Indian Health Serv. Council, NFFE, 31 FLRA 498, 507 (1988). That decision was affirmed in DHHS, Indian Health Serv., Oklahoma City v. FLRA, 885 F.2d 911 (D.C. Cir. 1989) [refer to Chapter 5, "Determination of Technology, Methods and Means of Performing Work," for negotiability of changes affecting work technology], and followed in DHHS, Pub. Health Serv., Indian Health Serv., Indian Hosp., Rapid City and NFFE Local 179, 37 FLRA 972, 979 (1990) (agency was required to bargain over union proposals that, although they might differ in details from the employer's preferred policy, do not interfere with purposes of the agency policy), reconsideration denied, DHHS, Pub. Health Serv., Indian Health Serv. and NFFE Local 179, 38 FLRA 867 (1990).

A proposal was negotiable to expand the interests of nonsmokers. NTEU and IRS, Indianapolis Dist., 30 FLRA 32, 32-33 (1987) ("[i]ndividuals occupying designated smoking areas will respect the interests of nonsmokers and will refrain from smoking at the request of nonsmoking employees or make other suitable accommodations"); cf. DOJ, INS and AFGE Nat'l INS Council, Local 2076, 43 FLRA 241, 251 (1991) (refusal to bargain case: "an agency's decision as to the designation of smoking areas is negotiable").

Proposals are negotiable that seek negotiations on smoking or changes in smoking policies. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 33 FLRA 183, 186 (1988). Office arrangements designed to accommodate the interests of smokers and nonsmokers were approved as negotiable. In NTEU and IRS, Los Angeles Dist., 32 FLRA 182, 184-92 (1988), negotiable proposals included: private offices, desks, work stations will be designated and signed as smoking areas at the request of employees; to the maximum extent possible, the employer will separate smokers and nonsmokers in the work area; the agency will provide and maintain an approved air purifier for each smoking area; management will install floor to ceiling partitions between smoking and nonsmoking areas. The proposals affected unit employees' conditions of employment: they did not conflict with government-wide regulations, 41 CFR 101-20.105-3 (1988), or decisions of the Comptroller General; they did not conflict with the methods or means or technology of performing work; they did not interfere with the right of the agency to determine its budget. But that decision was reversed in part by IRS, Los Angeles Dist. v. FLRA, 902 F.2d 998, 100-02 (D.C. Cir. 1990), ruling nonnegotiable as contrary to GSA regulations the proposal that private offices or work stations could be designated as smoking areas at the request of the employee. The decision should be read for a review of the technicalities of the GSA regulations.

POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 41 FLRA 795, 833 (1991), required bargaining on Provision 11, having two provisions involving rearrangements of office space to meet the needs of smokers and nonsmokers:

Smoking will be prohibited in rooms where meetings are being conducted, or in shared offices or work areas unless all professionals therein agree to such. Smoking professionals shall not be required to share an office or a work area with non-smoking professionals....

POPA declared as to arrangements for office space of smokers and non-smokers, 41 FLRA at 834-35:

The right to assign employees means the right to decide the particular position to which an employee will be assigned.... This right encompasses the authority to determine the particular qualifications and skills needed to perform the work of a position as well as the authority to determine which employees possess the requisite qualifications and skills.... This right does not normally encompass the decision as to where employees will perform duties previously assigned to their positions....

There is no indication in the record here that office assignment controls or affects the position to which an employee is assigned. That is, there is no indication that office assignment represents anything more than the location where the employee will perform duties previously assigned. Consequently, we reject the Agency's assertion that this provision directly interferes with management's right to assign employees.

The Statute does not define management's right to direct employees. In the absence of any indication that the phrase as used in the Statute has a meaning other than its ordinary meaning, the Authority has determined that the right to direct employees means to "supervise and guide [employees] in the performance of their duties on the job."... The right to direct employees is exercised through supervising employees and determining the quantity, quality and timeliness of work production and establishing priorities for its accomplishment....

This provision does not concern the direction of employees within the meaning of section 7106(a)(2)(A). It does not involve supervising employees or determining the quantity, quality and timeliness of work production or establishing priorities for its accomplishment. The Agency makes no showing in the record as to how this provision relates to directing employees, and a relationship is not otherwise apparent to us. Thus, we find that the provision does not interfere with management's right to direct employees....

The Agency makes no claim here, and it is not otherwise apparent to us, that the assignment of employees to specific offices involves "functional grouping" and, by extension, that such a grouping constitutes the methods and means of performing work under section 7106(b)(1).... Consequently, this case is distinguishable from those cases in which a demonstration has been made that the location of employees in office space bears a functional relationship to facilitating the performance of the agency's work....

Based on the foregoing, we reject the Agency's claim that Provision 11 is nonnegotiable because it directly interferes with management's rights under section 7106(a)(2) to assign and direct employees.... We conclude that Provision 11 is within the duty to bargain.

The Authority will not approve the negotiability of a proposal that, designed as a control on smoking or smoke, precludes management from assigning work to employees, e.g., those who come into contact with various levels of second-hand smoke. AFGE Local 1808 and Dept. of Army, Sierra Army Depot, 30 FLRA 1236, 1262 (1988) (Provision 15). Also not generally negotiable are proposals that allow employees control over smoking that is otherwise permitted by agency clients, e.g., patients in medical centers of the VA, although proposals that attempt to have volunteers escort patients who smoke, and proposals for "employee-only" smoking "shelters" are negotiable. See AFGE Nat'l VA Council and VA, 40 FLRA 1052, 1062 (1991).
