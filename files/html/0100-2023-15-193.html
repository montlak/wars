2. Assault

Physical altercations at the work site directly affect an agency's obligation to maintain a safe workplace and, by their nature, are disruptive to the efficiency of the service. "[A]n agency has both a right and a responsibility to maintain discipline and order among its employees." Bree v. DHHS, 49 MSPR 68, 72 (1991); see Dept. of Air Force, 315th AW v. FLRA, 294 F.3d 192, 197 n.1 (D.C. Cir. 2002) ("Congress has not shown any intention to render any workers in the federal workplace defenseless against assault, battery, or other crimes or torts committed by their co-workers whether or not in the course of a labor dispute. Employers may and must be allowed to punish such behavior to maintain basic civility."). Assaultive behavior may be variously characterized. If the agency alleges an assault, proof of intent may be significant. Facts that might lead to an allegation of assault if intent were assumed may, however, be cast as "disorderly conduct," a charge that does not require proof of intent. Colon v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 190, 197 (1993); see Omites v. USPS, 87 MSPR 223, 228-30 ¶¶ 10-15 (2000) (removal was mitigated to suspension on a charge of unacceptable conduct when the appellant threw a partially filled can of soda at a wall after the appellant became angry during their discussion; the supervisor was not injured and did not feel threatened by the incident; the appellant had an excellent past work record; the agency improperly decided that a "zero tolerance" policy concerning workplace violence trumped the Douglas mitigating factors in the circumstances of the case); Dukat v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 195, 196-97 (1981) (charge of unbecoming conduct was sustained on the basis of a disruptive incident involving name calling and general disruption). But see McMillan v. USPS, 48 MSPR 590, 593 (1991) (declining to credit a defense that an employee was mentally unable to form the intent to assault: "The agency did not charge that the appellant 'intentionally' assaulted his superiors. Hence, in order to sustain the charge, it was not necessary to establish that the assault resulted from the appellant's wrongful intent.").
