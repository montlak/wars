b. Duty of Appellant to Produce Evidence Concerning Timeliness

When a timeliness issue surfaces, the appellant must exercise care to avoid being defaulted. Ignoring an AJ's acknowledgment order to show good cause for an untimely appeal invites dismissal. Montalvo v. USPS, 91 MSPR 671, 674 ¶ 6 (2002) ("It is within the administrative judge's discretion to determine whether an appellant has demonstrated good cause for an untimely filing, and ignoring an administrative judge's acknowledgment order to show good cause is grounds for dismissal. Mendoza v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 966 F.2d 650, 653 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (appellant who ignores an order of the administrative judge does so at his peril; all litigants before the Board are obligated to respect the Board, its procedures, and the orders of the Board's judges); Sledge v. Department of Justice, 44 M.S.P.R. 455, 459, aff'd, 923 F.2d 870 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table), cert. denied, 502 U.S. 1099 (1992)."); cf. Riddle v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3214) ("[T]he Board, which has no way on its own to determine if good cause exists for an untimely appeal, determined that it had no choice but to dismiss Mr. Riddle's appeal as untimely.").

If an AJ raises timeliness of an appeal, the time for submitting evidence on timeliness is in response to the show cause or acknowledgment that raises the issue. The Board ordinarily will not consider evidence first submitted with a PFR that might have, if the evidence had first been submitted to the AJ, explained or excused the untimely filing. Lawson v. VA, 27 MSPR 610, 611 n.* (1985) ("The appellant's belated attempt to offer an explanation for his untimely filed appeal in his petition for review does not provide a basis for review under 5 CFR § 1201.115(a))."); see Pascasio v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3766) (appellant was obliged to respond to the show-cause order on timeliness) (relying upon Cheguina v. MSPB, 69 F.3d 1143, 1147 (Fed. Cir. 1995)); Scott v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3175) (appellant had an obligation to respond to the timeliness issue raised by the Board's "show cause" order); Cheguina v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 452, 455 (1995) ("The contention of the appellant's attorney that he did not understand that he was supposed to respond to the acknowledgment order as ordered is simply not credible."); O'Doherty v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 12, 13 n.* (1985) (appeal was untimely when counsel represented that the first appeal was lost in the mail; the second appeal was filed, but counsel failed to respond to a show cause order or to offer affidavits or other evidence to support the assertions).

Although for an untimely PFR, the explanation must be in the form of an affidavit or sworn statement, the regulations governing appeals do not require those explanations to be in any particular form. See King v. DHHS, 71 MSPR 22, 26 n.3 (1996); Steakley v. OPM, 63 MSPR 155, 157 & n.2 (1994) (Board credited statements by the appellant that were not sworn or made under penalty of perjury pursuant to 28 USC 1746, but the Board noted that the appellant asserted that the statement was "true and correct to the best of my knowledge"). But see Imler v. VA, DE-0752-15-0408-I-1 (NP 9/25/2015) ("The vague, unsworn descriptions of the appellant's conditions, submitted by her attorney, do not constitute evidence. See Burger v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 582, ¶ 48 (2003), aff'd sub nom. Hayes v. U.S. Postal Service, 390 F.3d 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2004)."). The response to a timeliness issue may include statements by the appellant as well as his representative. See Hendricks v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 163, 168-69 (1995) ("The statements of a party's representative in a pleading, however, do not constitute evidence. See Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 M.S.P.R. 211, 228 (1995). Rather, we find that the unchallenged assertions of the appellant's representative constitute a nonfrivolous allegation raising a factual question as to whether the appellant actually put an agency official on notice of her belief as alleged, and whether she filed her appeal promptly after learning of her appeal rights."). Hendricks was followed in Jenkins v. DHHS, DC-0752-12-0586-I-1 (NP 6/18/2013) ("We recognize that the appellant did not submit a sworn statement to corroborate his attorney's claim that the appellant did not receive the FAD prior to May 21, 2012. However, where an appellant establishes a factual dispute regarding timeliness and, as here, requested a hearing, he is entitled to a timeliness hearing. Hightower v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 567, 569 (1996). Although the appellant's attorney's 'affirmation' does not constitute evidence, we find that it does raise a nonfrivolous allegation of timeliness such that the appellant is entitled to a timeliness hearing. See Hendricks, 69 M.S.P.R. at 168-69 ('unchallenged assertions' of the appellant's representative regarding timeliness constituted a nonfrivolous allegation raising a factual question regarding timeliness and that, therefore, the appellant was entitled to a hearing on the issue of timeliness).").

The Board occasionally avoids defaulting an appellant who fails to respond to an acknowledgment order raising a timeliness issue but who, after the cutoff date in that order, provides information to the AJ showing that the appeal is timely. That occurred in Andrews v. DHHS, 70 MSPR 202, 206 (1996), declining to sanction the appellant by dismissing his appeal for failure to timely respond to an acknowledgment order:

To dismiss this timely-filed appeal because the appellant did not respond to the acknowledgment order would amount to imposing the most severe sanction available, namely, dismissing the appeal for failure to prosecute. Sanctions should be imposed only "as necessary to serve the ends of justice." 5 CFR § 1201.43; see Wright v. Dept. of the Treasury, 53 MSPR 244, 249 (1992). The extreme sanction of dismissal of a timely appeal for failure to prosecute should not be imposed for a single instance of failure to comply with a Board order. Carrier v. USPS, 65 MSPR 54, 57 (1994). In the absence of bad faith or evidence that the appellant intends to abandon his appeal, dismissal of a timely-filed appeal for failure to prosecute is generally inappropriate. See Slaughter v. USPS, 60 MSPR 10 (1993).

There is nothing to indicate that either the appellant or his attorney has acted in bad faith. Further, although the appellant's attorney should have understood that a response was required to the notice regarding timeliness in the acknowledgment order, there is nothing that would lead us to believe that the appellant intended to abandon his appeal. Indeed, the appellant, through his attorney, participated in the telephone conference with the administrative judge, and argued that the appeal should be considered on the merits.

It should be noted that the agency was prepared to litigate the merits of the appeal before the administrative judge dismissed it. The agency did not raise the timeliness issue before the administrative judge, nor did it argue, in response to the portion of the acknowledgment order that did raise the issue, that the appeal should be dismissed. Thus, adjudicating the appeal on the merits will not prejudice the agency.

See Burnett v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 34, 38 (1996) (refusing to sanction a failure to timely respond to an acknowledgment order when information belatedly supplied to the AJ demonstrated that the appeal was timely filed); cf. Fitzpatrick v. Dept. of State, 71 MSPR 318, 321 (1996) (appellant was not defaulted for failing to respond to an acknowledgment order raising timeliness when the Board excused as minimal the underlying one-day delay in filing the appeal). But see Sledge v. DOJ, 44 MSPR 455, 459 (1990) (late appeal was dismissed; the appellant failed to respond to the AJ's acknowledgment order providing her an opportunity to justify a late filing; no explanation for the late filing was provided with the PFR).
