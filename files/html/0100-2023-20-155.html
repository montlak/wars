17. Training

In an OSC disciplinary case under the WPA, Special Counsel v. Hathaway, 49 MSPR 595, 606-07 (1991), aff'd, Hathaway v. MSPB, 981 F.2d 1237 (Fed. Cir. 1992), the Board explained what constitutes training covered as an IRA personnel action:

Count three charged respondent with cancelling Livengood's training in MSPB procedures because of Livengood's protected disclosures in violation of 5 USCA 2302(b)(8). In order to find that the cancellation of training is a prohibited personnel practice as defined in section 2302(b)(8) it is necessary to show that the training meets the statutory definition of a personnel action contained in 5 USCA 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix).[11] The CALJ found that the training in question did not fall within this definition.[12]

[11] This section provides that a personnel action means: a decision concerning pay, benefits, or awards, or concerning education or training if the education or training may reasonably be expected to lead to an appointment, promotion, performance evaluation, or other action described in this subparagraph.

[12] The CALJ interpreted 5 USCA 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix) to mean that the cancelled training must be career enhancing. In light of the lack of any evidence that nonattendance at the MSPB symposium could affect Livengood's career we find it unnecessary to address the CALJ's finding that the cancelled training or education must be "career enhancing" to constitute a personnel action.

...

The plain language of the statute establishes that not all training and education are included within the meaning of the statute. It must be training that "may reasonably be expected to" lead to a personnel action as described in section 2302(a)(2)(A). 5 USCA 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix). The limiting language was contained in both the House and Senate versions of the bill. See H.R. 11280 and S. 2640, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. reprinted in 1 Legislative History of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 at 5 (Leg. Hist.). The Senate Report explains that a personnel action includes a decision concerning education or training if it may lead to some other personnel action. 2 Leg. Hist. at 1484. Thus, the legislative history demonstrates that there must be a link between the decision regarding training and a future personnel action.

That link is defined in the statute in terms of what it is reasonable to expect. The meaning of reasonable is "moderate: not demanding too much" or "being or remaining within the bounds of reason: not extreme." (Webster's Third International Dictionary (1971)). Expect has been defined as including the following "to consider probable or certain." Id. Thus, pursuant to the common meaning of the statutory terms, training that "may reasonably be expected to" lead to a personnel action can be interpreted to mean a moderate probability that the training will result in some type of personnel action. Surely these definitions contemplate more than evidence of a relationship between the cancelled training and one's job. However, it is unnecessary to decide in this case how much evidence is sufficient to meet the statutory standard because the Special Counsel has offered only speculation as to how the lack of training could affect Livengood in the future. Based on the evidence of record it is impossible to determine whether nonattendance at the two-day symposium could reasonably be expected to lead to a personnel action involving Livengood. Accordingly, the Board finds, in agreement with the CALJ, that because the Special Counsel failed to show that Livengood was subjected to a personnel action as defined in 5 USCA 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix) there cannot be a section (b)(8) violation.

Shivaee v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 383, 387 (1997), expanded on the IRA coverage of allegations concerning training:

The appellant also claims that he was denied training for a period of 2 years. A denial of training may be a personnel action if the training might reasonably have been expected to have led to an appointment, promotion, performance evaluation, or other covered action. See 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix). The plain language of the statute establishes that not all training is included within the meaning of the statute. There must be, at a minimum, a moderate probability that the training would have resulted in some type of personnel action. See Special Counsel v. Hathaway, 49 MSPR 595, 607 (1991), aff'd, 981 F.2d 1237 (Fed. Cir. 1992). The appellant does not explain how the alleged denial of the unidentified training affected or could affect him, much less that there was a link between the alleged denial of training and a personnel action. Accordingly, we find that the alleged denial of training does not constitute a personnel action.

Simone v. Dept. of Treasury, 105 MSPR 120, 125 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 69 (2007), echoed Shivaee:

With regard to the issue of training, a personnel action may include "a decision concerning...education or training if the education or training may reasonably be expected to lead to an appointment, promotion, performance evaluation, or other [personnel] action [as defined by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)]." 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix). Thus, not all training is included within the meaning of the statute. Shivaee v. Department of the Navy, 74 M.S.P.R. 383, 387 (1997). "There must be, at a minimum, a moderate probability that the training would have resulted in some type of personnel action." Id. With regard to the training issue, the AJ simply concluded in the initial decision that "the appellant has not furnished any evidence that the denial of training in any way impacted upon his career so as to constitute a personnel action under the [WPA]." The appellant asserts on review that the AJ failed to give him proper jurisdictional notice regarding the training issue and that his submissions clearly demonstrate that the agency's denial of training could have led to his failure to be made permanent by "making [him] non-competitive relative to his co-workers...since [the] appellant asserted that those co-workers received...training [that]he did not." We agree and find that the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation that there was "a moderate probability that the training would have resulted in some type of personnel action." See Shivaee, 74 M.S.P.R. at 387.

The Federal Circuit added, Wagner v. EPA (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 92-3123):

Mr. Wagner presents a novel argument that EPA's preclusion of his training was part of a pattern of exclusion. This court does not rule out the chance that the cumulative effect of several agency actions, not separately rising to the level of a personnel action, might show a pattern of harassment in retaliation for whistleblowing. However, this record does not show such a pattern.

Salazar v. VA, 2022 MSPB 42, ¶¶ 28-29 (2022), determined that a delay in training qualified as a covered personnel action:

Concerning his delayed training, 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix) provides that "a decision concerning...training" is a personnel action "if the...training may reasonably be expected to lead to...[a] performance evaluation or other [personnel] action" described in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A). There must be, at a minimum, a moderate probability that the training would have resulted in, or avoided, some type of personnel action. Simone v. Department of the Treasury, 105 M.S.P.R. 120, ¶ 9 (2007); Shivaee v. Department of the Navy, 74 M.S.P.R. 383, 387 (1997). Here, that standard is met.

In March 2014, a Fleet Management Analyst from the agency's Veterans Affairs Central Office offered to provide 2-day on-site training to the appellant and others in May 2014. The training would have given "assistance and oversight of Fleet Management responsibilities, mandates, and ensure policies/procedures are met." The appellant wanted to participate because he believed the training would assist the department in meeting fleet management goals and provide him with an opportunity to meet his standards. The appellant's supervisor denied his request for the training at that time. The appellant did eventually receive the training in September 2014, the same month that the agency placed him on a PIP for unacceptable performance pertaining to fleet management, which ultimately led to his removal. The appellant's first-level supervisor testified that the appellant "continued to be unsuccessful" after taking the training in September 2014. Nonetheless, we find that at the time the appellant requested the training, it was possible that the training could have improved his performance, which may have made the PIP unnecessary. We find the delay of training in March 2014 was, therefore, a personnel action.
