a. Jurisdictional or Timeliness Issue

Many appeals involve disputes over whether the Board has jurisdiction to entertain the claim presented. The dispute is really over the sufficiency of the evidence demonstrating jurisdiction, and the Board's methodology of resolving the dispute is akin to summary judgment, although the Board will not denominate its process summary judgment because of language in the legislative history of the Reform Act suggesting summary judgment is not to be used in Board proceedings.

Under 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A) (2022), the appellant has the burden of proof, by preponderant evidence, on jurisdiction. When confronted with appeals involving jurisdictional questions, e.g., constructive adverse actions or whistleblower reprisal claims, some agencies move to dismiss the claims for lack of jurisdiction while they simultaneously move to stay discovery pending rulings on the jurisdictional motions. This stratagem effectively denies the appellant the evidence that the appellant may need to establish preponderant evidence of jurisdiction. Many AJs grant the agency motion, and then they issue initial decisions finding lack of jurisdiction, leaving it to the appellant to try to figure out how to persuade the Board through a petition for review that the AJ made a material procedural error during the processing of the appeal. The appellant protects himself by objecting to a delay in discovery and by attempting to persuade the AJ that discovery should proceed before jurisdictional issues are determined by the AJ.

The regulations do not distinguish the use of discovery as to development of information as to the merits of an action clearly within the jurisdiction of the Board, e.g., an adverse action, and an action that may, depending upon the state of the facts, be within the jurisdiction of the Board, e.g., a claimed forced retirement. Santana v. USPS, SF-0752-14-0801-I-1 (NP 4/14/2015) ("An appellant may request discovery of relevant materials to help him meet his burden of establishing the Board's jurisdiction. See, e.g., Trotter v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 282, ¶ 14 (2002), overruled on other grounds by Deida v. Department of the Navy, 110 M.S.P.R. 408 (2009); Russo v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 8 (1999)."). The fruits of discovery may be used to support jurisdictional motions. The Federal Circuit recognizes that the party asserting jurisdiction should be permitted discovery of facts demonstrating jurisdiction, at least when the facts are particularly within the knowledge of the opposing party. Barrett v. Nicholson, 466 F.3d 1038, 1042-43 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (on appeal from the Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims). A jurisdictional dispute, e.g., a claimed forced resignation, may benefit from discovery if the merits and the jurisdictional issue are intertwined. The availability of discovery on a jurisdictional issue will depend on whether the appeal states a nonfrivolous jurisdictional allegation or explains what evidence sought in discovery would lend support to the jurisdictional allegation. See Sommers v. USDA, 62 MSPR 519, 523 (1994). "The appellant's access to discoverable information is especially important when, as here, the agency is likely to be in sole possession of evidence of internal agency rules and regulations necessary for the appellant to support his case. See Russo v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 6 (1999)." McCauley v. VA, CH-3443-16-0581-I-1 (NP 2/9/2023).

If the AJ sets a response date for a jurisdictional show cause order, or the agency motion needs to be answered, and discovery is needed but not completed, the appellant will need to request an extension of the response deadline for a period of time needed to complete the discovery. See Dowie v. OPM, DC-3443-21-0672-I-1 (NP 9/30/2022) ("Given the clear notice from the administrative judge that the appeal might be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction and the information in the acknowledgment order advising the parties of the requirements for requesting an extension, it was incumbent on the appellant to inform the administrative judge that she wished to receive the agency's discovery responses before addressing the jurisdictional issue. See Szejner v. Office of Personnel Management, 99 M.S.P.R. 275, ¶ 5 (2005) (concluding that an appellant was precluded from raising the agency's failure to respond to discovery on review when he failed to file a motion to compel below), aff'd, 167 F. App'x 217 (Fed Cir. 2006)."); cf. West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 563 (1990) (noting the lack of objection to a motion, granted by the AJ, staying discovery pending a determination on the jurisdictional issue).

Parker v. DHUD, 106 MSPR 329, 332 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 187 (2007), remanded to an AJ who prematurely foreclosed discovery in an employment practices claim:

The concerns raised by the appellant on PFR also deserve consideration on remand. The appellant alleges that he started the discovery process, but the agency filed a motion to defer discovery proceedings that the AJ granted. He argues that he would have been able to establish the Board's jurisdiction if he had been given access to the materials he requested. An AJ has wide discretion over matters pertaining to discovery, and the Board will not reverse his rulings on discovery matters absent an abuse of discretion. Wagner v. Environmental Protection Agency, 54 M.S.P.R. 447, 452 (1992), aff'd, 996 F.2d 1236 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table); see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(4). An appellant may request discovery of relevant materials to help him meet his burden of establishing the Board's jurisdiction, however. See, e.g., Trotter v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 282, ¶ 14 (2002); Russo v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 8 (1999). Indeed, some of the appellant's requested items seem to relate to the issue of jurisdiction. Moreover, the appellant initiated discovery in timely fashion. Russo, 85 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 9. Thus, on remand the AJ should allow discovery that could lead to relevant evidence regarding the Board's jurisdiction under 5 C.F.R. § 300.104(a).

In an involuntary retirement appeal, Agee-Long v. GSA, SF-0752-17-0518-I-1 (NP 1/20/2023), the Board noted:

Further, on remand, the administrative judge should rule on the appellant's request to extend the period to initiate discovery. The appellant filed a timely request to extend this deadline. The administrative judge did not rule on that request below. The appellant is entitled to request discovery of relevant materials to assist her in meeting her burden of establishing the Board's jurisdiction. Russo v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 8 (1999). On remand, the administrative judge should determine whether the appellant still wishes to engage in the discovery process and set appropriate deadlines.

There may be required, as a condition precedent to discovery, basic allegations establishing a colorable claim in a constructive adverse action appeal, Shelborne v. MSPB, 223 Fed. Appx. 990, 994 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Ms. Shelborne also contends that the administrative judge erred by staying discovery and resolving the question of the Board's jurisdiction based solely on her pleadings. We disagree.

The administrative judge dismissed Ms. Shelborne's appeal not because Ms. Shelborne was unable to document her allegations of jurisdiction, but because Ms. Shelborne's allegations were not sufficient, even if true, to establish the Board's jurisdiction. It was not an abuse of discretion for the administrative judge, before ordering the parties to engage in discovery, to require Ms. Shelborne to allege particular conduct that was sufficiently coercive to cause a reasonable person to resign.

See Nasuti v. Dept. of State, 112 MSPR 587, 591 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 218 (2009) (IRA case: "[i]n the absence of nonfrivolous IRA jurisdictional allegations, however, the AJ was not required to grant the discovery motions."); Sobczak v. EPA, 64 MSPR 118, 122 (1994) (IRA case: "[t]he administrative judge properly denied discovery because the appellant failed to plead facts sufficient to establish jurisdiction."); cf. Clark v. Addressing jurisdiction as a preliminary matter is also made clear in the Order laying out the deadlines for discovery."); cf. Adams v. DOD, DC-3443-22-0386-I-1, n.3 (NP 10/4/2022) (suggesting if there is no jurisdiction over the appeal, there is no need to decide a discovery dispute on PFR).

If there is jurisdiction, as in a VEOA appeal, the AJ should not close the record when there are discovery issues without taking into account, in orders affecting case processing, the appellant's right to pursue discovery, Lohr v. DHHS, DC-3330-12-0153-I-1 (NP 12/7/2012):

Because the administrative judge found that the appellant established Board jurisdiction under VEOA, and because the administrative judge declined to hold a hearing as she indicated she would in her acknowledgment order, she was responsible for advising the parties that there would be no hearing, for setting a date on which the record would close, and for affording the parties an opportunity to make submissions regarding the merits of the appeal before the date the record would close. See Jarrard v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 11 (2010). The administrative judge did not do so in this case, and it was therefore not clear to the parties that they would have no further opportunity to develop the record on the merits. See id. The administrative judge's failure to notify the parties of when the record would close was especially problematic in light of the appellant's assertion that there were discovery matters still outstanding. See id. This procedural error prejudiced the appellant's substantive rights because there remains the factual issue of whether the agency filled all of the applicable vacancies using its direct hiring authority, and the appellant would have had an opportunity to resubmit her motion to compel discovery.
