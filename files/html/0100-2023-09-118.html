a. Cancelation of Action Appealed

If an appealable action is canceled, the appeal that arose out of the action is dismissed by the AJ for lack of jurisdiction. Curtis v. VA, 19 MSPR 519, 520 (1984) (appeal was moot when an adverse action was canceled through a settlement before the issuance of the initial decision); Hunt v. TVA, 18 MSPR 470, 471-72 (1983) (citing Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 322, 324-25, 3 MSPB 406 (1980)). "[T]he desirability of an adjudicatory determination of important questions, standing alone, does not prevent a case from being dismissed as moot." Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 322, 325 (1980) (overruled as to the ability of a party to file a petition for enforcement after an appeal was dismissed as moot, Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616, 2007 MSPB 234 (2007)). But see Stivers v. USPS, 9 MSPR 346, 347 (1981) (indirectly suggesting that if an agency immediately reissues the action and the case is "pending" as a result of a PFR from the initial decision closing out the first action, the Board retains jurisdiction rather requiring another appeal if the employee complains of the reissuance of the action in his PFR). A retroactive pay correction--following a reassignment and prior to the initiation of an appeal--leaves the appellant with no appealable action. See Burkwist v. Dept. of Transp., 27 MSPR 419, 420 (1985) (base-pay problems were corrected following a reassignment; the Board declined jurisdiction over an alleged pay reduction resulting from the inability to secure pay shift differentials and from increased health insurance costs); cf. Todd v. VA, DE-0752-16-0409-I-1 (NP 2/10/2023) (when agency action was canceled for lack of due process, harmful error claim was moot).

For the appeal to be dismissed as moot, the agency's rescission of the appealed action must be complete. The employee must be returned to the status quo ante and not left in a worse position because of the cancelation than were the matter adjudicated. Harris v. Dept. of Air Force, 96 MSPR 193, 195-96 ¶ 5 (2004). Currier v. USPS, 72 MSPR 191, 197 (1996), determined that an unresolved claim for compensatory damages for EEO claims avoids a mootness dismissal for full relief when the agency cancels an appealed action if an EEO affirmative defense claim remains viable, i.e., if it is not settled or withdrawn.

Fernandez v. DOJ, 105 MSPR 443, 446 ¶ 5, 2007 MSPB 99 (2007), describes how an AJ is to assess whether restoration of the status quo renders an action moot following its cancelation by the agency--the AJ must determine if the employee received all the relief that he could receive had the matter been favorably adjudicated:

...An agency's unilateral modification of its action after an appeal has been filed cannot divest the Board of jurisdiction unless the appellant consents to such divesture or the agency completely rescinds the action. Id. Thus, the Board may dismiss an appeal as moot if the appealed action is canceled or rescinded by the agency. Harris v. Department of the Air Force, 96 M.S.P.R. 193, ¶ 5 (2004). For the appeal to be deemed moot, however, the employee must have received all of the relief that he could have received "if the matter had been adjudicated and he had prevailed." Harris v. Department of Transportation, 96 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 8 (2004); see also Murray v. Department of Defense, 92 M.S.P.R. 361, ¶ 16 (2002); Gillespie v. Department of Defense, 90 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 7 (2001); Bolton v. Department of the Army, 79 M.S.P.R. 333, 338 (1998); Alleman v. Department of the Army, 79 M.S.P.R. 233, 238 (1998). If an appeal is not truly moot despite cancellation of the action under appeal, the proper remedy is for the Board to retain jurisdiction and to adjudicate the appeal on the merits. Hagan, 99 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 6.[1]

[1] In some decisions, the Board has said that an appeal is moot if, after cancellation of the action under appeal, the employee has been placed in the position he would have been in if the action had "never occurred." E.g., Henry v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 399, ¶ 9 (2005); see also Reed v. U.S. Postal Service, 98 M.S.P.R. 585, ¶ 7 (2005); Delaney v. Agency for International Development, 87 M.S.P.R. 688, ¶ 5 (2001); Joyner v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 473, 475 (1997); Ray v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 73 M.S.P.R. 321, 325 (1997); Rojas v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 400, 404 (1996). These decisions and others that frame the test for mootness in this way are OVERRULED because the correct focus is on whether there is any relief that the appellant could receive if the Board ruled in his favor. See Church of Scientology of California v. United States, 506 U.S. 9, 12 (1992); Board of Pardons v. Allen, 482 U.S. 369, 371 (1987); Powell v. McCormack, 395 U.S. 486, 496 (1969).

See Kibler v. Dept. of Army, DC-531D-15-0914-I-1 (NP 5/12/2016) ("The Board has held that for a WIGI denial appeal to be moot, an agency must return the appellant to the status quo ante by cancelling the action, paying him retroactively for the period of the denial, removing all references to the action from the employee's personnel record, and amending the ALOC determination and the underlying performance evaluation. See Shaishaa v. Department of the Army, 58 M.S.P.R. 450, 452-53 (1992); Veal v. Department of the Army, 52 M.S.P.R. 66, 68 (1991)."); Rosato v. Dept. of Army, 111 MSPR 95, 97 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 53 (2009) (appeal is not moot if the agency fails to pay the appellant all appropriate back pay; an agency's representation that it intends to pay does not constitute evidence that it has done so); Antonio v. Dept. of Air Force, 107 MSPR 626, 629-30 ¶¶ 9, 11, 2008 MSPB 9 (2007) (restating the principles expressed in Fernandez and noting that the current test is not whether the employee is placed, as nearly as possible in the same situation that he would have been in if the action had never occurred, but whether the appellant received all of the relief he could receive if the matter were adjudicated and he prevailed; also noting that, under Haskins v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 616 (2007) (overruling Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 322, 325 (1980)), incomplete restoration results in reinstatement of the appeal; the employee need not file an enforcement petition); cf. Holsey v. DOJ, AT-0752-13-0060-I-1 (NP 10/29/2013) ("Applying these principles to this case, we find that the administrative judge erred by dismissing the appeal as moot because: (1) she did not make a determination that the relief that the agency conceded was due to the appellant constituted all of the relief he could have received if the appeal had been adjudicated and he had prevailed; and (2) she did not require the agency to submit evidence establishing that it has actually afforded such relief. Because the record is insufficient to establish that the appeal is moot, a remand is necessary.").

Ordinarily the concern is the effect on the appeal of the agency's cancelation of the action appealed. The same result may occur if the action is canceled before appeal to the Board, Anderson v. USPS, 75 MSPR 494, 498 (1997), or following the action of another adjudicator, e.g., a labor arbitrator. See Mackelprang v. USPS, 48 MSPR 23, 25-26 (1991) (employee grieved and filed an EEO complaint concerning removal; the arbitrator ordered the removal canceled and the agency complied; the appellant filed with the Board after receiving the final agency EEO decision; there was no Board jurisdiction since the appealable action was rescinded before the Board appeal was filed); Gaines v. USPS, 43 MSPR 594, 597 (1990) (agency reduced the removal to a 14-day suspension prior to the appeal and following the appellant's use of a grievance procedure); cf. Cantrell v. DHHS, 55 MSPR 600, 603 (1992) (because an arbitrator's decision provided full relief to the appellant from the removal, there was no longer a live controversy for the Board; the remaining EEO allegations would have to be processed as a nonmixed case before EEOC); Roja v. Dept. of Navy, 53 MSPR 326, 328 (1992) (on appeal to the Board from the agency's cancelation of an EEO complaint following the appellant's refusal of full relief, the agency's complete rescission of the adverse action left the Board without jurisdiction to consider the appellant's allegations of discrimination and reprisal). "The Board lacks jurisdiction over an appeal when an otherwise appealable action has been rescinded before a Board appeal is filed, regardless of whether the rescission has occurred before or after a formal EEO complaint is filed with the agency." Martin v. USPS, 101 MSPR 634, 636 n.1, 2006 MSPB 112 (2006).

Application of the mootness doctrine is illustrated by Codrington v. DOJ, 1 MSPR 333, 1 MSPB 321 (1980). Appellant was removed and appealed, contending that he was not a probationer. The agency decided that the employee was correct and reinstated him to a tenured position. Because the agency did not inform the AJ of its action, the AJ ordered the agency to cancel the separation and restore the employee. The Board reopened the case, dismissing the appeal. Citing Gordon v. Blount, 336 F. Supp. 1271 (D.D.C. 1971), the Board concluded that because the employee was restored to duty with back pay, the relief sought was moot. Codrington, 1 MSPR at 335. There was no chance that the employee would again be treated as a probationer. In Slocum v. USPS, 107 MSPR 129, 2007 MSPB 253 (2007), the appeal was from what the appellant contended was a suspension resulting from enforced leave. After the appeal was filed, the agency retroactively placed Slocum on administrative leave for all but 14 days. The appeal was not moot. Jurisdiction was determined based on the facts when the appeal was filed, and when the appeal was filed, the appellant was in a nonpay status of more than 14 days (permitting him to appeal that status as an adverse action). Additionally, the agency had not demonstrated that it removed all references to the appellant's placement in a nonduty status from Slocum's personnel file. In a constructive adverse action case resulting from a retirement challenged as the product of misleading information, the cancelation of the retirement following the initiation of the appeal will not moot the appeal if the appellant lost pay (and has a resultant constructive suspension appeal), Chichowski v. FEMA, 72 MSPR 474, 477-78 (1996):

...The administrative judge found under Himmel v. Department of Justice, 6 MSPB 408, 6 MSPR 484 (1981), that there was no appealable action because the appellant's retirement was canceled at the time of his appeal. We grant the appellant's petition for review to decide whether Himmel controls. We conclude that it does not.

...

The rule set forth in Himmel is clearly designed to prevent the divestiture of Board jurisdiction through post-appeal manipulation of facts. That is not the case here. Rather, in this appeal, OPM's disallowance of the retirement application was the first notice the appellant received that the agency may have provided him with misinformation. Applying the Himmel rule in such circumstances would mean that an alleged involuntary retirement of the type at issue here, i.e., alleged detrimental reliance on purported agency misinformation, would never come within the Board's jurisdiction. This would be so because the disallowance of the retirement application and the ordering of his restoration, while giving the appellant notice of the involuntariness issue, would at the same time invariably divest the Board of jurisdiction based on a finding that the retirement had been canceled. The Himmel rule does not require the creation of such a "Catch 22" for appellants, and we decline to so extend it.

Application of the Himmel rule under the circumstances of this case is also contrary to Board precedent regarding involuntary retirements. In Bannister, the appellant submitted, on the basis of agency misinformation, an application for retirement benefits to be effective September 2, 1986. See 42 MSPR at 364-67. OPM denied the application and the agency retroactively restored the appellant to its rolls, placed him on LWOP status from September 3, 1986, until April 30, 1987, and made his retirement effective April 30, 1987. Id. at 364. The appellant filed an appeal with the Board on August 19, 1988, alleging that the agency's actions in retiring him on September 2, 1986, and placing him on LWOP status until April 30, 1987, were involuntary. Id. at 365.

The appellant in Bannister was retired at the time he filed his appeal with the Board, and his appeal was untimely filed, i.e., it was filed well beyond the time limit for appealing the allegedly involuntary retirement of September 2, 1986, and the subsequent LWOP action. See id. at 368. If the appellant in Bannister had timely filed his appeal, however, he would have been in the same situation as the appellant here, namely, he would not have been retired at the time of filing.

In Bannister, the Board found good cause for the delay in filing and found on the merits that the retirement was involuntary and the LWOP was a constructive suspension. See id. at 368-69. Neither the cancellation of the retirement from September 2, 1986, through April 30, 1987, during the time limit for filing an appeal, nor the appellant's status of being retired when he filed the late appeal was a factor on which the Board relied in deciding the jurisdictional issue. Similarly, we find those considerations should not be factors in determining the jurisdictional issue in this appeal. Thus, we conclude that even though the appellant's retirement had been canceled at the time he filed his appeal, this fact does not divest the Board of jurisdiction.

Jankowski v. USDA, 63 MSPR 596, 598-99 (1994), concluded that jurisdiction over a constructive adverse action was not defeated by a subsequently-granted retirement retroactive to a date before the contested resignation: "As long as the action was effected prior to the grant of retirement by OPM, the Board would have jurisdiction over an appeal of that action regardless of the effective date of the retirement."
