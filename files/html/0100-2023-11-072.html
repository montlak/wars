9. Interpretation of Collective Bargaining Contract

The Board is not too clear on what to do with evidence concerning the intent of a negotiated agreement. Poirier v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 214, 217 n.4 (1986), concluded that evidence is appropriate concerning the intent of drafters of the contract or concerning contractual application if the meaning of the contract is in dispute:

We make this finding with the recognition that the collective bargaining agreement is a distinct type of contract not susceptible to interpretation exclusively under common-law concepts governing private contracts. Instead, it is necessary to consider the entire employment relationship reflecting the common law of the particular industry. See United Steelworkers of America v. Warrior & Gulf Nav. Co., 363 U.S. 574, 578-79 (1960).

An AJ will need to construe a collective bargaining contract only as to ambiguous sections. The AJ must attempt to determine and carry out the mutual intent of the parties. If there is no meeting of the minds, if there is a failure to express intent with clarity, the AJ is considered free, as an arbitrator would be in the same circumstances, to apply the rule that produces the better result. Simmons v. SSA, 10 MSPR 295, 297 (1982) (considering a contract clause calling for progressive discipline with an exception for cases involving criminal or serious misconduct). In Dominguez v. Dept. of Air Force, 803 F.2d 680, 682 (Fed. Cir. 1986), the Federal Circuit added to the law concerning contract interpretation:

...Interpretation of a contract provision is, of course, a matter of law.... We are not bound by the view expressed of this witness, even assuming it to be true and admissible evidence. Where the language of the contract is clear, it requires no such evidence to interpret it. We think it is clear and that the evidence is inapplicable to the interpretation here....

See Reed v. Dept. of Transp., 76 MSPR 126, 135 (1997) ("Interpretation of a contract provision is a matter of law, and the views of negotiators on the meaning of a provision are not binding where the terms of the provision are clear.").
