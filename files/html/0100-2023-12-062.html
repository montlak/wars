14. Entry of Settlement Agreement Into Record

A settlement agreement may be entered into the record through a request to reopen an appeal closed out by a final decision. The request is to made within a reasonable period of time, generally measured in weeks rather than years. Smith v. SSA, 102 MSPR 167, 169, 2006 MSPB 104 (2006). In Cruz v. VA, 99 MSPR 450, 451-52 ¶¶ 2-5 (2005), the Board reopened the record to permit the entry into the record of a supplement to a settlement agreement:

The appellant filed a petition for appeal from the agency action removing her from the GS-2010-08 position of Inventory Management Specialist. The appeal was ultimately dismissed as settled, and the settlement agreement was entered into the record for enforcement purposes in an initial decision that became final on January 15, 2004. Then, on July 15, 2005, the appellant's attorney submitted the parties' joint request to reopen the appeal and to enter the "Amendment to Settlement Agreement of December 3, 2003" into the record for enforcement purposes.

Under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(e)(1)(B) and 5 C.F.R. § 1201.118, the Board has the discretionary authority to reopen an appeal in which a final decision has been rendered. Moriarty v. Rhode Island Air National Guard, 56 M.S.P.R. 144, 149 (1992). In deciding whether to exercise its discretionary authority to reopen an appeal and modify a final decision, the Board balances the desirability of finality with the public interest in achieving the right result. Fischer v. Department of Treasury, 97 M.S.P.R. 546, 549 (2004). Ordinarily, the Board will not grant a reopening request to cure an untimely filed petition for review. Johnson v. Department of the Air Force, 92 M.S.P.R. 370, 372 (2002).

The Board favors settlement agreements that serve to avoid unnecessary litigation and encourage the fair and speedy resolution of issues. Gizzarelli v. Department of the Army, 90 M.S.P.R. 269, 277 (2001). Here, the parties have jointly requested that the Board enter their agreed-upon modification of their original settlement agreement into the record so as to avoid future disputes over the terms of the original agreement and their compliance therewith. We find that, under these unique circumstances, the parties have established a sufficient basis for granting the reopening request. We find further that the parties have, in fact, entered into a modification of the original settlement, that they understand the terms, and that they want the Board to enforce those terms. We find, additionally, that the modification to the settlement agreement is lawful on its face, that the parties freely entered into it, and that the subject matter of the underlying appeal--removal of a permanent federal employee in the competitive service--is within the Board's jurisdiction under 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511-7513.

Accordingly, we accept the amendment to the settlement agreement into the record for enforcement purposes.

See Davis v. Corp. for National & Community Serv., DA-1221-13-0040-R-1 (NP 2/3/2015) ("The parties submitted a document entitled 'SETTLEMENT AGREEMENT' signed and dated by the appellant on November 21, 2014, and by the agency on November 24, 2014. The document provides, among other things, for the vacature of the Board's May 22, 2014 Final Order, the administrative judge's February 21, 2013 Initial Decision, and dismissal of the underlying individual right of action (IRA) appeal as settled."); Duffy v. USPS, 101 MSPR 453, 454 ¶ 5 (2006) ("The Board has held that a joint request to enter a settlement agreement into the record following issuance of a final Board order serves as a sufficient basis for granting a reopening request because the Board favors settlements in resolving future disputes"; accepting settlement agreement into the record for purposes of enforcement).

A settlement of a dispute over enforcement may be entered at the PFR stage. E.g., Roberts v. DOJ, DA-1221-18-0421-X-1 (NP 9/26/2022); Dalaki v. Dept. of Army, AT-1221-20-0232-W-1 (NP 9/26/2022) (reopening an appeal to accept an amendment to a settlement). A settlement reached in another forum, e.g., EEOC, may provide for dismissal of a PFR. Although the parties may not agree that the settlement will then be entered into the Board's record for purposes of enforcement, it will, when presented to the Board, lead to dismissal of the PFR as settled. The appellant may seek to avoid the effect of the settlement by demonstrating coercion, although the settlement survived that challenge in Obasogie v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-14-0012-I-1 (NP 6/2/2015).
