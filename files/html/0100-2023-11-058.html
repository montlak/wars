b. Hearing Held

Appellants may provide unsworn statements in the course of appellate submissions, for example, as exhibits submitted with prehearing submissions. Agencies often offer (and the Board accepts) written statements in the appeal response. Statements may also be offered as evidence during the Board hearing. Not all written statements receive equal weight. The Board concluded that an AJ did not err by refusing to accept an unsworn statement offered by the appellant at the time of the hearing. Its "probative value would have been minimal." Hall v. GSA, 21 MSPR 200, 203 (1984). Mahnken v. USPS, 34 MSPR 1, 5 (1987), determined that a AJ erred in giving determinative weight to an unsworn and uncorroborated statement from a supervisor. The agency did not explain why the witness was unavailable to testify at the hearing or why his statement could not have been obtained in affidavit form. See Tojino v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 620, 623 (1982) (Board discounted the agency's unsworn, hearsay, and partially unexplained evidence when it was compared with the appellant's sworn testimony).

The probative value of an unsworn statement is diminished by the failure of an agency to show the unavailability of the witness. The value of the statement is further diminished if it is obtained only for the purpose of litigation. Trachy v. Defense Communications Agency, 18 MSPR 317, 322 (1983); see Isza v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 139, 141 (1982) (no error when the AJ admitted the statement of a warehouse supervisor concerning the value of items allegedly stolen from the government; the witness died shortly before the hearing and there was no contradictory evidence). As to remarks during a hearing, Addivinola v. GSA, 33 MSPR 660, 661 (1987), gave no probative value to statements made by an appellant while he was cross-examining an agency witness; the statements would be considered only if offered under oath in the course of regular testimony.

In an appeal involving an employee charged with unauthorized administration of medication to a patient, the patient's unsworn statement was discredited when the agency could have obtained a sworn statement or produced the patient at a hearing, Wallace v. DHHS, 89 MSPR 178, 180-81 ¶¶ 6-7 (2001):

First, the agency asserted that it did not call patient Doe to testify because of its concerns for client privacy and the confidentiality and integrity of the Baltimore City Sexually Transmitted Disease (STD) program. Yet, the Board has procedures in place that would have allowed Doe to testify without revealing his identity to the public. Specifically, the Board's regulations allow the AJ to order a hearing, or any part of a hearing, closed when doing so would be in the best interests of the appellant, a witness, the public, or any other person affected by the proceeding. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.52. The agency also could have filed a motion to seal the record to the extent that portions of the record identified the patient. See, e.g., Coons v. Department of the Treasury, 85 M.S.P.R. 631 ¶¶ 23-29 (2000). The record shows that the appellant and her counsel did not object to measures to protect Doe's identity. Given the procedures that the agency could have used to allow Doe to testify at the hearing while still protecting his identity, the appellant's property interest in her continued employment outweighed Doe's, and the clinic's, privacy and confidentiality interests. Thus, Doe should have been considered available to testify. Consideration of the first Borninkhof factor, the availability of persons with first-hand knowledge to testify at the hearing, therefore, weighs against assigning probative value to both his written statement and his oral statements reported by the agency witnesses.

The second Borninkhof factor, whether the statements were signed or in affidavit form and whether anyone witnessed the signing, provides some support for assigning probative value to Doe's written statement because it was signed and one of the agency's witnesses, Colleen Crowley, Field Operation Manager for the agency, testified that she witnessed the signing. The third Borninkhof factor, the agency's explanation for failing to obtain sworn statements, weighs against assigning such probative value, however. According to the agency, Crowley decided against bringing Doe before a notary out of concerns for his privacy. Yet, even if it were impractical for Crowley to obtain a sworn statement from Doe, the agency could have asked Doe to prepare a declaration pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1746, which would have had the same force and effect as a sworn statement. The agency could have assisted Doe in preparing such a declaration.

Ballard-Collins v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-13-0617-I-1 (NP 3/24/2016), discounted a sworn statement when the declarant could have testified at the hearing:

...We recognize that J.B.'s statement prepared during the agency investigation was sworn and that weighs in the agency's favor. See Borninkhof, 5 M.S.P.R. at 87. However, the agency provided no explanation for its failure to call J.B. as a witness at the hearing, and it did not demonstrate, or even assert, that he was unavailable to testify. See Womack v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 285, 291-93 (considering an agency's failure to explain why it failed to call a witness to testify in determining that the witness's hearsay statement was insufficient to prove a charge by preponderant evidence), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). Furthermore, as the administrative judge correctly noted, the agency investigation concluded that there was a "significant acrimonious history" between the appellant and J.B. Thus, it does not appear that J.B. was a disinterested witness. See McDonald v. U.S. Postal Service, 20 M.S.P.R. 587, 589 (1984) (considering the fact that two witnesses were not disinterested in finding that their hearsay statements were insufficient to prove a charge against an appellant). Finally, J.B.'s statement is inconsistent with the appellant's live testimony in which she denied yelling at J.B. in a parking lot, or making the statement alleged in the specification.

See Cook v. VA, 13 MSPR 534, 536 (1982) (because the agency did not use MSPB procedures to secure the presence at hearing of patients who were witnesses and who saw alleged patient abuse, their unsworn statements were insufficient to overcome appellant's testimony when it was weighed against testimony of the only other witness who appeared at hearing to describe incident).
