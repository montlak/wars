a. Competence to Testify

The testimony of a witness must be relevant, that is, it must relate to the substance of the case. It must be material, that is, it should add something to the understanding of the case. The testimony must also be competent, that is, the witness must be legally and mentally capable of presenting the testimony. The Board addresses the subject of witness competency at pages 23-25 of its Hearings Procedures Guide (although the Guide has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook, it is still used for training and orientation):

G. WITNESS COMPETENCY

1. General Rule: Witnesses are generally presumed to be competent until the contrary is demonstrated.

a. Qualified Persons

Fed. R. Evid. 601 provides that "every person is competent to be a witness except as otherwise provided in these rules."

Thus, none of the following are per se disqualifying: age, Deutsch v. Dept. of Defense, 12 MSPR 404, 407 (1982); mental incapacity, United States v. Lightly, 677 F.2d 1027 (4th Cir. 1982); alcoholism or drug addiction, even if the witness is under the influence while testifying, United States v. Van Meerbeke, 548 F.2d 415 (2nd Cir. (1976), cert. denied, 430 U.S. 974 (1977); and conviction for a crime, marital relationship, or religious beliefs, Banks v. United States, 520 F.2d 627 (7th Cir. 1975).

b. General Requirements of Competency

A witness need only be able to:

(1) perceive and remember an event;

(2) have personal knowledge of it (Fed. R. Evid. 602);

(3) understand the duty to tell the truth;

(4) take an oath or affirmation to that effect (Fed. R. Evid. 603); and

(5) understand questions and respond to them in an understandable manner. See United States v. Lightly, 677 F.2d at 1028.

c. Disqualified Persons

A witness is disqualified from testifying, if the witness:

(1) is unable to communicate effectively with the trier of fact;

(2) is unable to either know, appreciate or understand, the duty to tell the truth; or

(3) lacks personal knowledge.

Fed. R. Evid. 602--a witness may not testify to a matter unless the witness has personal knowledge of the matter. See McGowan v. VA, 30 MSPR 221, 224 (1986) (witness who was not present when incidents on which charges were based occurred was not allowed to testify). See also Carter v. Dept. of Labor, 29 MSPR 500 (1985).

d. Attorney, Representative, or Administrative Judge as Witness

Fed. R. Evid. 605 disqualifies the judge presiding at trial from testifying as a witness. It is preferable that the AJ not testify. The roles of an advocate and a witness are inconsistent. Courts are generally reluctant to allow advocates participating in a trial to take the witness stand.

A lawyer should not be allowed to be called as a witness, unless the calling party establishes that:

(1) The contemplated testimony relates solely to an uncontested matter;

(2) the testimony will relate solely to a matter of formality and there is no reason to believe that substantial evidence will be offered in opposition to the testimony;

(3) the testimony will relate solely to the nature and value of legal services rendered in the case by the lawyer or his firm to the client;

(4) as to any matter, if refusal would work a substantial hardship on the client because of the distinctive value of the lawyer or his firm as counsel in the particular case. Code of Professional Responsibility, DR 5-1-1 & 102. See also Gajewski v. United States, 321 F.2d 261 (8th Cir. 1963) (attorney should not be called when no showing of necessity is made and the testimony or evidence could be procured from other sources).

Although not directly involving attorney/representative as witness, but addressing a closely aligned issue, the Board in Special Counsel v. Winfield, 18 MSPR 402, 407 (1983), held that testimony of agency legal officer, not acting as representative when he advised employee, did not violate attorney-client privilege.
