4. Statistics

Statistical evidence may suggest discrimination. Although the Board rarely relies on statistical evidence, in proving a disparate impact case, statistical evidence is competent and serves a role if the existence of discrimination is in doubt. Although statistical proof, standing alone, without specific evidence of discrimination, may be used to establish a prima facie case of discrimination, the usefulness of the evidence depends on all the circumstances. Drucker v. Dept. of Interior, 14 MSPR 436, 439 (1983) (statistical evidence was insufficient to establish a disparate impact); cf. Padilla v. VA, 6 MSPR 494, 498, 6 MSPB 416 (1981) (as to evidence that 39% of the work force, but not one supervisor, was Hispanic, "[t]he statistical evidence offered by appellant, while possibly indicative of discrimination as to promotions to supervisory positions, is insufficient to establish that the agency reasons for his removal were pretextual"). A promotion case, Shidaker v. Carlin, 782 F.2d 746, 749-50 (7th Cir. 1986), analyzed statistical evidence of disparate impact:

The district court incorrectly found that Shidaker failed to make a prima facie showing of discriminatory impact. Once Shidaker proved that the Postal Service promotes from within and introduced statistical evidence showing that there is a gross disparity between the percentage of women occupying lower level positions and upper level positions, she made a sufficient prima facie showing of discriminatory impact. She need not, as the district court apparently thought...make a further showing of the percentage of female applicants for upper level positions or the percentage of lower level female employees qualified for upper level positions.

To make a prima facie showing of discriminatory impact Shidaker need only present evidence that the Postal Service's promotion practices, although facially neutral, select promotees "in a significantly discriminatory pattern.... This evidence may be, and most often is, statistical in nature.... Only in very limited instances may a court find discriminatory impact from non-statistical evidence....

Gross disparity between the racial or gender composition of the relevant labor pool and the composition of the group occupying the positions in question can, by itself, make a prima facie case of disparate impact.... Where a company is shown to promote from within, the relevant labor pool for upper level positions is the group of employees from which promotees will be drawn.... Thus, where the plaintiff can show that the employer promotes from within, evidence of a gross disparity between the percentage of a minority in upper and lower level positions is sufficient to make a prima facie case of disparate impact....

Once a plaintiff makes a prima facie showing with statistics of this nature, the burden shifts to the defendant to rebut the inference of discrimination raised by these statistics.... Defendants may rebut a statistical prima facie showing of disparate impact with statistical evidence of their own that is "more refined, accurate and valid,"...or by showing that the challenged standards causing the discriminatory pattern are job related....

Thus, it is not up to Shidaker to proffer evidence of lower level female employees' qualifications or evidence of the percentage of lower level female employees who applied for upper level positions. The burden is now on the Postal Service to bring forward evidence that few women occupying lower level positions have applied for upper level positions or are qualified for upper level positions, or to bring forward evidence of the job-relatedness of its promotion standards.

Armstrong v. ITC, 74 MSPR 349, 356-57 (1997), a RIF appeal, added:

...To establish a prima facie case of discrimination based on disparate impact, an employee must identify the specific employment practice that is challenged as responsible for the statistical disparities and show that the application of this particular practice created the disparate impact. If the employee establishes a prima facie case, the employer must offer a business justification for its use of the practice. The dispositive issue is whether a challenged practice significantly serves the legitimate employment goals of the employer. If the employer meets this burden, the employee may still establish disparate impact by showing the availability of alternative practices that achieve the same business ends with less impact on the protected group. Stern v. FTC, 46 MSPR 328, 333 (1990).

In rejecting the union's claim of disparate impact, the arbitrator properly considered the agency's evidence showing that, prior to the RIF, 78% of the employees in administrative and support positions were black, and 81.4% were women, whereas, of the employees adversely affected by the RIF, 62.5% were black, and 68.8% were women. Thus, considering the percentage of women and black employees separated in the RIF, the record evidences that both categories were actually favored. Despite finding no disparate impact, the arbitrator nonetheless considered whether the agency had shown a business necessity for the "asserted" disparate impact. He concluded that it had done so through evidence that, although it needed to reduce personnel in order to achieve savings, management felt compelled to maintain its core of expertise. Although the arbitrator failed to address the union's position that the agency could have achieved greater savings through furloughs or by abolishing the positions of higher-graded employees, that omission does not mean that his decision is contrary to law, rule, or regulation....[5] In any event, the appellants have not shown that the agency, faced with decreased funding, would have been able to maintain its core of expertise to the same extent, had it adopted the alternative practices suggested by the union. See Griffin v. If the sample providing the statistics is too small, conclusions will not be drawn about discrimination from the statistical evidence alone. Decker v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 119, 134-35 (1989) (sample too small and inconclusive in RIF case when the sample did not compare actions against employees in comparable positions); Fuller v. DOJ, 4 MSPR 497, 499, 5 MSPB 31 (1981) (170 employees, eight black employees, four removals of nonprobationers during a period of years was a sample too small to permit conclusions of discrimination in a removal case). Fuller, supra, said of small samples:

Statistical evidence is competent evidence which serves an important role in cases in which the existence of discrimination is in doubt. International Brotherhood of Teamsters v. United States, 431 U.S. 324, 339, 97 S. Ct. 1843, 1856, 52 L. Ed.2d 396 (1977); Adams v. Reed, 567 F.2d 1283, 1287 (5th Cir. 1978). However, as with any other circumstantial evidence, "their usefulness depends on all the surrounding circumstances." Teamsters, 431 U.S. at 340. Although statistical proof, standing alone, without specific evidence of discrimination, may be used in certain instances to establish a prima facie case of employment discrimination, Hazlewood School District v. United States, 433 U.S. 299, 307-308, 97 S. Ct. 2736, 2741, 53 L. Ed.2d 768 (1977); Davis v. Califano, 613 F.2d 957, 962 (D.C. Cir. 1979); Kinsey v. First Regional Securities, Inc., 557 F.2d 830, 839 (D.C. Cir. 1977), statistical evidence which is drawn from an extremely small sample does not produce highly probative evidence. Teamsters, 431 U.S. at 339 n.20, 97 S. Ct. at 1856 n.20; Adams, 567 F.2d at 1283; Morita v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group, 541 F.2d 217 (9th Cir. 1976).

If the sample is small, the Board may examine the components of the sample to determine whether discrimination occurred on the basis of the experience of the individuals within the group that was analyzed, Bechtel v. OPM, 11 MSPR 309, 313-14 (1982):

The Board finds that intervenor is correct in claiming that statistical data, introduced for the purpose of showing a general pattern of discrimination by the agency in proposing the reassignments, is relevant to appellant's affirmative defense of discrimination. It is well established that statistical evidence may be introduced to show that the employer's articulated reason for taking an allegedly discriminatory action was in reality a pretext for prohibited discrimination. McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 804, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 1825, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). In this respect, the presiding official's statement to the effect that "analysis of other personnel actions of the agency is found to be beyond the scope of review by the Merit Systems Protection Board" is incorrect. We find the presiding official remedied this misstatement, however, by analyzing the statistical data anyhow.

The statistics introduced by appellant reveal that the six individuals who were reassigned were the six oldest employees at the agency's Atlanta Regional Office. All of those individuals were in the protected age group. However, as the presiding official noted, two of those individuals who were older than appellant were merely reassigned to the Atlanta Field Office, a change that involved no geographic dislocation. In addition, as noted above, the agency has presented evidence that indicates appellant's experience and skills were especially well suited to the position to which he was assigned. We concur, moreover, with the presiding official's finding that appellant has failed to offer any evidence to establish that individuals younger than appellant who possess the same skills were available for the position to which he was reassigned but were not selected. We conclude, therefore, that any inference of age discrimination which may arise from appellant's statistical evidence has been rebutted. See International Brotherhood of Teamsters v.
