7. Certification and Proof of Interim Relief

The agency files a certification of interim relief. If the appellant challenges whether interim relief has been provided, the agency is required to provide additional information, 5 CFR 1201.116 (2022):

Compliance with orders for interim relief.

(a) Certification of compliance. If the appellant was the prevailing party in the initial decision, and the decision granted the appellant interim relief, any petition for review or cross petition for review filed by the agency must be accompanied by a certification that the agency has complied with the interim relief order either by providing the required interim relief or by satisfying the requirements of 5 U.S.C. 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii) and (B).

(b) Challenge to certification. If the appellant challenges the agency's certification of compliance with the interim relief order, the Board will issue an order affording the agency the opportunity to submit evidence of its compliance. The appellant may respond to the agency's submission of evidence within 10 days after the date of service of the submission.

(c) Allegation of noncompliance in petition or cross petition for review. If an appellant or an intervenor files a petition or cross petition for review of an initial decision ordering interim relief and such petition includes a challenge to the agency's compliance with the interim relief order, upon order of the Board the agency must submit evidence that it has provided the interim relief required or that it has satisfied the requirements of 5 U.S.C. 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii) and (B).

(d) Request for dismissal for noncompliance with interim relief order. If the agency files a petition for review or a cross petition for review and has not provided required interim relief, the appellant may request dismissal of the agency's petition. Any such request must be filed with the Clerk of the Board within 25 days of the date of service of the agency's petition. A copy of the response must be served on the agency at the same time it is filed with the Board. The agency may respond with evidence and argument to the appellant's request to dismiss within 15 days of the date of service of the request. If the appellant files a motion to dismiss beyond the time limit, the Board will dismiss the motion as untimely unless the appellant shows that it is based on information not readily available before the close of the time limit.

(e) Effect of failure to show compliance with interim relief order. Failure by an agency to provide the certification required by paragraph (a) of this section with its petition or cross petition for review, or to provide evidence of compliance in response to a Board order in accordance with paragraphs (b), (c), or (d) of this section, may result in the dismissal of the agency's petition or cross petition for review.

(f) Back pay and attorney fees. Nothing in this section shall be construed to require any payment of back pay for the period preceding the date of the judge's initial decision or attorney fees before the decision of the Board becomes final.

(g) Allegations of noncompliance after a final decision is issued. If the initial decision granted the appellant interim relief, but the appellant is not the prevailing party in the final Board order disposing of a petition for review, and the appellant believes that the agency has not provided full interim relief, the appellant may file an enforcement petition with the regional office under 1201.182 of this part. The appellant must file this petition within 20 days of learning of the agency's failure to provide full interim relief. If the appellant prevails in the final Board order disposing of a petition for review, then any interim relief enforcement motion filed will be treated as a motion for enforcement of the final decision. Petitions under this subsection will be processed under 1201.183 of this part.

Bryant v. Dept. of Army, 2022 MSPB 1, ¶ 8 (2022), approved straightforward evidence of interim relief:

With its petition for review, the agency filed the email in which it instructed the appellant to return to duty, and a Standard Form 52 reflecting her reinstatement effective October 20, 2017. Id. at 12-15. The Board previously has found such evidence sufficient to establish compliance with an interim relief order. Caryl v. Department of Treasury, 53 M.S.P.R. 202, 206 (1992). Additionally, with her petition for enforcement, the appellant provided evidence that the agency was processing her back pay and benefits between October 20 and November 27, 2017, the date the agency instructed her to report to duty. Because there is no dispute that the agency was in the process of providing the appellant with this relief at the time it filed its petition for review, we deny the appellant's request for additional back pay and benefits, and consider the agency's petition for review.

Through 1999 an agency was required to submit proof of interim relief with its PFR. The agency was also supposed to provide a copy of an undue disruption notice to the AJ as well as to the appellant. In 1999 the Board amended its regulations, earlier noted, to require the agency to file a "certification of compliance" with its PFR or cross-PFR. If the appellant challenges the compliance certification, the Board issues an order permitting the agency the opportunity to submit evidence of compliance. The appellant may then respond to the agency's submission. The appellant may also challenge the agency's interim relief efforts if the appellant (or intervenor) submits her own PFR or cross-PFR from an initial decision ordering interim relief. It appears that the certification may be made as part of the PFR. See Bartram v. USPS, 93 MSPR 74, 76 n.1 (2002).

Should the agency fail to provide the certification or evidence of compliance in response to a Board order, the Board may dismiss the agency's PFR or cross-PFR. The Board's dismissal authority is discretionary, Rodriguez v. VA, DA-0752-13-0183-I-1 n.2 (NP 3/5/2014):

If an agency files a petition or cross petition for review and has not provided the interim relief ordered, the appellant may request dismissal of the agency's petition. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(d). Here, the agency has failed to provide the certification of compliance required under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(a). Under these circumstances, the Board has discretion to dismiss the agency's petition pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(e), but is not required to do so. In this instance, the appellant has not responded to the petition for review or challenged the agency's failure to provide a certification of compliance with the interim relief order. Under these circumstances, we exercise our discretion and do not dismiss the agency's petition for review for failure to submit a certificate of compliance. See Merino v. Department of Justice, 94 M.S.P.R. 632, ¶¶ 5-6 (2003).

The law that follows was developed when the Board's regulations called for submission of evidence of interim relief when the agency filed its PFR or cross-petition. The law remains applicable under the 1999 regulatory changes when the Board issues an order allowing the agency the opportunity to submit evidence of compliance.

Under the earlier regulation, and presumably under the 1999 regulatory revisions, it was insufficient for an agency to file a PFR without proof (now certification) of compliance, to assert in the PFR that the agency would comply (provide certification of compliance) with the order of the AJ requiring interim relief, and to then move for termination of interim relief. See Gonzalez v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 439, 441 (1995) ("[T]he agency states generally that it has advised the parties of its compliance with the interim-relief Order. However, the agency has not submitted any documentary evidence with its petition demonstrating that it has complied...and the unsupported statement of the agency's representative is insufficient to establish compliance."); Ward v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 26, 28 (1993) ("The petition states that '[t]he agency will comply with the AJ's order to provide the interim relief pending the Board's decision.' The agency has submitted no proof with its petition, however, that it has complied. Accordingly, we conclude that dismissal of the agency's petition for review is appropriate."); Hanner v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 113, 116 (1992) (agency established that it provided interim relief through its submissions concerning restoration of the appellant to nonduty pay status and concerning health benefit election forms; the appellant improperly asserted that the agency owed him full back pay from the date of the action); cf. Brooks v. VA, 53 MSPR 93, 94 (1992) (interim relief regulations required petitioning the agency to submit evidence of compliance with the interim relief order, or an undue disruption determination with evidence of the restoration of pay and benefits, within the time period for filing a timely PFR).

Agencies were expected to know and follow Board interim relief regulations. Ignorance of these regulations and resulting delay in the implementation of interim relief were not lightly excused, Green v. USPS, 68 MSPR 684, 687 (1995):

Here, the agency filed its petition for review on August 17, 1995, the deadline for filing the petition. The petition was not accompanied by any evidence or argument on the interim relief issue, however. On September 14, 1995, the agency submitted for the first time evidence pertaining to interim relief. In doing so, the agency representative contended that, until September 12, 1995, he was unaware of the Board's regulatory requirement that evidence of interim relief be submitted with the petition for review. He contended that he "did not have access to the usual research materials due to the temporary assignment away from the domicile location in Atlanta, GA," and that "[u]pon learning of the requirement," he promptly submitted the evidence.

The agency representative's general allegation that he was unaware of the applicable Board regulations does not establish good cause for the late submission. See Jones, 65 MSPR at 415; see also D'Aquin v. OPM, 65 MSPR 499, 505 (1994); Barber v. Dept. of Agric., 63 MSPR 32, 35 (1994). He does not specifically explain how his temporary relocation prevented him from learning of the Board's regulations or why he could not have requested an extension of time, if necessary. See D'Aquin, 65 MSPR at 505; Averella v. USPS, 64 MSPR 441, 443-44 (1994); Johnson v. VA, 64 MSPR 257, 259 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 81 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). He cites Avant v. Dept. of the Navy, 60 MSPR 467 (1994), in arguing that his "inadvertent" and "promptly corrected" omission should be excused. Avant is distinguishable, however, because the agency there timely submitted prima facie evidence of compliance with the interim relief order. Avant, 60 MSPR at 475.
