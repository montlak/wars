4. Offer of Proof

Under Joshua v. SSA, 2 MSPR 280, 282, 2 MSPB 444 (1980), to show that the exclusion of a witness constituted harmful error, the appellant must make clear the nature of the proposed testimony of that witness. An offer of proof is useful both during the prehearing process of identifying or requesting witnesses and during examination of witnesses while the hearing is underway. The offer of proof need not be in the form of affidavits; one may use statements or evidentiary summaries or other forms permitted by Fed. R. Evid. 103(a)(12). See Johnson v. Burnley, 887 F.2d 471 (4th Cir. 1989); Edmond v. TVA, 23 MSPR 489, 492 (1984) (description by counsel of proffered testimony was a sufficient offer of proof; the offer did not need to meet the formal requirements of Fed. R. Evid. 103(a)(2)); Tracy v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 115, 118 (1984) ("Moreover, appellant has made no showing that he made an appropriate offer of proof at any time during the proceeding before the Regional Office to demonstrate that the witness' testimony would be relevant and material.").

A formal offer of proof may not be required if the substance of the testimony or evidence sought is clear from the context of the questions asked, and if counsel makes some attempt to make the trial judge aware of the evidentiary basis for admitting the information. See Beech Aircraft Corp. v. Rainey, 109 S. Ct. 439, 452 (1988). The Judges' Handbook suggests as to offers of proof, at Chapter 10, section 14(a) [misnumbering appears on the MSPB website] :

Offers of Proof. When an objection to a question is sustained by the AJ and the testimony of the witness is therefore not admitted, the party asking the question, at the discretion of the AJ, may make an offer of proof on the question.

If examination of proffered testimony of excluded witnesses demonstrates relevance, the AJ should permit the testimony. An AJ erred by excluding witnesses whose testimony could have affected credibility determinations concerning a critical witness against the appellant. Perkins v. VA, 21 MSPR 58, 60 (1984) (issue apparently was whether a supervisor had provoked the appellant's insubordinate behavior). For the Board to review on a PFR a refusal by the AJ to grant a request to subpoena a witness, the PFR must name the witness, state the harm caused by the refusal, or show why the denial was erroneous. Tibbits v. VA, 5 MSPR 61, 63-64, 5 MSPB 136 (1981). In its PFR, one agency was apparently permitted to supplement with more specific evidence a broadly stated offer of proof made at the hearing concerning a prospective rebuttal witness. The decision suggests that the agency was not permitted to expand upon its offer during the course of the hearing. Clarke v. DHHS, 37 MSPR 63, 67 (1988).

If an adverse witness whose presence is requested and approved does not appear at the hearing, the denial of the witness amounts to a denial of an opportunity to confront an opposing party. There should then be no need for an offer of proof. An accurate proffer of the expected testimony would be impossible, unless a deposition was taken. One AJ declined to schedule a hearing so that the proposing official, called as a witness by the appellant, could be present for examination. There was no indication of an offer of proof by the appellant for that witness. The exclusion was error: no formal proffer was needed. The agency presented the proposing official's testimony through a written statement, and the proposing official had "unique information" concerning the agency's decision to suspend the appellant rather than to take an alternative action, e.g., to reassign him. The agency presented no other witnesses with that information. The appeal was remanded for the proposing official's testimony. Ikramullah v. GSA, 34 MSPR 425, 427-28 (1987).

When is an offer of proof made? If a witness has been denied following submission of a witness list, then a supplemental written offer of proof or description of proposed testimony, or further argument concerning the significance of the testimony, should be made, together with an objection to the ruling and a request for reconsideration of that ruling. The offer, ideally, should have been made as part of the prehearing submission; but the offer can be made during the prehearing conference if the AJ seems inclined to reject the witness; or the offer may be made with a request, submitted after the prehearing conference, for reconsideration of the exclusion of a witness. An offer of proof may be made during the hearing. If a witness is approved and begins to testify, but is then precluded from answering a question, the offer of proof should be made at the point that the objection to the question is sustained or interposed by the AJ. The offer of proof may then be written or verbal. If the proffer is verbal, care should be taken to ensure that it is made on the record. When a verbal offer is made during a hearing, good practice calls for the party making the offer (or the opposing party) to request that the AJ excuse the witness under examination at the time of the offer. With the witness out of the hearing room, the offer may be made without the desired answer or line of testimony being suggested to the witness through the offer of proof. The proffer should be followed by a request for reconsideration of the decision sustaining an objection to the witness or portion of testimony of the witness.
