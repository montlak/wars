4. All Charges but Not All Specifications Sustained; Partial Specification Sustained

If all charges are sustained but not all the supporting specifications are sustained, the Board determines whether the agency's penalty is within the realm of the reasonable--that is, whether the penalty is entitled to deference, Payne v. USPS, 72 MSPR 646, 650-51 (1996):

[W]hen all of the charges are sustained, but some of the specifications supporting the charge are not sustained[, t]he agency's penalty determination is based on the charged misconduct. It is well-settled that proof of only one specification supporting a charge is sufficient to sustain the charge. Stein, 57 MSPR at 438. Thus, the ultimate basis of the agency's penalty determination--i.e., the charge--continues to exist, even though some of the supporting specifications are not sustained. In such circumstances, the penalty determination made by the agency is a reliable standard to review. It is entitled to deference and should be reviewed to determine whether it is "within the parameters of reasonableness." Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313, 5 MSPR 280, 305-06 (1981).

Payne was followed in Simpkins v. USPS, 79 MSPR 603, 605 ¶¶ 3-4 (1998):

...Although the administrative judge found that the agency proved the charges, he found that the preponderance of the evidence supported only the first and third specifications. He mitigated the penalty to a 30-day suspension, finding that the appellant's demotion to a nonsupervisory position was outside the bounds of reasonableness. The agency filed a timely petition for review in which it alleges that the administrative judge incorrectly substituted his judgment for that of agency management....

In its petition for review, the agency does not challenge the administrative judge's findings concerning the charges or the specifications. When all of the agency's charges are sustained, but not all of the underlying specifications are sustained, the agency's penalty determination is entitled to deference and should be reviewed to determine whether it is within the parameters of reasonableness. Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 MSPR 646, 650 (1996). If the penalty is not reasonable, the Board applies a maximum reasonable penalty standard and will only mitigate the penalty to the extent necessary to bring it within these parameters. Id. at 651. However, in applying this standard, the Board must take into consideration the failure of the agency to sustain all of its supporting specifications. Id.

Brown v. DHS, SF-0752-14-0816-I-1 (NP 1/19/2016), summarized the approach when not all specifications are sustained:

When all of the agency's charges are sustained, but some of the underlying specifications are not sustained, the agency's penalty determination is entitled to deference and should be reviewed only to determine whether it is within the parameters of reasonableness. Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 646, 650 (1996). In applying this standard, the Board must take into consideration the failure of the agency to sustain all of its supporting specifications. Id. at 651. That failure may require, or contribute to, a finding that the agency's penalty is not reasonable. Id. In such a case, the Board will look for evidence showing that the agency would have imposed the same penalty for the sustained specifications. Laniewicz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 83 M.S.P.R. 477, ¶ 9 (1999). Nevertheless, the Board's function is not to displace management's responsibility or to decide what penalty it would impose but to assure that management's judgment has been properly exercised and that the penalty selected by the agency does not exceed the maximum limits of reasonableness. Parker v. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 510, ¶ 9, aff'd, 355 F. App'x 410 (Fed. Cir. 2009). Thus, the Board will modify a penalty only when it finds that the agency failed to weigh the relevant factors or that the penalty the agency imposed clearly exceeded the bounds of reasonableness. Id. If the agency's penalty is beyond the bounds of reasonableness, the Board will mitigate only to the extent necessary to bring it within the parameters of reasonableness. Id.

Laniewicz v. VA, 83 MSPR 477, 483 ¶ 9 (1999), relying on Payne, summarized the law governing determination of a penalty when all of the charges, but not all of the supporting specifications, are sustained:

When the Board sustains all of the agency's charges, but not all of the specifications, it will review the agency-imposed penalty to determine whether it is within the parameters of reasonableness. Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 MSPR 646, 650-51 (1996). If the agency's penalty is beyond reasonableness, the Board will mitigate only to the extent necessary to bring it within the parameters of reasonableness, i.e., the Board will apply a maximum reasonable penalty standard. Id. In applying this standard, the Board will consider the agency's failure to sustain all of its supporting specifications. Id. That failure may require, or contribute to, a finding that the agency's penalty is not reasonable. Id.; accord LaChance v. Devall, 178 F.3d 1246, 1260 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (when the Board sustains fewer than all of the charges, "the Board may mitigate to the maximum reasonable penalty so long as the agency has not indicated in either its final decision or during proceedings before the Board that it desires that a lesser penalty be imposed on fewer charges"). In such a case, the Board will look for evidence showing that the agency would have imposed the same penalty for the sustained specifications. Cf. Devall, 178 F.3d at 1259 (if the agency makes clear before the Board that it would have imposed the same penalty on the basis of the sustained charges that it chose on the basis of the combined charges, "the nexus between the charges actually proved and the agency's chosen penalty" is not severed, and the Board may mitigate only if it provides the "requisite justification").

See Gaudin v. Dept. of Treasury, 109 MSPR 301, 305-06 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 151 (2008) ("[w]hen all of the agency's charges are sustained, but some of the underlying specifications are not sustained, as in this case, the agency's penalty determination is entitled to deference and should be reviewed only to determine whether it is within the parameters of reasonableness; however, an agency's failure to sustain all of its supporting specifications may require, or contribute to, a finding that the agency's penalty is not reasonable."); Russo v. USPS, 284 F.3d 1304, 1310 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (when the charge was "unbecoming conduct" and the agency failed to sustain a component of the supporting specification that the manager's comment was racist, mitigation was appropriate when the agency did not state that it would have removed the appellant in the absence of a finding that he made racial slur); Luciano v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 335, 340 ¶ 13 (2001) (citing Laniewicz for the proposition that "[a]n agency's failure to sustain all of its supporting specifications may require a finding that the agency's penalty is unreasonable"); Beardsley v. DOD, 55 MSPR 504, 510 (1992) ("[t]he administrative judge found it likely that the deciding official would have imposed a lesser penalty if he had only sustained two specifications. However, as the agency correctly notes in its petition for review, the question of whether the deciding official would have made the same decision in light of the fact that some specifications were not sustained by the administrative judge is irrelevant. See Patterson v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 6, 8 (1984), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1058 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table). The Board must determine if the sustained specifications merit the penalty imposed by the agency. Thus, we find that the administrative judge erred in relying on what penalty the deciding official might have imposed had he sustained fewer specifications. Id.").

Parker v. USPS, 111 MSPR 510, 513-14 ¶¶ 6-9, 2009 MSPB 117 (2009), aff'd, Parker v. USPS, 355 Fed. Appx. 410 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), distinguished the failure of the agency to sustain all its charges from the agency's ability to sustain its charges but not all the supporting specifications:

...The administrative judge erred in finding that the agency's penalty determination is not entitled to deference. In so finding, the AJ improperly relied on Byers v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 89 M.S.P.R. 655, ¶ 20 (2001). Byers involved an appeal in which only one of two agency charges were sustained. Byers, 89 M.S.P.R. 655, ¶ 20. The Board in Byers, in finding that the agency determination was not entitled to defense, cited Lachance v. Devall, 178 F.3d 1246, 1260 (Fed. Cir. 1999). See Byers, 89 M.S.P.R. 655, ¶ 20. It noted that Devall held that "where the agency proves fewer than all of its charges, the Board may not independently determine a reasonable penalty; rather, unless the agency has indicated that it desires a lesser penalty to be imposed on fewer charges, the Board may mitigate to the maximum reasonable penalty if a careful balancing of the mitigating factors warrants, or the Board may impose the same penalty imposed by the agency based on a justification of that penalty as the maximum reasonable penalty after balancing those factors." Byers, 89 M.S.P.R. 655, ¶ 20.

The rule applied in Byers does not apply to the instant appeal. The notice of proposed adverse action at issue here set forth two separate charges, i.e., unsatisfactory performance and failure to follow instructions. The proposal notice then described two specifications, i.e., the route count specification and the clock rings specification, both of which the agency claimed demonstrated the appellant's unsatisfactory performance and failure to follow instructions. Unlike in Byers, the administrative judge here sustained both charges but simply did not sustain one of the two specifications--i.e., the specification involving the clock rings--under each of the two charges. Accordingly, the administrative judge's finding that the agency's penalty determination is not entitled to deference based on Byers was in error.

In a situation where the Board sustains all of the agency's charges, Devall provides that the Board may mitigate the agency's original penalty to the maximum reasonable penalty when it finds the agency's original penalty too severe. Devall, 178 F.3d at 1260. Moreover, when all of the agency's charges are sustained, but some of the underlying specifications are not sustained, the agency's penalty determination is entitled to deference and should be reviewed only to determine whether it is within the parameters of reasonableness. Groeber v. U.S. Postal Service, 84 M.S.P.R. 646, ¶ 14 (2000), aff'd, 13 Fed. Appx. 973 (Fed. Cir. 2001); Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 646, 650 (1996). In applying this standard, the Board must take into consideration the failure of the agency to sustain all of its supporting specifications. Payne, 72 M.S.P.R. at 651. That failure may require, or contribute to, a finding that the agency's penalty is not reasonable. Laniewicz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 83 M.S.P.R. 477, ¶ 9 (1999). In such a case, the Board will look for evidence showing that the agency would have imposed the same penalty for the sustained specification. Id.

Nevertheless, the Board's function is not to displace management's responsibility or to decide what penalty it would impose, but to assure that management's judgment has been properly exercised and that the penalty selected by the agency does not exceed the maximum limits of reasonableness. Alberto v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 M.S.P.R. 50, ¶ 7 (2004), aff'd, No. 05-3090, 2005 WL 1368150 (Fed. Cir. June 10, 2005). Thus, the Board will modify a penalty only when it finds that the agency failed to weigh the relevant factors or that the penalty the agency imposed clearly exceeded the bounds of reasonableness. Dunn, 96 M.S.P.R. 166, ¶ 10. If the agency's penalty is beyond the bounds of reasonableness, the Board will mitigate only to the extent necessary to bring it within the parameters of reasonableness. Id.; Groeber, 84 M.S.P.R. 646, ¶ 14; Payne, 72 M.S.P.R. at 650-51.

Proof of a part of a specification may support adverse action, SSA v. Shapiro, CB-7521-11-0024-T-1 (NP 3/4/2014):

An agency is required to prove only the essence of its charge and need not prove each factual specification supporting the charge. Hicks v. Department of the Treasury, 62 M.S.P.R. 71, 74 (1994), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1235 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Thus, when more than one event or factual specification supports a single charge, proof of one or more, but not all, of the supporting specifications is sufficient to sustain the charge. Burroughs v. Department of the Army, 918 F.2d 170, 172 (Fed. Cir. 1990). Similarly, a partly sustained specification is sufficient to sustain a charge. See Bowen v. Department of the Navy, 112 M.S.P.R. 607, ¶ 6 n.2 (2009), aff'd, 402 F. App'x 521 (Fed. Cir. 2010); Crawford v. Department of the Treasury, 56 M.S.P.R. 224, 232 (1993).
