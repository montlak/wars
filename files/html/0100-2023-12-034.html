a. Internal Board Guidance

MSPB Order 1060.17 (May 17, 1996) provides the Board's internal guidance on PFR Processing:

1. PROCESSING PETITION FOR REVIEW CASES. All office procedures and actions must comply with the following basic policies for processing petitions for review (PFRs).

a. Process Controls. Each step of the PFR process must be clearly documented when case files are transferred between offices to comply with basic standards of internal control and to provide a basis of organized review and revision of process steps as needed.

b. Case Files. Case files must be protected from loss or misplacement. Case related materials must be properly tabbed and indexed by the office to which they are assigned as required by Chapter 4 of the MSPB Records Manual. The Office of the Clerk (OCB) provides a revised index and appropriately numbered tab for each document received in OCB upon transmission to the action office. Access to case files by anyone, other than Board staff and the parties to the appeal, is restricted by the Privacy Act of 1974. Others seeking access to case files should be referred to OCB.

c. Time Standards. The Board strives to process PFRs within 110 days of receipt. The 110 days are allocated as follows: OCB 15 days; Appeals Counsel (OAC) 55 days; Board 39 days; and as a general rule, the OCB has 1 day to review and issue a final Board decision. Offices are allotted 15 days to prepare rewrites and advisory opinions requested by the Board. If a case is returned for a rewrite or advisory opinion to an office other than the office and/or attorney to which it was originally assigned, however, that office is allotted 30 days to respond. These standards do not consider exceptional or special situations such as unusually difficult cases, cases of first impression requiring extensive research and analysis, filing of cross petitions, or an unexpected large surge in cases which may require the extension of all time goals.

2. INCOMPLETE, ILLEGIBLE, AND UNTIMELY PETITIONS FOR REVIEW

a. Incomplete Petition. An unsigned petition, a petition that does not properly list on the certificate of service all of the parties to be served at their correct addresses, or a petition lacking a certificate of service is an incomplete petition.

(1) Unsigned Petition (5 CFR § 1201.114(a)). OCB retains an unsigned PFR and returns a copy of the signature page to the appellant along with a notice advising the appellant to sign and return the page within 15 days (30 days for an overseas location). The notice also (1) instructs the appellant to serve the other parties with the PFR; (2) informs the other parties that they may respond to the PFR within 25 days of the postmarked date that the appellant returns the signature page; and (3) advises the appellant that if the appellant does not properly respond, the PFR will not be processed and the initial decision will remain the final Board decision. A letter or envelope that transmits a petition and which has a mark, fingerprint, or handwritten name will satisfy the regulatory requirement for submission of a signed petition. Facsimile signatures are also acceptable.

(2) Deficient Certificate of Service (5 CFR § 1201.114(h)). When a PFR does not properly list on the certificate of service all of the parties to be served at their correct addresses, OCB serves the PFR as appropriate and advises the parties that all future filings must be served on all parties at their correct addresses.

(3) No Certificate of Service (5 CFR § 1201.114(h)). When a PFR does not include a certificate showing how and when the petition was served on the other parties, OCB dockets the PFR and acknowledges it with a letter informing the appellant that OCB has served the other parties. The letter also advises the appellant that future unserved filings may not be accepted.

b. Illegible PFRs. When an illegible PFR is received, OCB returns the PFR to the filing party. OCB's acknowledgment of the PFR advises the filing party to refile a legible petition within 15 days of the date of the OCB's notice (30 days for an overseas location), or 35 days from the date of the initial decision, whichever is later.

c. Untimely PFRs. When a late filed PFR is received, OCB issues a "show cause" notice or "Motion to Accept Filing as Timely or to Waive Time Limit" form. The filer must complete and return the form within 15 days of the date of OCB's notice. An appellant in an overseas location is allowed 30 days to respond to OCB's notice.

4. SIMULTANEOUS PROCESSING OF SELECTED CASES

a. Identification of Cases. Cases suitable for simultaneous processing are identified by OAC or OGC. The types of cases approved for simultaneous processing are:

(1) Timeliness, jurisdiction, and other PFR cases processed by short forms, and opinions and orders (O&Os), including:

(a) Cases requiring O&Os to rule on an additional motion or to correct a minor error in the initial decision (ID).

(b) O&Os dismissing a PFR as untimely, withdrawn, or settled; forwarding allegations of noncompliance to the appropriate regional office as a Petition for Enforcement; or correcting an erroneous notice of appeal rights in the ID.

(c) Cases where the disposition rests on credibility determinations or other findings of fact (as opposed to applications of law) and no serious challenge has been presented.

(d) Cases where the Petition for Review (PFR) is confined to a penalty determination, the Administrative Judge (AJ) sustained the agency's action, and the PFR simply reargues the Douglas factors already considered by the AJ.

(e) O&Os dismissing an agency's PFR for failure to submit evidence of interim relief and those that are mooted by complete cancellation of the agency's action.

(2) Other simple and routine cases requiring O&Os or modified orders, including:

(a) Certain other timeliness and jurisdiction cases;

(b) Philippine retirement cases;

(c) Compliance cases; and

(d) EEOC non-concurrence cases.

(3) Time-urgent cases, including Special Counsel requests for extensions of stays.

b. Preparing Packages. For each case identified for simultaneous processing, OAC or OGC prepares a set of key documents, as set forth in the attached Appendix, for each Board member.

c. Forwarding Cases to the Board. The OAC or OGC forwards each case package to the Board as soon as processing in OAC or OGC is complete.

d. Action by OCB Staff. OCB staff who work in the Office of the Board (OB) to perform case tracking for the Board members, as appropriate, assign these cases simultaneously to each Board member and forward the packages to the members as they are received. OCB staff retain the official file in OB until all vote sheets are executed by the members.

e. Processing Board Vote Sheets. After receiving signed vote sheets, OCB staff dispose of the duplicate packages, add the vote sheets to the official file, and issue the decision.

5. PROCESSING CASES WHEN THERE ARE ONLY TWO BOARD MEMBERS VOTING AND THEY ARE DIVIDED ON THE DISPOSITION OF THE CASE

If a Board member does not concur in the proposed order or opinion provided by OAC or OGC, that member recommends a rewrite instruction that is given to the other Board member to consider. The second Board member may agree with the recommended rewrite instruction, agree with the original proposal, or recommend other rewrite instructions. When the two Board members do not agree on the disposition of the case at this stage of the process, they will attempt to reconcile their differences. If a compromise cannot be reached, the initial decision, recommendation, or order under review is deemed the final decision or order of the Board and the Chairman may direct the issuance of an order to that effect. When the Board members are unable to decide a matter in a case which does not involve a decision, recommendation, or order, the Chairman may direct referral of the matter to an administrative judge or other official for final nonprecedential disposition.

The goal of processing PFRs in 110 days is a hope. The Board's FY 2011 Performance and Accountability Report reports that 6% of PFRs were processed within 110 days; 80% took more than 150 days to process; the average processing time was 213 days. Toward the end of 2013, processing time was at about the 300 day mark. In 2015 adjudication accelerated; many PFRs were decided within four or five months from the completion of the parties' submissions. In January of 2017, the Board lost its quorum, restored in March of 2022; when there were about 3.500 undecided PFRs--three years and more of work for the Board worthies to decide --and PFRs keep arriving at a clip of 50-60 a month.

During their consideration of appeals and PFRs, Board members occasionally disagree over some dispositions and seek revisions in the decisions (or results) recommended by the Office of Appeals Counsel. In 1996 the Board adopted a "Policy for Rewrite Instructions":

1. Personal Footnotes

No personal footnotes, even in a rewrite, shall be included in opinions and orders issued by the Board.

2. Minor Edits

Minor edits may be made without a rewrite instruction and require the signature of at least one other Board Member to be executed.

Consistent with current practice, minor edits agreed to by a majority do not require the recirculation of a case among the Board Members. However, cases containing such minor edits (excluding typographical or punctuation errors which will continue to go directly to the Clerk) with majority support shall be transferred for changes to OAC (instead of the current practice of sending directly to the Clerk of the Board). OAC will execute the changes and then transfer the case to the Clerk of the Board for issuance.

If proposed minor edits do not receive majority support, a Board Member may always alternatively seek to place such a proposal in a circulating rewrite.

3. Requests to OAC

Consistent with the longstanding Board framework[1]:

[1] Each Board member has the option to adopt the proposed Order received from OAC or OGC, not adopt the proposed order, or initiate a rewrite. Two of the three members must agree to adopt before the decision can be issued. If a member requests a rewrite, the file must be reviewed by the other members and one of them must concur in the request. If a rewrite is initiated, the file is sent to the appropriate office (OAC or OGC) and is returned to the member initiating the rewrite when done.

Desired changes in a proposed opinion and order must be placed in a rewrite instruction and circulated to the Board for a vote.

A Board member seeking to establish an alternative point of view through either a dissent, a separate concurring opinion, or an opinion concurring in result only may request the OAC to draft such materials. Of course, Board Members may continue to request an explanation or written memoranda regarding legal or factual issues in a case.

Board Members are encouraged to advise OAC of apparent mistakes by using the minor edits process outlined above.

A member may also issue a dissent, a separate concurring opinion, or an opinion concurring in the result only. The member may have such an opinion drafted by his/her own staff, by OAC [or by OGC].

A November 16, 1996, memorandum from Chairman Erdreich to the Vice Chair and Members added as to rewrites:

Each Board member has the option to adopt the proposed Order received from OAC or OGC, not adopt the proposed order, or initiate a rewrite. Two of the three members must agree to adopt before the decision can be Issued. If a member requests a rewrite, the file must be reviewed by the other members and one of them must concur in the request. If a rewrite is initiated, the file is sent to the appropriate office (OAC or OGC) and is returned to the member initiating the rewrite when done.

A member may also issue a dissent, a separate concurring opinion, or an opinion concurring in the result only. The member may have such an opinion drafted by his/her own staff, by OAC, or by OGC.
