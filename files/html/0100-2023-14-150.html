G. LEAVE WITHOUT PAY

The granting of leave without pay (LWOP) is a matter of administrative discretion; that discretion is appropriate for negotiation in an agreement. AFGE Local 1917 and INS, E. Region, 13 FLRA 77, 78 (1983); see AFGE Local 2058 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Independence Nat'l Park, 68 FLRA 676, 684 (2015) (Member Pizzella dissenting in part) (Provision 6) ("use of the word 'normally' preserves the Agency's discretion to disapprove LWOP requests based on the Agency's need for the requesting employee's services"); NFFE, Council of VA Locals and VA, 31 FLRA 360, 375 (1988) (same result as to use of sick leave; also discussing proposals related to LWOP as to other VA employees). But see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 723, 725-28 (1992) (nonnegotiable: Provision 12, for a grant of LWOP to hold union office, or for study or illness, unless "work demands leave no other reasonable alternatives"; also nonnegotiable Provision 13, that LWOP be granted in a "fair and objective" manner; the proposals were not advanced as arrangements).

A proposal was nonnegotiable that required management to grant annual leave or LWOP for injuries or disabling conditions, without regard to work needs. It interfered with management's right to assign work and determine when work assigned would be performed. AFGE Local 2263 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq., 1606th Air Base Wing (MAC), Kirtland AFB, 15 FLRA 580, 583 (1984). The union could not require management to grant LWOP to employees for educational purposes, or for recovery from illness or disability, pending OPM action on disability retirement applications, or for certain relocation purposes. The proposal was contrary to the right to assign work. It limited the circumstances permitting management to deny leave. NFFE Local 15 and Army Armament Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 19 FLRA 48, 48-50 (1985); see IAM and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 33 FLRA 711, 726-27 (1988) (Proposal 7 nonnegotiable: proposal mandated LWOP status for employees who were medically unable to fulfill their duties during pendency of OWCP or OPM disability claims). But see VA Staff Nurses Council, Local 5032 and VAMC, Wood, 29 FLRA 849, 866-67 (1987) (proposal was negotiable for Title 38 employees who applied for disability retirement to receive LWOP).

AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, HCFA, Baltimore, 44 FLRA 1405, 1526-27 (1992), determined to be nonnegotiable Proposal 33, requiring LWOP for illness or disability. FLRA noted the analysis of Campana v. Dept. of Navy, 873 F.2d 289, 291 (Fed. Cir. 1989), that although authorization of LWOP when medical excuses are involved is within an agency's administrative discretion, it must be reasonable under the circumstances. Proposal 33 was not an appropriate arrangement. The burden on management of absolute entitlement to leave was excessive. A proposal was nonnegotiable that required management to grant LWOP for union officials. Illinois Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, N. Chicago, 27 FLRA 714, 733-34 (1987). A proposal to require the agency to grant LWOP without regard to the necessity for the employee's services, a proposal apparently designed to permit employees to seek other jobs during the course of a RIF, was a nonnegotiable infringement on the right to assign work. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 18 FLRA 418, 423 (1985) (Proposal 3). [Refer in this chapter to "Pending Disability Retirement Decision," and refer to Chapter 3, "Official Time."]

The union may bargain for a proposal that both vests discretion in management to approve outside teaching assignments that do not conflict with work responsibilities and that calls on management to grant LWOP for time spent in those approved teaching assignments. NFFE, Council of VA Locals and VA, 31 FLRA 360, 394 (1988). Proposals are negotiable that allow employees to request LWOP in the same manner that they request annual leave for reporting absences for emergency reasons. The submissions of the union demonstrated that the proposal did not require management to grant the requests without regard to whether the employee submitting the request could be spared from his work assignment. IAM and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 33 FLRA 711, 724-25 (1988) (Proposal 6).
