IV. EMPLOYEES WHO MAY APPEAL; APPEALABLE ISSUES

5 CFR 1201.2 (2022) identifies three types of original jurisdiction actions within the Board's jurisdiction; 5 CFR 1201.3 (2022) identifies more than 20 actions within the Board's appellate jurisdiction. For each of these two dozen actions that may come before the Board, there is an individual who is generally an appellant, or occasionally a respondent (e.g., Office of Special Counsel disciplinary or Hatch Act case or an action against an administrative law judge). The cases that are most commonly before the Board, according to the one of the Board's annual reports, are: adverse action (47%); retirement appeals (20%); and termination of probationers (6%). The remaining appeals are spread across the Board, so to speak: reductions in force (1%); performance cases (2%); step increase denials and suitability appeals (1% each); Individual Right of Action appeals (whistleblowing reprisal) (4%); and "other appeals" amounting to 18%, comprising, most likely, USERRA, VEOA, reemployment, and other cases nestled among those enumerated at 5 CFR 1201.2-.3. Chapters of this Guide describe those actions, the jurisdictional requirements of those actions, and any service requirements of individuals who may appeal those actions. [Refer to Chapter 10 for reduction in force appeals; Chapter 13 for Individual Right of Action cases; Chapter 14 for retirement and restoration rights; and to this chapter for descriptions of the jurisdictional requisites of USERRA and VEOA appeals.]

As to employees who appeal, they are either in the competitive or excepted service, and appeal rights differ for employees in those groups. 5 USC 7511 defines the federal employees with appeal rights to the Board. Before a person acquires ordinary appeal rights as an employee in the competitive service, that employee must either survive a probationary period of a year (or two years for DOD employees, 10 USC 1599e) or be in a position that does not require probation. For an excepted service employee to have an appeal right, the individual must either have veterans preference eligibility, allowing an appeal after a year in the position, or the individual without veterans preference status must have served a two year trial period in the position. A competitive service employee who is terminated during probation has limited MSPB appeal rights, later discussed. Although there are other statutes and sources of Board jurisdiction that may not depend on an individual being a statutorily-defined "employee," the singular importance of being an "employee," a subject of much discussion below, was summarized for adverse actions, DeSantis v. Dept. of Transp., NY-0752-14-0074-I-1 n.3 (NP 10/10/2014):

Only an "employee," as defined under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75, can appeal to the Board from an adverse action such as a removal. Hartman v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 77 F.3d 1378, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 1996); Lamberson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 80 M.S.P.R. 648, ¶ 33 (1999); see 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511(a)(1), 7512(1).

Board jurisdiction in most appeals will be clear. The employee has survived a probationary or trial period and the case, if timely filed, is adjudicated on the merits if the personnel action appealed is within the Board's jurisdiction. A great deal of law has developed, however, in appeals when the issue is whether the appellant has sufficient time in the federal service to appeal, and resolution of that issue often depends on the nature, duration, and continuity of service in a federal position occupied prior to the position held by the appellant at the time of termination, suspension, downgrading, or other appealable action.

Although the word "employee" will be used in its general sense throughout this Guide, jurisdiction of the Board over the bulk of its cases depends on the status of an individual as an "employee" as that word is defined by statute. The basic definition of an "employee" is provided by 5 USC 7511. The statute, amended from time to time, is remarkably opaque, necessarily leading to decisions from the Board and the Federal Circuit that are remarkably unclear. Despite a quarter century and more of elucidating litigation, the law defining employees with Board appeal rights continues to evolve.

For disciplinary cases, the significance of the legal definition of "employee" is best understood from review of several interrelated sections of Title 5 of the United States Code. 5 USC 7513(d) states:

An employee against whom an action is taken under this section is entitled to appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board under section 7701 of this title.

The "action" taken under § 7513 is described at 5 USC 7513(a):

Under regulations prescribed by the Office of Personnel Management, an agency may take an action covered by this subchapter against an employee only for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service.

The reference to the "subchapter" is to Subchapter II of Chapter 75, Title 5, United States Code, entitled "Removal. Suspension for More Than 14 Days, Reduction in Grade or Pay, or Furlough for 30 Days or less.," and Subchapter II covers from 5 USC 7511-7514. The actions covered under Subchapter II are listed at 5 USC 7512:

This subchapter applies to--

(1) a removal;

(2) a suspension for more than 14 days;

(3) a reduction in grade;

(4) a reduction in pay; and

(5) a furlough of 30 days or less;

but does not apply to--

(A) a suspension or removal under section 7532 of this title,

(B) a reduction-in-force action under section 3502 of this title,

(C) the reduction in grade of a supervisor or manager who has not completed the probationary period under section 3321(a)(2) of this title if such reduction is to the grade held immediately before becoming such a supervisor or manager,

(D) a reduction in grade or removal under section 4303 of this title, or

(E) an action initiated under section 1215 or 7521 of this title.

(F) a suitability action taken by the Office under regulations prescribed by the Office, subject to the rules prescribed by the President under this title for the administration of the competitive service.

"Employee," in turn, as the term is used in Subchapter II, is defined at 5 USC 7511, along with some other terms used in statutes describing Board jurisdiction:

7511. Definitions; application

(a) For the purpose of this subchapter--

(1) "employee" means--

(A) an individual in the competitive service--

(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or

(ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less;

(B) a preference eligible in the excepted service who has completed 1 year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions--

(i) in an Executive agency; or

(ii) in the United States Postal Service or Postal Regulatory Commission; and

(C) an individual in the excepted service (other than a preference eligible)--

(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment pending conversion to the competitive service; or

(ii) who has completed 2 years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency under other than a temporary appointment limited to 2 years or less;

(2) "suspension" has the same meaning as set forth in section 7501(2) of this title;

(3) "grade" means a level of classification under a position classification system;

(4) "pay" means the rate of basic pay fixed by law or administrative action for the position held by an employee; and

(5) "furlough" means the placing of an employee in a temporary status without duties and pay because of lack of work or funds or other nondisciplinary reasons.

(b) This subchapter does not apply to an employee--

(1) whose appointment is made by and with the advice and consent of the Senate;

(2) whose position has been determined to be of a confidential, policy-determining, policy-making or policy-advocating character by--

(A) the President for a position that the President has excepted from the competitive service;

(B) the Office of Personnel Management for a position that the Office has excepted from the competitive service; or

(C) the President or the head of an agency for a position excepted from the competitive service by statute;

(3) whose appointment is made by the President;

(4) who is receiving an annuity from the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund, or the Foreign Service Retirement and Disability Fund, based on the service of such employee;

[(5) Repealed. Pub. L. 114-328, div. A, title V, § 512(c), Dec. 23, 2016, 130 Stat. 2112.]

(6) who is a member of the Foreign Service, as described in section 103 of the Foreign Service Act of 1980;

(7) whose position is within the Central Intelligence Agency or the General Accounting Office;

(8) whose position is within the United States Postal Service, the Postal Regulatory Commission, the Panama Canal Commission, the Tennessee Valley Authority, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, an intelligence component of the Department of Defense (as defined in section 1614 of Title 10), or an intelligence activity of a military department covered under subchapter I of chapter 83 of title 10, unless subsection (a)(1)(B) of this section or section 1005(a) of title 39 is the basis for this subchapter's applicability;

(9) who is described in section 5102(c)(11) of this title; or

(10) who holds a position within the Veterans Health Administration which has been excluded from the competitive service by or under a provision of title 38, unless such employee was appointed to such position under section 7401(3) of such title.

(c) The Office may provide for the application of this subchapter to any position or group of positions excepted from the competitive service by regulation of the Office which is not otherwise covered by this subchapter.

Review of the statute establishes or implies several terms or concepts that require definition: competitive service; excepted service; preference eligible; probationary period; trial period; continuous service; current continuous service; same or similar positions. These concepts will be subsequently developed.

Board jurisdictional requirements may vary depending on the type of appeal that is being filed. The appeal that is discussed below is the adverse action, most commonly removal, demotion, or suspension of more than 14 days, or a constructive adverse action (as well as indefinite suspension or enforced leave of similar duration or effect) described in Chapter 6.

To illustrate some jurisdictional variations, for a performance-based removal or reduction in grade under Chapter 43 of Title 5 of the United States Code (described in Chapter 9 of the Guide), the definition of an employee eligible to appeal to the Board is less complex. Under 5 USC 4303(e):

(e) Any employee who is--

(1) a preference eligible;

(2) in the competitive service; or

(3) in the excepted service and covered by subchapter II of chapter 75,

and who has been reduced in grade or removed under this section is entitled to appeal the action to the Merit Systems Protection Board under section 7701.

(f) This section does not apply to--

(1) the reduction to the grade previously held of a supervisor or manager who has not completed the probationary period under section 3321(a)(2) of this title,

(2) the reduction in grade or removal of an employee in the competitive service who is serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment or who has not completed 1 year of current continuous employment under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less,

(3) the reduction in grade or removal of an employee in the excepted service who has not completed 1 year of current continuous employment in the same or similar positions, or

(4) any removal or demotion under section 714 of title 38.

For a reduction in force (discussed in Chapter 10), governed principally by regulations of the Office of Personnel Management, Board appellate jurisdiction is established, subject to exclusion of certain personnel actions (5 CFR 351.202(c) (2022)) at--

5 CFR 351.202:

(a) Employees covered. Except as provided in paragraph (b) of this section, this part applies to each civilian employee in:

(1) The executive branch of the Federal Government; and

(2) Those parts of the Federal Government outside the executive branch which are subject by statute to competitive service requirements or are determined by the appropriate legislative or judicial administrative body to be covered hereunder. Coverage includes administrative law judges except as modified by part 930 of this chapter.

(b) Employees excluded. This part does not apply to an employee:

(1) In a position in the Senior Executive Service; or

(2) Whose appointment is required by Congress to be confirmed by, or made with the advice and consent of, the United States Senate, except a postmaster.

5 CFR 351.901 [RIF Appeals]

An employee who has been furloughed for more than 30 days, separated, or demoted by a reduction in force action may appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board.
