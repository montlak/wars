c. Implicit Disruption Determination; Strong Overriding Interest

Under 5 CFR 1201.111 (2022), if interim relief is ordered and the agency elects to place the employee on pay status but not return the employee to the job, the agency must make a determination that returning the appellant would be unduly disruptive:

(c) Interim relief.

(1) Under 5 U.S.C. 7701(b)(2), if the appellant is the prevailing party, the initial decision will provide appropriate interim relief to the appellant effective upon the date of the initial decision and remaining in effect until the date of the final order of the Board on any petition for review, unless the judge determines that the granting of interim relief is not appropriate. The agency may decline to return the appellant to his or her place of employment if it determines that the return or presence of the appellant will be unduly disruptive to the work environment. However, pay and benefits must be provided.

(2) An initial decision that orders interim relief shall include a section which will provide the appellant specific notice that the relief ordered in the decision must be provided by the agency effective as of the date of the decision if a party files a petition for review. If the relief ordered in the initial decision requires the agency to effect an appointment, the notice required by this section will so state, will specify the title and grade of the appointment, and will specifically advise the appellant of his right to receive pay and benefits while any petition for review is pending, even if the agency determines that the appellant's return to or presence in the workplace would be unduly disruptive.

When the agency declines to return the employee to work as a component of interim relief, under 5 CFR 1201.116 (2022):

(a) Certification of compliance. If the appellant was the prevailing party in the initial decision and the decision granted the appellant interim relief, any petition or cross petition for review filed by the agency must be accompanied by a certification that the agency has complied with the interim relief order either by providing the required interim relief or by satisfying the requirements of 5 U.S.C. 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii) and (B).

Under regulations predating the 1999 revisions, the interim relief process was more complex. Under 5 CFR 1201.115 (1999):

1201.115 Contents of petition for review.

...

(b)(1) If the appellant was the prevailing party in the initial decision, and the decision granted the appellant interim relief, any petition for review or cross petition for review filed by the agency must be accompanied by evidence that the agency has provided the interim relief required, except when the agency has made a determination as described in paragraph (b)(2) of this section. The agency may comply by submitting an SF 50 or SF 52, a letter from an agency official directing the appellant to return to work and informing the appellant of his or her reinstatement as of the date of the initial decision, or an affidavit or declaration specifying the manner of the agency's compliance. The interim relief must be effected retroactively to the date of the initial decision.

(2) Under 5 U.S.C. 7701(b)(2), if the initial decision provides interim relief which requires that the appellant be returned to his or her place of employment pending the outcome of any petition for review and the agency determines that the return or presence of the appellant will be unduly disruptive to the work environment, the agency must notify both the appellant and the judge in writing. The agency must also provide evidence of such notification to the Board at the time of filing a petition or cross petition for review. The evidence must show that the agency has provided that the appellant will receive appropriate pay, compensation, and all other benefits as terms and conditions of employment from the date of the initial decision until a final decision is issued.

With the caveat that the interim relief structure was considerably eased for agencies by the Board in 1999, but recognizing that the agency must still make a statutory undue disruption determination and at least certify what it is doing to the Board when it files a PFR, we note here the many decisions, most involving appeals decided before the 1999 regulatory revisions, addressing implicit undue disruption notifications.

If an agency placed an employee back to work in his original position as interim relief, but materially changed the duties, the agency was deemed to have acted properly and made an implicit undue disruption determination if it provided to the Board a "strong overriding interest" or "compelling reason" for assigning duties other than those assigned prior to the appellant's termination. Under Haebe v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 167, 175-76 ¶¶ 14-15 (1999) (rev'd on other grounds, Haebe v. DOJ, 288 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2002)):

It is well-established that when the Board reverses an unwarranted personnel action the appropriate final relief is for the appellant to be returned as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. See Sink v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 MSPR 628, 632 (1994). In appropriate circumstances, however, the Board has held that the right to be returned to the status quo ante is not unconditional and does not require perfect consistency between all aspects of the appellant's pre- and post-removal positions. See id. at 633 n.4. While the law generally requires that an employee be reinstated to the position from which he or she was removed, the Board has held that an exception exists where the agency establishes that it had a "strong overriding interest" or "compelling" reason for placing the employee in a different position. See Spates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 438, 442 (1996); Briggs v. National Council on Disability, 68 MSPR 296, 301 (1995); Coffey v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 533, 537, review dismissed, 22 F.3d 1106 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Nielson v. Department of the Navy, 32 MSPR 332, 337 (1987). We find that this standard for complying with a final Board order requiring reinstatement should apply in interim relief cases as well. Thus, an agency should be found in compliance if it can show that it had a strong overriding interest or compelling reason, for assigning duties other than those assigned prior to an appellant's termination. If an agency can make such a showing, we will find that it has made an implicit undue disruption determination.

Here, we find that the agency has satisfied its burden of making an undue disruption determination by putting forward a strong overriding interest and compelling reason for assigning alternative duties to the appellant. Interpreting the agency's response to the show-cause order and the appellant's reply together, we find that the agency is not allowing the appellant to perform certain duties he previously performed because it does not want him to have to testify in a criminal proceeding, given that it charged him with an offense involving dishonesty.[2] Although this evidence was submitted after the agency filed its petition for review, we find that there was good cause for the lateness because, as in Pursycki v. General Services Administration, 81 MSPR 188, 198-99 (1999), the agency did not have clear notice from the initial decision of its interim relief obligations.

Lednar v. SSA, 82 MSPR 364, 369 ¶ 10 (1999), provides an example of an implicit undue disruption determination. The agency placed appellant on interim relief, paid him, assigned him to administrative leave, but did not issue a letter to him or the Board making an explicit interim relief determination. The appellant was dismissed for rude behavior, misconduct aggravated by past discipline. The Board declined to dismiss the agency's PFR in Lednar, instead finding that the undue disruption determination was implicit.

...We further find that the agency has satisfied its burden of making an undue disruption determination by putting forward a strong overriding interest and compelling reason for placing the appellant on administrative leave. Given the references in the record to the appellant's established pattern of exhibiting rude and discourteous behavior towards the public and management, and, in light of the AJ's finding that the appellant has a mental impairment that affects how he interacts with others, the agency's May 19, 1998 letter to the appellant's representative indicating its willingness to help the appellant apply for disability retirement provides sufficient explanation for its placing him on administrative leave. The letter shows not only the agency's willingness to support, and provide assistance for, the appellant's application for disability retirement but reflects its view that he should not be in the workplace.

When the Board reverses an unwarranted personnel action, the appropriate final relief would generally be to return an appellant as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. See Haebe, 81 M.S.P.R. 167 ¶ 14; Sink v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 M.S.P.R. 628, 632 (1994). However, the Board has permitted an agency to place conditions upon an appellant's return to duty, to alter an appellant's duties or work schedule, or to place an appellant in a different position, where the agency can show a "strong overriding interest" or "compelling" reason for not returning an appellant to the status quo ante. See Haebe, 81 M.S.P.R. 167 ¶ 14; Sink, 65 M.S.P.R. at 632-33 & n.4. The Board has held that this standard for complying with a final order of reinstatement applies to an agency's compliance with an interim relief order as well. See Lednar v. Social Security Administration, 82 M.S.P.R. 364 ¶ 9 (1999); Purzycki v. General Services Administration, 81 M.S.P.R. 188 ¶ 9 (1999); Haebe, 81 M.S.P.R. 167 ¶ 14.

The agency contends that the appellant has performed tasks during the interim relief period that she has always performed at the close of the fiscal year. Further, the agency contends that it has a strong overriding interest or compelling reason for altering the appellant's fiduciary duties due to: (1) the nature of the appellant's serious misconduct and its resulting loss of trust in her; and (2) a major reorganization of several maintenance organizations (divisions) at the base, subsequent to the appellant's removal, that resulted in the realignment of the duties of several positions, including the appellant's position. The agency asserts that, based on these strong overriding interests or compelling reasons, it has shown that it made an implicit undue disruption determination and, thus, it was permitted to alter the appellant's duties.

Given the agency's acknowledgement that it restored the appellant to her "old position description," and has not yet assigned the appellant to another position or altered her duties as a result if its reorganization, we find that its reorganization has not caused, and thus does not provide a strong overriding interest or compelling reason for, its failure to fully restore her position duties. However, we agree with the agency that the nature of the appellant's serious misconduct, and the agency's justified loss of trust in her, provided the agency with a strong overriding interest or compelling reason for altering her duties. See Lednar, 82 M.S.P.R. 364 ¶ 10; Haebe, 81 M.S.P.R. 167 ¶¶ 14-15. The agency's evidence of a compelling reason for altering the appellant's duties satisfies its burden of showing that it made an undue disruption determination. See Lednar, 82 M.S.P.R. 364 ¶ 10; Purzycki, 81 M.S.P.R. 188 ¶ 14; Haebe, 81 M.S.P.R. 167 ¶ 13. Although the agency's evidence of its undue disruption determination is untimely, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(b)(1), we find that there is good cause for the agency's delayed evidentiary submission due to the confusing language in the initial decision's penalty determination regarding the relief to which the appellant is entitled. See Purzycki, 81 M.S.P.R. 188 ¶ 13; Haebe, 81 M.S.P.R. 167 ¶ 15. Thus, we find that the agency has shown that it has complied with the interim relief order.

We disagree.

If an administrative judge's interim relief order requires that the appellant "shall return or be present at the place of employment," an agency may "prevent" such return or presence only upon determining that the employee's return or presence would be unduly disruptive to the work environment, and communicating this determination to the employee. See 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii); 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(2). "'Return,' in this context, means return to the employee's former position and duties...." Heath v. Dept. of the Navy, 60 MSPR 183, 185 (1993) (citation omitted). The "only action not associated with a determination of undue disruption is returning the employee to his or her former position and duties at his or her former duty station." Id. If an undue disruption determination is required, but not made, an agency's petition for review will be dismissed. See DeLaughter v. USPS, 3 F.3d 1522 (Fed. Cir. 1993); Heath, 60 MSPR at 186.

Before his demotion, the appellant performed the full range of General Foreman duties, however categorized, by himself. Under the principles set forth above, and regardless of the management considerations that led the agency to decide to divide the General Foreman position into "operations" and "administration," the agency was required to do one of the following: Restore the appellant to his former General Foreman position, with the same duties that he previously performed; or make an undue disruption determination and timely communicate that determination to the appellant. Cf. Shay v. Dept. of Defense, 67 MSPR 366 (1995) (the agency was required to make an undue disruption determination and expressly notify the appellant and the Board of this determination, even though its decision to place the appellant in a position other than her former position pursuant to an interim relief order was based on the fact that her former position no longer existed). The agency did not restore the appellant to his former position and duties, nor did it make an undue disruption determination and communicate such determination to the appellant. See Manning v. Dept. of Defense, 62 MSPR 456, 458-59 (1994) (although an undue disruption determination need not take a particular form, the agency must at least inform the appellant that his or her return or presence in the workplace would excessively or in an unwarranted fashion cause disorder or turmoil to the normal course of the agency's operation).

See Schultz v. USPS, 70 MSPR 633, 639 (1996) (only action not associated with an undue disruption determination was returning the employee to his former position and duties at his former duty station; the fact that the agency told the appellant he was to take a fitness examination, standing alone, did not constitute an undue disruption determination).

If an administrative judge's interim relief order requires that the appellant "shall return or be present at the place of employment," an agency may "prevent" such return or presence only upon determining that the employee's return or presence would be unduly disruptive to the work environment, and communicating this determination to the employee. See 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii); 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(2). The Board has held that "return" in this context means return to the employee's former position and duties. Heath v. Dept. of the Navy, 60 MSPR 183, 185 (1993). The only action not associated with a determination of undue disruption is returning the employee to his former position and duties at his former duty station. Id. If an undue disruption determination is required, but not made, an agency's petition for review will be dismissed. See DeLaughter v. USPS, 3 F.3d 1522, 1525 (Fed. Cir. 1993); Heath, 60 MSPR at 186.

Before his demotion, the appellant performed the duties of an area supervisor, supervising a team of ATCSs. As set forth above, and regardless of the management considerations that led the agency to assign the appellant the duties of TMU supervisor, the agency was required to either restore him to his area supervisor position, with the same duties he previously performed, or make an undue disruption determination and timely communicate that determination to him. Cf. Shay v. Dept. of Defense, 67 MSPR 366 (1995) (the agency was required to make an undue disruption determination and expressly notify the appellant and the Board of this determination, even though its decision to place the appellant in a position other than her former position pursuant to an interim relief order was based on the fact that her former position no longer existed). Here, the agency did not restore the appellant to his former position and duties, nor did it make an undue disruption determination and communicate such determination to him.[2] See Johnson, 67 MSPR at 499.

In the past, the Board would review an agency's undue disruption determination to see if it was discriminatory, demeaning, or inherently unsafe, and if it were, it was deemed to be made in bad faith, resulting in dismissal of the agency's petition for review. However, the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has recently rejected such a bad faith review, and has limited the scope of the Board's review of an agency's undue disruption determination to determining whether the agency actually made an undue disruption determination and whether the employee has received appropriate pay and benefits. See King v. Jerome and MSPB, 42 F.3d 1371, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1994). In any event, this analysis is irrelevant to the instant case since the agency here did not make an undue disruption determination.

Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 427, 431-33 (1995), considering evidentiary submissions of the parties on the nature of the appellant's position following her restoration through interim relief:

The appellant alleged in her motion to dismiss the petition for review that the agency had not returned her to her GS-5 Lead Medical Clerk position with all of its previous duties. As evidence of that allegation, she submitted copies of a June 13, 1994 memorandum reassigning to other employees her responsibilities for the Composite Health Care System (CHCS), the agency's computer data system, and her duties as Front Desk Supervisor.

Because the agency made a prima facie showing of compliance, we accept the further evidence of compliance that it filed after the deadline for filing a petition for review in response to our show cause order. In that response, the agency explained that the June 13, 1994 memorandum was in effect while the appellant was removed and the agency had not filled her vacant position. According to the affidavits of the appellant's supervisors, Dr. Mary Haluszka and Petty Officer in Charge Fausto Munoz, the appellant was placed back in her original position and given all of the duties that she had performed previously. Haluszka further stated that, contrary to the appellant's allegation, the appellant was given all of the supervisor's computer codes necessary to perform those duties.

The appellant replied, stating that her duties were not the same and that she was effectively demoted because Munoz, who had been her subordinate employee, was now her supervisor. She also stated that the agency had changed her position description and elements and standards in her performance appraisal after she returned to work.

The agency explained that the chain of supervision in the Urology Department was the same and that Munoz is now the appellant's supervisor because he was promoted to replace Chief Frank Roycroft who had been the appellant's supervisor. The appellant's position description was changed to reflect the different title of her supervisor from Administrative Assistant to Petty Officer in Charge and to reflect the use of CHCS, a different system for entering data which the Urology Department instituted in 1993 before the appellant's removal. Her position description still indicates that she is the lead clerk among three civilian clerks and, at times, three enlisted personnel.

Our review of the evidence shows that the agency changed the appellant's performance standards to include a critical element that she would be required to enter telephone consults into CHCS and the log-book at the time of the telephone call and ensure that all telephone consults were entered into CHCS for input into the End of Day Report. Chief Roycroft testified at the hearing below that the procedure for handling and inputting telephone consults was changed during the period that the appellant was removed so that the procedure is fully automated. This element conforms with the change that was made and does not appear to have been directed specifically at the appellant. We find that this change is not a change in the appellant's duties requiring the agency to make an undue disruption determination. See Browning v. Dept. of the Army, 61 MSPR 232, 235-36 (1994).

The appellant has submitted copies of handwritten notes regarding the agency's compliance with the interim relief order, the appellant's handwritten account of her workday from October 31 to November 8, 1994, the appellant's handwritten explanation of her understanding of her job elements and performance standards which she apparently prepared under the agency's direction, and the agency's reply clarifying her job elements and performance standards. After considering these documents in the light of the other evidence of compliance, we conclude that they do not show that the agency failed to place the appellant in the Lead Medical Clerk position with all of its previous duties.

If during the period of interim relief the agency decided to first restore and then remove the appellant from the workplace, the agency was to make an undue disruption determination and provide the required notice of the determination. This situation arose in Wilson v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 287, 294-96 (1995), when a removal was reversed, the employer filed a PFR and returned the employee to the workplace during interim relief, and the employer subsequently (but during the interim relief period) placed the employee on "home duty" as it investigated a separate offense that led to a subsequent removal attempt:

This is a case of first impression. Where an agency opts to comply with an administrative judge's interim relief order, not by returning the appellant to his former position and duty station, but by placing him on administrative leave or returning him to duty in some alternative position or duty station or with restricted duties, the agency must first make a determination that the appellant's return or presence in the workplace would be unduly disruptive.... That fact pattern is explicitly covered by the WPA. 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii).

Further, although an undue disruption determination need not take any particular form, the agency must at least inform the appellant that his return or presence in the workplace would excessively or in an unwarranted fashion cause disorder or turmoil to the normal course of the agency's operation. Manning, 62 MSPR at 458-59. If an agency does not return the appellant to his former position and duty station but fails to make a determination that his return or presence would be unduly disruptive to the workplace, the Board must dismiss the agency's petition for review for failure to provide interim relief. See DeLaughter v. USPS, 3 F.3d 1522, 1524 (Fed. Cir. 1993); see also 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(4); Shaishaa, 60 MSPR at 362; Heath, 60 MSPR at 185-86.

This case is different from the above-cited cases. In those cases, the agency made an initial determination not to return the appellant to his former position and duties or duty station but to some other position or set of duties or duty station, or not to return him to the workplace at all. Here, the agency initially returned the appellant to his former position and duties, and only later decided to place him in home duty status with full pay, compensation, and benefits. However, we discern no reason under the facts and circumstances of this case to depart from the established rule that, unless the agency places and retains the appellant in his former position and duties, it must make an undue disruption determination. The Board has previously held that "the only action not associated with a determination of undue disruption is returning an employee to his or her former position and duties at his or her former duty station." Heath, 60 MSPR at 185.

This holding is consistent with the analogous case law noted above concerning an agency's decision to detail or reassign or restrict the duties of an employee on interim relief. Storm, 64 MSPR at 24-27; Manning, 62 MSPR at 458-60; Shaishaa, 60 MSPR at 361-63; Heath, 60 MSPR at 184-86. The agency explicitly contends that home duty assignments are not disciplinary and refers to the agency's "reassignment" of the appellant to home duty status. It avers that home duty status is not leave, but full duty status. Thus, we discern no reason why the appellant's placement in home duty status in this appeal, two weeks after the agency had returned him to his former position and duties, should be treated differently from cases in which appellants are immediately, upon return to duty, detailed or reassigned out of their former positions or duty stations while on interim relief. In those cases, an agency is required to make an undue disruption determination before refraining from returning the appellant to the employee's former position or duty station. See Storm, 64 MSPR at 26-27; Manning, 62 MSPR at 459-60; Shaishaa, 60 MSPR at 363-63; Heath, 60 MSPR at 185-86.

Under the unique circumstances of this case, we find that the agency has failed to show that it made an undue disruption determination before placing the appellant in home duty status. There is no evidence showing that the agency made an explicit determination that it considered the appellant's return or presence in the workplace to be unduly disruptive. See 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A). Likewise, there is insufficient evidence for us to find that the agency made a de facto, implicit undue disruption determination. The agency's letter to the appellant informing him of his placement in home duty status does not set forth the reasons for its decision. In her sworn declaration, Loggins-Mitchell states that the appellant was placed in home duty status "pending an investigation concerning allegations of attempted incitement of an illegal strike which occurred subsequent to his return to duty...." The agency also submitted the December 9, 1993 sworn declaration of Warden R.E. Holt in which he explains the agency's policy concerning home duty status, and states that, although he was not the warden at the time the appellant was placed in home duty status, he would also have placed the appellant in home duty status had he been the warden. He further states that the appellant was placed in home duty status because he allegedly attempted "to incite a sick out by the correctional staff" which occurred after his return to duty on interim relief. There is no indication in Holt's declaration that the agency informed the appellant of the explicit reasons for its decision to place him in home duty status at the time that it made that decision.

It is obvious from the appellant's pleadings that he was aware in June 1993 that he was placed in home duty status pending an investigation into allegations of new misconduct. It is also clear that the appellant later became aware that the investigation concerned his purported attempt to incite a sick out when the agency proposed his removal for that alleged misconduct in July 1993. However, we cannot assume, based solely on his statement in a pleading before the Board that the agency placed him in home duty status pending an investigation, that the agency informed him that it considered his continued presence at the workplace to be unduly disruptive or that the agency informed him of the specific reasons for its decision to place him in home duty status at the time that it made that decision. The agency bears the burden of proving that it made an undue disruption determination and that it communicated this to the appellant. 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(2); Manning, 62 MSPR at 459. There is no evidence showing that the agency met this obligation. Therefore, we are compelled to find that the agency has failed to show compliance with the administrative judge's interim relief order.

The agency was permitted some flexibility. An employee was placed on AWOL status for failing to comply with a detail to another office. The agency, having made an undue disruption determination predicating the detail, was not precluded by the AJ's interim relief order from placing the appellant in a nonpay AWOL status. Rutberg v. OSHRC, 78 MSPR 130, 135-36 (1998).
