9. Failure to Report Misconduct

Some agencies have regulations requiring employees to report observations of employee misconduct. In Grubka v. Dept. of Treasury, 858 F.2d 1570 (Fed. Cir. 1988), the court implied that a reporting regulation might violate the Fifth Amendment prohibition against self-incrimination if it were construed to be a chargeable offense that an employee did not turn himself in for serious misconduct. If the agency is to properly charge an employee with failure to report misconduct, the policy should be clear and the reporting mechanism clearly defined. See Asberry v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 603, 607-09 (1994); cf. Losada v. DOD, 601 Fed. Appx. 940, 943 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) (concerning disclosure of possible child abuse in DOD education system: "We see nothing in MacLean to suggest that agency employees cannot be disciplined for failing to adhere to applicable agency regulations requiring them to report misconduct through agency procedures in addition to their whistleblower disclosures through other channels."). If a regulation requires the reporting of assaults on employees that occur on duty and the supervisor is aware of an off-duty assault, no discipline should be taken for the failure to report even though reporting the off-duty assault may have been more prudent. The reporting requirement was not clearly defined. Batchelder v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 312, 315, 6 MSPB 266 (1981) ("While it may have been prudent to report the assault, in the absence of agency proof on the record of a clearly defined duty to report it, appellant's failure to do so cannot form the basis of an adverse action against him."); see Hayes v. USPS, 42 MSPR 651, 656 (1989) (failure to report a fight and threatened assault on an employee; there was a question of what reporting requirement the agency regulations imposed); cf. VA v. Chiappetta, CB-7521-16-0014-N-1 (NP 12/2/2022) (individual in an ALJ-equivalent position was removed for reasons that included his failure to take more active steps to prevent inappropriate use of government email facilities by colleagues of the judge who did not report to him; the judge was in a position of high responsibility and played some part in the objectionable email traffic); Lewis v. USDA, 268 Fed. Appx. 952 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (sustaining charge that a supervisor failed to prevent subordinates from performing work on outside business ventures on government time).

Initially, the Board determined that if the employee is questioned as to knowledge of misconduct by another and the employee provides false answers, adverse action may be taken against the employee for the misrepresentations made during the investigation. See Cogman v. DLA, 12 MSPR 569, 573 (1982). However, the obligation to report misconduct of another was modified in Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 186, 201-02 (1994). There the Board recognized that if the report of another's misconduct would implicate the appellant in misconduct, the requirement of self-implication would be contrary to Walsh v. VA, 62 MSPR 586, 594 (1994) (overruled by LaChance v. Erickson, 118 S. Ct. 753 (1998)) determining that an agency may not separately charge an employee with both misconduct and making false statements about the alleged misconduct. Under Barrett, the agency could not invoke 5 CFR 2635.101(b)(11) to charge an employee with failing to disclose waste, fraud, abuse, and corruption to appropriate authorities if the disclosure would implicate the appellant in wrongdoing. Given the result in LaChance v. Erickson, Cogman appears to represent the current state of the law; Barrett no longer appears to control. Note the provision of the Office of Government Ethics regulations at 5 CFR 2635.101(b)(11), "Employees shall disclose waste, fraud, abuse, and corruption to appropriate authorities."
