4. Witness Fees; Travel Allowances

The subpoenaed witness is entitled to a witness fee to provide testimony at a deposition or hearing. Proper service on a witness who is not a federal employee includes tender of the witness fee for each day's attendance at the proceeding and for the cost of the witness's travel (mileage). 5 CFR 1201.37(b) (2022). Fees are set by 28 USC 1821 ($40 per day plus defined expenses). Mileage allowances are set by GSA travel regulations. See 5 USC 5704. The check for the fee is to be provided when the subpoena is served and the return of service should verify payment of the prescribed fees. 5 CFR 1201.84 (2022). If the subpoena is not accompanied by proper fees, it will not be enforced by the Board. Alejandro v. Dept. of Transp., 13 MSPR 463, 464 (1982), provided the Board's analysis of the financial prerequisite to subpoena enforcement:

Board subpenas are enforceable in the United States District Courts pursuant to 5 USC § 1205(c). Subpena [sic] enforceability, however, depends initially on proper service of the subpena [sic] . The service of the subpenas addressed by this Order was incomplete in one crucial aspect. The appellant failed to tender a daily witness fee of $30 plus mileage to each subpenaed [sic] witness.

Witness fees in Board proceedings are governed by 5 USC § 1205(b)(3) which states,

Witnesses (whether appearing voluntarily or under subpena [sic] ) shall be paid the same fee and mileage allowances which are paid subpenaed [sic] witnesses in the courts of the United States.

This statutory requirement is further detailed in the Board's regulations at 5 CFR § 1201.37(b). That regulation provides that witnesses who are not Federal employees are entitled to the same fees as those paid to subpenaed [sic] witnesses under 28 USC § 1821. 5 CFR § 1201.37(b)(2). The Board's regulations further require that the mandated witness fees be tendered to the witness at the time the subpena [sic] is served unless the witness is appearing voluntarily. 5 CFR § 1201.37(b)(3).

The daily attendance fee established by 28 USC § 1821(b) is currently $30 per day. Travel fees are determined by the distance and mode of travel. 28 USC § 1821(c). The fees tendered with the four subpenas [sic] addressed by this order are listed on the proof of service forms and range between $5.30 and $7.76. Although the basis of the tendered fees is not listed on the proof of service forms, the amounts appear to be mileage fees and most definitely do not include the required $30 per day attendance fee.

Tender of attendance and mileage fees is a precondition to proper service under the Board's regulations at 5 CFR § 1201.37(b)(3). The fees tendered with the subpenas [sic] addressed by this order are insufficient to meet this requirement.

Accordingly, for the foregoing reasons, the motion for enforcement of subpenas [sic] is DENIED.

Under the regulation, the government need not pay witness fees in advance. 5 CFR 1201.37(c) (2022). The regulation does not say when those fees should be paid. In practice, agencies will have the witness sign a voucher for fees and expenses at the time the witness appears for the hearing or deposition. But as a practical matter, the agency may be hard pressed to secure voluntary compliance with a subpoena if it commands a person to travel hundreds or thousands of miles at his own expense. Common sense is useful. The regulation provides, 5 CFR 1201.37 (2022):

(a) Federal employees. Employees of a Federal agency or corporation testifying in any Board proceeding or making a statement for the record will be in official duty status and will not receive witness fees.

(b) Other witnesses. Other witnesses (whether appearing voluntarily or under subpoena) shall be paid the same fee and mileage allowances which are paid subpoenaed witnesses in the courts of the United States.

(c) Payment of witness fees and travel costs. The party requesting the presence of a witness must pay that witness' fees. Those fees must be paid or offered to the witness at the time the subpoena is served, or, if the witness appears voluntarily, at the time of appearance. A Federal agency or corporation is not required to pay or offer witness fees in advance.

(d) A witness who is denied the witness fees and travel costs required by paragraphs (b) and (c) of this section may file a written request that the judge order the party who requested the presence of the witness to provide such fees and travel costs. The judge will act on such a request promptly and, where warranted, will order the party to comply with the requirements of paragraphs (b) and (c) of this section.

(e) An order obtained under paragraph (d) of this section may be enforced as provided under subpart F of this part.

Added to the discussion of witness fees is the matter of payment of expenses for agency employees who must travel considerable distances away from what would be the hearing site to attend a deposition. Brumley v. Dept. of Transp., 46 MSPR 666, 681 (1991), suggested that it was appropriate to condition depositions arranged through subpoenas on payment by the appellant of the deponents' expenses. What is unclear is why subpoenas were necessary--the deponents were federal employees who should have been on duty status during their depositions and who should have provided depositions without the necessity for subpoenas:

...In granting the appellant's request for the subpoenas in question, the administrative law judge modified the terms of the appellant's request by requiring the appellant to pay the travel expenses of the deponents to Arkansas, take the depositions at their place of business, or take the depositions by telephone. The administrative law judge also authorized the administrative judge to issue the subpoenas and to designate the time and place for the appearance of the witnesses. In accordance with this order, the administrative judge issued the subpoenas in question, including the location options set by the administrative law judge.

We find that neither the administrative law judge nor the administrative judge erred in modifying the place for the requested depositions. The Board's administrative judges have broad authority in discovery matters and, absent a showing of abuse of such discretion, the Board will not substitute its judgment for that of the administrative judge with respect to discovery.... We do not find an abuse of such discretion in the present case. As mentioned above, the appellant resides in Arkansas and has limited finances, and the agency would incur great expense in sending the deponents, who work and reside in Alaska, to Arkansas for the depositions. Under these circumstances, the administrative law judge appropriately provided the appellant with several options for conducting the deposition, including a telephonic alternative. We find the imposed limitations reasonable under the circumstances. See 5 CFR § 1201.74(b)(2).
