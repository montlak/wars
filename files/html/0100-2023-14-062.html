e. Temporary Condition; Stress; Alcoholism

Misconduct caused by temporarily disabling conditions may be subject to rehabilitation considerations. The removal of an employee with eight years of service was reduced to a 30 day suspension for reporting under the influence of alcohol and for being unable to stand watch on his ship; the individual was under stress resulting from personal problems and sought help from an alcohol treatment center. His prospects for rehabilitation were promising. Lott v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 513, 515, 7 MSPB 367 (1981); Hamilton v. USPS, 84 MSPR 635, 644, 645 ¶¶ 19, 22 (1999) (as to a medical condition that was causing absences, "[a]n appellant's action in seeking treatment for his medical condition indicates a potential for rehabilitation"; yet the removal was reasonable because of extensive failures to meet attendance requirements and because the medical evidence did not demonstrate the absences would stop in future). If stress is relied on as a mitigating factor, the appellant must show that the charged misconduct was directly related to the stressful conditions he experienced. See Wynne v. VA, 75 MSPR 127, 136 (1997); cf. Ciguzis v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-15-0015-V-1 (NP 7/24/2015) ("Nevertheless, the appellant failed to state whether there were any treatments or therapies available to ameliorate her medical condition, whether she availed herself of those treatments or therapies, or whether her condition and the side effects of any prescription drug she might have been taking for her condition played a part in the charged conduct. Hamilton v. U.S. Postal Service, 84 M.S.P.R. 635, ¶ 19 (1999) (finding that seeking treatment for a medical condition that played a part in the charged misconduct indicates a rehabilitative potential). Therefore, although we reviewed the appellant's arguments about her medical condition, we find that they are insufficient to mitigate the chosen penalty. See Archerda v. Department of Defense, 121 M.S.P.R. 314, 321-29 (2014) (finding that the chosen penalty was warranted where the agency proved that the appellant failed to follow instructions; although the appellant showed that he suffered from Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, he did not provide sufficient documentation about his medical condition and did not establish that he was impaired by that condition).").

Although successful participation in a rehabilitation program established to assist troubled employees is not a guarantee against discipline, it is a mitigating factor. When prospects were good for the employee, as demonstrated by improvement following program participation, the Board reversed removal and substituted a short suspension; management's disregard of the potential for rehabilitation was unreasonable. Tactay v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 363, 368 (1986). But the Board may dismiss alcoholism as a mitigating factor if the penalty is justified by the seriousness of the offense, e.g., lengthy AWOL, and the appellant's prior disciplinary record. Huettner v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 472, 475 (1992); see Wyrick v. Dept. of Transp., SF-0752-12-0524-I-3 (NP 5/21/2014) ("The administrative judge further correctly noted that, in any event, alcoholism may not operate to mitigate the penalty when, as here, it is outweighed by the seriousness of the offense in relation to the appellant's position, his prior disciplinary record, and the fact that there is no reason to conclude that his failure to be fully truthful was caused by his use of alcohol. See Huettner v. Department of the Army, 54 M.S.P.R. 472, 475 (1992) (finding that the appellant's defense of alcoholism was outweighed by the seriousness of the offense and the appellant's prior disciplinary record)."); Benally v. Dept. of Interior, 71 MSPR 537, 542 (1996) (removal was sustained of an employee for the loss of a driver's license following a charge of driving while intoxicated; it was immaterial that by the time the Board's initial decision was issued, only a short amount of time remained on the license suspension). If an agency is unaware of an appellant's medical condition, its failure to accommodate the appellant by reassignment is not harassment or discrimination. Spiegel v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 31, 34, 6 MSPB 40 (1981).
