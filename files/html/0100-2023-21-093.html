25. Calculation of Annuity

The Board addressed FERS disability annuity calculations, with consideration of a period of part-time employment, Siefring v. OPM, 94 MSPR 547, 549-51 ¶¶ 4-12 (2003):

Two sections of the FERS statute are implicated in this appeal. The first is 5 U.S.C. § 8415. That section governs the computation of an annuity under Subchapter II of Chapter 84 of Title 5 of the United States Code. Subchapter II applies to a "basic annuity," which means an immediate or deferred annuity. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8412 and 8413.

The second section of the FERS statute at issue is 5 U.S.C. § 8452. That section governs the computation of an annuity under Subchapter V of Chapter 84. Subchapter V applies to disability benefits. The appellant receives a disability benefit. Thus, Subchapter V applies to the appellant's retirement benefit unless otherwise provided by statute.

The way in which a basic annuity is computed under section 8415 of Subchapter II differs from the way in which a disability benefit is computed under section 8452 of Subchapter V. To compute a basic annuity, "1 percent of [an] individual's average pay [is] multiplied by such individual's total service." 5 U.S.C. § 8415(a). In contrast, a disability annuity "shall be equal to 60 percent of the annuitant's average pay" for the first twelve months of the annuity, and "shall be equal to 40 percent of the annuitant's average pay" thereafter. Id. § 8452(a)(1)(A), (B). For both computations, "average pay" is defined as "the largest annual rate resulting from averaging an employee's or Member's rates of basic pay in effect over any 3 consecutive years of service [the "high-3 years"]." 5 U.S.C. § 8401(3).

In calculating the appellant's disability annuity, OPM took into account the fact that the appellant had served for approximately one year in a part-time capacity during her high-3 years, and prorated her annuity downward to so reflect. Specifically, OPM applied 5 U.S.C. § 8415(e)(1), which requires proration of a retiring employee's total service to reflect the proportion of that service that was performed on a part-time basis. The appellant contends that this was error because section 8415 and the related regulatory provisions relied on by OPM are inapplicable to disability retirement. In its response to the petition for review, OPM states that its reliance on section 8415 was error. We agree with the appellant and OPM that OPM incorrectly used section 8415 to determine the amount of the appellant's disability benefit. As fully explained below, however, there is one statutory provision which can make section 8415 applicable to the appellant's disability benefit under certain conditions.[*]

[*] The administrative judge's conclusion that 5 U.S.C. § 8415(e)(1) is applicable to disability annuities was based on Roman v. Central Intelligence Agency, 297 F.3d 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2002). While it is true that the annuitant in Roman did initially receive a disability annuity, the appeal in that case was brought by the survivor of the annuitant. Roman, 297 F.3d at 1364. To compute the annuity for a survivor whose spouse dies before age 62, as was the case in Roman, OPM must first determine the amount of the annuity to which the disability annuitant would have been entitled under 5 U.S.C. § 8452(b) had he turned 62 on the day before he died. 5 U.S.C. § 8442(g)(2)(B)(i), (ii)(I). When a disability annuitant turns 62, which the present appellant has not yet done, her annuity is redetermined to "be equal to the amount of the annuity to which the annuitant would be entitled under section 8415." 5 U.S.C. § 8452(b)(2)(A). Accordingly, the reference to 5 U.S.C. § 8415 in Roman is inapposite to the present case; this case involves the computation of a disability annuity owed an annuitant who has not yet turned 62, whereas Roman involved the annuity owed the survivor of a disability annuitant.

Although the appellant argues that OPM mistakenly used section 8415 to compute her disability retirement, she contends that one part of section 8415 applies. She alleges that 5 U.S.C. § 8415(e)(1)(A) should be used to determine her "average pay" for her high-3 years of service.

Section 8415(e)(1)(A) states that "the average pay of the employee, to the extent that it includes pay for service performed in any position on a part-time basis, shall be determined by using the annual rate of basic pay that would be payable for full-time service in the position." [Emphasis added.] The appellant therefore wants OPM to determine her average pay for her high-3 years based on a percentage of what she would have earned as a full-time employee, rather than on a percentage of what she actually earned.

Section 8452 clearly states that the amount of a disability benefit shall be based on a percentage of the annuitant's average pay, that is, the pay actually earned. OPM's implementing regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 844.102 comport with the plain language of section 8452. Section 844.102 defines "basic pay" for the computation of a FERS disability annuity as "the pay an employee receives that is subject to deductions under FERS." [Emphasis added.] This regulatory provision has been in effect for over twelve years. Where an administrative interpretation of a statute is consistent and longstanding, it is particularly worthy of deference. DeJesus v. Office of Personnel Management, 63 M.S.P.R. 586, 592 (1994), aff'd, 62 F.3d 1431 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), cert. denied, 517 U.S. 1138 (1996). Thus, to the extent the appellant was in part-time service during her high-3 years, she received part-time pay, and the basic pay used to calculate her average pay and her resulting disability annuity should so reflect. The rules of statutory construction further support this result.

"[w]here Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another section of the same Act, it is generally presumed that Congress acts intentionally and purposely in the disparate inclusion or exclusion" Duncan v. Walker, 533 U.S. 167, 173 (2001). In Section 8415(e)(1)(A), Congress expressly mandated that, where an employee works part-time, her average pay for purposes of a basic annuity "shall be determined by using the annual rate of basic pay that would be payable for full-time service in the position." No comparable provision is contained in section 8452. The absence of such language in section 8452 shows that Congress intended for disability annuities to be computed using an average of the pay actually received by the annuitant in her high-3 years of service. There is, however, a statutory provision which may entitle the appellant to an annuity which is computed under section 8415.

Section 8452(d)(1) provides that the amount of a FERS disability annuity, after a reduction for any Social Security disability benefits, "shall not be less than the amount of an annuity computed under section 8415 (excluding subsection (f) of such section)." Subsection (f) of section 8415 pertains to reductions based on the age of a person receiving a basic annuity. OPM's regulation at 5 C.F.R. § 844.303, which is entitled "minimum disability annuity," states that notwithstanding any other provision relating to the computation of a disability benefit, a disability annuity "cannot be less than the amount of an annuity computed under 5 U.S.C. 8415 (excluding subsection (f) of that section) based on the annuitant's service." Thus, under the statute and the regulation, the appellant would be entitled to a disability retirement computed under 5 U.S.C. § 8415 if the application of that section results in a benefit amount that is greater than the amount of her benefit as calculated under 5 U.S.C. § 8452.

Of deductibility of Social Security benefits from a FERS disability annuity, Hunter v. OPM, SF-0845-15-0711-I-1 (NP 8/19/2016), explained:

A FERS disability annuity must be reduced by the Social Security disability amount to which the annuitant is entitled, as determined by the Social Security Administration (SSA). 5 U.S.C. § 8452(a)(2); Maxwell v. Office of Personnel Management, 78 M.S.P.R. 350, 355 (1998), overruled on other grounds by Conner v. Office of Personnel Management, 120 M.S.P.R. 670 (2014); Johnston v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 109, 112-17, aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table); 5 C.F.R. § 844.302(b)(2). During the first 12 months after a FERS annuity commences, the FERS annuity is reduced by 100% of the Social Security disability benefit to which the annuitant is entitled during that period; thereafter, the FERS annuity is reduced by 60% of the Social Security disability benefit to which the annuitant is entitled during that period. 5 U.S.C. § 8452(a)(2)(A); Maxwell, 78 M.S.P.R. at 355; 5 C.F.R. § 844.302(b)(2), (c)(2). The Board has found that OPM is correct in determining that it is required to reduce the appellant's FERS annuity based on the annuitant's entitlement, rather than actual receipt, of Social Security benefits. See Maxwell, 78 M.S.P.R. at 355; cf. Hicks v. Office of Personnel Management, 44 M.S.P.R. 340, 343 (1990) (observing that there is no requirement that the appellant actually receive Social Security benefits before his annuity will be offset under 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j)).

The Board reviewed the relevant statutory and regulatory provisions and concluded that, notwithstanding that a disability retirement annuitant is in receipt of a schedule award from the OWCP, OPM is correct in its determination that it is required to reduce the appellant's FERS annuity by the full, unreduced Social Security disability insurance amount to which he is entitled, as determined by the SSA, because such amount represents the assumed Social Security disability insurance amount used in computing the appellant's FERS annuity. See Johnston, 70 M.S.P.R. 109, 111. The Board found that nowhere in the relevant legislation as proposed or enacted is there any expression of Congress that a FERS annuitant's annuity should not be reduced by all of the Social Security benefits to which he is entitled. Id. at 116.

The Board noted that the Social Security component is the largest component of FERS and found that it is reasonable that any deductions required would be from the primary component and would be taken by the SSA from the full amount of the Social Security disability insurance benefit. The FERS disability annuity is offset by the unreduced Social Security disability insurance amount to avoid any duplicate payments. For example, if the SSA already offset the appellant's Social Security disability insurance benefit by an OWCP schedule award and the entire amount of such disability benefit is offset from FERS, the appellant receives no duplicate payments--the payments from OWCP, the payment from the SSA, and the FERS annuity contain no duplicate benefits. Id.

The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's reasoning. The court held that OPM's interpretation of the controlling statute, as manifested in the regulation designed to implement the statute, is not unreasonable. See Johnston v. Office of Personnel Management, 99 F.3d 1160, 1163 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). The court found that OPM's interpretation essentially leaves to the SSA the task of adjusting the SSA benefits in light of, e.g., OWCP awards and schedule payments. Id. Applying the Board's and the court's Johnston decisions in the overpayment context, OPM also properly uses the full amount that the SSA calculates as an employee's benefit entitlement in both the adjustment of the FERS annuity and the calculation of any overpayment caused by temporary dual payment by the SSA and FERS.
