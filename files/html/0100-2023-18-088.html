3. Decisionmaker, Reply Officer

The regulatory requirement is that a person receiving a reply to a proposed adverse action must have authority either to decide the case or to make a recommendation on the decision. 5 CFR 752.404(c)(2) (2022). An employee who claims that the oral reply was flawed must establish that the authority of the oral reply officer was erroneously restricted and that the error was harmful. Baker v. DHHS, 912 F.2d 1448 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (relying on Schultz v. Dept. of Transp., 773 F.2d 296, 299 (Fed. Cir. 1985), and Monroe v. Dept. of Treasury, 770 F.2d 1044, 1046-47 (Fed. Cir. 1985)); O'Brien v. United States, 284 F.2d 692, 695 (Ct. Cl. 1960) ("We do not think that the statute guarantees a veteran an interview with any particular official as long as he is given the opportunity to present such appeals as he may wish to make to a superior who has the authority to either recommend or take final action."). There is no requirement that the individual be the appellant's superior. Batchelder v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 312, 315, 6 MSPB 266 (1981); see Pelcher-Herring v. FMCS, DC-0752-13-0152-I-1 (NP 1/23/2014) ("Neither our decisions nor the Office of Personnel Management's regulations require that the oral reply official also serve as the deciding official. See Peterson v. Department of Health & Human Services, 25 M.S.P.R. 572, 573 (1985); Monroe v. Department of the Treasury, 20 M.S.P.R. 620, 621 (1984), aff'd, 770 F.2d 1044, 1046-47 (Fed. Cir. 1985); 5 C.F.R. § 752.404(c)(2) ('The agency will designate an official to hear the employee's oral answer who has authority either to make or recommend a final decision on the proposed adverse action.').").

Although 5 CFR 752.404(c)(2) (2022) requires an oral reply officer to recommend a final decision, the failure to make a recommendation is not harmful error without evidence showing that, if made, a recommendation would have been favorable. Jenkins v. GSA, 3 MSPR 117, 119, 3 MSPB 230 (1980). A reply officer must make an explicit recommendation to avoid the appearance that the reply is a formality. Swindell v. VA, 12 MSPR 148, 151 (1982) (evaluating a case under the harmful error standard). The recommendation may, apparently, be no more than a suggestion that the matter be settled. Baker, supra.

In Cheney v. DOJ, 720 F.2d 1280 (Fed. Cir. 1983), the deciding official from agency headquarters, after listening to the reply of the employee, essentially delegated the responsibility for making a recommendation to a field official. Based on the known facts, the deciding official made clear his intent to remove the employee. The deciding official then determined that he would be the person to consider the reply; there was some evidence that the field official would have recommended a lesser penalty, but the evidence was also that the field official did not have decisional authority because the authority to decide resided with the deciding official at headquarters. The court concluded that although the agency personnel policies were circumvented through the intervention of the deciding official in the reply process, the error was harmless: the deciding official could have awaited the recommendations for a lesser penalty and disregarded them; or the deciding official could have handled the case himself from the beginning without any assistance from a hearing officer who would make recommendations. A dissent from Judge Nies noted the possibility that the agency regulations may have vested the field official with the authority to make the adverse action decision--likely a suspension rather than a removal--and argued that the error in the swap had been harmful.
