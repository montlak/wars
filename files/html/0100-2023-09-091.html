broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section F: Justification for Late Filing, Subsection 08: Lack of Notice of Appeal Rights, Part n: Agency Misrepresentation
n. Agency Misrepresentation

The time for filing an appeal will be extended if the appellant was unaware of his appeal rights because the agency, deliberately or otherwise, affirmatively misrepresented to appellant that he had no appeal right, Butler v. Dept. of Interior, 3 MSPR 465, 466, 3 MSPB 534 (1980), a situation considered in Leonard v. Dept. of Army, 78 MSPR 492, 495-96 (1998):

The appellant asserted that he did not file an appeal at the time of his separation by RIF because James Long, Chief, Recruitment, Placement, and Career Management Division, told him and others at a meeting that they should not grieve the RIF actions because the agency would place them in a permanent position, and would extend their term appointments, until permanent positions became available. The appellant further asserted that Long met several times with Union President Joseph Dolan, who represented these employees, and Long repeated his assurances that the affected employees would not be terminated, but would be convened back to permanent positions, when vacancies became available.

...The record indicates that the parties dispute the representations that were made by the agency when the appellant was offered the term appointment and subsequently. The administrative judge should determine whether the agency made any representations about future permanent employment to the appellant and whether, if so, the appellant reasonably relied on such representations so that good cause for the delay in filing his appeal exists.

See Toyama v. MSPB, 481 F.3d 1361, 1363-67 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (discounting the effect of prior notice of Board appeal rights when mixed-case notices from the agency were incomplete and misleading); Hall v. Dept. of Army, 78 MSPR 222, 226 (1998) ("The administrative judge should determine whether the agency made any representations about future permanent employment to the appellant and whether, if so, the appellant reasonably relied on such representations so that good cause for the delay in filing her appeal exists."); Wrenn v. Smithsonian Inst., 21 MSPR 33, 35 n.3 (1984) (the filing time was waived when an agency letter notifying the appellant of a downgrade informed him that he was precluded from appealing the action to the Board); Stroup v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 174, 176, 6 MSPB 155 (1981) ("The appellant...was not informed of his appeal right. In fact, he was specifically informed that he could not appeal. Accordingly, the Board finds that the appellant's delay in appealing is for good cause."); cf. Freeman v. Dept. of Air Force, 89 F.3d Appx. 706 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (reversing the Board's determination as to the timeliness of a request for law enforcement officer benefits; the employee's SF-50s and other information provided by the agency led the employee to assume he in fact was covered for the benefit); Romane v. DOD, 27 MSPR 423, 424 n.* (1985) (good cause for an untimely appeal when the agency dismissed the appellant's EEO complaint and informed him that he had no right of appeal from an adverse action). But see Courtney v. Dept. of Transp., AT-0752-13-0755-I-1 (NP 9/15/2014) (opaque decision implying that the appeal deadline would not be extended when the appellant appeared to assert that a human resources specialist incorrectly informed him that his record, showing a demotion, would be clear after two years); Aldridge v. VA, 12 MSPR 601, 604 (1982) (personnel officer's assurances to an employee and counsel that his disability retirement application would be approved (it was not), lulling the appellant into a false sense that he need not pursue an adverse action appeal, were insufficient to warrant a finding of good cause).

Late filing was excused for an individual who was notified in a RIF notice of the 20-day appeal period, but who was also told by an agency official that he had 41 days to appeal. Bohanan v. TVA, 31 MSPR 275, 276 (1986). A late appeal was excused when the agency's notice of appeal rights incorrectly stated that the appeal could be filed in person or by certified mail. The appeal was delayed because the appellant lacked sufficient funds to pay for the certified mail. Elbert v. VA, 60 MSPR 524, 528 (1994); cf. Wallace v. MSPB, 728 F.2d 1456, 1459 ¶ 8 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (Board should have accepted an appeal mailed to an incorrect address of the MSPB regional office that was supplied by the agency: "Petitioner was not required to seek a waiver of time limits but to prove that the appeal had been mailed to the MSPB in accordance with the agency instructions."); Hunter v. Dept. of Interior, 79 MSPR 456, 458 (1998) (delay was excused when the agency representative provided a wrong Board fax number to the appellant's representative; the appeal was properly filed as soon as the appellant learned that the fax was misdirected). [Refer to "Appeal Misdirected Within MSPB, to OPM, or to OSC" in this chapter.] Under Floyd v. USPS, 44 MSPR 37, 42 (1990), good cause existed to extend the time for filing when, after the appellant's decision notice was delayed because of an improper address, agency officials told the appellant and his counsel that the time for filing ran from the date of receipt of the agency decision. See Devine v. Levin, 739 F.2d 1567 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

Good cause for a late appeal exists if the agency misinforms the appellant, to his or her detriment, as to a choice of forum. Qazi v. AID, 80 MSPR 398, 401 ¶¶ 8, 10 (1998) (delayed appeal was excused because the agency informed the appellant that he could utilize a grievance procedure and the review process of the Foreign Service Grievance Board; the appellant appealed to the MSPB soon after he learned that the Board had exclusive jurisdiction); see Chiovitti v. Dept. of Air Force, PH-0752-21-0212-I-1, n.4 (NP 7/12/2022) (remanding to determine if the appellant was misinformed of the right, exercised by the union on his behalf, to grieve his removal under a labor contract); Williams v. DHUD, 40 MSPR 506, 508 (1989) (appeal was accepted after the agency erroneously advised the employee that she had to withdraw her Board appeal as a condition to pursuing an EEO complaint); cf. Toyama v. MSPB, 481 F.3d 1361, 1363-67 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (mixed-case notices from the agency were incomplete and misleading); Terrell v. USPS, 70 MSPR 296, 300 (1996) ("The Board will excuse an untimely filing if the untimeliness was caused by the appellant's reliance on erroneous information given to him by the agency and if he acted promptly to pursue the matter once he became aware of the error.").

Confusing statements in an agency's decision letter pertaining to an election between an MSPB appeal and a negotiated grievance procedure excused a belated appeal. Lourie v. USPS, 82 MSPR 119, 122-23 ¶¶ 6-9 (1999); cf. Coleman v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 266, 268-69 ¶¶ 5-8 (2001) (alleged confusion in a decision letter concerning an appeal deadline--unrebutted by the agency--coupled with medical evidence of stress-related severe depression, caused the Board to excuse the delay of a day). But see Gamba v. USPS, 52 MSPR 75, 78 (1991) (agency did not misinform the appellant of his right to an immediate appeal to the Board while simultaneously pursuing a grievance when its decision notice informed him that he had the "right to 'appeal' the removal through the agency's negotiated grievance procedure 'and/or' to the Board"; noting that "while a preference eligible employee in the Postal Service may file both a grievance and an appeal with the Board at the same time, the collective bargaining agreement may preclude the employee from pursuing his grievance after he files an appeal with the Board"); Dotson v. USPS, 41 MSPR 412, 415 (1989) (supervisor's statement that an employee had about a month to file an appeal was an insufficient basis to excuse a delay when the agency notice advised the appellant of the exact amount of time and suggested that the appellant contact the postmaster if there were any questions concerning the appeal process).

A notice that an individual had probationary appeal rights was incorrect, excusing a late appeal of an individual who was a tenured employee. See Smart v. DOJ, 113 MSPR 393, 396-97 ¶ 10, 2010 MSPB 49 (2010) (notice of appeal rights in a probationary termination notice did not put the appellant on notice that he could file an appeal as an "employee" because his claims were not based on discrimination due to partisan political beliefs or marital status) (vacated for lack of jurisdiction, Smart v. DOJ, 116 MSPR 582, 2011 MSPB 72 (2011)); cf. Walker v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3367) (recognizing the substantial difference in pursuing an appeal as a probationer and as a tenured employee, a six year delay in appealing was excused when an attorney then determined that Walker was tenured).

Reliance on agency officials must be reasonable. Even bad advice from agency officials may not be enough to excuse a delay. An appellant who was put on notice of Board time limits did not reasonably rely on improper advice from the agency compensation officials that no further action was necessary to preserve his appeal. Bracy v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 631 (1984) (Table); see Sasso v. VA, 9 MSPR 361, 362 (1982) (delay was not excused for a GS-4 Nursing Assistant told by an agency personnel clerk that "even if he put it in writing 'it won't do any good because the VA is going to fire you anyway'"; the appellant's removal decision informed him of Board appeal rights). If the appellant receives a notice from the agency that correctly states the time for filing his appeal, followed by incorrect information provided by the agency during telephone conversations, untimely filing is not excused. Moore v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1015). But cf. Burch v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3046) (citing Gordy v. MSPB, 736 F.2d 1505 (Fed. Cir. 1984)) (failure by the agency to notify an employee of his appeal rights may constitute good cause for the employee's failure to file a timely appeal; however, the employee must file his appeal within the time limits once he becomes aware of the basis of his claim).

Allegations of misrepresentation may arise in contexts other than misstatements about appeal rights. The appellant may be misled by the agency concerning the underlying basis for the action taken against him, causing him to forego an appeal in the mistaken belief that the agency action was proper. Appellants arguing that they would have filed appeals earlier, had they been correctly informed of the factual basis for actions affecting them, have not received consistent treatment from the Board. The distinction in treatment may depend on whether the appellant was affirmatively misled or failed to ask the right questions at the time of the action. Reductions in force produced several cases addressing the timeliness of appeals when the appellant alleged she was unsure of the right to pursue an appeal. As to an appellant who was unsure about relying on his agency's advice that his RIF was properly conducted, Kotulak v. USDA, 35 MSPR 111, 114 (1987), noted:

On its face, this case is distinguishable from the kind of misrepresentation case where an employee has no reason to question the information that he is given by his agency at the time of the reduction in force. That the appellant apparently had some questions during the reduction in force about whether his assignment rights were violated was evidenced by his statement that he had been told that he would have bumping rights and his admitted search for documents during and after the reduction in force. In this case, if the appellant had filed a timely appeal, he could have obtained the documentation he sought through the Board's discovery process.

The Board expected an employee to exercise a timely inquiry or appeal if there is an indication that something is amiss in a RIF, McGill v. TVA, 12 MSPR 316, 318-19 (1982):

The record shows that appellant was informed in December 1979 that a RIF of iron workers would be occurring, but that he would not be among the five employees scheduled to be terminated. His foreman subsequently advised him that an error had been discovered in the retention standing of one employee scheduled for release, and thus appellant would be among the five employees to be separated. Since the foreman advised appellant that "everything had been checked out," he did not file an appeal. Appellant did not seek to appeal the RIF until 11 months later when he happened to hear through a coworker that a mistake had been made in granting a veteran's preference to a less senior employee who had been retained.

Although the agency did not meet the requirement of 5 CFR § 351.505 to keep accurate, current records for determining the retention standing of its employees, the agency had reason to expect that the personnel action against appellant had become final. Furthermore, appellant was affected by a RIF only after it had been determined that one error in the retention register already had been made. Therefore, appellant was on actual notice that errors in the retention register standing were possible. Yet appellant did not make any inquiries as to whether his separation was proper, even though the RIF notice invited questions and stated that records were available for review.[2] If appellant did not want to question agency officials, he could have appealed to the Board. In that appellant knew that he could grieve or appeal the action within specified time limits but failed to take any action for almost one year, it cannot be said that he exercised diligence or ordinary prudence. Accordingly, good cause has not been shown for waiver of the time limitation.

[2] Adequate grounds for a waiver of the time limitation might have existed if appellant had at least inquired whether there was any possibility of error with regard to his standing on the retention register and the agency still did not discover the error. However, appellant failed to take any timely action on his own initiative.

A result contrary to Koutulak, supra, was reached in what appears to be a significant, unfortunately nonprecedential, decision of Martin-Alvarez v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1492), determining that the Board should have accepted an untimely appeal after the agency affirmatively misrepresented facts to the employee concerning RIF procedures. Those misrepresentations led the employee not to appeal until the employee discovered information belying what the agency told him concerning the manner and extent that comparable positions were abolished. The court discounted the argument that the employee should have appealed and then sought information through discovery:

Clearly that argument misses the mark. Discovery is intended to be a tool for obtaining evidence in support of a claim which one has already pleaded with reasonable specificity. Discovery is not a fishing expedition if one has any possible basis for a claim.

The court continued its discussion, without deciding the question presented:

Martin-Alvarez's second basis for arguing that he demonstrated good cause is that, even apart from misrepresentation, he did not know and had no basis for even suspecting that he had a claim prior to receiving certain information through his FOIA suit. The issue raised by this assertion is one of first impression in this court. In essential respects petitioner's argument parallels one that might be made in connection with tolling a statute of limitations. In that setting, this court and its predecessor have recognized the following principles:

In certain instances the running of the statute will be suspended when an accrual date has been ascertained, but plaintiff does not know of his claim. Ignorance of rights which should be known is not enough. Plaintiff must either show that defendant has concealed its acts with the result that plaintiff was unaware of their existence or it must show that its injury was "inherently unknowable" at the accrual date. Japanese War Notes Claimants Ass'n. v. United States, 373 F.2d 356, 358-9 (Ct. Cl)...see also Welcker v. United States, 752 F.2d 1577, 1580 (Fed. Cir.)....

No precedent of this court has addressed whether good cause is shown for extending the time for filing where a party would not reasonably have had reason to question the bona fides of the RIF action as to him. Although the board has had the issue raised in at least two cases, see Smith v. USPS, 26 MSPR 168 (1985); McGill v. TVA, 12 MSPR 316 (1982), the employees in those cases had not exercised diligence or ordinary prudence once they became aware that there was a basis for a claim. The board did not in those cases reach the issue of whether absence of a reasonable basis to question the adverse action, such that the employee should have become aware of or should have at least started to investigate the claim, constitutes good cause for waiver of its time limit.

Delay was excused for an appellant who relied on uncontested representations of five different agency officials concerning the content of a retention register. Cohen v. Dept. of Labor, 20 MSPR 232, 234 (1984); see Shubinsky v. United States, 488 F.2d 1003, 1006 (Ct. Cl. 1973) (concerning misstatements in a RIF notice and a late appeal despite notice of appeal rights: "Plaintiff reasonably relied on the administrative misrepresentation that NASL had been 'liquidated' and, consequently, that plaintiff's VPA [retention] rights were lost to him. He acted with adequate promptness to perfect his appeal upon learning that the agency had been [reorganized] and VPA rights might still be available to him.").

Gometz v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 284, 287-89 (1996), remanded for a hearing on timeliness when the appellant argued she was misled by an agency official concerning the need to file an appeal from a RIF to secure repromotion:

Depending on the particular circumstances of the case, see Alonzo v. Dept. of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980), an appellant may establish good cause for the untimely filing of an appeal by showing that he or she reasonably relied on the statements of agency officials in delaying filing. In Cohen v. Dept. of Labor, 20 MSPR 232 (1984), agency officials told the appellant at the time of her RIF demotion from a Clerk-Typist position that there were no Clerk-Typist positions to which she could be assigned. About seven months later, when her attorney examined the retention register, the appellant came to believe that she had been misinformed, and filed an appeal. She established good cause for her untimely filing because, under the circumstances presented, she reasonably relied on the representations of agency officials, who led her to believe that she had no basis for an appeal. See also Shubinsky v. United States, 203 Ct. Cl. 199, 488 F.2d 1003 (1973) (where, based on misinformation in a general RIF notice, the appellant reasonably believed that he had no basis for filing an appeal, and where he filed an untimely appeal after learning that he might have had assignment rights in the RIF, the filing deadline was waived); Floyd v. USPS, 44 MSPR 37 (1990) (the appellant's delay in filing was excused, where he calculated the filing deadline based on the incorrect advice of agency officials). An appellant's delay in filing will not be excused, however, merely because the appellant chose to attempt to reach a settlement with the agency and was unsuccessful. See Schrum v. Dept. of the Treasury, 42 MSPR 103 (1989); see also Wall v. DHHS, 9 MSPB 401, 10 MSPR 139 (1982) (the vague and equivocal statements of agency officials that they were "looking into the matter" of returning the appellant to the position from which he was demoted were an insufficient basis for excusing his untimely filing, where the appellant was aware for many months of his right to appeal and that he had a basis therefor).

An administrative judge should hold a hearing if there are material facts in dispute regarding timeliness and the appellant has requested a hearing. See Secaida v. USPS, 44 MSPR 256 (1990). The appellant herein requested a hearing, and we find that there are material facts in dispute affecting the good cause determination. If the appellant reasonably believed, based on Hughes' statements, that the agency was going to return her to her former Boilermaker Supervisor position and that she therefore would receive all of the relief that she could receive by filing an appeal, then her delay in filing should be excused. In this connection, we note that she filed her appeal within the allowable time limits (19 days) after she allegedly first came to believe that Hughes had misled her, and that her overall delay in filing was not lengthy.

Comments from a union official to the effect that the appellant might be reinstated were not the result of misrepresentations from the agency, and they did not excuse an appeal four days late in Conyers v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-315H-17-0307-I-1 (NP 7/21/2022):

In support of her assertion that the agency caused her delay in filing, the appellant provided a declaration made under penalty of perjury recounting various conversations related to her termination and possible reinstatement. In her declaration, however, the appellant does not identify an instance after January 4, 2017--the date of the termination notice--in which an agency manager directly led her to believe that her termination would be rescinded. Id. Any suggestion that the appellant might have been reinstated came through the appellant's union representative, and there is no evidence that he was authorized to make a decision on the appellant's reinstatement. Under the circumstances, we find that the appellant failed to show that any agency official made misstatements that caused her to lose time in filing her appeal. Likewise, we find that the cases cited by the appellant in her petition for review do not support a finding that agency misrepresentation caused the appellant's filing delay. See Shubinsky v. United States, 488 F.2d 1003 (Ct. Cl. 1973); Gordy v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 736 F.2d 1505 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Gometz v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 284 (1996), overruled on other grounds by Sturdy v. Department of the Army, 88 M.S.P.R. 502 (2001). Both Shubinsky and Gometz are distinguishable because, unlike here, there was a finding in those cases that the employees actually received inaccurate information from agency officials. And, Gordy is distinguishable from the instant case because, unlike the employee in Gordy, the appellant here was fully aware of the basis for the claim on which she could file a Board appeal before the filing deadline had passed.
