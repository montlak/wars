7. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit

Section 144 of the Federal Courts Improvement Act of 1982, codified in part in 5 USC 7703, provided that petitions to review final orders of the MSPB be filed with the Federal Circuit. The Act applies to notices of appeal filed after October 1, 1982. See Hondros v. Civil Serv. Comm'n, 720 F.2d 278, 291 n.31 (3d Cir. 1983). Decisions of the predecessor tribunal, the Court of Claims, are part of the Federal Circuit's body of precedent. Collier v. OPM, 29 MSPR 38, 43 (1985) (citing South Corp. v. United States, 690 F.2d 1368, 1370 (Fed. Cir. 1982)); see Middleton v. DOD, 185 F.3d 1374, 1379 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 1999) ("The decisions of the Court of Claims are binding precedent for our court's panels. See South Corp. v. United States, 690 F.2d 1368, 1370 (Fed. Cir. 1982) (en banc)."). 28 USC 1295(a)(9) also vests in the Federal Circuit exclusive jurisdiction of an appeal from a final order or decision of the MSPB pursuant to 5 USC 7703(b)(1) and 7703(d). Section 108 of the Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act of 2012 amendments permit an appellant or OPM to secure review in circuit appellate courts "[d]uring the 2-year [now 5] period beginning on the effective date of the Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act of 2012." Under Section 202 of the statute, the effective date of the WPEA is 30 days after its enactment. It was enacted on November 27, 2012, making its effective date on or about December 27, 2012, and its terminus December 27, 2017. Discrimination cases go to district courts.

Subject to the WPEA amendments allowing some whistleblower cases to be appealed to the regional circuits, and subject to district court jurisdiction over discrimination cases under the civil rights acts, the Federal Circuit has exclusive jurisdiction over appeals brought from decisions of the Board issued pursuant to 5 USC 7701, encompassing most Board cases heard through its appellate jurisdiction over adverse actions and performance-related cases. The Board pays special regard to Federal Circuit decisions, Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 39 (1987):

Under 5 USC 7703(b)(1), as amended, the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has exclusive jurisdiction over petitions for judicial review of Board decisions with respect to cases other than those involving claims of prohibited discrimination. Given this exclusive grant of jurisdiction, the Board has held that decisions by that court are controlling authority on the Board, whereas decisions by other circuit courts are persuasive, but not controlling, authority.

Federal Circuit jurisdiction over MSPB decisions was summarized, Baca v. Dept. of Army, 983 F.3d 1131, 1137 (10th Cir. 2020):

The Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit generally has jurisdiction over final decisions by the MSPB, 5 U.S.C. § 7703(b)(1)(A), with two exceptions. First, an appeal that also challenges an adverse employment action based upon a claim of prohibited discrimination (termed a "mixed case") must be appealed to the appropriate federal district court. See Perry v. MSPB, --- U.S. ---, 137 S. Ct. 1975, 1988, 198 L. Ed.2d 527 (2017).[3] Second, a petition for judicial review of:

[A] final order or final decision of the [MSPB] that raises no challenge to the Board's disposition of allegations of a prohibited personnel practice described in [5 U.S.C.] section 2302(b) other than practices described in section 2302(b)(8), or 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C) or (D) shall be filed in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit or any court of appeals of competent jurisdiction.

5 U.S.C. § 7703(b)(1)(B) (emphasis added).[4]

[3] A "mixed case" or "mixed claim" raises "a personnel action serious enough to appeal to the MSPB and alleges that the action was based on discrimination." Perry, 137 S. Ct. at 1981 (quoting Kloeckner v. Solis, 568 U.S. 41, 44, 133 S. Ct. 596, 184 L. Ed.2d 433 (2012)); see also 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(a)(2).

[4] Previously, petitions for review of final decisions by the MSPB were exclusively reviewed by the Federal Circuit. See Avilés v. MSPB, 799 F.3d 457, 459 (5th Cir. 2015). The WPEA extended judicial review of final MSPB in whistleblower cases. See Pub. L. No. 112-199, 126 Stat. 1469 codified at 5. U.S.C. § 7703(b)(1)(B). Initially set to sunset after two years and then extended another three years, see Pub. L. No. 113-170, the jurisdictional expansion was permanently authorized in 2018 by the All Circuit Review Act. Pub. L. No. 115-195, 132 Stat. 1510 (2018).

See Gende v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 518, 523 (1987) (same as Fairall); Brooks v. Dept. of Transp., 33 MSPR 399, 402 n.4 (1987) (same holding as Fairall); cf. Bivens v. District courts assume judicial review over cases brought by employees seeking review of EEO issues. Through 1989, 5 USC 1207(c) provided that Special Counsel disciplinary cases be heard in the appeals court where the employee resided or was employed at the time of the action. Those cases are, however, now reviewed in the Federal Circuit along with Special Counsel disciplinary actions and Individual Right of Action decisions, subject to the possibility of review of whistleblower cases in regional circuit appellate courts following the 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act. [Refer to statutory citations in prior discussion.] Because of its dominance in the civil service arena, the Federal Circuit is enmeshed in many issues of both a substantive and a technical nature involving nexus, jurisdiction, counsel fees, retirement, and harmful error. [Refer to later discussion, "Discrimination Issues."]

The Federal Circuit, a court with a specialized subject matter jurisdiction (rather than the general jurisdiction of the other federal appellate courts), hears a variety of patent, tax, government contract, veterans rights, international trade, Court of Federal Claims, Tucker Act, and civil service appeals. Somewhere it was said that the court has jurisdiction to review the decisions of 100 lower tribunals, issued by more than 800 adjudicators. Average disposition time in the court for merits dispositions, that is, for cases involving disposition by decision of judges, is about 11 months. In 2008 about 425 appeals were filed with the court from MSPB decisions, about 28% of the court's docket of appeals totaling 1,432 new appeals filed for that year. As a percentage of the court's docket, Board appeals steadily decrease. For FY 2016, only 13% of the cases involved personnel matters. For FY 2017, there were about 200 Board cases of the approximately 1731 cases total for the court, or about 11% of the docket. For FY 2019 the numbers were approximately 180 Board appeals among the 1511 total appeals to the court, about 11%. During FY 2020-21, MSPB cases accounted for about 10% of the court's docket--about 125 or so appeals of personnel cases. The court affirms the Board most of the time--about 95% of the time. By comparison, in commercial disputes, generally involving patents, coming to the Federal Circuit from federal district courts, or involving international trade issues, the reversal rate ranged, depending on the type of case, from about 14-39% for FY 2011. About a third of the cases filed do not make it to judgment: they are dismissed as untimely or because they are in the wrong court; a few are settled, and more than a few are tossed out because appellants (or their counsel) fail to follow the court's procedural rules. Although these disposition figures represent decisions in many MSPB cases brought to the court pro se--and pro se cases probably account for about 70% or more of the MSPB appeals in the Federal Circuit--judicial dispositions do not turn on whether or not an appellant is represented by counsel. A high percentage of appellants represented by counsel lose in the appeals court. The standard of review is very restricted and, as Judge Nichols remarked in Ferrone v. Dept. of Labor, 797 F.2d 962, 963 (Fed. Cir. 1986), "many of our petitioners in MSPB cases are underachievers." Counsel should carefully consider whether to encourage a client to undertake the expense of appellate litigation.

Many Board decisions that find their way into the court are challenged either on the basis of factual disputes or on the basis of a sometimes well-founded belief that the Board failed to properly consider mitigating circumstances. Because, by law, the court must affirm the Board's factual findings on the basis of substantial evidence, dispositive findings of the Board may become almost unassailable in the court. Factual disputes and mitigation arguments are usually unsuccessful in the Federal Circuit, for it generally defers to the Board on factual resolutions and to both the Board and the agencies under Douglas on mitigation questions. Although factual findings of the Board are "almost unassailable," occasionally egregious circumstances arise that warrant reversals or remands based on inadequate or unsupported factual analysis by the Board, and these decisions crop up in various portions of the Guide. Some lawyers believe that a reasonable result may be obtained only by taking a case into district court on a discrimination issue, placing both civil service and EEO issues before a district judge (or jury) who hears the evidence de novo on EEO issues (civil service issues are considered "on the record" by the judge alone). Whether there is a disparity in results achieved in federal district court rather than Federal Circuit litigation cannot be said; district court decisions and jury verdicts are often unpublished.

What makes a compelling case to take to the Federal Circuit? Generally, an appeal will stand a decent chance in the Federal Circuit if it presents compelling facts or compelling law. The Federal Circuit is so likely to defer to the Board's interpretation of facts, and so likely to affirm the Board's factual findings, that an appeal amounting to no more than a factual dispute or strained interpretation of law is unlikely to succeed. Most significant civil service decisions in the Federal Circuit result either from the Board's inability to do something other than affirm an agency whose dismissal of an appellant could reasonably be considered outrageous or from the Board's (or agency's) fairly blatant denial of due process or tortured construction of a law or regulation. At least through 2010, when the Board began using nonprecedential final orders, because the Board issued so many pro forma denials of administrative petitions for review, the court was often directly reviewing the decision of a Board AJ; had that decision been more carefully reviewed at the Board's headquarters during the PFR process, judicial resort might have been unnecessary.

Although the distinction between precedential and nonprecedential decisions of the Federal Circuit is becoming blurred by publication in official reporters of nonprecedential decisions and by the MSPB's occasional reliance on nonprecedential court decisions, an opinion of the court is precedent for only the issues that it discusses. If an issue is not argued or if it is ignored in a decision, that decision is not precedent to be followed in a subsequent case raising the same issue. See Franklin Federal Sav. Bank v. United States, 431 F.3d 1360, 1372 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (dissenting opinion by Judge Rader). Federal Circuit rules should be consulted concerning citation of the court's nonprecedential decisions.

The Federal Circuit operates a mediation program. MSPB cases are not excluded from the program. Details of the program are at the court's website, www.cafc.uscourts.gov, and the program is described in the Federal Circuit's "Appellate Mediation Program Guidelines." (May 14, 2007). It is unknown how many civil service appeals are being addressed through the program. Federal Circuit mediation statistics show that in 2018 eight Circuit appeals were settled; twelve were not settled. Only a handful of mediated cases were reported settled in 2020.
