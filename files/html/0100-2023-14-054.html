10. Effect Of OPM Reconsideration Request

Before OPM can take the Board to court, it must request reconsideration of the Board's decision. The Board shows some deference to OPM's petitions for reconsideration. In York v. USPS, 11 MSPR 251, 254 (1982) (on a petition for reconsideration by OPM), the Board reversed its prior ruling and sustained the removal of an employee who violated his fiduciary responsibilities as a postal clerk by improperly cashing a blank money order for $30. The Board did not explain why it decided that the penalty warranted mitigation in its first opinion and why it did not on OPM's reconsideration petition. A similar result occurred in Vilt v. U.S. Marshals Serv., 16 MSPR 192, 202 (1983). The Board initially determined that because of a lack of nexus between charges of off-duty problems involving bad debts and the passing of a worthless check, a penalty sustained only by the remaining charges of duty-related misconduct was mitigated to a suspension. OPM petitioned for reconsideration after the Board's decision, whereupon the Board determined that because the bad debts involved, in part, appellant's own agency and coworkers and were demonstrated to have disrupted office operations, nexus was established. In Vilt, the Board said that normally it would have sustained the removal, despite the appellant's 10 years of service with only one disciplinary infraction, illness, and long tours of duty during the time involved in the charged misconduct. However, because the appellant had been reinstated after the Board's first decision mitigating the removal to a suspension and had been working without incident since that time, it was unfair to remove the appellant following the OPM reconsideration decision. The appellant was allowed to remain on duty. [Refer to Chapter 5, "OPM Reconsideration Requests."]
