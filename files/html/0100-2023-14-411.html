C. SAFETY COMMITTEES

The case law concerning proposals for safety committees is tangled. It seems as though there is a right to negotiate over the existence of a committee, a right generally to define its purpose, and a right to negotiate a provision for representatives of the employees to be on the committee. The more specifically the proposal defines the duties of the committee or its authority, the less likely the committee structure will be negotiable. Matters are confounded by the existence of "certified" and "non-certified" committees. The Merit Systems Protection Board offered some comments distinguishing the two types of committees, Owen v. Dept. of Air Force, 63 MSPR 621, 627-28 (1994):

Where certified employee/management occupational safety and health committees exist, reports of safety problems at federal facilities are addressed through those committees. 29 CFR §§ 1960.28(b)-(d) and 1960.36. Where certified committees do not exist, or when requested by half the members of a certified occupational safety and health committee, OSHA may conduct inspections of the workplace. 29 CFR §§ 1960.28(e)(3) and 1960.31. If the inspection reveals violations, the inspector, on behalf of the Secretary of Labor, notifies the agency, which is expected to abate the hazard. 29 CFR §§ 1960.30 and 1960.31. At no time during the process does a formal adjudication of the violation occur.

The right to assign employees includes the right to assign them to positions in the agency. The designation by proposal of an employee to serve on the Health and Safety Committee does not constitute an assignment to a position and it is not inconsistent with the right of the agency to assign employees. As to the right of an agency to assign work, the participation of union representatives on the committee does not concern official, prescribed duties and it does not involve the assignment of work. In NFFE Local 2059 and DOJ, U.S. Attorney's Office, S. Dist. of New York, 22 FLRA 136, 138-40 (1986), the proposal did not address specific duties of employees and did not prevent the agency from changing its designations of employees on the committee. Establishment of a safety committee did not prevent the agency from taking it upon itself to remedy unsafe conditions and the proposal did not interfere with the right of the agency to determine its own security practices. Intended as a forum to address safety and health issues, the committee contemplated by the proposal did not conflict with agency responsibilities and differed from the nonnegotiable proposal for an uncertified committee in AFGE Local 2786 and DMA, 20 FLRA 193, 195-98 (1985). The functions of the committee, to meet, report, and recommend corrective action for unsafe working conditions referred to the committee, were consistent with a negotiable proposal in AFGE Council of Prison Locals and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 11 FLRA 286, 287-89 (1983). An advisory committee was approved in NFFE Local 1256 and Sawyer AFB, 29 FLRA 171, 174-76 (1987). No specific functions were assigned to it, other than to meet quarterly. Management had the right to select from a list of union representatives.

Establishing a reporting requirement to a safety committee does not make negotiable a proposal that otherwise infringes on management rights. Because the proposals interfered with the right to assign work and did not constitute permissible bargaining arrangements, Provisions 4 and 5 were nonnegotiable in AFGE Local 2058 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Independence Nat'l Park, 68 FLRA 676, 682 (2015) (Member Pizzella dissenting in part) (union did not argue that the provisions were negotiable proposals or arrangements):

Provision 4

If there is a dangerous, unhealthful or potentially dangerous or unhealthful situation in any location, the first concern is for the employees and the public. Should it become necessary to evacuate a building, management will take precautions to guarantee the safety of employees and visitors. The Union will be notified in cases where an actual health and/or safety situation occurred. Once evacuated, a building will not be reopened until the Safety Officer and/or Division of Resource and Visitor Protection determine that there is no longer a danger to visitors and employees. The Health and Safety committee will be notified of the emergency at their next monthly meeting.

Provision 5

The park Safety Officer will review situations where information indicates that employees in a particular occupation are suffering from a pattern of accidents, disabling injuries and/or illnesses. Any written report will be supplied to the Health and Safety Committee.

A proposal was not negotiable that precluded the activity from disestablishing a management "deficiency abatement committee," created under the agency program implementing Exec. Order 12196. The proposal also sought appointment of union representatives to the committee. The work of the committee involved the deliberative process of the agency as to funding, priorities, or implementation of projects. The proposal for union representatives constituted an assignment of work to individuals not chosen by management. The proposal also interfered with the right of management to determine the formal organizational structure used to make decisions in the agency safety and health deficiency abatement program. Hawaii FEMTC and Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard, 23 FLRA 189, 190-91 (1986).
