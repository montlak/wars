broida flra Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 05: Issues Not Appropriate for Negotiability Appeals, Section A: Application or Interpretation of Negotiated Agreements, Subsection 02: Meaning of Agreement

2. Meaning of Agreement

One type of proposal not properly subject to a negotiability appeal is the provision purporting to interpret an existing agreement. The proposal in IFPTE Local 11 and Mare Island Naval Shipyard, 32 FLRA 380, 381 (1988) (Proposal 1), was that:

This instruction requires operators and passengers of motorcycles to wear certain protective equipment. Article 23, Section 5, of the Agreement between this union and the Shipyard states that the Shipyard will "furnish special issue protective clothing and equipment at no expense to the employee whenever it is required by the employer for safety or industrial health purposes." OPNAV Instruction 5100.12D requires the use of protective equipment by certain employees of the shipyard, therefore the shipyard is to provide this equipment at no cost to the employees.

Of the proposal in IFPTE Local 11, FLRA determined, 32 FLRA at 383:

We find that the wording of Proposal 1 does not concern a matter proposed to be bargained within the meaning of section 7117 of the Statute. Rather, Proposal 1 constitutes an asserted interpretation of a provision of the parties' collective bargaining agreement and a request that the Agency comply with that provision as interpreted by the Union. Questions concerning interpretation of provisions in the parties' collective bargaining agreement are not properly resolved through the negotiability appeal procedures set out in section 7117 of the Statute and Part 2424 of our Rules and Regulations, but rather, should be resolved in other appropriate proceedings, such as the parties' negotiated grievance procedure.

An issue of contract interpretation regarding whether a practice sought to be negotiated by the union was waived through agreement with management is to be resolved under the grievance procedure. But FLRA will not delay a decision on the agency's contention that the proposal is also nonnegotiable. NAGE Local R3-84 and Dist. of Columbia Air Nat'l Guard, 23 FLRA 536, 538 (1986).

Although AFGE Local 2736 v. FLRA, 715 F.2d 627, 629 (D.C. Cir. 1983) [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Issues Not Appropriate for Negotiability Appeals"], would now bring a different result, FLRA determined that if the negotiability dispute involved both interpretation of the controlling agreement and negotiability of proposals, the contract dispute was to be resolved first. If it determined that the matter in dispute was inconsistent with the agreement, FLRA need not decide the negotiability issue; a prior negotiability determination would lack finality if the parties could then review the matter in dispute under the terms of the negotiated agreement. Once the parties had a decision under their agreement that bargaining was not precluded on a specific proposal, FLRA would consider a negotiability appeal. AFGE Local 1661 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 2 FLRA 412, 412 (1980) (concerning dispute over coverage of master agreement and negotiation of local implementation of national sick leave policy); see AFSCME Local 3097 and DOJ, 24 FLRA 453, 455 (1986) ("Factual issues in dispute between the parties concerning the duty to bargain in the specific circumstances of this case should be raised in other appropriate proceedings."); AFGE Local 2879 and SSA, San Diego, Cal., 2 FLRA 733, 734 (1980) (dispute over whether union's flextime proposal was subject only to national negotiation or local negotiation would properly be resolved through "whatever procedures the parties themselves have adopted for such purposes through such agreements").
