1. Scope of Enforcement Authority

In the enforcement of settlements, the Board is not limited to matters that could have been raised through its appellate jurisdiction. "The Board has the authority to enforce a settlement agreement that has been entered into the record in the same manner as any final Board decision or order." Bernard v. USDA, DE-0752-11-0222-C-1 (NP 1/22/2014) (citing Vance v. Dept. of Interior, 114 MSPR 679, 682-83 ¶ 6 (2010)). The Board may enforce the entire settlement agreement, although the Board is confined to the matters contained in the filed and accepted agreement. Rhodes v. USPS, 13 MSPR 584, 586 (1982). "The Board has broad authority to ensure compliance with the terms of a settlement agreement entered into the record." Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 684, 694 (1997). The Board's authority to enforce settlements exceeds its ordinary remedial authority as to adverse actions. If the parties agree to terms and the AJ accepts the agreement into the record, those terms are enforceable. In Greco v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 408, 410-11 (1984), following remand, 30 MSPR 288, 292-93 (1986), the Board required the AJ to consider the appellant's complaints that the agency failed to fulfill terms in a settlement agreement concerning his participation in a priority placement program, payment of per diem and living quarters allowance, and assistance promised by the agency in the disposition of the appellant's stranded automobile. Another decision compelled the agency to abide by a provision of a settlement agreement requiring the agency to assist the appellant in his search for other employment and to provide the appellant with a letter of endorsement from the civilian personnel office. The letter that the agency provided, essentially no more than verification of employment, did not to contain the affirmative endorsement required by the agreement. Graff v. Dept. of Air Force, 39 MSPR 639, 643 (1989). Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 684, 699 (1997), concluded that an agency violated a settlement by denying an appellant LWOP and then removing him for AWOL. As a remedy, the Board ordered Mr. Joos reinstated, but left open the possibility that the agency could remove him for inability to perform his duties or excessive authorized absences.

The Federal Circuit endorsed the Board's authority to specifically enforce settlements in Lary v. USPS, 493 F.3d 1355, 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (denying rehearing on Lary v. USPS, 472 F.3d 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2006), on remand, Lary v. USPS, 106 MSPR 597, 2007 MSPB 220 (2007), dismissed as moot, Lary v. USPS, 108 MSPR 145, 2007 MSPB 291 (2007)):

The USPS also argues that the Board did not have jurisdiction to order specific performance. We again disagree. The Board's power to enforce a settlement agreement comes from its authority to enforce its own orders, set forth by statute. See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2) (2006); see also King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215, 1218 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The statute broadly grants the Board power to "order any Federal agency...to comply with any order or decision...and enforce compliance with any such order." 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2). On its face, the statute does not limit the Board's authority to any particular means of enforcing compliance with its orders or prevent it from ordering specific performance. See also 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(c)(2)(i) (regulation relating to settlements providing that "the Board will retain jurisdiction to ensure compliance with the agreement").

Enforcement may involve resolution of back pay disputes. E.g., Cobia v. VA, PH-0752-16-0283-X-1 (NP 1/6/2023) (settlement resulting in dispute over back pay and related accounting: requiring narrative statements and supporting documentation from VA regarding pay, leave, TSP, insurance premiums, notice concerning elections as to benefits restoration; appellant's problems with unexplained pay stubs referencing leave hours restored, lack of restoration of TSP benefits, termination of insurance benefits and lack of retroactive restoration; requirement that VA document its efforts to obtain related information from DFAS; requirement of biweekly status reports from VA [18 in all, stretching over about three years]).

Enforcement has limits. The Board may decline to deal with de minimis violations of agreements, and the Board may decline to estop an appellant's bona fide enforcement efforts due to a de minimis violation of the settlement by the appellant. See Joos v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 684, 699 & n.9 (1997) (de minimis violation for the appellant to fax rather than mail documentation to the agency when the agency did not assert nonreceipt of the documentation); cf. Schoetzow v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3172) (30-minute variance in the start time of a work shift was not a material breach of the settlement agreement); Galatis v. USPS, 110 MSPR 399, 401 ¶ 3, 2009 MSPB 6 (2009) (Board did not require an agency to calculate a particular benefit under the settlement when the agency convinced the Board that another benefit calculation resulted in an overpayment and that the net of the two adjustments in payments would result in appellant owing money to the agency). Another limitation on enforcement of an agreement may be prior enforcement litigation resolved by the appellant bargaining away his right to enforce a term of the underlying settlement. See McCrory v. Dept. of Navy, 91 MSPR 56, 59 ¶ 8 (2002), aff'd, McCrory v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-3266).
