f. Mistake

"The fact that appellant has now changed his mind is not a basis to grant review in light of a record which shows no improper action on the part of the presiding official." Rafferty v. Dept. of Treasury, 5 MSPR 554, 555 (1981) ("It appears likely that appellant withdrew his petition for appeal during a period when he was unsure of which remedy he wished to pursue."); see Keeling v. United States, 172 Ct. Cl. 246 (1965) (the CSC did not err by closing the appeal when the plaintiff mistakenly thought he was being offered reemployment; "[i]f plaintiff misunderstood the statements...it is regrettable; but this cannot be deemed a circumstances beyond plaintiff's control."); Teesateskie v. Dept. of Interior, 87 MSPR 618, 621 ¶ 6 (2001) ("[H]is mere regret in withdrawing his appeal does not satisfy the criteria for granting a petition seeking to rescind a freely made decision to withdraw."); Spiro v. Dept. of Labor, 14 MSPR 478, 479 (1983) (appellant who gave no reason for withdrawing an appeal could not through a PFR advise the Board that the withdrawal was based on the AJ's denial of his discovery requests and obtain reassignment of the case to another AJ); cf. Andrews v. DOD, PH-0752-11-0121-I-1 (NP 7/19/2011) ("Although the appellant asserts on review that he objects to the initial decision's dismissal of his appeal as voluntarily withdrawn because he now wishes to negotiate a settlement agreement with the agency, the appellant has not shown or even alleged unusual circumstances such as misinformation or new and material evidence that warrant reinstatement of his appeal.").

The withdrawal of the appeal is not excused by a later statement that the appellant withdrew the appeal in haste because of his discouragement. Jackson v. OPM, 31 MSPR 501, 502 (1986). Pursuit of other remedies through other fora will not likely convince the Board to permit reopening of a withdrawn appeal. See Brakas v. USPS, 90 MSPR 332, 335 ¶¶ 8-9 (2001) (declining to waive the time limit for an appeal that was withdrawn so the appellant could pursue his pending grievance; noting interplay of MSPB and grievance arbitration procedures under Article 16.9A of the national agreement), aff'd, Brakas v. MSPB, 41 Fed. Appx. 463 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP); Bankston v. Dept. of Army, 87 MSPR 507, 510-11 ¶ 8 (2001) (untimely filing of a "new appeal" resulting from withdrawal of the original appeal: unconditional withdrawal of the appeal would not be set aside based on appellant's explanation that, upon resorting to court to pursue a civil rights case, he was directed to exhaust administrative remedies; "risk of failing to exhaust administrative remedies before pursuing a court complaint under the ADEA were identified in case law published prior to the date that the appellant withdrew his appeal"). The appellant's lack of understanding of the appellate process did not avoid the effect of withdrawal of an appeal, Hummel v. DOD, PH-3330-16-0304-I-1 (NP 9/20/2016):

In his petition for review, the appellant states, without elaboration, that he filed his request to withdraw the appeal "prior to a thorough understanding of the process."...

Generally, an appellant's withdrawal of an appeal is an act of finality which removes the appeal from the Board's jurisdiction. Lincoln v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 486, ¶ 7 (2010). The withdrawal must be clear, decisive, and unequivocal. Id. In the absence of unusual circumstances, such as when the decision to withdraw was based on misinformation, or the appellant has submitted new and material evidence, the Board will not reinstate an appeal once it has been withdrawn. Nazario v. Department of Justice, 108 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶ 4 (2008). Here, the appellant's request to withdraw his appeal was clear, decisive, and unequivocal. He has not alleged or shown that his decision to withdraw was involuntary due to misinformation, and he has not submitted any new evidence on petition for review. Consequently, his petition does not meet the criteria for review under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115 and provides no basis for reinstating his appeal. See Lincoln, 113 M.S.P.R. 486, ¶ 8.

The Board may, however, consider whether the action of another adjudicator, OWCP, caused the appellant to be confused or mistaken relative to rights that the appellant initially sought through a Board appeal. See White v. USPS, 92 MSPR 113, 114-15 ¶¶ 4-7 (2002), following remand, White v. USPS, 95 MSPR 220, 225 ¶ 15 (2003) (determining that the appellant was not confused or mistaken as to benefits he thought he would receive through an OWCP determination; finding no reason to disturb withdrawal of appeal).
