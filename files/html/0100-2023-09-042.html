d. Appeal Misdirected Within MSPB, to OPM, or to OSC

Misfilings are usually excused if they are within the civil service infrastructure. Although docketing instructions in Chapter 2, section 1 of the Judges' Handbook suggest that a case lodged at the wrong Board office is not docketed until the date it reaches its proper destination, an otherwise timely appeal filed with the wrong Board office will not be rejected for that reason alone. Ellison v. USPS, 45 MSPR 244, 245 n.2 (1990). An appeal misdirected to the Board's central office, or clerk, will be forwarded to the right regional office; it is considered filed when it is received at the central office. Jones v. USPS, 31 MSPR 130, 131 (1986) (failure to direct the appeal to the appropriate Board office, rather than to the Board's Clerk, should not deny the appellant a determination of the appeal); see Branch v. Dept. of Army, 110 MSPR 663, 664-65 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 45 (2009) (appeal timely filed with the Board's Clerk should have been deemed timely filed with the Board regional office responsible for its adjudication when it was received by the region after the allowable 30-day filing period); Jackson v. Dept. of Army, 7 MSPR 506, 507, 7 MSPB 362 (1981) (appeal was timely that was first submitted to a regional office, dismissed as premature, and was then brought to the Board as a PFR: "although appellant's submission is untimely as a petition for review of the initial decision, it is timely as an appeal"); cf. Rogers v. DOD, AT-3443-13-3415-I-1 (NP 4/25/2014) ("The petition for review was filed less than 30 days after the appellant received the agency's decision to indefinitely suspend him, effective October 2013. The appellant has not filed a separate appeal of this suspension. Based on the proximity of the petition for review to the effective date of the indefinite suspension, as well as the contents of the petition for review, we find that the appellant, who is pro se, intended to appeal his indefinite suspension. See Patton v. Department of the Treasury, 88 M.S.P.R. 542, ¶ 8 (2001) (finding, based on arguments raised on petition for review, that the appellant intended to file an appeal from a new suitability determination)...."); Cordell v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 50, 51 (1981) (premature RIF appeal was dismissed by the region; appellant's petition to the Board for reinstatement of the appeal was referred back to the region to determine whether the appeal was ripe for adjudication).

Although the failure of an appellant to direct an otherwise timely appeal to the appropriate Board office should not have the effect of denying the appellant a determination on the merits, Stanley v. DOJ, 58 MSPR 354, 356 n.2 (1993) (timely filing with the wrong Board office constitutes a timely filed appeal), and Cade v. Dept. of Commerce, 19 MSPR 607, 608 (1984) (incorrect captioning of an appeal as a PFR was also effectively corrected through remand of the case to the regional office), if the appeal is misdirected, that information should be brought to the attention of the AJ who eventually receives the case in response to the "show cause" order from the AJ raising concerns about timely filing. In a determination that is questionable, given the Board's frequent statement that jurisdiction can be raised at any time, the Board stated it will not accept evidence of a misdirected appeal for the first time with a PFR when that evidence should have first been supplied to the regional office. Auzenne v. USPS, 14 MSPR 303, 304 (1983). The appellant should act diligently once he discovers that his appeal was misdirected to an incorrect address for a Board office. See Smith v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3256) ("Once it became clear that the initial filing was misdirected, Mr. Smith had an obligation to exercise due diligence in pursuing his appeal thereafter.").

A timely appeal from a disability determination directed to OPM rather than to the MSPB regional office would be accepted for processing if there was reasonable justification for the confusion and if there was no prejudice to OPM. Ewell v. OPM, 5 MSPR 185, 186, 5 MSPB 238 (1981); see Wood v. DOD, 71 MSPR 104, 106 (1996) (under 5 USC 7702(f), an adverse action appeal misdirected to OPM, but containing an allegation of discrimination, was considered timely filed with the MSPB when the filing with OPM was timely); cf. King v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 435, 438-39 ¶ 7 (1999) (excusing a late suitability appeal; instead of submitting the appeal to the MSPB, the appellant initiated a request for reconsideration to the employing agency that issued the suitability determination under delegated authority from OPM; the agency failed in its obligation to forward the appellant's letter to the MSPB as an appeal); cf. Koyner v. OPM, DC-0831-12-0065-I-1 (NP 5/3/2013) (excusing an appeal misdirected to OPM when the appellant's English fluency was limited).

Mohammed v. OPM, 108 MSPR 609, 613-14 ¶¶ 10-12, 2008 MSPB 98 (2008), reversing a timeliness dismissal of an appeal involving a death benefits claim, noted the effect of an erroneous but otherwise timely filing of the appeal with OPM rather than with the Board. The emphasis in Mohammed was on the lack of prejudice to OPM rather than a demonstration of the reasonableness of the appellant's error:

...OPM's reconsideration decision instructed the appellant that she should file her appeal with the Board's regional office, and the Board generally holds that an appellant's failure to follow explicit filing instructions does not constitute good cause for any ensuing delay. Maggard v. Office of Personnel Management, 102 M.S.P.R. 75, ¶ 9 (2006). The Board has recognized an exception to this rule, however, in cases where appellants have timely but mistakenly sent appeals of OPM reconsideration decisions to OPM rather than to the Board. In such cases, the Board has found good cause for the untimely filing when the following conditions have been met: The delay was caused in part by the appellant's failure to follow the directions set forth in the reconsideration decision and in part by OPM's failure to direct an otherwise timely appeal to the Board; the appellant clearly intended to seek further review of the reconsideration decision; the appellant was pro se; and there was no showing of prejudice to OPM by granting the waiver. E.g., Maggard, 102 M.S.P.R. 75, ¶ 9; Nicholas v. Office of Personnel Management, 96 M.S.P.R. 34, ¶ 8 (2004).

These conditions have been met here. The appellant failed to follow the directions in OPM's February 21, 2007 reconsideration decision and submitted an appeal to OPM rather than properly appealing to the Board. While the delay in filing was due in part to the appellant's failure to follow the directions in OPM's reconsideration decision, it was also caused in part by OPM's failure to direct an otherwise timely appeal to the Board. It is undisputed that the appellant received OPM's reconsideration decision on March 7, 2007. The appellant mailed her appeal to OPM on April 3, 2007, thereby clearly indicating that she intended to seek review of the reconsideration decision within the time period for filing an appeal. Further, the appellant is pro se and OPM has not shown or even suggested that it will be prejudiced by the granting of a waiver.

We therefore find that the appellant has shown good cause for the untimely filing of her appeal, and so we waive the time limit for filing the appeal. See Maggard, 102 M.S.P.R. 75, ¶ 10.

The Board may extend its case law concerning misdirected appeals to other pleadings. Calfee v. OPM, 64 MSPR 309, 312-13 (1994), excused the misfiling, with OPM, of evidence concerning appellant's medical condition (bearing on the timeliness of his appeal) that should have been sent to the Board's regional office. The evidence was sent to OPM by the appellant's physician, who misconstrued the AJ's "show cause" order. OPM was not prejudiced, and the appellant was unable to immediately determine that the material was misdirected. However, it was error for a regional office to accept as an appeal a letter from OPM referring an employee's OPM inquiry to the Board. Stroup v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 174, 176, 6 MSPB 155 (1981). If the appellant presents to the Board an appeal that is properly directed to OPM, the Board should not docket the appeal; if the appeal is docketed by the region, the Board may correct the error by vacating the appeal and referring the matter to OPM. Brooks v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 258, 261 (1991). [Refer to earlier in this chapter, "Negative OPM Reconsideration Decision Appeal."]

If counsel erroneously files an appeal with OSC rather than with an MSPB regional office, it should not be dismissed if the appeal is otherwise timely. Casey v. Soldiers' and Airmen's Home, 5 MSPR 91, 92, 5 MSPB 161 (1981) ("The failure of counsel to direct an otherwise timely appeal to the appropriate office of the Board should not have the effect of denying appellant a determination on the merits."); Evans v. USPS, 20 MSPR 370, 371 (1984) (same as Casey). An appeal was accepted, although one day late, after it was mistakenly sent by a pro se appellant to OSC; the appellant corrected his mistake immediately after he learned of it. Caligiuri v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 372 (1985).
