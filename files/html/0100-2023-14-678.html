D. WORK AND SHIFT ASSIGNMENTS TO UNION OFFICIALS

A nonbargainable proposal precluded sending union officials on assignments that made it impossible for them to communicate with management. The proposal interfered with the right of the agency to assign work. AFGE Local 2272 and DOJ, Marshals Serv., 9 FLRA 1004, 1014 (1982) (Proposal 7); see IAM Local 726 and Naval Air Rework Facility, North Island, 31 FLRA 158, 159 (1988) (Provisions 1 and 2) (nonnegotiable as arrangement: provision making agency's ability to reassign stewards to shops and shifts outside their representation area without union's agreement; "[n]o allowance is made for the Agency to retain its ability to 'transfer' stewards without the Union's agreement, even in circumstances where it has compelling reasons to effect a transfer"). Nonnegotiable was a proposal that essentially protected union officials against charges of conflict of interest or position by insulating them against unwanted short-term assignments to supervisory responsibilities. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 31 FLRA 181, 183 (1988) (Provisions 1 and 2); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 769-71 (1992) (portion of Provision 41, essentially allowing union official to avoid details or temporary promotions to supervisory positions, was nonnegotiable as interfering with right to assign work; proposal was not considered under arrangements test).

A proposal was negotiable, following judicial review and remand, that gave union officials first preference for certain auditing assignments at the office, given management's option of assigning individuals to office instead of field work, NTEU v. FLRA, 810 F.2d 1224, 1229-30 (D.C. Cir. 1987), rev'g 17 FLRA 379 (1985):

The U.S. Marshals Service case is clearly distinguishable from the one before us. In that case, management could never assign certain work to union officials. In this case, the IRS can assign any work to the named union officials that it had previously assigned to them. The proposal merely provides that these union officials will have first preference for office audits. If the union officials do not prefer office audits, or if none are available, or if there is just cause for denying their first preference, the union officials may be assigned to field audits. The proposal in no way excepts these officials from a certain type of work.

All procedures for selecting qualified employees for certain work "excepts" them from doing other work at the same time. Proposal 2 has the same effect as one that requires that certain assignments "be offered to qualified and available employees with requisite skills on the basis of seniority," American Fed'n of Gov't Employees, 5 F.L.R.A. 83 (1981), or one that requires management to assign work to an employee with the "greatest Federal employment service" when two or more employees are "equally well qualified and capable of performing the detail work." Laborers Int'l Union of North Am, AFL-CIO Local 1276, 9 F.L.R.A. 703, 704 (1982).... [W]e grant the petition for review of the FLRA's decision that Proposal 2 is nonnegotiable because it directly interferes with the IRS' right to assign work. Because the FLRA considered only the IRS' claim that the proposal violated section 7106(a)(2)(B), we remand for consideration of the IRS' claims that Proposal 2 was nonnegotiable because it directly interfered with its right to direct employees, 5 U.S.C. § 7106(a)(2)(A), and to determine the number and type of employees assigned to an organizational subdivision or work project, id. § 7106(b)(1)....

On remand, NTEU and IRS, 28 FLRA 40, 43, 46 (1987), the proposal was negotiable. The proposal did not interfere with the right of the agency to direct its employees; it did "not involve supervising employees or determining the quantity, quality and timeliness of work production or establishing priorities for its accomplishment." The proposal did not interfere with the right of the agency to assign employees, for it concerned only where certain employees would perform duties previously assigned to their positions. The proposal did not explicitly relate to the agency's staffing pattern or directly and integrally relate to the numbers, types, and grades of employees assigned.

The agency was required to bargain over a proposal that stewards be reassigned to different shifts or work areas on the basis of seniority. There was no change in duties involved. the proposal also provided that the representatives could be assigned to another shift by mutual consent of the parties. The proposal fit within the class of cases that permitted negotiation of shift assignments of employees with interchangeable duties. Int'l Plate Printers Local 2 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 25 FLRA 113, 122 (1987) (Provision 16); see Nat'l Union of Hosp. and Health Care Employees, Dist. 1199 and VAMC, Dayton, 28 FLRA 435, 482 (1987) ("Proposal 3 would require that certain Union officials be assigned to permanent day tour of duty. This proposal is to the same effect as Provision 3 which was found to be within the duty to bargain in International Plate Printers, Die Stampers and Engravers Union of North America, AFL-CIO, Local 2 and Department of the Treasury, Bureau of Engraving and Printing, Washington, D.C., 25 FLRA No. 9 (1987). As in that case, there is no indication here that the Union representatives would perform duties other than those which the Agency had already assigned to their positions. Rather, the proposal is concerned with when, that is, on what shift, the Union representatives will perform those duties, and is within the duty to bargain. Accordingly, Proposal 3 is within the duty to bargain."). Proposals were negotiable that the agency assign the union president to the day shift and required that union officers' and stewards' work hours be adjusted to conform to those of the bulk of the workforce. Proposals concerning when employees will perform duties previously assigned to their positions do not violate the right of management to assign employees. NAGE, SEIU and Nat'l Guard Bureau, Adjutant Gen., 26 FLRA 515, 529 (1987) (Proposal 1).

FLRA signaled some limits on proposals concerning assignments of union officials in AFGE Local 2182 and Propulsion Lab., Army Research and Tech. Labs., 26 FLRA 600, 603-04 (1987) (Provisions 2 and 3), determining that two proposals were nonnegotiable: one, that the employer would make every effort to assign union officials to day shifts and that if other assignments were necessary for other than voluntary or emergency reasons, the employer would give the union two weeks advance written notice with an explanation for the tour change; the second, that the employer agree that no steward would be transferred to a different tour of duty without two weeks notice, except in emergencies:

We find that Provisions 2 and 3 are outside the duty to bargain. They limit the Agency's ability to revise work schedules of the employees involved in a manner inconsistent with the applicable statutory framework.

The fact that these provisions would apply to employees who are union representatives has no bearing on our decision. Under the Statute, an agency has the right to assign work to all employees whether or not they are union officials. American Federation of Government Employees Local 2272 and U.S. Department of Justice, Marshals Service, 9 FLRA 1004, 1014 (1982).

In National Association of Government Employees Local R7-23 and Department of Air Force, Scott AFB, 23 FLRA No. 97 (1986), we held that Proposal 1, which required the agency to give 14 days' notice before changing work schedules, except in emergencies, was outside the duty to bargain....

...

Provisions 2 and 3 are to the same effect.... The provisions do not incorporate the statutory and regulatory exceptions to the notice period stated above [5 USC 6101(a)(3)(A) and 5 CFR 610.121(a)]....
