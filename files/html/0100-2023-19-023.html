broida mspb Chapter 12: Discrimination Chapter 12: Discrimination, Subchapter 01: Discrimination Issues, Section A: Procedures in Mixed Cases, Subsection 06: Election of Remedies, Part c: Effect of Subject Matter of Preexisting Complaint on Subsequent Appeal; Complaint Involving Proposed Action
c. Effect of Subject Matter of Preexisting Complaint on Subsequent Appeal; Complaint Involving Proposed Action

Not every discrimination issue raised in an EEO complaint prior to an appeal forecloses Board jurisdiction on related issues pleaded in the appeal. See Smith v. Dept. of Energy, 49 MSPR 110, 114-15 (1991) (Chapter 43 demotion for unacceptable performance was within the Board's jurisdiction while the appellant processed an EEO complaint involving a performance appraisal and unsuccessfully attempted to amend the complaint to include a step increase denial).

The Board had jurisdiction over a discharge appeal when the appraisal underlying the removal was the subject of an EEO complaint. The discharge itself was not raised as part of the EEO complaint. Tinsley v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 189, 191 n.3 (1984). A denial of a promotion, raised in an EEO complaint that made no mention of a RIF and downgrade, was insufficient to divest the Board of immediate jurisdiction over an appeal from a downgrading through reduction in force. South v. Dept. of Air Force, 8 MSPR 91, 93, 7 MSPB 579 (1981). But a preexisting EEO complaint on an earlier matter may be amended to include an adverse action; that election may bar immediate (and otherwise timely) to the Board. Thompson v. DHS, AT-0752-15-0303-I-1 (NP 7/22/2015) ("We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant made an informed election to challenge her removal through the EEO complaint process by sending the email before she filed her Board appeal, and that such election was binding. See also Carey v. Department of the Interior, 103 M.S.P.R. 534, ¶ 11 (2006). Therefore, we find that the administrative judge properly dismissed her appeal without prejudice as prematurely filed under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(c).")

An EEO complaint as to a proposal filed before the appealed removal will not divest the Board of jurisdiction, even though the processing of the complaint continues past the removal and filing of the Board appeal, without a clear showing of the intent of the parties to treat the complaint as applying to the removal. Coleman v. Dept. of Treasury, 22 MSPR 519, 520-21 (1984); see Brewer v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-15-0216-I-1 (NP 7/22/2015) ("We cannot determine from the record whether that EEO complaint encompassed the agency's removal action. The complaint included the agency's proposed removal action, but this is not dispositive. See Hay v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 151, ¶ 14 (2007) (filing of an EEO complaint regarding a proposed removal does not affect the Board's jurisdiction over the removal unless there is a clear showing that the parties intended the complaint to include the removal action)."); Hay v. USPS, 106 MSPR 151, 157-58 ¶¶ 13-15, 2007 MSPB 156 (2007) ("The filing of an EEO complaint regarding a proposed removal does not affect the Board's jurisdiction over the removal "'unless there is a clear showing that the parties intended the complaint to include the removal action.'" Scarboro v. Department of the Navy, 55 M.S.P.R. 494, 497 (1992) (alteration in original) (quoting Yartzoff v. Environmental Protection Agency, 34 M.S.P.R. 105, 107 (1987))"; applying the rule through construction of the intent of appellant's EEO complaint); Scarboro v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 494, 497 (1992) ("Nevertheless, '[u]nlike the filing of an EEO complaint with respect to a removal action, the filing of an EEO complaint with respect to a proposal to remove does not affect the Board's jurisdiction, unless there is a clear showing that the parties intended the complaint to include the removal action.' Yartzoff v. EPA, 34 MSPR 105, 107 (1987)"); Yartzoff v. EPA, 34 MSPR 105, 107 (1987) (filing an EEO complaint against a proposal to remove does not affect MSPB jurisdiction without a clear showing that the parties intended the complaint to embrace the removal action); cf. Weaver v. USPS, 32 MSPR 330, 331 (1987) (different bases of discrimination were raised in an EEO complaint challenging proposal and removal decisions).

Galloway v. SSA, 111 MSPR 78, 84-86 ¶¶ 17-20, 2009 MSPB 46 (2009), demonstrated the need for a clear demonstration that preexisting EEO proceedings, although related to a defense to the removal, were meant to extend after the date of the removal to the removal action itself to preempt a Board appeal or resort to grievance arbitration that would later lead to a Board appeal:

The record shows that: the appellant filed a formal EEO complaint and an amended complaint on March 4, September 26, and October 2, 2007, that asserted that she was subject to harassment based on various forms of prohibited discrimination when the agency, inter alia, failed to give her adequate time to train for and learn her position, placed her on a PAP, and proposed to remove her; and the Union, on behalf of the appellant, timely invoked arbitration of the appellant's removal on November 21, 2007, after the agency informed the appellant on October 26, 2007, that she would be removed effective October 29, 2007. The agency does not assert in its response to the appellant's request for review, and the record does not show, that the appellant took any actions other than invoking arbitration following her receipt of the agency's October 26, 2007 notice of removal. Thus, because the appellant's first and only action following her receipt of the notice of removal was to have the Union, on her behalf, invoke arbitration of the removal action, we find that the appellant, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d), elected the negotiated grievance procedure as her choice of forum to grieve the removal action that she alleges was based on prohibited discrimination. See Goodwin, 106 M.S.P.R. 520, ¶ 20 (the appellant made a binding election under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) to remedy the agency's actions through a statutory procedure because the first election she made following her involuntary reassignment was to file a formal EEO complaint of that action, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.301(a), even though she filed a grievance of that same action two days after filing the formal EEO complaint).

Additionally, we find that the arbitrator's finding that the appellant's prior EEO complaint on her proposed removal constituted her election of forum with respect to the removal action is inconsistent with Board case law on election of forum. The Board has held that in the analogous case, where an appellant filed an EEO complaint on a proposed removal and then filed a Board appeal only after the agency made a decision to take the removal action, in the absence of a clear showing that the parties intended to act on the formal EEO complaint as a complaint concerning the ongoing procedure, including all steps of the removal action, the appellant's prior filing of the EEO complaint concerning the proposed removal did not divest the Board of jurisdiction over the removal appeal pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a). Coleman v. Department of the Treasury, 22 M.S.P.R. 519, 520-21 (1984); see also Yartzoff v. Environmental Protection Agency, 34 M.S.P.R. 105, 107 (1987) (the Board had jurisdiction over the appellant's removal appeal because even though the appellant, following his removal, filed a formal EEO complaint with the agency on his proposed removal, there was no clear showing that the parties intended the EEO complaint to include the removal action); Weaver v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 M.S.P.R. 330, 331 (1987) (the Board had jurisdiction over the appellant's removal appeal because his formal EEO complaint of his proposed removal and his Board removal appeal raised different bases of discrimination, thus indicating that he considered different issues in the two actions, and because there was no clear showing that it was the intent of both parties that the EEO complaint cover the ongoing process of the appellant's removal).

Here, our review of the record does not show that the appellant's original and amended EEO complaint intended to prospectively include the appellant's removal, and the record is devoid of evidence showing that the appellant took any further action on her formal EEO complaint between when she was notified of her removal on October 26, 2007, and when the Union invoked arbitration of her removal on November 21, 2007, that could be construed as an intent to include the removal action in the EEO complaint. We find that there is not a clear showing that the parties intended to act on the appellant's formal EEO complaint as a complaint concerning the ongoing procedure, including all steps of the removal action. See Coleman, 22 M.S.P.R. at 520-21. Thus, pursuant to the statutory language of section 7121(d), the Board's case law on election of forum, and in the absence of a clear showing that the appellant intended to appeal her removal action in another forum, the appellant made a valid election of forum when the Union, on her behalf, invoked the negotiated grievance procedure as the forum through which to appeal her removal action.

Therefore, we find that the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation in his decision to find that the appellant's formal EEO complaint of the notice of proposed removal, filed prior to the agency's removal action, constituted an irrevocable choice of forum with respect to the appellant's election to grieve her removal.

The filing of the EEO complaint against a proposal may have the effect of preserving Board jurisdiction if no complaint is subsequently filed against the adverse action decision and if a Board appeal is not filed within the Board's regulatory time period for appealing an adverse action to the Board. If the employee neither files a discrimination complaint with the agency nor appeals the agency action to the Board, the complaint on the proposal may include the agency final decision. Tate v. USPS, 34 MSPR 653, 655 (1987) (appeal from a removal); cf. Jordan-Berry v. Dept. of Interior, 32 MSPR 412, 414 (1987) (Board could accept an appeal once the agency canceled the appellant's complaint on the proposal following the appeal on the subsequent agency decision resulting from that proposal). Compare Lee v. DHS, DC-531D-11-0569-I-1 (NP 3/15/2012) ("[t]he appellant's EEO complaint concerned the agency's March 27, 2007 initial decision regarding the denial of her WIGI, as opposed to the agency's August 1, 2007 reconsideration decision sustaining its initial decision. We further find that the appellant did not amend her complaint to include the August 1, 2007 reconsideration decision. Consequently, the timeliness standards of 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(b) do not apply, and we must instead consider whether the appeal was timely filed under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b) and, if not, whether the appellant has shown good cause for the delay."), with Myvett v. CSOSA, DC-0752-12-0189-I-1 (NP 1/14/2013) (permitting otherwise belated Board appeal of a removal after the appellant unsuccessfully attempted to add the removal claim to an existing EEO complaint that included discrimination and reprisal allegations as to several actions, including the proposed removal; the Board likened the situation to the appellant in Williams v. USPS, 115 MSPR 318, 319-20 ¶¶ 3-4 (2010), whose appeal, first filed as a mixed case with EEOC, was considered to have been timely filed but filed with the wrong agency).

If the Board cannot determine what the appellant's EEO complaint encompassed, a remand may be in order to ascertain whether the MSPB appeal is timely. See Green v. Dept. of Interior, 72 MSPR 667, 671 (1996). Alternatively, if the EEO complaint is initiated as a complaint against a proposal and a confusing series of events ensues triggered by the agency's post-complaint reinitiation of the adverse proposal and unclear jurisdictional rulings from an EEOC administrative judge, the Board may find the ensuing adverse action appeal timely if the appellant demonstrated due diligence in pursuing his appeal rights and would otherwise be denied a hearing on the merits of his case. See Mincey v. USPS, 79 MSPR 663, 666 (1998), followed, Kirkland-Zuck v. DHUD, 85 MSPR 180, 182-83 ¶¶ 4-10 (2000) (a pro se appeal just seven days late was accepted by the Board when there was no prejudice to the agency; the appellant filed an EEO complaint challenging the proposal and the agency did not inform the appellant that she had to refile her EEO complaint to incorporate the removal action).
