broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 02: Restoration Rights Upon Recovery From Occupational Injury or Illness, Section G: Remedy, Subsection 01: Same or Equivalent Position, Part a: Return to Status Quo
a. Return to Status Quo

The employee who fully recovers within a year of a compensable injury goes back to his job, but if the duties of the job were limited before the compensable injury, as when an employee was under investigation and given limited duties, there is no violation of the right to restoration if the employee is returned to the status quo, i.e., limited duties pending the outcome of the investigation, Jinn v. DOJ, 106 MSPR 305, 308-10 ¶ 9-12, 2007 MSPB 177 (2007):

An employee who fully recovers from a compensable injury within one year from the date eligibility for compensation began is entitled to be restored immediately and unconditionally to his former position or an equivalent one. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(a); Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 81 M.S.P.R. 92, ¶ 6 (1999). The Board has jurisdiction over such an appeal if the appellant makes non-frivolous allegations that: (1) He is an employee of an executive branch agency; (2) he suffered a compensable injury; (3) he fully recovered from the compensable injury within 1 year from the date his eligibility for compensation began; (4) the agency failed to restore him or improperly restored him; and (5) if he was separated from his position prior to the alleged failure to restore or improper restoration, his separation was from a position without time limitation and substantially related to the compensable injury. See, e.g., Frye v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 695, ¶ 9 (2006); Burgess v. Department of the Interior, 95 M.S.P.R. 134, ¶ 8 (2003); Sullivan v. Department of Transportation, 59 M.S.P.R. 18, 19 (1993).

Thus, the administrative judge erred when he applied a "preponderance of the evidence" standard of jurisdiction to the appellant's allegations. Instead, the administrative judge should have determined whether the appellant raised non-frivolous allegations of jurisdiction and was entitled to the hearing on the merits that he requested. See Burgess, 95 M.S.P.R. 134, ¶ 8. This adjudicatory error, however, provides no basis for reversal of the initial decision. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984). We have considered the appellant's allegations under the proper "non-frivolous" standard and find that those allegations, even if proven, are insufficient to establish a non-frivolous allegation of Board jurisdiction.

The record indicates that, shortly before the appellant's injury, the agency assigned him duties that were outside those of his supervisory General Foreman position. This assignment was pending the completion of an investigation into alleged misconduct committed by the appellant in his supervisory capacity. After his return to duty on March 10, 2006, the agency restored the appellant to his General Foreman position, but again assigned the appellant duties outside of his General Foreman duties, as it had done before his injury, due to the ongoing investigation.

Under the circumstances of this case, we discern nothing improper in the agency's assigning the appellant duties other than his supervisory General Foreman duties upon his return to work, pending the outcome of the investigation. To hold otherwise would place the appellant in a better position than he would have been in had he not been absent from the agency. Such is not required. See Raicovich v. U.S. Postal Service, 675 F.2d 417, 424 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (while injured employees are not to suffer any loss of rights or benefits, they are "not to be accorded greater rights than they would have had had their employment been continuous"; rather, they are to be accorded treatment equal to that which they would have received but for their injury); King v. Department of the Navy, 98 M.S.P.R. 547, ¶ 19 (2005) (finding that the imposition of a security clearance requirement before rehiring the appellant, who had priority consideration rights as a fully-recovered employee, did not violate the restoration order because, at the time of her separation, the appellant's position required a security clearance, as did all positions at the agency's facility), aff'd, 167 F. App'x 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006); cf. Sherrod v. Department of the Navy, 90 M.S.P.R. 347, ¶¶ 13, 17 (2001) (where a personnel action is cancelled, the appellant is entitled to status quo ante relief, but is not entitled to a better position than she was in at the time of the agency action). Here, the appellant has received treatment equal to that which he would have received but for his injury, i.e., his assignment to non-supervisory duties pending the completion of the investigation into his alleged wrongdoing.

The remedial approach of a retroactive job search was explored upon in Tram v. USPS, 118 MSPR 388, 390-92 ¶ 7-10, 2012 MSPB 92 (2012) (following remand, Tram v. USPS, 120 MSPR 208, 2013 MSPB 77 (2013)), distinguishing Latham, supra, involving discontinuation of modified work assignments:

The purpose of a Board order canceling a wrongful action is to place the appellant as nearly as possible in the status quo ante, i.e., in the situation she would have been in had the action not occurred. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Bullock v. Department of the Air Force, 80 M.S.P.R. 361, ¶ 5 (1998); see 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(1), (2). In order to accomplish status quo ante relief, the Board will often be required to exercise broad remedial powers. Lavelle v. Department of the Navy, 40 M.S.P.R. 329, 331 (1989). The Board will consider the facts and circumstances of a particular case in deciding upon an appropriate remedy. Cf. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d) (an agency must make every effort to restore a partially recovered individual within the local commuting area according to the circumstances of each case).

Under the particular circumstances of this case, we agree with the agency that retroactive restoration is not the appropriate status quo ante remedy. A partially recovered individual does not enjoy an unconditional right to restoration. Delalat v. Department of the Air Force, 103 M.S.P.R. 448, ¶ 16 (2006); Irlanda v. U.S. Postal Service, 23 M.S.P.R. 289, 291-92 (1984); see 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(a), (d). Rather, an agency's decision to deny restoration to a partially recovered individual constitutes a reviewable action only to the extent that it is "arbitrary and capricious." 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(c); see Zysk v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 520, ¶ 6 (2008); see also Bledsoe v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 659 F.3d 1097, 1103-04 (Fed. Cir. 2011). In this case, the appellant has not established that the agency should have retained her in her former modified assignment beyond April 9, 2009.

However, upon discontinuing the appellant's modified assignment, the agency was obligated to conduct a legally sufficient job search for an alternative assignment. Sanchez v. U.S. Postal Service, 114 M.S.P.R. 345, ¶ 14 (2010); Urena v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 13 (2009); 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d). The administrative judge found that the agency failed to conduct such a search in a timely manner and that the agency's action was arbitrary and capricious in that regard. The agency has not challenged the administrative judge's finding, which is supported by the record and the law.

The Board will not order the appellant restored to an assignment that was properly discontinued, nor will it order back pay based on such an assignment because that would put the appellant in a better position than if the wrongful action had not occurred. Cf. Hagan v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 8 (2005) ("A status quo ante remedy does not require that the appellant be placed in a better position than he was in at the time of the agency's action."). Rather, in a case like this one where the denial of restoration was arbitrary and capricious for lack of a proper job search, the Board has found that the appropriate remedy is for "the agency to conduct an appropriate search within the local commuting area retroactive to...the date of the appellant's request for restoration, and to consider her for any suitable vacancies."[1] Sapp v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 411, ¶ 21 (1999). The remedy of a retroactive job search will be sufficient to correct the wrongful action and substitute it with a correct one based on an appropriate search. However, it will not put the appellant in a better position than the one she was in before the wrongful action because it leaves open the possibility that the agency might still be unable to find an appropriate assignment available as of April 9, 2009. The appellant may be entitled to back pay only if the agency's retroactive job search uncovers available work to which it could have restored her. Therefore, this case is distinguishable from Latham v. U.S. Postal Service, 117 M.S.P.R. 400, ¶¶ 77, 83 (2012), where the Board ordered the agency to retroactively restore two appellants to their former modified assignments. In those situations, the Board found that the agency had acted arbitrarily and capriciously because the appellants established that the limited circumstances under which the agency could legitimately discontinue their modified assignments were not present. Latham, 117 M.S.P.R. 400, ¶¶ 42, 49.

[1] Under the circumstances of this case, we find it appropriate to treat the date that the agency discontinued the appellant's limited duty assignment as the date that she "requested" restoration within the meaning of Sapp.
