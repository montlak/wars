2. Policy on Continuances

5 USC 7701(i)(4) states "It shall be the duty of the Board, an administrative law judge, or employee designated by the Board to hear any proceeding under this section to expedite to the extent practicable that proceeding." The Board made the point in Gipson v. VA, 1 MSPR 435, 436-37, 1 MSPB 420 (1980), that, by law, cases involving discrimination must be decided within 120 days from an appeal. See 5 USC 7702. Board internal policy directives and case law have, over the years, extended the 120-day limit to all cases (with suspension of that deadline in 2015 as a result of the large number of furlough appeals and the limited number of AJs). Dickerson v. District of Columbia Gov't, 3 MSPR 181, 185, 3 MSPB 282 (1980), noted that Board policy is to hold hearings promptly unless extraordinary circumstances are shown. That policy comports with congressional concern over administrative delays that occurred in the processing of appeals by the Board's predecessor, the Civil Service Commission. As stated in the legislative history of the Reform Act, delays are especially troublesome because they adversely affect, to a significant extent, employees who might be unemployed while their appeals are pending. The future course of the career of the employee is subject to great uncertainty as long as the appeals are pending. Dickerson resolved that an attorney who agrees to accept a case pending before the Board must be prepared to move expeditiously toward an equitable resolution of the matter, including adherence to the 120-day time limit. The Board's policy on timely regional adjudication is followed. Unfortunately, the Board itself allows cases to languish far beyond an additional 120 days when a PFR is filed. Some appeals sit with the Board for years before they are decided. Briley v. NARA, 236 F.3d 1373, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 2001), noted of delay at all levels of an IRA appeal:

We note that the prolonged history of this case calls into question the Board's ability to provide prompt justice to both the complaining employee and to the government agency. Briley filed her first appeal with the Merit Systems Protection Board in January of 1995, and the Board's final order was not issued until June of 1999--a period of four and a half years. The costs of such extensive and time-consuming litigation weigh heavily on the complaining employee and the civil service generally. In the future we expect that the Board, the government, and complaining employees will take appropriate steps to avoid such protracted proceedings.

Notwithstanding what happens at the MSPB headquarters level in the processing of petitions for review (where the processing time has been reduced over the years), the processing of appeals at the regional level is usually sprightly, with exceptions for some appeals that become protracted because of discovery or other issues, and some that languish for many months with AJs before initial decisions are issued. The Board's guidance on those timelines strongly influences or controls decisions on requests by parties for adjustment in hearing or prehearing submission dates, subject to reasonably liberal use of DWOPs and suspensions to extend the time for regional adjudication.

AJs (or their supervisors, regional chief administrative judges) issue hearing notices setting times and places for hearings. Unlike judicial proceedings and labor or commercial arbitrations, the AJ usually does not consult with the parties in advance to determine what dates appear reasonable for the hearing. It is common practice for AJs to schedule hearings and to then alter the schedule as necessary. Bergstein v. USPS, 28 MSPR 495, 497 (1985). There are reasons for the failure of AJs to negotiate hearing dates: first, it is easy to pick a date and let the parties take the time and go to the effort to seek an adjustment; second, it may be difficult to negotiate a date with pro se parties who are not used to administrative procedures. Some AJs will respond to reasonable requests concerning hearing dates. If counsel writes to the AJ soon after the appeal is filed and lists dates known to be good or bad, or invites the AJ to contact all counsel to solicit their preferences for hearing dates, it is likely that a responsive AJ will attempt to set a date that is convenient to the parties and avoid later time, trouble, and motions. Parties occasionally inform an AJ, through pleading or e-filing, of the dates of unavailability of a key witness, to be considered when the hearing is scheduled. The very best AJs take the initiative and convene a conference call to discuss among counsel mutually acceptable hearing dates.

At least when continuances were in use (ere widespread use of case suspensions and DWOPs), under Risher v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 430, 432 (1982), "if a motion for a continuance clearly relates to a matter that is material to the case, the motion should be granted in the absence of a showing that it would be unduly burdensome, harassing, or cause unwarranted delay." Board policy on continuances is stated in Roberson v. DHHS, 24 MSPR 240, 241 (1984): assuming no lack of due diligence by the requestor or prejudice to the opponent, the right to a continuance depends upon a showing of good cause, an elastic concept resting upon principles of equity and justice. "If good cause is shown, the stay must be granted." Ormerod v. OPM, 10 MSPR 201, 203 (1982); cf. Koch v. SEC (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 01-3347) ("While specific prejudice to the opposing party is often a valid reason for a court to deny a continuance, the absence of a showing of specific prejudice is not sufficient to render the denial of a continuance an abuse of discretion.").

The Board restated and modified its past guidance concerning continuances (now generally supplanted by case suspensions, DWOPs, or routine postponements of hearing dates) in Mann v. Dept. of Air Force, 44 MSPR 197, 200 (1990), stating that "[T]he basis for a continuance...rests on the sound policy considerations that employees be given a hearing on the merits of their cases and be ensured a fair opportunity to secure an independent review of an agency's action." The Board must also balance the competing interests of avoiding unwarranted delays and expediting the administrative resolution of employee appeals. There is some flexibility, however, in the 120-day rule. Biberstine v. DODDS, 37 MSPR 248, 254 (1988), involved the appellant's assertion that a necessary continuance was denied because the AJ felt the Board's 120-day adjudication policy prohibited a continuance that would extend the regional adjudication:

If...a motion for a continuance presents good cause and clearly relates to a matter that is material to the case, in the absence of a showing that it would be unduly burdensome, harassing, or cause unwarranted delay, the motion should be granted. Cf. Pedersen v. Dept. of Transp., 9 MSPR 195, 199 (1981). A motion for a continuance which, if granted, would cause an administrative judge to issue an initial decision beyond the 120th day does not, for that reason, cause unwarranted delay. Although an administrative judge should attempt to comply with the 120-day adjudication policy, denial of a continuance solely because of that policy in a non-discrimination case constitutes an abuse of discretion. To hold otherwise would exalt expedition over the substantive rights of both of the parties.[3] If there is, in fact, good cause to grant a lengthy continuance, the administrative judge must grant the continuance. If the time requested is both lengthy and indefinite, the administrative judge may dismiss a case without prejudice, subject to refiling at a date or event certain in the future. See Toma v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 408 (1981).

[3] To the extent that the Board has indicated in other cases that it will consider the 120-day adjudication policy as a reason for denying a continuance...the Board rejects this principle as a factor for denying a requested continuance in a non-discrimination case. Although we need not resolve the question of whether the 120-day provision in 5 USC 7702 may be a factor in cases involving discrimination allegations, we note that such determinations must evolve from a balancing of all of the procedural entitlements in sections 7701 and 7702.

Kent v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-10-0652-I-1 (NP 9/19/2011), summarized the law on continuances:

The appellant argues that the administrative judge erred in rejecting his request for a continuance to allow new counsel to prepare for the appeal. He asserts that she denied the continuance based entirely upon her concern about her 120-day deadline for processing the appeal.

The Board reviews an administrative judge's ruling on a motion to continue for abuse of discretion. See Milner v. Department of Justice, 87 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 9 (2001). A request for a continuance that presents good cause and relates materially to the appeal should be granted unless it is unduly burdensome, harassing, or will cause unwarranted delay. See Bloom v. Department of the Army, 101 M.S.P.R. 79, ¶ 8 (2006); Pecina v. Department of the Army, 65 M.S.P.R. 100, 103 (1994). An administrative judge's denial of a continuance solely based on the 120-day case processing deadline can constitute an abuse of discretion. See Friedrick v. Department of Justice, 52 M.S.P.R. 126, 130 (1991), aff'd, 980 F.2d 742 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); see also Milner, 87 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 9 (the administrative judge abused his discretion by dismissing an appeal without prejudice solely to avoid exceeding the 120-day adjudication standard). To warrant reversal, the appellant must establish that the improper denial of a continuance adversely affected his substantive rights. See Friedrick, 52 M.S.P.R. at 130.

Although the record does not clearly document the administrative judge's reasons for denying the appellant's motion for a continuance, it does not show that she abused her discretion in denying the motion or that the denial adversely affected the appellant's substantive rights because her ruling was otherwise fully supported by the record. In particular, the appellant never previously indicated that he was seeking counsel; he provided no advance notice that he had retained counsel; he had represented himself vigorously throughout the proceeding; the agency was prepared to proceed at the hearing; and the agency's witnesses were present for the hearing. Under these circumstances, the appellant's belated request would appear burdensome and create undue delay. See generally Bloom, 101 M.S.P.R. 79, ¶ 8. Furthermore, as acknowledged by the appellant, the administrative judge attempted to ameliorate any difficulties his new counsel might have had by assisting him with formulating questions, explaining certain legal issues to him, and allowing him frequent recesses.

Biberstine, supra, was followed in Friedrick v. DOJ, 52 MSPR 126, 130-31 (1991) (suggesting the possibility of a continuance were there a surprise witness at a hearing, but then determining that the appellant should have anticipated the presence of the particular witness). But see Townsend v. USDA, 14 MSPR 242, 243-44 (1982) (AJ was not faulted for refusing to schedule another hearing session to permit the appellant's rebuttal witnesses when the 120-day limit was near; the AJ thought that the record was already sufficiently developed, and the principal hearing session was delayed by the appellant's failure to promptly respond to the AJ's orders). Law v. IRS, 5 MSPR 141, 145, 5 MSPB 203 (1981), determined that there was no harmful error after the AJ declined to provide a continuance to the appellant's representative to review 500 pages of documents produced by the agency the day before the hearing; although the burden on the representative was acknowledged, there was no showing of a single instance of disadvantage to the appellant in the hearing presentation or examination of agency witnesses. Nor was there harmful adjudicatory error without a showing of prejudice if the AJ continued a hearing on the motion of an agency, even though the motion was not supported by the affidavit required by Board regulations. Miller v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 482, 485 (1984). Miller also decided that no harmful adjudicatory error was presented when an AJ denied a continuance to the appellant to secure additional witnesses, gave the appellant the opportunity to submit affidavits from those witnesses, and the appellant failed to submit the affidavits: there was no proof of prejudice. See Hoyt v. Dept. of Treasury, 22 MSPR 264, 266 (1984) (motion for postponement is to be written, accompanied by an affidavit stating reasons for the request, and granted only upon a showing of good cause); Davis v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 305, 307 (1983) (continuance for medical reasons was denied for lack of an affidavit); Schwartz v. OPM, 3 MSPR 151, 153 (1980) (no error by an AJ who denied a continuance of the hearing when the appellant failed to support his request with an affidavit); cf. Sutton v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 512, 513 n.* (1984) (no error warranting a remand when the appellant did not assert at the time of the hearing that she received a hearing notice in an insufficient time to prepare).
