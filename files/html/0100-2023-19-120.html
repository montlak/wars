a. Agency Request for Information

Although agencies are entitled to actual or constructive knowledge of a disabling condition, they are not entitled to an unlimited amount of information about an appellant's alcoholism treatment as a condition of accommodation, as Avritch v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 542, 544-46 (1985), explained, reversing a removal based on unacceptable performance for lack of sufficient accommodation:

Because appellant was a qualified handicapped employee, the agency was obligated to afford him reasonable accommodation by offering him rehabilitative assistance and allowing him to take leave for treatment, if necessary, before initiating any action for continuing performance or misconduct problems related to his alcoholism. See Ruzek, supra, at 311, 7 MSPR at 443. Appellant contends that the agency failed to do this, even though it referred him to its Counseling and Assistance Center (Center), since the Center personnel refused to assist him because he would not sign disclosure forms he believed violated his rights of confidentiality.

Appellant was effectively denied assistance. The agency first referred appellant to the Center for an appointment on April 21, 1981. On the advice of his attorney, however, appellant refused to sign the Privacy Act Statement presented to him by Center personnel on the grounds that it violated statutory and regulatory provisions requiring confidentiality of patient records. Appellant's position was confirmed by the Office of the Staff Judge Advocate, Naval District Washington (SJA), in a memorandum to the Center's director dated June 10, 1981. In the memorandum, the SJA recommended that the Center discontinue using its Privacy Act Statement in cases involving civilian clients because the Statement conflicted with 42 USC § 4582 (now 42 USC § 290dd-3).

The agency scheduled a second appointment for appellant on July 15, 1981. At the appointment, appellant was presented with a blank release form to sign. When appellant asked to have his attorney, who was not present, review the form, or to have the form filled in, the Center personnel refused and told the agency that appellant would not be considered for further screening. Appellant's attorney again requested an opinion from the SJA asserting that the release form did not meet the requirements of 42 CFR §§ 2.31(a); 2.38(c), and 2.38(d). In response, the SJA informed the Center director that the regulations apply to the release form as well as to the Privacy Act Statement and that any disclosures of information must be in strict compliance with the regulations.

Our review of the statutory and regulatory provisions cited by appellant confirms the SJA's conclusions. The statute provides for disclosing information in patient records, without the patient's written consent, in only three limited circumstances: to medical personnel to meet a bona fide medical emergency, to qualified personnel to conduct research, audits, or evaluations if the individual patient's identity is not disclosed, and pursuant to an appropriate court order. See 42 USC § 290dd-3. The Center's Privacy Act Statement, however, also permitted the nonconsensual release of appellant's records to personnel performing official Department of Defense duties, including law enforcement, with a requirement to view appellant's records in support of their duties. The statement provided that the personnel could review appellant's records to determine his suitability or eligibility for retention or return to full duty, discharge or type of discharge warranted, and security clearance actions. Therefore, the form clearly went beyond the release of information contemplated by the statute.

Further, we find that the blank release form also conflicted with the regulations identified by appellant's counsel. The regulations require that a consent for a disclosure must contain specific information including: the names of the disclosing program, the person or organization receiving the information, and the patient; the reason for and extent of the disclosure; and the procedure for revoking consent to disclose. See 42 CFR § 2.31(a). The regulations further limit the information to be disclosed even with patient consent. See 42 CFR § 2.38. The release form submitted to appellant failed to identify the persons to whom the information would be disclosed and provided for the release of a broad range of information including the extent and appropriateness of appellant's action on the Center's recommendations and evidence of his positive response to treatment. We find, therefore, that the release form did not include the safeguards provided for in the regulations.

Throughout this time, appellant expressed a willingness and desire to participate in the Center's program. At appellant's first appointment, his attorney suggested that the Center proceed with an intake interview and counseling services while waiting for the opinion of the Staff Judge Advocate's Office on the Privacy Act Statement. In letters to the agency and Staff Judge Advocate's Office, appellant's attorney further expressed appellant's interest in proceeding with the program. We find, therefore, that the agency failed to reasonably accommodate appellant's alcoholism because it required him to sign improper forms before admitting him to the program in which he was otherwise willing to participate.
