broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 15: Pay, Premiums, and Allowances, Section F: Pay and Employment for Employees of Particular Agencies, Subsection 03: Military School Systems

3. Military School Systems

20 USC 241(a) (now repealed) provided for compensation to be set by the agency for certain educational agencies operating schools for military installations. The Supreme Court entered and ended the debate over negotiability of military school teachers' salaries in Ft. Stewart Sch. v. FLRA, 495 U.S. 641, 110 S. Ct. 2043 (1990), post, affirming negotiability determinations by the Eleventh Circuit and FLRA. Salary-related proposals were earlier reviewed in Ft. Stewart Sch. v. FLRA, 860 F.2d 396, 400-03, 405-06 (11th Cir. 1988). The circuit court affirmed FLRA's negotiability determination. The court explained, distinguishing Dept. of Navy, Military Sealift Command v. FLRA, 836 F.2d 1409 (3d Cir. 1988):

[T]he absence of the terms "wages" and "hours" from the FSLMRA does not prove that Congress intended to exclude them from negotiations.... Congress simply used the general term "conditions of employment," which encompasses wages, to define the scope of negotiable matters.

...

...The legislative history indicates that Congress intended to treat wages and fringe benefits as other conditions of employment; the parties must bargain over them unless a federal statute specifically provides for them or the proposed matters would conflict with law....

...

We agree with the Second Circuit that section 241 does not specifically provide for the schools' teachers' and other employees' wages....

...

[T]he legislative history demonstrates that Congress did not intend comparable education to require identical salaries....

...

We similarly defer to the FLRA's decision that the Union's proposals do not invade the Army's right to make its budget. A proposal does not infringe on an agency's right to determine the budget merely because the proposed matter will increase the agency's costs....

...The proposals would not necessarily increase the Army's costs; the Army did not specify any amount by which the proposed matters would increase its budget. Further, any increase in the employees' salaries would not significantly increase the Army's budget; the Army concedes that its budget includes bases, troops, weapons, vehicles, other equipment, salaries for all other officers, and expenses for its eight other schools. Finally, the Army did not establish that no compensating benefits would offset such costs even if its costs increased....

Rounding out judicial consideration by the circuit courts, the D.C. Circuit decided pay proposals for DODDS teachers were nonnegotiable in DODDS v. FLRA, 863 F.2d 988, 993-94 (D.C. Cir. 1988). The Sixth Circuit followed suit in Ft. Knox Dependent Sch. v. FLRA, 875 F.2d 1179, 1181-82 (6th Cir. 1989) (also deciding that teachers' pay proposals were nonnegotiable), determining that "conditions of employment" under the FSLMRA implies a narrower range of bargainable topics than under the NLRA, that Congress did not intend to subject pay of federal employees to bargaining, and that wages and other compensation matters for federal employees fall outside the duty to bargain.

Ft. Stewart Sch. v. FLRA, 495 U.S. 641, 645-57, 110 S. Ct. 2043 (1990), resolving the split in the circuits, and considering Ft. Stewart Ass'n of Educators and Ft. Stewart Sch., 28 FLRA 547 (1987), concluded that the pay proposals were negotiable:

...Petitioner claims that...7103(a)(14) defines "conditions of employment" as matters affecting "working conditions," and because the latter term most naturally connotes "the physical conditions under which an employee labors."...

...

...Petitioner seeks to persuade us...that the term "conditions of employment"...does not include the insisted-upon prerequisite par excellence, wages.... To describe this position is sufficient to reject it, but we nonetheless examine briefly the elements petitioner sets forth to establish that "conditions of employment" clearly has a meaning here that it bears nowhere else.

Petitioner points to the National Labor Relations Act...which authorizes bargaining over "wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment,"...and to the Postal Reorganization Act...which grants postal workers the right to bargain over "wages, hours, and working conditions."... Because each of these statutes specifically refers to wages, the argument runs, we must infer from the absence of such a reference in the FSLMRS that Congress did not mean to include them. But those other statutes deal with labor-management relations in entirely different fields of employment, and the FSLMRS contains no indication that it is to be read in pari materia with them....

Petitioner discusses at great length the legislative history...from which it has culled a formidable number of statements suggesting that certain members and committees of Congress did not think the duty to bargain would extend to proposals relating to wages and fringe benefits....

The trouble with these statements, to the extent they are relevant to our inquiry, is that they may have been wrong. The wages and fringe benefits of the overwhelming majority of Executive Branch employees are fixed by law, in accordance with the General Schedules of the Civil Service Act, see 5 USC 5332, and are therefore eliminated from the definition of "conditions of employment" by the third exception in 7103(a)(14)...--which excludes "matters...specifically provided for by Federal statute."... Employees of schools established under sec. 241 are among a minuscule minority of federal employees whose wages are exempted from operation of the General Schedules.... The legislative materials to which petitioner refers display no awareness of this exception.... There is no conceivable persuasive effect in legislative history that may reflect nothing more than the speakers' incomplete understanding of the world upon which the statute will operate....

Petitioner next argues that, even if the Union's proposals relate to "conditions of employment" subject to bargaining under 7102, they are exempted from the statutory duty to bargain by 7106, which provides that "nothing in this chapter shall affect the authority of any management official of any agency...to determine the...budget...of the agency...."

...

Petitioner does not take issue with the Authority's premise that 7106 does not make a proposal nonnegotiable simply because it "imposes a cost upon the agency which requires the expenditure of appropriate agency funds."... Rather, petitioner argues that "the application of the FLRA's rule here--particularly the conclusion that the proposal calling for a 13.5% pay raise would not significantly affect the agency's budget--is plainly flawed."...

...

...Petitioner has challenged neither the Authority's requirement that an agency show a significant and unavoidable increase in its costs, nor the Authority's finding that petitioner failed to submit any evidence on that point in this case. Rather, it asks us to hold that a proposal calling for a 13.5% salary increase would necessarily result in a "significant and unavoidable" increase in the agency's overall costs. We cannot do that without knowing even so rudimentary a fact as the percentage of the agency's budget attributable to teachers' salaries....

Petitioner's final argument rests upon 20 USC 241, which directs the agency establishing a school thereunder to "ensure that the education provided pursuant to such arrangement is comparable to free public education provided for children in comparable communities in the State,"...and to limit expenditures "[t]o the maximum extent practicable" to "an amount per pupil which will not exceed the per pupil cost of free public education provided for children in comparable communities in the State."...

...

...According to petitioner, "it is a fair reading of [sec. 241] to conclude that Congress excepted [wages and fringe benefits] from the civil service laws so that they would be set in comparison with those at public schools."... That is not so.... The statute requires equivalence...in total per pupil expenditure, not in each separate element of educational cost....

[P]etitioner chose not to assert a claim that Army Regulation 352-3 was either "essential...to the accomplishment of the mission or the execution of the functions of the agency," 5 CFR 2424.11(a), or "necessary to insure the maintenance of basic merit principles," 2424.11(b). But, those alternatives were available, and suffice to give the regulation for which there is a "compelling need" an existence quite independent of the regulation whose elimination would be inconsistent with law.

Earlier decisions from FLRA found substantial room for negotiability of teachers' salaries, despite some contrary judicial determinations. A proposal for particular salaries did not interfere with the right of the agency to determine its budget. The proposals did not prescribe that the budget include specific provisions or amounts for compensation of employees. Agency procedures did not require that budget submissions include itemization of salaries and benefits as separate budget items. The costs involved did not create such an increase in the budget as to be outside the duty to bargain under Wright-Patterson. Costs of proposals cannot be aggregated for that determination. Ft. Bragg Unit of N.C. Ass'n of Educators and Ft. Bragg Dependents Sch., 12 FLRA 519, 522-23 (1983), followed in AFGE Local 1770 and DODDS, Ft. Bragg, 25 FLRA 1132 (1987) (disclaiming undue reliance by an agency on costs of pay proposals as basis for negotiability determination), rev'd, DODDS, Ft. Bragg v. FLRA, 838 F.2d 129 (4th Cir. 1988), vacated as moot, 838 F.2d 129, 130-38 (4th Cir. 1988); see NEA and DODDDESS, Ft. Bragg, 53 FLRA 898, 919-21 (1997) (approving the negotiability of Proposal 4 (second sentence) for a 4% annual bonus for Career Status II teachers who attain National Board Certification); Ft. Stewart Ass'n of Educators and Ft. Stewart Sch., 28 FLRA 547, 551-53 (1987) (reviewed in Supreme Court Ft. Stewart decision); Ft. Knox Teachers Ass'n and Ft. Knox Dependents Sch., 28 FLRA 179, 181-82 (1987) (approving negotiability of incentive stipend for service as reading improvement teachers). The Second Circuit affirmed the Authority's reasoning upholding the negotiation of proposals concerning compensation of military school teachers in West Point Elem. Sch. Teachers Ass'n v. FLRA, 855 F.2d 936, 943 (2d Cir. 1988) (specifically rejecting employer's argument that negotiated compensation would defeat the education and cost parity requirements of 5 USC 241(a)); cf. FEA and DODEA, 73 FLRA 262 (2022) (Member Kiko concurring in part and dissenting in part) (reviewing some decisions from the courts and FLRA relating to the negotiability of pay systems for teachers at dependents’ schools).

Overtime entitlement was negotiable for school teachers within the statutory system. Ft. Knox Teachers Ass'n and Ft. Knox Dependent Sch., 25 FLRA 1119, 1123 (1987); see AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., 29 FLRA 1447, 1450-51 (1987); Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 628, 651-52 (1987) (Proposal 1e; additional pay for the loss of preparation time); Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 485, 509-10 (1987); Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 28 FLRA 936, 942 (1987) (Proposal 2). Following the same reasoning, proposals were negotiable that permitted paid year-long sabbaticals for education or travel of employees following 10 years of continuous service. The agency demonstrated neither statutory conflicts nor compelling need to preclude negotiation of the proposal. A section of the proposal requiring that upon return from sabbatical teachers would be reinstated to their original position conflicted with the right of the agency to assign employees under § 7106(a)(2)(A). Ft. Knox Teachers Ass'n and Ft. Knox Dependent Sch., 26 FLRA 934, 939-40 (1987); see Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 628, 652-54 (1987) (requirement of reimbursement for university training expenses in exchange for loss of in-service days). A proposal was approved that unit employees receive additional compensation for all time required to perform duties beyond the normal duty day. Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 28 FLRA 700, 709-11 (1987) (Proposal 1). For additional analysis of the statutory relationship between salaries for the Ft. Bragg teachers and DODDS teachers, refer to DOD, Ft. Bragg Dependents Sch. and Ft. Bragg Ass'n of Educators, 49 FLRA 333, 358-57 (1994) (Proposals 8 and 9).

More than 40 proposals were considered in Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 27 FLRA 492 (1987), involving living quarters allowances, post differentials, allowances for dependent travel, transportation agreements, and other allowances for overseas employees. The decision is discussed in other sections of this chapter in the context of particular allowances. It provides considerable analysis of the allowance system for overseas employees. Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 485, 500-01, 509-10 (1987), also considered proposals concerning pay adjustments under 20 USC 902-3. Other pay proposals were considered in Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 734, 770-72 (1987) (Proposals 32-34). Nonnegotiable as matters specifically provided for by law were proposals pertaining to overseas teachers' basic compensation and pay-fixing procedures. Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 45 FLRA 1185, 1187, 1191-95 (1992) (Proposal 1-5) (following Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 734 (1987), and interpreting March v. United States, 506 F.2d 1306, 1315-20 (D.C. Cir. 1974)). Antilles Consol. Ed. Ass'n. and DODEA, 73 FLRA 282 (2022), approved as negotiable Proposal 4, requiring that bargaining unit employees' aggregate salary--base pay, locality pay, and COLA--be no less than the salary in the salary schedule for teachers at the school with the highest salary schedule in DODEA's Mid-Atlantic District.

As to a different type of military school system, the Child Care system established by the Military Child Care Act of 1989, 10 USC 113 note, refer to NAGE Local R4-6 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Eustis, 42 FLRA 687, 691-98 (1991), concerning the Act and several proposals involving pay-setting practices relating to Child Care workers.
