c. Discriminatory Application of Interim Relief

Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 73 MSPR 590, 595-96 (1997), determined that the Board would not entertain claims for compensatory damages arising out of discrimination by the agency in the application of interim relief:

In her Memorandum in Support of Petition for Review to the EEOC, the appellant contended that she "suffered and continued to suffer emotional distress after being denied the opportunity to return to employment in April of 1993," that the agency "deliberately denied her right to be reinstated by barring her from the base claiming that her presence would have a disruptive influence on personnel," that she "remained in a depressed emotional state for almost two years after the case was initially decided," and that a psychologist who testified at the hearing stated that she "would remained [sic] ill until she went back to work."

These assertions appear to set forth a claim for compensatory damages arising out of the agency's decision to provide interim relief to the appellant by making an undue disruption determination and then placing her in nonduty status pending adjudication of its petition for review by the Board. In King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1994), the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that even if an agency, in the guise of providing interim relief, engages in discrimination against an employee, Congress has not given the Board jurisdiction to consider such claims. Id. at 1375. Rather, the court stated that if an employee has evidence that the agency discriminated against her in the interim relief process, her recourse is with the EEOC, not with the Board. Id.

Decisions by the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit constitute precedent that is binding on the Board. See Gende v. Department of Justice, 35 MSPR 518, 523 (1987). Based on the court's decision in King v. Jerome, we are compelled to find that we lack authority to consider any claim by the appellant that the agency discriminated against her in effecting interim relief by making an undue disruption determination and placing her in nonduty status with the pay and benefits of her position--a course of action which, we note, is expressly permitted by statute. See 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)-(B). Consequently, we lack jurisdiction to consider whether any discrimination in the interim relief process caused the appellant emotional distress resulting in compensatory damages. See generally Shaw v. Department of the Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 588 (1989) (the Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by statute or regulation).

Indeed, EEOC's decision does not state that we consider any claim made by the appellant related to the agency's compliance with the Board's interim relief order. Rather, EEOC's decision states that we should consider only compensatory damages which may have resulted from the action that the Board previously found to be discriminatory, viz., the September 16, 1992 removal action. Hence, we find that EEOC's decision is not in conflict with the Federal Circuit's decision in King v. Jerome.

Moreover, regarding purely procedural issues related to mixed cases, the Board owes no deference to the EEOC. See Crawford, 70 MSPR at 422. Thus, even if EEOC's decision were contrary to the court's holding on this point, we would find that the issue of whether Congress has given the Board jurisdiction over an action (and, concomitantly, the authority to order a remedy) is a matter of procedure to which we owe no deference to the EEOC. See, e.g., Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 MSPR 211, 239 (1995) (in deciding the retroactivity of an amendment setting forth the rule for establishing contributing factor, the Board likened the amendment to "intervening statutes that confer or oust jurisdiction" because the new rule was procedural and "speaks more to the power of the court, rather than to the rights and obligations of the parties"' (quoting Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 114 S. Ct. 1483, 1502 (1994))), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). The administrative judge therefore shall not consider any evidence or argument on the issue of whether the agency's attempts to comply with the interim relief order constituted discrimination and, if so, whether they gave rise to post-hearing compensatory damages apart from any compensatory damages resulting from the removal action.
