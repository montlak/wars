a. Deficient but Timely PFR

If a party's PFR is deficient, the Board ordinarily permits that party a period of time to cure the deficiency, e.g., by properly serving opposing parties and certifying service, or by furnishing good cause for a late filing. See Labinghisa v. OPM, 52 MSPR 69, 72 (1991). Through a show cause order the Board's Clerk notifies the filing party of the deficiency and provides a set time for a response curing or explaining the deficiency. That order is not issued if the petitioner files an extension request with his late PFR; the Board then adjudicates the timeliness issue without calling for further explanation from the petitioner. See Smith v. USDA, 61 MSPR 309, 312 (1994).

In 1996 the Board determined that in the absence of bad faith or evidence that an appellant intended to abandon his appeal, it was inappropriate to dismiss a PFR only because the appellant did not timely perfect his PFR in response to the Board's first deficiency notice. Smith v. USPS, 69 MSPR 420, 423 (1996) ("To dismiss the petition--which was timely filed--because the appellant did not provide a timely and complete response to the Clerk's first notice, would amount to imposing the most severe sanction available: Dismissing the appeal for failure to prosecute."); see Tamayo v. OPM, 88 MSPR 685, 687 ¶ 5 (2001) (citing Smith v. USPS, supra: "In 1996, the Board changed its approach and held that, in the absence of bad faith or evidence that an appellant intended to abandon his appeal, it was inappropriate to dismiss a petition for review merely because the appellant did not timely perfect his petition in response to the Board's first notice.").

Before 1996, the Board admonished that it "routinely dismisses petitions for review as untimely filed when a party fails to respond with relevant material to a show cause order regarding the timeliness of a petition for review." Brown v. USPS, 52 MSPR 118, 122 n.3 (1992); see Gallegos v. Dept. of Interior, 60 MSPR 5, 8 (1993) (by failing to respond to the Clerk's show cause order, the agency failed to demonstrate good cause for an untimely filing; the agency response was also deficient because it was unverified and unsworn); Washington v. Dept. of Energy, 59 MSPR 487 (1993) (Table) ("Because the appellant did not respond to the Clerk's show-cause notice or otherwise proffer any explanation for her delay in filing the corrected petition for review, her petition must be dismissed as untimely filed."); McKinney v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 600, 601 (1990) (dismissing a corrected PFR late by a day without an explanation of untimeliness); Martinez v. OPM, 42 MSPR 434, 435 (1989) (declining to consider OPM's PFR when OPM failed to respond to the clerk's notice to show good cause for a late filing); cf. White v. OPM, 37 MSPR 320, 322-23 (1988) (show cause response to the Clerk was not legible; the appellant did not respond to the Clerk's request for a typewritten reply to the show cause order); McCuistion v. USPS, 2 MSPR 27, 31, 2 MSPB 201 (1980) (appellant received an extension to file materials supporting a PFR but no materials were filed; PFR was dismissed for lack of prosecution).

The response to a notice concerning a deficient petition should respond fully to what the Board requests. If the Board wants the appellant to correct his filing by refiling his PFR with a certificate showing service, the Board may decline to entertain it if the appellant fails to file the corrected PFR with the Board, even though it becomes apparent that the agency was served with the PFR. See Caballero v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 298, 302 (1993) (if the appellant fails timely to refile a PFR with a proper certificate of service, the PFR may be dismissed as untimely even though the Board's Clerk served a copy of the PFR on the respondent); Wooten v. VA, 56 MSPR 497, 499 (1993) (declining to accept belatedly refiled PFR that lacked a sworn explanation for the delay in refiling); Hudgies v. USPS, 55 MSPR 311, 314 (1992) (although the appellant proved service of his PFR, the Board dismissed it when the appellant did not submit timely a corrected PFR that included a service certificate; explanations for the untimely corrected filing were either in unsworn form, belated, or too vague); cf. Allen v. USPS, 44 MSPR 369, 372 (1990) (PFR was dismissed as untimely when the appellant failed to return it to the Clerk, with deficiencies corrected, in the time allowed; further delay was not excused because of the appellant's failure to comply with clearly stated directions).
