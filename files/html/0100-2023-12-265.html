c. EEO Issue

Of a disparate treatment EEO defense raised in review of an arbitrator's award, Pleasant v. DHUD, 98 MSPR 602, 610-11 ¶ 15 (2005), determined:

The appellant has additionally raised a pro forma claim that the agency's action constituted discrimination based on race, color, sex, disability, and age because the agency allegedly did not suspend any other union official for failing to request official time to engage in union activities in accordance with the requirements of the CBA and the agency's regulations. To establish a prima facie case of discrimination based on disparate treatment, the appellant must show by preponderant evidence that she is member of a protected group, she was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of the protected group, and she was treated more harshly than the individual who was not a member of her protected group. Hidalgo, 93 M.S.P.R. 645 ¶ 9. For comparison employees to be considered similarly situated to an appellant,

all relevant aspects of the appellant's employment situation must be nearly identical to those of the comparison employee. Among other things, comparative employees must have engaged in conduct similar to the appellant's without differentiating or mitigating circumstances that would distinguish their misconduct or the appropriate discipline for it.

Id. ¶ 10. Moreover, the appellant and the comparison employees must have been supervised by the same individual. Id.; Bell v. Department of the Treasury, 54 M.S.P.R. 619, 629 (1992). Assuming arguendo that the appellant has provided the Board with evidence of the first criterion for establishing a prima facie case of discrimination based on disparate treatment, the appellant has not presented the Board with evidence establishing the second or third criterion and, therefore, has failed to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. See De Bow, 97 M.S.P.R. 5 ¶ 7; Weaver, 94 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶ 19; Hidalgo, 93 M.S.P.R. 645 ¶¶ 9-10.

The appellant also maintains for the first time on review that the agency took its action in reprisal for her activities that were allegedly protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8). Although the Board has found that an appellant may raise a claim of discrimination prohibited under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1) for the first time in a request for review of an arbitrator's decision, the Board has determined that there is no statutory basis under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) to permit an appellant to raise other claims of prohibited personnel practices (i.e., under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)) unless they were first raised before the arbitrator. Means v. Department of Labor, 60 M.S.P.R. 108, 115-16 (1993); see also Luna v. Social Security Administration, 85 M.S.P.R. 301 ¶ 13 (2000); Chandler v. Social Security Administration, 80 M.S.P.R. 542 ¶ 8 (1999). Thus, we find that the appellant's failure to raise this reprisal claim before the arbitrator precludes a finding that the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation and provides no basis upon which to set aside or modify the arbitrator's decision. See Luna, 85 M.S.P.R. 301 ¶ 13; Means, 60 M.S.P.R. at 116.

Disparate treatment analysis was reviewed in Godesky v. DHHS, 101 MSPR 280, 285-86 ¶¶ 12-14, 2006 MSPB 48 (2006):

In any event, we find that the comparison employees cited by the appellant are not similarly situated to him. For comparison employees to be considered similarly situated, "all relevant aspects of the appellant's employment situation must be 'nearly identical' to those of the comparison employees." Spahn v. Department of Justice, 93 M.S.P.R. 195, ¶ 13 (2003). Among other things, comparison employees must have engaged in conduct similar to the appellant's without differentiating or mitigating circumstances that would distinguish their misconduct or the appropriate discipline for it. Id. Moreover, the appellant and the comparison employees must have been supervised by the same individual. Id.; Bell v. Department of the Treasury, 54 M.S.P.R. 619, 628-29 (1992).

Here, the record indicates that the appellant, a GS-15 Senior Technical Advisor, and the two alleged comparison employees, an administrative assistant and a security officer, had different job titles, different grade and series identifiers, were assigned to different divisions, and, presumably, had significantly different duties. See, e.g., Doe v. An arbitrator's analysis of a disability and sex discrimination defense to a Chapter 43 removal was inadequate in Tatsis v. DHUD, CB-7121-16-0003-V-1 (NP 6/16/2016):

We further find that the arbitrator's analysis regarding the appellant's discrimination claim was cursory and that he failed to consider all potentially relevant issues. See Hollingsworth v. Department of Commerce, 115 M.S.P.R. 636, ¶ 8 (2011) (finding that an arbitrator's decision is not entitled to deference where the arbitrator decides an issue without making specific findings, citing the correct legal standard, or employing the proper analytical framework). As an initial matter, the arbitrator failed to cite any legal standard or set forth any analytical framework for adjudicating the appellant's discrimination claim. See Brookens v. Department of Labor, 120 M.S.P.R. 678, ¶ 15 (2014) (vacating an arbitrator's findings where he failed to set forth any analytical framework for his findings regarding the appellant's claims of discrimination and retaliation for union activity); Sadiq, 119 M.S.P.R. 450, ¶ 12 (vacating an arbitrator's findings regarding an appellant's discrimination claims where, among other things, the arbitrator did not set forth any analytical framework for adjudicating the claims). However, it appears that the arbitrator construed the appellant's claim that the agency failed to provide her with a reasonable accommodation for her medical conditions solely as a claim of disability discrimination.

The arbitrator also should have considered whether the appellant's allegations raised a claim of discrimination based on sex under the Pregnancy Discrimination Act (PDA), Pub. L. No. 95-555, 92 Stat. 2076 (1978), codified at 42 U.S.C. § 2000e(k). The PDA amended title VII of the Civil Rights Act by expanding the definition of sex discrimination to include discrimination "because of or on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions." 42 U.S.C. § 2000e(k); see Thome v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 315, ¶ 26 (2015). The PDA further specifies that women affected by pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions must be treated the same for employment-related purposes as other persons not so affected "but similar in their ability or inability to work." 42 U.S.C. § 2000e(k). The arbitrator should have requested that the appellant clarify whether she was alleging that the agency discriminated against her based on a pregnancy-related medical condition, and if so, considered the applicability, if any, of Young v. United Parcel Service, Inc., 135 S. Ct. 1338, 1353-55 (2015), in which the United States Supreme Court recently clarified the analytical framework for proving a claim of disparate treatment discrimination under the PDA.

Moreover, as the appellant correctly notes in her request for review, in addition to the alleged oral request for a reasonable accommodation in January 2013, she also alleged that she made a second request for a reasonable accommodation in one of her September 2014 responses to the notice of proposed removal. Although the arbitrator mentioned this allegation in the arbitration decision, he failed to make any findings or conclusions regarding this claim; id. at 28-29. Accordingly, the Board has no basis upon which to defer to the arbitrator on this issue. See Sadiq, 119 M.S.P.R. 450, ¶ 12 (finding that the Board had no basis upon which to defer to an arbitrator where the arbitrator failed to make findings on certain claims).

For these reasons, we vacate the arbitrator's finding that the appellant failed to prove her affirmative defense of discrimination, and, pursuant to the Board's authority in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.155(e), we forward the matter to the Board's Northeastern Regional Office for assignment to an administrative judge to make recommended findings on the appellant's discrimination claim under the appropriate legal standards. See Brookens, 120 M.S.P.R. 678, ¶¶ 15-17 (vacating an arbitrator's findings that an agency did not retaliate against an appellant due to his union activities and discriminate against him based on age and race, and forwarding the case to a regional office for further adjudication).
