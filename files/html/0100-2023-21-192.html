broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 01: Retirement, Section N: Overpayments, Subsection 02: Basis for Waiver, Part f: Ordinary and Necessary Living Expenses; Future Events
f. Ordinary and Necessary Living Expenses; Future Events

Financial hardship encompasses consideration of ordinary and necessary living expenses.

For CSRS, 5 CFR 831.1404-05 provides:

831.1404 Financial hardship.

Financial hardship may be deemed to exist in--but not limited to--those situations where the annuitant from whom collection is sought needs substantially all of his/her current income and liquid assets to meet current ordinary and necessary living expenses and liabilities.

...

831.1405 Ordinary and necessary living expenses.

An individual's ordinary and necessary living expenses include rent, mortgage payments, utilities, maintenance, food, clothing, insurance (life, health and accident), taxes, installment payments, medical expenses, support expenses when the annuitant is legally responsible, and other miscellaneous expenses which the individual can establish as being ordinary and necessary.

For FERS, under 5 CFR 845.304-05:

845.304 Financial hardship.

Financial hardship may be deemed to exist in, but not limited to, those situations when the annuitant from whom collection is sought needs substantially all of his or her current income and liquid assets to meet current ordinary and necessary living expenses and liabilities.

...

845.305 Ordinary and necessary living expenses.

An individual's ordinary and necessary living expenses include rent, mortgage payments, utilities, maintenance, transportation, food, clothing, insurance (life, health, and accident), taxes, installment payments, medical expenses, support expenses for which the annuitant is legally responsible, and other miscellaneous expenses that the individual can establish as being ordinary and necessary.

"Whether the types and amounts of enumerated living expenses are 'ordinary and necessary' is determined by using a 'reasonable person test'...the enumerated expenses claimed are comparable to what a person of ordinary prudence would require under similar circumstances." Maples v. OPM, 48 MSPR 572, 575 (1991). Loans incurred before awareness of an overpayment may be ordinary and necessary expenses, allowing for the possibility that the loan for a purchased item, e.g., a car, may be discharged by selling that item. See Wagner v. OPM, 83 MSPR 355, 360 (1999). "[i]f the appellant is legally responsible for the school loans and they were incurred before the appellant became aware of overpayment, they are ordinary and necessary." Hulvey v. OPM, 93 MSPR 568, 572 ¶ 11 (2003); cf. Cline v. OPM, SF-0845-15-0690-I-2 (NP 2/28/2023) (declining to honor detrimental reliance argument: "Thus, her student loan debt was a preexisting obligation. See Maples, 48 M.S.P.R. at 578 (finding that, although the appellant used the overpayment to repay her home mortgage loan, she did not detrimentally rely on the overpayment because the loan predated the overpayment). We therefore find that the appellant did not relinquish a valuable right as a direct result of the overpayment.").

Pierotti v. OPM, 124 MSPR 103, 2016 MSPB 46, ¶¶ 8-9 (2016), said of living expenses:

An annuitant who is ineligible for waiver nevertheless may be entitled to an adjustment in the recovery schedule if he shows that it would cause him financial hardship to make payment at the rate scheduled. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1401. Pursuant to OPM's regulations, financial hardship may exist when the annuitant needs substantially all of his income and liquid assets to meet current ordinary and necessary living expenses and liabilities. 5 C.F.R. § 831.1404. In determining whether living expenses are "ordinary and necessary," the Board applies a reasonable person test, regardless of the individual's accustomed standard of living. Zelenka v. Office of Personnel Management, 107 M.S.P.R. 522, ¶ 9 (2007). As to the matter of financial hardship, we have reviewed the accuracy of the administrative judge's calculations in determining the appellant's income and expenses, and we apply our calculations to determine whether the appellant is entitled to a reduction of the recovery schedule.

Ordinary and necessary living expenses include rent, mortgage payments, utilities, maintenance, food, clothing, insurance (life, health, and accident), taxes, installment payments, medical expenses, support expenses when the annuitant is legally responsible, and other miscellaneous expenses which the individual can establish as being ordinary and necessary. Wright, 105 M.S.P.R. 419, ¶ 6; 5 C.F.R. § 831.1405. The Board will give the appellant the benefit of the doubt unless the expense clearly constitutes an extravagance or a luxury. Wright, 105 M.S.P.R. 419, ¶ 6. In the absence of a specific challenge by OPM, an appellant seeking to reduce a repayment schedule should not be required to substantiate his expenses and income unless the information appears incomplete or unreasonable on its face. Derrico v. Office of Personnel Management, 42 M.S.P.R. 491, 496-97 (1989).

Vestal v. OPM, 55 MSPR 433, 438-39 (1992), considered the expense of sending children to a private school:

Next, he considered the appellant's monthly bill of $333 for "education." He surmised, without support from the evidentiary record, that this monthly payment represented the cost of the appellant's children's private school education. He questioned, though, whether a "private school tuition is an ordinary and necessary living expense," because 5 C.F.R. § 845.305 does not list such an expense.

The appellant acknowledges that the expense is for his children's private school tuition. He argues, though, that the administrative judge erred by finding that sending his children to a private school is not an "ordinary and necessary" living expense. According to 5 C.F.R. § 845.305, "ordinary and necessary living expenses" include:

rent, mortgage payments, utilities, maintenance, food, clothing, insurance (life, health, and accident), taxes, installment payments, medical expenses, support expenses when the annuitant is legally responsible, and other miscellaneous expenses which the individual can establish as being ordinary and necessary.

The Board held in Fusco v. Office of Personnel Management, 42 M.S.P.R. 501, 507 (1989), that, in determining whether living expenses are "ordinary and necessary," it will follow the OPM Policy Guideline rule that an annuitant "should be given the benefit of the doubt" with respect to the expense, "unless the expense clearly constitutes an extravagance or luxury." 42 M.S.P.R. at 507. The reasonable person test must be applied, regardless of the annuitant's accustomed standard of living, and discrete circumstances particular to that individual's situation must be taken into account. Id. at 507-08. In addition, the annuitant must show by substantial evidence that the amount claimed for the expense meets the reasonable standard. 5 C.F.R. § 845.305.

The Board has considered expenses not specifically listed in 5 C.F.R. § 845.305 to be ordinary and necessary.[9] We conclude that the administrative judge's determination to disallow the expense listed for education on the appellant's FRQ as not being an ordinary and necessary living expense is not supported by the evidence of record.

[9] For example, the Board has found that ordinary and necessary living expenses included: the care, cleaning and purchase of clothing, the purchase of a new car for travel to work, and child care expenses. See Mason v. Office of Personnel Management, 48 M.S.P.R. 479, 483-04 (1991); Portello v. Office of Personnel Management, 54 MSPR 261m 266-67 (1992); Mauro v. Office of Personnel Management, 50 M.S.P.R. 28, 33 (1991); and Nixon v. Office of Personnel Management, 52 M.S.P.R. 672, 676-77 (1992). In contrast, an annuitant's claim of $430 a month for the care of five horses was found not to be an ordinary and necessary living expense. See Maples v. Office of Personnel. Management, 48 M.S.P.R. 572, 576 (1991).

See Shaw v. OPM, 54 MSPR 576, 577 (1992) (in an overpayment case, the Board has jurisdiction to consider entitlement to an adjustment in a repayment even when it is not raised before or considered by OPM); Lopez v. OPM, 47 MSPR 186, 191 (1991) ("When determining an annuitant's ability to repay an overpayment, it is proper to consider anticipated changes in income and expenses during the projected period of collection"; considering effect of anticipated retirement of appellant's wife and diminution of family income).

"Pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 831.1404(a)(2), the income of other family members must be considered if their ordinary and necessary living expenses are included in those reported by the annuitant." Fruth v. OPM, 44 MSPR 235, 238 (1990). See Joyce v. OPM, 69 MSPR 158, 162 (1995) (need to consider effect of anticipated change in income by reason of impact of annuitant's separation from his wife).

Investments in business ventures may not qualify as ordinary and necessary living expenses, Mason v. OPM, 48 MSPR 479, 483 (1991):

To be considered in determining an individual's ability to repay an overpayment, an expense must be an ordinary and necessary living expense. Such expenses include rent, mortgage payments, utilities, maintenance, food, clothing, insurance (life, health, and accident), taxes, installment payments (on debts previously incurred), medical expenses, support expenses when the individual is legally responsible, and other miscellaneous expenses which the individual can establish as being ordinary and necessary. See Policy Guidelines on the Disposition of Civil Service Retirement Overpayments, (Policy Guidelines) March 1985, at 7. The Policy Guidelines also provide that: "In most circumstances, ordinary and necessary living expenses do not include investment-related expenses (i.e., purchase and maintenance costs of investments)."Id.

The appellant's business related expenses appear to be investment-related expenses rather than ordinary and necessary living expenses.[6] The appellant has not shown why his continued investment of large sums of money in the laundromat constitutes an ordinary and necessary living expense, and thus these expenditures cannot be considered in determining the existence of financial hardship.

[6] The fact that an investment is a poor one does not alter its characterization as an investment.

Short term loans are considered in the overpayment analysis. An individual's financial ability to pay is considered the time collection is scheduled to be made, and not at some point in the future. Delange v. OPM, 30 MSPR 177, 178-79 (1986); see Eaton v. OPM, 38 MSPR 216, 217-18 (1988) (proper for AJ to consider changes in the annuitant's expenses and income that are anticipated to occur during the projected period of collection for an overpayment). For discussion of business expenses and status as an independent contractor, refer to Drummond v. OPM, 55 MSPR 119 (1992).

Gross v.
