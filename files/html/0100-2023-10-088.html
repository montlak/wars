13. Opening Statement

At the onset of Board operations in 1979, opening statements were generally received unless the parties affirmatively waived them. The usual practice now is to dispense with opening statements on the theory that they are unnecessary if the case was properly prepared and presented to the AJ at the prehearing stage. Some AJs still accept opening statements; the statements may sharpen the issues or at least remind the parties and AJ of the issues and essential facts they (preferably concisely) convey. See Manuel v. OPM, DA-844E-15-0277-I-1 (NP 4/15/2022) ("opening and closing arguments are committed to the administrative judge's sound discretion.").

All representatives: if you have the opportunity to provide an opening statement, do not read it to the AJ. A recitation from a piece of paper is boring to watch and difficult to follow. It is often read with the incantation of a liturgical chant and the intonation of a maladjusted electronic voice simulator. Instead, try the following. Stand up. Put the paper down. Look at the AJ. Speak with conviction. Know the subject matter and law of your case. Explain the nature of your case and provide a brief summary of expected testimony. Stop. Sit down. Soon enough the world will know of your prowess.

Appellants' representatives: if you have the opportunity to present an opening statement, resist, if possible, the temptation to defer it until the opening of your case (assuming the agency has the burden of proof). If for no other reason than the deferred statement is frequently forgotten in the crush of events, it should be presented at the onset of the hearing. Also, the AJ is not a jury swayed most--one hopes--by that heard last. The AJ has likely heard scores or hundreds of appeals and wants to know what facts and defenses are being emphasized early during the hearing so that the testimony and cross-examination make sense. Deferral of the appellant's opening statement may be justified, however, if there is a good reason to avoid early disclosure of evidence not already known to the opposition. Once the agency rests its case, "surprise" at learning the true nature of the appellant's evidence is not a good reason for reopening the case. It is too late. Discovery is supposed to be used to eliminate surprises; if it is not used, the agency has no reason to complain if, after resting its case, it first hears the appellant's evidence.
