a. Interactive Process; Medical Documentation

When an accommodation is required, the need for the accommodation must be known or made known to the agency. The employee and the agency are to then pursue discussion and exchange of information leading to a reasonable accommodation, if one is possible. The process of identifying an accommodation should involve participation and interaction by the employee and the employer. See Mengine v. Runyon, 114 F.3d 415, 420 (3d Cir. 1997) (citing 29 CFR 1630.2(o)(3); 29 CFR Part 1630, App. at 351). Collins v. USPS, 100 MSPR 332, 336-37 ¶¶ 10-12 (2005), determined to be adequate what amounted to an agency hearing on the accommodation for a disabled employee:

We note that 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(3) provides that an employer may need "to initiate an informal, interactive process" with the employee in order to determine what actions might constitute a reasonable accommodation of the employee's disability. The same section provides further that "[t]his process should identify the precise limitations resulting from the disability and potential reasonable accommodations that could overcome those limitations." The appellant seems to argue that the agency failed to carry out its responsibility under this provision by failing to search for suitable positions to which he could be assigned.

Both parties, however, have an obligation to assist in the search for an appropriate accommodation, and both have an obligation to act in good faith in doing so. Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296, 312 (3d Cir. 1999). In this case, as noted above, the agency held a hearing to address the appellant's request for accommodation. The letter the agency issued to the appellant following the hearing suggests that the agency had not conducted a search for appropriate positions at that time, and that it had instead considered only the possibility of modifying the appellant's city carrier duties. It did specifically note, however, that one of the options available to the appellant was to apply for a position for which he physically qualified. Instead of indicating that he was interested in that option, the appellant submitted his application for retirement within a few days after issuance of the letter.... The appellant has asserted on petition for review that he did not pursue the option of seeking reassignment because he "reasonably believed he would not have been given an opportunity to get another collective bargaining position because of retaliation for his protected disclosures." We find this assertion unpersuasive. The appellant's alleged belief that he would not be treated fairly does not relieve him of the obligation to cooperate in the "interactive process" mentioned above.

Under the circumstances described above, we see no basis for finding that the agency was obligated to continue the "interactive process" by searching for positions to which the appellant might be assigned. The appellant's submission of his retirement application, and his failure to provide any indication that he would be interested in assignment to a vacant position, showed clearly that he was not interested in pursuing any option other than retirement.

Applying Collins, the Board decided that if an appellant refuses to cooperate in the interactive process, an accommodation argument is not likely to succeed, Sansone v. USPS, CH-0752-12-0485-I-1 (NP 6/6/2013) (Member Wagner dissenting):

The Board has held that, where an appellant refuses to cooperate in the interactive process, he cannot establish his disability retirement was involuntary based on an agency's alleged failure to accommodate his disability. Collins v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 332, ¶¶ 11-12 (2005) (stating that the appellant, had not established his retirement was involuntary as there was no basis for finding that the agency was obligated to continue the interactive process because, instead of responding to an accommodation proposal, the appellant submitted his application for retirement); see Simpson v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 346, ¶¶ 9, 16-18 (2010) (finding no jurisdiction in a constructive suspension appeal where the appellant could not prove he was denied a reasonable accommodation because he did not engage in good faith in the interactive process, including failing to provide requested medical documentation). The appellant admits that, when he received the RAC request for medical documentation to facilitate his parking request, he "gave up," asked his supervisor to tell the RAC "to stop pursuing this issue," and indicated his intent to request a "disability package." Because the appellant voluntarily terminated the interactive process, we see no basis to find that the agency failed to accommodate him or that his disability retirement was involuntary....

See Anderson v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-16-0451-I-1 (NP 1/31/2023) ("However, when the existence or nature of a reasonable accommodation is not obvious, and the employee fails to respond to reasonable requests for medical information and documentation, the agency will not be found to have violated its duty to provide a reasonable accommodation because the appellant failed to fulfill his obligations in the interactive process. White v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 120 M.S.P.R. 405, ¶ 12 (2013)."; also concluding the appellant did not comply with agency documentation requests necessary for an agency job search).

The interactive process was also implicit in Savage v. Dept. of Navy, 36 MSPR 148, 152 n.2 (1988):

The extent to which an appellant is able to articulate a reasonable accommodation will vary depending on the issues and circumstances of the case. In general, however, the agency can be expected to know more than the appellant about the essential duties of the position or positions at issue, about its own ability to modify duties or working conditions, and about the availability of positions that could be filled by employees with certain limitations. See Prewitt v. USPS, 662 F.2d 292, 308 (5th Cir. 1981). On the other hand, the appellant can be expected to know more than the agency about his qualifications and capabilities, and, as we have noted above, he bears the ultimate burden of persuasion in establishing his affirmative defense of handicap discrimination. Accordingly, although the agency has the ultimate burden of showing an inability to accommodate the appellant's handicap, the appellant "initially has the burden of coming forward with evidence to make at least a facial showing that his handicap can be accommodated." Treadwell v. Alexander, 707 F.2d 473, 478 (11th Cir. 1983).

DHS, 99 MSPR 307, 310-11 ¶ 7 (2005). An agency does not fail to provide reasonable accommodation if, without readily apparent accommodation, the employee does not suggest an accommodation. Webb v. To identify an appropriate accommodation, the parties will undergo an informal, interactive process during which they ascertain "the precise limitations resulting from the disability and potential reasonable accommodations that could overcome those limitations." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(3). Agencies are obligated to accommodate only the known limitations resulting from an employee's disability. Although an agency may inquire as to whether an employee needs an accommodation, it is generally the employee's responsibility to inform the agency that one is needed. When the need for an accommodation is not obvious, an agency may require the employee to document the need. 29 C.F.R. pt. 1630 App'x § 1630.9. The employee is required to cooperate with the interactive process in good faith, which includes providing the requested medical documentation. Her failure to do so may preclude a finding that the agency discriminated against her. See Simpson v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 346, ¶¶ 16-18 (2010).
