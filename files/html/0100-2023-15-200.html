3. Fighting; Self Defense

The agency has both a right and a responsibility to maintain discipline and order among its employees. Fighting on the job site is clearly sufficient to establish a nexus between the offense and discipline imposed to promote the efficiency of the service. Lindsey v. Dept. of Navy, 9 MSPR 468, 471 (1982) (mitigating removal of combatants with no prior discipline and satisfactory evaluations; a penalty less than removal was consistent with the agency schedule of disciplinary penalties for a first offense of fighting). Fighting on duty time in a work area is a serious offense affecting the service efficiency. For two employees alleged to have been fighting, the agency should not mechanically fire both employees involved in the altercation. The agency should examine the circumstances. An employee who is acting in self-defense, or is struck by another without fault, should not be fired. Williams v. USPS, 5 MSPR 5, 6-7, 5 MSPB 88 (1981). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Mitigation; Aggravation," for other cases involving physical confrontations.]

In an appeal from adverse action based on fighting or assault charges, Fuller v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 187, 190 (1993), aff'd, Fuller v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3190), determined that if an agency proves a charge that employees engaged in a physical altercation while on duty, self-defense can be a mitigating factor:

...To support her assertion that she acted in self defense, the appellant must prove by preponderant evidence, inter alia, that she used only so much force as was reasonably necessary to free herself.... Factors to consider in determining whether the level of force was reasonably necessary include the amount of force exerted, the means or instrument used, the manner or method of applying the force, and the circumstances under which the force was applied.... Moreover, the doctrine of self defense may not be successfully invoked if the person raising it was not free from fault in bringing on the difficulty, unless that person retreats in good faith intending to abandon the difficulty that eventually led to the aggression.

The Federal Circuit affirmed, Fuller v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3190):

A person is entitled to defend herself when it would appear to a reasonable person under similar circumstances, that she is in immediate danger of bodily harm. However, she is justified in using only such force as appears to be reasonably necessary to resist the unlawful attack. Davis v. Freels, 583 F.2d 337, 340-41 (7th Cir. 1978). Factors to consider in determining whether the level of force was reasonably necessary include the amount of force exerted, the means or instrument used, the manner or method of applying the force, and the circumstances under which the force was applied. Burton v. Waller, 502 F.2d 1261, 1276 (5th Cir. 1974). Based on our application of these factors to the undisputed facts, we conclude the Board's finding that Fuller used excessive force is supported by substantial evidence.

Marra v. DOJ, NY-0752-21-0039-I-1 (NP 4/28/2022), citing Fuller, determined:

On review, the appellant argues, among other things, that the administrative judge erred in not addressing his claim of self-defense. He asserts that he was not the aggressor but rather the victim of an assault by multiple ferryboat crew members. He characterizes his claim as an affirmative defense, but it is more properly addressed within the context of whether the agency proved the specification. The Board has considered claims of self-defense in such a way. See Mahan v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 140, ¶ 6 (2001) (considering an appellant's claim that she used her agency-issued firearm in self-defense in deciding whether the agency proved its charge of conduct unbecoming).

The doctrine of self-defense is not applicable if the person raising it was not free from fault in bringing on the difficulty, unless that person retreats in good faith intending to abandon the difficulty that eventually led to the aggression. Fuller v. Department of the Navy, 60 M.S.P.R. 187, 190 (1993), aff'd, 40 F.3d 1250 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Here, the appellant caused the circumstances that led to the altercation by improperly boarding the ferryboat and pushing the ferryboat captain. Further, the record does not indicate any good faith effort by the appellant to "abandon" the difficulty after the initial confrontation; rather, he appears to have escalated the conflict by punching a crew member (and perhaps the ferryboat captain). The appellant only ran off the dock after, upon falling to the ground, the crew members saw his gun and yelled "gun" several times. Under these circumstances, we find that the appellant's actions do not constitute self-defense.

To the extent that the administrative judge erred in not addressing the appellant's claim of self-defense, any such error did not prejudice his substantive rights because, as discussed above, he failed to establish his claim. Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984) (finding that an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision)....

See McCoy v. VA, AT-0752-15-0197-I-1 (NP 9/25/2015) ("The doctrine of self-defense may not be successfully invoked if the employee raising it was not free from fault in bringing on difficulty, unless that person retreats in good faith intending to abandon difficulty that eventually led to aggression. Fuller v. Department of the Navy, 60 M.S.P.R. 187, 190 (1993), aff'd, 40 F.3d 1250 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table)."); cf. Thompson v. Analyzing the facts leading to and following the initiation of a confrontation, the Board determined that an appellant was properly removed, rejecting an explanation of self-defense, when the appellant had not done what was reasonable to avoid a fight with a coworker. Cunningham v. USPS, 109 MSPR 402, 408 ¶ 14, 2008 MSPB 167 (2008) (rev'd and remanded, Cunningham v. USPS, 328 Fed. Appx. 646 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (following remand, mitigating penalty, Cunningham v. USPS, 112 MSPR 457, 2009 MSPB 200 (2009)) (discussed in Chapter 17, "Credibility"):

To support an assertion of self-defense, the appellant must prove, inter alia, that he used only so much force as was reasonably necessary to free himself from another's unwanted grasp. See Mahan v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 140, ¶ 8 (2001); Fuller v. Department of the Navy, 60 M.S.P.R. 187, 190 (1993), aff'd, 40 F.3d 1250 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). The doctrine of self-defense may not be successfully invoked if the person raising it was not free from fault in bringing on the difficulty, unless that person retreats in good faith, intending to abandon the difficulty that eventually led to the aggression. Fuller, 60 M.S.P.R. at 190.

See Layton v. VA, SF-0752-13-1060-I-1 (NP 7/7/2014) (applying Fuller and Cunningham to a claim of self-defense in an altercation with a patient); Rogers v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-11-0170-I-1 (NP 11/23/2011) ("We agree with the administrative judge's analysis on this issue and explicitly find that the appellant was not entitled to claim that he acted in self-defense because he was not free from fault in bringing on the difficulty and he did not retreat in good faith nor exhibit any intent to abandon the difficulty that led to the aggression.").

Self-defense was considered, but not accepted, as a defense to a disciplinary charge asserting that the appellant, a law enforcement officer, improperly displayed a weapon during an off-duty traffic confrontation, Rodriguez v. DOJ, NY-0752-10-0081-I-1 (NP 10/7/2011):

The appellant does not dispute that he displayed the weapon, but asserts that it was a justified use of force in self defense because he perceived that Mr. Mamaras posed a threat to him following the "pissing match" and the appellant's striking of Mr. Mamaras's car. The Board has held that "the doctrine of self defense may not be successfully invoked if the person raising it was not free from fault in bringing on the difficulty, unless that person retreats in good faith intending to abandon the difficulty that eventually led to the aggression." Fuller v. Department of the Navy, 60 M.S.P.R. 187, 190 (1993), aff'd, 40 F.3d 1250 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). The administrative judge concluded that the display of the weapon was justified under the circumstances because, while the appellant provoked Mr. Mamaras, Mr. Mamaras "was going to pummel the appellant with his fists." However, an administrative judge errs if he accepts an appellant's claim of self defense without first properly considering whether the appellant was free from fault in bringing on the difficulty or physical altercation. See Cunningham v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 402, ¶¶ 14-21 (2008) (citing Fuller, 60 M.S.P.R. at 190), rev'd on other grounds, 328 F. App'x 646 (Fed. Cir. 2009).

The agency's policy is that a use of force must be "objectively reasonable under all the circumstances known to the deputy at the time and may range from verbal coercion to the use of firearms." In this situation, the appellant created the danger of harm. The appellant told the grand jury that Mr. Mamaras did not leave his car or approach the appellant until after the appellant hit Mr. Mamaras's car. Displaying a weapon was not necessary when a far simpler means to avoid harm was available to the appellant--not instigating the conflict at all by not blocking the street, not entering a verbal argument with a civilian, not approaching the civilian's car, and not hitting the civilian's car. Even once the situation had escalated to the extent that Mr. Mamaras appeared interested in striking the appellant, the appellant could have taken steps to verbally de-escalate the situation by, for example, apologizing to Mr. Mamaras. The record does not indicate any good faith effort by the appellant to "abandon" the difficulty until after his weapon was displayed to Mr. Mamaras. The appellant clearly caused the conflict that night and made no efforts to diffuse it prior to drawing his weapon. Under these circumstances, we cannot see the display of the weapon as a manifestation of any intent to "retreat" from the conflict. Accordingly, consistent with the holdings of Fuller and Cunningham, self defense does not apply to this situation and the charge of unnecessary display of a weapon is sustained.

Disparate treatment and provocation are factors considered in setting a penalty for fighting. See Faucher v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3261) (reviewing court deemed inadequate the Board's attempt to distinguish other incidents of scuffles leading to disciplinary action short of removal; the court noted the Board's explanation: "merely [a] conclusory statement that, for example, the incident occurred at a different agency location, or the incident occurred two years ago, or there was no showing that the incidents were as serious as the incident here"). Mitigation was directed in Faucher when the Board did not properly consider provocation involved in a scuffle. See Faucher v. USPS, 41 MSPR 336, 339 (1989) (on remand). The Board remanded another case for credibility findings about provocation in a fight. That the appellant injured his coworker did not relieve the AJ of the obligation to determine whether the coworker provoked the blow. Angelo v. USPS, 52 MSPR 664, 668 (1992). When the Board could not figure out who started the fracas, but determined that the appellant had a good work record despite a suspension four years earlier for assaulting a fellow employee, removal was reduced to a 60-day suspension "sufficient to impress the seriousness of appellant's conduct on appellant and deter such conduct by others." Grimm v. USPS, 10 MSPR 244, 248 (1982). Balouris v. USPS, 107 MSPR 574, 2008 MSPB 1 (2007); see Simmons-Roberts v. DOD, 443 Fed. Appx. 539 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) (sustaining removal for involvement in physical altercation); cf. Johnson v.
