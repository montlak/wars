f. Certification by Military Authority

At issue in Kingsley v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-09-0533-I-1 (NP 8/27/2010), was removal of an airplane flight instructor who lost Army Aviator flight qualifications. The decision by the military to disqualify the appellant was an unreviewable military determination:

The appellant served as an Airplane Flight Instructor and was subject to several conditions of employment. Specifically, the appellant was required to maintain active military reserve membership, meet both military and civilian position skill compatibility, and satisfy the military membership requirements of Army regulation 140-315. The agency removed the appellant because he lost his Army Aviator flight qualifications, specifying that the Army's Flying Evaluation Board (FEB) terminated the appellant's Army Aviation Service, causing the loss of the appellant's Army Aviator flight qualifications. The FEB based its action on the appellant's failure to provide an updated flight physical. In sustaining the appellant's removal, the administrative judge found that the Board lacked authority to examine the merits of the military determination.

On review, the appellant argues that the agency did not follow its own regulations in removing him, that the administrative judge denied him a witness who would have testified about the FEB proceeding, and that the administrative judge should have considered the propriety of the FEB proceeding in analyzing whether the agency proved its charge....

While an argument can be made that the Board has authority to consider whether the agency complied with its own procedural regulations in making its military decision, see Romero v. Department of Defense, 527 F.3d 1324, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2008), we find that the appellant's claim here is actually a substantive, rather than procedural, challenge. The appellant argues that the agency violated section 6-3 of Army Regulation 600-105. That section is titled "Procedures," and sets forth how the FEB ensures that it both receives all relevant information pertaining to a person's qualifications and objectively evaluates that information. Subsection (d) provides guidance for FEB recommendations, stating that the FEB will make its recommendations using certain enumerated guidelines. One guideline states that recommendations to disqualify an officer are normally not made on the basis of an isolated incident or action, unless an officer clearly shows a dangerous or unacceptable change in pattern of performance. The appellant asserts that the agency violated this subsection by terminating his Army Aviation Service based upon his isolated failure to provide an updated flight physical and that there was no showing of dangerious [sic] or unacceptable change in his pattern of performance.

We note, however, that this factor is only one of several other considerations that the FEB uses to make its determination. Indeed, this regulation also includes a guideline that provides that "[i]n the final analysis, the best interest of the Army remains the prime criterion in evaluating each case." The appellant's claim that the agency violated its regulations by terminating his Army Aviation Service based upon an isolated instance is, therefore, really a disagreement with the FEB's ultimate determination on the merits of his case. Thus, rather than challenging an alleged procedural violation, the appellant is, instead, attempting an improper challenge to the substance of the military determination. See Archer v. Department of the Army, 44 M.S.P.R. 427, 430 (1990).

A court (and the Board) must accept a military determination of medical fitness for a job that requires military flight status. Kelleher v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-731). [Refer in this chapter to "Removal From Reserve Status."]
