q. Lawyer

With good news for government counsel, Wilcox v. IBWC, 103 MSPR 73, 78-79 ¶¶ 12-14, 2006 MSPB 237 (2006), determined that an excepted service attorney was not excluded from status as a covered employee under the WPA in an IRA appeal:

The appellant also asserts that he occupied a "covered position." He contends that he was a Schedule A attorney in an excepted service position. He claims that he was not in a position of a "confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating character."

Again, we agree with the appellant that he occupied a "covered position." A "covered position" does not include any position that is, prior to the personnel action, excepted from the competitive service because of its confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating character. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(B)(i). The Standard Form 50 reflecting the appellant's appointment shows that he was appointed to his Schedule A, excepted service position pursuant to the "legal authority" of 5 C.F.R. § 213.3102(d). That is the regulatory section designated by OPM for "positions which are not of a confidential or policy-determining character,...for which it is not practicable to examine." 5 C.F.R. § 213.3101. Subsection (d) of 5 C.F.R. § 213.3102 refers specifically to attorneys. The vacancy announcement under which the appellant applied for his position also indicates that it is a Schedule A position. In contrast, Schedule C positions are designated as "positions which are policy-determining or which involve a close and confidential working relationship with the head of an agency or other key appointed officials." 5 C.F.R. § 213.3301(a).

The documents relied on by the agency do not show that the appellant occupied a non-covered Schedule C position. The agency relied on two documents prepared by Robert Ortega, the Acting U.S. Commissioner during the appellant's tenure with the agency, wherein Ortega expressed his opinion that the appellant was in a "critically confidential position," and referred to the "criticality of [his] confidential position. The agency relied as well on a sworn statement (apparently prepared in connection with its response to this appeal) from one of the IBWC's Principal Engineers, who stated that both Principal Engineers and the Legal Advisor "serve in a close and confidential" relationship with the U.S. Commissioner. Despite these documents, the appellant has established that his position was "covered" since it was excepted from the competitive service, not because of its confidential, policy-determining, policy-making, or policy-advocating character, but based on its attorney designation.
