5. Effect of Agreement That Matter Is in Dispute; Ineffectiveness of Agreement of Parties to Extend Time for Appeal

The date for filing the negotiability petition may be triggered by a joint agreement of the parties, e.g., a settlement of some proposals that expresses the parties' understanding that the agency maintains other proposals to be nonnegotiable. The settlement constitutes both the request for and the allegation of nonnegotiability. AFGE Local 491 and VAMC, Bath, 28 FLRA 30, 31-32 (1987). A memorandum of understanding, reflecting an agreement that management had declared a subject matter nonnegotiable and that FLRA would be asked for a negotiability decision by the union, constituted notice from management that a proposal was nonnegotiable and triggered the union's time for filing its petition for review. A separate written request for negotiations from the union was not a prerequisite to the commencement of the time limits for filing an appeal. AFGE Local 3790 and Dept. of Interior, BLM, 7 FLRA 393, 394-95 (1981). If the parties enter into a memorandum of understanding recognizing that the employer maintains that a proposal is nonnegotiable, the memo serves as a written request by the union for an allegation and a simultaneous written response by the agency alleging the matter proposed to be nonnegotiable. AFGE Nat'l GSA Council and GSA, 20 FLRA 448, 448-49 (1985); see NTEU and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 48 FLRA 919, 922-23 (1993) (appendix to parties' negotiated agreement identifying provisions declared to be nonnegotiable by agency served as declaration that controlled the union's obligation to petition FLRA for review of negotiability dispute); AFGE Local 2145 and VAMC, Richmond, Va., 44 FLRA 1055, 1061 (1992) (MOU of parties agreeing that union would file negotiability appeal as to a contract article constituted written request by union for allegation of nonnegotiability and simultaneous written response by agency alleging that proposed matter was not negotiable).

AFGE Nat'l INS Council and DOJ, INS, 45 FLRA 391, 404-05 (1992), precluded the parties from creating a contractual process to hold negotiability disputes in abeyance, following bargaining on indisputably negotiable proposals, until the union later decides to reopen bargaining. Disputed was the negotiability of Provision 4:

It is understood that the Union's position of negotiability and the Service's position of non-negotiability on the items listed below will not be jeopardized or prejudiced as a result of implementing the Agreement. The parties agree that the items listed below can be reopened for negotiations on the basis decided by the Federal Labor Relations Authority. The parties agree that such negotiations will be reopened only once and that all items listed below as on which reopening is desired will be negotiated collectively at that time. Except as provided below, such negotiations shall, during the life of the Agreement, commence within sixty (60) days of written notice by either party. If, during the life of the agreement, less than one year remains between the receipt of such written notice and the anniversary date of the Agreement, negotiations will be conducted in accordance with the contract Article entitled Effective Date and Duration.

That provision was nonnegotiable, 45 FLRA at 407-08:

Section 7117(c)(2) permits an exclusive representative to file a petition with the Authority "on or before the 15th day after the date on which the agency first makes [an] allegation" that a matter proposed to be bargained is outside the duty to bargain. See also 5 C.F.R. § 2424.3. The time limit for filing a petition for review of an agency allegation of nonnegotiability may not be extended or waived by the Authority. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.23(d). See International Association of Fire Fighters, Local F-33 and U.S. Department of the Navy, Naval Air Station, San Diego, California, 37 FLRA 460 (1990). The Union claims that the provision would allow the Union to determine when to institute a negotiability appeal by requesting a written allegation of nonnegotiability. However, the Authority previously has found language comparable to that contained in the first sentence of Article 52 to constitute both a written request for an allegation of nonnegotiability and a written response to the request under section 2424.3 of our Regulations. See, for example, American Federation of Government Employees, Local 2145 and U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, Richmond, Virginia, 44 FLRA 1055, 1060 (1992), and cases cited therein. Consequently, we find that the allegation of nonnegotiability was made by INS at the time the parties entered into Article 52. Provision 4 thus constitutes an attempt to extend the time period in which a negotiability appeal may be filed by permitting the Union to file a petition for review at an unspecified future date, rather than in compliance with the statutory filing requirements. Therefore, the provision is inconsistent with section 7117(c)(2) of the Statute and is nonnegotiable.

We also find that the petition for review as to the five proposals listed under Provision 4 is untimely. Because the first sentence of Article 52 constitutes both a written request for an allegation of nonnegotiability and a written response to that request, the Union had 15 days from the date the parties agreed to such language to file a petition for review. The record reveals that the parties agreed to Article 52 on May 16, 1990. The petition for review was filed with the Authority on July 6, 1990. Clearly, the Union's petition for review as to the proposals was untimely filed. Therefore, the proposals are not properly before us in this proceeding and we will dismiss the petition for review as it relates to those proposals.
