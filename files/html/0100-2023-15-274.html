broida mspb Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses, Subchapter 03: Integrity, Section B: Removal, Misuse of Property, Subsection 05: Unauthorized Use, Theft, or Destruction of Specific Property, Part d: Government Credit Card
d. Government Credit Card

Use of a government credit card for purchases of property for an employee's own use is a taking of government property. Waymon v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 374, 375 (1983) (gasoline for a private vehicle); see Meneese v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3485) (demotion of a customer service manager for the use of a government credit card "for personal use and gain"); Casteel v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 521 (2004) (removal for delinquency in payment of a government credit card, coupled with a false statement concerning the credit card and a prior incident of delinquency in payment of a credit card); Brown v. Dept. of Army, 96 MSPR 232, 236 ¶ 11 (2004) (removal of materials handler who misused his credit card 67 times, allowed his account to remain delinquent for an extended period of time, and who had not paid off the bill when the decision was made to remove him); Nelson v. VA, 22 MSPR 65, 70 (1984) ("[M]isuse of a government credit card is a serious offense."); Drayton v. EEOC, 11 MSPR 43, 46 (1982) (mitigating removal to suspension; the employee did not profit from the transactions--he was trying to obtain reimbursement for expenditures he made on behalf of the agency; the appellant had a long service record); cf. Baracker v. Dept. of Interior, 70 MSPR 594, 598-602 (1996) (discipline against an SES member for credit card misuse: the statute governing SES discipline does not require that misconduct by an SES member be intentional to be actionable).

Misuse of a government phone credit card, amounting to $261.46 for 45 personal calls, warranted dismissal of a law enforcement officer. See Coleman v. Dept. of Commerce (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3155); see Boppre v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-14-0386-I-1 (NP 10/11/2016) (removal for use of government credit card for excessive cash advances, repayment, and other problems); D'Annunzio v. USPS, NY-0752-14-0148-I-1 (NP 2/26/2015) (removal of a sales manager for repeated misuse, although without financial loss to the agency, of a government credit card over a period of years); McMullen v. Dept. of Labor, SF-0752-12-0343-I-1 (NP 8/22/2013) ("The charge of misuse of government property, which includes misuse of a GCC, does not require a showing of intent; rather, all that the agency must demonstrate is that the appellant's use of the credit card was unauthorized. See, e.g., Edwards v. U.S. Postal Service, 116 M.S.P.R. 173, ¶ 12 (2010); Quarters v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 97 M.S.P.R. 511, ¶ 4 (2004)."); Quarters v. VA, 97 MSPR 511, 513 ¶ 5 (2004) (suspension for inadvertent use of credit card involving a purchase, repaid by the employee, of $226; intent was not an element of the charge of misuse, although the accidental nature of the appellant's behavior was a mitigating factor); Baracker v. Dept. of Interior, 70 MSPR 594, 598-602 (1996) (suspension of a SES member for improper use of a credit card for personal business: no showing of intentional misuse was required); Doran v. Dept. of Interior, 11 MSPR 270, 273 (1982) (removal of an administrative technician for misuse of a credit card and other charges; removal was warranted because of the employee's position of trust, the continuing nature and severity of conduct, and the consistency of the penalty with the penalty table).

Removal was mitigated to a 45-day suspension of a GS-12 Supervisory General Supply Specialist who failed to safeguard a government Diners Club Card and PIN, who failed to follow instructions for reporting loss, theft, or compromise of the card and PIN, and who on one occasion misused the card. Mitigating against removal were the factors that the appellant's record was otherwise unblemished and that on her own volition the appellant took full responsibility for repaying the improper charges. Kye v. DLA, 64 MSPR 570, 574 (1994), reopened and modified on other grounds, 82 MSPR 399, 400 ¶¶ 1-2 (1999); see Edwards v. USPS, 116 MSPR 173, 178-80 ¶¶ 15-17, 2010 MSPB 176 (2010) (mitigating demotion of a supervisor to 60-day suspension for the sustained charge of credit card misuse based on lack of clear guidance to the appellant concerning when the government credit card could be used for personal purchases and also based both on the deciding official's misjudgment of the extent of the demotion and his testimony that a suspension might have been appropriate for the sustained charge if other, unrelated, charges were not sustained); Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 15 MSPR 731, 736-37 (1983) (mitigating removal to a demotion of a supervisor, responsible for monitoring the use of credit cards by others, who misused a credit card; there was no financial gain to the appellant, who used the card to purchase gas for a personal vehicle being used for official business); cf. Phillips v. GSA, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (employee was not insubordinate in failing timely to pay a government credit card bill when she did everything within her power to pay the bill within the time allowed). But see Hickmon v. USPS, 282 Fed. Appx. 812 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (discharge for, among other charges, problems relating to payment of government credit card; no personal gain was shown; the card was eventually paid); Brown v. Dept. of Army, 96 MSPR 232, 237 ¶ 15 (2004) (declining to consider as mitigating factors as to repeated misuse of a government-issued credit card the appellant's divorce, eviction, and bankruptcy).

Removal was sustained for failure to make timely payments on a government credit card in Chandler v. DHS, DC-0752-10-0368-I-1 (NP 6/30/2011), also involving card misuse:

Although the appellant asserts that there is no nexus between the agency's proven charges of "Non Sufficient Funds in Payment of Travel Card" (two specifications) and Travel Card Delinquency (four specifications) and the efficiency of the service, the administrative judge correctly found such a nexus in light of the appellant's failure to timely pay the full outstanding balance in contravention of agency directives and his cardholder agreement. See Allen v. U.S. Postal Service, 466 F.3d 1065, 1067, 1070-72 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (affirming a removal based on a failure to timely pay a government-issued credit card where the cardholder agreement required the appellant to timely pay the outstanding balance in full and the deciding official indicated that the misconduct destroyed the appellant's credibility and trust). The courts and the Board have on many occasions upheld the imposition of discipline based on a failure to timely make government-issued credit card payments. See Allen, 466 F.3d at 1072; Boyd v. Department of the Treasury, 222 F. App'x 997, 998 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Casteel v. Department of the Treasury, 97 M.S.P.R. 521, ¶¶ 2, 9-10 (2004), review dismissed, 120 F. App'x 340 (Fed. Cir. 2005); Garner v. Department of the Treasury, 97 M.S.P.R. 362, ¶¶ 2-3, 7 (2004), aff'd, 127 F. App'x 477 (Fed. Cir. 2005); Wood v. Chertoff, 523 F. Supp.2d 509, 514, 517 (W.D. Texas 2007).

Even assuming, however, that there was no nexus with respect to the charges disputed by the appellant on review, the administrative judge found that the agency's deciding official testified that he would have removed the appellant based solely on the sustained charge of Unauthorized Use of the Government Issued Travel Card (sixteen specifications). We find that even if this had been the only sustained charge, the penalty of removal would have been within the bounds of reasonableness in light of the nature and seriousness of the misconduct, the fact that the misconduct was intentional, frequently repeated, and for personal gain, the appellant's 7 years of service as a Federal Air Marshal, the clear notice he had that such use was prohibited, the high standard that law enforcement officers like the appellant are held to, the appellant's prior 5-day suspension for late credit card payments and a suspension for missing a scheduled mission, and the nature of the duties of a Federal Air Marshal, which include spending a majority of time traveling and using government travel cards to pay for expenses. Although the appellant claims that some of the agency's rules regarding government credit card usage violated his constitutional rights, this assertion was not raised below, and therefore may not be raised for the first time on petition for review, see Crowe v. Small Business Administration, 53 M.S.P.R. 631, 634-35 (1992).

Allen v. USPS, 99 Fed. Appx. 924, 927-28 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP), remanded an appeal because it was unclear whether the agency removed the employee because he failed to pay his card (the charge) or whether, when it later turned out that the card was paid, the employee was not sufficiently forthcoming concerning his credit card transactions (the implication of the decision letter), the difficulty being that an agency should act only on the grounds included in the proposal. Allen returned to the region for further analysis, Allen v. USPS, Docket No. DE-0752-01-0289-M-1 (Sept. 27, 2004). After the Board again sustained the removal, the Federal Circuit sustained the Board, based tardy repayment by Allen to Citibank of funds paid to Allen by the Postal Service. Allen v. USPS, 466 F.3d 1065 (Fed. Cir. 2006).

Minor and technical misuse of a government credit card, coupled with delayed filing of vouchers (in technical violation of agency regulations), to some extent resulting from the complexities of mixing government and personal travel, costing the government nothing in financial losses, led to a 30-day suspension, mitigated from a demotion and a suspension (based on a series of charges, some not sustained), in Johnson v. SBA, 97 MSPR 571, 589 ¶ 38 (2004). See Quarters v. VA, 97 MSPR 511, 513 ¶ 5 (2004) (setting aside mitigation by AJ and sustaining a 30-day suspension for relatively minor, inadvertent use of a government card for a personal purchase; the agency was not required to prove intentional misuse of the card; although there was no financial loss to the government, there was a prior, unrelated disciplinary offense, and "[m]isuse of a government credit card is a serious offense"). Failing to make timely payment on a government credit card, coupled with a false statement concerning past problems with government credit cards, resulted in removal of a GS-8 Tax Examining Assistant in Casteel v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 521 (2004). Scere v. DHS, NY-0752-14-0157-I-1 (NP 9/9/2016), having no precedential value at all, involved a Board final order affirming the AJ because the Board members were split in their vote (no third member then being in office). The AJ mitigated a removal, based on cancelation of a government travel card, to reassignment to a position not requiring the card, when the basis for the cancelation appeared to be a good faith effort by the appellant to make required payments on the card.
