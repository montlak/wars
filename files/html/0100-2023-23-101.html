4. Union Dues; Insurance; Medicare

The Federal Labor Relations Authority provided its view of the nature of deductions and elections for the various items funded by federal payroll checks in VAMC, Northport and AFGE Local 1843, 25 FLRA 523, 526-28 (1987), involving a dispute over the ability of a union to recapture union dues that would have been paid out of the employee's check had the employee not been discharged:

When an agency calculates an employee's backpay award, some payments must be withheld from the award, some payments may not be credited to the employee, and some matters require the exercise of discretion by the affected employee. Thus, the courts have held that an agency must deduct from an employee's backpay award those payments for which the employee is legally obligated, including Federal and state taxes, Federal Insurance Contributions Act ("FICA") payments, retirement and Medicare deductions, annual leave payments, and any withdrawals which were made by the employee from the Civil Service Retirement Fund at the time of his separation. Tanaka v. Dept. of Navy, 788 F.2d 1552 (Fed. Cir. 1986)....

Some Federal employee benefit programs require the employee to exercise options concerning whether or not to participate in the program and concerning the nature of their participation. When employees who are found to have been wrongfully terminated are reinstated, they are given the option of retroactively participating in these optional programs and paying for them out of their backpay award. Thus, employees may refuse to "purchase" life insurance through the government's program or they may choose varying levels of participation. For employees who are reinstated the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) provides that there will be no life insurance withholdings made from the retroactive pay adjustment of an employee who is retroactively restored to duty with pay after an erroneous separation, suspension or removal. However, if death or accidental dismemberment occurs during the period between the employee's separation, suspension or removal and the finding that the agency action was erroneous, premiums will be withheld from the backpay awarded for the period of separation, suspension or removal and the employee or their estate will be entitled to the benefits which would have accrued to them but for the erroneous agency action.

Similarly, Federal employees can decide whether or not to participate in the health benefits program, and they have a variety of options regarding the medical insurance they want to choose. An employee who is removed or suspended without pay and whose enrollment in the health benefits program has been terminated is given the option when he is reinstated of having his prior health benefits reinstated retroactively to the date they were terminated or he may enroll in the same program as a new employee. If the employee elects to have the prior enrollment reinstated retroactively, withholdings and contributions must also be made retroactively just as though the erroneous suspension or removal had not taken place and the health benefits coverage is considered to have been continuously in effect, with the employee and any covered family members being entitled to the full benefits of the plan.

From the above, we conclude that the Federal Personnel Manual's policy regarding retroactive payments for optional programs by an employee who is reinstated after a finding that the employee's removal was unwarranted or unjustified allows an employee to choose whether or not to have those payments made retroactively.

We find that an employee's decision to become a union member and to pay union dues through checkoff is a voluntary decision like those which an employee makes regarding life insurance and health benefits. Therefore, we conclude, in the absence of any statutory or regulatory constraint requiring a different policy, that the purposes of the Statute are best served by a policy which gives to an employee who is reinstated after a determination that his termination was unwarranted or unjustified the option of having his union membership retroactively restored. If the employee so chooses, he will incur a retroactive obligation for the dues not paid during the period of his termination and the union will be obligated to provide him with any benefits which might have accrued to him as a union member during that period. A reinstated employee should be given the option, alternatively, of joining the union as if he were a newly hired employee and his obligation to the union would then begin only with signing of a new dues authorization form.

The Board adopted the FLRA's approach in Samuels v. USPS, 95 MSPR 30, 33-36 ¶¶ 7-11 (2003), reopened as to deduction of union dues, Samuels v. USPS, 95 MSPR 512 (2004), post:

We first address the appellant's claim that the agency improperly deducted union dues from his back pay award. As noted, according to evidence that the appellant does not dispute, he was a member of both APWU and NAPFE at the time of his removal. He argues, however, that he did not have the benefit of union representation during the period following his removal, and that he never signed any document requesting that union dues be taken out of his back pay award.

The Back Pay Act provides, in pertinent part, that an employee who is found to have been affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action which has resulted in the withdrawal or reduction of all or part of his pay, allowances, or differentials, is entitled, on correction of the personnel action, to receive for the period for which the personnel action was in effect an amount equal to all or any part of the pay, allowances, or differentials, as applicable, which the employee normally would have earned or received during the period if the personnel action had not occurred. 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(A)(i). The Back Pay Act applies to preference eligible Postal Service employees who, like the appellant, prevail in appeals before the Board. Andress v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 501, 506-08 (1993). Regulations promulgated by OPM pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 5596(c) set forth how back pay is to be computed. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805. Those regulations specifically provide that, in computing the net amount of back pay payable under § 5596, an agency must make certain offsets and deductions from the gross back pay award, including authorized deductions of the type that would have been made from the employee's pay (if paid when properly due) in accordance with the normal order of precedence for deductions from pay established by the agency, subject to any applicable law or regulation. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(c)(3). Those deductions include, but are not limited to, mandatory employee retirement contributions, social security and Medicare taxes, health benefit and life insurance premiums under certain circumstances, and federal income tax withholdings. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3)(i)(v); see also Tanaka v. Department of the Navy, 788 F.2d 1552, 1553 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

In deducting dues payable to both unions from the appellant's back pay award, the agency reasoned that, had he not been removed, it would have continued to automatically deduct dues from his salary pursuant to his own authorizations. And, the agency asserted, the appellant did not show that he was removed from membership with the unions during the back pay period.

The Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA) is charged with using its specialized experience in the field of labor relations to give content to the principles and goals set forth in the Civil Service Reform Act. Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco & Firearms v. FLRA, 464 U.S. 89, 97, 104 S. Ct. 439, 78 L. Ed. 2d 195 (1983). "[t]he [FLRA] is entitled to considerable deference when it exercises its special function of applying the general provisions of the [Civil Service Reform] Act to the complexities of federal labor relations." Id. (internal quotations omitted). Section 7115(b) of that statute provides that an employee's authorization for deduction of union dues terminates when the agreement between the agency and the exclusive representative involved ceases to be applicable to the employee. In examining the case of a federal employee whose removal was reversed by the Board, the FLRA found that an agreement for dues deduction authorization no longer applies to an employee when his employment is terminated. American Fed'n of Gov't Employees Local 1843, 25 F.L.R.A. 523, 526, 1987 WL 90307 (1987), petition denied, American Fed'n of Gov't Employees Local 1843 v. FLRA, 843 F.2d 550 (D.C. Cir. 1988). In the FLRA's view, an employee's decision to become a union member and to pay union dues through automatic payroll deduction is a voluntary decision, and that, in the absence of any statutory or regulatory constraint requiring a different policy, the purposes of its statute are best served by a policy which gives to an employee who is reinstated after a determination that his removal was unwarranted or unjustified the option of having his union membership retroactively restored. Id. at 528. In other words, if the reinstated employee so chooses, he would incur a retroactive obligation for the dues not paid during the period of his removal, and the union would be obligated to provide him with any benefits which might have accrued to him as a union member during that period. Alternatively, he should be given the option of joining the union as if he were a newly hired employee, in which case his obligation to the union would begin with the signing of a new dues authorization form. Id. In affirming the FLRA's decision finding it proper for the agency not to have deducted union dues from the employee's back pay award, the Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit found no indication that Congress specifically intended that an employee's dues withholding authorization, having been terminated pursuant to § 7115(b)(1), should be revived automatically by a subsequent reinstatement of that employee after a finding of wrongful discharge. American Fed'n of Gov't Employees Local 1843, 843 F.2d at 556.

The Board will usually defer to an administrative interpretation of a statute by an agency charged with its administration when that interpretation is reasonable. See, e.g., True v. Office of Personnel Management, 44 M.S.P.R. 251, 254 (1990), aff'd, 926 F.2d 1151 (Fed. Cir. 1991). Because the FLRA's interpretation of its statute is reasonable, we find it appropriate to, and do, defer to it.[3] We find, therefore, that the appellant's obligation to pay dues ended on May 9, 1999, when he was removed, and that, because he did not indicate that he wished his prior dues-paying status to be restored retroactively, the obligation did not automatically revive on July 8, 1999, when he was restored to duty. Therefore, the agency's deduction of dues from his back pay award for the back pay period was improper, and this case must be remanded for a determination of the amount to which the appellant is now entitled. The extent of his entitlement may be affected by the provision found in both authorizations of deductions he signed to the effect that the assignment of funds is irrevocable for the period of one year from the date of delivery, unless written notice is provided to the agency within a distinct time frame. On remand, the parties shall offer evidence and argument in support of their respective positions on this point.

[3] We do not defer to the rule applied by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) in Vu v. Postmaster General, EEOC No. 04950020, 1996 WL 197412 (1996), wherein EEOC held that union dues are properly deducted from an employee's back pay award. EEOC is not charged with the administration of either the Federal Labor-Management Relations Statute or the Back Pay Act. We also note that, in finding that the dues deduction was proper, the AJ relied on Konieczko v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 660 (1993), wherein the Board stated in a footnote, and without explanation, that it concurred in the AJ's holding that a return to the status quo ante, under which the appellant was a dues-paying union member, required deduction of union dues. Id. at 663 n.2. To the extent that our holding today is inconsistent with the statement in Konieczko, it is hereby overruled.

In Samuels, the Board treated the Postal Service as being subject to the Federal Service Labor Management Relations Statute administered by the FLRA when, in fact, the Postal Service is subject to the National Labor Relations Act, as amended, administered by the NLRB. The distinction in the laws governing labor-management relations, as well as a peculiarity in Postal Service employee relations that retains an individual as a nominal employee after a removal and pending an appeal, led the Board to reopen the case, at the request of the Postal Service and reach the result that for preference-eligible employees of the Postal Service, dues deductions remain in effect during a period of termination unless the employee affirmatively discontinues dues deduction during that period in accordance with provisions of the collective bargaining contract, Samuels v. USPS, 95 MSPR 512, 514-19 ¶¶ 2-16 (2004):

The appellant was removed from his position as a Motor Vehicle Operator, effective May 8, 1999, based on the charge of threatening his supervisor. On appeal, the administrative judge (AJ) sustained some, but not all, of the specifications the agency cited in support of the charge and found that the appellant had not established his claim of racial discrimination. The AJ mitigated the penalty to a 60-day suspension. The agency filed a petition for review (PFR) and the appellant filed a cross PFR. The Board issued a final order denying both petitions. Samuels v. U.S. Postal Service, 90 M.S.P.R. 29 (2001) (Table).

Thereafter, the appellant filed a petition for enforcement, claiming, among other things, that the agency improperly deducted union dues from his back pay award. Samuels, 95 M.S.P.R. 30 ¶ 3. With regard to that issue, the AJ found that, according to the evidence presented, the appellant was a dues-paying member of both the American Postal Workers Union (APWU) and the National Alliance of Postal and Federal Employees (NAPFE) prior to his removal, and that, because of that status, the agency's deduction of the union dues from his back pay award was proper. Id. ¶ 4. The appellant filed a petition for review that included a challenge to this finding. Id. ¶ 5.

In reviewing the administrative judge's finding on the union dues issue, the Board took guidance from the Back Pay Act and the Federal Labor Relations Authority's (FLRA's) interpretation of the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute (FSLMRS). Id. ¶¶ 7-11. The Board found that, under the Back Pay Act and its implementing regulations, the Office of Personnel Management defines some offsets and deductions from a gross back pay award and provides that the employing agency take these and other appropriate offsets and deductions "subject to any applicable law or regulation." The Board found that: in this case, the FSLMRS was the law applicable to the appellant's obligation to pay unions dues; under the FLRA's interpretation of the FSLMRS, that obligation ended when he was removed; and, because he did not indicate that he wished his prior dues-paying status to be restored retroactively, the obligation did not automatically revive when he was restored to duty. However, the Board found that the extent of the appellant's entitlement to the improperly deducted dues may be affected by the provision found in both authorizations of deductions he signed to the effect that the assignment of funds is irrevocable for the period of one year from the date of delivery, unless written notice is provided to the agency within a distinct time frame. Id. ¶ 11. Thus, it remanded for a determination of the amount of improperly deducted union dues to which the appellant was entitled. Id. ¶¶ 11, 16.

The PRA, Pub. L. No. 91-375, 84 Stat. 719 (1970), converted the cabinet-level Post Office Department into the U.S. Postal Service, an "independent establishment of the executive branch." 39 U.S.C. § 201. As part of this reorganization, labor-management relations in the Postal Service were largely placed under the regulation of the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) and the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB), effective July 1, 1971. Old Dominion Branch No. 496 v. Austin, 418 U.S. 264, 266 n.1, 94 S. Ct. 2770, 41 L. Ed.2d 745 (1974).

To the extent that the decision in Samuels, 95 M.S.P.R. 30, suggested that labor-management relations in the Postal Service is wholly subject to the FSLMRS, we hereby modify it to reflect that the U.S. Postal Service back pay issues raised by the parties in Samuels are governed by provisions of the PRA and the NLRA. See National Labor Relations Board v. U.S. Postal Service, 833 F.2d 1195, 1197 (6th Cir. 1987) (the PRA specifically assigns to the NLRB some of the responsibilities that the NLRB exercises under the NLRA). Thus, we find that the FSLMRS is not applicable law to determine whether the agency properly deducted union dues in computing the appellant's back pay.

Additionally, we find that the FSLMRS does not provide analogous authority to decide whether the agency properly deducted union dues from the appellant's back pay. Under the FSLMRS, a dues allotment with respect to any employee terminates when the collective bargaining agreement (CBA) ceases to be applicable to the employee. 5 U.S.C. § 7115. Generally, when a federal employee is terminated, he is removed from the rolls and the CBA ceases to be applicable to him. Thus, under the FSLMRS, an authorization for union dues deduction no longer applies to a terminated employee. See American Federation of Government Employees Local 1843 v. Federal Labor Relations Authority, 843 F.2d 550, 552 (D.C. Cir. 1988).

However, in contrast to federal employees subject to the FSLMRS, preference eligible postal employees remain on the agency rolls during the pendency of a removal appeal before the Board. The Agreement between the United States Postal Service and the American Postal Workers Union, AFL-CIO, Article 16.5, provides that a preference eligible Postal employee who appeals a discharge to the Board "shall remain on the rolls (non-pay status) until disposition of the case has been had either by settlement or through exhaustion of his/her MSPB appeal." Thus, preference eligible Postal employees, such as the appellant, are not terminated while they appeal agency discharge actions to the Board in the same sense that federal employees subject to the FSLMRS are terminated during the Board appellate process. We find that one effect caused by the fact that Postal employees remain on the agency's rolls, i.e., remain employees, during the pendency of Board discharge appeals is that the CBA between the agency and the exclusive representative does not cease to be applicable to the employees. Cf. Vaca v. Sipes, 386 U.S. 171, 176-78 (1967) (under the NLRA, a union has a duty of fair representation to union members to enforce the CBA grievance procedures even after their discharge from employment). Therefore, we reopen our decision in Samuels to consider the agency's contention that it properly deducted union dues for the APWU and the NAPFE from the appellant's back pay under the PRA.

The PRA provides as follows:

(a) When a labor organization holds exclusive recognition,...the Postal service shall deduct the regular and periodic dues of the organization from the pay of all members of the organization in the unit of recognition if the...Postal Service has received from each employee, on whose account such deductions are made, a written assignment which shall be irrevocable for a period of not more than one year.

(b) Any agreement in effect immediately prior to the date of enactment of the Postal Reorganization Act between the Post Office Department and any organization of postal employees which provides for deduction by the Department of the regular and periodic dues of the organization from the pay of its members, shall continue in full force and effect and the obligation for such deductions shall be assumed by the Postal Service. No such deduction shall be made from the pay of any employee except on his written assignment, which shall be irrevocable for a period of not more than one year.

39 U.S.C. § 1205. Thus, the PRA provides that the Postal Service may deduct union dues for the union that is the exclusive representative of the employee and for unions that are not the exclusive representative of the employee but have dues deduction privileges sheltered by § 1205(b). U.S. Postal Service v. Mail Handlers, 248 NLRB 5, 7-8 WL 11959 (1980). Further, the PRA allows a CBA to provide that a dues authorization that is irrevocable for not more than one year automatically will be renewed for an additional interval unless revoked by the employee during a specific yearly period. National Labor Relations Board v. U.S. Postal Service, 833 F.2d at 1197.

The agency has the burden of proof to show compliance with the Board's final order. O'Connell v. Department of the Navy, 73 M.S.P.R. 235, 238 (1997). The agency must submit evidence clearly proving its compliance with a final order. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a); Malloy v. Office of Personnel Management, 73 M.S.P.R. 179, 181-82 (1997). With regard to the issue of whether it properly deducted union dues from the appellant's back pay, the agency submitted the CBA between the Postal Service and the APWU and evidence to establish that the appellant made a written assignment of dues to the APWU on November 11, 1986, and a written assignment of dues to the NAPFE on January 19, 1993. The agency asserts that the appellant never revoked these authorizations. Although, under the Board's regulations, 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a), the appellant may rebut these assertions by the agency, he failed to do so.

The 1998-2000 CBA between the Postal Service and the APWU provided that a union dues authorization will be irrevocable for a period of up to one year and that it may be automatically renewed. Specifically, the CBA provided:

A. In conformity with...39 U.S.C. § 1205, without cost to the Union, the Employer shall deduct and remit to the Union the regular and periodic Union dues from the pay of employees as instructed in writing by the Union and the employee, which written assignment by the employee shall be irrevocable for a period of not more than one (1) year.

B. The authorization of such deductions shall be made in accordance with the terms of Standard Form 1187. Revocation of authorization shall be made in accordance with the terms of Standard Form 1186.

C Notwithstanding the foregoing, employees' dues deduction authorizations (Standard Form 1187) which are presently on file with the Employer on behalf of the Union party to this Agreement, shall continue to be honored and given full force and effect by the Employer unless and until revoked in accordance with their terms.

(Agreement between the United States Postal Service and the American Postal Workers Union, AFL-CIO, Article 17.6). We find that, with regard to the appellant's assignment of dues to the APWU, the agency established that its deduction of dues from the appellant's back pay for the entire back pay period, July 8, 1999, through July 15, 2000, was authorized by the PRA, the applicable CBA, and the appellant's unrevoked written Authorization for Deduction of Dues to the APWU.

With regard to the appellant's assignment of dues to the NAPFE, we find that the agency established that its deduction of dues from the appellant's back pay for the entire back pay period was authorized by the appellant's unrevoked written Authorization for Deduction of Dues to the NAPFE. That Authorization provides, in relevant part:

This assignment...shall be irrevocable for a period of one (1) year from the date of delivery hereof to you, and I agree and direct that this assignment...shall be automatically renewed and shall be irrevocable for successive periods of one (1) year, unless written notice is given by me...prior to the expiration of each period of one year.

This assignment is freely made pursuant to the provisions of the Postal Reorganization Act and is not contingent upon the existence of any agreement between you, my Union, or any other Union.

We find that this provision in the Authorization allows for the deduction of dues from the appellant's back pay by the agency even without the existence of a CBA provision between the NAPFE and the agency similar to that between APWU and the agency. Moreover, the NAPFE is among the nonexclusive unions whose dues deduction privilege is sheltered by 39 U.S.C. § 1205(b). See National Alliance of Postal and Federal Employees v. Klassen, 514 F.2d 189, 192 (D.C. Cir. 1975).

Accordingly, we find that the agency established that the deduction of union dues payable to the APWU and the NAPFE was proper under the regulations implementing the Back Pay Act, specifically 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3), which authorizes an agency to make "[a]uthorized deductions" from the gross back pay award. We also find that, with regard to the issue of the deduction of unions dues from the appellant's back pay, the agency is in compliance with the Board's order mitigating the agency's action.

Vice Chair Wagner, in a concurrence to a nonprecedential decision, Tawadrous v. Dept. of Treasury, DA-0752-08-0227-X-2, 2011 MSPB 21 (2011) (NP), voiced her disagreement with the approach adopted in the first iteration of Samuels. Ms. Wagner expressed her belief that, if not properly terminated by the employee, union dues should continue during a period of back pay entitlement (and be set off from back pay):

In its prior pleadings below, the agency contended that it properly deducted union dues from the back pay award because it was required under 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3) to offset from gross back pay all authorized deductions that would have been made from the appellant's pay if he had continued in pay status. In response to the administrative judge's most recent recommendation, the agency has paid the appellant the amount of dues withheld, and appears to have abandoned its objection that such payment is inconsistent with 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3). Consequently, I agree that the appellant's petition for enforcement is moot. However, I write separately in order to express my view that such payment is not warranted under the Back Pay Act.

Under the Back Pay Act, an employee, on correction of an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action, is entitled "to receive for the period for which the personnel action was in effect...an amount equal to all or any part of the pay, allowances, or differentials, as applicable which the employee normally would have earned or received during the period if the personnel action had not occurred...." 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(A). Thus, the Board is required, upon correcting a wrongful personnel action, to ensure that the employee is returned, as nearly as possible, to the status quo ante. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In other words, the remedy should place the injured party as nearly as possible in the situation that he or she would have occupied if the wrong had not been committed. Kerr, 726 F.2d at 733 n.3 (citing Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405, 418-19 (1975)).

The Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute (FSLMRS) provides for a one-year period during which an employee's written consent to an automatic pay deduction for union dues is not revocable. 5 U.S.C. § 7115(a); see, e.g., In re Margaret Jackson, 59 Comp. Gen. 666, 667 (Aug. 14, 1980) (where an employee signs an allotment form, she may not revoke that authorization before one year). Here, the record does not establish that the appellant lawfully revoked his dues deduction authorization under 5 U.S.C. § 7115(a) prior to his termination. Rather, it appears that he was in a dues-paying status as of January 18, 2008, the effective date of his removal. Thus, as of the date of his termination, the appellant's pay was subject to a lawful dues deduction authorization under 5 U.S.C. § 7115(a) that--had he not been terminated--would have been revocable only upon completion of its one-year term. While it is true that the agency appropriately terminated the dues allotment following his removal pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7115(b), the subsequent restoration of the status quo ante under the Back Pay Act in this case voided this action and requires that the appellant's back pay award be subject to the one-year term of his irrevocable dues authorization and reduced accordingly.

I am aware that in Samuels v. U.S. Postal Service, 95 M.S.P.R. 30, ¶¶ 10-11 (2003) ("Samuels I"), vacated in part, 95 M.S.P.R. 512 (2004) ("Samuels II"), the Board deferred to the decision of the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA or Authority) in American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE), Local 1843, 25 F.L.R.A. 523, 526 (1987), petition denied, AFGE, Local 1843 v. FLRA, 843 F.2d 550 (D.C. Cir. 1988), in holding that the agency's deduction of union dues from back pay was improper. In Samuels II, the Board vacated Samuels I, ¶¶ 10-11, on the basis that the FSLMRS does not apply to the U.S. Postal Service. Samuels II, 95 M.S.P.R. 512, ¶¶ 8-10.

Apart from its having been vacated, I otherwise do not find the Board's decision in Samuels I to be persuasive on the question of whether the Back Pay Act mandates reducing a back pay award by the amount of union dues that the employee would have been obligated to pay had he not been terminated. In answering that question, the Board relied entirely on the FLRA's decision in AFGE, Local 1843 where the issue was whether the agency's failure to deduct union dues from a back pay award constituted an unfair labor practice (ULP) under 5 U.S.C. § 7116. 25 F.L.R.A. at 523. The FLRA did not determine whether such a deduction was required under the Back Pay Act. Id. at 526-528.[*]

[*] Indeed, on review, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit adopted the "not [ ] unreasonable" assumption that the employee would not have been entitled under the Back Pay Act to recover the dues for the period of time covered by an irrevocable dues authorization. See AFGE, Local 1843, 843 F.2d at 554. The court thereafter found that "nothing in the Back Pay Act or the FSLMRS compels a conclusion that an agency payment to a reinstated employee in excess of his Back Pay Act rights, rather than to the union, 'interferes with' his exercise 'of any right' under the FSLMRS" for purposes of establishing an unfair labor practice. Id. at 555 (emphasis added).

Rather, in AFGE, Local 1843, the Authority summarily concluded that an employee's obligation to pay union dues is a voluntary decision analogous to other optional programs, such as life insurance and health benefits, and that the employee, therefore, should be given the option to have his union membership retroactively restored upon reinstatement. 25 F.L.R.A. at 528. However, the FLRA's analysis is devoid of any discussion reconciling its position with the plain language of 5 U.S.C. § 7115(a) creating a legally binding obligation to allot dues for one year after the employee voluntarily assumes membership. Id. at 526-528.

In my view, the restoration of a successful appellant to status quo ante under the Back Pay Act requires that we place him as nearly as possible in the situation that he would have occupied if he had not been wrongfully removed. Thus, if the record shows that the appellant had made an irrevocable election under 5 U.S.C. § 7115(a) to have union dues withheld from his pay at the time of his removal, then the agency properly withheld the union dues from the back pay award, at least until such time as the appellant could have revoked his election.

The ability of the employee to make an election to have, or not to have, life insurance retroactively applied (and paid for) may depend on whether there is a finding that the employee's separation was erroneous, a finding that is explicit in Board decisions reversing discharges or other separations, but that finding would not ordinarily occur in a settlement, Alfonso v. DOJ, 96 MSPR 266, 269-70 ¶¶ 7-8 (2004):

Citing 5 U.S.C. § 8706(d) the appellant argues that the retroactive deduction for life insurance was improper because he did not suffer a loss during the separation period. Section 8706(d) reads:

If the insurance of an employee stops because of separation from the service or suspension without pay, and the separation or suspension is thereafter officially found to have been erroneous, the employee is deemed to have been insured during the period of erroneous separation or suspension. Deductions otherwise required by section 8707 of this chapter shall not be withheld from any backpay awarded for the period of separation or suspension unless death or accidental dismemberment of the employee occurs during such period.

The agency's November 7, 2003 submission argues that this section does not apply in the appellant's case. The agency contends that in order for section 8706 to be applicable, the statute requires there to have been an official finding that the appellant's separation was erroneous and that there has been no such finding in this case. The agency's position is that the life insurance deduction is required in this back pay award because it agreed to pay back pay and full benefits as part of paragraph 2(b) of the settlement agreement, which states (emphasis added):

[The agency agrees] to reinstate Mr. Alfonso as a Border Patrol Agent with such reinstatement to be effective from February 9, 2002, thereby providing back pay and full benefits from that date up to and including April 26, 2002.

The agency agreed to retroactively reinstate the appellant for a designated period of time with full benefits and to allow the appellant to resign from the agency. We find that, because of the settlement agreement, the appellant had the opportunity to bargain for or to reject coverage thus making section 8706(d) inapplicable. Additionally, the settlement agreement at paragraph 4 states (emphasis added):

The parties agree that this agreement is not an admission of wrongdoing, and that they make this agreement solely to avoid the consumption of time, money, and other resources associated with litigation.

We thus agree with the agency that the required finding of an erroneous separation was not made and find that the appellant is not entitled to reimbursement for the $93.84 life insurance premium deduction.

Title Five, United States Code, Section 8908(a) provides as follows:

An employee enrolled in a health benefits plan under this chapter who is removed or suspended without pay and later reinstated or restored to duty on the ground that the removal or suspension was unjustified or unwarranted may, at his option, enroll as a new employee or have his coverage restored, with appropriate adjustments made in contributions and claims, to the same extent and effect as though the removal or suspension had not taken place.

Thus, under this provision, upon his reinstatement the appellant had the option of being enrolled in the health insurance program as a new employee or having his insurance coverage restored, with appropriate adjustments, as if he had not been separated. See Fernandez v. Department of Justice, 105 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 14 (2007).

The appellant has provided a copy of a June 9, 2009 e-mail to an agency human resources employee stating that he did not want to be charged for health insurance during the back pay period. The agency did not challenge the validity of this e-mail. Thus, consistent with the statutory provision set forth above, following the appellant's reinstatement, the agency should have enrolled the appellant in the Federal Employees Health Benefits Program as a new employee. Because of the appellant's election, the agency should not have deducted from the appellant's back pay award health insurance premiums for the period prior to the appellant's enrollment as a new employee. See Fernandez, 105 M.S.P.R. 443, ¶ 16 (where an employee elects not to have his health insurance benefits retroactively restored, he is entitled to reimbursement of the premiums improperly deducted from his back pay). Accordingly, the agency is in noncompliance in this regard. To be in compliance, the agency must pay the appellant the amount of health insurance benefits improperly withheld from the back pay award with appropriate interest. Because it has failed to do so, the agency remains in noncompliance.

As to cancelation of an adverse action, Fernandez v. DOJ, 105 MSPR 443, 448-49 ¶ 13-17, 2007 MSPB 99 (2007), also explained the rule concerning treatment of health insurance premiums:

The appellant also asserts on review that he is entitled to reimbursement for the health benefits premiums that the agency withheld from his back pay when it restored his health benefits retroactive to the effective date of his removal because he did not elect to have his health benefits retroactively restored.

The restoration of health benefits for employees who have been removed and are subsequently reinstated is governed by 5 U.S.C. § 8908(a) and 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3)(iii). Title 5 U.S.C. § 8908(a) provides as follows:

An employee enrolled in a health benefits plan under this chapter who is removed or suspended without pay and later reinstated or restored to duty on the ground that the removal or suspension was unjustified or unwarranted may, at his option, enroll as a new employee or have his coverage restored, with appropriate adjustments made in contributions and claims, to the same extent and effect as though removal or suspension had not taken place.

(Emphasis added.) Title 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3)(iii) requires agencies to deduct health benefits premiums when computing the amount of back pay payable to an employee who has been removed and is later reinstated if coverage is retroactively reinstated at the employee's election under 5 U.S.C. § 8908(a). See Gutkowski v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 399, ¶ 10, review dismissed, 204 Fed. Appx. 6 (Fed. Cir. 2006).

Thus, the retroactive restoration of an employee's health benefits under 5 U.S.C. § 8908(a) is contingent upon the employee's election to have these benefits retroactively restored. In this case, it appears that this condition has not been met. The agency merely stated below that it would retroactively restore the appellant's health benefits. In its response to the PFR, the agency does not rebut the appellant's assertion that he did not elect to have his health benefits retroactively restored, nor is there any evidence in the record that the appellant made such an election.

Therefore, on remand, the AJ shall direct the agency to afford the appellant the opportunity to elect to have his health benefits retroactively restored under 5 U.S.C. § 8908(a). If the appellant does not exercise this option, he is entitled to reimbursement for the health benefits premiums that the agency deducted from his back pay when it retroactively restored his health benefits coverage. Under those circumstances, however, he would not be entitled to reimbursement for the out-of-pocket medical costs he incurred as a result of the cancellation of his health benefits due to his removal, which he requested below. See Raymond v. Department of the Army, 102 M.S.P.R. 665, ¶ 13 (2006).

Conversely, if the appellant elects to have his health benefits coverage retroactively restored, he would not be entitled to reimbursement for the health benefits premiums that the agency deducted from his back pay when it retroactively restored his coverage. He would, however, be entitled to reimbursement for the medical expenses he incurred during the period of his removal. As the AJ explained, if the appellant wishes to be reimbursed for medical expenses he incurred during the period of his removal, the proper procedure is to submit any claims for those expenses to his health benefits carrier. See, e.g., Raymond, 102 M.S.P.R. 665, ¶ 13.
