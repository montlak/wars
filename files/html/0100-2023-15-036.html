12. Cancelation or Conversion of Approved Leave

An agency may cancel previously approved leave in an emergency. The Board will uphold the cancelation, that is, the Board will sustain adverse action based on ensuing AWOL charges, unless there is a showing that it was an unreasonable exercise of managerial discretion under the circumstances. McPartland v. FAA, 14 MSPR 506, 509 (1983). If an agency cancels leave and then seeks to remove the employee for failure to report and AWOL, it must demonstrate that it effectively communicated to the employee its action canceling the leave. Foulk v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 32, 35 (1983); see Carpenter v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 84, 87 (1983) (notice is inadequate if presented to appellant's wife at home while the appellant is on a camping trip and cannot be reached). But see Rojas v. USPS, 74 MSPR 544, 548 (1997) (assuming that the agency did not effectively notify the appellant that he was on AWOL rather than administrative leave, the error was harmless because the appellant was incarcerated and could not have returned to work had he received proper notice).

Circumstantial evidence tending to establish dispatch of notice of leave cancelation supports the inference of receipt of the notice. Eads v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-1576) (relying on Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 537 (Fed. Cir. 1984)). An agency may announce, in advance, the cancelation of leave because of an imminent labor strike, but a memo to that effect removed from a mandatory reading file several weeks prior to a strike was not sufficient notification of leave cancelation. Smith v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 582, 584 (1983). Once an employee is notified of the likelihood of leave cancelation, the employee is obliged to determine his leave status. Lack of knowledge of leave cancelation due to negligence or conscious avoidance of the truth did not relieve the employee from AWOL or related charges. Bambury v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 417, 425 (1983).

If an agency changes an employee's status from leave without pay to absence without leave because the agency decides the employee is fit to return to duty, the agency must demonstrate by preponderant evidence both the employee's fitness and the reasons for its decision to change the leave status. Bavier v. Dept. of Transp., 4 MSPR 548, 550, 5 MSPB 73 (1981). Although under Bavier, removal of an employee for absence without leave can be sustained only on a showing that the employer considered an employee's claim of medical disability and determined that he was fit to return to duty, the agency may remove an employee for excessive unscheduled leave of more than a year should the agency need to fill the position through a regular, full-time or part-time employee. Dornan v. IRS, 7 MSPR 674, 676, 7 MSPB 499 (1981). An agency may properly remove an employee who is out because of injury if approved leave is converted to AWOL after the employee supplies insufficient evidence to support a continuation of sick leave and if the absence is without foreseeable end. See Bentley v. USPS, 20 MSPR 208, 210 (1984).
