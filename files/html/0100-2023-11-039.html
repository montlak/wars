a. Repudiated or Recanted Admission

Adverse action may be sustained, despite denials of misconduct at a hearing, if the employee previously supplied a sworn affidavit admitting the misconduct and there is further corroborating unsworn testimony attributed to him by the disinterested and credible testimony of agency investigators. Vandergrift v. USPS, 26 MSPR 516, 518 (1985). Repudiated admissions can support the agency's burden of proof if the misconduct is demonstrated to exist and if evidence exists corroborating the admissions. Hodges v. Dept. of Treasury, 27 MSPR 51, 54-55 (1985), remanded for credibility determinations:

The presiding official found that although the agency furnished evidence that misfiling of tax documents had been a problem, no evidence was presented by the agency, aside from unsworn statements attributed to the appellant by Brown and Eason which were repudiated by appellant under oath, connecting appellant with any misfiled document. Citing Wohlwend v. Department of Health and Human Services, 16 MSPR 458 (1983), and Roberts v. Department of the Treasury, 8 MSPB 323, 8 MSPR 674 (1981), the presiding official held that the agency failed to support its case by a preponderance of the evidence, and, accordingly, reversed the removal action.

...

Our review of the record reveals that the presiding official's application of the Board's holdings in Roberts and Wohlwend are misplaced and inapplicable to the particular circumstances of the case. In Roberts, supra, the agency presented no independent evidence, other than the employee's admissions, tending to establish the existence of misconduct or that the employee actually engaged in such misconduct. Additionally, there was a complete absence of any evidence corroborating appellant's alleged misconduct (use and sale of drugs). Thus, the employee's repudiated admissions, without more, could not support a finding that the employee used and sold narcotics.

In Wohlwend, supra, the agency wholly failed to establish that the alleged misconduct (temporarily disconnecting a patient from a respirator) had actually occurred or any injury had resulted therefrom. Thus, the Board went on to hold that in absence of some evidentiary showing that the act had occurred, apart from the employee's admissions, witness credibility could not be considered determinative.

Here, the agency has shown that the alleged misconduct (misfiling of tax documents) had occurred and that it occurred within the division where the appellant was employed. Thus, the agency has met its initial obligation of showing misfeasance or nonfeasance by someone. The remaining fact to be determined now is whether it is more likely true than not that appellant made the "admissions" as alleged by Brown and Eason, or whether it is more likely true than not that Brown and Eason misunderstood the essence of appellant's admissions (that she had put NIFs in the bin, not that she had willfully misfiled tax returns). This issue cannot be determined, however, without assessing the credibility and demeanor of the relevant parties in this case....

As to admissions later withdrawn, repudiated, or denied, Gulso v. Dept. of Air Force, 46 MSPR 478, 481-82 (1990):

[T]he administrative judge erred by failing to consider what effect, if any, the appellant's attempt to recant his admission would have had on the outcome of the case. In Wohlwend v. DHHS, 16 MSPR 458, 461 (1983), we found that an administrative judge erred in considering an agency's witnesses' credibility to be a determinative factor, when the agency failed to support its removal action by preponderant evidence because it proffered no evidence except an employee's admission uttered in the "heat of the moment," which she later recanted.

We find, however, that the administrative judge's error in this matter is harmless, because the appellant's recantation is both late and unsupported by evidence or, indeed, argument that he did not engage in the misconduct, so that his withdrawal lacks credibility and weight as evidence on his behalf. Additionally, unlike in Wohlwend, the agency in this case offered sufficient proof that the appellant committed the misdeeds. The affidavits of Koch and Lewis, who actually witnessed the appellant's transgression of agency rules, support the two charges that the administrative judge sustained.... Therefore, even absent the appellant's admission as evidence, the charges are supported to that extent....

Compare Bowers v. USPS, CH-0752-15-0084-I-1 (NP 8/3/2016) (AJ declined to credit the recantation of a charge), with Mitchell v. Dept. of Air Force, 91 MSPR 201, 205-06 ¶¶ 10-12 (2002) (appellant's comments to another employee admitting misconduct should have been evaluated in context of employee's later denial of misconduct, coupled with explanation that statements to other employees were no more than flippancy).

The Board again pointed out that recanted admissions may carry evidentiary weight, if there is corroborating evidence, Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 548-49 (1994), aff'd, Uske v. USPS, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995), post:

The administrative judge treated this appeal as one in which the agency's only evidence in support of the charge is the appellant's admission, which he later recanted. He determined that, under Wohlwend v. DHHS, 16 MSPR 458, 461 (1983), and Roberts v. Dept. of the Treasury, 8 MSPB 323, 8 MSPR 674, 677-79 (1981), "it must first be shown by the agency that its charge is supported by preponderant evidence in the face of the appellant recanting his prior admissions." He seems to suggest that the Board must disregard a recanted admission and evaluate only the other evidence against an appellant to see if the agency can meet its burden of proof on the charge.

Presumably, under the administrative judge's theory, if the agency cannot prove its charge without relying on the recanted admission, then the action fails. If the agency can prove its charge without relying on the recanted admission, then it need not rely on the recanted admission at all. The application of the administrative judge's theory therefore leads to nonsensical results because it renders the recanted admission utterly inconsequential whether the recantation is eventually adjudged to be credible or not. In addition, this theory could effectively insulate alleged employee wrong-doers from all legal consequences of their admissions so long as they later recant.

Furthermore, the administrative judge's reliance on Wohlwend and Roberts is misplaced in this appeal. The Board held in Wohlwend and Roberts that an appellant's unsworn extrajudicial statements, repudiated by him under oath and standing alone, do not constitute preponderant evidence that the appellant committed the charged misconduct. In those cases, the agency presented no evidence in support of its charge except the appellant's unsworn recanted admissions. In this appeal, the agency has presented not only the appellant's unsworn recanted admission, but also, inter alia, the testimony and sworn written statements of five agency witnesses, the testimony of the agency investigator, an investigative memorandum, and the photographs themselves. Because the agency has submitted corroborative evidence in support of its charge, the Board may properly consider the appellant's unsworn recanted admission, including whether the recantation is credible or not, as one of the pieces of evidence against him.

The Federal Circuit affirmed, Uske v. USPS, 56 F.3d 1375, 1377-78 (Fed. Cir. 1995):

The opinion below relied on Roberts v. Dept. of the Treasury, 8 MSPB 323, 8 MSPR 674 (1981), and Wohlwend v. DHHS, 16 MSPR 458 (1983). In Roberts, the board held that without independent evidence tending to show that the misconduct occurred, an unsworn admission cannot satisfy the preponderant evidence standard. 8 MSPR at 679. Likewise, in Wohlwend, the board reversed the adverse action because the agency had not introduced independent evidence tending to show that the alleged conduct had occurred. 16 MSPR at 461. Both board opinions looked for authority in Wong Sun v. United States, 371 U.S. 471, 83 S. Ct. 407, 9 L. Ed.2d 441 (1963), and Opper v. United States, 348 U.S. 84, 75 S. Ct. 158, 99 L. Ed. 101 (1954), which involved criminal convictions that involved alleged improper reliance on judicial statements. The Opper Court held that in order to support a conviction "the corroborative evidence need not be sufficient, independent of the statements, to establish the corpus delicti.... It is sufficient if the corroboration supports the essential facts admitted sufficiently to justify a jury inference of their truth." 348 U.S. at 93, 75 S. Ct. at 164. In Wong Sun, the Court held that it "is a settled principle of the administration of criminal justice in the federal courts that a conviction must rest upon firmer ground than the uncorroborated admission or confession of the accused." 371 U.S. at 488-89, 83 S. Ct. at 418.

The extent to which a recanted admission or confession can be relied on by an agency to support an adverse action is an issue of first impression in this court. At the outset, we note that the agency's burden in sustaining an adverse action does not rise to the level needed for a criminal conviction. See Novotny v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 521, 524 (Fed. Cir. 1984) ("Contentions that government employees faced with removal are 'entitled to all the protections of a criminal trial' have been authoritatively rejected."). Thus, the agency need only meet the lower preponderance of the evidence standard. 5 USC § 7701(c)(1)(B) (1988 & Supp. V 1993); 5 CFR § 1201.56(a) (1994). As part of its case, the agency must of course prove that the events giving rise to the misconduct charged actually occurred. If the only proof the agency brings forth are later recanted statements by the employee, an adverse action cannot be justified. Wohlwend, 16 MSPR at 461; Roberts, 8 MSPR at 679.

This is not such a case. Here, the board found that the agency had proved by a preponderance of the evidence that the photographs published in Lips magazine were taken in the old Maintenance Office at the DMU. This finding was based on the statements and testimony of five of Uske's co-workers regarding the background appearing in the photos, which constitutes substantial evidence. When the agency has proved that the charged conduct has occurred, the agency may rely on recanted admissions along with other evidence to support the other aspects of its charge. All elements of the agency's charge do not have to be proved by evidence that is independent of recanted admissions. Uske's statements to his co-workers and to the postal inspectors, even though recanted or denied by Uske, together with the other evidence constitute substantial evidence to support the agency's charges.

See Valerius-Simonds v. DOJ, PH-0752-12-0155-I-1 (NP 7/3/2013) ("In Uske, the court found that, '[i]f the only proof the agency brings forth are later recanted statements by the employee, an adverse action cannot be justified.' 56 F.3d at 1378 (citing Wohlwend v. Department of Health & Human Services, 16 M.S.P.R. 458, 461 (1983), and Roberts v. Department of the Treasury, 8 M.S.P.R. 674, 679 (1981)). However, '[W]hen the agency has proved that the charged conduct has occurred, the agency may rely on recanted admissions along with other evidence to support the other aspects of its charge.' Uske, 56 F.3d at 1378."); Ignacio v. Dept. of Air Force, 12 MSPR 648, 650 (1982) (as to statements from witnesses who testified at the hearing and retracted or contradicted prior statements, these "signed statements that resulted from the agency investigation do not have sufficient reliability...to meet the preponderance of the evidence standard"); cf. SSA v. Long, 113 MSPR 190, 206-07 ¶ 36-39, 2010 MSPB 19 (2010) (the Board evaluated various factors to determine whether a statement, later recanted by a witness, should have been credited by the AJ).

The Board will not readily credit repudiations submitted with a PFR that attempt to void earlier sworn hearing testimony. "We find...live testimony in an adversarial context, subject to cross-examination, to be inherently more credible than [a] subsequent affidavit repudiating...former testimony, without a reasonable explanation for doing so." Kuwada v. Dept. of Navy, 12 MSPR 9, 11 (1982) (no evidence of coercion or other reasonable explanation for repudiation).
