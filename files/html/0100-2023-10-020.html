a. Postponement Denied

If an appellant requests a hearing, then asks for a continuance that is properly denied, his failure to attend the hearing waives it. Lyons v. Dept. of Air Force, 14 MSPR 448, 450 (1983). The Board's regulations provide that AJs may dismiss an action when a party fails to prosecute an appeal, if the sanction is necessary to serve the interests of justice. But "where a party has only failed to appear at a scheduled hearing...the Board has held that the presiding official may proceed to adjudicate the case on the record." Nicholson v. DHHS, 14 MSPR 513, 515 (1983); cf. Rutila v. Dept. of Transp., 801 Fed. Appx. 745, 750 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) ("Mr. Rutila argues that he 'felt coerced into agreeing' to waive his hearing during the telephone conference with the AJ because, absent a postponement, he 'could not reasonably prepare for a hearing' that was to take place two days after the conference, and had 'not even received a decision on his motion for a subpoena or motion to compel discovery' until the telephone conference. Mr. Rutila failed to preserve his objection to the denial of his motion to postpone by foregoing his right to a hearing.").

In Clark v. USPS, 29 MSPR 315, 316 (1985), the AJ properly adjudicated the case on the record after the appellant failed to appear at a hearing. The appellant apparently relied on the agency's motion for a postponement of the hearing:

On July 31, the agency filed a motion requesting postponement of the hearing until the week of August 26, 1985. By Order dated August 5, 1985, the presiding official rescheduled the hearing for August 23.

In his petition for review, the appellant asserts that he did not appear at the hearing because he did not know that his hearing was scheduled for August 23 and "the last letter (he) had in (his) possession was a letter which mentioned a postponement to the 26th of August." The letter referred to by the appellant is the agency's motion for postponement.

There is no evidence that the appellant did not receive the Board's order rescheduling the hearing for August 23. Even assuming that the appellant did not receive his copy of the order rescheduling the hearing, it would not be reasonable for the appellant to assume that the agency's motion requesting a postponement would be granted. Furthermore, the agency's motion requested a postponement until the week of August 26 and did not indicate the exact date and time of the hearing.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant has not shown that his absence from the hearing was justifiable. We also find that the presiding official properly decided the case on the basis of the evidence in the record.

Once the appellant is notified of the hearing date and informed that motions for postponements of the hearing must be accompanied by an affidavit, the AJ should issue a decision based on the record after the appellant fails to appear for the hearing. Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984), post; cf. Jeffcoat v. USPS, 22 MSPR 593, 595 (1984) ("We find that remand is unnecessary in this case, as the Board's hearing notice, in addition to setting forth the time and place of the hearing, advised appellant that the case could be adjudicated on the record if he failed to appear for the hearing. Under the circumstances, we find that appellant has waived his right to a hearing and is not entitled to submit any supplementing evidence."); Newby v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 424, 425, 3 MSPB 498 (1980) (AJ properly decided case on the basis of the record when the appellant failed to file a request for a continuance of the hearing and failed to notify the field office of his inability to appear at the hearing).
