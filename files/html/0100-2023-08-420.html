a. Discretionary Review; Likelihood of Success

Board regulations at 5 CFR Part 1203 provide for discretionary acceptance of regulation review and regulation implementation requests. Factors guiding the exercise of that discretion were summarized in Early v. OPM, CB-1205-17-0011-U-1 (NP 4/4/2023), involving a challenge to limitations on veterans preference laws to excepted service hiring of attorneys:

...To guide us in deciding whether to exercise our discretion, we consider, among other things, the likelihood that the issue will be timely reached through ordinary channels of appeal, the availability of other equivalent remedies, the extent of the regulation's application, and the strength of the arguments against the validity of its implementation. McDiarmid v. U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, 19 M.S.P.R. 347, 349 (1984)....

To confer jurisdiction, there must be an initial showing of the validity of the request for review. If the review request appears on its face to lack merit, the Board will deny the request for lack of a reasonable likelihood that a prohibited personnel practice has been committed. See Brown v. OPM, 91 MSPR 314, 317 ¶ 6 (2002) (implementation review petition seeking to compel one facility of an agency to screen applications, rather than selectees, against OPM qualification standards: the petitioner's position was "not very strong"; agency policy had "very limited application"); Padilla v. OPM, 83 MSPR 12, 13-14 ¶ 2 (1999) (noting issues concurrently raised in retirement appeals and implementation proceedings by the same parties or their "privies" and barring their consideration through res judicata: "[t]o the extent that petitioners' claims of invalid implementation of 5 C.F.R. § 831.201(a)(13) in this proceeding are distinguishable from petitioners' claims made in the prior retirement appeals and regulation review proceedings, the instant claims could have been raised in the earlier proceedings"); Enrique v. OPM, 82 MSPR 305, 307 ¶ 4 (1999) (court decision affirming exclusion from CSRS retirement coverage of nonpermanent appointments barred a regulation review proceeding on the same issue); Amarille v. OPM, 76 MSPR 182, 183-84 (1997) (same challenge was barred from a second appeal by res judicata); Amarille v. OPM, 68 MSPR 424, 426 (1995), aff'd, 78 F.3d 605 (Fed. Cir.) (Table), cert. denied, 116 S. Ct. 2534 (1996) (challenge to the exclusion from retirement coverage of employees with indefinite appointments was not reviewed by the Board because the exclusion was found valid in a decision of the Federal Circuit), followed, Arede v. OPM, 83 MSPR 516, 518 ¶¶ 5-6 (1999) (no basis to review a challenge to OPM regulations excluding indefinite appointees from CSRS annuity coverage); In re Implementation of 5 CFR Part 771, 27 MSPR 379, 380 (1995) (on challenge to an agency's processing of an administrative grievance); In re Invalidation of 5 CFR 752.404(d)(3), 13 MSPR 621, 623 (1982) (review will not be undertaken if there is little likelihood that the issue will succeed on its merits); cf. Ward v. OPM, 79 MSPR 623, 626 (1998) (the Board declined to consider a challenge to OPM Bulletin 40--permitting agencies to combine merit promotions with OPM competitive procedures: "The weakness of petitioner's argument persuades us to decline his request for a review of the agency's implementation of OPM Bulletin 40."), aff'd in part and dismissed in part, Ward v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3044); Berromilla v. OPM, 76 MSPR 226, 228-29 (1997) (rejecting a petition for weakness of its arguments; the appellant's position was rejected in court litigation by another party on the same issue); Alver v. OPM, 75 MSPR 465, 470 (1997) (Civil Service Commission retirement coverage exclusion of indefinite appointees was not shown to require the commission of a prohibited personnel practice); Pollard v. OPM, 52 MSPR 566, 570 (1992) (challenge to the application of regulations governing leave for military reservists: the impact complained of as a prohibited practice must be a "reasonably foreseeable result" of the rule that has been challenged); Larson v. OPM, 29 MSPR 317, 320 (1985) (after performing statutory analysis, the Board concluded that the appellant's claim that the Social Security coverage provisions of OPM regulations were invalid were not "strong enough to create a reasonable likelihood that a prohibited personnel practice has been committed").
