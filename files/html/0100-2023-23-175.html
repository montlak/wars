e. Intervening Adverse Action

"An agency is not precluded from taking personnel actions pending Board disposition of, or after Board reversal of, other appealable actions as long as a valid reason exists for the later action." Jones v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 183, 187 (1997). However, "the Board's enforcement authority of its final decisions reversing agency actions continues even in an appeal from a separate disciplinary action." Holtgrewe v. FDIC, 62 MSPR 14, 17 (1994). Noble v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 524, 528 (1995), noted in response to an appellant's claim, submitted at the Board level during enforcement proceedings, of reprisal against him for exercising his appeal rights:

The appellant's claim that the agency proposed his removal immediately upon the rescission of the reversed removal in reprisal for his appeal is also properly considered in these compliance proceedings. Although the agency's proposed removal of the appellant upon his return to duty is not a separate appealable action to the Board, the Board can examine whether an action was taken in reprisal for exercising appeal rights even if the agency action would otherwise be beyond the Board's jurisdiction. 5 USC § 7512; Anderson v. Department of Agriculture, 21 MSPR 468, 470-71 (1984), aff'd, 790 F.2d 93 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 851 (1986). However, this does not mean that the agency may not institute a subsequent personnel action after a Board reverses the agency's decision as long as the action is taken for legitimate reasons. Mascarenas v. Department of Defense, 57 MSPR 425, 430 (1993).

Since the agency proposed the appellant's removal while the petition for enforcement was pending at the Board, this action was not addressed by the administrative judge. Agency actions occurring after an administrative judge has issued an initial compliance decision, which call into question whether the agency is in compliance with a final order of the Board, are properly considered in the first instance by the administrative judge and not the Board. James v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 504, 509 (1994); Mascarenas at 433. Therefore this case is being remanded only for the administrative judge to consider the appellant's newly raised allegation of reprisal based on the agency's action taken subsequent to the issuance of the administrative judge's recommended decision.

Under Hicks v. Dept. of Navy, AT-0752-13-7338-X-1 (NP 5/17/2022), an enforcement proceeding:

Events subsequent to the compliance initial decision have rendered the appellant ineligible to occupy his Engineer Technician position, and the agency was entitled to effectuate a second removal action based on the appellant's inability to meet the requirements of that position. The appellant does not dispute that the agency has provided him with the back pay and benefits to which he was entitled pursuant to the Board's final order, and that the agency placed him on paid administrative leave through the effective date of his second removal action. Accordingly, there is no further relief that the Board can provide in the context of his claims in these compliance proceedings, and we must dismiss the appellant's petition for enforcement as moot. See Allen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 111, ¶ 3 (2008) (recognizing that a petition for enforcement is moot when there is no effective remedy that the Board can provide); Adair v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 431, 435 (1996) (concluding that a matter is moot when the Board can grant no meaningful or effective relief). Any relief that the appellant may be entitled to following the effective date of his second removal will be resolved in his separate Board appeal challenging that removal, once that appeal has been refiled. See Strope v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 539, 542 n.1 (1997) (finding that an appellant's right to relief following a second removal action would be resolved in a separate Board appeal challenging that second removal, rather than in a compliance matter regarding an order reversing the appellant's first removal).

Legitimate agency actions taken during the pendency of an appeal by a former incumbent may be taken into account in determining compliance. An employee who has been successful in an appeal is not insulated from bona fide agency actions. Grant v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3294); Strope v. USPS, 76 MSPR 539, 542 (1997) (agency did not fail to comply with order reversing action on procedural grounds when it renewed its adverse action and followed proper procedures). On reinstatement following a successful Board appeal, an appellant may be required to undergo drug testing or rehabilitation to meet the requirements for a sensitive position. If the appellant fails to comply, he may be removed for failing to follow the steps necessary to hold the position. See Bradstreet v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3108).

During enforcement proceedings, if the agency again finds reason to take adverse action against the employee, the employee may contend that the adverse action is a violation of the agency's compliance responsibilities. From the perspective of the appellant, it is probably most straightforward and least risky procedurally to timely and separately appeal the later adverse action and let the AJ assigned to the appeal determine whether or not to join the new appeal with an enforcement proceeding, or to determine how else to treat the new appeal. See Moore v. USPS, 83 MSPR 533, 537 ¶ 6 (1999) ("Board's enforcement authority of its final decisions reversing agency actions continues even in an appeal from a separate disciplinary action"; Board joined, sua sponte, enforcement action and appeal from removal that followed reinstatement); Lucas v. DOD, 64 MSPR 172, 177 n.3 (1994) ("[a]n appeal of a second removal action based on the appellant's failure to report for work to a position other than the one he had held prior to the first removal action that the Board reversed, should either be joined with the pending enforcement case, or, in the alternative, dismissed without prejudice until the enforcement proceeding is resolved."); cf. Bullock v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 361, 367 ¶ 7 (1998) (treating as an enforcement problem a back pay claim for a period of absence during an improper detail following restoration).

Senior v. USPS, 85 MSPR 283, 287 (2000), summarized the procedural nuances of an adverse action that intervenes during enforcement proceedings:

When an agency removes an employee during the pendency of a compliance proceeding involving that employee, and the removal case involves a controlling issue that will be determined in the compliance proceeding, the Board usually joins the two cases, or, in the alternative, dismisses the appeal of the second removal without prejudice until the enforcement proceeding is resolved. See Lucas v. Department of Defense, 64 MSPR 172, 177 (1994), overruled in part on other grounds, Stabile v. Defense Commissary Agency, 76 M.S.P.R. 658 (1997); Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 62 MSPR 14, 17 (1994), amended on other grounds, 63 MSPR 291 (1994). Accordingly, we join the two cases, because both involve the issue of the appellant's ability to perform the duties of her position.

If a second adverse action was appealed and sustained, an earlier compliance issue pertaining to the nature of duties assigned to the appellant upon reinstatement may be moot. See Lunkin v. USPS, 27 MSPR 289, 292 (1985). Gannon v. USPS, 67 MSPR 660, 664-65 (1995), elaborated:

The agency argues that the issue of the appellant's return to duty is now moot because the appellant resigned. The appellant argues that his resignation was involuntary because he resigned rather than submit to a fitness-for-duty examination ordered by the agency contrary to the settlement agreement.

The settlement agreement between the appellant and the agency settled the restoration to duty issue by providing that the appellant was to be restored to duty effective April 9, 1994, and paid for his duty from that date to April 23. The agreement relegated the issue of the appellant's restoration to duty beyond April 23 to the appellant's April 25 appeal challenging his placement on enforced leave, which is a separate action. Therefore, the appellant's claim that he has not been restored to duty, the agency's contention that the restoration to duty issue is moot, and the appellant's counterclaim of constructive removal should all be considered by the regional office separately, as a petition for enforcement of the August 23, 1994, initial decision reversing the agency action of placing the appellant on enforced leave status on April 25, 1994 (MSPB Docket No. PH-0752-94-0389-I-1).[2]

[2] This case is distinguishable from Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 62 MSPR 14, 17, amended on other grounds, 63 MSPR 291 (1994), in which the Board held that when, during the pendency of a petition for enforcement of a Board order reversing a removal action, the agency takes a second removal action based on the employee's failure to report for work to a position other than the one he held prior to the first removal action, the appeal of the second removal action should be joined with the pending enforcement case or dismissed without prejudice until the enforcement proceeding is resolved. The instant petition for enforcement was not pending at the time of the placement on enforced leave that was the subject of the appellant's April 25, 1994, appeal.
