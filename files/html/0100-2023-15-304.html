f. Lack of Improvement Period

If work errors cannot be corrected through an improvement period without unreasonable cost or risk to the health or safety of others, even a few work errors permit removal. Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 51-52 (1987) (medical technologist), aff'd, Fairall v. VA, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987) [discussed in Chapter 9, "Lovshin; Combined Action Under Chapters 75 and 43"]. On review, the Federal Circuit concluded in Fairall that the prior notification of deficient performance necessary to a Chapter 43 removal is conspicuously and purposely absent from Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826 (Fed. Cir. 1985), permitting agencies to use either Chapter 75 or Chapter 43 procedures to take action against poor performers. The point was made in Mealy v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 187, 191 (1987), involving discipline of a third-level supervisor for failing to ensure proper safety precautions were maintained during an asbestos rip-out:

An agency is not required to provide a performance improvement period in a Chapter 75 performance-based action.... The lack of an improvement period, however, is relevant to the mitigating factor identified in Douglas...that addresses the extent to which the employee was on notice that his or her performance was deficient. Fairall. But, where the employee's position description or other evidence gives sufficient notice of the kind of performance that is considered deficient, the lack of an improvement period is not a sufficient basis for mitigating the penalty....

In this case, the appellant contends that he had no warning concerning asbestos precautions and no responsibility to instruct workers concerning safety. The administrative judge...found that the appellant had seen a copy of the rip-out job order alerting him of the health hazard. The administrative judge also found that even if the special instructions that provided the details for handling asbestos were not attached to the job order, the appellant could have located them. She found that the appellant was responsible for explaining work requirements to subordinate supervisors. The appellant's position description indicates that his duty was to assign and explain work requirements to subordinate supervisors and to promote and administer the Branch safety program. In light of these findings, it is clear that the appellant had sufficient notice of the kind of performance that was considered deficient. Accordingly, the lack of an improvement period provides no basis for further mitigating the penalty in this case.

The Board again reviewed whether improvement periods are required in Chapter 75 actions in Gende v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 518, 524-25 (1987). The appellant was demoted under Chapter 75 from WS-8 Electronic Foreman to GS-7 Correctional Officer for careless workmanship and failure to follow the agency electrical policy and the National Electrical Code, deficiencies that allegedly resulted in a $4,000 loss to the agency. Applying Lovshin, the Board decided that the agency was free to use either Chapter 43 or Chapter 75 procedures, but that the agency could not circumvent Chapter 43 by charging that an employee should have performed better than he was required to perform under standards established under Chapter 43. The Board noted that under Fairall, supra, no improvement opportunity period is required in a Chapter 75 action, although the lack of this opportunity could be considered a relevant factor in assessing the reasonableness of the penalty. If an appellant challenges an action because he received no opportunity period, the agency could rebut the challenge by showing that (1) the appellant's performance deficiencies were willful or intentional, or (2) providing an improvement period would have resulted in unreasonable costs or placed the safety and health of others at risk. As to notice of deficiencies in his work, the appellant knew or should have known he was required to follow the National Electrical Code and that his failure to follow the Code could have dangerous results associated with unsafe electrical systems and inoperable security devices at the prison where the appellant worked. An improvement period would have placed the health and safety of inmates and employees at risk; the agency had rebutted the challenge to the penalty. The procedural history of Gende is that an earlier decision, Gende v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 604, 619 (1984), determined that the action should have been handled under Chapter 43. The case was remanded for the agency to determine whether to pursue the action under Chapter 43. The action was affirmed by the AJ and again appealed by the appellant. Meanwhile, the Federal Circuit issued Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826 (Fed. Cir. 1985), resolving that Chapter 75 remained available for performance-based actions, irrespective of the availability of Chapter 43 procedures.

Under Fairall, supra, although lack of an improvement period may be a mitigating factor in some cases involving employees not on notice of poor performance, if an appellant is removed for failure to succeed during a training program, "a performance improvement period is not relevant to a consideration of the propriety of the penalty...because the developmental program which the appellant failed is, by its very nature, a continuous opportunity to improve performance." Pawlak v. FAA, 40 MSPR 546, 554 (1989) (leaving open whether an "up and out policy" may be subject to mitigation through the application of an agency reassignment policy); Billings v. Dept. of Transp., 36 MSPR 421, 424 (1988) (vacating 25 MSPR 201, 204 (1984) (placing employee under Chapter 43 removal action for the failure to complete a training program)), followed in Cortright v. Dept. of Transp., 37 MSPR 565, 571-72 (1988) (an air traffic control developmental position).

Of the lack of a performance improvement period in a Chapter 75 case, Solomon v. VA, AT-0752-10-0370-I-1 (NP 5/27/2011), noted:

The appellant additionally argues on review that his demotion is improper because he was never afforded an opportunity to improve his performance. The appellant, however, is not entitled to a performance improvement period under a chapter 75 performance-based action. Pawlak v. Department of Transportation, 40 M.S.P.R. 546, 552 (1989). Further, although the appellant was not afforded a performance improvement period, he nonetheless was aware of what kind of performance would be considered deficient through the results of the 2007 inspection of the Police and Security Service. Indeed, the 2007 inspection results identified similar deficiencies that management instructed him to correct. Therefore, the lack of a performance improvement period does not warrant mitigation of the demotion penalty. See Pawlak, 40 M.S.P.R. at 552.
