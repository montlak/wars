k. Repeated Misconduct; Last Chance Agreement

Repeated misconduct suggests lack of rehabilitative potential. Guzman-Muelling v. SSA, 91 MSPR 601, 607 ¶ 16 (2002), provides an example of the Board's analysis of prior discipline:

Moreover, both the 3-day and the 10-day suspensions, imposed for some of the same misconduct as that presently charged, put the appellant on notice that further misconduct could result in the issuance of even more severe disciplinary action. As the AJ found, the appellant's continued misconduct of a serious and intentional nature demonstrates that she has thus far failed to exhibit positive rehabilitative potential, and suggests that a lesser disciplinary penalty than that imposed would be unlikely to deter future misconduct.

In Isom v. DLA, 22 MSPR 79, 82 (1984), the failure of an appellant to correct a sick leave abuse problem, despite counseling and the issuance of a leave restriction letter, cast doubt on the appellant's potential for rehabilitation. Given repeated misconduct, the agency's evaluation of the potential for rehabilitation warranted deference by the Board. Goulet v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 492, 495 (1988); see Grubb v. Dept. of Interior, 96 MSPR 377, 397 ¶ 49 (2004) (several suspensions within six months: "[t]his prior record has not impressed upon the appellant the need to behave with proper professional decorum"); Wooten v. OPM, 87 MSPR 680, 687 ¶ 12 (2001) ("[t]he record of the warning memoranda and counselings not only reflects that the appellant was clearly on notice that this behavior was inappropriate, but it also demonstrates a very poor potential for rehabilitation."); Fuqua v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 173, 178 (1986) (past record of warnings, counselings, and disciplinary actions); Skram v. USPS, 12 MSPR 597, 600 (1982) (the AJ erred by substituting her judgment for that of the agency as to whether further efforts at rehabilitation would be fruitful).

An appellant with a history of failing to follow instructions and of being unable to accept counseling showed little potential for rehabilitation. Sherwin v. Dept. of Air Force, 44 MSPR 144, 152 (1990); Eirstedt v. Dept. of Navy, 13 MSPR 603, 606 (1982) (agency attempted to assist the appellant with performance and behavioral problems by referring him for counseling in lieu of discipline; the appellant ceased participation in the program and continued to demonstrate the same problems); see Lynch v. Dept. of Treasury, 9 MSPR 597, 599 (1982) (prior reprimand concerning the failure to respond to the direction of supervisors supported removal for current charges that included failing to respond readily to the direction of the appellant's supervisor).

Repeated conduct is placed in context. A series of errors in a single work product may be evaluated differently than the same or similar errors occurring over a period of time and after notice or warning of the deficiencies, Brown v. DHS, SF-0752-14-0816-I-1 (NP 1/19/2016):

We also have considered the agency's assertion that the administrative judge erred when she determined that the appellant did not engage in a deliberate pattern of improper conduct. As the agency notes, whether an offense was frequently repeated is an element to be considered in the Douglas factor that discusses the nature and seriousness of the offense. See Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305. The deciding official testified that he considered the seriousness of the submitting false reports charge, but he acknowledged on cross examination that many of the incidents of submitting false reports were contained within a single report. The record does not reflect, however, that he considered this fact in his Douglas factors analysis. Because the administrative judge properly noted that the only two specifications of the submitting false reports charge that she sustained were contained in the same report, we discern no error with her evaluation of this Douglas factor or her conclusion that the appellant did not engage in a deliberate pattern of misconduct.

If an appellant enters into and then violates a settlement or "last chance" agreement that essentially exchanges continued employment for a promise not to repeat certain misconduct, violation of that agreement will sway the Board against mitigation when disciplinary action is taken for a repetition of the conduct described by the agreement. See Hart v. USPS, 37 MSPR 633, 636 (1988); cf. Burrell v. DLA, 58 MSPR 372, 378-79 (1993) (sustaining a removal, and overturning mitigation of the removal, based on positive drug test during the completion of a drug treatment plan). An agency may offer a last chance arrangement and the appellant's rejection of the offer may be considered a sign of poor rehabilitative potential when adverse action is ultimately imposed. See Banks v. DLA, 29 MSPR 436, 439 (1985). A statement of continued indifference to agency policy suggests a lack of rehabilitative potential. Holland v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 592, 597 (1993), rev'd on other grounds, Holland v. Dept. of Air Force, 31 F.3d 1118 (Fed. Cir. 1994), made a point of discounting mitigation arguments by noting that "at the oral reply to the proposal notice, the appellant reasserted his inappropriate attitudes regarding the employment of women in his work unit."
