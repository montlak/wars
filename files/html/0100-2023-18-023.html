1. Due Process

The Board examines the conduct of agency investigations to ensure fundamental due process. An elastic concept, due process is somewhat constrained under Board interpretations. In Vann v. Dept. of Navy, 1 MSPR 487, 488-89, 1 MSPB 472 (1980), the employee charged that the investigation of his misconduct was improperly manipulated by agency investigators who attempted to clarify information concerning the incident resulting in the adverse action. The inference was that witnesses were improperly influenced. Although the Board noted that Camero v. United States, 375 F.2d 777 (Ct. Cl. 1967), requires fundamental due process in agency adjudicative proceedings, if all persons with relevant knowledge are available for examination at the adverse action hearing, improper investigative techniques affect the credibility of the agency case, not the right of the agency to take the adverse action. The failure of an agency to provide an employee with an opportunity to rebut evidence developed during an internal investigation does not constitute harmful error. Harper v. USPS, 7 MSPR 264, 267, 7 MSPB 165 (1981). Nor under Harper is the agency's subsequent submission of that evidence at a Board hearing a denial of due process. [Refer to Chapter 4, "Exclusion of Evidence."] Ruffin v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 499, 504 (1987), decided that an agency has no legal duty to interview an appellant before issuing a proposed removal. Its failure to do so was not reason to mitigate a penalty. The agency's failure to maintain notes used in an investigation, as required by agency regulations, did not warrant reversal of an action when the appellant failed to show how the error likely affected the suspension decision. See Kimm v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 198 (1994). Under McCoy v. VA, AT-0752-15-0197-I-1 (NP 9/25/2015):

...The Board has held that an agency does not have a duty to investigate the appellant's misconduct before proposing his removal, but only to make reasonable inquiries into exonerating facts brought to its attention by an appellant before removing him. Uske v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 544, 550 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995). Therefore, the thoroughness or lack of thoroughness of the agency's investigation is not a proper basis for not sustaining the agency's charge. Id. Consequently, we find this allegation provides no basis to disturb the initial decision.

An agency will not ordinarily be faulted for investigating charges of misconduct by a means arguably different from the investigatory technique required by regulation or past practice. See Hatch v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 260, 264 (1989) (agency was not required to refer sexual harassment complaints to the EEO process rather than to separately investigate the problem and take disciplinary action based on the investigatory results); Cain v. Dept. of Army, 13 MSPR 252, 253 (1982) (agency could investigate alleged sexual harassment without going through the discrimination complaint procedure); cf. Clark v. DHS, DC-0752-13-0661-I-1 (NP 2/21/2023) (declining to extend due process protections to include "argument that the agency violated her right to due process by withholding certain favorable evidence during the removal process...that the agency withheld certain exculpatory information from both her and the deciding official," citing Yee v. Bureau of Prisons, 348 Fed. Appx. 1, 2 (5th Cir. 2009); Ashton v. Whitman, 94 Fed. Appx. 896, 900-02 (3rd Cir. 2004); Lee v. Hutson, 810 F.2d 1030, 1030-34 (11th Cir. 1987); National Labor Relations Board v. Nueva Engineering, Inc., 761 F.2d 961, 969 (4th Cir. 1985)).

As part of the reply process, some agency regulations provide for something more than an oral or written reply before the agency determines whether to take adverse action. One agency's regulations required that before disciplinary action could be taken, the supervisor make inquiry into the facts; however, when disciplinary action was taken without the investigation, the Board declined to reverse it. There was no demonstration that the inquiry would have affected the result, no proof of prejudice, and no harmful error. Osokow v. OPM, 25 MSPR 319, 325 (1984); see Gallmon v. GSA, 21 MSPR 643, 644-45 (1984) (agency's failure to properly convene a panel to consider charges against the appellants was not harmful error when a different result was only a possibility); cf. Davis v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 662, 663 (1983) (failure of the agency to consider evidence in support of sick leave prior to removal was not harmful error; considering the evidence, the Board concluded it was insufficient to support the requested leave).
