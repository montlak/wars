6. Grade and Pay Retention; Pay Rate Change; Demotion; Reduction in Hours

An action that is the basis for grade and pay retention benefits may be appealed only if it arose as a result of a RIF taken by the agency under 5 CFR Part 351 (2022). Warfield v. GSA, 3 MSPR 233, 234 n.1, 3 MSPB 328 (1980). The agency action involving grade and pay retention alone may not be presented to the Board on appeal in a RIF case. Mack v. Dept. of Labor, 6 MSPR 80, 81, 6 MSPB 78 (1981). However, an employee's entitlement to retained grade and pay has no effect on the employee's right to appeal a reduction in force action. Brown v. USPS, 58 MSPR 345, 350 (1993); 5 USC 5366(a)(2); see Sapp v. USPS, DC-3443-13-0644-I-1 (NP 12/27/2013) ("Even placement of an employee in an indefinite saved pay and saved grade status will require compliance with the RIF regulations if the new position is at a lower grade level. Robinson, 63 M.S.P.R. at 313-325. The relevant inquiry in determining whether an employee moved from one pay method category to another was demoted during a RIF is not the actual pay received by the appellant, but rather the representative rate of the new position as compared with the representative rate of the prior position. Smith v. Department of Air Force, 117 M.S.P.R. 488, ¶¶ 6-7 (2012) 5 C.F.R. § 210.102(b)(4)(ii)."; remanded to determine whether the position move was voluntary). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Grade and Pay Retention"; refer to separations of Title 38 VA medical professionals through RIFs in this chapter, "VA Health Care Professional"]. For discussion of the concept of "basic pay" as to a retirement claim, refer to Lisanti v. OPM, 573 F.3d 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2009).

Under 5 USC 5363 and 5 CFR 536.104(a)(2) (2022), an employee affected by a RIF is entitled to pay retention in his rate of basic pay, defined by 5 CFR 536.102 (2022) to be the rate fixed for the position and exclusive of additional pay differentials. The appellant, reassigned out of a dormitory counselor position that paid a premium for working extra time, had no right to that additional pay following the RIF and assignment to a new position. Etter v. DOD, 14 MSPR 367, 370 (1983). Should the appellant receive pay retention following a RIF, and should there be disputes substantially later in time over the amount of periodic salary increases, the Board declines jurisdiction over an appeal based on the salary dispute. The Board's "jurisdiction, based on reduction in pay, arises only when an ascertainable lowering, at the time of the personnel action, of an employee's present or future pay occurs." Chaney v. VA, 906 F.2d 697 (Fed. Cir. 1990).

A change in classification from GS-9 Architect, paid through a special salary rate, at $33,436, to a different and lower pay category to Engineer Technician, GS-10, nonspecial salary rate of $31,211, did not constitute a demotion for purposes of a RIF. The definition of "demotion" contemplates a lower rate of pay for positions in different pay method categories. See McGauley v. VA, 29 MSPR 686, 689 (1986). Within the Title 38 pay system for the VA, a demotion did not occur, and there was no appealable RIF, when a VA head nurse was reassigned to a position of staff nurse at a different locality but with no cut in her pay grade under the nursing pay plan. It was immaterial that the appellant's pay rate actually diminished because of the difference between the new position and its pay rate under the nursing pay plan. Glennon v. VA, 86 MSPR 340, 343-44 ¶¶ 10-11 (2000), followed, McClue v. VA, 86 MSPR 362, 364 ¶ 5 (2000); see Wadhwa v. MSPB, 296 Fed. Appx. 17 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (geographical reassignment did not constitute a RIF; loss of locality pay and a cash performance award did not affect base pay).

A reassignment of a dentist who alleged, as a result of the reassignment, an adjustment of a special pay rate under 38 USC 7431, did not constitute a RIF, McDonald v. VA, 86 MSPR 539, 543-44 ¶¶ 12-14 (2000):

A reassignment resulting from a RIF is not appealable to the Board; a demotion is. 5 CFR § 351.901. The definition of a demotion, as found at 5 CFR § 210.102(b)(4) requires that an employee be changed to (i) "a lower grade when both the old and the new positions are under...the same type graded wage schedule," or (ii) "a position with a lower rate of pay when both the old and the new positions are under the same type ungraded wage schedule." The AJ's finding implicitly assumed that the appellant's old and new positions are in an ungraded wage schedule. To the contrary, however, the record shows that he was reassigned from a position as VM-0680, Chief, Step/Rate 10, with an annual salary of $97,201, to another position as VM-0680, Chief, Step/Rate 10, with an annual salary of $97,201.

Pursuant to 38 USC § 7404, which establishes "[t]he grades for positions provided for in paragraph (1) of section 7401 of this title," there is a "Physician and Dentist Schedule," pursuant to which seven "grades" are set forth, including one for "Chief grade." 38 USC § 7404(b)(1). (A range of rates, established by Executive Order, for each grade, is set out at Schedule 3 appended to the section.) The law therefore shows, on its face, that the appellant occupied a "graded" position before and after his reassignment. Thus, that he is receiving "a lower rate of pay," 5 CFR § 210.102(b)(4)(ii), is irrelevant to the determination of whether an appealable RIF action was effected.

Rather, to show that he was demoted, the appellant must establish under subsection (b)(4)(i) that he was changed to a lower grade. This he has not done. The agency's evidence all indicates that he was simply reassigned with his grade and designation intact, and the appellant does not claim to the contrary, but relies instead on his loss of duties and the commensurate loss of the additional pay he received for doing them. Accordingly, as in Glennon, BN-0351-99-0130-I-1 ¶ 11, although his pay may have been reduced, he did not suffer a RIF demotion because he was not reduced in grade. He therefore cannot appeal to the Board.* See McGauley v. Veterans Administration, 29 MSPR 686, 689 (1986); cf. Di Pietro v. U.S. Postal Service, 58 MSPR 430, 434-35 (1993) (the appellant's assignment to a lower-graded position when both positions are in the same Postal Service pay method category constituted an appealable RIF demotion).

[*] Nor can the appellant appeal his reassignment as an adverse action (reduction in pay). His position is excluded from coverage under subchapter II of chapter 75 by 5 USC § 7511(b)(10) because it has been excluded from the competitive service by or under a provision of Title 38. See Pichon v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 67 MSPR 325, 327 (1995); Exum v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 62 MSPR 344, 346 (1994).

A RIF demotion occurs through an actual reduction in grade. An individual cannot claim a RIF demotion from a grade never held, even though the appellant may have performed the duties of another position or claims that she should have been placed in a higher grade. Marcheggiani v. DOD, 90 MSPR 212, 218 ¶ 11 (2001) (citing Brock v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 564, 566 (1991)). Martin v. DOD, SF-0351-16-0620-I-1 (NP 7/19/2022), determined there was no RIF jurisdiction over reduction to half-time of a school nurse:

The appellant, a school nurse at Daegu High School (DHS) in the Korea District of the agency's Department of Defense Education Activity (DoDEA), filed a board appeal alleging that the agency subjected her to a reduction-in-force (RIF) action when it eliminated her half-time teaching position, reducing her to a half-time school nurse. In its response to her appeal, the agency explained that the appellant occupied a mixed teacher/specialist position at DHS and that, based on anticipated enrollment for the following school year, its headquarters office eliminated the half-time English as a Second Language (ESL) position at DHS that the appellant occupied the previous school year. Consequently, the agency "excessed" the appellant from that position in keeping with the procedures set forth in the memorandum of understanding on that issue between DoDEA and the union.

...

To be entitled to a jurisdictional hearing over a RIF claim, the appellant must make a nonfrivolous allegation that she was subjected to an appealable RIF action in the form of a demotion, separation, or furlough for more than 30 days. See Harrell v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 492, ¶ 11 (2009); Wolf v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 87 M.S.P.R. 33, ¶ 7 (2000); 5 C.F.R. § 351.901. Here, the appellant claims that she was subjected to an appealable RIF action when the agency demoted her from a full-time to a part-time position. For the following reasons, we agree with the administrative judge's decision to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

...

A demotion is defined in pertinent part as a change of an employee, while serving continuously within the same agency, to a lower grade or to a position with a lower rate of pay. 5 C.F.R. § 210.102(b)(4). Although the appellant may have lost half of her hours as a consequence of excessing the ESL teacher position that she held, she has not alleged that the agency reduced her rate of pay or assigned her to a lower-graded position. Therefore, she has failed to nonfrivolously allege that she was demoted. See Wolf, 87 M.S.P.R. 33, ¶¶ 8-10 (finding that a "staff adjustment" resulting in a reduction of hours but not in a reduction of the rate of pay or grade was not a demotion). Accordingly, we find that the appellant has failed to nonfrivolously allege that she was subjected to an appealable RIF action. See id., ¶ 11
