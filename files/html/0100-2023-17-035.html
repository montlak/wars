E. RIF

Under 5 USC 7121(a)(1), the grievance procedure is the exclusive means of resolving matters within its coverage. Excepted from the statutory exclusivity are matters involving EEO allegations (§ 7121(d)), adverse or performance-based actions (§ 7121(e)), and other prohibited personnel practices (e.g., whistleblowing reprisal) (§ 7121(g)). The result of the statutory scheme is that there are some actions, most notably reductions in force and denials of step increases, that are within the exclusive domain of the contract grievance procedure, unless an allegation of prohibited discrimination is asserted as to that action, or unless a matter is excluded from the coverage of the contract. [Refer later in this chapter to "Particular Matters Excluded or encompassed by Contract."] The prohibited practice allegation then permits the employee to elect an available statutory appeals procedure.

The convoluted interplay of a contract, 5 USC 7101, and 5 USC 7121 in the context of a RIF case was explored in Bonner v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 202, 203-06 (Fed. Cir. 1986). The court offered analysis supporting the Board's result excluding the RIF appeal from MSPB jurisdiction and leaving the appellant to his remedy through the grievance procedure. The grievance procedure provided:

Art. XI; Sec. 1 (Grievances)

This Article provides a method for settlement of employee grievances. This procedure will be the only avenue available to employees for the processing and final disposition of grievances. The scope of this grievance procedure is only restricted as provided in Section 7121 of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978.

Art. XIII; Sec. 1 (RIF)

Reduction-in-force as used herein is defined as the Employer's action to reduce the number of occupied positions within the unit requiring the use of reduction-in-force procedures set forth in applicable regulations to implement such actions....

Sec. 2

All reductions-in-force will be executed in strict compliance with applicable laws and regulations.

Bonner explained, id. at 204-06:

This appeal presents two questions for our review. The first question is whether this RIF action is a "matter" within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. § 7121. The second question is whether this RIF action is excluded from the application of the negotiated grievance procedure.

...

...We hold that this RIF action is a "matter" within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. § 7121(a)(2). Just as the statute excludes from the negotiated grievance procedure the underlying action of "a suspension or removal under Section 7532," so the agreement may exclude from the negotiated grievance procedure the underlying action of a RIF. The significance of our holding is that if a collective bargaining agreement does exclude the underlying action of a RIF, then the RIF action is not a "matter" covered by a negotiated grievance procedure and the Board retains its customary appellate jurisdiction over an appeal from that RIF action.

In deciding whether the agreement here excluded RIFs from the negotiated grievance procedure, the Board applied an incorrect legal standard. The Board cited one of its own cases for the proposition that the collective bargaining agreement must "specifically" exclude a matter. Sirkin v. Dept. of Labor, 16 MSPR 432, 436 (1983). That gloss is no part of the statute, its legislative history, or the regulation. The correct standard is the standard contained in the statute, whether the collective bargaining agreement excludes a matter from the application of the negotiated grievance procedure.

...

...For the following reasons, we hold that upon applying the correct legal standard RIFs were not excluded from the negotiated grievance procedure....

First, regarding the course of dealing of the parties, there is evidence in the record indicating the agency's interpretation. By a form letter dated October 18, 1983, the agency notified Bonner of the RIF; that letter also contained information on the degree of some rights Bonner did or did not have [noting his right to file a grievance and not a Board appeal].... The letter is consistent with the view that one of the contracting parties, the agency, understood the contract not to exclude a RIF from the negotiated grievance procedure.

Second, Bonner disputes the agency's interpretation of the agreement. She argues that the agency's interpretation of the agreement renders the RIF article, Article XIII, meaningless. If correct, that might be a strong argument in Bonner's favor. Cf. W.M. Schlosser Co. v. United States, 767 F.2d 870, 874 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (rejecting an interpretation which renders contract language superfluous). However, we do not agree that it is correct. Initially, we note that the six sections of Article XIII grant to employees and to the union rights that are greater than the rights required by regulation. Further, even if Bonner's argument were understood to be restricted to the language of the first and second sections quoted above, we do not agree with it on the factual circumstances of this case. The first section refers to an employer's action and the regulatory procedures for implementing a RIF. Similarly, the second section requires strict compliance in executing a RIF. It is possible that the contracting parties were addressing actions the employer must take and were not addressing the employee's choice of forum for resolving a dispute about those actions. In sum, we are not persuaded that Article XIII excludes a RIF from the negotiated grievance procedure.

Parnell v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 128, 129-31 (1993), determined that an allegation of reprisal for filing an EEO complaint was sufficient to preserve MSPB jurisdiction, even though the RIF appeal would otherwise have been within the scope of the collective bargaining agreement's grievance procedure:

There is no dispute that the appellant is covered by a collective bargaining agreement that does not exclude RIF actions. As the administrative judge correctly stated below:

The Board's jurisdiction extends to employees demoted in a reduction in force, but this jurisdiction is limited for those employees, such as the appellant, [who are] covered by a collective bargaining agreement that does not exclude reduction-in-force actions from coverage. If the employee is covered by such a collective bargaining agreement, the grievance procedures contained in the agreement are the exclusive procedures for resolving the appeal unless the employee alleges discrimination prohibited by 5 USCA 2302(b)(1) (West 1980). See 5 CFR 1201.3(c)(1) (1992).

Id. at 2.

In her petition for appeal, the appellant alleged that the agency's RIF action was based on reprisal for her having filed formal complaints of discrimination with the agency. The administrative judge found that the appellant's claim of reprisal did not rest upon any of the bases stated in 5 USC 2302(b)(1), and that, since her appeal did not fall within the exception provided by 5 CFR 1201.3(c)(1), her avenue of appeal was solely within the negotiated grievance procedure. She therefore dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

As the administrative judge noted, the plain language of 5 USC 2302(b)(1) refers to prohibited discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, handicapping condition, marital status, and political affiliation; reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint is not included in that list. The administrative judge cited Patterson v. Department of the Air Force, 39 MSPR 413, 415-16, aff'd, 878 F.2d 1446 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table), and Poirier v. Department of the Navy, 31 MSPR 214 (1986), in support of her finding that, since a claim of reprisal is not included in the above list, the appellant's right of appeal was limited to the negotiated grievance procedure. We note, however, that the appellants in those cases did not allege reprisal for having filed a discrimination complaint. We therefore find that those decisions are not controlling.

Some of the court's language in Spruill v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 978 F.2d 679, 691-92 (Fed. Cir. 1992), suggests that reprisal taken against an individual because of the filing of a discrimination complaint is limited to a violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(9), so that one could argue that it could not also be violative of section 2302(b)(1). The court's comments must be taken in context, however. The issue before the court in Spruill was whether reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint constituted whistleblowing under section 2302(b)(8). 978 F.2d at 689. Thus, we view the court's comments as leading to the conclusion that there is no overlap between sections 2302(b)(8) and 2302(b)(9).

This reading of Spruill is reinforced by the court's reference to In re Frazier, I MSPB 159, 1 MSPR 163 (1979), aff'd sub nom. Frazier v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 672 F.2d 150 (D.C. Cir. 1982). 978 F.2d at 691. The court approved the distinction the Board drew in Frazier between sections 2302(b)(8) and 2302(b)(9), without mentioning or otherwise disapproving the lengthy footnote in Frazier, stating that a claim of reprisal for EEO activities sets forth a cognizable claim under section 2302(b)(1)(A), as well as under section 2302(b)(9). Frazier, 1 MSPR at 191 n.36. Further, the court noted in Spruill at 682 that the administrative judge in that case relied on Williams v. Department of Defense, 46 MSPR 549 (1991), without mentioning or otherwise disapproving the footnote in Williams reiterating the Board's position that reprisal for filing an EEO complaint violates both sections 2302(b)(1) and 2302(b)(9). Williams, 46 MSPR at 553 n.4.

We find the reasoning in Frazier to be instructive. Frazier noted that 42 USC 2000e-16, referenced in section 2302(b)(1), has been interpreted by the courts to include claims of reprisal for opposition to employment practices made unlawful by Title VII. 1 MSPR at 191 n.36. See also Jordan v. Clark, 847 F.2d 1368, 1376 n.8 (9th Cir. 1988), cert. denied, 488 U.S. 1006, 109 S. Ct. 786, 102 L. Ed.2d 778 (1989). We note, in addition, that the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, the agency responsible for the administration and enforcement of equal opportunity in Federal employment, includes claims of reprisal for filing discrimination complaints as within the ambit of 42 USC 2000e-16 in its regulations. See 29 CFR 1614.101 and 1614.103(a); see also Spruill, 978 F.2d at 692 n.17.

We therefore conclude that a claim of reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint is cognizable under 5 USC 2302(b)(1). Because the appellant raised such a claim in connection with her RIF appeal, her appeal falls within the Board's jurisdiction under 5 USC 7121(d).

FLRA affirmed an arbitrator's award that dismissed a union challenge to RIF competitive levels. FLRA ruled that the agreement excluded any and all matters subject to a statutory appeals procedure, one matter being the competitive level of a RIF that could be raised by an employee before the MSPB. DHUD and AFGE, 12 FLRA 41, 41-42 (1983). In SBA and AFGE Local 2532, 32 FLRA 311, 315 (1988), an exception based on an election argument was not sustained: although the employee sought initial review of a RIF with the MSPB, the election was ineffective because the negotiated agreement covered RIF appeals and divested the Board of jurisdiction.
