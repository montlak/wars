d. Social Security Benefit--FERS

FERS is linked to Social Security benefits. The determination of the amount of the FERS annuity benefit, the reduction for Social Security entitlements, the "high-three" calculation of the basis for the annuity, and the Board's dependence on the accuracy of agency pay records, particularly the Individual Retirement Record (IRR), were explored in Maxwell v. OPM, 78 MSPR 350, 352, 356-59 (1998):

The appellant's application for disability retirement under the Federal Employees' Retirement System (FERS) was approved effective February 10, 1990, and, on May 15, 1990, the appellant retired from her position with the U.S. Postal Service (USPS). The appellant received a FERS annuity benefit for one month until her FERS annuity payments were suspended on March 10, 1990, when she elected to receive benefits from the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP) in lieu of a FERS annuity. The appellant continued to receive OWCP benefits until she was declared recovered by the Department of Labor, and her OWCP benefits were consequently terminated effective February 15, 1994. On February 16, 1994, the appellant's FERS annuity payments resumed. In the meantime, on September 23, 1994, the appellant was found entitled to social security disability benefits retroactive to August 1, 1991.

In May 1996, OPM learned of the appellant's entitlement to social security benefits and, on June 30, 1996, reduced the appellant's FERS benefit by the amount of her social security benefit. OPM then sought to collect an alleged overpayment of $9,078 that occurred during the period of February 16, 1994, and June 30, 1996, when OPM should have, but did not, adjust the appellant's FERS retirement annuity to offset her receipt of social security benefits. The appellant requested reconsideration of the existence or amount of the debt and waiver of the collection of the overpayment, claiming that she was without fault because she had promptly notified OPM when she became entitled to social security benefits, and that repayment would cause her financial hardship.

With some exceptions, e.g., the social security offset, an annuitant entitled to FERS disability benefits receives, during the first 12 months after the FERS annuity commences, an annuity equal to 60% of her "average pay"; thereafter she receives an annuity equal to 40% of her "average pay." 5 U.S.C. § 8452(a)(1)(A); 5 C.F.R. § 844.302(c)(1); see also 5 U.S.C. § 8455(b)(2),(3); 5 C.F.R. § 844.404. The term "average pay" means the largest annual rate resulting from averaging the individual's rates of "basic pay" in effect over any 3 consecutive years. 5 U.S.C. §§ 8401(3), 8331(4). For FERS retirement matters, "basic pay" is the pay an employee receives that is subject to deductions under FERS. 5 C.F.R. § 844.102. However, bonuses, allowances, overtime pay, military pay, and pay given in addition to base pay are specifically excluded from "basic pay." 5 U.S.C. § 8331(3); see 5 U.S.C. § 8401(4).

OPM requires that an employing agency, which has employees or Members subject to FERS, maintain a record of an individual's retirement deductions in the individual's IRR. 5 C.F.R. §§ 841.106(a), .602. This IRR is the "basic record for action on all claims for annuity or refund." 5 C.F.R. § 841.106(b)(1); see also 5 C.F.R. §§ 841.106(a), .602. An employing agency must record the appropriate amount of employee deductions on the employee's IRR and, when the employee separates, the agency must close the IRR and forward it to OPM. 5 C.F.R. § 841.504(c), (d). When an employing agency determines that it has paid less than the correct total amount of the normal cost for its current or past employees, for any reason whatsoever, including correction of the amount of "basic pay," the agency must withhold the appropriate employee deduction from any payment of additional "basic pay" which is part of, or the result of, the corrective action. 5 C.F.R. § 841.505(a),(b). Additionally, the employing agency must report all corrections, related agency payments, and employee deductions to OPM. 5 C.F.R. § 841.505(e).

Hence, it is the employing agency, not OPM, which determines an individual's "basic pay" by withholding FERS deductions from the individual's pay. We therefore find that the Board's review in such matters is limited to determining that OPM, in computing the amount of the individual's FERS retirement annuity, properly relied on the employing agency's certification or noncertification in the individual's IRR of the individual's "basic pay."

As OPM notes, this finding is consistent with previous decisions where the Board has held that it lacks jurisdiction to review agency certifications regarding retirement matters. See Bacani v. Office of Personnel Management, 64 M.S.P.R. 588, 592-94 (1994) (neither OPM nor the Board could consider the annuitant's claim that the IRR maintained and certified by his employing agency, the Department of Veterans Affairs (DVA), erroneously excluded special pay from the computation of his civil service annuity benefits because the IRR maintained by an employing agency is the basic record for action on all claims for annuity or refund and, under 38 U.S.C. § 7422(d), compensation determinations by the DVA cannot be reviewed by any other agency), appeal dismissed, 56 F.3d 79 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); Carmack v. Office of Personnel Management, 47 M.S.P.R. 397, 401 (1991) (the AJ properly found that the SSA's certification regarding the commencement of the annuitant's social security benefits was sufficient to determine that OPM's determination of the overpayment amount was correct), aff'd in part and vacated in part on other grounds, 956 F.2d 1173 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); Werley v. Office of Personnel Management, 39 M.S.P.R. 686, 691-92 (1989) (in a retirement appeal concerning whether certain contract service is creditable service for retirement purposes, the Board's review is limited to determining that, in making its decision, OPM properly relied upon the employing agency's certification or noncertification of the contract service claimed by the annuitant); Dickson v. Office of Personnel Management, 37 M.S.P.R. 465, 468-70 (1988) (OPM properly relied on the Department of Defense's certification regarding the cause of a disability in calculating the annuitant's civil service annuity because the Board lacks jurisdiction to review such certifications and also lacks jurisdiction to review an annuitant's entitlement to have his military service credited in calculating his civil service annuity).

Here, the appellant presented below a letter from USPS dated September 14, 1995, which she claims is material to the determination of her "basic pay." This letter indicated that, pursuant to a collective bargaining agreement or a "1990 compensation package for non-bargaining employees," USPS awarded the appellant a supplemental annuity benefit in the form of a retroactive lump-sum payment (hereinafter "retroactive USPS lump-sum payment") representing the difference between the total amount of the monthly annuity payments she had received from OPM from February 10, 1990, to January 1, 1995, and the amount she would have received from OPM for that same period had the USPS COLAs been included in her basic salary while she was employed by USPS Although the letter stated that this retroactive USPS lump-sum payment would "not affect the monthly annuity [the appellant is] now receiving from OPM," it also indicated that deductions for retirement contributions would be withheld from the retroactive USPS lump-sum payment. Because OPM did not include this retroactive USPS lump-sum payment in its determination of her "basic pay," the appellant argued below, OPM miscalculated her "average pay."

As discussed above, however, it is the employing agency, not OPM, which determines an individual's "basic pay," which it certifies in the individual's IRR forwarded to OPM. It is undisputed that USPS provided OPM with a certified "Final" IRR, dated May 17, 1990, that showed that USPS did not include any part of the retroactive USPS lump-sum payment in the appellant's "basic pay" for February 1987, through January 1990, the 3 calendar years used to compute the appellant's "average pay. There is no evidence that USPS subsequently amended or corrected this "Final" IRR to show either that FERS deductions were actually taken from this retroactive USPS lump-sum payment or that this retroactive USPS lump-sum payment was otherwise part of the appellant's "basic pay." In the absence of such a corrected or amended IRR, we find that OPM properly relied on USPS's certified "Final" IRR when it computed the amount of the appellant's FERS disability annuity. Because OPM has shown that it based its computation of the appellant's FERS disability retirement annuity on the "basic pay" certified by USPS on the appellant's "Final" IRR, we will not consider the appellant's claim that OPM improperly excluded the retroactive USPS lump-sum payment from her "basic pay" further.

Accordingly, we find that OPM properly did not include the appellant's retroactive USPS lump-sum payment in her "basic pay" when it computed the amount of her FERS disability annuity. Because, OPM showed, as discussed above, that, until July 1, 1996, it delayed in adjusting the appellant's FERS disability annuity payments to offset the amount of the social security benefit to which she was entitled from February 16, 1994, to June 30, 1996, OPM proved that the appellant received an overpayment of $9,078. We therefore find, contrary to the initial decision, that OPM satisfied its burden of proving the existence and the amount of the overpayment by preponderant evidence.

If the appellant still believes that the retroactive USPS lump-sum payment should be included in her "basic pay," she should petition USPS, to amend her IRR accordingly and to forward it to OPM for recalculation of her annuity payments and the amount of the overpayment. See generally 5 C.F.R. §§ 841.504(c),(d),(e); 505(a),(b),(e). In the event that USPS forwards such an amended IRR to OPM, the appellant must then obtain an OPM final decision concerning her request for reopening and reconsideration of OPM's overpayment decision and/or for adjustment of the amount of her annuity payments based on USPS's adjustment of her "basic pay," before the Board's jurisdiction attaches to hear an appeal of OPM's final decision on this matter. See 5 U.S.C. § 8461(d),(e).
