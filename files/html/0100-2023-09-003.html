1. Dates of Filing

Filing is the process of delivering documents to the Board (placing them into the adjudicative process). In order to expedite and to control its caseload, the Board is quite insistent that appeals and petitions for review be filed on time. Most types of case adjudicated by the Board have filing deadlines set by regulation or statute. Appeals must be timely filed if the Board is to adjudicate them. Many, many Board cases have been lost by appellants because their appeals were not filed on time. To determine when a document is filed, the Board's regulation explains, 5 CFR 1201.4(l) (2022):

Date of filing. A document that is filed with a Board office by personal delivery is considered filed on the date on which the Board office receives it. The date of filing by facsimile is the date of the facsimile. The date of filing by mail is determined by the postmark date; if no legible postmark date appears on the mailing, the submission is presumed to have been mailed five days (excluding days on which the Board is closed for business) before its receipt. The date of filing by commercial delivery is the date the document was delivered to the commercial delivery service. The date of filing by e-filing is the date of electronic submission.

The regulatory periods for filing an appeal, and for taking other actions, are calculated by the number of days permitted for a particular action. When Board regulations speak of a day or days, they speak of calendar days, not business or work days. 5 CFR 1201.4(h) (2022). But see Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578, 1583 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (noting ambiguity in the manner of calculating "days" in notice of appeal rights); Urban v. VA, CH-0752-10-0261-I-1 (NP 3/7/2011) (delay of a day in filing an appeal was excused, in part, because of incorrect calculation of the time for filing: "The appellant's explanation that she believed Federal holidays were excluded from the calculation of the timeframe for filing, while inaccurate, is not unreasonable. As such, it is grounds for finding good cause for her filing delay. See Walls, 29 F.3d at 1582 (appellant demonstrated due diligence and ordinary prudence in filing the appeal within the regulatory time period as construed by him); Fitzpatrick v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 318, 320-21 (1996) (pro se appellant's misconstruction of the filing period was a reasonable excuse for his 1-day delay)."); Munir v. OPM, 67 MSPR 328 (1995) (allowing flexibility when a pro se appellant is not on notice of the distinction between "calendar" and "working" days). 5 CFR 1201.23 (2022), post, defines the effect of intervening federal holidays and weekends.

Although the Board regulation speaks of the postmark on a mailed appeal as the filing date, there is a lot of law that has developed, discussed below, that essentially determines the actual date of mailing, rather than the postmark, as the date of filing. The postmark becomes a rebuttable presumption as to the filing date. Read the caselaw carefully to follow the specialized rules that have developed through evolving decisions to determine the actual filing date of an appeal, however submitted.
