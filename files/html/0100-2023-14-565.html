1. Representatives; Observers

Negotiable was a proposal permitting the union to have an observer present at a discrimination complaint hearing unless the employee requesting the hearing objected out of concern for privacy and the hearing examiner determined the objection to be valid, AFGE Local 2182 and Propulsion Lab., Army Research and Tech. Labs., 26 FLRA 600, 602 (1987):

Under EEOC regulations, agencies have the responsibility for establishing regulations governing the processing of complaints of discrimination. Agencies must ensure that their regulations comply with certain principles and requirements established by the EEOC. See 29 C.F.R. 1613.211 through 1613.222. One of these principles is that agencies control the powers vested in the complaints examiner who conducts the hearing on a complaint of discrimination.

We have previously addressed the authority of EEOC's procedural regulations relating to agencies' equal employment opportunity responsibilities. In U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, Kansas City District and National Federation of Federal Employees Local 29, 22 FLRA. No. 74 (1986), we held that EEOC procedural regulations serve only as guidelines to agencies. That is, they involve matters which are within an agency's discretion....

Provision 1 concerns who can attend a discrimination complaint hearing. In our view, like the issue in Corps of Engineers, Kansas City District, this is a matter which is covered by an EEOC procedural regulation and therefore is within the Agency's discretion. The Agency does not claim in this case that the provision: (1) does not involve conditions of employment, or (2) is otherwise inconsistent with applicable law or regulation. We find that no bases for such assertions are apparent. Consequently, the provision is within the duty to bargain.

Another proposal considered and approved for negotiation was that at any stage in the processing of an EEO complaint the employee shall have the right to be accompanied, represented, and advised by a representative of his or her choosing. The proposal reinforced the rights of bargaining unit employees to representation in the EEO complaint process. AFGE Nat'l INS Council and INS, 27 FLRA 467, 470 (1987) (Provisions 2 and 7). In AFGE Local 1770 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Bragg Dependent Sch., 28 FLRA 493, 496 (1987) (Provision 1, Section 8), a proposal was negotiable that allowed the union the right to representation at all formal discussions between the employer and employee concerning an EEO complaint. IRS, Fresno Serv. Ctr. v. FLRA, 706 F.2d 1019, 1023-25 (9th Cir. 1983), was distinguished. It involved representation at EEO precomplaint conciliation conferences. [Concerning representation at EEO-related meetings, refer to Chapter 8, "Representation During Formal Discussions," on ULPs.]

There was no bar to the negotiability of Proposal 27 in NFFE Local 405 and Dept. of Army, Army Info. Sys. Command, St. Louis, 42 FLRA 1112, 1146 (1991), that "the Union itself may be a representative and may itself file a complaint under the statutory EEO procedures on behalf of employees." The proposal was consistent with EEOC regulations then providing that a complaint may be filed by an organization for the aggrieved person with that person's consent. There was no bar to the negotiability of a provision that an employer's selection decisions concerning reassignments would be subject to the EEO procedure or the grievance procedure. The portion of the provision allowing challenges to management's selections was consistent with the definition of grievance contained in § 7103(a)(9), permitting employees to challenge any claimed violation of law, rule, and regulation. The proposal was also consistent with § 7121(d), permitting an aggrieved party to utilize the services of the EEOC under various circumstances. AFGE Local 3172 and DHHS, SSA, Modesto, 49 FLRA 302, 310 (1994) (Proposal 2).
