4. Disruptiveness; Lack of Cooperation

Akin to insubordination is an inability to comply with expected standards of workplace civility. In Hawes v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 591, 594 (1983) (aff'd, Hawes v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-992)), appellant's removal was sustained, following his earlier reinstatement to the job as a result of Board litigation, when his conduct became obstructive:

...Appellant made disparaging comments about the qualifications and experience of his coworkers and his supervisors, demanded that all instructions be in writing, and refused to speak to his supervisors on the phone when they called him at home about work....

The Board further finds that the charge that appellant used improper procedures to resolve matters of personal concern is also supported by a preponderance of the evidence. Appellant disregarded established means of airing grievances and continued to do so despite being warned to stop or be disciplined.

Employees are not privileged to set the terms of their conversations with supervisors. See Yartzoff v. EPA, 38 MSPR 403, 408 (1988) (employee was not justified in refusing to discuss his performance before the date normally set for the discussion). Removal was sustained of an employee who disrupted the workplace through repeated communications to coworkers concerning the employee's displeasure with the office dress code, or lack of one. The employee was repeatedly instructed to stop distracting his coworkers and using office resources (including email) to express his displeasure with the office dress policy. Bieber v. Dept. of Army, 287 F.3d 1358, 1364-65 (Fed. Cir. 2002); see Muldrow v. VA, 22 MSPR 586, 588 (1984) (removal sustained for insulting, disrespectful speech and gestures, coupled with threatening behavior; no evidence of physical or mental ailments or disabilities affecting the appellant's judgment; the appellant's conduct "was especially serious, completely undermining his employer/employee relationship with the agency by his actions towards his supervisor"). Hemelt v. VA, 21 MSPR 74, 77 (1984), mitigated removal to a suspension:

The agency removed appellant from her position as a Secretary, GS-5, effective July 9, 1982. The agency took this action based on one charge that appellant was negligent in the performance of her duties and three charges that appellant was disrespectful and used insulting, abusive, and obscene language toward her supervisor.

...

Finally, appellant asserts that the agency-imposed penalty of removal is excessive in light of the sustained charges and her prior satisfactory service without a disciplinary record. We agree with appellant. The presiding official cited Jefferson v. Veterans Administration, 6 MSPB 297 (1981), to support his decision upholding removal. In Jefferson, however, the Board upheld the appellant's removal after the presiding official found that two specifications of disrespect toward supervisors, together with the appellant's prior record which included a reprimand and a one-day suspension for a threatening remark, revealed a continuing insolent, insubordinate attitude toward supervision. Similarly, in McKowen v. Defense Logistics Agency [10 MSPR 98, 9 MSPB 365 (1982)], also cited by the presiding official, the agency discharged appellant only after he had previously been suspended twice for using profane, abusive language toward his supervisors. Noting that the agency allowed appellant a "third strike before calling him out," the Board upheld the removal as appropriate. In the present case, although three charges of improper conduct were sustained against appellant, all the charges occurred on the same day and, in essence, involved one incident. Thus, they do not reveal the type of continuing, insolent attitude that warranted removal in the above-cited cases. Moreover, appellant had nine and a half years of satisfactory service with the federal government and no prior disciplinary record, factors supporting mitigation of the penalty. See Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313, 332 (1981). Thus, despite the seriousness of appellant's conduct which affected her relationship with her supervisor, we find that it is outweighed by the factors favoring mitigation. In light of this, the Board finds that a 60-day suspension is the maximum reasonable penalty in this instance.

Abusive language to an agency security officer is serious because it undermines the officer's mission of maintaining order and security in the workplace. The conduct is particularly serious when the officer did not provoke the appellant. See Thomas v. DOD, 66 MSPR 546, 552 (1995). Material obstruction or interference by a law enforcement officer with agency law enforcement procedures or equipment was sufficient to sustain removal in Rodriguez v. DHS, 108 MSPR 76, 2008 MSPB 25 (2008) (aff'd, Rodriguez v. DHS, 314 Fed. Appx. 318 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP)) (overruled on other grounds, Thomas v. USPS, 116 MSPR 453, 458-61 ¶¶ 8-13, 2011 MSPB 62 (2011), and Pickett v. USDA, 116 MSPR 439, 440-44 ¶¶ 2-12, 2011 MSPB 58 (2011)).
