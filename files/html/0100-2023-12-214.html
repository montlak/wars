7. Lack of Transcript

5 CFR 1201.114(b) (2022) states that a PFR should be supported by references to applicable laws or regulations and by specific reference to the record. In most Board hearings, there is no written transcript because the Board record is an audio recording of the hearing. That audio recording is included with the Board's copy of the appeal file. In practice, it ordinarily seems to be placed on the electronic repository. If one or both parties wish a transcript, it has to be ordered by the requesting party from the commercial firm that supplied the person who recorded the hearing. That firm will usually then provide a copy of the transcript to the Board, and the AJ ordinarily will place a PDF file of the transcript into the electronic repository. If the transcript is prepared after the AJ issues the initial decision, and if the commercial firm that prepared the audio record files the transcript with the Board, either the AJ or someone in the Board's Office of the Clerk should enter the transcript into the electronic repository. Under 5 CFR 1201.53(c), the Board will provide a copy of the recording of the hearing (or other components of the case that were recorded) or a copy of any filed transcript without charge to the requesting party. Board regulations do not specifically provide a procedure for the Board to have the hearing transcribed at Board expenses; nothing prevents the Board from doing so. Assuming, for example, that a transcript is desired for purposes of review of a final Board decision in an appellate court, nothing prevents a party without the financial means of paying the firm that recorded the hearing to prepare a transcript from filing a motion with the Board requesting, for good cause shown, that the Board pay for and supply a transcript. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Hearing Recording Omissions; Transcript Errors."]

A transcript may be helpful in writing a PFR. In the absence of a transcript, it is not necessary to quote testimony precisely. It is sufficient to provide the Board with the identity of witnesses, the subject matter of their testimony, the context of its presentation, the relevance of the testimony to the matter at issue, and other factors pertinent to the testimony or information in the hearing record. Burris v. USPS, 27 MSPR 304, 304-05 (1985); see Davis v. Dept. of Education, 13 MSPR 575, 576 (1982). Parties may cite to the Board recordings by referring to the elapsed time shown on the playback display for a particular witness or segment of the hearing. The Board uses this method in its decisions. No reasonable citation system will be ignored.

Some appellants attempt to transcribe hearing recordings themselves, creating "unofficial transcripts" that they then cite in their PFRs. The Board does not prohibit the practice (as good or better than recollection of testimony unaided by a review of an "official" hearing recording); the agency may respond by pointing out inaccuracies in the transcription offered by the appellant. Fidelibus v. DLA, 21 MSPR 280, 281 (1984). Whether this practice will continue to be permitted is unknown, given the current regulatory definition of the Board's record as the official record of the proceedings. See 5 CFR 1201.53(a) (2022). If an untranscribed off-the-record conference is significant, it is acceptable for affidavits to be submitted with the PFR describing the conference. Porter v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 301, 304 n.2, 6 MSPB 257, 259 n.2 (1981).
