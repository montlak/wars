g. Complete Relief Through Other Means; Stay of Pending Litigation

An appeal may be moot if complete relief was achieved outside of Board channels. If an appellant obtains all the relief from an EEO or other case that he could with the Board, the Board case is dismissed as moot. See Morman v. DOD, 90 MSPR 197, 204 ¶ 20 (2001) (remanding for determination by AJ of whether a related EEOC case provided the full relief the Board could order if the appellant prevailed on a constructive removal claim); Williams v. USPS, 31 MSPR 604, 606 (1986) (mooting an employee's claim of enforced leave through a grant of OWCP benefits for the period of leave); Young v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 541, 543 (1984) (relief, other than counsel fees, provided in EEO process; fee entitlement would be addressed through any motion filed for fees); Finley v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 320, 321 (1981) (OPM approved grade retention rights for the appellant: "[T]he facts in this case indicate that the question of the appellant's right to grade and pay retention, which was originally in contention, has been resolved in the appellant's favor while the appeal was pending. The Board is prohibited by 5 USC 1205(g) [now 1204(h)] from issuing advisory opinions."); cf. Rivas v. USPS, 62 MSPR 484, 486 (1994) (complex constructive suspension appeals: as to one appeal, the appeal is moot if there is no "effective relief" that the Board could grant). The Board dismissed disability annuity appeals when the appellant secured optional retirement in the interim, as long as the benefit amount is at least as high. Optional retirement is considered equal to or better than disability retirement because the appellant need not annually reestablish his disability. See Kozer v. OPM, 45 MSPR 505, 507 (1990).

An appeal is not moot if the appellant concurrently pursues a case in another forum to obtain the same relief sought through the Board. The Board may, however, exercise its discretion to stay the litigation before it while the other litigation is resolved, as the Federal Circuit explained in Kagel v. Dept. of Army, 126 F.3d 1455, 1459 (Fed. Cir. 1997). Kagel involved an IRA appeal and a district court case, both designed to enjoin the agency from proceeding against the employee on the basis of an alleged apparent conflict of interest created by business activities of the employee's spouse:

As an alternative ground for dismissing Kagel's appeal, the administrative judge invoked the doctrine of "discretionary mootness." See In re AOV Indus., Inc., 792 F.2d 1140, 1147 (D.C. Cir. 1986). The administrative judge found the conflict-of-interest issue common to both Kagel's IRA appeal and Nancy Kagel's district court action and concluded that it would be "awkward" if the Board adjudicated the conflict of interest issue and the district court later came to a contrary conclusion. In addition, the administrative judge noted the potential for duplicative discovery requests and conflicting rulings by the two tribunals.

Although the Board enjoys broad discretion in controlling its docket, see Rowe v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 802 F.2d 434, 437 (Fed. Cir. 1986), it may not dismiss an appeal over which it has jurisdiction simply because of a potential overlap of subject matter with a proceeding pending in another forum. It may be appropriate for the Board, in the exercise of its discretion over matters within its jurisdiction, to stay the administrative proceedings in this case pending further developments in Nancy Kagel's district court action. Outright dismissal of the appeal, however, is another matter and cannot be justified on the basis of concerns about possible conflicts with another proceeding. Kagel is entitled to have his whistleblowing claim adjudicated before the Board, notwithstanding his wife's temporary cessation of her business in the district. Accordingly, it was improper for the administrative judge to dismiss Kagel's appeal.

According to Malone v. Dept. of Treasury, 57 MSPR 12, 15 (1993), if the appellant's appeal is moot because of agency action removing the substantive basis of the appeal, the Board will not entertain a claim that the agency's action constituted a prohibited personnel practice or violation of his constitutional rights. Malone is open to question if the appeal is an IRA case involving a claim for damages.
