broida flra Chapter 05: Subjects of Bargaining: Substantive Limitations on Negotiability Determinations Chapter 05: Subjects of Bargaining: Substantive Limitations on Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 02: Employment Conditions, Section A: Matters Covered by Law, Subsection 01: Proposal Incorporating Statutory Requirements

1. Proposal Incorporating Statutory Requirements

There is a limited exception to the preclusion of negotiability of matters covered by law. The proposal is negotiable if it requires the agency to follow the law or if the proposal does no more than call on the agency to incorporate into the contract statutory provisions that undeniably cover unit employees. The dividing line between negotiable and nonnegotiable proposals is whether they require negotiations over the substantive content of matters that are specifically provided for by statute, i.e., whether the proposal modifies the substantive statutory provisions. NTEU Chap. 213 and Dept. of Energy, 32 FLRA 578, 582-84 (1988) (Proposal 2, requiring the contract to include the statutory provisions concerning within-grade increases); see AFGE Local 2119 and Army, Mfg. & Tech. Center, Rock Island, 72 FLRA 706, 707-08 (2022) (Member Abbott concurring) (Provision 2, affirming the right of the union to act as representatives and present its views to the agency and officials of the government. Was negotiable a restatement of 5 USC 7102(1); it did not implicitly authorize inappropriate uses of official time); NLRB Professional Ass'n and NLRB, 62 FLRA 397, 401 (2008) ("The exercise of management's rights under § 7106(a)(2) is limited by 'applicable laws.' NTEU, 42 FLRA 377, 388-91 (1991), enforcement denied on other grounds, 966 F.2d 1246 (D.C. Cir. 1993). Thus, proposals that require an agency to exercise its management's rights in accordance with applicable laws do not interfere with such rights and are within the duty to bargain. See id. The Authority has found that an agency regulation constitutes an 'applicable law' where that regulation has 'the force and effect of law.' Id. At 391."). [Refer in this chapter to "Proposal to Enforce Agency Regulations and Policies."]

AFGE Local 997 and Dept. of Air Force, Maxwell AFB, 66 FLRA 499, 500-01 (2012), involving a proposal concerning wearing of military uniforms by ARTs, summarized FLRA's position on proposals requiring management to exercise management rights in accordance with applicable laws:

The Union argues that the proposal does not affect management's right because it is consistent with AFI 36-704. The exercise of management rights under § 7106(a)(2) is limited by "applicable laws." 5 U.S.C. § 7106(a)(2); NLRB Union, 62 FLRA 397, 402 (2008) (NLRBU). Proposals that require management to exercise its management rights in accordance with applicable laws do not impermissibly affect such rights and are within the duty to bargain. NLRBU, 62 FLRA at 402. An agency regulation constitutes an applicable law when the regulation has the force and effect of law. Id.

But under § 2424.25I(1)(iv)4 of the Authority's Regulations, a union must set forth arguments and supporting authorities for any assertion that a proposal enforces an applicable law within the meaning of § 7106(a)(2). Id. Although the Union relies on AFI 36-704, the Union does not specifically argue that the proposal requires management to exercise its right to discipline in accordance with AFI 36-704. And the Union does not argue that AFI 36-704 constitutes an applicable law within the meaning of § 7106(a)(2). As such, the Union fails to state whether and why the proposal enforces an applicable law, as required by § 2424.25. See id.

Under § 2424.32I, when a union does not argue that a proposal that affects a management right under § 7106(a)(2) constitutes an exception to management rights under § 7106(b) or enforces an applicable law, the Authority finds that the proposal is outside the duty to bargain. AFGE, Local 1164, 65 FLRA 924, 926 (2011); NLRBU, 62 FLRA at 402-03. Here, the Union does not argue that the proposal is an exception to management rights under § 7106(b), or that it enforces an applicable law under § 7106(a)(2). Consequently, we find that the proposal affects management's right to discipline under § 7106(a)(2)(A) and that the Union has failed to establish that the proposal is otherwise negotiable. Accordingly, we conclude that the proposal is outside the duty to bargain.

When an agency claims that a proposal is barred because its subject matter is covered by law, the agency objection under 5 USC 7103(a)(14)(C) must, at a minimum, cite the statute and explain why it removes the proposal from mandatory bargaining. DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau v. FLRA, 982 F.2d 577, 580 (D.C. Cir. 1993). If a law establishes a specific program and leaves the specifics of the program to the discretion of an agency, those specifics may be negotiable. Incentive pay rates, permitted but not specified by law, may be negotiable. NTEU and IRS, 27 FLRA 132, 138 (1987) (discussed in Chapter 6, "Incentive Pay; Awards"). To exclude a proposal as nonnegotiable because the subject matter of the proposal is already covered by law, it is not sufficient for the statute to refer to a particular matter covered by the proposal. Instead, the statute precludes bargaining only if it specifically provides for or establishes a particular aspect of a matter; then that aspect of the matter is not included within the conditions of employment subject to bargaining. AFGE Local 1931 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Weapons Station, Concord, 32 FLRA 1023, 1060-62 (1988) (Provision 33); see Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 45 FLRA 1185, 1194-95 (1992) (proposals concerning basic pay were preempted by complex statutory scheme for setting pay of overseas teachers). AFGE 1931 determined negotiable a proposal that the agency make specific transportation arrangements for employees with job-related injuries. Workers' compensation statutes contained references to transportation, but the statutes did not cover the particular matter addressed by the proposal.

The negotiability exclusion under 5 USC 7103(a)(14)(C) involves only those matters specifically provided for by federal statute. A government-wide regulation, OMB Circular A-76, establishing procedures for administering the rents for government quarters, does not constitute a federal statute for purposes of the statutory definition. See DHHS, Indian Health Serv. And LIUNA Local 580, 57 FLRA 902, 907 (2002) (citing IAM Lodge 2135, 50 FLRA 677, 690 n.22 (1995); NTEU, 37 FLRA 147, 151-52 (1990)). Government-wide regulations have other implications. [Refer later in this chapter to "Government-Wide Regulations" and "Compelling Need Determinations," concerning the effect on negotiability of agency regulations.]

In 1988 the policy that statutory discretion makes a proposal negotiable was modified in the Third Circuit following an agency's challenge to FLRA's ruling that proposals concerning pay rates and practices of prevailing rate employees were negotiable. Dept. of Navy, Military Sealift Command v. FLRA, 836 F.2d 1409, 1415-16 (3d Cir. 1988), rev'g Nat'l Maritime Union and Dept. of Navy, Military Sealift Command, 25 FLRA 105 (1987), declared:

Simply put, FLRA asserts that matters within an agency's reviewable discretion, including pay practices, are within the duty to bargain subject to certain narrow statutory exceptions which it reserves unto itself the power to define.... The Navy asserts that the FLRA places an insurmountable burden on agencies by exempting them from collective bargaining only where a statute explicitly prohibits such bargaining. Given the recent enactment of the Labor-Management Statute, few statutes would include such an explicit exemption. Furthermore, the statute explicitly exempts matters from collective bargaining where to do so would be inconsistent with law...as well as matters specifically provided for by statute. The Navy's argument has force since the FLRA argues that matters left to agency discretion are, in general, not specifically provided for by statute. The implication of that argument is that sole and exclusive discretion is an empty set.

...

The FLRA's holding of negotiability in this case depends on the validity of its across-the-board conclusion that matters entrusted to agency discretion are not "specifically provided for by federal statute." Such an interpretation is possible if one looks only to this section of the Labor-Management Statute. However, it ignores 7117(a) by imputing to Congress an intent to deprive the Navy of the discretion Congress gave it under the prevailing rate system and substitute the collective bargaining process for that discretion....

The Third Circuit decision is discussed in Chapter 6, "Wages Set by Statute." FLRA now states that, as to the negotiability of pay-related proposals, if a law or applicable regulation vests an agency with unfettered discretion over a matter, the agency's discretion is not subject to negotiation. AFGE Local 3295 and Dept. of Treasury, Office of Thrift Supervision, 47 FLRA 884, 893 (1993) (relying on Illinois NG v. FLRA, 854 F.2d 1396, 1398-1400 (D.C. Cir. 1988)); see Colorado Nurses Ass'n v. FLRA, 851 F.2d 1486, 1489 (D.C. Cir. 1988) ("Section 4108 underscores the exclusive nature of the Administrator's authority by exempting its exercise from 'any law, Executive order, or regulation.' The purpose of this language is clear: when the Administrator prescribes regulations concerning conditions of employment, he is to be unhampered by the range of federal personnel statutes and regulations that might otherwise constrain his authority."). But cf. AFGE Local 1917 and DOJ, INS, 55 FLRA 228, 236-37 (1999) (proposal identifying weapons that employees could carry was at least permissively negotiable; statute authorizing agency head to permit employees to carry weapons neither excluded weapons use as an employment condition nor divested management of discretion concerning conditions of weapons use).
