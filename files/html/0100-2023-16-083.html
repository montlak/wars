5. Proposal, Decision Letter

The agency should create a proposal letter that states the essentials of a performance case, Kadlec v. Dept. of Army, 49 MSPR 534, 540 (1991):

[I]n its notice of proposed removal, although the agency stated that the appellant's performance under the four critical elements was unacceptable, it did not specify what performance standards she had failed to meet. Rather, it simply set forth deficiencies in the appellant's performance during the improvement period without relating the deficiencies to any standards. Of course, by failing to relate the deficiencies to any standards, it also did not show that the appellant's failure to meet a particular standard justified a finding that her performance of a critical element containing multiple performance standards warranted a finding of below fully successful performance of the critical element as a whole. It is true that an agency may give content to an employee's otherwise valid written performance standards by informing the employee of the specific requirements and applications of the standards to her work situation through a performance improvement plan.... However, an agency may not prove an employee's below fully successfully performance of a critical element without regard to the written performance standard for that critical element....

The agency's decision letter does nothing to remedy this situation, simply repeating the phrase for all critical elements that "The evidence supports unsatisfactory performance on this critical element." Thus, we agree with the appellant that even if the agency has proved the specific deficiencies it cited in its notice of proposed removal, it has still not proved that they warrant a finding that the appellant failed to meet the critical elements for her position.

Proposal letters may be confusing in their choice of language in performance-based actions. But the notice requirements of 5 USC 4303 are procedural in nature and subject to a harmful error analysis. Faust v. Smithsonian Inst., 29 MSPR 496, 499 (1985). The Board considers whether the confusion harmed the appellant in her defense of an action pursued under Chapter 43 or Chapter 75. Penaloza v. DHHS, 4 MSPR 322, 326-27, 4 MSPB 389 (1980), analyzes an ambiguous proposal:

Section 7513 affords certain procedural safeguards to an employee whose removal is contemplated. It contains no requirement that the proposed or final decision cite the statute or regulation which authorizes the action. An agency action must contain the elements required by statute, however, and failure to do so may result in reversal. The issue in the instant case, thus, is whether the agency action sufficiently notified appellant of the reason(s) for her removal pursuant to Chapter 75.

It is undisputed that the proposal and decision letters cited "unacceptable job performance" as one ground for the action. "Unacceptable performance" is a term of art defined in Section 4301(3), and its usage could reasonably lead one to conclude that Chapter 43 was the basis for the action, at least as to that charge. The proposal letter is misleading to some extent in this regard. However, an action will not be reversed merely "because of technical procedural oversights which have not substantially prejudiced or impaired the employee's rights." Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 MSPB 489, 1 MSPR 505 (1980). Therefore, the Board must consider whether the reference to unacceptable performance caused any harm to appellant; in other words, whether appellant was misled by the reference to such an extent as to impair her right to notice under section 7513.

Even though "unacceptable performance" was cited as one ground for the action, the second ground cited gave sufficient notice that the action was taken pursuant to Chapter 75. The events cited in the second charge, in fact, are the sole basis for the action--concealment of 246 pieces of unassociated mail. The details recited in support of both charges, and the exhibits incorporated by reference, addressed the charge of concealment.

Therefore, appellant was notified with sufficient particularity that she was charged with concealing mail matter. Moreover, appellant was informed before the hearing that the action was based solely on Chapter 75, and reliance on Chapter 43 was disavowed. Also, in response to oral pre-hearing motions, the agency reiterated its position. This being so, there was no substantial harm to appellant's rights when the agency presented its case as a Chapter 75 action. Further, by virtue of having taken the action under Chapter 75, the agency subjected itself to the greater burden of proof applicable to such cases. We conclude that appellant has failed to demonstrate harm by the use of the term "unacceptable performance" in charge 1. See 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(A) and 5 CFR § 1201.56(b)(1) and (c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1) and 1201.4(r)] .
