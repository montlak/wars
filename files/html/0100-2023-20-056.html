4. Influencing Employment Decision; 5 USC 2302(b)(5)

Under 5 USC 2302:

(b) Any employee who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action, shall not, with respect to such authority--

...

(5) influence any person to withdraw from competition for any position for the purpose of improving or injuring the prospects of any other person for employment[.]

It is not a necessary element of the prohibited practice that the violator's efforts be directed specifically toward assisting another person in obtaining a position. The improper efforts need not be successful to be actionable. Special Counsel v. Filiberti, 27 MSPR 498, 504-05, 508-09 (1984); cf. Special Counsel v. Waddams, 34 MSPR 84, 86 (1987) ("[t]o influence or attempt to influence candidates to withdraw from competition, the conduct prohibited by [5 CFR] section 330.601, is a prohibited personnel practice as defined in 5 USC 2302(b)(5)."). The Filiberti decision was reviewed by the Ninth Circuit in Filiberti v. MSPB, 804 F.2d 1504 (9th Cir. 1986). The respondent was involved in the selection of an individual for an agency position. OPM subsequently reviewed the selection process and found that it was defective because an applicant with veterans preference should have been ranked higher than the selectee. OPM directed the agency to rerun the selection. Rerunning it, the respondent attempted to "pass over" the veteran, with an explanation that OPM rejected. OPM directed the respondent to offer the job to the veteran. Instead of making a job offer, the respondent corresponded with the veteran applicant in a manner that was characterized as pessimistic. The applicant read between the lines, said he was not interested in becoming involved in a local feud, and withdrew his application, asking to be kept in mind should vacancies again occur. OPM reviewed the correspondence and referred the matter to OSC. On complaint of OSC, MSPB imposed a 60-day suspension. The respondent retired the day his suspension was to begin. OSC then obtained an order from the Board directing that a sum equal to the salary respondent would have forfeited had he served his suspension be withheld from his accrued leave pay, a penalty the court reversed for reasons discussed concerning OSC penalty decisions. On review the court determined that 2302(b)(5) should not be restricted to applicants for federal employment, in the sense that the actions of Filiberti were construed to "secure" or prolong the employment of the individual first selected for the position.

Sustaining charges of sections 2302(b)(5) and (6) (granting unwarranted preferences or advantages not authorized by law) involving personnel practices by a personnel officer and a personnel specialist, the Board directed demotion of the personnel officer by one grade and the personnel specialist by two grades for a year. The two employees attempted to prevent outside candidates from applying for a newly created position that was intended for an in-house employee. The improper conduct of the employees included falsely recording that two candidates for the position declined consideration, intentionally failing to mail inquiries to four candidates concerning their availability for a position, subsequently falsely reporting to OPM the status of certain candidates, and making false statements during the ensuing OSC investigation. Special Counsel v. Ross, 34 MSPR 197 (1987).

Special Counsel v. Brown, 61 MSPR 559, 565-67 (1994), sustained disciplinary action against officials who interfered with the employment of a candidate who had priority consideration rights under the OPM Displaced Employee Program. The Board emphasized comments made to the candidate, misleading information provided by agency officials to OPM, and the agency's failure to take corrective action at the request of OPM:

Count one charged respondents with violating 5 USC § 2302(b)(5) (1988). That section prohibits an employee from "influenc[ing] any person to withdraw from competition for any position for the purpose of improving or injuring the prospects of any other person for employment." Actual withdrawal of an applicant is not required to establish a violation; an attempt to influence withdrawal is sufficient. Special Counsel v. Filiberti, 27 MSPR 498, 508-09 (1984), aff'd in part, rev'd in part and remanded on other grounds, 804 F.2d 1504 (9th Cir. 1986). It is not the consequences of the conduct which is prohibited, but the conduct itself. Id. Thus, the statute requires a two-part showing: (1) that an employee influenced or attempted to influence a person to withdraw from competition and (2) that the influence was exerted to improve or injure the employment prospects of another.

The Special Counsel alleged that Brown influenced Nielson to withdraw from competition for the public affairs specialist position for the purpose of injuring his employment prospects or improving the prospects of Friday. The CALJ found that preponderant evidence supported the charge as to Brown. We agree.

In finding that Brown influenced Nielson to withdraw, the CALJ credited Nielson's testimony and discredited Brown's testimony. Nielson testified that following the panel interview Brown told him "[W]e want someone who is more of [sic] flag waver. OPM referred you to me, and I can ask you to verbally waive your priority."...

Brown next argues that the CALJ erred in finding that he acted for the purpose of improving Friday's prospects for employment. Brown's action, however, belies this contention. At the time of Brown's first interview with Nielson, Friday had already been selected for one of two positions in the Dallas district office and personnel management specialist Sara Watson had advised Brown that the DEP [Displaced Employee Program] eligible Nielson had not been selected. Because Nielson met the basic qualifications for the public affairs position, OPM regulations required his selection or the holding of one position on the certificate while an objection was pending. See 5 CFR §§ 330.305(b) and 332.406(c) and (d). Thus, if Brown simply wanted to avoid appointing Nielson for a legitimate reason, he could have left the position vacant pending OPM's decision on an INS objection or filled the position noncompetitively with someone other than Friday. That Brown did not hold one of the two district positions open for Nielson can only be explained by a desire to advantage Friday. Friday lacked competitive status, and therefore could not have been placed in the regional office position for which no certificate was prepared. Thus, it must be inferred that Brown influenced Nielson to withdraw because he blocked Friday's appointment to the district position. Accordingly, the CALJ properly found that Brown acted for the purpose of improving Friday's prospects for employment within the meaning of 5 USC § 2302(b)(5).

The Special Counsel charged Brown with influencing Nielson to withdraw from competition to improve Friday's employment prospects or to harm Nielson's employment prospects. The CALJ, however, made no specific finding as to whether Brown acted for the purpose of harming Nielson's employment prospects. The statute requires a showing inter alia, that Brown acted "for the purpose of" injuring the employment prospects of any other person. 5 USC § 2302(b)(5). Consistent with Filiberti v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 804 F.2d 1504, 1509 (9th Cir. 1986) (rev'd in part and remanded on other grounds), influencing Nielson to withdraw from competition for the purpose of injuring Nielson's employment prospects is a sufficient basis on which to sustain a violation of 5 USC § 2302(b)(5).

Here the Board finds ample evidence that Brown acted for the purpose of injuring Nielson's employment prospects. Brown suggested to Nielson that he waive his right to the priority consideration afforded displaced employee candidates. Additionally, Brown provided misleading information to OPM about Nielson's fitness for the public affairs position by misrepresenting that Nielson used the invective "wets" to refer to Mexicans. Finally, Brown ignored OPM's orders to take corrective action for an extended period of time. These actions support a finding of intent to harm Nielson's chances of employment. See Filiberti, 804 F.2d at 1510 (court found strong suggestion of intent to harm individual's employment prospects where agency provided misleading information to individual and ignored OPM's direction to hire him).
