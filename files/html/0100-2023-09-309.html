2. Sanction Against Appellant

Sanctions up to and including dismissal may be applied against appellants who do not comply with discovery orders. Butler v. Smithsonian Inst., 10 MSPR 644, 646 (1982). Sanctions are appropriate, under Abbott v. Dept. of Commerce, 18 MSPR 429, 432-433 ¶ 6 (1983), against an appellant who fails to respond to discovery requests calling for information about what specifically the appellant believes was improper about an agency RIF action. In Abbott, the AJ effectively imposed the sanction of precluding the appellants from obtaining a hearing. If an appellant fails to answer interrogatories on an affirmative defense, e.g., discrimination, raised during the appeal, the AJ may strike that claim as a sanction for the failure to comply with a discovery order requiring a response to the interrogatories. Moody v. Dept. of Air Force, 14 MSPR 453, 454 (1983), following remand from EEOC, 30 MSPR 9, 12 (1986) (AJ need not find an appellant's actions to be willful or in bad faith before applying sanctions); see Robinson v. VA, 94 MSPR 509, 517 ¶ 16 (2003) (AJ had authority to impose some sanction short of dismissal against a pro se appellant who failed to attend a deposition that was required by an AJ's order). In Nickerson v. USPS, 49 MSPR 451, 460-61 (1991), the AJ declined to consider a defense of harmful error after the appellant failed to obey an order directing a response to an interrogatory requiring the appellant to identify and explain allegations of harmful error that he intended to raise. It was immaterial that the appellant mentioned harmful error in his statement of issues at another stage of the case. Because the appellant had discovery processes available to him, Nickerson found unpersuasive the appellant's contention that he could not answer the interrogatory until he heard the hearing testimony of two agency witnesses.

The Federal Circuit determined that the AJ could refuse to permit an appellant to testify in support of an affirmative defense after the appellant failed to respond to interrogatories relating to that defense. Harp v. Dept. of Army, 791 F.2d 161 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Cancelation of a hearing was affirmed as a sanction, Nasuti v. Dept. of State, 675 Fed. Appx. 975, 980 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP):

Contrary to Mr. Nasuti's contention, 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a), which provides that a federal employee who appeals to the Board "shall have the right...to a hearing for which a transcript will be kept," did not entitle him to a hearing regardless of whether he complied with the administrative judge's discovery orders. In Ahlberg v. Department of Health & Human Services, 804 F.2d 1238 (Fed. Cir. 1986), we held that § 7701(a) did not preclude the Board from dismissing an appeal under § 1201.43 for failure to prosecute. Id. at 1244-45. There, the presiding official "warned" the appellants that their refusal to comply with an order to provide certain requested information would result in the dismissal of their appeal. Id. at 1242. We upheld the Board's actions, explaining that the appellants' failure to comply, despite the warning, "waived any right they may have had to a hearing." Id. at 1243.

Here, the administrative judge warned Mr. Nasuti that his continued failure to comply with the administrative judge's discovery orders would result in the cancellation of the hearing. Nevertheless, Mr. Nasuti refused to comply with those orders and did not substantiate his reasons for doing so. Mr. Nasuti further asserted that the Board proceedings were "moot" because the administrative judge was not interested in holding an "expeditious or thorough hearing on the merits." As we explained in Ahlberg, § 7701 is not so absolute as to require the Board to hold a hearing in the face of such repeated defiance. See Ahlberg, 804 F.2d at 1243.

To the extent that Mr. Nasuti argues that the administrative judge abused his discretion in imposing sanctions under § 1201.43, we disagree. The administrative judge cancelled the hearing only after Mr. Nasuti refused to comply with the administrative judge's discovery orders on two separate occasions--and only after Mr. Nasuti was warned that continued refusal would result in cancellation. This is not a case in which a hearing was cancelled despite the presence of a good excuse for the conduct that led to the cancellation. Compare Habtemariam v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 180 Fed. Appx. 968, 970-71 (Fed. Cir. 2006). We cannot say that Mr. Nasuti's behavior did not rise to the level of "contumacious conduct or conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice" for which cancellation of a hearing is within the Board's discretion. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43.

Yet the Board modified a sanction of issue preclusion in Thorne v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 408, 410 (1986), declaring that it would not preclude an EEO affirmative defense for failure to answer interrogatories. The need for a complete record outweighed the need for the sanction if the case went to court or to EEOC. The appeal was remanded for rehearing, with permission for the AJ to consider a lesser sanction, e.g., an adverse inference against the appellant. Care should be taken to avoid a negative inference, or negative finding, when the lack of evidence is the result of a sanction barring the introduction of evidence for failure to comply with a discovery order. Dunbar v. Dept. of Transp., DC-0752-14-1003-I-1 (NP 11/15/2016) ("Our reviewing court has ruled that it is an abuse of discretion to exclude evidence during a Board proceeding that would tend to support an employee's claim of retaliation for whistleblowing, and then use that lack of evidence to find against the employee on that claim. Whitmore v. Department of Labor, 680 F.3d 1353, 1369-70 (Fed. Cir. 2012)."). But see Pettus v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0353-13-0409-B-1 (NP 4/4/2022) (disability discrimination claim properly struck: "The record reflects that the appellant not only failed to respond to the agency's discovery request, but that she also failed to respond to the agency's motion to compel discovery and the administrative judge's orders to file her discovery responses. Smets v. Department of the Navy, 117 M.S.P.R. 164, ¶ 13 (2011) (finding that an administrative judge is not required to provide the appellant with an opportunity to oppose sanctions for failure to comply with an order that warned of the possibility of sanctions), aff'd per curiam, 498 F. App'x 1 (Fed. Cir. 2012). In granting the agency's motion to compel, the administrative judge warned the appellant that failure to comply could result in sanctions").

The sanction of dismissal of an appeal is a serious matter reserved for extreme misconduct. Although a pro se appellant cannot avoid sanctions because of his unrepresented status, it is inappropriate to dismiss his appeal because he failed, after attempting a response, to provide a complete reply to an agency motion for a more definite statement of the basis for his appeal. Also, "when there are significant adjudicatory facts in dispute, such factor weighs against dismissal." Hoyt v. Dept. of Treasury, 19 MSPR 21, 23 (1984). The Board will not dismiss an appeal for failure by the appellant to obey a discovery order, but the Board may impose a sanction that essentially results in a dismissal. In Roth v. Dept. of Transp., 54 MSPR 172, 176-77 (1992), an involuntary resignation case, the appellant failed to obey an order directing her to appear for a deposition called by the agency. The AJ, citing deliberate disobedience and prejudice to the agency, sanctioned the appellant by drawing an inference that all of the appellant's claims of involuntariness lacked support. He then canceled the hearing for lack of a basis for Board jurisdiction. An order was then issued dismissing the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. The Board approved the AJ's approach:

Rule 37 identifies sanctions available if a party fails to obey an order to provide discovery. See Fed.R.Civ.P. 37(b)(2). The evidentiary sanction imposed in the case at hand, drawing "an inference favorable to the agency that all of [the appellant's] claims of involuntariness of her resignation [were]lacking in support," is within the sanctions available under Rule 37. Further, the Board does not prohibit the imposition of an evidentiary sanction for a single failure to obey a discovery order. Cf. Murdock v. GPO, 38 MSPR 297, 299 (1988) (a single failure to comply with a Board order is insufficient to support a dismissal for failure to prosecute). Although the sanction imposed here led to dismissal for lack of Board jurisdiction, because jurisdiction could only be established by proof that the appellant's resignation was involuntary, the sanction itself was not dismissal.

Traditionally, courts have not allowed an imposed sanction to result in dismissal on the merits except on the clearest showing that this course was required. See Wright and Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure: § 2284. The reason for the party's failure to comply has been the pivotal consideration in determining what sanction to impose. See Societe Internationale Pour Participations Industrielles et Commerciales, S.A. v. Rogers, 357 U.S. 197, 78 S. Ct. 1087, 2 L. Ed.2d 1255 (1958). In the past twenty years or so, courts have taken a stricter view of sanctions for discovery violations. See National Hockey League v. Metropolitan Hockey Club, Inc. 427 U.S. 639, 96 S. Ct. 2778, 49 L. Ed.2d 747 (1976). If the failure to make discovery is willful, however, a court may well order dismissal or a default judgment even though less drastic sanctions are available. See Morgan v. Massachusetts General Hosp., 901 F.2d 186, 195 (1st Cir. 1990).

We find that a sanction for a single failure to obey a discovery order should allow the extreme result of dismissal in Board appeals only if the appellant's defiance of a discovery order is willful. Here, although the CALJ did not specifically find whether the appellant's actions were willful, upon examination of the record, we find that they were. "Willful" is defined as "proceeding from a conscious motion of the will; voluntary. Intending the result which actually comes to pass; designed; intentional; not accidental." See Black's Law Dictionary, 5th Edition at 1434. The appellant's failure to appear at the deposition was willful to the extent that she acted voluntarily, intentionally, and with the specific intent to disobey the order of the CALJ to attend the deposition.

The appellant had not received an amended order from the judge after she explained her reasons for objecting to his order to attend the deposition, and thus knew that she was required to attend. Further, the appellant did not show that incapacitation because of illness kept her from attending the deposition. In her opposition to the agency's motion for sanctions, the appellant stated that she was fearful of being subjected to a hostile deposition and she determined that she could not risk that stress and exasperation. The appellant did not, however, present any medical evidence of incapacity or medical consequences as a result of appearing for the deposition. As the CALJ stated, more is required than fear of stress and exasperation to establish a valid reason to evade the legal obligation to appear at the deposition. The appropriate action for the appellant would have been to attend the deposition and, if it turned unduly stressful or abusive, to seek a ruling from a judge limiting questioning or continuing the deposition. The CALJ's sanction, which resulted in dismissal of the appeal, was not, therefore, an abuse of discretion. See Bilger v. Department of Justice, 33 MSPR 602, 607 (1987), aff'd, 847 F.2d 842 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table).

Wagner v. DHS, 105 MSPR 67, 70-73 ¶¶ 9-15, 2007 MSPB 58 (2007), reversing an AJ's ruling imposing the sanction of dismissal of an appeal against an appellant who was tardy in responding to an AJ's discovery order, emphasized that dismissal of an appeal as a result of a discovery dispute should not be employed as a sanction unless there is no alternative and the sanction is necessary to protect the integrity of the discovery process and the ability of the agency to prosecute its case:

The Board has the authority, through its administrative judges, to order compliance with its orders and to enforce compliance through sanctions. See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43. Such sanctions may be imposed upon the parties as necessary to serve the ends of justice. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43. As a general rule, appellants are subject to the same sanctions as agencies; consequently, administrative judges may apply sanctions up to dismissal against appellants who do not follow discovery orders in the same fashion as they apply them to agencies. Butler v. Smithsonian Institution, 10 M.S.P.R. 644, 646 (1982); see Moody v. Department of the Air Force, 14 M.S.P.R. 453, 454 (1983). Once an administrative judge imposes a sanction, the Board will not ordinarily disturb such a determination unless it is shown that the administrative judge abused his discretion or that his ruling adversely affected a party's substantive rights. Bilger v. Department of Justice, 33 M.S.P.R. 602, 607 (1987), aff'd, 847 F.2d 842 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table).

On review, the appellant argues that the administrative judge erred by imposing sanctions because he provided a response to the agency's discovery requests on the due date imposed by the administrative judge's second order compelling discovery. According to the agency, the appellant submitted only a partial response to its discovery request, and submitted that partial response untimely, i.e., after 5:00 P.M. on February 3, 2006. We note that neither party ever informed the administrative judge that the appellant had filed any discovery responses, whether partial or otherwise, although both parties had an ample opportunity to do so at some point between the date the responses were filed, February 3, 2006, and the date the initial decision was issued, May 23, 2006. Under these circumstances, we find that the administrative judge did not abuse her discretion in imposing a sanction because the record before her did not show that the appellant had complied with her discovery order. However, we find it appropriate to reopen this appeal and reconsider the agency's motion for sanctions for the following three reasons.

First, a mere 81 minutes after the agency notified the administrative judge that the appellant had not provided it with discovery responses, the appellant faxed his responses to the agency. The agency could easily have amended its notice of non-compliance to inform the administrative judge that the appellant had responded, and, in fact, the administrative judge did not impose sanctions until three days after the agency filed the notice of non-compliance. The agency may have believed that 6:18 P.M. was after the "close of business," but whether it truly was, and whether any tardiness should have been excused, were determinations for the administrative judge to make, not the agency. Cf. Williams v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 75 M.S.P.R. 144, 148-49 (1997) (criticizing the agency's "gamesmanship" in arguing that an appeal should be dismissed as untimely filed when all along the agency possessed a certified mail return receipt proving that the appeal had been timely filed).

Second, the administrative judge's final warning to the appellant before she imposed sanctions did not explain what "close of business" meant. "Close of business" is not defined in the Board's regulations either. When a document must be filed with the Board, it is considered timely if it is transmitted electronically or placed into the mail stream any time before midnight on the due date. See Fisher v. Department of Defense, 59 M.S.P.R. 165, 169 (1993); Dooley v. Department of the Air Force, 57 M.S.P.R. 684, 686 (1993), aff'd, 22 F.3d 1105 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). The record does not reveal any reason why it was important that the appellant send his discovery responses to the agency by "close of business" on February 3 instead of simply any time before February 4. There is also no evidence, or even any allegation, that the agency was prejudiced because it received the discovery responses at 6:18 P.M. on February 3 rather than some earlier time that day. Under the circumstances, we deem the appellant to have substantially complied with the deadline for discovery responses imposed by the administrative judge. Cf. Stauner v. Department of the Interior, 86 M.S.P.R. 179, 182, ¶ 2 (2000) (where the appellant's attorney appeared not to understand that the administrative judge expected him to file a brief by 5:00 P.M. on a particular day, and he faxed his brief to the administrative judge at 10:00 P.M. that day, the administrative judge should have considered the brief).

Third, even assuming that the appellant's discovery responses were inadequate as the agency alleges, and that the appellant did not comply with the administrative judge's order, the sanction imposed was excessive. An administrative judge may impose sanctions "as necessary to serve the ends of justice." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a). Because this appeal involves a removal action taken under 5 U.S.C. §§ 7512, 7513(d), the agency bears the burden of proving its charges by a preponderance of the evidence, 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B), and it should not have removed the appellant, a tenured public employee, if it did not have evidence to support its charges. The discovery requests at issue were not aimed at obtaining evidence to help the agency meet its burden of proof, but instead sought information on how the appellant might attempt to explain his conduct or otherwise rebut the charges. Accordingly, deeming the agency to have proven its charges because the appellant did not respond to the agency's discovery requests was not appropriate. Rather, an appropriate sanction would have been to preclude the appellant from putting on evidence in support of any defense or rebuttal to the charges with respect to which he had not provided discovery responses. The Board's regulation envisions exactly such a sanction in an appropriate case, 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a)(2), and assuming arguendo that the appellant's discovery responses did not comply with the administrative judge's order, this is such a case.

Furthermore, while the Board is not bound by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, it may look to them for guidance. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.72(a); Kravitz v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 M.S.P.R. 44, 47 (1997). Doing so is appropriate here because the standard under the Federal Rules for imposing sanctions for failure to cooperate in discovery--what the court determines is "just" under the circumstances--is similar to the "necessary to serve the ends of justice" standard in the Board's regulations. Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(b)(2); see Chimie v. PPG Industries, Inc., 402 F.3d 1371, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (a district court has broad discretion to impose a "just" sanction for a party's failure to provide discovery responses, such as precluding the party from introducing designated matters into evidence).

A sanction under the Federal Rules should be "proportionate" to the offense, and, when the possibility of a severe sanction is raised, a court should consider carefully whether a lesser sanction would be more appropriate for a particular violation. Bonds v. District of Columbia, 93 F.3d 801, 808 (D.C. Cir. 1996). A "litigation-ending" sanction or a sanction that "approaches a default judgment" is a last resort, considering the value that the American judicial system places on getting to the merits of controversies. Id. Here, relieving the agency of its burden of proving its charges and sustaining those charges was not proportionate to the appellant's failure to respond to discovery requests directed at obtaining information on the evidence he intended to produce to defend against or rebut the charges. Thus, principles developed under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure also lead to the conclusion that a more appropriate sanction would have been to preclude the appellant from presenting evidence related to the disputed discovery requests. Such a sanction is "just" because it recognizes that, although "relevant and perhaps dispositive [evidence]" is being excluded, fairness dictates that evidence be subject to "fair testing [by the opposing party] in an even handed process." Perez-Perez v. Popular Leasing Rental, Inc., 993 F.2d 281, 287 n.4 (1st Cir. 1993).

The remaining issue here is whether the appellant should be sanctioned at all. The agency contends that the appellant's discovery responses were incomplete but, because the agency does not elaborate and neither party has submitted those responses, it is unclear what evidence, if any, the appellant should be precluded from introducing. If, for example, the appellant responded to discovery requests relating to the first and second charges but not to the third charge, precluding him from presenting evidence to rebut the first and second charges would be disproportionate to the offense and would not "serve the ends of justice." See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a). We find that it is necessary to remand this appeal in order to determine whether the appellant complied with the administrative judge's discovery order and, if not, what sanction, if any, is appropriate for his non-compliance, taking into account the principles discussed above.

The Federal Circuit, reviewing a dismissal of a case in the Claims Court, emphasized the need to proceed with caution before dismissing a case for discovery failings, Intelligent Investments, Inc. v. United States, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP):

Dismissal of a case with prejudice as a discovery sanction is a "harsh remedy," Ingalls Shipbuilding, Inc. v. United States, 857 F.2d 1448, 1451 (Fed. Cir. 1988); "severe," National Hockey League, 427 U.S. at 643; and "universally recognized as a sanction of last resort," Genentech, Inc. v. U.S. International Trade Commission (ITC), 122 F.3d 1409, 1423 (Fed. Cir. 1997); see also 9 Charles A. Wright & Arthur R. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure § 2369 (4th ed. updated Apr. 2022). In Genentech, which interpreted Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 37 in applying the counterpart ITC rule, we held that "courts are required, before imposing that sanction, to consider fully all the surrounding circumstances, such as the degree of culpability, the amount of prejudice, and the availability of less drastic sanctions." 122 F.3d at 1423; see id. at 1411, 1418 (explaining the ITC rule's relationship to Rule 37).

It is enough for us, in this appeal, to focus on an aspect of the culpability component. Specifically, following the Supreme Court, we have long held that dismissal as a discovery sanction "is authorized only when the failure to comply with [a] court order is due to willfulness or bad faith and not from the inability to comply with the order." Hendler, 952 F.2d at 1382 (citing National Hockey League, 427 U.S. at 640); see Societe Internationale, 357 U.S. at 212; Ingalls Shipbuilding, 857 F.2d at 1451; Adkins v. United States, 816 F.2d 1580, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (citing National Hockey League, 427 U.S. at 640); 8B Charles A. Wright & Arthur R. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure § 2283 (3d ed. updated Apr. 2022) ("[T]he holding of Societe Internationale is that a failure to respond to a court order to produce [discovery] is a noncompliance, and brings Rule 37 into play,...but that the sanction of dismissal cannot be imposed if the failure was due to inability to comply."). The Claims Court's decision here departed from that legal rule.

A possible sanction, should the appellant fail to comply with discovery orders, is an order canceling a hearing, Fushikoshi v. USDA, SF-0752-14-0599-I-1 (NP 10/29/2015), or an order barring the appellant's hearing testimony. See McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR 192, 206 (1992) ("A party's refusal to cooperate with reasonable discovery requests is a serious matter; the Board has not hesitated to impose sanctions where an administrative judge has failed to do so when a party willfully and flagrantly disobeys a legitimate discovery order of the administrative judge."); cf. Butler v. Dept. of Air Force, 73 MSPR 313, 317-18 (1997) (AJ properly disregarded the testimony of the appellant's witness, who refused to be subjected to cross-examination). If the hearing is canceled as a sanction against the appellant, the AJ must take care to ensure the agency is not inadvertently prejudiced through an inability to challenge the appellant's evidence. See Fushikoshi, supra. Brite v. Dept. of Army, DC-0432-15-0940-I-1 (NP 3/7/2023), determined the AJ could cancel a hearing for failure of the appellant to cooperate in discovery:

The Board has held that, when an appellant fails to satisfy an order to provide adequate responses to an agency's discovery requests, it would be appropriate to sanction the appellant by precluding him from introducing evidence concerning the information sought. Wagner v. Department of Homeland Security, 105 M.S.P.R. 67, ¶ 13 (2007) (citing 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a)(2)). In addition, the Board's regulations provide that an appropriate sanction in such a case might include, among other things, drawing an inference in favor of the agency regarding the information sought. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a)(1). Here, despite the appellant's failure to satisfy an order to provide adequate discovery responses, the administrative judge neither precluded the appellant from introducing any evidence nor drew any inferences in favor of the agency.

The Board has held that, when an appellant fails to satisfy an order to provide adequate responses to an agency's discovery requests, it would be appropriate to sanction the appellant by precluding him from introducing evidence concerning the information sought. Wagner v. Department of Homeland Security, 105 M.S.P.R. 67, ¶ 13 (2007) (citing 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a)(2)). In addition, the Board's regulations provide that an appropriate sanction in such a case might include, among other things, drawing an inference in favor of the agency regarding the information sought. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a)(1). Here, despite the appellant's failure to satisfy an order to provide adequate discovery responses, the administrative judge neither precluded the appellant from introducing any evidence nor drew any inferences in favor of the agency.

Instead, the administrative judge canceled the hearing and provided the parties the opportunity to introduce additional evidence and argument before she decided the appeal based on the written record. The Board has recognized that an appellant's right to a hearing should not be denied as a sanction absent extraordinary circumstances and that a single failure to comply with an order is generally not sufficient to justify such a drastic sanction. Sims v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 101, ¶ 7 (2001). The Board has also held, however, that a single failure to comply with a discovery order may allow for the more extreme sanction of dismissal if the appellant's defiance of the discovery order is willful. Roth v. Department of Transportation, 54 M.S.P.R. 172, 176-77 (1992), aff'd, 988 F.2d 130 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).

Here, considering the administrative judge's granting of the agency's motion to compel, the clarity of the administrative judge's instructions, the appellant's failure to comply with those instructions, and the appellant's responses to the show cause order, we find that preponderant evidence demonstrates that the appellant intended to provide nonresponsive answers to the agency's discovery requests and disobey the administrative judge's order. We further find that the appellant's failure to answer the agency's discovery requests in good faith constituted conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice and that cancelling the hearing was appropriate to prevent unfair prejudice to the agency. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(e). We also find that the appellant's arguments that the administrative judge failed to give appropriate consideration to his pro se status, that the information sought by the agency was immaterial, and that the Board should have appointed an attorney to represent him, are all without merit.

In Smets v. Dept. of Navy, 117 MSPR 164, 169-71 ¶¶ 11-14, 2011 MSPB 97 (2011), the AJ properly precluded evidence to support a disability discrimination claim when the appellant repeatedly failed to cooperate in providing her deposition testimony:

An administrative judge may impose sanctions upon a party as necessary to serve the ends of justice. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43. When a party fails to comply with an order, the administrative judge may "[p]rohibit the party failing to comply with the order from introducing evidence concerning the information sought, or from otherwise relying upon testimony related to that information." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a)(2). The imposition of sanctions is a matter within the administrative judge's sound discretion, and absent a showing that such discretion has been abused, the administrative judge's determination will not be found to constitute reversible error. Bilger v. Department of Justice, 33 M.S.P.R. 602, 607 (1987), aff'd, 847 F.2d 842 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table).

The appellant has not shown that the administrative judge abused her discretion when she precluded the appellant from submitting additional evidence regarding her claim of disability discrimination after the appellant did not comply with the administrative judge's order to appear for a deposition. See Simon v. Department of Commerce, 111 M.S.P.R. 381, ¶¶ 12, 14 (2009) (although the administrative judge "went too far" in striking the appellant's affirmative defenses as a sanction for her failure to respond to an order regarding affirmative defenses, the administrative judge might have achieved the same result consistent with the Board's regulations by barring the appellant from presenting evidence regarding her affirmative defenses); Wagner v. Department of Homeland Security, 105 M.S.P.R. 67, ¶¶ 13, 15 (2007) (an appropriate sanction for the appellant's failure to respond to the agency's discovery requests would have been to preclude the appellant from putting on evidence in support of any defense or rebuttal to the charges with respect to which he had not provided discovery responses; such a sanction is "just" under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure because it recognizes that although "relevant and perhaps dispositive evidence is being excluded, fairness dictates that evidence be subject to fair testing by the opposing party in an even[-] handed process") (internal citations omitted). To the extent that the appellant claims that the administrative judge precluded her from testifying, the appellant withdrew her request for a hearing and requested a decision based on the written record before the administrative judge ruled on the motion for sanctions. Thus, any failure to testify resulted from the appellant's withdrawal of her hearing request, not from any action by the administrative judge.

Although the appellant contends that the administrative judge erred in imposing the sanction because she did not first provide written notice of an intent to impose a sanction and an opportunity to respond, the appellant has identified no Board regulation or other authority supporting such a requirement. While chapter 3, paragraph 7 of the Judge's Handbook, entitled "Acknowledgment and Show Cause Orders," does provide that "the Board requires that even beyond jurisdiction, the [administrative judge] must provide an explanation of the burdens and methods of proof of any claim as to which the appellant has some or all of the burden of proof or production in an appeal," it does not require written notice and an opportunity to respond prior to imposing sanctions for a party's failure to comply with an administrative judge's written order that notifies the party that sanctions may be imposed for failure to comply. In fact, chapter 8, paragraph 9 of the Judge's Handbook provides that administrative judges have the authority to order the taking of depositions, and that the Board has upheld the imposition of sanctions for a party's failure to comply with discovery orders.

As set forth above, the sanction at issue prohibited the appellant from presenting additional evidence regarding her claim of disability discrimination. Because such a claim of discrimination may not be brought in an IRA appeal, the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge's sanction affected her substantive rights in the IRA appeal. See Marren v. Department of Justice, 51 M.S.P.R. 632, 638-39 (1991) (IRA appeals are not subject to the provisions of 5 U.S.C. § 7701 or § 7702; thus, the Board lacks the authority to decide, in conjunction with an IRA appeal, the merits of an allegation of discrimination), aff'd, 980 F.2d 745 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table), and modified on other grounds by Robinson v. U.S. Postal Service, 63 M.S.P.R. 307, 323 n.13 (1994).

See Huffman v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0752-14-0047-I-1 (NP 4/5/2023) (affirming sanction precluding appellant from testifying or from submitting evidence that was the subject of the order granting the agency's motion to compel).

The AJ's rulings on discovery, and associated sanctions for failure to comply with discovery orders, are subject to review for abuse of discretion. Sanctions were set aside in Dunbar v. Dept. of Transp., DC-0752-14-1003-I-1 (NP 11/15/2016). The AJ failed to explain the status of discovery during a case suspension, the agency filed motions concerning discovery without first consulting with the appellant, and the AJ ruled on those motions without allowing the appellant a reasonable time to respond to the motions. One or more of the AJ's rulings were issued during the period of the case suspension, when the better procedure would have been to take the case off suspension to adjudicate those motions.
