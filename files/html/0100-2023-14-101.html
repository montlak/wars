b. Reassignment

The Board expressly declined to state whether it would order reassignment of an appellant, other than through demotion, in mitigating an unreasonable penalty. See Hernandez v. USPS, 12 MSPR 275, 276 n.1 (1982). The Board reviewed reassignments with appealable adverse actions, e.g., demotions, McCoy v. DOD, 20 MSPR 284, 287 (1984):

[W]e note that although the Board has previously held that the reasoning in Douglas is generally applicable to performance-based actions taken under 5 USC Chapter 75, Springer v. U.S. Postal Service, 7 MSPB 63 (1981), the Board has also noted that such factors as the employee's past disciplinary record, past work record and the adequacy and effectiveness of alternative sanctions are not equally applicable to performance cases. Van Gilder v. Department of the Air Force, 9 MSPB 572 at 574 (1982). The Board has further held that a relevant factor in determining the appropriateness of an agency penalty of removal for unsatisfactory performance is whether the nature of appellant's deficient performance could adversely affect his performance in a different position. Hatler v. Department of the Air Force, 6 MSPB 605, 607 (1981). In the instant case, the record shows that despite appellant's 26 years of satisfactory service prior to the action involved in this case, he failed to exert sufficient effort to perform basic receiving procedures as a Warehouse Worker, including such simple tasks as counting items of property. Thus, the agency had reasonable grounds to believe that his performance would not change if he was suspended, reassigned or demoted to another position.

The Board implied the ability of an AJ to authorize an agency to reassign an employee, as part of a sustained adverse action penalty, Chavez v. SBA, 121 MSPR 168, 184 ¶ 26, 2014 MSPB 37 (2014), although reassignment was not justified:

In rendering his penalty determination, the administrative judge additionally found that the agency could reassign the appellant "to a different position for which he is qualified." Prior Board decisions which have upheld reassignment as a part of an employee's penalty under chapter 75, however, have involved factual findings that the employee cannot perform his prior position given the nature of his sustained misconduct and that there exist vacancies within the agency to which the appellant could be reassigned. See, e.g., Wilson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 74 M.S.P.R. 65, 69 (1997) (finding that reassignment of a law enforcement official to a vacant maintenance worker position was appropriate due to the appellant's lack of rehabilitation potential in the law enforcement field and the nature of responsibility and trust in supervisory law enforcement positions). The administrative judge, however, made no such findings in this case, and we find that the appellant's sustained charges do not preclude the agency from returning him to his prior position as a Regional Communications Director. See id.; see also Blyther v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 537, ¶ 8 (2009) (an initial decision must identify all material issues of fact and law, as well as the authorities on which that reasoning rests). Accordingly, the administrative judge's order permitting the agency to reassign the appellant to a vacancy within the agency for which he is qualified is VACATED.

Wilson, cited in Chavez, involved an agency-initiated demotion and reassignment that was sustained. Ortolano v. SSA, NY-0752-13-0155-I-1 (NP 2/4/2015), seemingly cut off the ability of an AJ to direct reassignment as mitigation, drawing a (bad) analogy to cases involving indefinite suspensions or removals of individuals whose clearances are suspended or revoked. Ms. Ortolano was removed for excessive absence. The agency decision process was challenged as lacking in due process because the deciding official testified that she did not have the authority to reassign the appellant to another location:

...We disagree that lacking authority to reassign an employee to a different facility renders unconstitutional a deciding official's decision to sustain the removal. The Board has recently clarified in the security clearance context that due process requires that an employee be permitted to invoke the discretion of a deciding official who has the authority to select from among alternative penalties, to the extent such alternatives are permitted, feasible, and within management's purview. Ryan v. Department of Homeland Security, 121 M.S.P.R. 460 (2014). Reassignment, however, is not an alternative penalty.[4] Further, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held in Griffin v. Defense Mapping Agency, 864 F.2d 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1989), and Lyles v. Department of the Army, 864 F.2d 1581 (Fed. Cir. 1989), that if an appellant does not have a right to be considered for a reassignment following the suspension of her security clearance unless granted by agency policy, statute, or regulation, then she does not have a greater due process right to be considered for such a reassignment. Here, it is undisputed that the agency was not obligated to reassign the appellant. Applying the foregoing principles, because the appellant did not have right to be considered for reassignment, it was not a violation of due process for the deciding official to lack the unilateral authority to reassign her.

[4] For purposes of this decision, when we are discussing a "reassignment," we are not referring to situations involving management-directed reassignments, which also are not per se penalties. Such reassignments may occur when the agency is trying to avoid a reduction-in-force or for other reasons such as when management laterally moves an employee within the organizations or between organizations to better use that employee's skills in another equivalent position.

If the employee does not establish disability discrimination and is removed for physical inability to perform the job, the Board considers the agency's ability to have avoided removal by demotion to a different job as a component of its mitigation analysis. See Marshall-Carter v. VA, 94 MSPR 518, 523-25 ¶¶ 12-14 (2003).
