3. Filing by Facsimile

5 CFR 1201.4 (2022), providing definitions, states:

(l) Date of filing. ...The date of filing by facsimile is the date of the facsimile....

As the regulation states, facsimile filing is acceptable. See Meister v. OPM, 52 MSPR 508, 512 (1992) (noting as well that the agency also filed the PFR by mail). The fax number for MSPB Headquarters is 202-653-7130. Under Biter v. ICC, 76 MSPR 82, 86-87(1997):

When a facsimile is transmitted at a time when the Board is not open for business, the date of filing is normally considered to be the date imprinted on the facsimile.... If, however, a party can show by credible, unrebutted evidence that a facsimile was actually transmitted on a date other than the machine-generated date shown on the facsimile, the machine-generated date will not govern.

If timeliness depends on the date and time of a fax, and if the Board has no record of receipt of the PFR, the appellant should submit evidence, e.g., a fax confirmation sheet, to support the claim of timely filing. See Oglesby v. TVA, 90 MSPR 595, 597 ¶¶ 3-5 (2002). Dooley v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 684, 686 (1993), considered the date and time stamped by the appellant's fax machine in determining the date and time of filing. See Young v. VA, 83 MSPR 187, 191 n.2 (1999) (PFR due on September 4 was timely filed when faxed to the Board at 10:55 P.M. on September 3 and stamped as received by the Board on September 4 at 6:40 A.M.); Biter v. ICC, 76 MSPR 82, 86 (1997) (same analysis; proof of an improper fax clock setting was buttressed by a telephone bill showing the actual time of transmission to the MSPB); Casarez v. Dept. of Army, 70 MSPR 131, 132 n.1 (1996) (quoting Dooley, ("[T]he date of filing by facsimile 'is the date of the facsimile.'")); Thompson v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 145, 147 (1994) (accepting unrebutted evidence that the clock imprinter on the sender's fax was approximately an hour fast due to time changes and power interruptions); Jackson v. OPM, 62 MSPR 6, 9 (1994) (where a fax is transmitted when the Board is not open for business, the filing date is determined by the imprint on the fax).

The Board considered a faxed PFR untimely when the evidence was not clear concerning the fax transmittal from the appellant's representative and the Board's equipment showed the PFR was filed a day late, Stafford v. USPS, DA-0353-11-0481-B-1 (NP 8/13/2013):

In response to the second show-cause order, the appellant submits a declaration under penalty of perjury indicating that she filed her petition for review by facsimile from her representative's machine at 23:25 and 23:31 central standard time as shown on the Query Call Details and that she did not fax any documents on September 14, 2012, at 02:21 to anyone. The appellant's representative submits a declaration indicating that the appellant filed her petition for review on September 13, 2012, at 23:25 and 23:31 central standard time.

Based on our review of the record, we find that the appellant has failed to meet her burden of showing that her petition for review was timely. If the appellant submitted her petition for review via facsimile on September 13, 2012, at 23:25, as she contends, it follows that the date imprinted on the facsimile of the petition for review received by the Clerk's office would be September 13, 2012, at 23:25, rather than September 14, 2012, at 02:21. Although the date and time imprinted on the facsimile of the appellant's petition for review do not correspond with the date and time of the transmissions from the appellant's representative's facsimile number to the Clerk of the Board's facsimile number, as reflected on the "Query Call Details," the appellant has made no effort to explain this discrepancy. Given the appellant's failure to address why the facsimile of her petition for review reflects a filing date of September 14, 2012, when she claims to have filed her petition for review by facsimile on September 13, 2012, we find that the documentation submitted by the appellant in support of her motion to accept her petition for review as timely does not provide a sufficient basis for establishing that her petition for review was timely filed. Cf. Thompson, 62 M.S.P.R. at 146-47 (finding that the petition for review was timely filed where the appellant's representative submitted a sworn statement explaining that there was a discrepancy between the time he submitted the petition for review to the Board via facsimile and the time imprinted on the cover sheet of the facsimile because the clock on his fax machine did not show the correct time).

Moreover, other than the "Query Call Details" for September 13, 2012, the appellant has failed to provide any documentation to support her claim that her petition for review was timely. Although afforded an opportunity to do so, the appellant has not submitted a report of the call activity for the appellant's representative's facsimile number on September 14, 2012. This documentation would be relevant to the issue of whether the appellant's petition for review was sent to the Board via facsimile from the appellant's representative's facsimile number on September 13, 2012, at 23:25, rather than on September 14, 2012, at 02:21. Specifically, a report of the call activity from the appellant's representative's facsimile number on September 14, 2012, would show whether there was a transmission from the appellant's representative's facsimile number to the Clerk of the Board's facsimile number at 02:21 on September 14, 2012, as the imprint on the facsimile reflects. Such a transmission would indicate that, contrary to the appellant's apparent assertion, the appellant submitted her petition for review via facsimile on September 14, 2012, at 02:21. Conversely, the absence of such a transmission would support the appellant's claim that the appellant submitted her petition for review via facsimile on September 13, 2012, rather than on September 14, 2012.

In sum, based on the paucity of evidence in support of the appellant's claim that she filed her petition for review on September 13, 2012, and the appellant's failure to explain the discrepancy between the date and time she claims to have filed her petition for review and the date and time imprinted on the facsimile of her petition for review, we find that the appellant has failed to show that her petition for review was timely. A declaration subscribed as true under penalty of perjury, if uncontested, proves the facts it asserts. Woodall v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, 30 M.S.P.R. 271, 273 (1986). Here, however, the declarations submitted by the appellant and her representative are contradicted by other evidence in the record, and no explanation for this contradiction has been provided. See Schaefer v. U.S. Postal Service, 42 M.S.P.R. 592, 595 (1989) (finding an affidavit attesting to a timely mailing rebutted by other evidence).

If the facsimile time stamp on the sender's machine is obviously wrong, the Board looks at its own records to determine the time and date of transmission (or receipt). See Wright v. VA, 85 MSPR 358, 361 ¶ 6 (2000) (when the imprint of the sending machine was clearly wrong, the Board considered a PFR filed on the date shown on the certificate of service when the PFR was actually faxed to the Board on that date). If the Board's fax machine suffers a disabling condition, the Board makes allowances for the failings of its equipment by permitting what might otherwise be a belated fax filing of a PFR. See Jimenez v. SSA, 100 MSPR 287, 290 n.* (2005). Under earlier law the date generated on a fax served as proof of the time that the fax was sent, but it was unclear whether the controlling date on the fax was the one stamped on it by the sender's or the receiver's machine. See Sacchino v. EPA, 57 MSPR 469, 472 (1993) ("the date of a filing by facsimile is...deemed to be the date and time automatically recorded on the facsimile sheet") (citing Jude v. Dept. of Treasury, 52 MSPR 5, 8 (1991) (referring to the date stamp of the receiving office)); Williams v. DLA, 53 MSPR 269, 272-73 (1992) (noting the existence of an index maintained by the Clerk of the Board); cf. SSA v. Price, 94 MSPR 337, 341 ¶ 7 (2003) (excusing 34 minute delay in fax filing caused by "technical difficulties"). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Facsimile" for decisions discussing proof of time of faxed transmissions of appeals.]

One may fax as well as mail a PFR. The fax confirmation provides some proof of filing, and one hopes the mail will get through. If the fax is late but the PFR is mailed on time, the PFR is timely. See Bane v. USPS, 59 MSPR 13, 15-16 (1993).
