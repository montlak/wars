e. Response of Agency to Disclosure

If the agency acts on disclosures by the appellant, the appellant's contention that his disclosure involved a matter covered by the IRA will be bolstered. See Delgado v. DOJ, 966 F.3d 556, 562 (7th Cir. 2020) (amended, without change to quoted portion, on petition for rehearing, Delgado v. DOJ, 977 F.3d 1295 (7th Cir. 2020)) ("Delgado's supervisors agreed in substance with his account of his disclosure. They took his report seriously enough that they relayed it to the ATF's Special Agent in Charge in Chicago and to the United States Attorney's Office."); Armstrong v. DOJ, 107 MSPR 375, 384 n.6, 2007 MSPB 280 (2007) (overruled on other grounds, Edwards v. Dept. of Labor, 2022 MSPB 9 (2022)) ("Additionally, as the AJ noted, the fact that the agency investigated the promotion practices at PRAO weighs in the appellant's favor. See Conrad v. Department of Justice, 99 M.S.P.R. 636, ¶ 13 (2005)."); Gonzales v. Dept. of Navy, 99 MSPR 97 (2005) (considering reasonableness of allegation of threat to public health and safety based on outcome of subsequent agency investigation of the incident); Wojcicki v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 628, 634, 640 (1996) (agency actions to remedy the appellant's safety concerns supported the conclusion that those concerns were based on a reasonable belief); cf. Swinford v. Dept. of Transp., 107 MSPR 433, 437 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 296 (2007) ("OIG considered the appellant's allegations to be sufficiently viable so as to require conducting an investigation and, ultimately, corrective action."); Conrad v. DOJ, 99 MSPR 636 ¶ 13 (2005) ("Given that the appellant's second-line supervisor believed that the matter should be reported, that OPR's own employee suggested the method of reporting, and that the agency investigated the matter, we find that the AJ erred in concluding that the appellant's belief was unreasonable.").

But the agency's evaluation of a situation is not determinative. "[t]he agency's failure to recognize the protected nature of the disclosures does not affect the disclosures' coverage under 5 USC 2302(b)(8)," Bump v. Dept. of Interior, 69 MSPR 354, 362 (1996), although agency evaluations in the form of IG reports arising out of the allegedly protected disclosures may furnish background information that is helpful in evaluating the reasonableness of the disclosure from the perspective of what is known among informed personnel in the agency. See Chianelli v. EPA, 86 MSPR 651, 657-58 ¶ 15 (2000) (reasonableness of the appellant's belief was undercut by the results of an OIG report showing proper program management); cf. Special Counsel v. Spears, 75 MSPR 639, 658-59 (1997) (referencing an IG report evaluating funds spent on training). The issue is not whether the appellant proves that the condition reported established gross mismanagement or any of the other situations detailed under 5 USC 2302(b)(8)(A)(ii). Instead, "[h]e must show that the matter reported was one which a reasonable person in his position would believe evidenced" the statutorily described situation. Carolyn v. Dept. of Interior, 63 MSPR 684, 690 (1994), overruled in part, Thomas v. Dept. of Treasury, 77 MSPR 224 (1998), overruled, Ganski v. Dept. of Interior, 86 MSPR 32 (2000); cf. Weber v. Dept. of Army, 9 F.3d 97, 101 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("In general, the WPA does not give the Board the right to review the substance of whistleblowing claims.").

Stevens v. Dept. of Transp., AT-1221-15-0481-W-1 (NP 7/1/2016), determined that an IG report lent no support to the reasonableness of the appellant's whistleblowing allegations:

In addition, the appellant's assertion that the IG's lengthy investigation establishes the reasonableness of his belief, does not provide a basis for disturbing the initial decision, as many factors could delay or extend an investigation. We further note that the IG found no violation of policy or merit system principles and that the agency's authority and rationale to reassign employees noncompetitively appeared sufficient on its face. At most, notwithstanding the IG investigation and report, the appellant's disclosure constitutes a general philosophical or policy disagreement with the agency regarding the reorganization, which does not otherwise constitute a protected disclosure under the WPEA. Webb v. Department of the Interior, 122 M.S.P.R. 248, ¶ 8 (2015) (citing 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(D)); Luecht v. Department of the Navy, 87 M.S.P.R. 297, ¶ 12 (2000) (concluding that the appellant's statement that he met with the agency IG to report "ongoing fraud in [Resource Sharing] contracts" lacked the detail necessary to constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of a violation of law, rule or regulation, gross mismanagement, gross waste of funds, abuse of authority, or danger to public health or safety). After extensively reviewing the appellant's written submissions, we conclude that he failed to allege nonfrivolously any of the types of wrongdoing set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), and he did not make a protected disclosure.
