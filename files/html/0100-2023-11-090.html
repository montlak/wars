D. PREVIOUS DETERMINATIONS OF DISPUTED FACTS, ISSUES, CLAIMS: RES JUDICATA, COLLATERAL ESTOPPEL, AND LAW OF THE CASE--THE DOCTRINES

Federal employees have a variety of appellate channels to challenge decisions of their superiors. Important is the idea that cases should not be relitigated; once the appellate channel is chosen, the resolution there achieved should not be retested through another appellate channel concerned with the same issues and facts. The Board will not relitigate issues decided between parties in previous Board proceedings or in other administrative appeals, unless there is new or material evidence that was unavailable when the record in the prior proceedings closed. See McKenzie v. USPS, 1 MSPR 496, 498, 1 MSPB 480 (1980), followed in Ellis v. USPS, 33 MSPR 685, 686 (1987). If the appellant files an appeal on a matter already closed out by a prior final decision, the AJ may forward the later appeal to the Board for consideration as a motion to reopen the prior appeal; however, the AJ should not treat the submission as a new appeal and decide it on the merits because the AJ believes the first (and final) Board decision was wrongly decided. See Golden v. USPS, 60 MSPR 268, 271-72 (1994). Our discussion focuses upon what criteria must be met for past litigation to bar current or future litigation of the same issues, facts, claims, and cases. [Refer to Chapter 11, "Double Jeopardy" for decisions involving arguably repetitive penalties for the same conduct.]

To avoid repeated adjudication of decided issues, courts apply forms of issue preclusion. Del Mar Avionics, Inc. v. Quinton Instrument Co., 836 F.2d 1320, 1323-24 (Fed. Cir. 1987), explained in patent infringement litigation:

...As described by the doctrine of "issue preclusion," the relitigation of issues previously decided is barred on principles of finality and repose. 18 C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice & Procedure Sec. 4402 at 10 (1981 ed.). See Commissioner v. Sunnen, 333 U.S. 591, 601, 68 S. Ct. 715, 721, 92 L. Ed. 898 (1948):

Of course, where a question of fact essential to the judgment is actually litigated and determined in the first...proceeding, the parties are bound by that determination in a subsequent proceeding even though the cause of action is different.

See also Messenger v. Anderson, 225 U.S. 436, 444, 32 S. Ct. 739, 740, 56 L. Ed. 1152 (1912); Stewart-Warner v. City of Pontiac, Michigan, 767 F.2d 1563, 1568, 226 USPQ 676, 679 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

Res judicata is of the same effect with respect to a final decision between the parties as to the identical dispute. As the Court stated in Cromwell v. County of Sac, 94 U.S. (4 Otto) 351, 353, 24 L. Ed. 195 (1876):

But where the second action between the same parties is upon a different claim or demand, the judgment in the prior action operates as an estoppel only as to those matters in issue or points controverted, upon the determination of which the finding or verdict was rendered.

Res judicata and collateral estoppel are doctrines applied to prevent relitigation of past events already subjected to litigation. See Mohammed v. Dept. of Army, 780 Fed. Appx. 870, 879 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP) ("Res judicata and collateral estoppel require litigation of some sort."). Res judicata, Latin for "a thing judged," is invoked to avoid successive appeals to the Board of claims that were or should have been treated in an earlier appeal. Application of the doctrine avoids the subsequent assertion of additional causes of action springing from the same transactional facts that should have been litigated together in the first case. See Heim v. United States (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-5004); cf. Sowinski v. Calif. Air Resources Bd., 971 F.3d 1371, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) ("The application of preclusion 'encourages reliance on judicial decisions, bars vexatious litigation, and frees the courts to resolve other disputes,' Brown v. Felsen, 442 U.S. 127, 131 (1979)."); Coronel v. OPM, 101 MSPR 407, 410 ¶ 5, 2006 MSPB 67 (2006) (under the doctrine of res judicata, the petitioners "are not entitled to return to the Board based on a new theory of their cases"), (aff'd, Coronel v. OPM, 193 Fed. Appx. 967 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP).

Lexicographers have their way with words. They say more with less. From the author's dilapidated copy of Black's Law Dictionary (Revised Fourth Edition):

Res judicata: "Rule that final judgment or decree on merits by court of competent jurisdiction is conclusive of rights of parties or their privies in all later suits or points and matters determined in former suit."

Collateral estoppel: "The collateral determination of a question by a court having general jurisdiction of the subject."

Shall we stop there?

Black v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP No. 2015-3223), summarized the doctrines:

...Res judicata bars relitigation of issues that were, or could have been, raised in a prior action, Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980), and applies when: "(1) the prior decision was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases," Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005). Collateral estoppel applies when: "(i) the issue previously adjudicated is identical with that now presented, (ii) that issue was actually litigated in the prior case, (iii) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the end-decision then made, and (iv) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action." Morgan v. Dep't of Energy, 424 F.3d 1271, 1274-75 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

...Both res judicata and collateral estoppel apply even if new evidence exists or the aggrieved party believes the earlier case was wrongly denied. "[R] es judicata and collateral estoppel relieve parties of the cost and vexation of multiple lawsuits, conserve judicial resources, and...encourage reliance on adjudication." Allen, 449 U.S. at 94....

The law of the case doctrine avoids repeated litigation of a single issue at various phases of a single case or appeal.

Judicial estoppel, prevents an appellant from arguing a position in one case, receiving a favorable result based on the position, and then in another case arguing a position contrary to that argued in the first case.

A doctrine designed to preclude relitigation of issues is "stare decisis": when a question of law has been settled, it forms a precedent that is then applied to all future cases only if the same, or substantially similar, question is involved. Stare decisis is the business of decisional precedent--the justification for this Guide.

The doctrines of issue preclusion are complex, intertwined, and sometimes confused by the Board, by the courts, and by writers of law texts. Res judicata applies to decisions of agencies acting in a judicial capacity. An agency, for example, OPM, may correct a decision and change a result without running afoul of the doctrine of res judicata. See Rogers v. OPM, 87 F.3d 471, 475 (Fed. Cir. 1996). For a brief discussion of the doctrines of res judicata and collateral estoppel, and their distinctions, refer to McCandless v. MSPB, 996 F.2d 1193 (Fed. Cir. 1993), McCandless v. USPS, 54 MSPR 76, 81 (1992), and Arkla, Inc. v. United States, 37 F.3d 621, 623-24 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (reviewing the doctrine of collateral estoppel). For application of the doctrines of res judicata and the law of the case, discussed below, in enforcement litigation, refer to Vargo v. USPS, 62 MSPR 156, 159 (1994) [discussed in Chapter 16, "Multiple Enforcement Petitions; Res Judicata"].

A party or the AJ may raise res judicata at the onset of an appeal. See Winston v. Dept. of Labor (Fed. Cir. 1997 NP No. 96-3245); Moss v. Dept. of Air Force, 82 MSPR 309, 313 ¶ 11 (1999) (Board raises and decides an issue of collateral estoppel sua sponte). The initial decision dismissing for res judicata or collateral estoppel should, under Mycka v. OPM, 56 MSPR 675, 679 n.4 (1993), be based on a record that provides an evidentiary basis for the dismissal. The decision of the AJ should fully articulate the evidentiary and legal basis for the dismissal. When an appeal is dismissed on the basis of res judicata, the Board will inform the appellant of his right to further review by the Federal Circuit but the notice of appeal rights will not include information as to any right of review of the EEOC or a federal district court. Donahue v. USPS, 100 MSPR 387, 398 n.7 (2005).

If the Board does not have jurisdiction over a case, according to Murry v. DOJ, 411 Fed. Appx. 309, 311-12 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), it cannot rule that res judicata precludes its consideration of the matter:

In addition to a lack of jurisdiction, the MSPB found that Murry's whistleblower claim was barred for res judicata based on the previously filed MSPB Removal Appeal. The Department of Justice, in response to Murry's appeal, concedes that this finding by the Board was erroneous. The Board cannot make a decision on merits issues when it is without jurisdiction to hear and decide a case. Schmittling v. Dep't of Army, 219 F.3d 1332, 1337 (Fed. Cir. 2000) ("If it lacks jurisdiction, the Board is without authority to decide the issues presented by a petitioner. The [Board] has only the jurisdiction conferred on it by Congress. This jurisdiction, provided by statute, creates the power of the [Board] to hear and decide a case, i.e., the Board's subject matter jurisdiction." (citations and internal quotation marks omitted)). Thus, the Board lacked jurisdiction here to render any judgment on Murry's whistleblower claim. However, this error by the Board was harmless. Id. ("Without jurisdiction, the Board's decision on the merits of a petition is a nullity.").

See Linn v. OPM, AT-0831-13-0193-I-1 (NP 12/27/2013) ("Where, as here, the Board lacks jurisdiction over a claim, it may not also render a decision on the merits with regard to that matter. See Schmittling v. Department of the Army, 219 F.3d 1332, 1337 (Fed. Cir. 2000). Under circumstances similar to this appeal, our reviewing court determined in Murry v. Department of Justice, 411 F. App'x 309, 311 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) that the Board properly dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, but erred in dismissing the appeal, in the alternative, as barred by res judicata. Following Schmittling, the court found that the Board lacked jurisdiction to render any judgment on the merits of the appellant's claim, including making a finding of res judicata. Id.").

On occasion, an adjudicator dispenses with references to res judicata or collateral estoppel and instead speaks simply of claim preclusion, Payton v. DHS, 502 Fed. Appx. 942, 944 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP):

The doctrine of claim preclusion prevents parties from litigating claims that were, or could have been, raised in an earlier action. See Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005). It applies when "(1) the prior decision was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases." Id. Claim preclusion forecloses matters that, although never litigated, could have been raised in an earlier suit. Id. at 1372 n.8 (citing Migra v. Warren City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Ed., 465 U.S. 75, 77 n.1 (1984)).

Ms. Payton's whistleblower retaliation claim meets all the requirements for claim preclusion. The Board has jurisdiction to hear claims of retaliation for whistleblower activities and it could have entertained Ms. Payton's claim in 2004 had she raised it. See, e.g., Carson, 398 F.3d at 1374. In 2005, the Board entered a final decision upholding Ms. Payton's removal on the merits. See Payton v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., Docket No. AT-0752-05-0043-I-1, 99 M.S.P.R. 669 (M.S.P.B. Sept. 15, 2005) (Table). And the same cause of action (a challenge to the 2004 removal) and same parties (Ms. Payton and the agency) were involved in both this and the 2004 appeal. Accordingly, Ms. Payton's current appeal is barred under the doctrine of claim preclusion.

Burroughs v. Dept. of Army, 559 Fed. Appx. 1002, 1006 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), similarly stated:

Second, although there is some dispute about whether or not Mr. Burroughs properly presented an allegation of a violation of 38 U.S.C. § 4311(b) in his earlier Board appeal, his claim now is barred regardless. The crucial fact is that he identifies nothing that prevented him from making that allegation in the first Board appeal. If the same transactional facts are involved, claim preclusion "prevents litigation of all grounds for, or defenses to, recovery that were previously available to the parties, regardless of whether they were asserted or determined in the prior proceeding." Brown v. Felsen, 442 U.S. 127, 131 (1979); see Levi Strauss & Co. v. Abercrombie & Fitch Trading Co., 719 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2013) ("Claim preclusion does not depend on an earlier court's resolution of a particular issue, but prevents a litigant, in certain circumstances, from pressing issues in a second suit that it could and should have raised in earlier litigation even if it did not."); Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005) ("[C]laim preclusion forecloses matters that, although never litigated or even raised, could have been advanced in an earlier suit.").

Again, from Renville v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP No. 2015-3193):

Mr. Renville's claim under USERRA is also barred under the doctrine of res judicata. Res judicata serves to limit not only claims that a party actually raised, but also claims that the party could have raised in an earlier action arising from the same transaction or occurrence. See Ammex, Inc. v. United States, 334 F.3d 1052, 1055 (Fed. Cir. 2003); see also Migra v. Warren City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Educ., 465 U.S. 75, 77 n.1 (1984) ("Claim preclusion refers to the effect of a judgment in foreclosing litigation of a matter that never has been litigated, because of a determination that it should have been advanced in an earlier suit."). Because Mr. Renville's USERRA claim relates to the same transactional facts--his separation from the Federal service--which were resolved by the settlement agreement, the Board was correct in dismissing the claim on res judicata grounds.

Cf. Slocum v. USPS, 629 Fed. Appx. 992, 994 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) ("Because Mr. Slocum could have challenged the USPS's compliance with the 2008 order in his first petition, he cannot bring that same claim in a second petition under the doctrine of res judicata. See Phillips/May Corp., 524 F.3d at 1271 ('[T]he general rule is that claim preclusion applies when "the second claim is based on the same set of transactional facts as the first."' (quoting Ammex, 334 F.3d at 1055))."); Ware v. DHHS, DC-0752-14-0313-I-1 n.3 (NP 7/9/2014) ("Collateral estoppel was the proper doctrine to rely upon, even though the term 'res judicata' is sometimes used broadly to encompass not only the doctrine of 'claim preclusion' but also the doctrine of 'issue preclusion,' or 'collateral estoppel.' Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 336 (1995) (citations omitted). The doctrines of claim preclusion and issue preclusion both concern the preclusive effects of a prior adjudication and are based on similar policy concerns: to 'relieve parties of the cost and vexation of multiple lawsuits, conserve judicial resources, and, by preventing inconsistent decisions, encourage reliance on adjudication.' Id. at 336-37 (quoting Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980)). However, there are significant differences in their prerequisites for application, as well as in their preclusive effects. Id. The Board's practice, in general, has been to use the term 'res judicata' in the narrow sense, to refer solely to the doctrine of claim preclusion. Id. at 337."). Res judicata and collateral estoppel, along with their kin, have been around so long and been subject to so many judicial interpretations that the distinctions among the doctrines are not crisp.
