4. Mixed and Whistleblowing Cases

Mixed cases (ordinarily appealable actions that contain an EEO defense) that are initiated through the agency EEO complaint process for later hearing by the Board cannot be filed with the Board until after at least partial exhaustion of the EEO complaint process. If an AJ issues an initial decision in a mixed case that is prematurely appealed, that decision is issued without statutory authority and is void ab initio. See Key v. Dept. of Interior, 12 MSPR 73, 74-75 (1982). If an AJ dismisses an appeal based on a preexisting EEO complaint that has not ripened into an appealable action, the Board may remand the case to the region for adjudication if the appeal would have become timely for regional adjudication during the pendency of the appellant's PFR. See, e.g., Price v. VA, 105 MSPR 126, 129-30 ¶ 10, 2007 MSPB 70 (2007) ("Further, she filed her petition for review on December 30, 2006, within 30 days of her receipt of the FAD. In the alternative, 120 days have now elapsed since the appellant filed her amended EEO complaint. Under either circumstance, this appeal is now ripe for adjudication. See Summerset, 100 M.S.P.R. 292, ¶ 9 (where more than 120 days had elapsed since the appellant filed his EEO complaint, the Board found that the appeal was ripe for adjudication and forwarded it to the regional office); cf. Gray v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 161, ¶ 13 (2002) (the appellant's appeal was timely filed where he filed it within 30 days of the date the agency issued the FAD in the appellant's EEO complaint)."); Ayer v. VA, 54 MSPR 638, 640 (1992) ("At the time the administrative judge issued his initial decision, dismissal was proper because 120 days had not elapsed since the time the appellant filed her formal discrimination complaint. Because 120 days have now passed since the appellant filed her formal complaint on December 31, 1991, the appeal is now properly before the Board. See Ward v. Department of the Navy, 22 M.S.P.R. 441, 443 (1984)."); 5 CFR 1201.154 (2022). But see Jones v. MSPB, 154 Fed. Appx. 197, 198 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP) (premature case did not ripen when, during the pendency of the PFR from the earlier dismissal of the appeal as premature, the agency issued its final agency decision and the appellant did not again appeal within 30 days following the issuance of that final decision). [Refer to Chapter 12, "Jurisdictional Problems with EEO Complaints," "Filing Dates," and "Procedures in Mixed Cases," for decisions discussing the timeliness of mixed case appeals and the effect of an EEO complaint challenging a proposed adverse action notice.]

IRA cases that can only be appealed through initial resort to OSC and cases that are otherwise appealable and are initiated with OSC under the Whistleblower Protection Act are subject to OSC exhaustion requirements before they may be appealed to the Board. [Process described in Chapter 13, "Whistleblowing Reprisal" and "Pleading to OSC," and "Time for Initiating IRA Appeal With OSC and MSPB."] See 5 CFR 1209.2, 1209.5 (2022). An IRA appeal, premature when filed with the Board, may become timely if OSC issues a necessary closeout letter during the pendency of a PFR; the Board then remands the case to the regional office for further adjudication. See Simnitt v. VA, 113 MSPR 313, 317 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 38 (2010); Becker v. VA, 112 MSPR 516, 519 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 206 (2009); Becker v. VA, 107 MSPR 327, 331-32 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 281 (2007) (120-day exhaustion period for an OSC complaint lapsed during the pendency of the PFR, making the matter ripe for consideration by the AJ); Morales v. SSA, 108 MSPR 583, 586 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 97 (2008); Kochanoff v. Dept. of Treasury, 98 MSPR 405, 408 ¶ 7 (2005); Brooks v. DHS, 95 MSPR 464, 467 ¶ 8 (2004).

The Board noted that the AJ properly dismisses an appeal without prejudice to its refiling if the matter appealed was, before the appeal, raised as a whistleblowing reprisal matter with OSC and the OSC process has not been exhausted by a closeout letter from OSC or the lapse of sufficient time from the initiation of the matter with OSC, Hillard v. DOD, AT-1221-10-0617-W-1 (NP 9/13/2010):

...When an employee raises a claim of reprisal based on whistleblowing and the personnel action giving rise to the claim is an otherwise appealable action, as here, the employee may elect to seek corrective action from OSC before appealing to the Board or she may file the appeal directly with the Board. Tubesing v. Department of Health & Human Services, 109 M.S.P.R. 420, ¶ 11 (2008); see 5 U.S.C. §§ 1214(a)(3), 1221(a), (b); 5 C.F.R. § 1209.2(b)(2). Therefore, if an appellant files an appeal with the Board and indicates during the proceedings that she filed a claim with OSC on the same matter prior to filing her Board appeal, it is proper for the administrative judge to dismiss the appeal if the appellant has not yet exhausted her remedy before the OSC. Tubesing, 109 M.S.P.R. 420, ¶ 11. An appellant has exhausted her remedy before OSC if she has received notice from OSC that it has terminated its investigation into her complaint, or upon the expiration of 120 days after the complaint was filed if OSC has not notified the appellant that it will seek corrective action on her behalf. See id. (citing 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3), Westmoreland v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 77 M.S.P.R. 315, 317 (1998), and 5 C.F.R. § 1209.5(a), (b)). As the administrative judge correctly explained, once the appellant exhausts her remedies with OSC, she must abide by the time limits set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3) if she decides to refile her appeal. See also 5 C.F.R. § 1209.5(a).

If a case is dismissed as premature because of an inaccurate belief that it was earlier filed with the OSC, the Board corrects the error on PFR and sends the case back to the AJ for adjudication. See Tubesing v. DHHS, 109 MSPR 420, 2008 MSPB 179 (2008). We are informed by Jundt v. VA, 113 MSPR 688, 689 n.1, 2010 MSPB 101 (2010), that if the otherwise premature IRA case ripens into maturity while pending on PFR with the Board, the Board will "affirm and forward" the case to the region rather than "vacate and remand." Jundt was relied on in Barbour v. DHHS, DC-1221-21-0517-W-1 (NP 3/31/2022), remanding to the region an IRA appeal, initially dismissed for failure to exhaust remedies with OSC, when the OSC process was completed during the pendency of the PFR: "The Board's practice is to adjudicate an appeal that was premature when it was filed but becomes ripe while pending with the Board." [Refer in Chapter 13 to "Premature Appeal Following Resort to OSC."]
