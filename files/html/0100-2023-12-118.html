C. REVIEW OF FACTUAL AND CREDIBILITY FINDINGS

Many PFRs are filed contesting factual and credibility resolutions. Few succeed. Although the Board has the authority to correct factual errors, the Board rarely does more than dismiss factual objections with a suggestion that the objections constitute "mere disagreement" with the findings of the initial decision. The decisions that follow suggest the limitations and requirements of a PFR seeking to obtain reversal on factual grounds. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Required Analysis," "Credibility Findings," concerning initial decisions.]

Although most initial decisions will explicitly resolve credibility disputes based on observations described by the AJ, when the AJ hears "live testimony" credibility determinations must be at least implicitly based on the demeanor of witnesses. Aldridge v. USDA, 111 MSPR 670, 674-75 ¶ 9, 2009 MSPB 146 (2009). But in appropriate circumstances, the Board may substitute its judgment for that of the AJ concerning the credibility of witnesses. Marotta v. DHHS, 34 MSPR 252, 257-58 (1987); see Thomas v. Dept. of Army, 2022 MSPB 35, ¶ 8 (2022) ("Moreover, the Board may overturn an administrative judge's credibility determinations when they are incomplete, inconsistent with the weight of the evidence, and do not reflect the record as a whole. Faucher v. Department of the Air Force, 96 M.S.P.R. 203, ¶ 8 (2004)."). When documents or objective evidence contradict the witness's story or when the story itself is so internally inconsistent or implausible on its face that a reasonable factfinder would not credit it, the reviewing authority may find clear error in the credibility determination. Bradley v. VA, 39 MSPR 598, 602 (1989); see Giltner v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 209, 213 (1991) (weighing notations in contemporaneous patient records against the recollections of witnesses). Although the Board will not lightly set aside AJs' credibility findings, those findings are entitled to no deference if they are based on improper considerations unrelated to the conduct of the witnesses at the hearing. Bennett v. Dept. of Air Force, 84 MSPR 132, 139 ¶ 12 (1999). Lawyers beware: AJs may give less weight to testimony elicited by leading questions of one's client. See Betts v. VA, DE-0752-13-0190-I-1 (NP 1/22/2014) (citing Zeidat v. VA, 59 MSPR 106, 109 (1993)).

For the Board to review credibility or other factual findings, the petitioner must identify the specific evidence allegedly improperly considered (or not considered) by the AJ and explain why he believes the AJ's determination to be in error--for example, by citing internal inconsistencies or inherent improbability in the determinative evidence. See Seavello v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 155, 156, 4 MSPB 239 (1980); Walsh v. USPS, NY-0752-13-0134-I-2 (NP 10/20/2015) ("The Board has held that, where the appellant presents bare assertions of perjury or falsification of documents with no evidence or argument to support those assertions, they do not provide a basis for disturbing the initial decision. Vaughn v. Department of the Treasury, 119 M.S.P.R. 605, ¶ 9 (2013)."); Tanner v. Dept. of Transp., 65 MSPR 169, 172 (1994) ("Regarding the appellant's innumerable arguments challenging the administrative judge's findings on the race/gender discrimination issue, we find it unnecessary to individually address the arguments because they are not supported by specific and persuasive factual and/or legal grounds."); Weaver v. Dept. of Navy, 2 MSPR 129, 133, 2 MSPB 297 (1980), post, aff'd, 669 F.2d 613 (9th Cir. 1982); cf. Brown v. USPS, CH-0752-08-0818-I-2 (NP 6/16/2011) ("The appellant contends that a critical witness was lying. The administrative judge found that that witness was credible based on his observation of her testimony at the hearing. The appellant's mere assertion that the witness was lying is not a sufficiently sound reason to overturn the administrative judge's determination that she was a credible witness. See Diggs v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 114 M.S.P.R. 464, ¶ 8 (2010).").

If the appellant contends that the AJ did not give sufficient weight to the appellant's evidence, the appellant is actually contending in his PFR that the AJ made an error of law by failing to apply the proper evidentiary standard. See Andrews v. USPS, 9 MSPR 86, 88-89 (1981). Without regard to how the PFR is parsed, the Board ordinarily will not reconsider the factual findings made by the AJ on the basis of allegations that he failed to give sufficient weight to the evidence introduced by the agency or that he gave too much credence to the evidence submitted by the appellant. Woodard v. Dept. of Army, 18 MSPR 492, 494 (1983);see Chin v. DOD, 2022 MSPB 34, ¶ 11 (2022) (Member Limon dissenting) ("The Board will not reconsider the factual findings of an administrative judge based on an allegation that the administrative judge failed to give sufficient weight to the evidence introduced on behalf of one party and gave too much credence to the evidence submitted by the other. Meier v. Department of the Interior, 3 M.S.P.R. 247, 256 (1980)."). An AJ cannot be said to have improperly ignored evidence because she assigned less weight to it than a party would have wished. Smith v. USPS, 41 MSPR 70, 76 (1989); see Kuriakose v. VA, 800 Fed. Appx. 889, 898 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) ("That the Board did not find this evidence persuasive does not mean that it failed to consider it."). If references to the record demonstrate only that different conclusions might have been drawn, there is no significant evidentiary question warranting full reassessment of the facts. Wickramasekera v. VA, 21 MSPR 707, 710 (1984). But AJs' conclusions should be supported. See Boyd v. Dept. of Labor, 561 Fed. Appx. 973, 977 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) ("It is true that in Whitmore we admonished that "[a]ny determination by an administrative judge that is based on findings made in the abstract and independent of the evidence which fairly detracts from his or her conclusions is unreasonable and, as such, is not supported by substantial evidence." 680 F.3d at 1376. However, in that case we found that there was "considerable countervailing evidence" that the administrative judge had "manifestly ignored, overlooked, or excluded." Id.").

The precise nature of the record references required on a PFR to challenge a credibility determination is not defined in Board decisions. The Board's review process is flexible, but there should be some reference to components of the record suggesting a reversible error in the AJ's analysis. See Ransom v. VA, CH-0752-11-0748-I-1 (NP 5/24/2013) ("The petitioning party must explain why the challenged factual determination is incorrect and identify the specific evidence in the record which demonstrates the error. Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 M.S.P.R. 129, 133 (1980). The appellant did not provide specific citations to the record, but, from our review of the investigative interviews and the testimony, we conclude that she assigns too much significance to any slight inconsistencies that might exist.... We conclude that the administrative judge considered the evidence as a whole, drew appropriate inferences, and made reasoned conclusions on issues of credibility and thus find that the administrative judge properly sustained charge 2. See Broughton v. Department of Health & Human Services, 33 M.S.P.R. 357, 359 (1987)."). As to the specificity of the PFR, the Board reaffirmed in Brotherton v. Dept. of Air Force, 54 MSPR 366, 368 (1992), citing Lawrence v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 556, 557 (1983), that although the language of a PFR may be simply drawn, the PFR must contain at least enough specifics to enable the Board to ascertain whether there is a serious evidentiary question warranting Board review of the record. The appellant must explain why challenged determinations are incorrect or otherwise demonstrate error by the AJ with specific reference to the record. See Wallin v. DHUD, 6 MSPR 396, 397 (1981) (PFR must be specific enough to enable the Board to determine if there is a serious evidentiary question justifying a review of the record). If a factual misstatement is identified in an initial decision, the Board should be made aware of how that error determines the outcome of the case. See Bronfman v. GSA, 40 MSPR 184, 189-90 (1989). An error by the AJ in characterizing the action as a removal rather than as a demotion is harmless without a demonstration that the error affected the appellant's substantive rights. See Forbes v. USPS, 24 MSPR 459, 461 & n.* (1984).

Review of factual findings may be warranted if the AJ offers only general conclusions concerning testimony without identifying the witnesses relied on. If a challenge is raised concerning the factual determinations made by the AJ, the Board is required to review the evidence presented on material issues. The Board will give no deference to credibility choices made on the basis of an inadequate reason or no reason at all. Macijauskas v. Dept. of Army, 34 MSPR 564, 567 n.1 (1987) (citing NLRB v. Moore Business Forms, Inc., 574 F.2d 835 (5th Cir. 1978), and Local 138 Int'l Union of Operating Eng'rs v. NLRB, 321 F.2d 130, 138 n.1 (2d Cir. 1963)); see Copeland v. USPS, 46 MSPR 127, 129-30 (1990) (Board completely reevaluated evidence in a misconduct case when the AJ did not consider all relevant evidence concerning the credibility of witnesses).

The Board may consider testimony evaluated by the AJ and consider written statements and set aside a AJ's credibility determinations that the Board decides are inconsistent with the weight of the evidence. See Moore v. EEOC, 97 MSPR 684, 690 ¶ 12 (2004). But see Ketchum v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 149, 151 (1983) (where there are no other indicia of credibility in the record, due deference must be given to the AJ's assessment of credibility (citing Weaver v. Dept. of Navy, 2 MSPR 129 (1980)). If due to other factors, the failure by the AJ to make a proper credibility finding on a charge is immaterial (that is, the same result would be reached whether or not the charges were sustained), the Board will not remand the case for further consideration by the AJ of the credibility issue. See Parker v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 343, 347 n.2 (1991).
