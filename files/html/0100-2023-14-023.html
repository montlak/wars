broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 02: Hours of Work; Tours of Duty, Section A: Shifts, Tours, Hours of Duty, Subsection 16: Days Off; Rotations

16. Days Off; Rotations

A proposal defining pairs of rotating days off was negotiable, there being no demonstration by the agency of how the proposal interfered with management rights. AFSCME Local 2477 and Library of Congress, 14 FLRA 59, 59 (1984). Without a showing that personnel shortages would result, a proposal was negotiable that the employer make efforts to ensure that employees received three consecutive days off if they were scheduled on tours for more than five consecutive days. The agency failed to meet its burden of demonstrating a direct or integral connection between the proposal and the numbers, types, and grades of employees assigned to a tour of duty. AFGE Local 2094 and VAMC, New York, N.Y., 22 FLRA 710, 723 (1986). A proposal that the agency provide ordinarily consecutive days off permitted the agency to decline that type of scheduling if the agency determined that patient care would be adversely affected. The proposal did not conflict with management rights. NAGE Local R14-8 and VAMC, Topeka, 24 FLRA 126, 130 (1986); see NAGE, SEIU and VAMC, Dept. of Memorial Affairs, 40 FLRA 657, 659 (1991) (Provision 1 was negotiable: "[w]henever possible, employees will be given two consecutive days off, preferably Saturday and Sunday"; management retained discretion under 5 CFR 610.121 to vary work schedule); VA Staff Nurses Council, Local 5032 and VAMC, Wood, 29 FLRA 849, 856-58 (1987) (negotiable proposals required efforts to schedule consecutive days off, equitable distribution of weekends off, and other shift restrictions among registered nurses when the proposals contemplated that scheduling would always be consistent with patient care needs; declining to approve as negotiable provision limiting rotations of nurses to schedules unless basic care needs otherwise required).

A union could require negotiation of a proposal binding management to schedule administrative nonduty days for weekends in an equitable manner. Requiring that assignments be made equitably permitted management to take into account its work needs. NFFE Local 1798 and VAMC, Martinsburg, W.Va., 27 FLRA 239, 261 (1987) (Proposal 8). A proposal that National Guard technicians' days off be consecutive, on Saturday and Sunday, prescribed a basic workweek of Monday through Friday. The proposal did not preclude management from requiring employees to work on weekends. For Guard employees, who cannot receive overtime, the effect would be that if they were assigned weekend work they would be granted compensatory time under 32 USC 709(g). The proposal did not interfere with the right to assign work and was negotiable. NFFE Local 1655 and Illinois NG, 26 FLRA 654, 665 (1987).

The matter of consecutive days off was addressed in Tidewater Va. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Navy Pub. Works Ctr., Norfolk, 25 FLRA 3, 9 (1987). The proposal was that the employer schedule the basic workweek so that employees have two consecutive days off, unless mutually agreed by the employer and the union. The proposal was nonnegotiable:

5 U.S.C. § 6101(a)(3)(B)...states that except when the head of an agency determines that his organization would be seriously handicapped in carrying out its functions or that costs would be substantially increased, he shall provide that the basic 40-hour workweek of his employees is scheduled on 5 days, Monday through Friday when possible, and that the 2 days outside the basic workweek are consecutive. 5 C.F.R. § 610.121(a)(2)...contains the same language. Both law and regulation, therefore, authorize the Agency head in this case to schedule the basic workweek of his employees in a manner which does not provide for two consecutive days off where he determines that the agency would otherwise be seriously handicapped in carrying out its functions or that costs would be substantially increased. The proposal prevents the Agency head from scheduling the basic workweek in a manner which does not provide for two consecutive days off, unless the Union agrees. Thus, Proposal 3 in this case is distinguished from Proposal 3 in National Association of Government Employees Local R-14 and Veterans Affairs Medical Center, Topeka, Kansas, 24 FLRA No. 17 (1986), and Proposal 4 in National Association of Government Employees, SEIU, AFL-CIO and Veterans Affairs Medical Center, Grand Junction, Colorado, 24 FLRA No. 21 (1986). The cited proposals in those cases, unlike Proposal 3 in this case, expressly permitted management to take into account actual work requirements in scheduling days off and, as interpreted by the Authority, thereby incorporated the statutory and regulatory requirements concerning the scheduling of days off.

What of a proposal that "nurses will have every other weekend off"? The proposal was negotiable only at the election of the agency under § 7106(b)(1). AFGE Local 3669 and VAMC, Minneapolis, 2 FLRA 641, 644 (1980). Noting the NTEU Chap. 66 decision, supra, FLRA maintained that, although the proposal did not relate explicitly to the numbers, types, and grades of employees assigned to a tour of duty, it so directly and integrally related to those factors as to determine employees' numbers, types, and grades on a tour of duty. The VA must establish tours of duty with sufficient numbers and types of medical personnel to provide patient care. The proposal negated management's ability to determine the skills of nurses assigned to weekend work. No matter what skill levels were required, the ability to assign those nurses was determined on the basis of who had the previous weekend off. To accomplish the mission, the agency would be required to hire additional nurses, a requirement integrally connected with the numbers, types, and grades of employees assigned to a tour of duty. See Illinois Nurses' Ass'n and VAMC, Hines, 28 FLRA 212, 229-31 (1987) (proposal was not negotiable that agency make every effort to give nurses every third weekend off).

A proposal for rotation of nurses was nonnegotiable in New York State Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, Manhattan, 12 FLRA 540, 541-42 (1983); see New York State Nurses Ass'n and VA Bronx Med. Ctr., 30 FLRA 706, 738 (1987) (Proposal 13 § 8.03I was nonnegotiable: requiring employer to grant nurse 15 hours off after completion of scheduled workday); Nat'l Union of Hosp. and Health Care Employees, Dist. 1199 and VAMC, Dayton, 28 FLRA 435, 464-65 (1987) (Proposal 15 for 15 hours between duty assignments was nonnegotiable); AFGE Local 695 and Dept. of Treasury, U.S. Mint, Denver, 3 FLRA 43, 44-45 (1980) (proposal that work assignments be rotated weekly was nonnegotiable). Colorado Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, Ft. Lyons, Colo., 25 FLRA 803, 815 (1987), distinguished NAGE Local R14-8 and VAMC, Topeka, 24 FLRA 126 (1986), on the basis that in the latter case, the word "ordinarily" in the proposal protected the agency right to vary the employees' days off to provide for its patient care needs. See Int'l Bhd. Of Police Officers and GSA, Region 2, 47 FLRA 397, 405 (1993) (negotiability appeal dismissed as to Proposal 6, seeking rotation of employees into positions with ordinary five-day workweek; no demonstration that employees eligible for rotation would have qualifications for assignment into positions); Illinois Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, N. Chicago, 27 FLRA 714, 721-22 (1987) (considering various sections of proposals concerning shift assignments and specialty transfers with consideration of limitations imposed on provision of patient care, skills required of nursing personnel, and limitations of sources of personnel available for transfer and assignment to positions); NFFE Local 1798 and VAMC, Martinsburg, W.Va., 27 FLRA 239, 259-60 (1987) (distinguishing AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 15 FLRA 825 (1984), and AFGE Local 2272 and DOJ, Marshals Serv., 9 FLRA 1004 (1982), because agencies there did not demonstrate, nor was it otherwise apparent, how restrictions on shift changes and requirement of an advance notice period prevented management from assigning work or employees).

A nonnegotiable proposal prohibited the agency from scheduling an employee to work on a shift if that employee worked during the previous 11 hours, or from scheduling an employee for two different tours of duty in the same week except to provide the employee with an 11-hour interval between shifts; it infringed on the right to assign work. NAGE Local R14-8 and VAMC, Topeka, 24 FLRA 126, 129 (1986). A proposal requiring employees to have at least 64 hours off between shift or tour changes was nonnegotiable. It violated the right to assign work, regardless of the agency's actual work requirements and regardless of whether the employee's particular skills were needed on a particular shift. The proposal was not an appropriate arrangement: it abrogated the exercise of management's right to assign work. NFFE Local 1655 and Illinois NG, 26 FLRA 654, 664 (1987).

A flat preclusion of weekend or holiday work was nonnegotiable. It interfered with the right to assign work. The proposal was not a negotiable arrangement, for it precluded management from exercising its right to assign work. Int'l Plate Printers Local 2 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 25 FLRA 113, 118-19 (1987). A proposal directly interfered with the right to assign work--the right to determine what duties will be assigned, when work assignments will occur, and to whom or what positions duties will be assigned--that management withdraw working leaders from weekend coverage and allow the weekend crew to function as the evening crew in a nonsupervised capacity, or, alternatively, to hire a permanent weekend leader. NAGE Local R1-109 and VAMC, Newington, 26 FLRA 532, 533-34 (1987).

Determined to be nonnegotiable in AFGE Local 701 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 58 FLRA 128, 129-30 (2002), was Provision 1, that supervisors fairly and equitably, and to the greatest extent possible, grant military reservists' requests for work schedule adjustments to attend weekend military drills. The provision interfered with the right to assign work. It applied without regard to whether there were qualified employees available to perform weekend work. The provision required the agency to assign work to an employee during the week to accommodate the employee's request for time off; the union's statement of position did not suggest that all employees were equally qualified to perform assigned duties.

A negotiable proposal established a procedure for management to consider requests by employees for Saturdays off and to consider volunteers for temporary duty assignments. The employer could refuse the requests for time off and could decline to use volunteers for temporary assignments. AFGE Meat Grading Council of Locals and USDA, Meat Grading and Certification Branch, 22 FLRA 496, 500 (1986). If more than one employee is qualified to perform the work of a position, a union can negotiate procedures for the selection of the particular employee who will perform that work. It was negotiable to propose that management permit a particular individual to work on only certain weekend days. NAGE Local R1-109 and VAMC, Newington, 26 FLRA 532, 535 (1987).
