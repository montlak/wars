b. Incorrect Advice From OPM

Counsel fees may be awarded if OPM provides clearly incorrect procedural advice. That occurred, and fees were awarded, after OPM gave a guardian incomplete and misleading instructions on the procedures needed to obtain standing to prosecute a retirement application on behalf of another person. Peters v. OPM, 34 MSPR 570, 573-74 (1987) ("OPM's failure to advise the guardian of the information that was needed to prevail on reconsideration needlessly compounded the guardian's difficulty in balancing the necessity of furnishing enough evidence to obtain an annuity for his daughter with the need to limit her access to information about her condition for the sake of her mental health. We find that OPM's decision to deny standing was therefore clearly without merit[.] "). The fee award is conditioned on improper advice from OPM. Fees against OPM are not supported by a showing that the employing agency did not properly advise the appellant on how to fill out the annuity application. Kinard v. OPM, 39 MSPR 265, 271 n.5 (1988) (assessing a fee petition under the clearly without merit standard).

Attempting to distinguish the clearly without merit and knew or should have known criteria, Thomas v. OPM, 59 MSPR 31, 35-36 (1993), determined:

We have reopened the appeal in order to correct an adjudicatory error. In her initial decision, the administrative judge confused the "clearly without merit" and "knew or should have known" categories set forth in Kent for determining whether an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice. As noted above, the administrative judge found that the appellant had not shown that OPM knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits. She reached that conclusion after finding that OPM had not misled the appellant or given him insufficient information and that the evidence submitted by him on the disability issue was not so compelling that reasonable minds could not differ as to his eligibility for disability benefits. Those two factors, however, are applicable not to the "knew or should have known" category but to the "clearly without merit" category, and the administrative judge thus erred by confusing the two categories. See Kent, 33 MSPR at 366-69. However, given our finding above that the appellant has not established his entitlement to an award of attorney fees, the error does not warrant reversal of the initial decision. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984).
