a. Proof of Intent

Insubordination requires a showing of intent; the failure to perform work or the failure to carry out a directive, standing alone, does not constitute insubordination. See Webster v. Dept. of Army, 911 F.2d 679, 684 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (insubordination is a "willful and intentional refusal to obey an authorized order of a superior officer which the officer is entitled to have obeyed"); Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 603 ¶ 8 (2001) ("To prove a charge of 'defiance of authority,' the agency must establish by preponderant evidence that an employee engaged in insolent or defiant behavior or challenged a superior's authority."); Gallagher v. Dept. of Labor, 11 MSPR 612, 614 n.3 (1982) (the appellant could not perform an assignment due to illness; as to the insubordination charge, "[p]art of the agency's burden is to establish that the appellant's refusal to obey such order was intentional and willful disobedience"). An agency must prove intent if an appellant presents a credible explanation for alleged insubordination. If a meritorious defense is not presented, however, there is no requirement that a charge of refusal to comply with specific instructions be attributed by the agency specifically to insubordinate intent. Gatling v. NTSB, 17 MSPR 43, 48 (1983); see Hamilton v. USPS, 71 MSPR 547, 555 (1996) (clarifying the law: intent is not a required element of proof when the charge is failure to follow instructions), post, followed in Eichner v. USPS, 83 MSPR 202, 205 ¶ 5 (1999) ("Unlike a charge of insubordination, failure to obey orders does not require proof of intent."); Johnson v. DOJ, 61 MSPR 110, 113 (1994) ("[A] charge of failing to follow a supervisor's instructions is equivalent to a charge of insubordination.").

The insubordinate nature of an employee's failure to properly perform work--the evidence of intent needed to show something other than carelessness--can be demonstrated through circumstantial evidence, Alvarado v. Dept. of Air Force, 103 MSPR 1, 17-19 ¶¶ 37-40, 2006 MSPB 230 (2006):

As noted above, "insubordination" is a willful offense requiring proof of intent. See, e.g., Phillips v. General Services Administration, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989). Because the agency charged the appellant with the insubordinate defiance of authority, the agency must prove that the appellant's defiance of authority was willful and intentional. See Hawkins, 73 M.S.P.R. at 401. Proof that the appellant's actions were negligent or unintentional will not suffice. See Hamilton v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 547, 555 (1996) (a charge of insubordination requires proof of intentional and willful refusal to obey an order entitled to be obeyed, rather than a negligent or otherwise unintentional failure to obey an order).

The Board does not require that agencies in all cases prove their charges by direct evidence. See Suarez v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 96 M.S.P.R. 213, ¶ 25 (2004) (if the agency provides enough circumstantial evidence to lead the Board to conclude that it is more likely than not that the appellant committed the charged misconduct, this is sufficient to meet the agency's burden of proof, regardless of any direct evidence), aff'd, 125 Fed. Appx. 1010 (Fed. Cir. 2005). In cases requiring proof of intent, the Board has long held that intent is a state of mind that is generally proven by circumstantial evidence. Suarez, 96 M.S.P.R. 213, ¶ 40; Mendez v. Department of the Treasury, 88 M.S.P.R. 596, ¶ 15 (2001); Johnson v. Department of the Army, 48 M.S.P.R. 54, 57 (1991), aff'd, 960 F.2d 156 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). The Board has held that intent can be proven by circumstantial evidence in the context of an insubordination charge. See Redfearn v. Department of Labor, 58 M.S.P.R. 307, 312 (1993); Forgett v. Department of the Army, 45 M.S.P.R. 198, 204 n.5 (1990); Acker v. U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, 22 M.S.P.R. 15, 17 (1984). In considering whether the agency has proven intent, the Board must examine the totality of the circumstances. Suarez, 96 M.S.P.R. 213, ¶ 40; Johnson, 48 M.S.P.R. at 57.

MSgt. Rettinger testified that he considered the appellant's failure to perform inspections properly was intentional and defiant because the appellant continued to get a large number of inspection rejects after being told what was expected of him. He also explained that it was the cumulative effect of the repeated inspection rejects, not any one individual reject, that convinced him that the appellant's conduct was intentional. Mr. Rariden testified that he considered the appellant's conduct to be intentional because Mr. Rariden had personally counseled the appellant on the need to conduct inspections when he had caught the appellant "[n]ot even going down to the outlying sections and inspecting these vehicles." Mr. Rariden stated, "I reminded him of the requirement that we are inspecting 100 percent of the vehicles. That includes refueling, fire truck, and base maintenance heavy-mobile equipment-type vehicles," and that the appellant acknowledged that he had not been doing that but understood the importance of inspections and would comply in the future. He did not do so, however. Mr. Rariden testified that the appellant had once told him that he did not believe that, as an inspector, his work should be subject to inspection.

We find that the agency has proffered sufficient circumstantial evidence to prove that the appellant's misconduct was intentional. Contrary to the AJ's finding that the appellant's factual disputes concerning some of the specifications shows that his conduct was unintentional, in fact, the evidence shows the opposite. While never actually admitting or denying that he failed to check to see whether the ether bottle was full, the appellant offered his opinion that it was unsafe to require a full ether bottle in warm weather and that the operator of the vehicle should obtain a full ether bottle later when the weather warranted it. The appellant also testified, as noted above, that a new oil filter was not needed for new vehicles and that mechanics could not be expected to do painting work. In addition, he complained that he was being held responsible for cosmetic issues that were frivolous and unrelated to vehicle safety. This testimony, coupled with his insistence that the agency improperly failed to bargain over perceived changes to the reject system, shows that the appellant disagreed with at least some of the agency's inspection requirements and decided not to adhere to them. Further, we find convincing the agency's evidence that the appellant performed his duties satisfactorily during the first weeks of the PIP, but not thereafter when MSgt. Rettinger was not available to monitor his work closely. This evidence shows that the appellant was aware of the agency's requirements and was able to perform them capably when he knew he was being watched, but chose not to perform those duties with which he disagreed when he thought he would not get caught. See Smith v. Department of the Air Force, 48 M.S.P.R. 594, 598 (1991) (employees do not have the unfettered right to disregard orders which they believe are improper). Under the circumstances of this case, we find that the agency has shown that it is more likely true than not true that the appellant's refusal to complete his inspection duties was intentional. We further find that the appellant's decision to correctly complete his inspections duties only when he believed he was being watched reflects his disrespect for those aspects of the agency's reject system with which he disagreed, and establishes that his conduct was defiant and insolent. See Redschlag, 89 M.S.P.R. 589, ¶ 19; Bennett, 84 M.S.P.R. 132, ¶ 14. Accordingly, we sustain the charge of insubordinate defiance of authority.

Redfearn v. Dept. of Labor, 58 MSPR 307, 312-13 (1993), explained the intent to be insubordinate:

...Since intent is a state of mind, there will seldom be direct evidence of it. The Board has therefore held that intent may be inferred from circumstantial evidence.... Here, with respect to the first specification, the agency showed that the appellant was given written instructions, and that she did not follow them. Further, aside from her general denial that she was insubordinate, the appellant did not contest that she had failed to follow the instructions, nor did she offer any reason, e.g., impossibility, lack of knowledge, danger, etc., for her failure to follow the instructions she was given by the agency. Under these circumstances, we find that the agency established by preponderant evidence that the appellant's failure to follow her written instructions was willful and intentional.

Circumstantial proof of intent is established when an important assignment is given to an employee with no other responsibility, who then fails to alert the agency that the deadline for the project could be missed. Acker v. Comm'n on Civil Rights, 22 MSPR 15, 17 (1984); see Cowins v. VA, 64 MSPR 551, 557 (1994) (insubordination was established: the agency proved the appellant was specifically instructed to personally contact his designated acting chief during his absences and he did not do so; the appellant offered no reason for failing to follow the instructions). Parbs v. USPS, 107 MSPR 559, 566-67 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 302 (2007) (aff'd, Parbs v. USPS, 301 Fed. Appx. 923 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)), added as to circumstantial evidence of insubordination:

Insubordination is the willful and intentional refusal to obey an authorized order of a superior officer which the officer is entitled to have obeyed. See Phillips v. General Services Administration, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989); Alvarado, 103 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 23. As noted above, it requires proof of intent. See Phillips, 878 F.2d at 373; Alvarado, 103 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 37. Intent is a state of mind that is generally proven by circumstantial evidence in the context of an insubordination charge. Id., ¶ 38. In considering whether the agency has proven intent, the Board must examine the totality of the circumstances. Id. We can determine at this level whether the agency proved the charge in this case because the appellant did not request a hearing, and, thus, no credibility determinations based on demeanor are required. See, e.g., Bradley v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 78 M.S.P.R. 296, 300 n.2 (1998).

Although insubordination may be established by circumstantial evidence, the charge cannot be sustained unless the employee's action or inaction was intentional or willful. See Forgett v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 198, 204 n.5 (1990). "The fact that the appellant may have disagreed with his supervisors on a complex jurisdictional determination that was unclear or controversial does not, on its own, show that any continued disagreement by itself constituted insubordination." Rosario-Fabregas v. Dept. of Army, NY-0752-13-0142-I-2 (NP 7/1/2016).

Attempts, albeit unsuccessful, to comply with a directive negate the willfulness required to sustain a charge of insubordination, as in Southerland v. DOD, 117 MSPR 56, 61 ¶ 15, 2011 MSPB 92 (2011) [replacing the decision at 2011 MSPB 78 (2011)], (overruled on other grounds, Southerland v. DOD, 119 MSPR 566, 2013 MSPB 46 (2013)) involving a charge that the employee was insubordinate when he allegedly failed to comply with a directive to supply medical information to the agency:

Insubordination is the willful and intentional refusal to obey an authorized order of a superior officer which the officer is entitled to have obeyed. Phillips v. General Services Administration, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989); Alvarado v. Department of the Air Force, 103 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 23 (2006), aff'd, Alvarado v. Wynne, 626 F. Supp.2d 1140 (D.N.M. 2009). Part of the agency's burden is to establish that the appellant's refusal to obey any such order was intentional and willful disobedience. Gallagher v. Department of Labor, 11 M.S.P.R. 612, 614 n.3 (1982). Thus, if an employee attempted to comply with an order, he was not insubordinate. Phillips, 878 F.2d at 373-74.

Intent presupposes knowledge of the order. Bonanova v. Dept. of Educ., 49 MSPR 294, 302 (1991). A charge of failing to follow instructions cannot be sustained unless there is a finding that instructions were given. Boscoe v. USDA, 54 MSPR 315, 320 (1992); cf. Harriss v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 427, 434 (1995) (although performance standards need not detail every duty for a position, an employee may not be disciplined for failing to perform a duty that the agency did not make her aware she had).
