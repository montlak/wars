23. Rescission of Disability Retirement Grant

OPM may rescind its approval of a disability retirement application, but only under limited circumstances, set by regulation, described in Longoria v. OPM, 78 MSPR 242, 243-45 (1998):

While employed by the Department of the Navy, the appellant, who is deaf, applied for a disability retirement annuity under FERS based on her chronic bronchitis, asthma, cough, arthritis, and allergies to dust, air conditioning, and chemical sprays. OPM granted her application, and, on October 5, 1992, the employing agency separated the appellant as retired on disability. About a year later, on October 29, 1993, OPM rescinded the appellant's disability retirement annuity because, during an audit, OPM discovered that her "application for disability retirement was approved in error. OPM reprocessed her application for a disability retirement annuity. OPM afforded her an opportunity to submit additional evidence to show that she was entitled to a disability retirement annuity. OPM thereafter issued an initial decision and then a reconsideration decision denying the appellant's application. The appellant petitioned for appeal. The administrative judge sustained the reconsideration decision. The appellant has now petitioned for review. OPM has not responded to the petition.

...

OPM's regulation is specific with regard to the bases upon which it is authorized to rescind a disability retirement annuity under FERS. The applicable regulation, which has remained unchanged since 1988, provides in pertinent part as follows:

OPM may rescind a decision to allow an application for disability retirement at any time if OPM determines that the original decision was erroneous due to fraud, misstatement of fact, or upon the acquisition of additional medical or other documentation.

5 C.F.R. § 844.203(c)(2).

It is well settled that an agency is bound by the regulations which it has promulgated, even though absent such regulations the agency could have exercised its authority to take the same action on another basis, and that the agency must abide by its regulations as written until it rescinds or amends them. See United States v. Nixon, 418 U.S. 683, 695-96 (1974); Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535, 539-40 (1959). Despite the regulatory limitation on OPM's authority to rescind a disability retirement annuity, OPM submitted no evidence below to support a finding that the rescission of the appellant's annuity was in compliance with the regulation noted above.

Because the issue of OPM's authority for its action had not been raised below, the Board afforded OPM an opportunity to produce the statutory or regulatory basis for its action by issuance of a Notice to Show Cause providing OPM with such an opportunity. In its response, OPM summarily stated that, approximately one year after it approved the appellant's application for disability retirement benefits, it conducted an internal review of its disability rolls and determined that the appellant was not entitled to disability retirement because the approval of her application was based on insufficient medical evidence.

OPM has not set forth any statutory or regulatory basis for its action. Moreover, OPM has not asserted that the rescission was based on fraud, misstatement of fact, or the acquisition of additional medical or other documentation. See 5 C.F.R. § 844.203(c)(2). Thus, we find that OPM has no statutory or regulatory authority to reconsider and rescind the appellant's disability retirement annuity. OPM has made no showing of the propriety of its action. Accordingly, the rescission is not sustained.

Langoria was followed in Cerone v. OPM, 85 MSPR 380, 382-83 ¶ 4-7 (2000):

OPM advised the appellant that it was rescinding its original approval of her disability retirement in accordance with 5 C.F.R. § 831.1206(b)(2). However, OPM erroneously relied on 5 C.F.R. § 831.1206(b)(2), which applies only to disability retirement applications under the Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS). In this case, because the appellant's disability retirement application is under FERS and not CSRS, 5 C.F.R. § 844.203(C)(2) is the applicable regulation.

As the Board found in Longoria v. Office of Personnel Management, 78 M.S.P.R. 242, 244-45 (1998), OPM's regulation is specific with regard to the bases upon which it is authorized to rescind a disability retirement annuity under FERS. The applicable regulation, which has remained unchanged since 1988, provides in pertinent part as follows:

OPM may rescind a decision to allow an application for disability retirement at any time if OPM determines that the original decision is erroneous due to fraud, misstatement of fact, or upon the acquisition of additional or other documentation.

5 C.F.R. § 844.203(C)(2).

It is well settled that an agency is bound by the regulations it has promulgated, even though absent such regulations the agency could have exercised its authority to take the same actions on another basis, and that the agency must abide by its regulations as written until it rescinds or amends them. United States v. Nixon, 418 U.S. 683, 695-96, 94 S. Ct. 3090, 3100-02 (1974), Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535, 539-40 (1959). Despite the regulatory limitation on OPM's authority to rescind a disability retirement annuity under FERS, OPM submitted no evidence below to support a finding that the rescission of the appellant's annuity was in compliance with the regulations noted above. Rather, OPM merely stated that upon further review of the medical evidence in the case file, it had determined that the evidence failed to support the existence of a disabling medical condition. OPM then discussed the medical evidence in the file and found that the appellant's file lacked objective medical evidence showing that she had a medical condition which prevented her from successfully performing the duties of her position.

OPM has not set forth any statutory or regulatory basis for its action. OPM has not asserted that the rescission was based on fraud, misstatement of fact, or the acquisition of additional medical or other documentation. 5 C.F.R. § 844.203(c)(2). Thus, we find that OPM has no statutory or regulatory authority to reconsider and rescind the appellant's disability retirement annuity. Longoria, 78 M.S.P.R. at 245. Accordingly, the rescission is not sustained.
