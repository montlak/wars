a. Conduct Postdating Proposal

The employing agency cannot proceed at the hearing on the basis of other charges that might have been, but were not in fact, made. Alfaro v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 628, 630 (1983) (citing Johnston v. GPO, 5 MSPR 354, 357, 5 MSPB 376 (1981)). An agency automatically limits the charges against an appellant to offenses occurring on or before the date of the notice of proposed adverse action. To sustain the action on the basis of charges that could have been, but were not, made violates basic procedural rights of employees. Brooks v. FAA, 18 MSPR 178, 181 (1983); see Creggett v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 584, 588 (1989) ("since the agency must afford an employee with adequate notice of the nature of the charges, the administrative judge properly declined to consider testimony that the appellant had committed violations other than those specified in the proposal notice"). However, submission of new allegations of misconduct to the AJ, in response to an appellant's appeal, by itself does not show that the agency considered reasons other than those in the proposal notice in reaching the decision to take action against the appellant. There was no harmful error if the AJ properly ignored the additional reasons irrelevant to the case. Svejda v. Dept. of Interior, 7 MSPR 108, 112, 7 MSPB 36 (1981); cf. Powers v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 256, 263 (2000) ("[T]here is no reason to conclude that the appellant would have denied the postproposal AWOL allegations had he been informed of them below, nor is there any reason to believe that the deciding official would not have sustained the charges even in the absence of his knowledge of the appellant's post-proposal conduct. Under these circumstances, we agree with the agency and find that the information the deciding official obtained through ex parte communications was not so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that no employee can fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances, and therefore we find that it was not material and that the agency did not commit a due process violation."); Morales v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 24, 24 n.* (1985) (no error when the AJ admitted evidence of a prior incident not referenced in the proposal; the deciding official testified that he did not rely on the incident even though he was aware of it); Cusick v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 161, 162 (1982) (although the agency may have considered absences beyond the date of the proposal notice, error was not shown to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it reached).
