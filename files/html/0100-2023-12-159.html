a. Misguided Facsimile

As to lost fax transmissions, the Board explained the law and evidentiary requirements, Odom v. USPS, 67 MSPR 511, 513-14 (1995):

...The Board received correspondence from the agency dated February 13, 1995, which was captioned as a response to the appellant's petition for review, but no record of receipt of such a petition for review existed. After the Clerk of the Board issued a show-cause Order directing the appellant to explain why she had not timely filed a petition for review, her counsel (Mr. Weiser) submitted a declaration made under penalty of perjury, in which he affirmed that he had filed the appellant's petition for review with the Board by facsimile transmission at 11:34 P.M. on January 18, 1995. In support of his declaration, Mr. Weiser enclosed a facsimile confirmation sheet indicating that a transmission had been made to the Clerk of the Board at 11:34 P.M. on January 18, 1995, and that the result of the transmission was "OK."

A party may submit credible evidence, in the form of an affidavit or sworn statement, to establish that, despite a postmark date that appeared to indicate untimely filing, the pleading was actually placed in the Postal Service mail stream before the deadline passed, and thus was timely filed. See Raphel v. Dept. of the Army, 50 MSPR 614, 618 (1991); see also Thompson v. Dept. of the Navy, 62 MSPR 145, 147 (1994), appeal dismissed, Thompson v. MSPB, 36 F.3d 1114 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table) (filing by facsimile covered by the Raphel rule). The Raphel rule also applies to pleadings not received by the Board; if a party can show that a submission was properly placed in the mail stream, it will be treated as filed on the date it was placed in the mail stream, regardless of whether the Board receives it. See Gaydon v. USPS, 62 MSPR 198, 201-02 (1994), review dismissed, Gaydon v. MSPB, 36 F.3d 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Because the appellant has submitted credible, unrebutted evidence that her petition for review was submitted by facsimile before the deadline passed, we find that the petition was timely filed. See Thompson, 62 MSPR at 147; Gaydon, 62 MSPR at 202.

Another PFR faxed and allegedly lost was discussed in Duran-Arcelay v. OPM, 70 MSPR 13, 15-16 (1996):

Where a party shows by preponderant evidence that a pleading was properly addressed to the Board with postage prepaid and placed in the Postal Service mail stream, it will be treated as filed on the date it was placed in the mail stream, regardless of whether the Board receives it. See Freeze v. VA, 65 MSPR 149, 152 (1994) (citing Gaydon v. USPS, 62 MSPR 198, 202, review dismissed, 36 F.3d 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table)). We find no reason not to apply this same principle to FAX transmissions which are considered filed on the date of the facsimile. See 5 CFR § 1201.4(l). Thus, the appellant here must establish by preponderant evidence that he timely submitted a petition for review by FAX to the Board, or that there was good cause for the delayed filing.

The appellant has failed to meet this burden. A party must present specific details concerning the mailing, or, in this instance, the FAX submission. See Freeze v. VA, 65 MSPR at 152. Here, the appellant's mere assertion that he faxed a petition within the deadline is too vague and general. There is nothing specific concerning the time or date the submission was allegedly faxed, and no copy of the petition for review that was allegedly faxed, despite the Clerk's statement in both of his notices that such a document should be filed, and the appellant's assertion in both of his responses that he has a copy of the originally faxed letter. Thus, the nature and quality of the evidence does not establish, by preponderant evidence, timely submission of any document to the Board by FAX.

See Jeanmarie v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-3330-14-0445-I-1 (NP 3/20/2015) ("Because the pro se appellant has submitted credible unrebutted evidence that he attempted to file his petition for review by fax on the day it was due, and the agency has not shown that it will be prejudiced by a waiver of the time limit to file this petition, see Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980), we waive the filing deadline, see, e.g., Hunter v. Department of the Interior, 79 M.S.P.R. 456, ¶ 8 (1998)."); Njoku v. DHS, 111 MSPR 469, 471 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 79 (2009) ("Beyond his vague assertions in the memorandum, the appellant has not provided any proof of his alleged efforts to file this PFR in September. Acceptable proof might have included a facsimile machine transaction report showing that he sent the transmission to the Board, or notes from a telephone call identifying the person with whom he claims to have spoken.").
