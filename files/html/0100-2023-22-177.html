7. Investigator

If an attorney hires an investigator to do work that could be done by counsel or her staff, and if the investigator's charges are billed to the client as part of counsel fees, those expenses may be recovered, Shimotsukasa v. USPS, 78 MSPR 679, 681-84 (1998):

The appellant argues on review that the investigator was a licensed attorney, that the services he performed were similar to those performed by an attorney or paralegal, and that, if the investigator had not been engaged, an associate or other para-professional in the employ of the firm would have performed and billed for those services. He further asserts that the investigator's work was not clerical or otherwise part of the firm's overhead but rather would have been billed directly to the client regardless of who performed it. Under these circumstances, he contends, the investigator's fees should be compensable. We agree.

The Board has held that an attorney fee award under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1) may include reimbursement for services rendered by paralegals, law clerks[,] librarians, and statisticians where the fees are routinely billed to the client, are not taxable costs or prohibited by statute or regulation, and are not expenses incurred for the mere convenience of counsel. See Social Security Administration v. Goodman, 33 MSPR 325, 337 (1987). See also Bennett v. Department of the Navy, 699 F.2d 1140, 1145 (Fed. Cir. 1983). The rationale behind allowing recovery of these expenses is that such personnel "provide necessary services which, were they performed by attorneys, would be more costly." Goodman, 33 MSPR at 337 (citing Keith v. Volpe, 501 F. Supp. 403, 413 (C.D. Cal. 1980)). The fact that a non-attorney is not a party to an attorney-client relationship does not preclude recovery of fees for his or her services. The existence of an attorney-client relationship between the appellant and the attorney, for whom the non-attorney is acting as an agent in all aspects of the case, triggers the possibility of an award of fees for the non-attorney's services. See Anderson v. Government Printing Office, 55 MSPR 548, 551 (1992); Mitchell v. U.S. Postal Service, 6 MSPB 33, 6 MSPR 22, 23 (1981).

Here, the legal investigator's fee was based on the following services: conducting interviews with certain witnesses; locating other witnesses; photographing evidence; and, assisting in various aspects of case preparation. We agree with the appellant that these services were the type of services that an attorney, or possibly a paralegal, would have been required to perform had the investigator not been hired, and that the administrative judge erred in finding to the contrary.

In determining whether certain types of fees or costs may be recovered under 5 USC § 7701(g), the Board has frequently looked to federal court interpretations of fee-shifting statutes other than the Civil Service Reform Act. See, e.g., Goodman, 33 MSPR at 337 (the Board referred to a district court case that interpreted the Civil Rights Attorney's Fee Award Act of 1976, 42 USC § 1988, in holding that fees for non-lawyer personnel employed by a law firm may be compensable); O'Donnell v. Department of the Interior, 2 MSPB 604, 2 MSPR 445, 456 (1980) (the Board relied on three U.S. Court of Appeals cases that construed attorney fee provisions in other federal statutes in deciding what costs a prevailing appellant's attorney can recover), overruled in part on other grounds, Koch v. Department of Commerce, 19 MSPR 219 (1984). In Garmong v. Montgomery County, 668 F. Supp. 1000, 1011 (S.D. Tex. 1987), the court expressly found that 42 USC § 1988 allows for recovery of fees expended for investigator work that is traditionally done by an attorney. The court held that the investigator's tasks of assisting counsel at trial, locating and interviewing witnesses, reviewing records and participating in conferences with lawyers and witnesses fell within that definition, and were therefore compensable. Id. See Harris v. Marhoefer, 24 F.3d 16, 19 (9th Cir. 1994) (stating that section 1988 allows recovery for an investigator's fees that would normally be charged to a fee paying client). Since the work performed by the investigator in the instant case was very similar to that of the investigator in Garmong, and since both statutes are silent on the issue of recovery of investigator fees but require that fee awards be "reasonable," the court's holding in Garmong lends further support to the view that here, the investigator fees are compensable.

It is undisputed that an attorney-client relationship existed between the appellant and the law firm, Smith Himmelmann, and that the firm hired the investigator to perform services on the appellant's behalf. As we stated above, in Bennett the court held that charges that are taxable costs under 28 USC § 1920 are not compensable as attorney fees. 699 F.2d at 1143. That section does not bar recovery here, however, as investigator fees are not included within the statute's list of taxable costs.[3] Finally, we find that the appellant has satisfied the requirements in Goodman that the investigator's fees are of the type that are routinely billed to the client and that the services were not provided for the mere convenience of counsel. 33 MSPR at 337. The appellant's attorney submitted an unrebutted sworn statement that the investigator's fees would normally be billed directly to the client. The record also contains affidavits from other attorneys practicing in the same community that indicate that their law firms routinely use investigators to perform legal services, and that such services are not covered by the attorney's hourly rate, and are specifically billed to the client.

[3] In re Media Vision Technology Securities Litigation, 913 F. Supp. 1362, 1367 (N.D. Cal. 1996), the court held that fees for investigator services were recoverable as expenses, implicitly finding that such fees were taxable costs. The type of investigative services provided in that case, however, involved locating and contacting witnesses and class members in a class action case, and therefore the services were not similar to those provided by a paralegal or an attorney, as in the instant case. Accordingly, that case does not convince us that the investigator's services here are taxable costs.

Accordingly, we find that the administrative judge erred in holding that the investigator fees are not compensable under 5 USC § 7701(g). Because the investigator's bills show that all of the services he provided would have been performed by an attorney or paralegal and billed separately to the appellant, and since the appellant has presented unrebutted evidence that the investigator's hourly rate was reasonable, and we find that the number of hours claimed was reasonable, we AWARD the appellant $7,625.00 in additional attorney fees.
