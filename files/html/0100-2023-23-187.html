a. Appellant's Agreement to Resign or to Take Other Action; Agency Self Help

Bales v. Dept. of Transp., 54 MSPR 187, 191-92 (1992), informed agencies that they may do what they wish as to the appellant's agreement to resign--a form of self-help--to enforce terms of agreements they reach with appellants, subject to an enforcement petition by the appellant:

...The administrative judge correctly found that the obligation to resign arose upon appellant's receipt of the directed reassignment. The agency argued to the administrative judge that when appellant failed to submit his resignation it was authorized to treat the settlement agreement as a resignation and to effectuate the resignation on April 6, 1990, following its issuance of an amended letter directing appellant's reassignment.

The appellant argues that the agency did not have the authority to effectuate his resignation without first filing a petition for enforcement with the Board asking it to authorize such action, as was done in McClain v. United States Postal Service, 40 MSPR 66 (1989). The agency replies that it was authorized to effectuate the resignation on the date contemplated by the agreement, also relying on McClain.

The law of contracts applies to cases involving settlement agreements in Board cases. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Appellant's argument is essentially that the agency should have asked the Board to order specific performance of the resignation provision of the agreement. However, specific performance of a contract may be denied if the party requesting it has an adequate remedy available through non-judicial means, including some type of self-help, whether provided by the terms of the contract itself or by some other means. 5A Corbin on Contracts, 1158 (1964). Appellant's promise to resign was evidence of his voluntary decision to resign upon receipt of the directed reassignment. See McClain, 40 MSPR at 70. The agency has authority to accept resignations of its employees. 5 CFR 715.202(a). Therefore the remedy of specific performance might not have been available to the agency even if it had filed a petition for enforcement. Cf. Eaton v. United States Postal Service, 50 MSPR 377, 380 (1991) (dismissing agency's petition for enforcement because there was no need for the agency to obtain Board's authorization to remove an employee for breach of a settlement agreement); Moseley v. United States Postal Service, 48 MSPR 667, 668 (1991) (dismissing as beyond Board authority an agency's petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement provision authorizing the agency to remove appellant upon appellant's breach). Accordingly, we conclude that the agency had the authority to use self-help to accomplish compliance with the agreement in this case. In light of this conclusion, we find unpersuasive appellant's argument that the agency tried to "intimidate" him into resigning, and we need not address appellant's arguments concerning the agency's alleged noncompliance with other provisions of the agreement at that time.

See Taylor v. Dept. of Interior, 34 Fed. App. 766 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP) (agency enforces retirement agreement in EEO settlement); Hight v. Dept. of Navy, 98 MSPR 266, 267 (2005) ("appropriate remedy for the appellant's breach of the settlement provision to tender his resignation is to consider the signed settlement agreement to be the appellant's voluntary resignation"); Bouck v. USPS, 66 MSPR 226, 229-30 (1995) (agency essentially implemented appellant's agreement to resign based on violation of a last chance settlement agreement); Spencer-Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 65 MSPR 93 (1994) (Table) (approving agency's use of settlement agreement to effect appellant's resignation), aff'd, Spencer-Grant v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3117); McClain v. USPS, 40 MSPR 66, 70 (1989) ("Here, the agency, as the non-breaching party, has requested that the appellant comply with the terms of the settlement agreement. The Board has broad authority to enforce its orders. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 732-33 (Fed. Cir. 1984); 5 U.S.C. § 1205(a)(2). Therefore, because paragraph two of the settlement agreement evidences the appellant's voluntary decision to resign immediately upon its execution, the Board finds that the signed and executed settlement agreement constitutes the appellant's resignation from the United States Postal Service."); cf. Alfonso v. DOJ, 96 MSPR 266, 271-72 ¶ 11 (2004) (when appellant violated settlement requiring that reasons he placed on an SF-52 requesting resignation not be adverse to the agency, Board found it proper for agency to process the SF-50 by stating appellant resigned voluntarily for personal reasons). But see Virgil v. USPS, 75 MSPR 109, 114 (1997) ("We hereby ORDER the appellant to submit to the agency and the Board within 15 days of the date of this order a written resignation from his position at the agency effective June 12, 1996. In the event the appellant fails to submit such a writing, we authorize the agency, upon notification to the Board, to consider the settlement agreement to be the appellant's resignation and on that basis to place appropriate papers effectuating the resignation in the appellant's official personnel folder.")
