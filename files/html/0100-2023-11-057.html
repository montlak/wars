a. No Hearing

If no hearing is held, the AJ must analyze whatever evidence and statements are provided by the appellant, even though they are not made under oath or subject to cross-examination. When no hearing is held, it is possible that submissions from the agency and appellant alike may not be under oath or subject to prehearing cross-examination. Walker v. VA, 4 MSPR 78, 80, 4 MSPB 166 (1980) ("By rejecting appellant's statements of explanation for the reason that, as they were not made under oath and subject to cross-examination, they were of no probative value, the presiding official, in effect, denied the appellant his right to due consideration of his affirmative defenses to the action taken by the agency."). The general rule is that written statements are admissible and constitute substantial evidence to support an administrative determination despite their hearsay character. Evans v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-683).

A problem surfaces when the statements conflict and there is no hearing to determine credibility issues. Should all the statements be unsworn and contradictory, the Board, recognizing that it is the agency's burden to prove a case through the best evidence available, will carefully examine the amount and quality of the evidence presented and reverse the agency if the unsworn statements offered by the appellant appear more convincing than those offered by the agency. See Raines v. USPS, 32 MSPR 56, 58 (1986); Taylor v. VA, 30 MSPR 460, 460-01 (1986) (agency's evidence was insufficient when it consisted of an unsworn statement by the appellant's supervisor and unsworn statements containing a contrary version of the incident from two coworkers and the appellant); cf. Gunville v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-13-0220-I-1 (NP 12/5/2014) (crediting sworn declaration offered by the agency and noting: "Here, because the appellant did not request a hearing, it is immaterial whether persons with firsthand knowledge were available to testify at the hearing. Moreover, as stated above, the statement is sworn.").

In Sanders v. USPS, 801 F.2d 1328, 1331-32 (Fed. Cir. 1986), involving a removal based on the alleged sale of cocaine, the record consisted of conflicting sworn and unsworn statements. Affirming the Board's affirmation of removal, Sanders stated:

[H]earsay must be evaluated on a case-by-case basis to determine if the hearsay is inherently truthful and more credible than the evidence offered against it. Therefore, hearsay has been held to be substantial evidence in some cases and not in others.

...Sanders sought to rebut with his uncorroborated flat denial.... He made no sworn statement to show how the police were wrong or that he was right. There is a strong presumption in the law that administrative actions are correct and taken in good faith. It takes "well-nigh irrefragable proof" to overcome the presumption.... Sanders failed to present any proof and only attacked the quality of the government's evidence. True, the paperwork of the police was not exemplary. But, it was all the MSPB had to go on and it was evidence which, as to its accuracy, was sworn to by Postal Inspector Gordon before Sanders was removed.

...

Further, it would be a most peculiar and unjust procedure to permit Sanders to overcome the government's documentary evidence on the grounds of hearsay when he offers nothing to counter it but his unsworn, self-serving denial and is the one who precluded the government from offering live testimony, subject to cross-examination, by waiving a hearing, which under our precedent prevented the presiding official from granting the government's request for a hearing.... He cannot have it both ways....
