11. Government Corporation

Although Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation employees may appeal Chapter 75 actions, the government corporation is not covered by Chapter 43. Shelton v. FDIC, 38 MSPR 303, 305 n.1 (1988); see Jonson v. FDIC, 121 MSPR 56, 2014 MSPB 22 (2014) (dissent by Member Robbins) (Board has jurisdiction to review removal of an FDIC employee); Dockery v. FDIC, 64 MSPR 458, 460-62 (1994) (detailed opinion explaining why the FDIC is a government-controlled, but not a government-owned, corporation and why its employees may not appeal denials of within-grade increases under 5 USC chapter 51 and 5 USC 5331(b)); cf. NTEU v. Helfer, 53 F.3d 1289 (D.C. Cir. 1995) (sustaining agency authority to appoint Schedule A liquidation grade employees without competitive examination). Calhoun v. Federal Nat'l Mortgage Ass'n, 823 F.2d 451 (11th Cir. 1987), determined that FNMA employees are not subject to civil service laws. FNMA is a private corporation with civil service coverage only under the retirement laws. Koszola v. FDIC, 393 F.3d 1294 (D.C. Cir. 2004), analyzed the Resolution Trust Corporation Whistleblower Act.

Although employees of government corporations may not obtain Board review of arbitration awards disposing of challenges to some actions that would otherwise be appealable to the Board, grants of Board jurisdiction over government corporation employees' claims appear to include whistleblower allegations and reductions in force. The 1994 amendments to the Whistleblower Protection Act provided jurisdiction to the Board over whistleblower cases brought by government corporation employees. Pub. L. 103-424 § 5, codified at 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(C)(i), Snead v. PBGC, 74 MSPR 501, 502-03 (1997):

The Board has jurisdiction to review an arbitration decision under 5 USC 7121(d) where the subject matter of the grievance is one over which the Board has jurisdiction, the grievant alleges discrimination as stated in 5 USC 2302(b)(1) in connection with the underlying action, and a final decision has been issued. See Schulte v. Department of the Navy, 72 MSPR 466, 468, dismissed, No. 973138, 1997 WL 61200 (Fed. Cir. Feb. 4, 1997) (Table). The Board has jurisdiction here over the appellant's removal, see 5 USC 7512(1), 7513(d), 7701(a), and a final arbitration decision has been issued. The appellant has also claimed in his request for review that the agency effected his removal in retaliation for his filing of "numerous" equal employment opportunity (EEO) complaints. A claim of reprisal for filing an EEO complaint is an allegation of discrimination under 5 USC 2302(b)(1). See Parnell v. Department of the Army, 58 MSPR 128, 130-31 (1993). The agency, however, argues that it is excluded from coverage under 5 USC 2302, with the exception of 5 USC 2302(b)(8), because it is a wholly-owned United States government corporation; it therefore contends that the appellant may not invoke 5 USC 2302(b)(1) in alleging that the agency discriminated against him and that the Board thus lacks jurisdiction over his request for review.

A "Government corporation" is excluded from coverage under 5 USC 2302 except in the case of an alleged prohibited personnel practice described under subsection (b)(8). See 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(C)(i). For purposes of title 5, "Government corporation" means a corporation owned or controlled by the Government of the United States; a "Government controlled corporation" does not include a corporation owned by the Government of the United States. See 5 USC 103. As the Board has previously noted in Dockery v. FDIC, 64 MSPR 458, 461-62 (1994), title 5 does not explain what a corporation owned by the Government of the United States is, but Congress has classified corporations elsewhere for budget and accounting purposes as either "mixed-ownership" corporations controlled by the Government of the United States or corporations that are "wholly owned" by the Government of the United States. See 31 USC 9101.

The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation was created by Congress, which specified by statute its powers, duties, and organization, and is explicitly classified as a "wholly owned" Government corporation. See 29 USC 1302; 31 USC 9101(3)(J). As a corporation owned by the Government of the United States, then, it is a "Government corporation" for purposes of title 5 and is excluded from coverage under 5 USC 2302 with the exception of subsection (b)(8). See 5 USC 103, 2302(a)(2)(C)(i). Congress did not include under section 2302(b)(8) matters covered under section 2302(b)(1). See Mitchell v. Department of the Treasury, 68 MSPR 504, 510 (1995). The appellant as an employee of the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation thus can only allege a violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(8) but cannot allege that it violated 5 USC 2302(b)(1). See 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(C)(i); Mitchell, 68 MSPR at 510. Absent an allegation of a violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(1) that we may consider, then, we lack jurisdiction over the appellant's request for review and dismiss it on that basis. See Schulte, 72 MSPR at 468-69.
