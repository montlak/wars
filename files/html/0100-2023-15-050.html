19. Mitigating Factors

On the mitigation side, in appeals from attendance-related adverse actions the most significant factors are usually the length and frequency of the AWOL, although other factors should be considered. The employee's defenses or arguments and mitigation may be raised for the first time during the appeal. If, for example, the appellant's defense to AWOL is that he was in fact on approved leave, he may raise and prove the defense despite his failure to do so during the reply process preceding the termination. See Orokos v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 639, 641 (1983). The Board may consider the appellant's medical problems and other mitigating factors, including treatment of arguable comparators, relating to AWOL charges. On review of an arbitration award, Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 (NP 5/2/2013), sustained an adverse action based on AWOL despite a claim of medical impairment and with consideration of several arguments in mitigation:

We agree with the appellant that his 19 years of service with the agency is a mitigating factor. See Lloyd v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 342, ¶ 14 (2005), aff'd, 180 F. App'x 911 (Fed. Cir. 2006). Although the appellant's medical and psychological conditions were not the proximate cause of his removal, they were indirectly related to it. We therefore agree with the appellant that this is a mitigating factor as well. See Roseman v. Department of the Treasury, 76 M.S.P.R. 334, 345 (1997). But see Hanna v. Department of the Army, 42 M.S.P.R. 233, 241 (1989) (the lack of any direct connection between the appellant's mental condition and his misconduct reduced that condition's value as a mitigating factor).

The appellant also argues that other mitigating factors include his prior performance, the nature of his position, and the lack of notoriety of the offense. We find that these factors do not weigh "heavily" in favor of mitigation as the appellant argues. Rather, we find that they are not especially significant in this case. See Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 306 (not every penalty factor will be pertinent in every case). According to the appellant, in the 4 years leading up to his removal, he was rated "fully successful" twice, "minimally successful" once, and "effective" once. We find that these summary ratings are rather unremarkable and that the appellant's performance can fairly be described as adequate. This factor, therefore, does not weigh strongly in the overall penalty analysis. As for the appellant's position itself, although the position was not high-level or high-profile, the prohibition against AWOL applies equally to all federal employees. Therefore, we find that this is a neutral penalty factor. As for the lack of notoriety of the offense, we find that this will rarely be a mitigating factor and that this case presents no exception. We agree with the appellant, however, that this is at least not an aggravating factor.

The appellant further argues that his AWOL did not affect his ability to perform the duties of his position, as evidenced by his previous summary ratings. He argues that his absences were not disruptive. We find, however, that the appellant is merely disagreeing with the arbitrator's finding that the appellant's AWOL was disruptive. This disagreement does not establish that the arbitrator's decision was contrary to civil service law. In addition, noting again that the appellant's past performance was merely adequate, we find the offense did affect the appellant's ability to perform in his position because he cannot perform any work at all while he is AWOL.

The appellant argues that his misconduct was unintentional. However, regardless of the appellant's subjective intent in failing to secure leave for his absences, we find no basis to disagree with the arbitrator that his conduct was in reckless disregard for the consequences. The appellant further argues that he desires to return to work with an accommodation. However, like the appellant's lack of intent, his desire to return to work is outweighed by his repeated failure to adhere to the rules of his workplace and his disregard for the consequences.

The appellant also argues that the arbitrator's failure to make findings on each AWOL specification impaired the penalty analysis. He argues that the agency only properly charged him with a total of 1.75 hours of AWOL, but even if all 19.75 hours of AWOL were sustained, this would still not be enough to warrant removal. As explained above, the arbitrator sustained all twelve AWOL specifications, for a total of 19.75 hours of AWOL, and there is no basis for the Board to disturb that finding. Although no federal employee is entitled to be AWOL for any period of time, we agree with the appellant that, in terms of raw hours, this is by no means the most egregious case of AWOL that the Board has ever seen, but the number of hours at issue does not tell the whole story. If the appellant had incurred 19.75 hours of AWOL as part of a discrete incident, the matter would be less serious. However, the appellant incurred this AWOL day after day over the course of two months. This ongoing pattern of unreliability is not captured in the grand tally of AWOL hours, but we find that it is the most problematic aspect of the appellant's conduct.[9] We agree with the arbitrator that this repeated AWOL constitutes a serious offense. See generally Gartner v. Department of the Army, 104 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶ 13 (2007) (the Board places primary importance upon the nature and seriousness of the offense and its relation to the appellant's duties, position, and responsibilities).

[9] The appellant argues that the agency "piled on" the AWOL specifications in an effort to have him removed. Although the Board would frown upon an agency sandbagging one of its own employees in such a way, we find that that is not what happened here. The record shows that the appellant's supervisor warned him at least once during this time period that he was accruing significant amounts of AWOL. Yet the appellant continued to accrue more AWOL, thus piling the incidents on himself. Moreover, we find that the specifications refer to discrete acts of misconduct and are not duplicative in any way. Cf. Southers v. Veterans Administration, 813 F.2d 1223, 1225-26 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

The appellant argues that two higher-graded employees received lesser discipline for similar offenses. However, we find that the cited arbitration decisions show that the agency treated these employees the same as it treated the appellant. The agency removed them for AWOL. The agency's treatment of these two employees is consistent with its treatment of the appellant, and it therefore supports the agency's penalty selection in this case. See Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305. It was the arbitrators who decided to mitigate the comparison employees' removals. We find that, even if those arbitrators excised their judgment differently than did the arbitrator in this case, this does not establish a violation of civil service law, and it provides no basis for the Board to disturb this arbitrator's penalty analysis. For the same reason, we find that the greater leniency that the appellant received in a previous arbitration in front of a different arbitrator is immaterial to the outcome of the instant appeal.

The appellant argues that removal in this case would be inconsistent with the concept of progressive discipline. We disagree because the appellant already received two suspensions for AWOL in the 2 1/2 years leading up to his removal. Accordingly, we find that the agency already attempted twice, albeit unsuccessfully, to remediate the appellant's behavior through progressive discipline. The appellant further argues that an arbitrator's mitigation of his previous 25-day suspension to a 13-day suspension is evidence that the penalty in the instant appeal should be mitigated as well. Like the arbitrator in this case, however, we fail to see the connection.

The appellant argues that his rehabilitative potential weighs in favor of mitigation because he admitted to some of the AWOL specifications and has sought medical treatment. We disagree that the appellant shows rehabilitative potential. The appellant's leave problems have been ongoing for years, and the agency has given him numerous opportunities to rehabilitate himself during that time, including counseling, leave restriction letters, and lesser discipline. The appellant's failure to avail himself of these many opportunities is the best evidence of his rehabilitative potential.

The appellant argues that, because the AWOL charge was related to his disability, the maximum reasonable penalty was a 60-day suspension. He cites several cases in support of his argument. However, we do not interpret these decisions as creating a hard-and-fast rule that removal is never appropriate when there are such mitigating factors involved. Cf. Thom, 114 M.S.P.R. 169, ¶ 7 (removal for AWOL was appropriate even though the appellant's absences were related to a medical condition). We find that the arbitrator's failure to mitigate on this basis does not constitute a violation of civil service law.

Finally, the appellant argues that the deciding official was not a credible witness and that the agency failed to present credible evidence of his rationale for the removal decision. We find, however, that the removal decision speaks for itself and adequately explains the deciding official's rationale. To the extent that the appellant is disputing the arbitrator's credibility determinations, he has not explained how civil service law would require different credibility determinations. Therefore, this matter is beyond the scope of Board review. See Robinson, 30 M.S.P.R. at 395-96.
