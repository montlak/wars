j. Denial of Misconduct; Vigorous Defense

In ascertaining the reasonableness of removal as a penalty, the Board may take into account an employee's deliberate misleading of agency investigators and presentation of false testimony at a Board hearing. DeWitt v. Dept. of Navy, 747 F.2d 1442 (Fed. Cir. 1984); see Reynolds v. USDA, 54 MSPR 111, 115 (1992) (finding of a lack of rehabilitative potential was justified, in part, by the failure of the appellant to admit that he erred concerning a conflict of interest). A disingenuous defense should, however, be distinguished from a case involving a vigorous defense. Defending oneself does not demonstrate a lack of remorse. See Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 616, 621 (1994) ("[i]t is inappropriate to consider an appellant's denial of misconduct as an aggravating factor in determining the maximum reasonable penalty."); Dubiel v. USPS, 54 MSPR 428, 434 (1992) (agency deciding official improperly considered factors that included the appellant's attempts to defend himself against charges as an indication that he lacked remorse and failed to comprehend the nature of his wrongdoing).

In a footnote to Gose v. USPS, 451 F.3d 831, 834-35 n.3 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (on remand, 105 MSPR 101, 2006 MSPB 361 (2006), the Federal Circuit observed that there should be support for a conclusion that an appellant lacked remorse:

As for lack of remorse, Ashworth stated:

I am unable to find mitigating circumstances for your response that you needed to have a membership card to gain entry into the VFW. I have considered your seeming lack of remorse for the impact your continued drinking while in uniform had with our customers. Postal records in this file, and your own acknowledgement, indicates that you were previously notified of your responsibility regarding drinking in uniform. You have not acted appropriately in your own behalf in this matter even after being forewarned.

It is not clear whether Ashworth ever entertained the possibility that Gose was not exhibiting a "lack of remorse" but rather felt he had been wrongly accused. After all, whether indignation or remorse is appropriate turns entirely on whether or not Gose had actually violated an ELM provision. Gose believes that he did not.

Under Johnson v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 46, 52 n.1 (1994):

Although an employee's expression of remorse for his or her conduct may demonstrate potential for rehabilitation, see, e.g., Dolezal v. Dept. of the Army, 58 MSPR 64, 71 (1993), aff'd, 22 F.3d 1104 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table), we do not foreclose other circumstances under which an employee may, while denying the charged misconduct, still demonstrate potential for rehabilitation. We have, however, found no such circumstances in this appeal. Moreover, an employee's potential for rehabilitation, is only one factor to be considered in determining whether the agency-imposed penalty is appropriate. See Douglas v. VA, 5 MSPR 280, 305-06 (1981).

Von Muller v. Dept. of Energy, 101 MSPR 91, 101 ¶ 21, 2006 MSPB 18 (2006), noted as to an agency's assertion that the appellant lacked rehabilitative potential when there was a defense but also some recognition of wrongdoing:

We agree with the administrative judge that the record does not support Norman's negative view of the appellant's rehabilitative potential. While the appellant's responses to the proposed removal are at times defensive, they also include acknowledgements of wrongdoing, expressions of remorse, and assurances that the misconduct will not be repeated. We find that the appellant's potential for rehabilitation was at worst neutral, and should not have been considered as an aggravating factor.

See Rosario-Fabregas v. Dept. of Army, NY-0752-13-0142-I-2 (NP 7/1/2016) ("The appellant offered the agency an apology for 'having failed to understand that having corrected some letters helping citizens could offend the agency reputation nationwide.' The fact that the appellant may have vigorously contested the agency's charges in his response to the proposal and on appeal to the Board does not indicate a lack of remorse. See Smith v. Department of the Navy, 62 M.S.P.R. 616, 621 (1994) (finding it inappropriate to consider an appellant's lack of remorse for the misconduct when that lack of remorse is a consequence of the denial of misconduct)."); Raco v. SSA, 117 MSPR 1, 9 ¶ 16, 2011 MSPB 87 (2011) ("[w]e agree with the administrative judge that the appellant admitted to wrongdoing and expressed remorse. The appellant's reply included an explanation of her behavior and the mitigating factors that weighed in her favor. That the appellant mounted a defense against the agency's charges does not indicate that she lacked remorse or failed to admit to wrongdoing, and we are not persuaded by the agency's attempt to argue otherwise.").

But the Board can determine, as in Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 500, 509 (1995), that a strong defense demonstrates a lack of rehabilitative potential:

Moreover, as we have indicated, there is no evidence of potential rehabilitation in this case. To the contrary, the appellant's petition for review is a continuation of his attempts to rationalize his actions, without acknowledging that they were inappropriate. Indeed, as we have stated, the appellant had been warned 3 years earlier that such actions were inappropriate and impermissible....

See Talavera v. AID, 104 MSPR 445, 448 ¶¶ 10-11, 2007 MSPB 1 (2007) (although the deciding official may consider the appellant's denial of misconduct as a component of the appellant's potential, or lack of potential, for rehabilitation, the denial should not serve as a separate but unstated charge in support of the adverse action); Richardson v. RTC, 66 MSPR 302, 313 (1995) (crediting the agency's contention that it lost trust and confidence in the appellant, an asset marketing specialist, when the appellant presented, "under oath at the Board's hearing, a version of events which we do not credit to explain the purported circumstances of her misdemeanor offense."); cf. Obiedzinski v. USPS, PH-0752-15-0522-I-1 (NP 10/5/2016) ("The Board has found that an appellant's attempt to shift the blame for his misconduct to others or his display of an arrogant attitude during the removal process reflects poor potential for rehabilitation. Alberto v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 M.S.P.R. 50, ¶ 10 (2004); Adam v. U.S. Postal Service, 96 M.S.P.R. 492, ¶ 9 (2004), aff'd, 137 F. App'x 352 (Fed. Cir. 2005)."); Stanford v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0444-I-1 (NP 9/15/2016) ("Finally, although not discussed by the proposing or deciding officials, the appellant's attempt to blame his supervisors for failing to detect his falsification reflects a lack of remorse for his actions, which is an aggravating factor. See Neuman v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 26 (2008) (finding that an appellant's rationalizations and lack of remorse were aggravating factors)."); Grubb v. Dept. of Interior, 96 MSPR 377, 397 ¶ 49 (2004) ("[t]he appellant's refusal to acknowledge that she has done anything wrong reflects very poorly on her potential for rehabilitation."); Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 637 ¶ 93 (2001) (noting the appellant's unwillingness to acknowledge that she engaged in any misconduct and also noting "the extent to which her attitude had deteriorated"); McKoy v. DOD, 33 MSPR 195, 199 (1987) ("the agency's argument that the appellant's denial of the charge serves to support a finding that the misconduct occurred is without merit").

In 1998 the Supreme Court determined that agencies could separately charge employees with misconduct and lying when they denied the misconduct during the course of administrative investigations. LaChance v. Erickson, 118 S. Ct. 753 (1998) [Discussed in Chapter 8, "Investigative Misrepresentation; 'Exculpatory No'."] Before the decision of that Court, the Board held that an appellant should not be separately charged with falsification for denying a charge and putting an agency to its proof. See Chatman v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 582, 587 n.2 (1997) (simple denial of a charge and its related facts may not be considered in determining the penalty); Hamilton v. USPS, 71 MSPR 547, 558 (1996) (appellant's failure to admit misconduct could not be considered by the deciding official determining the penalty); Alexander v. USPS, 67 MSPR 183, 187-90 (1995) (sexual harassment case: if a penalty was otherwise justified, it would not be set aside because the deciding official considered as an aggravating factor the appellant's denial of the charges); Fowler v. USPS, 77 MSPR 8, 15 (1997) ("[I]f an employee denies a charge or its underlying misconduct, that fact can not be considered in determining the reasonableness of the penalty." (citing Jefferson v. USPS, 73 MSPR 376, 383 (1997))).
