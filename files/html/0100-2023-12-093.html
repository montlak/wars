broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section B: Interim Relief, Subsection 05: Dismissal of PFR for Failure to Provide Interim Relief, Part b: Motion to Dismiss PFR for Lack of Proper Interim Relief
b. Motion to Dismiss PFR for Lack of Proper Interim Relief

5 CFR 1201.116 (2022), provides:

Compliance with orders for interim relief.

(d) Request for dismissal for noncompliance with interim relief order. If the agency files a petition for review or a cross petition for review and has not provided required interim relief, the appellant may request dismissal of the agency's petition. Any such request must be filed with the Clerk of the Board within 25 days of the date of service of the agency's petition. A copy of the response must be served on the agency at the same time it is filed with the Board. The agency may respond with evidence and argument to the appellant's request to dismiss within 15 days of the date of service of the request. If the appellant files a motion to dismiss beyond the time limit, the Board will dismiss the motion as untimely unless the appellant shows that it is based on information not readily available before the close of the time limit.

...

(g) Allegations of noncompliance after a final decision is issued. If the initial decision granted the appellant interim relief, but the appellant is not the prevailing party in the final Board order disposing of a petition for review, and the appellant believes that the agency has not provided full interim relief, the appellant may file an enforcement petition with the regional office under 1201.182 of this part. The appellant must file this petition within 20 days of learning of the agency's failure to provide full interim relief. If the appellant prevails in the final Board order disposing of a petition for review, then any interim relief enforcement motion filed will be treated as a motion for enforcement of the final decision. Petitions under this subsection will be processed under 1201.183 of this part.

Before and after various regulatory changes in the Board's interim relief regulations, procedural issues concerning interim relief are or were addressed or resolved by case law. Objections by the appellant to interim relief, or the lack of it, should be made by a motion to dismiss the agency PFR. The motion, as part of or separate from the response to the agency PFR, should be filed within the time allowed for the appellant's response to the PFR. See Simien v. USPS, 99 MSPR 237, 242 ¶ 10 (2005) ("nothing in the February 12 submission, or in any other document in the record, indicates that the appellant's challenge was based on information not readily available before the close of the 25-day time limit. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(a). The appellant's claim of noncompliance with the interim relief order therefore is not properly before us."); Bartram v. USPS, 93 MSPR 74, 76 n.1 (2002) (motion to dismiss a PFR for noncompliance with an interim relief order must be filed before the record on review closes unless it is based on new and material evidence that was not readily available before the close of the record); Coates v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 362, 366 (1997) ("Where, as here, an appellant's motion to dismiss an agency's petition for review is filed after the close of the record on review, the Board will consider it only if it is based on information not readily available before the record closed."); Moultrie v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 153, 158 (1994) ("Any motion to dismiss for failure to effect interim relief must be filed within 25 days of the date that the agency files a PFR, except for good cause shown."); Rothwell v. USPS, 64 MSPR 473, 476 (1994) (denying a motion to strike because the motion was filed after the record closed and was based on information available before the record closed); Holt v. USPS, 63 MSPR 198 (1994); Forma v. DOJ, 57 MSPR 97, 102 (1993) (declining to consider interim relief issues when the appellant's pleading is filed after the close of the record without showing that it was based on information not readily available before the record closed); cf. Armstrong v. DOJ, 107 MSPR 375, 381 n.4, 2007 MSPB 280 (2007) (overruled on other grounds, Edwards v. Dept. of Labor, 2022 MSPB 9 (2022)) (although 5 CFR 1201.116 calls for the agency to respond within 15 days of service to the appellant's motion to dismiss a PFR for failure to provide interim relief, the Board considered an agency response filed after the regulatory deadline but before the close of the record on review); Alston v. SSA, 95 MSPR 252, 255 ¶ 6 (2003) (as to information submitted after the close of the PFR record concerning employment conditions during the appellant's interim relief: "[t]he appellant has not explained why she is raising these complaints at this late date, nor has she shown that they are based on new and material evidence").

Should the appellant move to dismiss a PFR for lack of interim relief, and if that motion is based on factual assertions, those assertions should be made through an affidavit or statement signed under penalty of perjury. See Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 325, 335 (1993). An allegation is frivolous if it is not supported with specific assertions of fact that, if proved, would establish that the agency was not in compliance with the AJ's interim relief order. See Dennis v. VA, 62 MSPR 462, 464 (1994).

The Board may issue a show-cause order if the agency's submissions call into question its compliance and compliance cannot be determined on the record. See Johnson v. DOJ, 67 MSPR 494, 497 (1995) ("If an appellant makes a non-frivolous challenge to the sufficiency of what otherwise appear to be adequate interim relief measures, the Board will permit the agency to explain its position. Forma v. Department of Justice, 57 M.S.P.R. 97, 102, aff'd, 11 F.3d 1071 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). Normally the Board will issue a show-cause order for this purpose, id., but here, the appellant's motion to dismiss put the agency on specific notice of potential deficiencies in its interim relief measures, and the agency has addressed each of the points raised by the appellant."); Harvey v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 120, 123-24 (1994) ("When an appellant's submission raises nonfrivolous issues of fact relating to an agency's compliance with an interim relief order, the Board issues a show-cause order requiring it to respond with evidence and argument to the allegations of noncompliance...before a show cause order could be issued, the agency submitted evidence and argument regarding compliance in response to the appellant's allegations."); cf. Dennis v. VA, 62 MSPR 462, 464 (1994); Abbott v. VA, 67 MSPR 124, 128 (1995) ("An agency's own evidence of compliance with interim relief may raise the issue, even where it has not been asserted by the appellant."). No show-cause order will be issued if the agency responded to the allegations of noncompliance and the allegations can be resolved on the basis of the existing record. Forma v. DOJ, 57 MSPR 97, 102 (1993).

A show cause order is not issued if the interim relief issue is addressed by the parties. See Rothschild v. Dept. of Treasury, 78 MSPR 93, 95-96 (1998) ("Because it appears that the parties have fully addressed the compliance issue, we need not issue a show-cause Order and resolve the matter here."); Luciano v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 441, 448 (1997) ("Because the appellant's motion to dismiss and response to the agency's supplemental evidence of compliance put the agency on notice of his perceived deficiencies in its interim relief measures, and the agency has addressed each of the points that he raised, the Board need not issue a show-cause order here."); Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 73 MSPR 476, 483 (1997) (show-cause order was unnecessary when the agency answered the appellant's motion to dismiss for the lack of interim relief before the Board could issue a show-cause order); Sherron v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 70 MSPR 498, 502 (1996) ("[W]e find that the issuance of a show-cause order is unnecessary because we find that the appellant's motion to dismiss placed the agency on notice of his perceived deficiencies in the interim relief provided."); Barron v. Dept. of Transp., 68 MSPR 446, 448-49 (1995) ("Normally, the Board will issue a show-cause order for this purpose, but here, the appellant's motion to dismiss and response to the agency's supplemental evidence of compliance put the agency on notice of the appellant's perceived deficiencies in its interim relief measures."); Pencook v. USPS, 67 MSPR 409, 413 (1995) (the Board did not issue a show cause order because the agency quickly responded to the appellant's allegations concerning interim relief). Should the appellant incorporate a motion to dismiss into the appellant's cross-PFR, the Board may allow the agency to respond to the motion in the time allowed from response to the cross-petition rather than the shorter time allowed for response to the motion to dismiss for lack of proper interim relief. O'Regan v. VA, 74 MSPR 134, 139 (1997) (agency relied on the date set by the MSPB Clerk for response to a cross-PFR).

If based on new evidence or defects in the AJ's decision awarding interim relief the Board questions whether interim relief should have been granted, the Board may decline to penalize the agency that does not provide interim relief. The Board may vacate the relief order and remand the case to the AJ to evaluate that aspect of the remedy properly, with any necessary reevaluation of the appeal. See Kizer v. Dept. of Navy, 87 MSPR 249, 255 ¶ 16 (2000) (remanding a RIF appeal for proper assessment of the competitive level based on a veterans preference determination; the remand order vacated the initial decision as well as the interim relief order, leaving to the discretion of the AJ the reinstatement of interim relief upon issuance of a remand initial decision); Gaddas v. OPM, 61 MSPR 650, 653 (1994) (vacating interim relief order rather than dismissing OPM's PFR because of information, that the AJ erroneously failed to mention, placing into question the accuracy of the AJ's determination that interim relief was warranted). The Board is not inclined to accept evidence of the agency's failure to provide interim relief that was readily available before the PFR record closed. Sims v. DOD, 58 MSPR 131, 134 (1993); see Gamber v. USPS, 58 MSPR 142, 144 n.3 (1993) (declining to consider the appellant's protestation of noncompliance when it was filed after the close of the record on review--defined as the expiration of the period for filing the response to the PFR or, if applicable the cross PFR); cf. Jacobs v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 688, 690-91 (1994) (applying the same rule to an untimely submission from the appellant concerning agency noncompliance with an interim relief order).
