d. Against Others

Resisting arrest and assault on an agency police officer warrants removal; the nexus "speaks for itself." Depte v. United States, 715 F.2d 1481, 1485 (Fed. Cir. 1983); see Pollard v. USPS, 24 MSPR 472, 474 (1984) (removal of a letter carrier who struck a police officer in face; although no injury resulted, striking a police officer can be expected to have a more adverse effect on the ability of the agency to provide a safe working environment than striking a fellow employee). Excessive force (kicking a citizen in the head after he was already subdued) during an arrest led to removal of a Secret Service officer. Goldstein v. Dept. of Treasury, 62 MSPR 622, 629 (1994) (The appellant violated agency directives and training concerning the means of controlling an individual: "The misconduct itself, which involved the use of unnecessary force against a criminal suspect who had already been subdued, went to the heart of the appellant's duties as a law enforcement officer and could readily damage the public's confidence in the agency's ability to fulfill its law enforcement role in a responsible manner."), rev'd and remanded for reconsideration, Goldstein v. Dept. of Treasury, 51 F.3d 1570 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Board relied too much on how bystanders perceived an arrest and too little on what proper police procedures required for the protection of the officer and the public); cf. Crickard v. VA, 92 MSPR 625, 628-32 ¶¶ 5-14 (2002) (remanding for more specific findings on a charge that an agency police officer used more than necessary force to subdue an individual). Demotion was warranted for a correctional officer who used excessive force to restrain an inmate. Cristia v. DOJ, 36 MSPR 75, 79 (1988); cf. Brenner v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3456) (arbitrator properly concluded that a police officer failed to exercise professional demeanor in response to a traffic control incident at a Navy station).

A letter carrier's removal was affirmed for striking a five-year-old boy and engaging in a controversy with a member of the public while delivering mail, in violation of agency regulations. Guevara v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1438); see Taylor v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 438, 444 (1987) (removal of a clerk-typist based on a conviction for the involuntary manslaughter of the appellant's child; supervisors lost confidence in the appellant; coworkers stated that her misconduct impaired her ability to work with them); Brown v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 132, 135 (1987) (removal for voluntary manslaughter: "[c]ontinued employment of a person convicted of violent criminal conduct can diminish public respect for the agency and undermine its efficiency by dissuading qualified job applicants who prefer not to work with or near a co-employee who has been convicted of shooting a human being. Abrams v. United States Department of Navy, 714 F.2d 1219, 1223 (3rd Cir. 1983).")).

Removal was sustained, after a plea of self-defense was discredited, of a foreman who struck and injured a nonemployee during an altercation at a parking lot of the work site. Whelan v. Dept. of Army, 8 MSPR 486, 490 (1981); see Scofield v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 179, 189 (1992) (GS-12 Special Agent was removed for an off-duty altercation with his girlfriend, leading to a conviction for battery, and misuse of a government vehicle); cf. Bristen v. Dept. of Treasury, 9 MSPR 447, 449-50 (1982) (removal for frequent outbursts and threats against employees; some outbursts occurred in the presence of taxpayers). An assistant store manager's removal was mitigated to a suspension for using abusive and offensive language and committing an assault on a salesman, whose firm provided merchandise for a commissary, by grabbing the salesman by the tie and shaking him two or three times. Boese v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 325, 327-28 (1984); cf. Venson v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 375, 377, 9 MSPB 606 (1982) (removal for an off-duty incident in a NCO club involving the infliction of harm to club patrons and abusive language to agency police).

One kick to the buttocks of a personnel specialist netted an administrative law judge a 14-day suspension. Dept. of Commerce v. Dolan, 39 MSPR 314, 318 (1988); cf. Long v. SSA, 635 F.3d 526 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (Judge Dyk, concurring) (removal of an administrative law judge for conduct involving an off-duty domestic altercation). A supervisor of customer services was removed when, after verbal and possible physical abuse or provocation from a customer, the supervisor struck the customer. The supervisor could have defused or avoided the escalating confrontation with the customer but did not do so. Harris v. USPS, 100 MSPR 613, 620-21 ¶ 14 (2005). Thompson v. USPS, DA-0752-15-0590-I-1 (NP 12/16/2022) ("[W]hile the individual's use of racial slurs was offensive and should be considered as a mitigating factor, it was insufficient to relieve the appellant of his obligation to retreat when possible to avoid a physical confrontation, even if the events occurred in a short period of time....The appellant argues that Harris is distinguishable because there the appellant was a supervisor and held to a higher standard. We disagree. The presence of that additional aggravating factor does not alter the separate finding that provocation does not justify violence if the appellant had an opportunity to retreat."); Allman v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-09-0562-I-1 (NP 7/2/2010), sustained removal of a criminal investigator for abuse of Taliban detainees during interrogations. Davis v. DHHS, DE-0752-10-0062-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010), sustained removal of a counselor aid with IHS who was alleged to have hit a male resident youth. [Refer to Chapter 7, "Violent Off-Duty Misconduct."]
