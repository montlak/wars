broida flra Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards, Subchapter 03: Statutory Elections of Remedies That Preclude Grievance Arbitration, Section A: Grievance or ULP Filings; 5 USC 7116(d), Subsection 05: Timing of Filing

5. Timing of Filing

Timing may be determinative. If the union first files a grievance and then files a ULP charge on a related matter, the arbitrator has jurisdiction even though the demand for arbitration postdates the ULP charge or complaint. The arbitrator must resolve the grievance and cannot decline to do so because the case is also under review or in litigation before FLRA. Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs, Chemawa Indian Boarding Sch. and NFFE Local 241, 39 FLRA 1322, 1324 (1991). If the grievance is filed before the ULP and then an amended grievance is filed, FLRA allows the arbitrator to apply the contract and the statute to permit the amended grievance to relate back to the original grievance so that the original grievance, and not the amendment, controls the filing date for purposes of resolving a § 7116 election issue. See DOD, DCA, Defense Commercial Communications Office, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 49 FLRA 1492, 1496 (1994). For a filing prior to a ULP charge to constitute an election, it must truly be a grievance. A letter or protest not constituting a grievance under the contract will not block the later ULP. Norfolk Naval Shipyard and Tidewater Virginia FEMTC, 9 FLRA 458, 471-72 (1982) (ALJ Decision).

If the question is whether a grievance is precluded by the earlier filing of a ULP charge, the issue is whether the charge raises the same matter under the ULP procedure. It is immaterial that the charge is later withdrawn short of final disposition or complete adjudication of the matter presented by the charge. Hq., Space Div., Los Angeles Air Force Station and AFGE Local 2429, 17 FLRA 969, 970-71 (1985); see SPORT and Edwards AFB, 71 FLRA 626, 628 (2020) (Member DuBester dissenting) ("the Authority has consistently held that an issue is raised for purposes of § 7116(d) upon the filing of a ULP charge, even if that charge is subsequently withdrawn"); IAM Lodge 39 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Aviation Depot, Norfolk, 44 FLRA 1291, 1298-99 (1992) ("[T]he theories relied on by the Union in support of its contentions were the same in the ULP charge and in the grievance. The Union argued in both the ULP charge and in the grievance that the Agency had violated its obligation to negotiate over the Union's request for a higher rate of EDP.... We find no merit in the Union's contention that the ULP charge could not be a bar to the Arbitrator's consideration of the grievance because the ULP charge was withdrawn and never adjudicated. The Authority has consistently held that an issue is "raised" within the meaning of section 7116(d) of the Statute at the time of the filing of a grievance or a ULP charge, even if the grievance or ULP charge is not adjudicated on the merits"); DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Butner and AFGE Local 3696, 18 FLRA 831, 832 (1985) ("[T]he issue raised in the grievance procedure was not the same as the issue raised in this unfair labor practice proceeding. See Department of Defense Dependents Schools, Pacific Region, 17 FLRA No. 135 (1985). Thus, the grievance complains that the suspension of Officer Painter was 'without just cause and for other than reasons to promote efficiency of service.' The gravamen of the charge and complaint herein concerned the interference and restraint imposed on Officer Painter's exercise of his protected right under the Statute to investigate and secure information in the processing of the grievance, not the merits of the suspension.").

When a union filed a grievance before a ULP, clearly the selection favored the grievance procedure and an arbitrator correctly ruled that the case was not precluded by the later filing of a ULP. Adjutant Gen. of Pennsylvania and Pennsylvania State Council ACT, 8 FLRA 208, 209 (1982); see ACT Show-Me Army Chap. and Dept. of Army, Mo. NG, 58 FLRA 154, 155 (2002) (grievance was not precluded by subsequent ULP charge; contract language mirrored § 7116(d)). Section 7116(d) blocked a ULP concerning the failure of the employer to disclose information, a crediting plan, to the union after the union filed, but did not pursue, a grievance on the same problem. Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. Region VIII, San Francisco and NTEU, 13 FLRA 631, 634 (1984). The matter is raised sufficiently to constitute an election even if the grievance is returned without action and is not decided on the merits. Lowry AFB, Denver and AFGE Local 1974, 32 FLRA 792, 794-95 (1988).

Although the case law often addresses the blocking effect of an earlier-filed ULP on a later-filed grievance (or the reverse), the law does not permit simultaneous filing, Dept. of Transp., FAA and NATCA, 62 FLRA 54, 55-56 (2007):

In order for a grievance to be precluded under § 7116(d) by an earlier-filed ULP charge: (1) the issue that is the subject matter of the grievance must be the same as the issue that is the subject matter of the ULP charge; (2) such issue must have been earlier raised under the ULP procedure; and (3) the selection of the ULP procedure must have been in the discretion of the aggrieved party. See United States Dep't of the Army, Norfolk Dist., Army Corps of Eng'rs, Norfolk, Va., 59 FLRA 906, 909 (2004) (citing United States Dep't of Health & Human Servs., Soc. Sec. Admin., Office of Hearings & Appeals, Region II, 36 FLRA 448, 451 (1990)). The Agency disputes the Arbitrator's finding, with respect to the second requirement, that it did not establish that the ULP charge was filed before the grievance. For reasons different than those asserted by the Agency, we find that the Arbitrator erred as to the second requirement.

The Arbitrator found, as matters of fact, which are undisputed, that the ULP charge and the grievance were filed on the same day and that the Union "denied on the ULP form that [it] 'raised this matter in any other procedure[.]'" However, contrary to the Agency's claim, the Union official's assertion does not require a conclusion that the ULP charge was filed first. In this regard, as the Arbitrator pointed out, the Union official's assertion leaves open the possibility that the grievance was filed first if the Union official's assertion was based on the belief that the issues raised in the earlier-filed grievance were different than those being raised in the ULP charge. The Union claims that this is the case. Thus, the Union official's statement on the ULP charge form, standing alone, does not resolve the issue of which filing was first.

Nevertheless, even assuming the Union's filings were simultaneous, this would not establish that the grievance was proper under § 7116(d). In this connection, § 7116(d) provides that "issues which can be raised under a grievance procedure may...be raised under the grievance procedure or as an unfair labor practice under this section, but not under both procedures." As such, the plain wording of § 7116(d) requires the Union to make an election between the ULP procedure and the grievance procedure. See, e.g., Headquarters, Space Div., L.A. Air Force Station, Cal., 17 FLRA 969, 971 (1985) (interpreting § 7116(d) as requiring an "election of procedures"). The Statute makes no exception to the election requirement for simultaneous filings. Thus, the award is inconsistent with § 7116(d) unless the grievance was filed before the ULP charge. It is undisputed that there has been no finding that the grievance was filed first. Indeed, the Union has never claimed that the grievance was filed first. Rather, the Union has consistently maintained--both in arbitration and before the Authority--that the ULP charge and the grievance were filed "simultaneously." As there is no claim or arbitral finding that the grievance was filed before the ULP charge, and as there is no dispute that the other two requirements of § 7116(d) were satisfied, the award is deficient.

Based on the foregoing, we conclude that the Union's grievance over the Agency's alleged unilateral change in work schedules is barred by § 7116(d) of the Statute. As the award reflects, the Union withdrew its ULP charge over the same issue. Consequently, the Union has exhausted its options to pursue this claim in either of the procedures provided for in § 7116(d) of the Statute.

If a ULP charge is dismissed as untimely filed, it still blocks a later grievance addressing the same subject matter, Dept. of Air Force, Minot AFB and AFGE Local 4046, 70 FLRA 867, 867-68 (2018) (Member DuBester dissenting) (recon. den., Dept. of Air Force, Minot AFB and AFGE Local 4046, 71 FLRA 188 (2019) (Member DuBester dissenting)):

In this case, we reaffirm that § 7116(d) of the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute (the Statute) precludes the filing of a grievance which presents issues that are substantially similar to those raised in an earlier-filed unfair-labor-practice (ULP) charge. This is no less true when the earlier-filed ULP charge was dismissed because it was not filed by the Union in a timely manner.

...

In May 2016, the Union filed a ULP charge, alleging that the Agency violated the Statute when it unlawfully changed the grievants' working conditions, "resulting in the loss of pay and premiums." Specifically, the Union alleged that the Agency unlawfully: (1) changed overtime practices for the grievants without bargaining with the Union; (2) revoked an agreement that allowed the grievants to teach a certain class; (3) retaliated against the grievants by investigating their use of hazard pay; (4) arbitrarily determined that previous hazard-pay approval was "no longer valid and declared [that] prior approval was void"; and (5) "[d]irected the [grievants] to change time[-]card documentation on [hazard pay] and change the way [that they] enter coding for payment."

The FLRA's Chicago Regional Office investigated the Union's overtime and retaliation claims and, on November 23, 2016, Regional Director Sandra J. LeBold dismissed the ULP charge.

...

The Agency argues that the award is contrary to § 7116(d) of the Statute because the Union's earlier-filed ULP charge bars the grievance. As relevant here, an earlier-filed ULP charge bars a grievance under § 7116(d) of the Statute if the ULP charge and a grievance involve the same issue.[10]

[10] In addition, for an earlier-filed ULP charge to bar a grievance under § 7116(d) of the Statute, the issue that the grievance raises must have been previously raised under the ULP procedure, and the selection of the ULP procedure must have been at the discretion of the aggrieved party. See, e.g., U.S. Dep't of the Navy, Naval Air Eng'g Station, Lakehurst, N.J., 64 FLRA 1110, 1111 (2010) (citing U.S. Dep't of HHS, Indian Health Serv., Alaska Area Native Health Servs., Anchorage, Alaska, 56 FLRA 535, 538 (2000)).

The Authority has held that a ULP charge and a grievance involve the same issue where they: (1) arise from the same set of factual circumstances, and (2) advance substantially similar legal theories.[11] In U.S. Department of the Navy, Navy Region Mid-Atlantic, Norfolk, Va. (Navy),[12] the Authority recently clarified that the legal theories in the ULP charge and the grievance need not "be identical" in order for the ULP charge to bar the grievance.[13]

[11] See, e.g., U.S. DHS, ICE, L.A., Cal., 68 FLRA 302, 304 (2015).

[12] 70 FLRA 512 (2018).

[13] Id. at 516-17.

Here, the Union's earlier-filed ULP charge alleged, in relevant part, that: (1) the Agency violated the Statute when, in March 2016, it arbitrarily determined that the grievants' previous hazard-pay approval "was no longer valid," and (2) such change "result[ed] in the [grievants'] loss of pay." The Union's later-filed grievance alleged, as relevant here, that the Agency violated the Statute by terminating its practice of paying the grievants hazard pay without bargaining with the Union and, as a remedy, requested retroactive hazard pay beginning in March 2016. Thus, the ULP charge and the grievance arise from the same set of factual circumstances: the Agency's decision to change its hazard-pay practices. Further, the ULP charge and the grievance allege statutory violations based on that change and seek pay remedies back to March 2016--the time of the initial change. As the Authority noted in Navy, "[w]e cannot simply turn a blind eye when parties, through carefully crafted pleadings, try to avoid the § 7116(d) bar in order to get two bites of the proverbial apple." In our view, that is what the Union's grievance attempts to do here. Therefore, we find that the Union's earlier-filed ULP charge and the grievance raise substantially similar issues, and that the Arbitrator erred as a matter of law in concluding that § 7116(d) does not bar the grievance. Accordingly, we set aside the award.
