f. Reassignment During or Following RIF

When during a RIF an individual's position is abolished and he displaces someone in the same type of position and same grade on a retention register, he has not been released from his competitive level and has no MSPB RIF appeal. He has essentially been reassigned; that the agency provides a notice of appeal rights is insufficient to confer jurisdiction on the Board. Saxton v. District of Columbia Gov't, 7 MSPR 364, 365, 7 MSPB 244 (1981) ("5 C.F.R. 351.201 states that each agency shall follow the reduction-in-force procedures 'when it releases a competing employee from his/ her competitive level...." "Since the appellant was not released from his competitive level, the procedures were not applicable."). If an agency releases a competing employee from his competitive level by reassignment requiring the displacement of another employee, the agency must follow RIF procedures. These procedures are optional if the employee is released for the purpose of a RIF through a reassignment that does not require displacement; the agency has the option of reassigning the employee without a RIF. Mantick v. DHUD, 6 MSPR 358, 359, 6 MSPB 305 (1981).

The Board has jurisdiction over a reassignment to a position of the same grade, pay, title, and series, if the reassignment is directed for RIF reasons and the agency uses RIF procedures to accomplish the reassignment. Marcinkiewicz v. FAA, 10 MSPR 631, 632 (1982). If an agency uses RIF procedures to reassign an employee when it would not have had to use a RIF, the employee still has an appeal. When an agency effected release and reassignment under RIF procedures, the appellant was entitled to the procedures and protections of RIF regulations. The Board determines the propriety of an agency action solely on the grounds invoked by the agency for taking the action. Patton v. Smithsonian Inst., 26 MSPR 609, 610 (1985) (RIF reassignment); Payne v. Dept. of Interior, 26 MSPR 159, 160 (1985) (RIF reassignment); see Mantick, supra. But see Saxton, supra. The employee who is reassigned through a RIF may challenge the RIF on the basis that the employee is unqualified to perform the position. The agency may waive qualification requirements, and the Board is inclined to honor the agency's waiver, as in Ratkus v. GSA, 12 MSPR 464, 465-66 (1982), involving a personnel management specialist who declined a reassignment to the position of safety manager:

In rejecting the agency's offer of reassignment, appellant contended that she was unqualified for the position because she lacked the specialized experience required. She further contended that she was incapable of meeting the physical demands of the position.

Under 5 CFR § 351.702, an agency is permitted to waive qualifications for a position to which an employee is offered reassignment pursuant to a RIF where it determines that the employee satisfies "any minimum educational requirements for the position" and "has the capacity, adaptability and special skills needed to satisfactorily perform the duties and responsibilities of the position." In Petranek v. Department of the Army, 4 MSPB 472, 473, 8 MSPR 419, 421 (1980), the Board, citing Miatech v. United States, 553 F.2d 105, 213 Ct. Cl. 688, 689 (1977), and Losure v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 2 MSPB 361, 2 MSPR 195 (1980), stated that "[a]lthough it has been held that the determination as to the qualifications for a position is a matter within the discretion of the agency...such determination is subject to review by the Board under 5 CFR Part 351."

In the instant case, the agency asserted that there were no minimum education requirements for the Safety Manager position, and appellant did not contradict that assertion. Also, appellant's supervisor, the agency's Regional Personnel Officer, determined that appellant was able to perform the duties of the new position without undue interruption and that appellant "would not be asked to do anything in that job which she felt would be detrimental to her health." Although appellant had alleged that she was physically unable to perform satisfactorily in the new position because of her back problems, the new position was essentially sedentary. Furthermore, appellant's supervisor stated that the agency was willing and able to restructure the job or otherwise accommodate appellant. Since the agency would have accommodated appellant and appellant was not otherwise shown to be physically unqualified for the position, we find that the agency satisfied its burden of proof under 5 CFR § 351.702. Therefore, we find that the presiding official erred in finding that the RIF was not conducted pursuant to regulations.

See Monk v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 560, 564 (1995) (noting OPM regulations allowing agencies to waive qualification requirements when filling vacancies as part of a RIF); Haataja v. Dept. of Labor, 25 MSPR 594, 601 n.11 (1985) (recapitulating Ratkus); Murphy v. Dept. of Energy, 8 MSPR 695, 697 (1981) (rejecting appellant's contention that he did not receive a valid offer of assignment because he was unqualified for the job assigned to him through the RIF; concluding that he had the necessary qualifications; "an agency may properly waive the qualification requirement(s) in making an assignment to an employee who has been released from his competitive level"); cf. O'Connor v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 400, 402 (1982) (appellant questioned the agency's best offer because, he maintained, he was not physically qualified for the position; the objection was known to the agency, at least at the MSPB hearing; the agency had the burden of proof on the appellant's qualifications for the assigned position).
