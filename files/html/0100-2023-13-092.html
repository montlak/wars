c. Evaluation of Leave Enforced Following Notice to Employee

Norrington v. Dept. of Air Force, 83 MSPR 23, 26-27 ¶¶ 8-13 (1999), provides an example of enforced leave imposed with due process and adequate medical justification based on an employee's physical inability to perform his job as an aircraft mechanic:

The agency's placement of an employee on enforced leave for more than fourteen days, based on the employee's physical disability, constitutes a suspension within the Board's jurisdiction because the absence from the workplace is involuntary. Pittman v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 832 F.2d 598, 599-600 (Fed. Cir. 1987). See also 5 USC §§ 7512(2), 7513(d) and 7701(a). To sustain a suspension, the agency must prove by preponderant evidence that the charged conduct occurred, that a nexus exists between the conduct and service efficiency, and that the penalty is reasonable. Pope v. U.S. Postal Service, 114 F.3d 1144, 1147 (Fed. Cir. 1997). However, once an agency learns that the employee is fit for duty, the employee must be restored immediately to active duty status. Mercer v. Department of Health & Human Services, 772 F.2d 856, 858 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

We agree with the administrative judge's findings that the appellant was properly placed on enforced leave based on the evidence available to the agency at the time it made that decision. Cf. Barresi v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 MSPR 656, 664 (1994) (the critical factor in determining whether an indefinite suspension may be imposed pending investigation into the appellant's conduct is the evidence the deciding official had before him at the time he acted). Further, we agree that the appellant failed to show disparate treatment. Because there is no reason to disturb these findings, we find that the agency's action was procedurally correct, and that the agency supported its basis for the action by preponderant evidence.

We reopen this case to modify the initial decision to find that the appellant failed to provide sufficient medical evidence to show that he was fit to return to duty. The agency's enforced leave decision provides that the appellant's leave will continue until he submits medical evidence "reflecting that [he is] physically capable of performing the duties of [his] position without endangering [his] health." On May 15, 1998, he submitted to the administrative judge both the March 4, 1998 letter from Dr. Palmer and the March 17, 1998 letter from Dr. Buafo, both of which released him to return to work without any restrictions. Dr. Palmer's March 4, 1998 letter simply stated that: "After further review of Dr. Buafo's (cardiologist) evaluation of [the appellant's] cardiac status, I concur with Dr. Buafo that [he] may return to full work duty without restrictions." Dr. Buafo's March 17, 1998 letter noted that: "[The appellant] was seen in my office on September 15, 1997. At this visit [the] patient state[d] that he could not perform his job safely because his duties involved climbing up and down and getting into remote areas of the airplane. At patient['] s request he is now being released to return to work."

These two work releases do not constitute sufficient medical evidence to show that the appellant is physically capable to perform his duties without endangering his health. Nothing in the record indicates that the releases were supported by additional medical tests or that they were issued based on the appellant's medical condition. Moreover, in his April 22, 1998 deposition, Dr. Buafo clearly testified that his March 17, 1998 release letter had been prepared only because the appellant indicated to him that he wanted to return to work. Also in his deposition, Buafo emphasized that the nature of the appellant's coronary artery disease could not have improved since his most recent evaluation.

Additionally, we note that, after receiving the work release letters from Drs. Buafo and Palmer, the agency's occupational physician, Dr. Luppino, reevaluated the appellant's status in light of those letters. Luppino concluded that the appellant's condition had not changed and that his prior work restrictions (i.e., involving lifting etc.) remained consistent with his diagnosis. The appellant did not offer any evidence to rebut the medical opinions of Drs. Buafo and Luppino.

Because the appellant did not submit any objective medical evidence to indicate that his medical condition had improved, we find that he failed to meet his burden to produce competent medical evidence demonstrating his physical capacity to perform his duties without endangering his health. See Barnes v. United States Postal Service, 49 MSPR 21, 26 (1991). Thus, we affirm the initial decision, as modified by this Opinion and Order, still sustaining the enforced leave action.
