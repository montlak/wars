broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 10: Filing PFR With Wrong Agency or MSPB Office, Part a: Mixed Case Appeals
a. Mixed Case Appeals

Petitions for review are filed with the Clerk of the Board, but sometimes they go astray. The mixed case procedural section of the Reform Act provides, 5 USC:

§ 7702. Actions involving discrimination

...

(f) In any case in which an employee is required to file any action, appeal, or petition under this section and the employee timely files the action, appeal, or petition with an agency other than the agency with which the action, appeal, or petition is to be filed, the employee shall be treated as having timely filed the action, appeal, or petition as of the date it is filed with the proper agency.

[Refer to Chapter 2, "Irregular and Misdirected Filings."]

The savings provision was touched on in Schlottman v. Perez, 739 F.3d 21, 22 (D.C. Cir. 2014):

Because the federal government's administrative procedures for resolving complaints of discrimination are complex and confusing, individuals sometimes file their complaints with the wrong agency. In an effort to deal with this problem, Congress adopted a savings clause: "[i]n any case in which an employee is required to file any action...under this section and the employee timely files the action...with an agency other than the agency with which the action...is to be filed, the employee shall be treated as having timely filed the action...as of the date it is filed with the proper agency." 5 U.S.C. § 7702(f) (emphasis added)....

Under 5 USC 7702(f), a provision of the Reform Act concerning the processing of mixed cases, the Board will waive its timeliness requirements if an appeal involving EEO claims is timely, but mistakenly, filed with an agency that lacks jurisdiction to hear the appeal. See Moore v. Dept. of Commerce, Bureau of the Census, 55 MSPR 451, 455-57 (1992) (after the appellant sought EEOC review of an arbitration award based on erroneous written advice from the agency, he was not required to withdraw the EEOC appeal and take his case to the Board when the agency belatedly verbally notified him of the correct review route and the appellant decided instead to await the EEOC decision); Rupp v. DHHS, 51 MSPR 456, 463 (1991) (delay in seeking an arbitration review was excused after the appellant improperly invoked review by the EEOC; the appellant did not receive notice of review rights from the agency, union, or arbitrator). But see Williams v. USPS, 51 MSPR 186, 188 (1991) (good cause for delay is not provided if the decision to go to EEOC instead of MSPB is for the purpose of seeking EEOC review; "the appellant chose to file a petition for review with the EEOC, and the Board will not waive its timeliness requirements when an appellant consciously elects another forum"); Van Meter v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 468, 471-72 (1989) (request for review of an arbitration award was untimely because the initial review request to the EEOC did not meet the Commission's deadline; other reasons were not presented to excuse the delayed filing) (citing Marenus v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 498, 503 (1989)); cf. Gallegos v. Dept. of Air Force, 78 MSPR 626, 627 n.* (1998) (appellant's submission to the EEOC was not considered a misdirected PFR "because it was filed 5 days after the September 4, 1997 initial decision became final").

The Board accepted a PFR addressed to EEOC, Board of Review and Appeals, noting that procedural steps taken by employees should be liberally construed in order to find compliance with jurisdictional requirements, Portlock v. VA, 4 MSPR 306, 308, 4 MSPB 376 (1980):

The Board is aware that the provisions of the Civil Service Reform Act, combined with Reorganization Plan No. 1 of 1978 have occasioned substantial confusion on the part of both employees and agencies concerning the various procedures, choices of forum, and possible election of remedies in adjudicating cases made appealable to the Board which also raise issues of prohibited discrimination. 5 USC § 7702. We believe that procedural steps taken by employees in these cases should be liberally construed in order to find compliance with jurisdictional requirements. See, e.g., 5 USC § 7702(f); 5 CFR § 1201.151(b).
