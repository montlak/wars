d. Counsel Authorized to Appear in Appeal; Federal Circuit Admission

If a party seeks representation, the representation must be provided by an attorney who is admitted to practice before the Federal Circuit. The admissions process is straightforward. Request for admission to the bar of the Circuit is through Form 21, found on the court's website. If a party seeks the assistance of an attorney who does not or will not qualify for admission to the court, it seems the attorney may prepare the pleadings or briefs for the party, who then files them pro se. See Mendoza v. MSPB, 966 F.2d 650, 652 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 1992) ("While pro se, Mendoza's pleadings appear to have been phrased by someone familiar with legal procedures. Foreign counsel who do not become members of the bar of this court may not sign or file pleadings for a party."). There are ethics and court opinions on whether counsel's participation in preparation of pro se submissions--ghostwriting--should be disclosed to the court. Rules of the Federal Circuit change over time, and those rules should be consulted to determine if the court must be informed of assistance by counsel who prepares the brief or court pleadings for a pro se appellant. See Bhojani, S. Attorney Ghostwriting for Pro Se Litigants--A Practical and Bright-Line Solution to Resolve the Split of Authority Among Federal Circuits and State Bar Associations, 65 SMU L. Rev. 653 (2012). https://scholar.smu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1195&context=smulr.
