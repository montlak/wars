1. Filing Dates; Untimely Exceptions

FLRA described the application of its regulations governing calculation of a filing date when the exceptions are served by mail, Army Armament Research Dev. and Eng'g Ctr. Picatinny Arsenal and NFFE Local 1437, 52 FLRA 527, 530 (1996):

Under section 2423.28(b) of the Authority's Regulations, the 10-day time limit for filing an opposition is calculated from the date the exceptions are served, not from the date they are received. U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Social Security Administration, 50 FLRA 296, 298 (1995). Under section 2429.22, when exceptions are served on a party by mail, only 5 days are added to the 10-day limit for filing an opposition, even when, as alleged here, it takes more than 5 days from the date of service before the exceptions are actually received. Id. Section 2429.21 provides that an opposition's filing date "[is] determined by the...postmark date[.]"

The time for filing an opposition to the exceptions, and the mail extension are computed separately. If either period expires on a weekend or federal holiday, the period is deemed not to expire until the end of the next workday. Air Force Flight Test Ctr., Edwards AFB and AFGE Local 3854, 55 FLRA 116, 118 (1999). The five day extension for mailed documents is added at the end of the filing period. Fed. Bureau of Prisons, FCI Bastrop and AFGE Local 3828, 55 FLRA 848, 848 (1999). During the Pandemic starting in early 2020, FLRA is not accepting in-person filings. The FLRA website should be checked to determine when in-person filings will again be accepted.

Failure to timely file exceptions will not lightly or likely be waived. The postmark ordinarily determines the filing date. See USDA, Farm Serv. Agency and NTEU, 55 FLRA 22, 23 (1998) (problems with internal mail systems do not present extraordinary circumstances for untimely exceptions to ALJ Decision); VA, Los Angeles Reg'l Office and AFGE Local 490, 44 FLRA 15, 17 (1992) (postmark controls; failings of party's internal mail system is no excuse for untimely filing). Section 2429.21 of the FLRA regulations was amended on December 31, 1986, to change the definition of "filing" from receipt at FLRA's national office to the date of mailing. Under the regulation, if there is no postmark date on the mailing, the submission is presumed to have been mailed five days prior to receipt. If certified mail is used, the postmarked certified mail receipt is proof of a date in lieu of a postmarked envelope. Under USPS regulations, metered or franked mass mailings by government agencies do not require a postmark unless the agency requests that a specific mailing be postmarked.

Given the ability of a party to obtain a postmark, under VAMC, Muskogee and AFGE Local 2250, 29 FLRA 51, 53 (1987) (a decision denying reconsideration), that an agency's mailing that lacks a postmark cannot be shown through extrinsic evidence to have been mailed on a different date than established by FLRA regulations:

[S]ection 2429.21(b) does not state a rebuttable presumption. Regarding postmarked mailings, the date of the postmark itself establishes the date of filing and cuts off any dispute over the actual mailing date. The postmark provides fair, uniform, and objective evidence of the date of filing. For mailings without a postmark, the use of the word "presumed" in the Authority's 5-day rule merely describes how the rule is implemented rather than establishing a rebuttable presumption.

Irregular filings of exceptions may result in denial of exceptions, as occurred in DOJ, INS, Border Patrol, El Paso and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 40 FLRA 792, 793 (1991). The exceptions were found in FLRA's "in-box" in the FLRA docket room on the morning of the day after they were due. There was no postmark or evidence of mailing attached to the submission, and no date stamp or other indication of how or when the exceptions arrived. They were untimely.

Tinker AFB v. FLRA, 321 F.3d 1242, 1245-47 (10th Cir. 2002), upheld FLRA's strict construction and application of its regulation detailed precisely how exceptions to ULP ALJ decisions are to be filed. The agency's exceptions were apparently timely sent, but to the wrong room of FLRA, and apparently FLRA's case processing unit did not learn of the exceptions until opposition briefs were filed by the union and FLRA's General Counsel:

...Tinker AFB...attempted to file its exceptions on time but simply failed to follow the rules and sent its exceptions to the wrong place. It did not address its submission to the Case Control Office and it sent them to Room 440 instead of suite 415.

...

Tinker AFB further contends that enforcement of the FLRA's regulations about the time and place of filings "elevates form over substance and amounts to an abuse of discretion." Yet, the general rule is well established that reviewing courts mill not overturn an agency's strict application of its own procedural regulations so long as the rule is applied uniformly or with reasoned distinctions. See, e.g., Gilbert v. NTSB, 80 F.3d 364, 367 (9th Cir. 1996) (upholding strict application of agency filing deadlines); Hooper v. NTSB, 841 F.2d 1150, 1151 n.2 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (same); Green Country Mobilephone, Inc. v. FCC, 765 F.2d 235, 237 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (same). Here, the evidence is that the Authority consistently applies its filing requirements in a strict fashion. See, e.g., Cross R.B. at 2; United States Dept of Agric., Farm Serv. Agency, Kansas City, Mo. and United States Dept of Agric., Office of the Inspector Gen., Kansas City, Mo., 55 F.L.R.A. 22, 22-23, 1998 WL, 901702 (1998) (refusing to excuse one-day filing delay caused by party's internal mail system); Dept of Justice, United States Immigration and Naturalization Serv., United States Border Patrol, El Paso, Tex. and American Fed'n of Gov't Employees, AFL-CIO, Natl Border Patrol Council, 40 F.L.R.A. 792, 793, 1991 WL 133659 (1991) (considering as untimely exceptions found in the Authority's Case Control Office the morning after they were due without evidence of timely delivery); Dep't of the Treasury, Customs Serv., Washington, D.C. and Natl Treasury Employees Union, 38 F.L.R.A. 875, 877, 1990 WL 223197 (1990) (dismissing as untimely a filing made forty minutes late due to delays at the courier service hired to deliver the papers). Because Tinker AFB has provided no evidence nor cited any cases indicating that the FLRA enforces its filing regulations inconsistently, we should not overturn its strict enforcement of its regulations. See Gilbert, 80 F.3d at 368 ("[Petitioner] has presented no evidence that the NTSB has failed to apply its dismissal policy consistently. The NTSB, therefore, is free to enforce its strict rule."). "[T]he agency's strict construction of a general rule in the face of waiver requests is insufficient evidence of an abuse of discretion." Mountain Solutions, Ltd. v. FCC, 197 F.3d 512, 517 (D.C. Cir. 1999).

Tinker AFB also urges that its late filing should be excused because that mistake did not cause prejudice to any party. On April 13, 2001, Tinker AFB sent its exceptions to the FLRA as an entity, the Chief ALJ for the FLRA, the General Counsel and Deputy General Counsel, the regional director for the FLRA, the union, and unspecified others. Two of these entities, the union and the counsel for the General Counsel, timely filed oppositions to the exceptions with the case control office. It was the receipt of these responses that prompted the FLRA to issue its show cause order, offering Tinker AFB the opportunity to furnish proof that it had timely filed the exceptions. Tinker AFB contends that this chain of events demonstrates a lack of prejudice that should have resulted in the FLRA's acceptance of the exceptions. However, it is clearly established that "although absence of prejudice is a factor to be considered in determining whether the doctrine of equitable tolling should apply once a factor that might justify such tolling is identified, it is not an independent basis for invoking the doctrine and sanctioning deviations from established procedures." Baldwin Cty Welcome Ctr. v. Brown, 466 U.S. 147, 152, 104 S. Ct. 1723, 80 L. Ed.2d 196 (1984). Tinker AFB has not identified any independent factor that would justify the FLRA's acceptance of the exceptions.

Furthermore, it does not appear that Tinker AFB requested a waiver from the FLRA as contemplated by the regulation. 5 C.F.R. § 2429.23(b) ("Request for a waiver of time limits shall state the position of the other parties and shall be served on the other parties.").... Tinker AFB should not be heard to complain now that it was an abuse of discretion for the FLRA to deny them a waiver they never requested.

FLRA does not appear to excuse delays in exceptions caused when parties send the exceptions to the wrong FLRA office. The exceptions are to be filed in FLRA's Docket Room at its headquarters offices in Washington. AFGE Local 1457 and Dept. of Army, Letterman Army Med. Ctr., San Francisco, 36 FLRA 253, 254 (1990); 5 CFR 2429.24(a) (2022). The AFGE Local 1457 result was, however, set aside on a motion for reconsideration. FLRA noted that it could extend or waive the time for filing exceptions to ULP decisions under 5 CFR 2429.23 (2022). It was appropriate to permit a waiver of the time limit because the party filing the exceptions had been misled by FLRA agent as to the proper place for mailing the exceptions. AFGE Local 1457 and Dept. of Army, Letterman Army Med. Ctr., San Francisco, 39 FLRA 519, 520-21 (1991). If, however, a series of extensions is requested and granted and the filing deadline is still missed, FLRA is not likely to waive the late filing unless there are extraordinary circumstances. See Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 691 (1991) (bad weather did not justify waiver of expired time limit). Distinguished from irregular filing of exceptions is erroneous service of exceptions. If a party claims not to have been properly served with exceptions, FLRA is inclined to waive the defective service if it is quickly cured. An extension of time will then be allowed for filing a response to the exceptions. See Long Beach Naval Shipyard and FEMTC, 44 FLRA 1021, 1023-24 (1992). Each party is responsible for securing any necessary extension for filing a document with FLRA; an extension granted to one party, e.g., the charging party, will not operate to the advantage of an allied party, e.g., the General Counsel. IRS, Philadelphia Serv. Ctr. and NTEU, 54 FLRA 674, 681 (1998) (marking departure from prior practice permitting extension to allied party).

Exceptions a day late were excused due to weather conditions, including closure of the FLRA offices, DOJ, Bureau Of Prisons, FCI Coleman and AFGE Local 506, 67 FLRA 632, 633-34 (2014):

The Respondent filed, and the Authority granted, a motion for an extension of time--until Monday, March 17, 2014--to file exceptions to the Judge's decision. When the Respondent filed its exception on Tuesday, March 18, 2014, the Authority issued an order directing the Respondent to show cause why the exception should not be dismissed as untimely. In its response to the Authority's order, the Respondent argues that it was unable to file its exception timely due to a snowstorm and federal-government closure on March 17. According to the Respondent, because both it and the Authority were closed on the Monday due date, "[a]ny attempt at service on the Authority was a physical impossibility" even though the Respondent was otherwise "ready [and] willing" to file its exception on time. The Respondent further argues that service on the Authority by courier or the U.S. Postal Service was "not possible" due to the emergency weather conditions, and that electronic service was not possible because the Respondent's counsel had no access to his files and "is not currently set-up" to file electronically through the Authority's electronic-filing system. The Respondent requests that the Authority waive the expired time limit for filing its exception on the ground that the snowstorm and government closure constitute "extraordinary circumstances" under § 2429.23(b) of the Authority's Regulations.

Under § 2429.23(b) of the Authority's Regulations, "the Authority...may waive any expired time limit...in extraordinary circumstances." The Authority has found extraordinary circumstances where the reason for the filing being untimely was outside of the filing party's control, such as, for example, where the U.S. Postal Service delivered a properly addressed and timely postmarked filing to the incorrect agency.[16] In contrast, the Authority has declined to find extraordinary circumstances where the filing party demonstrated a lack of diligence, such as, for example, when the filing party or its internal mail system erred.[17] With specific regard to weather, the Authority declined to find extraordinary circumstances in both SSA[18] and U.S. Department of Transportation & FAA (FAA).[19] In SSA, a snowstorm closed the union and agency offices on the due date, the union filed its opposition eight days later, and the union's counsel was unaware of the delay because he was on personal leave.[20] The Authority stated that "[e]ven if the Authority were to find that the office closings constituted an extraordinary circumstance, the use of 'personal leave' does not constitute an extraordinary circumstance" that excused "the additional filing delay after the office closings."[21] In FAA, the agency filed exceptions five days late and argued to the Authority that its decision to release employees early on the due date in anticipation of both severe weather and the Thanksgiving holiday occurring the following day was an extraordinary circumstance excusing its late filing.[22]

Here, unlike in SSA and FAA, the weather (and the resulting government closure) is the sole reason for the Respondent's untimely filing. Specifically, both the Respondent and the Authority's Office of Case Intake and Publication (CIP) closed, at the direction of the Office of Personnel Management, on the Monday due date because of a snowstorm.[23] It is undisputed that the Respondent's counsel is a federal employee who could not access his office on the due date because of the closure, and, because the storm occurred on a Monday, could not anticipate the need to bring files home before the weekend. Further, the Respondent could not request an additional extension for filing because the Authority was also closed. And, unable to file on the due date, the Respondent promptly filed its exception the following day, when the government offices reopened. Thus, unlike cases where filing parties demonstrated a lack of diligence, in this case, the Respondent acted diligently to file as quickly as it could. And because the Respondent's inability to file its exceptions timely was due to circumstances beyond its control, this case is akin to decisions where the Authority waived an expired deadline based on extraordinary circumstances.[24]
