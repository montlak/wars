g. Hearing

Under 5 CFR 1201,183(a)(3) (2022), concerning procedures for processing enforcement petitions, "the judge may convene a hearing if one is necessary to resolve matters at issue." A hearing may be convened when necessary to resolve matters at issue, but a hearing is not required. Espinoza v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 679, 683 (1996) ("Under Board regulations governing petitions for enforcement, an administrative judge may convene a hearing if one is necessary to resolve matters at issue."); see Allen v. VA, 420 Fed. Appx. 980, 989 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) ("There is no right to an evidentiary hearing in a petition for enforcement, and whether to hold a hearing is within the discretion of the Board. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)(3). Mr. Allen, moreover, has not alleged that he suffered any prejudice when he was denied a hearing."); Weed v. SSA, 367 Fed. Appx. 144, 147 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (no fault with the Board's practice that there is no right to a hearing regarding an enforcement petition and the Board's practice of considering hearsay in enforcement cases); King v. Dept. of Navy, 98 MSPR 547, 552 ¶ 9 (2005) ("The Board's regulations clearly provide that the decision to hold a hearing in a compliance matter is discretionary. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(b)(3). Moreover, the Board has consistently held that there is no right to a hearing regarding a petition for enforcement. Galliart v. Department of the Treasury, 84 M.S.P.R. 15 ¶ 9 (1999), aff'd, 232 F.3d 911 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table); Hawkins v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 633, 641 n.3 (1993)."); Green v. USPS, 67 MSPR 62, 66 (1995) (directing a compliance hearing); Thomas v. DHUD, 63 MSPR 649, 666 (1994) (remanding to AJ for consideration, among other matters, whether a compliance hearing was necessary; collecting cases concerning compliance hearings); Bolson v. DODDS, 55 MSPR 8, 11 (1992); Davis v. USPS, 52 MSPR 305, 310 (1992) (hearing was necessary to resolve a factual dispute involving a credible issue material to an allegation of breach of an agreement; citing 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(3)); Connor v. USPS, 50 MSPR 389, 394 (1991) (in a compliance case, a hearing is discretionary and the appellant failed to demonstrate that a hearing was necessary to resolve the issues or that the AJ abused his discretion by denying a hearing); Rolle v. USPS, 44 MSPR 323, 326 (1990) (AJ may require a hearing in an enforcement proceeding if one is needed to resolve matter at issue); Rickels v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 596, 602 (1989) (AJ did not abuse his discretion by denying a compliance hearing when he was able to resolve the issues based on the evidence the parties submitted).

"In enforcement proceedings, where the Board cannot determine from the agency's submission whether the agency is in compliance with the Board's order, the appellant is entitled to a hearing to determine whether the agency has fully complied." Vargo v. USPS, 78 MSPR 66, 73 (1998). The Board may direct a hearing, if there is no settlement of the enforcement petition, as to material factual disputes, e.g., whether the appellant has been restored to a position with duties commensurate to his grade and classification. See, e.g., Vaughan v. USPS, 77 MSPR 541, 549 (1998) (a breach of settlement: a hearing was discretionary); Davis v. USPS, 52 MSPR 305, 310 (1992) (remanding for a hearing to resolve credibility issues material to the petition to enforce a last chance settlement agreement); Wiatr v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 623 (1991) (Table) (hearing may be required whether or not it was requested by the appellant). Regardless of whether or not there is a hearing, a party may seek to transfer an enforcement case to another Board office, and that motion will be governed by the same venue considerations, discussed in Chapter 2, that govern changes in venue for hearings in other Board proceedings. See Fitzpatrick v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 556, 562 ¶¶ 14-15 (2002) (compliance proceedings involving settlement agreements).

The AJ should determine whether a hearing is appropriate after considering the evidence presented supporting and opposing the enforcement petition. If a hearing is to be secured, it should be requested when the enforcement petition is filed. Without this request, the right to a hearing will be deemed waived. Spezzaferro v. FAA, 807 F.2d 169 (Fed. Cir. 1986). A hearing request concerning compliance may be liberally construed. In Beaudin v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 597, 601 n.* (1988), a hearing was requested by an appellant's statement of his readiness to "testify, under oath" about matters at issue in his petition seeking compliance with a settlement agreement. If the appellant requests a hearing, the AJ should rule on the request before closing the record. See Burford v. USPS, 56 MSPR 460, 464 (1993).

The authority to direct a hearing includes a requirement that there be an explanation why a hearing is necessary. Covert v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 376, 382 (1986) (a hearing and discovery are discretionary in compliance cases); Covert v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 474, 477 n.* (1985), on appeal from remand, 31 MSPR 376, 382 (1986) (citing Green v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 3, 3 n.* (1986) (AJ has discretion to hold a hearing in an enforcement case), and Crispin v. Dept. of Commerce, 732 F.2d 919 (Fed. Cir. 1984)); Carlson v. USPS, 22 MSPR 650 (1984) (Table). The failure of the region to hold a hearing on compliance will not result in reversal if the compliance decision can be issued on the basis of written submissions. Compton v. Dept. of Energy, 706 F.2d 260 (8th Cir. 1983). But under Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 34 MSPR 632, 635 (1987), if there are disputes concerning restoration of the appellant to a position in the status quo ante, the AJ should fully develop the record, to the extent of supplementing written evidentiary submissions with an evidentiary hearing.

The Board has difficulty explaining when factual submissions sufficiently define disputes to require a hearing. The Board's lack of clarity on what it takes to get the Board to grant a hearing on an issue of compliance was the subject of a dissent by Judge Newman in Castle v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 91-3120):

It is the Board's obligation to require full compliance with settlements, by not only the employee but the agency. Denying the petitioner access to the Board's procedures (by requiring that she prove her case, before the Board will hear her case, while depriving her of the opportunity to develop the facts through the hearing process) simply acquiesces in agency carelessness or wrongdoing. It does not contribute to employee confidence in fair administration of the laws of federal employment, and thus disserves the nation.
