w. Military Service

Service on active duty in the military, coupled with a failure to receive a communication from the Board concerning a PFR, excused a delay in responding to a Board inquiry involving the PFR. Gaylor v. Dept. of Army, 31 MSPR 432, 432 (1986); see Abintenya v. VA, 15 MSPR 43, 44 n.1 (1983) (extending the time limit for PFR: "Appellant alleges that she was on active duty with the U.S. Army when the initial decision was rendered and consequently did not receive a copy of the decision"). But see Stoglin v. Dept. of Labor, CH-4324-12-0389-I-1 (NP 6/5/2013) (military service did not cover the complete period that appellant had to either file a PFR or request an extension of time to do so) (following remand, Stoglin v. Dept. of Labor, CH-4324-12-0389-M-1 (NP 2/27/2015), post); Dougherty v. FDIC, 52 MSPR 311, 314 n.2 (1992) (period of military leave of the agency representative was not an excuse for an untimely response to a PFR when the representative had 11 working days to complete the response and did not request an extension of time); Ferguson v. Dept. of Navy, 18 MSPR 689, 690 (1984) (declining to excuse a late PFR on the basis of a letter submitted verifying the appellant's service in the armed forces).

The Board requested a remand when Mr. Stoglin took his case to the Federal Circuit, but the Board reaffirmed its earlier rejection of his PFR untimely, Stoglin v. Dept. of Labor, CH-4324-12-0389-M-1 (NP 2/27/2015):

Generally, a petition for review must be filed with the Board within 35 days after the date of issuance of the initial decision. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(e). The appellant's September 7, 2012 petition for review was filed 17 days after the August 21, 2012 filing deadline. At issue on remand is whether the appellant's military duties from July 29, 2012, to August 10, 2012, and August 18, 2012, to August 24, 2012, constitute military service within the meaning of the SCRA and, as a result, if the Board's filing period was sufficiently tolled to render timely the appellant's petition for review.

The tolling provision of the SCRA provides that the "period of a servicemember's military service may not be included in computing any period limited by law, regulation, or order for the bringing of any action or proceeding in a court, or in any board, bureau, commission, department, or other agency of a State (or political subdivision of a State) or the United States by or against the servicemember or the servicemember's heirs, executors, administrators, or assigns." 50 U.S.C. app. § 526(a); Neighoff v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 86, ¶ 7 (2015). The SCRA defines "military service" for National Guard members to include: "service under a call to active service authorized by the President or the Secretary of Defense for a period of more than 30 consecutive days under section 502(f) of Title 32, United States Code, for purposes of responding to a national emergency declared by the President and supported by Federal funds." 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(ii). Additionally, in the case of a servicemember who is a member of the Army, "military service" means "active duty," as defined in 10 U.S.C. § 101(d)(1), which includes "full-time duty in the active military service of the United States," but "does not include full-time National Guard duty." 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(i); 10 U.S.C. § 101(d)(1).

Neither the appellant's service under the May 30, 2012 order nor under the July 23, 2012 order meet the definition of military service under 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(ii). Such service was clearly not active service for a period of more than 30 consecutive days to respond to a national emergency. Thus, to constitute military service, the appellant's Quartermaster Captain's Career training and Equal Opportunity Leader Course Instructor National Guard duty must qualify as "active duty" under 10 U.S.C. § 101(d)(1). See 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(i). Since full-time National Guard duty is expressly excluded from the definition of active duty, 10 U.S.C. § 101(d)(1), we find that the appellant's military duties pursuant to the July 23, 2012 order do not constitute military service within the meaning of the SCRA. Thus, the Board filing period was not tolled from August 18, 2012, to August 24, 2012.

The appellant's service under the May 30, 2012 order constituted active for duty training that would appear to fall within the definition of "active duty" under 10 U.S.C. § 101(d)(1). Cf. Brown v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 15 (2007) (the fact that "inactive-duty training" is defined separately from "active duty" in Title 10 and is not otherwise referenced in or by the SCRA makes it likely that it does not constitute "military service" under the SCRA). In any event, even assuming that it were military service that tolled the filing period from July 29, 2012, to August 10, 2012, the appellant's petition for review was still 4 days untimely. Accordingly, having applied the SCRA, we find that the appellant's petition for review was properly dismissed as untimely filed and the Board's prior final order finding the appellant failed to establish good cause for his untimely filing remains undisturbed.

Back to the Federal Circuit, but the court affirmed, Stoglin v. MSPB, 621 Fed. Appx. 662, 662-63 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP):

First, the Board did not err in its analysis of the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act of 2003, Pub. L. No. 108-189, 117 Stat. 2835 (2003) ("SCRA"). The SCRA tolls certain time periods provided certain conditions--including "military service"--are met. See 50 U.S.C. app. § 526(a). Service may constitute "military service" under either 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(i) or (ii).

Under subsection (i), the Board properly determined that only Mr. Stoglin's thirteen-day service period potentially qualified as "military service," and that, even if that were true, Mr. Stoglin's seventeen day late filing would still have been untimely. See 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(i); 10 U.S.C. § 101(d)(1). Furthermore, the Board also properly determined that Mr. Stoglin's other period of service did not qualify as "military service" because it was for full time National Guard duty. See 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(i); 10 U.S.C. §§ 101(d)(1),(5). Under subsection (ii), the Board properly determined that neither of Mr. Stoglin's two periods of service constituted "military service" because neither period lasted more than thirty consecutive days. See 50 U.S.C. app. § 511(2)(A)(ii).

Second, the Board did not err in determining that Mr. Stoglin failed to establish good cause to excuse the untimely filing. Mr. Stoglin does not raise any meritorious argument regarding this issue. In these matters, we "'will not substitute [our] own judgment for that of the Board.'" Walls, 29 F.3d at 1581 (citation omitted).
