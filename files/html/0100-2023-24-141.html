a. Implied Preclusion Under the CSRA

Back pay remedies may be limited by implication as a result of the Reform Act's structure. In United States v. Fausto, 108 S. Ct. 668 (1988), a nonpreference eligible excepted service employee was removed without being informed of his right to file an administrative grievance. Later, he was informed and permitted to pursue his grievance with the agency. In the interim, his term of employment expired. The agency granted him back pay, less the 30-day suspension, to the time his term of employment would have expired had he not been removed. He brought an action in the Claims Court [now the U.S. Court of Federal Claims] on the basis that his suspension was unwarranted and that he should have been granted back pay from the time of his removal until the time of the agency's decision to reverse the removal. The Claims Court dismissed the case because the Civil Service Reform Act precluded the suit. The Federal Circuit reversed, deciding that the Reform Act did not repeal remedies available to excepted service employees prior to its passage. Fausto v. United States, 783 F.2d 1020, 1023 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("If a federal civilian employee is granted rights by an executive department regulation and if that employee is accorded no right of redress by appeal to the Board under the CSRA, then the CSRA does not operate to repeal the Tucker Act/Back Pay Act jurisdiction of the Claims Court.").

In a decision written by Justice Scalia, joined by Justices Rehnquist, White, O'Connor, and Blackmun, the Court found that the CSRA replaced a previous "patchwork" of federal personnel law and provided exclusive remedies for those covered by the Act. The Court's Fausto decision stated, 108 S. Ct. at 674:

...The comprehensive nature of the CSRA, the attention that it gives throughout to the rights of nonpreference excepted service employees, and the fact that it does not include them in the provisions for administrative and judicial review contained in Chapter 75, combine to establish a congressional judgment that those employees should not be able to demand judicial review for the type of personnel action covered by that chapter. Their exclusion from the scope of those protections can hardly be explained on the theory that Congress simply did not have them in mind, since, as noted earlier, Congress specifically included in Chapter 75 preference eligible excepted service employees, § 7511(a)(1)(B), and specifically provided for optional inclusion (at the election of OPM) of certain excepted service employees with respect to certain protections of the chapter, including MSPB and judicial review....

Justice Blackmun filed a concurring opinion stating that, although the majority cited "persuasive evidence" of congressional intent to preclude judicial remedies for excepted service employees, he did not interpret the majority opinion to extend the Court's doctrine on "implied" repeal of previously recognized forms of judicial review. The dissent of Justice Stevens, joined by Justices Marshall and Brennan, charged the majority with creating preclusion out of "thin air," 108 S. Ct. at 683:

...Not surprisingly, the CSRA generally provides for review of an adverse personnel action by the Merit Systems Protection Board only when such review is necessary to protect the systems of hiring and promoting federal employees on the basis of merit. By definition...employees in the excepted service are not part of the system of merit-based hirings and promotions. Thus, their general exclusion from the protection of the MSPB is quite understandable. Since the availability of judicial review under the Act is tied to initial review by the MSPB, 5 USC 7703(a)(1), the CSRA does not provide any means by which a nonpreference eligible may seek judicial review. Thus, the failure to provide an avenue of judicial review for nonpreference eligible excepted service employees within the CSRA is not a preclusion of such review in all contexts, rather it is merely the consequence of the fact that actions against persons who are not in the competitive service do not pose a threat to the merit protection system....(footnotes omitted).

Although the opinion involved an excepted service employee, the majority in Fausto made it plain that they would extend the same holding to probationary employees. The case has since been applied to bar judicial review of dismissals of Postal Service employees who are not preference eligible. Witzkoske v. USPS, 848 F.2d 70 (5th Cir. 1988); see Elgin v. Dept. of Treasury, 132 S. Ct. 2126 (2012) (Justices Alito, Ginsburg, Kagan dissenting) (following Fausto, further developing the concept the preclusive effect of the CSRA). [Elgin is discussed earlier in this chapter, "Judicial Relief Outside the Reform Act."]

Fausto was relied on to bar a claim by a Bureau of Indian Affairs employee who had no direct appeal under CSRA to the MSPB and instead took her case directly to district court. The Federal Circuit, deciding that the trial court had no jurisdiction, directed dismissal of the case. Mullenberg v. United States, 857 F.2d 770, 772 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Other courts relied on Fausto to preclude district court review of dismissals of excepted service, nonpreference eligible, employees of the Postal Service. Royals v. Tisch, 864 F.2d 1565 (11th Cir. 1989); see Diaz v. USPS, 853 F.2d 5 (1st Cir. 1988) (a postmaster's discharge, apparently unreviewable other than through Postal Service grievance process, could not be heard as to nonconstitutional claims in federal court; jurisdiction was precluded, under Fausto, by remedial structure of Reform Act); Witzkoske v. USPS, 848 F.2d 70 (5th Cir. 1988) (under analysis similar to Diaz, a postmaster whose discharge was not then reviewable by MSPB could not challenge the discharge in court because of the preclusive effect of the Reform Act).

In a dispute over the negotiability of a labor contract provision concerning the scope of a grievance procedure, U.S. Capitol Police v. Office of Compliance, 908 F.3d 748, 763 (Fed. Cir. 2018), summarized Fausto:

Fausto presented the question of whether the Civil Service Reform Act ("CSRA") precluded a nonpreference eligible employee in the excepted service from seeking judicial review for an allegedly improper job suspension. Such review was admittedly not available before the MSPB under the CSRA. But the employee asserted that review was available in the Court of Claims under the Back Pay Act. Id. at 440-41. The Supreme Court noted that the CSRA "comprehensively overhauled the civil service system" and created an elaborate "new framework for evaluating adverse personnel actions against" executive branch employees, including the right to appeal agency decisions to the MSPB and then to this court. Id. at 443, 446 (citation omitted). But "[n]o provision of the CSRA gives nonpreference eligible members of the excepted service the right to administrative or judicial review" of adverse personnel actions. Id. at 443. Because the employee was classified as a "nonpreference eligible [employee] in the excepted service," the Court held that the CSRA precluded judicial review under the Back Pay Act, as well as under the CSRA. Id. at 455.

This was so because Congress intended the CSRA to provide "an integrated scheme of administrative and judicial review" of personnel action taken against executive branch employees. Id. at 445. The Court found that "the comprehensive nature of the CSRA [and the failure to provide review to nonpreference eligible employees as part of the comprehensive scheme for review], combine to establish a congressional judgment that those employees should not be able to demand judicial review." Id. at 448. Thus, the CSRA's "deliberate exclusion of employees in respondent's service category from the provisions establishing administrative and judicial review for personnel action of the sort at issue here prevent[ed] respondent from seeking review in the Claims Court under the Back Pay Act." Id. at 455.

Fausto may have further repercussions. Fausto and Bush v. Lucas, 462 U.S. 367 (1983), may be collectively interpreted to preclude any implied cause of action for monetary recovery against agencies or individuals. There is little doubt that if the case is against the government and the plaintiff could pursue his claims before the Board, an independent action in district court will be impliedly barred by the Reform Act remedial scheme. See Booth v. United States, 990 F.2d 617, 619 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (precluding Tucker Act suit in Court of Federal Claims); USIA v. Krc, 989 F.2d 1211, 1217 (D.C. Cir. 1993) (applying Fausto to suit when employee had remedy through Foreign Service Act).
