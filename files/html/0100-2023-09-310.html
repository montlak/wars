D. FAILURE TO FOLLOW PREHEARING OR HEARING PROCEDURES

Sanctions during the hearing may be imposed for several reasons. Appellants are sanctioned for failing to comply with prehearing orders. Agencies may fail to produce witnesses whose appearance the AJ directed, or agencies may be guilty of other sins of omission. The imposition of sanctions for failure to abide by prehearing procedures is within the discretion of the AJ, whose decision will not be set aside unless an abuse of discretion is shown. See Felter v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 132, 133-34 (1983) (no error when the AJ failed to sanction the agency for failing timely to file a statement of facts and issues; the appellant responded to the submission). But sanctions are rationally imposed with a view to the purpose of a sanction. Sanctions leading to the denial or curtailment of a hearing granted by statute are not to be imposed lightly. The Board questioned, but did not decide, "the appropriateness of...sanctions for conduct which does not directly involve the Board's adjudication of the case before it, or otherwise violate the Board's rules and regulations." White v. SSA, 76 MSPR 303, 307 n.6 (1997). If a submission, e.g., a brief, is late, its exclusion is serious because it impinges on the right to be heard. If timeliness of the submission does not involve a jurisdictional question, a submission should be excluded only if the tardiness affects the other party or the Board's orderly and timely processing of cases. If the tardiness is insubstantial, and if the party has made a good faith effort to comply with Board procedures, exclusion of the submission is inappropriate. Goodale v. USDA, 5 MSPR 65, 5 MSPB 139 (1981).

The Board's disinclination to sanction an appellant by denying her a hearing when she attempts to comply with Board procedures was emphasized in Bailey v. USPS, DC-0752-16-0463-I-1 (NP 5/25/2022):

An administrative judge has the authority to sanction a party when necessary to serve the ends of justice, which includes the right to sanction a party for failure to comply with an order. Heckman v. Department of the Interior, 106 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶ 8 (2007); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(a). An appellant's right to a hearing, however, should not be denied as a sanction absent extraordinary circumstances. Heckman, 106 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶ 8; see 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a)(1) (providing an appellant a right to a hearing in matters "appealable to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation"); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43(e) (explaining that a hearing may be cancelled for contumacious conduct or conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice). An appellant's single failure to comply with an administrative judge's order is not sufficient to show a lack of due diligence, negligence, or bad faith in her noncompliance with the order so as to justify the drastic sanction of cancelling the appellant's requested hearing. Caracciolo v. Office of Personnel Management, 82 M.S.P.R. 532, ¶¶ 6-7 (1999).

We disagree with the administrative judge that the appellant did not demonstrate good cause for her failure to submit prehearing submissions and appear at the prehearing conference. The record reflects that, as asserted by the appellant below, the regional office mailed all pleadings to the appellant's representative, despite her status as an e-filer. It was not until after the order requiring prehearing submissions and scheduling the prehearing conference was issued that this error was corrected at the appellant's request. When, as here, the order to which the appellant failed to respond was not electronically served on her representative, as elected, then all doubts about whether the appellant was prejudiced by the regional office's failure to follow the Board's regulations should be resolved in her favor. Gordon v. Department of the Air Force, 104 M.S.P.R. 358, ¶ 5 (2006) (finding that an administrative judge abused his discretion by dismissing an appeal for failure to comply with orders that were not served on the appellant's representative via his chosen method, which was electronic service), clarified on other grounds by Williams v. U.S. Postal Service, 116 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 8 (2011); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.14(j)(1) (providing that when the Board issues documents, email messages will be sent to e-filers that notify them of the issuance).

Further, this is not a case where the appellant displayed a pattern of noncompliance prejudicial to the administration of justice. Cf. Heckman, 106 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶¶ 6, 9 (finding that the administrative judge did not abuse her discretion by cancelling the appellant's requested hearing in light of his repeated failure to respond to multiple orders over a 2-month period). Although the appellant was notified that failure to appear at the hearing may result in its cancellation, she was not apprised that she could be sanctioned for failing to file her prehearing submission or appearing for the prehearing conference. Moreover, she indicated that she was participating in discovery at the time and actively pursuing her appeal. She responded promptly to the administrative judge's hearing cancellation and show cause order, was apologetic in explaining her noncompliance, and complied with all prior and subsequent orders. Therefore, we find that the record does not show that she exhibited a lack of due diligence sufficient to justify the severe sanction imposed here. See Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 78 M.S.P.R. 615, ¶¶ 3-6 (1998) (finding that the appellant's failure to timely comply with a single Board order did not constitute a lack of due diligence, negligence, or bad faith justifying an order that she could not present witnesses or additional evidence at the hearing, despite the fact that her excuses were that she needed to concentrate on her job, was unsure whether to retain her attorney, and was embarrassed that she had forgotten the name of her therapist). Nor has the agency claimed that it was prejudiced by the appellant's conduct.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant's actions in this appeal do not rise to the level of "extraordinary circumstances" sufficient to cancel the requested hearing, and that the administrative judge abused her discretion by doing so.

Watkins v. DOD, DC-1221-13-0230-W-3 (NP 1/31/2023), found cancelation of a hearing appropriate when "the appellant's refusal to cooperate in the prosecution of his appeal created a situation in which there was no realistic chance that a hearing could actually take place."

In IRA cases, AJs may issue prehearing orders requiring disclosures by appellants of specific information on jurisdictional issues. In Hoback v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 425, 429-30 (2000), the Board suggested that, although sanctions may be imposed for failure to provide prehearing submissions, the AJ should not dismiss an IRA appeal without examining the existing record to determine whether jurisdictional requirements are met.
