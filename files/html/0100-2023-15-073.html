4. Lack of or Loss of Minimum Qualification; Position Requirement

Occasionally an employee will be placed into a position without the necessary qualifications established by either OPM or the employing agency. The employee may be without fault, the error being that of a staffing specialist whose job it is to determine what qualifications are required before the individual is considered for or appointed to the job. The employee may perform well in the job, despite the absence of technical minimum qualifications. Unless there is evidence of agency bad faith or patent unfairness (a phrase not further defined), an agency may downgrade the employee through adverse action, and the Board defers to an agency determination concerning the requirements that must be fulfilled for an individual to qualify for and retain a particular position. Santaloci v. Dept. of Air Force, 18 MSPR 603, 605 (1984) (OPM X-118 qualification requirements for a personnel management specialist); cf. Cortright v. Dept. of Transp., 37 MSPR 565, 571 (1988) (deferring to an agency's determination concerning training requirements unless there is shown bad faith or patent unfairness).

The Board sustains the downgrading of an individual who, after appointment to a position, is discovered not to possess the minimum educational requirements for the position, even though the appellant did not contribute to the position assignment. Thomas v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 261, 263, 4 MSPB 336 (1980). The Board sustained as an action promoting service efficiency the agency's demotion of an employee who did not possess minimal educational requirements of the position under OPM standards. Travaglini v. Dept. of Educ., 18 MSPR 127, 141 n.20 (1983) (citing Thomas v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 261, 263, 4 MSPB 336 (1980)). If an appellant is demoted for lack of specialized experience for a position, the Board considers the testimony and affidavits of supervisors and coworkers that relate information about the appellant's experience (described in terms of experience and performance) as that experience is called for by OPM X-118 standards. See Martin v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 95, 96 (1980) (setting aside demotion when the agency presented no evidence refuting the appellant's qualifications or successful performance in the position held prior to demotion). Thompson v. Dept. of Air Force, 104 MSPR 529, 531-33 ¶¶ 2-10, 2007 MSPB 22 (2007) (aff'd, Thompson v. Dept. of Air Force, 289 Fed. Appx. 406 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)), reemphasized the agency's ability to determine qualifications for its positions and to remove employees who no longer possess those qualifications:

The agency proposed to remove the appellant, a GS-11 Air Traffic Control Specialist (Terminal), for failure to maintain an Air Traffic Control Specialist (ATCS) certificate. The agency specified that the appellant tested positive for marijuana in a random drug test and that, as a result, his ATCS certificate was withdrawn. The agency found that the charge was supported by the evidence and removed the appellant from the position.

...

Based on the parties' written submissions, the AJ found that the agency failed to show that the ATCS certificate is a necessary condition of the appellant's position. The AJ examined the Board's precedent involving other agency-controlled certifications and found that the Board has the authority to examine the merits of the agency's decision to withdraw the appellant's ATCS certificate, as well as the merits of the subsequent removal based on that withdrawal.

The AJ next found that the agency failed to show any legal basis for requiring employees in the Air Traffic Control Specialist (Terminal) position to have an ATCS certificate. The AJ found that the agency's regulations and the appellant's position description required that Air Traffic Control Specialists hold both an ATCS certificate and a Control Tower Operator (CTO) certificate. The agency repeatedly asserted that its regulations and the appellant's position description were based solely on the requirements of 14 C.F.R. part 65, subpart B. However, the AJ found that 14 C.F.R. part 65, subpart B only requires a CTO certificate. The AJ therefore concluded that the possession of the ATCS certificate was not legally mandated and that it was not a necessary requirement of the Air Traffic Control Specialist (Terminal) position. Given that conclusion, the AJ reversed the agency's removal action, but he did not order interim relief.

...

The AJ found that there was no legal basis for the agency to require that the appellant maintain his ATCS certificate for his Air Traffic Control Specialist (Terminal) position. However, it is well settled that, absent evidence of bad faith or patent unfairness, the Board defers to the agency's determination as to the requirements that must be fulfilled in order for an individual to qualify for appointment to a particular position, and to retain that position. See Cortright v. Department of Transportation, 37 M.S.P.R. 565, 571 (1988); Catalano v. U.S. Postal Service, 23 M.S.P.R. 432, 436 (1984); Bryant v. General Services Administration, 12 M.S.P.R. 270, 273 (1982).

We note that the appellant's position description and the agency's regulations clearly state that an occupant of the position is required to hold an ATCS certificate. In addition, we find that the withdrawal of the appellant's ATCS certificate was directly related to his qualifications to perform the duties of the Air Traffic Control Specialist position. See Currie v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 21 M.S.P.R. 720, 724-25 (1984) (suspension of the appellant's law license was directly related to his performance of the duties of the Attorney-Advisor position). As a result, the Board defers to the agency's determination that an ATCS certificate was a necessary condition of the appellant's Air Traffic Control Specialist (Terminal) position because the appellant has not presented any evidence of bad faith or patent unfairness. See Cortright, 37 M.S.P.R. at 571; Catalano, 23 M.S.P.R. at 436-37; Bryant, 12 M.S.P.R. at 273. Thus, the agency properly required that the appellant hold an ATCS certificate as a necessary condition of his employment.

Archer v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 427, 430-31 (1990), distinguished the military requirements of a civilian job from the qualification requirements for the position. The appellant lost his military flying status and the agency contended that he no longer met qualification standards for the position of supervisory aircraft pilot. The appeal had to be further developed through remand:

Although generally the Board does not have jurisdiction to make collateral inquiry into military requirements...its inquiry in cases involving such requirements does include consideration of whether the military requirement is also a requirement of the civilian position encumbered by the employee.... In the case at hand, the administrative judge concluded that military flying status was required by the qualification standard of the appellant's position without making the necessary factual inquiries and determinations.

Qualification standards are developed by OPM for positions in the federal service. Qualification standards must bear a rational relationship to the job to which they apply.... Each qualification standard of the Federal government must be based on a job analysis which identifies: (1) the basic duties and responsibilities; (2) the knowledge, skills and abilities required to perform the duties and responsibilities; and (3) the factors that are important in evaluating candidates. See 5 CFR 300.103(a).

Further, when an agency requests certification of eligibles for a position based on selective factors it wishes to add to the minimum qualification standards developed by OPM, OPM must approve those factors. Absent proof of approval by OPM, an agency may not rely on selective factors that are additions to OPM's minimum qualifications. See Federal Personnel Manual Chapter 332, Appendix B-12....
