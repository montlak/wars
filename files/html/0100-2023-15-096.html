D. CONFLICTS OF INTEREST; APPARENT CONFLICTS

Straight is the path and narrow the gate. One problem for agencies is how to select among discipline, admonishment, or counseling in situations involving conflicts or apparent conflicts of interest. Frequently, to avoid any impropriety, all impropriety is forbidden and punished by severe sanctions. Courts defer to agency rules of conduct. Rotolo v. MSPB, 636 F.2d 6 (1st Cir. 1980). The problem may be one of overreach or merely one of defining what constitutes a conflict or an apparent conflict. Often, there are no clear definitions and much is left to the beholder's imagination. Depending on the circumstances, a luncheon with a friend may appear to be a conflict to an outsider if the friend is a government contractor and the outsider becomes a disappointed bidder. Even if there is no conflict or apparent conflict, the simple matter of who pays for the lunch and how expensive the lunch was may involve a violation of regulations of the Office of Government Ethics or the employing agency. OGE and agency regulations should be studied with care. If counsel is in the fortunate position of having a client who asks before he acts, the client should be told that agencies have ethics counselors who will provide formal guidance on whether contemplated activities constitute a conflict or violation of agency policy concerning outside employment or investments. The employee is better off obtaining advice in advance instead of challenging the reasonableness of agency regulatory interpretations through adverse action litigation. If constitutional (associational or free speech) interests are raised as to a standards of conduct allegation, the appellant must first show that the action leading to discipline was speech protected by the First Amendment. The adjudicator must then balance the interests in accordance with the principles earlier outlined. England v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 89-3165).

Appellant contends that the agency failed to follow its regulations requiring it to bring suspected ethical violations, such as using a position to effect personal gain, to the employee's attention, and requiring it to pursue the matter with the agency's designated ethics official.

In order to establish the affirmative defense of harmful procedural error, an appellant must show error by the agency in the application of its procedures that, in the absence or cure of the error, would have been likely to cause the agency to reach a conclusion different from the conclusion reached in the case at issue. See Baracco v. Department of Transportation, 15 MSPR 112, 123 (1983), aff'd, 735 F.2d 488 (Fed. Cir. 1984); 5 CFR §§ 1201.56(b)(1), 1201.56(c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1), 1201.4(r)] . In the present case, however, appellant has not established that the regulation at issue prohibits the agency from taking an action against an employee for the violations that are the subject of the regulation. He also has not shown how the agency's compliance with the regulation would have been likely to cause the agency to reach a conclusion different from the conclusion it reached in this case. Thus, appellant has not established that any failure to comply with the regulation likely had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency. He therefore has not established the affirmative defense of harmful procedural error.

Similarly, interpreting Postal Service conflict regulations, Schumacher v. USPS, 52 MSPR 575, 582 (1992), considered alternatives available for remedying a conflict:

The appellant interprets 39 CFR § 447.32 to state that the agency must consider alternatives before removing an employee for a conflict of interest. Our review of 39 CFR § 447.32 does not support the appellant's interpretation. The regulation merely states that, when a conflict of interest exists, an ethical conduct officer within the agency may take remedial action, which includes disciplinary action such as the present removal action, and does not mandate less severe alternatives to removal. While we note that 39 CFR § 447.32(b) provides that remedial action may include disqualifying an employee for a particular assignment or changing his assigned duties, neither of those options would solve the appellant's conflict because, regardless of any changes in his assignment or duties, his continual employment with UPS alone would constitute a conflict of interest under Section 661.42(g) of the E & LRM. We further note that the appellant fails to state any mitigating factor that would warrant a less severe action by the agency or avoid the conflict of interest. We therefore reject his allegation that the agency improperly failed to consider other remedial action, and we find that the administrative judge's failure to address this argument did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights.
