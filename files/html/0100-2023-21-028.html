a. Qualifying Position

Even if an individual has prior service in a covered position, a disability retirement application cannot be based on a disability incurred during a later, temporary appointment that does not constitute a covered position, Odum v. OPM, 73 MSPR 247, 249-50 (1997):

An employee who has completed five years of civilian service and becomes disabled while serving in a position subject to the CSRS is entitled to a disability annuity. 5 U.S.C. § 8337(a); 5 C.F.R. § 831.1203(a). In general, receipt of a refund of retirement contributions voids all annuity rights based on the service for which the refund is made, unless the employee redeposits the contributions while serving in a position subject to the CSRS. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8334(d), 8342(a); 5 C.F.R. § 831.112(a); Deleon v. Office of Personnel Management, 49 M.S.P.R. 369, 373 (1991); Rint v. Office of Personnel Management, 48 M.S.P.R. 69, 72-73, aff'd, 950 F.2d 731 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table).

The appellant, relying on Crowder v. Office of Personnel Management, 21 M.S.P.R. 293 (1984), argues, as she did below, that the general rule governing refunded service does not apply when disability retirement benefits are sought. In Crowder, the Board applied a 1978 rule promulgated by the Civil Service Commission to find that an individual's receipt of a refund of retirement contributions did not void his entitlement to a disability annuity. 21 M.S.P.R. at 295.

We need not reach the issue of how the rule applied in Crowder, or any subsequent rules that may have been promulgated by OPM, might affect the creditability of the appellant's service with DoJ. The appellant is not eligible for a disability annuity because, as explained below, she was not serving in a position subject to the CSRS at the time she claims to have become disabled. Thus, the creditability of the appellant's DoJ service is not relevant to the disposition of this appeal.

According to the appellant, her 1993 appointment with RTC was under a temporary appointment, not-to-exceed one year. RTC extended her appointment for a single 81-day period. (The appellant states that her "appointment was scheduled to expire after one (1) year, i.e., June 27, 1994"); (Effective June 28, 1994, RTC extended the appointment, not-to-exceed September 17, 1994). Thus, the appellant's employment with RTC was excluded from CSRS coverage. See 5 U.S.C. § 8347(g) (authorizing OPM to exclude temporary appointees from CSRS coverage); 5 C.F.R. § 831.201(a)(1) (OPM's regulations exclude from CSRS coverage individuals under temporary appointments limited to one year or less). The fact that the appellant's total service with RTC exceeded one year does not make the exclusion inapplicable, since she served under appointments limited to one year or less. See Portacio v. Office of Personnel Management, 52 M.S.P.R. 396, 398-400 (1992). Accordingly, we hold that, because the appellant was not serving in a position subject to the CSRS in August of 1994 when she claims to have become disabled, she does not meet a basic eligibility requirement for CSRS disability benefits. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.1203(a).

The discussion above assumes that the appellant's entitlement to federal retirement benefits, if any, would arise under the CSRS, and not the Federal Employees' Retirement System (FERS), since she completed five years of service under the CSRS prior to December 31, 1986. See generally 5 U.S.C. § 8402(b) (setting out the rules for determining coverage under FERS). Assuming that the appellant's receipt of a refund of her CSRS contributions for her DoJ service means that her rights upon post-1986 federal employment are governed by FERS, however, she is not entitled to FERS disability benefits, for reasons similar to those given above. To be eligible for a FERS disability annuity, an individual must have 18 months of creditable civilian service and become disabled while serving in a position subject to FERS. 5 U.S.C. § 8451(a)(1)(A); 5 C.F.R. § 844.103(a). The appellant was not serving in a position subject to FERS at the time she claims to have become disabled, because her temporary employment with RTC was excluded from FERS coverage. See 5 U.S.C. § 8402(c)(1); 5 C.F.R. § 842.105(a)(1).
