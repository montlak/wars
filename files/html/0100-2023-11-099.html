2. Collateral Estoppel

Collateral estoppel is generally considered to involve preclusion of issues previously litigated that surface in subsequent litigation between the same parties. For example, if parties litigate through a contract grievance proceeding the validity of a performance standard in an appeal from the denial of a within-grade increase, and the same parties are now before the Board examining the validity of the same performance standard in a subsequent performance-based removal action, a fair argument is made that collateral estoppel precludes relitigation of the validity of the performance standard in the Board removal appeal.

The Federal Circuit said of collateral estoppel, and its application to administrative agencies (and MSPB is one of those), Synqor, Inc. v. Vicor Corp., 988 F.3d 1341, 1346-47 (Fed. Cir. 2021):

"[T]he determination of a question directly involved in one action is conclusive as to that question in a second suit." B & B Hardware, Inc. v. Hargis Indus., Inc., 575 U.S. 138, 147 (2015) (quoting Cromwell v. County of Sac, 94 U.S. 351, 354 (1877)). "It is well established that collateral estoppel, also known as issue preclusion, applies in the administrative context." MaxLinear, Inc., 880 F.3d at 1376. In fact, "because the principle of issue preclusion was so 'well established' at common law, in those situations in which Congress has authorized agencies to resolve disputes, 'courts may take it as given that Congress has legislated with the expectation that the principle [of issue preclusion] will apply 'except when a statutory purpose to the contrary is evident.'" B & B Hardware, 575 U.S. at 148 (quoting Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Ass'n v. Solimino, 501 U.S. 104, 108 (1991)).

Thus, administrative decisions have preclusive effect "[w]hen an administrative agency is acting in a judicial capacity and resolves disputed issues of fact properly before it which the parties have had an adequate opportunity to litigate." B & B Hardware, 575 U.S. at 148-49 (quoting Univ. of Tenn. v. Elliott, 478 U.S. 788, 797-98 (1986)). "Although administrative estoppel is favored as a matter of general policy, its suitability may vary according to the specific context of the rights at stake, the power of the agency, and the relative adequacy of agency procedures." Astoria, 501 U.S. at 109-10.

Gossage v. MSPB, 861 Fed. Appx. 426 (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP), explained collateral estoppel:

Under the doctrine of collateral estoppel, "once a court has decided an issue of fact or law necessary to its judgment, that decision may preclude relitigation of the issue in a suit on a different cause of action involving a party to the first case." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980). "It is well established that collateral estoppel, also known as issue preclusion, applies in the administrative context." SynQor, Inc. v. Vicor Corp., 988 F.3d 1341, 1347 (Fed. Cir. 2021) (quoting MaxLinear, Inc. v. CF CRESPE LLC, 880 F.3d 1373, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2018)); see also Thomas v. GSA, 794 F.2d 661 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("Like other judicial or quasijudicial tribunals deciding on the basis of an adversary, litigated record, the MSPB can apply the doctrine of issue preclusion in the appropriate circumstances."). "Thus, administrative decisions have preclusive effect '[w]hen an administrative agency is acting in a judicial capacity and resolves disputed issues of fact properly before it which the parties have had an adequate opportunity to litigate.'" SynQor, 988 F.3d at 1347 (quoting B&B Hardware, Inc. v. Hargis Indus., 575 U.S. 138, 148-49 (2015). The application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel is an issue of law that we review de novo. Id. (citing Shell Petroleum, Inc. v. United States, 319 F.3d 1334, 1338 (Fed. Cir. 2003)). But, as always, we are bound by the Board's factual determinations underlying its legal conclusions unless those findings are not supported by substantial evidence. See Smith v. GSA, 930 F.3d 1359, 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2019).

Collateral estoppel is applicable when the following criteria are met: (i) the issue previously adjudicated is identical to the issue currently presented; (ii) the issue was actually litigated in the prior case; (iii) the previous determination of the issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (iv) the party to be precluded had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action. See Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Serv., 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988). We have, on multiple occasions, held that the doctrine of collateral estoppel applies to bar a litigant from relitigating a timeliness issue. See Fernandez v. Dep't of the Army, 86 F. App'x 410, 413 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Estacio v. U.S. Postal Service, 34 F. App'x 677, 679 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Abellanes v. OPM, No. 1996-3219, 1996 U.S. App. LEXIS 29098, at *2-3 (Fed. Cir. Nov. 7, 1996).

Cf. Siegler v. Sorrento Therapeutics, Inc., ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP) ("Under collateral estoppel, once a court has decided an issue of fact or law necessary to its judgment, that decision may preclude relitigation of the issue in a suit on a different cause of action involving a party to the first case. Hydranautics v. FilmTec Corp., 204 F.3d 880, 885 (9th Cir. 2000). For the doctrine to apply, the party asserting preclusion must establish, inter alia, that the first proceeding ended with a final judgment on the merits. See id.").

No firm rules govern application of collateral estoppel, sometimes referred to as administrative estoppel. Although administrative estoppel is favored as a matter of general policy, its suitability varies according to the specific context of the rights at stake, the power of the agency, and the relative adequacy of agency procedures. See Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Ass'n v. Solimino, 111 S. Ct. 2166, 2170 (1991) (ADEA suit was not precluded by the findings of a state administrative agency). The party seeking to impose estoppel must demonstrate the propriety of its application. Quittley v. USPS, 54 MSPR 70, 74 (1992) (citing Chisolm v. DLA, 656 F.2d 42, 50 (3d Cir. 1981)). For a discussion of collateral estoppel, both affirmative and defensive, in patent infringement litigation, refer to Intellectual Ventures I v. Capital One Financial, 937 F.3d 1359, 937 F.3d 1359, 1373-76 (Fed. Cir. 2019) (including review of how collateral estoppel applies to alternate grounds for disposition of a claim). Jennings v. SSA, AT-4324-11-0442-B-1 (NP 4/10/2023), explained:

Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when the following conditions are met: (1) the issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action. Hau v. Department of Homeland Security, 123 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 13 (2016), aff'd sub nom. Bryant v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 878 F.3d 1320 (Fed. Cir. 2017). In determining whether an issue is identical for collateral estoppel purposes, differences precluding the application of collateral estoppel may be in facts, subject matter, periods of time, case law, statutes, procedural protections, notions of public interest, or qualifications of tribunals. Harris v. U.S. Postal Service, 119 M.S.P.R. 583, ¶ 6 (2013). Before a party can invoke collateral estoppel, the legal matter raised in the subsequent proceeding must involve the same set of events or documents and the same "bundle of legal principles" that contributed to the rendering of the first judgment. Tanner v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 11 (2003).

The Federal Circuit provided its collateral estoppel analysis, Salazar v. OPM, 495 Fed. Appx. 64, 67 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP):

"It is well established that the doctrine of collateral estoppel contributes to efficient judicial administration, serving the public interest in judicial economy as well as the parties' interests in finality, certainty of affairs and avoidance of unnecessary relitigation." Collateral estoppel will be applied where: "(1) the issue previously adjudicated is identical with that now presented; (2) that issue was 'actually litigated' in the prior case; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the end-decision then made; and (4) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action." Rice v. Dep't of the Treasury, 998 F.2d 997, 999 (Fed. Cir. 1993); see also Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Serv., 865 F.2d 235, 238 (Fed. Cir. 1988).

Collateral estoppel was considered as to Postal Service employees whose statutory appeal rights permit them to pursue an appealable action, e.g., removal, through both arbitration and a Board appeal. Deciding that a prior arbitration award would be given great deference, but would not establish collateral estoppel, Williams v. USPS, 35 MSPR 581, 584-85 (1987), offered:

What is the doctrine of collateral estoppel, what are its requirements, and what is the effect of its application, generally, on current proceedings?

The term "collateral estoppel" has generally been used to refer to the binding and conclusive effect given a prior judgment in a subsequent action. Although collateral estoppel is considered part of the broad doctrine of res judicata, the former applies only to issues actually litigated and decided in a prior proceeding, while the latter also applies to claims which could have been raised in an earlier action. See Chisholm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 656 F.2d 42, 47-48, n.10 (3d Cir. 1981). Despite their differences, the public policy served by these doctrines is their contribution to efficient judicial administration and judicial economy, as well as the private parties' interest in finality, certainty of affairs, and avoidance of unnecessary litigation. Id. at 46; I-B Moore's Federal Practice § 0.405[i] at 186-87.

Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when:

1. The issue is identical to that involved in the prior action;

2. The issue was actually litigated in the prior action; and

3. The determination in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment.

See Chisholm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 3 MSPB 273, 3 MSPR 171, 175 (1980), rev'd on other grounds., 656 F.2d 42 (3d Cir. 1981).

If all three prerequisites are met, the judgment precludes relitigation of the issues actually litigated and determined in the prior suit, regardless of whether it was based on the same cause of action as the second suit. See Lawlor v. National Screen Service Corporation, 349 U.S. 322, 326, 75 S. Ct. 865, 867, 99 L. Ed. 1122 (1955).

The considerations of public economy and private repose discussed above, however, must be balanced against the competing interests of fundamental fairness and due process. Thus, even if the prerequisites for collateral estoppel exist, the reviewing body may refrain from applying the doctrine if injustice would result or other public policy considerations would be compromised. See Payer v. Department of the Army, 19 MSPR 534, 538 (1984). For example, redetermination of issues may be warranted due to the different nature of one forum as compared to the other, or where there is reason to doubt the quality, extensiveness, or procedural and substantive fairness of the prior adjudication. See Montana v. United States, 440 U.S. 147, 163-64, 164 n.11, 99 S. Ct. 970, 978-79, 979 n.11, 59 L. Ed.2d 210 (1979); Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322, 330-31, 331 n.15, 99 S. Ct. 645, 651 n.15, 58 L. Ed.2d 552 (1979); Hooper v. United States, 326 F.2d 982, 985, 164 Ct. Cl. 151, cert. denied, 377 U.S. 977, 84 S. Ct. 1882, 12 L. Ed.2d 746 (1964).

On its face, a Postal Service arbitration decision, rendered in connection with an action appealable to the Board, would appear to meet the three prerequisites for the application of collateral estoppel since: (1) The issue before the Board, the propriety of the action appealed, would be the same as that involved in the proceeding before the arbitrator; (2) the issue before the Board would have been actually litigated before the arbitrator; and (3) the determination of the issue before the arbitrator would have been necessary to the resulting judgment. It remains to determine, however, given the unique characteristics of the Postal Service as a federal agency, whether application of the collateral estoppel doctrine would be appropriate in these cases.

Attempting to clarify the law defining collateral estoppel, McNeil v. Dept. of Defense, 100 MSPR 146, 151-52 ¶¶ 12-16 (2005), explained that four elements would control: (1) the issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action. The fourth element, a full and fair opportunity to litigate, directly or indirectly, differed, intentionally so in the analysis of the Board, from earlier decisions that the appellant needed to be represented in an earlier proceeding for collateral estoppel to apply:

Perhaps the Board's first major published decision applying the doctrine of collateral estoppel was Chisholm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 3 M.S.P.R. 171 (1980). In Chisholm, the Board adopted what it described as the "traditional" three-part test for the application of collateral estoppel: (1) The issue to be resolved is identical to that involved in a prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; and (3) the determination of the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment. Chisholm, 3 M.S.P.R. at 175. Even when these three elements were met, however, the Board declined to apply collateral estoppel when it would be unjust to do so, such as where the appellant was not a party to the earlier proceeding. See Mortensen v. Department of the Army, 27 M.S.P.R. 433, 436 (1985).

In Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 238 (Fed. Cir. 1988), the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit considered for the first time whether the decision of a postal arbitrator could be given collateral estoppel effect by the Board. In considering which legal standard for the application of collateral estoppel it should apply, the court relied on its own precedent in Mother's Restaurant, Inc. v. Mama's Pizza, Inc., 723 F.2d 1566 (Fed. Cir. 1983). Kroeger, 865 F.2d at 239. In Mother's Restaurant, the appellant argued that it was not bound by the result of prior litigation to which it was not a party. The court disagreed, finding that the earlier judgment was against a licensee of Mother's Restaurant and that the interests of Mother's Restaurant were "virtually represented" by the licensee. Mother's Restaurant, 723 F.2d at 1572-73. Applying Mother's Restaurant, the court in Kroeger devised the following four-part collateral estoppel test: (1) The issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action. Kroeger, 865 F.2d at 239.

Since then, the Board has recognized that the fourth inquiry under Kroeger is ordinarily made only when an individual who was not a party to an earlier proceeding contests an issue that was decided in that proceeding; the purpose of the "fully represented" element is to ensure that the party to be estopped has had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue. See Fisher v. Department of Defense, 64 M.S.P.R. 509, 515 (1994). The Board has not always been consistent in this interpretation of the fourth prong of the Kroeger test and, at times, has suggested that whether to give preclusive effect to a ruling in an earlier action to which the appellant was a party depends on whether the appellant had an attorney or other representative in the earlier action. E.g., Slater v. General Services Administration, 95 M.S.P.R. 378 ¶ 16 (2004); Readout v. Office of Personnel Management, 78 M.S.P.R. 637, 643 (1998).

To dispel any further confusion on this matter, we now announce a clarified test for collateral estoppel, and overrule Slater, Readout, and similar cases to the extent that they may be inconsistent with this clarified test. Specifically, collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when: (1) The issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action.

Under consideration in this appeal is whether collateral estoppel should be applied to previously adjudicated jurisdictional issues, so as to preclude their relitigation. While a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction does not necessarily preclude a second action on the same claim, the second claim will only be viable if the prior jurisdictional defect is cured, such as by filing the second action in a forum that does have jurisdiction. Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 338 (1995). However, a second action in the same forum is generally precluded by the doctrine of collateral estoppel, which would preclude relitigation of the same jurisdictional issue. Id. at 338.

See Morton-Hamlet v. USPS, SF-3443-16-0626-I-1 (NP 4/5/2023) ("[T]he Board in McNeil explained that the matter of representation is ordinarily at issue only when an individual who was not a party to the earlier proceeding contests an issue that was decided in that proceeding, McNeil, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 14. Here, the appellant was a party to the earlier proceeding, where she had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue of Board jurisdiction. Accordingly, all alleged bases for jurisdiction that the appellant reasserted from her prior appeal are barred by collateral estoppel, even though she did not have an attorney or other representative in the earlier action. See McNeil, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶¶ 14-15."); Ware v. DHHS, DC-0752-14-0313-I-1 (NP 7/9/2014) ("[A]s the Board observed in McNeil, a second action is only viable if the prior jurisdictional defect is cured, for example, by filing in the proper forum, and that a second action in the same forum is generally precluded by the doctrine of collateral estoppel. Here, the appellant's claim of age discrimination does not cure the jurisdictional defect in his first appeal because, as stated above, the Board does not have jurisdiction over discrimination claims absent an otherwise appealable action not present here. Pridgen, 117 M.S.P.R. 665, ¶ 7.").

Decisions before McNeil in 2005 often depended, in part, on an element of representation in a prior proceeding. "Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when (1) an issue is identical to that involved in the prior action, (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action, (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment, and (4) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action." Hoback v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 315, 318 ¶ 7 (2001). A party was considered to have been "fully represented" in a prior action if he had a "full and fair chance to litigate" the issue in question. Estacio v. USPS, 34 Fed. App. 677, 678 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP) (citing Mother's Rest., Inc. v. Mama's Pizza, Inc., 723 F.2d 1566, 1569 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 1983)).

Special Counsel and Hubbard v. EPA, 79 MSPR 542, 547 (1998), summarized the concept of judicial collateral estoppel:

...This Board, like other administrative and judicial bodies, generally defers to a final court order unless we determine that the order is flawed. See, e.g., McGean v. National Labor Relations Board, 15 MSPR 49, 53 (1983) (the Board does not examine reasons for bar decertification where employee is removed for failure to maintain bar membership, as long as the court had general subject matter jurisdiction to enter the order); Williams v. U.S. Postal Service, 35 MSPR 581, 589 (1997) (the Board will refuse "to collaterally attack the decision of another tribunal, statutorily charged with the authority to render the decision under review"); Dargel v. Office of Personnel Management, 20 MSPR 48, 52 n.4 (1984) (the Board refused to collaterally attack the dates established in a final order in a federal administrative proceeding in calculating the appellant's annuity); see also Smith v. United States, 823 F.2d 532 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (the Federal Circuit affirmed a Claims Court decision deferring to a final order of a federal district court, as affirmed and modified by the Third Circuit, in determining the claimant's appointment date for purposes of its jurisdiction to award back pay); Sonya v. Arizona School for the Deaf, 743 F. Supp. 700 (D. Ariz. 1990) (once a judgment has been entered by a court of general jurisdiction, a presumption of regularity attaches that cannot be collaterally attacked unless the rendering court lacked personal jurisdiction, subject matter jurisdiction, or jurisdiction to enter the particular order involved, or if the court acted in excess of its jurisdiction or if "extrinsic" fraud is shown).

Collateral estoppel was considered in Otherson v. DOJ, 711 F.2d 267, 272 (D.C. Cir. 1983). The employee was removed following a criminal conviction for abusing aliens. MSPB precluded him from relitigating facts established at the criminal trial and sustained the discharge. Relying on Chisolm v. DLA, 656 F.2d 42 (3d Cir. 1981), Otherson considered appropriate preclusion in administrative proceedings of issues litigated in criminal cases:

The fact that Congress guaranteed employees one full opportunity to be heard...does not mean that Congress intended them to have more than one. Issue preclusion is only appropriate when a party has a full and fair opportunity to present his case at a prior hearing...and the employee may always argue in his hearing before the MSPB that the prior proceeding failed to meet this standard. Just as application of issue preclusion in civil trials does not unlawfully deprive litigants of their day in court, neither does application of issue preclusion in MSPB hearings deprive employees of their statutory hearing rights. Moreover, only those issues determined against the employee at the earlier proceeding may not be contested again. Employees whose misconduct is established preclusively will thus still have an undiminished opportunity to press other arguments before the Board, such as whether removal would promote the efficiency of the service.

Otherson provided extensive discussion of the requirements of issue preclusion or collateral estoppel. First, the issue sought to be precluded must have been actually litigated, that is, contested by the parties and submitted for determination by the court, even if some facts were stipulated. Second, the issue must have been actually and necessarily determined by a tribunal of competent jurisdiction. Whether an issue has been to determined is resolved through review of the pleadings and outcome of the first case and a determination of what the first tribunal had to consider in order to reach its conclusion. Third, the preclusion must not be unfair by binding the party to a result he had no incentive to litigate in the first case; however, there is no unfairness if the party against whom preclusion is sought vigorously litigated the issues involved in the first case, whether or not the stakes of that case were significant.

Otherson was followed in a Federal Circuit decision, Thomas v. GSA, 794 F.2d 661, 664 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (on appeal from Thomas v. GSA, 27 MSPR 401 (1985)):

Like other judicial or quasi-judicial tribunals deciding on the basis of an adversary, litigated record, the MSPB can apply the doctrine of issue preclusion in the appropriate circumstances. Graybill v. USPS, 782 F.2d 1567, 1571 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Otherson v. Dept. of Justice, 711 F.2d 267 (D.C. Cir. 1983); Chisholm v. DLA, 656 F.2d 42 (3d Cir. 1981). Issue preclusion is normally applicable if (i) the issue previously adjudicated is identical with that now presented, (ii) that issue was "actually litigated" in the prior case, (iii) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the end-decision then made, and (iv) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action. See Mother's Restaurant, Inc. v. Mama's Pizza, Inc., 723 F.2d 1566, 1569 (Fed. Cir. 1983)....

Under Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 92 MSPR 370, 375-76 ¶ 13 (2002), the "actually litigated" element is satisfied when an issue was properly raised by the pleadings, was submitted for determination, and was determined. Johnson declined to apply collateral estoppel to a USERRA claim when the appellant did not submit evidence or argument concerning the claim and the AJ discussed only the procedural history of the issue and then went on in the initial decision to dismiss resignation and retirement claims for lack of jurisdiction. See Alford v. OPM, DC-844E-13-0283-I-1 (NP 2/4/2014) ("An issue is considered 'actually litigated' when it was properly raised by the pleadings, was submitted for determination, and was determined. Banner v. United States, 238 F.3d 1348, 1354-55 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (mere disagreement with a legal ruling does not mean that a party was denied a 'full and fair' hearing).").

Mercer v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3287), determined that "[T]he requirement of actual litigation can be satisfied when one party raises a dispositive issue of fact and an adverse party with opportunity and motive to challenge the issue fails to actually challenge it." Mercer cited Harris Trust & Sav. Bank v. Ellis, 810 F.2d 700, 705 (7th Cir. 1987). Killeen v. OPM, 106 MSPR 666, 671, 2007 MSPB 237 (2007), noted that an issue litigated in a compliance proceeding serves to collaterally estop litigation of the same issue in a subsequent appeal from an OPM reconsideration decision. Under Killeen, citing Hamiter v. USPS, 96 MSPR 511, 517-18 ¶ 17 (2004), an issue is "actually litigated" when it is contested by the parties and submitted for determination by a court or other neutral adjudicator.

Collateral estoppel does not apply if a change in circumstances has occurred or if, for some other reason, the issues in the adjudications are not the same. See Quittley v. USPS, 54 MSPR 70, 74 (1992) (declining to apply the doctrine to whether a position was managerial when the prior adjudication was in 1978 and there was no showing that the nature or duties of the position remained the same). Nor, apparently, does it apply if the appellant could have, but did not, litigate an issue in another forum because he failed to appear for scheduled hearings in that other forum and did not receive a merits determination in that forum. See Lehman v. OPM, 40 MSPR 286, 289 (1989) (suitability appeal; misconduct in a prior job was not actually litigated before the county civil service commission; despite the appellant's appeal to that organization, the appellant failed to show up for scheduled hearings and the county commission's minutes did not reflect consideration of the merits of his removal). Collateral estoppel does not apply if the issue determined in a prior tribunal was not necessary to the judgment in that case. See Oshiver v. OPM, 38 MSPR 191, 196 (1988); cf. Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 73 MSPR 574, 579-80 (1997) (adjudicator may refrain from applying collateral estoppel if an injustice would result or other public policy considerations would be compromised). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Method and Scope of MSPB Review," for the degree of deference or preclusion the Board and reviewing courts give to arbitration awards.]

Collateral estoppel applies when the burden of proof in the later litigation is the same or less than the burden of proof in the earlier litigation. If the appellant's burden of proof in a civil contempt proceeding in district court is "clear and convincing evidence," the result of the district court case, if against the appellant, does not preclude the appellant from litigating an issue before the Board that must be established by preponderant evidence. The preponderance standard is a less stringent burden of proof than the district court standard. Having failed to convince the court under the more stringent standard, the appellant is not estopped from his attempts to persuade the Board under a less stringent standard of proof. See Matosian v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 689, 694 (1993).

If an earlier adverse action appeal was found untimely, collateral estoppel precludes consideration of a subsequent appeal of the same adverse action raising a newly-stated affirmative defense, e.g., discrimination. See Gibson v. USPS, CH-0752-13-0384-I-1 (NP 5/15/2014) ("We conclude that the appellant is precluded under the doctrine of collateral estoppel from relitigating the timeliness of his appeal of the agency's removal action. Because this is a dispositive basis for dismissing a subsequent adverse action appeal of the appellant's removal in 2000, it also precludes him from raising matters that could only be raised as affirmative defenses to an adverse action, such as discrimination claims"; noting that the Board was not determining whether collateral estoppel would bar a USERRA or VEOA claim). But if a prior action was reversed for lack of due process, collateral estoppel does not preclude continued litigation of the facts underlying the agency's charges. See Heath v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-14-0233-I-1 (NP 2/24/2015) ("[B]ecause the administrative judge assigned to the appellant's appeal of the first removal action reversed the agency's action on the basis of due process, her determination on the substantive charges, was not necessary to the resulting judgment. Thus, the administrative judge's application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel to the substantive charges was inappropriate[.] ").

The application of collateral estoppel, although frequent, is discretionary, Luna v. VA, DA-0752-14-0378-I-1 (NP 4/29/2015):

Assuming, without deciding, that all of the collateral estoppel requirements are met regarding the appellant's prior appeal, we decline to apply collateral estoppel to that appeal. The application of collateral estoppel is discretionary and the Board need not apply it in every situation in which the criteria are met. See Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (even when the requirements for applying collateral estoppel are met, it would not be error to decline to apply collateral estoppel). The considerations of public economy and private repose that underlie the doctrine of collateral estoppel must be balanced against the competing interests of fundamental fairness and due process. Tanner v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 15 (2003) (citing Williams v. U.S. Postal Service, 35 M.S.P.R. 581, 585 (1987), modified on other grounds by Berkey v. U.S. Postal Service, 42 M.S.P.R. 24, 27-28 (1989)). Thus, even if the prerequisites for collateral estoppel exist, the reviewing body may refrain from applying the doctrine if injustice would result or other public policy considerations would be compromised. Tanner, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 15.
