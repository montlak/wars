8. Lack of Notice of Appeal Rights

Appeal rights are best exercised if they are known.

A separate topic is election of remedies: the civil service system is complex and there are choices, depending on the nature of an action under protest, among remedies. The choice of one remedy, sought through an appeal or complaint or grievance, may exclude others. A misguided choice may mean that no tribunal has jurisdiction over a personnel action that an employee seeks to challenge. [Refer to Chapter 12, "Election of Remedies" for treatment of mixed-case appeals (involving EEO affirmative defenses); refer to Chapter 13, discussing Individual Right of Action (whistleblower) appeals, "Jurisdiction Limitations of an IRA Rather Than an Adverse Action Appeal," and "Election and Preclusion of Remedies; Managers and Supervisors; Notice of Rights," and to Chapter 14, "Lack of Notice of Appeal Right," involving restoration appeals.]

5 CFR 1201.21 (2022) requires an agency to notify the appellant of Board and other appeal or complaint rights with a decision appealable to the Board, and the notice should include information about elections among procedures that exist to challenge the action covered by the notice

When an agency issues a decision notice to an employee on a matter that is appealable to the Board, the agency must provide the employee with the following:

(a) Notice of the time limits for appealing to the Board, the requirements of § 1201.22(c) [relating to the timeliness of the appeal], and the address of the appropriate Board office for filing the appeal;

(b) A copy, or access to a copy, of the Board's regulations;

(c) A copy, or access to a copy, of the MSPB appeal form available at the Board's Web site (http://www.mspb.gov), and

(d) Notice of any right the employee has to file a grievance or seek corrective action under subchapters II and III of 5 U.S.C. chapter 12, including:

(1) Whether the election of any applicable grievance procedure will result in waiver of the employee's right to file an appeal with the Board;

(2) Whether both an appeal to the Board and a grievance may be filed on the same matter and, if so, the circumstances under which proceeding with one will preclude proceeding with the other, and specific notice that filing a grievance will not extend the time limit for filing an appeal with the Board;

(3) Whether there is any right to request Board review of a final decision on a grievance in accordance with § 1201.155 of this part; and

(4) The effect of any election under 5 U.S.C. 7121(g), including the effect that seeking corrective action under subchapters II and III of 5 U.S.C. chapter 12 will have on the employee's appeal rights before the Board.

(e) Notice of any right the employee has to file a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission or to grieve allegations of unlawful discrimination, consistent with the provisions of 5 U.S.C. 7121(d) and 29 CFR 1614.301 and 1614.302.

(f) The name or title and contact information for the agency official to whom the Board should send the Acknowledgment Order and copy of the appeal in the event the employee files an appeal with the Board. Contact information should include the official's mailing address, email address, telephone and fax numbers.

A requirement that agencies provide notice of appeal rights was enacted into law by the FY 2018 National Defense Authorization Act (Pub. L. 115-91, div. A, title X, § 1097(b)(2), Dec. 12, 2017, 131 Stat. 1617), codified as a note to 5 USC 7503:

Information on Appeal Rights

(A) In general.--Any notice provided to an employee under section 7503(b)(1), section 7513(b)(1), or section 7543(b)(1) of title 5, United States Code, shall include detailed information with respect to--

(i) the right of the employee to appeal an action brought under the applicable section;

(ii) the forums in which the employee may file an appeal described in clause (i); and

(iii) any limitations on the rights of the employee that would apply because of the forum in which the employee decides to file an appeal.

See Kaszowski v. Dept. of Air Force, 2023 MSPB 15, ¶¶ 5-7 (2023) (not deciding retroactivity of NDAA provision, but determining that the agency's notice of rights to the employee must explain not only the choice but the consequences of the choice made between a grievance and a Board appeal).

The subsections of Chapter 75 referenced by the 2017 statute are, technically, to proposals for action. The fair intent of the statute addresses decisions that would lead to appeals. The statute could be amended for clarity.

A regulation from OPM issued in 2020, 5 CFR 752.203 (2022), added:

(b) Notice of proposed action. The notice must state the specific reason(s) for the proposed action, and inform the employee of his or her right to review the material which is relied on to support the reasons for action given in the notice. The notice must further include detailed information with respect to any right to appeal the action pursuant to section 1097(b)(2)(A) of Public Law 115-91, the forums in which the employee may file an appeal, and any limitations on the rights of the employee that would apply because of the forum in which the employee decides to file.

For actions against SES members, notice of appeal rights is required in a proposal notice. See 5 CFR 752.604(b)(1) (2022).

Under Cargile v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-14-0056-I-2 (NP 10/3/2022), "An agency's failure to provide information on Board appeal rights may excuse a late-filed Board appeal, but it does not constitute a due process violation that would warrant reversal of the action. Porter v. Department of Defense, 98 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 36 (2005)."

Most agencies notify most appellants of their MSPB appeal rights most of the time, but exceptions occur: the mail does not get through, the agency erroneously concludes the individual has no appeal right, or the agency misleads or downright obstructs the appellant who would otherwise exercise the right to take a Board appeal. Good cause for late filing may exist if the agency's failure to provide the employee with the required appeal rights notice effectively contributed to or caused the untimely filing. Rivera v. Dept. of Transp., 94 MSPR 216, 221 ¶ 13 (2003) (at the time of the operative events, the appellant had no appeal rights, so the agency was not at fault for the delayed appeal). The decisions noted in this section involve individuals who ultimately learn of the Board, file an appeal, and seek waiver of a filing deadline because of either the agency's failure to provide notice of the appeal right or the agency's obstruction of that right once proper notice is provided to the individual. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Failure to Receive Initial Decision; E-Filer," involving failure to receive judges' initial decisions containing notice of deadline for filing PFR.]

Although an agency has no regulatory authority to extend the time for the appellant to appeal to the Board from an agency action, the Board will find good cause for a waiver of the appellate time limit when an appellant relies on an agency extension. See Hoagland v. Marine Corps, 20 MSPR 6, 6 (1984) (citing Baker v. OPM, 7 MSPR 379 (1981)). [Refer to "Request for Extension of Time to Appeal" in this chapter.]

The agency's duty is to provide full and accurate information to an employee regarding his appeal rights. Drose v. USPS, 85 MSPR 98, 101 ¶ 8 (2000). When terminations of temporary appointees were not within the Board's jurisdiction, the agency was not required to give the appellants notice of Board appeal rights, and the lack of notice was not an excuse for delayed filing. Vaught v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 554, 558 (1993); see Prior v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 561, 570 (1993) (when the agency placed employees into a nonpay status under an "on-call" employment agreement, the agency took no appealable action and had no duty to supply appeal rights).

Under Linen v. Dept. of Treasury, AT-0752-12-0175-I-1 (NP 6/19/2012):

The Board will not excuse filing delays based on asserted confusion where the appellant has been placed on clear notice of a filing time limit. Melendez v. Department of Homeland Security, 112 M.S.P.R. 51, ¶ 14 (2009); Crook v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 553, ¶¶ 5-6, aff'd, 301 F. App'x 982 (Fed. Cir. 2008).
