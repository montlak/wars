5. Failure to Hire

Aside from appealable discriminatory practices under VEOA, USERRA, or the WPA, no law, rule, or regulation supports Board review of an individual's claim that he was not hired for a particular position. See Denson v. USPS, 4 MSPR 439, 440, 4 MSPB 490 (1980). A failure to hire does not rise to a violation of regulations governing employment practices (5 CFR 300.103 (2022)) unless a particular practice was applied to the applicant by OPM during the hiring process. Phillips v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 620, 623 (1989); Wizi v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 192, 193, 6 MSPB 169 (1981) (same analysis); see Juhas v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3399) (Board lacked jurisdiction over a federal annuitant's appeal from the failure of the agency to reemploy him); Polen v. DOD, 72 MSPR 1, 4 (1996) (other than rights to restoration to duty or reemployment under 5 CFR Parts 352 and 353, the appellant has no right to be rehired or reassigned to the former employing agency); Scipio v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 337, 339-40 (1984) (Board lacked jurisdiction over an allegation that the agency failed to comply with the Minority Recruitment Program when it failed to hire the applicant for an engineering position). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Cancelation of Appointment, Promotion," concerning the appointment process; refer earlier in this chapter to VEOA and USERRA decisions and to Chapter 13 for Individual Right of Action appeals under the Whistleblower Protection Act.]

As to an agency decision not to select an employee, the Board lacked appellate jurisdiction, Grigg v. Dept. of Interior, 5 MSPR 439, 441 (1981):

Appellant bases his contention that the presiding official erred upon his belief that 5 CFR 1201.3(a)(8), 5 CFR 300.103 and 5 CFR 300.104, when read together, provide this Board with appellate jurisdiction over his appeal. Appellant's belief is in error and the presiding official was correct in finding that appellant's appeal was not within the appellate jurisdiction of the Board.

The action which appellant seeks to have the Board review is the agency's non-selection of him for a position as an archaeologist. It is, thus, an agency action and not an action by the Office of Personnel Management ("OPM") which could constitute "an employment practice which was applied to him..." by OPM. Consequently, the action does not fall within the appellate jurisdiction conferred on this Board by 5 CFR 300.104. See Roeder v. OPM, 3 MSPB 467, 3 MSPR 387 (1980); Cunningham v. ICC, 3 MSPB 473, 3 MSPR 394 (1980).

Appellant's allegations that the agency committed prohibited personnel practices, even assuming such allegations can be established, similarly do not confer appellate jurisdiction on this Board since 5 USC 2302(b) is not an independent basis for Board jurisdiction. See Wren v. Dept. of the Army, 2 MSPB 174, 2 MSPR 1 (1980); Chandler v. ICC, 3 MSPB 173, 3 MSPR 55 (1980).

Shipp v. DHHS, No. 2012-3141 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP), noted that as to individualized objections to hiring decisions:

As a general matter, however, an agency's failure to select an applicant for a vacant position is not appealable to the Board. Prewitt v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 133 F.3d 885, 886 (Fed. Cir. 1998). Mr. Shipp claims an exception to that general principle because he alleges he was the victim of racial discrimination and retaliation for lodging an equal employment opportunity complaint against HHS. The Board, however, correctly concluded that the alleged discrimination and retaliation, even if accepted as true, would amount only to an isolated incident affecting Mr. Shipp individually. "[A]n individual agency action or decision that is not made pursuant to or as part of a rule or practice of some kind does not qualify as an 'employment practice.'" Id. at 887; see 5 C.F.R. § 300.104. Mr. Shipp has not identified a policy of discrimination or retaliation promulgated by OPM or practiced by HHS, so the Board correctly held that it lacked jurisdiction over his retaliation and discrimination claims.

"[T]he mere filling of a particular vacancy is not an 'employment practice' within the meaning of 5 CFR 300.101." Carroll v. EEOC, 6 MSPR 228, 230 (1981). Banks v. USDA, 59 MSPR 157, 159-60 (1993), explained why a failure to hire will not ordinarily fall within the Board's jurisdiction over employment practices:

We find that the appellant has failed to establish the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal under 5 CFR Part 300. The term "employment practice" includes the "development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests, and other measurement instruments." 5 CFR 300.101; Kelly v. OPM, 53 MSPR 511, 516 (1992). Under 5 CFR 300.104(a), an "employment practice" that was applied to a "candidate" by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) may be appealed to the Board if it violates a basic requirement in 5 CFR 300.103. An agency's misapplication of a valid OPM requirement under 5 CFR Part 300 also constitutes an employment practice that is appealable to the Board. See Maule v. OPM, 40 MSPR 388, 393, aff'd, 892 F.2d 1050 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table).

In the instant appeal, the agency asserted below that the appellant did not meet the qualifications set out in the job announcement for the GS/GM-12/13 Geneticist position because he did not have the requisite training in molecular biology. The appellant has not shown that he possessed this knowledge and that the agency misapplied to him any measurement of employment that OPM developed or applied to him an OPM measurement of employment that was invalid. His bare allegation that the agency failed to fully consider his education and experience in making a selection for the position does not establish that the agency subjected him to an employment practice that falls within the Board's jurisdiction as provided under 5 CFR Part 300. See Kelly, 53 MSPR at 516. Rather, the appellant appears to be merely challenging his nonselection for the position and the agency's alleged irregularities in the selection process, matters that are not otherwise appealable to the Board. See Robins v. Dept. of Justice, 48 MSPR 644, 650-51 (1991)....

Making the same point, Roeder v. OPM, 3 MSPR 387, 388-89 (1980):

The presiding official found that 5 CFR 300.101 relates to federal employment practices generally, "employment practices" being defined therein as "the development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests, and other measurement instruments." He concluded that the filling of a particular vacancy is thus not an "employment practice" within the meaning of Part 300. The Board agrees with that conclusion under the circumstances of this case. Appellant did not take issue with OPM's failure to rate properly his application for a specific position or his application for placement on the register. Rather, he contended that the hiring practices of the agencies involved were unfair; that proper procedures were not followed; and that one of the agencies relied on improper information, i.e., that he had neither Civil Service status nor was reachable on the Civil Service Register, in making its decision not to hire him. We find that he has thereby alleged no "employment practice" which is appealable to the Board.

The presiding official also interpreted 5 CFR 300.104(c)(2), 44 Fed. Reg. 48951 (1979), as providing that employment practices administered or required by the agency which violate a basic requirement of 5 CFR 300.103 shall be grieved within the agency. The Board agrees with this interpretation and with the finding of the presiding official that appellant had no Part 300 right of appeal to the Board because his failure to be selected for the Air Safety Investigator and Airspace System Inspector Pilot positions was within the control of the agencies and did not concern a matter applied to him by the Office of Personnel Management.

Cf. Nowlin v. FDIC, AT-0731-16-0078-I-1 (NP 8/10/2016) ("By letter dated September 28, 2015, the agency notified the appellant that it was rescinding its tentative offer of employment because the security clearance process revealed a 'pattern of dishonesty and omission of relevant facts.' Specifically, the agency cited the appellant's failure to disclose prior criminal history on his Standard Form 85P, Questionnaire for Public Trust Positions, and his OF-306, Declaration of Federal Employment....[T]he agency's decision to rescind its tentative offer of employment is not an appealable suitability action under 5 C.F.R. part 731.").

Alford v. MSPB, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP), affirmed MSPB's lack of jurisdiction over a nonselection:

Ms. Alford argues that her non-selection for employment by OPM was the result of improper "employment practices." We have held that "an unsuccessful candidate for a federal civil service position generally has no right to appeal his or her non-selection to the [B]oard." Ricci v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 953 F.3d 753, 757 (Fed. Cir. 2020). An exception to that rule exists when an applicant believes that OPM applied an improper "employment practice." Prewitt v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 133 F.3d 885, 887 (Fed. Cir. 1998); 5 C.F.R. § 300.104(a). An employment practice is defined by regulation as any practice that affects "the recruitment, measurement, ranking, and selection of individuals for initial appointment and competitive promotion in the competitive service." 5 C.F.R. § 300.101. Although "'employment practice' is...construed broadly," it does not encompass "an individual agency action or decision that is not made pursuant to a rule or practice," such as an irregularity in the selection process. Prewitt, 133 F.3d at 887.

Here, Ms. Alford has not alleged any improper employment practice that OPM allegedly applied to her application. Although her appeal to the Board cited to basic requirements for federal employment practices, see 5 C.F.R. § 300.103, she failed to identify which of the basic requirements OPM allegedly violated by not selecting her for employment.

As to an appellant's concerns relating to his allegedly superior qualifications for a position, Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-300A-10-0760-I-1 (NP 5/12/2011), noted:

Further, whether the appellant was best qualified for the position in question is not at issue in his employment practices case. The Board has jurisdiction in such a case to consider whether an OPM employment practice violates one of the basic requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 300.103. Meeker v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 319 F.3d 1368, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2003); 5 C.F.R. § 300.104(a). The basic requirements set by 5 C.F.R. § 300.103 are that an employment practice (in this case, the time-in-grade requirement) be based on a job analysis, rationally related to performance in the position to be filled, and non-discriminatory. Metzenbaum v. General Services Administration, 96 M.S.P.R. 104, ¶ 13 n.7 (2004). Thus, the Board's jurisdiction in an OPM employment practices case does not extend to considering whether the appellant was best qualified for the position at issue.

As to alleged mismanagement by an agency of an application for a position, the Board explained its lack of jurisdiction under its authority to review a complaint of an improper employment practice, Perez v. DHS, NY-3443-09-0286-I-1 (NP 8/5/2010):

...In order to establish Board jurisdiction over an employment practice appeal under 5 C.F.R. § 300.104(a), an appellant must: (1) show that the appeal concerns an employment practice; (2) show that OPM was involved in administering the employment practice; and (3) make a non-frivolous allegation that the employment practice violated one of the "basic requirements" for employment practices set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 300.103. See Meeker v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 319 F.3d 1368, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Mapstone v. Department of the Interior, 110 M.S.P.R. 122, ¶ 7 (2008).

Under part (1) of the test, the appellant must show that the appeal concerns an employment practice. In other words, the basis for the challenge to the agency's action must not concern inaccuracies or irregularities in the agency's rating and handling of an application per se. Rather, it must concern the validity or the applicability of an employment practice underlying the agency's rating and handling of the application. In determining whether an appeal concerns an employment practice, it is ordinarily helpful to consider first whether the basis for the challenge to the agency's action does not concern an employment practice. See Saya v. Department of the Air Force, 68 M.S.P.R. 493, 496 (1995) ("[A]n individual agency action or decision that is not a rule or practice of some kind does not qualify as an "employment practice.'"); Dow v. Office of Personnel Management, 68 M.S.P.R. 285, 288-89 (1995) (employment practices appealable to the Board "must encompass matters that are amenable to a general application to a group"; they "encompass 'merit' considerations that are generally applied in making Federal hiring decisions"); Banks v. Department of Agriculture, 59 M.S.P.R. 157, 159-60 (1993) (the agency's failure to consider all relevant education and experience, and other alleged irregularities in the selection process did not constitute "employment practices").

Here, we find that the appellant's petition for review fails to establish that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal. The appellant alleges that the agency failed to properly credit his prior experience in such a way as to find him eligible for the Aviation Enforcement Officer position. As such, the basis for the appellant's challenge appears to concern the agency's rating and handling of his individual application, and not the validity or applicability of the employment practice underlying the agency's action. In other words, his challenge appears to be directed at a particular agency decision rather than at a broadly-applicable rule or policy. Thus, the appellant has failed to satisfy part (1) of the jurisdictional test. See Richardson v. Department of Defense, 78 M.S.P.R. 58, 61 (1998) (the appellant's allegation that the agency incorrectly evaluated her credentials for available positions did not fall within the Board's employment practice jurisdiction).

Employment practice cases involve the competitive service. The Board lacks employment practice appellate jurisdiction over nonselection for an excepted service position. Nowezki v. DOJ, DC-3443-09-0780-I-1 (NP 10/7/2010), explained:
