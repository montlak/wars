b. Retirement Appeal

The burden of legal expenses is a heavy one for disability retirement applicants who typically have limited financial resources. Stephens v. OPM, 33 MSPR 292, 295 (1987). The Board now entertains fee petitions in retirement cases. But it was not always so. Through 1985 the Board concluded that because employee-initiated retirement cases are not cases against employees, voluntary retirement appeals did not qualify for fee awards, whether based on optional or disability retirement claims that are resolved in favor of the employee. Acosta v. OPM, 26 MSPR 288, 289 & n.2 (1985); Vergagni v. OPM, 12 MSPR 494, 498 (1982), post. The Federal Circuit then rewrote the law, allowing fee awards in retirement cases. Antal v. MSPB, 775 F.2d 287 (Fed. Cir. 1985), post; Simmons v. MSPB, 768 F.2d 323 (Fed. Cir. 1985), post; Bronger v. OPM, 740 F.2d 1552 (Fed. Cir. 1984), post.

The law relating to fees in employee-initiated retirement cases was rewritten after the Supreme Court's decision in Lindahl v. OPM, 470 U.S. 768 (1985) (permitting judicial review of all MSPB decisions in retirement appeals). [Lindahl is discussed in Chapter 17, "Retirement Appeals."] Simmons v. MSPB, 768 F.2d 323, 325-26 (Fed. Cir. 1985), considered whether Lindahl required reconsideration of earlier decisions barring fees in annuity appeals. The court determined that the Board could award counsel fees:

The only express statutory provision authorizing the MSPB to award attorney fees is 5 USC 7701(g).... The bottom-line is whether this subsection, an integral part of 7701, applies to a federal employee seeking disability retirement.

...

[T]his court has already specifically held that an employee seeking non-disability retirement falls under 7701 (citing Bronger v. OPM, 740 F.2d 1552 (Fed. Cir. 1984)).... From the procedural and jurisdictional point of view, there is now absolutely no difference between seekers of non-disability retirement (like Bronger) and those requesting disability retirement. Both types obtain their right to appeal to the MSPB from 5 USC 8347(d)(1)....

...

It is also very significant that the MSPB applies 7701 procedures to retirement claims (including disability ones)....

Once we recognize that 7701 applies and has been applied to petitioner's disability retirement claim, we cannot exclude application of subsection (g)(1)...authorizing attorney fees (at least for employees and applicants for employment) to the prevailing party "in the interest of justice." The statutory terms are all-inclusive; there is no exclusion, in the text of the subsection or its legislative history, of any type or class of case. Retirement claims are necessarily covered, and there is nothing to suggest their omission. The canon that waivers of statutory immunity are strictly construed can have no impact on such a broad and literally-encompassing provision which fully covers the present case.

The Board applied the Simmons result retroactively, meaning that it reopened decisions denying fees for past favorable retirement decisions. Stephens v. OPM, 33 MSPR 292, 294 (1987). Antal v. MSPB, 775 F.2d 287 (Fed. Cir. 1985), also determined that appellants may obtain counsel fees in employee-initiated disability retirement cases. The court reaffirmed that result in Bronger v. OPM, 740 F.2d 1552 (Fed. Cir. 1984). See Francois v. OPM, 64 MSPR 191, 194 (1994) ("The Board has the authority to award attorney fees to an appellant who has prevailed on a claim of entitlement to a disability retirement annuity.").

The Board has authority under 5 USC 7701(g) to consider applications for attorney fees in survivor annuity appeals, that is, in nondisability retirement cases. Gonczi v. OPM, 41 MSPR 671, 673 (1989); Leppelman v. OPM, 29 MSPR 263, 264 (1985) (survivor annuity). Fees may be awarded to individuals who successfully pursue appeals under 5 USC 8336(c)(1), providing early retirement for law enforcement officers. Obremski v. OPM, 31 MSPR 554, 557 (1986). Fees may also be secured if OPM approves an annuity but fails to pay it, meaning that the appellant must appeal to the Board to force OPM to honor its annuity determination. See Evans v. OPM, 67 MSPR 357, 359 (1995) (likening the ensuing MSPB appeal to a proceeding to secure compliance with a Board decision or settlement).

Before the revision in the law governing entitlement to fees for individuals prevailing in annuity appeals, Vergagni v. OPM, 12 MSPR 494 (1982), distinguished Frazier v. MSPB, 672 F.2d 150 (D.C. Cir. 1982), permitting counsel fee awards in cases brought by the Special Counsel to secure Reform Act rights for aggrieved employees, by determining that annuitant applicants were not "aggrieved" because they voluntarily initiated their disability applications. The Board also concluded that an administrative determination of an annuity application did not constitute "agency action" under 5 USC 7701(g); awards of fees could only be made as to an agency-initiated application for an employee's retirement under 5 CFR Part 831 Subpart L, equivalent to an adverse action, and then the award was to be against the agency that initiated the proceeding, not against OPM. The Board extended the reasoning of the disability retirement appeal cases to those involving appeals from OPM decisions denying eligibility for supplemental annuities or redetermined annuities; because the appeals arose under 5 USC 8347(d)(1) and 5 USC 8337, 5 USC 7701(g)(1) could not be construed as a waiver of sovereign immunity in an award of fees against the government. Edwards v. OPM, 21 MSPR 141, 143 (1984); cf. Cheeseman v. OPM, 21 MSPR 118, 126-27 (1984) (fees were not available under 7701(g)(1) for litigation successfully challenging the procedures governing survivor benefits elections). The Board decided in Clarkson v. OPM, 19 MSPR 235, 236 (1984), that the Equal Access to Justice Act, 5 USC 504, did not permit an award of fees in retirement appeals because the Act included only those adjudications that, under 5 USC 554, are required to be determined on the record after an opportunity for hearing. Because the statute vesting jurisdiction in the Board to hear retirement appeals, 5 USC 8347(d)(1), did not require a hearing on the record, the Board decided the Equal Access to Justice Act [discussed later in this chapter, "Equal Access to Justice Act"] is inapplicable to this class of cases.

Under Vergagni, supra, fees could be awarded in agency-initiated retirement cases, but only against the employing agency, not against OPM. See Williams v. OPM, 24 MSPR 316, 317 (1984). Fees were not awarded every time the employee was successful. The usual Board standards governing interest of justice determinations applied. As to an agency that initiated a disability retirement application on the basis of a psychiatric report supporting its actions, fees were denied. It could not be said that the agency knew or should have known it would not prevail. No indication of bad faith was shown (to the contrary, OPM allowed the application). Williams v. OPM, 24 MSPR 316, 318 (1984).

The courts generally concurred with MSPB's analysis. Dawson v. MSPB, 712 F.2d 264 (7th Cir. 1983), determined that the Board lacked authority to award counsel fees in employee-initiated disability retirement cases processed under 5 USC 8347(d) and 5 USC 8337. The Board's jurisdiction to hear retirement appeals, arose from § 8347(d), not from 5 USC 7701. Because the latter section, also including attorney fee provisions, did not provide the Board with jurisdiction to hear retirement appeals, and because counsel fee provisions were strictly construed when applied against the government, the court discerned no statutory authority for a fee award. The court determined that its conclusion was consistent with the congressional intent of awarding fees in personnel cases brought against employees for improper reasons, contrasted with what the court considered neutral OPM decisions on employee-initiated retirement applications. Williams v. OPM, 718 F.2d 1553 (Fed. Cir. 1983), reached the same result. Section 7701(g)(1) could not "reach over" to 5 USC 8347(d)(1) to authorize an award of fees by the Board. When Congress distinguished under § 8347(d) the treatment of involuntary mental disability retirements (to permit judicial review and presumably to allow for fee awards by the Board) and physical disability retirement appeals, the fair implication, considering the strict construction of counsel fee provisions required by the doctrine of sovereign immunity, was that fees were not authorized in the latter appeals. Williams and Dawson were followed in Banda v. OPM, 727 F.2d 471 (5th Cir. 1984). The Eleventh Circuit reached an opposite result, determining that § 7701(g) provided a separate basis for an award of fees after MSPB reviewed an OPM determination under Chapter 83 of Title 5. Scarborough v. OPM, 723 F.2d 801 (11th Cir. 1984).
