3. Maintaining Order

The Board has reviewed relatively few cases involving termination of hearings due to participants' misconduct. Termination of a hearing may severely prejudice an appellant's ability to develop a record. Counsel, parties, and nonlawyer representatives should avoid creating disruption and antagonism so great that the AJ has no choice but to preserve adjudicatory dignity by curtailing the hearing. If feelings run that high, a representative would do well to call for a short recess to permit attitudinal readjustment. If objections must be entered into the record that will obviously antagonize the AJ, it is less disruptive--yet just as effective--to put the objections in writing and file them with the AJ rather than stating, repeating, and arguing them on the record before an AJ who is unlikely to sustain the objections. This does not mean that one should adopt a posture of subservience toward AJs. Use some tact.

The AJ may act to maintain order, even if it means curtailing the hearing. A hearing was properly terminated when appellant's counsel became disruptive through numerous irrelevant objections, failed to heed warnings, and was advised of, but did not use, the interlocutory appeals procedure upon termination of the hearing. Allen v. VA, 22 MSPR 204, 206 (1984). It was also proper for the AJ to terminate the hearing when appellant's counsel ignored repeated warnings as to the scope of the evidence to be presented. Roberts v. FAA, 23 MSPR 112, 114 n.* (1984) (five months elapsed before the initial decision without objection by the appellant to the AJ's action). If an appellant's attorney is excluded for being disruptive, the AJ should permit the appellant to continue without the disruptive representative rather than terminate the proceedings. Williams-Scaife v. DODDS, 26 MSPR 338, 340 (1985); see Snowden v. Dept. of State, 12 MSPR 487, 489 (1982) (although the appellant's attorney was ejected from the hearing for disruptive conduct, the appellant was not prejudiced; three days of hearing testimony were taken, the attorney would not alter his conduct, and the AJ took action near the conclusion of the hearing, still offering the appellant the opportunity to proceed pro se; the AJ considered all the evidence of record in reaching a decision); Howard v. Dept. of Labor, 13 MSPR 611, 614 (1982) (if the appellant elects not to continue with the hearing after his representative has been cautioned about disruptive behavior, the appellant was not denied a fair hearing); cf. Blanton v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 605, 607 (1983) (AJ properly closed the hearing when, after repeated warnings to counsel that his behavior was unacceptable and that the hearing would be closed if it continued, the representative "continually disrupted the proceeding with rude and arrogant behavior showing disrespect and contempt for both the presiding official and the Board"; the appellants were unable to proceed pro se since they failed to appear at the hearing). Control of the hearing usually takes action less drastic than termination of the proceedings. It was appropriate for the AJ to prohibit discussion between the appellant and his representative after the appellant attempted to interrupt his representative's cross-examination of a witness. Zych v. USPS, 21 MSPR 638, 642 (1984). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Control and Exclusion of Representative or Participant."]

We are unaware of what circumstances propelled the Board to act, but in January 2020 it issued its "Policy on Prohibited Conduct":

The U.S. Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB or the Board) is committed to promoting a safe and productive work environment for its personnel and the people it serves so that it may carry out its mission to "Protect the Merit System Principles and promote an effective Federal workforce free of Prohibited Personnel Practices." While parties to an appeal and other MSPB stakeholders may disagree strongly with each other and/or MSPB personnel during the appeal process or through interactions regarding MSPB's jurisdiction and processes, no individual is permitted to abuse the Board's adjudicatory process or personnel.

Unacceptable conduct includes:

1. Physically or verbally assaulting parties, witnesses, or Board personnel;

2. Harming or threatening harm (in person, over the telephone, by mail, by fax, or in electronic communications) to parties, witnesses, Board personnel or Board offices;

3. Engaging in harassing, abusive, disruptive, or contumacious conduct, including but not limited to the use of profanity or excessively persistent telephone, email, fax, or in-person communications, that impedes Board personnel from performing their duties;

4. Using disparaging language based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, sexual orientation, disability, marital status, and/or political affiliation;

5. Recording conversations with Board personnel without the Board's consent; and

6. Contacting or attempting to contact Board personnel at their personal addresses, telephone numbers, or emails, or through social media.

Engaging in such prohibited conduct may result in the Board taking steps to protect its personnel and resources such as banning the offending individual from entering the Board's offices, ceasing in-person, electronic or telephonic communications with the individual, and/or barring the individual from filing pleadings. In addition, the Board may impose sanctions as set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.43, or, as a last resort, terminate an appeal with prejudice. The Board may also report such prohibited conduct to Federal law enforcement for appropriate action.

The Board will provide appropriate warnings to an offending individual and any representative, allow a response to the actual or proposed sanction when feasible, and document the reasons for any resulting sanction in the appeal record for pending matters and/or in separate written communications to the offending individual. In the event the Board imposes any limits on an individual as a result of prohibited conduct, the Board will provide information regarding whether the individual may request permission to file pleadings and/or communicate with MSPB in any pending or future case, and the method by which the individual may request such permission.
