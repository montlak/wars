e. Misappropriation of Funds

Seibert v. Dept. of Treasury, 41 MSPR 133, 137-38 (1989), involving a charge that an employee made false claims for emergency salary payments, determined that no causal connection existed between the offense and addiction:

In Brinkley v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR 682 (1988),[1] the Board held that a claim of general drug dependency cannot insulate an employee from discipline for willful acts of misconduct committed independently of that condition, i.e., the statutory proscription of discrimination is inapplicable to employee misconduct that is not itself the concurrent product of mental or physical impairment caused by drug addiction or alcoholism.

[1] In Brinkley, the Board modified its decision in Ruzek v. General Services Administration, 7 MSPB 307, 7 MSPR 437, 443-44 (1981). In Ruzek, the Board held that rehabilitative assistance is mandated before discipline may be imposed for performance or misconduct problems "related to" alcoholism. In Brinkley, the Board found that the problems must be "caused" by the addictive condition before rehabilitative assistance must be afforded. In Brinkley, the Board also overruled Green v. Department of the Air Force, 31 MSPR 152 (1986), as to its holding on the causation issue.

The Board's causation standard, however, does not require an appellant to prove that his inebriated condition rendered him unable to have mens rea, the guilty mind of the criminal standard for innocence due to intoxication. Rather, it requires only that an appellant show that he was so impaired by alcohol or drug intoxication at the time of his misconduct that he lacked control over his actions. See Brinkley, 37 MSPR at 686.

The appellant here did not prove he was under the direct influence of drugs or alcohol at the time of his misconduct. He did not show that his misconduct was the concurrent product of mental and/or physical impairment caused by drug addiction or alcoholism or that he did not know fully what he was doing. His proof that generally he suffered impaired judgment and compulsion to obtain funds as a result of his addictions fails to meet the standard of causation for proof of discrimination on the basis of a handicapping condition. That standard requires a showing that the appellant was under the direct influence of alcohol and/or drugs at the time of his misconduct. See Brinkley, 37 MSPR at 685. Here, at the times that the appellant cashed his paycheck and thereafter filled out forms for obtaining dual salary payments, he was not under the influence of drugs or intoxicated.

Because the appellant's repeated thefts of government funds occurred independently and separately from his alleged addiction, the Board finds that the agency had no obligation to provide accommodation to the appellant under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, 29 USC § 791 et seq., the Drug Abuse Prevention, Treatment, and Rehabilitation Act, 42 USC § 290ee-1, and the Comprehensive Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism Prevention, Treatment and Rehabilitation Act, 42 USC § 290dd-1.

Relying on Seibert, Walsh v. USPS, 53 MSPR 478, 481 (1992), concluded that no causal connection linked misappropriation of postal funds and drug addiction. The evidence did not demonstrate that the appellant was so impaired by drug use at the time he misappropriated the funds that he lacked control over his actions. The Seibert causation test is a narrow one, strictly interpreted by the Board. Noguera v. USPS, 45 MSPR 156, 160-61 (1990), determined, as to an addiction defense asserted by an individual who extracted some credit cards from the mails:

Subsequent to the issuance of the initial decision, the Board issued two decisions applying and interpreting the causation burden of proof standard that an appellant must establish by preponderant evidence a causal connection between the drug addiction handicap and the misconduct which led to the adverse personnel action. See Brinkley v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR 682 (1988); Campbell v. Defense Logistics Agency, 37 MSPR 691 (1988). In Brinkley v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR at 686, we defined the requisite causation standard as requiring evidence that, at the time of the charged misconduct the appellant was suffering from the impairing effects of drugs or alcohol. In Campbell v. Defense Logistics Agency, 37 MSPR at 695, the Board held that an appellant must demonstrate the existence of an impairment of faculties to the extent that he or she lacked control over the actions at issue.

Later, in Seibert v. Department of the Treasury, 41 MSPR 133, 137 (1989), the Board clarified that the causation standard does not require an appellant to prove that his inebriated condition rendered him unable to have mens rea, the guilty mind of the criminal standard for innocence due to intoxication. Rather, the Board reiterated that the standard requires only that an appellant show that he was so impaired by alcohol or drug intoxication at the time of his misconduct that he lacked control over his actions.

The record here shows that the appellant failed to prove that he was under the active influence of cocaine at the time that he stole the credit cards. Even crediting the appellant's assertion that he took the credit cards for the purpose of obtaining more cocaine in the future, that fact is irrelevant to the issue of whether the appellant engaged in intoxicating use of cocaine concurrently with or shortly before his misconduct. Absent such a showing, as in this case, it is immaterial that the appellant may have had some secondary intent or other motive. See Brinkley v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR at 686; Campbell v. Defense Logistics Agency, 37 MSPR at 695.

Although the appellant argues that he was addicted to cocaine and that, because of his substance abuse, he suffered a general loss of judgment, he did not prove that his judgment or ability to form intent was actually affected by cocaine use on either of the two days that he committed the misconduct.[4] Because the appellant was not under the direct influence of cocaine when he stole the credit cards, the discipline imposed was for the independent sustained misconduct and not in any part, much less "solely," because of his handicap. See Richardson v. United States Postal Service, 613 F. Supp. 1213, 1216 (D.D.C. 1985). Therefore, the appellant's claim of cocaine addiction cannot insulate him from discipline for willful acts of misconduct committed independently from that condition. See Seibert v. Department of the Treasury, 41 MSPR at 137.

[4] Since the appellant did not meet the first prong of the Brinkley test (under the influence), it is unnecessary to decide the extent or the effects of any alleged impairment. However, to the extent that the administrative judge suggested that, in order to establish causation, an appellant must show that he was totally out of control of his actions so that he had no choice but to commit the misconduct, the Board finds that this is an incorrect standard. The extent of impairment must be such that the appellant lacks substantial control over his actions, i.e., that his capacity for analytical judgment, free will, and/or intent is diminished. Seibert v. Department of the Treasury, 41 MSPR at 137; Brinkley v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR at 687-88; Campbell v. Defense Logistics Agency, 37 MSPR at 695.

We conclude that, because the appellant's misconduct stands separate and apart from his alleged handicapping condition, the agency did not have a statutory or regulatory obligation to offer accommodation. The agency's decision to remove the appellant rather than first offer him rehabilitation does not constitute handicap discrimination under the 1973 Rehabilitation Act. See Campbell v. Defense Logistics Agency, 37 MSPR at 696.
