1. Authority to Order Compliance; Sanctions

The Board has plenary statutory authority to hear, adjudicate, or provide for the hearing or adjudication of all matters within its jurisdiction. It also has authority to order any federal agency or employee to comply with any order or decision issued and to enforce compliance with the order or decision. 5 USC 1204(a)(1), (2); Chappell v. SSA, 12 MSPR 633, 635 (1982). The Board has authority under 5 USC 1204(e)(2)(A) to enforce any order issued under § 1204(a)(2) by ordering that an employee (other than a presidential appointee) shall not be entitled to be paid until there has been compliance, a measure occasionally threatened but not yet applied by the Board. See Stone v. OPM, 5 MSPR 68, 170, 5 MSPB 142 (1981). [Refer later in this chapter to "Lack of Authority to Enforce Compliance."]

Board Order 1559.14A (September 15, 1987), adds some explanation to the Board's view of its authority regarding noncompliance:

If the agency or responsible official refuses to comply, the Board may take one of the following actions:

(1) Certify to the Office of the Comptroller General that an agency official found to be in noncompliance shall not be paid until he/she complies.

(2) Initiate court action to force an agency or appellant to comply with a Board decision.

Order 1559.14A also provides that the Board may refer to OSC, for the investigation and consideration of disciplinary action, cases involving willful noncompliance with Board orders.

Gannon v. USPS, 67 MSPR 660, 667 n.5 (1995), added:

Section 1204(a)(2) provides that the Board may order a federal employee to comply with its orders and enforce compliance. Section 1204(e)(2)(A) provides that the Board may order that an employee charged with complying with such an order "shall not be entitled to receive payment for service as an employee during any period that the order has not been complied with." The procedure for implementing these provisions is set out at 5 CFR § 1201.183. If the agency does not identify the official responsible for compliance with the Board's Order, we will presume that the highest ranking appropriate agency official who is not appointed by the President is charged with compliance. 5 CFR § 1201.183(a)(2) (1994).

In practice, the Board does not impose sanctions in enforcement proceedings. It cajoles agencies to comply, threatens those who delay compliance, and eventually everyone complies--it is just a matter of time--lots of time, while the appellant goes without a complete remedy. The Board may order periodic reporting on compliance disputes to ensure there is some progress as time goes by. See Rhodes v. VA, AT-0752-12-0316-X-1 (NP 9/25/2015) (agency to submit biweekly reports; appellant then to submit responses within two weeks). The Board views sanctions as a means to enforce compliance. Once the agency complies with a Board order, even if prior noncompliance is arguably in bad faith, sanctions are inappropriate. See North v. DHS, DC-0752-12-0771-X-1 (NP 12/30/2013) ("With respect to the appellant's request for sanctions, we note that the Board normally does not impose punishment or sanctions once compliance has been obtained. See Bruton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 14 (2009), review dismissed, 370 F. App'x 90 (Fed. Cir. 2010)."); Vance v. Dept. of Interior, DC-0752-08-0733-C-2 (NP 3/1/2013) ("Although the administrative judge found that the agency failed to remain in compliance with the parties' settlement agreement, because the agency established in the proceedings below that it is once again in compliance, the imposition of sanctions, which Board law indicates are solely a means to obtain such compliance, are not appropriate. E.g., Martin v. Department of Justice, 86 M.S.P.R. 13, ¶ 2 (2000); Mavronikolas v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 113, 116 (1992) (even where the agency is responsible for delays in compliance, sanctions are inappropriate once compliance is complete), aff'd 979 F.2d 216 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). Moreover, the Board is without authority to award compensatory damages for the breach of a settlement agreement. E.g., Young v. U.S. Postal Service, 117 M.S.P.R. 211, ¶ 1 n.1 (2012)."); Vitanza v. USPS, 94 MSPR 385, 391 n.5 (2003) ("[w]e find that the agency has at least tried to comply with the Board['] s Order, and for this reason, sanctions are inappropriate"); Camastro v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 267, 275 ¶ 18 (2000) ("[i]t would be inappropriate to impose sanctions at this time where the agency has attempted to demonstrate compliance."); Lyon v. OPM, 45 MSPR 179, 186 (1990) ("Here OPM has submitted evidence of compliance and therefore, despite the inordinate amount of time it has taken OPM to comply with the Board's Opinion and Order, it would be inappropriate for the Board to impose sanctions at this time."); White v. USPS, 40 MSPR 23, 25 (1989) (declining to impose sanctions when the agency complied with the Board's order and terms of a settlement agreement). If the appellant or the agency at least attempts compliance, lack of complete success does not warrant sanctions. See Vitanza v. USPS, 94 MSPR 385, 391 n.5 (2003). The Board does not invent remedies in lieu of sanctions during compliance proceedings. See Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 62 MSPR 212, 219 (1994) (AJ correctly ruled that the appellant's claim for "litigation costs" was beyond the scope of the enforcement petition).

The Board threatened to use its statutory authority to withhold the salary of an OPM official who allegedly would not comply with a Board order in a disability retirement case. Circle v. OPM, 5 MSPR 121, 122, 5 MSPB 187 (1981); see Keenan v. USPS, 67 MSPR 135, 136-37 (1995) (upon a third finding of noncompliance, the MSPB directed the agency's responsible official to appear at the Board's Office of General Counsel to show cause why the Board should not impose sanctions for agency noncompliance); Marshall v. GPO, 45 MSPR 62, 66-67 (1990) (agency's compliance position demonstrated an attitude of disrespect and obstinate refusal and to be frivolous and devoid of merit, the Board ordered the agency officials responsible for compliance to appear before the Board and show cause why their salaries should not be withheld); cf. Marren v. DOJ, 34 MSPR 520, 524 (1987) (referring to OSC under 5 USC 1206(g)(1) for "appropriate action" agency's willful violation of Board order directing restoration of pay to appellant); cf. Perkins v. VA, 105 MSPR 289, 296 ¶ 20, 2007 MSPB 80 (2007) ("The agency has previously identified its responsible agency official as '11735.' The agency must submit the name and address of the agency official. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)(2) (2007). If the agency fails to do so, the Board will regard the highest-ranking career official at the agency as the responsible agency official."). But a two-month delay in making the appellant whole for back pay was not sufficient to warrant sanctions under 5 USC 1204(e)(2). Norris v. USPS, 29 MSPR 434, 435-36 (1985).

If the Board ultimately determines that an agency has complied with a settlement agreement, no sanctions will be imposed if the agency missed a time limit in responding to an AJ's directive during the enforcement proceedings. See Welber v. USPS, 72 MSPR 244, 246 (1996) (sanctions were not justified by the appellant's argument that the agency's compliance delay harmed him because he could have earned more interest on money due him than he was ultimately paid under the Back Pay Act); Holtgrewe v. FDIC, 65 MSPR 137, 141 (1994) (where an agency complies with a Board order, sanctions to enforce compliance, e.g., an order directing that an employee not be paid during a period of noncompliance, are inappropriate); Fuller v. USPS, 47 MSPR 211, 217-18 (1991) ("[t]he agency's submission of evidence of, compliance with the recommendation was untimely filed. Because we have found, however, that the agency was never in breach of the parties' settlement agreement, we find that the imposition of sanctions against the agency for its untimely filing of compliance with the administrative judge's recommendation would be inappropriate[.]"); see also Cioce v. Dept. of Army, 69 MSPR 564, 566 (1996) (following Holtgrewe). Modrowski v. VA, 97 MSPR 224, 228 ¶ 13 (2004), noted:

The appellant also complains about the agency's delays in complying with the settlement agreement and seeks the imposition of sanctions against the agency. We agree with the appellant that agencies are expected to promptly comply with the terms of settlement agreements into which they enter. See Woodson v. Department of Agriculture, 94 M.S.P.R. 289 ¶ 6 (2003). There is, however, no basis to impose sanctions against the agency in light of its compliance. Kruger v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 94 M.S.P.R. 106 ¶ 5 (2003) (where an agency is in compliance, "the imposition of sanctions would serve no purpose"); Mavronikolas v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 113, 116 (the imposition of sanctions is a means to enforce compliance), aff'd, 979 F.2d 216 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).
