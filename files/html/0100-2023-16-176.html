broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section G: Interference, Subsection 13: Restraint on Union Internal Organization, Part a: Recognition of Representatives
a. Recognition of Representatives

The employer must respect the union's designation of individuals who will represent the union on labor management matters, including meetings and joint committees. If the employer's officials meet and deal with representatives of their choice rather than those chosen by the union, they effectively bypass the union in violation of §§ 7116(a)(1) and (5). Dept. of Air Force, 915th Tactical Fighter Group, Homestead AFB and NFFE Local 1167, 13 FLRA 135, 150 (1983) (ALJ Decision); see VA and NAGE Local R5-136, 57 FLRA 515, 518-19 (2001) (management unlawfully refused to permit union to designate attorney as representative during grievance meeting; the contract did not preclude representation by counsel). It was an act of interference for a manager to tell employees that they could not act as stewards until the manager was notified by his labor relations offices of their official status. Dept. of Labor, OWCP, Branch of Special Claims and Garland, 11 FLRA 77, 84 (1983) (ALJ Decision); cf. Dept. of Army, Ft. Rucker and AFGE Local 1815, 71 FLRA 734 (2020) (Member DuBester dissenting) (vacating portion of award setting aside order barring union official from agency premises when the statutory basis for the award was not an issue before the arbitrator); DOJ, Bureau of Prisons USP McCreary and AFGE Local 614, 71 FLRA 538, 541-43 (2020) (Member DuBester concurring) (ULP for agency to bar union counsel from facility to observe staff movements relative to possible compensation claims). When the parties agreed to a union observer on a rating panel to ensure that merit promotion plan procedures were followed, management could not refuse to honor the designation of a representative named by the union vice president, who was delegated the task by the union president. The union had the right to select its own representative by its own procedures; a single violation of the recognition by management was a statutory violation not to be dismissed as de minimis. DHHS, FDA, Region II, New York Reg'l Lab. and AFGE Local 2637, 16 FLRA 182, 193-94 (1984) (ALJ Decision).

In the unusual case of, Bureau of Indian Affairs, Isleta Elementary Sch. and IEF, 54 FLRA 1428, 1439-42 (1998), the respondent's school principal expressed to the Governor of the Native American reservation the principal's concerns about the aggressive or offensive tactics of a union representative. In turn, the Governor, who had a close and cooperative relationship with the principal, banned the union representative and other union officials from the reservation, effectively denying the unit employees the opportunity to meet with their representatives concerning working conditions. FLRA determined that the agency effectively solicited the Governor's ban, decided that the agency's actions constituted interference with protected activities, and decided that none of the conduct of the union representative was sufficiently blatant to warrant loss of statutory protection for his union activities. The agency was ordered to advise the Governor that the union's activities were lawful and protected and to provide transportation of unit employees to meetings with their representatives off the reservation (at agency expense) until matters were resolved with the Governor. See Philadelphia Naval Shipyard and Philadelphia Metal Trades Council, 4 FLRA 255, 269 (1980) (ALJ Decision) (management unlawfully interfered with exclusive representative who was barred from workplace; employer reduced representative's ability to provide representation through grievance presentations or collective bargaining.).

A contract clause that stewards will normally restrict union activities to assigned work areas or organizational locations they are appointed to serve was not a clear and unmistakable waiver of the union's right to designate its representatives on some basis other than work areas. The agency violated §§ 7116(a)(1) and (5) by refusing to recognize a steward appointed for disabled employees and by refusing to recognize a steward who had been terminated from employment with the agency. DOD, Dept. of Army, HQ, XVII Airborne Corps, Ft. Bragg and AFGE Local 1770, 15 FLRA 790, 792-93 (1984).

Section 7114(a)(5) provides that an employee may have his own attorney except in the case of grievance or appeal procedures negotiated under the Statute. Ordinarily either the employee, the union, or both of them may file a grievance, but the right to invoke arbitration rests with the union alone. The union generally has the right to determine whether the employee may designate his or her own counsel in the course of the grievance proceeding. The right of unions to control the grievance procedure is an important one. A private attorney acts only in the interest of his client and may negotiate a settlement that is contrary to the interest of other unit employees. "If individual employees were permitted to cut their own deals, it would dilute and diminish the right of unions to act as exclusive representatives, for employers would undoubtedly prefer to play one employee against another, or buy them off, one by one." If a conflict arises, the employee may present his grievance directly to management under § 7121(b)(3)(B), or the employee may have a remedy against the union through a fair representation charge if the union provides improper representation. Sheet Metal Workers Int'l Ass'n Local 97 and Cosden, 7 FLRA 799, 815 (1982) (ALJ Decision); see Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation and Runnels, 59 FLRA 101 (2003) (5 USC 7102 was not violated by contract provision requiring union's approval of representative in grievance proceeding; employee could always represent himself).
