1. Common Possession/Multiple Causation

If it is unclear who is responsible for alleged misconduct, careful scrutiny is required of the charges and proof. The AJ should look to several factors: motive; habit; past conduct and performance in the job; the appellant's character; and the reasons why others would or would not come forward to confess the misconduct and exculpate the appellant. See Remy v. VA, 8 MSPR 141, 148, 7 MSPB 624 (1981) (appellant was accused of making harassing phone calls; the calls could have been originated by three or more persons); cf. Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 554-55 ¶¶ 15-16 (1999) (analysis of evidence in a conspiracy case was based principally upon a sworn statement and other statements made through the plea bargain of a convicted felon); Guerrero v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 472, 474 (1984) (inconclusive and inconsistent evidence of whether the appellant was at the crime scene and involved with other individuals who stole government property).

One situation calling for the evaluation of circumstantial evidence involves a vehicle or toolbox used by several people and found to contain contraband or stolen property. How is it to be determined whether the appellant should be held responsible? The general rule is that circumstantial evidence of charges not reasonably and satisfactorily explained permits a strong inference of culpability. See Haebe v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 167, 176 ¶ 18 (1999), rev'd on other grounds, Haebe v. DOJ, 288 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2002). The strong inference of culpability from unexplained possession of agency property by an employee leaving the agency premises after his work shift may constitute preponderant evidence of the charge of attempted theft. However, for a strong inference to be drawn from unexplained possession, the property must be within the exclusive possession of the accused. If the place where the goods are found was easily accessible to others, the strong inference of culpability cannot be drawn from the lone fact of possession, although that fact may be considered with other facts to establish preponderant evidence of the charge. Other circumstances to consider may include factors suggesting the appellant should have known that he was carrying property that was not his, e.g., silence during questioning concerning the items in his possession. See Davis v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 521, 524 (1985) (not treating the Fifth Amendment issue involved in implications of guilt resulting from silence in response to incriminating questions); cf. Mojica-Otero v. Dept. of Treasury, 30 MSPR 46, 49 (1986) (shoplifting charge: "An inference can be drawn from appellant's unexplained possession of the shorts, especially when they were within his exclusive control.").

Maston v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 168, 170-71 (1987), concluded that the appellant concealed some agency property in his lunchbox:

In Davis v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 521 at 524 (1985), the Board held that in a case of alleged attempted theft of government property, it could not draw a strong inference of culpability from an employee's unexplained possession of agency property while leaving at the end of his work shift because he did not have exclusive possession of the sack in which it was found. In the instant case, the unrebutted evidence establishes that the appellant kept his lunch box, in which the items were found, on a flat in the main aisle of the warehouse and that the lunch box was accessible to numerous employees, vendors, and customers of the commissary. Thus, the appellant did not have exclusive possession and control of the lunch box, and we cannot draw an inference of culpability from his mere possession of the commissary items.

The administrative judge found implausible the appellant's contention that he did not know how the items got in his lunch box and that he did not feel the added weight of the items, slightly less than three pounds, in the lunch box. The administrative judge also relied on the testimony from Ms. Cebulski, who was working at the security desk, that the appellant failed to stop, that he knew that he was required to stop when requested to do so by the personnel at the security desk, and that he had stopped on his own in the past without being told to do so.

The administrative judge implicitly found Ms. Cebulski's testimony more credible than the appellant's testimony that he did not put the items in his lunch box, that he did not realize the items were in his lunch box, and that he did not realize that he was the person being called over to the security desk.... Accordingly, we affirm the administrative judge's finding that the agency proved its charge of unauthorized possession by the preponderance of the evidence.

Other decisions illustrate common possession problems. An employee was removed for unauthorized possession of government property even though others had access to his garage. The appellant presented no evidence suggesting that anyone else put the government tools there. Bowman v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1724). Smith v. TVA, 18 MSPR 694, 694-96 (1984), applied the rule after marijuana was found in a car used by several people. Betts v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 37, 41 (1984), also involved a charge of drug possession. The appellant was the owner and driver of the vehicle involved and was nearest in proximity to the drug. He apparently attempted to conceal it when discovery was imminent. The Board concluded that he was the possessor and sustained the charge.

Rice v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 632, 633-34 (1982), involving possession of drugs in a vehicle, resolved the charge against the appellant based on circumstantial evidence:

...In his petition, appellant argues that the agency had failed to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that appellant had possession of the substance found in his vehicle. He argues that the record does not contain any evidence that appellant had knowledge that the substance found in his vehicle was a controlled substance, and that the presiding official erred by considering the prior reprimand for entering the Base with marijuana in his vehicle as a predisposition to be involved with marijuana and thus some measure of circumstantial evidence of knowingly possessing marijuana.

In arriving at his finding that appellant had possession of a controlled substance, the presiding official considered that: 1) appellant was the only person in the vehicle at the entry check point; 2) that the leafy substance in the car organizer and partially burned cigarettes, determined to be contraband, were found in the front passenger compartment and in each of the ashtrays in the rear passenger compartment; 3) appellant was accustomed to using the car to commute to work; and 4) there was no evidence that appellant was not in control of the vehicle from the time he left his worksite to the time that he was stopped. Proof of actual possession need not be shown; it may be established by circumstantial evidence. United States v. Raysor, 294 F.2d 563 (3d Cir. 1961). The presiding official determined that appellant's uncorroborated explanations that his wife and others had use of the car and that his wife was a marijuana user were not credible, and sustained the charge. The decision of the presiding official on this point will not be reviewed.... We find that the presiding official's factual determination of possession, coupled with appellant's familiarity based on personal use and prior reprimand for possession, gives rise to an inference that appellant knew that the substance found in his vehicle was a controlled substance. The matter of the prior reprimand was properly entered in the record since it formed a basis for the removal action. Therefore, we find that the charge has been proved by a preponderance of the evidence.

But see Day v. USDA, SF-0752-14-0218-I-1 (NP 9/11/2014) (considering whether the appellant was in possession of marijuana that was in his wife's purse, the Board applied court decisions defining possession in terms of knowledge of the existence of contraband and the power to exercise dominion and control over it).

Analogous decisions consider the issue of who orchestrated the actions attributed to the appellant. When the appellant was charged with throwing an object that hurt another employee, circumstantial evidence was insufficient in light of direct, uncontradicted, reasonable, and unimpeached testimony establishing an alternate theory of how the harm occurred. Jefferson v. DLA, 22 MSPR 10, 13 (1984); see Facciponti v. USPS, 15 MSPR 183, 186 (1983) (issue of who altered some documents). Evidence of direct observation of the appellant is sufficient to refute alternate theories of how charged misconduct occurred. See Leftridge v. USPS, 56 MSPR 340, 344 (1993) (charge of mishandling mail). [Refer to Chapter 8, "Common Possession" under "Removal, Misuse of Property."]
