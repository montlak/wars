f. Comparative Qualifications as a Factor in Selection of Positions for RIF

Unless there is bad faith or an underhanded scheme to remove an employee, an agency may consider comparative qualifications of two employees in determining what position to abolish if a reorganization is otherwise bona fide. Gandola v. FTC, 23 MSPR 383, 386-87 (1984) (citing Gibson v. United States, 176 Ct. Cl. 102 (1966)), followed in Anderson v. DOD, 48 MSPR 388, 392 (1991) ("[a]n agency could consider the comparative qualifications of two non-competing employees to determine which position to abolish when that decision was made in the interest of office efficiency.").

Judicial review of Gandola provided guidance distinguishing permissible management discretion in the selection of positions to be eliminated from impermissible use of a RIF to achieve a disguised adverse action. The court's discussion should be compared with the Board's analysis in Losure v. ICC, 2 MSPR 203 (1980), supra. At issue in Gandola v. FTC, 773 F.2d 308, 310-11 (Fed. Cir. 1985), was agency consideration of personal qualifications in a national RIF resulting in some attorneys and other professionals being separated in regional offices. Criteria used to select employees for RIF included their experience, individual effectiveness, and personal abilities. Office grade structures were not considered. The separated employees challenged the RIF because the individuals selected for separation were chosen on the basis of personal qualifications. The court found nothing improper about the RIF:

A reduction in force involves two steps. First, the agency determines which positions to abolish. Second, it selects the people to be eliminated serving in those positions on the basis of their retention standing. In this case, for example, the Commission first decided to eliminate four attorney positions at grades GS-13 and 12 in the Cleveland office, and then selected the four attorneys in those categories to be removed according to the retention credits of all the attorneys at those levels.

...

The petitioners...contend (A) that the Commission improperly considered the qualifications of particular employees in deciding which positions to eliminate; and (B) that although the overall reduction in force was instituted for the bona fide reason of budgetary limitations, the implementation of the program in the Cleveland office was tainted because improper personal motivations rather than legitimate managerial considerations were the basis of the action taken there.

When an agency is required to reduce its staff through a reduction in force, the agency's primary concern is to insure that the remaining staff will be as well qualified as possible to continue effectively to perform the agency's duties. In deciding which four attorney positions to eliminate in the Cleveland office, the Commission was required to determine what would be the best structural organization with only nine attorneys instead of the thirteen previously employed. That decision on the composition and structure of the work force reflects the kind of managerial judgment that is the essence of agency discretion, and...not...for judicial reevaluation....

...

[I]n the present case it was appropriate for the Commission, in deciding which positions to eliminate in the Cleveland office, to consider the "relative ability" of the remaining employees to "handle the work" by themselves....

[N]othing...in the language or legislative history of [the Reform Act] or the implementing regulations...specifically bars an agency from considering the abilities of individual employees in determining which positions to eliminate in a reduction in force.

Concerning the argument made in Gandola that the RIF was predicated upon personal hostility, the court stated, id. at 312-13:

A reduction in force may not be used as a disguised adverse action to remove or demote a particular employee.... The Board has held that if an employee is removed through a reduction in force because of dissatisfaction with his performance, the reduction in force is improper with respect to that employee, even though it is valid with respect to other employees....

...

We cannot say that the facts upon which the petitioners rely--consisting largely of suspicion and surmise--were sufficient to overcome the Commission's case that the implementation of the reduction in force in the Cleveland office was for bona fide management considerations. The director of personnel...testified that the Commission considered the experience and effectiveness of each employee "[i]n attempting to determine what the most effective structure would be for the remaining positions in the office...." Such consideration does not reflect a bad faith scheme to get rid of certain employees under the guise of a reduction in force, but rather a managerial decision made in the interest of office efficiency....

An agency has wide discretion in conducting a reduction in force. This court will not disturb a reduction in force absent a clear abuse of discretion or a substantial departure from applicable procedures.... If the agency proves that the reduction in force regulations were invoked for a legitimate reason and that those regulations were properly applied to the individual employees--as was the case here--the agency action will be sustained.

Gandola relied in part on Gibson v. United States, 176 Ct. Cl. 102, 109-10 (1966), considering whether an agency could take into account comparative qualifications of incumbents when a decision was made concerning elimination of a position:

It is settled that, absent bad faith or the kind of underhanded scheme plaintiff asserts, the hospital could decide with propriety to abolish Gibson's job instead of Jungkind's, and could rest that decision on the relative ability of the two men to handle the work by himself. Management's reasonable belief that the photographic workload could be carried by one employee was sufficient for elimination of one of the two positions. The Civil Service Commission regulations governing reductions in force authorized use of that corrective mechanism for "lack of work" (5 CFR § 20.2 (a) (1961)). Commission policy likewise permitted the agency to select which of the two separate jobs to erase, and to make that choice on the basis of the comparative capacity of the incumbents. It is conceded that nothing in Section 12 of the Veterans' Preference Act, 5 USC 861--the reduction in force provision--disabled the Commission from allowing employing agencies to make such a selection from among jobs which were not competing (because at different grade levels).
