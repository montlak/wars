7. Criminal Charge

Should an agency charge an individual with a criminal offense, it must prove the elements of that crime. In Knuckles v. USPS, 1 MSPR 358, 359, 1 MSPB 346 (1980), the appellant was charged with the offense of converting postal funds to his own use. At the hearing, however, the agency presented evidence only of violation of accounting procedures. To prove the charge, the agency was required to show that there was an unlawful taking with intent to wrongfully deprive the Postal Service of the funds. The difference between the proof and the charges could have been significant, for the appellant was forced to defend against a charge that the Postal Service did not intend to prove. The case was remanded to the AJ to determine whether the mislabeling of the charge substantially prejudiced the appellant's rights and constituted harmful error. If an agency charges an employee with particular conduct, the charge will be sustained on proof of that conduct even if the proposal notice references a criminal statute. The conduct, not a violation of the criminal statute, is the issue. See Hickman v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 153, 155 (1982).

Larry v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 348, 355 (1997), elaborated on the distinction between a charge based on criminal proceedings and a charge that references criminal proceedings:

The appellant further argues on review that "it is significant" that the criminal charges related to this misconduct charge were dismissed. The record below shows that the criminal charges were indeed dismissed. To the extent the appellant is arguing that the dismissal of the criminal charges warrants not sustaining the "off-duty misconduct" charge, we disagree. The undisputed fact that the appellant was criminally "charged" based on his misconduct was included as part of the agency's factual description in support of the overall misconduct charge; the misconduct charge was based on the appellant's conduct rather than the merits of the criminal charges themselves. Nor did the agency's proof rely on the criminal proceedings. In such situations, the misconduct charge may be sustained notwithstanding the dismissal of the criminal charges. See Farrier v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 60 MSPR 71, 74 & n.3 (1993); Hofmann v. Department of Agriculture, 31 MSPR 399, 402 (1986); cf. Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 503, 508 (1996) (where the appellant was removed solely based on the fact of his criminal conviction, not on the underlying misconduct, the reversal of the conviction warrants reversing the removal).
