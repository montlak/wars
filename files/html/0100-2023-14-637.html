broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 19: Critical Elements and Performance Standards, Section I: ALOC, Step-Increase Determinations, Subsection 02: Within-Grade Increase

2. Within-Grade Increase

The within-grade increase (WIGI) is a relatively routine salary step increase for competitive service employees. It is earned by longevity of a particular type: service within a particular grade for a period of time determined by the step at that grade. The step increase is independent of government-wide cost of living adjustments. It is awarded only if the employee has maintained what is referred to as an "acceptable level of competence" (ALOC), during the waiting period for the increase. The ALOC essentially equates to fully satisfactory performance. Ordinarily, the WIGI is routinely granted. If the step increase is denied, the employee has the right to seek reconsideration of the denial through agency procedures defined by OPM regulations. If reconsideration is denied, the employee may seek review through MSPB or grievance arbitration, depending upon the employee's appeal rights or contract coverage. 5 USC 5335(c). Evolution of the WIGI system is described in Griffith v. FLRA, 842 F.2d 487, 496-97, 499-505 (D.C. Cir. 1988).

A proposal was negotiable that did no more than define, in terms of regulations, what was meant by an "acceptable level of competence (ALOC)." NTEU and USDA, Food and Nutrition Serv., W. Region, 42 FLRA 964, 967-68, 969-70 (1991) (Provision 1). A proposal contravened OPM regulations governing WIGIs that required the step increase to be granted if performance in at least one critical element "exceeded the minimum standard for satisfactory." OPM regulations required that a step increase be denied if performance in any critical element fell below minimally satisfactory. AFSCME Local 2027 and ACTION, 12 FLRA 643, 645-66 (1983). A proposal for the periodic grants of step increases as long as performance exceeded "minimally satisfactory" was nonnegotiable. It violated regulations requiring that the performance be at least fully satisfactory. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 14 FLRA 6, 12-14 (1984) (Proposal 6). A proposal was improper that had the effect of denying a WIGI only if the employee performed below the minimal level in a critical element. NFFE Local 29 and Dept. of Army, Kansas City Dist., 14 FLRA 283, 283-86 (1984); see AFGE Local 1858 and Army Ordnance Missile and Munitions Ctr. and Sch., Redstone Arsenal, 26 FLRA 102, 105-07 (1987) (nonnegotiable, portion of Provision 3: that rating of marginal may be cause for denial of a within-grade increase, because implication that increase could be granted with marginal rating was contrary to government-wide regulations). A proposal that granted a step increase to employees whose overall performance was satisfactory was nonnegotiable. It was contrary to OPM regulations precluding the increase if one critical element of the employee's performance was at the unsatisfactory level. AFGE Local 15 and IRS, N. Atl. Region, 15 FLRA 954, 955-57 (1984); see AFGE Local 1940 and USDA, Plum Island Disease Ctr., 16 FLRA 816, 824 (1984) (Proposal 5, that unsatisfactory rating on critical element may be basis for denial of step-increase was contrary to government-wide regulations requiring that step-increase be denied for that level of rating).

A negotiable proposal required satisfactory performance for a step increase, defined to exceed minimum level required for retention. The proposal essentially defined the fully successful overall rating, a function within the exclusive province of management. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 16 FLRA 948, 949-50 (1984). Under NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Pub. Debt, 3 FLRA 769, 769-70 (1980), a proposal that standards be the same for job retention and a WIGI was nonnegotiable. It was contrary to OPM government-wide regulations setting different levels of performance for these two determinations. Nothing barred a proposal that ALOC determinations be made in a fair and objective manner, free of personal favoritism. But the provision became nonnegotiable when it required the ALOC determination to be made by the employee's immediate supervisor. A proposal that negotiates a task to a particular individual interferes with management's right to assign work. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 734-38 (1992) (Provision 18).

A negotiable proposal established a rebuttable presumption of fully satisfactory work for step-increase purposes if a supervisor failed to provide timely notice of work that was not fully satisfactory. FLRA determined that the agency would still be free to determine the actual level of the employee's performance from any other source. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 14 FLRA 6, 15 (1984). But a provision was nonnegotiable that required the employer to grant a WIGI if it failed to provide a written notice of a negative ALOC determination within a prescribed limit. It was contrary to the then-controlling FPM Ch. 531, subch. 4-9.c(2), establishing procedures for ALOC determinations when agencies fail to make timely determinations because of oversight, error, or delay. POPA and PTO, Dept. of Commerce, 29 FLRA 1389, 1409-11 (1987). A proposal that set a given level of performance as demonstrating entitlement to a WIGI was contrary to the right of management to set performance standards. It was also contrary to the manner that those performance standards are to be applied under government-wide regulations. POPA and PTO, Dept. of Commerce, 29 FLRA 1389, 1406-09 (1987). A proposal was not negotiable that required step increases to be conditioned on the last "audit of record" and that precluded further audits. OPM regulations allow management the option of using a more recent evaluation should the most recent rating of record not properly reflect the employee's performance. AFGE Local 1760 and DHHS, SSA, 23 FLRA 168, 168-70 (1986). [Refer later in this chapter to "Performance Measurement; Statistical Applications; Forced Distributions," for proposals concerning evidence of performance.]

Under POPA and PTO, Dept. of Commerce, 29 FLRA 1389, 1406-09 (1987), a union could compel negotiations on a proposal to determine the length of the performance period before management reevaluates an employee who was previously denied a WIGI. OPM regulations at 5 CFR 531.411 (2022) left that determination to the agencies, and the agencies could bargain within the limits of that discretion. A union could properly propose that an agency provide a 60-day notice and improvement period when a step increase was denied, followed by reconsideration. But the union could not compel negotiations on a proposal to make the redetermination decision retroactive. Under the then-governing regulation, 5 CFR 531.412(a), a retroactive payment was permissible only when, due to administrative error, oversight, or delay, a positive determination is made after the waiting period is completed. Section 531.412(b) stated that "[w]hen an acceptable level of competence is achieved at some time after a negative determination, the effective date is the first day of the first pay period after the acceptable determination has been made." AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 14 FLRA 6, 16 n.14 (1984) (Proposal 8); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 734-38 (1992) (Proposal 18). Another proposal that established a period of retroactivity for a WIGI ultimately granted after being initially withheld was nonnegotiable. It conflicted with OPM regulations. NFFE Local 1623 and S. Carolina NG, Columbia, 28 FLRA 633, 639-41 (1987) (Provision 4).

A negotiable procedure called on the employer to agree that: an employee be given a 60-day period to improve her work once a supervisor determined that the employee's work would not qualify for a WIGI; the notice include the critical elements and performance standards involved in the unacceptable work; the employer provide advice on how the employee can improve; a warning that the performance would be deemed unacceptable without improvement; and a warning that the WIGI will be denied absent improvement to an acceptable level of competence. Other procedural elements for step increase denials were negotiable in POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 90-91 (1993), considering Provision 47:

2. Negative Determination. A negative determination that an employee's performance is not at an acceptable level of competence shall be given to the employee in writing no later than the pay period after the completion of the employee's waiting period and shall include:

a. The performance standard that the employee failed to perform at the satisfactory level.

b. The factual basis for the determination that the employee failed to perform at the satisfactory level.

c. The fact that the employee has the right to request reconsideration from management under the procedures and requirements and time limits of 5 CFR 531.410 and then to grieve the propriety of the reconsidered decision under the negotiated grievance procedure.

d. An identification, where appropriate, of the options available to the employee for improving performance.

FLRA explained in the 1993 POPA decision, 47 FLRA at 91-92:

Provision 47 requires that, if the Agency determines that an employee's performance is not at an acceptable level of competence, management will notify the employee of its determination in writing no later than the pay period after the completion of the employee's waiting period.

We reject the Agency's assertion that Provision 47 is inconsistent with FPM chapter 531, subchapter 4-9.h. Initially, we note that Provision 47 concerns only the timing of notice to an employee that performance is not at an acceptable level of competence, and the Agency does not argue that the timing of the notice is inconsistent with FPM provisions. Rather, the Agency's objection is based on the possible consequences of management's failure to comply with the time frame established by the Provision 47. However, the provision is silent concerning the penalty for noncompliance. Therefore, nothing in Provision 47 prevents the Agency from denying a within-grade increase, based on an employee's performance during the waiting period, when a negative determination is not made on a timely basis because of administrative oversight, error, or delay. Moreover, if an arbitrator were to resolve a grievance concerning this provision in a manner that was contrary to FPM chapter 531, subchapter 4-9.h., the award could be set aside under section 7122(a)(1) of the Statute. Consequently, we find that Provision 47 is not inconsistent with FPM chapter 531, subchapter 4-9.h. See National Treasury Employees Union and Department of the Treasury, Office of Chief Counsel, Internal Revenue Service, 40 FLRA 849, 866-67 (1991), aff'd in part, vacated and remanded as to other matters sub nom. Department of the Treasury, Office of Chief Counsel, Internal Revenue Service v. FLRA, 960 F.2d 1068 (D.C. Cir. 1992) (provision requiring only that employees be notified within specified time of intent to deny within-grade increase found not to require within-grade increase for failing to meet time limit).
