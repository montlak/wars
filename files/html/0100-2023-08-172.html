c. 1996 Legislation Removing MSPB Jurisdiction

Effective January 1, 1996, FAA was authorized to implement a new personnel system in accordance with § 347 of Pub. L. 104-50, the 1996 DOT Appropriations Act. Although the statute permitted FAA employees to pursue whistleblower complaints through the civil service infrastructure, FAA employees were otherwise removed from Title 5 protections. On March 28, 1996, FAA established a "Personnel Management System" that created a tripartite arbitration system to review adverse actions against employees not covered by negotiated agreements. Panel decisions were reviewable in the D.C. Circuit or in the regional circuit court of appeals of the employee's residence. For unit employees covered by labor agreements, traditional arbitration arrangements provided review of challenged adverse actions.

The 1996 legislation removed jurisdiction from the Board for appeals from adverse actions. The effect of the legislation was to rescind preexisting tenure (appeal rights), as the Federal Circuit pointed out in Allen v. MSPB, 127 F.3d 1074, 1076 (Fed. Cir. 1997), over the appellant's protests that the legislation improperly divested rights secured by other statutes, including the Veterans Preference Act. The 1996 legislation precluded the Board from awarding back pay under the Back Pay Act. See Ivery v. Dept. of Transp., 102 MSPR 356, 2006 MSPB 174 (2006); cf. Mitchell v. DHS, 104 MSPR 682, 2007 MSPB 62 (2007) (no interest to TSA nonscreeners under the Back Pay Act); McFarland v. Dept. of Transp., 107 MSPR 449, 452 ¶ 6, 2007 MSPB 299 (2007) (precluding an order directing back pay when a suspension was mitigated; recognizing that the statutory omission may have been an oversight by Congress but requiring explicit waiver of sovereign immunity). But see Bennett v. Dept. of Transp., 105 MSPR 634, 638-42, 2007 MSPB 133 ¶¶ 10-20 (2007) (precluding back pay remedy but permitting an order adjusting sick leave balances in an enforced leave case involving an FAA employee).
