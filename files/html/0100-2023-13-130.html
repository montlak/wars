2. Affirmative Defenses

An employee may present as an affirmative defense the commission of discrimination or a prohibited personnel practice, harmful error (the commission of harmful application of agency procedures in the adverse action process), or legal error. 5 USC 7701(c)(2). [Refer to Chapter 12 for discrimination, to Chapter 11 for "Harmful Error, Defined" and to Chapter 13 for "Whistleblowing Reprisal."] For an affirmative defense, the burden of proof is on the appellant. The appellant's burden is the same as that of the agency: the preponderance of evidence. Pinto v. Dept. of Labor, 11 MSPR 422, 423-24 (1982); cf. Davis v. OPM, 96 Fed. Appx. 665, 668 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (appellant must initially prove to OPM his entitlement to disability retirements, a determination then reviewed by the Board; the employing agency does not determine eligibility for disability retirement benefits); Searcy v. OPM, 98 MSPR 598, 601 ¶ 8 (2005) ("[T]he Board has found that the strict application of the adversarial model of adjudication is not always appropriate in a case involving retirement benefits."). But cf. Joyce v. OPM, 69 MSPR 158, 161 (1995) (appellant must demonstrate by substantial evidence his eligibility for a waiver of the collection of an overpayment of retirement annuity benefits). [Since 1989 the standards of adjudication in Individual Right of Action whistleblower reprisal cases brought by employees against agencies, or involving affirmative defenses of reprisal, have been set by statute. Refer to prohibited personnel practices, and to the concept of "irrefragable proof" of improper agency motivation in whistleblowing cases in chapter 13, "Whistleblowing Reprisal."] Shockro v. FCC, 5 MSPR 113, 117, 5 MSPB 181 (1981), rejected the agency argument that a presumption of administrative regularity required "well nigh irrefragable evidence" (a phrase coined by the Court of Claims) to demonstrate wrongdoing by government officials taking personnel actions against employees. But see Sanders v. USPS, 801 F.2d 1328 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (still alluding to the "irrefragable" proof necessary to overcome a presumption of administrative good faith). Shockro held that the elements of a whistleblowing defense must be proved by preponderant evidence: a protected disclosure, actual or constructive knowledge of the disclosure by the agency officials who took the action against the employee, and retaliatory intent constituting a significant factor in the action.

Hundley v. OPM, 83 MSPR 632, 635 ¶ 8 (2000), involving an assertion that OPM discriminated as to marital status in an annuity determination, pointed out that affirmative defenses may extend beyond those typically raised in adverse action or performance-based appeals:

...Although the scope of the Board's review is limited in some kinds of cases, such as individual right of action appeals, see Kewley v. Department of Health & Human Services, 153 F.3d 1357, 1366 (Fed. Cir. 1998), appeals governed by 5 CFR § 315.806, see id., and cases brought under the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994, see Bodus v. Department of the Air Force, MSPB Docket No. CH-3443-97-0520-I-1, slip op. ¶¶ 12-17 (June 16, 1999), the Board has broad authority to review the merits of OPM reconsideration decisions such as that at issue here. Moreover, while we know of no statutory or regulatory provision specifically addressing the authority of the Board to review allegations of marital status discrimination in cases other than those covered by 5 CFR §§ 315.806 and 315.908,[3] no such provision would appear to be necessary; implicit in the authority to review the merits of an action is the authority to review issues relating to the propriety of that action. See, e.g., Heelen v. Department of Commerce, 75 MSPR 366, 369-70 (1997) (allegation that reduction-in-force action was motivated by partisan political reasons is reviewable by Board because it concerns bona fides of action), rev'd on other grounds, 154 F.3d 1306 (Fed. Cir. 1998); Pope v. Department of the Navy, 44 MSPR 289, 293-94 (1990) (Board considered allegation that removal was based on harassment, i.e., that supervisor was unfairly demanding and critical of employee). Evidence that OPM's denial of the appellant's request for a waiver was based improperly on her marital status would lend support to a finding that the denial was not based on the regulatory criteria governing waiver requests. See 5 CFR §§ 845.301-.307. The appellant's allegation therefore would appear to be within the scope of the Board's review as a challenge to the merits of OPM's reconsideration decision unless considerations such as those mentioned below call for its exclusion.

[3] In light of our finding below, we need not determine whether discrimination on the basis of marital status against annuitants such as the appellant in this case is "prohibited under any law, rule, or regulation," and whether it therefore constitutes a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1)(E). See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(B) (the agency's decision may not be upheld by the Board if the appellant "shows that the decision was based on any prohibited personnel practice described in [5 U.S.C. §] 2302(b)"); cf. Mitchell v. Espy, 845 F. Supp. 1474, 1492 (D. Kan. 1994) (claim of marital status discrimination under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1)(E) failed because the employee identified no statute, rule, or regulation providing cause of action for such discrimination or providing basis for court's jurisdiction over claim), Craighead v. Department of Agriculture, 6 MSPR 159, 162-63 (1981) (the Board reviewed an allegation of marital status discrimination under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1)(E) in an adverse action appeal).
