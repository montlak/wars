broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 08: Disciplinary Procedures and Standards, Section A: Investigatory Protections, Subsection 07: Form and Notice of Complaints, Investigation

7. Form and Notice of Complaints, Investigation

Nothing precluded an agency from negotiating a provision requiring the agency to notify an employee as soon as practicable after receipt of a non-criminal complaint concerning that employee and requiring the agency to provide to the employee a copy of the complaint and of the agency's response. There was no showing of how an investigation would be hampered by the disclosure. Safeguards could be taken to protect confidential sources or other employees involved in the complaint. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 9 FLRA 983, 987 (1982) (Section 13). A proposal was negotiable that required the agency to provide to the union a summary of noncriminal allegations received on its hotline. There was no requirement as to when the summary was required, and there was no requirement that names of individuals be released. The proposal did not infringe on the right of the agency to determine its security practices. NTEU Chap. 153 and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 21 FLRA 841, 846-47 (1986). A proposal was within the duty to bargain that the agency notify nurses of all actions of the Professional Standards Board within 30 days after the Board meeting, although it was not negotiable to specify the supervisors who would be required to discuss Board recommendations with the employees. Illinois Nurses' Ass'n and VAMC, Hines, 28 FLRA 212, 218 (1987). There were, apparently, no concerns with this type of investigation as to internal security practices. Nat'l Union of Hosp. and Health Care Employees, Dist. 1199 and VAMC, Dayton, 28 FLRA 435, 446 (1987).

A nonnegotiable proposal required that employees be notified when higher-level management made an inquiry concerning their work assignment. It involved the employees in discussions pertaining to the exercise of managerial deliberations about the exercise of management rights. AFGE Local 3804 and FDIC, Madison Region, 21 FLRA 870, 875 (1986). An agency could not be compelled to negotiate a proposal that the union be made aware of the conclusions and results of investigations of unit employees. The proposal interfered with the right of the agency to determine its own internal security practices by forcing disclosure of information. AFGE Local 1738 and VAMC, Salisbury, 27 FLRA 52, 54-55 (1987) (Proposal 5) (relying on NFFE Local 1300 and GSA, 18 FLRA 789 (1985) (discussed below)). Nonnegotiable--an infringement upon management's right to determine internal security practices--was Provision 3 in AFGE Local 701 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 58 FLRA 128, 133-34 (2002), requiring that the agency notify the union president at least a day in advance of the arrival of internal affairs investigators seeking to interview a bargaining unit member. The proposal eliminated the element of investigative surprise, even though the proposal did not require advance identification of the individuals being interviewed. The union was not restricted from sharing its information with unit employees.

Procedures for complaints against employees were nonnegotiable to the extent they required sworn statements and prosecution of individuals filing false statements. The proposal interfered with management's right to determine its internal security practices. AFGE Local 2272 and DOJ, Marshals Serv., 9 FLRA 1004, 1010 (1982). NTEU and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 41 FLRA 1349, 1350 (1991), considered, but determined to be nonnegotiable, Provision 1:

When someone from outside the TMEO communicates with the Office about a Unit employee, no part of the communication will be used against the employee unless:

1. it is reduced to writing, which includes a notation of the dates;

2. the employee is given a copy of the complaint and is given the opportunity to make a written response to the complaint, which shall be attached to the complaint wherever it is filed; and

3. the employee gets copies of other investigatory reports management develops from the communication which are used in an action against an employee.

Under NTEU, 41 FLRA at 1353-55:

The Authority has previously held that management's right to take disciplinary actions under section 7106(a)(2)(A) encompasses the right to obtain and use evidence to support actions. For example, NAGE and VAMC, Brockton and West Roxbury, Massachusetts, 41 FLRA 529, 533 (1991) (VAMC, Brockton). In addressing proposals that would restrict an agency's use of various materials and documentation as evidence, we have concluded that those proposals directly interfere with management's rights to discipline employees under section 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute.

As written, this provision does not allow the Agency to use information against an employee unless, among other things, a copy has been given to the employee. The Agency asserts that this provision would apply to the use of adverse medical information in the context of disciplinary actions. In addressing the Agency's argument, the Union does not dispute this assertion. Under Office of Personnel Management (OPM) regulations, certain medical or psychological records may be given only to a physician who has been designated by the subject of the records, and who in turn has "full authority to disclose those records to the [employee] when appropriate." 5 C.F.R. § 297.205. This regulatory requirement can be a factor in adverse action proceedings.... More specifically, OPM regulations that govern adverse actions, such as removals, suspensions for more than 14 days, and reductions in grade or pay, prohibit agencies from using "material that cannot be disclosed to the employee of [sic] his or her representative or designated physician under § 297.204(c) of this chapter to support the reasons" in a notice of proposed action. 5 C.F.R. § 752.404(b). Thus, 5 C.F.R. § 752.404(b) also contemplates that an agency may be required to disclose certain information to a designated physician in lieu of the employee.

Under Provision 1 as written, the Agency could be prohibited from using medical or psychological records that under 5 C.F.R. § 297.205 it may provide only to a physician to support a disciplinary action. Consequently, this provision directly interferes with management's right to take disciplinary action by restricting the Agency's use of materials and documentation to support disciplinary and adverse actions....

A provision that directly interferes with management's rights is nonnegotiable unless it constitutes an appropriate arrangement under section 7106(b)(3) of the Statute. Here, the Union describes the provision as providing a procedural safeguard for employees because it is intended to allow employees to defend themselves against allegations that may result in negative consequences. We view these arguments as an assertion that the provision constitutes an appropriate arrangement. In previous cases, the Authority has concluded that although proposals and provisions that would limit an agency's use of materials in supporting disciplinary and adverse actions may constitute arrangements, they are not appropriate because they excessively interfere with management's rights to discipline employees....
