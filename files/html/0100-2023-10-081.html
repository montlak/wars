c. Raising Issue Sua Sponte

An AJ may address, on his own initiative, an error implicating an employee's basic procedural rights if treatment of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice. The AJ may also raise and consider a prohibited personnel practice issue. There is greater leeway for the AJ to do so if the appellant is not assisted by counsel. However, the AJ is not prevented from considering these issues when the appellant is represented by counsel, Chance v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 583, 588-89 (1983):

[T]he agency argues that the presiding official erred both procedurally and on the merits of the constructive suspension. First, the agency contends that the presiding official is precluded from raising an error sua sponte where, as here, appellant was literate and represented by counsel. The Board has not so limited the basic powers of a presiding official. The Board has stated that it may be appropriate for a presiding official to address sua sponte an error that implicates an employee's basic procedural rights, when cognizance of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice. See Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 MSPB 489, 496 n.9 (1980). The Board noted that slightly greater leeway may be justified where appellant is unassisted by counsel. Id. The Board has also approved under appropriate circumstances a presiding official's sua sponte action in raising and considering a prohibited personnel practice. See Fekete v. Department of Justice, 8 MSPB 130 at 130 (1981); Ketterer v. Department of Agriculture, 2 MSPB 459, 463 n.10 (1980). In Speaker v. Department of Education, 11 MSPB 430 at 431 (1982), the Board noted that under the circumstances of the case the presiding official could have raised a substantive right of appellant on his own motion.

The determinative factor in deciding whether a presiding official abused his discretion in raising an action sua sponte is whether such action was necessary to prevent injustice. In this case, the presiding official found that appellant had not participated in the strike and was not AWOL. Therefore, the agency erred in placing appellant in a non-pay, non-duty status. The Board finds that the presiding official's raising of the constructive suspension sua sponte was a proper exercise of his remedial powers to make appellant whole.

Although the Board has not issued an encompassing definition of "manifest injustice" as to the AJ's authority to raise issues on her own volition, Mouser v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 619, 622 (1986), made plain that manifest injustice includes avoidance of an unwarranted discharge:

[T]he agency challenges the presiding official's finding of harmful procedural error, arguing initially that the appellant had never raised the specific procedural error upon which the presiding official decided the case, and that the presiding official should not have raised it sua sponte in this instance. The agency acknowledges that it is appropriate for a presiding official to address, on his own motion, an error that affects an employee's basic procedural rights, if it would be necessary to do so to prevent manifest injustice, see Chance v. Department of Transportation, 16 MSPR 583, 588-589 (1983); Knab v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 13 MSPR 509, 514 n.1 (1982), but argues that the presiding official failed to explain what manifest injustice would have ensued here had he not raised this issue. In fact, the presiding official clearly set forth his belief that the agency's alleged procedural error violated the appellant's basic right to be allowed to make an informed response to specific charges against him. Thus, while we may, and herein do, consider the propriety of the presiding official's finding of harmful procedural error, we do not believe he erred in raising and considering the possibility of its existence in this case.

The Board also states that "the presiding official is not bound to address possible agency errors sua sponte as an appellant bears the burden of establishing an affirmative defense," Eng v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 220, 222 (1983), and although an AJ may raise, sua sponte, issues implicating basic procedural rights, apparently it is not proper to do so as to substantive rights, e.g., whether an appellant's position was actually abolished by a RIF. Caselli v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 196, 199 & n.4 (1985); see Logan v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 573, 581 (1986) (because the appellant did not raise the affirmative defense of disability (alcoholism) discrimination, the AJ's analysis of that claim was inappropriate). But see Ortolano v. SSA, NY-0752-13-0155-I-1 n.5 (NP 2/4/2015) ("the Board may raise potential due process violations sua sponte. See Powers v. Department of the Treasury, 86 M.S.P.R. 256, ¶ 10 n.3 (2000)."); Powers v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 256, 261-62 ¶ 10 (2000) (AJ properly questioned whether agency decisional processes involved ex parte communications in violation of constitutional safeguards); Stephen v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 672, 678-79 (1991) (AJ appropriately inquired whether appellant was a probationer--an issue of jurisdictional as well as basic procedural significance); Luger v. VA, 29 MSPR 261, 262 n.* (1985) ("It is appropriate for a presiding official to address an error which implicates basic procedural rights accorded under a collective bargaining agreement when cognizance of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice."); Knab v. NASA, 13 MSPR 509, 514 n.* (1982) (AJ raised issues concerning the appellant's assignment rights; had the agency been "surprised," it could have requested a continuance of the hearing to prepare and present additional evidence).

The Federal Circuit informs us that a AJ should not "try the case" on behalf of the appellant represented by counsel: it is not the job of the AJ to raise mitigation if it was not placed squarely before him in the course of the appeal. Allred v. DHHS, 786 F.2d 1128, 1132 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("[I]n those cases in which an employee is represented by counsel, it is not the duty of the presiding official to substitute for counsel and to 'try the case' for the represented employee[.] "). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Mitigation or Aggravation of Penalties," for Allred.] In Checo v. Shinseki, 748 F.3d 1373, 1382-85 (Fed. Cir. 2014), Judge Meyer offered a dissent as to whether a subordinate court should have raised a nonjurisdictional timeliness issue as to a veteran's claim:

..."In our adversary system, in both civil and criminal cases, in the first instance and on appeal, we follow the principle of party presentation. That is, we rely on the parties to frame the issues for decision and assign to courts the role of neutral arbiter of matters the parties present." Greenlaw v. United States, 554 U.S. 237, 243 (2008). The Veterans Court's regular practice of addressing, sua sponte, the question of whether a veteran's appeal is timely filed is contrary to the Supreme Court's admonition that a court should independently consider a statute of limitations defense only "in exceptional cases." Wood v. Milyard, 132 S. Ct. 1826, 1834 (2012). Regularly raising an affirmative defense on behalf of the Secretary creates the appearance that the court functions not as a "neutral arbiter," Greenlaw, 554 U.S. at 243, but instead as a mere appendage of the Department of Veterans Affairs ("VA"), as even the Veterans Court once recognized. See MacWhorter v. Derwinski, 2 Vet. App. 133, 135 (1992) ("[F]erreting out...implicit or possible contentions" on behalf of the Secretary "would be the antithesis of the adversarial judicial appellate process."); see also Hodge v. West, 155 F.3d 1356, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 1998) ("[I]n the context of veterans' benefits where the system of awarding compensation is so uniquely pro-claimant, the importance of systemic fairness and the appearance of fairness carries great weight.").

...

..."[A] federal court does not have carte blanche to depart from the principle of party presentation basic to our adversary system." Wood, 132 S. Ct. at 1833. Instead, the Supreme Court has repeatedly cautioned that a court can sua sponte address an affirmative defense only in a narrow set of circumstances. See id. at 1834 (concluding that an appellate court abused its discretion by raising a timeliness defense on its own initiative); Greenlaw, 554 U.S. at 244 (Because our justice "system is designed around the premise that the parties know what is best for them, and are responsible for advancing the facts and arguments entitling them to relief," courts "normally decide only questions presented by the parties." (citations and internal quotation marks omitted)); Arizona v. California, 530 U.S. 392, 413 (2000) (Because it "erod[es] the principle of party presentation so basic to our system of adjudication," courts must be "cautious" about raising an affirmative defense sua sponte.)....

...

Many veterans who seek redress from the Veterans Court suffer from significant service-connected physical and psychiatric disabilities. See Dixon v. Shinseki, 741 F.3d 1367, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2014). Such veterans, moreover, are often unrepresented when they file their notices of appeal. See id. The Secretary, by contrast, is represented by a regiment of skilled and experienced attorneys. Given that the Secretary generally has a clear advantage--in terms of resources and experience--it defies understanding why the Veterans Court believes it necessary to routinely raise the timeliness defense on his behalf. See Greenlaw, 554 U.S. at 244 ("Counsel almost always know a great deal more about their cases than we do, and this must be particularly true of counsel for the United States, the richest, most powerful, and best represented litigant to appear before us." (citations and internal quotation marks omitted)).

"The rule that points not argued will not be considered is more than just a prudential rule of convenience; its observance, at least in the vast majority of cases, distinguishes our adversary system of justice from the inquisitorial one." United States v. Burke, 504 U.S. 229, 246(1992) (Scalia, J., concurring in the judgment)....

Nevertheless, if the AJ makes it reasonably clear that he is considering a defense, e.g., disability discrimination, that the appellant may not have clearly articulated, the agency cannot raise for the first time on PFR the AJ's the treatment of the issue at the hearing. The agency should object to treatment of the defense when the AJ suggests he will consider it. See Harbo v. USPS, 53 MSPR 450, 458-64 (1992) (majority opinion joined by Chairman Levinson, who offered an extensive concurring opinion collecting related cases and suggesting that the Board should clarify law as to when AJs may determine unpleaded issues without the parties' consent).

The appellant should not be required, at the hearing or in his PFR, to rebut arguments and evidence never advanced by either the agency or competing employees in a RIF, either expressly or by implication; it was error for the AJ to raise and address an appellant's right to displace other employees in the course of a RIF. Putnam v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 427, 430 (1986) (citing Caselli v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 196, 199 (1985) (it was inappropriate for the AJ to raise and address, sua sponte, similarity between pre- and post-RIF positions when the appellant never advanced those allegations and the issue did not implicate the appellant's basic procedural rights)). Conversely, if the appellant, represented by counsel, expressly waives a defense, that defense should not be resurrected by the AJ when the opposing party believed it would not need to defend against it, McKowen v. DLA, 10 MSPR 98, 100-01 (1982):

As we held in Clancy v. Department of the Navy, 6 MSPB 173, 175, 6 MSPR 196, 200 (1981), an allegation of discrimination on the basis of a handicapping condition is an affirmative defense which must be pled and proved by the appellant who asserts it. 5 CFR § 1201.56(b) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)] . In this case, the appellant's attorney argued at the hearing that the appellant had a psychiatric condition related to the misconduct with which he was charged, and that the agency had an obligation to assist the appellant to a greater degree than it did, proffering both documentary and testimonial evidence on those issues. However, when the presiding official specifically inquired, "Is the appellant alleging discrimination because of a handicapping condition," the appellant's attorney responded, "We're not alleging today that [the appellant] was discriminated against based on a handicap...." Disregarding this exchange, the presiding official found in his initial decision that, while handicap discrimination was not expressly alleged in the petition for appeal, it was the "essence" of the testimonial and documentary evidence. He then based his disposition of the case on his finding of discrimination.

The appellant argued in his response to the petition for review that the Board's decision in Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 MSPB 489, 1 MSPR 505 (1980), supports the presiding official's exercise of discretion to consider an affirmative defense that had not been raised by the appellant. We did hold in Parker at 496 n.9, 1 MSPR at 517 n.9 that, although a procedural error should not generally be considered harmful unless the appellant so alleges, "[I]t may be appropriate for a presiding official to address on his or her own initiative an error that implicates an employee's basic procedural rights, when cognizance of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice." A procedural error is an affirmative defense by statutory and regulatory definition, as is handicap discrimination. 5 USC §§ 2302(b)(1)(D) and 7701(c)(2)(A) and (B); 5 CFR § 1201.56(b)(1) and (2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1) and (2)] . It follows, therefore, that the principle of sua sponte consideration of the affirmative defense of harmful error under circumstances that would otherwise work manifest injustice against the appellant, applies equally to the affirmative defense of handicap discrimination.

In this case, however, the appellant was represented by an attorney with the National Association of Government Employees who acknowledged that he had considered raising the defense but decided not to do so. Cf. Parker at 496 n.9, 1 MSPR at 517 n.9 (noting that slightly greater leeway may be justified in raising a defense sua sponte where the appellant is unassisted by counsel). As pointed out by OPM in its brief, because the appellant's attorney expressly waived reliance on the affirmative defense of handicap discrimination, neither the presiding official nor the agency invoked the regulation of the Board which provides that "[W]hen an appellant alleges prohibited discrimination for the first time during the course of a proceeding,...the agency shall be given a reasonable opportunity to refute the allegation through a responsive pleading, testimony, production of documents or as otherwise permitted by the presiding official." 5 CFR § 1201.155(d). Under these circumstances, we find that the presiding official erred in finding that the agency failed to rebut the appellant's prima facie showing of handicap discrimination when the agency was never put on notice that the matter was in issue....
