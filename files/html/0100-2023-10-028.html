a. Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile

Most AJs (perhaps concerned about how lengthy continuances will affect their productivity elements in their performance standards or the regional office's case processing statistics) prefer to delay hearings through dismissals of appeals without prejudice to refile (DWOP) the appeals after the lapse of a set time. If the AJ is not inclined to continue a hearing but there is a good reason for delay--such as active settlement discussions or complex discovery--there is good reason for permitting the appellant to take a DWOP for a certain period of time. The parties can then pursue and conclude settlement discussions or negotiations; the appellant then refiles the appeal on or before the date set in the initial decision dismissing the appeal without prejudice to its refiling.

The Board regularized the DWOP process through regulation in 2012. 5 CFR 1201.29 (2022). Summarizing the DWOP process, as an alternative to a continuance, the AJ may dismiss an appeal without prejudice to its refiling. The procedure may be invoked for many reasons. If, for example, an appellant cannot attend a hearing because of a long-term illness without foreseeable end, the AJ should DWOP the appeal rather than denying a continuance of the hearing and exceeding the 120-day period for issuing an initial decision. Under current Board practice, the AJ should set a definite deadline for the refiling of the appeal. If the condition necessitating the DWOP is not been met upon refiling, another DWOP motion may be filed. Some older decisions allowed cases to be DWOP'd until a condition of indefinite duration was met. If the DWOP was conditioned on the appellant's recovery, rather than on the passage of a specified period of time, when the appellant refiled the appeal he would offer evidence justifying the delay and demonstrating that he filed the appeal as soon as his incapacity concluded. Poudrier v. OPM, 18 MSPR 646, 647 (1984); see Toma v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 408, 410, 5 MSPB 420 (1981) (AJ acted properly by using a DWOP rather than granting a continuance, when the appellant was medically unable to proceed with the appeal and the AJ established timetables to expedite the processing of the appeal when the appellant could proceed). An appellant was required to reinstate the appeal within 20 days after medical certification of his competence to proceed. Documentation establishing that the refiled appeal was timely was to accompany the reinstatement request. Vournazos v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 442, 443 (1984). The agency was not prejudiced by allowing the appellant to refile if sufficient medical evidence was not presented with the initial application for the continuance; the evidence was to be supplied when appellant refiled. Paine v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 135, 137 (1987); cf. Rodriguez v. DOD, supra (there is no strict requirement of medical evidence to support a motion to dismiss without prejudice to refiling; there was no objection by the agency to a dismissal without prejudice to refile even though the appellant had not then submitted the medical evidence requested by the AJ to support his motion). There are many reasons other than illness commending and allowing a DWOP. An alternative to the DWOP or continuance is the case suspension procedure, described earlier and at 5 CFR 1201.28 (2022), to obtain an additional 30 days for discovery or settlement negotiations.

The AJ's initial decision will contain a date, or permit calculation of a date (e.g., 90 days from the date of the initial decision), for the appeal to be refiled. Too many otherwise timely appeals that are untimely refiled are dismissed as untimely, meaning the appellant's appeal does not receive merits adjudication. To avoid the possibility of a belated refiling, the AJ can be requested to automatically refile the appeal at the conclusion of the DWOP period. If automatic refiling is granted at the request of the appellant, or included in the DWOP initial decision at the AJ's own initiative, the risk for an untimely refiling is avoided.
