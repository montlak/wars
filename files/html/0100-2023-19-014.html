g. Premature Appeal

Under regulation, 5 CFR 1201.154(c), and under past case law, if an appellant filed an EEO complaint with the agency challenging the adverse action and prematurely files a Board appeal, the AJ may hold the petition, rather than dismiss it to be later refiled, if there is only a short time before the appeal would become timely. Howard v. Dept. of Commerce, 15 MSPR 23, 26 n.1 (1983). Shalwala v. SBA, 70 MSPR 168, 170 (1996), provided some indication of what constitutes the "short time" that an AJ may hold an otherwise premature appeal filed in a mixed case:

The appellant argues that the administrative judge should have held the appeal until it became timely rather than dismiss without prejudice so that the processing of her appeal would not be delayed. The Board's regulations provide that an administrative judge may hold a premature appeal for a "short time" rather than dismiss it, if doing so would allow it to become timely. 5 CFR 1201.154(c). The appellant argues that her appeal would have become timely by January 1, 1996, a short time after the November 28, 1995 initial decision dismissing it without prejudice. An administrative judge has broad discretion to control the proceedings before her, and dismissal without prejudice is a procedural option that is committed to her sound discretion. Gardner v. Department of the Treasury, 68 MSPR 258, 261 (1995). We find that the administrative judge determined reasonably that the approximately 30 days until the appeal became timely was not a "short time."

If, due to a discrimination complaint, the Board appeal is premature, the AJ dismisses the appeal, and a PFR is filed challenging the dismissal, the Board will vacate the initial decision dismissing the appeal and remand for adjudication if, between the time of the initial decision and the decision on the PFR, the Board appeal became timely under the mixed case regulations. See Johnson v. USPS, 76 MSPR 289, 292 (1997); Posey v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 422, 424 (1992); Nichols v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 41, 42-43 (1988); Nugent v. USPS, 31 MSPR 290, 291 (1986); Rogers v. CPSC, 21 MSPR 113, 114 (1984); cf. Galvan v. EEOC, 113 MSPR 322, 324-25 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 36 (2010) (unnecessary to determine if the initial contact with the agency following the removal constituted initiation of the EEO process making his Board appeal premature when, by the time the PFR from the dismissal of that appeal was adjudicated, 120 days passed; remanding for a merits determination, citing Summerset v. Dept. of Navy, 100 MSPR 292, 295 ¶¶ 8-9 (2005)).

As an exception to the usual run of Board cases determining that a case that was premature when filed, by reason of an earlier EEO complaint, ripens during the pendency of the PFR, in Jones v. MSPB, 154 Fed. Appx. 197, 199 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP), on February 12, 2004, the AJ dismissed, for lack of jurisdiction, a Board appeal that was filed a few hours after an EEO complaint was filed to contest the same adverse action. Jones filed a timely PFR. While that PFR was pending, on October 20, 2004, the agency issued a final agency decision on the EEO complaint. On January 27, 2005, the Board issued its final order affirming the AJ's decision that there was no jurisdiction over the appeal. Under one calculus, Jones could have refiled after 120 days from the EEO complaint until the date of the final agency decision, and under another calculus, Jones could have filed within 30 days from the final agency decision. The court determined that under either scenario, Jones could file an MSPB appeal from May 1, 2004 until approximately November 19, 2004. The court implied that the Board was not obliged to consider the case timely filed during the pendency of the PFR through January 27, 2005, when the Board issued its final decision. The court decided the Board properly dismissed the appeal:

The Board interprets 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(b) to mean that "the right to appeal does not vest until either the agency issues a final decision on the discrimination complaint or until 120 days elapse from the date the discrimination complaint was filed." Jones v. Dep't of Navy, No. AT-0752-04-0238-I-1 (M.S.P.B. February 12, 2004) ("Initial Decision") (citing Kozak v. Dep't of Health & Human Serv., 90 M.S.P.R. 398, 401 (2001)); see, e.g., Grunfelder v. United States Postal Serv., 56 M.S.P.R. 119, 122 (1992). The Board also recognizes that if the 120-day period has elapsed and the case is pending before the Board on petition for review, "the appeal is...ripe for adjudication, and...remand [to the regional office for a hearing] is appropriate." E.g., Kozak, 90 M.S.P.R. at 401 (deciding the petition for review after the expiration of the 120-day period and before a final decision by an agency).

If the right to appeal vested after April 30, 2004 then it is arguable that the Board should have remanded the appeal to the regional office for a hearing on May 1, 2004 even though the Board had not issued the final order. The Board, however, argues, and we agree, that the appeal could not be remanded to the regional office because the final order, on January 27, 2005, was apparently issued more than 30 days after her receipt of the final decision by the agency, which was issued on October 20, 2004.

Ultimately, Jones's appeal is subject to the timing of the final order of the Board. If the final order had been issued anytime between May 1, 2004 and October 19, 2004 then the Board would have had jurisdiction. The final order, however, was issued on January 27, 2005, more than 30 days after the final decision by the agency.

Substantial evidence in the record supports the findings of the Board as to the filing dates of the discrimination complaint, appeal to the Board, initial decision of the Board, and final order of the Board. Furthermore, Jones did not prove that the Board's actions were not in accordance with the law. Jones fails to show that the Board properly had jurisdiction over her appeal.
