5. Official Time; Resources Available to Appellant

Not much law describes the AJ's ability to equalize resources between the government and the appellant by requiring the agency to place the appellant (assuming she is still employed) on official time or to require the agency to grant to the appellant access to agency resources during the course of Board proceedings. White v. SSA, 76 MSPR 447, 466-67 (1997), involving an IRA and constructive adverse action appeal by an ALJ, determined that the authority of the Board's AJ or ALJ does not extend to requiring the agency to grant the appellant official time or use of official facilities:

...Part 2635 of Title 5 of the Code of Federal Regulations contains Standards of Ethical Conduct for Employees of the Executive Branch, including the petitioner. 5 CFR 2635.102(h). Subpart G, Misuse of Position, contains provisions relating to the proper use of official time and authority, and of information and resources to which an employee has access because of his Federal employment. Id. at § 2635.701. This subpart provides that, unless authorized in accordance with law or regulations to use such time for other purposes, an employee shall use official time in an honest effort to perform official duties, id. at § 2635.705, and that an employee shall not use government property for other than authorized purposes, which are defined as those purposes authorized in accordance with law or regulation, id. at § 2635.704(1) and (b)(2). The petitioner asks the Board to find that 5 USC 7521, broadly construed, impliedly authorizes ALJs under 5 CFR 2635.705 and .704 to use official time and resources to bring claims before the Board. In support, he cites to Tcherepnin v. Knight, 389 U.S. 332, 336, 88 S. Ct. 548, 552-53, 19 L. Ed.2d 564 (1967), for the general proposition that any remedial legislation should be construed broadly to effectuate its purposes and Benton v. United States, 488 F.2d 1017, 1023, 203 Ct. Cl. 263, 272 (1973) wherein the court described the Administrative Procedure Act, from which 5 USC 7521 derived, as a sweeping piece of remedial legislation which should be given a broad and generous interpretation in light of the objectives sought to be accomplished. Notwithstanding the validity of these basic principles, we are not inclined to interpret section 7521 as requiring agencies to allow appellants official time and resources to pursue their appeals with the Board, particularly in the absence of any agency regulation specifically providing for such.

With one exception not here applicable,[12] the Board has no specific statutory or regulatory authority for allowing the use of official time to pursue an appeal before it. Forrest v. Dept. of Agric., 74 MSPR 213, 221 (1997)....

[12] Section 1201.33, 5 CFR, provides that employees, including appellants, who furnish sworn statements or appear as witnesses in Board hearings are entitled to be in official duty status for those times, and to receive pay and benefits including travel and per diem, where appropriate. See Sapp v. USPS, 73 MSPR 189, 196 (1997). Having determined that it had the authority to act as necessary to perform its functions, the Board prescribed the above-referenced regulation concerning witness fees. In re Maisto, 28 MSPR 436, 440 (1985). No comparable Board regulation exists, however, with regard to an appellant's use of official time for pursuing an appeal.

Forrest v. USDA, 74 MSPR 213, 221 (1997), confirmed the unavailability of official time in Board proceedings for the purpose of attorney-client consultations, although the case added that restoration or reimbursement for leave may be recovered as a component of compensatory damages. As to its authority to direct the agency to permit the use of official time, the Board distinguished official time entitlements in cases governed by FLRA and EEOC regulations:

[N]o authority exists for allowing time spent to consult a representative regarding a Board appeal to be considered official time or administrative leave. Unlike the FLRA or the EEOC, the Board has no specific statutory or regulatory authority for allowing the use of official time to pursue an appeal. 5 USC 7105(g)(3) and 7131 pertain to the FLRA and authorize that agency to take any remedial action it considers appropriate to carry out its statutory purpose, and provide for official time in connection with an employee's participation for, and on behalf of, labor organizations. 29 CFR 1614.605 of the EEOC's regulations allows a complainant employee a reasonable amount of official time to prepare an equal employment opportunity complaint and to respond to agency and EEOC requests for information. The EEOC was specifically granted administration and enforcement authority of equal opportunity in Federal employment, and the authority to issue regulations regarding Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967, the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, and the Fair Labor Standards Act, pursuant to Reorganization Plan No. 1 of 1978, 43 Fed. Reg. 19,807 (1978), and Executive Order 12106, 44 Fed. Reg. 1053 (1978). See 54 Fed. Reg. 45,747 (1989). The Board, however, has no comparable statutory or regulatory authority authorizing such leave to pursue an appeal before the Board....

See SSA v. Glover, 23 MSPR 57, 66 (1984) (under agency regulations, "[t]he agency did not improperly deny [Respondent] unrestricted official time and use of his office and free access to agency files in preparing for hearing"); cf. White v. SSA, 76 MSPR 303, 306-08 (1997) (dismissing the ALJ's appeal when he failed to obey an order of the Board AJ to cease the use of penalty mail and to refile appeal without using penalty envelope).
