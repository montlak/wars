b. Multiplicity and Merger of Charges

Agencies may take a single offense and break it down into constituent components--each component stated as a separate charge. In the classic case, an employee was asked 19 times during the course of one interview about a job incident, and 19 times the employee gave the same response. When the agency decided the employee was lying, 19 separate charges resulted. A reviewing court concluded that the agency should have stated its case in one charge. Southers v. VA, 813 F.2d 1223 (Fed. Cir. 1987). The single charge may be less serious. See Southers v. VA, 36 MSPR 213, 216 (1988) (remand decision finding removal appropriate for falsification of reports of work and investigative testimony). But "[t]he Board has held that merger of a duplicative charge into another charge does not mean that the merged charge is not sustained. Mann v. Department of Health & Human Services, 78 M.S.P.R. 1, 6 (1998)." Moreno v. Dept. of Army, DE-0752-12-0066-I-1 (NP 12/17/2013). Merger of charges may, however, have an impact on the outcome of an appeal, Torres v. DHS, AT-0752-10-0189-I-1 (NP 3/24/2011):

We do note two legal errors by the administrative judge, neither of which impacts upon the final result. First, the administrative judge erred in concluding that none of the agency's charges was duplicative. When the charges are based upon the same incident and involve the same misconduct, and there is no specific additional conduct particular to the individual charges, the charges are duplicative and must be merged. See Beaudoin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 99 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶¶ 11-13 (2005). Where the bulk of the charges (or most significant charges) should have been merged, the error may be deemed harmful and warrant a remand to ascertain whether the penalty selected was appropriate for the reduced number of charges. See Southers v. Veterans Administration, 813 F.2d 1223, 1225-26 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

Here, charges 1 and 6 are essentially identical and should have been merged. But the mere fact that charges should have been merged does not make the appellant's misconduct less serious or imply that the penalty selected was unreasonable. See generally Shiflett v. Department of Justice, 98 M.S.P.R. 289, ¶ 12 (2005). In this case, the bulk of the charges remain, including the lack of candor charge (charge 4), which the deciding official stated was the most significant and prompted the appellant's removal.

A single incident may properly lead to two or more charges, each describing "entirely separate acts of misconduct." Miles v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 633, 636 (1992) (use of government vehicle to kill a deer could be split into a charge of misuse of a GOV and a charge of conduct unbecoming to a federal employee); see Cotton v. Smithsonian Institution, DC-0752-10-0824-I-1 (NP 1/19/2012) ("Where two charges are based on the same act of misconduct and proof of one charge automatically constitutes proof of the other, the charges should be merged. Alvarado v. Department of the Air Force, 103 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 18 (2006), aff'd, 626 F. Supp.2d 1140 (D.N.M. 2009). However, merger is not appropriate where proof of a single act does not automatically result in proof of multiple charges. Id."). If the conduct described by the agency describes one incident, the multiple components of that incident should be stated in one or more charges that interrelate the significant components of the misconduct. See Jones v. USPS, AT-0752-10-0788-I-1 n.2 (NP 4/6/2012) ("the Board has merged charges when, like here, the agency relies on the same specification, the charges are based on the same incident, and involve the same misconduct. See Patton v. Department of the Treasury, 94 M.S.P.R. 562, ¶ 14 (2003) (more general charge of personal misconduct merged into falsification charge where the agency merely repeated the same specifications); Delgado v. Department of the Air Force, 36 M.S.P.R. 685, 688 (1988); see Ruffin v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 499, 502-03 (1987), aff'd, 852 F.2d 1293 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table). The agency's decision to drop the more specific of its charges, because it relied on the same narrative specification for both charges, effectively merged the charges."); Delgado v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 685, 688 (1988) (charges of AWOL and leaving the job without permission were merged into one offense; charges of destruction of government property and unauthorized use of government property should have been merged); cf. Moreno v. Dept. of Army, DE-0752-12-0066-I-1 (NP 12/17/2013) ("Here, the misconduct described in the narrative under the agency's charge of failure to follow a direct order is essentially based on the same misconduct described in the agency's second specification under its charge of failure to follow instructions. In addition, the agency necessarily proved the charge of failure to follow a direct order by proving the charge of failure to follow instructions. Accordingly, it is proper to merge the second charge of failure to follow a direct order into the first charge of failure to follow instructions. See Shiflett, 98 M.S.P.R. 289, ¶ 5.").
