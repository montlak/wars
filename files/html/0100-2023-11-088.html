1. Issues of Fact and Law

As a matter of law, the parties may stipulate as to any matter of fact, and that stipulation satisfies the party's burden of proof of the fact alleged. 5 CFR 1201.63 (2022). The AJ errs by overstepping the boundaries of a factual stipulation. See Davis v. USPS, 54 MSPR 374, 377 (1992) (the parties stipulated to the existence of a vehicular accident, not to the appellant's fault). As a matter of law, the Board is not bound by a stipulation of law. See Alston v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 19, 22 n.2 (1994); see also Targan v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3498) (stipulation concerning the interpretation of an agency regulation: "While courts, and the board, may be bound by factual stipulations, they are not bound by parties' stipulations concerning questions of law."); Wojcicki v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 628, 634, 640 (1996) (notwithstanding the parties' stipulation that the appellant made protected disclosures, the Board must resolve whether the appellant had a reasonable belief that the condition he reported established a substantial and specific threat to public health or safety).

The parties may stipulate that the outcome of an appeal depends on resolution of a single issue of fact, Alexander v. USPS, CH-0752-09-0737-I-1 (NP 3/3/2011):

...The parties stipulated that the charge dealt solely with the appellant's conduct toward the complaining witness. The parties further stipulated that if the Board "credit[s] the testimony of [the complaining witness], the Agency will have shown that its action was taken for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service and that the penalty of removal is within the tolerable limits of reasonableness."...

...

As noted above, the parties stipulated that "[T]he sole issue in this case is the credibility of Appellant and the [complaining witness]." The parties agreed that if the Board credits the testimony of the complaining witness, "the Agency will have shown that its action was taken for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service and that the penalty of removal is within the tolerable limits of reasonableness." The Board permits such stipulations. Sullivan v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 196, 200, aff'd, 11 F.3d 1073 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table) (holding that a stipulation can establish that an agency had just cause for implementing a removal action); see Tanner v. Department of Transportation, 65 M.S.P.R. 169, 171 (1994) (holding that an appellant's stipulations can be sufficient to establish a charge); Frye v. Department of the Army, 63 M.S.P.R. 242, 252 (1994) (holding that "a stipulation is sufficient to prove the fact alleged").

Accordingly, consistent with the stipulations reached during the pre-hearing discussions, we find that the disciplinary action was for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service and that the penalty of removal is within the tolerable limits of reasonableness.

Wright v. Dept. of Labor, supra. Anderson v. TVA, 77 MSPR 271, 275-76 (1998), explained the limited value of a stipulation that involves an issue of law:

...The appellant's petition for review assertion, however, contradicts the stipulation that he agreed to below, that is, that his competitive level was established in accordance with 5 CFR § 351.403. Under Board regulations, parties may stipulate to any matter of fact, and the stipulation will satisfy a party's burden of proving the fact alleged. 5 CFR § 1201.63; Swift v. Office of Personnel Management, 48 MSPR 441, 445 (1991). However, whether the appellant's competitive level was properly drawn is a matter of mixed fact and law. See McCray v. Department of Defense, 68 MSPR 186, 192 n.5 (1995). Stipulations concerning matters of mixed fact and law are not binding on the Board. Id.; Alston v. Department of the Navy, 62 MSPR 19, 22 n.2 (1994), rev'd on other grounds, 75 F.3d 657 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Thus, notwithstanding the stipulation by the parties that the appellant's competitive level was established in accordance with 5 CFR § 351.403, to the extent that the stipulation involved a matter of law, the Board must resolve for itself whether the agency proved by preponderant evidence that the appellant's competitive level was properly drawn. See Wojcicki v. Department of the Air Force, 72 MSPR 628, 634 (1996).

Board jurisdiction is not determined by the stipulation of the parties. McCarty v. EPA, 108 MSPR 45, 50 ¶ 10, 2008 MSPB 21 (2008); see Chiovitti v. Dept. of Air Force, PH-0752-21-0212-I-1, n.3 (NP 7/12/2022) ("The appellant argues that the agency agreed not to challenge that good cause existed or that the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal. Although an agency can stipulate to matters of fact, legal conclusions, such as Board jurisdiction or the existence of good cause, are not subject to stipulation. See King v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 105 M.S.P.R. 21, ¶ 16 n.2 (2007) (stating that, while the parties can stipulate to facts, the question of Board jurisdiction is a legal question not subject to stipulation); Ludlum v. Department of Justice, 87 M.S.P.R. 56, ¶ 27 (2000) (stating that the Board is not bound by stipulations of mixed questions of fact and law), aff'd, 278 F.3d 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2002)."). The agency may not be able to stipulate away its defenses, particularly if those defenses are intertwined in Board jurisdiction. [Chapter 13, "Whistleblowing Reprisal," considers decisions that essentially require an appellant to prove, rather than allowing an agency to stipulate to, elements of a whistleblowing appeal.] But in an appeal involving a disability defense, McCray v. DOD, 68 MSPR 186, 192-93 n.5 (1995), the Board allowed a stipulation concerning the existence of a disability:

We note that the administrative judge referred to the parties as having "stipulated" that the appellant was a mentally disabled individual. Under Board regulations, "[T]he parties may stipulate to any matter of fact. The stipulation will satisfy a party's burden of proving the fact alleged." See 5 CFR § 1201.63. In general, the question of whether an individual is disabled within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act and its implementing regulations is a mixed question of fact and law; that is, whether the facts of record establish that a particular legal standard or criterion has been met. However, because the parties do not dispute that the appellant is disabled within the appropriate definitions, we will not review that matter and we accept the "stipulation" and the parties' conclusion that the appellant is disabled and covered by the Act and the regulations, although the precise nature and extent of the appellant's disability is not specified in the stipulation and remains an open question.

See Ludlum v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 56, 68 ¶ 27 (2000) ("Even assuming, however, that the parties' stipulation covered the nexus and penalty issues, the Board is not bound by those determinations because a stipulation involving a mixed question of law and fact is not binding on the Board.").
