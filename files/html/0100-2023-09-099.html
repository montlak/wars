a. MSPB

If Board officials mislead the appellant concerning the necessity to file an appeal or its timing, the appellant's delay due to the misleading advice will be excused. Meehan v. USPS, 7 MSPR 193, 196, 7 MSPB 106 (1981); see Annunziata v. USPS, 36 MSPR 192, 196-97 (1988) (reasonable for a pro se appellant to rely on the AJ's misstatement concerning the timing of his appeal); Perlman v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 591, 593 (1984) (excusing delay when a Board employee refused to accept the appellant's appeal because it was thought to relate to a nonappealable classification decision rather than a RIF action; the appellant's union then referred the matter to the Comptroller General, who referred the appellant back to the Board). The time allowed for filing an appeal will be extended if the agency did not notify the appellant of his appeal rights and if the appellant communicated with the Board and was confused as to whether his letters to the Board constituted either a new appeal or a continuation of a prior appeal. Farrior v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 160, 162 (1985) (couple of years of delay in filing a restoration rights appeal was excused; several letters from the agency denying restoration over a period of years failed to identify a Board appeal right). But see Harris v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3125) ("In order to establish good cause on the basis of lack of understanding of Board procedures, the appellant's confusion must relate to a specific ambiguity in either the instructions received from the Board or in a Board procedure."); Clark v. Dept. of Interior, 31 MSPR 28, 28 n.* (1986) (counsel, having been notified by the AJ that the deadline for an appeal had passed, had the duty to cure the error quickly and not delay the appeal for another two months). A delay in filing an appeal was excused as a result of confusion generated by an initial decision that did not clearly explain the distinctions in dates stated in the decision for filing a PFR and for filing an involuntary retirement appeal. Daniel v. TVA, 69 MSPR 376, 377 (1996). If the allegation is that an AJ provided incorrect information regarding filing requirements, and if the AJ must supply evidence for the issue to be resolved, a new AJ should be appointed to consider the evidence. See Sanders v. EEOC, 45 MSPR 693, 698 (1990) (involving a late petition for counsel fees). We add to this group of cases the reversal and remand that may occur if the appellant fails to receive the Board's acknowledgment order directing a response on a timeliness issue. Davis v. OPM, 45 MSPR 397, 400 (1990). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Appeal Misdirected Within MSPB, to OPM, or to OSC."]
