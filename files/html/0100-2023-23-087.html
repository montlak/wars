11. Insurance Benefit

The Back Pay Act does not require the government to purchase, or to reimburse for the purchase, of private health insurance for a period of unemployment caused by an unjustified separation for the federal service. The appellant may obtain retroactive coverage of insurance that she had prior to separation, or the employee may avoid deduction from back pay for premiums that would otherwise be deducted for the period following separation until reinstatement, Sowa v. VA, 100 MSPR 5, 7-8 ¶¶ 6-8 (2005):

The agency must place the appellant in the status quo ante when the Board orders the cancellation of an agency action. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The appellant argues that under the back pay regulation, 5 C.F.R. § 550.805, and DVA's own regulation the status quo ante requires that the agency reimburse her for the health benefits she purchased during the back pay period because these constitute "pay, allowances and differentials" within the meaning of the regulations. The back pay regulation cited, however, merely provides that health benefits premiums will be deducted from back pay if coverage is retroactively reinstated at the employee's option. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(3)(iii). Similarly, the DVA regulation cited by the appellant in support of her position specifically states that "[i]f the employee elects to have the enrollment reinstated retroactively, withholdings and contributions must also be made." Here the appellant specifically elected to have her benefits retroactively reinstated, and deductions were made consistent with that election.

The appellant argues, however, that the agency's reimbursement of her out-of-pocket expenses for health insurance premiums incurred during the period of COBRA coverage, July 1997 through December 1998, demonstrates that reimbursement is required for the non-COBRA-covered period January 1999 through June 1999. As the administrative judge correctly found, however, the Board's final Order did not obligate the agency to reimburse the appellant for expenses incurred to maintain private health insurance. The fact that the agency was able to obtain reimbursement for the appellant from the National Finance Center for the COBRA health insurance premiums does not require a different result. There is simply no authority for awarding the appellant out-of-pocket expenses for the health insurance she purchased after separation. See Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 65 M.S.P.R. 137, 140 (1994) (Board lacks authority to order an agency to compensate an employee for the cost of maintaining extra health insurance during the period that he was removed). If the appellant wished to receive reimbursement of the premiums already paid, she could, as the administrative judge suggested, opt not to have her health benefits reinstated and receive the amount that would have been deducted from back pay for the health benefits. She would also receive interest on that additional pay.

Contrary to the appellant's assertion, Kennedy v. U.S. Postal Service, 42 M.S.P.R. 429 (1989), does not support her position. In Kennedy, the Board held that the agency was required to reinstate the appellant's health benefits retroactively, which the agency here has done. Similarly, Worsowicz v. Nashua Corp., 612 F. Supp. 310 (D. N.H. 1985), and Pedreyea v. Cornell Prescription Pharmacies, 465 F. Supp. 936 (D. Colo. 1979), cited by the appellant, are inapposite. Neither case was governed by the Back Pay Act, and neither case refers to a situation where, as here, an individual requested reinstatement of her medical benefits. Accordingly, the appellant has not shown that she is entitled to reimbursement for her out-of-pocket expenses for medical insurance....
