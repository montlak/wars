broida mspb Chapter 04: Evidence Chapter 04: Evidence, Subchapter 03: Substitutes for Direct Evidence, Section E: Res Judicata and Collateral Estoppel--The Doctrines Applied, Subsection 04: Successive MSPB Cases, Part a: Dismissal for Adjudicatory Efficiency
a. Dismissal for Adjudicatory Efficiency

What is the AJ to do when faced with successive appeals but no final decision? He should dismiss the second appeal on the basis of "adjudicatory efficiency," Holloway v. VA, 56 MSPR 422, 425 (1993):

We reopen this appeal...to address the administrative judge's incorrect application of the doctrine of res judicata. Under the doctrine, a final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action.... The doctrine of res judicata cannot be applied, though, where the action is not yet final, such as where a petition for review is pending before the Board.... Rather, such appeals should be dismissed in the interest of adjudicatory efficiency where an identity of issues exists and the controlling issues in the appeal will be determined by a prior appeal. Kinler v. GSA, 44 MSPR 262, 263 (1990).

Defining adjudicatory efficiency dismissals, under Carson v. OSC, AT-1221-15-0092-W-1 (NP 8/17/2015):

When an appellant files an appeal that raises the same claims raised in an earlier appeal before the decision in the earlier appeal has become final, the Board may dismiss the subsequent claims based upon adjudicatory efficiency. Bean v. U.S. Postal Service, 120 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶ 5 (2013); Zgonc v. Department of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 6 (2006) (same), aff'd, 230 F. App'x 967 (Fed. Cir. 2007). Appeals may be dismissed in the interest of adjudicatory efficiency where an identity of issues exists and the controlling issues in the appeal will be determined in a prior appeal. Kinler v. General Services Administration, 44 M.S.P.R. 262, 263 (1990).

See Watkins v. MSPB, 737 Fed. Appx. 996, 998 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) ("[W]e have determined in a past non-precedential opinion that when an appellant files an appeal with the Board that presents the same claims as an earlier appeal and the earlier appeal remains pending before the full Board, an administrative judge does not violate the law in dismissing the later appeal in the interest of adjudicatory efficiency. Boyd v. Dep't of Labor, 561 Fed. Appx. 978, 982 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (citing Zgonc v. Dep't of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 666, 669 (2006)). We have no reason to draw a different conclusion here."); Boyd v. Dept. of Labor, 561 Fed. Appx. 978, 982 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) ("The Board has often ruled that it is appropriate to dismiss a second appeal on the grounds of adjudicatory efficiency when it raises claims already decided in an initial decision in an earlier appeal. See Zgonc v. Dep't of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 666, 669 (2006). Thus, the only real question is whether the two appeals in fact raise the same claims."); Bean v. USPS, 120 MSPR 447, 449 ¶ 5, 2013 MSPB 98 (2013) ("the Board will dismiss on the basis of adjudicatory efficiency where an identity of issues exists and the controlling issues in the appeal will be determined in a prior appeal. Kinler v. General Services Administration, 44 MSPR 262, 263 (1990)"); Carson v. VA, AT-1221-11-0746-W-1 (NP 6/15/2012) ("when an appellant files an appeal that raises claims raised in an earlier appeal after the initial decision in the earlier appeal has been issued, but before the full Board has acted on the appellant's petition for review, it is appropriate to dismiss the subsequent appeal on the grounds of adjudicatory efficiency. See Zgonc v. Department of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 6 (2006), aff'd, 230 F. App'x 967 (Fed. Cir. 2007); McNeil v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 11 (2005); Holloway v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 56 M.S.P.R. 422, 425 (1993).").

The later claim dismissed for adjudicatory efficiency must be the same as the earlier claim, Castillejos v. OPM, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP):

...The government urges this court to affirm the dismissal of the case on grounds of adjudicatory efficiency because "[t]he Board has often ruled that it is appropriate to dismiss a second appeal on the grounds of adjudicatory efficiency when it raises claims already decided in an initial decision in an earlier appeal." Boyd v. Dep't of Lab., 561 F. App'x 978, 982 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (citing Zgonc, 103 M.S.P.R. at 669). See generally In re Vox Populi Registry Ltd., 25 F.4th 1348, 1352 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2022) (doctrine of administrative preclusion may preclude repetitive applications).

The problem with the government's argument and the Board's apparent conclusion that Mr. Castillejos is filing "serial applications seeking the same annuity based on the same period of Federal civilian service," is that Mr. Castillejos raises different claims in this case. Castillejos III involved only the period of employment between 1974 and 1986. Here, Mr. Castillejos additionally argues for benefits based on his employment between 1987 and 1992. Unlike in Boyd, these are facially different claims. Under these circumstances, we decline to affirm the Board's dismissal on grounds of adjudicatory efficiency.

Cf. Anderson v. DHHS, DC-0752-16-0845-I-1 (NP 3/24/2023) (citing Bean, supra: "To the extent that the appellant raised issues in her third appeal regarding the voluntariness of her separation from service pursuant to the settlement agreement, we find that the administrative judge should have declined to consider them anew in the interest of adjudicatory efficiency. We find no basis for considering this issue twice under different labels.").

At the PFR level, the Board may reach approximately the same result as an appeal dismissed by an AJ for "adjudicatory efficiency" by vacating an AJ's initial decision and remanding a related appeal involving essentially the same case for consideration along with the earlier appeal. Groom v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 221, 225 ¶ 10 (1999); cf. Peterson v. VA, PH-752S-14-0445-I-1 (NP 8/11/2014) (at the PFR level the Board dismissed an appeal when the appellant had another, apparently identical, appeal pending at the regional level that may have cured the jurisdictional deficiency leading to the AJ's dismissal of the earlier appeal); The Board may apply res judicata to terminate a second appeal that is the subject of a PFR when it was dismissed for reasons of adjudicatory efficiency after the underlying initial decision is affirmed during the pendency of both PFRs. See Zgonc v. DOD, 103 MSPR 666, 670, 2006 MSPB 315 (2006) (performance-based action with a whistleblowing defense followed by an IRA case based on the same removal and defense), aff'd, 208 Fed. Appx. 850 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP), reh'g den., 230 Fed. Appx. 967 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP). At the PFR level, a remand may become unnecessary if there is a pending appeal raising issues that would otherwise be subject to the remand. See Lentz v. Dept. of Interior, SF-1221-15-0688-W-1 (NP 6/30/2022) ("Courts have the inherent authority, in the interest of judicial efficiency, to dismiss an action because of the pendency of another action, so long as an identity of issues exists and the controlling issues in the dismissed action will be determined in another lawsuit. Kinler v. General Services Administration, 44 M.S.P.R. 262, 263 (1990). In most cases of judicial efficiency, we would dismiss the subsequent appeal and the prior appeal would be adjudicated. Id.; O'Leary v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 124, ¶ 7 (2001)."). If an initial decision dismissing for adjudicatory efficiency becomes final and the earlier, underlying appeal is later decided while on PFR, the initial decision may be modified to show that it is barred by res judicata. See Barnhart v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-15-0082-C-2 (NP 12/15/2022).
