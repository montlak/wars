g. PFR

The Board considered it unreasonable to request $115 per hour for 23 hours of time to respond to a PFR when the appeal consumed 76 hours at $50 per hour. The Board considered the 23 hours excessive: the time exceeded one-quarter of the time spent on the appeal. The Board allowed fees of $50 per hour for 16 hours. Arthur v. Dept. of Army, 20 MSPR 421, 422 (1984). In Smith v. Dept. of Labor, 20 MSPR 605, 607 (1984), a claim for 10 hours spent preparing the PFR was excessive; compensable time was reduced to six hours. The Board did not describe in Arthur the length of the PFR or the extent of the case record. In May v. Dept. of Transp., 28 MSPR 357, 363 (1985), involving a one-day hearing with testimony of three witnesses concerning coercion during the strike of the air traffic controllers, 82.5 hours charged for researching and preparing the PFR and 15.5 hours reviewing it were cut to 20 and five hours, as the maximum, reasonable hours. A claim was denied for 36 hours spent by counsel seeking permission from the Federal Circuit to file an amicus brief in related litigation. The request to file the brief was denied by the court as untimely; counsel did not cite authority allowing attorney fees for that work.

Time of counsel was compensable when it was devoted to researching and preparing a pleading related to the timeliness of a response to a PFR, Boltz v. SSA, DE-0752-08-0436-A-1 (NP 8/20/2010):

The agency also reasserts on review that the administrative judge should have excluded the 8.41 hours of time related to the appellant's motion to accept her response to the agency's petition for review of the initial decision as timely filed. Below, the appellant asserted, as good cause for accepting her 30-minute untimely filed pleading, that she had encountered technical difficulties in filing her response and attachments thereto through the Board's e-filing system, such that she ultimately had to file the pleading and attachments separately. Thus, the technical difficulties were not solely attributable to the appellant; rather, they were partly attributable to the Board's e-filing system. Despite the fact that the filing delay was minimal and that the Board's e-filing system had contributed to the delayed filing, the agency filed a 5-page objection to the appellant's motion. The record reflects that appellant's counsel then spent a total of 7.83 hours of time (2.50, 1.33, and 4.00 hours) researching the agency's stated objections and drafting a reply to those objects. As a result, of the 8.41 hours of time requested, .58 hours were necessitated in part by the Board's e-filing system and the remaining 7.83 hours were necessitated by the agency's objection to the motion. Under such circumstances, we see no error in the administrative judge's finding that the fee request was reasonable, and the agency has failed to show actual error in that finding.

In the author's experience with briefs before various courts and administrative agencies, record review, research, citation checking and updating, writing, rewriting, and proofreading of a double-spaced brief takes about an hour and a half or two hours per page, a figure some say is too low. It would be difficult to state fairly that the time reasonably invested in a PFR is directly related to the time invested in an appeal that may or may not have already required extensive research or briefing. Much of the time spent in the appeal is devoted to interviewing witnesses, discovery, preparing for the hearing, and the actual hearing. PFRs involve different uses of time. See Howard v. Dept. of Air Force, 673 Fed. Appx. 987, 990-91 (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP) ("Some documents are short because they contain little content; others are short because the author has taken the time and effort necessary to concisely convey the complex ideas therein. The Board has broad discretion to determine that an attorney of Mr. White's experience spent an unreasonable amount of time preparing a document, but the document's length, on its own, cannot be the Board's only explanation for doing so. There must be some nexus between the time spent preparing a document and the content of that document."). [Refer to earlier in this Chapter to "PFR."]
