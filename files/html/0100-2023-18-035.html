a. Reinitiation of a Proposal After an Adverse Action Is Canceled

An adverse action charge may be proposed, canceled, and again proposed without the case being dismissed for "double jeopardy." Reynolds v. United States, 454 F.2d 1368, 1375 (Ct. Cl. 1972) ("It is not unusual or wrongful for an agency to begin anew an adverse action based on charges which were previously brought when the initial action was invalidated on procedural grounds."); see Davis v. Dept. of Navy, No. 2011-3229 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP) ("Because Davis' original termination was rescinded on procedural grounds, Davis did not suffer two adverse personnel actions as a result of her improper use of a government credit card."); Archer v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-595); Foster v. Civil Serv. Comm'n, 369 F.2d 399 (5th Cir. 1966) ("The reversal was on technical grounds as distinguished from the merits of the discharge, and in no way barred the subsequent discharge."); Evans v. MSPB, 306 Fed. Appx. 587, 591 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) ("an invalidated suspension action, if invalidated on procedural grounds, does not bar an agency from taking a subsequent removal action, even though the two actions arose out of the same alleged misconduct. See Cox-Vaughn v. U.S. Postal Serv., 100 M.S.P.R. 246, 247, 254-55 (2005) (reversing suspension but affirming removal for the same conduct.); Reynolds v. United States, 454 F.2d 1368, 1374 (Ct. Cl. 1972) ('It is not unusual or wrongful for an agency to begin anew an adverse action based on charges which were previously brought when the initial action was invalidated on procedural grounds.'"); Callan v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 657 (1985) (citing Cabral v. DHHS, 7 MSPR 372, 374 (1981) (a proposed, but canceled, reprimand did not bar subsequent adverse action for vehicle misuse)); Dejoy v. DHHS, 2 MSPR 577, 580, 3 MSPB 96 (1980) (citing Stevenson v. United States, 155 Ct. Cl. 592 (1962), and Jenkins v. Macy, 357 F.2d 62 (8th Cir. 1966)); Andrews v. USPS, 9 MSPR 86, 87 n.1 (1981) (agency was not precluded from pursuing a removal action when it settled a related "indefinite emergency suspension" by canceling the suspension, giving the appellant back pay, and obtaining through the settlement agreement a provision that the agreement was "without prejudice to the position of the United States Postal Service in this or any other case regardless of forum").

If the Board reverses an action based on a procedural problem, the agency need not request the Board's permission to reinstitute the action. See Price v. Dept. of Treasury, 7 MSPR 72, 73 (1981) (a performance action reversed under Chapter 43 for lack of a proper regulatory basis could be reinstituted under Chapter 75 without permission from the Board); cf. Nelson v. VA, 27 MSPR 133, 135 n.2 (1985) (agency was not faulted for placing the appellant in nonduty with pay status pending a second removal on misconduct occurring after the reversal of a prior removal).

Jenkins v. Macy, 357 F.2d 62, 67 (8th Cir. 1966), explained the rule that an action may be canceled, due to procedural error, then reinstituted:

...Res judicata refers to the thing adjudicated. The adjudication at the time of both appellant's first and second removals from the government service herein was that he was guilty of the conduct charged. In other words, whether or not we grant appellant's contention, he is not benefitted since the facts never were adjudicated in his favor. His removal was postponed because of procedural fault, but this fault was corrected. Appellant, who in effect got a second chance, cannot now complain. Unlike the typical res judicata situation, appellant never prevailed on the merits. If anything, appellant's contention sounds more of "double jeopardy" rather than of res judicata. But the criminal concept of double jeopardy clearly is not applicable herein. At a minimum, appellant was not being punished twice for the same offense, for he never was punished after his first removal, having been fully reimbursed for his time lost on that occasion. This might be likened to the retrial of a criminal case where error in the first trial necessitates its being tried again. No double jeopardy results.

But see Blagaich v. Dept. of Transp., 90 MSPR 619 (2001) (implying that res judicata precludes the agency from taking action a second time based on the same charge that was held substantively invalid in a prior case decided by an arbitrator; action could proceed, however, when the causes of action differed); Davis v. VA, 69 MSPR 627, 630 (1996) (settlement agreement that resolved an adverse action impliedly barred a subsequent adverse action arising from the same incident when the agreement "resolved the issues raised in these appeals"). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Res Judicata."]

If an agency is to avoid a "double jeopardy" defense, cancelation of the earlier action must be complete; the appellant must be returned to the status quo. Mavronikolas v. USPS, 39 MSPR 442, 444-45 (1989) (once canceled, an "agency is not precluded from renewing an adverse action based on charges brought in an earlier proceeding where the adverse action in that proceeding was invalidated on procedural grounds"). If the agency fails to properly cancel one action, e.g., a suspension, and then removes the appellant for reasons substantially related to the acts predicating the suspension, the Board may direct the agency to cancel the removal and restore the appellant. See Storm v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 14, 20-21 (1994). Assuming, however, that an agency rescinds an adverse action that leads to a dismissal by the Board, the rescission is not invalid because the agency institutes a new removal action based on the same alleged misconduct that served as the basis for the first removal action. The appellant's remedy is to pursue an appeal of the second action. See Frias v. USPS, No. 93-3472 (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP). Nor is an agency precluded from using evidence gathered in a rescinded action as a basis for a second action. West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 560 (1990). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Mootness of Appeal" and "Cancelation of Action Appealed."]

In Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 232, 235 (1986), the appellant's removal for misconduct was proposed, but the agency deciding official reduced the removal to a 60-day suspension. Before the suspension was served, it was canceled, and in its place removal was again proposed and effectuated by a different deciding official. The Board saw nothing improper about reconstitution of the adverse action following further investigation into the charges resulting from arguments raised by the appellant in his answer to the proposed removal. Rose resulted in a reversal of the removal and restoration of the suspension because of the unexplained change in deciding officials and because no new evidence developed that justified enhancing a suspension first determined after a careful weighing of mitigating factors. The decision is discussed below concerning errors involving the reply officer's recommendation. See Middleton v. VA, 64 MSPR 294, 297 (1994) ("There is no intrinsic agency error...in its reinstitution of adverse action proceedings against an employee based on the same charges that were included in a prior, rescinded action."). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals."]

Just as an agency may cancel a prior proposal and replace it with one carrying another penalty, the agency may enhance a proposal for disciplinary action as a result of misconduct intervening between the original and a second proposal for adverse action. It is not harmful error for an agency to propose an adverse action, e.g., suspension, then cancel that action and take a more severe action, e.g., removal, against an employee who repeated his misconduct before the first proposal ripened into a decision. Lancaster v. Dept. of Army, 8 MSPR 3, 4, 7 MSPB 504 (1981). The agency properly suspended a decision on a proposed removal pending a one-year probationary period and fired the employee for absences that occurred after the imposition of the probation. Banks v. DLA, 29 MSPR 436, 439 (1985).

In Parker v. USPS, 46 MSPR 214, 219, 220 n.5 (1990), the agency removed the employee effective April 25 on the basis of a March 1 decision letter. On April 22, the agency initiated another removal action for different reasons. The removal was effective May 13. The employee appealed the April 25th removal, and then he appealed the removal effective May 13. The AJ initially dismissed the second appeal for lack of jurisdiction on the basis that the appeal in the first removal action had not yet been decided. When the initial appeal was decided in favor of the appellant, that decision became final and the agency advised the employee that he was being given back pay from April 25 through May 13. The employee's second appeal was reinstituted and the Board sustained the second removal with the observation: "[a]gencies are not precluded from taking legitimate personnel actions pending Board disposition or after Board reversal of other appealable actions so long as a valid reason exists for the later action." The agency properly restored the status quo; the employee was constructively restored to the agency rolls; and the May 13 removal was properly resurrected. Parker distinguished Marshall v. GPO, 39 MSPR 272, 276 (1988). In Marshall, the agency retroactively removed an employee after the Board reversed the agency's first removal action; the Board concluded that because of the retroactive nature of the second removal, it constituted noncompliance with the order reversing the first removal. In Parker, the second removal was effective immediately on the occurrence of the misconduct leading to the second removal. Cf. Wilkins v. USPS, 31 MSPR 579, 584 (1991) (discipline was reactivated when the appellant allegedly failed to meet expectations during a last chance agreement).
