A. FAILURE TO RESPOND TO APPEAL

Sanctions have (rarely) been occasionally imposed for failure to file a timely response to the appeal. Generally, however, a sanction is reserved for egregious misconduct. A short delay in filing the response to an appeal is not likely to result in a sanction without a showing of prejudice to the appellant. Hey v. USPS, 57 MSPR 443, 448-49 (1993) ("A party is not entitled to sanctions against an agency for the untimely filing of submissions, absent a showing of prejudice to such party."); see Osler v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 539, 542 (1994) (appeal response was a day late and the agency representative belatedly initiated settlement discussions: "a party is not entitled to sanctions against an agency for the untimely filing of a submission absent a showing of prejudice"); Wall v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 252, 255 (1984) (delay caused by foreign mail did not justify the imposition of sanctions on the agency for the late submission of an appeal response); cf. Matthews v. EPA, 18 MSPR 533, 535 (1984) (15-day extension for the agency to file a response was "quite minimal"; no prejudice was shown by appellant); Oliver v. USPS, 11 MSPR 541, 543 (1982) (although the agency offered no excuse for a delayed response to the appeal, the appellant suffered no harm and no sanctions were imposed).

But the Board decided that an appropriate sanction against an agency that repeatedly failed to provide an appeals response was exclusion of all of its evidence. In an adverse action case, this likely means that the appellant will prevail. The agency will fail to meet its burden of proof. See Henry v. USPS, 13 MSPR 19, 22 (1982); Chappell v. SSA, 12 MSPR 633, 635 (1982) (AJ appropriately sanctioned the agency by refusing to consider its reconsideration file in a step increase denial appeal when the agency failed to meet deadlines for submitting the material and when the agency provided no explanation when the file was submitted beyond the deadline); Auerhahn v. EEOC, 9 MSPR 437, 438 (1982) ("The presiding official rejected the agency's entire submission as a sanction for its untimeliness. Accordingly, the Board finds that the agency failed to meet its burden of proof. Hence, the RIF action was properly reversed.").

Early Board decisions gave little credence to agency claims of internal delays or misrouting of Board correspondence when affirming dismissal of an adverse action following the imposition of the sanction of exclusion of all agency evidence (including the belated appeal response). Lynn v. DHHS, 14 MSPR 217, 220 (1982) (poor agency work planning; failure to request a sufficient extension from judge); Harris v. VA, 14 MSPR 213, 216 (1982) ("the agency...repeatedly failed to act diligently and in a reasonable manner to assure compliance with" service and timeliness requirements). Nor did protests about inexperience of agency representatives gain sympathy. One decision imposed sanctions because the agency failed to respond to three Board orders concerning filing of an appeal response. The agency blamed the delay on its representative. Irrelevant was evidence of criminal conduct by the employee who won the appeal after the belated response was stricken from the record. Stanley v. USPS, 19 MSPR 86, 87 (1984). As to an agency that unreasonably failed to respond to notice from the regional Board office that an appeal was filed, the AJ could sanction the agency by reversing the appealed RIFs. Johnson v. District of Columbia Bd. of Educ., 7 MSPR 652, 654, 7 MSPB 481 (1981). Because an agency filed its response four days after an extension for the filing expired, the AJ correctly struck the untimely response and reversed an unacceptable performance action for lack of evidence. Morin v. Dept. of Army, 18 MSPR 642, 645 (1984). But a delay was excused for "administrative confusion at the agency due to lack of familiarity with Board procedures." Beamer v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 281, 283 (1986).

Minor infractions will not likely result in sanctions. Although an agency filed a late response to an appeal, the Board did not require sanctions because there was no evidence of repeated violations of Board rules and no prejudice to the appellant. Lawrence v. District of Columbia Gov't, 4 MSPR 300, 302, 4 MSPB 370 (1980). An AJ did not err by failing to sanction the late appeal response presented by a small post office. There was a misunderstanding concerning its role in the appeal and no intent to avoid compliance. Schaules v. USPS, 19 MSPR 390, 392 (1984). When an agency response to an appeal was late by 15 days, no sanction was imposed. The appellant was not denied the right to a hearing or an appeal. A single delay in an agency response was not a failure to prosecute warranting a dismissal of the adverse action. Conner v. USPS, 5 MSPR 131, 133, 5 MSPB 194 (1981), followed by Reinert v. Dept. of Interior, 22 MSPR 600, 601 (1984). Sanctions are inappropriate if an agency cannot produce documentation called for by an acknowledgment order, and the agency explains that the documents cannot be located despite its best efforts to find them. The failure to comply fully with the order is not a result of negligence, bad faith, or lack of due diligence. Smith v. USPS, 54 MSPR 631, 635 (1992).
