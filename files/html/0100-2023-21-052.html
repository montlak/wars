13. Application After Employee's Death

Lazarski v. OPM, 58 MSPR 475, 478-79 (1993), declined to require OPM to process a disability retirement application filed by the widow on behalf of the employee after his death:

The appellant bears the burden of establishing her entitlement to a survivor annuity. See Cheeseman v. Office of Personnel Management, 791 F.2d 138, 140-41 (Fed. Cir. 1986), cert. denied, 479 U.S. 1037 (1987). As noted above, the appellant's claim to such entitlement rests on the assertion that she is the surviving spouse of a former Federal employee who was entitled to disability retirement benefits before his death. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8337(a), 8341(e)(2). Under relevant statutes, an application must be made to OPM in order to establish entitlement to disability retirement benefits. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8337(a), 8345(i)(1). The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has stated in this regard that the right to file an application for retirement benefits is personal to the employee alone and that an application for disability retirement benefits on that employee's behalf cannot first be filed after his death. See Davis v. Office of Personnel Management, 938 F.2d 1283, 1285 (Fed. Cir. 1991).

The appellant argues that her situation falls under an exception to the personal application rule provided in Davis. The appellant misinterprets Davis.[2] In Davis, OPM advanced its view that, even where an agency may be obligated to file on behalf of an employee, the employee's death ends the possible filing period. See id. The court accepted that view. Id. Therefore, even if the former employing agency had an obligation to file an application for Mr. Lazarski because he involuntarily resigned from his position, that obligation ended when he died.

[2] The appellant quotes Davis v. Office of Personnel Management, 938 F.2d 1283 (Fed. Cir. 1991), out of context when she asserts that the court established an exception to the personal application rule "where an agency must file on the employee's behalf. In fact, in the remainder of that sentence, the court noted and approved OPM's view that "an application for such annuity cannot be filed in any event after the death of the employee." Davis, 938 F.2d at 1285. Similarly, the appellant misstates the exception established in Oshiver v. Office of Personnel Management, 896 F.2d 540 (Fed. Cir. 1990). In that case, as further explained by the court in Davis, the court reserved the question of whether a retirement application could be filed by a guardian or trustee on behalf of an employee who was declared a missing person. "This court in Oshiver did not suggest that a disability annuity may be filed on behalf of a person who is deceased." Davis, 938 F.2d at 1285.

See Teng v. OPM, 85 MSPR 176, 179 ¶ 7 (2000) ("The right to file for a retirement annuity is personal to the annuitant and an application may not be filed on behalf of the annuitant after his death. See Davis v. Office of Personnel Management, 938 F.2d 1283, 1285 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (administratix cannot file a posthumous application for disability retirement benefits on behalf of deceased spouse); Davis v. Office of Personnel Management, 918 F.2d 944, 946 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (representative of survivor not entitled to annuity where survivor did not apply during her lifetime); and Oshiver v. Office of Personnel Management, 896 F.2d 540, 541-42 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (spouse of "missing" employee cannot file for a retirement annuity on behalf of the employee)."); cf. Murray v. OPM, NY-844E-21-0093-I-1 (NP 4/25/2023) ("[T]he Board has ordered OPM to grant disability retirement benefits to deceased applicants. See, e.g., Widmer v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 363, ¶¶ 7, 20 (2006); Thomas v. Office of Personnel Management, 54 M.S.P.R. 686, 688, 691 (1992). Nevertheless, in both Widmer and Thomas, the deceased applicants were separated from their respective agencies before their deaths, opening the possibility for them to qualify as 'retirees' or 'annuitants' entitled to disability retirement annuities. These cases thus do not require that OPM adjudicate the decedent's disability retirement application as, as noted, he was still an employee at the time of his death.").
