d. Individual Serving as Conduit of Information; Internal Disclosure

The WPA protections extend to individuals who serve as conduits of information because their jobs require them to pass allegations along to the agency IG, Shively v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 531, 537 (1993):

The legislative history of the WPA "stress[es] that any disclosure is protected (if it meets the requisite reasonable belief test and is not required to be kept confidential)." S. Rep. No. 413, 100th Cong., 2d Sess. 13 (1988) (emphasis in original). It also specifically indicates that "it is inappropriate for disclosures to be protected only if...the employee is the first to raise the issue." Id. The statutory language and legislative history thus indicate that the reasonable belief test is the only relevant inquiry in determining whether a particular disclosure is protected. In addition, finding that an employee's disclosure is unprotected because he is acting as a conduit for the whistleblowing disclosures of another employee would run counter to Congress's intent that disclosures of wrongdoing are to be encouraged. See S. Rep. No. 413 at 13. We therefore hold that, even if an employee is a "conduit" for the whistleblowing disclosures of another employee, his disclosures are protected under section 2302(b)(8) as long as he has a reasonable belief that the information disclosed evidences one or more of the types of wrongdoing specified in the statute.

The appellant here repeatedly alleged in his petition for appeal and responses to the administrative judge's jurisdictional order that he believed the information he disclosed to the IG evidenced illegality, mismanagement, and waste of funds. The agency has not disputed these allegations. To the contrary, it has stated that, based on the disclosures in question and a resulting investigation by the Army Criminal Investigation Command (CID), two agency employees were "titled," i.e., CID made a determination that there was probable cause to believe that a criminal offense had been committed. Accordingly, we find that the administrative judge erred in finding that the "secondary" nature of the appellant's disclosures to the IG rendered them unprotected under 5 USC 2302(b)(8).[7]

[7] Whether the appellant was "merely" a conduit for the whistleblowing allegations of another employee may be relevant to whether the appellant can prove by preponderant evidence that the disclosures were a contributing factor in the reassignment and, if so, whether the agency can prove by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action absent the protected disclosures. See Clark v. Department of the Army, 997 F.2d 1466, 1472-73 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (the administrative judge should consider all relevant and competent evidence in deciding both issues), petition for cert. filed, 62 U.S.L.W. 3275 (U.S. Sept. 29, 1993). We note in this regard that, even under the agency's version of events, the appellant did more than pass on information provided by another employee. The agency stated that, after meeting with the IG's investigators regarding the subordinate's allegations, the appellant met with the corporate president of the contractor involved in the allegations, and that, based on the information provided by this individual, the appellant again met with the IG investigator.

The "protected conduit" path has its limitations. A letter to prospective witnesses in a grievance action warning that the witnesses might be subject to harassment did not constitute a protected disclosure under 5 USC 1221(e)(1). Kidd v. Dept. of Interior (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 91-3546); cf. Mount v. DHS, 937 F.3d 37 (1st Cir. 2019) (considering a claim that an employee assisted a whistleblower by passing along information that helped the recipient to pursue his own whistleblower claim; not explicitly considering the "conduit" approach), supra. Disclosures to supervisors or others who would not be expected to act on the disclosures will not likely receive WPA protection. See Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 397, 402-03 (1994), aff'd, Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 66 F.3d 279 (Fed. Cir. 1995).
