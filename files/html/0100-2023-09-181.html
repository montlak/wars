A. ESSENTIAL INFORMATION

5 CFR 1201.24 (2022) states the essential information to be provided by an appeal. The regulation also explains some matters collateral to initiation of the appeal:

(a) Content. Only an appellant, his or her designated representative, or a party properly substituted under § 1201.35 may file an appeal. Appeals may be in any format, including letter form. An appeal may be filed in electronic form provided that the requirements of § 1201.14 have been satisfied. All appeals must contain the following:

(1) The name, address, and telephone number of the appellant, and the name and address of the agency that took the action;

(2) A description of the action the agency took and its effective date;

(3) A request for hearing if the appellant wants one;

(4) A statement of the reasons why the appellant believes the agency action is wrong;

(5) A statement of the action the appellant would like the judge to order;

(6) The name, address, and telephone number of the appellant's representative, if the appellant has a representative;

(7) Where applicable, a copy of the notice of proposed action, the agency decision being appealed and, if available, the SF-50 or similar notice of personnel action. No other attachments should be included with the appeal, as the agency will be submitting the documents required by 1201.25 of this part, and there will be several opportunities to submit evidence and argument after the appeal is filed. An appellant should not miss the deadline for filing merely because he or she does not currently have all of the documents specified in this section.

(8) A statement telling whether the appellant or anyone acting on his or her behalf has filed a grievance or a formal discrimination complaint with any agency regarding this matter; and

(9) The signature of the appellant or, if the appellant has a representative, of the representative. If the appeal is electronically filed, compliance with § 1201.14 and the directions at the Board's e-Appeal site (https://e-appeal.mspb.gov) satisfy the signature requirement.

(b) An appellant may raise a claim or defense not included in the appeal at any time before the end of the conference(s) held to define the issues in the case. An appellant may not raise a new claim or defense after that time, except for good cause shown. However, a claim or defense not included in the appeal may be excluded if a party shows that including it would result in undue prejudice.

(c) Use of Board form or electronic filing. An appellant may comply with paragraph (a) of this section, and with § 1201.31, by completing MSPB Form 185, or by completing all requests for information marked as required at the e-Appeal site (https://e-appeal.mspb.gov). MSPB Form 185 can be accessed at the Board's website (www.mspb.gov).

(d) Right to a hearing. An appellant generally has a right to a hearing on the merits if the appeal has been timely filed and the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal.

Under 5 U.S.C. 7701, an appellant has a right to a hearing.

(e) Timely request. The appellant must submit any request for a hearing with the appeal, or within any other time period the judge sets for that purpose. If the appellant does not make a timely request for a hearing, the right to a hearing is waived.

For appeals raising discrimination issues, 5 CFR 1201.153 (2022) requires that the appeal state that there was discrimination as to the matter appealed; state how the appellant was discriminated against; and state if the appellant has filed a discrimination complaint or grievance with his employer or any other agency, the date of filing, and any action taken by the agency in response to the complaint or grievance:

(a) Contents. An appeal raising issues of prohibited discrimination must comply with § 1201.24 of this part, with the following exceptions:

(1) The appeal must state that there was discrimination in connection with the matter appealed, and it must state specifically how the agency discriminated against the appellant; and

(2) The appeal must state whether the appellant has filed a grievance under a negotiated grievance procedure or a formal discrimination complaint with any agency regarding the matter being appealed to the Board. If he or she has done so, the appeal must state the date on which the appellant filed the complaint or grievance, and it must describe any action that the agency took in response to the complaint or grievance.

(b) Use of Board form or Internet filing option. An appellant may comply with paragraph (a) of this section by completing MSPB Form 185, or by completing all requests for information marked as required at the e-Appeal site (https://e-appeal.mspb.gov). MSPB Form 185 can be accessed at the Board's website (http://www.mspb.gov).

The regulatory requirements should be readily met when appeals are filed through the e-filing process, involving a template designed to elicit necessary information from filers. Appeals submissions made by paper, through mail, fax, commercial or personal delivery to the Board, should conform to the regulatory requirements. Although technical requirements may be waived, there must be a reasonable attempt to comply with Board regulations and to provide the information required for an appeal. One appeal that consisted of nothing more than a hearing demand was rejected. Neither the agency nor the action appealed was identified. Peoples v. VA, 30 MSPR 164, 165 (1986). The Board suggested in Highley v. USPS, 78 MSPR 63, 65 (1998), that a representative's letter to a Board regional office requesting it to "kindly consider this Letter as my client's Appeal Notice" did not serve as an appeal under 5 CFR 1201.24(a)(1-5). When technical errors are made, Board Order 1050.10 (Oct. 27, 1988) requires that the appeal be returned to the appellant with a statement noting the deficiency and requesting correction and return of the appeal to the Board within 15 days (or 25 days for overseas cases). But a submission to the Board need not be elaborate to constitute an appeal, DeVaughn v. Dept. of Commerce, CH-0752-14-0040-I-1 (NP 7/17/2014):

[7] In arguing that she did not previously file her appeal, the appellant relies on the fact that she sent a "letter of inquiry" to the Board. She alleges that this letter "did not contain any of the elements that the MSPB states must be contained in an appeal." We disagree. The necessary elements of an appeal are relatively simple, and include such items as the appellant's name, address, and telephone number; a description of the action at issue; the appellant's contentions as to why the agency's action was wrong; etc. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.24(a). The appellant's letter and its attachments were sufficient to satisfy these criteria.

The appeal is considered to be filed on the date it was originally filed, not the date it is refiled after having been rejected. If it is not timely refiled, the appellant will be directed to provide evidence and argument demonstrating that the submission was timely or that good cause existed for the delay. The Federal Circuit affirmed dismissal of an appeal that was not timely corrected. The court determined that if an appeal is technically deficient because it fails to provide the Board an extra copy of attachments or it does not provide the name and address of the agency taking the action, or if the appeal is based on an IRA and does not clearly identify the nature and date of the alleged retaliation, the AJ may require the appellant to correct or refile the appeal within a set time. If the corrections are not timely made, or if any delay in making the corrections is not excused, the AJ may dismiss the refiled appeal. See Chafin v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3347). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Irregular and Misdirected Filings," for discussion concerning docketing dates assigned to appeals received without signatures or misdirected to incorrect Board offices.]

In the ordinary adverse action or performance-based action appeal, the appellant will attach a copy of the adverse action decision to the appeal. But an agency may effect an appealable disciplinary action without issuing the written decision letter required by 5 USC 7513(b)(4). The agency's failure to issue a written decision is a procedural error, but it does not deprive the Board of jurisdiction. Aldridge v. USDA, 110 MSPR 21, 24-25 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 209 (2008) (still determining that the agency did not issue a removal decision or effect the appellant's removal).

Before 2012, Board regulations provided the option, at 5 CFR 1201.24(a)(7) (2011), for attachment of relevant documentation. This was an important, yet often neglected, right. The appellant (and representative) could attach anything they wanted to their appeal. Agencies routinely file complex and comprehensive appeal responses replete with documentation. Appellants rarely fully documented their appeals. If there was likely to be an issue of jurisdiction or timeliness of the appeal, an affidavit or sworn declaration on the issue and attached to the appeal provided the AJ an evidentiary basis for accepting the appeal. For example, if the appellant was fired as a probationer but the appellant had prior federal service that would establish an appeal right, the information on that prior service could be detailed, with attached personnel documents, in a sworn declaration filed with the appeal that immediately furnished its jurisdictional basis.

The broad right to attach relevant documentation to the appeal was eliminated in the 2012 revisions to the Board's regulations. That was unfortunate from the perspective of the appellant, although the Board maintains that there is no detriment because the appellant may supply documentation in response to agency motions, show cause orders from the AJ, or with the prehearing submission. There are two good reasons supporting earlier submission of documentation. First, the AJ will be better educated about the case before the hearing; second, the absolute right to submit documentation with the appeal avoids all sorts of evidentiary objections, use of exhibit lists, and other problems that may arise if documents are first identified at the prehearing stage for use at the hearing. True, the right of inclusion can be abused. But the AJ can regulate the abuse by striking material that clearly has no place in the record. The 2012 regulations do not, strictly speaking, prohibit the submission of material supplementing the appeal; they do not openly permit it. If some document is of such significance that it should be filed prior to the prehearing submission, a party may file it, with an explanation of its significance and then respond to a motion to strike the material or respond or object to an order from the AJ disallowing the submission.
