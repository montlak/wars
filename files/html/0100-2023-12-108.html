a. Retirement Appeals

Steele v. OPM, 57 MSPR 458, 461-64 (1993), determined that retirement cases should not be excluded from the interim relief provisions, but that "caution should be exercised in awarding interim relief in retirement appeals":

The Board has found that the interim-relief provisions of Section 6 of the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989 (WPA), 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(A), are not applicable to appeals involving retirement rights under either the Civil Service Retirement System or the Federal Employees Retirement System. See Cassity v. OPM, 55 MSPR 25, 27-28 (1992). In Cassity, we concluded that the provision for interim relief in 5 USC 7701(b)(2) was limited to employees or applicants for employment and that retirement annuitants fit neither category; we also stated that the interim relief provision contemplated the restoration of an employee or applicant to the agency's rolls and that such restoration had no relevance in retirement appeals. See id. We now reconsider and overrule our holding in Cassity.

The issue of whether retirement annuitants are entitled to the same rights as employees or applicants for employment under Chapter 77 was extensively litigated before the Board and the courts under the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (CSRA). The Board has found that all CSRA appeals, including disability retirement cases, are covered by section 7701 and that appellants in disability retirement cases are therefore entitled to a de novo hearing before the Board under section 7701(a). See Chavez v. OPM, 6 MSPB 343, 6 MSPR 404, 411-13 (1981). In another case, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit rejected OPM's argument that a former Federal employee seeking legal retirement benefits was not an "employee" for the purpose of appealing to the Board and the court under, respectively, sections 7701(a) and 7703(a). See Bronger v. OPM, 740 F.2d 1552, 1555-56 (Fed. Cir. 1984), rev'd on other grounds, 769 F.2d 756 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (en banc), cert. denied, 474 U.S. 1101, 106 S. Ct. 882, 88 L. Ed.2d 918 (1986). And, the court later found that section 7701 applied to both legal retirement and disability retirement cases in concluding that the attorney fee provision in section 7701(g) covered retirement appeals as well as adverse action cases. See Simmons v. MSPB, 768 F.2d 323, 326-27 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

Taken together, the decisions in Chavez, Bronger, and Simmons indicate that the term "employee" in sections 7701(a), (g) and 7703(a) may not be read to exclude retirement annuitants. When Congress enacted the WPA and amended the CSRA provision at section 7701(b) to provide interim relief to a prevailing "employee or applicant," it must be presumed to have known that the same phrase had been interpreted under the CSRA as including retirement annuitants.... Nothing in the amended section 7701(b) or the legislative history of the WPA reveals that Congress intended to overrule this prior interpretation. See 5 USC 7701(b); H. Rep. 100-274, 100th Congress, 1st Sess. (1987); S. Rep. 100-413, 100th Congress, 2d Sess. (1988). Therefore, it appears that a retirement annuitant must be considered an "employee" for the purpose of the interim relief provision in section 7701(b).

Our holding in Cassity was also based on the fact that the amended section 7701(b) and its legislative history addressed adverse action appeals and did not mention retirement appeals.... In Vergagni v. OPM, 11 MSPB 125, 12 MSPR 494, 496-97 (1982), overruled by Stephens v. OPM, 33 MSPR 292, 302 (1987), the Board provided a similar rationale for excluding disability retirement annuitants from the coverage of the attorney fee provision in section 7701(g)(1); it relied on the fact that the legislative history of the section's interest-of-justice requirement mentioned adverse action cases but not retirement cases. As noted above, the court in Simmons held that section 7701(g)(1) did apply to retirement appeals, basing this conclusion in part on the fact that there was "no exclusion, in the text of the subsection or its legislative history, of any type or class of case." See Simmons, 768 F.2d at 326. The court's reasoning thus depended on whether retirement appeals were specifically excluded from the statute, not on whether they were specifically included. Because neither the text of section 7701(b) nor its legislative history excludes retirement cases from the coverage of the interim relief provision, the reasoning in Simmons indicates that such cases are covered by the provision. See id.; H. Rep. 100-274, 100th Congress, 1st Sess. (1987); S. Rep. 100-413, 100th Congress, 2d Sess. (1988). Therefore, for the reasons stated above, we overrule our prior holding in Cassity and conclude that the Board may award interim relief in retirement appeals.

Nothing in the above analysis, however, suggests that the Board is precluded from finding that interim relief is not appropriate in a particular retirement case or class of retirement cases. The statute makes clear that the decision whether or not to grant interim relief is an exercise of discretion. See 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(A). Further, the Board has concluded that interim relief may not be appropriate in all cases in which the appellant prevails, and that the administrative judge must make a determination on this issue based on the facts of the particular case, balancing the benefits and burdens to the parties anticipated by the process of effecting the interim relief order. See Ginocchi v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 62, 67 n.3 (1992).

We find that the administrative judge erred here by granting interim relief to the appellant. In adverse action cases, typically, an appellant granted interim relief by the Board is restored to the agency's rolls and often returns to work; in any event, even if the appellant eventually loses his appeal, his award of interim relief is not deemed an expenditure of funds not permitted by law, and the agency is not forced to collect the monies paid to him during the period of interim relief. On the other hand, in retirement appeals, a Board award of interim relief to an appellant would typically force OPM to grant benefits that it believed were not permitted by law, violating its statutory authority. See OPM v. Richmond, 496 U.S. 414, 416, 434, 110 S. Ct. 2465, 2467, 2476, 110 L. Ed.2d 387 (1990) (OPM may not grant benefits not permitted by law). And, if the appellant lost his appeal, OPM would find it necessary to collect the monies paid to the appellant during the interim relief period, because those monies would be benefits that the Board had ultimately found not to be authorized by the retirement statutes. These considerations suggest that caution should be exercised in awarding interim relief in retirement appeals.

Here, the appellant already receives a reduced monthly survivor annuity from OPM and thus is not dependent on an interim relief award as her sole source of monies from OPM. And, granting interim relief here as a prospective award of increased monthly annuity benefits would entail some administrative costs to OPM in terms of effecting a change in the level of benefits; if the appellant loses the appeal, as we noted, OPM would bear more costs in order to collect the additional payments. After balancing the considerations stated above, we find that an award of interim relief was not appropriate in this appeal, and we therefore vacate the administrative judge's interim relief order....
