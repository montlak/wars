E. TIMELINESS OF APPEALS

The Board's dismisses untimely appeals unless the delay is excused for good reason, 5 CFR 1201.23(c) (2022):

Timeliness of appeals. If a party does not submit an appeal within the time set by statute, regulation, or order of a judge, it will be dismissed as untimely filed unless a good reason for the delay is shown. The judge will provide the party an opportunity to show why the appeal should not be dismissed as untimely.

A lawsuit for negligence is typically filed within two or three years after the event. For contract violations, the limitation is often up to six years--the same limit for some claims under the Back Pay Act. For an unfair labor practice charge, the time allowed for filing is six months. An IRA appeal must be filed no later than 65 days after OSC issues its notice that it is taking no action on the complaint. See Koury v. DOD, 84 MSPR 219, 223 ¶ 6 (1999) (noting that if the OSC notice is received more than five days after its issuance, the appellant may appeal within 60 days after receipt of the notice). A complainant with an EEO charge has 45 days to contact an EEO counselor. If a federal worker loses his job or fails to secure retirement benefits through OPM, he must appeal within 30 days. The Reform Act does not impose the restriction; rather, it is imposed by MSPB through regulation as a means of case management. The more employees who miss deadlines, the fewer cases the Board need decide on the merits. The 30-day appeal period is short indeed. [Refer to Chapter 13, "Time for Initiating IRA Appeal With OSC and MSPB" and earlier in this chapter, "Individual Right of Action Appeals."]

As the Supreme Court noted in Burnett v. Grattan, 104 S. Ct. 2924 (1984), the initiation of a case is a complex undertaking. Speaking of a civil rights claim, the Court made these observations: an injured person must recognize the legal dimensions of his injury; he must obtain counsel or prepare to represent himself; he must conduct enough investigation to draft pleadings that meet the requirements of governing rules; he must file those pleadings; and he must still look ahead to the responsibilities, e.g., conduct of discovery, that immediately follow initiation of the case. Even the Board, in a nonprecedential moment of candor, exclaimed that "modern Board law, with its complex body of precedent and many areas of jurisdiction, presents enormously difficult legal and factual issues in a niche area that few attorneys are well-equipped to handle." Tanner v. DOD, DC-0752-12-0209-A-1 (NP 8/1/2014). The Board failed to recognize the acumen of users (particularly purchasers) of this Guide. Complexities involving the initiation of litigation aside, the Federal Circuit admonished that "an agency suffers prejudice when a delay hampers its ability to adequately and effectively defend its action because of the untimely filing." Santiago v. MSPB, 281 Fed. Appx. 981, 982 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP).

Considering the relative complexity of Board rules, patterned as they are after the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (governing litigation by skilled advocates in federal courts), it is not surprising that some Board appeals are filed late.

The Board's general policy concerning late appeals was summed up, Lourie v. USPS, 82 MSPR 119, 121-22 ¶ 5 (1999):

Generally, an appeal must be filed during the period beginning on the day after the effective date of the action being appealed and ending 30 days after the effective date. See 5 CFR § 1201.22. The Board may waive its regulatory filing time limit for good cause shown. See 5 CFR § 1201.12. To establish good cause for the untimely filing of an appeal, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980). To determine whether an appellant has shown good cause, the Board will consider the length of the delay, the reasonableness of his excuse and his showing of due diligence, whether he is proceeding pro se, and whether he has presented evidence of the existence of circumstances beyond his control that affected his ability to comply with the time limits or of unavoidable casualty or misfortune which similarly shows a causal relationship to his inability to timely file his petition. Moorman v. Department of the Army, 68 MSPR 60, 62-63 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167, 1996 WL 102697 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). If the appellant gives a reasonable excuse for the delay, it should be accepted by the administrative judge, absent a showing of substantial prejudice to the agency caused by the delay in filing. See Alonzo, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR at 184.

The Board's historically strict policy against acceptance of untimely appeals has slightly mellowed over the years. If the delay is short, e.g., a couple of days, the delay, if there is some explanation from the appellant, tends to be excused, particularly if the appellant is pro se. See Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1994), post. If the appellant is represented, gross neglect or outright obstruction by counsel or his staff that results in a delayed appeal may be excused if the appellant closely monitored his case and immediately steps in to file an appeal as soon as his representative's failings are discovered. See Dunbar v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 640, 643 (1990), post. In retirement appeals, where the benefit is vested and there is no prejudice to the respondent through a delayed appeal, short delays are being excused. See Herring v. MSPB, 778 F.3d 1011 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (Judge Reyna dissenting) (excusing belated disability retirement based in part on the nature of the appeal and in part on the appellant's reasonable reliance on her attorney to get the appeal filed on time). [Herring is discussed in this chapter, "Delay Excused; Counsel's Delay Balanced Against Appellant's Best Efforts; Retirement Cases."] ; Edney v. OPM, 79 MSPR 60, 63 ¶ 7 (1998), post. Finally, "the Board encourages a liberal interpretation in order to minimize the number of appeals which are rejected as untimely." Fye v. USPS, 3 MSPR 315, 317 (1980) (determining whether correspondence from the appellant constituted an appeal). Those mitigating comments aside, a lot of appeals continue to be rejected because they are late by a day or more--particularly if no reasonable excuse is offered for the delay.

Although the Board may deem a timeliness objection waived if it is not raised directly in response to an appeal, e.g., McDuffie v. OPM, 26 MSPR 106, 107 n.* (1985), the Board may also determine that an appeal is late without a timeliness objection from the respondent. The lack of objection to the appellant's belated appeal does not establish good cause for waiving the regulatory deadline. See Lifschitz v. DLA, 48 MSPR 487, 492-93 (1991) ("[A]lthough Colonel Cashman stated that his command would not object to the Board's waiving its time limit, the Board, in the interest of judicial efficiency and fairness, will not waive its timeliness requirements in the absence of good cause shown [.] "). Hapgood v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3336), affirmed dismissal of an appeal as untimely with the observation:

Hapgood contends that OPM waived the timeliness issue when it failed to raise the issue in its response. As stated in 5 CFR 1201.22(c)(1), it is the appellant's responsibility, when an appeal is filed out of time, to present evidence and argument with the petition for appeal showing why the time limit should be waived. Since Hapgood failed to do this, 5 CFR 1201.22(c)(2) indicates that the AJ can raise the issue and require Hapgood to show cause why the appeal should not be dismissed. There is no requirement in 1201.22(c)(2) that OPM raise the timeliness issue before the AJ can consider it. Therefore, the AJ did not err in raising the timeliness issue sua sponte.

Cf. Thomas v. OPM, AT-0831-15-0226-I-1 (NP 9/28/2015) ("Although the appellant filed the appeal outside of the 30-day time limit for appealing reconsideration decisions to the Board, OPM did not challenge the timeliness of the appellant's Board appeal or his contention that he did not receive a copy of the reconsideration decision until December 2014. The administrative judge did not make a specific finding as to the timeliness of the appellant's Board appeal. As the agency has not challenged the timeliness of the appeal either below or on review, and this matter can be decided on the merits, we discern no need to address the timeliness issue. See Ferrin-Rodgers v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 140, ¶ 7 (2010).").

The Federal Circuit has not been consistent in its views of how the Board should handle issues of timeliness, Wallace v. MSPB, 728 F.2d 1456, 1458 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 1984):

Since the time for filing an appeal to the MSPB is fixed only by regulation which provides for waiver, timeliness is not appropriately viewed as a matter of jurisdiction, and we question the apparent practice of the board in raising the issue sua sponte. The failure of the agency to raise the issue may be intentional, as it apparently was in this case.

The Federal Circuit's more recent view is that Board AJs may raise timeliness without prompting by the parties. See Hamilton v. MSPB, 75 F.3d 639, 645 (Fed. Cir. 1996).

Although the Board announced no firm rule on the issue, it deferred a timeliness ruling to resolution of whether an earlier appeal was res judicata as to the appeal later challenged as untimely. See Corbin v. Dept. of Labor, 43 MSPR 318, 321 (1990).
