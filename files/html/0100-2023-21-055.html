16. Evidence of Disability

The scope of evidence considered by the Board in a disability retirement appeal is encompassing, Ogden v. OPM, PH-844E-18-0305-I-1 (NP 1/27/2023):

In determining an appellant's entitlement to disability retirement, the Board will consider all pertinent evidence, including objective clinical findings, diagnoses and medical opinions, subjective evidence of pain and disability, and evidence relating to the effect of the appellant's condition on his ability to perform the duties of his position. Dunn v. Office of Personnel Management, 60 M.S.P.R. 426, 432 (1994).

The approval of an application for disability retirement is based on evidence that an individual being unable to perform specific duties of a position. Robinson v. OPM, 21 MSPR 744, 746 (1984); see Confer v. OPM, 111 MSPR 419, 429 ¶ 27, 2009 MSPB 102 (2009) ("An individual can prove that a medical condition is incompatible with useful and efficient service by showing that the condition is inconsistent with performing assigned duties. See Thieman, 78 M.S.P.R. at 120-21 (chronic depression, which negatively affected concentration, memory and judgment, was incompatible with useful and efficient service or retention in the position of medical officer); Gometz, 69 M.S.P.R. at 121 (pinched nerve affecting both hands was incompatible with working as a mechanic)."); McDonald v. OPM, 50 MSPR 164, 169 (1991) (removal for inability to perform duties is supporting evidence, as is the testimony of the appellant's wife, who describes, with reference to a disability, her husband's inability to perform household tasks); Sansom v. OPM, 8 MSPR 185 (1981) (evaluating medical evidence showing lack of improvement). One factor to be considered is whether the appellant's continued performance is a threat to himself or others. See Moore v. OPM, 57 MSPR 318, 321 (1993) ("We find that the administrative judge did not consider the risks involved in the appellant's continued performance of his duties and that such risks render the appellant incapable of performing useful and efficient service. The Board may consider whether the appellant is a danger to himself or others in determining whether the appellant is able to render useful and efficient service."); cf. Dudar v. OPM, 93 MSPR 292, 296 ¶ 9 (2003) ("an appellant may establish entitlement to a disability retirement annuity by showing that, because of his medical condition, continued employment would pose an unacceptable risk to his health). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Retirement Appeals."]

Inability to perform will not qualify for a disability retirement unless it is demonstrated that the disability causes the service deficiency. Reese v. OPM, 8 MSPR 10, 12 n.3 (1981), decided against appellant's claim because of the lack of evidence clearly relating physical impairments with the failure to perform at the expected level of performance:

Hearing testimony indicated that the appellant took regular breaks from his duties to treat his physical discomforts, used excessive leave time, and failed to meet production requirements for the grade of his position. But the psychiatrist who was commissioned by OPM to examine the appellant reported that the appellant had no real interest or motivation in continuing his job, and that he very much wanted to be retired on physical disability. No evidence clearly supported the conclusion that the appellant's poor physical condition was the immediate cause of his failure to perform at the level expected for his grade or class.

Medical documentation called for by the SF-3112 in support of a disability retirement application is described by OPM on the form:

1. A comprehensive history of this patient's medical condition(s). This must include detailed information regarding the symptoms and history, past and current physical findings, results of laboratory studies and therapy of this condition(s). The medical documentation must contain specific information to show why this patient is not able to perform his or her duties. The medical documentation should not be conclusory. Provide a discussion of patient compliance with therapy, response to therapy, and plans for future therapy. Also, provide copies of pertinent hospitalization summaries and operative reports.

2. Copies of reports of all applicable diagnostic laboratory tests (e.g., hematologic, chemistry, electrophysiologic, radiologic, nuclear medicine, etc.). In the case of psychiatric disorders, provide the results of mental status examinations, personality tests, test of cognitive function, educational evaluation, neuropsychiatric tests, etc.

3. Diagnosis of patient's condition(s). Preferably each diagnosis should be found in the current publication "International Classification of Disease." In the case of psychiatric disorders, diagnostic titles and codes from the DSM III(R) should be used.

4. An assessment of the degree to which the medical condition(s) has or has not become static and an estimate of the expected date of full or partial recovery or remission.

5. If restrictions have been placed on this patient's activities, please state what they are, why they have been imposed, and how long you expect these to be in effect.

One can become lost in the Board's linguistic gymnastics describing how to prove disability. We start by noting the statutory and significant regulatory requirements and definitions.

First, for CSRS, under 5 USC 8337:

(a) An employee who completes 5 years of civilian service and has become disabled shall be retired on the employee's own application or on application by the employee's agency. Any employee shall be considered to be disabled only if the employee is found by the Office of Personnel Management to be unable, because of disease or injury, to render useful and efficient service in the employee's position and is not qualified for reassignment, under procedures prescribed by the Office, to a vacant position which is in the agency at the same grade or level and in which the employee would be able to render useful and efficient service....[emphasis supplied]

The CSRS statute is interpreted through OPM regulation, 5 CFR 831.1202 (2022):

Useful and efficient service means (1) acceptable performance of the critical or essential elements of the position; and (2) satisfactory conduct and attendance.

Second, for FERS, under 5 USC 8451:

(a)(1)(B) For purposes of this subsection, an employee shall be considered disabled only if the employee is found by the Office to be unable, because of disease or injury, to render useful and efficient service in the employee's position.

The FERS statute is implemented through OPM regulation, 5 CFR 844.102 (2022):

Useful and efficient service means acceptable performance of the critical or essential elements of the position; and satisfactory conduct and attendance.

Onto these seemingly straightforward definitions, the Board engrafts case law through decisions that state, restate, modify, and again restate twisted evidentiary paths leading to proof of disability. Worley v. OPM, DC-844E-13-0150-I-1 (NP 6/6/2014), summarized caselaw:

In Henderson v. Office of Personnel Management, the Board noted that OPM's implementing regulation describes two ways to meet the statutory requirement that an employee be unable, because of disease or injury, to render useful and efficient service in the employee's position: (1) by showing that the medical condition caused a deficiency in performance, attendance, or conduct; or (2) by showing that the medical condition is incompatible with either useful and efficient service or retention in the position. 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 16 (2012) (citing Gometz v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 115, 121 (1995)); see also Jackson v. Office of Personnel Management, 118 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 7 (2012) (finding that the standard is the same under both the Civil Service Retirement System and FERS).

Under the first method discussed above, an appellant can establish entitlement by showing that his medical condition affects his ability to perform specific work requirements, prevents him from being regular in attendance, or causes him to act inappropriately. Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 16. Under the second method, an appellant can show that the medical condition is inconsistent with working in general, working in a particular line of work, or working in a particular type of setting. Id. Regardless of the particular method of establishing an inability to render useful and efficient service, the appellant bears the burden of proof in every case by a preponderance of the evidence, i.e., more likely true than not. Id.; 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.56(a), (c)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b), 1201.4(q)] . To require medical evidence that is unambiguous and without contradiction is to impose a higher burden of proof, one that is not authorized by law or regulation. Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 16.

In Mullins-Howard v. Office of Personnel Management, the Board considered an administrative judge's findings that medical evidence at the time of OPM's denial of benefits failed to demonstrate how an employee's impairments prevented her from performing the specific duties of her position. 71 M.S.P.R. 619, 625 (1996). Reversing that finding, the Board noted that the appellant's entitlement to disability retirement did not turn on finely tuned correlations between particular medical impairments and specific job requirements. Id. at 627.

After Mullins-Howard, it appeared as if what began as an observation about the probative value of expert opinion evidence was later viewed as a substantive rule governing entitlement to disability retirement. Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 17. However, the Board has since adjusted course by overruling Bynum and its progeny to the extent that they state that Mullins-Howard reflects an "exception" to a "general rule" that medical evidence must unambiguously and without contradiction show how the medical condition affects specific job duties or indicate that the appellant cannot meet the requirements of her position. Id., ¶ 18 (citing Bynum v. Office of Personnel Management, 89 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 14 (2001); Mullins-Howard, 71 M.S.P.R. at 627).

In his initial decision, the administrative judge referenced the Bynum rule as if it was still applicable. As detailed above, Henderson overruled Bynum, so it does not apply to the appellant's case. Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 18. Nevertheless, based on the evidence of record, the judge concluded that "because [the appellant] has not established by preponderant evidence he cannot perform the Letter Carrier job duties in general, he has failed to establish he meets the criteria for receiving disability retirement benefits under FERS." We agree.

The Board will consider all pertinent evidence in determining an appellant's entitlement to disability retirement: objective clinical findings, diagnoses and medical opinions, subjective evidence of pain and disability, and evidence relating to the effect of the applicant's condition on his ability to perform the duties of his position. Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 19. The ultimate question, based on all relevant evidence, is: Do the employee's medical impairments preclude him from rendering useful and efficient service in his position. Id., ¶ 20. This question must be answered in the affirmative if the totality of the evidence makes that conclusion more likely to be true than not true. Id.
