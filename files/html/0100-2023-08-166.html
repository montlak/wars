(i) Probationer

The status of TSA probationers, i.e., whether they have Board appeal rights upon termination if they assert discrimination based on political affiliation or marital status discrimination, was raised but not decided in Corbett v. DHS, 97 MSPR 336, 337-40 ¶¶ 2-10 (2004):

On April 28, 2002, the appellant received an excepted service appointment to the position of Civil Aviation Security Specialist (Federal Air Marshal (FAM)) FV-1801-G with the Department of Transportation (DOT), Federal Aviation Administration (FAA), Transportation Security Administration (TSA). His appointment was subject to his receipt of credentials and a top-secret security clearance followed by a 12-month probationary period. On March 1, 2003, pursuant to the Homeland Security Act of 2002, Pub. L. No. 107-296, TSA was transferred to the newly created Department of Homeland Security (DHS) (hereinafter, "the agency").

On August 14, 2003, the agency notified the appellant by letter that he was terminated from his FAM position for tardiness and circumventing airport security procedures. The agency stated that the appellant was terminated in accordance with DOT's Human Resources Management (HRM) Letter 300-2, Interim Policy on Probationary Periods, dated July 29, 2002. The agency apprised the appellant that he could appeal his termination to the Board if he was a preference eligible and had completed one year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency, the United States Postal Service or Postal Rate Commission. Effective August 15, 2003, the agency removed the appellant from service. At the time of his removal, the appellant had not received a top-secret security clearance.

This timely Board appeal followed. The appellant, by his counsel, asserted that he was an employee under chapter 75 with appeal rights to the Board and that the agency had summarily removed him without affording him minimum due process, including a removal proposal notice and an opportunity to respond to its charges, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7513. The agency responded that the appellant was not entitled to minimum due process, but he had the same Board appeal rights as he previously had as a TSA employee with FAA. Specifically, the agency asserted that pursuant to the Aviation and Transportation Security Act (ATSA), codified at 49 U.S.C. § 114(n), TSA adopted FAA's personnel system granting Board appeal rights to FAA employees under the Wendell H. Ford Aviation Investment and Reform Act for the 21st Century (the Ford Act), codified at 49 U.S.C. § 40122 (g)(2)(H), (g)(3). The agency asserted that its TSA employees had no procedural rights before removal, however, because the Ford Act had not granted FAA employees section 7513 procedural rights of Title 5, and such rights had not existed prior to the Ford Act or pursuant to ATSA. Further, the agency asserted that probationary TSA employees were subject to HRM Letter 300-2, Interim Policy on Probationary Periods, section 5(g), dated July 29, 2002, and issued pursuant to ATSA, which provided that TSA could remove probationers immediately for performance or conduct deficiencies. The agency asserted that its removal action against the appellant was supported by a preponderance of the evidence.

Thereafter, the administrative judge (AJ) determined in a telephonic prehearing conference that the appellant had the same Board appeal rights afforded FAA employees. He held the requested hearing, at which the parties stipulated that the appellant was an employee as defined under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B). Following the hearing, the AJ issued an initial decision (ID), first finding that the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal pursuant to ATSA, 49 U.S.C. § 114(n), and FAA's personnel system set forth at 49 U.S.C. § 40122(g)(3), but that, under 49 U.S.C. § 40122(g)(2)(H), the appellant was not entitled to section 7513 procedural protections of Title 5. The AJ sustained the agency's removal action based on the charges of tardiness and circumventing airport security procedures, found a nexus between the sustained charges and the removal, and found that the removal was a reasonable penalty. Regarding the appellant's minimum due process claim, the AJ found that the appellant had no constitutional right to minimum due process given his probationary status. The AJ found that the agency did not commit harmful error since, as he noted earlier, FAA's personnel system set forth at 49 U.S.C. § 40122(g)(2)(H) does not include section 7513 procedural protections of Title 5. The AJ found that the agency had properly issued the appellant a written explanation for its removal action in accordance with HRM Letter 300-2.

The Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is limited to those matters over which it has jurisdiction by law, rule, or regulation. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a)(1); Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The appellant has the burden of proof on the issue of jurisdiction, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)], and must be given explicit notice of the criterion for establishing the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal, see Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

Here, the appellant argues the merits of his appeal on review but the record contains evidence that the Board may have lacked jurisdiction over the appeal ab initio. Neither the parties nor the AJ addressed this evidence. We therefore vacate the initial decision and reopen this appeal on our own motion to remand the appeal on the issue of jurisdiction.

Specifically, ATSA, 49 U.S.C. § 114(n), on which the AJ partly relied in finding Board jurisdiction in this appeal, states in pertinent part:

The personnel management system established by the Administrator of the [FAA] under section 40122 shall apply to employees of the Transportation Security Administration, or, subject to the requirements of such section, the Under Secretary [for TSA] may make such modifications to the personnel management system with respect to such employees as the Under Secretary considers appropriate, such as adopting aspects of other personnel systems of the Department of Transportation.

(Emphasis added.) The agency submitted into the record below HRM Letter 300-2, Interim Policy on Probationary Periods, which was issued pursuant to ATSA. HRM Letter 300-2, Section 5(i), Grievance and Appeal Rights, states:

Probationary employees, other than screeners, may appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board if they believe that the termination was based on discrimination because of political affiliation or marital status. These are the only issues that may be raised with the Board.

Here, it is undisputed that the appellant was a probationary/nonscreener employee at the time of his removal from the agency and that he challenged only the merits of his removal in this appeal. Thus, in light of the language in 49 U.S.C. § 114(n) permitting the Under Secretary of TSA to "make such modifications to the personnel management system," a question arises as to whether HRM Letter 300-2, section 5(i), suffices as a "modification" and thereby limits the Board appeal rights of TSA's probationary/nonscreener employees such as the appellant. Further questions arise as to the effect of HRM Letter 300-2, section 5(i), on the Board's jurisdiction in this case because HRM Letter 300-2 was issued as an interim policy and was signed by an Associate Under Secretary for Finance and Administration rather than the Under Secretary for TSA; and it was issued after DOT hired the appellant and prior to TSA's transfer from DOT to DHS. Accordingly, on remand, the AJ shall order the parties to submit evidence and argument on whether or not HRM Letter 300-2, section 5(i), constitutes a modification under 49 U.S.C. § 114(n), thereby providing the agency's probationary/nonscreener TSA employees with Board appeal rights limited to a claim of termination due to political affiliation and/or marital status discrimination.
