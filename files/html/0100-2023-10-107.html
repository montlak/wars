3. Past Internal MSPB Guidance on Transcript

The 1981 budget restrictions were echoed in Board Notice R83-12 (April 4, 1983) ("Project SOS [Save our Staff] "), stating that budget constraints forced economies of operations. Point 7 of the cost limitation measures included a caution to "be conservative on ordering transcripts of hearings; plan on ordering transcripts for no more than 10% of your cases."

The budget problems passed, but the economies achieved by discontinuing use of formal transcripts continued. The Board issued two directives in 1982 concerning the purchase of transcripts. Those directives are still useful as guidance if an application is made for the printing of the transcript at public expense under the Board's "extenuating circumstance" regulation. Notice 82-11, issued on April 16, 1982, was called "Board Policy Regarding Hearing Transcripts." In pertinent part, it stated:

Following issuance of the Board's current interim regulations at 5 USC 1201.53(a), which eliminated the provision of free transcripts to parties in hearings...a number of incidents have come to our attention which indicate that the intent of the revised regulation may be subject to misinterpretation, leading to an overly stringent application of the current Board policy regarding access to transcripts.

[T]he basic aim of the policy was to reduce the substantial amount of funds which the Board previously expended in obtaining hearing transcripts for all cases, and furnishing free copies to the parties. Under the new policy, it is anticipated that as a general rule, cases will be processed and adjudicated initially without preparation of a hearing transcript. However, regional directors retain authority to order a transcript in cases involving lengthy hearings, numerous witnesses, credibility judgments, complex and sensitive issues, or where the likelihood of a petition for review or court suit may be foreseen. Presiding officials and regional directors should be alert to the proper need of an official transcript to document the record in such cases.

Earlier "Guidance Regarding Hearing Transcripts and Tapes," issued on June 24, 1982, Board Notice 82-20, provided, in part:

[R]egional directors and presiding officials should be alert to the probable need of an official transcript at any stage in the processing of a case to adjudication, and preferably at the earliest point of development as possible. Of course, the need for a transcript in any particular case possibly may not be foreseen on the basis of the preliminary record development, and will not become apparent until the case has progressed into or through the hearing stage. If any case, however, in order to determine whether a transcript should be ordered either at an early point of the case processing, or at a later stage of the proceedings, the basic factors to be considered are essentially the same:

--Type of appeal case.

--The complexity of the legal and factual issues to be decided.

--The number of witnesses, and the nature of the testimony to be developed.

--Sensitive credibility determinations involved.

--Public interest in the case and the likelihood of publicity.

--The case involves novel or precedent-setting issues.

As a general principle, particular types of appeals in which need for an official transcript may not be expected would include the following:

--Reemployment or restoration right appeals.

--Probationary termination appeals.

--Disability retirement appeals.

--Annuity overpayment recovery appeals.

--Reduction in force appeals, unless a large consolidated and complex case.

On the other hand, there would be a general presumption that official transcripts may be required in the following types of cases:

--Adverse action appeals generally, although many may not require a transcript, such as single issue or single incident cases, depending on review of the record as it developed.

--Actions based on unsatisfactory performance, including denial of within-grade increases, where there may be issues of valid performance standards applied, as opposed to improperly subjective standards.

In any case for which preparation of a transcript is ordered, there generally should be no need for regional offices to expend the additional costs required to obtain expedited delivery of a transcript. As to transcript copies furnished to the parties, the general rule to be emphasized is that no free copies are to be provided; exceptions to the payment requirement for extenuating circumstances, based on a showing of good cause under 5 CFR 1201.53, will require close review and exercise of good judgment by regional directors.
