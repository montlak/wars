4. Time and Attendance Record

In the air traffic controller appeals of the 1980s, time and attendance records were relied on, although they were considered hearsay, to establish prima facie evidence of absence during the strike, i.e., the records were sufficient to establish absence when no contradictory evidence was introduced by the appellant. See Ackley v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 243, 246 (1983). Records may also be the basis for testimony concerning attendance and leave if the records are reliable compilations of information. The records may be reliable evidence even if the personnel who prepared them no longer have an independent recollection of the appellant's attendance or use of leave. See Watson v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 656, 662 (1987); cf. Enos v. USPS, 8 MSPR 59, 62, 7 MSPB 554 (1981) ("The leave request/notification forms relating to the other six instances, however, bear the signature of the appellant's supervisor and his brief explanation of why he denied leave. Thus, these forms constitute signed statements routinely made by a person with first-hand knowledge of the events. Since the appellant did not contest the statement of events contained in the forms, but only challenged the agency's decision to charge him with AWOL based on those events, we find that the forms suffice to establish by preponderant evidence that in six instances the appellant was absent, requested leave, and was charged with AWOL for the reasons indicated on the forms."); Rint v. OPM, 48 MSPR 69, 72 (1991), post (discussing the evidentiary weight of time and attendance records); Abbott v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 527, 529 (1983) (AJ properly admitted time and attendance records and watch schedules as evidence of the striking of controllers who did not appear for work).

Electronic records of personnel entering or leaving a facility have been used. See Noffke v. DOD, 809 Fed. Appx. 877 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) (discrepancy between swipe records and time sheet entries); Wilson v. Dept. of Army, 625 Fed. Appx. 543, 545 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) ("The AJ found Mr. Wilson's statements less persuasive than the testimony of his coworkers, noting that Mr. Wilson was unable to account for any of his movements during these absences, and in favor of testimony that at least one turnstile was operational at all times and that security personnel would not routinely allow employees to circumvent security protocols."); Ward v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-0752-13-0380-I-1(NP 2/20/2015) (charge of receipt of pay for time not worked: badge swipes compared to T&A entries); Barber v. USPS, CH-0752-10-0277-I-1 n.1 (NP 12/17/2010) ("This charge was based upon door ring records--an electronic database that records each time an employee uses his electronic access card to enter or leave the building."); cf. Koch v. SEC, DC-0752-10-0413-I-1 (NP 11/14/2011) (Vice Chairman Wagner concurring) (commenting on effect of a union agreement concerning the use of swipe card records of the dates and times of employees' entry to and exit from agency offices).
