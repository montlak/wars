h. Unauthentic, Ambiguous or Conditional Resignation

If an individual other than the employee, e.g., the employee's wife, signs the employee's name to a resignation and the agency does not verify the resignation with the employee, it is invalid should the employee deny its validity. Boyling v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 276, 279, 6 MSPB 236 (1981). Under Boyling, the issue is not whether the agency had a reasonable belief that the appellant resigned, but whether he resigned in fact. See Vargo v. USPS, 78 MSPR 66, 73 (1998) (implying that a hearing was required when appellant challenged the effectiveness of a brief letter constituting an unsigned resignation).

Assuming the signature to what purports to be a resignation is the employee's, the intent behind a resignation will be determined from all the circumstances of a case. "[A]lthough a resignation is not necessarily ambiguous because it is conditional, the conditional nature of the resignation is a factor to be considered in assessing the totality of the circumstances." McDermott v. DOJ, 82 MSPR 19, 25 ¶ 11 (1999). An ambiguous letter that appeared to contain a resignation that was conditioned on the agency's denial of a leave request was not a voluntary resignation; the intent of the appellant appeared to be to request a grant of leave, followed by consideration of resignation if the agency did not provide the leave. Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 644 (1987); see McDermott v. DOJ, 82 MSPR 19, 24-27 ¶¶ 9-12 (1999) (when the appellant offered the agency the alternative of allowing her LWOP or accepting her resignation, the agency could accept the resignation; there was no abuse of discretion or disability discrimination implicated in the refusal to grant LWOP; nevertheless, "an agency is entitled to rely on an employee's expression of a present intent to resign only when that expression is definite and unequivocal."). Under Hammond, supra, an employee's expression of a present intent to resign must be definite and unequivocal. If it is ambiguous, the agency is not entitled to rely upon it. When in doubt, the agency should contact the employee for a clarification of an ambiguous resignation. Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 174, 181 (1991).

Contreras v. Dept. of Navy, 78 MSPR 281, 285-87 (1998), involved an employee who resigned based on his ability to obtain a voluntary separative incentive payment (VSIP). The agency effected his resignation, even though he did not obtain the incentive payment, without checking with the appellant to see if he still desired to resign. The agency improperly placed the burden on the employee to resolve the ambiguity in his resignation:

The voluntariness of a resignation is determined based on whether the totality of the circumstances supports the conclusion that the employee was effectively deprived of free choice in the matter. See Braun v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 50 F.3d 1005, 1007 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1574; Christie, 518 F.2d at 587; Perlman, 490 F.2d at 933; Heining, 68 MSPR at 519-20. The application of the totality of the circumstances test must be gauged by an objective standard rather than by the employee's subjective evaluation. See Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1575. Additionally, where that expression of a resignation is ambiguous, the agency is not entitled to rely on it. See Hammond v. Department of the Navy, 50 MSPR 174, 181 (1991). An employee's expression of a present intent to resign must be definite and unequivocal for an agency to rely on it. See Hammond, 50 MSPR at 181. Further, unless there is an apparent reason why the agency could not have contacted the employee to clarify any ambiguity in a resignation, it should do so. Id.

In his petition for review, the appellant alleges essentially that the administrative judge erred in finding that his resignation was voluntary because it was effected before the agency had made a decision on his entitlement to a VSIP. We agree. The evidence of record in this case establishes that the circumstances surrounding the appellant's resignation did not allow him to exercise free choice and, therefore, his resignation is involuntary....

The administrative judge correctly found that the appellant had discussed the possibility of resigning with Acosta, that the agency had informed the appellant that his VSIP determination might not be made by July 14, 1996, and that the appellant must notify agency officials if he wanted a postponement of the resignation date. Further, there is no evidence of record that the appellant objected to the agency's giving him the responsibility to postpone his resignation if no VSIP determination was made by July 14, 1996. We find, however, that the record is insufficient under the circumstances of this case to establish that the appellant's resignation was voluntary. On its face, the appellant's statement on the SF-52 does not evidence a definitive, unconditional resolve to resign on July 14, 1996, because it is conditioned on the agency's VSIP determination. Thus, it is ambiguous. See Hammond v. Department of the Navy, 35 MSPR 644, 648 (1987).

Further, agency officials apparently recognized the ambiguity in the appellant's expression of his resignation. Acosta acknowledged that he, and not the appellant, selected the July 14, 1996 effective date of the resignation. He stated that the date was arbitrary, but based on his assumption that the agency would be able to give the appellant a determination on whether or not he actually would be able to receive a VSIP buyout by that time. Labor Relations Specialist Janet Longacre, who actually processed the SF-52 effecting the appellant's resignation, testified that both she and Acosta expected the appellant to contact them to extend the resignation's effective date if the agency had not made a VSIP determination by July 14, 1996. Also, Longacre testified that she did not want to rush the processing of the resignation, so she waited from July 14, 1996, until Acosta returned from vacation on July 24, 1996, on the chance that the appellant had left a message with Acosta during Acosta's absence. The testimony of Acosta and Longacre shows that, notwithstanding that they had informed the appellant that he must contact them to extend the effective date of his resignation, they recognized the importance of the conditional statement on the SF-52, i.e., that the resignation depended on the agency making a VSIP determination. Thus, because the appellant's expression of his resignation was ambiguous, the agency was not entitled to rely on it.

Neither Acosta nor Longacre, however, contacted the appellant to clarify the ambiguity. Longacre testified that she did not contact the appellant to resolve the ambiguity because she thought it would appear as if she were trying to coerce him into changing his requested resignation. Acosta testified that he did not contact the appellant after he had returned from his vacation because he had made it clear to the appellant that it was his responsibility to inform the agency if he wanted to postpone the resignation after July 14, 1996, and that he (Acosta) could not be expected to follow-up on the SF-52 of one employee when he was busy counseling many employees.

Thus, neither Acosta or Longacre contacted the appellant to clarify the ambiguity that they recognized existed on the face of his resignation, although they had the opportunity to do so. We find that, notwithstanding that the appellant could have postponed the effective date of his resignation by calling the agency, factors within the agency's control operated on the employee's decision-making process that deprived him of freedom of choice. These included the agency's failure to make a VSIP determination by July 14, 1996, and the failure of Acosta and Longacre to contact the appellant to provide him with that information so that he could clarify the ambiguity in his resignation regarding the VSIP determination. See Hammond, 50 MSPR at 181. Accordingly, we find that the appellant has established that his resignation was involuntary, and thus tantamount to a removal action.
