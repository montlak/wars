5. Hearing

If it is requested, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on the merits of the IRA appeal, Mason v. DHS, 116 MSPR 135, 140 ¶ 7, 2011 MSPB 39 (2011):

...If the appellant establishes Board jurisdiction over his IRA appeal by exhausting his remedies before OSC and making the requisite nonfrivolous allegations, he has the right to a hearing on the merits of his claim. Spencer v. Department of the Navy, 327 F.3d 1354, 1356 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Rusin v. Department of the Treasury, 92 M.S.P.R. 298, ¶ 20 (2002).

An AJ does not commit harmful error by making a preliminary determination that an IRA case is within the jurisdiction of the Board, holding a hearing, and then determining that the case is beyond the Board's jurisdiction, Shope v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 590, 593-94 ¶¶ 5-6, 2007 MSPB 219 (2007):

In an IRA appeal, an appellant is not entitled to a hearing on the threshold issue of jurisdiction because the Board has jurisdiction over an IRA appeal if the appellant makes nonfrivolous allegations that the elements of his claim are satisfied and whether allegations are nonfrivolous is determined on the written record. See Spencer v. Department of the Navy, 327 F.3d 1354, 1356 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Oscar v. Department of Agriculture, 103 M.S.P.R. 591, ¶ 7 (2006). If the appellant makes the required nonfrivolous jurisdictional allegations, he is entitled to a hearing on the merits. See Spencer, 327 F.3d at 1356-57; Rusin v. Department of the Treasury, 92 M.S.P.R. 298, ¶ 20 (2002).

Here, the administrative judge denied the agency's motions to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction and proceeded to a hearing. Because the administrative judge found Board jurisdiction, this hearing should have been on the merits of the claim, and the administrative judge therefore erred in dismissing the appeal for lack of jurisdiction after holding the hearing. We find, however, that this error was harmless because we agree with the administrative judge's ultimate conclusion that the documentary evidence does not show that the appellant presented a nonfrivolous allegation that his disclosure was protected under the WPA. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981) (the administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown to have adversely affected a party's substantive rights).

The appellant does not have a right to a hearing on separate jurisdictional components, for example, the OSC exhaustion requirement. See Abou-Hussein v. MSPB, 557 Fed. Appx. 979, 982 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) ("nothing in that case or any of our precedent suggests that a jurisdictional hearing is required on the issue of OSC exhaustion. Rather, exhaustion is determined based on the complaint or other written materials submitted to OSC").

Under English v. SBA, DE-1221-15-0090-W-1 (NP 4/8/2015):

[A]ny doubt or ambiguity as to whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation should be resolved in favor of affording the appellant a hearing. Huffman v. Office of Personnel Management, 92 M.S.P.R. 429, ¶ 13 (2002).

Huffman v. OPM, 92 MSPR 429, 435 ¶ 13 (2002), determined:

Any doubt or ambiguity as to whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of a reasonable belief should be resolved in favor of affording the appellant a hearing. See Sood v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 88 M.S.P.R. 214, ¶ 13 (2001); Czarkowski v. Department of the Navy, 87 M.S.P.R. 107, ¶ 9 (2000); Keefer v. Department of Agriculture, 82 M.S.P.R. 687, ¶ 15 (1999); Ayers v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 79 M.S.P.R. 19, 24 n.* (1998).[6] We note in this regard that an employee might understate the strength of his belief that wrongdoing has occurred if he is calling the attention of his superiors to problems of a sensitive nature, and particularly if such problems are likely to engender a defensive or angry reaction from superiors. He also might not disclose fully the information he possesses in support of a belief that wrongdoing has occurred. For example, some information may have been provided by co-workers whom the employee making the disclosure does not want to identify.

[6] Under our former Geyer standard, such a hearing was considered a jurisdictional hearing. Under the Yunus/Dick standard, however, such a hearing is on the merits.

If the Board declines to holding a hearing on some of the alleged incidents of reprisal, the Federal Circuit may decide that, based on the nature of the allegations, the denial was harmless error. See Stern v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP) (hostile environment may be a covered personnel action, but the circumstances presented did not warrant reversal of the Board's conclusion that a hostile environment was not adequately alleged or proved; if there was error as to analysis of the sufficiency of the allegations, a hearing denial was harmless error).
