a. Linkage of ALOC and Appraisal System

OPM regulations provide, 5 CFR 531.404 (2022):

531.404 Earning within-grade increase.

An employee paid at less than the maximum rate of the grade of his or her position shall earn advancement in pay to the next higher step of the grade or the next higher rate within the grade (as defined in § 531.403) upon meeting the following three requirements established by law:

(a) The employee's performance must be at an acceptable level of competence, as defined in this subpart. To be determined at an acceptable level of competence, the employee's most recent rating of record (as defined in § 430.203 of this chapter) shall be at least Level 3 ("Fully Successful" or equivalent).

(1) When a within-grade increase decision is not consistent with the employee's most recent rating of record a more current rating of record must be prepared.

(2) The rating of record used as the basis for an acceptable level of competence determination for a within-grade increase must have been assigned no earlier than the most recently completed appraisal period.

(b) The employee must have completed the required waiting period for advancement to the next higher step of the grade of his or her position.

(c) The employee must not have received an equivalent increase during the waiting period.

The Board mustered up an explanation of the linkage between WIGIs and appraisals in Nalls v. Dept. of Air Force, 46 MSPR 603, 607 (1991):

Employees who have not reached the maximum rate of pay for the grade of their positions and who have completed the necessary waiting period are eligible to receive within-grade salary increases if their work is at an acceptable level of competence. See 5 USC 5335(a). An acceptable level of competence means fully successful performance by the employee of the duties and responsibilities of her assigned position and is determined by the employee's most recent rating of record as defined in the agency's Performance Management Plan, which must be a rating of no less than fully successful. See 5 CFR 531.403 and 531.404(a).

The appraisal supporting the ALOC determination is conducted pursuant to the performance appraisal system. Under 5 USC 4302(c)(6), an agency must provide the employee an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance, but only if the agency contemplates a performance-based action of reassignment, reduction in grade, or removal. If the agency is going to withhold a step increase, it need not provide an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance; that "opportunity" comes at the end of the waiting period when the step increase is denied and the employee is told what must be done to obtain the step increase in the future. Lance v. Dept. of Energy, 28 MSPR 467, 470 (1985).

Although the step increase is linked to a performance appraisal, the lack of an appraisal does not preclude denial of the salary increase. In Bowden v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 662, 666-69 (1993), the appellant's prior rating of record was fully successful, but he was denied a step increase after he was placed on a PIP. The issue was whether the agency's error in denying the WIGI without first issuing a new written performance rating was harmful error. The OPM regulation relied on by the appellant, 5 CFR 531.409(b) (2022), provided, in part, that "[w]hen applicable, an acceptable level of competence determination shall be based on a current rating of record made under part 430, subpart B, of this chapter." The Board discerned no error in denying the step increase without a current rating of less than fully satisfactory:

Both the agency and OPM contend that the requirement of basing the appellant's WIGI determination on his rating of record is procedural in nature and not a substantive right. Specifically, OPM contends that its regulations under 5 CFR Part 531 are recognized by the Board as being procedural in nature, citing Lance v. Department of Energy, 28 MSPR 467 (1985). In Lance, the Board found that under 5 USC 4302 an agency must provide an employee with an opportunity to improve when, on the basis of a performance rating, an agency contemplates reassignment, reduction in grade, or removal, but not when it contemplates a denial of a WIGI. Therefore, in Lance, the Board stated that the right to an opportunity to improve when denial of a WIGI is contemplated is not a substantive right granted by 5 USC 4302. Id. at 470. The Board's review of and holding on what is a substantive right in a WIGI case was limited to that matter. More importantly, however, Lance was decided prior to the 1986 changes in Part 531, and thus did not address the "rating of record" issue raised in this appeal.

In performance-based actions, an employee is entitled to certain substantive due process rights. See Stephen v. Department of Air Force, 47 MSPR 672, 680-81 (1991), citing Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532 (1985). Because Part 531 is intertwined with 5 CFR Part 430, we reject OPM's unqualified statement that the Board recognizes Part 531 as a whole, as being procedural in nature. We find, however, that because the appellant was afforded his right of notice, and the right to respond orally and in writing, he was provided with all the substantive due process rights he was entitled to receive.[3]

[3] The Board has held in actions brought under 5 USC Chapter 43, that the appellant has a substantive right prior to removal or demotion to be placed on notice that his performance is unacceptable, not just marginal, and to an opportunity to improve. See Colgan v. Department of the Navy, 28 MSPR 116, 119 (1985); Grant v. Department of Transportation, 24 MSPR 663, 667 (1984). The basis for that holding, however, is the employee's substantive right to an opportunity to improve as one of the Chapter 43 requirements. As stated above, though, there is no such right with respect to ALOC denials. See also, Cross v. Department of the Air Force, 25 MSPR 353, 358 n.3 (1984), aff'd, 785 F.2d 320 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table) (construing the requirement in Chapter 43 that performance standards be "communicated" to be substantive, but holding that the substantive right is to be made aware of the standards against which the employee's performance is to be measured, not to communication, per se). We find here, similarly, that the substance of the appellant's right under Part 531 is not to a formal evaluation, but to be put on notice that his performance has been found inadequate so that he can work to gain his WIGI at a later date. See 5 CFR § 531.411.

Moreover, a WIGI is not granted to an employee as a matter of right. See Griffith v. Federal Labor Relations Authority, 842 F.2d 487, 495-96 (D.C. Cir. 1988). In Griffith, the court found that a WIGI was not a constitutionally protected right and that Congress intended that agencies have broad discretion in granting or denying a WIGI. In summary, then, we conclude that where an appellant is granted substantive due process, because he is not entitled to a WIGI as a matter of right, he is not deprived of a substantive right when his WIGI denial is not based on his rating of record. Because the agency's error was procedural, the issue becomes whether that error was harmful to the appellant.

Under 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(a), the agency's decision may not be sustained if the employee or applicant for employment shows harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision. The Board's regulations define "harmful error" as:

Error by the agency in the application of its procedures that is likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error.

5 CFR 1201.56(c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(r)] ; Coughlan v. Department of Air Force, 35 MSPR 230, 232 (1987). Reversal of an action is warranted only where a procedural error, whether regulatory or statutory, is likely to have had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency.... The burden is on the appellant to prove harmful procedural error by a preponderance of the evidence. 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(1) and (c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1) and 1201.4(r)] .

Applying this definition to the present facts, we find that the appellant has not met his burden of showing that the agency committed harmful procedural error in effecting its denial of his WIGI. The agency did err by failing to base its denial of the WIGI on the appellant's current rating of record. 5 CFR 531.409(b). The issue, therefore, is whether the agency's error in denying his WIGI without first issuing a new written performance rating was harmful.

OPM added the requirement in 1986 that denial of a WIGI must be based on a rating of record. In discussing this requirement, OPM commented that:

...use of a written justification which is contrary to the employee's rating of record as a basis for within grade increase determinations would be inconsistent with pay-for-performance principles.

51 Fed. Reg. 8402 (1986). This comment suggests that the regulation was changed to avoid a situation where an employee would receive a fully successful rating at the end of a performance period, but then be denied his WIGI. What was not addressed in OPM's regulations is the situation, as here, where the employee's performance may be less than fully successful at the end of the rating period and the agency denies the WIGI without reducing the employee's rating to writing. To grant a WIGI, even though an employee has not met the fully successful standard in his just completed rating period, does not appear to be the result contemplated in OPM's regulations. Such an outcome would itself violate the pay-for-performance principles. As was true in Stephen, the standard applied by the administrative judge "creates considerable risk of doing exactly what Congress sought to avoid in enacting the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, Pub. L. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111 (1978), i.e., reversing agency actions for procedural violations that did not cause harm to the employee." See Stephen, 47 MSPR at 680-81.

OPM contends that unacceptable performers should not receive a windfall because the agency errs in applying an OPM regulation. We agree. Under 5 USC 5335(a), before an employee may receive a WIGI, the agency must determine that the work of the employee is of an acceptable level of competence. We thus find merit to OPM's argument that a WIGI may not be granted by default but must be earned by successful performance under the principles of pay-for-performance. See Griffith, 842 F.2d at 495-96.[7]

[7] The appellant argues in response to OPM's brief that the administrative judge properly relied on our decision in Hudson v. Department of the Army, 49 MSPR 202, 206 (1991). We find that reliance to be misplaced, however. In Hudson, the agency denied the employee's WIGI based on a performance rating issued before the employee's entire waiting period had elapsed. We held that a denial of a WIGI based on a rating of record for a period less than the full waiting period was acceptable because it was made pursuant to the most recent rating of record. Thus, in Hudson, although we recognized the 1986 change in 5 CFR § 531.404(a) regarding the "rating of record" requirement, we did not address the issue of whether an agency's failure to base its determination on a rating of record was harmful procedural error.

Finally, we note that deference should be given to OPM's interpretation of the law as the agency charged with its implementation. See Newman v. Love and Penn, 962 F.2d 1008, 1012 (Fed. Cir. 1992). This is true unless its interpretation is contrary to the plain wording of the law. Id. OPM's position, that a WIGI may not be granted by default as the result of a procedural error, coincides with the plain language of 5 USC 5335(a), that made the granting of a WIGI subject to a determination by the agency that the employee's work is of an acceptable level of competence.
