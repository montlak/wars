E. BYPASSING UNION

As the exclusive representative, the federal sector union stands as partner with management in collective bargaining. Management impairs that bargaining relationship if it deals with employees directly about negotiable working conditions or the impact of changes in those working conditions on bargaining unit employees. Under the Statute, and through evolution of case law, the union represents not only the bargaining unit as a community of agency employees but also the individuals in the unit for the purposes of involvement in grievances or appeals and complaints; assisting during certain types of formal discussions between representatives of management and employees; and providing representation to employees who desire union assistance during investigations that may lead to disciplinary action against them. To provide effective leadership in bargaining and representation of employees in discussions and investigations, along with other union functions, the union and its officials need protection against interference or acts of retaliation that preclude or discourage either union representatives in their union functions or that tend to discourage individuals from becoming members of or supporting unions. The union itself needs protection against management practices that interfere with its ability as an organization to carry on its mission and perpetuate itself as an organization. Our discussion begins with problems that arise when management intentionally or unintentionally bypasses the union and instead deals directly with employees concerning negotiable working conditions or adjustment of grievances.

Management's disregard of a union and its attempts to deal with employees individually concerning grievances and personnel policies and practices are acts in derogation of the representative's right to represent employees. IRS and NTEU, 4 FLRA 488, 497 (1980) (ALJ Decision). A well-meaning attempt by management to resolve a personnel problem with an employee's spouse, rather than through the union, after the employee had pursued the union grievance procedure with union assistance through at least several steps of that procedure, amounted to an unlawful bypassing of the union. Dept. of Treasury, IRS Memphis Serv. Ctr. and NTEU, 17 FLRA 107, 115 (1985) (ALJ Decision). Contract language for joint efforts by the agency and union to minimize adverse effects of reassignments did not authorize the agency to bypass the union in dealings with employees concerning shift changes. See Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU Chap. 66, 57 FLRA 126, 130 (2001). Direct discussion with an employee concerning changes and reorganization of work operations was an unlawful bypass of the union. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 22 FLRA 91, 114-15 (1986) (ALJ Decision). The union is improperly bypassed if the agency delivers grievance decisions and responses directly to employee grievants and not to designated union representatives. SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 16 FLRA 434, 435 (1984); see Dept. of Air Force, 355th SPTG and AFGE Local 2924, 63 FLRA 635 (2009) (agency improperly bypassed union by delivering grievance answer to employee and not designated union representative). The result is the same if the agency advises the employee that a contract grievance is nongrievable, declines to process the grievance, and then suggests to the employee that he process his complaint through the agency administrative grievance procedure. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 3937, 39 FLRA 298, 311-13 (1991).

If, after an adverse action proposal and decision, the employee meets directly with the union and designates the union as his representative, management undermines the union's status by meeting with the employee and telling him that he does not need his representative after he asks whether the union representative should be present. 438th Air Base Group, McGuire AFB and AFGE Local 1778, 28 FLRA 1112, 1124 (1987) (ALJ Decision); cf. Dept. of Air Force, Sacramento Air Logistics Ctr., McClellan AFB and AFGE Local 1857, 35 FLRA 345, 357 (1990) (ALJ Decision) (unlawful bypass when management met with employees to present disciplinary actions, employees were represented by union in preliminary steps of disciplinary process, and management failed to allow union to be present). If local management negotiates with employees directly, even though it has done so at the direction of higher-level management, it appears that local management will have committed a ULP. DHHS, SSA, Dallas Region and AFGE, Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, 23 FLRA 807, 813 (1986). The union may waive its right to representative status if it suggests to management by words or conduct that management may meet with an employee over a matter known to the union. See Air Force Logistics Command, Ogden Air Logistics Ctr., Hill AFB and AFGE Local 1592, 43 FLRA 736, 737-38 (1991). [Refer later in this chapter to "Representation During Formal Discussions."]

To determine if a communication amounts to a bypass, the communication must be judged independently, and it must be ascertained whether that communication constitutes an attempt by agency management to deal or negotiate directly with unit employees or to threaten or promise benefits to employees. The content of the communication and the circumstances surrounding it must be considered. Not all communications between agency management and unit employees over matters relating to the collective bargaining relationship are improper. Communications violate the Statute when they amount to an attempt to bypass the representative and bargain directly with employees, urge employees to put pressure on the representative to take a certain course of action, or threaten or promise benefits to employees. Div. of Military and Naval Affairs, New York and New York State Council of ACT, 8 FLRA 307, 321-22 (1982) (ALJ Decision). An attempt to bypass the union suggests direct dealings with employees. An absence of any dealings is not a bypass; it could be a refusal to bargain, but refusal to bargain does not amount to an illegal bypass. DHHS, SSA, Dallas Region and AFGE, Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, 23 FLRA 807, 813 (1986).

A quick meeting to announce a change in working conditions to employees did not result in a bypass of the union. There was no negotiation or other direct dealing. To prove a bypass, there must be proof that the employer attempted to deal directly with employees over employment conditions. DLA, Defense Depot Tracy and Laborers Int'l Union, Local 1276, 14 FLRA 475, 478 (1984). Notice directly by a manager to employees affected by a change in agency operations is not an unlawful attempt to bypass the union if the manager did not attempt to bargain with the employees over negotiable issues. The union would not be bypassed if management, trying to accommodate a union request to assist individual employees, reasonably makes some suggestions to the employees to provide the assistance requested. Dept. of Air Force, 47th Air Base Group and AFGE Local 1749, 4 FLRA 469, 477-78 (1980) (ALJ Decision).

Management did not bypass the union when it asked for suggestions from employees to improve the workplan brought about by a reorganization that the union was given an opportunity to negotiate. SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 1501, 20 FLRA 768, 770 (1985). Management did not bypass the union by interviewing employees to gather factual data concerning the effectiveness of a work assignment plan. There was no attempt to deal directly with unit members or undermine the status of the union. IRS and NTEU, 14 FLRA 698, 700-01 (1984). But the union was unlawfully bypassed when a supervisor asked individual employees for their views concerning development of a new watch or work schedule and solicited their assistance in establishing alternative schedules. The supervisor was on notice of the union official authorized to bargain and failed to bargain with the union over the changes or the manner of employee participation. Dept. of Transp., FAA, Los Angeles and PASS, Local 503, 15 FLRA 100, 103-04 (1984). Memos sent by management directly to employees soliciting their comments on accountability plans (essentially performance standards) constituted unlawful bypass of the union. DHHS, SSA, Dallas Region and AFGE, Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, 23 FLRA 807, 813 (1986). The union was improperly bypassed when a supervisor asked his employees by memo for their opinions concerning a proposed change in conditions of employment accomplished by eliminating an evening shift on weekends, and by soliciting their opinions again on the subject in a follow-up memo and meeting. Management was not just attempting to gather information or opinions concerning its operations; it directly sought the opinions of employees as to proposed changes in their conditions of employment. Dept. of Transp., FAA and PASS, 19 FLRA 893, 895 (1985). [Refer later in this chapter to "Polls; General Inquiries; Questionnaires" and "Representation During Formal Discussions."]

The union was bypassed when management first said it would meet with the union concerning establishment of new work centers that would employ, or at least arguably affect the working conditions of, unit employees and then, instead of meeting with the union, management solicited applications from unit members for employment at the centers. Reviewing an arbitration award, SSA ODAR and Ass'n of ALJs, IFPTE, 66 FLRA 193, 196 (2011), explained:

The Arbitrator found that, after the Union became aware of rumors that the Agency was about to open the first NHC, the Agency agreed to meet with the Union. However, despite a promise not to implement its plan to create the NHCs until after the meeting, the Agency sent out job solicitations for the NHC ALJ positions to all the ALJs in the bargaining unit before the meeting took place. This was actually the first official notice the Union received from the Agency regarding the NHCs. This behavior led the Arbitrator to conclude that "[t]he creation of the NHCs was all but over and done with before the...meeting ever took place" and that the matter was a "done deal."

The Agency's conduct circumvented the Union. The Agency dealt directly with unit employees by issuing the job solicitation for the NHC positions prior to the meeting. Such actions constitute a ULP because they bypass the Union, undermining its authority as the exclusive representative. See, e.g., SSA, 55 FLRA 978, 982 (1999); U.S. Dep't of Justice, Bureau of Prisons, Fed. Corr. Inst., Bastrop, Tex., 51 FLRA 1339, 1346 (1996); Dep't of Health & Human Servs., SSA, Baltimore, Md., 39 FLRA 298, 311 (1991); cf. Tra-Mar Commc'ns, Inc., 265 NLRB 664, 681 (1982) (employer violated § 8(a)(1) of National Labor Relations Act by unilaterally granting wage increases to certain employees because such bypassing of collective bargaining representative undermined its authority as representative of employees).

Of unclear significance in the context of labor relations is an OPM regulation that suggests a degree of protection for consultations between management and employees as long as those consultations do not infringe on labor-management responsibilities. 5 CFR 251.101 (2022), notes:

(a) The regulations in this part apply to all Federal executive branch departments and agencies and their officers and employees.

(b) This part provides a framework for consulting and communicating with non-labor organizations representing Federal employees and with other organizations on matters related to agency operations and personnel management.

(c) The purposes of consultation and communication are: the improvement of agency operations, personnel management, and employee effectiveness; the exchange of information (e.g., ideas, opinions, and proposals); and the establishment of policies that best serve the public interest in accomplishing the mission of the agency.

(d) An agency's consultation and communication with organizations representing Federal employees and with other organizations under this part may not take on the character of negotiations or consultations regarding conditions of employment of bargaining unit employees, which is reserved exclusively to labor organizations as provided for in Chapter 71 of title 5 of the U.S. Code or comparable provisions of other laws. The regulations in this part do not authorize any actions inconsistent with Chapter 71 of the U.S. Code or comparable provisions of other laws.

(e) The head of a Federal agency may determine that it is in the interest of the agency to consult, from time to time, with organizations other than labor organizations and associations of management officials and/or supervisors to the extent permitted by law. Under section 7(d)(2) and (3) of Executive Order 11491, as amended, recognition of a labor organization does not preclude an agency from consulting or dealing with a veterans organization, or with a religious, social, fraternal, professional, or other lawful association, not qualified as a labor organization, with respect to matters or policies which involve individual members of the organization or association or are of particular applicability to it or its members.

(f) Federal employees, including management officials and supervisors, may communicate with any Federal agency, officer, or other Federal entity on the employee's own behalf. However, Federal employees should be aware that 18 U.S.C. 205, in pertinent part, restricts Federal employees from acting, other than in the proper discharge of their official duties, as agents or attorneys for any person or organization other than a labor organization, before any Federal agency or other Federal entity in connection with any matter in which the United States is a party or has a direct and substantial interest. An exception to the prohibition found in 18 U.S.C. 205 permits Federal employees to represent certain nonprofit organizations before the Government except in connection with specified matters. Agency officials and employees are therefore advised to consult with their designated agency ethics officials for guidance regarding any conflicts of interest that may arise.
