broida flra Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards, Subchapter 08: Grounds for Review, Section G: Contrary to Law; Award is Inconsistent with Law, Rule, or Regulation, Subsection 19: Performance-Related Issues, Part c: Performance Appraisal--Through 1987
c. Performance Appraisal--Through 1987

Decisions issued before Newark Air Force Station and AFGE Local 2221, 30 FLRA 616 (1987), permitting arbitrators to examine performance standards and elements to determine whether they comply with legal requirements, provide some guidance concerning permissible awards involving standards and elements. An arbitrator's award sustaining a grievance challenging a critical element as improperly characterized and changing the designation to noncritical was set aside. It infringed the right of management to identify performance standards and critical elements of positions. AFGE Local 1917 and INS, 15 FLRA 781, 782 (1984). FLRA overturned an award that enforced a contract provision that required performance standards and critical elements to be reasonable. The contract provision was unenforceable. By limiting the development of standards and elements, it abridged management's right to assign work. NTEU and Customs Serv., 17 FLRA 38, 39 (1985). An award could require that management provide advance notice to a union before revising employee work plans. The union had the right to an opportunity to respond concerning the impact and implementation of the changes. INS and AFGE Nat'l INS Council, 27 FLRA 488, 490-91 (1987).

One award was set aside when the arbitrator considered factors that "the Arbitrator conceded were not required to be considered by management," and so, it was concluded, had conducted an independent evaluation of performance. SSA, DHHS and AFGE Local 3231, 28 FLRA 731, 733 (1987). FLRA confronted the problem of how an arbitrator could properly assess performance under standards and direct that a particular rating be granted without conducting an independent evaluation of the employee's performance or substituting his judgment for that of management, SSA, Office of Hearings and Appeals, Region II and AFGE Local 1760, 21 FLRA 672, 676-77 (1986):

[I]t was appropriate for the Arbitrator to have determined that the appraisal by management of the grievant's production violated the collective bargaining agreement because in applying the established performance standards to the grievant, management failed to consider factors that were beyond the control of the grievant. However, in the circumstances of this case, the Arbitrator was in no manner authorized to determine, as he in effect did, that the grievant's case production would have been rated at least as "fully satisfactory" but for the violation of the agreement. As noted, management's appraisal of the grievant's numerical case production was based on the statistical determination of the grievant's average daily production and the rating of that production under established standards. The Authority finds that this is not the type of appraisal that would permit an arbitrator in an objective, nondiscretionary, and essentially mechanistic manner to determine without an independent evaluation that an aggrieved employee was entitled to a different rating under the established standards.... Although the Arbitrator properly could have ordered the grievant reevaluated on the finding that management had failed to consider factors that were beyond the control of the grievant, the Arbitrator instead independently determined what that rating would have been....

Unless the standards permitted an arbitrator in an objective, nondiscretionary, and essentially mechanistic manner to determine without an independent evaluation that an employee was entitled to a different rating rather than the one issued under the established standards, the arbitrator could only direct reevaluation. GSA and AFGE Council 236, 22 FLRA 82, 86 (1986); see HCFA and AFGE Local 1923, 25 FLRA 725, 726-27 (1987) (setting aside award: "[t]he arbitrator conducted an independent evaluation of the grievants' performance under the elements and standards established by management and substituted his judgment for that of management as to what the grievants' evaluations and ratings should have been"). FLRA judged whether the performance standards were objective, nondiscretionary, and essentially mechanistic. See DHHS, SSA and AFGE Local 3231, 28 FLRA 961, 964-65 (1987).

As an example of an award that was sustained after an arbitrator directed that management assign a particular rating for a particular standard, Warner Robins Air Logistics Ctr. and AFGE Local 987, 28 FLRA 652, 654-55 (1987) (Member McKee concurring in the result but urging less constraint on arbitrators than expressed by the majority opinion), stated:

...We have found that an arbitrator may resolve a grievance over whether an employee was adversely affected in his or her performance appraisal by management's application of the established performance standards. The arbitrator may sustain the grievance on finding that management had not properly applied to the grievant the standards which it has established. In sustaining the grievance, the arbitrator may direct when appropriate that the grievant's work product or performance be granted the rating to which entitled under the established elements and standards. As to when such a direction would be appropriate, we have indicated that the disputed appraisal and elements and standards should be of the type that permits the arbitrator "in an objective, nondiscretionary, and essentially mechanistic manner to determine without an independent evaluation that the aggrieved employee was entitled to a different rating under the established standards."...

We find that the appraisal and the standards in dispute in this case permitted the Arbitrator...to determine without an independent evaluation that the grievant was entitled to ratings of "met" under the established standards for both subelements of the disputed element, and consequently a rating of "met" for the element, itself. Based on the evidence and testimony presented, the Arbitrator resolved the objectively verifiable dispute over whether the grievant was tardy more than 5 times during the rating period. Once he found that the grievant had not been tardy more than 5 times, the Arbitrator determined without an independent evaluation that the grievant was entitled to a rating of "met" for that subelement [job element 5E, pertaining to time and attendance, establishing a standard of 3-5 instances of tardiness during the rating period].

An arbitrator reviewing an appraisal was not to limit management's discretion on reevaluation by specifying the circumstances governing performance evaluation by, for example, directing comparison of the grievant's work with a sample of five appraisals selected under a precise method determined by the arbitrator. Fed. Prisons Sys., Med. Ctr. for Fed. Prisoners and AFGE Local 1612, 23 FLRA 372, 374 (1986). An award was set aside if, in reviewing a performance appraisal grievance, an arbitrator directed the agency to reevaluate the grievant's performance, retained jurisdiction, and, finding the reevaluation to be insufficient, directed the agency to adopt a particular rating. The arbitrator substituted his judgment for that of management as to what should have been the grievant's performance evaluation and rating under established standards. Mare Island Naval Shipyard and IFPTE Local 11, 23 FLRA 259, 261 (1986).

An arbitrator was precluded from directing that management could not, on reevaluation, rate an employee below a certain level. VA Hosp., Bath and AFGE Local 491, 22 FLRA 1063, 1064-65 (1986). An award that ruled a manager lacked the authority to lower a performance appraisal was set aside. The award interfered with management's right to assign performance appraisal review duties to the reviewing official. The award also eliminated the discretion inherent in that right. DHHS, SSA and NTEU, 17 FLRA 561, 562 (1985). An arbitrator was not to render an award precluding a reviewing official from carrying out his review functions and changing performance appraisals. AFGE Local 2092 and VAMC, Ann Arbor, 26 FLRA 246, 248 (1987). An award improperly set aside reduction by a reviewing official of a rating issued to an employee on the ground that the reviewer should, under the contract, have been someone with daily knowledge of the employee's performance. The award enforced a provision that restricted the right of management to assign work, i.e., restricted management in the assignment of reviewing duties. AFGE Local 1509 and VA Hosp., Sioux Falls, 23 FLRA 17, 18 (1986).

A violation of a contract provision requiring a 12-month appraisal was properly remedied by a directive to reevaluate the employee at the conclusion of the full rating period. SSA and AFGE Local 1760, 21 FLRA 401, 402-03 (1986). The arbitrator could not direct the modification of an appraisal because of a procedural violation. AFGE Local 2092 and VAMC, Ann Arbor, 26 FLRA 246, 248 (1987). The arbitrator could not, in setting aside an appraisal, preclude management directly or indirectly from conducting a proper appraisal of the employee. HCFA and AFGE Local 1923, 26 FLRA 860, 862 (1987). An arbitrator could not direct management to treat an employee's performance at a certain level for a promotion or cash award. SSA and AFGE Local 1122, 26 FLRA 137, 139 (1987). An arbitrator could establish the timeframes for reappraisal of a grievant, depending on the timing of changes in performance standards and the date the employee became aware of those changes. AFGE Local 2014 and SSA, 21 FLRA 991, 998 (1986). Although an arbitrator could direct an agency to reevaluate an employee, the arbitrator could not add to the provisions of the negotiated agreement or agency regulations by establishing evaluation procedures or by ordering the training of supervisors or by directing the parties to negotiate over performance standards and critical elements. Those procedures either violated management rights or had the effect of assigning work to supervisors, an indirect violation of management rights. VAMC, Leavenworth and AFGE Local 85, 26 FLRA 375, 377-78 (1987).
