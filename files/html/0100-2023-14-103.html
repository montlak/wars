1. Notice Requirement

"It is improper for an agency to rely on the appellant's past disciplinary record where it was not cited in either the notice of proposed removal or in the decision notice and was mentioned for the first time when the agency's proposing and deciding officials testified that they relied on the prior offense in determining the penalty." Lentine v. Dept. of Treasury, 94 MSPR 676, 680 ¶ 10 (2003). [If past discipline were considered but not cited, there would like also be a due process issue. Refer later in this chapter to "Failure to Include Past Discipline in Proposal: Due Process."] If an agency relies on past misconduct to enhance a penalty, the agency need not formally charge the misconduct. The only requirement is that the proposal notice reference the past misconduct in enough detail to permit an informed reply. Clardy v. VA, 13 MSPR 598, 601 (1982) ("the wording of the proposal notice was sufficient to inform appellant that his past disciplinary actions would be considered in determining an appropriate penalty"); see Lybbert v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-12-0528-I-1 (NP 7/23/2013) ("Further, an agency may consider an employee's record of prior discipline as an aggravating factor for imposition of an enhanced penalty. Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 304-05 (1981). To do so, the agency must give the employee notice that it will consider his record of prior discipline when it proposes the new disciplinary action. Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274, 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2011)."); Dangerfield v. USPS, 77 MSPR 678, 686 (1998) ("[The deciding official] did not consider appellant's past disciplinary record as indicated in the proposal notice, and, thus, we will not consider it.").

If the agency does not reference past events in the advance notice of charges, the agency may not raise those past problems during the course of the hearing. The Board will not sustain an agency action on the basis of charges that could have been brought, but were not. Carson v. VA, 29 MSPR 631, 633 (1986); see Leaton v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 331, 340 (1994) ("The agency contends on review that we should consider the appellant's previous 30-day suspension for a government vehicle-related offense, but we cannot do so because the agency did not advise him in the notice of proposed suspension that it intended to take his past disciplinary record into account."). A penalty may not be enhanced if the agency does not recite the prior discipline relied upon in the proposal letter. Harris v. Dept. of Transp., 29 MSPR 430, 432 (1985) (relying upon Burnette v. USPS, 12 MSPR 230, 233, 10 MSPB 760 (1982)); see Huisman v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 378, 381 n.* (1987) (when the AJ was required on remand to reconsider the penalty, the AJ could not consider a prior suspension that was not relied upon in the proposal and that was not relied upon by the deciding official); Reed v. USPS, 33 MSPR 9, 11 (1987) (there was no reference to past discipline in either the proposal or the decision letters); cf. Baldwin v. USPS, 26 MSPR 383, 386 (1985) (there was no error when a deciding official learned of past discipline that was not mentioned in the proposal or decision letters; the deciding official testified that he did not consider the prior discipline in determining to remove the appellant). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Past Discipline, Performance Record; Aggravating Factors" and "Framing of Charge."]

It is insufficient for past discipline to be mentioned in a proposal or decision letter. The record must contain documentary evidence showing that the appellant was informed of the prior actions in writing, that the actions were a matter of record, and that the appellant was permitted to dispute the charges before an authority different from the authority that took the actions against him. Lacking this record foundation, past discipline does not support an agency's penalty determination, meaning mitigation may be appropriate. See Schoeffler v. USDA, 47 MSPR 80, 89 (1991). Mingledough v. VA, 88 MSPR 452, 456-58 ¶¶ 7-10 (2001), helped to explain the necessity for clear notice in the adverse action proposal of the agency's intent to rely upon past discipline:

In its seminal decision in Douglas, the Board observed that

aggravating factors on which the agency intends to rely for imposition of an enhanced penalty, such as a prior disciplinary record, should be included in the advance notice of charges so that the employee will have a fair opportunity to respond to those alleged factors before the agency's deciding official, and the decision notice should explain what weight was given to those factors in reaching the agency's final decision.

5 MSPB 313, 5 MSPR at 304. In Westmoreland v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 83 MSPR 625 ¶ 9 (1999), a decision cited by the AJ, the Board found that it was improper for the agency to rely on the appellant's past disciplinary record where it was not cited in either the notice of proposed removal or in the decision notice, was not documented in the agency file, and was mentioned for the first time during the Board proceeding when the agency's proposing and deciding officials testified that they relied on the prior offenses in determining the penalty.

In its petition for review, the agency contends that the instant case can be distinguished in some respects from Westmoreland. Specifically, the agency argues that, unlike in Westmoreland, the decision notice here did cite the appellant's "past record" in determining that removal was appropriate, and the agency's file submitted in response to the appeal contained copies of the appellant's past disciplinary actions.... These distinctions do not, however, warrant the conclusion that the agency could properly consider the appellant's past disciplinary record. The Board has found that an agency's failure to state its reliance on an appellant's past disciplinary record in its notice of proposed removal is sufficient to prevent consideration of that disciplinary, record by the agency's deciding official or by the Board. See Thompson v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 41, 45 (1991), aff'd, 965 F.2d 1065 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

The agency contends, in effect, that any error in failing to include notice of reliance on the appellant's past disciplinary record in its notice of proposed removal was "cured" by the fact that the appellant himself addressed his past discipline. It cites, inter alia, Coppola v. U.S. Postal Service, 47 MSPR 307 (1991) in support of this contention. In Coppola, the Board found that the AJ properly considered and sustained a specification of absence without leave even though it was not mentioned specifically in the decision notice, where the proposal notice contained an allegation of unauthorized absence, the particulars of which were contained in an investigative memorandum to which the appellant responded. Id. at 313. Coppola is inapposite; it involved a failure to include information in a decision letter, not a failure to include information on which the agency intended to rely in a proposal notice. Moreover, the appellant's response to the March 3, 1999 proposal notice did not address his past disciplinary record. It is true, as the agency points out in its petition for review, that. the appellant addressed the circumstances of some of this past discipline in his hearing testimony. As the Board and our reviewing court have observed, however, the issue is one of due process at the agency level--the opportunity to respond to--the agency's deciding official. See Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Westmoreland, 83 MSPR 625 ¶ 8; Douglas, 5 MSPB 313, 5 MSPR at 304. Thus, the fact that the appellant had an opportunity to address his past discipline before the Board does not remedy the agency's failure to include it in the proposal notice.

Accordingly, we find that the AJ correctly found that the agency erred in considering the appellant's past disciplinary record. As the AJ properly noted, however, this error did not necessarily require mitigation of the penalty; the question was whether the removal penalty was within the bounds of reasonableness for the sustained charges, considering the pertinent factors other than the appellant's disciplinary record. See Groeber v. U.S. Postal Service, 84 MSPR 646 ¶¶ 10-11 (2000).
