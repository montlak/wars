4. Problem With Representation

The statutory right to be represented, 5 USC 7701(a)(2), is limited by the ability to obtain and retain counsel. Feathers v. OPM, 27 MSPR 485, 487 (1985). The Reform Act does not strictly provide a right to counsel, but is written broadly to confer a right to representation of the employee's choice. The representative need not be a licensed attorney. No statute or regulation requires the Board to appoint a representative for employees appealing to the Board. Yanopoulos v. Dept. of Navy, 796 F.2d 468 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Klant v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 374, 380-81, 5 MSPB 393 (1981) ("Appellant's final argument is that he is indigent and is therefore entitled to have a representative appointed by the Board free of charge." "[T]he Board's regulations do not provide for the appointment of counsel."). The appellant is not the victim of harmful error if he lacks counsel at an adverse action hearing. It is the appellant's responsibility, not the Board's, to secure legal counsel if he desires legal assistance. Dinkins v. USPS, 5 MSPR 165, 167, 5 MSPB 222 (1981). But see French v. OPM, 37 MSPR 496, 499 (1988) (suggesting that MSPB regional offices should attempt to secure pro bono counsel for mentally incompetent unrepresented appellants in retirement cases). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Assistance to Mentally Incompetent Appellant; French Procedure."] If an agency violates its own regulations on the release of its employees to serve as representatives at Board hearings, but the appellant secures other competent representation, there is no harmful error in the denial of the representative initially chosen. Padilla v. VA, 6 MSPR 494, 496-97, 6 MSPB 416 (1981). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Representatives."]

Appellants (and occasionally agencies) frequently complain that their representatives have inadequately prosecuted or defended their appeals. They argue that incompetent counsel, not misconduct or substandard performance, is responsible for the outcome of the appeal. As an adjudicative defect, incompetence of counsel probably ranks slightly below allegations of bias of AJs as a basis for attack on the initial decision. Although both complaints are common, neither has resulted in a reversal by the Board. The Board has not yet found one of its AJs biased, although it has on occasion reassigned cases to different AJs following complaints of bias or related concerns. As to counsel, the rule is that if the appellant or agency chooses incompetent counsel, the Board will not remedy the problem. Inadequate assistance of counsel is not reason for reversal on a PFR; the appellant is responsible for the action or inaction of his counsel. Tilley v. Frank, 728 F. Supp. 1293, 1297 (M.D. La. 1990) (mixed case review of MSPB result: "Plaintiff...is bound by the actions of the representative, both favorable and unfavorable."); Archuleta v. Dept. of Air Force, 16 MSPR 404, 406 (1983) ("With regard to the claim of ineffective counsel, appellant alleges that, despite his request, his representative failed to call any witnesses, including his doctors, to testify at the hearing. Additionally, appellant asserts that his representative did not know how to proceed at several points during the hearing. Inadequate counsel, however, even if true, is not grounds for reversal as appellant is held responsible for the action or inaction of his counsel."); Wilson v. USPS, 14 MSPR 129, 130 (1982) ("appellants are responsible for the actions, or inactions, of their representative, and thus cannot avoid the consequences of their choice"). Nor will a result be set aside because of an allegation that a nonlawyer representative chosen by an appellant was ineffective or inadequate. Mosely v. Dept. of Navy, 229 Ct. Cl. 718 (1981); Barnett v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 40, 42 (1984) (dismissing allegation of inadequate union representative).

Ineffective assistance of counsel is a defense that relates only to criminal proceedings, not to administrative cases. There is no denial, through ineffective assistance, of the statutory right to have representation under 5 USC 7701(a)(2). Rockwell v. Dept. of Transp., 789 F.2d 908 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Hijar v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 133, 134 n.1, 6 MSPB 121 (1981) ("while the Sixth Amendment relates to criminal proceedings and not civil administrative proceedings, appellant was not denied his statutory right to be represented"). An appellate court will not review the conduct of a hearing by a petitioner's chosen representative for alleged negligence. Catron v. Dept. of Energy (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-507). An appellant is responsible for research errors by his lawyer. Fontaine v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 457, 459 (1984). A charge that an appellant's attorney was incompetent because he did not call a favorable witness at a Board hearing did not alter the unfavorable result. Clancy v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-532). The failure of an appellant's attorney to provide the Board with information concerning a change of address did not excuse the failure to file information with the Board on a timely basis in response to orders affecting the conduct of the appeal. Corbett v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 368, 369 (1984).

An assertion of ineffective assistance of counsel was addressed in Bowen v. Dept. of Transp., 769 F.2d 753, 755 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Appellants alleged that their counsel was ineffective before the Board and that the employees' cases should be remanded for further hearing. Of the argument, the court said:

The statute, 5 USC 7513(b)(3) (1985), provides that an employee facing an adverse action is entitled to "be represented by an attorney or other representative." There is no statutory or regulatory requirement that representation be "effective." On the contrary, the Supreme Court has stated that a petitioner who has voluntarily selected an attorney to represent him in a civil action, "cannot...avoid the consequences of the acts or omissions of this freely selected agent." See Link v. Wabash Railroad Co., 370 U.S. 626, 633-34 (1962). The Court concluded:

Any other notion would be wholly inconsistent with our system of representative litigation, in which each party is deemed bound by the acts of his lawyer-agent and is considered to have "notice of all facts, notice of which can be charged upon the attorney."...

Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 721 F.2d 361, 365 (Fed. Cir. 1983), added:

...It is not the Board's duty, when the chosen representative shows a lack of diligence in his representation, to counsel an appellant in that regard, or substitute for the representative.... When chosen counsel appears, the Board cannot be expected to monitor that representative's actions or to assume that those actions fail to reflect the employee's own desires.

Regarding neglect of counsel causing untimely submissions, the Federal Circuit held in Buchanan v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1028):

While we are sympathetic to the exigencies of solo practice, including the problem of reading and responding to mail received during periods of vacation, it was counsel's responsibility nevertheless to make arrangements as necessary to ensure that his client's interests were protected during his absence. Petitioner is unfortunately bound by the inaction of his chosen representative.
