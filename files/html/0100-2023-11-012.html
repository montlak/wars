c. Manipulation or Redaction of Evidence

It is within the Board's discretion to make determinations as to the materiality of evidence. An agency may not intentionally circumvent Board authority by obliterating a portion of the evidentiary submissions it deems to be immaterial. To warrant remand, it need not be demonstrated that the obliterated material was exculpatory; it is sufficient that it might have led to exculpatory evidence or to other witnesses. See Harms v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 165, 166 (1986) (agency improperly redacted information from a portion of appellant's work product that an expert witness needed to review to offer his opinion of the appellant's work); Miller v. Dept. of Navy, 21 MSPR 731, 734 (1984) (citing Bize v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 155, 3 MSPB 261 (1980) (appellant is entitled to a full investigation report on the subject matter of his case, even though the agency claims it did not rely on the material in the removal proceedings)), following remand, 27 MSPR 227 (1985).

In Miller, supra, the Board remanded the case to the AJ for reconsideration based on a complete, rather than partially redacted, agency incident report that led to adverse action against the appellant, id. at 733-34:

In its response to appellant's petition for review, the agency admits that the referenced report is not the complete report of Captain Loving. It contends, however, that the omission of that information is totally appropriate; that the omitted information was not relied upon by the proposing or deciding officials, and was therefore immaterial. We cannot agree.

As the trier of fact, it is within the Board's discretion to make determinations on the materiality of evidence and to weigh the probative value to be accorded evidentiary submissions. See United States v. Evan, 572 F.2d 455, 490 (5th Cir. 1978), cert. denied, 439 U.S. 870, 99 S. Ct. 200, 58 L. Ed.2d 182 (1978); Compton-Richmond Co., Inc. v. Briggs, 560 F.2d 1195, 1201 n.12 (5th Cir. 1977); see also Borninkhof v. Department of Justice, 5 MSPB 150, 154 (1981). The agency may not intentionally circumvent the discretionary authority of this Board by obliterating certain segments of evidentiary submissions that it simply deems "immaterial." In Bize v. Department of the Treasury, 3 MSPB 261 (1980), the Board stressed that the appellant was entitled to be furnished all of the investigatory report relevant to the subject matter of his case even though the agency claimed it did not rely on that evidence in its decision to remove him.

In her initial decision the presiding official expressly stated that her findings were, in part, based on the report of investigation by Captain Loving. Since the report contained detailed elements of an investigation into the charges levied against appellant, it is apparent that the report was relied upon, as well, by the agency in arriving at its decision to take disciplinary action against appellant. It is reasonable, therefore, to conclude that even if the obliterated portion of the report was not exculpatory in nature, its contents could possibly lead to exculpatory evidence, other witness, etc. Hence, that portion of the report might be helpful to the appellant, as well as the presiding official, and might result in an outcome different than the one reached below.

Therefore, we conclude that the agency wrongfully obliterated the report of investigation by Captain Loving. While we find no apparent error in the evidentiary findings made by the presiding official on the basis of the present record, we find that the appellant, as well as the presiding official, should have been furnished the complete report. Therefore, this case must be remanded for further proceedings. Upon remand, the presiding official shall: (1) require the agency to produce the full and complete report of investigation by Captain Loving; (2) allow appellant an opportunity to make further presentations as may be warranted by the evidence; (3) determine if further proceedings are appropriate; and (4) issue a new initial decision taking into consideration the evidence and arguments advanced after production of the complete report insofar as they raise matters not already fully decided.

An agency's documentary evidence, though altered, will not be automatically excluded or denied probative value. The weight of the evidence will be determined upon consideration of the documents with explanatory testimony and evidence of the intent of custodians or authors of the documents. Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 827 F.2d 1564 (Fed. Cir. 1987). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Effect of Denial of Discovery."]

The right to a hearing implies due process and a fair hearing. Evidence must be made available to a party. [There is an exception for in camera submissions involving disputes over claims of privilege, a matter discussed in Chapter 2, "In Camera Inspection."] One party cannot seek to introduce into the record evidence from another Board case unless it makes available to the other party (as opposed to only providing access to the party) the evidence it seeks to have considered by the Board. Strauss v. OPM, 39 MSPR 132, 136-37 (1988); cf. Berry v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 95, 96 (1984) ("an initial decision issued in another case does not constitute new and material evidence" supporting a PFR; distinguishing outcome of two seemingly similar cases with different results). [Refer to "In Camera Inspection" in Chapter 2.]

One agency decided to hold its own impromptu hearing prior to the scheduled Board hearing and invited the appellant and his representative to attend. The agency then had a hearing of sorts: it transcribed the testimony from its own witness. The appellant and his representative did not attend. On the day of the Board hearing, the agency representative provided the AJ with a transcript of the ersatz agency hearing and provided no other evidence at the adverse action hearing convened by the Board. The Board concluded that it was error for the AJ to consider the agency transcript: there was extensive use of leading questions of agency witnesses; the agency representative did not appear to have the authority to administer oaths; and the agency's evidence was not of sufficient value to overcome contradictory live evidence from the appellant. The agency was admonished for its attempt to deny the appellant his statutory right to a hearing; counsel for the appellant was advised of his right to submit a fee petition. Riethmaier v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 73, 75-76, 6 MSPB 73 (1981). The decision is interesting because the Board had to go to some trouble to explain why the hearing transcript was not sufficient to establish a case. Many adverse action files prepared by agencies are little more than materials, including transcripts of investigatory interviews, gathered or created ex parte. Those interviews may be developed through leading questions. The agency procedure in Riethmaier was unusual, in the Board's estimation unfair, but it was a use of hearsay as evidence.

Evidence is supposed to be submitted by the parties. If a nonparty, e.g., an agency EEO office, insists upon transmitting evidence directly to the AJ, that information should be served by the AJ on the parties for their responses concerning its admissibility and significance. If the information cannot be provided to the parties, it cannot be considered by the AJ. See Sarich v. Dept. of Interior, 12 MSPR 144, 146-47 (1982).

The AJ is bound by considerations of fundamental fairness. In Lockner v. Dept. of Transp., 14 MSPR 352, 354 (1983), the Board remanded after determining that the AJ made a substantial number of incorrect evidentiary rulings concerning examination of witnesses and introduction and exclusion of evidence. The hearing was unfair because the AJ repeatedly allowed the agency representative to ask leading questions, permitted critical agency witnesses to testify from previously prepared statements without any indication that they could not recollect events, and chastised appellant's counsel for raising objections that were properly made. The Board said that although it would not strictly apply the Federal Rules of Evidence, the AJ's evidentiary errors severely curtailed appellant's substantive right to a fair and impartial hearing. See Borninkhof v. Dept. of Justice, 5 MSPR 77, 5 MSPB 150 (1981) (AJ improperly relied on witnesses who testified by reading from the investigatory record).

In one appeal that was determined not to be within its jurisdiction, there were sufficient questions concerning the agency's handling of documents that the Board admonished, Grose v. DHS, AT-3443-10-0427-I-1 (NP 9/14/2010):

...However, our review of the record reveals inconsistencies between the copy of the notice the appellant received from the agency, and the copy that the agency submitted to the Board as evidence. Specifically, it appears that the agency has altered the original document by attempting to erase boxes that were checked on the notice the appellant received that indicate that his application was disqualified for the following reasons: (1) Your application was not considered because it was received after the Vacancy Announcement closing date May 05, 2007; (2) You did not include the announcement number in your application as required by the Vacancy Announcement; (3) You are not a United States Citizen; and (4) There are currently no positions available. In addition, it also appears that the agency added the comment that the Selective Placement Factor box "WAS NOT CHECKED" after the notice was sent to the appellant, as that comment does not appear on the copy of the notice submitted by the appellant. Thus, it appears that this notation was written on the notice prior to submitting it to the administrative judge as evidence. We find the appearance that the agency has altered/falsified evidence that it has submitted to the Board in this case to be disturbing and unacceptable. Nevertheless, the agency's reason for not selecting the appellant for the position is irrelevant because the administrative judge correctly found that the Board does not have jurisdiction over nonselections.
