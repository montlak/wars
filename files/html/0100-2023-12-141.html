f. Reversal of Criminal Conviction

An appellant who is charged with conduct that is also subject to adjudication in the criminal justice system may seek to reopen a Board decision when subsequent criminal proceedings result in an acquittal or dismissal of the criminal charges. The evidence of the disposition of the criminal case is new, and if the civil service charges depended on the existence or outcome of the criminal charges, the evidence is material. Czubinski v. Dept. of Treasury, 76 MSPR 552, 556 (1997) (reversal of a conviction resulting in the appellant's removal was new evidence requiring the Board to grant a petition for review and warranting a reversal of the removal); see Pawn v. USDA, 82 MSPR 284, 286 ¶ 5 (1999) (reversal of a criminal conviction on judicial appeal warrants reversal of an adverse action based solely on conviction). Garriga v. VA, 13 MSPR 204, 207 (1982), stated of the materiality of reversal of a conviction when the civil service charges are based upon the conduct leading to the conviction:

With regard to the fact that appellant was arrested and indicted but subsequently acquitted by a jury at his trial, the Board further finds that the subsequent acquittal is irrelevant in this administrative proceeding inasmuch as the agency action was based, not on the fact of the arrest and indictment, but on the misconduct that led to appellant's apprehension and arrest. Since the cause relied on was the appellant's act of wrongdoing, the adverse action and administrative proceedings thereupon is not affected by any subsequent court action in a criminal case. Austin v. Department of Justice, 10 MSPB 221, 224, 11 MSPR 255, 260 (1982); see, also, Finfer v. Caplin, 344 F.2d 38 (2d Cir.), cert. den., sub nom. Finfer v. Cohen, 382 U.S. 883, 86 S. Ct. 177, 15 L. Ed.2d 124 (1965)....

If the adverse action depended on proof of facts and not proof of criminal charges or a conviction, evidence of the disposition of the criminal proceedings is not material, Larry v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 348, 355 (1997):

The appellant further argues on review that "it is significant" that the criminal charges related to this misconduct charge were dismissed. The record below shows that the criminal charges were indeed dismissed. To the extent the appellant is arguing that the dismissal of the criminal charges warrants not sustaining the "off-duty misconduct" charge, we disagree. The undisputed fact that the appellant was criminally "charged" based on his misconduct was included as part of the agency's factual description in support of the overall misconduct charge; the misconduct charge was based on the appellant's conduct rather than the merits of the criminal charges themselves. Nor did the agency's proof rely on the criminal proceedings. In such situations, the misconduct charge may be sustained notwithstanding the dismissal of the criminal charges. See Farrier v. VA, 60 MSPR 71, 74 & n.3 (1993.); Hofmann v. Dept. of Agric., 31 MSPR 399, 402 (1986); cf. Payne v. USPS, 69 MSPR 503, 508 (1996) (where the appellant was removed solely based on the fact of his criminal conviction, not on the underlying misconduct, the reversal of the conviction warrants reversing the removal).

To the extent that the agency's charge may be construed as including a separate specification that the appellant was criminally charged, the dismissal of the criminal charges would negate such a specification. This would not warrant reversing the charge, however. See Hicks v. Dept. of Treasury, 62 MSPR 71, 74 (1994) (the agency is required to prove only the essence of its charge, and need not prove each factual specification in support of the charge), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1235 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); Royster v. Dept. of Justice, 58 MSPR 495, 498 (1993) (proof of one or more of the supporting specifications may be sufficient to sustain the charge). We therefore find that the appellant has failed to show that the AJ erred in sustaining this charge.

See Pflanz v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 71, 73 (1984) ("[A]n appellant's acquittal on criminal charges is not relevant in the administrative proceeding where the agency action is based upon the misconduct which led to the criminal charge and not on the fact of arrest or indictment."); see also DePappe v. USPS, 27 MSPR 565, 565 (1985) (same result as Pflanz); cf. Doucet v. Dept. of Navy, 63 Fed. Appx. 512 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (acquittal in a criminal case related but not tied to misconduct charged in a removal did not require the Board to set aside a settlement of the removal action). Addressing not reversal of a criminal conviction but post-removal restoration of a medical license required to be a VA medical professional, Cerwonka v. VA, 915 F.3d 1351, 1358 (Fed. Cir. 2019), noted that "[t]he Board's role in these cases is limited to reviewing Federal agency personnel actions and determining whether those actions were proper at the time they were made."
