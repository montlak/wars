4. Withdrawal of Hearing Request

The right to a hearing is significant enough that the Board requires its AJs to ensure that when a hearing request is withdrawn, the withdrawal is clear and informed, Graves v. USPS, 106 MSPR 224, 226-27 ¶¶ 4-6, 2007 MSPB 171 (2007):

An appellant before the Board has the right to withdraw his request for a hearing. Conant v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 148, 150 (1998). However, there is a strong policy in favor of granting an appellant a hearing on the merits of his case, and therefore withdrawal of a hearing request must come by way of clear, unequivocal, or decisive action. Id. Further, the decision to withdraw a hearing request must be informed, i.e., the appellant must be fully apprised of the relevant adjudicatory requirements and options, including the right to request a postponement or continuance of the hearing and a dismissal of the appeal without prejudice to its timely refiling. Id. at 150-51.

The prehearing conference in this appeal took place on November 7, 2006. There is no indication in the AJ's summary of that conference that the appellant expressed any interest in withdrawing his hearing request at that time. The hearing was scheduled for 7 days later. On the eve of the hearing, the appellant submitted a handwritten note stating that he wanted to withdraw from the hearing and that he would instead file a written statement in support of his appeal. The appellant then wrote, "However[,] the [appeal] trail is still active." The AJ issued an order the same day stating that the appellant had indicated during a November 13, 2006, teleconference that he wished to withdraw his hearing request and have his appeal decided on the written record.

His statement that "the [appeal] trail is still active" raises doubts as to whether the appellant fully understood that he was completely waiving his right to have a hearing in this matter. Given that statement, it is possible that the appellant thought he was merely postponing the hearing. Moreover, there is nothing in the record establishing that the appellant was apprised of any alternatives to withdrawing his hearing request. Therefore, given the strong policy in favor of granting an appellant a hearing on the merits of his appeal, and the lack of any memorialization in the record of what occurred during the November 13 teleconference when the appellant sought to withdraw his hearing request, we must remand the appeal in order to provide the appellant the hearing he initially requested. See Gallegos v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 90 M.S.P.R. 159, ¶ 8 (2001) (the Board remanded the appeal for further adjudication in part because it was unclear whether the appellant fully understood the consequences of withdrawing her hearing request).

Peraza v. OPM, 114 MSPR 457, 463-64 ¶ 15, 2010 MSPB 152 (2010), emphasized that an AJ should ensure that the withdrawal of a hearing request is informed:

On remand, the administrative judge shall also provide the appellant with a renewed opportunity for a hearing at which he can present evidence and argument regarding equitable estoppel. In his response to OPM's PFR, the appellant appears to allege that he did not make an informed decision to withdraw his request for a hearing. Specifically, the appellant states as follows:

When Judge Hudson suggested that we eliminate hearings and confine ourselves to submitting written documents, we did not think to ask her what we would be giving up or what risks we would be taking, or to compare the two alternatives to each other, nor did she raise these possibilities or volunteer explanations.

An appellant before the Board has the right to withdraw his request for a hearing; however, there is a strong policy in favor of granting an appellant a hearing on the merits of his case, and therefore, withdrawal of a hearing request must come by way of clear, unequivocal, or decisive action. Pariseau v. Department of the Air Force, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 9 (2010); Conant v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 148, 150 (1998). Further, the decision to withdraw a hearing request must be informed, i.e., the appellant must be fully apprised of the relevant adjudicatory requirements and options. Pariseau, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 9. In light of the Board's policy in favor of granting an appellant a hearing on the merits of his appeal, it was incumbent on the administrative judge to inform the appellant of his alternatives to withdrawing his hearing request. Here, the appellant alleges that the administrative judge failed to do so, and the record does not indicate otherwise, as there is no evidence that the administrative judge apprised the appellant of his alternatives to withdrawing his hearing request. Moreover, as discussed above, the appellant made the decision to withdraw his request for a hearing without notice of the proper standard to be applied in asserting a claim of equitable estoppel. Therefore, the administrative judge should afford the appellant a hearing on remand if he requests one. See Blaha, 106 M.S.P.R. 265, ¶ 12 (directing the administrative judge to afford the appellant a hearing on remand if she requested one, given that her decision to waive a hearing below was made without notice of the proper standard to be applied in asserting a claim of equitable estoppel).

Informed waivers were reemphasized, Pour v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-14-0672-I-1 (NP 3/28/2016):

During the recorded portion of the October 2, 2014 conference call, the appellant's representative clearly indicated that the appellant wished to withdraw his request for a hearing and obtain a decision on the written record. However, the record does not contain audio or written documentation of the circumstances surrounding the withdrawal of the hearing request. Crucially, the administrative judge did not document that the appellant or his representative, neither of whom is an attorney, was informed of any alternatives to withdrawing the hearing request, such as a postponement or continuance of the hearing or dismissal without prejudice. See Pariseau v. Department of the Air Force, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 10 (2010); Graves v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 6 (2007); Conant, 79 M.S.P.R. at 151. Hence, even though the appellant's decision was clear and unequivocal, the record does not show that his decision was informed. See Pariseau, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 10. Therefore, given the strong policy in favor of granting a hearing on the merits of his appeal, we must remand the case to provide the appellant the hearing he initially requested and evidently still desires. See Pariseau, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 10; Graves, 106 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 6; Conant, 79 M.S.P.R. at 151-52.

Coons v. Dept. of Treasury, SF-1221-99-0385-B-7 (NP 2/11/2011), summarized the law:

An appellant before the Board has the right to withdraw his request for a hearing. Pariseau v. Department of the Air Force, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 9 (2010); Graves v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 4 (2007); Conant v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 148, 150 (1998). However, the withdrawal of a hearing request must come by way of clear, unequivocal and decisive action. Pariseau, 113 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 9. In addition, the decision to withdraw a hearing request must be informed. Id.

The appellant does not withdraw a hearing request by advising the AJ that "no hearing was necessary" and who moves "for immediate reinstatement," if the appellant does not withdraw the hearing request that she initially made. Aldridge v. USDA, 111 MSPR 670, 673 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 146 (2009); cf. Sattlefield v. OPM, CH-844E-13-4542-I-1 (NP 10/7/2014) ("Regarding the appellant's withdrawal of her hearing request, although the appellant may now regret that decision, she is ultimately responsible for the prosecution of her own appeal and cannot escape the consequences of her decision to rely on the advice of her attorney. See Fairchilde v. Department of the Interior, 36 M.S.P.R. 586, 588 (1988).").

Although a hearing is the employee's option, an AJ may dismiss an appeal with prejudice if a hearing withdrawal occurs as part of what the Board concludes is a pattern of events demonstrating failure properly to prosecute an appeal or, alternatively, failure to honor an AJ's orders governing the processing of the appeal. See Davis v. Dept. of Commerce, 120 MSPR 34, 40 ¶ 17-18, 2013 MSPB 56 (2013).
