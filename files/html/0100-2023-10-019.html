c. Nursing Personnel

Section 5 USC 7103(a)(10) classifies nurses as supervisors only if a preponderance of their employment time is devoted to the exercise of supervisory authority.

Head nurses at a VA hospital were supervisors. Although they were responsible for direct patient care, and although they were on-call through the day and week, their primary working hours were shifts involving the exercise of supervisory functions. They approved leave, recommended hiring, promotions, step increases, awards, and completed proficiency reports of employees. They effectively recommended retention and discharge of probationary employees. They attended management meetings and served on hospital committees. The preponderance of the head nurse's time was in a supervisory role, giving direction to subordinates, checking patients to ensure that they were properly attended. It was immaterial that the head nurse spent some time, especially during emergencies or absences of regular staff, on direct patient care. VAMC, Fayetteville, N.C. and AFGE, 8 FLRA 651, 657 (1982); see VAMC, Lyons, N.J. and AFGE, 14 FLRA 46, 47 (1984) (head nurses were supervisors when they assigned and directed work, effectively recommended disciplinary actions, and effectively recommended subordinates for awards; their duties were not routine or clerical, but required consistent exercise of independent judgment); VA and AFGE, 11 FLRA 176, 178 (1983) (head nurses were supervisors: they assigned work, disciplined employees, and effectively recommended employees for award; although they were directly involved in patient care, their principal work was devoted to supervision). Head nurses for the operating and recovery wards and head nurses for wards, who performed functions similar to the nursing care supervisors, were statutory supervisors. VA Hosp., Tucson and Nurses Comm'n on Collective Bargaining, Arizona Nurses Ass'n, 4 FLRA 122, 123-25 (1980). Under VA Hosp., Tucson, a VA Nursing Home Care Unit supervisor was a supervisor. Most of her time was devoted to administering the unit rather than personally participating in direct patient care. She assigned work and rated employees. Her authority included granting leave, initiating discipline, and settling grievances. A nursing care supervisor qualified as a statutory supervisor. She approved proficiency reports and scheduling, assigned nurses to units, initiated discipline, resolved grievances, initiated promotion recommendations for head nurses, concurred in promotion decisions for staff nurses, authorized leave requests, and conducted staff meetings to discuss patient care problems and new policies. VAMC Asheville and AFGE, 68 FLRA 28 (2014), determined, following detailed analysis of their work, that nursing coordinators were supervisors: they routinely assigned and directed work of employees, spending a preponderance of their employment time exercising that authority.

A clinical infection control nurse, who made on-the-spot corrections to other employees, was not a supervisor when those corrections did not generally result in discipline and the nurse performed no other supervisory duties. Dept. of Army, Womack Army Med. Ctr. and AFGE Local 1770, 63 FLRA 22 (2008). "Nurse officers of the day" were not supervisors. The record did not show that they spent a majority of their employment time on assigning and directing employees, although that was a part of their responsibilities. VAMC Hampton and AFGE, 65 FLRA 364 (2010). For consideration of whether nurses are supervisors under the National Labor Relations Act, refer to NLRB v. Health Care & Retirement Corp., 114 S. Ct. 1778 (1994).
