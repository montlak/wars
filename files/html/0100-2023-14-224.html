broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 08: Disciplinary Procedures and Standards, Section J: Fiduciary Employees; Pecuniary Liability; Claims

J. FIDUCIARY EMPLOYEES; PECUNIARY LIABILITY; CLAIMS

A cap on financial liability of employees for loss, damage to, or destruction of government property was inconsistent with the agency right to control its internal security practices by establishing a management plan to eliminate or minimize risks to government property. The proposal directly infringed management's rights and was not merely a procedure under § 7106(b)(2). NFFE Local 29 and Dept. of Army, Kansas City Dist., Corps of Eng'rs, Kansas City Dist., 21 FLRA 233, 233-35 (1986). A similar limit on pecuniary liability for damage to government property (limited to an amount no greater than one month's salary, and only for gross negligence), was outside the duty to bargain because it interfered directly with management's right to determine its internal security practices. The revision of the existing standard from negligence to gross negligence abrogated management's right to set the standard of recovery and excessively interfered with management's rights, removing it from the shelter of an appropriate arrangement. NAGE Local R7-23 and Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB, 23 FLRA 753, 758 (1986), aff'd on remand, NAGE Local R7-23 and Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB, 27 FLRA 768 (1987) (following NFFE Local 29 and Dept. of Army, Army Corps of Eng'rs, Kansas City Dist., 27 FLRA 404 (1987), post); see AFGE Local 3529 and DOD, DCA, 57 FLRA 456, 458 (2001) (definition of "gross negligence," for purposes of financial liability, set standard for agency plan to prevent damage or loss to agency property, interfering in agency's right to determine internal security practices).

A proposal limiting pecuniary liability for loss, damage, or destruction of property to gross negligence, rather than to a negligence standard, was not negotiable. It infringed the right to determine internal security practices. AFGE Council 214 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq., Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB, 21 FLRA 244, 244-46 (1986), aff'd on remand, 27 FLRA 814 (1987), on the basis of NFFE Local 29 and Dept. of Army, Army Corps of Eng'rs, Kansas City Dist., 27 FLRA 404, 406 (1987), post; see Air Force and NAGE Local R1-8, 21 FLRA 957, 959-60 (1986) ("union proposals which seek to limit the amount of money an employee may be required to pay for lost or damaged property, or proposals which seek to establish the standard to be used in determining whether an employee will be held pecuniarily liable for the loss, damage, or destruction of government property, directly interfere with management's right to determine its internal security practices under section 7106(a)(1) of the Statute."). If management sets the recovery standard pursuant to statute at negligence, the union may pursue to an arbitrator whether the facts demonstrated the employee's negligence. See NTEU Chap. 15 and IRS, 33 FLRA 229, 237-38 (1988) (on exceptions to arbitration award).

A union could not compel negotiation of a proposal that the agency indemnify employees or excuse their losses incurred as certifying officers. A statute provided that employees would reimburse the government for improper losses. The proposal nullified the statutory requirement. A proposal was not negotiable that altered the burden of proof and the procedures involved under the statute to determine liability of the certifying officer to the government. NTEU and IRS, San Francisco, 14 FLRA 65, 67-70 (1984). A nonnegotiable proposal required management to choose between assessment of financial liability or disciplinary action for loss, damage, or destruction of government property. The proposal interfered with the right of management to impose discipline by conditioning it on the exercise of an internal security practice. Additionally, a cap of $150 on pecuniary liability was nonnegotiable. NFFE Local 29 and Army Corps of Eng'rs, Kansas City Dist., 21 FLRA 233, 233-36 (1986). The Local 29 case was reconsidered by FLRA after remand from the D.C. Circuit. On remand, FLRA concluded, NFFE Local 29 and Dept. of Army, Army Corps of Eng'rs, Kansas City Dist., 27 FLRA 404, 406 (1987):

On reconsideration, we find that the determinations of when to hold employees pecuniarily liable as well as of the amount of such liability are both directly related to the Agency's adoption of pecuniary liability as an internal security practice. They are substantive determinations as opposed to being merely procedural in nature. The proposals to limit the extent of liability on the one hand, and to foreclose it under certain circumstances on the other, are inconsistent with the Agency's right under the...Statute to determine the substance of its internal security practices, that is, whether and to what extent to impose pecuniary liability.

Additionally, on reconsideration, we find that Proposal 2 interferes with the Agency's right to determine its internal security practices by conditioning the exercise of that right, that is, the imposition of pecuniary liability, on the relinquishment of its right to impose discipline. As noted in our original decision in this case, a proposal which establishes a condition on the exercise of a management right interferes with the exercise of that right.

FLRA added in UPTO and Army Corps of Eng'rs, N. Pac. Div., 30 FLRA 639, 644 (1987), declaring nonnegotiable another proposal designed to limit employees' pecuniary liability:

In our Decision on Remand in National Federation of Federal Employees Local 29 and Department of the Army, Kansas City District, U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, Kansas City, Missouri, 27 FLRA 404 (1987), we resolved the apparent inconsistency in our original decision. We held that proposals which limit the extent of an employee's pecuniary liability or which foreclose pecuniary liability under certain circumstances are inconsistent with the Agency's right under section 7106(a)(1) of the Statute to determine the substance of its internal security practices, that is, whether and to what extent to impose pecuniary liability.

But see VA Jefferson Barracks Nat'l Cemetery and NAGE Local R14-116, 61 FLRA 861 (2006) (arbitrator did not violate right of management to determine its internal security by determining that agency erred in concluding that employee was negligent and liable for damage to agency property).
