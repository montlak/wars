1. Lifetime Annuity

The significance of court orders apportioning annuities was emphasized, Liversedge v. OPM, 36 MSPR 80, 82 (1988):

OPM is only authorized to make payments to an individual other than the annuitant as provided under 5 U.S.C. § 8345(j)(1). This statutory provision provides in pertinent part that:

Payments...which would otherwise be made payable to an annuitant based upon his service shall be paid (in whole or in part) by the Office to another person if and to the extent expressly provided for in the terms of any court decree of divorce, annulment, or legal separation, or the terms of any court order or court-approved property settlement agreement incident to any court decree of divorce, annulment, or legal separation.

Thus, OPM is only authorized to apportion an annuity pursuant to a court order. See Chapman v. Office of Personnel Management, 33 M.S.P.R. 426, 429 (1987). Because the appellant's claim to a portion of her former spouse's annuity is not based on a court order but on Puerto Rican law, OPM cannot, by statute, apportion her former spouse's annuity. Moreover, the appellant has not challenged the administrative judge's determination that she is not entitled to a survivor annuity based on the federal government service of her former spouse.

The apportionment authority and processes were explained, Chapman v. OPM, 33 MSPR 426, 427-30 (1987):

Appellant retired in 1981 and was granted a final decree of divorce by a California Superior Court in 1984. Although his marriage was finally dissolved as of September 6, 1984, final judgment on the disposition of appellant's civil service retirement pension was reserved by the court. That matter was finally decided by a court order issued June 26, 1985.

As set forth in the initial decision, appellant had contended before the administrative judge that the judgment issued by the court was in error because it did not reflect the fact that appellant's former spouse had agreed, during the proceedings on the disposition of the property, to receive only one-half of the apportioned share of appellant's annuity allotted to her by the court order of June 26, 1985. The administrative judge found that the only method of altering an erroneous court order was to seek an amendment in the court where the judgment was entered, and he therefore affirmed OPM's reconsideration decision of December 16, 1985, which held that it would honor the court order and would allocate payments in accordance with it.[2]

[2] Pursuant to the express terms of an appropriate court order, OPM is authorized to allocate, to another person, annuity payments which would normally be payable to an annuitant. See 5 U.S.C. § 8345(j); 5 C.F.R. § 831.1701, et seq.

In connection with his appeal to the Board from this reconsideration decision, appellant submitted a copy of the supplemental trial brief prepared by his former spouse's attorney in connection with the court proceedings on the pension issue. This brief stated that appellant's former spouse's share of appellant's civil service pension should be one-half of the amount the court subsequently ordered. Appellant also submitted a notarized statement by him and his former spouse addressed to OPM and dated January 20, 1986, stating that she and the appellant had agreed that she would receive only a 50% share of the amount of the annuity that had been allotted by the court. On the basis of this notarized statement, the administrative judge found that the parties had entered into a settlement pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 831.1718[3] and ordered OPM to adjust the annuity of appellant's former spouse to reflect that she would receive only 50% of the apportioned share.

[3] This regulation provides in pertinent part as follows:

The former spouse may request that an amount be withheld from the retirement benefits that is less than the amount stipulated in the court order. This lower amount will be deemed a complete fulfillment of the obligation of OPM for the period in which the request is in effect.

OPM contends in its petition for review that the administrative judge improperly modified its reconsideration decision because a request for such modification must be filed with OPM under 5 C.F.R. § 831.1718, so that the administrative judge improperly and prematurely assumed jurisdiction over the matter. OPM states that, when it receives a request pursuant to this section, it contacts the former spouse to make certain the request is genuine before rendering its decision. OPM states further that it will immediately begin processing the request for modification by the appellant's former spouse in this case.

...

OPM is authorized to make annuity payments to an individual other than the annuitant only as provided for at 5 U.S.C. § 8345(j)(1). This statutory provision expressly provides, in pertinent part, as follows:

Payments under this subchapter which would otherwise be made to an annuitant shall be paid in whole or in part by the Office if and to the extent expressly provided by the terms of any court decree of divorce, annulment or legal separation, or the terms of any court order or court-approved property settlement incident to any court decree of divorce, annulment, or legal separation.

Thus, since the only court decree apportioning appellant's annuity in the instant case is that of June 26, 1985, OPM was obliged to follow it pursuant to the above-cited statute. See also 5 C.F.R. § 831.1704 et seq.; Office of Personnel Management v. Dargel, 20 M.S.P.R. 48 (1984) (OPM is obligated by applicable statute and regulation to give effect to a qualifying court order.)

Moreover, there is no provision in applicable statute or regulation pursuant to which the administrative judge could give effect to the notarized statement of appellant's former spouse as a settlement agreement. This statement is not a "court-approved property settlement" within the terms of the relevant statute. In addition, as the OPM notes in its petition for review, applicable regulations pertaining to settlements provide that appellant's former spouse may request a lesser share of appellant's annuity. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.1718.

Thus, since OPM is giving effect to the existing valid court ordered property settlement in this case, and since it is now considering the request of appellant's former spouse under section 831.1718, there is no matter properly before the Board because OPM has not yet issued its reconsideration decision on the request. See Lawrence v. Department of the Army, 9 M.S.P.R. 419 (1982) (appeal to the Board held premature where appeal was from agency action and OPM had not issued a reconsideration decision).

See Drob v. OPM, 59 MSPR 91, 93 (1993) (relying on Liversedge: a court decree, not state law, governs apportionment for former spouse); cf. Irving v. OPM, DE-0843-16-0327-I-1 (NP 7/13/2022) ("To the extent the appellant challenges on review the administrative judge's finding that she is not entitled to an apportionment of her former husband's annuity, we discern no basis upon which to disturb that finding. Regardless of the fact that the 2012 court order purported to assign the appellant an interest in her former husband's annuity, the annuity 'was not in pay status,' at the time of his death because he had not yet retired. 5 C.F.R. § 838.222(c). Therefore, there was no annuity to be apportioned.").

With extensive statutory analysis, the Board concluded in Evans v. OPM, 59 MSPR 94, 101-02 (1993), that although an annuitant may waive an annuity pursuant to 5 USC 8345(d), once a qualified court order is entered granting an apportionment of an annuity to a former spouse, the waiver may not defeat the annuity awarded to the beneficiary by the state court. Donlan v. OPM, 907 F.2d 1132, 1133 (Fed. Cir. 1990), approved an apportionment, noting in the court's discussion of the unsuccessful appeal by the annuitant to prevent the division ordered by a state court:

On November 13, 1987, Mr. Donlan filed a request with the Office of Personnel Management to receive a lump-sum payment of his retirement contributions. However, Ms. Donlan filed several certified copies of divorce decrees which had been issued by the Circuit Court of Montgomery County, Maryland and requested to receive a portion of Mr. Donlan's Civil Service Retirement System benefits. The state court's Amended Judgment of Absolute Divorce, dated December 13, 1984, provided, in part, as follows:

ORDERED, that if, as and when Defendant [Mr. Donlan] receives retirement pension benefits, whether he may elect one lump sum or several periodic payments, Plaintiff [Ms. Donlan] shall receive a fraction of each payment in accord with her marital contribution. Such amount shall be calculated as follows: 27 (number of years of marriage) divided by the total number of years of Defendant's employment credited toward his retirement; this fraction shall then be multiplied by 50%....
