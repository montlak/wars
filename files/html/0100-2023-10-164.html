d. Examination of Witness

AJs often ask questions of witnesses. No rule prevents the practice, and Federal Rule of Evidence 614(b) allows a judge to ask questions of a witness. Floch v. VA, 348 Fed. Appx. 573, 576 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP). Vigorous questioning of an appellant by the AJ does not reflect bias, despite a critical tone in the examination: the AJ is responsible for developing a clear record and he may question a witness, before or after examination of the witness by counsel, to forestall prolonged examination or to clarify points raised during testimony. The existence and reflection of feeling in the course of a trial does not disqualify an AJ or call for reversal. Richardson v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 186, 195 (1982); see Conley v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 20, 22 (1984) (bias is not demonstrated by an AJ's attempts during the examination of a witness to clarify testimony and to define relevant legal terms). Bias is not demonstrated by objections to questions from an AJ, even without objections from the opposing representative. McGhee v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 122, 125 (1982). Interruption of a hearing to take the testimony of witnesses in another case is not bias, even though it may cause some inconvenience or confusion for the appellant. Madison v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 465, 469 (1987). Interruption of testimony, without its resumption, does not necessarily demonstrate bias. See Matosian v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 689, 696 (1993) ("[T]hat the administrative judge overruled the appellant's objections, sustained the agency's objections, and cut off the appellant's testimony and his questioning of agency witnesses also does not constitute bias"; "[T]he limits...were consistent with his ruling that collateral estoppel applied to the appellant's disability discrimination claim"[.] ); cf. Jenkins v. DHHS, DC-0752-12-0586-B-2 (NP 2/2/2016) ("The appellant also contends that the administrative judge improperly coached a Human Resources (HR) Specialist's testimony regarding whether the proposing official wanted the appellant to be removed from the outset of the agency's investigation into the appellant's misconduct. At the hearing, the appellant, who was represented by an attorney, did not object to the administrative judge's conduct in addressing the witness. As such, he is precluded from raising this argument now. See Brown, 57 M.S.P.R. at 625."); cf. Bennett v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-12-0183-I-3 (NP 1/20/2023) ("In fact, the administrative judge's rulings, which permitted an extensive number of witnesses, a lengthy hearing, and several extensions and dismissals without prejudice to refiling, suggest that she conducted a fair and impartial proceeding for this pro se appellant. Thus, we find no showing of bias by the administrative judge.").
