g. Retirement Credit, Contributions, and Incentives; Individual Retirement Record

In Grasso v. IRS, 4 MSPR 119, 4 MSPB 205 (1980), aff'd, 657 F.2d 224, post, the appellant was suspended for five days and reassigned from a GS-7 Criminal Investigator position to a GS-7 Internal Revenue Agent position. He claimed that he was a victim of an adverse action, asserting that the reassignment resulted in reduction of his retirement credit by 1% per year, deprived him of an optional 20-year retirement right, and prevented his promotion to GS-9. The Board affirmed the initial decision dismissing the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. The Board did not have jurisdiction over the short suspension, the reassignment, or deprivation of promotion. As to reduction in pay, although a reduction is appealable under 5 USC 7512(4), "pay" is defined by 5 USC 7511(a)(4) as the rate of basic pay established by 5 USC 5332, a pay level unaffected by the reassignment. The Board also considered whether it had jurisdiction under 5 USC 8347(d), granting appeal rights based on the loss of certain retirement benefits. The alleged loss was of benefits under 5 USC 8336(c), providing optional retirement for law enforcement officers after 20 years of service, and § 8339(d), setting the method for computing the annuity of one who retires under 8336(c). Because the appellant was a law enforcement officer before the reassignment, he was able to accrue service toward meeting the requirements of 8336(c) and was subject to higher deductions toward his account than are regular employees. The Board noted that under Hein v. OPM, 1 MSPR 410, 416, 1 MSPB 396 (1980), its scope of review under 5 USC 8347(d) is specified by what is now 5 CFR 1201.3(a)(2) (2022), providing that the Board's appellate jurisdiction includes determinations relating to disability retirement and annuities. Because MSPB only becomes involved in a retirement determination when the employee becomes eligible to file a claim, the Board had no jurisdiction over the retirement aspect of the appeal. The appellant had not then met age and service requirements necessary for annuity entitlement. A court affirmed the Board's view that in determining whether a downgrading has occurred, only basic pay entitlements are considered; changes in possible retirement benefits do not confer Board jurisdiction. See Grasso v. IRS, 657 F.2d 224, 225 (8th Cir. 1981) ("While the loss of the 20-year retirement eligibility benefit does seem a significant one, there is nothing in the case law or legislative history to suggest that it must be construed as a reduction in pay under section 7512."). The Board declined jurisdiction over an appellant's reassignment from a secondary to a primary LEO position. The Board could have taken jurisdiction over an agency's final decision denying approval of a job as an LEO position, but the appellant's position remained LEO. Kirwan v. Dept. of Transp., 88 MSPR 445, 448 ¶ 6 (2001).

The Board does not have jurisdiction to direct a refund of retirement contributions, a process initially within the province of OPM, subject to an MSPB appeal from the OPM annuity determination. See Smith v. Dept. of Interior, 29 MSPR 19, 20 n.* (1985). Nor does the Board have jurisdiction over a claim that a retirement incentive should have been offered or that it should have been offered in a larger amount, Johnson v. USPS, 67 MSPR 573, 577 (1995):

The Board does not have jurisdiction over all matters involving a Federal employee that are allegedly unfair or incorrect. See Dockery v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 64 MSPR 458, 462 (1994); Weyman v. Department of Justice, 58 MSPR 509, 512 (1993). Rather, the Board's jurisdiction is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by statute or regulation. Lary v. OPM, 65 MSPR 291, 298 (1994); Shaw v. Department of the Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 588 (1989). We are unaware of any statute or regulation that gives the Board jurisdiction over a claim that an employee was not offered as generous a retirement buyout as were other agency employees. Further, the record readily reflects, and the appellant admits, that he occupied the same grade and position and received the same pay before and after the agency's nationwide restructuring. Thus, there is no indication in the record that he has suffered an adverse action or RIF over which the Board has jurisdiction.

To the extent that the appellant's assertion on petition for appeal that older Postmasters were offered incentives to encourage them to retire and "make room for minorities and non-veterans" could be construed as a claim of prohibited discrimination, we note that the Board does not have jurisdiction to hear a discrimination claim absent an otherwise appealable action, see Tanner v. Dept. of Transportation, 65 MSPR 169, 172 (1994); Hundley v. USPS, 64 MSPR 598, 602 (1994). Because there is no underlying appealable action in this appeal, the Board lacks jurisdiction to hear the appellant's discrimination claim. Tanner, 65 MSPR at 172; Hundley, 64 MSPR at 602.

Hoxie v. DHS, SF-752S-13-0482-I-1 (NP 3/6/2014), disclaimed jurisdiction over a claim that a one-day suspension would lead to an adverse impact on a future pay increase and retirement benefits:

...The appellant's argument that his suspension will have an adverse effect on his future pay and potential promotional opportunities also fails to establish the Board's jurisdiction over this appeal. See Deida v. Department of the Navy, 110 M.S.P.R. 408, ¶ 13 (2009) (it is well settled that promotions generally are not appealable to the Board); Scott v. Department of Justice, 105 M.S.P.R. 482, ¶ 8 (2007) (finding that a loss of pay based on "speculative future earnings" does not establish Board jurisdiction).

...The Board, moreover, lacks jurisdiction over an otherwise nonappealable action based on the possible future impact that action will have on retirement benefits. McEnery v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 963 F.2d 1512, 1514-15 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Grasso v. Internal Revenue Service, 4 M.S.P.R. 119, 122-23 (1980), aff'd, (8th Cir. 1981). The appellant's theory that any action which may impact an employee's retirement benefits establishes Board jurisdiction, if adopted, would enlarge the Board's jurisdiction to near limitless proportions. Such a theory is counter to the long-standing principle that the Board's jurisdiction is not plenary; it is strictly defined and confined by statute and regulation. Bolton v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 154 F.3d 1313, 1316 (Fed. Cir. 1998).

For years, the Board declined to involve itself in disputes over the accuracy of an agency individual retirement record (IRR) showing service and retirement contribution information for employees. Then, as a result of Lisanti v. OPM, 573 F.3d 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2009), matters changed and now, according to Denney v. OPM, 117 MSPR 269, 274-75 n.4, 2012 MSPB 9 (2012), the accuracy of an IRR can be challenged through a retirement appeal:

In Lisanti, the court modified prior Board decisions holding or implying that OPM and the Board are bound by the employing agency's IRR and that an appellant's remedy for a claim that the IRR is erroneous lies with seeking an amended IRR from the employing agency. See Lee v. Office of Personnel Management, 108 M.S.P.R. 321, ¶ 7 (2008); Rainone v. Office of Personnel Management, 104 M.S.P.R. 423, ¶ 7 (2007); O'Connell v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 579 (2006). The court specifically rejected OPM's reliance on these decisions, finding that the IRR is not necessarily binding, and that, absent a clear Congressional intent to the contrary, both OPM and the Board are required to "entertain" a challenge to the employing agency's interpretation of the term "basic pay" on the IRR. Lisanti, 573 F.3d at 1239-40.

Lisanti v. OPM, 573 F.3d 1334, 1339-40, 1343 (Fed. Cir. 2009), determined that an IRR can be challenged before OPM, whose decision can be reviewed by the Board:

The government cites several Board decisions and two of our nonprecedential decisions, Rainone v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 249 Fed. Appx. 823 (Fed. Cir. 2007), and Lee v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 301 Fed. Appx. 926 (Fed. Cir. 2008), to argue that an employing agency's certified individual retirement record is binding on OPM and on the Board, so that OPM may not consider evidence outside of the certified individual retirement record. However, the government fails to cite any precedential decision of this court supporting the idea that OPM may avoid interpreting the CSRS, a statute that OPM itself is required to administer. See 5 U.S.C. § 8347(a) ("[OPM] shall administer this subchapter [the CSRS]."). Thus, even when an employee's certified individual retirement record states his "basic pay," if the employee challenges the employing agency's interpretation of the term "basic pay," OPM, and subsequently the Board, are required to entertain that claim, absent some clear congressional intent to the contrary. Cf. Bacani v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 64 M.S.P.R. 588, 592 (1994) (determining that the Board could not interpret employee's "special pay adjustment" because such an adjustment, under the employing agency's statute "may not be reviewed by any other agency." (citing 38 U.S.C. § 7422(d))). The Board therefore erred in stating that neither it nor OPM had the authority to question the Administrative Office's determination of Lisanti's basic pay.

In sum, we disagree with the Board's determination that it did not have the authority to review OPM's reliance on the employing agency's individual retirement record.

News of Lisanti did not seem to filter down to the Board in one subsequent decision declaring that if an issue arises concerning the accuracy of retirement records maintained by the agency, the Board does not adjudicate the issue in a retirement appeal involving OPM. The appellant is left to other means to sort out the record with his employing agency, Castro v. OPM, SF-0841-10-0452-I-1 (NP 12/13/2010):

The appellant appealed the final decision of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) regarding the calculation of his retirement annuity. The Individual Retirement Record (IRR) is the "basic record for action on all claims for annuity or refund" under the Federal Employees' Retirement System. E.g., Rainone v. Office of Personnel Management, 104 M.S.P.R. 423, ¶ 6 n.2 (2007); 5 C.F.R. § 841.106(b)(1). The Board's review in determining whether OPM properly calculated a retirement annuity is limited to whether OPM properly relied upon the employing agency's certified IRR. O'Connell v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 579, ¶ 3 (2006) (citing Maxwell v. Office of Personnel Management, 78 M.S.P.R. 350, 356-57 (1998)). Further, the Board lacks jurisdiction to review the employing agency's certification of an IRR. Bacani v. Office of Personnel Management, 64 M.S.P.R. 588, 593-94 (1994). In this matter, the record reflects that OPM relied on the IRR certified by the appellant's employing agency. OPM is entitled to do so. O'Connell, 103 M.S.P.R. 579, ¶ 4. If the appellant believes that the data certified by his employing agency to OPM is in error, he must seek a correction from his employing agency, not from OPM. Id.

But the Board fell into line with Lisanti in Conner v. OPM, 120 MSPR 670, 674 ¶ 6, 2014 MSPB 26 (2014):

Precedential decisions of the Federal Circuit such as Lisanti and Billinger are controlling authority for the Board. Garcia v. Department of Agriculture, 110 M.S.P.R. 371, ¶ 12 (2009). The Board is bound to follow them unless they are overruled by the court sitting en banc. Johnston v. IVAC Corp., 885 F.2d 1574, 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1989); Schibik v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 M.S.P.R. 591, ¶ 8 (2005). Therefore, to the extent that Lee, 108 M.S.P.R. 321, Rainone, 104 M.S.P.R. 42, Maxwell, 78 M.S.P.R. 350, Bacani, 64 M.S.P.R. 588, O'Connell, 13 M.S.P.R. 579, ¶ 2, and any similar cases are inconsistent with Lisanti, 573 F.3d 1334, and Billinger, 206 F.3d 1404, they are hereby overruled. Consistent with Federal Circuit precedent, we find that the Board has jurisdiction to review the accuracy and completeness of IRRs in the context of appeals from OPM final decisions that rely on them.

Parker v. OPM, DC-0842-15-0928-I-1 (NP 9/8/2022), noted that the IRR is not conclusive, but subject to review:

Under U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and Board case law, OPM and the Board may review the accuracy and completeness of the determinations and certifications made by the employing agency in an appellant's IRR. Lisanti v. Office of Personnel Management, 573 F.3d 1334, 1338-40 (Fed. Cir. 2009); Conner v. Office of Personnel Management, 120 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 6 (2014), aff'd, 620 F. App'x 892 (Fed. Cir. 2015). The Board and nonprecedential Federal Circuit case law finding to the contrary, and on which the appellant relies, is not the prevailing law. See Lisanti, 573 F.3d at 1339-40 (overruling Board and nonprecedential Federal Circuit decisions establishing that an employing agency's certified IRR is binding); Conner, 120 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶¶ 5-6 (expressly overruling the Board case law relied on by the appellant)....
