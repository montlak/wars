B. AUTHORITY TO TRANSFER

Under 28 USC 1631, the circuit court may, in the interest of justice, transfer a case to the court where the case could have been brought at the time it was filed. A transfer will not be ordered if it is clear that another court would not have jurisdiction over the subject matter of the case because the appellant has no right to bring his case into another court. See Burke v. USPS, 888 F.2d 833, 834-35 (Fed. Cir. 1989); cf. Schafer v. Dept. of Interior, 88 F.3d 981, 987 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (under 28 USC 1631, the court may transfer a case to a court but not to another federal administrative agency, e.g., the FLRA). The Federal Circuit may transfer what amounts to an EEO case to a district court for that court to determine if it has jurisdiction over a claim even when the district court first, and possibly incorrectly, dismissed the claim because of possible jurisdiction in the Federal Circuit. See Campbell v. Geren, 318 Fed. Appx. 884 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP).

The Federal Circuit declined to transfer a case to another court when the underlying action, refusal of the Federal Labor Relations Authority General Counsel to issue an unfair labor practice complaint involving the appellant's termination, would have been unreviewable in any court, Morrison v. Dept. of Interior, No. 2012-3046 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP):

Because this petition does not arise out of a decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board or an arbitrators' award, Morrison concedes that the petition is outside of this court's limited jurisdiction. See 5 U.S.C. § 7121(f); 5 U.S.C. § 4303; 5 U.S.C. § 7512. Morrison, however, contends that this court should transfer the matter to either the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit or the DC. Circuit pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1631. That statute authorizes transfer to a court in which the appeal could have been brought at the time it was filed "if it is in the interest of justice."

We cannot say that transfer here would be in interest of justice. Courts, including the Ninth Circuit and D.C. Circuit, have held that decisions by the FLRA General Counsel to file (or not file) unfair labor practice complaints are unreviewable. See Patent Office Profl Ass'n V. Fed. Labor Relations Auth., 128 F.3d 751, 753 (D.C. Cir. 1997) ("[B]ecause the court is without jurisdiction to review the General Counsel's decision not to issue an unfair labor practice complaint, we dismiss the petition for review."); Columbia Power Trades Council v. United States Dep't of Energy, 671 F.2d 325, 329 (9th Cir. 1982) (same).

Should the Federal Circuit receive a case by transfer from another court, the Federal Circuit may again transfer (or retransfer) or dismiss the case if it believes that the original transfer was clearly erroneous. Simanonok v. Simanonok, 918 F.2d 947, 953 (Fed. Cir. 1990). If the appellant improperly initiates a case in district court to review an arbitration award and then seeks transfer of the case to the Federal Circuit, the district court is obliged to consider the transfer request under 28 USC 1631. The district court should consider whether the action would have been timely had it been filed in the proper forum on the date it was filed, and if so, whether the transfer would be in the interest of justice. The district court's decision is reviewable for abuse of discretion, but the discretion must be exercised; the trial court may not dismiss the case once transfer is requested for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. Taylor v. SSA, 842 F.2d 232 (9th Cir. 1988); see Hays v. Postmaster Gen., 868 F.2d 328 (9th Cir. 1989) (concerning a case to review an MSPB decision brought in district court by an appellant who did not properly raise EEO allegations before the Board; transfer was sought to the Federal Circuit). If a district court reviewing a mixed case decides that the discrimination issue is frivolous, i.e., not having even an arguable basis in law or in fact, Neitzke v. Williams, 490 U.S. 319, 325 (1989), the district court may transfer the remaining civil service component of the mixed case to the Federal Circuit for disposition. The Federal Circuit reviews the transfer decision for clear error. Mattos v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3203); see Shaw v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 93-3518) ("[I]n accordance with Christianson v. Colt Industries Operating Corp., 486 U.S. 800, 819 (1988), this court is required to accept the transfer under the law-of-the-case principles, for 'if the transferee court can find the transfer decision plausible,' the 'jurisdictional inquiry is at an end.'").

If because of erroneous advice from his employer an employee appeals to MSPB rather than to EEOC, the court may use the authority of 5 USC 7702(f) to direct the Board to transfer the case to EEOC. Ramey v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1563). The court ordered the Board to transfer a tangled suitability case to OPM in Clayton v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3440), even though the Board apparently correctly determined that it did not have jurisdiction over the appeal. The court may also transfer an appeal involving discrimination issues to district court, but if the Board correctly concludes that an appellant presents no more than a frivolous allegation of discrimination, the Federal Circuit may deny the appellant's motion to transfer the appeal to a district court. Norton v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 90-3384) (as to an EEO claim presented by an appellant in a probationary termination case: "In view of the fact that we have jurisdiction to review the Board's action and have affirmed the Board's dismissal for lack of jurisdiction, we deny the motion to transfer to a District Court."). Should the appellant submit no evidence of discrimination at the MSPB hearing, no basis exists for the Federal Circuit to transfer an appeal to a district court. Hauges v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3299). If because of erroneous advice from the MSPB the Federal Circuit receives a case on appeal, the court may send the case back to the MSPB so that the Board may issue a new decision with proper notice of the appellant's right to pursue EEO issues to a district court. See Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 906 F.2d 689, 691-92 (Fed. Cir. 1990).
