11. Case or PFR Misfiled in Court

Marino v. OPM, 96 MSPR 294, 298 ¶ 8 (2004), noted that "generally when the ID clearly informs an appellant where to file his PFR and the appellant misdirects a PFR to the Federal Circuit, good cause does not exist for the untimely filing with the Board." In Gribcheck v. USPS, 41 MSPR 195, 196 (1989), the Board declined to extend the period for filing a PFR that was improperly filed with the Federal Circuit. The Board's reasoning was that cases allowing PFRs that were otherwise timely filed with other offices of the Board did not apply to a court that was not an office of the Board. See Evans v. OPM, 85 MSPR 36, 39 ¶ 9 (1999) ("[A] mistaken filing with the Federal Circuit prior to the deadline for filing a petition for review with the Board generally does not excuse a later untimely filing with the Board."); Colon v. USPS, 71 MSPR 514, 517 (1996) (weighing the factors contributing to a delayed filing with the MSPB, but declining to excuse a late filing following the incorrect initial filing of an administrative PFR with the Federal Circuit). The Board did not mention or distinguish past holdings excusing petitions filed with EEOC and the Office of Special Counsel. Gribcheck was followed in Allen v. Dept. of Air Force, 43 MSPR 192, 195 (1990) (appellant sent PFR to Federal Circuit rather than to Board), and Lindsay v. USPS, 45 MSPR 541, 542 (1990) (Federal Circuit filing); see Carter v. USPS, 55 MSPR 183, 184 (1992) ("when the initial decision clearly informs the appellant where to file his petition for review and the appellant misdirects a petition for review to the Federal Circuit, good cause does not exist for the untimely filing with the Board"); Alexander v. VA, 51 MSPR 368, 370 (1991) (late PFR was not excused when the appellant claimed confusion and filed the petition with the federal court system).

A district court directed the Board to determine whether a complaint filed in court would have been timely filed were it filed with MSPB as a PFR. The Board accepted the direction from the court, but concluded that the case untimely filed. Hall v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 274, 275, 277 (1990); see Luna v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 69, 72-73 (1995) (a district court filing, apparently to challenge the Board decision accepting settlement, did not excuse an untimely PFR when the initial decision clearly informed the appellant of the filing deadline and location for filing a petition to the Board).

Buckser v. EPA, 45 MSPR 274, 278-79 (1990), considered whether late filing with the Board of a PFR of an arbitration award involving claims of disability and age discrimination was excused by an earlier, arguably timely, filing of a petition seeking review in a district court. Buckser did not excuse the delay:

...As a general rule, initiating an action in another forum does not excuse a late filing with the Board. See, e.g., Grasty v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 438, 441 (1986); Shrider v. USPS, 26 MSPR 238, 240 (1985); Turner v. USPS, 25 MSPR 588, 589 (1985). In "mixed cases," however, 5 USC § 7702(f) alters this rule by requiring the Board to consider timely a petition filed on time but with "an agency other than" the Board. In Dearsman v. Dept. of Treasury, 16 MSPR 631, 636 n.4 (1983), we recognized, but left open, the question of whether a United States District Court is an "agency" covered by this statutory exception to the general rule. Reading section 7702(f) and 5 USC § 105 together convinces us the district court is not an "agency." It is not an "Executive department, a Government corporation," or an "independent establishment" for the purpose of Title 5 of the United States Code. The District Court, moreover, is not a part of the Executive branch at all but a court of general jurisdiction, within the judicial branch of government, established under Article III of the Constitution. Accordingly, section 7702(f) does not require the Board to treat the appellant's late-filed petition as timely....
