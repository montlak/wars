1. Hearing Error: Measure of Damage

The Board's regulations require AJs to conduct fair, impartial hearings. 5 CFR 1201.41(b) (2022). Beyond the requirements of the rule, there is the notion of due process, explained in McGowan v. Int'l Communications Agency, 3 MSPR 83, 86, 2 MSPB 198 (1980), involving the AJ's failure to provide for a response to a dispositive agency motion:

It is well settled that due process should be utilize[d] to protect a public employee's interest in his employment. Jenkins v. Macy, 357 F.2d 62, 68 (8th Cir. 1966). Due process in administrative proceedings of a judicial nature generally requires conformance to fair practices of Anglo-Saxon jurisprudence. Hornsby v. Allen, 326 F.2d 605, 608, reh. denied, 330 F.2d 55 (5th Cir. 1964). Although administrative agencies such as the Board are not bound by the same details of procedure as the courts, they are governed by the same basic requirements of fairness and notice, including specificity of notice and opportunity to respond if what is instituted (the agency's motion to dismiss) is intended to be a procedure for summary disposition without a hearing. Cf. Hess & Clark, Division of Rhodia, Inc. v. Food and Drug Administration, 495 F.2d 975, 984 (D.C. Cir. 1974). Under all of the circumstances of the instant appeal, although appellant was served with a copy of the agency's motion to dismiss three weeks prior to the issuance of the initial decision and failed to object to it, we find that the presiding official did not afford appellant fair notice of the deadline for his response, and that appellant was thereby denied a reasonable "opportunity to object" to the agency's motion under 5 CFR § 1201.55(b).

For the Board to review an objection presented in PFR to a AJ's hearing conduct, that objection should first be made to the AJ. See Watson v. Dept. of Treasury, 49 MSPR 237, 242 (1991) ("[W]e find that the appellant's representative did not object to the length of the hearing sessions and presentation of oral closing arguments below, and the appellant is therefore precluded from pursuing those arguments here."). If the AJ rejects documents during the hearing process, the party whose documents were excluded may have them reviewed during the hearing process for the Board to determine whether the AJ committed adjudicatory error. See, e.g., Klant v. Dept. of Army, 5 MSPR 374, 378 n.2, 5 MSPB 393 (1981). There is no harm if rejected documents are offered with the PFR, although AJs ordinarily keep a rejected exhibits file to preserve the record of excluded evidence.

Not every error by a AJ warrants remand. An initial decision will be set aside only if it is shown that an AJ's error substantially affected a party's rights. Ragland v. IRS, 6 MSPR 674, 676, 6 MSPB 565 (1981). According to the oft-cited Karapinka v. Dept. of Energy, 6 MSPR 124, 127, 6 MSPB 114 (1981), "a 'mere deviation' from the best procedure does not constitute reversible error," and, in accordance with Fed. R. Civ. P. 61, the Board will disregard a procedural error or defect that does not affect the substantive rights of the parties. See Jeffers v. VA, 40 MSPR 567, 572 (1989) (the appellant failed to demonstrate that alleged procedural errors of the AJ precluded him from presenting material evidence or adversely affected that party's substantive rights); Panter v. Dept. of Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984) (adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to the party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversing initial decision). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Required Analysis," for the analysis required in an initial decision and the measure of harm when errors are made.]

An example of a nonprejudicial hearing error is provided by Tisdell v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 44, 51 ¶¶ 13-14 (2003). The AJ allowed the introduction at the hearing of an agency exhibit that was not identified in the prehearing submission. Whether the AJ erred by admitting the document was debatable, for the AJ has the responsibility for developing a full record. But if there was error, it was not prejudicial. The substance of the exhibit, a safety instruction, was covered by testimony of agency managers:

In his cross petition for review, the appellant contends that the administrative judge erred in sustaining the charge of failure to observe standard safety procedures based on Technical Order 1-1-8, which was introduced "at the eleventh hour" of the hearing over his objection. We disagree. An administrative judge has wide discretion to control the proceedings before him, to receive relevant evidence, and to ensure that the record on significant issues is fully developed. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(3), 5(ii), (6); Satlin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 60 M.S.P.R. 218, 221 n.8 (1993). We note that Technical Order 1-1-8 is a published agency order that was available to the agency prior to the hearing and should have been part of the agency's pre-hearing exhibit file. Nevertheless, we discern no error on the part of the administrative judge in admitting Technical Order 1-1-8 into the record, as it was clearly relevant to determine whether the agency had met its burden of proving the charge by preponderant evidence. See Mitchell v. Department of the Air Force, 91 M.S.P.R. 201 ¶ 5 (2002) (the agency has the burden to prove the charges by preponderant evidence, and in determining whether the agency met that burden, the administrative judge's initial decision must identify all material issues of fact and law, summarize the evidence, resolve issues of credibility, and include his conclusions of law and legal reasoning, as well as the authorities on which that reasoning rests).

Moreover, in sustaining the charge, the administrative judge credited the testimony of Mr. Tabb and Mr. Moon that the appellant was aware of the requirement to ground his work stand during all sanding operations, and we discern no reason to disturb this credibility determination. See Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987). Thus, even assuming it was error for the administrative judge to receive Technical Order 1-1-8 into the record, the appellant has failed to show how he was prejudiced by this error, based on the credible testimony of Mr. Tabb and Mr. Moon, and the appellant's admission that he was aware of the requirement to ground his work stand during sanding operations. [S] ee Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 MSPB 114, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981) (the administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown to have adversely affected a party's substantive rights). Consequently, the appellant has failed to provide a basis for reversal of the initial decision in this regard. Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984)....

The key is substantial prejudice. See Pinto v. Dept. of Labor, 11 MSPR 422, 424-25 (1982) (no showing of harmful error by the appellant who was shown unredacted copies of agency documentation only one day prior to the hearing); Anderson v. DODDS, 3 MSPR 553, 555 (1980) (failure to serve the agency with the appellant's submission was an error, but the error was harmless because it was not "regarded as decisionally significant"). Deviation from the best procedure does not constitute reversible error. In Ragland v. IRS, 6 MSPR 674, 676, 6 MSPB 565 (1981), the AJ apparently spoke with the agency representative, but not with appellant's representative, concerning continuance of the hearing. That was not an ex parte communication, for it did not concern the merits of the case. It was not prejudicial, although it may have been disquieting to the appellant. The appellant did get his hearing. If, however, the agency submits material evidence to an AJ that is not provided to the appellant, the case will be remanded on a PFR for the appellant to have the opportunity to respond to the evidence. Rucker v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 82, 83, 6 MSPB 80 (1981). Should the AJ unintentionally mislead an appellant into failing to present evidence on a charge by creating an impression that the charge was dropped, the appeal will be remanded for a hearing on that charge. Palmer v. USPS, 36 MSPR 263, 267 (1988). An arguably untimely evidentiary submission, provided to the appellant immediately prior to the hearing, does not constitute harmful error on the basis of an allegation that the AJ should have allowed the appellant additional time to review the document prior to the hearing. See Copeland v. OPM, 9 MSPR 127, 128 (1981). [Refer to this chapter, "Closing Record."]

The Board looks to Fed. R. Civ. P. 61 for guidance in curing errors by AJs. Karapinka v. Dept. of Energy, 6 MSPR 124, 127, 6 MSPB 114 (1981) (Rule 61 then provided: "The court at every stage of the proceeding must disregard an error or defect in the proceedings which does not affect the substantial rights of the parties." The current version of Rule 61 states:

Unless justice requires otherwise, no error in admitting or excluding evidence--or any other error by the court or a party--is ground for granting a new trial, for setting aside a verdict, or for vacating, modifying, or otherwise disturbing a judgment or order. At every stage of the proceeding, the court must disregard all errors and defects that do not affect any party's substantial rights.

The approach is demonstrated by Hutchins v. VA, 21 MSPR 770, 771 n.* (1984). The AJ's failure to tell the appellant that the voluntariness of his retirement would become a jurisdictional issue was not reversible error. The hearing produced all evidence necessary to adjudication of the issue, meaning there was no prejudice to the appellant's substantive rights. In Mitchell v. DOD, 46 MSPR 154, 160 (1990), the AJ did not rule on the appellant's request for a hearing. The denial of a written ruling explaining why a hearing would not be convened was error, but the error was harmless: the appellant had not made out a prima facie basis for invoking Board jurisdiction over the subject matter of his appeal. See Garcia v. USPS, 67 MSPR 263, 267 (1995) (as to a notice from the AJ concerning the timeliness issue: "[n]or does she assert that, if she had known that the timeliness question had not been resolved, she would have presented any other arguments or otherwise changed her strategy in pursuing her appeal.").
