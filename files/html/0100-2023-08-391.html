A. APPELLATE PROCEDURES

Board appellate procedures for suitability actions are invoked under 5 CFR 731.501 (2022):

Appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board.

(a) Appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board. When OPM or an agency acting under delegated authority under this part takes a suitability action against a person, that person may appeal the action to the Merit Systems Protection Board (hereinafter "Board").

(b) Decisions by the Merit Systems Protection Board.

(1) If the Board finds that one or more of the charges brought by OPM or an agency against the person is supported by a preponderance of the evidence, regardless of whether all specifications are sustained, it must affirm the suitability determination. The Board must consider the record as a whole and make a finding on each charge and specification in making its decision.

(2) If the Board sustains fewer than all the charges, the Board must remand the case to OPM or the agency to determine whether the suitability action taken is appropriate based on the sustained charge(s). However, the agency must hold in abeyance a decision on remand until the person has exhausted all rights to seek review of the Board's decision, including court review.

(3) Once review is final, OPM or an agency will determine whether the action taken is appropriate based on the sustained charges and this determination will be final without any further appeal to the Board.

(c) Appeal procedures. The procedures for filing an appeal with the Board are found at part 1201 of this title.

Subpart F--Savings Provision

§ 731.601 Savings provision.

No provision of the regulations in this part is to be applied in such a way as to affect any administrative proceeding pending on June 16, 2008. An administrative proceeding is deemed to be pending from the date of the agency or OPM "notice of proposed action" described in §§ 731.302 and 731.402.

A suitability appeal is filed and adjudicated at the regional office. The AJ is not bound by OPM's determinations or evidence. The appellant may controvert OPM's factual assertions and the legal assertions concerning nexus between conduct and ability to perform a federal position. See Kirkland v. OPM, 27 MSPR 159, 162 (1985) (AJ improperly limited the appellant's testimony, and OPM was not estopped from contesting suitability because OPM, relying upon erroneous information provided by the appellant in an employment application, initially approved appellant's placement on register); cf. Rougemont v. OPM, 60 MSPR 264, 265-66 (1993) ("An individual may appeal his separation to the Board if the separation was ordered by OPM following a suitability disqualification determination. See 5 CFR 731.508 [(1993)] ; Munson v. DOJ, 55 MSPR 246, 252 (1992)."). Hearsay contained in the OPM investigation report is received and evaluated by the Board as in other appeals. See Morris v. OPM, 33 MSPR 619, 621 (1987). OPM's regulations provide that a remanded suitability case will be held in abeyance until the appellant has exhausted all rights to seek review. Lane v. OPM, DA-0731-13-0504-I-1 (NP 1/16/2015) (citing 5 CFR 731.501(b)(2)). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Hearsay."] Adjudication procedures follow those involved in other Board litigation.

Under regulations in effect before 2008, "the Board is to affirm a negative suitability determination on appeal if one or more, but not all, of the charges on which the determination is based are supported by preponderant evidence, and it is to remand the appeal to the agency so that the agency may determine whether the action taken is still appropriate based on the sustained charge(s)." Saunders v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 38, 40 n.3 (2003); see Folio v. DHS, 402 F.3d 1350 (Fed. Cir. 2005), post. If there was a remand from the AJ to the agency for reconsideration of the suitability determination, the time for the appellant to file a PFR appeared to run from the date of the issuance of the initial decision that remands the matter to the agency, not from the date that the agency makes its decision on whether to maintain the negative suitability determination. See Santillan v. DHS, 98 MSPR 78, 80-81 ¶ 7 (2004). The notice of appeal rights in the initial decision was to be carefully reviewed. Revised OPM regulations governing suitability determinations. 65 Fed. Reg. 82,239 (2000), requiring the Board to remand the case to the agency if the Board sustains fewer than all the charges, did not apply to administrative proceedings pending on January 29, 2001. Id. at 82,246 (5 CFR 731.601, as revised). Merino v. DOJ, 94 MSPR 632, 635 ¶ 8 (2003). See Doerr v. OPM, 104 MSPR 196, 2006 MSPB 347 (2006) (example of a suitability case involving a remand to OPM for further consideration of its determination when one of its charges was not sustained and another charge was sustained by the Board). The current regulation, 5 CFR 731.501 (2022), provides for a remand or reconsideration process when one but not all the suitability charges are sustained.

Stewart v. OPM, 8 MSPR 289, 293-94 (1981), described the Board's de novo approach to review of suitability determinations:

An applicant or eligible subject to a negative suitability determination under 5 CFR 731.202 has the right to appeal that action to the Board, under 5 CFR 731.401(a) which expressly provides that right to "[a]ny applicant or eligible who is disqualified from examination or appointment by the Office [of Personnel Management]." Consequently, the Board properly exerts jurisdiction under 5 USC 1205(a)(1) and 7701(b) to hear and adjudicate that appeal, and, under 5 USC 7701(a), the appellant has the right "to a hearing for which a transcript will be kept." Thus, under those statutory and regulatory provisions, the Board is both authorized and mandated to conduct a hearing upon request, and, accordingly, to consider de novo all available relevant evidence. As we noted in Chavez v. OPM, 6 MSPB 343, 349, 6 MSPR 404, 412 (1981) (footnotes omitted)[1] :

The Board has consistently rejected the theory that its scope of review is limited to that of the appellate courts or to consideration of the administrative record established before the agency.... [Restriction of review to the agency record] would render nugatory the right of appellant to a hearing pursuant to 5 USC 7701(a) and 1205(a)(1), and our implementing regulations at 5 CFR 1201.24(c).

[1] See also Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 MSPB 489, 499, 1 MSPR 505, 520 (1980), where we held that § 7701(c)(1) requires consideration of the entire record before the Board, including any relevant evidence adduced at the Board's hearing. In Chavez, supra, we pointed out that "the [Civil Service] Reform Act contains no provision for Board consideration of 'appeals,' whether authorized under that Act or under some other statutory or regulatory authority, which are not to be governed by Section 7701." Id., 6 MSPB 348-49, 6 MSPR 411-12.

Since the Board's review is not restricted to the administrative record but is a de novo review, the Board may properly consider evidence relating to a period of time extending beyond the date of OPM's decision to disqualify appellant inasmuch as OPM has "a full and fair opportunity to cross-examine or otherwise rebut appellant's evidence at the Board's hearing." Chavez v. OPM, supra, 350, 6 MSPR 414. Therefore, we find that the presiding official properly admitted and considered the testimony of appellant and his forensic psychologist at the hearing as well as evidence adduced at the hearing which may have related to a period of time extending beyond July 30, 1979, the date of appellant's disqualification by OPM.
