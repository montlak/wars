d. Jurisdictional Analysis in Untimely Appeal

The Board carved an exception to the morass concerning jurisdictional issues in constructive adverse action appeals: it decided that if an appeal is untimely, it need not delve into jurisdiction. This rule of convenience--perhaps necessity--is explained in Bosworth v. USPS, 51 MSPR 36, 39-40 (1991) (asserting that the agency constructively furloughed the appellant by placing him in a nonpay status):

The Board recently reconsidered the question of whether a jurisdictional determination is always a preliminary requirement in the case of an untimely-filed appeal. In Popham v. USPS, 50 MSPR 193 (1991), the Board held that a jurisdictional determination may not be required where, by assuming arguendo that it has jurisdiction over an appeal, the Board finds that the appeal can be properly dismissed on timeliness or other grounds.

Specifically, the Board held in Popham that, while the existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, a jurisdictional determination would not be in the Board's interest of adjudicatory efficiency and fairness to the parties involved in an untimely appeal in which (a) the Board arguably may have jurisdiction over the appeal and (b) the record developed on the timeliness issue shows no good cause for the untimely filing, or (c) other grounds exist for the dismissal of the appeal.... Thus, the Board found that, if the record is sufficiently developed on the issue of timeliness or other grounds to show that an appeal should be dismissed on other than a jurisdictional basis, then an administrative judge may properly determine, by assuming arguendo the appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction (without actually making fact findings and conclusions of law on the jurisdictional issue), that dismissal of the appeal is warranted. In all other cases, however, the administrative judge must: (1) Make an actual jurisdictional determination; (2) make a determination on the issues of timeliness or other grounds, if necessary; (3) conduct further proceedings on the merits; and (4) make a determination on all other material issues of fact and law.

If the timeliness question is close, the Board decides the jurisdictional issue. See Vitale v. VA, 107 MSPR 501, 508 ¶ 16, 2007 MSPB 300 (2007). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Jurisdiction or Timeliness as Alternate Bases for Dismissal of Appeal."]
