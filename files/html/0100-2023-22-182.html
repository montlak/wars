12. EEO, Whistleblower Protection Act, USERRA, and VEOA Cases; Broader Coverage

In appeals involving a finding of discrimination or reprisal, costs are awarded under standards established by 42 USC 2000e-5(k). See 5 USC 7701(g)(2)). In those cases full costs (including photocopying and transcripts) are recoverable. Chin v. Dept. of Treasury, 55 MSPR 84, 86 (1992) (reimbursing out-of-pocket expenses of appellant for postage, fax, copying, and mailing); D'Iorio v. DHUD, 43 MSPR 531, 537 (1990) (disability discrimination allegations warranted the recovery of photocopying expenses); Bartel v. FAA, 30 MSPR 451, 457 (1986) (Title VII violation warranted recovery of postage, copying, and toll calls); Mitchell v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 206, 215-16 (1984) (Title VII authorized recovery of expert witness fee, depositions, postage, copying, travel, and postage). McGovern v. EEOC, 42 MSPR 399, 414 (1989), determined that EEO standards permit recovery for transcripts, depositions, subpoenas, and necessary duplicating.

The authority of the Board to award costs under the EEO laws is apparently tied to a finding of discrimination, Page v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3145):

On appeal, Page argues that the board erred in analyzing Page's claim for out-of-pocket expenses and expert witness fees under 5 USC 7701(g)(1) (1994) rather than 5 USC 7701(g)(2) (1994). We disagree with Page's contention. Section 7701(g)(2) applies only if the board's decision was based upon a finding of prohibited discrimination. In this case, the board's decision was not based upon such a finding. Thus, the board was correct in not analyzing Page's claim under § 7701(g)(2).

Of witness fees, in West Virginia Univ. Hosps., Inc. v. Casey, 111 S. Ct. 1138 (1991), the Supreme Court explained that statutes providing for awards of counsel fees and costs do not allow the shifting of these expenses. These fees may be assessed only under statutes that explicitly provide for recovery of expert witness fees. The provision for "costs" in a statute was construed, as to 42 USC 1988, to include only statutory witness fees allowed under 28 USC 1821(b) ($40 per day plus mileage). The Board applied Casey to Board practice in Fishback v. USPS, 54 MSPR 257, 259-60 (1992), not an EEO case, denying, as part of counsel fees, payment for work "performed by a non-attorney labor consultant employed by appellant's counsel":

We find that the present case is governed by the United States Supreme Court's recent decision in West Virginia University Hospitals, Inc. v. Casey, __ U.S. __, 111 S. Ct. 1138, 113 L. Ed.2d 68 (1991). In this case, the Court examined whether fees for services rendered by testimonial and nontestimonial experts in civil rights litigation may be shifted to the losing party under 42 USC 1988, which permits an award of "a reasonable attorney's fee."

The Court first found that nontestimonial expert fees may not be recoverable as costs under 28 USC 1920, because they do not fall under any of the enumerated fees contained in that statute. The Court then found that expert fees and attorney fees are distinct items of expenses, and that Congress has recognized this distinction by specifically authorizing the recovery of such fees, in addition to attorney fees, in various legislation. Thus, the Court held that expert fees may only be awardable as attorney fees where the attorney fee statute expressly authorizes such fee shifting. Because 42 USC 1988 only provides for attorney fees, and does not expressly authorize the recovery of expert fees, the Court held that the amount of these fees may not be shifted to the losing party.

Congress provided the Board with the power to award attorney fees in 5 USC 7701(g)(1) and (g)(2). Subsection (g)(1) provides that the Board may require an agency to pay, under certain circumstances, "reasonable attorney fees" incurred by an employee or applicant who prevails before the Board. Subsection (g)(2) further provides that, if the Board's decision is based upon a finding of discrimination prohibited under 2302(b)(1), payment of attorney fees shall be made in accordance with the standards prescribed under section 706(k) of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.

Neither of these statutory provisions contains any reference to an award of fees for work performed by experts in either a testimonial or nontestimonial capacity. We, therefore, find that under the Supreme Court's decision in West Virginia University Hospitals, the Board lacks authority to award such fees. Because the nonattorney consultant in the present case has performed expert services, the appellant is not entitled to have the costs of those fees shifted to the agency. Accordingly, we conclude that the administrative judge correctly denied the appellant's request for those fees.

Earlier the Board advised that a fee award in a case resulting in a finding discrimination may include the cost of expert witnesses. Cason v. NASA, 46 MSPR 401, 405 (1990) (citing Mitchell v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 206, 215 (1984)); see McGovern v. EEOC, 42 MSPR 399, 414 (1989); Mitchell v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 206, 215-16 (1984) (section 7701(g)(2) permits an award, following a finding of discrimination, of costs such as expert witnesses, transcripts, depositions, subpoenas, and duplicating).

As to other cases not involving Title VII, the Supreme Court suggested that if a statute awards only attorney fees, but does not speak to other expenses or expenses for experts (as does the EAJA), the legislative silence may be indicative of a congressional determination that experts' fees are not to be awarded as part of or in addition to counsel fees. West Virginia Univ. Hosps., Inc. v. Casey, 111 S. Ct. 1138 (1991).

The Whistleblower Protection Act provides for the recovery of reasonable costs in addition to counsel fees for appellants prevailing on whistleblowing claims. Wiatr v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 441, 454 (1991). It is expected that the WPA provision will embrace the usual litigation costs that Bennett v. Dept. of Navy, 699 F.2d 1140 (Fed. Cir. 1983), precluded from recovery under the CSRA as items included in the coverage of federal statutes defining recoverable costs. Smit v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 612, 623 (1994), allowed photocopying costs under the WPA:

The agency disputes the amount of costs allowable and cites 28 USC 1920 and Harris v. Department of Agriculture, 31 MSPR 219, 224 (1986), as authority for its argument that the appellant is not entitled to reimbursement for photocopying costs. Under 28 USC 1920, certain items are awardable as costs when an appropriate statute allows reimbursement for costs. Among those items are copies that are reasonably necessary. This has been interpreted to include the costs of photocopying documents submitted to the court or the opposing party. See Northbrook Excess and Surplus Insurance Co. v. Procter and Gamble Co., 924 F.2d 633 (7th Cir. 1991) (court awarded over $50,000 in photocopying costs under 28 USC 1920); Koch v. Department of Commerce, 19 MSPR 219, 222 (1984). In Harris, 31 MSPR at 224, the Board case cited by the agency, the Board denied a request for photocopying costs because they were not attorney fees which are distinguished from the costs allowed by 28 USC 1920. Thus, the Board does not award costs unless the authorizing statute specifically includes costs as does 5 USC 1221(g)(1), the statute relied upon in this appeal. We find that the administrative judge correctly reimbursed the appellant's representative for the costs of photocopying. See D'Iorio v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 43 MSPR 531, 537 (1990), overruled on other grounds, Mitchell v. Dept. of the Navy, 51 MSPR 103 (1991). We also find that the amount awarded is reasonable, given the volume of documents included in the record.

USERRA, 38 USC 4324(c), provides for the costs of litigation:

(4) If the Board determines as a result of a hearing or adjudication conducted pursuant to a complaint submitted by a person directly to the Board pursuant to subsection (b) that such person is entitled to an order referred to in paragraph (2), the Board may, in its discretion, award such person reasonable attorney fees, expert witness fees, and other litigation expenses.

VEOA, 5 USC 3330c(b), also provides for customary costs:

A preference eligible who prevails in an action under section 3330a or 3330b shall be awarded reasonable attorney fees, expert witness fees, and other litigation expenses.
