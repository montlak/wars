a. Appendix

Ordinarily the Federal Circuit will not review the entire administrative record in a civil service case. It reviews materials that both counsel in the appeal believe will significantly aid the court in its consideration of the points raised on review. Those materials are placed in what is called an appendix. Some materials must be included in the appendix: the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure require that the appendix include the certified index of the record and the decision that is under review (a Board case would ordinarily encompass the initial decision and the final decision from the Board). If a certified index cannot be supplied--for example, if an arbitrator fails to create or provide the index for the court--the court may direct that the parties stipulate to the record or file an agreed statement of the case. See Rubert v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3610). The remainder of the material in the appendix--dozens or thousands of pages--are documents drawn from the record before the Board and chosen by the parties' counsel to explain or support arguments made in their briefs.

Careful designation of materials to be included in the appendix reflects careful analysis of issues and careful briefing. Some practitioners, including government counsel, wish to include the entire record of the case in the appendix, perhaps because it is feared that if some document or segment of testimony is not included, the reviewing judges will believe that a reference in the brief to that document or testimony is unsupported. Careful briefing, and focus on key issues, avoids the need for over-designation of the appendix.

Rule 30 of the Federal Rules, as supplemented by the Federal Circuit rules, generally describes the procedure for compiling the contents of the appendix. The Advisory Committee notes to Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure 30 describe the desired contents of the appendix:

The essential characteristics of the appendix... are: (1) the entire record may not be reproduced; (2) instead, the parties are to set out in an appendix to the briefs those parts of the record which in their judgment the judges must consult in order to determine the issues presented by the appeal; (3) the appendix is not the record but merely a selection therefrom for the convenience of the judges of the court of appeals; the record is the actual trial court record, and the record itself is always available to supply inadvertent omissions from the appendix.

Counsel will determine what should be in the appendix, but everything there should be referenced in the brief. Federal Circuit Rule 30 advises that without leave of the court, the following are not to be included in the appendix: briefs and memoranda (except as otherwise provided in Federal Circuit Rule 30); notices; subpoenas (except where the enforcement or validity of a subpoena is at issue); summonses; motions to extend time. Briefs and memoranda may be included if the only issue is the propriety of summary judgment.

Both parties may include materials in the appendix, but generally its assembly is the appellant's responsibility. Government counsel must cooperate in preparing the appendix, even though it is filed by petitioner's counsel. Federal Circuit Rule 30(b) governs the arrangement of the appendix. The Circuit Rule should be studied in its entirety and followed. Redoing a brief and appendix at the direction of the clerk can be a tedious process suggesting to counsel the desirability of alternate careers or early retirement. Because under the court rules the designation of the appendix must occur early in the briefing period, counsel should begin preparation of the brief and appendix before the case is docketed.

The court rules call for each party to inform the other of the materials it intends to include in the appendix, a process known as designation of the appendix--a process that is supposed to be completed before the first brief is filed. Added here is a word concerning how the appendix procedure generally works in practice in civil service appeals. Because the Justice Department often does not have the record at the time the designation is due from petitioner's counsel, the Justice Department attorney on the case may not be able to offer a timely counter-designation of materials for the appendix. A procedure that works well in practice requires the petitioner to provide to government counsel a copy of his portion of his appendix (suitably indexed and paginated) at the time petitioner files his brief. Petitioner defers filing the appendix with the court. When government counsel files his brief, he will list any additional items for the appendix. At that point, petitioner's counsel adds those items to the index, completes the numbering and indexing, and files the appendix at the time his reply brief is due. An appendix of 100 pages or less may be included with the appellant's initial brief under Federal Circuit Rule 30(d).
