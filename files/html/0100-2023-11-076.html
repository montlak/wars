A. OFFICIAL NOTICE

There are some matters that should not require proof because they are common knowledge. On request, official notice may be taken of a matter to avoid the need for proof. Under Federal Rule of Evidence 201, official (or judicial) notice may be taken of facts not subject to reasonable dispute, defined to include either those facts generally known within the territorial jurisdiction of the trial court or those facts capable of accurate and ready determination by resort to sources whose accuracy cannot reasonably be questioned. Under 5 CFR 1201.64 (2022), the AJ may take official notice of any matter that can be verified. An AJ errs by not allowing parties the opportunity to comment on the taking of official notice. See Ketchem, post. The term of art sometimes employed is "administrative notice." See Senior Executives Ass'n v. OPM, 67 MSPR 643, 653 n.5 (1995) (Board takes administrative notice of a study by the General Accounting Office concerning use by agencies of alternative work schedules).The Board took official notice of a description of fibromyalgia from the website of the American College of Rheumatology and from a medical dictionary in Thomas v. OPM, DE-844E-15-0524-I-1 (NP 5/6/2016). Decisions of the Board are not evidence. Copies need not be provided for the record. The parties need only cite Board decisions they wish the Board to consider. Parker v. USPS, 59 MSPR 603, 607 n.2 (1993).

The discussion that follows suggests the boundaries of official notice in Board litigation. First, the Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.64 (2022), states:

Official notice is the Board's or judge's recognition of certain facts without requiring evidence to be introduced establishing those facts. The judge, on his or her own motion or on the motion of a party, may take official notice of matters of common knowledge or matters that can be verified. The parties may be given an opportunity to object to the taking of official notice. The taking of official notice of any fact satisfies a party's burden of proving that fact.

The Board may take administrative notice of public documents. See Azdell v. OPM, 88 MSPR 319, 323 (2001) (notice taken of letters from OPM to ALJ applicants notifying them of rescored ratings). The Board may take official notice of publicly-recognized conditions, for example, the effect of anthrax contamination on the mail system during the fall of 2001. Terrance v. OPM, 94 MSPR 77, 80 ¶ 8 (2003). "[T]he Board may take official notice of matters that can be verified, including documents or actions in other Board appeals." Smith v. USPS, 107 MSPR 410, 413 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 289 (2007) (notice taken of an initial decision in a different appeal by the same appellant when the initial decision considered evidence of the appellant's preference eligible status, a jurisdictionally-significant element of the case before the Board on PFR); cf. Clark v. DOJ, CH-0752-15-0288-I-2, n.2 (NP 10/21/2022) ("The CSRS and FERS Handbook is a public document, of which we take administrative notice. See Azdell v. Office of Personnel Management, 88 M.S.P.R. 319, 323 (2001).").

A fact subject to substantial dispute is not properly the subject of official notice. Ketchem v. FAA, 12 MSPR 1, 3-5 (1982). Under Ketchem, there is an implicit right to controvert, through evidence, a matter within the scope of a request for official notice; it has no other effect than to relieve one of the parties of the burden of introducing evidence and does not prevent the other party from disputing that matter by evidence. It is improper for an AJ to take official notice of an unverifiable assertion. Affsa v. USDA, 7 MSPR 446, 450, 7 MSPB 314 (1981) (federal employees routinely exceed their lunch breaks). An appellant's state of mind, thought, and knowledge are not matters of common knowledge and therefore not appropriate subjects for official notice. Presley v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 291, 294 (1983); see Thompson v. Dept. of Treasury, 16 MSPR 294, 297 n.1 (1983) ("[M]edical officer's opinion as to the likely behavior of paranoid persons is not a matter of common undisputed knowledge that may be officially noted under the Board's regulations."); cf. Seamans v. Dept. of Interior, 62 MSPR 502, 506 (1994) ("It appears that the administrative judge may have relied on extra-record evidence, i.e., his own familiarity with travel authorizations, in sustaining charge (2). To the extent that he did so, it was improper, as the administrative judge's personal knowledge is irrelevant to the appellant's state of mind when he took the trips home.").

Official notice of the parameters of a competitive area is inappropriate if the only evidence on the issue is conclusory and if the issue is disputed. See Anderson v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 13, 16 (1983) (since the official notice was the only support for the definition of the competitive area, the Board reversed the appellant's separation). Government ownership of a vehicle was not necessarily a matter of official notice, particularly without a request for official notice. See Johnson v. IRS, 14 MSPR 236, 240 (1982). The AJ need not take official notice of the evidence in a prior appeal; the party seeking to have that evidence considered in a pending appeal should submit that evidence in the later appeal. Wagner v. EPA, 54 MSPR 447, 454 (1992); cf. Strauss v. OPM, 39 MSPR 132, 135-36 (1988) (evidence from a different but similar appeal could be incorporated into the record, but the appellant must be given, not just have access to, the material; further, the evidence from the other case cannot foreclose the appellant's opportunity to have a full evidentiary hearing); Berry v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 95, 96 (1984) ("an initial decision issued in another case does not constitute new and material evidence" supporting a PFR; distinguishing outcome of two seemingly similar cases with different results). But see Smith v. USPS, 107 MSPR 410, 2007 MSPB 289 (2007), supra.

An AJ should not take official notice without providing notice and allowing a response, Hope v. Dept. of Army, 108 MSPR 6, 10-11 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 13 (2008):

The AJ found that, had the agency provided the appellant with written notice and an opportunity to respond, he likely would have convinced the agency that he was sufficiently qualified for appointment to the position of Optometrist. In reaching this conclusion, however, the AJ relied primarily upon evidence outside the record, of which he took official notice. This evidence consisted of the appellant's medical licensure in Oklahoma and the Oklahoma statutory scheme for licensure of Optometrists. The Board or an AJ may take official notice of certain facts that are matters of common knowledge or matters that can be verified. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.64. While the appellant's license to practice medicine in Oklahoma may be verified, the effect of an Oklahoma statutory scheme is something that may be subject to reasonable dispute and so should not have been taken as proven. See Anderson v. Department of Health & Human Services, 15 M.S.P.R. 13, 14-15 (1983). In addition, while the Board's regulations allow only that "parties may be given an opportunity to object to the taking of official notice," the Board has stated that it regards parties as having a right of refutation. See id. at 15 n.1; Ketchem v. Department of Transportation, 12 M.S.P.R. 1, 3 (1982). The agency was given no opportunity to refute the facts of which the AJ took official notice. We therefore find that it was error for the AJ to take official notice of these facts without allowing the agency the opportunity to dispute them with evidence. See Ketchem, 12 M.S.P.R. at 3.
