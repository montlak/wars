B. JUDGE'S RESPONSIBILITY

USERRA and VEOA are complex. Many of these appeals are litigated by unrepresented appellants against government counsel expert in personnel litigation. An AJ must inform the appellant of burdens and methods of proof in a USERRA petition for remedial action, as well as when a violation of USERRA is raised as an affirmative defense. Skellham v. USPS, 90 MSPR 361, 364 ¶ 8 (2001); see Noble v. USPS, 93 MSPR 693, 700 ¶ 13 (2003) ("When an appellant raises a USERRA claim or affirmative defense, an AJ must inform an appellant of the burden of proving this claim or defense, the burden of going forward with the evidence, and the type of evidence necessary[;] " the acknowledgment order provided no jurisdictional information and the agency's motion to dismiss did not notify the appellant what was required to establish a nonfrivolous USERRA discrimination allegation). Before dismissing a VEOA appeal for lack of jurisdiction, the AJ must ensure the appellant was placed on specific notice of what must be shown or alleged to establish jurisdiction, and the appellant must have then failed to make the requisite showing or allegations. Hamner v. DHUD, 93 MSPR 84, 90 ¶ 18 (2002). If it is apparent that the appellant is invoking a USERRA claim in a VEOA proceeding, the AJ should inform the appellant of his rights and burdens under USERRA. Walker v. DHHS, 99 MSPR 367, 371 ¶ 10 (2005). Several cases emphasize the AJ's responsibility to provide the appellant enough information to structure his or her claim.

If a pro se appellant makes a claim that she was discriminated against because of her military status, the AJ's acknowledgment order must inform her of what is required to establish a jurisdictional issue, assuming that an agency motion to dismiss did not serve the same purpose. See Riordan v. USPS, 84 MSPR 410, 411 ¶ 4 (1999). Wright v. VA, 73 MSPR 453, 455-56 (1997), summarized the responsibilities of an AJ who receives an appeal containing a claim under USERRA that an individual's employment was terminated as a result of prior military service:

USERRA provides that a person may submit an appeal against a Federal executive agency alleging discrimination on the basis of prior military service directly to the Board or he may submit such an appeal to the Board after he has filed a complaint with the Secretary of Labor, and proceedings there are exhausted. See 38 USC 4324(b). On remand, the administrative judge therefore must determine whether this appeal is currently ripe for adjudication because the appellant has chosen not to file a complaint with the Secretary of Labor, and filed directly with the Board, or whether adjudication must be delayed because the appellant sought the assistance of the Secretary by filing a complaint within the meaning of 38 USC 4322, and proceedings before the Secretary have not been exhausted. See 38 USC 4324(b); Jasper, [73 MSPR] at 370.

...

Further, if the appeal is ripe for adjudication, the administrative judge must determine which of the following methods of proof the appellant wishes to use in supporting his claim that the agency discriminated against him in violation of USERRA: (1) the USERRA standard of review if the appellant supports his claim of a violation of USERRA through the use of direct evidence of discriminatory intent, see 38 USC 4311(b); Jasper, at 371; Petersen, 71 MSPR at 239-40; or (2) analogous burdens of proof and production under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 for analyzing discrimination claims based on circumstantial evidence if the appellant supports his claim more indirectly by showing that discriminatory intent could be inferred from relevant circumstantial evidence, see Jasper, at 371. Based on that determination, the administrative judge shall afford the parties the opportunity to develop the record more fully, and he shall then make findings on the appellant's USERRA discrimination claim under the relevant method of proof.

As with other appeals involving disputed jurisdictional issues, the AJ must, before dismissing a USERRA (or VEOA) appeal for lack of jurisdiction, ensure that the appellant is aware of what he must plead to establish Board jurisdiction, unless the appellant is placed on notice of the pleading requirements by an agency motion to dismiss. Smyth v. USPS, 85 MSPR 549, 551 ¶¶ 7-8 (2000), following remand, Smyth v. USPS, 89 MSPR 219, 221 ¶ 2 (2001), aff'd, Smyth v. USPS, 41 Fed. Appx. 475 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP). Fox v. USPS, 88 MSPR 381, 386-87 ¶¶ 11-13 (2001), determined:

...The AJ had informed the appellant of the requirements for establishing the Board's USERRA jurisdiction over his case, yet he did not inform the appellant of the USERRA burdens of proof or the different methods of proving USERRA claim. Neither did the AJ determine the method(s) that the appellant wished to use to support his claim....

...

[W]e hold that in a case of a USERRA petition for remedial action as well as in a case where a violation of USERRA is raised as an affirmative defense, an AJ must inform an appellant of the USERRA burdens of proof and the different methods of proving a USERRA claim. This will enable an appellant to make a reasoned decision on the method(s) he wishes to use in supporting his claim. An AJ must then determine the method(s) that the appellant wishes to use to support his claim. Accordingly, we find in this case that it was error to fail to inform the appellant of the methods and burdens of proof in establishing a USERRA claim, and by failing to determine the method(s) that the appellant wished to use to support his claim. See Tindall, 84 MSPR 230 ¶ 12; Morgan, 82 MSPR 1 ¶¶ 10-11; Jasper, 73 MSPR at 370-71; Duncan, 73 MSPR at 93-95.

Remanding a case to the region, Russell v. EEOC, 104 MSPR 14, 21 ¶¶ 17-18, 2006 MSPB 319 (2006), noted the AJ's the responsibility to provide notice to the appellant of the elements of a USERRA case:

We also find that further adjudication is required in this appeal. Although the administrative judge noted possible jurisdictional questions, he held a hearing on the merits and found that the appellant did not show that her military status was a motivating or substantial factor in the agency's decision to change her duty station. On review, however, the appellant claims, among other things, that the administrative judge failed to inform her of the appropriate burden of proof in a USERRA appeal.

The record establishes that the administrative judge only informed the appellant that she was required to prove that the agency discriminated against her based upon her military status. This notice is insufficient because it does not fully inform the appellant of the distinctions between USERRA reemployment cases and USERRA discrimination cases, and the respective methods and burdens of proof regarding these different claims. See Wyatt v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 28, ¶¶ 12-13 (2006). If the appellant claims that the agency violated her reemployment rights, the administrative judge's finding that the appellant did not show that her military status was a motivating or substantial factor in the agency's decision to change her duty station does not resolve the appeal. Id. We therefore find that remand is required to inform the appellant of the different types of USERRA appeals and the respective burdens and methods of proof for these appeals and to conduct any further adjudication that may be required in light of the appellant's response to this notice.

See Martir v. Dept. of Navy, 81 MSPR 421, 426 ¶ 13 (1999) (noting the AJ's duty to inform the appellant of what he would need to show to establish that his appeal of a USERRA matter was timely); cf. Milner v. DOJ, 77 MSPR 37, 47 (1997) (advising the appellant of the right to file a Board appeal with a regional office upon exhaustion of a pending USERRA complaint with the Department of Labor). If during the course of a VEOA case the judge becomes aware that the appellant is invoking USERRA, the Aj must ensure that the appellant is informed of his rights and burdens under USERRA. Walker v. DHHS, 99 MSPR 367, 371 ¶ 10 (2005). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Jurisdictional Issues" and "Judge Must Ensure Jurisdictional Issues Are Identified; Burgess Notice."]
