c. OSC File; Executive and Investigative Privileges

In a four-year discovery dispute that began as a subpoena enforcement proceeding, In re Subpena Addressed to OSC, 16 MSPR 596 (1983) (see also 18 MSPR 454, 457-58 (1983) and 20 MSPR 245, 248 (1984)), guidance was offered on claims of executive and work product privilege raised by agencies holding documents sought in Board litigation:

In order for a claim of executive privilege to be properly invoked, it must be raised by the head of an agency in an affidavit after actual personal consideration of the underlying documents. United States v. Reynolds, 345 U.S. 1 at 7-8 (1953).... The purpose of this procedural requirement is to insure that the privilege is not lightly or mistakenly invoked in circumstances not warranting its application. Assertion of the executive privilege within the affidavit of the agency head requires a specific designation and description of the documents that are alleged to be within its scope, as well as precise and certain reasons for preserving their confidentiality....

[W]e find that the Office of Special Counsel has not properly invoked the executive privilege in a manner consistent with the law. The privilege has been invoked only by subordinates of the office. The Special Counsel himself has not asserted the privilege.... It has provided little more than the broad claim that the executive privilege removes its investigative file from the scope of allowable discovery.

...In the absence of proper assertion, the Board must deem the privilege as waived.

A few months later, the Board again addressed the problems raised in the Special Counsel subpoena enforcement proceedings, after OSC had the chance to substantiate its executive privilege claim. In re Subpena Addressed to OSC, 18 MSPR 454, 457 (1983). OSC again failed properly to claim the executive privilege, instead claiming an "investigatory file privilege," likened by the Board to executive privilege and discounted because the Special Counsel failed to make a personal review and exemption claim for the documents:

The Special Counsel apparently believes that a claim of "investigative privilege" is not a claim of "executive privilege" and that, accordingly, it does not have to be asserted by the agency head in the manner prescribed by the Board. In the context of civil discovery, however, the investigatory file privilege is usually subsumed in and subject to the same invocatory requirements as the executive privilege. See Black v. Sheraton Corp. of America, 564 F.2d 531, 541-47 (D.C. Cir. 1977); Coastal Corp. v. Duncan, 86 F.R.D. 514, 519 (D. Del. 1980). On occasion a distinction has been made between an "executive privilege," which protects documents that contain military or diplomatic secrets and the statements and deliberations of high executive officials, and a "law enforcement evidentiary privilege," which protects investigative files. E.g., McClelland v. Andrus, 606 F.2d 1278, 1286 n.53 (D.C. Cir. 1979); Association for Women in Science v. Califano, 566 F.2d 339, 343 (D.C. Cir. 1977). However, a claim of law enforcement evidentiary privilege must be asserted in the same manner as a claim of executive privilege, i.e., the head of the agency involved must lodge a formal claim of privilege, after personal consideration of the documents in question. Women in Science, supra, at 347. Accord, Black v. Sheraton Corp. of America, supra, at 543-44; National Lawyers Guild v. Attorney General, 96 F.R.D. 390, 396 n.12 (S.D.N.Y. 1982); Coastal Corp., supra; United States v. American Telephone & Telegraph, 86 F.R.D. 603, 605 (D.D.C. 1979). Therefore, since the Special Counsel has not properly invoked the privilege asserted here, whether it be called by the broader term "executive" privilege, or by the narrower "investigative" or "law enforcement evidentiary" privilege, we have no choice but to hold, as we cautioned in our order of September 22, 1983, that the privilege has been waived. We accordingly make no determination on whether a properly asserted claim of government investigatory file privilege would have protected the Special Counsel's investigative file.

The Federal Circuit has not shown much interest in compelling discovery of Special Counsel files in WPA cases, King v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 92-3299):

King also challenges the AJ's denial of his motions to compel discovery of OSC materials and for the deposition of an OSC investigator.... The privileged nature of the material strongly cuts against such discovery, and King does not demonstrate that it would cause him undue hardship to gather the privileged information himself.

OSC will make available to appellants the files that the appellants submitted to OSC. See Hoback v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 425, 433 ¶ 13 (2000) (suggesting that IRA appellants employ Board discovery procedures to obtain them).

Congress seemed disinclined to direct OSC to furnish its investigation files to complainants when it amended the Whistleblower Protection Act in 1994. Instead, Congress required OSC to provide detailed closeout letters and to informally advise complainants concerning matters pertaining to investigation of their complaints. In commentary on Pub. L. 103-424, Senate Report 103-358, 1994 USCCAN 3549, 3560-61, stated:

[M]any whistleblowers complain that OSC's close-out letters are perfunctory and give little information about OSC's investigation or the facts of the case. Some have sought to remedy this problem by gaining access to the OSC's investigative files.

Giving whistleblowers a right of access to OSC investigative files may not be appropriate, because such files might contain confidential information about persons other than the whistleblower. Also, witnesses might be less candid with OSC investigators if they knew that OSC files would be open to the whistleblower. On the other hand, OSC is statutorily required to assist whistleblowers, and should not treat them the same way as a federal agency would treat any routine FOIA request.

S. 622 would address this problem by requiring OSC to provide, in each close-out letter terminating an investigation of a prohibited personnel practice, the name and telephone number of an OSC employee who will be available to respond to reasonable questions from the person who initiated the investigation regarding OSC's investigation, the relevant facts ascertained, and the law applicable to the person's allegations. This approach should provide whistleblowers with information necessary for them to make informed decisions about how to proceed with their cases.

Why all the fuss about the Special Counsel's files? The Special Counsel becomes a target for discovery when it investigates issues pertaining to prohibited personnel practice allegations that relate either to Individual Right of Action appeals or to affirmative defenses raised in adverse action appeals. OSC resources are greater than the average appellant's and the possibility is always present that the Special Counsel may obtain some material information otherwise unavailable to an appellant who pursues his own discovery.

For discussion of executive privilege, refer to McClelland v. Andrus, 606 F.2d 1278 (D.C. Cir. 1979), noting that executive privilege consists of two categories: first, matters relating to state secrets or national security; second, other forms of official information that, if disclosed, would be injurious to government consultative functions. Following Vaughn v. Rosen, 523 F.2d 1136 (D.C. Cir. 1975), McClelland determined that it is insufficient to demonstrate that requested information was prepared to aid a decisionmaker in the decisional process, id. at 1289:

[T]o come within the privilege...the document must be a direct part of the deliberative process in that it makes recommendations or expresses opinions on legal or policy matters. Put another way, pre-decisional materials are not exempt merely because they are pre-decisional; they must also be a part of the agency give-and-take of the deliberative process by which the decision itself is made.

Under McClelland, if an executive privilege claim is meritorious, the adjudicator balances the employee's need for the information against the agency's need to protect its deliberative process, and the adjudicator is then to "excise those portions where the balance tips in favor of protecting the deliberative process." Id. at 1290.
