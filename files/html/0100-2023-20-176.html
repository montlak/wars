D. OSC DISCIPLINARY ACTION

Under the statutory provisions now codified at 5 USC 1215, OSC may, by written complaint, seek disciplinary action against an employee who commits a prohibited personnel practice. OSC operates with autonomy in its investigation and in the initiation of disciplinary actions, as in Special Counsel v. Harvey, 28 MSPR 595, 599 (1984), declining to bar a disciplinary complaint after OSC first closed out and then reopened the case:

A primary duty of the Special Counsel under the Reform Act is to protect employees against prohibited personnel practices. Section 1206(a)(1)-(3) sets out his investigative authority and responsibilities. The Special Counsel is empowered to investigate allegations made to him to the extent necessary. The Special Counsel may investigate even in the absence of allegations. Neither expressly nor implicitly does the statute prohibit the reopening of a terminated investigation and pursuing it without further allegations. Contrary to Harvey's assertion, closing an investigation is not tantamount to "dismissing" charges.

We conclude that the Special Counsel may use whatever information he has available, whether derived from a closed or reopened file; consolidate; reinstitute; or change the course of an investigation from a corrective to a disciplinary focus or the reverse. The decision on whether to pursue an investigation is a matter for the Special Counsel's discretion. Whether an investigation culminates in no action or in initiation of a proceeding that is remedial or disciplinary is similarly a matter for his discretion.

For the Board to have jurisdiction over the complaint, the respondent should be an employee at the time the OSC complaint is filed with the Board's Clerk. If the individual is an employee at the time the action is filed, but retires or resigns before the complaint is served, the Board retains jurisdiction. See Special Counsel v. Santella, 46 MSPR 99 (1990). If the respondent is not an employee at the time the action is initiated through the filing of a complaint, the Board has no jurisdiction over that individual. Special Counsel v. Eidmann, 49 MSPR 614, 619 n.4 (1991); Special Counsel v. Owens, 11 MSPR 128, 129 (1982) (no jurisdiction over an employee who left federal service before the OSC complaint was filed and served).
