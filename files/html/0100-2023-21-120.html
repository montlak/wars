5. Calculation

Hathaway v. OPM, 118 MSPR 678, 681 ¶ 7, 2012 MSPB 128 (2012), described the basic financial structure of the survivor annuity:

Under the CSRS, the surviving spouse of a retired federal employee is entitled to an annuity equal to 55 percent of the retiree's annuity unless the survivor consented in writing to receive no such survivor annuity or a reduced annuity at the time of the employee's retirement. 5 U.S.C. §§ 8339(j)(1), 8341(b)(1); Luten, 110 M.S.P.R. 667, ¶ 10; 5 C.F.R. § 831.614. A retiree may, within 18 months after retirement, choose to elect a survivor annuity for the spouse to whom he was married at retirement if he did not previously do so or to increase the size of such an annuity.5 U.S.C. § 8339(o)(1); Nunes v. Office of Personnel Management, 111 M.S.P.R. 221, ¶¶ 10-11 (2009); 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(b)(1).

The full CSRS base survivor annuity calculus was explained in Fortunato v. OPM, 87 MSPR 43, 45-47 ¶ 7-11 (2000):

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8341(b), a surviving spouse "is entitled to an annuity equal to 55 percent...of an annuity computed under section 8339(a)-(i), (n), (p), and (q) of this title as may apply with respect to the annuitant...." Thus, OPM and the AJ correctly found, and it is undisputed on review, that the appellant's base survivor annuity is 55 percent of the employee's base annuity.

Regarding the employee's annuity, the employee retired after 55 years of age with more than 30 years of service, and was apparently not in a special retirement category such as that for firefighters. Hence, 5 U.S.C. § 8339(a) is applicable in computing his annuity since it applies to annuities whose computation is not "otherwise provided" by the other subsections of section 8339. See Wassenaar v. Office of Personnel Management, 55 M.S.P.R. 517, 524 (1992), rev'd on other grounds, 21 F.3d 1090 (Fed. Cir. 1994). In computing an annuity under section 8339(a), section 8339(f) states in pertinent part that the annuity "may not exceed 80 percent of--(1) the average pay of the employee...." Our review of the record shows that when the employee retired in October 1971, OPM computed his annuity based on 80 percent of his average pay of $1,463.00. Specifically, OPM multiplied $1,463.00 by 80 percent to arrive at $1,170.40; subtracted a "10% unpaid deposit" of $25.20 to arrive at $1,145.20; and multiplied the result by .975 to account for a "[d]eduction for survivor base," to arrive at $1,116.57, which was then divided by 12 to arrive at a monthly annuity of $93.00, rounded to the nearest dollar. (OPM's Annuity Rate Work Sheet).

At the same time that OPM calculated the employee's annuity, OPM calculated the appellant's potential survivor annuity. Id. at 16. Because the employee elected to use all of his annuity as the survivor base, OPM computed the survivor annuity based on 55 percent of the employee's gross annuity of $1,145.20 (80 percent of the employee's average pay of $1,463.00, less the "10% unpaid deposit"), which was divided by 12 to arrive at $52.00 per month rounded to the nearest dollar. Id. at 16. When the employee died in 1989, OPM applied the applicable COLAs to $52.00, to arrive at a survivor annuity of $149.00 per month, which it began paying the appellant. Her survivor annuity gradually increased due to COLAs, and, as of December 1998, was $199.00 per month.

Regarding the appellant's claim that the employee was entitled to disability retirement benefits, the record shows that the employee stated on his retirement application that he was totally disabled due to degenerative osteoarthritis. In addition, it appears from the record that OPM considered the employee to have been entitled to disability retirement. A disability retirement annuity is computed under 5 U.S.C. § 8339(g), "unless the employee...is eligible for a higher annuity computed under section 8339(a)-(e), (n), or (q)." 5 U.S.C. § 8337(a). Under section 8339(g), "[t]he annuity of an employee...retiring under section 8337 of this title is at least the smaller of--(1) 40 percent of his average pay; or (2) the sum obtained under 5 subsections (a)-(c), (n), or (q) of this section after increasing his service of the type last performed by the period elapsing between the date of separation and the date he becomes 60 years of age." Thus, under the two formulas set forth in section 8339(g), the maximum disability annuity is 40 percent of an employee's average pay. See Rogers v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 154, ¶ 22 n.4 (1999). Under section 8337(a), however, a disability retirement annuitant who is also entitled to regular retirement and whose regular retirement annuity would be greater is entitled to the greater annuity.

Thus, even though it appears that the employee was entitled to disability retirement, he was entitled to receive the greater, nondisability retirement annuity computed under section 8339(a), limited by the 80 percent maximum specified in section 8339(f). The record shows that the employee did receive the greater, nondisability retirement annuity, which OPM computed based on 80 percent of his average pay, in lieu of the lesser annuity he would have received strictly as a disability retirement annuitant, based on 40 percent of his average pay. Because the appellant's survivor annuity was computed based on the employee's annuity, which was based on 80 percent of his average pay, we find that OPM correctly concluded that the appellant received the maximum survivor benefits to which she was entitled.
