a. Inadequate Representation

The right of representation may be a mixed blessing. Some appellants obtain excellent representation--they have lots of time to research issues, engage in motion practice, develop information through careful discovery, and brief the issues--and at a reasonable cost; some appellants do not fare so well. Many Board decisions attribute to appellants the omissions or commissions of their representatives. Faults of agency representatives are similarly attributed to their principals. See Meyers v. EEOC, 45 MSPR 488, 493 (1990) ("We therefore hold that agencies are bound by the actions of their representatives just as appellants are."). Ineffective assistance of counsel is not a basis for setting aside a Board decision, Hijar v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 133, 135 n.1, 6 MSPB 121 (1981):

Appellant further contended that he was denied effective assistance of counsel during his hearing in violation of the Sixth Amendment. In support of this contention, he submitted four affidavits, including three from witnesses who testified in his behalf at the hearing. The affidavits assert that the appellant's counsel advised them not to bring up at the hearing certain statements allegedly made to them by agency officials. Since the attorney chosen to represent the appellant during the hearing was selected by him, and as the three witnesses testified under oath in behalf of the appellant, we fail to find that appellant was denied his right to be represented by counsel. Link v. Wabash Railroad Co., 370 U.S. 626, 632, 82 S. Ct. 1386, 1389, 8 L. Ed.2d 734 (1962). Thus, while the Sixth Amendment relates to criminal proceedings and not civil administrative proceedings, appellant was not denied his statutory right to be represented under 5 USC § 7701.

Archuleta v. Dept. of Air Force, 16 MSPR 404, 406 (1983), added:

With regard to the claim of ineffective counsel, appellant alleges that, despite his request, his representative failed to call any witnesses, including his doctors, to testify at the hearing. Additionally, appellant asserts that his representative did not know how to proceed at several points during the hearing. Inadequate counsel, however, even if true, is not grounds for reversal as appellant is held responsible for the action or inaction of his counsel. See Bennett v. Navy, 2 MSPB 93, 99 (1980).

See Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-0752-09-0691-I-1 (NP 12/15/2010) ("The appellant also argues that his attorneys "submitted only a portion" of the "substantial documentary evidence" he provided in support of his appeal. The appellant is bound by the acts and omissions of his chosen representative and allegations that his attorneys failed to represent him properly below are not grounds for granting his petition for review. See Fox v. U.S. Postal Service, 81 M.S.P.R. 522, ¶¶ 4-5 (1999)."); Hubbard v. USPS, 32 MSPR 505, 507 (1987) (Board declined to honor claims that the representative was inexperienced, did not provide adequate representation, and failed to select the best forum for the appeal: "The appellant is responsible for the acts or omissions of his designated representative.... Therefore, the Board will not consider further the appellant's allegations of misconduct or neglect of his chosen representative.").

The Board has not relaxed the rule on detrimental reliance by appellants on representatives, Wynn v. USPS, 115 MSPR 146, 149 ¶ 7, 2010 MSPB 214 (2010):

Further, the appellant's claim of inadequate representation does not constitute a basis for reversal of the initial decision. Sparks v. Department of the Interior, 62 M.S.P.R. 369, 371 (1994). Even if true, the presence of inadequate counsel is not a basis for reversal, because the appellant is held responsible for the action or inaction of his counsel. Id.; Sofio v. Internal Revenue Service, 7 M.S.P.R. 667, 670 (1981) (the appellant is responsible for the errors of his chosen representative).

Inadequate union representation was insufficient for a remand, Anthony v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 271, 272 (1985):

We find no merit in appellant's assertion that grossly inadequate representation by her union representative during the proceedings below precluded her from receiving a fair and impartial hearing and thus amounted to a denial of due process requiring a remand. As appellant recognizes, she is responsible for the actions or inactions of her representative and cannot avoid the consequences of her choice. See Wilson v. USPS, 12 MSPB 296, 14 MSPR 129 (1982), and cases cited therein. Moreover, to the extent that she contends that she was unable to understand the proceedings in which she was involved because of the alleged deficiencies in her representation, we find her argument belied by her participation in the hearing below and the aid afforded by the presiding official. See Thompson v. US. Coast Guard, 10 MSPB 397, 11 MSPR 461 (1982).

See Anthony v. SSA, PH-0432-15-0405-I-1 (NP 7/28/2016) ("The appellant states that, occasionally, her union attorney representatives did not 'work with' her and that she did not know the law. She also asserts that she missed the hearing and a prehearing settlement opportunity because of poor representation, and she feels like she 'was set up for failure.' Having considered the appellant's arguments, we find that she has not shown that her union representatives' negligence or malfeasance thwarted her diligent efforts to prosecute her appeal.").

The Federal Circuit noted as to the removal of an employee who lost a security clearance and asserted that her attorney should have, but did not, defend against the clearance revocation, Brewer v. Dept. of Navy, 118 Fed. Appx. 495, 497 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP):

The law of the land is clear. Misbehavior of an attorney is taxed to the client. Link, 370 U.S. at 633-34, 82 S. Ct. 1386. The law in our circuit is the same, and is equally clear. When a client relies in good faith on incorrect advice, the client is bound by the actions of the attorney. See Massingale v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 736 F.2d 1521, 1523 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In Rowe v. Merit Systems Protection Board, we were faced with a client who placed good faith reliance on the lawyer who negligently failed to meet a filing deadline at the Board. 802 F.2d 434 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Citing Link, we stated:

Therefore, Rowe cannot escape the consequences of the lateness of his appeal that was filed by his union attorney after the deadline, even though Rowe himself acted in good faith in relying on the advice and actions of his attorney.

Id. at 437.

Notwithstanding the clarity of the law which burdens the client with the misbehavior of the attorney, the Board has decided that, in some circumstances, for purposes of its own filing deadlines, it will excuse the client from deceptive conduct of the attorney. We have no cause in this case to decide whether the Board's discretion over its own procedure is sufficiently broad so as to allow it to adopt a more generous rule in instances of attorney misconduct than is otherwise applicable.

On the related issue of reliance on counsel's advice as a defense to a misconduct charge (a topic discussed in Chapter 8, "Reliance on Advice of Counsel or Other Individual"), Jones v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 495, 498 n.5 (1983), suggested that the defense is not likely to be successful:

Generally, reliance on advice of counsel is not an absolute defense to malfeasance. See, e.g., Licovali v. United States, 294 F.2d 207, 209 (D.C. Cir.) (advice of counsel is no defense to willfully defaulting to Congressional subpoena), cert. denied, 366 U.S. 936, 81 S. Ct. 1660, 6 L. Ed.2d 847 (1961); Taylor v. United States, 221 F.2d 809 (6th Cir.) (advice of counsel is no defense to refusing to obey a court order), cert. denied, 350 U.S. 834, 76 S. Ct. 69, 100 L. Ed. 744 (1955)....
