1. Inability to Travel to Hearing Site

A letter indicating financial inability to attend a hearing at a location remote to the appellant should be viewed as an attempt to show good cause for changing the hearing location. Gray v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 460, 461, 5 MSPB 1 (1981). If the appellant appears to desire a hearing but also appears to be unable to travel to attend the hearing, the AJ should ensure that the appellant understands the availability of a telephonic hearing. See Oribio v. OPM, 47 MSPR 588, 591-92 (1991). An AJ should ensure that an appellant is made to understand his hearing rights if his appeal form is ambiguous or confused as to the request for and location of a hearing, Rossett v. OPM, 87 MSPR 415, 417-18 ¶¶ 5-6 (2001):

There is a strong policy consideration that an appellant receive a hearing on the merits of his appeal. Siman v. Department of the Air Force, 80 MSPR 306 ¶ 6 (1998); Apiado v. Office of Personnel Management, 58 MSPR 33, 35 (1993). Here, the record does not show that the AJ adequately informed the appellant of his options regarding his right to a hearing. On his appeal form, the appellant did not check either of the boxes marked "Yes" or "No" in response to the query, "Do you want a hearing?" Instead, he stated: "UNABLE TO TRAVEL PLEASE CALL ME AT HOME." Under these circumstances, the AJ should have informed the appellant of the alternatives to an in-person hearing, specifically, a hearing by telephone or by videoconference. See Morgan v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 MSPR 1 ¶ 14 n.4 (1999), aff'd, No. 99-3435 (Fed. Cir. May 17, 2000) (Table).... The record does not show that he did so.

During a January 27, 2000 telephone conference, the AJ confirmed that the appellant "want[ed] the appeal decided on the basis of the written submissions" without a hearing. However, nothing in the record shows that the appellant was apprised by the AJ or was otherwise aware of the option of a hearing by telephone or videoconference. Thus, the record does not show that the appellant's waiver of his right to a hearing was an informed one. See Conant v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 MSPR 148, 150 ¶ 1 (1998) (the waiver of a right to a hearing must be an informed one). Although this pro se appellant does not argue on review that the AJ improperly denied him a hearing, he states on review that he "should have requested a personal hearing," and requests a hearing on review. We find, under these circumstances, that a remand is necessary to ensure that his right to a hearing is not unduly abridged....

The appellant does not waive a hearing if the AJ cannot travel to the hearing site. In Davila v. OPM, 50 MSPR 5, 6-8 (1991), the appellant, seeking an annuity under the CSRS, sought a hearing in the Philippines. The AJ could not travel there, however, because "the international crisis arising out of the war in the Persian Gulf" resulted in "foreign travel by administrative judges" being "indefinitely curtailed." The AJ informed the appellant that he could elect a hearing in Hawaii or withdraw his request for a hearing and obtain adjudication on the written record. The appellant first elected to have a decision on the record, but later, with his PFR, reiterated his request for a hearing. The Board remanded Davila, noting that the original hearing request was not waived:

...There is a strong policy consideration that an appellant receive a hearing on the merits of a case.... The appellant indicated his desire for a hearing several times below, and he would have received one in the Philippines, but for the Board's own travel restrictions, and international events.

An appellant's right to a hearing may only be waived by implication if there is a clear, unequivocal, and decisive act showing such a purpose.... Under the circumstances of this case, we will not, at this juncture, hold the appellant to the apparent hearing waiver that he submitted with his petition for review. Clearly, his only reason for offering to forego his desired hearing at that time was his initial lack of a means to finance a trip to Hawaii--which burden was imposed upon him by the Board, perforce, for reasons that were beyond his control.

The initial decision did not repeat the possibility that the appellant could request and receive a hearing in Hawaii, nor did it advise him that he could obtain a telephonic hearing.... Upon remand, the administrative judge shall advise the appellant of his options for obtaining a hearing on the merits of his appeal.

See Manoharan v. OPM, 103 MSPR 159, 163-64 ¶ 11, 2006 MSPB 252 (2006) ("The appellant, who has been acting pro se throughout this appeal, clearly indicated on his petition for appeal form that he wanted a hearing 'if possible,' and that a hearing could be held at a United States consulate in India. He also indicated that he could not appear before the AJ in person 'due to the distance involved,' and that he could answer any questions by telephone. The appellant further provided the AJ with the time difference between India and Washington, D.C., for purposes of telephone contact, and alternatively requested an opportunity to appear before the AJ on his next visit to the United States. We find, under these circumstances, that the appellant initially requested a hearing and thereafter repeatedly suggested ways in which he could present his case to the AJ over the telephone or in person. There was no clear, unequivocal, and decisive act by the appellant waiving a hearing, and the AJ should have informed him of his alternatives to an in-person hearing, including a hearing by telephone or videoconference"); Morgan v. USPS, 82 MSPR 1, 6 n.4 (1999) (implied waiver of a hearing was negated by AJ's failure to inform the appellant of the availability of a telephonic hearing as an alternative to a change of venue unsuccessfully sought by the appellant); Siman v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 306, 308-09 ¶¶ 5-6 (1998) (pro se appellant who explained to the AJ that he could not afford the expense of traveling to an in-person hearing should have been advised of the options of a telephonic or video hearing); Sincero v. OPM, 41 MSPR 239, 241-43 (1989) (cautioning AJs against construing as a waiver of a hearing, rather than as a request for a change in the location of a hearing, an appellant's statement that he wants but cannot afford a hearing); Apiado v. OPM, 58 MSPR 33, 35-36 (1993) (AJ should not have presumed that the appellant waived a hearing at a site far from her after she failed to respond to a notice requiring her to state whether she would attend the hearing; the AJ should have informed the appellant of her right to a telephonic hearing); Collier v. USPS, 53 MSPR 282, 284 (1992) (appellant's treating physician had stated that the appellant was unable to travel; the appeal was remanded).

If the appellant cannot afford to travel, he must let the AJ know of the problem. See Lee v. OPM, PH-0831-13-0051-I-1 (NP 2/14/2014) ("The appellant states on review that he did not request a hearing because he believed the hearing would be held in Pennsylvania and he could not afford to travel there. However, we see no indication in the record that the appellant ever advised the administrative judge of the reason why he did not request a hearing, so this argument does not provide a basis to disturb the initial decision. Cf. Manoharan v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 159, ¶ 11 (2006) (remanding an appeal for a hearing where the appellant repeatedly indicated that he wished to have a hearing, although he would have difficulty appearing in person, because the administrative judge should have informed the appellant of alternatives to an in-person hearing under those circumstances).").
