1. Charges

To require an appellant to prove that he did not commit the alleged offense by a preponderance of the evidence is legal error. The agency always has the burden of proof as to its charges. Because an employee has no burden to disprove the charges against him, it is the agency's obligation to submit a well-developed record to the AJ to prove its case by a preponderance of the evidence. Fagan v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 59, 61 (1983). The agency must prove all the essential elements of a charged substantive offense. King v. Nazelrod, 43 F.3d 663, 666 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

An agency is required not only to prove the charges levied, but also to prove that removal based upon the charges promotes the efficiency of the service. The burden is not on the appellant to prove, as an affirmative defense, that the removal will not promote the efficiency of the service. Starkey v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 289, 293, 3 MSPB 377 (1980); see Martinez v. DOD, 4 MSPR 15, 17, 4 MSPB 109 (1980) (agency must prove, under 5 USC 7513(a), that it took action for such cause as will promote service efficiency). An employee could demonstrate that service efficiency is not enhanced through her removal because the work unit experienced difficulties in accomplishing its work without the appellant. See Payne v. USPS, 72 MSPR 646, 653 (1996) (Member Amador dissenting) ("We find that this appellant's failure to perform a duty on one occasion does not negate her overall ability to perform her job at a satisfactory level, especially when her mistake resulted from an out-of-the-ordinary hiring situation, the appellant was not clearly on notice of her responsibility to perform the duty, and a much higher-level agency official vouched for Shaler as a good employee and waived review of Shaler's OPF. Indeed, based on Webb's testimony, we find that the agency's mission is being negatively affected by the appellant's demotion."). An agency is not estopped from discharging an employee for cause where its written evaluation of the employee's performance is satisfactory. Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 268 n.2 (D.C. Cir. 1977).

The Federal Circuit offered its analysis of the agency burden, Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

The review by the MSPB of a Chapter 75 action is denominated "an appeal" of agency action. 5 USC 7701(a) (1982). However, even though the employee is the initiating party, the agency is required to establish its case to the board by a "preponderance of the evidence." 5 USC 7701(c). Under this standard, the "appeal" requires a de novo determination of the facts. By seeking "review," an employee puts the agency in the position of a plaintiff bearing the burden of first coming forward with evidence to establish the fact of misconduct, the burden of proof, and the ultimate burden of persuasion, with respect to the basis for the charge or charges. The employee (while denominated appellant) has the advantageous evidentiary position of a defendant with respect to that aspect of the case.

"We give wide berth to agency decisions as to what type of adverse action is necessary to 'promote the efficiency of the service,' provided that the agency's decision bears some nexus to the reason for the adverse action." Einboden v. Dept. of Navy, 802 F.3d 1321, 1325-26 (Fed. Cir. 2015)

There is generally no requirement that an agency official have personal knowledge of charged conduct before taking an adverse action against an employee. Rabourn v. DOJ, 38 MSPR 103, 106-07 n.2 (1988) (citing Williamson v. DHHS, 3 MSPR 18, 21-22 (1980); Roane v. DHHS, 8 MSPR 339, 342-43 (1981)).

Although the statute requires adjudication of adverse actions on the basis of a preponderance of the evidence, a disciplinary case often encompasses issues other than those dealing purely with the ultimate fact of whether misconduct occurred. The agency may also be required to show the reasonableness of its actions toward the appellant if the agency, through its own decisions, has placed the employee in the position of committing misconduct. In In re Van Sciver, 1 MSPR 99, 99, 1 MSPB 94 (1979), the appellant was discharged for unauthorized absence. The employee defended on the basis that the agency had unreasonably denied a request for leave. The AJ determined that the discharge would be upheld because the agency had not been unreasonable or arbitrary in denying the leave. The Board, on appeal, remanded the case and vacated the AJ's decision, concluding that the AJ used an incorrect analysis concerning the burden of proof: the preponderance of the evidence standard must be applied to every element of proof of the agency's case, including the reasonableness of the agency action denying the leave.
