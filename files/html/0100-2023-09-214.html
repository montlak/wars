F. JURISDICTIONAL ISSUES

Chapter 1 constitutes a long essay on Board jurisdiction. In brief, "[t]he Board has limited jurisdiction, restricted by certain statutes and regulations." Flowers v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 167, 169 (1993). The Board always has jurisdiction to decide its jurisdiction. "While a decision of an adjudicatory agency like the Board as to its own jurisdiction is not the last word, it must assuredly be the first." Cody v. DOJ, 7 MSPR 218, 228-29, 7 MSPB 127 (1981). "[T]he issue of Board jurisdiction is always before the Board and may be raised by either party or sua sponte by an administrative judge or the Board at any time during a Board proceeding." Engler v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 109, 114 (1995); see Gainey v. USPS, DC-0752-16-0167-I-1 (NP 9/2/2016) ("It is incumbent on the administrative judge to address jurisdiction sua sponte if there is reason to believe that jurisdiction might be an issue in the appeal. See Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 678 (1991)."); Styslinger v. Dept. of Army, 105 MSPR 223, 231 ¶ 12, 2007 MSPB 53 (2007) ("See Covington v. Department of the Army, 85 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 9 (2000) ('The issue of Board jurisdiction is always before the Board and may be raised by either party or sua sponte by the Board at any time during a Board proceeding.')"); Robinson v. USPS, 92 MSPR 37, 43 n.2 (2002) (issue of jurisdiction may be raised sua sponte by the Board any time during the proceeding); Giove v. Dept. of Transp., 89 MSPR 560, 564 (2001) ("The existence of Board jurisdiction is a threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, and the appellant bears the burden of establishing jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence."). "[J]urisdiction is determined by the nature of the action at the time the appeal is filed." Fidler v. USPS, 53 MSPR 440, 443 (1992). Board (and judicial) decisions apply retroactively to pending cases, but retroactive application is not compelled; by definition, a jurisdictional ruling may never be prospective only. Williams v. DOD, 53 MSPR 23, 26 (1992).

Many appeals establish Board jurisdiction through identification of both an action defined by statute or regulation to be within the Board's jurisdiction and an adversely affected employee, the appellant, who is also classified by statute or regulation as an employee within the appellate jurisdiction of the Board. Some cases are not clearly within the jurisdiction of the Board, for example, when the employee's status does not permit him to appeal the action, or the action itself is normally considered voluntary--most commonly resignation or retirement--but is appealed as constructively adverse. Other cases, common in Board jurisprudence, require that Board jurisdiction be established through allegations involving both exhaustion of administrative remedies and averment of jurisdictional facts. Included in this group are Individual Right of Action appeals requiring both initial resort to OSC and pleading of whistleblowing allegations that conform to statutory definitions of protected activity. These appeals, appeals by probationers, and appeals from individuals asserting constructive adverse actions, require a jurisdictional showing of facts going beyond pro forma allegations. See Beadle v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 193, 197 (1989); cf. Herring v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3443) ("Assuming that Ms. Herring is again asserting her claim of marital-status discrimination, her bare assertion of discrimination, without any factual allegations in support, is insufficient to confer jurisdiction on the Board. See Briscoe v. VA, 55 F.3d 1571, 1573 (Fed. Cir. 1995) ("Although an appellant need not prove her entire case before she is entitled to a hearing, the board may request sufficient evidence to determine if, in the first instance, there is any support for what otherwise might be bald allegations."); Hill v. Department of Air Force, 796 F.2d 1469, 1470-71 (Fed. Cir. 1986).").

"Jurisdiction cannot be determined by any act of the parties," e.g., stipulation or concession of jurisdiction by the opposing party. See Murdock-Doughty v. Dept. of Air Force, 74 MSPR 244, 248 (1997) ("While the agency's representative did in fact make an unsworn statement in the agency's response to the acknowledgment order that the appellant was an 'employee' as defined by 5 USC 7511(a)(1), it is well settled that such statements do not confer jurisdiction on the Board."); Allen v. USPS, 73 MSPR 73, 76 (1997) ("jurisdiction cannot be determined by any act of the parties"); Mas v. Dept. of Army, 46 MSPR 46, 48 (1990) (the parties cannot confer jurisdiction on the Board by stipulation; the AJ must find jurisdiction as a matter of law); Metz v. USPS, 1 MSPR 693, 695 (1980) ("[T]he agency's advice to appellant regarding a right of appeal does not constitute a 'rule' under § 7701(a) and cannot confer a right of appeal to the Board.").

A jurisdictional issue is usually brought to fore by the AJ at the onset of the case, either by a section in the acknowledgment order or in a jurisdictional show cause order. The AJ will allow the appellant to submit a response on jurisdiction within a set number of days and the agency will be informed that it has a set period to respond to the appellant's jurisdictional submission. The agency may raise the jurisdictional issue through a motion to dismiss the appeal. At times the AJ raises the issue through an order to show cause and the agency raises the same issue by motion.

When it is an issue, ultimately "an appellant bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that his appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction." Keller v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 183, 187 (1996). To get to a hearing (on the merits, on jurisdiction, or both), the Federal Circuit noted in a restoration rights appeal, Bledsoe v. MSPB, 659 F.3d 1097, 1101-02 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (Judge Newman dissenting), that it is the appellant's initial burden to establish jurisdiction by first making allegations of nonfrivolous jurisdictional facts and then proving those facts at a jurisdictional hearing:

As this court has explained, "Congress has not 'directly spoken' to an employee's burden of proof for establishing the Board's jurisdiction." Garcia, 437 F.3d at 1338 (quoting Chevron v. Natural Res. Def. Council, 467 U.S. 837, 842 (1984)). But 5 U.S.C. § 7701(k) provides that the "Board may prescribe regulations to carry out the purpose of this section" and, as this court noted in Garcia, "[p]ursuant to this statutory authority, the Board has promulgated regulations to carry out § 7701." 437 F.3d at 1338. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)] states that the "appellant has the burden of proof, by a preponderance of the evidence, with respect to...[i]ssues of jurisdiction." In Garcia, this court held that this regulation is "neither 'arbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute,' and is therefore entitled to deference and is lawful." 437 F.3d at 1338. As this court explained, "the regulation clearly requires that the claimant establish jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence, independent of whether facts relevant to deciding the merits overlap with the facts relevant to deciding jurisdiction." Id. at 1340.

In Garcia, this court distinguished numerous cases regarding Article III jurisdiction as inapposite in rejecting the view that Board "jurisdiction attaches when an employee makes non-frivolous claims." Id. at 1335 (citing Duke Power Co. v. Carolina Envtl. Study Grp., Inc., 438 U.S. 59 (1978); Montana-Dakota Utils. Co. v. Nw. Pub. Serv. Co., 341 U.S. 246 (1951); Bell v. Hood, 327 U.S. 678 (1946); The Fair v. Kohler Die & Specialty Co., 228 U.S. 22 (1913)). Cf. Arbaugh v. Y&H Corp., 546 U.S. 500 (2006).

Rather, in Garcia, this court explained that "[t]here is nothing in the plain language of the statute requiring or even suggesting a non-frivolous standard...[nor that] Congress intended that, in cases in which jurisdictional issues and merits issues overlap, the Board is required to follow the standards for jurisdiction applicable to Article III courts." 437 F.3d at 1336. "[i]nasmuch as the Board is not an Article III court, we fail to see how the backdrop of standards applicable to Article III court jurisdiction suggests that Congress intended those standards to apply to this quasi-judicial administrative agency." Id. at 1337 (also finding "suggestions that Congress specifically did not intend concepts from Article III courts to be used by the Board.").

As this court further explained in Garcia, establishing jurisdiction at the Board is a two-step process. First, a petitioner must meet the threshold requirement of making non-frivolous allegations of fact which, if true, would be sufficient to establish the Board's jurisdiction. Id. at 1330. If the petitioner makes non-frivolous allegations to support jurisdiction, then and only then will the petitioner be entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which the petitioner must prove jurisdiction by preponderant evidence. See id. at 1344 ("[O]nce a claimant makes non-frivolous claims of Board jurisdiction, namely claims that, if proven, establish the Board's jurisdiction, then the claimant has a right to a hearing. At the hearing, claimant must prove jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence."). After the petitioner has proven jurisdiction by preponderant evidence, then and only then is the Board free to reach the merits of the petitioner's appeal. "If the Board determines that the claimant fails to prove jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence, then the Board does not have jurisdiction and the case is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction." Id. When the Board lacks jurisdiction, "it cannot decide the merits of the case." Id. at 1340.

Because the Board's jurisdiction is limited rather than plenary, and because the petitioner must prove jurisdiction by preponderant evidence, including on issues that overlap with the merits, it is crucial to identify at the outset the jurisdictional requirements of any given appeal.

Judge Newman pointed out in her dissent in Bledsoe what several judges of the Federal Circuit noted in past decisions: the distinction between jurisdiction (established by statute or regulation) and the ability to state a claim on the merits, id. at 1106-09:

Ms. Bledsoe appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board, requesting review of the Postal Service's denial of her request for restoration to light duty service. The Board held a telephonic conference, requested evidence on the relevant factors, and upon consideration of the evidence and argument, and application of the prescribed standard of review, the Board concluded that the agency's decision should be affirmed. The Board then, curiously, did not affirm the agency's decision, but instead dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. This inapt and illogical resolution "obscures the issue," as the Supreme Court remarked in Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U.S. 500 (2006), counseling against "unrefined" jurisdictional rulings:

Judicial opinions, the Second Circuit incisively observed, "often obscure the issue by stating that the court is dismissing 'for lack of jurisdiction' when some threshold fact has not been established, without explicitly considering whether the dismissal should be for lack of subject matter jurisdiction or for failure to state a claim." Da Silva, 229 F.3d, at 361. We have described such unrefined dispositions as "drive-by jurisdictional rulings" that should be accorded "no precedential effect" on the question whether the federal court had authority to adjudicate the claim in suit. Steel Co., 523 U.S. at 91, 118 S. Ct. 1003.

546 U.S. at 511. As in Arbaugh, the issue here is not whether the Board had authority to adjudicate Ms. Bledsoe's claim, but whether her claim was properly denied on the merits. As in Arbaugh, the dismissal of the claim as outside the Board's jurisdiction "obscures the issue." Id. The Board undoubtedly has jurisdiction to decide whether to sustain the agency's denial of restoration, for the Board is assigned this jurisdiction by statute.

...

The MSPB found that Ms. Bledsoe had made non-frivolous allegations of the elements of her claim and considered the reasons given by the Postal Service for denying restoration. The Postal Service stated that there was no "necessary and productive work available" within Ms. Bledsoe's medical restrictions. The administrative judge, at a status conference with the parties, stated that "in order to raise a non-frivolous allegation that denial of restoration was arbitrary and capricious, the appellant must identify a vacant position, or positions, within 50 miles" of her former work location, as this would constitute "specific, independent evidence corroborating her allegation that the denial was arbitrary and capricious." Ms. Bledsoe, aided by a union representative, identified three "door monitor" positions at her prior location, provided a description of the duties of this position, and explained why Ms. Bledsoe could perform those duties within her medical limitations. She also presented evidence and argument as to the seniority of various employees at that location, as well as medical evidence of her partial recovery. The Postal Service and Ms. Bledsoe presented opposing evidence and argument as to the availability of suitable positions within the commuting area and Ms. Bledsoe's suitability for those positions.

The Court has often reminded the lower courts and the administrative tribunals that "the term 'jurisdictional' properly applies only to 'prescriptions delineating the classes of cases (subject-matter jurisdiction) and the persons (personal jurisdiction)' implicating that authority." Reed Elsevier, Inc. v. Muchnick, _ U.S. _, 130 S. Ct. 1237, 1243 (2010) (quoting Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U.S. 443, 455 (2004)). In Reed Elsevier the Court reiterated the inaptness of "drive-by jurisdictional rulings":

In Arbaugh, we described the general approach to distinguish "jurisdictional" conditions from claim-processing requirements or elements of a claim: "If the Legislature clearly states that a threshold limitation on a statute's scope shall count as jurisdictional, then courts and litigants will be duly instructed and will not be left to wrestle with the issue. But when Congress does not rank a statutory limitation on coverage as jurisdictional, courts should treat the restriction as nonjurisdictional in character."

Id. at 1244 (quoting Arbaugh, 546 U.S. at 515-16); see also Bell v. Hood, 327 U.S. 678, 682 (1946) ("Jurisdiction, therefore, is not defeated as respondents seem to contend, by the possibility that the averments might fail to state a cause of action on which petitioners could actually recover."). The standard of review is not an element of jurisdiction. The standard of "arbitrary and capricious" is deferential, not jurisdictional. In Spruill v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 978 F.2d 679 (Fed. Cir. 1992) this court explained that: "The forum had jurisdiction to hear the matter in the first instance--that is, subject-matter jurisdiction existed--as long as the petitioner asserted nonfrivolous claims," id. at 687-88, and pointed out that: "Sometimes the question of subject matter jurisdiction gets confused with the question of entitlement to relief, that is, whether a cause of action has been stated in the complaint, or later proved." Id. at 686. Such confusion is here apparent.

Entitlement to relief was the dispute in Ms. Bledsoe's appeal to the MSPB, not subject matter jurisdiction or personal jurisdiction. In Union Pacific Railroad Co. v. Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers & Trainmen, _ U.S. _, 130 S. Ct. 584 (2009), where the agency was the National Railroad Adjustment Board, the Court explained that "there is surely a starting presumption that when jurisdiction is conferred, a court may not decline to exercise it. The general rule applicable to courts also holds for administrative agencies directed by Congress to adjudicate particular controversies." Id. at 590 (citation omitted); see also Pruidze v. Holder, 632 F.3d 234, 240 (6th Cir. 2011) ("[A]n agency cannot contract its power to hear claims that fall plainly within its statutory jurisdiction.").

The Board's jurisdiction is set by statute. No statute states that jurisdiction is ousted if the petitioner does not make her case. The standard by which the Board reviews agency action is not a matter of jurisdiction, but of procedural law. A review of the evidence going to the merits leads to a decision on the merits, not denial or grant of jurisdiction depending on whether the petitioner wins or loses. See Bell, 327 U.S. at 682 ("[I]t is well settled that the failure to state a proper cause of action calls for a judgment on the merits and not for a dismissal for want of jurisdiction."); Spruill, 978 F.2d at 687 ("[F]ailure of proof of an element of the cause of action means the petitioner is not entitled to the relief he seeks."). The Board incorrectly characterized its decision as jurisdictional, denying its own jurisdiction assigned by 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a) and 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(c). This court compounds the error. From this court's flawed "drive-by jurisdictional" analysis, I must, respectfully, dissent.

But see Wright v. USPS, DA-0353-15-0517-I-1 n.2 (NP 5/17/2016) ("Consistent with the decision of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit in Bledsoe v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 659 F.3d 1097, 1102 (Fed. Cir. 2011), the administrative judge informed the appellant that, to prove jurisdiction over his appeal, he would need to first make nonfrivolous allegations of Board jurisdiction, and then he would be entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which he would be required to prove jurisdiction by preponderant evidence. However, the Board's revised regulation, effective March 30, 2015, and applicable in any appeal filed on or after March 30, 2015, modified the burden of proof for establishing jurisdiction over restoration appeals to require only nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.57(b)."). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Jurisdictional Issue; Right to Hearing," for jurisdiction over probationers' appeals; refer to jurisdictional issues in Chapter 6, "Adverse Actions."]

The Board may avoid a jurisdictional determination if the appeal is not timely filed and is dismissed on that basis, Persons v. USPS, 75 MSPR 428, 431 (1997):

While the existence of Board jurisdiction is the threshold issue in adjudicating an appeal, a jurisdictional determination would not be in the Board's interest of adjudicatory efficiency and fairness to the parties involved in an untimely appeal if the record is sufficiently developed to show that the appeal should be dismissed because no good cause exists for the untimely filing.

See Andrews v. Dept. of Army, 76 MSPR 147, 150 (1997) (following Persons).

Alternatively, if an appeal is beyond the Board's jurisdiction, the appeal may be dismissed without a timeliness determination. See Ford v. USPS, 82 MSPR 327, 331 ¶ 14 (1999) (citing Conover v. Dept. of Army, 78 MSPR 605, 614 (1998)). "[W]here a case...appears not to be within the jurisdiction of the Board, it is more appropriate for the presiding official to dismiss on jurisdictional grounds rather than to dismiss on procedural grounds of questionable validity." In re Bohannon, 1 MSPR 15, 17, 1 MSPB 15 (1979). On occasion the AJ may permit the parties to reserve a jurisdictional issue, such as veterans preference status, pending Board review of an initial decision on a merits issue, e.g., whether the agency engaged in a RIF or adverse action when appellants accepted positions during a reorganization. If, through its consideration of a PFR, the Board then determines that the merits issue requires further examination, the appeal returns to the AJ for analysis of that issue as well as the jurisdictional issue. See Burger v. USPS, 88 MSPR 579, 583 ¶ 12 (2001). The same result could occur if the merits issue were presented to the Board through an interlocutory appeal. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Jurisdiction or Timeliness as Alternate Bases for Dismissal of Appeal."]

The Board's analytical standard as to jurisdictional issues parallels the standard applied in federal courts, Miller v. USPS, 105 MSPR 89, 94-95 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 68 (2007):

...An appeal within the Board's jurisdiction may be properly dismissed for failure to state a claim if the appellant cannot obtain effective relief before the Board even if his allegations are accepted as true. Kennedy v. Department of the Air Force, 102 M.S.P.R. 524, ¶ 7 (2006); Young v. Federal Mediation & Conciliation Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 99, ¶ 5 (2002), aff'd, 66 Fed. Appx. 858 (Fed. Cir. 2003). In appraising the sufficiency of an appeal, the Board follows the accepted rule that an action should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the appellant can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief. Kennedy, 102 M.S.P.R. 524, ¶ 7. Taking as true the allegations in the appellant's petition for appeal, we note that it is not beyond doubt that the appellant could not prove such a set of facts.

Miller is tempered by the need of the appellant to establish nonfrivolous factual allegations supporting Board jurisdiction, and "nonfrivolous" allegations often really amount to a requirement of fairly detailed factual pleading. The amount of detail depends on the nature of the case and the approach of the assigned AJ. The ability to pursue an appeal and obtain a hearing depends on a statement of the claim with sufficient particularity to demonstrate that the appellant and his claim are at least arguably within the Board's jurisdiction. What is ordinarily required to avoid dismissal of an appeal is a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction, constituting an allegation of fact that, if proved, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal. See Tedesco v. Dept. of Air Force, 90 MSPR 367, 372 ¶ 9 (2001).

If there are material facts in dispute concerning jurisdiction, the case should proceed to a hearing, sometimes dubbed a "jurisdictional hearing." To obtain a jurisdictional hearing, the appellant need only allege facts that, if proved, could establish a claim; the appellant need not make the jurisdictional showing by preponderant evidence. Morman v. DOD, 90 MSPR 197, 201 ¶ 8 (2001). A nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction is not the same as proving facts that would establish the Board's jurisdiction over an appeal. Burger v. USPS, 93 MSPR 582, 592 ¶ 25 (2003); see Frison v. Dept. of Army, 94 MSPR 431, 433 ¶ 4 (2003) ("A nonfrivolous allegation is an allegation of fact that, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal.... [A]n appellant need not allege facts which, if proven, definitely would establish that the retirement was involuntary; he need only allege facts which, if proven, could establish such a claim."). In practice, there are not many jurisdictional hearings. Jurisdiction is decided based on the evidence and argument submitted in response to a jurisdictional order or motion to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction. If there are nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction, and if a hearing is requested on the merits, jurisdiction will likely again be discussed in the initial decision on the merits. An AJ could hold a jurisdictional hearing and a merits hearing on, for example, a constructive adverse action--jurisdiction and merits merging for disposition through an initial decision.

There are mixed pronouncements on jurisdictional allegations. There is support for the proposition that jurisdiction is established by more than just allegations: the allegations should be sworn, or supported by evidence, and there is some contrary precedent. See, e.g., Edem v. Dept. of Commerce, 64 MSPR 501, 503-06 (1994) ("Although the Board stated in Bedynek-Stumm that the particular 'unsupported and unsworn assertions' made in that case were insufficient to establish a right to a jurisdictional hearing, 57 MSPR at 179, it did not hold that sworn statements are essential to establishing a right to a hearing, and no such requirement exists."). A review of the cases involving constructive adverse actions in Chapter 6, and Individual Right of Action cases, in Chapter 13, and USERRA or VEOA cases in Chapter 1, shows that the caselaw requires different levels of initial jurisdictional allegations and evidence, depending on the nature of the appeal. Because it is not uniform, how properly to plead and provide evidence supporting jurisdictional allegations can be confusing. The amount of evidence necessary to support particularized allegations is generally left unsaid in MSPB acknowledgment orders. AJs' show cause orders are often so broadly stated that it is difficult to discern exactly where the pleading deficiencies lie and how they are cured.

If evidence is required to demonstrate jurisdiction, then the Board is really employing an attenuated form of summary judgment. See Marcino v. USPS, 344 F.3d 1199, 1204 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (citing Dick v. VA, 290 F.3d 1356, 1361 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (partially overruled on other grounds, Garcia v. DHS, 437 F.3d 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2006)), for the proposition that the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal if the employee makes nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction supported by affidavits or other evidence). Over time the Board and the Federal Circuit have established a variety of pleading standards to establish jurisdiction over significant components of the Board's caseload. These standards are not consistent. They are not precisely defined. [Readers should examine discussion of VEOA, USERRA, constructive adverse actions, and whistleblower (IRA) appeals in this chapter as well as in Chapters 1, 6, and 13 of the Guide for cases that define what allegations and evidence has to be established in the underlying appeal to establish jurisdiction over these cases.] The safest route for an appellant to travel in establishing jurisdiction is to supply in the appeal, or in response to a show cause order, not only allegations but also evidence (through documents and affidavits or sworn statements) establishing facts supporting jurisdiction. That will not be required in every appeal, but it should be sufficient in any case, to establish jurisdiction--depending on whether allegations and evidence can be compiled that will meet the jurisdictional standards for the appealed action.

Under Hurst v. GSA, 2 MSPR 497, 499, 3 MSPB 21 (1980), if the issue involves jurisdiction, the facts or allegations supporting jurisdiction should not be raised for the first time in a PFR. The information ought first to be placed before the AJ. If the agency alleges in its response to the appeal that the employee's position is beyond the jurisdiction of the Board, e.g., the employee is in the excepted service, and if the appellant does not counter the allegation with an assertion that she occupies a position within the Board's appeals jurisdiction, the Board will not consider the appellant's jurisdictional argument raised in a PFR. Robinson v. FEC, 33 MSPR 254, 256 (1987); see Gallucci v. VA, 67 MSPR 360, 365 (1995) (declining to consider the evidence of the appellant's argument that he expected his attorney to properly respond to the show-cause order from the AJ; there was no showing that the appellant contacted his attorney about responding to the order or that the lawyer misled the appellant on how or when to respond to the order). But see Stewart v. TVA, 77 MSPR 565, 567-68 (1998) (considering at PFR stage an agency jurisdictional submission timely filed but not considered by AJ); Di Iorio v. Holocaust Memorial Council, 69 MSPR 100, 104 (1995) ("Although not argued by the appellant on review, we reopen this appeal on our own motion because we find that he raised a nonfrivolous allegation of Board jurisdiction that entitled him to a jurisdictional hearing."); Miller v. DOJ, 41 MSPR 353, 356 (1989) (the Board considered a jurisdictional allegation raised in a PFR because the AJ did not inform the appellant of what he needed to plead to invoke jurisdiction over his appeal). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Jurisdictional Issue," for decisions explaining when evidence of jurisdiction may first be submitted with a PFR or with a request for reopening of a final decision.]

The rule that jurisdictional information ought to be provided by the appellant to the AJ and not for the first time in a PFR, assuming fair notice of the jurisdictional issue when the appeal was pending before the AJ, does not necessarily operate in the reverse: lack of jurisdiction may be raised at any time during a proceeding. For example, lack of jurisdiction was raised when disability retirement was retroactively effective on or before the date of a removal. Morgan v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 477, 478 (1985); see Campbell v. OPM, 90 MSPR 68, 71 ¶ 8 (2001) (argument that the Board lacks jurisdiction may be raised any time; the agency was not estopped when it raised a position contrary to that taken below); Sonneborn v. DOD, 80 MSPR 215, 217-18 ¶¶ 4-5 (1998) (agency was permitted to raise at the PFR level exclusion of the appellant's RIF appeal from Board jurisdiction as a result of coverage under the collective bargaining contract); Becker v. VA, 76 MSPR 292, 296 (1997) (Board may always examine its jurisdiction over an IRA case, even if the appellant does not explicitly raise the issue); Waldrop v. USPS, 72 MSPR 12, 15 (1996) ("[T]he agency cannot be estopped from arguing that the Board lacks jurisdiction, notwithstanding the position it took before the administrative judge, and notwithstanding the findings and the testimony of the deciding official."), followed, Reedy v. USPS, 84 MSPR 453, 456 ¶ 4 (1999), and Preyor v. USPS, 83 MSPR 571, 573 ¶ 4 (1999); Tetrault v. USPS, 71 MSPR 376, 378 (1996) (noting the rule that the Board may raise jurisdiction sua sponte at any time); Fitzgerald v. Dept. of Navy, 70 MSPR 152, 154 (1996) ("As a limited-jurisdiction federal tribunal, the Board has an obligation to inquire into its own jurisdiction sua sponte."); Fish v. USPS, 67 MSPR 540, 542 (1995) (allowing the agency to raise at the PFR stage the appellant's entitlement to preference eligibility, a prerequisite to the Board's jurisdiction); Williams v. EEOC, 64 MSPR 436, 438 (1994) ("[J]urisdiction is always at issue and may be raised by either party or sua sponte by an administrative judge or the Board at any time during a Board proceeding"; there is no time limit for raising a question of jurisdiction); Chan v. VA, 53 MSPR 617, 619 (1992) ("The issue of the Board's jurisdiction may be raised at any time during a proceeding."); cf. Dunklebarger v. MSPB, 130 F.3d 1476, 1480 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (jurisdictional issue may be raised irrespective of prior positions on jurisdiction of the parties or the MSPB).

There have been some direct, and a few indirect, critiques of the Board by the Federal Circuit for the Board's failure or inability to distinguish an appeal outside its jurisdiction from a case that the appellant fails to prove. See Spruill v. MSPB, 978 F.2d 679 (Fed. Cir. 1992). Walley v. VA, 279 F.3d 1010 (Fed. Cir. 2002), distinguished an appeal outside the Board's jurisdiction and one within the Board's jurisdiction but decided against an appellant who fails to state or prove a claim:

We have recently had occasion to review the question of whether Board jurisdiction is established by a non-frivolous allegation on a jurisdictional issue. Yunus v. Dept. of Veterans Affairs, 242 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Based on a long line of this court's decisions, we concluded that the Board can properly reach the merits of a claim because a non-frivolous allegation is sufficient to confer jurisdiction. See id. ("This court has held that the Board has jurisdiction over an IRA appeal if the appellant has exhausted his administrative remedies before the [Office of Special Counsel] and makes 'non-frivolous allegations'...."); Spruill v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 978 F.2d 679, 687-88 (Fed. Cir. 1992) ("The forum had jurisdiction to hear the matter in the first instance--that is, subject-matter jurisdiction existed--as long as the petitioner asserted nonfrivolous claims."). Once the Board has jurisdiction, it may address the merits.

Under our decision in Yunus, there are issues before the Board that are at one and the same time jurisdictional issues--requiring a non-frivolous allegation for Board jurisdiction--and merits issues--requiring a determination in favor of the employee if relief is to be awarded. As we discuss below, in the present context the question of whether a termination occurred because of a compensable injury is one of those issues. Board jurisdiction is established by a non-frivolous allegation of a termination because of a compensable injury, but the employee can succeed on the merits only if after a hearing there is a finding that such a termination occurred.

Section 1201.56 of the regulations, placing the burden of proof on the employee with respect to "jurisdictional issues," is plainly not concerned with the first issue of technical jurisdiction (the need for a non-frivolous allegation), but rather with the second issue (concerning the ultimate merits). It places the burden of proof on the employee at a merits hearing with respect to merits issues that are also jurisdictional issues. Thus, although the regulation characterizes the issues with which it is concerned as "jurisdictional" in nature, the regulation is directed, in fact, to the allocation of the burden of proof on the merits. The consequence is that if a merits issue is one that is also "jurisdictional," the burden of proof on that merits issue remains with the employee even though, as a technical matter, the Board's jurisdiction is established by a non-frivolous allegation. The Board itself has interpreted this regulation as governing the burden of proof on those issues that the Board has viewed as "jurisdictional." Smith v. Dep't of Energy, 89 M.S.P.R. 430, 434 (2001); Giove v. Dep't of Transp., 89 M.S.P.R. 560, 564 (2001); Mobley v. U.S. Postal Serv., 86 M.S.P.R. 161, 164 (2000). We owe deference to the Board's interpretation. See Am. Express Co. v. United States, 262 F.3d 1376, 1382-83 (Fed. Cir. 2001). We accordingly conclude that this regulation allocates the burden of proof to the employee on merits issues which are also "jurisdictional issues."

In Spencer v. Dept. of Navy, 327 F.3d 1354, 1356-57 (Fed. Cir. 2003), Judge Plager expressed the court's distress concerning the continuing refusal or inability of the Board to distinguish jurisdictional analysis from analysis of the merits of a claim within the Board's jurisdiction:

This court for more than ten years, beginning with Spruill v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 978 F.2d 679 (Fed. Cir. 1992), has tried to get the Board to clearly separate the issue of jurisdiction from that of the merits of a petitioner's case. We recently once again patiently explained the difference, and how that difference bears on a fair hearing process. See Yunus, 242 F.3d at 1371-72 & n.1; see also Langer v. Dep't of the Treasury, 265 F.3d 1259, 1264-65 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Indeed, we held recently in Dick v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 290 F.3d 1356, 1361-62 (Fed. Cir. 2002), that an employee is not entitled to a hearing on the threshold issue of jurisdiction, because the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal if the employee makes non-frivolous allegations that the elements of his claim are satisfied. Langer, 265 F.3d at 1265; Spruill, 978 F.2d at 687-88. Whether allegations are non-frivolous is determined on the basis of the written record. Dick, 290 F.3d at 1361. Only after Board jurisdiction is established is the Board required to provide an employee with a hearing under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a)(1), and that hearing is a hearing on the merits, not a jurisdictional hearing. Id. at 1362.

It is axiomatic that at that hearing the appellant must establish the elements of his claim by a preponderance of the evidence. See Langer, 265 F.3d at 1265. Failure to do so is a failure to state a claim on which relief can be granted; it is not, repeat not, a matter of lack of subject matter jurisdiction to decide the merits. The consequence of such a failure is a judgment on the merits, not a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction.

In an oral argument in the summer of 2008, Judge Newman of the Federal Circuit succinctly explained to counsel: jurisdiction involves the right to complain, not the determination of the merits of the complaint. See Bledsoe v. MSPB, 659 F.3d 1097, 1101-02 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (dissent by Judge Newman) (distinguishing jurisdiction and the failure to state a claim with Board jurisdiction).

In practical application, the distinction between the failure to establish jurisdiction and the failure to state or prove a claim is unclear. The Board tends to merge the concepts when there is a jurisdictional issue, i.e., an appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction only if certain factual elements are proved by the appellant, for example, an IRA or USERRA claim. Among the other possible examples, the appellant may allege that his resignation or retirement was truly a discharge. The agency may file a motion to dismiss the appeal for lack of jurisdiction or the AJ may, without motion, tell the appellant by letter or by a show-cause order that he must explain why the Board has jurisdiction over the case. Unrepresented appellants may have particular difficulty figuring out how to respond. They may not be familiar with litigation practice or the peculiarities of Board jurisdiction. More than a few lawyers share the problem.
