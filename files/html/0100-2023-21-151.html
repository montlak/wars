L. APPORTIONMENT OF ANNUITY BY COURT ORDER

The significance of court orders involving spousal annuity interests following divorce is understood when it is recognized that "divorce terminates a prior election of spousal survivor benefits." Painter v. OPM, 106 MSPR 385, 388-89 ¶ 5-10, 2007 MSPB 189 (2007) (citing 5 USC 8339(j)(5)(A); Hernandez v. Office of Personnel Management, 450 F.3d 1332, 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2006)). But cf. Evans v. Diamond, 957 F.3d 1098 (10th Cir. 2020) (TSP beneficiary designation of former spouse was not overcome by divorce or divorce decree waiving the interest of the former spouse in the decedent's retirement accounts). [Refer below to "Former Spouse Survivor Annuity."]

The right to a survivor annuity was summarized by Irving v. OPM, DE-0843-16-0327-I-1 (NP 7/13/2022) (FERS):

The appellant's right to a survivor annuity as a former spouse is governed by the portion of the FERS statute codified at 5 U.S.C. § 8445. That section of the FERS statute provides that a former spouse of a deceased employee is entitled to a survivor annuity "if and to the extent expressly provided for" in either: (1) an election under § 8417(b) of the FERS statute; or (2) the terms of any decree of divorce or annulment or any court order or court-approved property settlement agreement incident to such decree. See 5 U.S.C. § 8445(a). Although the "expressly provided for" provision of § 8445(a) does not require the use of "magic words," the intent to provide a survivor annuity must be clear, definite, explicit, plain, direct, and unmistakable, not dubious or ambiguous. See Holzman v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 M.S.P.R. 254, 257 (1994) (interpreting identical language found in 5 U.S.C. § 8341(h)(1)), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1237 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Under 5 C.F.R. § 838.804(b), in order to expressly award a former spouse survivor annuity or expressly direct an employee or annuitant to provide a former spouse survivor annuity, the court order must expressly state that the former spouse is entitled to a survivor annuity using terms similar to those described by § 838.912. See 5 C.F.R. § 838.804(b)(2). For example, the court order must contain language such as "survivor annuity," "death benefits," or "former spouse survivor annuity." 5 C.F.R. § 838.912(a).

See Williams v. OPM, DC-0831-14-1065-I-1 (NP 9/15/2015) (CSRS: "The former spouse of a retired federal employee is entitled to a survivor annuity if and to the extent the retiree expressly provided for one in an election under 5 U.S.C. § 8339(j)(3), or in the terms of a divorce decree or any court order or court-approved property settlement issued in connection with the divorce decree. 5 U.S.C. § 8341(h)(1).").

OPM will honor court orders apportioning lifetime annuities upon divorce, separation, or annulment, but modifications must come by court order or court-ordered property settlement submitted initially to OPM, not for the first time by request to the Board. The statute and implementing regulations governing former spouse survivor annuities are more complex than those governing apportionment of lifetime annuities. The statutory provision applicable to court orders affecting the apportionment of retirement annuities does not prohibit modification of the court order after the employee's retirement or death. See 5 USC 8345(j). But by statute, OPM may not honor a modification to a court order providing a former spouse survivor annuity if the modification is made after the retirement or death of the federal employee. 5 USC 8341(h)(4); 5 CFR 838.806(a). The law interpreting and applying court orders affecting annuity interests is fascinating in its complexity, baffling to the parties, and a reminder to lawyers handling divorce cases that they would do well to review OPM regulations and guidance on the subject of court decrees, often involving divorce property settlements, apportioning retirement benefits.
