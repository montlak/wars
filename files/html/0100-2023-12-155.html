1. Request for Extension of Time

The time limit for filing a PFR is not absolute. Under the statute, 5 USC 7701(e):

(1) Except as provided in section 7702 of this title, any decision under subsection (b) of this section shall be final unless--

(A) a party to the appeal or the Director petitions the Board for review within 30 days after the receipt of the decision; or

(B) the Board reopens and reconsiders a case on its own motion.

The Board, for good cause shown, may extend the 30-day period referred to in subparagraph (A) of this paragraph....

The Board's regulations provide for extensions of time to file a PFR, 5 CFR 1201.113(d) (2022):

Extensions. The Board may extend the time limit for filing a petition for good cause shown as specified in § 1201.114 of this part.

5 CFR 1201.114 (2022) requires an affidavit or sworn statement supporting a request for an extension of time to file a PFR:

(f) Extension of time to file. The Board will grant a motion for extension of time to file a pleading described in paragraph (a) only if the party submitting the motion shows good cause. Motions for extensions must be filed with the Clerk of the Board on or before the date on which the petition or other pleading is due. The Board, in its discretion, may grant or deny those motions without providing the other parties the opportunity to comment on them. A motion for an extension must be accompanied by an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746. (See Appendix IV.) The affidavit or sworn statement must include a specific and detailed description of the circumstances alleged to constitute good cause, and it should be accompanied by any available documentation or other evidence supporting the matters asserted.

See Cole v. Dept. of Treasury, 4 MSPR 228, 229, 4 MSPB 304 (1980) ("The time limit for filing a petition for review is not absolute, however, because the Board's regulations provide at 5 C.F.R. 1201.113 for extensions of time 'for good cause shown.'").

The Board is generous in granting extensions, not generous in forgiving late filings. Parties and representatives should request extensions when timely filing might be an issue. There is no penalty or dishonor for obtaining an extension and not later needing it.

It was implied in Mashack v. USPS, 96 MSPR 174, 177 ¶ 11 (2004), that a request for an extension may be filed after the due date for the PFR has passed, suggesting that if it becomes apparent that the PFR, already late, will be further delayed, an extension request is filed rather than filing a showing of good cause for the first time with the PFR. See Smith v. OPM, DC-0845-10-0323-I-1 (NP 3/17/2011) ("Although the finality date of June 3, 2010, had already passed, the Clerk of the Board found sufficient cause for a limited extension of time in which to file, and on July 7, 2010, issued an order extending the time for filing."). The proper practice is to request the extension before the PFR filing deadline. Hotchkiss v. DOJ, 72 MSPR 291, 294 (1996), implied that if counsel knows that there will be a considerable delay in filing a PFR, counsel should request an extension of time before the deadline for the PFR and avoid a request for waiver of a late filing. The Board suggested the possibility of an extension to file a PFR when a court case is pending that would affect Board decisional law, although in Taylor v. OPM, 23 MSPR 284, 285 (1984), the extension was denied because, in the Board's estimation, the case in court would not affect the issue that the appellant sought to have reviewed.

The operative component of the regulation is the phrase "good cause." A request devoid of a showing of good cause will likely be denied. See Lanes v. OPM, 43 MSPR 364, 365 (1990) (declining to grant an unexplained motion to waive a late filing).

In 1983 the Board's Assistant Managing Director for Management issued a Case Flow Management Study. One concern expressed in the study was that extensions were being requested in approximately one-third of all PFRs submitted to the Board, with the resultant use of significant amounts of staff time to process the requests and delay in the issuance of final decisions on PFRs. The study recommended that because the liberal granting of extensions was a major reason for overall case delays, the Board should consider strict enforcement of time restraints governing the filing of PFRs.

In 1994 the Federal Circuit repudiated the Board's theory that any otherwise unexcused minimal delay must result in dismissal of an appeal with Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1994). [Walls is discussed at length in Chapter 2, "Current Law on Waiver; Multifactor Analysis."] The Board remains adamant that late PFRs will be excused only with a justification, even if the delay is minimal. No matter what tension may exist between the views of the Board and its reviewing court concerning timeliness, and no matter how many generations of cases may be necessary for the Board to adopt a more flexible approach toward filing delays, given the choice between requesting an extension of time before the PFR deadline closes and attempting to justify a missed deadline after having not secured an extension, the former procedure is preferred. The failure to request an extension of time for filing the petition "weighs against a finding of due diligence or ordinary prudence." Holloway v. OPM, 54 MSPR 507, 510 (1992).

A late request for an extension must be justified under the standards of Alonzo v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 180, 184 (1980), applicable to late appeals. See Dooley v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 684, 689 (1993). There is no list of reasons that will or will not result in an extension of time from the Clerk of the Board for the filing of a PFR. The extension request does itself not serve to extend the filing time. See Adams v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-610) (if a request for an extension is made to the Board's Clerk, the time for filing the PFR is not automatically extended pending the decision on the extension request).; cf. Olivares v. MSPB, 17 F.3d 386 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (delineating the effect on the period for filing a petition for judicial review of the belated filing of an administrative PFR). If the extension request is not granted, good cause must be shown to justify filing the PFR after the 35th day. If there is no good cause shown, the Board will dismiss a late PFR as untimely. Bradley v. USPS, 19 MSPR 451, 452 (1984) (appellant's decision to file a PFR, made a few days before the deadline, was not good cause for extending the deadline). This approach places a significant risk upon the appellant (or counsel) who files an extension request expecting routine approval. If the extension request is denied (and there is no guarantee that the Board will decide the request prior to the last day for filing the PFR), the appellant is without a Board review right, a serious penalty for an expectation that the extension will be routinely granted. If a request for an extension of time is filed and there is no response from the Board, it is not excusable neglect to wait eight months to inquire if the request has been granted. Burkett v. Dept. of Air Force, 26 MSPR 539, 541 (1985). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Waiver of Time Limit for Appeal; Alonzo."]

Any extension request should be in proper form. A voice mail message to the Board will not suffice: the extension request must be accompanied by a sworn statement of the circumstances justifying the request, accompanied by pertinent documentation. McNevin v. Dept. of Air Force, 77 MSPR 108, 112 (1997). In practice, the Board's Clerk seems reasonable about granting a short extension to file a PFR if the extension is filed before the PFR deadline. As a practical matter, it is efficient to fax the extension request to the Clerk, 202-653-7130, in letter or pleading format, with the factual statements sworn to as true under the penalty of perjury pursuant to 28 USC 1746. Then call the Clerk (202-653-7200) and speak with someone with reference to the case docket number to confirm receipt of the extension request and to quickly determine if the request for the extension is granted. E-filers request extensions by e-filing. Those who are not e-filers use mail, commercial delivery, personal delivery, or fax. Emails to the Board's Clerk are not an authorized means of filing, and the time of filing of an extension request is not clocked from the date of the email. See Winter v. DOD, SF-0752-15-0467-I-1 (NP 2/18/2016).

An extension request that is directed to the wrong Board office will be considered filed as of that date. See Andrus v. IRS, 14 MSPR 500, 502 n.2, 13 MSPB 150 (1983). The PFR may be filed with a request for an extension of time to file supporting materials. see Dooley, supra ("The appellant was also free to file a timely petition for review and to concurrently seek permission to supplement the record..."). If the appellant provides evidence that an extension request was submitted to the Board, and the Board lacks a record of receipt of the request, the Board will not default the employee who then files a PFR based upon his belief that the extension was granted. See Aviles v. USPS, SF-0353-09-0968-I-2 (NP 7/2/2012). There is a difference between the extension requested to file supporting materials and an extension requested to seek supporting materials. The mere allegation that some future report or decision may provide relevant and material evidence does not provide a basis for the Board to either reverse the initial decision or to delay its consideration of appellant's PFR. Payne v. USDA, 26 MSPR 554, 555 n.* (1985).

Once the extension is granted, appellant and counsel are obliged to meet the deadline for filing their PFR or, if they have filed a summary PFR and requested additional time for filing a brief, for filing the later submission. If the deadline is missed, the Board will not consider the late submission without good cause assessed by the same standards--discussed below--governing the filing of untimely PFRs. See Goldberg v. DOD, 39 MSPR 515, 518 (1989); McCuistion v. USPS, 2 MSPR 27, 31, 2 MSPB 201 (1980) (appellant received an extension to file materials supporting a PFR but no materials were filed; PFR was dismissed for lack of prosecution). Should the appellant contend that he did not meet a deadline set by the Board's Clerk because the Clerk sent the notice out too close to the deadline for receipt of the return submission, the burden is on the party alleging the Clerk's error to come up with some evidence showing improper mailing, or late receipt of the mailing, from the Clerk. The party's affidavit should at least state when she received the Clerk's notice. Harrell v. VA, 43 MSPR 281, 283-84 (1990).

It is unclear what occurs if the appellant allows the initial decision to become final and files a court appeal, while the agency concurrently files a request for an extension of time to file its own PFR and, when it is granted, files the PFR with the Board after the employee filed his case in court. This apparently occurred, but the Board offered no substantive guidance on the problem, in Dockterman v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 522, 524 (1987), ruling on technical grounds that it retained jurisdiction of the agency PFR. Cf. Flescher v. USPS, 34 MSPR 643, 643-44 (1987) (once the appellant petitions the Federal Circuit for review, the Board loses jurisdiction to decide the appellant's requests to the Board for reopening and reconsideration).
