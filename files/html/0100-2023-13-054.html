VII. NEGOTIABLE PROCEDURES

Even as to reserved management rights, the FSLMRA authorizes collective bargaining over the "procedures which management officials of the agency will observe in exercising [their] authority." DOD, AAFES v. FLRA, 659 F.2d 1140, 1146 (D.C. Cir. 1981) (Dix-McGuire). 5 USC 7106 establishes that:

(b) Nothing in this section shall preclude any agency and any labor organization from negotiating--

...

(2) procedures which management officials of the agency will observe in exercising any authority under this section.

The difficulty is determining how a proposal should be characterized. The issue is whether it is a substantive infringement on management rights or a procedure affecting the exercise of management rights. Put another way, a proposal that directly interferes with a substantive right reserved by § 7106(a) to management discretion is by definition not "procedural" under § 7106(b)(2). AFGE Local 32 v. FLRA, 728 F.2d 1526, 1528 (D.C. Cir. 1984); see AFGE Local 1164 and SSA Dist. Office, New Bedford, 54 FLRA 1327, 1340 (1998) ("[P]roposals or provisions that 'directly interfere' with the exercise of management's rights under section 7106(a) do not constitute negotiable procedures."). The definition of what is procedural becomes, in part, a matter of classifying and eliminating proposals that substantively affect, or unduly interfere with, management rights.

The substance/procedure distinctions drawn by FLRA have received judicial attention. Under Navy Pub. Works Ctr., Pearl Harbor v. FLRA, 678 F.2d 97, 99 (9th Cir. 1982), involving the negotiability of a proposal that employees have the right to remain silent during investigations that might lead to discipline:

Application of section 7106 turns upon the elusive distinction between substantive and procedural proposals. This distinction is always troublesome because each type extended unduly diminishes the scope of the other. In addition, the distinction frequently will not be clear. On one hand, unions could use procedural language in framing proposals, the impact of which would be primarily substantive.... On the other hand, federal employers could assert as a management prerogative the authority to act in a manner totally inconsistent with proposals designed to provide bona fide procedural protections. In each case, the federal agencies and courts must attempt to fix the limits of "the range of proposals to be deemed 'procedural' within the contemplation of the statute."... If a proposal does not fit comfortably within the procedural pigeon-hole of (b)(2), the question becomes whether it intrudes upon the managerial authority protected by (a)(2).

A limitation on the negotiability of procedures involves measures concerning internal security of an agency, NTEU and DHS, C&BP, 60 FLRA 367, 371 (2004):

Proposals that require the adoption of security measures to ensure a specific level of security do not constitute negotiable procedures under § 7106(b)(2) of the Statute. See, e.g., NTEU, 59 FLRA at 754 (citing NFFE, Local 1482, 44 FLRA 637, 648 (1992)); Local 987, 37 FLRA at 202. By requiring the Agency to make changes and adopt measures at all duty locations where the Agency deems security currently insufficient to adequately secure firearms stored during off-duty hours, the proposal in this case does not constitute a procedure within the meaning of § 7106(b)(2) of the Statute. See id.

Several standards distinguish procedural from substantive proposals.
