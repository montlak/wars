b. Deceit; Misrepresentation

Retirement is involuntary if the employee is given incorrect information by his employing agency concerning retirement rights and decides to take retirement rather than face removal. Beaty v. USDA, 24 MSPR 658, 662 (1984). "[A] decision to retire made with inadequate information upon which an employee reasonably relied to her detriment based on an objective evaluation of the surrounding circumstances and within a limited time frame can support a finding that the retirement was involuntary." Isabelle v. OPM, 69 MSPR 176, 182 (1996); see Webb v. OPM, 47 MSPR 275, 278 (1991) ("The applicable standard for determining whether an employee's election of retirement options is voidable due to improper information is whether a reasonable person would have been confused in the particular circumstances. See Frantz v. OPM, 778 F.2d 783, 786 (Fed. Cir. 1985)."); Moriarty v. OPM, 47 MSPR 280, 287 (1991) ("Misleading statements upon which an employee reasonably relied to his detriment are sufficient to render an action involuntary regardless of whether there was an intent to deceive by the agency. See Scharf v. Dept. of Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1575 (Fed. Cir. 1983).... [T]he agency need not be aware that the statements were misleading--they can be negligently or even innocently provided to the employee. See Covington v. DHHS, 750 F.2d 937, 942 (Fed. Cir. 1984).").

Petric v. OPM, 108 MSPR 342, 345-46 ¶¶ 9-10, 2008 MSPB 59 (2008), noting that the appellant should support the jurisdictional allegations by a sworn statement, provided a summary of the law concerning constructive adverse action appeals based on retirements that result from materially misleading information:

A retirement is involuntary if an agency made misleading statements upon which the employee reasonably relied to his detriment. The appellant need not show that the agency intentionally misled him. That is, the agency could have provided the misleading information negligently or even innocently; if the appellant materially relied on the misinformation to his detriment, his retirement is considered involuntary. See, e.g., Wallendorf v. Department of the Treasury, 102 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶ 6 (2006). An appellant is entitled to a hearing on the issue of Board jurisdiction over an appeal of an alleged involuntary separation if he makes a non-frivolous allegation casting doubt on the presumption of voluntariness. E.g., Williams v. Department of Agriculture, 106 M.S.P.R. 677, ¶ 10 (2007).

The appellant has failed to make a non-frivolous allegation that the agency misinformed him and that he reasonably relied on the misinformation to his detriment. In the show-cause orders, the AJ informed the appellant of the above cited standard for proving that his retirement was involuntary based on agency misinformation. The AJ notified the appellant that he would be entitled to a hearing if he made non-frivolous allegations sufficient to show that a material fact relating to jurisdiction was in dispute. The AJ further notified the appellant that statements to support a jurisdiction claim must be in the form of an affidavit, sworn statement, or declaration, and directed him to a form for the statement in the Board's regulations. Despite those instructions, the appellant failed to submit a sworn statement that the agency told him that he would be granted workers' compensation benefits in lieu of disability retirement if he voluntarily applied for and accepted disability retirement. Thus, he has not shown that the Board should remand this case for further proceedings on the issue. See, e.g., Hanna, 101 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶¶ 11-12; cf. Wallendorf, 102 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶¶ 7-11 (Board remanded for further findings on jurisdiction and timeliness where the appellant submitted a declaration asserting that the agency misinformed her that she must resign to retain her health insurance).

Petric added that the appellant's allegations must demonstrate that his "reliance on any agency misinformation was reasonable." Id., ¶ 11. Cf. Vinson v. USPS, CH-0752-15-0593-I-1 (NP 10/24/2016) (appellant's reliance on retirement information from the agency was not reasonable, when the information provided was clearly inapplicable to the appellant); Komatar v. Dept. of Army, CH-3443-10-0748-I-1 (NP 2/23/2011) ("The crux of the appellant's argument is that he relied on the agency's representation that he could obtain a VSIP to his detriment in planning his early retirement. It is undisputed, however, that the agency subsequently informed the appellant that his VSIP had not been approved, and the appellant had the options of remaining in his position or applying for early retirement knowing that he would not receive a VSIP, and he chose the latter. See Covington, 750 F.2d at 942.").

Salazar v. Dept. of Army, 115 MSPR 296, 300-02 ¶¶ 9-12, 2010 MSPB 235 (2010), emphasized that materially incorrect information provided by the agency concerning retirement benefits, relied on by the appellant, may be innocently provided--the result, however, is that the retirement is involuntary and constructively adverse:

The administrative judge correctly found that an employee's retirement is presumed to be a voluntary action and, as such, is not within the Board's jurisdiction. Aldridge v. Department of Agriculture, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 7 (2009). An involuntary retirement or resignation, however, is tantamount to a removal, and, accordingly, is appealable to the Board. Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322, 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc); Aldridge, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 7. The presumption that a retirement is voluntary can be rebutted by evidence showing that the retirement was the result of agency misrepresentation, coercion, or duress. Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574-75 (Fed. Cir. 1983). Where, as here, there is a claim that an involuntary action resulted from misinformation, an appellant must show: (1) that the agency made misleading statements; and (2) that the appellant reasonably relied on the misinformation to his detriment. Id.; Aldridge, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 8. The appellant need not show that the agency was intentionally misleading. Covington v. Department of Health & Human Services, 750 F.2d 937, 942 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Aldridge, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 8. An agency is required to provide accurate information to permit an employee to make an informed, and thus voluntary, decision regarding resignation or retirement. Aldridge, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 8. A decision based on misinformation is not binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process. Covington, 750 F.2d at 943.

Here, the appellant reasserts on review that the agency provided him with incorrect information regarding his annuity that caused him to involuntarily retire. Specifically, the appellant contends that the Army had a responsibility to verify the accuracy of the post-1956 deposit for his military service made in 1997, because it was supposed to conduct a comprehensive review of his retirement eligibility and provide him an accurate estimate on which he was to base his retirement decision.

In this case, the administrative judge credited "as straightforward" the appellant's testimony that he would have delayed his retirement had the Army, the agency which processed his retirement, properly detected and corrected the error made by the INS in collecting the post-1956 deposit under FERS rules. ID at 6.[2] Further, the record includes an affidavit, sworn under oath, from Carla M. Gilbert, Human Resources Specialist, in which she states that she spoke with the appellant on January 9, 2009, and provided him with a "final counseling." Ms. Gilbert's affidavit states that the final counseling included an estimate for the appellant's monthly annuity and that, while she and the appellant did not specifically discuss his post-1956 military deposit, the counselor based her estimates on the proof of payment in full of a military deposit the appellant included in his retirement packet. This proof of payment information, however, was wrong. The appellant had not paid his post-1956 military deposit in full at the CSRS rate. As a result, because the Army did not verify the accuracy of the appellant's military deposit, the Army's estimate of the appellant's monthly annuity was, therefore, also incorrect. Thus, the Army provided the appellant with misinformation, albeit unintentionally. The Board has repeatedly held that whether the agency provided the misinformation intentionally, unintentionally, negligently, inadvertently, or even innocently is immaterial to whether it was, in fact, misinformation, and that a decision based on misinformation is not binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process. See Aldridge, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 8; Baldwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 109 M.S.P.R. 392, ¶ 26 (2008); Petric v. Office of Personnel Management, 108 M.S.P.R. 342, ¶ 9 (2008).

[2] An accurate deposit for the appellant's post-1956 military service gives him retirement credit for an additional 7 years of service. The failure to pay the deposit thus has serious consequences.

Because the appellant has shown that the Army provided him with misinformation and that he reasonably relied on it in deciding to retire, he has proven that his retirement was involuntary. See Baldwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 586, ¶ 46 (2009). Accordingly, because the appellant has proven that his retirement was involuntary and thus tantamount to a removal, there is Board jurisdiction and he prevails on the merits. See Shoaf v. Department of Agriculture, 260 F.3d 1336, 1341 (Fed. Cir. 2001); Schultz v. United States Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Aldridge, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 20. The appellant is therefore entitled to reinstatement.

A retirement was set aside as involuntary when the agency provided the employee an estimate of retirement benefits based on an immediate retirement date, the employee then separated from the agency thinking he was retired, and it turned out that he was too young to secure an immediate rather than a deferred retirement, Notyce v. DHS, AT-0752-12-0846-I-1 (NP 5/29/2014):

In contesting the administrative judge's finding that the appellant's retirement was involuntary, and therefore a constructive discharge, the agency makes three arguments. First, it contends that the appellant's decision to retire could not have been due to agency misinformation because the appellant made his decision and retired on February 29, 2012, before any alleged misinformation was provided. This argument ignores the fact that, on February 17, 2012, the agency provided a "Federal Retirement Benefits Estimate" that indicated that the appellant was eligible for immediate retirement benefits, listing the Annuity Start Date as May 1, 2012. The agency had an affirmative duty to provide the appellant information that was not only correct in nature but adequate in scope to allow the appellant to make an informed decision to retire. See Cortinas v. Office of Personnel Management, 32 M.S.P.R. 513, 515 (1987). The agency clearly violated that duty in this case and, as subsequent events showed persuasively, there is no reason to believe that the appellant would have retired on February 29, 2012, if he had known that he would not be eligible to start receiving a retirement annuity until January 2013.

The agency secondly argues that, even if the appellant were able to point to some misinformation he received from the agency, he would have to show that he would have made a different decision if he had been correctly informed of the consequences of his choices, and it points out that, after the appellant received his retirement package in March 2012, he did not question the written estimate that showed January 1, 2013 as his annuity start date. As discussed above, the administrative judge made credibility determinations, which we affirm, that the appellant did not become aware that he was eligible only for a deferred retirement starting in January 2013 until a later point in time, and that he acted promptly and diligently once he realized that he was not eligible for immediate retirement benefits and that the agency did not intend to compensate him for the period during which he was separated.

The agency thirdly argues that, even if the appellant were able to show some misinformation by the agency and that he would have made a different decision had he been given correct information, his reliance was not reasonable. It notes that the appellant could have waited for an OPM determination before retiring, that he was fully aware that the agency did not have the authority to make a final determination of his eligibility for immediate retirement, and states that, as "a well-educated and experienced Federal employee," he "knew, or should have known, that he was not eligible to retire before age 62." The agency is here trying to hold the appellant to a higher standard of knowledge than it holds its own retirement experts, who should have known and immediately advised the appellant that he was not eligible for immediate retirement, but failed to do so.

The bottom line is that inaccurate information concerning benefits may result in an involuntary retirement. See Nazaroff v. DOD, DC-0752-11-0959-I-4 (NP 11/20/2013) ("[T]he agency provided the appellant with misinformation, that the appellant erroneously believed that he was eligible for a law enforcement retirement without waiving his military retired pay, and that the DFAS supervisor did not correct this erroneous belief. The appellant detrimentally relied on the agency's misinformation by filing a retirement application that OPM ultimately denied. Accordingly, we find that the appellant has shown that his retirement was involuntary and therefore tantamount to a forced removal within the Board's jurisdiction, and he prevails on the merits. See Salazar, 115 M.S.P.R. 296, ¶¶ 11-12 (finding a retirement involuntary where the agency failed to correct the appellant's mistaken assumption regarding payment of a deposit for military service)."); Hosford v. OPM, 107 MSPR 418, 422 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 295 (2007) ("Although neither the Board nor the Federal Circuit has ever extended the holding in Scharf to an allegedly involuntary disability retirement, we find that it is appropriate to do so under the circumstances presented in this case. The appellant argues that her employing agency informed her that her 7 years of service under CSRS were not creditable for purposes of determining retirement eligibility. She argues that, based on that misinformation from her employing agency, she believed that she did not have sufficient service credit to be eligible for immediate optional retirement. The appellant has therefore alleged that her decision to apply for disability retirement, rather than immediate optional retirement, was the result of her reasonable reliance on misleading advice from her employing agency. Accordingly, we find that she has raised a claim that her retirement was involuntary. See Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1575."); Beverly v. USPS, 88 MSPR 247, 250-51 ¶¶ 9-10 (2001) (resignation was involuntary when based on misleading information from the agency concerning the appellant's OWCP status and a requirement that he return to work); Williams v. Dept. of Army, 49 MSPR 405, 413-14 (1991), following remand from 44 MSPR 449 (incomplete information concerning various retirement options invalidated both the retirement and the position change taken in anticipation of retirement); Baier v. OPM, 43 MSPR 210, 213 (1990) (improper information concerning the level of retirement benefits; the employing agency, rather than OPM, was the proper respondent); Siedschlag v. OPM, 38 MSPR 517, 520 (1988) (claim of involuntariness was linked to improper information concerning retirement eligibility); Cochran v. OPM, 38 MSPR 504, 508 (1988) (an appeal from a partial denial of an overpayment waiver: the appellant was not informed that without a waiver of his military pension he was not eligible for retirement; the Board ordered joinder of the employing agency to litigate the voluntariness of retirement); Webb v. OPM, 37 MSPR 541, 542-43 (1988) (complaint of involuntary retirement raised in an appeal from an OPM overpayment determination should have been litigated as an involuntary retirement challenge; the appellant asserted that he elected to retire based on information provided by his employing agency, and that he would not have retired had the agency provided him with correct information concerning the amount of his monthly annuity); cf. Cortinas v. OPM, 32 MSPR 513, 516 (1987) (involving a disability retirement where the benefits information from the employing agency was incorrect; OPM was directed to provide proper information to the annuitant and permit him to make a change in his annuity election). But see Bente v. USPS, 104 Fed. Appx. 728, 730 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (employee's unhappiness about the processes used by the agency to collect a debt from his salary did not render his retirement involuntary when the agency did not make an incorrect representation to the employee concerning the effect of the debt collection process upon the amount of pay he would receive when he retired; the employee did not show that the agency "knew of his alleged misunderstanding of the debt collection procedures upon retirement"; nor did he show that the agency's actions would have misled a reasonable person in making a retirement decision); Benavidez v. Dept. of Navy, 241 F.3d 1370 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (retirement was not involuntary; the agency did not mislead an employee concerning benefits available through retirement as opposed to the receipt of worker's compensation benefits).

Misleading advice from personnel specialists of employing agencies or OPM may be the basis for invalidating as involuntary a choice between retirement options. See Fox v. OPM, 54 MSPR 525, 528 (1992) (misleading information from an employer concerning the ability to receive an annuity refund if eligible for an annuity within 31 days from the date of application); Tiffany v. Dept. of Treasury, 48 MSPR 334, 337 (1991) (involuntariness claim based upon the allegation that the appellant retired under CSRS rather than FERS after she received incorrect information from an agency retirement counselor concerning FERS eligibility); Wilber v. OPM, 47 MSPR 444, 447 (1991) ("As the agency charged with the responsibility of administering civil service retirement law, OPM has an affirmative duty to fully and accurately inform claimants of their rights and obligations."); Bannister v. GSA, 42 MSPR 362, 367 (1989) (incorrect information on retirement eligibility and service credit from OPM); Barthel v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 245, 254 (1988) (alleged incorrect information was received during agency retirement counseling concerning the disclosure of all options pertaining to retirement and a possible Board appeal of a proposed Chapter 43 action).

An employee who was told by agency or OPM personnel that he may retire and still contest a proposed adverse action through a Board appeal stated a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness and had the right to a hearing on the issue. McGowan v. Int'l Communications Agency, 7 MSPR 569, 571, 7 MSPB 4 (1981); see Niederhofer v. USDA, AT-0752-11-0389-I-1 (NP 2/9/2012) ("However, the Board has previously held that, whether the employing agency or OPM is the cause of the lack of information does not affect the involuntariness of a retirement appeal. See Isabelle v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 176, 182 (1996); Bannister v. General Services Administration, 42 M.S.P.R. 362, 366-67 (1989)."). The appellant's claim of misrepresentation by an agency is belied, however, if the appellant knew from other, reliable sources the correct state of affairs. See Miller v. DHS, 111 MSPR 325, 336 ¶ 19, 2009 MSPB 85 (2009) (remanding to determine if the appellant was entitled to a notice of RIF rights concerning disestablishment of an overseas position and reassignment back to the mainland, with a possible effect upon his decision to retire, rather than accepting the downgrade and challenging it upon appeal) (aff'd, Miller v. MSPB, 361 Fed. Appx. 134 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)); Lawson v. USPS, 68 MSPR 345 (1995) (although the agency was charged with failing to give the appellant proper notice of his RIF rights at the time of his retirement, the appellant was then represented by counsel and was in the process of litigating whether the agency should have used RIF procedures when it demoted him); cf. Jenson v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 94-3520) (remanding: it was unclear whether the appellant was given information, at or before the time he retired, concerning his right to appeal a threatened RIF).

The appellant had a right to challenge a retirement based on an EEO settlement procured through what the appellant characterized as misleading information concerning the right to appeal a conduct-based removal, Perry v. Dept. of Commerce, DC-0752-12-0486-I-1 (NP 6/12/2013):

An appellant may establish that the Board has jurisdiction over an action taken pursuant to the terms of a settlement agreement wherein he agreed to waive his appeal rights if he can establish that the agreement was invalid due to misrepresentation by the agency.[7] Simmons v. Small Business Administration, 115 M.S.P.R. 647, ¶ 11 (2011). A showing that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency's statements is sufficient to show misrepresentation. Hazelton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 357, ¶ 11 (2009).

[7] We discern no basis in this matter to distinguish between settlement agreements entered into before the Board and agreements entered into in other forums. Swink v. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 9 (2009), aff'd, 372 F. App'x 90 (Fed. Cir. 2010) ("[T]he Board will review the terms of a settlement agreement and the surrounding circumstances to determine if it retains jurisdiction over an appeal of an action that was settled in another procedural avenue.").

The appellant alleged that he was coerced into signing the settlement agreement under "threat of termination at the Agency's sole discretion." He further alleged that the agency provided him with "misinformation," "misrepresent[ed] " information, and did not inform him "that [he] had appeal rights."[8] Id. The appellant's allegations can be fairly interpreted to mean that the agency advised him that his only alternative to termination was to accept the terms of the settlement agreement and indicated that he would not otherwise have appeal rights. Indeed, on review, the appellant reiterates that the agency provided him with "misinformation with regard to [his] due process rights and MSPB appeal rights" and "stat[ed] that there were no appeal rights." We therefore find that the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness sufficient to warrant a jurisdictional hearing. If the appellant is able to prove his allegation that the agency provided him with misinformation upon which he detrimentally relied in signing the settlement agreement (namely, that he would not otherwise have appeal rights), this would demonstrate that he did not enter into the agreement voluntarily. See Gutierrez v. U.S. Postal Service, 90 M.S.P.R. 604, ¶ 9 (2002) (finding that the appellant's allegation that the agency incorrectly "told him that, if he did not resign and the agency followed through with its proposal to remove him, he would not have appeal rights with regard to the removal action" constituted a "nonfrivolous allegation that his resignation was involuntary").

[8] To the extent the appellant alleges that the agency was required to notify him of his appeal rights before he signed the settlement agreement, this is incorrect. An agency is not obligated to notify an employee of his Board appeal rights until it "issues a decision notice...on a matter that is appealable to the Board." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.21; Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 583, 587 (1995) (stating that an agency is obligated to notify an employee of his Board appeal rights when it takes an appealable action).

Even if a settlement is not filed with the Board for enforcement, breach of the agreement may support a claim of involuntary retirement resulting from promises in the settlement that the agency did not intend to honor, Pearson v. Dept. of Army, DC-0752-15-0507-I-1 (NP 10/13/2015):

The Board has found that a retirement, predicated on an agreement breached by the agency or a promise the agency did not intend to fulfill, is involuntary and the Board has jurisdiction over it. An example of a retirement appeal based on misleading or incomplete information is Douglas v. DOD, 108 MSPR 244, 248 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 48 (2008). The agency informed employees that their worksite was scheduled for closure. Employees were informed of priority placement programs, the possibility of relocating to other locations, and employees who were eligible for retirement were told of that option and offered a voluntary separation incentive payment. The appellant accepted the retirement option and the incentive payment and, following retirement, appealed a constructive adverse action, asserting the agency did not inform her, shortly after her agreement for the separation payment and before her retirement date, that the agency announced that it would retain fifteen positions at her location that would be filled through RIF procedures. Aldridge v. USDA, 110 MSPR 21, 26 ¶¶ 11-12, 2008 MSPB 209 (2008), remanded for a hearing on whether the appellant's retirement was involuntary based on an agency's threat that if she did not retire immediately she would lose all retirement benefits upon termination. The appellant would, even if terminated, have the right to both appeal the termination and claim a deferred annuity at age 62. Following remand, on another PFR, the Board concluded that the appellant was misled and that her retirement was involuntary, Aldridge v. USDA, 111 MSPR 670, 678-80 ¶¶ 16-20, 2009 MSPB 146 (2009):

For the reasons discussed above, we find that the appellant has proven by preponderant evidence her assertion that Jackson and Henry represented to her in the October 14, 2004 meeting that she would lose her retirement benefits if she were terminated. In reaching this conclusion, we rely on the appellant's clear and specific account of what she was told, the lack of any contradiction by Jackson and only a vague and ambiguous response by Henry, and the testimony from Solomon corroborating the appellant's account, as well as the lack of the contradiction cited by the AJ to find the appellant not credible. See Hillen, 35 M.S.P.R. at 460 (contradiction by or consistency with other evidence as factors to be considered in determining credibility). The appellant has therefore shown that the agency made a misleading statement to her and has met the first prong of the evidentiary test to show that her retirement was involuntary. See Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1574-75; Baldwin, 109 M.S.P.R. 392, ¶ 20.

We also find that the appellant reasonably relied on this misinformation to her detriment and that she has thus met the second prong of her burden of proof. Id. A determination as to whether the appellant's resignation was voluntary must be based on an objective evaluation of all the surrounding circumstances. Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1574; Beverly, 88 M.S.P.R. 247, ¶ 9. Thus, we have considered the fact that the appellant at least cursorily considered retirement in September 2004 and that she was told by Maddrey and Solomon, as well as by Jackson and Henry, that termination would result in the loss of these benefits. However, we are persuaded that the statement by Jackson and Henry in the October 14, 2004 meeting was what resulted in the appellant's decision to retire. While the appellant received the same information from Maddrey and Solomon, Jackson and Henry were more authoritative figures whose statements would carry more weight. As stated in the Board's previous decision, "it was reasonable for the appellant to rely on these sources," i.e., to believe that information Jackson and Henry provided was correct. Aldridge, 110 M.S.P.R. 21, ¶ 12. This was particularly true in the context of being told by Henry that she faced the option of retiring or being terminated. See Covington, 750 F.2d at 942 (employee receiving RIF notice reasonably relied on erroneous statement that he had no right of reassignment and could retire); Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1575 (employee who could not work due to health problems reasonably relied on misinformation from retirement counselor indicating that there would be no adverse consequences to taking optional retirement prior to disability retirement).

In addition, we are persuaded that the statement regarding loss of retirement benefits if she were terminated was the decisive factor in the appellant's decision to retire because she made an appointment the very next day to sign her retirement papers. Although she had previously been told by her union representative that the agency's pending removal action would put an end to her retirement benefits, the appellant did not retire until after she was told this by Jackson and Henry. As in cases where an individual alleges coercion, the showing of a short period of time between an employer's provision of misleading information and the employee's decision to resign or retire is probative evidence that the employer's action was the triggering event. Cf. Shoaf, 260 F.3d at 1342; Axsom, 110 M.S.P.R. 605, ¶ 15.

Moreover, there is no evidence to show that the appellant knew at the time of her retirement that the information she received during the October 14, 2004 meeting was incorrect or that she was in fact eligible for a deferred annuity at age 62. The appellant testified, without rebuttal, that she did not know about the option of a deferred annuity until she read about it in the Board's prior decision. She also stated that she did not ask Nero, when they met on October 18, 2004, to effect her retirement, whether what Jackson and Henry had told her was true. Nero did not testify that she told the appellant about the deferred annuity option. The AJ's statement that Nero testified that "she explained to the appellant she would always be eligible to receive a deferred annuity at age 62, even if the agency had removed her," is not supported by a review of the hearing tape. Finally, in finding that the appellant relied on the agency's misinformation to her detriment, we note the appellant's unrebutted testimony that she would not have retired but for the statement made in the October 14, 2004 meeting.

Because the appellant has shown that the agency made a misleading statement and that she reasonably relied on it in deciding to retire, she has proven that her retirement was involuntary. See Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1574-75; Baldwin, 109 M.S.P.R. 392, ¶ 20. As noted above, in an involuntary separation case, the issue of Board jurisdiction and the merits of the case are inextricably intertwined. Accordingly, because the appellant has proven that her retirement was involuntary and thus tantamount to a removal, there is Board jurisdiction and she prevails on the merits. See Shoaf, 260 F.3d at 1341; Schultz, 810 F.2d at 1136; Porter, 98 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 22. She is therefore entitled to reinstatement.

To the same effect, the appellant stated a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency incorrectly secured his retirement by telling him he would lose all his retirement benefits if he was discharged, Morrison v. Dept. of Navy, 122 MSPR 205, 208-09 ¶¶ 7-9, 2015 MSPB 15 (2015):

A retirement is involuntary if it is obtained by agency misinformation or deception. Covington v. Department of Health & Human Services, 750 F.2d 937, 942 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The misleading information can be negligently or even innocently provided; if the employee materially relies on such misinformation to his detriment, based on an objective evaluation of the circumstances, his retirement is considered involuntary. Id. A decision based on misinformation or lack of information cannot be binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process. Id. at 943. The Board has stated that the principles set forth in Covington require an agency to provide information that is not only correct in nature but also adequate in scope to allow an employee to make an informed decision. Baldwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 586, ¶ 16 (2009). This includes an obligation to correct any erroneous information an agency has reason to know an employee is relying on. Id.

The appellant alleges that an agency manager told him that he would lose his retirement benefits if he were removed. This appears to be misinformation. Retirement benefits earned over the course of one's federal career are generally available upon separation from federal service, even when that separation is agency initiated. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8312-15 (codifying limited circumstances under which an annuity is subject to forfeiture). This is an allegation that the appellant lacked a meaningful choice due to the agency's act of providing incorrect advice, and if true, would constitute an appealable constructive removal. See Bean, 120 M.S.P.R. 397, ¶¶ 8-9.

The record includes evidence that the agency had reason to know the appellant was relying on this misinformation. The same manager who allegedly told the appellant that he would lose his retirement benefits if he were removed stated that in the months leading up to the removal decision the appellant was "very concerned" about how a removal would affect his retirement benefits. Failing to correct erroneous information that it has reason to know an employee is relying on is a wrongful action by an agency that may deprive an employee of a meaningful choice. Baldwin, 111 M.S.P.R. 586, ¶ 16.

Retirement was involuntary when a supervisor failed to correct the incorrect impression of the appellant that if he did not retire before a removal decision was issued he would lose his retirement benefits, Morrison v. Dept. of Navy, PH-0752-14-0669-B-1 (NP 6/28/2016):

The agency argues that, because it was not the source of the misinformation the appellant relied on in deciding to retire, his retirement was voluntary. The agency also argues that the administrative judge erred by expecting its manager to act as a "full-blown benefits counselor" to the appellant, anticipating and answering questions the appellant had not asked. The agency's arguments are unavailing. The agency had an obligation to provide the appellant with information that was adequate in scope to make an informed decision whether to retire, including an obligation to correct misinformation it had reason to know the appellant was relying on, whether or not the agency was the source of that misinformation. See Williams v. Department of the Army, 44 M.S.P.R. 449, 454-55 (1990). The administrative judge's findings on the dispositive facts are largely undisputed and are fully supported by the record as explained in the remand initial decision. The appellant repeatedly expressed concern to his supervisor that he would lose his retirement benefits after receiving the notice of proposed removal, the supervisor told the appellant that he would give him the opportunity to retire prior to issuing a decision, and the appellant retired when his supervisor subsequently gave him that opportunity. The supervisor testified that he did not know whether the appellant's belief about losing his retirement benefits was accurate, but he made no attempt to provide the appellant with adequate information so that the appellant could make an informed decision.

The agency cites to Covington v. Department of Health & Human Service, 750 F.2d 937 (Fed. Cir. 1984), and Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572 (Fed. Cir. 1983), for support of the proposition that the agency must be the source of the relied upon misinformation to render a retirement involuntary. However, the Board has found that, although Covington and Scharf involved affirmative misrepresentation by the agencies, those decisions go further and require that an agency provide information that is correct in nature and adequate in scope to allow an employee to make an informed choice. Wills v. Department of the Navy, 37 M.S.P.R. 137, 141 (1988). The agency is obligated to correct any erroneous information on which it has reason to know an employee is relying. Id.

Moreover, even if the agency was not the source of the misinformation, the agency reinforced the appellant's belief that he had to choose to retire before the decision to remove him was issued to avoid adverse consequences to his retirement benefits. After the appellant raised his concerns that a removal would adversely affect his retirement benefits, rather than disabuse him of this misunderstanding, the appellant's supervisor reassured him that he would have an opportunity to retire prior to being removed. At no point did the agency provide the appellant with the critical information he needed to make an informed choice, specifically, that even after the agency issued its decision the appellant could choose to retire and retain his right to appeal the removal decision to the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(j); Mays v. Department of Transportation, 27 F.3d 1577, 1579-81 (Fed. Cir. 1994); Norton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 248, ¶ 2 (2009). Instead, the agency allowed the appellant to believe that he had to choose between removal and retirement, and then argued that his retirement was voluntary and his appeal should be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction.

The agency argues that the administrative judge misstated and mischaracterized the appellant's supervisor's testimony. We do not agree. The administrative judge found that the appellant's supervisor admitted that it was "policy" for employees to first go to their supervisors prior to approaching human resources with questions. The agency argues that the supervisor testified that employees contacted him prior to contacting human resources as a "courtesy only."

We find, however, whether it was a policy, past practice, or mere courtesy is irrelevant. The testimony from both the appellant and his supervisor establishes that the appellant brought human resources issues to his supervisor prior to contacting human resources directly. The agency notes that this same supervisor testified that he "continuously" forwarded information regarding benefits and retirement to the employees under his supervision via email. This testimony supports the administrative judge's conclusion that the appellant's supervisor operated as an intermediary between human resources and the appellant.

The appellant's supervisor testified that he knew he was providing advice that the appellant would rely on in making his decision to retire. He was asked what he did after the appellant asked if he would be given a chance to retire prior to being removed, and he testified, "I made a phone call because I wanted to provide [the appellant] accurate information." He contacted his supervisors, including the deciding official, and confirmed that the appellant would be given an opportunity to retire prior to issuing the decision on the proposed removal, and he communicated this to the appellant.

While there is no requirement that this supervisor act as a "full-blown benefits counselor," an agency is required to provide employees with adequate information in order to make an informed retirement decision. See Freeborn v. Department of Justice, 119 M.S.P.R. 290, ¶ 10 (2013) (stating that a decision made based on lack of information cannot be binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process). After careful review of the record, we find no basis to disturb the administrative judge's finding that the appellant's retirement was involuntary. See Crosby v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 98, 105-06 (1997) (finding no reason to disturb the administrative judge's findings when she considered the evidence as a whole, drew appropriate inferences, and made reasoned conclusions).

An early example of a retirement appeal based on misleading information is the oft-cited Scharf v. Dept. of Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574 (Fed. Cir. 1983), rev'g Scharf v. Dept. of Air Force, 12 MSPR 418 (1982). The employee, who had the choice between taking optional and disability retirement, was counseled that no adverse effect would result if optional retirement preceded the disability retirement. The advice was incorrect. Under an optional retirement, accumulated sick leave is added to the length of the retiree's civil service to calculate his annuity. Under disability retirement, the retiree is allowed to stay on the payroll to exhaust his sick leave at full pay before receiving retirement benefits. This advantage to disability retirees is not available if an optional retirement precedes a disability retirement. Although the employee relied on the advice that he would be able to use up his sick leave if he filed for and received optional retirement before he was approved for disability retirement, there was no intention to deceive. The Board determined the retirement was voluntary, a ruling that was reversed by the Federal Circuit. The court's discussion provided its analysis of claims of involuntariness of personnel actions:

With freedom of choice as the guiding principle, it has been held that the element of voluntariness is vitiated when (1) an employee resigns under duress brought on by government action, see, e.g., McGucken v. United States, 407 F.2d 1349, 1351 (Ct. Cl.), cert. den. 396 U.S. 894 (1969); (2) an employee unsuccessfully tries to withdraw his resignation before its effective date, see Cunningham v. United States, 423 F.2d 1379, 1384-85 (Ct. Cl. 1970); (3) an employee submits a resignation under time pressure, see Perlman v. United States, 490 F.2d [928], 932-33 (Ct. Cl. 1974); or (4) an employee fails to understand the situation due to mental incompetence, see Manzi v. United States, 198 Ct. Cl. 489, 492 (1972). Several other cases have also indicated that a resignation will be held involuntary if obtained by agency misrepresentation or deception. See Taylor v. United States, 591 F.2d 688, 692 (Ct. Cl. 1979); Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d 584, 588 (Ct. Cl. 1975).

Concluding that a reasonable person would have relied on the agency's improper advice concerning retirement options and that the employee did rely on that advice in good faith, Scharf determined there was no need to demonstrate that agency personnel intended to deceive the employee. The test was whether a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency's statements. The court observed that the test involving misrepresentation or deception is objective; inquiring into the subjective perceptions of the employee or the subjective intentions of the agency was inappropriate. However, the court noted at footnote 5 of its opinion that if an employee is able to show an intent to deceive on the part of the agency, then an objective test may not apply; in this situation, it may be sufficient that the employee was actually misled, even though a reasonable person would not have been. Determining that the retirement was involuntary and treating it as an adverse action without procedural protections, the court ordered the agency to cancel the retirement and place the employee back on the rolls on sick leave status, with deductions for prior retirement payments received and conversion to disability retirement, already approved by OPM, once the sick leave was exhausted. The MSPB decision holding the retirement voluntary was reversed as contrary to law.

Scharf was the subject of commentary in another involuntary retirement case, Funesti v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 90-3369):

In Scharf v. Dept. of Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572 (Fed. Cir. 1983), this court held that it is not necessary to show that alleged agency misrepresentations were intentional in order to establish involuntary retirement.... By the same token, it is also not necessary to show that any misrepresentations were made negligently. Instead, what Funesti is required to show is that the agency did, through Ms. Smith, make statements which were, under an objective test, materially misleading.

The Federal Circuit offered additional guidance on involuntary retirement claims concerning an employee who retired on the effective date of an agencywide RIF under the belief that he had no retention rights, a belief later refuted by the outcome of judicial and administrative litigation concerning the retention and transfer rights of the employees whose positions were eliminated in the RIF. Covington v. DHHS, 750 F.2d 937, 940-41 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Writing for the court, Judge Newman said of the Board's jurisdiction over Covington's appeal and his right to a hearing:

Absent evidence to the contrary, retirement is presumed to be a voluntary act. Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d 584, 587, 207 Ct. Cl. 333, 338 (1975); Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Supp. 752-1, subchapter S1-2(a)(1). Voluntary action initiated by the employee precludes a subsequent appeal to the MSPB. 5 CFR § 752.401(c)(3) (1984).

To determine whether a retirement is voluntary, a court must examine "the surrounding circumstances to test the ability of the employee to exercise free choice." Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (citing Perlman v. United States, 490 F.2d 928, 933, 203 Ct. Cl. 397, 407-08 (1974)). The fact that an employee is faced with an inherently unpleasant situation or that his choice is limited to two unpleasant alternatives does not make an employee's decision any less voluntary. Taylor v. United States, 591 F.2d 688, 692, 219 Ct. Cl. 86, 92 (1979); FPM Supp. 752-1, subchapter S12a(3). But the decision must ultimately be the employee's decision, not the government's; whether the employee made an informed choice is the touchstone of our analysis.

The presiding official found that Covington made no allegation that his retirement had been involuntarily extracted through coercion or duress. However, a retirement need not be "coerced" to be involuntary. A resignation or retirement is involuntary if it is obtained by agency misinformation or deception. Scharf, 710 F.2d at 1574; FPM Supp. 752-1, subchapter S1-2(a)(1). Scharf held that an objective test applies to such situations and that a court in applying this test will not inquire into the "subjective perceptions of the employee" or "the subjective intentions of the agency." 710 F.2d at 1575. Unlike a retirement which is induced through duress, there is no requirement that an employee be intentionally deceived about his employment options, it being sufficient that "the employee shows that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency's statements." Id. The misleading information can be negligently or even innocently provided; if the employee materially relies on the misinformation to his detriment, his retirement is considered involuntary.

...

[T]he law also requires that a choice between two alternatives, however unpleasant, must be understood by the employee and that such a decision be freely made. In this case, the agency was responsible for Covington's lack of an informed choice. A decision made "with blinders on," based on misinformation or a lack of information, cannot be binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process. Cf. Shubinsky v. United States, 488 F.2d 1003, 1006, 203 Ct. Cl. 199, 204 (1973) (Government's misleading notice of preference-eligible rights and withholding of information concerning plaintiff's right to appeal adverse action entities plaintiff to adjudication on the merits).

Since Covington was not informed that a transfer of function had occurred (and actually was informed to the contrary), he had no grounds to believe an appeal of his RIF action might be productive. The agency's additional failure to inform him that a retirement election would preclude a later appeal denied him the right to consider this fact in making his decision. Covington was given a Hobson's choice, that is to say no real choice at all.

Covington also asserts that he was improperly denied a hearing on the issue of the voluntariness of his retirement. The decision of the Fifth Circuit in Gonzalez v. Department of Transportation, 701 F.2d 36 (5th Cir. 1983), is instructive on this point. In that case petitioner received a termination notice which indicated that she could appeal and also either resign or accept involuntary separation and still receive retirement pay. The Fifth Circuit held that the voluntariness of her resignation could not be presumed by virtue of her application for retirement pay, and that petitioner was entitled to a hearing on the issue of the voluntariness of her resignation:

Neither the initial reduction-in-force notice from the Agency, however, nor the subsequent letter from the Board requesting jurisdictional argument, informed her of her applicable rights. The Board's regulations furnished her do not show that a voluntary resignation waives the right to appeal the termination action of an agency-employer. Nor does the record indicate that Gonzalez was adequately informed, by the Agency or by the Board, of her applicable rights relative to voluntarily resigning and applying for discontinued employment retirement or, in the alternative, accepting involuntary separation and also applying for discontinued employment retirement. In the first instance, she would lose her right to appeal, and in the second, she would not. However, the notice to Gonzalez as a whole indicated that she could both appeal and also either resign or accept involuntary separation and still receive retirement pay.

Id. at 39.

As in Gonzalez, the presiding official in the present appeal issued a decision on the merits of the jurisdictional issue, based solely upon the pleadings and written submissions of the parties. This violated procedures required by law that guarantee an employee a hearing on the issue of involuntary retirement. See Gratehouse v. United States, 512 F.2d 1104, 1108, 206 Ct. Cl. 288, 296 (1975); McCormack v. United States, 204 Ct. Cl. 371, 373, 378 (1974). Precedent within the MSPB is also in accord. See, e.g., Murray v. Defense Mapping Agency, 1 MSPB 338 (January 17, 1980)....

Wylie v. USDA, 99 MSPR 71, 74-76 ¶¶ 7-12 (2005), presents another example of a discontinued service retirement challenged as constructively adverse because of the agency's failure to provide proper information concerning what amounted to a RIF:

The November 20, 2001 letter that the appellant received from the agency falls woefully short of the regulatory requirements for a RIF notice. It was issued fewer than 60 days before the appellant's expected separation date, 5 C.F.R. § 351.801(a)(1), and it did not contain, as a RIF notice must, a statement of the appellant's competitive area, competitive level, retention subgroup, service computation date, "the place where the employee may inspect the regulations and record," or his right to appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board once the RIF is effected. See 5 C.F.R. § 351.802(a). The letter seems designed to deceive, or at the very least contained inadequate information. The letter created the clear impression that the appellant would be separated involuntarily on January 3, 2002 because his position was slated for elimination, but the letter did not use the words "RIF" or "reduction in force" and did not identify any other legitimate basis for separating the appellant if he did not choose to retire.

The agency denies that the November 20, 2001 letter was a RIF notice leading to the question of just what it was. According to guidance issued by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), when an agency decides to abolish the position of an employee who meets the age and service requirements for discontinued service retirement, it should advise the employee in writing that: You "may be separated from the service"; "official retention registers will be used to determine your assignment rights in accordance with reduction in force procedures"; "you will receive specific advance notice at least sixty days prior to the effective date of the personnel action to be taken"; you are eligible for discontinued service retirement but "are under no obligation or pressure" to apply for it; and "this is not a reduction in force notice." CSRS & FERS Handbook, § 44C1.1-1 (ch. 44 at 39); see DeLaet v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 390, 394 (1996) (OPM's CSRS & FERS Handbook is an authoritative interpretation of employee rights and agency responsibilities under the federal retirement laws). The November 20, 2001 letter did not meet these requirements.

In sum, the agency exacted the appellant's decision to accept a discontinued service retirement by threatening to separate him on a date certain. The administrative judge viewed the appellant's decision to retire as a voluntary choice between the unpleasant alternatives of retiring or "litigat[ing] the agency's inevitable termination action by RIF," but in fact the agency did not provide the appellant with either a RIF notice or the information that OPM says should be provided to a retirement-eligible employee when the agency is attempting to avoid a RIF. On this record, it appears that the appellant's decision to accept discontinued service retirement was involuntary because it was made "with blinders on," that is, without adequate information that the agency should have given him. See Covington v. Department of Health & Human Services, 750 F.2d 937, 943 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Johnson v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 620, 626 (1995).

The administrative judge relied on Jones v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 M.S.P.R. 306, 311 (1994), to hold that the agency's failure to advise the appellant about his RIF rights was excusable, but Jones was a very different case. In Jones, the appellant retired during the Postal Service's 1992-93 restructuring. Although it was later determined by the Board that the Postal Service should have followed OPM's RIF rules, Brown v. U.S. Postal Service, 58 M.S.P.R. 345, 347-48 (1993), at the time of the restructuring the Postal Service could have reasonably believed, based on its quasi-independent status and the fact that a large portion of its workforce was not covered by civil service rules, that it did not need to follow OPM RIF procedures. Accordingly, the Postal Service was not required to inform retirement-eligible employees about their RIF rights during the 1992-93 restructuring. Jones, 65 M.S.P.R. at 311-12.

Here, by contrast, the agency (Forest Service) does not have any special status outside the executive branch, nor is there any indication that the appellant's competitive-service Archaeologist position was not covered by the civil service rules. Simply put, the agency could not have reasonably believed that if the appellant did not retire, it could nevertheless separate him involuntarily without running a RIF, offering him a directed reassignment and having him decline it, or taking some other legally-authorized action. Indeed, if the administrative judge was right in observing that the appellant's RIF separation was "inevitable" if he did not retire, then plainly Jones does not control because in Jones agency officials reasonably believed that they could restructure without running a RIF.

On this record, then, it appears that the appellant retired after the agency provided him with information that the agency knew or should have known was inadequate. This amounts to a non-frivolous allegation that the appellant's retirement was involuntary, which under the facts alleged also amounts to a non-frivolous allegation that the agency should have informed him of his right to file an appeal with the Board claiming that he was constructively removed. In this way, jurisdiction and timeliness are intertwined. See Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 583, 586-87 (1995) (jurisdiction and timeliness were intertwined, precluding a disposition on timeliness alone, where the agency failed to advise an employee of his appeal rights upon retirement but under the circumstances should have so advised him; an individual who should have been advised of his appeal rights but was not has no duty to exercise due diligence in attempting to discover those rights, but instead must act reasonably promptly only after discovering his potential right of appeal from an alleged constructive removal).

The Board applies Covington and Scharf to require an agency to avoid actively misleading an employee, and to require the agency to advise the employee of an error it knew or should have known the employee was making in his reliance upon retirement-related information. But the employee is expected to read retirement forms, front and back, to determine benefit eligibility. The agency's responsibility to correct an employee error that was not the result of improper advice from the agency does not extend to a searching inquiry concerning the employee's actions leading to retirement; the agency is required only to attempt to correct erroneous impressions or actions of the employee that come to the attention of agency personnel specialists. See Bland v. VA, 31 MSPR 592, 592-93 n.1 (1986) ("Appellant may have wished to appeal to the Board and may have been unaware that if he retired, he could not appeal, but the record shows that he did not request information on his appeal rights. The agency could not have known what was in appellant's mind[.]"); Kolstad v. USDA, 30 MSPR 143, 146-47 (1986) (employee resigned, incorrectly believing that he could obtain a refund of retirement contributions; the agency was not at fault; it was unaware of the appellant's erroneous assumption concerning his entitlement to a refund).

An involuntary retirement appeal advanced to adjudication on the strength of the appellant's allegation that his retirement was induced by agency misstatements concerning his eligibility for a discontinued service annuity, Lawson v. USPS, 68 MSPR 345, 349-53 (1995):

An employee-initiated action such as a resignation or retirement is presumed to be voluntary.... An appellant may rebut this presumption, however, by presenting sufficient evidence to show that his retirement was based on agency-supplied misinformation.... A retirement is deemed involuntary not only when owing to an affirmative misrepresentation, but also when it results from the agency's failure to correct erroneous information that it has reason to know an employee is relying on. Drummonds v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 58 MSPR 579, 583 (1993).

...

[T]he appellant provides an undisputed statement sworn under penalty of perjury that the agency misrepresented his eligibility for a discontinued service annuity. Because the appellant was informed of this by OPM after the close of the record below, we find that it is new and material evidence that was not previously available. Avansino v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 MSPB 308, 3 MSPR 211, 214 (1980). Thus, under the Board's regulations, 5 CFR § 1201.115, this evidence is admissible and will be considered.

Although the appellant did not argue below that his retirement was involuntary as a result of not receiving the discontinued service annuity, he did express his belief generally that he was not adequately informed regarding his rights and options during the agency's reorganization. The appellant's statements also indicate that he made his decision to retire based on the information he received from the agency, in a context where he had a limited amount of time to make that choice (if he wanted to exercise the special retirement option offered by the agency).

The appellant's allegation of misrepresentation concerning a discontinued service annuity has two premises: (1) That based on the agency's statements, he reasonably believed that he was eligible for a discontinued service annuity and applied for it; and, (2) that in fact he was not eligible for such a retirement. Although the record is void of any documentation demonstrating that the agency advised the appellant that he was eligible for a discontinued service annuity, the appellant argued below, as he argues now, that the agency encouraged his retirement and offered him a discontinued service annuity with a lump-sum benefit if he retired by October 15, 1993. The agency has not challenged or denied the appellant's assertions, either below or on petition for review.

Misleading statements upon which an employee reasonably relied to his detriment are sufficient to render an action involuntary. Ogden, 61 MSPR at 40. Further, the agency need not be aware that the statements were misleading, but may instead have provided them to the employee negligently or innocently. Id. at 40-41; see also Gammill v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 MSPR 484, 488 (1985) (finding that the appellant's reliance on the agency's unintentional misrepresentation as to his eligibility for a discontinued service retirement annuity rendered his retirement involuntary).

In this instance, if the agency offered the appellant a discontinued service annuity with lump-sum benefits in exchange for his retirement by October 15, 1993, the appellant's reliance upon such an offer would have been reasonable. However, because it appears that the appellant was not eligible for a discontinued service annuity, since he met the requirements for an immediate optional retirement, see 5 USC § 8336(a), (d), such an offer could reasonably have misled him. While the agency may not have intended to mislead the appellant regarding his retirement options, the appellant is not required to show that the agency had such an intent in order to establish that his retirement was involuntary. Covington, 750 F.2d at 942. Because the appellant has indicated throughout the appeal process that his decision to retire was influenced by the incentive of a discontinued service annuity with its lump-sum benefit, we find that he has raised a nonfrivolous allegation that he had inadequate and incorrect information upon which to make an informed decision. Hence, we find that the appellant has raised a nonfrivolous allegation of an involuntary retirement based on misinformation by the agency concerning his eligibility for a discontinued service annuity.

An employee's disappointment in not securing a discontinued service annuity did not make his FERS early retirement involuntary. The agency did not mislead the appellant or improperly take action resulting in the retirement, Miller v. DHS, 111 MSPR 325, 330-31 ¶¶ 8-10, 2009 MSPB 85 (2009) (aff'd, Miller v. MSPB, 361 Fed. Appx. 134 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)):

An employee-initiated action, such as a retirement, is presumed to be voluntary unless the appellant presents sufficient evidence to establish that the action was obtained through duress or coercion or shows that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency. Talley v. Department of the Army, 50 M.S.P.R. 261, 263 (1991). A retirement action is involuntary if, for example, the agency made misleading statements upon which the employee reasonably relied to his detriment. Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574-75 (Fed. Cir. 1983). A decision made "with blinders on," based on misinformation or a lack of information, cannot be binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process. Middleton v. Department of Defense, 185 F.3d 1374, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 1999). In this regard, there is no requirement that the misinforming be knowingly deceptive. Id. Moreover, an agency is required to provide information that is not only correct in nature, but adequate in scope to allow an employee to make an informed decision. Exum v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 62 M.S.P.R. 344, 348 (1994). In order to be entitled to an evidentiary hearing on a jurisdictional issue such as involuntariness, an appellant must make nonfrivolous allegations that, if proven, could establish Board jurisdiction. Middleton, 185 F.3d at 1379-80.

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8414(b), an employee who, among other things, is "separated from the service involuntarily," except by removal for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency, after becoming 50 years of age and completing 20 years of service, is entitled to an annuity. OPM's regulations similarly provide that an employee who "separates from the service involuntarily" after becoming age 50 and completing 20 years of service is entitled to an annuity. 5 C.F.R. § 842.206(a). In the context of the Civil Service Retirement System version of the above statute, which is set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d) and which is substantially similar to section 8414(b), the Board's reviewing court has held that the type of involuntary separation to which the statute speaks is one that results from a lawful agency action, rather than a coerced, unlawful separation. Nebblett v. Office of Personnel Management, 237 F.3d 1353, 1359 (Fed. Cir. 2001).

Here, as the AJ and the agency found, the appellant was not lawfully involuntarily separated from his position when he decided to retire under the "MRA + 10" provision instead of accepting the offered assignment to Arlington. In fact, the agency had not even proposed the appellant's separation at the time the appellant retired; instead, it merely informed him that he would be assigned to the position of Transportation Security Specialist at TSA headquarters at the end of his overseas tour of duty, and that he would be expected to report to work on August 6, 2008. Thus, contrary to the appellant's claims, he did not nonfrivolously allege that he met all of the criteria for a discontinued service retirement, and that the agency therefore provided him with misinformation in this regard upon which he reasonably relied in deciding to retire under the "MRA + 10" provision. The fact that the appellant's choices may have been limited to unpleasant alternatives does not make his decision to retire involuntary. See Lawson v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 M.S.P.R. 345, 350 (1995).

In Silvestri v. Dept. of Army, 60 MSPR 594, 599-603 (1993), the Board faced the situation of an appellant seeking to rescind his OPM-approved retirement as involuntary because of allegedly misleading or incorrect advice concerning his eligibility for retirement. The Board determined that it could adjudicate the case without joining OPM. The Board decided it would review the representations made by the agency to both the appellant and, apparently, OPM:

Unlike the appellant's allegation of coercion, his allegation that the agency misrepresented his eligibility to take discontinued service retirement could, if true, provide a basis for a finding of involuntariness. An incorrect representation in this regard would have materially altered the appellant's perceived options in the face of the abolishment of his Physical Scientist position. Instead of having three options--retirement, taking the Space Training position, or separation from the agency--he would have been faced with only two alternatives, separation or taking the Space Training position. The putative detrimental reliance, then, is that the agency may have misled the appellant into selecting discontinued service retirement instead of accepting the Space Training position, when he in fact was not eligible for such retirement.

The appellant's allegation of misrepresentation has two premises: That he was in fact not eligible for discontinued service retirement; and that he reasonably believed, based on the agency's statements, that he was eligible for such retirement. Regarding the latter issue, we note that the agency's letter of July 25, 1989 did not specifically advise the appellant that he was eligible for discontinued service retirement. It advised him that he met the age and service requirements and that he was "eligible to apply" for such retirement. We nonetheless conclude that a reasonable person in the appellant's position would have understood that the agency was telling him that he was eligible for such retirement. Lurita Williams, Civilian Personnel Officer, was asked by agency counsel whether she provided the July 25 letter "notifying [the appellant] that he was eligible for discontinued service retirement," and she replied affirmatively, stating that such a letter is "routinely given to those who have that entitlement." William McNamee, Chief of the Recruitment and Placement Branch of the Civilian Personnel Office, similarly testified that, before the agency resorts to a reduction in force, it "give[s] a formal notification that the employee is entitled to a discontinued service retirement based on the abolishment of their job." In addition, both the agency and the appellant referred to the agency as making the appellant an "offer" of discontinued service retirement. We think that a reasonable person would conclude that the agency would make such an offer only if it believed the employee was entitled to accept it.

Turning to the question of the appellant's entitlement to discontinued service retirement, an employee meets the age and length of service requirements if he is separated from the service involuntarily, except by removal for cause on charges of misconduct, after completing 25 years of service or after becoming 50 years of age and completing 20 years of service. 5 USC § 8336(d). An employee is not eligible for such retirement, however, if he has declined a reasonable offer of another position. Id. OPM's implementing regulations specifically provide that an offer of another position is not reasonable unless it is made in writing. 5 CFR § 831.504(b)(1).

In finding "[n]o evidence in the appeal record support[ing] the appellant's claim that he was offered continuing employment," the administrative judge cited only the agency's July 25, 1989, letter advising the appellant that his position was being abolished, noting that this letter did not even refer to, much less offer him, the position of Space Training Specialist. The administrative judge should also have considered two other pieces of evidence bearing on this issue. Covington v. Department of Health and Human Services, 750 F.2d 937 (Fed. Cir. 1984), is cited in the concurring opinion for the proposition that "the crucial inquiry is whether the appellant made an informed choice," and for the proposition that a "decision made 'with blinders on,' based on misinformation or a lack of information, cannot be binding as a matter of fundamental fairness and due process." The decision does support those statements. One of the bases for the court's finding that the employee's retirement (in the face of a separation by reduction in force) was involuntary was that the employee had not been informed that a transfer of function had occurred, and that he therefore had no grounds for believing an appeal of a separation by reduction in force might be "productive." See Covington, 750 F.2d at 943. In relying on this failure, however, the court pointed out that the employee "actually was informed to the contrary," i.e., the agency told him that there had been no transfer of function. Id. Furthermore, although the court also stated that the "agency's additional failure to inform [the employee] that a retirement election would preclude a later appeal denied him the right to consider this fact in making his decision," id., this does not appear to be a major basis for the court's conclusion that the appellant was "given a Hobson's choice, that is to say no real choice at all." Id. That failure is mentioned briefly in a single sentence near the end of the analysis, while the agency's incorrect denial that there had been a transfer of function that could have given the employee a right to another job is mentioned repeatedly in the decision. See id. at 939-43. The agency's failure to provide information about the effect of the employee's retirement apparently became significant only in light of the misinformation the agency provided.

Williams v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 449 (1990), is cited in the concurring opinion for the proposition that "the Board has found that an agency is required to provide information that is not only correct in nature but adequate in scope to allow an employee to make an informed decision." The concurring opinion also indicates that, in Williams, "the Board specifically noted that this includes an obligation to correct any erroneous information that the agency has reason to know that the appellant is relying on." Again, the cited decision supports the propositions for which it is cited. See Williams, 44 MSPR at 451. It does not indicate, however, that an agency has an obligation to correct erroneous information absent "reason to know that the appellant is relying on" that misinformation, and there appears to be no indication in the present appeal that the agency knew the appellant misunderstood his appeal rights. Most likely, the agency assumed the appellant's decision to retire was, in effect, a decision to give up his appeal right in return for a relatively clean record, an end to his job-related problems, and an annuity. This would seem to be a reasonable assumption.

An employee-initiated action such as a resignation or retirement is presumed to be voluntary, unless the appellant establishes that the action was obtained through duress or coercion, or shows that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency. See Drummonds v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 58 MSPR 579, 583 (1993). A retirement is deemed involuntary not only when owing to an affirmative misrepresentation, but also when it results from the agency's failure to correct erroneous information that it has a reason to know an employee is relying on. Id. Cf. Nicoletti v. Department of Justice, 60 MSPR 244, 247-248 (1993) (whether the appellant put the agency on notice that he viewed his demotion as involuntary generally implicates the issue of the timeliness of his subsequent appeal, not the Board's jurisdiction over the demotion).

In the present case, the appellant alleges that the agency was aware that he considered his retirement to be involuntary, and that therefore it was obligated to inform him that his retirement would prevent him from appealing the RIF action. The appellant cites to the fact that he wrote "Job abolishment-RIF" on his SF-52 form, and that his SF-50 uses the same language to describe the reason for his "resignation." However, the Board has found that such notations, of themselves, do not render a retirement involuntary. See Bravman v. Department of the Navy, 26 MSPR 169, 172-73 (1985).

The appellant also cites to testimony from agency witnesses indicating that they knew the appellant was leaving his position involuntarily. This testimony is consistent, however, with the theory, asserted by these same witnesses, that the appellant, in retiring rather than being separated under the RIF, was simply choosing between unpleasant alternatives. In any case, such evidence falls far short of establishing either that the agency knew the appellant intended to appeal his RIF action, or that he misunderstood the effect his retirement would have on his appeal rights. For example, in Drummonds, relied upon as support by the appellant, the Board found that an employee's resignation was involuntary where he notified the agency in writing that he intended to appeal his demotion action at the time he tendered his resignation, thereby alerting the agency that he was relying upon erroneous information regarding his appeal rights. Drummonds, 58 MSPR at 584.

In the present case, by contrast, Lisa Johnson (Johnson), the agency's employee relations specialist, testified that the appellant never discussed appealing the RIF action. Although the appellant initially stated that "I believe I told [Johnson] on each occasion when I met her that I intended to appeal the job abolishment," he contradicts this testimony in his petition for review: "[T]he Appellant had no reason to discuss his appeal rights with Ms. Johnson, as the RIF notice was clear as to the fact that the Appellant had appeal rights to the Board." From this evidence, we conclude that the administrative judge acted within her discretion in crediting the agency's testimony over the appellant's as to this point. Accordingly, we find that the agency had no reason to know that the appellant intended to appeal his RIF action subsequent to retiring, and that therefore it was under no obligation to disabuse him of this notion.

Based on the agency's own evidence and argument regarding the appellant's motivation for retiring, we find that the appellant reasonably relied upon misleading statements to his detriment, however. The agency asserts that "[t]he Administrative Judge correctly found that Respondent 'retired rather than face a removal pursuant to a RIF and lose health benefits.'" It is undisputed that the appellant lacked the requisite five consecutive years of service in a health benefits plan prior to his retirement, as prescribed under 5 USC § 8905(b). Johnson testified that she advised the appellant to retire before the effective date of the RIF in order to ensure that he would obtain a waiver of this requirement, allowing him to carry his health coverage under the Federal Employees Health Benefit Program (FEHBP) into retirement. Johnson conceded, however, that it was possible for the appellant to obtain such a waiver following separation by RIF, a fact of which the agency should have been aware. See 5 CFR § 890.108, setting forth the considerations the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) will apply in acting on waiver requests. Indeed, OPM, in exercising its discretion to waive the 5-year requirement, explained that the appellant was prevented from meeting the requirement by circumstances beyond his control, a factor which was based on the abolishment of his position during the RIF.

Misleading statements upon which an employee reasonably relied to his detriment are sufficient to render an action involuntary. See Moriarty v. Office of Personnel Management, 47 MSPR 280, 287 (1991), aff'd, 989 F.2d 1202 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). Further, the agency need not be aware that the statements were misleading, but may instead have provided them to the employee negligently or innocently. Id.

In the present case, the agency expressly affirms that the appellant's decision to retire prior to the effective date of the RIF was strongly influenced by his perception, engendered by the agency's official, that such an election was necessary to preserve his health benefits. Regardless of any other advantages or disadvantages of so electing, this advice was clearly misleading.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant's decision to retire was not voluntary, and that the appellant is entitled to a hearing on his appeal of the RIF action, and on his claim of age discrimination.
