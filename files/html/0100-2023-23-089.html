13. Leave Account; FMLA; Military Leave

"The Board has held that an award of back pay includes the restoration of leave. See Toth v. U.S. Postal Service, 85 M.S.P.R. at 407, ¶ 9; Rivera v. U.S. Postal Service, 107 M.S.P.R. 542, ¶ 9 n.5 (2007)." Galatis v. USPS, 110 MSPR 399, 403 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 6 (2009). The Back Pay Act provides for recovery of leave lost as a result of an unwarranted or unjustified personnel action. Hart v. Dept. of Transp., 109 MSPR 280, 282 n.1, 2008 MSPB 149 (2008) (constructive adverse action involving alleged forced use of leave). "Annual leave is an allowance that the agency must recredit to a prevailing appellant's account under the Back Pay Act." Carter v. USPS, 89 MSPR 45, 51 ¶ 21 (2001). If the employee takes leave because of the effects of personnel actions caused by reprisals for whistleblowing, the employee is entitled to restoration of the leave he used. See Special Counsel v. VA, 81 MSPR 601, 606 ¶ 10 (1999) (sick and annual leave).

A discharged federal employee is not entitled to a lump sum payment based on accrued sick leave. When the employee is reinstated, the agency should recredit sick leave lost at termination. Andreko v. Dept. of Transp., 30 MSPR 65, 67 (1986); see Raphel v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 596, 604 (1992) (enforcing settlement); Gay v. USPS, 49 MSPR 219, 232 (1991) ("It is clear that in remedying a wrongful action, the employee must be made whole to the maximum extent possible, including restoration of all annual leave the employee would have earned during the period of non-duty status."); Clements v. Dept. of Navy, 21 MSPR 275, 277 n.2 (1984) (citing Manzi v. United States, 198 Ct. Cl. 489 (1972) (concerning enforced leave prior to removal)). The Postal Service Employee Labor Relations Manual (ELM) requires that a nonpreference eligible employee receive annual leave she would have earned during the period of a reversed removal. Driscoll v. USPS, 112 MSPR 498, 505 ¶ 20, 2009 MSPB 205 (2009). "[U]nlike the Back Pay Act regulations, the ELM in some instances makes leave determinations dependent on whether any net back pay is received in a particular pay period. See ELM Section 512.311-E-1." Hunt v. USPS, SF-0353-09-0586-X-1 (NP 11/4/2011); see Martin v. USPS, DC-0752-15-0108-C-2 (NP 3/9/2023) (leave administration, including leave accumulation requirements, for nonpreference eligibles is governed by Postal Service ELM, not the Back Pay Act).

Decisions abound involving leave computations and recredits. See Bullock v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 361, 368 ¶ 8 (1998) ("Annual leave is an allowance that the agency must recredit to a prevailing appellant's account under the Back Pay Act. 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(B); 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(g)"); Forrest v. USDA, 74 MSPR 213, 220 (1997) ("Although this section does not specifically discuss an award of leave, 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(B) and OPM's regulations implementing the Back Pay Act do. Subsection 5596(b)(1)(B) provides the procedures for accounting for annual leave restored pursuant to the Act that exceeds the maximum leave accumulation permitted by law. 5 C.F.R. § 550.803 states that the term 'pay, allowances, and differentials' means monetary and employment benefits to which an employee is entitled by statute or regulation by virtue of the performance of a Federal function. The Board has also specifically found that an award under the Back Pay Act includes crediting the appellant with all of the leave he would have accrued but for the unwarranted personnel action."); Reid v. USPS, 67 MSPR 105, 108 (1995) ("Under the Back Pay Act, an employee is entitled to restoration of annual leave which is 'in excess of the maximum leave accumulation permitted by law.' 5 USC § 5596(b)(1)(B); Konieczko v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 MSPR 660, 663 (1993)"); Vathke v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 8, 12 (1991) (the entitlement to payment for accrued annual leave upon separation from the service arises, "not by virtue of any assurances by the agency representative or by agreement in the enforcement action, but by operation of law," 5 USC 5551(a)).

On separation, the employee obtains accrued annual or vacation leave through a lump sum payment. On reinstatement, the lump sum payment will be deduced from back pay and leave is recredited. See Biter v. ICC, 82 MSPR 421, 424-25 ¶ 10 (1999) (agency was required to recredit annual leave and recover its value for employee who, following a RIF and prior to the reversal of the RIF, joined another agency). Golsby v. DHS, 100 MSPR 25, 30 n.2 (2005), explained treatment of accrued leave on termination:

Individuals with accrued but unused annual leave are entitled to be paid for the unused annual leave when they separate from the federal service. 5 U.S.C. § 5551(a). While individuals with accrued but unused sick leave are not entitled to payment for this leave upon separation, they are entitled to have their sick leave balance restored if they are reemployed by the federal government, 5 C.F.R. § 630.502(b), and employees subject to the Civil Service Retirement System receive service credit for their unused sick leave for the purpose of computing the amount of the retirement annuity to which these employees are entitled, 5 U.S.C. § 8339(m).

Jordan v. USPS, 65 Fed. Appx. 308, 313 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP), explained the relation between sick and annual leave benefits on restoration of an employee:

It is well established that a retiring employee may use accrued sick leave in calculating his years of service for annuity purposes, but that he is not entitled to receive the cash value of such leave. 5 C.F.R. § 831.302 (2000); Carman v. U.S., 602 F.2d 946, 948 (Ct. Cl. 1979). In contrast he is entitled to receive the cash value of accrued annual leave. 5 C.F.R. § 550.1203 (2000). The Postal Service's Employee and Labor Relations Manual so provides. It states that "[n]o payment is made for accumulated sick leave when an employee retires or separates," but that such leave may be used "in calculating retirement or survivor annuity at the time of the employee's retirement or death...[e]ach 8 hours of sick leave represents 1 day of retirement credit." U.S. Postal Serv., Employee & Labor Relations Manual, §§ 518.81, 513.821 (1989). Under this standard, which constitutes a regulation of the Postal Service to the extent it states agency policy, 39 C.F.R. § 211.2(a)(2) (2000), Jordan is not entitled to be paid for his accrued sick leave. See also Stewart v. U.S. Postal Serv., 926 F.2d 1146, 1149 (Fed. Cir. 1991).

When the employee is reinstated, the lump sum payment for annual leave must be restored to the agency so that the accrued annual leave may be recredited. The complexities of that process were explained in Clemons v. Smithsonian Inst., 54 MSPR 1 (1992), discussed below. On restoration, the lump sum payment for accrued unused annual leave received by the separated employee is deducted from back pay, and the annual leave is recredited to the employee, Collins v. USPS, 64 MSPR 684, 691 (1994):

...In determining the appropriate deduction for the accrued annual leave payment, the agency should credit the appellant with whatever amount it has already recovered via deductions from his salary. Although the appellant appears to wish to "waive" his right to receive a recredit of his leave, ostensibly to avoid repaying the amount that he received in payment for his accrued leave, the Back Pay Act and its implementing regulations require the agency to recredit his leave and to recover its value. See 5 USC § 5596(b)(1)(B) (an appellant is "deemed to have performed service for the agency" during the period of an unjustified personnel action "for all purposes") (emphasis supplied); 5 CFR § 550.805(e)(2) (an agency should deduct "[a]ny erroneous payments received from the Government" from a back pay award).

See Kinney v. VA, 103 MSPR 602, 608 ¶ 15, 2006 MSPB 309 (2006) (citing Clemons, supra, for the proposition that "the amount of lump sum annual leave payment must be deducted from the net amount of back pay award after the required deduction for taxes and retirement contributions"); cf. Eberhardt v. USPS, AT-0353-11-0436-C-1 (NP 9/25/2015) ("The appellant cannot receive her regular salary and leave payments without charge to her leave balance for the same duty hours because that exceeds what she would have earned if the unjustified personnel action had not occurred. Cf. Markham v. Department of the Navy, 70 M.S.P.R. 244, 248 (1996) (allowing an employee to keep a lump sum payment for leave and have the leave recredited would be a windfall to the appellant).").

The agency is not to deduct from the annual leave credit debt that the employee might otherwise owe to the agency, Markham v. Dept. of Navy, 70 MSPR 244, 247-48 (1996):

The agency claims that it could not credit the appellant with annual leave because of his indebtedness to the agency. In support of this contention the agency cites its own regulation which states:

The order of precedence for deducting erroneous payments from backpay awards when the net amount of backpay is insufficient to cover all the deductions is as follows: retirement annuity payments or refunds of retirement contributions, severance pay, and lump sum payment for accrued annual leave.

This language does not address the question here. It merely states the order in which deductions will be made from the back pay award for erroneous payments made to the appellant when he was separated. It does not state that the appellant may not be credited with the actual leave hours he would have earned during the period of unlawful separation. The agency cites no other regulation or case law to support its position.

The crediting of annual leave is distinguishable from a lump sum payment paid to an appellant for annual leave upon his separation. A lump sum payment for annual leave that was accrued prior to the appellant's separation would have to be reimbursed to the agency before the appellant could be recredited for that leave. Andreko v. Department of Transportation, 30 MSPR 65, 67 (1986). A different result, allowing an employee to keep a lump sum payment for leave and have the leave recredited would be a windfall to the appellant. However, crediting a wrongfully terminated employee with the annual leave he would have earned during the period of his unlawful separation is placing the appellant in the position he would have been in absent the wrongful action and is required by Kerr [v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730 (Fed. Cir. 1984)]. Because the agency has not credited the appellant with his annual leave, the agency is in noncompliance with the Board's order.

According to Eaton v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 12, 14 (1992), when the employee is separated, the employee is paid a lump sum amount for accrued leave and taxes are deducted from the amount. When the separation is reversed, the agency repays to the employee the interim salary and deducts from that amount the gross accrued leave lump sum amount. The employee is left to sort out with the IRS how to recover the taxes paid earlier on the lump sum that was paid to him at the time of the separation. Eaton, like Clemons before it, added that "[w]e direct the agency to assist the appellant to the extent possible by providing any necessary forms or other assistance within the agency's purview that the appellant may need in seeking recourse from the Internal Revenue Service." As to annual leave, under Andreko v. Dept. of Transp., 30 MSPR 65, 67 (1986), the employee is entitled to have his leave account recredited upon his repayment of a lump sum annual leave payment. It is within the discretion of the agency to refuse to grant the annual leave represented by the required refund until the refund is made in full.

The back pay obligation extends to annual leave for the period from the removal to the date of restoration. See Dwyer v. USPS, 32 MSPR 181, 186 (1987). "An appellant is eligible to receive all the annual leave that he would have accrued notwithstanding any agency rule regarding the maximum amount of leave that can be carried over to the next pay year." "The restoration may be done by a lump-sum payment or annual leave credit." Carter v. USPS, 89 MSPR 45, 52 ¶ 21 (2001) ("[t]he agency must show that the appellant was afforded a reasonable opportunity to use his accrued annual leave, and must recredit the appellant with any annual leave he was not afforded a reasonable opportunity to use by the end of the prior year or give him a lump-sum payment for the annual leave."). If the employee secured new employment with another federal agency before the Board overturns the removal from the original employer, the subsequent agency employer may be joined in compliance proceedings to accomplish the recrediting of leave. Okonski v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 620, 622 (1989).

Annual leave carryover must be complete; the employee should not be limited by a maximum carryover restriction of 240 hours. Kellus v. USPS, 35 MSPR 335, 338 & n.4 (1987) (noting a modification in the Back Pay Act in 1975 on the 240-hour leave accumulation restriction and its effect on the interpretation of Postal Service regulations); Broadnax v. Because the agency's obligation under the settlement agreement to return the appellant to status quo ante requires the return of all annual leave in excess of the maximum leave accumulation, we find that the agency is not in compliance with paragraph 2(b) of the settlement agreement."); Cavanagh v. USPS, 65 MSPR 232, 240-41 (1994) (reaffirming the position, despite a Postal Service argument, that its collective bargaining agreement limited carryover of leave); Davis v. USPS, 57 MSPR 81, 84 (1993) ("Granted, the appellant would have been able to carry over only a certain amount of annual leave each year, but, due to a wrongful removal, he was not given the option of accumulating or using annual leave during each year."); Knoieczko v. USPS, 56 MSPR 660, 663-64 (1993); Wilkins v. USPS, 56 MSPR 648, 652 (1993) (same); Lunkin v. USPS, 56 MSPR 642, 645 (1993) (same); Hawkins v. USPS, 42 MSPR 111, 114 n.2 (1989) ("We note that restoring accrued leave beyond the carry over is part of the make whole remedy.").

The treatment of excess annual leave was described in Tubesing v. DHHS, 115 MSPR 327, 338-39 ¶¶ 24-28, 2010 MSPB 242 (2010):

Title Five, United States Code, Section 5596(b)(1)(B)(i) provides that annual leave credited to an employee under the Back Pay Act, "which is in excess of the maximum leave accumulation permitted by law shall be credited to a separate leave account for the employee and shall be available for use by the employee within the time limits prescribed by regulations of the Office of Personnel Management."[5] Here, the appellant was retroactively awarded annual leave for 27 pay periods as a result of the reversal of the agency's termination action, and he earned eight hours of annual leave per pay period. Thus, he earned 216 hours of annual leave during the back pay period (27 x 8).

[5] The relevant regulation promulgated by the Office of Personnel Management provides that a full-time employee shall use excess annual leave of 416 hours or less "by the end of the leave year in progress 2 years after the date on which the annual leave is credited to the separate account." 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(g)(1); see Simons v. Department of Health & Human Services, 89 M.S.P.R. 685, ¶ 6 (2001). The record shows that the agency has properly afforded the appellant until the end of the 2011 leave year on December 31, 2011, to use the restored annual leave.

Contrary to the appellant's contention, he is not entitled to have this entire amount of annual leave credited to a separate leave account. Rather, only the amount of annual leave "which is in excess of the maximum leave accumulation permitted by law shall be credited to a separate leave account." 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(B)(i). For general schedule employees, such as the appellant, the maximum leave accumulation permitted by law is 240 hours. See 5 U.S.C. § 6304(a).

The agency has not provided any information regarding the appellant's annual leave balance at the time of his termination. The appellant, however, provided a detailed chart in which he listed his annual leave balance at the time of his termination as 144 hours. The agency has not contested that representation.

Accordingly, when the appellant was reinstated, his annual leave balance was 360 hours (144 + 216). Pursuant to the statutory provision discussed above, the amount of annual leave in excess of 240, that is 120 hours, should have been credited to a separate leave account. See 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(B).

The earnings and leave statement submitted by the appellant shows that the agency credited the appellant with 128 hours in a separate restored leave account. Thus, the agency has placed eight hours more than required in a separate leave account for the appellant's use. Accordingly, the agency is in compliance regarding the amount of annual leave credited to the appellant and how that leave has been made available for the appellant's use.

[A]ccording to the Office of Personnel Management's regulations, a full-time employee earns leave during each full biweekly pay period while in a pay status or in a combination of a pay status and a nonpay status. 5 CFR § 630.202(a). The tax complications of the process of leave restoration were explored in Clemons v. Smithsonian Inst., 54 MSPR 1, 2-4 (1992):

When the appellant was terminated in August, 1989, he was issued a lump-sum payment for 312 hours of annual leave. The gross amount of the payment was $2,936.40 with a net receipt after taxes of $1,622.16. This amount was, or should have been, reported to the Internal Revenue Service on the appellant's 1989 tax return.

Pursuant to the Back Pay Act, 5 USC § 5596 (1980), when an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action is corrected, the employee's pay and leave are recomputed as if the personnel action had not occurred. 5 CFR § 550.805(a)(2). The lump-sum payment for annual leave is statutorily authorized only when the employee is separated from service. 5 USC § 5551(a). See also Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Supplement, § 990-2, S8-7(d)(4). When the separation is cancelled and the employee is restored to duty, the lump-sum payment for annual leave is considered to be an erroneous payment and must be set off against the employee's back pay award and the annual leave recredited to the employee. FPM Supp. § 990-2, S8-7(d)(4). Consequently, because the appellant was reinstated to his former position, the lump-sum payment for annual leave must be deducted from his back pay award. Vincent T. Oliver, 59 Comp. Gen. 395 (1980).

The parties concur that the lump-sum payment must be offset, but they differ as to whether the amount should be deducted from the gross or net amount of the back pay award. In essence, the issue is whether the Federal and state taxes and retirement contributions due on the back pay should be calculated before or after the lump-sum payment is deducted. The appellant argues that the lump-sum annual leave payment ($2936.40) must be offset from the gross back pay award ($3055.72), which leaves a difference of $119.32 on which appropriate taxes and deductions should be calculated and from which they should then be deducted. The agency argues that it must deduct the lump-sum annual leave payment from the remaining net amount of the back pay award after required deductions for taxes and retirement contributions have been calculated on the gross amount and deducted.

In support of its position, the agency provided portions of the Federal Personnel Manual Supplement concerning deductions from back pay awards of lump-sum annual leave payments issued to employees erroneously separated from service. FPM Supp. § 990-2, S8-7(d)(4).[1] The applicable section of the FPM Supplement contains an example which makes it clear that the amount of taxes and retirement contributions due on the back pay awards is calculated based on the gross back pay amount, without offset of the previously issued lump sum payment. FPM Supp. § 990-2, S8-7(f), example 2. Therefore, we must reject the appellant's argument that he should be taxed only on the difference between the gross amount of back pay due him and the previous gross amount received.

Under Moore v. USPS, 83 MSPR 533, 538-41 ¶¶ 9-15 (1999), the Board may adjudicate FMLA claims during enforcement proceedings. Moore rejected the argument that those issues are reserved to adjudication by the Department of Labor or a federal district court under 29 CFR 825.400(a). The Board could require an agency to reverse a removal imposed against an employee, reinstated by Board order from an underlying adverse action, who was fired because he sought to take leave under FMLA and who was denied that leave because the agency maintained that the employee had not accrued hours of work for FMLA purposes during the period of his dismissal. Moore explained that the employee's period of unwarranted separation from the agency counted as hours of work for purposes of FMLA administration:

Whether the agency[5] was required to deem the appellant to be an employee eligible for leave under the FMLA requires our consideration of both the FMLA and the Back Pay Act. An employee is "eligible" for FMLA leave if he was employed for at least 12 months by the employer from whom leave is requested and performed at least 1,250 hours of service during the 12-month period prior to the leave request. 29 USC § 2611(2). If eligible, the employee has the right in specified circumstances, including where the employee's own serious health condition makes the employee unable to do his job, to take job-protected, unpaid leave, or paid leave if it has been earned, for up to 12 work weeks in any 12-month period. 29 USC § 2612(a); 5 USC § 6382(a)(1). [5] With respect to its bargaining unit employees, such as the appellant, the agency is subject to the FMLA. See Ramey v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 463, 466 (1996), modified on other grounds, Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 76 MSPR 54 (1997).

The Back Pay Act applies to the appellant because he is a preference eligible veteran. See Andress v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 MSPR 501, 508 (1993). The Back Pay Act provides, in pertinent part:

An employee of an agency who...is found by appropriate authority under applicable law, rule, regulation, or collective bargaining agreement, to have been affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action which has resulted in the withdrawal or reduction of all or part of the pay, allowances, or differentials of the employee...(B) for all purposes, is deemed to have performed service for the agency during that period [for which the employee received back pay]....

5 USC § 5596(b)(1)(B) (1996) (emphasis supplied). See also 5 CFR § 550.805(a)(1) (1998) ("When an appropriate authority corrects or directs the correction of an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action...[t]he employee shall be deemed to have performed service for the agency during the period covered by the corrective action.").

In recommending that the Board grant the petition for enforcement, the administrative judge found that the agency had not restored the appellant to the status quo ante because "but for the agency's unwarranted personnel action, the appellant would have worked 1,250 hours prior to the date he made the leave request." The agency maintains that this conclusion is error because finding that the appellant "would have worked at all is pure speculation, as his record upon reinstatement demonstrates." Because the Back Pay Act deems the employee, "for all purposes," to have worked for the entire period for which he or she received back pay, the agency's argument conflicts with the plain language of the statute. 5 USC § 5596(b)(1)(B). Under the statute, the corrective action period is deemed an actual period of service. No speculation was involved; the administrative judge merely applied the clear command of the Back Pay Act. Thus, we agree with the administrative judge that the agency failed to restore the appellant to the status quo ante because the agency did not deem the appellant, for purposes of FMLA eligibility, to have performed service during the period for which the appellant received back pay.

The agency next maintains that the Back Pay Act restores "traditional benefits of employment, i.e., leave, health insurance, life insurance" to employees receiving back pay, but does not affect "statutory rights" such as the entitlements provided under the FMLA....

...

To the extent that the agency is arguing that the Back Pay Act does not apply to benefits that are conferred by statute, we find no such distinction in the language of the Back Pay Act. More particularly, for purposes of applying the Back Pay Act, we perceive no meaningful conceptual difference between an employee's eligibility for leave rights created by the FMLA and his eligibility or qualifications for other types of leave rights created by other statutes and regulations.

Finally, the agency argues that the Board does not have authority to order an employing agency to "waive" the eligibility requirements of the FMLA. In support of this proposition, the agency cites two cases in which Courts struck down a Department of Labor regulation prohibiting employers from challenging an employee's FMLA eligibility when the employer had previously confirmed the employee's eligibility at the time the leave request was made. In each case, the court found that the challenged regulation directly contradicted Congress's expressed intent regarding employee eligibility under the FMLA. Seaman v. Downtown Partnership of Baltimore, Inc., 991 F. Supp. 751, 753 (D. Md. 1998); Wolke v. Dreadnought Marine, Inc., 954 F. Supp. 1133, 1137 (E.D. Va. 1997). In both cases, and a third "analogous" case cited by the agency, Cox v. Autozone, Inc., 990 F. Supp. 1369 (M.D. Ala. 1998), the courts attempted to reconcile inconsistencies between the FMLA and its implementing regulations.

We find these cases inapposite. Here, the issue concerns the relationship between two separate and distinct statutes--the FMLA and the Back Pay Act--and we see no inconsistency between them. As the Supreme Court has observed, the "classic judicial task of reconciling many laws enacted over time, and getting them to 'make sense in combination, necessarily assumes that the implications of a statute may be altered by the implications of a later statute." United States v. Fausto, 484 U.S. 439, 453 (1988) (holding that the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 precluded judicial review of a nonpreference eligible employee's action based on the Back Pay Act of 1966).

Although the Back Pay Act and the FMLA were enacted for different purposes, their terms affecting the same subject matter must be construed harmoniously and consistently with one another, if reasonably possible. See Woodyard-Hamilton v. Office of Personnel Management, 64 MSPR 150, 154 (1991); see also Singer, Sutherland Statutory Construction § 57.01 (5th Ed. 1992). The Back Pay Act states that "for all purposes" an employee is deemed to have performed service during the period for which the employee received back pay, and the FMLA states that an employee must have performed 1,250 hours of service during the 12 months prior to the date of the leave request. The question before us is whether the phrase "for all purposes" under the Back Pay Act encompasses the eligibility requirements of the FMLA. We find that the remedial purposes of the Back Pay Act and the FMLA require an affirmative answer. Therefore, the appellant is an eligible employee under the FMLA and the agency, by refusing to consider the appellant's requests for FMLA leave, failed to restore him to the status quo ante pursuant to the Board's final decision at 78 MSPR 31. See Kerr, 726 F.2d at 733.
