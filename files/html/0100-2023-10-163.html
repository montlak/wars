c. Improper Communication

In re King, 1 MSPR 146, 1 MSPB 144, 153 (1979), determined that the inadvertent receipt of agency evidence by the adjudicator before it is reviewed by appellant's counsel will not sustain a charge that the adjudicator is unable to render a fair hearing, as long as the adjudicator considers rebuttal evidence. Improper ex parte communications can be cured by placing the communications in the record and allowing the respondent an opportunity to comment. An ill-advised conversation by an AJ with one party, but not the other, concerning the continuance of the hearing did not substantiate a claim of bias. Ragland v. IRS, 6 MSPR 674, 677, 6 MSPB 565 (1981). Ritchey v. USPS, 19 MSPR 170, 172-73 (1984), determined there was no reversible error when the agency submitted with its posthearing brief documents not earlier entered into the record. The AJ did not appear to consider the materials, meaning there was no harm to the appellant:

Appellant also contends that he has been denied the right to a fair hearing because the agency submitted ex parte evidence with its post-hearing brief. The record indicates that the agency posthearing submission included an attachment which included maintenance department work orders that were not previously part of the record and which the agency admits appellant was not provided. We find that although appellant was not given an opportunity to rebut the contents of the documents, there has been no substantive harm to appellant's rights. There is nothing to indicate that these documents were given any consideration by the presiding official. Furthermore, the presiding official stated that it was unnecessary to introduce the documents during the course of the hearing because in and of themselves they did not prove that there were problems requiring technical expertise. Therefore, we find that there was no harmful adjudicatory error in the presiding official's acceptance of these documents as filed. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 MSPB 114 (1981).

The AJ may send the parties a copy of a Board or court decision that the AJ feels may be pertinent to the disposition of the case. The party who is not favored by the decision may believe that the AJ is performing legal research for the opponent and educating the opposing party on matters that might otherwise go without notice. There is no harm in the practice: informed parties are better able to settle. See Lewis v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 373, 377 (1990). The Board adds that the AJ is not required to seek out and produce all pertinent decisions, for the function of the AJ "is not to act as a party's representative." Henry v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 482, 487 (1989). As to allegations that an AJ became too involved in settlement negotiations, Stone v. DHHS, 46 MSPR 34, 37 (1990):

...The appellant claims...that the administrative judge was biased against her because he suggested certain terms for the parties' consideration in settlement discussions, and because he did not hold the agency bound to certain offers it made during those negotiations....

An administrative judge may properly encourage the parties to try to settle cases, given the public policy which favors settlement agreements in civil actions in order to avoid unnecessary litigation and to encourage the fair and speedy resolution of issues.... Moreover, a settlement agreement is a contract between the parties.... Neither party is bound by terms of settlement negotiations unless and until an "act of finality," that is, agreement, occurs. See Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 132, 135-36 (1988).

An inquiry about the health of agency counsel during a phone conversation with the AJ did not establish bias or basis for assignment of another AJ, Chiovitti v. Dept. of Air Force, PH-0752-21-0212-I-1, n.7 (NP 7/12/2022):

On review, the appellant objects to the administrative judge having telephoned the agency's representative to inquire after her health, after she filed a motion to stay deadlines/responses citing health issues as the basis for the motion....Administrative judges are not prohibited from engaging in ex parte conversations regarding procedural matters, such as extensions of time. See Vidal v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 254, ¶ 6 (2010) (finding that ex parte communications regarding procedural matters are not prohibited); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.102 (prohibiting ex parte communications regarding the merits, as opposed to procedural aspects, of matters before the Board). The administrative judge's conversation with the agency's representative does not violate the prohibition on ex parte communications, and the appellant's argument provides no basis to assign this appeal to a new administrative judge on remand.

If ex parte communications are determined to be inappropriate, the objecting party must show how he was harmed by the matter. Martinez v. USPS, 89 MSPR 152, 163 (2001). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Coercion or Bias by Judge," for decisions evaluating charges that settlements were coerced.]
