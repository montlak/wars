4. Changes in Employment Conditions

Initially, FLRA insisted that an employer is required to maintain existing conditions of employment during the pendency of a question concerning representation (QCR). That rule does not prevent management from changing negotiable personnel policies, practices, and matters affecting working conditions when those changes are essential to the necessary functioning of the agency. DOJ, INS and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 9 FLRA 253, 255 (1982). The requirement of maintaining the status quo precluded negotiation of a merit promotion plan. Changes in the plan would lead to changes in employment conditions. INS and AFGE, Nat'l INS Council, 16 FLRA 80, 86-87 (1984).

DOJ v. FLRA, 727 F.2d 481, 486, 488 (5th Cir. 1984), reviewing 9 FLRA 253 (1982), implied that FLRA could not wholly preclude management from exercising statutorily reserved management rights during the pendency of a QCR:

In this case, the Authority has promulgated a rule that an employing agency, such as INS, may not change existing conditions of employment while a question concerning representation under 5 USC § 7111 is pending before the Authority, except insofar as a change is consistent with the necessary functioning of that agency. Applied to this case, this rule means, according to FLRA, that the INS should have refrained from establishing or reestablishing highway checkpoints at night; from failing to assign backup units to checkpoints; and from using uniformed agents to conduct city patrols. To decide whether such an application is appropriate, we consider the FLRA's election rule in the light of the statute's management-rights section.

...

The decision to establish or reestablish a highway checkpoint...[or] to assign a backup unit...involves...assignment of work...; the decision as to whether an agent will wear a uniform...involves a determination of the "means" of performing work.... Consequently, the decision of INS with regard to these practices implicated rights which are reserved to management by section 7106.

...

...Congress provided specifically in 5 U.S.C. § 7106 that "nothing in this chapter shall affect the authority of any management official of any agency" to exercise the rights reserved to management by that section.... The use of such words makes it obvious that Congress did not intend to let the Authority decide whether, in its judgment, it was "necessary" for the INS to establish highway checkpoints...or to require uniformed agents to conduct "city patrols."... Construing the statute to allow the Authority to promulgate a rule which would bar management from exercising its reserved rights during the pendency of a representation question would hardly lead to an INS which was as effective and efficient as possible. This particular question concerning representation has been pending nearly five years....

...

...We conclude, therefore, that the Authority's rule suspending the exercise of these management rights during the pendency of a representation question is prohibited by the statute and thus the rule was improperly applied to the acts of management here in question.[11]

[11] Our decision here should not be read as invalidating the rule. We only decide that its application to these facts cannot stand in view of the conflict presented with the statutory provisions of section 7106.

Department of Justice suggested that the employer may modify its operations pending a QCR, with consequential impact on working conditions, if those changes are reasonably related to and necessitated by agency operational requirements. Additionally, management does not act improperly by changing a condition of employment that has become unlawful even if the change must occur while there is a pending QCR. HCFA and AFGE Local 1923, 17 FLRA 650, 654 (1985).

Changes in employment conditions that constitute reprisal against union adherents and advocates may invalidate an election. A second election rerun was directed in Marine Corps Logistics Base, Barstow and AFGE Local 1482, 9 FLRA 1046, 1047-48 (1982) (ALJ Decision), because during the pre-election period the agency changed the status, reduced the hours of work, reprimanded, and revoked the "excellent" performance rating of an employee who was open and active in supporting the union and soliciting others to join the union. Given the small size of the unit, 65 employees, the centralized work locations of the employees, and the visibility of the union supporter, FLRA concluded that the agency's improper conduct had a reasonably foreseeable negative effect on the voters' attitude toward the union and interfered with the employees' freedom of choice in the election.
