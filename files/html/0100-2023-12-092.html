broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section B: Interim Relief, Subsection 05: Dismissal of PFR for Failure to Provide Interim Relief, Part a: Remedy for Inadequate Interim Relief
a. Remedy for Inadequate Interim Relief

If the agency's interim relief is insufficient, the appellant's sole remedy--and the maximum relief from the Board--is dismissal of the agency PFR. Further relief may be obtained through proceedings seeking compliance with the Board's final order. See Mascarenas v. DOD, 57 MSPR 425, 429-30 (1993). The Board will not entertain a motion to enforce an interim relief order; the motion is treated as a motion to dismiss the PFR. Batten v. USPS, 101 MSPR 222, 225 ¶ 6, 2006 MSPB 35 (2006); see Cruz v. DOD, NY-0752-20-0029-I-1 (NP 4/18/2023) ("The administrative judge erred in granting the appellant's petition for enforcement of the interim relief order. Our regulations do not allow for a petition for enforcement of an interim relief order. Ayers v. Department of the Army, 123 M.S.P.R. 11, ¶ 7 (2015); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.182(a)-(b). Instead, the Board treats motions to enforce an interim relief order as a motion to dismiss the petition for review. Batten v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 222, ¶ 6, aff'd per curiam, 208 F. App'x. 868 (Fed. Cir. 2006); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(d)."); Bryant v. Dept. of Army, 2022 MSPB 1, ¶ 6 (2022) ("The appellant's petition for enforcement is denied because the Board's regulations do not allow for a petition for enforcement of an interim relief order. Elder v. Department of the Air Force, 124 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 20 (2016); see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.182(a)-(b) (providing for petitions for enforcement of final Board orders). We instead consider the appellant's pleading as a challenge to the agency's certification of compliance. Elder, 124 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 20; 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(b)."); Parbs v. USPS, 107 MSPR 559, 2007 MSPB 302 (2007) (aff'd, Parbs v. USPS, 301 Fed. Appx. 923 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)) (same analysis as Batten). If the appellant believes that the agency did not provide him proper interim relief, his objections to the agency action or inaction may take the form of a motion to dismiss the agency's PFR. See Wayne v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 322 (1992); Nicoletti v. DOJ, 53 MSPR 610, 614 (1992) (treating as a motion to dismiss the agency PFR the appellant's assertion in his response to the PFR that the agency did not comply with the AJ's order directing interim relief). The Board treats objections by the appellant to interim relief measures as a motion to dismiss the agency PFR, whether or not the objections are formally designated as a motion. See Lewis v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 325, 335 (1993) (treating as a motion to dismiss objections to interim relief asserted in a petition for compliance with an interim relief order); Bonggat v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 402, 405 n.1 (1993) (treating a request for enforcement of interim relief as a motion to dismiss the agency's PFR). A court determined that a back pay claim involving an AWOL charge while an employee was on interim relief was preempted by MSPB's authority to adjudicate disputes over interim relief. Ramos v. USDA, 22-CV-20190 (N.D. Fla. Aug. 17, 2022).

An interim relief objection to a PFR may be deemed moot when the agency PFR is denied and the appellant receives the relief directed by the AJ as a result of the Board's final decision. Elder v. Dept. of Air Force, 124 MSPR 12, 2016 MSPB 41, ¶ 20 (2016) ("If the agency fails to provide evidence of compliance in response to such an order, the Board may, at its discretion, dismiss the agency's petition for review. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.116(e). In this case, however, we find that the agency's petition does not meet the criteria for review in any event, and the issuance of our final decision renders moot any dispute concerning the agency's compliance with the interim relief order. See Ayers, 123 M.S.P.R. 11, ¶ 8 (reaching the same conclusion where the Board affirmed the administrative judge's reversal of the appellant's removal based on whistleblower reprisal)."); Smith v. Dept. of Army, 2022 MSPB 4, ¶ 12 (2022) ("We exercise our discretion in this case not to dismiss the petition for review because the issue of the agency's compliance with the interim relief order is now moot by virtue of our final decision ordering corrective action."); Coffey v. USPS, 77 MSPR 281, 286 (1998) (appellant received a Board order directing full relief through retroactive back pay; because "appellant has received the only remedy to which he was entitled, any matter related to compliance with the interim relief order is now moot"); cf. Anderson v. USPS, 64 MSPR 233, 238 (1994) (on occasion, the Board will declare a controversy over interim relief moot by taking another action in the appeal that grants full relief to the appellant: "Here, the appellants would be in no better position were we to adjudicate any claims based on interim relief, since as a result of our decision on the issue of jurisdiction, they are already entitled to retroactive cancellation of their demotions and restoration to the status quo ante.").

The Board addresses as a dismissal motion objections to interim relief raised in an appellant's cross-PFR. See Browning v. Dept. of Army, 61 MSPR 232, 234 (1994). Should the appellant believe that the agency's interim relief is inadequate, the time for challenging the agency's action is through a challenge to its PFR. An enforcement petition by appellant raising interim relief problems following resolution of the PFR in the agency's favor is barred by res judicata, since the interim relief issue should have been raised in earlier proceedings. See Mazzarella v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3039). Once the Board issues a final order, disputes over remedies are resolved through enforcement proceedings and any question regarding compliance with an interim relief order is moot. Owens v. Dept. of Transp., 99 MSPR 377, 382 ¶ 10 (2005).

Although the Board expects the appellant to timely challenge the agency's denial of interim relief, the Federal Circuit suggested in Harding v. VA, 451 Fed. Appx. 947, 950 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP), that the Board has an independent responsibility to assess the adequacy of interim relief: "Yet, even without a timely challenge to the agency's interim relief, the Board remains obligated to ensure that the agency has complied with the interim relief order either by providing the required relief or satisfying the exceptions to such relief. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(b)(1)."

The Board does not permit appellants to litigate, through challenges to interim relief, issues that are involved in the back pay remedy that will accompany a final decision favoring the appellant. Those issues are addressed through enforcement proceedings following the Board's final order directing permanent relief, Slaughter v. USDA, 56 MSPR 349, 352 n.9 (1993):

The appellant also alleges that the agency failed to comply with the interim relief order by not crediting him with sick and annual leave for the period he was out of work, by failing to pay him his full salary for his last pay period before his removal, by failing to provide him a full 30 days of temporary quarters at his new duty station, and by wrongfully planning to collect a $500 travel advance that he never received. These allegations are not appropriate for review at the interim relief stage of the proceedings. See Ginocchi v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 62, 71 n.6 (1992)....

The remedy for incomplete interim relief, i.e., striking an agency PFR, is a drastic sanction. It is one that the Board previously employed with some frequency, and with far less frequency in recent years following relaxation of interim relief technical regulatory requirements in 1999. Failure to certify compliance with an interim relief order resulted in dismissal of an agency PFR in Wheatley v. USPS, 51 MSPR 238, 240 (1991). Another PFR was dismissed when the agency did not certify interim relief with its PFR. Instead, the agency waited a month after filing the PFR to attempt to cure the procedural deficiency. The Board noted, however, that it was not reaching the "issue of whether an agency may, within the time period for filing a petition for review, supplement its petition with a showing of interim relief." Baughman v. Dept. of Army, 49 MSPR 415, 418 n.3 (1991); see Edwards v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 536, 538 (1992) (same result). The Board implied in Nelson v. DHHS, 51 MSPR 621 (1991) (Table), that it would not permit an agency to demonstrate interim relief by a filing after the final date for filing a PFR. See Brown v. USPS, 54 MSPR 278, 281 (1992) (agency PFR was dismissed when it contained information showing that the appellant was directed to report for duty, but the reporting date was not the date of the initial decision: "The agency's subsequent effort to comply was made only pursuant to the Board's show-cause order and did not occur until several months after the final date for filing a petition for review had passed."); Purdy v. Dept. of Air Force, 53 MSPR 693, 696 (1992) (interim relief requirements were not met when the agency complied a month after the date passed for filing a PFR); Grady v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 225, 226-27 (1992) (noting that the agency's correction of its interim relief certification was well after the last date it could have filed the PFR); Stanford v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 96, 98 (1992, 95) (interim relief shortly after last the day for a PFR was insufficient); Weinstein v. OPM, 50 MSPR 92 (1991) (agency could not demonstrate interim relief on the day after the period expired for filing its PFR; also dismissing the PFR because the agency retroactively canceled an adverse action).
