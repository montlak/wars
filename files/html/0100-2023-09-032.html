b. Significance of Postmark: Presumptive Filing Date

If an appeal is filed by regular mail, the postmark date is considered the filing date. See 5 CFR 1201.22(d), 1201.4(l) (2022); Simonton v. USPS, 35 MSPR 594, 596 (1987) (postmark, not the date of receipt of an appeal by the regional office, is the filing date); Isip v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 401, 402 (1980) (postmark controlled even though the appeal was not received by the Board until two months later); Stevenson v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 356, 358, 3 MSPB 437 (1980). In one esoteric situation, the Board decided that the postmark was the filing date even after the Board rejected the appeal itself because there was postage due on the envelope. Conley v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 492, 493 (1984).

If there is a conflict between the date on metered mail and a USPS canceling machine date, the cancelation is the date of filing because the Postal Service cancels metered mail only if it bears an incorrect date. Rousselle v. Dept. of Army, 69 MSPR 531, 533 (1996); Hunt v. USPS, 41 MSPR 381, 384 (1989) (rejecting the contention that an undesignated representative mailed the appeal on time and that the Postal Service misplaced and repostmarked the envelope). If there is a meter marking and a hand cancelation date stamp that conflicts with the date the appellant states he posted an appeal, the issue may prove important. Under Lawrence v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 7, 9 (1992), the AJ should take evidence and determine whether the appeal was posted on the date the appellant claims, or whether it was posted on the date of the metered postal strip. See Steakley v. OPM, 63 MSPR 155, 157 (1994). In Glascock v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 595, 596 (1983), the Board summarily ruled against an appellant who maintained that a postage meter date was incorrectly set, one day too late for his appeal.

For various reasons, a delay may occur between the time of mailing and the entry on the envelope of a postmark. The Board will not default an appellant who provides a reasonable explanation of how his appeal was timely placed into the mail stream but was not timely postmarked, Williams v. USPS, 61 MSPR 213, 215-16 (1994):

The Board considers the postmark date to be the date of filing of a mailed submission. 5 CFR § 1201.4(l). In Raphel v. Dept. of the Army, 50 MSPR 614 (1991), the Board held that a credible, unrebutted affidavit or sworn statement averring that, despite a late postmark date, the appeal was placed in the Postal Service mail stream before the filing deadline is sufficient to establish timely filing.

Here, the appellant's affidavit and the statement of his representative say that the appellant signed and mailed his appeal to the Board on June 29, within the 20-day time frame for filing. Although the appellant did not state specifically that he placed the petition for appeal in a "mailbox," we have no reason to find that his use of the term "mail" does not mean the Postal Service mail stream. The appellant's arguments that he should not be held responsible for the speed of the mail service, and that the Postal Service's reorganization and its implementation of remote barcoding in his area made the mail flow sluggish, support this finding. Thus, we find that the appellant's unrebutted and uncontradicted statements are sufficient to establish timely filing. See Nicoletti v. Dept. of Justice, 53 MSPR 610 (1992) (a representative's legal secretary's affidavit representing that she had placed the submission in the mail box on the last day for filing was sufficient to establish that fact). Contrary to the implication of the administrative judge's finding, the appellant need not provide reasons for the Postal Service's delay in affixing a postmark.
