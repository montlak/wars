b. Case That May Be Either Initiated With OSC or Appealed Directly to MSPB

Assuming an otherwise appealable action that should be filed within the normal Board appellate deadline, the question of whether the same appellate deadline would be applied to cases initiated with OSC under the WPA arose in Augustine v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 648, 651-53 (1991). The Board decided that there was no statutory or regulatory limitation period for initiating the case with OSC:

The administrative judge further found that, even if the WPA was applicable, the appellant's appeal was not timely filed. He reasoned that, because the appellant was affected by an otherwise appealable action (his removal) that must be appealed to the Board within 20 days of its effective date under 5 CFR 1201.22, he was required to choose within that appealable time limit whether to appeal directly to the Board or first go through OSC.

The statute and implementing regulations do not support the administrative judge's position. Under 5 USC 1214 (a)(3), an employee, former employee, or applicant for employment may appeal to the Board from agency personnel actions alleged to have been threatened, proposed, taken, or not taken, because of the appellant's whistleblowing activities. See 5 CFR 1209.2(a). The Board exercises jurisdiction over Individual Right of Action (IRA) appeals, where the appellant must seek corrective action from OSC before appealing to the Board, and otherwise appealable action appeals. Id. at 1209.2(b)(1) and (2). As to otherwise appealable action appeals, the Board's regulations state that the appellant may choose either to seek corrective action from OSC before appealing to the Board or to appeal directly to the Board. Id. at 1209.2(b)(2).

The appellant in this case chose to seek corrective action from OSC before appealing to the Board. The statute places no time limit on an employee's right to seek such corrective action from OSC. See 5 USC 1214(a)(1)(A). See also 5 CFR Part 1800, OSC's regulations imposing no time limit for the filing of complaints. Thus, although the appellant did not, in fact, seek corrective action from OSC until after he had unsuccessfully grieved his removal, his complaint to OSC was timely.

Having chosen to seek corrective action from OSC before appealing to the Board, the appellant's time limit for appealing to the Board is governed by 5 CFR 1209.5(a). See 5 CFR 1209.5(b). Those time limits provide only that, if OSC has not notified the appellant that it will seek corrective action on his behalf within 120 days of the date of filing of the request for corrective action, then his appeal may be filed at any time after the expiration of 120 days. Id. at 1209.5(a)(2); 5 USC 1214(a)(3). The legislative history of the WPA supports our position that no other time limit is applicable. In the Report of the Committee on Governmental Affairs of the U.S. Senate, S. Rep. No. 413, 100th Cong., 2d Sess. 18 (1988), the Committee indicated that it did not intend that any time limit be applied to appeals by whistleblowers whose cases have not been formally closed by OSC. Since the appellant filed his appeal with the Board after 120 days had passed without OSC's having notified him that it would seek corrective action on his behalf, his appeal was timely filed. Cf. Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 475, 478-80 (1991) (the statutory language of the WPA indicates by its own terms that IRA appeal rights are independent of the EEO complaint process and vest upon the expiration of the time limits set forth at 5 USC 1214(a)(3), which are the only exhaustion requirements that Congress intended to be imposed on such actions).

The Board summarized the dual track for IRA cases, Carolyn v. Dept. of Interior, 63 MSPR 684, 687 n.1 (1994):

The agency argued, in its motion to dismiss, that, because the appellant filed his IRA appeal 25 days after the termination, it should be dismissed as untimely. The administrative judge did not consider this issue in his initial decision, however, because he dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. We find that the IRA appeal was timely because it was filed not more than 60 days after OSC provided notification to the appellant that its investigation was terminated. 5 USC § 1214(a)(3). See Wood v. Dept. of the Air Force, 54 MSPR 587, 591-92 (1992).

See Tubesing v. DHHS, 109 MSPR 420, 424-25 ¶ 11, 2008 MSPB 179 (2008) (explaining the OSC exhaustion scheme for an otherwise appealable action involving a probationer's dismissal); cf. Hornby v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 246 (1994) (if the appellant simultaneously files with the Board and with OSC, he must await OSC exhaustion).

If the appellant challenges a personnel action in an appeal, and if that case is resolved, the appellant cannot refile the case as an IRA appeal. Vogel v. Dept. of Navy, 106 MSPR 451, 455 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 200 (2007) ("His subsequent filing with OSC does not afford him the right to file another Board appeal of the same action."); see Jackson v. DOD, 107 MSPR 317, 2007 MSPB 279 (2007) (in a complex procedural setting, the Board implied that if the appellant first filed a removal appeal that could have raised a whistleblowing reprisal claim, and that appeal was dismissed based on withdrawal of the appeal to pursue an EEO case, the whistleblower claim could not again be raised through an IRA appeal; but the Board went on to construe the dismissal as a DWOP and resolved the appeal on other issues); Tubesing, supra, 109 MSPR at 425 n.* (if an appellant appeals an otherwise appealable action directly to the Board and receives a final merits decision, he is barred from bring, after exhausting the OSC process, a second appeal challenging the same action under a new legal theory). But see Ferry v. Hayden, 954 F.2d 658 (11th Cir. 1992) (a probationer who appealed and lost before the MSPB as to limited claims that could be raised in a probationer's appeal could subsequently file with OSC and pursue his whistleblower reprisal case: "[n]either the statutory provisions, see 5 U.S.C. § 1214, nor the regulations, see 5 C.F.R. § 1800.1, regarding the filing of complaints and allegations with the OSC place any time limit on the exercise of that right."). The IRA appeal is independent of whatever action the appellant pursues under the EEO complaint system. The WPA does not condition the right to file an IRA appeal on the appellant's exhaustion of his EEO administrative remedies after filing a formal EEO complaint on the underlying personnel action. Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 475, 479 (1991) ("[B]y setting forth time limits for tiling an IRA appeal without regard to the agency resolution of the EEO complaint, we find that the statutory language indicates by its own terms that IRA appeal rights are independent of the EEO complaint process and vest upon the expiration of the time limits set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3). We note that to find otherwise would create an inescapable conflict between the time limits established at 5 U.S.C. § 7702 and those established at 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3).").
