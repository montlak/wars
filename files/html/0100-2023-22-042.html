broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 01: Entitlement Standards, Section E: Clearly Without Merit or Wholly Unfounded, Subsection 03: Lack of Legitimate Basis for Agency Action, Part a: Development of Dispositive Information During Appeal
a. Development of Dispositive Information During Appeal

Discussed in later in this chapter, "Retirement Appeal," is Kent v. OPM, 33 MSPR 361, 367 (1987), providing analysis applied to counsel fee petitions filed in retirement appeals. Under Kent, the Board examines the evidence made available to OPM prior to the Board appeal and during the MSPB appeal from an unfavorable reconsideration decision. The issue then is whether OPM's decision was clearly without merit on the evidence. Gollis v. Dept. of Navy, 54 MSPR 38, 42-44 (1992), applied Kent to an adverse action appeal. In Gollis the appellant was removed for physical inability to perform his work. The appellant did not reply to the proposed removal. During the Board appeal, the appellant introduced evidence negating the existence of a disabling condition. He prevailed in his appeal and filed a petition for fees. Fees should not have been awarded by the AJ because, without more information, a reasonable person would not have resolved the case in the appellant's favor on the basis of the evidence available to the agency before the AJ's adjudication of the case:

[W]e find that this case is analogous to those in which the Board has reversed a decision by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to deny an appellant's application for a disability retirement annuity on the basis of evidence submitted while the case was pending before the Board. In the attorney fee cases arising from those decisions, the Board has found that the proper standard to apply is whether the agency's action was clearly without merit. See, e.g., Kent v. Office of Personnel Management, 33 MSPR 361, 367-69 (1987) (when the agency's reconsideration decision would not have been reversed absent evidence adduced during the appellate process, it is appropriate to resolve the request for a fee award based on an analysis of the "clearly without merit" category).

In Kent, 33 MSPR at 368-69, the Board found that to award an appellant attorney fees under this criterion, the Board must determine that, at some point prior to the close of the appellate record, the agency's failure to acknowledge the appellant's entitlement to benefits was blameworthy. To make this determination, it considers: (1) Whether the appellant was misled by OPM or whether OPM failed to put him on notice of the kind of evidence needed to prevail on reconsideration; (2) the extent to which reversal was based on evidence not presented by the appellant but readily available to OPM; and (3) the extent to which the appellant produced evidence that was so compelling that reasonable minds could not differ as to his eligibility for an annuity and OPM's continued refusal to approve the annuity prolonged the proceedings.

In adapting this test to the present case, we find that the evidence does not show that the appellant was misled by the agency, that the agency failed to put him on notice of the kind of evidence he needed to prevail, or that the reversal of his removal was based on evidence that he did not present but that was readily available to the agency. The notice of proposed separation informed the appellant that the agency's action was based on his physical inability to perform, notified him that he could review the material the agency relied on in proposing his separation, and specifically gave him an opportunity to submit a response and any updated medical documentation concerning his disability. The record indicates that the appellant did not submit any response to the notice of proposed separation. Moreover, the administrative judge sustained the charge against the appellant because he found that the evidence possessed by the agency before it removed the appellant showed by a preponderance of the evidence that the appellant was unable to perform the duties of his position. The Board does not normally review the merits of an appeal in an attorney fee case. See, e.g., Goldbach v. Office of Personnel Management, 49 MSPR 9, 16 (1991); Yorkshire, 746 F.2d at 1458-59. Thus, we find no basis for awarding attorney fees for work performed prior to October 9, 1990, the date on which the administrative judge found that the agency was aware of the letter from Dr. Tourtellotte.

We also find that the appellant is not entitled to attorney fees for work performed after October 9, 1990, under the third Kent factor, the "compelling evidence" test.[3] Although we found that Dr. Tourtellotte's October 3, 1990 letter was sufficient evidence under the standard established in Street to show that the agency should have been on notice that the appellant was probably able to return to work, we do not find that it is sufficient evidence under the higher standard established in Kent to show that the appellant is entitled to attorney fees. The letter itself was not sufficiently detailed, nor was there supporting documentation or evidence, that a reasonable person would have had to accept it without more. See Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPR 404, 423 (1981) (the probative value of medical opinions may depend in part upon such factors as whether the opinion provides a reasoned explanation for its findings as distinct from mere conclusory assertions). In addition, the agency had no clear way to try to rebut the letter, since it received the letter after all agency action was completed, during appeal to the Board. As the agency no longer employed the appellant, it could not require the appellant to report for a "fitness for duty" examination.

[3] An attorney fee award based on an agency's continued, improper actions is limited to fees incurred after the agency is made aware of the dispositive evidence. See, e.g., Lode v. OPM, 43 MSPR 676, 685 (1990).

Accordingly, we find that the appellant is not entitled to attorney fees.
