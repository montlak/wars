7. Protecting Record; Missing Witness

A party must use the Board's witness production procedures before it can complain in a PFR that a witness was absent through the fault of another party. Baldwin v. TVA, 5 MSPR 125, 127, 4 MSPB 190 (1981). If a party is surprised by the failure of the other party to call a witness, then the first party must make a timely request for a continuance to obtain the presence of that witness. Dubiel v. USPS, 54 MSPR 428, 432 (1992); see Sanders v. SSA, 114 MSPR 487, 491 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 155 (2010) ("Thus, the appellant failed to preserve this issue for review by objecting to the administrative judge's ruling excluding her requested witnesses when given the opportunity to do so."); Quinn v. USPS, 53 MSPR 671, 674 n.4 (1992) ("After the administrative judge denied the agency's request to call Williams as a witness, the appellant could have requested Williams as his own witness. Because the appellant failed to do so, he cannot now claim that the administrative judge erred by denying him the opportunity to examine or cross-examine this witness and then concluding that he withheld information from the witness. See, e.g., Washington v. United States Postal Service, 35 MSPR 195, 199 (1987); Lohr v. Department of the Air Force, 24 MSPR 383, 386 (1984)."); Lohr v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 383 (1984) (if the other party did not call a listed witness, the appellant should have requested a subpoena and continuance); Morgan v. USPS, 12 MSPR 23, 25 (1982) (no harmful error when, despite the refusal of a witness to appear at the hearing, the appellant's representative failed to request sanctions and did not describe the expected testimony or state how the appellant was harmed by the lack of the testimony).

Phillipp v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-11-0065-I-1 (NP 11/25/2011), noted of the problem of the witness who, possibly tired of waiting to be called, departs:

On petition for review, the appellant asserts that he was denied the appearance of a federal employee, Gloria Baker, who was on the appellant's witness list and was purportedly present outside the hearing room but later departed, thereby becoming unavailable to testify. However, the record does not contain an effort by the appellant's attorney to call Ms. Baker. An appellant must object on the record to the failure of a requested witness to testify at the hearing in order to preserve the issue. See Herring v. U.S. Postal Service, 40 M.S.P.R. 342, 346 (1989) (holding that when the record is silent as to why a requested witness did not testify, an objection cannot be raised before the Board); see also Sanders v. Social Security Administration, 114 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 9 (2010). Even if we assume arguendo that the agency failed to produce Ms. Baker, the appellant failed to preserve the issue and therefore cannot raise it on petition for review. See Herring, 40 M.S.P.R. at 346.

For the party who makes and, after discussion with the AJ, withdraws a request for a witness, no ruling is made that the Board can review in a PFR. The record is not preserved. Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 324, 325-26, 6 MSPB 277 (1981); see Nichols v. USPS, 80 MSPR 229, 231-32 ¶ 7 (1998) (appellant failed to object to the denial of witnesses at the time of the hearing); Dunn v. Dept. of Army, 9 MSPR 459, 460-61 (1982) (appellant's representative rested his case without seeking to call certain witnesses; a complaint could not then be made in a PFR that the witnesses were not allowed to testify); cf. Corbett v. USPS, 29 MSPR 512, 513 (1985) (appellant abandoned the request for witnesses when his representative did not object to a letter from the agency representative reciting their agreement that the testimony of witnesses was not necessary because they had no direct knowledge of the appellant's case). [Refer to this chapter, "Offer of Proof," and to Chapter 4, "Offer of Proof."]

Although there are decisions just noted that suggest a continuance ought be requested if the other party does not call or produce a witness, most AJs will not permit a continuance unless the party who wishes to have the testimony taken listed the witness in the prehearing submission and that witness was approved. If a party wants a witness, that party must list and secure approval from the AJ for the witness. Do not depend on the AJ to protect your right to examine a witness who you believe will attend the hearing because the other side listed that individual as a witness. Protect yourself: list the witness.

If a witness was requested and no objection is made to the failure of the witness to appear at the hearing, the request for the witness is deemed abandoned. Mahnken v. USPS, 34 MSPR 1, 6 (1987). An agency cannot complain in a PFR of the failure to obtain the testimony of a requested witness at a Board hearing when, if the witness subpoenaed did not appear at the hearing, the agency representative did not request enforcement and did not request the AJ to hold open the record for the receipt of the testimony of that witness. Porter v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 301, 306, 6 MSPB 257 (1981) ("[T]he agency by its inaction had abandoned its subpoena request at the conclusion of the hearing."). If an adverse witness who was subpoenaed fails to appear and the appellant has the opportunity to request a continuance to obtain the witness but declines, preferring to rest on the record developed without that testimony, the AJ does not err by refusing to strike the written statement of that witness placed into the record by the agency. Prestien v. OPM, 8 MSPR 698, 702-03 n.4 (1981). If a witness is substituted for another who cannot be located, there is no basis to reverse the initial decision without a demonstration that the substitution of the witness impaired the proceedings, prejudiced the appellant's substantive rights, or denied him a fair hearing. Washington v. USPS, 35 MSPR 195, 198-99 (1987). If during a hearing it is agreed that a witness will be deposed, the party desiring to offer the deposition in evidence should ensure that the record remains open for its receipt. Ramos v. SBA, 27 MSPR 607, 608-09 n.* (1985). If a deposition is offered in lieu of live testimony at the hearing, a party will not be heard to complain that he has not been furnished with a transcript of the deposition if no request is made to the Board or to the commercial stenographer for a copy of the transcript. Smith v. USPS, 55 MSPR 348, 352 n.2 (1992).
