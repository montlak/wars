d. Undue Interruption

Assignment rights are conditioned on the capacity, adaptability, and any special skills needed to perform satisfactorily the duties and responsibilities of the position without undue interruption to the activity. 5 CFR 351.702(a)(4) (2022). Section 351.702(a)(4) (2022) authorizes an agency to determine that an otherwise qualified employee is not entitled to exercise assignment rights to a position if the displacement would involve undue disruption. Narcisse v. Dept. of Transp., 32 MSPR 232, 234 (1987).

5 CFR 351.203 (2022) provides OPM's definition of "undue disruption":

Undue interruption means a degree of interruption that would prevent the completion of required work by the employee 90 days after the employee has been placed in a different position under this part. The 90-day standard should be considered within the allowable limits of time and quality, taking into account the pressures of priorities, deadlines, and other demands. However, a work program would generally not be unduly interrupted even if an employee needed more than 90 days after the reduction in force to perform the optimum quality or quantity of work. The 90-day standard may be extended if placement is made under this part to a low priority program or to a vacant position.

When considering the defense of undue disruption concerning claimed assignment rights, the appellant need not demonstrate that he can perform the optimum quality and quantity of work for the position, even within the 90 days alluded to by the former FPM. The nature and priority of the work should be considered. Those considerations govern the construction of "undue disruption" both as to assignment rights and as to definition of the competitive level. Porter v. Dept. of Commerce, 13 MSPR 177, 179-80 (1982) (citing Rasmussen v. United States, 543 F.2d 134 (Ct. Cl. 1976)); see FPM Ch. 351-3-4b-1; Fass v. Gray, 197 F.2d 587 (D.C. Cir. 1952) (history of the concept of undue disruption).

A trade specialist, although minimally qualified for an examiner's position, was not entitled to the position because of the undue disruption that would flow from her relative inexperience with complex cases involving ship financing applications and the administration of guarantee contracts, both requiring exposure to shipping economics and financing. LaPrade v. Dept. of Transp., 27 MSPR 277, 283 (1985). An undue interruption in an agency training program would have occurred if an appellant was placed in the position sought through assignment rights. At the time of the RIF, the program was in its second six-month period; the employee needed the first six months of training to qualify for a position in the latter portion of the training. Harris v. Dept. of Treasury, 5 MSPR 545, 548, 6 MSPB 8 (1981).

Undue interruption is relative. The process of evaluating qualifications for vacant positions differs from evaluation of qualifications for the exercise of RIF assignment rights. If an employee is highly qualified for a vacant position sought through competitive promotion, without unusual circumstances, there is no concern about undue interruption of activities. The vacant position has no ongoing activities to disrupt. Lewellen v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 525, 527 & n.1 (1985); see Manescalchi v. USPS, 74 MSPR 479, 488 (1997) (same analysis).

If displacement is involved it may be that the same qualified employee will cause undue disruption if placed into the job due to the amount of training and orientation necessitated by the assignment. Jamison v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 513, 515-18 (1984), compared the relative impact of assigning an employee to an incumbered rather than to a vacant position:

The appellant has filed a timely petition for review of the initial decision contending that, subsequent to the RIF action, the agency determined that he was among the best qualified candidates for two GS-13 vacancies, announced through the agency's merit promotion program, which appellant alleges are identical to the GS-12 position for which he was found not qualified during the RIF. In essence, the appellant argues that the agency's subsequent determinations with regard to his qualifications for the GS-13 vacancies constitute new and material evidence under 5 CFR § 1201.115(a) establishing that the agency and presiding official erred in finding that the appellant was not qualified to exercise RIF assignment rights to the GS-12 position.

Based on the evidence which the appellant has submitted, and in the absence of any rebuttal by the agency, we find that the two GS-13 vacancies and the GS-12 position are substantially similar in terms of their duties, responsibilities and qualifications. It is evident that all three positions require a knowledge of the transportation of hazardous materials. Notwithstanding this finding, however, we conclude, for the reasons discussed below, that the process of evaluating qualifications for vacant positions is inherently different in one key respect from the process of evaluating qualifications for the exercise of RIF assignment rights, and that an agency may therefore justifiably reach different results.

In asserting that the appellant was not qualified under 5 CFR § 351.701 for the GS-12 position, the agency relied exclusively on subsection (a)(4) which provides that, in addition to meeting the minimum OPM standards and requirements [subsections (a)(1)-(3)], the employee must also have "the capacity[,] adaptability, and any special skills needed to satisfactorily perform the duties and responsibilities of the position without undue interruption." Thus, this section authorizes an agency to find that an otherwise qualified employee is not entitled to exercise assignment rights to an encumbered position if the displacement would result in undue interruption. When an appellant raises the issue of his assignment rights, the burden is on the agency to prove that it properly followed RIF regulations in effecting appellant's assignment.[4] Harris v. Department of the Treasury, 6 MSPB 8 (1981).

[4] Both the Board and the courts have upheld an agency's reliance on the undue interruption test in denying an employee's RIF assignment to a specific position. See, e.g., Powell v. Brannan, 196 F.2d 871 (D.C. Cir. 1952); Ciaffone v. United States, 126 Ct. Cl. 532 (1953); Shultz v. Department of the Interior, 11 MSPB 42 (1982). Compare Rasmussen v. United States, 543 F.2d 134, 211 Ct. Cl. 260 (1976) (agency did not rebut appellant's evidence that he could perform the duties of a GS-12 position without undue interruption and Board of Appeals and Review erred in accepting agency's additional evidence); Porter v. Department of Commerce, 11 MSPB 441 (1982) (presiding official did not err in finding that employee was qualified to perform position without undue interruption within 90 day period, citing Rasmussen, supra).

In this case, we find that the agency established, by preponderant evidence, that appellant's assignment to the GS-12 position would result in undue interruption of the activities associated with the position. The agency presented unrebutted evidence that: (1) substantially all the duties of the GS-12 position involved regulations governing the packaging and shipping of hazardous materials; (2) these regulations, set forth at 49 CFR Parts 171-79, comprised approximately 1,000 pages and were quite technical in terms of the commodities covered and the safety requirements set forth for the transportation of various commodities; (3) the incumbent of the GS-12 position must evaluate and act on proposed technical and procedural changes to these regulations; and (4) without a thorough working knowledge of these regulations, an individual could not properly evaluate and act on these proposals. The agency also analyzed appellant's experience and training and found that he had no experience related to the transportation of hazardous materials and had only one training course which was arguably related to one aspect of this field. On this basis the agency concluded that appellant's assignment to the GS-12 position would result in undue interruption of the ongoing regulatory activities associated with the position. The appellant contended that his prior experience involving the shipping of cargo and cargo loss prevention qualified him for the position, but in light of the agency's unrebutted evidence regarding the duties and requirements of the position, we find no error in the presiding official's finding that the appellant lacked an essential knowledge required to perform in the position without undue interruption, and was therefore not "qualified" to displace the incumbent of the position, as that term is defined for RIF purposes.

In contrast to its determination regarding the GS-12 position, the agency's evaluation of appellant's qualifications for the GS-13 vacancies did not involve any considerations of undue interruption. The filling of a vacant position does not involve the displacement of another employee; hence, there are no ongoing activities associated with a vacancy which could be subject to undue interruption. Thus, in filling a vacant position, an agency can normally expect an interruption of the activities associated with the position until the selected employee receives the orientation and training required to function at the full performance level. Consequently, because the undue interruption test is applicable to the exercise of RIF assignment rights involving displacement but not to the filling of vacancies, we find that an agency may justifiably reach different results in evaluating an employee's qualifications for these two different kinds of placement actions....

Treese v. USPS, 77 MSPR 187, 190-91 (1998), added as to relative qualifications for filling encumbered positions and vacancies during a RIF:

Section 351.702, entitled "Qualifications for assignment," provides, in pertinent part, as follows:

(a) Except as provided in § 351.703, an employee is qualified for assignment under § 351.701 if the employee:

(1) Meets the OPM [Office of Personnel Management] standards and requirements for the position, including any minimum educational requirement, and any selective placement factors established by the agency;

(2) Is physically qualified, with reasonable accommodation where appropriate, to perform the duties of the position;

(3) Meets any special qualifying condition which the OPM has approved for the position; and

(4) Has the capacity, adaptability, and special skills needed to satisfactorily perform the duties of the position without undue interruption. This determination includes recency of experience, when appropriate.

Section 351.703, entitled "Exception to qualifications," provides:

An agency may assign an employee to a vacant position under § 351.201(b) or § 351.701 of this part without regard to OPM's standards and requirements for the position if:

(a) The employee meets any minimum education requirement for the position; and

(b) The agency determines that the employee has the capacity, adaptability, and special skills needed to satisfactorily perform the duties and responsibilities of the position.
