4. Change Directed by OPM

A letter from OPM giving contingent approval of an agency plan does not prove approval without confirmation that the contingency was satisfied. Wapinski v. Dept. of Army, 28 MSPR 616, 619 (1985) (affidavit of an agency employee established, however, that the modified appraisal plan was approved by OPM); see Jones v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 105, 106 (1984) (remanding for a determination of whether pen and ink changes on a performance plan complied with OPM's mandated modifications). This analysis applies as well to plans initially approved and then modified and resubmitted to OPM. If changes are directed on matters of substance and if there is no proof that the agency made the changes, the action against the employee will not be sustained. Harris v. USDA, 29 MSPR 93, 94 (1985). If the OPM approval depends on a modification being made in the definition of the term "critical element" and if there is no evidence that the modification was made, then the agency has not established OPM approval; it is immaterial that the definition in itself was not crucial to the disposition of the facts of the case. Foss v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 535, 537 (1985) (performance action failed when the agency did not demonstrate compliance with an OPM directive that it revise its appraisal plan definition of a critical element); Nothman v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 488, 489-90 (1984) (agency failed to demonstrate that a modification required by OPM was implemented in the performance appraisal system). The operation of an unapproved system does not cure a defect causing the lack of approval; a deficient definition of "critical element" may lead to other inadequacies not readily apparent from the definition itself. Rice v. GPO, 25 MSPR 254, 256-57 (1984).

A performance appraisal plan will not support an unacceptable performance action--even if it is approved by OPM--if OPM's approval letter requires that changes of substance be made and the changes are made but not approved. Brown v. Dept. of Labor, 27 MSPR 255, 258 (1985). Under Wapinski, supra, an affidavit from a HR official is a means sufficient to provide evidence that OPM later approved the modified plan if the affidavit provides a clear and thorough history of OPM communications with the agency concerning its plan. A letter from OPM stating that agency modifications have brought a plan into compliance with law and with OPM regulations was sufficient, when there was no rebuttal evidence, to prove the system was approved. Renshaw v. Dept. of Army, 28 MSPR 638, 643 (1985). OPM approval of an appraisal system is not invalidated by agency changes in the plan not required by statute or regulation and not requiring OPM approval. Wood v. Navy, 27 MSPR 659, 662 (1985).
