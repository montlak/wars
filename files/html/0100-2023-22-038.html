broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 01: Entitlement Standards, Section E: Clearly Without Merit or Wholly Unfounded

E. CLEARLY WITHOUT MERIT OR WHOLLY UNFOUNDED

There does not have to be a finding of bad faith or ill will to justify a grant of attorney fees. Bad faith is but one fee determinant. Erdman v. Dept. of Labor, 6 MSPR 51, 53, 6 MSPB 54 (1981). Bad faith is not a necessary condition to an award that may be made if the agency decision was "clearly without merit" or "wholly unfounded," or if the agency "knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits when it brought the proceeding." Jarze v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 164, 167, 4 MSPB 247 (1980). An action clearly without merit and wholly unfounded warrants fees because there is no validity to the charges. As to the bottom line of fee entitlement, however, it is immaterial whether the appellant was innocent of the charges or whether the agency knew or should have known it would not prevail. Clark v. Dept. of Navy, 12 MSPR 428, 429 (1982) ("[t]he Board finds that the reason an award is warranted is not because of the reasons set forth in the addendum decision but rather because the action was 'clearly without merit' under 5 USC 7701(g) and 'wholly unfounded.' Therefore, whether or not appellant was innocent of the charges or the agency knew it could not prevail on the merits is irrelevant because there was no validity to the charges.").

An action need not be free from doubt to be clearly without merit; only when the standard is invoked in an employee-initiated retirement case must the Board conclude that fees are warranted because the evidence during the hearing was so compelling that reasonable minds could not differ as to the appellant's eligibility for an annuity. See Ortiz v. OPM, 34 MSPR 47, 50 (1987) (relying on Callan v. OPM, 34 MSPR 150, 154-55 (1987), post). "The agency's decision to settle a case is not evidence that the agency lacked a substantial basis for its action." Griffith v. USDA, 96 MSPR 251, 257 ¶ 17 (2004) ("The appellant has not shown that the agency presented incredible or unspecific evidence to the Board that he fully countered or that the accusations against him were unsubstantiated."). [Compare the "clearly without merit" and "wholly unfounded" criteria with the "knew or should have known" and "substantial innocence" criteria, discussed later in this chapter, "Knew or Should Have Known" and "Substantially Innocent."]

Rabago v. Dept. of Army, 28 MSPR 403, 404-05 (1985), overruled by Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 161, 166 (1991), post, decided that an action would not be clearly without merit and unfounded if the agency had a good faith belief in the soundness of its actions:

...The agency initiated the action based upon charges that appellant failed to maintain a proper work schedule.... [T]he Board reversed the removal, determining that the agency had acted improperly in removing appellant for absence from duty when appellant had obtained either sick leave, annual leave, leave without pay, or continuation of pay for all of her absences....

...

...However, the record...indicates that the agency did not act hastily in removing appellant, and acted with a reasonable belief that appellant's situation invoked an exception to the general rule that an employee cannot be disciplined on the basis of approved leave....

...

In this instance, appellant was not substantially innocent of the charge against her, i.e., excessive absence from the workplace. The record establishes that appellant had a history of excessive absences, and that the agency had a valid concern as to the effect of such absences on the agency's performance of its mission. Furthermore, as the agency had a good faith belief it was acting in accordance with the provision from the FPM, we concur with the presiding official insofar as he determined that appellant fails to establish that the agency action was clearly without merit and unfounded....

Rabago met its demise with Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 161, 165-66 (1991), concluding that the result of the action, not its motivation, controls:

The agency's argument that attorney fees are not warranted because it had a reasonable belief in the correctness of its action is not a valid bar to the award of attorney fees under Allen category 2, which includes the subcategories of "clearly without merit," "wholly unfounded," and "substantially innocent." See Allen v. United States Postal Service, 2 MSPB 582, 2 MSPR at 434. In support of its argument, the agency relies on the Board's decision in Rabago v. Department of the Army, 28 MSPR 403, 405-06 (1985), which holds that a showing by an agency that it had a good faith belief in the correctness of its action bars an appellant's entitlement to an award of attorney fees under the "clearly without merit" and "wholly unfounded" standards of Allen category 2. The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has specifically held, however, that Allen category 2 applies to the result before the Board, not to the evidence and information available prior to the hearing. See Yorkshire v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 746 F.2d 1454, 1457 (Fed. Cir. 1984). We find the "reasonable belief" holding in Rabago to be inconsistent with the Federal Circuit's holding in Yorkshire. Therefore, Rabago is hereby overruled.

Morrison v. NSF, 423 F.3d 1366 (Fed. Cir. 2005), reviewing an arbitration award resulting from an indefinite suspension, cited Yorkshire v. MSPB, 746 F.2d 1454, 1457 (Fed. Cir. 1984), for the proposition that "[w]hether an award of fees in the interest of justice under the second Allen factor must be determined by examining the arbitrator's decision, not the evidence and information available to the arbitrator. See AFGE Local 3599 v. EEOC, 920 F.3d 794, 798 (Fed. Cir. 2019) ("The court [in Morrison] did not hold that the arbitrator's decision as to the fee award is limited to those factual findings and conclusions set forth in the opinion on the merits; the only prohibition set out by the court was that the arbitrator may not 'revise the merits decision,' i.e., make findings contrary to the merits decision, in determining whether to award fees.").
