b. Process Due to Employee

In Vargo v. USPS, 49 MSPR 284, 287 (1991), although the Board set aside a period of enforced leave, it explained that the action was procedurally invalid for lack of constitutional rather than regulatory protections, an approach that followed the Board's earlier change in direction concerning procedurally defective probationary terminations:

In Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 MSPR 672 (1991), the Board modified its prior decisions reversing agency actions for "harmful error" based solely on an agency's failure to afford employees their statutory or regulatory procedural rights. The Board held that, when an appealable action against a nonprobationary Federal employee has not been effected in accordance with the minimum procedures that satisfy the constitutional requirements of due process of law under Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532, 546 (1985), the action will be reversed because it cannot withstand constitutional scrutiny, rather than because the action constitutes harmful error. In this case, the agency did not provide any of the procedural protections of 5 USC 7513(b). Nor did it provide the appellant with any other notice or opportunity to be heard. Thus, the Board finds that the agency action must be set aside.

For constitutional due process to be provided to an individual placed on enforced leave, the process must be offered in a timely manner. It will not do to provide the appellant "advance" notice of the leave at the same time the agency initiates the leave and then issue the "decision" on the leave proposal after the appellant returns to work, Guillory v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 244, 250 (1991):

...At that point, it would have been necessary for the agency to "undo" a completed personnel action in order to render a decision in the appellant's favor. We find that the statutory purpose of the absolute right to prior notice and reply, i.e., an opportunity to persuade the agency not to take the action, is clearly not served by such procedures as were used in this case....

If constitutional prerequisites are met, but regulatory or statutory provisions are violated during removal proceedings, the action is reversible under the harmful error standard if the record shows that the error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a different result had there been no error. See Marr v. USPS, 49 MSPR 196, 202 (1991). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Errors in Proposal Notice and Reply Process," concerning harmful error and constitutional due process in the notice and decision processes.]
