a. Properly Ordered

The fitness-for-duty examination is a useful procedure to determine an employee's competency to perform his duties. Failure to submit to the examination, if there are good reasons for directing it, is insubordination justifying discharge or other discipline. Risner v. FAA, 7 MSPR 480, 486, 7 MSPB 342 (1981), aff'd, Risner v. FAA, 677 F.2d 36 (8th Cir. 1982); Thompson v. Dept. of Treasury, 16 MSPR 294, 298 (1983) (as to a properly ordered fitness exam: "[t]he agency could properly discipline appellant for her failure to appear for the fitness-for-duty examination"); Pellum v. VA, 12 MSPR 208, 212 (1982) (removal for a prolonged absence and the failure to take four requested medical or fitness exams to determine the appellant's physical ability to perform her duties).

Failure to obey an agency order to report for a mental fitness-for-duty examination, even on the advice of a union representative or counsel, is improper and a basis for discipline unless it would clearly place the employee in a dangerous situation; the employee may take whatever steps he thinks appropriate to challenge the ultimate validity of the order. Davis v. Smithsonian Inst., 14 MSPR 397, 399-400 (1983) (removal of a guard who held a sensitive position and who was held to a "high standard of conduct"; "appellant was the individual who placed his mental condition at issue by claiming that his actions regarding the theft charge should be mitigated because he was suffering from mental problems at the time"); see Tracy v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 115, 117 (1984) (agency denied a grievance challenging a directive to take a fitness exam; the Board declined to credit the appellant's allegation that he was misled by his representative to believe the grievance was valid).

Sellman v. USPS, 63 MSPR 145, 152 (1994), noted that Postal Service Employee and Labor Relations Manual provisions permit the use of fitness exams required in determining whether an employee is able to perform the duties of the position because of medical reasons. Managers can order the examinations "at any time" and "repeat as necessary to safeguard the employee or coworker"; "a specific test or consultation may be required in the judgment of the examining medical officer." Although the agency may neither force an employee to respond to its requests nor force an employee to take a fitness-for-duty examination, the agency may consider an employee's intentional failure to take the examination as grounds for disciplinary action. Daniel v. USPS, 16 MSPR 486, 490 (1983) (examination was authorized under § 343.34 of the USPS Employee and Labor Relations Manual; the agency could not place an employee into duty status until he took the fitness exam; the resulting absence interfered with service efficiency); see Fuentes v. USPS, 54 MSPR 4, 8 (1992) (employee should have obeyed an order to take a fitness exam and then grieved; also, the agency was not required to follow the procedures at § 568 of its Employee Labor Relations Manual when it directed the appellant to attend a fitness exam to verify his claim of a work-related injury).

The right of an agency to direct a fitness exam for an employee who had received OWCP benefits was considered in Abatecola v. VA, 29 MSPR 601 (1986), a decision notable for its review of revisions to the fitness exam regulations. The revised regulation governing the fitness exam then provided, at 5 CFR 339.301(b) (1986):

An agency may require an employee receiving workers compensation benefits or assigned to limited duties as result of an on-the-job injury to report for medical evaluation when the agency has identified an assignment or position (including the employee's regular position) which it reasonably believes the employee can perform consistent with the medical limitations of his/her condition. If the medical information (consistent with generally accepted medical principles and practice) indicates that the employee is capable of performing the duties identified, the agency will promptly return the employee to corresponding duty and pay status.

The Board noted of the regulation, effective in 1984, that OPM restricted the authority of agencies to order fitness exams. The Federal Register notice accompanying the regulation stated (49 Fed. Reg. 1321), 29 MSPR at 605 n.2:

These regulations were necessary (and, indeed, have been under development for several years) because previous authorities to obtain and use medical information, particularly in so-called "fitness-for-duty" situations were unclear; and there was substantial evidence that those authorities had been abused, especially in the case of psychiatric examinations and agency-filed disability retirements.... Agency authority to order medical examinations has been significantly limited in recognition that, except in certain limited circumstances, the burden of proof is on the employee and, if he/she fails to provide adequate evidence for the agency to conclude that a medical condition exists which needs to be taken into account, the agency is free to proceed with a decision (adverse action, denial of request) having fully met any procedural obligation related to the health status question.

After Abatecola clarified the regulatory basis for fitness exams, the decision addressed the central point: whether an OWCP finding that the employee was suffering from a compensable injury precluded the agency from using a fitness exam to reach a conclusion that the employee was fit to work. The Board accepted a comment by OPM at 49 Fed. Reg. 1323 (1984), providing guidance on the new regulation, that an agency could require a fitness exam to assess whether an employee receiving compensation benefits can be assigned to a position consistent with the limitations of his or her condition. The exam, according to the OPM guidance, could be directed when the agency had reason to believe that an employee receiving compensation benefits may be capable of performing the duties of his original position, a modified or restructured job, or a different position. The regulation, then 5 CFR 339.301(b), "clearly authorizes the agency to order an examination even if the employee is receiving OWCP compensation at the time the agency orders the examination." Abatecola, id. at 607. Having concluded that the fitness exam was properly ordered and that the appellant improperly failed to attend it, the Board upheld the dismissal.

Further regulatory changes in fitness examination regulations were noted, Fogg v. Ashcroft, 254 F.3d 103, 111 (D.C. Cir. 2001):

Under regulations implemented in 1984, the OPM did in fact require an agency to "identif[y] an assignment or position...which it reasonably believes the employee can perform" before ordering a medical exam for an employee receiving worker's compensation for an on-the-job injury. 5 CFR § 339.301(b) (as quoted in Medical Determinations Related to Employability, 49 Fed. Reg. 1321, 1329 (Jan. 11, 1984)). But the version of the regulation applicable to Fogg's case (and currently in effect) makes no mention of this requirement: An agency may require an employee who, has applied for or is receiving continuation of pay or compensation as a result of an on-the-job injury or disease to report for an examination to determine medical limitations that may affect placement decisions." 5 CFR § 339.301(c).

Sweeney v. DHS, 248 Fed. Appx. 179, 181 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), determined that the Board correctly sustained discharge of a law enforcement employee whose fitness for duty examination was ordered and who refused to supply a release for medical records:

The Board found that Mr. Sweeney's misconduct warranted termination. Mr. Sweeney maintains that the direct order to sign the medical release form violates 45 C.F.R. § 164.508, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), and 5 U.S.C. § 552(a), the Privacy Act. With respect to Mr. Sweeney's HIPAA claim, HIPAA does not provide a private cause of action and clearly authorizes employers to require disclosure of medical records in connection with a fitness exam. 45 C.F.R. § 164.512 (b)(1)(v). With respect to the Privacy Act claim, Mr. Sweeney even admitted that the Department of Homeland Security had the authority to order him to undergo the fitness exam.

See Smith v. DOD, DC-0752-13-0065-I-1 (NP 5/16/2014) (removal when the appellant did not appear for fitness examinations to determine if the appellant could meet the lifting and other requirements of his position); cf. Savage v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-11-0634-B-1 (NP 10/31/2022) ("The Board has held that an agency may offer, rather than order, a medical examination, including a psychiatric evaluation, in any situation where the agency needs additional medical documentation to make an informed management decision. Doe v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, 117 M.S.P.R. 579, ¶ 27 (2012); 5 C.F.R. § 339.302.").
