9. Misuse of Dispute Resolution Procedure

Employees do not have unfettered discretion to disobey or ignore agency orders that may later be found to have violated a collective bargaining agreement or that are later found to have violated law. Almost all employees have available to them either an administrative (agency) grievance procedure or a grievance system under a collective bargaining contract. Redress for an improper order or for a contract violation is through the grievance procedure. McPartland v. FAA, 14 MSPR 506, 511 (1983), aff'd, 795 F.2d 1017 (Fed. Cir. 1986); see Kays v. FAA, 15 MSPR 62, 66 n.7 (1983) ("As we also noted in McPartland, supra, at 7, employees do not have discretion to disobey agency orders they feel are improper. Rather, they must seek redress pursuant to contractual grievance procedures which are provided, in this case, in Article 7 of the bargaining agreement.").

Recognizing the availability of redress, it should not become a full-time occupation. Boyle v. United States, 515 F.2d 1397, 1401-02 (Ct. Cl. 1975), observed that:

[T]he prime duty and foremost obligation of any employee is to exert effort and energy in the accomplishment of assigned tasks. It is not too much to ask to require a person to function in job he or she was hired to do. Those in the working force certainly have a legitimate interest in seeking to better their working conditions, and to that end an employee has a right to express his dissatisfactions to those in positions of higher authority. But he is assuredly not free to simply drop assigned work in order to protest management's policies; nor is an employee permitted to devote all of his labor--at the expense of his normal duties--to convince superiors that his approach to management techniques is more enlightened than theirs. This court has admonished Government employees in the past they may not refuse to do work merely because of disagreements with management, and that if they fail to perform their duties, they do so at the risk of being insubordinate. See Burton v. United States, 186 Ct. Cl. 172, 404 F.2d 365 (1968), cert. denied, 394 U.S. 1002 (1969).
