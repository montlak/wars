f. Allegation of Discrimination

For a short time the Board was required to adjudicate a discrimination claim even if it lacked jurisdiction over the underlying appeal. Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 906 F.2d 689 (Fed. Cir. 1990). The panel decision in Cruz was vacated; the Federal Circuit en banc determined that the Board is not required to decide an EEO allegation unless there is an action within its appellate jurisdiction. Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 934 F.2d 1240 (Fed. Cir. 1991). The Board observed in Price v. USPS, 50 MSPR 107, 110 (1991), that it may still make an initial determination on a claim of prohibited discrimination if that claim is asserted as the sole basis of an involuntary action and Board jurisdiction. But if the Board lacks jurisdiction over an appellant's constructive adverse action appeal, it lacks jurisdiction to review an appellant's discrimination or reprisal claims pertaining to the appeal. See Carlisle v. DOD, 93 MSPR 280, 284 ¶ 9 (2003) (declining to consider a claim of disability discrimination when the appellant's period of leave was voluntary). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Jurisdictional Showing" for consideration of Cruz, also discussed in Chapter 12.]

Vazquez v. MSPB, 296 Fed. Appx. 20 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP), noted in its discussion of discrimination or similar claims in constructive adverse action appeal:

...The Board has no jurisdiction to consider discrimination, perjury, procedural misconduct, whistleblowing or reprisal allegations unaccompanied by an appealable adverse action over which the Board has jurisdiction. Garcia, 437 F.3d at 1342-43; Cruz v. Dep't of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1245-46 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc); see also, Mintzmyer v. Dep't. of Interior, 84 F.3d 419, 423 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (a district court's finding that alleged discrimination did not result in a constructive discharge barred litigating whether an alleged reprisal for whistleblowing constituted constructive discharge). To the extent that Mr. Vazquez's references to reprisal constitute an effort to prosecute an individual right of action appeal for whistleblowing reprisal, he has failed to exhaust his administrative remedies, as the AJ pointed out.

Vasquez and the decisions it relied on addressed collateral claims of discrimination; it did not directly address the assertion that discrimination caused, and was not a byproduct of, an involuntary adverse action. Cruz v. Dept. of Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1245-46 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc) (Judges Skelton and Newman dissenting), involved an employee who resigned after receiving and replying to a proposed removal but before a decision was made on the proposal. In the ensuing appeal Cruz raised, among other contentions, an allegation of Title VII reprisal, but, after hearing, the Board dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. On review, the court affirmed the Board's conclusion that it lacked jurisdiction. The court determined that the reprisal charge was invalid because the agency had ample basis for proposing the removal of Mr. Cruz. The court merged analysis of the reprisal claim with the analysis of the strength of the agency evidence supporting the removal proposal and concluded not that Cruz's claim failed on the merits--and should have been denied on that basis, with notice of further appeal rights to EEOC or to court--but that the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appeal for want of sufficient evidence of involuntariness:

It must be remembered that the asserted act of reprisal here is the Navy's bringing of the charges. The sole issue here is the Board's jurisdiction, and that issue resolves into whether Cruz' resignation was voluntary or involuntary because obtained by coercion, deception or the like. All evidence of involuntariness must be considered, but the reprisal assertion, if the Board had independent jurisdiction over it, would be subsumed by the findings of no coercion, or deception, or other improper agency act. Though we obviously do not here decide whether reprisal may or may not have been among the motives of Cruz' supervisors, we cannot hold that the bringing of numerous, valid, serious and sustainable charges of conduct that destroys the efficiency of the service constitutes coercion to resign. To so hold would be contrary to precedent and to common sense. This court has repeatedly held that the imminence of a less desirable alternative does not render involuntary the choice made. Sammt v. United States, 780 F.2d 31, 32 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Schultz v. United States Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Griessenauer v. Department of Energy, 754 F.2d 361, 364 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Covington v. Department of Health and Human Services, 750 F.2d 937, 942 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (dicta); Taylor v. United States, 591 F.2d 688, 692, 219 Ct. Cl. 86 (1979); Christie v. United States, 518 F.2d 584, 587-88, 207 Ct. Cl. 333 (1975).

Cruz' primary assertion in his appeal here is that the Board should have decided his reprisal claim and notified him of a right to appeal that decision to the EEOC or a district court because the agency's January 29, 1988 letter was in reprisal for filing his 1987 EEOC complaint. But that mere assertion does not provide a basis for Board jurisdiction in this voluntary resignation case. First, a petitioner's mere assertion cannot create jurisdiction. Second, as above indicated, bringing charges, for whatever reason, is not in itself an agency action appealable to the Board. Nor are discriminatory acts in themselves agency actions appealable to the Board. A statute, 5 U.S.C. Sec. 2302(b), makes reprisal a prohibited personnel practice, but that statute is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction. Wren v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982).

Nor would a source of jurisdiction be created by accepting, arguendo, Cruz' label for his reprisal assertion: "Title VII claim of discrimination." The Board has not been granted jurisdiction over Title VII claims per se, i.e., over "pure" or "naked" Title VII claims unaccompanied by an appealable action over which the Board does have jurisdiction. Cruz on this appeal attempts to convert his attempted appeal under Title 5, U.S.C. to a suit for discrimination under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act. As above indicated, when discrimination is asserted in an appeal from a removal or other appealable adverse agency action over which the Board has jurisdiction, the case is a "mixed case" and the Board must decide the merits of the appealable action and whether it was a discriminatory act. 5 U.S.C. Sec. 7702. In such cases, the Board routinely notifies appellants of a right to EEOC review. Where, as here, it has been found that there never was an appealable adverse agency action, the case is not a "mixed case," Sec. 7702 is not involved, and the Board correctly determined that it was without jurisdiction to consider Cruz' claim of procedural error or his reprisal assertion, or any other claim beyond the Board's jurisdiction. That holding and our affirmance break no new ground. See Manning v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 742 F.2d 1424 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (jurisdiction lacking over reassignment, no jurisdiction to consider allegation reassignment was in reprisal for exercise of First Amendment rights); Synan v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 765 F.2d 1099, 1101 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (rejecting reliance on Sec. 7702 because suspension was not an appealable action and refusing to transfer discrimination claims to district court where Board lacked jurisdiction over suspension of 14 days or less: "Reliance on Sec. 7702(a)(1) is of no help to petitioner either, because the discrimination-based action must be one that is appealable."); Sammt v. United States, 780 F.2d 31 (Fed. Cir. 1985); and Wren, supra. For the same reason, the Board correctly declined to refer to any right of EEOC review and Cruz' motion for transfer of this case to a district court must be denied.

The dissent in Cruz argued that the claim of involuntary resignation was a substantive claim requiring due process rights of appeal, hearing an decision, including a determination on the reprisal claim.

Mintzmyer v. Dept. of Interior, 84 F.3d 419 (Fed. Cir. 1996), involved the appeal of a manager who, following an undesired reassignment, filed an EEO complaint asserting age and sex discrimination, as well as whistleblowing reprisal complaint with OSC. Ms. Mintzmyer retired, amended her EEO complaint, and appealed the retirement to the Board as constructively adverse. The court's discussion dealt with the application of collateral estoppel to some court litigation that the appellant pursued relating to her employment claims. Deciding that collateral estoppel was properly applied by the Board, the jurisdictional dismissal by the Board was affirmed by the court with almost no analysis of the allegations constructive adverse action appeal.

Garcia v. DHS, 437 F.3d 1322, 1341 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (en banc) (dissent by Judges Dyk and Newman), involving a demotion claimed by Ms. Garcia to be constructively adverse because the agency did not properly accommodate her disability in the job she held prior to the job she assumed through a geographical reassignment and downgrading. The court examined Cruz and decisions postdating Cruz that analyzed Board threshold jurisdictional requirements. The court reaffirmed the approach of Cruz, but added clarification. The court recognized that evidence of discrimination may lend support to the question of whether an action was involuntary. If the evidence does not demonstrate involuntariness, the case is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. If the evidence, including proof of discrimination or reprisal, demonstrates that the action was involuntary, the Board has jurisdiction over the constructive adverse action case and it has both the jurisdiction to decide claims under the EEO laws and the obligation to provide notice of further appeal rights:

...Furthermore, in mixed constructive adverse action cases, there are additional differences between the jurisdictional issues and the merits of the discrimination claim. As stated by the administrative judge in this case,

evidence of discrimination goes to the ultimate question of coercion, i.e., whether under all of the circumstances working conditions were made so difficult by the agency, that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to request a reduction in grade. Once the Board's jurisdiction has been established in a constructive demotion appeal, issues of discrimination and reprisal may be adjudicated under the standards applicable for proof of discrimination and/or retaliation under Title VII as the case is then a mixed case.

Initial Decision, slip op. at 3 (citing Markon v. Dep't of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 578 (1996)). Thus, in a mixed constructive adverse action case, jurisdiction is determined by 5 U.S.C. § 7512 and the legal standards applicable to establishing involuntariness. Discrimination issues may be considered insofar as they illuminate involuntariness. In contrast, the merits of the discrimination claim are determined by the applicable anti-discrimination statute.

In these cases, undoubtedly, facts relevant to jurisdiction may also be relevant when deciding the merits. We fail to see, however, how this circumstance makes the current approach illogical. As a practical matter, there need not be any duplicative effort. If a fact determined for jurisdiction is later relevant for the merits determination, there is no reason why that previous finding cannot easily be applied to the merits determination. See also Markon, 71 M.S.P.R. at 580 (noting that "if the appellant establishes that she has been constructively discharged, much of the same evidence of discrimination or retaliation relied upon to establish coercion may also be relevant to proof of discrimination and retaliation under Title VII and/or the WPA"). Importantly, as noted, in mixed cases the discrimination claim itself is adjudicated using the elements, burdens, and defenses specific to the underlying discrimination statute.

The Board's views concerning the place of discrimination allegations in constructive adverse action appeals were summarized in Markon v. Dept. of State, 71 MSPR 574, 578-80 (1996), cited in Garcia, essentially permitting consideration of EEO allegations to show the involuntariness of an action, but not imposing upon constructive adverse action litigation the standards of proof governing Title VII hostile environment discrimination and reprisal cases. Markon, an appeal from a constructive retirement, determined that the analysis is "whether under all of the circumstances working conditions were made so difficult by the agency, that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to" resign or retire:

[W]hen allegations of discrimination and reprisal are alleged in connection with a determination of voluntariness, it is well settled that such allegations may only be addressed insofar as they relate to the issue of voluntariness. That is, evidence of discrimination may be considered only in terms of the standard for voluntariness in a particular situation--not whether such evidence meets the test for proof of discrimination or reprisal established under Title VII. Thus, evidence of discrimination or EEO retaliation goes to the ultimate question of coercion--whether under all of the circumstances working conditions were made so difficult by the agency, that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign [or retire]." ...Burke, 53 MSPR at 438-39. This is so because the Board has no jurisdiction over presumed voluntary actions unless and until they are found to be involuntary.

The Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit's en banc decision in Cruz v. Department of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240 (Fed. Cir. 1991), where Cruz had resigned in the face of a threatened removal and alleged, inter alia, that the proposed removal was based on reprisal for his EEO complaint filings, definitively resolved the issue of whether the Board has independent jurisdiction over claims of discrimination and retaliation, and resolved it in the negative. The court noted that 5 USC § 2302(b), involving prohibited personnel practices, including discrimination, "is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction." See Cruz, 934 F.2d at 1245 (citing Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 MSPB 174, 2 MSPR 1, 2 (1980), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982)). Nor has the Board been granted jurisdiction over Title VII discrimination claims, "per se." Id. Because the Board may initially consider only the issue of jurisdiction, this case is not a mixed one, under 5 USC § 7702, involving discrimination as an affirmative defense.[2] See id. at 1246. See also Fair v. Department of the Navy, 66 MSPR 485, 488 (1995) (an allegation of discrimination may not bring an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction; "[a]lthough the Board is authorized to adjudicate a claim of discrimination of the sort listed in 5 USC § 2302(b)(1) where the claim is interposed as a defense to an appealable action, see 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(B), section 2302(b) is not a source of Board jurisdiction.").

[2] The court did not overrule Durden v. Department of the Navy, 18 MSPR 373 (1983). That case involved an appellant who alleged that he was forced to retire on disability when the agency threatened him with removal for inability to perform. The EEOC had found that the appellant was discriminated against on the basis of handicap; the Board found that no arguable basis for discharge existed, that the appellant's retirement was involuntary, and that he had thus been constructively discharged. The Board gave the appellant mixed judicial review rights, noting that although it was not the usual mixed case where the issue of discrimination is raised in connection with an otherwise appealable action, discrimination must be analyzed in order to determine whether the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal. See Durden, 18 MSPR at 374 & n.1. Among other things, the court in Cruz distinguished Durden because the Board had said "it was a narrow exception since handicap discrimination was the sole reason for the threatened discharge." See Cruz, 934 F.2d at 1247. We note, however, that the Board has since overruled Durden to the extent that it is incompatible with Cruz; that is, to the extent that Durden suggested that actions not within the Board's jurisdiction may nevertheless be treated as mixed cases for appeal rights purposes. See Price v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 107, 110 (1991), involving an alleged involuntary retirement over which the Board declined jurisdiction. The Board opined, however, that under Cruz, it "may still make an initial determination on a claim of prohibited discrimination where, unlike here, that claim is asserted as the sole cause of an involuntary action." See id. Because Durden involved a unique circumstance, we need not reach the issue of the narrow exception to Cruz. However, our sequential analysis of the discrimination issue, that evidence of discrimination may be considered only for the purpose of determining jurisdiction and for Title VII purposes only if the Board has jurisdiction, might well dictate a different analysis in Durden, if not a different result.

The Board has followed the Cruz analysis in many cases involving an allegation of discrimination and/or reprisal in connection with jurisdiction over a constructive discharge and has found that allegations of discrimination and reprisal must initially be considered only as relevant to the test for coercion and, thus, as relevant to the question of the Board's jurisdiction. See Swift v. U.S. Postal Service, 61 MSPR 29, 32 (1994) ("where an employee's working conditions are rendered so intolerable by alleged discrimination or harassment that he is 'forced' into resignation, there has been a constructive discharge and the employee's resignation is involuntary...the ultimate question to be decided in this regard is whether working conditions were so difficult that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign.") (Citations omitted.) See also Taylor v. Environmental Protection Agency, 61 MSPR 188 (1994); Hurwitz v. Department of the Army, 61 MSPR 436 (1994); Tripp v. Department of the Air Force, 59 MSPR 458, 461 (1993) (involving allegations of reprisal for whistleblowing and citing to, inter alia, Burke, 53 MSPR at 438; Day v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 50 MSPR 680, 684 (1991), aff'd, 975 F.2d 870 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table)).

Once it is established that the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal because there has been a constructive discharge, issues of discrimination and reprisal may be adjudicated under the standards applicable for proof of discrimination and/or retaliation under Title VII as the case is then a mixed case. See Cartwright v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 54 MSPR 338 (1992); Price v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 107, 110 (1991).

Thus, the fact that the agency in the instant case, while adjudicating the appellant's formal complaint of age discrimination, found that evidence of age discrimination supported a prima facie case of such discrimination, is, in-and-of-itself, not relevant to the appellant's burden of proof on the voluntariness issue. Rather, for the reasons described above, the administrative judge must consider any evidence of discrimination, retaliation for EEO activities, and retaliation for whistleblowing, in light of the coercion test for involuntariness. The administrative judge must consider any such evidence of discrimination and retaliation, together with all other evidence concerning incidents of harassment, or allegations of unfavorable treatment, or any other allegations regarding coercion. Only if a constructive discharge is established based upon consideration of all such evidence, need she reach the issue of discrimination and/or retaliation under Title VII or the Whistleblower Protection Act. Accordingly, we decline to overrule Burke and other similar cases, as the appellant urges us to do.

Markon was summarized by Neice v. DHS, 105 MSPR 211, 216-17 ¶¶ 8-9, 2007 MSPB 85 (2007) (affirming dismissal for lack of jurisdiction following remand, King v. VA, 296 Fed. Appx. 40 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)), also involving an alleged hostile environment leading to resignation:

Where, as here, an appellant claims that the agency intentionally made working conditions so intolerable that he was driven to an involuntary resignation, the Board will determine whether, under all the circumstances, the agency made working conditions so difficult that a reasonable person in the appellant's position would have felt compelled to resign. See id. In making such a determination, evidence of discrimination or retaliation may only be considered insofar as it relates to the issue of voluntariness. Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 578-80 (1996). Thus, the Board will consider any evidence of discrimination or retaliation together with all other evidence bearing on the voluntariness of the resignation, but it does not need to determine whether discrimination or retaliation has been established under the standards of Title VII or the Whistleblower Protection Act, unless and until it is established that the appellant was constructively discharged. Id. at 580; see Garcia, 437 F.3d at 1341.

As explained in the initial decision, the appellant failed to establish by preponderant evidence that he was forced to resign because of intolerable working conditions. The appellant contends that his decision to resign was based on a series of retaliatory and harassing actions by the agency beginning in October 2003. The "last straw" appears to have been his receipt, on February 1, 2005, of the decision on his first-step grievance of his "unsatisfactory" performance rating for 2004. The appellant submitted his letter of resignation two days later on February 3, 2005. Although the appellant was clearly dissatisfied with his performance rating and the first-step official's decision on his grievance, we find no reason to disturb the administrative judge's explained finding that a reasonable person would not have felt compelled to resign under the circumstances. See Miller v. Department of Defense, 85 M.S.P.R. 310, ¶ 32 (2000) ("An employee is not guaranteed a working environment free of stress. Dissatisfaction with work assignments, a feeling of being unfairly criticized, or difficult or unpleasant working conditions are generally not so intolerable as to compel a reasonable person to resign.").

Anderson v. VA, CH-0752-10-0288-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010), reiterated Markon:

When an appellant raises allegations of discrimination in connection with an involuntariness claim, evidence of discrimination may be considered only in terms of the standard for voluntariness. Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 578 (1996). Thus, in an involuntary retirement or resignation appeal, evidence of discrimination goes to the ultimate question of coercion, i.e., whether under all of the circumstances working conditions were made so difficult by the agency, that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign or retire. Id.

Gruspe v. MSPB, 324 Fed. Appx. 917, 919-20 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), similarly noted that discrimination and related hostile environment arguments are relevant only to establishing an involuntary resignation:

The Board lacks jurisdiction to review voluntary resignations. It may evaluate a resignation only if the employee can show that her resignation was actually involuntary. The employee must show that (1) the agency effectively imposed the terms of her resignation, (2) the employee had no realistic alternative, and (3) the resignation resulted from the agency's improper acts. Garcia v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., 437 F.3d 1322, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2006); see also Staats v. U.S. Postal Serv., 99 F.3d 1120, 1124 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (noting that an "unpleasant" or "unattractive" choice does not make resignation involuntary). The test for voluntariness is objective, under the totality of the circumstances. Garcia, 437 F.3d at 1329. Resignations are presumed voluntary, and the employee must overcome this presumption by a preponderance of the evidence. Id.

Gruspe argues that the Board incorrectly weighed the evidence in deciding that her resignation was voluntary. She contends that the administrative judge failed to consider evidence of discrimination, and "in no case considered whether the supervisors [sic] conduct exhibited toward Gruspe was similar in nature to conduct exhibited toward other employees." Additionally, Gruspe claims that the administrative judge failed to discuss the fact that she submitted an Equal Employment Opportunity report against Joe on January 7, 2004, which might have motivated Joe to retaliate.

While the Board may review "mixed case" claims of discrimination combined with improper separation, it must have jurisdiction over the non-discrimination claim. Garcia, 437 F.3d at 1324-25. In Gruspe's case, this requires a finding of involuntary resignation before the Board can review her discrimination claims. Thus, in this appeal, her discrimination arguments are relevant only to establishing an involuntary resignation. See Cruz v. Dep't of Navy, 934 F.2d 1240, 1245-46 (Fed. Cir. 1991).

The Board properly considered Gruspe's arguments in the context of her resignation. In her appeal to the Board, Gruspe did not present evidence of discrimination on the basis of race, national origin, or other protected categories. Rather, in her prehearing statement to the Board, she alleged discrimination in the way her supervisor, Joe, treated her as compared to her co-workers. Gruspe alleges, for example, that Joe "excessively monitored" her more than her colleagues. However, the administrative judge found no discriminatory treatment, crediting testimony from co-workers that they remembered no such excessive monitoring. Gruspe contends that the administrative judge did not consider her report of January 7, 2004. Although the administrative judge did not specifically discuss that report, he did consider the circumstances noted in the report, and discussed Gruspe's meetings with an Equal Employment Counselor and a February 19, 2004 mediation session with Gruspe, Joe, and the Counselor....

The scope of evidence of discrimination as a component of a constructive adverse action appeal is not limited to allegations considered in an EEO investigation, Bullock v. Dept. of Treasury, PH-0752-14-0665-I-1 (NP 8/6/2015):

The permissible scope of the Board's review of an appellant's discrimination claims on the involuntary separation question is not confined solely to the specific allegations the agency accepted for investigation; rather, it may extend to any discrimination similar or related to the substance of the allegations in the claim and which reasonably can be expected to grow out of the investigation triggered by the claim. Williams v. Department of Agriculture, 106 M.S.P.R. 677, ¶ 12 (2007).

Cf. Carter v. SSA, NY-0752-16-0120-I-1 (NP 10/6/2022) ("The Board addresses allegations of discrimination and retaliation in connection with an alleged involuntary retirement only insofar as those allegations relate to the issue of voluntariness. Brown v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶ 10, aff'd, 469 F. App'x 852 (Fed. Cir. 2011)."); Spearman v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-12-0306-I-1 n.4 (NP 11/1/2012) ("Because the Board has no jurisdiction over this alleged involuntary resignation appeal, the Board has no independent jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's disability discrimination allegation. See Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 580 (1996); Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982)."); Jones v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-11-0468-I-1 n.4 (NP 2/23/2012) ("Because the Board has no jurisdiction over this alleged involuntary retirement appeal, the Board has no independent jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's discrimination and retaliation claims. See Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 580 (1996); Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982)."); Fahrenbacher v. VA, 89 MSPR 260, 266 (2001) ("[I]f the appellant establishes that he was presented with a choice between continuing to work or accepting disability retirement, the administrative judge shall permit the appellant to present evidence concerning his discrimination and reprisal claims, including his USERRA claim, for the purpose of establishing that his choice to accept disability retirement was involuntary.").

Collins v. USPS, 100 MSPR 332, 338-39 ¶¶ 15-17 (2005), noted that when the appellant had not demonstrated that his retirement was caused by disability discrimination, the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appeal and, without jurisdiction over the appeal, could not consider other allegations of discrimination:

As noted above, the administrative judge considered the merits of the appellant's discrimination and reprisal claims despite her finding that the appellant's retirement was voluntary. At the time she did so, our reviewing court, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, had issued decisions that could be interpreted as consistent with this approach. In Dick v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 290 F.3d 1356, 1362-63 (Fed. Cir. 2002), a case in which the employee claimed that his agency coerced him into accepting a demotion in reprisal for disclosures he had made, the court held that the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal if the employee made nonfrivolous allegations that his separation was involuntary; and in Dorrall v. Department of the Army, 301 F.3d 1375, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2002), a case in which the employee claimed that his retirement was coerced, the court held that the Board had jurisdiction over such an appeal if the employee made nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction supported by affidavits or other evidence. See also Walley v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 279 F.3d 1010, 1019 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (Board jurisdiction over an appeal in which the employee alleged that the agency had denied her restoration rights was established by a nonfrivolous claim that she had been separated because of a compensable injury). The same court, however, had previously held in Cruz v. Department of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc), that Board jurisdiction over an allegedly involuntary resignation was established only upon proof that the resignation was a constructive removal, i.e., involuntary, and not by the mere assertion of a nonfrivolous claim that the separation was involuntary.

Shortly after the initial decision in this case was issued, the Board issued its decision in Lloyd v. Small Business Administration, 96 M.S.P.R. 518, 522 (2004). In that decision, the Board found that the holdings in Dick and Dorrall that are described above could not be reconciled with the holding in Cruz; it noted that en banc holdings were the controlling law of the Federal Circuit unless and until they were overruled by the court sitting en banc; it noted further that Cruz was an en banc decision, while Dick and Dorrall were not; and it concluded, therefore, that it was bound by the holding in Cruz. Lloyd, 96 M.S.P.R. at 523-25; Bartels v. U.S. Postal Service, 98 M.S.P.R. 280 (2005).

Consistent with Lloyd and Bartels, we find that the appellant's claim that his retirement was involuntary--whether that claim constitutes a nonfrivolous claim of jurisdiction or not--is insufficient to confer on the Board jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's appeal. Because the appellant has not established by preponderant evidence that his retirement was in fact involuntary, he has not established that the Board has appellate jurisdiction over his separation; and, in the absence of such jurisdiction, the Board lacks the authority to determine whether the evidence of discrimination and reprisal that has been presented here is sufficient to prove that the agency discriminated against, or took reprisal against, the appellant in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act. See Cruz, 934 F.2d at 1248. The administrative judge's findings that the appellant failed to substantiate his claims of discrimination and reprisal are therefore VACATED.[3]

[3] The administrative judge did not err in considering evidence concerning those claims to the extent that the evidence related to the question of whether the appellant's separation was voluntary. See Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 578 (1996) (when an appellant raises allegations of discrimination and reprisal in connection with a claim that the personnel action at issue in the appellant's appeal was involuntary, evidence concerning the allegations of discrimination and reprisal may be considered insofar as they relate to the issue of voluntariness).

If the appellant is found to have been constructively removed by reason of misleading advice concerning entitlement, or loss of entitlement, to retirement benefits, the Board adjudicates an associated disability discrimination claim, Aldridge v. USDA, 111 MSPR 670, 680-81 ¶¶ 21-23, 2009 MSPB 146 (2009):

Where an appellant raises allegations of discrimination in an appeal of an alleged involuntary retirement or resignation, the evidence on discrimination goes first to the question of whether the action was voluntary and thus whether there is Board jurisdiction. Neice v. Department of Homeland Security, 105 M.S.P.R. 211, ¶ 8 (2007); Markon v. Department of State, 71 M.S.P.R. 574, 578 (1996). Once it has been determined that the retirement or resignation was involuntary and the Board has jurisdiction in the appeal, the Board will adjudicate the discrimination on the merits under the substantive standards of antidiscrimination law. See Garcia, 437 F.3d at 1341; Neice, 105 M.S.P.R. 211, ¶ 8; Markon, 71 M.S.P.R. at 580.

In her initial appeal, the appellant alleged disability discrimination, i.e., failure of the agency to accommodate her brain surgery and her allergies. In her prehearing submission on remand, the appellant also appeared to raise chronic depression and chronic fatigue as disabling conditions. See Nugent v. U.S. Postal Service, 59 M.S.P.R. 444, 447-48 (1993) (an appellant may raise a claim or defense that was not included in the appeal at any time before the end of the conference held to define the issues in the case), review dismissed, 36 F.3d 1107 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.24(b). The appellant did not present any evidence at the hearing regarding disability discrimination, and the AJ did not address it in the RID.

Because the appellant has established that her retirement was involuntary, tantamount to a removal, and the Board thus has jurisdiction over her appeal, her disability discrimination claim must be addressed, even though she is entitled to reinstatement. Schibik v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 M.S.P.R. 591, ¶ 11 (2005); Totten v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 M.S.P.R. 255, 257 (1995); Polite v. Department of the Navy, 49 M.S.P.R. 653, 657 (1991). Thus, this case must be remanded for a hearing on, and adjudication of, the appellant's allegation of disability discrimination.
