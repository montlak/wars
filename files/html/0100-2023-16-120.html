broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section F: Failure to Cooperate in Arbitration

F. FAILURE TO COOPERATE IN ARBITRATION

The contract grievance process, including arbitration, is a principal means the parties to the collective bargaining agreement use to resolve their differences. If management fails to cooperate in the grievance or arbitration process or fails to honor a final arbitration award or grievance settlement, the labor-management relationship is impaired.

Initially it was determined that if binding arbitration is invoked by one party and the other party refuses to participate, and if the arbitrator proceeds ex parte and issues an award, the refusal by the second party to participate does not constitute a patent breach or rejection of the negotiated agreement in violation of §§ 7116(a)(5) and (1). FAA, Alaskan Reg'l Office and PATCO, Pac. Region, 7 FLRA 164, 170 (1981). Relying on FAA, Alaskan Reg'l Office, FLRA ruled in AFGE Local 3723 and Dept. of Navy, Navy Exch., Naval Station San Diego, 8 FLRA 70, 73 (1982), that the union did not commit a ULP by refusing to refer a management grievance to arbitration. Section 7121(b)(3)(C) provides that either party may refer an unsettled grievance to arbitration. The agency could proceed to arbitration by itself without approval of the submission from the union.

But Dept. of Labor, Employment Standards Admin./Wage and Hour Div. and AFGE Local 12, OPM, 10 FLRA 316, 320-21 (1982), reconsidered the ramifications of ex parte arbitration. It reaffirmed that one side or the other may proceed to arbitration ex parte should the other side fail to cooperate in the procedure. But, FLRA added, a refusal to cooperate in arbitration is inconsistent with § 7121 of the Statute and constitutes a violation of §§ 7116(a)(1) and (8), or §§ 7116(b)(1) and (8):

[I]t would be anomalous for Congress to have required agencies and unions to include provisions for binding arbitration in their negotiated agreements if it were not contemplated that both parties would participate therein and share the costs thereof. In this regard, a recent Court of Claims decision...Ables v. United States, No. 666-80C (Ct. Cl. March 26, 1982), must be considered. In that case, the court indicated that an arbitrator who proceeds with an ex parte arbitration may not be entitled to recover the portion of his or her fee which would otherwise be payable by the Federal agency that refused to participate in the proceedings. Thus, in those instances where agencies refuse to proceed to or participate in arbitration proceedings, exclusive representatives may be impeded in submitting unresolved grievances to binding arbitration as mandated by Congress, or may be obstructed in doing so by a requirement to pay the entire costs of such proceedings contrary to the contemplation of Congress in enacting section 7121....

Dept. of Air Force, Langley AFB, Hampton and NAGE Local R4-26, 39 FLRA 966, 969 (1991), summarized FLRA's position on cooperation in arbitration:

[A] refusal by one party to an agreement to participate in the procedures for the resolution of grievances, including questions of arbitrability, conflicts with the requirements of section 7121.... Such a refusal may not be justified by a party's contention, however arguable or reasonable, that the parties intended the subject matter of the grievance to be excluded from the coverage of the negotiated grievance and arbitration procedures....

Similarly, confirming an award allocating the costs of an arbitration, Dept. of Air Force, Fairchild AFB and Fairchild Fed. Employees Union Local 987, 59 FLRA 434, 435 (2003), declared:

We disagree with the Agency's contention that the Arbitrator exceeded his authority "by forcing the Agency to participate in the arbitration hearing[,]" despite the fact that the grievance was not arbitrable. In this regard, the Statute expressly provides that collective bargaining agreements "shall provide procedures for the settlement of grievances, including questions of arbitrability." 5 U.S.C. § 7121(a)(1) (emphasis added). Accord Dep't of the Air Force, Langley Air Force Base, Hampton, Va., 39 FLRA 966, 969 (1991) (Air Force). Consistent with this, the Authority has held that a refusal by one party to participate in the procedures for the resolution of grievances, including questions of arbitrability, conflicts with the requirements of § 7121. See Air Force, 39 FLRA at 969 (citing, inter alia, Dep't of Labor, Employment Standards Admin./Wage & Hour Div., Wash., D.C., 10 FLRA 316, 321 (1982)). Such a refusal may not be justified by a party's contention, "however arguable or reasonable," that the parties intended the subject matter of the grievance to be excluded from the coverage of the negotiated grievance and arbitration procedures. Id. (citing Dep't of the Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, Portsmouth, N.H., 11 FLRA 456 (1983)).

FLRA admonished that refusal by a party to participate in the procedures for settlement of grievances conflicts with § 7121 of the Statute. Dept. of Air Force, Griffiss AFB, Rome and AFGE Local 2612, 38 FLRA 276, 280 (1990). An agency may not unilaterally decide an arbitrator's jurisdiction under a contract. Refusal to proceed to arbitration on a contracting-out problem was a ULP. HCFA and AFGE Local 1923, 22 FLRA 437, 439 (1986); see AFGE Local 1457 and Dept. of Army, Letterman Army Med. Ctr., San Francisco, 39 FLRA 519, 528 (1991) (ALJ Decision). An agency is not relieved of its obligation to participate in arbitration by making an offer to participate in a bifurcated proceeding, first to decide an arbitrability question. Dept. of Army, 83rd Army Reserve Command, Columbus and Council of Locals No. 213, AFGE, 11 FLRA 55 (1983). An agency may not hold a related group of cases hostage and refuse to go through the procedure needed to invoke arbitration because processing of one of the grievances may depend upon the future issuance of an FLRA decision in a related ULP case involving the agency's refusal to disclose information requested by the union. Rolla Research Ctr., Bureau of Mines and NFFE Local 934, 29 FLRA 107, 113-15 (1987).

An agency's refusal to continue to participate in arbitration decisions involving retention of jurisdiction is a violation of § 7121. If an instruction or directive not to participate in the arbitration proceedings comes from higher-level authority, that authority commits a ULP. Hq., Dept. of Army, US Army Training Ctr, Ft. Leonard Wood and NAGE Local R14-32, 22 FLRA 647, 651-52 (1986). But cf. Dept. of Air Force, Seymour Johnson AFB and NAGE Local R5-188, 57 FLRA 847, 850 (2002) (award was set aside following grievance filed, at the suggestion of an arbitrator, seeking to require the agency to pay a portion of arbitration fees; grievance machinery was unavailable when the arbitrator should have pursued the dispute with the contracting officer, agency board of contract appeals, or in court). If the agency declines to timely participate in the selection of an arbitrator, and the union relies on its contract to request FMCS to appoint an arbitrator, the arbitrator's refusal to dismiss the grievance for procedural irregularity would not be set aside as a violation of management rights or a as a violation of the prohibition against management officials from participating in the management of a labor organization. Dept. of Air Force, Willow Grove ARS and NAGE Local R3-32, 58 FLRA 516, 518 (2003).

In an unusual case, the parties' contract provided for a panel of arbitrators. Under the contract, either party could remove an arbitrator from the panel. A member of the panel was chosen to decide a dispute. The arbitrator issued an initial award finding the grievance to be arbitrable. The agency filed exceptions. But while the exceptions were pending, the agency notified the union, but not FLRA, that it was invoking its contract right to unilaterally remove the arbitrator from the national panel. FLRA later issued a decision on the exceptions and issued an order identifying the arbitrator by name and directing the parties to resubmit the matter to the arbitrator if the matter were not settled. The agency refused to submit the case to that arbitrator. A ULP charge was filed, leading to a complaint, and then to FLRA decision that the agency committed a ULP by taking action to prevent the arbitrator previously jointly selected from continuing and concluding the arbitration proceeding. FLRA rejected the contention that the arbitrator would be biased by the dispute over his jurisdiction. FLRA reasoned that the agency should not be able to argue that the arbitrator was biased by the agency's unlawful attempt to remove him. Dept. of Transp., FAA and NATCA, 65 FLRA 208 (2010).

Although an agency may not unilaterally obstruct proceedings by refusing to participate, it was permissible for an agency to enter a special appearance before an arbitrator, state its objection to the arbitrator's jurisdiction and then withdraw from the hearing room, allowing the union to carry on with its case, VAMC Phoenix and AFGE, 60 FLRA 405, 406-07 (2004):

The Authority has held that an agency's refusal to participate in an arbitration proceeding pursuant to a negotiated grievance procedure conflicts with § 7121 of the Statute and therefore constitutes a violation of § 7116(a)(1) and (8). See Army Reserve Command, 11 FLRA at 56; DOL, 10 FLRA at 320;. In this regard, the Authority has noted that a party's refusal to participate in an arbitration proceeding results in the "obstruction" of the grievance resolution process, contrary to the mandate and intent of Congress in enacting § 7121. Headquarters, Dep't of the Army, Wash., D.C., 22 FLRA 647, 651 (1986) (Headquarters, Army); see also DOL, 10 FLRA at 321.

Here, the Respondent did not refuse to participate in the arbitration proceeding. The Respondent agreed to submit the parties' dispute to arbitration, participated in selecting the arbitrator, provided a room for the arbitration hearing, and arranged for a court reporter. As acknowledged by the Charging Party, the Respondent participated in the hearing by making a "special appearance" to challenge the arbitrator's jurisdiction. The Respondent also filed a post-hearing brief with the arbitrator.

Further, the Respondent's actions did not hinder or obstruct the grievance resolution process. After the Respondent's departure, the arbitrator continued with the proceeding, hearing the Charging Party's evidence and testimony. Subsequently, the arbitrator issued an award sustaining the Charging Party's grievance. Based on the foregoing, the Charging Party has not demonstrated that the Respondent refused to participate in the arbitration proceeding or that the Respondent's actions resulted in the "hindrance or obstruction of grievance resolution by binding arbitration[.]" Headquarters, Army, 22 FLRA at 650.

In arguing that the Respondent refused to participate in the arbitration proceeding, the Charging Party relies on Army Reserve Command, where a scheduled arbitration hearing never took place because the respondent refused to participate in the hearing and considered it canceled. In Army Reserve Command, as in other decisions where the Authority has found that a party refused to participate in an arbitration proceeding, the respondent's refusal to participate actually obstructed resolution of the grievance. See, e.g., Dep't of the Air Force, Langley Air Force Base, Hampton, Va., 39 FLRA 966, 969 (1991); Am. Fed'n of Gov't Employees, Local 1457, 39 FLRA 519 (1991); Headquarters, Army, 22 FLRA at 650; and Am. Fed'n of Gov't Employees, Local 2782, AFL-CIO, 21 FLRA 339 (1986). Nothing in Army Reserve Command, or the authority cited in that case, indicates that a party such as the Respondent here, who takes the position that an arbitrator lacks jurisdiction over a matter, but nevertheless does not impede the arbitration and disposition of the case, violates the Statute. See Army Reserve Command, 11 FLRA at 56 (citing DOL, 10 FLRA at 320-32). As the Respondent's conduct here did not obstruct the resolution of the grievance, the Charging Party's reliance on Army Reserve Command is misplaced.

The special appearance at least avoids a charge of the refusal to cooperate in the arbitration process. The failure of the agency to cooperate in submitting an arbitrability issue to an arbitrator constitutes a ULP, irrespective of the merits of the agency position. See Dept. of Labor and AFGE Local 12, 61 FLRA 825 (2006) (dispute over whether a probationer could grieve a separation). But Director of Admin., Hq., Air Force and AFGE-GAIU Council of Hq. USAF Locals, 17 FLRA 372, 375 (1985), found no ULP when the agency declined to proceed to arbitration concerning the termination of a probationer, ruling that the law on nonarbitrability of the subject matter of the grievance was "clearly established":

...In the instant case, the issue involves only that matter of clearly established law. Thus, the instant case presents no threshold question or any other question of interpretation or statutory construction which can legitimately be resolved by an arbitrator. Accordingly, the Authority shall dismiss the section 7116(a)(1) and (8) allegations of the complaint in Case No. 3-CA-1700. The Authority shall also dismiss the section 7116(a)(5) allegation of the complaint. Thus, even assuming that the parties' negotiated agreement purported to require the Respondent to proceed to arbitration concerning the termination of probationary employees, a matter which is subject to differing and arguable interpretations, such requirement would be contrary to law and regulation and hence unenforceable. See, e.g., Wisconsin Army National Guard and Association of Civilian Technicians, 14 FLRA 57 (1984). In a word, it would be a pointless and hollow exercise to require the parties to proceed to arbitration over an issue which, as a matter of law, is not cognizable under any grievance procedure negotiated under the Statute.

Air Force was distinguished in VA Canteen Serv. and NAGE Local R4-78, 65 FLRA 224, 228 (2010), determining that the agency committed a ULP by refusing to participate in the arbitration of a terminated Canteen Service employee. The agency's argument was that the MSPB decided that employees of the Canteen Service did not have appeal rights to the Board, and so they should not be able to take their cases to arbitration. FLRA ruled that the law governing the grievant's ability to have the termination grievance considered was not "clearly established," and FLRA directed that the case return to arbitration.

An agency committed a ULP when it declined to participate in arbitration, holding local grievances in abeyance until a national grievance was resolved, DHS, ICE and AFGE Local 1917, 69 FLRA 72, 73-75 (2015) (Member Pizzella concurring and dissenting in part):

A. Under § 7121, the Agency must present its arguments regarding mootness, standing, arbitral jurisdiction, and res judicata to an arbitrator.

The Agency argues that the Judge's decision is contrary to law because: (1) the disputed grievances are moot; (2) Local 1917 does not have standing to arbitrate its grievances, so § 7121 allegedly does not apply here; (3) an arbitrator would not have jurisdiction to decide the disputed grievances; and (4) arbitrating the disputed grievances would violate the doctrine of res judicata.

As the Judge noted, § 7121(a) of the Statute requires that collective-bargaining agreements contain "procedures for the settlement of grievances, including questions of arbitrability."[19] Additionally, § 7121(b)(1)(C)(iii) of the Statute requires that all such negotiated grievance procedures "provide that any grievance not satisfactorily settled under the negotiated grievance procedure shall be subject to binding arbitration."[20] These negotiated grievance and "arbitration procedures 'must be read as providing that all questions of arbitrability not otherwise resolved shall be submitted to arbitration.'"[21] And the Authority has repeatedly held that a party refusing to arbitrate unresolved grievances violates § 7116(a)(1) and (8) of the Statute.[22] But, as the Judge noted, the Authority has found a limited exception to this rule where "clearly established law" precludes arbitrating a grievance, in which case an agency would not violate § 7116(a)(1) and (8) by refusing to arbitrate.[23]

[19] 5 U.S.C. § 7121(a).

[20] Id. § 7121(b)(1)(C)(iii).

[21] VCS, 65 FLRA at 228 (quoting DOL, Emp't Standards Admin./Wage & Hour Div., Wash., D.C., 10 FLRA 316, 318 (1982)).

[22] See id. (citing Dep't of the Air Force, Langley Air Force Base, Hampton, Va., 39 FLRA 966, 969 (1991) (Langley AFB); AFGE, Local 1457, 39 FLRA 519, 521-22 (1991)).

[23] Id. (quoting HQ, 17 FLRA at 375).

Consistent with the principles set forth above, it is well established that questions of arbitrability are solely for an arbitrator to decide.[24] Of particular relevance here, the Authority has found that arbitrability questions include disputes over whether: (1) grievances are moot;[25] (2) a local union has standing to file and arbitrate a grievance;[26] (3) an arbitrator has jurisdiction to resolve a grievance;[27] and (4) the doctrine of res judicata should bar grievances.[28]

[24] Id. (citing 5 U.S.C. § 7121(c); Langley AFB, 39 FLRA at 969).

[25] AFGE, Local 2328, 66 FLRA 149, 150-51 (2011) (Local 2328) (citing NFFE, Council of Consol. Locals, 52 FLRA 137, 139-40 (1996)) (finding that mootness is procedural-arbitrability question).

[26] INS, DOJ, U.S. Gov't, 7 FLRA 549, 552 (1982) (DOJ) (finding that local union's standing was procedural "question[ ] for resolution by an arbitrator").

[27] E.g., AFGE, Local 953, 68 FLRA 644, 645-46 (2015) (Local 953) (finding that arbitrator's jurisdiction over grievance involving classification matter was substantive-arbitrability question).

[28] AFGE, Local 2459, 51 FLRA 1602, 1606-07 (1996) (Local 2459) (citing U.S. Dep't of the Air Force, Air Logistics Ctr., Tinker Air Force Base, Okla., 41 FLRA 303, 305 (1991) (finding decision whether to apply res judicata should be resolved as arbitrability question).

The Agency does not cite any clearly established law to justify its refusal to arbitrate the disputed grievances. And, as mentioned above, the Authority's precedent indicates that questions of mootness, standing, arbitral jurisdiction, and res judicata are questions of arbitrability.[29] Thus, like all unresolved arbitrability questions, they must be submitted to an arbitrator for resolution, consistent with § 7121.[30] As such, the Judge correctly declined to resolve those questions in the first instance, and his decision in that respect is not contrary to law.

[30] E.g., VCS, 65 FLRA at 228.

B. Sections 7101(b) and 7112(a) of the Statute do not support excusing the Agency from complying with § 7121.

The Agency notes that, under § 7101(b), the Statute's provisions "should be interpreted in a manner consistent with the requirement of an effective and efficient [g]overnment."[31] Further, the Agency notes that § 7112(a) states that the Authority should find a bargaining unit appropriate for exclusive representation only when that unit "will promote effective dealings with, and efficiency of the operations of the agency involved."[32] According to the Agency, the Judge's decision is inconsistent with the principles underlying §§ 7101(b) and 7112(a) because: (1) the Agency did "not impede the arbitration and disposition" of the matters in dispute; (2) the Agency had no "obligation to engage in multiple[,] duplicative arbitration cases"; and (3) a rule requiring local arbitration over national policies "could not only consume significant [A]gency resources..., but would [also] produce inconsistent and chaotic results."

[31] 5 U.S.C. § 7101(b); e.g., Exceptions at 13, 15 (citing § 7101(b)).

[32] 5 U.S.C. § 7112(a); e.g., Exceptions at 13, 15 (citing § 7112(a)).

With regard to the Agency's first argument, the Authority has held that refusing to arbitrate a grievance--even where the refusing party has a reasonable basis for its position--violates § 7121.[36] In this case, the Agency refused to select an arbitrator to resolve the disputed grievances and unilaterally placed them "in abeyance." As such, we reject the Agency's contention that it did "not impede the arbitration."

[36] See FAA, 65 FLRA at 211; Langley AFB, 39 FLRA at 969.

Concerning the Agency's second argument, as discussed in Section III.A. above, the Agency must present to an arbitrator its contention that the disputed grievances should be precluded by Council 118's grievances.

As for the Agency's third argument, the Judge found that the parties have arbitrated local and national grievances without the Agency "actually experienc[ing]" problems regarding insufficient resources or contradictory outcomes. In addition, the Authority has stated that a party may not refuse to arbitrate a grievance on the ground that it wastes resources, no matter how "arguable or reasonable" that party's position is.[40]
