a. Liability for Event Preceding Adverse Action

Normally, back pay is calculated from the date of the adverse action through the date of reinstatement. There may, however, have been actions taken in anticipation of an adverse action. Those preliminary measures may influence the back pay calculations, Lavelle v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 86, 91 (1988) (on second PFR, 40 MSPR 329, 332 (1989), overruled in part, Mattern v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 65, 72 ¶ 16 (2001), supra):

In fashioning a make-whole remedy which is consonant with the Back Pay Act and the Board's broad remedial powers, our analysis of what appellant "normally" would have earned must not be rigidly circumscribed by the effective date of the personnel action. Where, as here, an agency waits a significant length of time between an appellant's act and the imposition of punishment and, in the intervening time, takes an action against the employee which is not appealable but results in a loss of pay to the employee, then the agency must show that the action was for a legitimate management reason unrelated to the unlawful personnel action.[3] Compare Summers v. United States, 227 Ct. Cl. 353, 648 F.2d 1324 (1981) (overtime pay due employee for reassignment period preceding unlawful suspension), with Reynaud v. United States, 225 Ct. Cl. 624, 628 (1980) (premium pay not due when employee validly reassigned); see also Vol. XI Comp. Gen. No. 3, Case No. B-163142 (Feb. 28, 1968) (employee who was reassigned to day shift prior to removal may be paid premium pay if he normally would have earned the pay had he not been removed, to be computed on basis of average premium time of similarly situated employees).

[3] This standard is consonant with the Board's holding in Pickard v. Department of Transportation, 25 MSPR 404, 407 (1984), that if an alleged constructive suspension preceded a removal but was not appealed, either separately or as a part of the removal, back pay for the alleged suspension period cannot be awarded in the compliance proceeding relating to the removal. In Pickard, the alleged constructive removal was an appealable action; in this case, the reassignment is not appealable.

Thus, in this case, the agency must show more than simply that appellant was on the day shift when the suspension was served. Because appellant has alleged that but for the contemplated disciplinary action he would not have been reassigned from the second shift to the day shift, the agency must show that the reassignment was for a reason unrelated to the alleged theft of property. If the agency cannot show this, then appellant is entitled to night differential pay.

Entitlement to premiums will not necessarily encompass a period of administrative leave prior to a removal, Rolle v. USPS, 44 MSPR 323, 325 (1990):

When a wrongful personnel action is reversed, the subject of that action is to be restored as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. Kerr v. National Endowment of the Arts, 726 F.2d 730 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In this case, the wrongful personnel action was the removal, not the agency's placement of appellant on administrative leave. Indeed, the Board has not made any determination concerning the placement of the appellant on administrative leave. Consequently, appellant's status quo ante remedy relates solely to correcting the harm suffered from the removal action. As the agency's placement of appellant on administrative leave was not a harm suffered as a result of the wrongful removal, appellant is not entitled to any adjustments to his pay while he was on administrative leave.

If an employee was removed in reprisal for union activities on the pretext of medical inability to perform duties, a period of enforced leave preceding the removal was a constructive suspension. It was part of the improper disciplinary action and the employee was ready, willing, and able to work. Clements v. Dept. of Navy, 21 MSPR 275, 277 (1984) (citing Manzi v. United States, 198 Ct. Cl. 489 (1972)). The Board suggested "for clarity" in Clements that if an agency has improperly forced an employee to use leave, restoration of leave, and not its monetary equivalent, is the appropriate remedy (citing Ainsworth v. United States, 185 Ct. Cl. 110 (1968)). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Collateral Result of Restoration Order; Action Related to Case Under Appeal."]
