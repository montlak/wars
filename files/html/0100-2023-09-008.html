1. Treatment of Timeliness Issues

Timeliness is a procedural matter; however, similar to an issue of jurisdiction, timeliness may be raised sua sponte by the AJ without prompting from the respondent (although the respondent-agency will normally also object to an untimely appeal). Hamilton v. MSPB, 75 F.3d 639, 642-45 (Fed. Cir. 1996), considered the nature of a timeliness issue:

In the event that an appeal to the Board is untimely filed, section 1201.22(c) provides that the appeal "will be dismissed as untimely filed unless a good reason for the delay is shown."

This regulation was issued pursuant to 5 USC § 7701(j) (1988) which provides that the MSPB "may prescribe regulations to carry out the purpose of this section." As this court stated in Phillips v. USPS, 695 F.2d 1389, 1390-91 (Fed. Cir. 1982), the Board has discretion to "requir[e] that appeals be processed in accordance with regulations prescribed by the Board."

The issue of the timeliness of an appeal to the Board has had a surprisingly complex and tortuous history. In its earliest decisions, the Board considered a timely appeal necessary to establish the Board's jurisdiction. See, e.g., Butler v. Dept. of Interior, 10 MSPR 25 (1982) (vacating initial decision and remanding case for hearing on "the jurisdictional issue" of the timeliness of the petition). Thus, it followed that the Board could, indeed, was obligated to raise the timeliness issue sua sponte and to place the burden on the appellant to prove timeliness as with any other jurisdictional issue. See Hambsch v. United States, 857 F.2d 763, 764-65 (Fed. Cir. 1988), cert. denied, 490 U.S. 1054 (1989). In Wallace, 728 F.2d at 1458 n.2, this court noted that the time for filing an appeal to the MSPB was fixed only by regulation, not statute, and, thus, was not jurisdictional. Not being a jurisdictional issue, we questioned at that time the practice of the Board in raising a nonjurisdictional issue sua sponte, id., but the Wallace case was decided on other grounds. In any event, the MSPB has continued to raise the issue of timeliness sua sponte in its Acknowledgement Orders, as it did in this case.

...

Given the entirety of this jurisprudential and statutory history, we must conclude that Congress, by reinstating the Hopkins [v. MSPB, 725 F.2d 1368 (Fed. Cir. 1984)] rule, mandated that the regulations fixing time periods be treated as a procedural matter in which the MSPB is the party in interest, not a substantive defense of the employing agency, comparable to a statute of limitations....

Timeliness regulations being directed to MSPB docket control and orderly procedures, and the MSPB being the litigating respondent on appeals involving timeliness, we conclude the Board may sua sponte raise the issue of an appellant's compliance with its regulations respecting timeliness.

See Hemann v. VA, 2022 MSPB 46, n.2 (2022) ("Both the Board and our reviewing court have held that time prescriptions are not jurisdictional. Kirkendall v. Department of the Army, 479 F.3d 830, 842 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (en banc); Heimberger v. Department of Commerce, 121 M.S.P.R. 10, ¶ 13 (2014).").

The reference to "docket control" is another way of saying that the appeal summarily dismissed as untimely filed is one less appeal for the Board to adjudicate through the processes of discovery, prehearing submissions, hearing, issuance of an initial decision, and possible MSPB petition for review or judicial review. At times the Board's reviewing court finds docket control weak justification for the Board's refusal to waive a filing deadline, Pyles v. MSPB, 45 F.3d 411, 415-16 (Fed. Cir. 1995):

Nor can we accept counsel's assertion at oral argument that the Board's need to manage and limit its caseload somehow justifies the Board's refusal to find excusable delay here. The Board's standard on waiver of the deadline, whatever it is, must be applied consistently across cases; a waiver cannot be denied merely because the Board's docket may be increasing, as suggested by the Board's counsel at oral argument. Nor can our standard of what constitutes an abuse of discretion by the Board when it has denied a waiver.

Although a timeliness dismissal of an appeal is ordinarily addressed through a PFR, the Board may excuse an untimely filing if, sua sponte, the Board discerns a fundamental error by the AJ who made the timeliness determination. See Henry v. USPS, 69 MSPR 555, 559 (1996) (even though the parties did not address the issue, the Board found that the appeal was likely not untimely because of the provisions of the Soldiers' and Sailors' Civil Relief Act). Ordinarily, if the AJ dismisses a case as untimely, the appellant will seek review of that determination through a petition for review filed with the Board. [Refer to Chapter 5 for petitions for review.]

If the AJ raises a question of timeliness, the AJ should inform the appellant of how timeliness will be resolved, e.g., if the timely filing of an appeal requires proof of the date that a document, such as a notice of VEOA appeal rights from the Department of Labor, was received by the appellant. See Perkins v. USPS, 94 MSPR 425, 428-29 ¶ 10 (2003). The AJ must inform the parties, preferably in the acknowledgment order, of the dates that he intends to rely on to support the dismissal for untimeliness. Mohammed v. OPM, 108 MSPR 609, 613 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 98 (2008). An appellant responds to an AJ's order raising a timeliness issue by filing a submission that provides information concerning the circumstances of the filing of the appeal. For an apparently untimely petition for review, the explanation must be in the form of an affidavit or sworn statement; however, the regulations governing appeals do not require that the explanations be in any particular form. Unsworn but credible explanations are accepted by the Board. See Perkins v. USPS, 94 MSPR 425, 429 ¶ 11 (2003); Burnett v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 34, 37 (1996) ("In the case of an apparently untimely petition for review, the explanation must be in the form of an affidavit or sworn statement, but the regulations governing petitions for appeal do not require that such explanations be in any particular form."); King v. DHHS, 71 MSPR 22, 26 n.3 (1996) ("This submission is in the form of a letter from the appellant's current representative, and two statements from Ms. Sturm, the appellant's former representative. Although the latter statements are styled as "affidavits," they do not constitute affidavits because they are not sworn under oath or affirmation. We note, however, that evidence regarding the timeliness of a petition for appeal need not be in the form of an affidavit or declaration made under penalty of perjury. See Cohen v. Department of Commerce, 56 M.S.P.R. 578, 582 n.1 (1993)."). Proper practice is to provide a sworn declaration from someone personally familiar with the events pertaining to the timeliness issue. Unsworn statements of representatives are not a proper evidentiary basis for a showing of the good cause necessary to waive a deadline unless the representative has personal knowledge of the event described in the declaration, e.g., whether a document was mailed and how it was mailed from the representative's office. [Refer to Chapter 4, "Statements of Representative, Party."]

Assuming that an issue of timeliness would be dispositive of the appeal, the AJ should ordinarily resolve timeliness issues before engaging in prehearing procedures and the hearing. Pitre v. OPM, 39 MSPR 693, 696 (1989). Where timeliness requires factfinding, the AJ should hold a "timeliness hearing." See Lewis v. EPA, 82 MSPR 269, 272 ¶ 10 (1999); Hightower v. USPS, 72 MSPR 567, 569 (1996) ("[I]f an appellant can establish a factual dispute as to whether he timely filed his petition for appeal...and he requested a hearing, he is entitled to a timeliness hearing.").
