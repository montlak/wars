a. Appeal Right With EEO Allegation

What 5 USC 7121 taketh away from the Board, it restoreth if a claim of discrimination is asserted. 5 USC 7121(d) provides:

An aggrieved employee affected by a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b)(1) of this title which also falls under the coverage of the negotiated grievance procedure may raise the matter under a statutory procedure or the negotiated procedure, but not both. An employee shall be deemed to have exercised his option under this subsection to raise the matter under either a statutory procedure or the negotiated procedure at such time as the employee timely initiates an action under the applicable statutory procedure or timely files a grievance in writing, in accordance with the provisions of the parties' negotiated procedure, whichever event occurs first. Selection of the negotiated procedure in no manner prejudices the right of an aggrieved employee to request the Merit Systems Protection Board to review the final decision pursuant to section 7702 of this title in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board, or, where applicable, to request the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to review a final decision in any other matter involving a complaint of discrimination of the type prohibited by any law administered by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission.

5 USC 2302(b)(1) covers the usual bases of prohibited discrimination, plus marital status or political affiliation prohibited by law, rule, or regulation:

(b) Any employee who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action, shall not, with respect to such authority--

(1) discriminate for or against any employee or applicant for employment--

(A) on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin, as prohibited under section 717 of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (42 U.S.C. 2000e-16);

(B) on the basis of age, as prohibited under sections 12 and 15 of the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (29 U.S.C. 631, 633a);

(C) on the basis of sex, as prohibited under section 6(d) of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 U.S.C. 206 (d));

(D) on the basis of handicapping condition, as prohibited under section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (29 U.S.C. 791); or

(E) on the basis of marital status or political affiliation, as prohibited under any law, rule, or regulation[.]

Section 7121 has several salient features. Under 5 USC 7121(e)(1), an action under 5 USC 4303 (performance) or 5 USC 7512 (adverse action) can be grieved or taken to the Board by direct appeal, not both. Under 5 USC 7121(d), as to matters not involving actions under 5 USC 4303 or 5 USC 7512 that might otherwise be appealed to the Board (e.g., WIGI denial, RIF), the grievance procedure is exclusive (unless it excludes those matters), but the employee may appeal to the Board (if a grievance is not first used) to challenge the action otherwise within Board jurisdiction if discrimination (or EEO reprisal) is alleged. If any action otherwise within Board appellate jurisdiction is grieved and makes its way of an arbitration decision and if discrimination (or EEO reprisal) was alleged in the grievance, Board review at the headquarters level is available to the appellant (grievant). 5 USC 7121(d). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards."]

A few decisions explain the statute. An allegation of discrimination places an action covered by contract and otherwise within the Board's jurisdiction back into the MSPB appellate jurisdiction, should the appellant elect that route. Jones v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 680, 683-84 (1990). [Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards," discusses the process permitting review at the headquarters level of the Board of an arbitrator's award on an action otherwise within the Board jurisdiction when an allegation of discrimination is made; refer in this chapter to "Appeal Right With Arbitration Award; Employees of Agencies Other Than Postal Service."]

Avila v. DLA, 21 MSPR 91, 93-94 (1984), involving an appeal from the denial of a within-grade increase, explained application of 5 USC 7121:

There is no dispute that an appellant is entitled to appeal to the Board from an agency reconsideration decision denying a within-grade increase. 5 U.S.C, § 5335 (c); 5 C.F.R. § 531. 410 (d). Section 531. 410 (d; further provides as follows:

... However, for an employee covered by a collective bargaining agreement a reconsideration decision that sustains a negative determination is only reviewable in accordance with the terms of the agreement.

Further, there is no dispute that the collective bargaining agreement in the instant case provides that it "...is the exclusive procedure available to bargaining unit employee(s) for the resolution of grievances." The agreement, however, also provides, that "[A]n employee alleging discrimination...may at his/her option raise the matter under the appropriate statutory appellate procedure or under the provisions of this Article, but not both."

Such provision is in conformity with the applicable statute, 5 U.S.C. § 7121, which provides for two exceptions to the provision that a collective bargaining agreement may limit appeal rights to the Board. These exceptions are set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) and (e). Section 7121 (d) provides:

An aggrieved employee affected by a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302 (b)(l) [discrimination] of this title which also falls under the coverage of the negotiated grievance procedure may raise the matter under a statutory procedure or the negotiated procedure, but not both.

The second exception found at section 7121(e)(1) pertains to matters arising under 5 U.S.C. § 4303 and 5 U.S.C. § 7512 and is not relevant to this case.

Thus, the Board is not divested of jurisdiction by the terms of a collective bargaining agreement providing for exclusivity of remedy where an appellant alleges discrimination in connection with an otherwise appealable matter In the instant case, the appellant alleged discrimination on the basis of both age and sex, and thus is entitled to appeal to the Board. See Greer and Jennings v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 19 M.S.P.R. 90 at 91-92 (1984). We therefore find that the presiding official erred in dismissing the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. Accordingly, the initial decision is REVERSED and the case is REMANDED for adjudication on the merits.

Parnell v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 128, 129-31 (1993), concluded that an allegation of reprisal for filing an EEO complaint served as a predicate for Board jurisdiction in a RIF case:

In her petition for appeal, the appellant alleged that the agency's RIF action was based on reprisal for her having filed formal complaints of discrimination with the agency. The administrative judge found that the appellant's claim of reprisal did not rest upon any of the bases stated in 5 USC 2302(b)(1), and that, since her appeal did not fall within the exception provided by 5 CFR 1201.3(c)(1), her avenue of appeal was solely within the negotiated grievance procedure. She therefore dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

As the administrative judge noted, the plain language of 5 USC 2302(b)(1) refers to prohibited discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, handicapping condition, marital status, and political affiliation; reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint is not included in that list. The administrative judge cited Patterson v. Department of Air Force, 39 MSPR 413, 415-16, aff'd, 878 F.2d 1446 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table), and Poirier v. Department of Navy, 31 MSPR 214 (1986), in support of her finding that, since a claim of reprisal is not included in the above list, the appellant's right of appeal was limited to the negotiated grievance procedure. We note, however, that the appellants in those cases did not allege reprisal for having filed a discrimination complaint. We therefore find that those decisions are not controlling.

Some of the court's language in Spruill v. MSPB, 978 F.2d 679, 691-92 (Fed. Cir. 1992), suggests that reprisal taken against an individual because of the filing of a discrimination complaint is limited to a violation of 5 USC 2302(b)(9), so that one could argue that it could not also be violative of section 2302(b)(1). The court's comments must be taken in context, however. The issue before the court in Spruill was whether reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint constituted whistleblowing under section 2302(b)(8). 978 F.2d at 689. Thus, we view the court's comments as leading to the conclusion that there is no overlap between sections 2302(b)(8) and 2302(b)(9).

This reading of Spruill is reinforced by the court's reference to In re Frazier, 1 MSPB 159, 1 MSPR 163 (1979), aff'd sub nom. Frazier v. MSPB, 672 F.2d 150 (D.C. Cir. 1982). 978 F.2d at 691. The court approved the distinction the Board drew in Frazier between sections 2302(b)(8) and 2302(b)(9), without mentioning or otherwise disapproving the lengthy footnote in Frazier, stating that a claim of reprisal for EEO activities sets forth a cognizable claim under section 2302(b)(1)(A), as well as under section 2302(b)(9). Frazier, 1 MSPR at 191 n.36. Further, the court noted in Spruill at 682 that the administrative judge in that case relied on Williams v. Department of Defense, 46 MSPR 549 (1991), without mentioning or otherwise disapproving the footnote in Williams reiterating the Board's position that reprisal for filing an EEO complaint violates both sections 2302(b)(1) and 2302(b)(9). Williams, 46 MSPR at 553 n.4.

We find the reasoning in Frazier to be instructive. Frazier noted that 42 USC 2000e-16, referenced in section 2302(b)(1), has been interpreted by the courts to include claims of reprisal for opposition to employment practices made unlawful by Title VII. 1 MSPR at 191 n.36. See also Jordan v. Clark, 847 F.2d 1368, 1376 n.8 (9th Cir. 1988), cert. denied, 488 U.S. 1006, 109 S. Ct. 786, 102 L. Ed.2d 778 (1989). We note, in addition, that the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, the agency responsible for the administration and enforcement of equal opportunity in Federal employment, includes claims of reprisal for filing discrimination complaints as within the ambit of 42 USC 2000e-16 in its regulations. See 29 CFR 1614.101 and 1614.103(a); see also Spruill, 978 F.2d at 692 n.17.

We therefore conclude that a claim of reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint is cognizable under 5 USC 2302(b)(1). Because the appellant raised such a claim in connection with her RIF appeal, her appeal falls within the Board's jurisdiction under 5 USC 7121(d).

See Martinesi v. EEOC, 24 MSPR 276, 281 (1984) (WIGI appeal: the Board had jurisdiction, despite the existence of a negotiated grievance procedure, when the appellant alleged that the agency's action was in reprisal for filing a discrimination complaint).

As to an appeal (and hearing) of a case by an MSPB AJ, for an EEO allegation to vest jurisdiction in the Board over an appealable matter (a RIF) that would otherwise be exclusively covered by a contractual grievance procedure, the EEO allegation is ordinarily raised in the appeal. There are, however, circumstances permitting the EEO allegation to be raised as late as the petition for review, although Weslowski v. Dept. of Army, 80 MSPR 585, 590-92 ¶¶ 9-14 (1999), aff'd on other grounds, Weslowski v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3168), decided that the facts did not permit consideration of appellant's belated EEO allegations:

Initially, in his appeal of the merits of his separation by RIF procedures, the appellant stated that he was not a member of the union, and reiterated his request that the Board adjudicate the merits of his RIF separation. If an employee is covered by a CBA, matters that customarily would be within the appellate jurisdiction of the Board are deemed to be covered by the negotiated grievance procedure, and thus beyond the Board's jurisdiction, unless a matter is excluded from the application of the grievance procedure. See DiSera v. Department of the Army, 71 MSPR 120, 122 (1996). However, an aggrieved employee affected by a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC § 2302(b)(1), i.e., discrimination on the bases of race, color, national origin, sex, religion, disability, age, equal pay, marital status, and political affiliation, which also falls under the coverage of the negotiated grievance procedure may raise the matter under a statutory procedure, i.e., a Board appeal, the EEO complaint process, or the negotiated grievance procedure. 5 USC § 7121(d). The employee shall be deemed to have exercised his option to raise an allegation of discrimination with the Board, in the EEO complaint process, or under the negotiated procedure at such time as the employee timely initiates an action under the applicable statutory procedure or timely files a grievance in writing, whichever occurs first. Id.

Here, the applicable CBA does not exclude demotions and separations taken pursuant to a RIF from the negotiated grievance procedure. Therefore, notwithstanding that the appellant is not a member of the union, the negotiated grievance procedure is the exclusive appellate avenue for employees separated by operation of RIF procedures and covered by the CBA, except for those who allege discrimination prohibited under section 2302(b)(1). 5 USC § 7121(d). There is no evidence of record that the appellant alleged such discrimination when he filed his complaint with OSC, when he filed his appeal with the Board from the conclusion of OSC's investigation of his whistleblower reprisal complaint, or when he filed this appeal of the merits of the RIF action.

For the first time in this petition for review, the appellant states that he will file an equal employment opportunity (EEO) complaint against the agency and OSC for violating the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) by not accommodating his disabling conditions of depression and attention deficit disorder. We construe this statement on petition for review as an assertion by the appellant that the Board has jurisdiction over the merits of his RIF separation under 5 USC § 7121(d), i.e., that the Board's jurisdiction is predicated on the appellant's assertion that his RIF separation was discriminatory on the basis of disability. See Walters v. U.S. Postal Service, 65 MSPR 115, 119 (1994).

An appellant may raise an allegation of discrimination at any time during the Board's consideration of the appeal of the agency's action, including on petition for review, if the appellant did not know of the existence of a basis for the allegation at the time that the petition for appeal was filed. See Anderson v. Veterans Administration, 3 MSPB 188, 3 MSPR 71, 74 (1980). See also Schermerhorn v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940729 (Feb. 10, 1995) (the time limit to begin the EEO complaint process is not triggered until a complainant should have reasonably suspected discrimination). The appellant explains that he is raising his allegation of disability discrimination eighteen months after the agency effected his separation because he "recently discovered" that "[b]oth Depression and Attention Deficit Disorder are recognized by [the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission] as being in the scope of the [ADA]."

An appellant must raise a discrimination claim when he has sufficient knowledge of facts and circumstances to form a reasonable suspicion that prohibited discrimination has occurred. See Ball v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05880247 (July 6, 1988). An appellant's responsibility to raise the issue of discrimination is triggered when he possessed sufficient facts that he should have reasonably suspected discrimination, but before all of the facts that would have supported a charge of discrimination have become apparent. Id. Here, the appellant admits that he had been treated for depression and attention deficit disorder for many years, and alleges that the Health Clinic and his place of employment had this information in his personnel file. He also was aware that the agency had not accommodated his conditions. Further, he was fully aware of the facts around his separation by September 29, 1996, the effective date of the agency's separation action. Thus, he had sufficient information to reasonably suspect that his RIF separation might be discriminatory on the basis of disability.

He alleges on petition for review, however, that he did not know he could file an EEO complaint based on his conditions because he was unaware that they were within the scope of the ADA. However, the additional information that the appellant discovered after he had filed his petition for appeal was not information that could reveal a discriminatory motive on the part of the agency. Therefore, we find that this additional information is not a basis for waiving the time limit for raising a claim of discrimination on the basis of disability, and consequently the appellant's allegations of disability discrimination on petition for review do not provide a basis for the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal from the conclusion of the merits of his RIF separation.[1] Accordingly, we dismiss this appeal for lack of Board jurisdiction.

One decision determined that if through an EEO allegation the appellant seeks the Board to assume jurisdiction over a RIF that is otherwise relegated to contract grievance procedures, the appellant should make the EEO allegation when he files the appeal. If an appellant's appeal is dismissed as beyond the Board's jurisdiction in the absence of an EEO allegation, the appellant may seek to cure the jurisdictional defect by filing a new appeal claiming discrimination, although he would have to explain why he did not earlier amend his pleadings in the first appeal when he was put on notice of the jurisdictional problem. McLaughlin v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 481, 485-86 (1997); see Honga v. DHHS, DE-0351-16-0072-I-1 (NP 5/6/2016) (RIF: "Finally, the appellant has not alleged that he previously lacked sufficient knowledge to suspect that discrimination occurred. Consequently, the appellant's allegations of discrimination on review do not provide a basis for the Board's jurisdiction over this appeal. See Weslowski v. Cooper v. Dept. of Defense, 98 MSPR 313, 317-19 ¶¶ 9-11 (2005), resolved that an allegation of discrimination need not be supported by factual allegations to provide the predicate for Board review of a RIF that could otherwise only be challenged through a contract grievance procedure:

As noted above, the appellant has maintained that his separation was the result of discrimination based on race, disability, and reprisal for having filed a number of equal employment opportunity (EEO) complaints. In Currier v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 177, 180 (1998), the Board held that an appellant has a right to a hearing on his claims of discrimination prohibited by the laws enumerated at 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1)(B), and that it is error for an AJ to strike such a claim prior to a hearing regardless of whether the appellant has made a non-frivolous allegation of facts to support it. In arriving at this finding, the Board expressly relied on its decision in Bennett v. National Gallery of Art, 79 M.S.P.R. 285 (1998), in which the appellant had requested review of an arbitration decision and raised a claim of such prohibited discrimination for the first time on review.

In Bennett, the Board observed that, under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d), "[a]n aggrieved employee affected by a prohibited personnel practice under [5 U.S.C. §] 2302(b)(1)" may "request [the Board] to review the final [arbitration] decision pursuant to [5 U.S.C. §] 7702...in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board[.] " Bennett, 79 M.S.P.R. at 289. The Board further observed that section 7702(a)(1) provides that, "in the case of any employee or applicant for employment who" appeals an action under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75 and who "alleges that a basis for the action was discrimination...the Board shall...decide both the issue of discrimination and the applicable action[.] " Id. (emphasis in original). Upon reviewing the legislative history of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (CSRA), Pub. L. No. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111, and the plain language of the statute, the Board in Bennett concluded that section 7702 does not distinguish between frivolous and non-frivolous allegations or between facts sufficient to support a prima facie case of discrimination and facts insufficient to support a prima facie case of discrimination. Id. at 290. The Board similarly concluded that the statute does not distinguish between Board decisions which would find that a discrimination claim is frivolous and decisions in which the Board would find that the appellant has made allegations sufficient to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. Id. Consistent with this reasoning, the Board held that it had jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's decision under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d) in any case in which an appellant alleges discrimination prohibited by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1), irrespective of whether the allegations are non-frivolous. Id. at 294 (noting that the appellant also must meet any other jurisdictional requirements for consideration of an arbitration decision).

The Board's Currier decision adopted the analysis set forth in Bennett and applied it to cases in which the appellant has raised a discrimination claim with respect to an appeal brought to the Board under law, rule, or regulation. See Currier, 79 M.S.P.R. at 180. Here, the appellant has raised his discrimination claims in an appeal brought to the Board under 5 C.F.R. § 351.901 following the appellant's election to proceed before the Board under 5 U.S.C. §§ 7121(d) and (g). We therefore find that the initial decision, which dismissed these claims without affording the appellant a hearing, was contrary to Board precedent as discussed above.[2] See also Farquhar v. Department of the Air Force, 82 M.S.P.R. 454 ¶ 12 (1999) (under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1), an appellant who raises a discrimination claim with respect to any appeal brought to the Board under any law, rule, or regulation is entitled to a hearing and to have the Board decide the merits of such a claim in accordance with the procedures set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 7701).[3]

[2] To the extent that the Board's decisions in cases such as Foland v. Department of the Air Force, 75 M.S.P.R. 92 (1997), Begay v. Department of the Interior, 73 M.S.P.R. 532 (1997), and Smith v. Department of the Air Force, 73 M.S.P.R. 408 (1997) hold that an appellant must allege facts which, if proven, would establish a prima facie case of discrimination prohibited by the authorities listed under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a)(1)(B) in order to establish Board jurisdiction over an appeal that an appellant has elected to bring to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d), they are hereby overruled.

In its seminal decision, Ogden Air Logistics Center v. Thereafter, in Livingstone v. More recently, in Smith v. Because marital status discrimination is a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1), but is not prohibited by any of the authorities listed under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1)(B), Smith is not inconsistent with our decision in the instant case.

Before the 2005 Cooper decision, although the existence of an EEO allegation in an appeal that might otherwise be within the exclusive coverage of a contract preserved Board jurisdiction, there were occasional issues concerning the jurisdictional sufficiency of the allegation. The decisions in Foland, Begay, and Smith were overruled by Cooper. [Refer to Chapter 12, "Authority of Judge to Strike Frivolous EEO Allegation; Summary Judgment."]

McCann v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 288, 293-95 (1993), predating Cooper, supra, discussed the specificity of the allegations necessary to vest jurisdiction in the Board through a discrimination claim:

...In its response to the appeal, the agency contended that, because the collective bargaining agreement covering the appellant did not exclude reduction-in-force actions from the list of employment actions subject to the negotiated grievance procedure, and because he had not raised a claim of discrimination, the Board lacked jurisdiction over his appeal. The appellant, however, argues that he did allege discrimination below, on the bases of "age, reclassification, and seniority."

Under 5 USC 7121(a), the grievance procedures provided in the applicable collective bargaining agreement generally are the exclusive means of resolving disputes related to matters not excluded from the agreement's coverage. An aggrieved employee who has been "affected by" a personnel practice prohibited under 5 USC 2302(b)(1), however, may file an appeal with the Board concerning an action otherwise covered by the collective bargaining agreement. See 5 USC 7121(d). The Board has construed the words "affected by" to mean that the Board is not divested of jurisdiction by the terms of a collective bargaining agreement providing for exclusivity of remedy where an appellant alleges discrimination.[4] ...Personnel practices prohibited under section 2302(b)(1) include discrimination based on age. 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(B).[5]

[4] The appellant's rights in this appeal, then, are analogous to those of a probationary employee seeking to establish Board jurisdiction over his or her appeal. Such an employee may appeal his or her termination to the Board if he or she alleges that the termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status discrimination. See 5 CFR 315.806(b); Robinson v. Department of Justice, 48 MSPR 141, 143-44 (1991). In such cases, the Board exercises an initial jurisdiction, based on the mere allegation, to determine whether it does in fact have jurisdiction to proceed further and decide the case. See Stokes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 761 F.2d 682, 685 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The Board has interpreted the regulation's requirement of an allegation of discrimination to mean a nonfrivolous allegation. To meet the jurisdictional burden of proof requires more than "the mere filing of allegations," but a proffer of evidence sufficient to enable an administrative judge reasonably to conclude that they are not frivolous. See In re Stephens, 52 MSPR 522, 528 (1992).
