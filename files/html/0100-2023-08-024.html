1. Nonprecedential Decisions, Final Orders

Beginning in earnest in mid-2010, in an effort to reduce the number of "short form" final decisions and better inform parties of the reasons that the Board declined to grant a petition for review, the Board started to issue a large volume of nonprecedential final decisions, known as nonprecedential final orders (NPFO). A 2010 regulatory amendment provided for nonprecedential decisions that present reasoning that cannot be relied on as precedent. That development--as opposed to simply doing the same thing without calling the decisions nonprecedential--had no clear justification and results in lack of clarity in Board law and corresponding lack of reliable guidance on the law to AJs and to parties conducting research. There was the additional problem that the nonprecedential decisions were not posted on the Board's website (as are the precedential decisions) while those nonprecedential decisions were being posted on commercial reporting services. The Board addressed the limited (microscopic) outcry over its publication practices by placing the nonprecedential decisions on its website, along with their precedential siblings, at www.mspb.gov publishing an interim rule creating the nonprecedential final order system, at 75 Fed. Reg. 61321 (Oct. 5, 2010), informs us:

This amendment, adding a new paragraph (c) to 5 CFR 1201.117, which reflects recent changes in the Board's internal procedures, is intended to give the parties greater insight into the reasoning supporting the Board's decision in a particular case without requiring the Board to issue a precedential decision. The Board believes that including more information in its nonprecedential decisions will be beneficial to both appellants and agencies because both parties will more fully understand the Board's reasoning and have added assurance that the Board fully considered their arguments on appeal.

This amendment to 5 CFR 1201.117 also revises paragraph (b) to make clear that the Board may issue a final decision and, when appropriate, order a date for compliance with that decision.

See 76 Fed. Reg. 60706 (Sept. 30, 2011) (publishing the final order and responding to comments stemming from the 2010 Federal Register notice publicizing the interim rule).

The Board's development of nonprecedential decisions, the rule that they could not be cited in other cases, and the initial difficulty in locating them, led to expressions of some concerns from Board followers. Whether or not those concerns led to reconsideration of its policies, in October 2011 the Board revised its regulation defining the types of decisions, nonprecedential included, that it issues, 5 CFR 1201.117 (2022):

Board decisions; procedures for review or reopening.

(a) In any case that is reopened or reviewed, the Board may:

(1) Issue a decision that decides the case;

(2) Hear oral arguments;

(3) Require that briefs be filed;

(4) Remand the appeal so that the judge may take further testimony or evidence or make further findings or conclusions; or

(5) Take any other action necessary for final disposition of the case.

(b) The Board may affirm, reverse, modify, or vacate the initial decision of the judge, in whole or in part. The Board may issue a final decision and, when appropriate, order a date for compliance with that decision.

(c) The Board may issue a decision in the form of a precedential Opinion and Order or a nonprecedential Order.

(1) Opinion and Order. An Opinion and Order is a precedential decision of the Board and may be appropriately cited or referred to by any party.

(2) Nonprecedential Orders. A nonprecedential Order is one that the Board has determined does not add significantly to the body of MSPB case law. The Board may, in its discretion, include in nonprecedential Orders a discussion of the issue(s) to assist the parties in understanding the reason(s) for the Board's disposition in a particular appeal. Nonprecedential Orders are not binding on the Board or its administrative judges in any future appeals except when it is determined they have a preclusive effect on parties under the doctrines of res judicata (claim preclusion), collateral estoppel (issue preclusion), judicial estoppel, or law of the case. Parties may cite nonprecedential Orders, but such orders have no precedential value; the Board and its administrative judges are not required to follow or distinguish them in any future decisions. In contrast, a precedential decision issued as an Opinion and Order has been identified by the Board as significantly contributing to the Board's case law.

Cf. Murray v. OPM, NY-844E-21-0093-I-1 (NP 4/25/2023) ("Nonprecedential Board decisions and initial decisions have no precedential value and the administrative judge erred in relying on them.").

The Federal Register notice announcing the 2011 change in practice concerning nonprecedential orders is at https://www.mspb.gov/publicaffairs/federal_register_publications/76_FR_60706_Procedures_Regarding_Nonprecedential_Orders_646388.pdf.

After a five-year hiatus in decisions resulting from an absence of a Board quorum from January 2017 through March 2022, the Board regained its quorum and started issuing decisions. They are now either precedential or nonprecedential. Nonprecedential decisions come in two varieties: those that describe the case and analyze arguments presented on PFR (and some of these nonprecedential decisions are extensive); and those that decide the PFR through no more than a reference to the appeal, the PFR, and an abbreviated summary stating that no grounds were found for modifying the initial decision.

Many nonprecedential decisions are summarized in this Guide. The cited cases may include dissents or concurrences--and any decision that demonstrates division by the Board is worthy of public notice, not because of the division itself but because of the importance of Board members' different approaches to the law. Cited nonprecedential decisions often explain the law in a manner that makes it more readily understood. There are plenty of nonprecedential decisions that announce, directly or indirectly, intentionally or unintentionally, new points of law. There are precedential decisions that add nothing to the law. The decision by Board employees to classify a decision as precedential or nonprecedential depends on the extent of the knowledge of those employees of a body of law dating back to 1979. If someone is not aware of past decisions, it becomes more difficult to say that a current decision adds to the body of precedent. The nonprecedential and precedential decisions follow the same analysis and a similar review process, although a precedential decision may be reviewed or edited more intensively within the Board bureaucracy, and although factual descriptions in nonprecedential decisions may be attenuated. For the sake of establishing a reliable body of law, some individuals familiar with Board operations will press the Board, as its membership changes, to eliminate the distinction between nonprecedential and precedential decisions. See Ortiz v. DOJ, 103 MSPR 621, 627 ¶ 14, 2006 MSPB 305 (2006) ("The Board is a public body and the public has a right to be informed of the Board's decisions. See 5 U.S.C. § 552(a)(2)(A) (requiring that agencies make available, for public inspection and copying, their final opinions and orders made in the adjudication of cases). Moreover, our published opinions provide guidance to appellants, their representatives, and federal agencies as to how we might rule in similar cases in the future.").
