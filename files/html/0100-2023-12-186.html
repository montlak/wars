broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part o: Withdrawal, Absence of Representative
o. Withdrawal, Absence of Representative

Because the appellant is responsible for the acts of his counsel, withdrawal of an attorney before the PFR is due is no excuse for its lateness. Scott v. USPS, 52 MSPR 439, 441 (1992); see Walker v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-20-0334-I-2 (NP 5/5/2023) ("In addition, the appellant's argument that he was left 'powerless' by his former counsel's failure to communicate with him is unpersuasive. The Board has long held that an appellant is responsible for the actions or inaction of his chosen representative, and inadequate representation does not constitute good cause for a waiver of the Board's filing time limits. Young v. Department of Labor, 69 M.S.P.R. 695, 697 (1996); see also Reynolds v. Department of the Army, 23 M.S.P.R. 269, 270 (1984) (finding that any communication failure between the appellant and his attorney would not constitute a valid reason for untimely filing) (citing Sofio v. Internal Revenue Service, 7 M.S.P.R. 667, 670 (1981)), aff'd, 765 F.2d 162 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table)."); Boehning v. DOJ, DA-4324-16-0126-I-1 (NP 1/25/2023) ("Moreover, the appellant's arguments that his representative breached their side agreement and was suspended from the practice of law while representing him do not establish good cause for his untimely filing because the appellant is responsible for the errors of his chosen representative....Further, the appellant's assertions that his representative misrepresented his status as an attorney and failed to file a response to the agency file below, while unfortunate, do not establish good cause for his delay in filing....Indeed, even if the appellant could establish that his otherwise diligent efforts to prosecute his appeal were thwarted by the negligence or malfeasance of his representative, it would not excuse the over 8 months that he allowed to elapse after he first learned of his representative's alleged misconduct and the time he filed his petition."); Bolton v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-12-0515-I-1 (NP 12/23/2013) ("The appellant also asserts that his attorney informed him in a January 9, 2013 letter that she would not be filing a petition for review on his behalf. According to the appellant, up until that time "[h]e had a reasonable expectation that he was being represented." The appellant also asserts that his attorney did not send him information that he needed to file his petition for review until January 19, 2013. The appellant, however, does not provide a specific explanation of the information that his attorney possessed or why he was unable to file a petition for review during the more than 2-week period after his receipt of the information and the petition for review filing deadline."); Green v. Dept. of Air Force, 83 MSPR 333, 334-35 ¶ 6 (1999) (appellant's delay of two years in filing a PFR was not excused by the disbarment of his attorney), aff'd, Green v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3446) (declining to excuse a delay even though the appellant "had the enormously bad luck to retain an attorney who failed to represent him adequately"); Dominguez v. OPM, 47 MSPR 365, 367-68 (1991) ("The appellant has not shown why he could not have timely refiled the petition for review himself, or informed the Board prior to the deadline for filing that he needed additional time to secure a new representative, and that his appeal would be delayed because his new representative needed to become familiar with the area of Federal employment and personnel law."); Mills v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 403, 405 n.3 (1986) (agency whose representative took scheduled leave should have arranged for an extension to be requested for filing a response to the PFR); Hatcher v. USPS, 27 MSPR 471 (1985) (late PFR was not excused because the former representative had misrepresented himself as an attorney and had provided untimely, ineffective representation); McGovern v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 119, 120 (1984) (vacation of counsel should not have hindered the appellant's preparation of a PFR); cf. Tolton v. OPM, 14 MSPR 127, 128 (1982) (explanation that the appellant no longer had a representative and needed to familiarize himself with the facts and law of his case did not excuse a seven-week delay in filing).

Withdrawal of counsel by death does not necessarily excuse the failure to file a PFR on time. Balanay v. OPM, 47 MSPR 219, 221 (1991) (citing Baldos v. OPM, 36 MSPR 606, 608 (1988)); see Murphy v. Dept. of Treasury, 91 MSPR 239, 244 ¶ 8 (2002) (delay was not excused by the attorney's need to assist his wife during her illness); Cooper v. USPS, 70 MSPR 87, 90-91 (1996) (death in the family of the appellant's counsel: "[w]hile such an event might well constitute good cause for a late filing, he has not shown that it prevented him from at least timely noting a petition for review or informing the Board that he needed additional time and requesting an extension, nor that this event justified a filing delay of 36 days"); Bagalayos v. OPM, 65 MSPR 96, 98-99 (1994) (delay of two years in filing a PFR: the appellant did not explain when the representative passed away); Taylor v. USPS, 53 MSPR 27, 28-29 (1992) (death in the appellant's representative's family did not excuse an untimely PFR: it was unstated when the death occurred, when the representative left the area, when he returned, why no one else could have filed in his absence, or why the appellant could not have filed the PFR; no extension was requested); Baldos v. OPM, 36 MSPR 606, 608 (1988) (delay was not excused by the death of the representative more than two months after the date of the initial decision); Filamor v. OPM, 32 MSPR 217, 218 (1987) (delay in filing is not excused for an individual whose representative died eight months before the initial decision).

If an appellant's attorney withdraws from a case, the employee must act prudently by filing a PFR himself or by filing a request for an extension of time. Buchanan v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1659), aff'g Buchanan v. USPS, 21 MSPR 479, 480 (1984) ("Appellant's allegation that the delay was caused by his attorney's failure to contact him is without merit since appellant is responsible for the action or inaction of his chosen representative."). When the appellant's representative withdrew from the case two days before a PFR was due, and appellant delayed two and a half months before filing the PFR, the delay was not excused. The appellant could have filed a timely PFR. Aspinwall v. VA, 6 MSPR 93, 6 MSPB 90 (1981). [Refer to the subsections that follow concerning neglect of representatives.]

Agency protestations of understaffing did not excused a belated PFR, Bajrami v. VA, CH-0752-14-0376-I-1 (NP 9/20/2016):

The agency also asserts that it has been chronically understaffed since 2012, and has had five attorney vacancies until the new agency corepresentative started working there. The agency does not explain how large its office is, however. For example, there is a great deal of difference between an office with 5 out of 8 positions vacant, and 5 out of 20 positions vacant, or 5 out of 100 positions vacant. Therefore, the agency's argument that it is understaffed by an abstract number without providing any context for that number is not informative.

Aside from stating that it is understaffed, the agency proffers no reason why it could not have filed its petition for review or requested an extension of time before April 8, 2016, except that the agency representative bore a heavy caseload. The Board has held, however, that the "press of business," an attorney's heavy workload, and a shortage of staffing does not establish good cause. Lands v. Department of the Air Force, 95 M.S.P.R. 593, ¶ 7 (2004); Murphy v. Department of the Treasury, 91 M.S.P.R. 239, ¶ 8 (2002); Averella v. U.S. Postal Service, 64 M.S.P.R. 441, 443 (1994).
