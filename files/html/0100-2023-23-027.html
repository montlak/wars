a. Unemployment Compensation

Before an appellant's agreement not to initiate or pursue litigation in any forum relating to her employment with an agency is construed to waive a statutory right, e.g., unemployment compensation benefits, the waiver must be the result of an informed, intentional abandonment of a known right. Wethington v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 285, 286-88 (1988), concluded that the appellant's waiver of the "right to sue or appeal over matters raised in this appeal" was not a knowing relinquishment of the right to apply for unemployment compensation:

...The Board notes in this regard that, under the law of the State of Texas, no agreement by an individual to waive his rights to unemployment compensation benefits is valid....

...

The law is clear that a waiver of a right in a settlement agreement must be "comprehensive, freely made and fair."... Moreover, in order to be enforceable, a waiver of a statutory right must constitute "the informed, intentional relinquishment of a known right."...

The settlement agreement in the instant case does not provide for an explicit waiver of the appellant's right to apply for State of Texas unemployment compensation benefits. Nor does the agreement contain a provision showing that the appellant clearly understood what the waiver provision meant.... The terms of the settlement agreement disclose that the intent of the parties at the time that they contracted was to resolve the entire appeal before the Board.... Therefore, we find that the settlement agreement does not constitute a waiver of the appellant's right to apply for...unemployment compensation benefits.

The statutory right to benefits is not waived without a statement by the appellant indicating that he knew he was waiving his right to pursue unemployment benefits. Miura v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 663, 665, 668-69 & n.1 (1989) (citing Wethington v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 285, 287-88 (1988)). In Miura, the settlement provided that "[a]ppellant agrees that he shall neither initiate nor pursue the litigation in any forum (MSPB, EEOC, etc.) of any matter related to his employment with the Agency." The waiver was ineffective as to the appellant's claim for unemployment compensation:

A settlement agreement is enforceable and will not be set aside by the Board provided it is not tainted with invalidity, either by fraud or by a mutual mistake with regard to a material fact under which both parties acted, see, e.g., Asberry v. United States Postal Service, 692 F.2d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1982); Tucker v. United States Postal Service, 35 MSPR 376, 377-378 (1987), and if it is a final decision under the Board's appellate jurisdiction which is made part of the record. See Richardson v. Environmental Protection Agency, 5 MSPB 289, 5 MSPR 248, 250 (1981). Further, the law is clear that a settlement agreement is a contract and that the interpretation of its terms is a question of law. See Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Therefore, before the Board can determine whether to grant the agency's request for compliance, it must be determined, in accordance with law, whether the settlement agreement is enforceable and whether paragraph 5 constitutes a waiver of the appellant's right to pursue unemployment insurance benefits with the State of Hawaii.

In construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the words of the agreement itself are of paramount importance in order to determine the intent of the parties at the time that they contracted. See Wethington v. Department of the Army, 39 MSPR 285 (1988). If the terms of the agreement are ambiguous, parol evidence is permissible. See Greco, 852 F.2d at 560. We find that in this case the terms of paragraph 5 in the agreement are clear and unambiguous and that parol evidence is unnecessary. Indeed, the Board has found that a very similar paragraph in the Wethington case was not ambiguous.[4] However, in finding the terms of the agreement unambiguous, we also find that the settlement agreement did not constitute a waiver of the appellant's right to seek State of Hawaii unemployment compensation benefits.

[4] The settlement agreement in the Wethington case provided in pertinent part:

The appellant agrees that he waives his right to sue or appeal over matters raised in this appeal and they will not be the subject of future litigation. Wethington, 39 MSPR at 287.

In the analogous case of Wethington, the Board found that an agreement to "waive [one's] right to sue or appeal over matters raised in [the appeal of the disciplinary action]" did not include waiving one's right to pursue unemployment insurance benefits. In Wethington, because the agreement did not specify that the appellant waived his right to unemployment compensation, the appellant did not effect a waiver of this right.

Specifically, in order to effectuate an enforceable waiver of a statutory right in a settlement agreement or contract, it must be the result of an informed, intentional abandonment of a known right, free of any coercion or duress. See McCall v. United States Postal Service, 839 F.2d 664, 668 (Fed. Cir. 1988), citing Ferby v. United States Postal Service, 26 MSPR 451, 456 (1985). See also Riese v. United States Postal Service, 37 MSPR 364, 366-367 (1988); Roberson v. Veterans Administration, 32 MSPR 173, 175 (1987). Here, as in Wethington, the right to unemployment insurance benefits is statutory. Indeed, as pointed out by the appellant, it is against public policy in Hawaii to agree to waive one's right to benefits. Further, as in Riese and Wethington, there is no explicit waiver of this right. Here, there is no statement signed by the appellant indicating that he knew that he was waiving his right to pursue unemployment benefits, and his many submissions to the Board, including his response to the agency's petition for enforcement, support the fact that he did not know he was waiving this right in signing the settlement agreement. Therefore, we find that the settlement agreement does not constitute a waiver of the appellant's right to pursue his claim for State of Hawaii unemployment compensation benefits. See Wethington, 39 MSPR at 288.

Further, because paragraph 5 does not constitute a waiver of unemployment insurance rights, the settlement agreement is not "tainted with invalidity" by having a term inconsistent with Hawaiian law. Thus, the settlement agreement entered into the record is enforceable by the Board.

We find no merit to the agency's arguments that the appellant's right to unemployment benefits never vested because the state of Hawaii was bound by the Federal findings of the appellant's Federal employer regarding his termination. If the State is bound by Federal findings, that fact does not preclude the appellant from pursuing unemployment benefits. Rather, it is up to the state of Hawaii to determine that, in light of the Federal findings, the appellant is ineligible for benefits.

A waiver of unemployment compensation benefits again surfaced in Cianteo v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 40, 43-44 (1993), when the Board construed a settlement waiving the right to "seek any type of relief and/or damages against the [a]gency" except for workers' compensation and disability retirement claims. The waiver did not preclude an unemployment compensation claim:

The Board has previously considered the question of an appellant's waiver of his right to unemployment compensation benefits in a settlement agreement. See Miura v. Department of the Navy, 39 MSPR 663, 668-69 (1989); Wethington v. Department of the Army, 39 MSPR 285, 287 (1988). In those appeals, the Board held that the waiver of a statutory right to unemployment compensation benefits under State law must be comprehensive, freely made, fair, and must constitute the informed, intentional relinquishment of a known right. Miura, 39 MSPR at 668-69; Wethington, 39 MSPR at 287. Further, we note that, like Hawaii law, which applied in Miura, and Texas law, which applied in Wethington, Indiana law, which applies here, provides that "any agreement by an individual to waive, release or commute his rights" to unemployment compensation benefits is void as against public policy. Ind. Code Ann. 22-4-33-1 (Burns 1991); see Ind. Code Ann. 22-4-1-1 (Burns 1991)....

Although the appellant here waived his right to seek any relief and/or damages against the agency with regard to any violation of law, rule, or regulation, except for workers' compensation and disability retirement claims, the settlement agreement does not contain an explicit waiver of the appellant's right to apply for State unemployment compensation benefits. Nor does the agreement contain a provision showing that the appellant made an informed and knowing waiver of his statutory right to unemployment compensation benefits. Therefore, we find that the settlement agreement does not constitute a waiver of the appellant's right to apply for State of Indiana unemployment compensation benefits....

Cases involving challenges to waivers of unemployment compensation through settlements were summarized, Jones v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-12-0426-X-1 (NP 8/13/2013):

In deciding whether unemployment benefits are waived under a negotiated settlement agreement, the Board considers whether the law of the state in which the appellant was employed voided any waiver of a person's right to unemployment compensation. See, e.g., Cianteo v. Department of the Navy, 59 M.S.P.R. 40, 44 (1993), aff'd, 34 F.3d 1079 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Miura v. Department of the Navy, 39 M.S.P.R. 663, 669 (1989); Wethington v. Department of the Army, 39 M.S.P.R. 285, 286 (1988). Here, the appellant worked for the agency in Georgia and filed for unemployment benefits in Georgia, a state which voids any waiver of a person's right to unemployment compensation. Ga. Code. Ann. § 34-8-250; see Ga. Code. Ann. § 34-8-195.

To the extent that the settlement agreement requires the appellant to withdraw her claim for unemployment compensation benefits in the state of Georgia, we find that it is void. Accordingly, we find no duty by the appellant to withdraw her claim for unemployment compensation benefits. We find that the parties intended to enforce the remainder of the settlement to remain in effect should this provision be voided. The settlement agreement thus otherwise remains in effect. See American Sav. Bank, F.A. v. United States, 519 F.3d 1316, 1325 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (the court will "consider the language of a contract and the intent of the parties in determining whether a contract is divisible"); see also 15 Williston on Contracts § 45:7 ("[t]he parties' intent to enter into a divisible contract may be expressed...through a so-called 'severability clause'").
