a. Assigned Post of Duty; Travel Requirement

To support an AWOL charge, "the agency must show both that the appellant was absent and that either the absence was not authorized or that a request for leave was properly denied." Cooke v. USPS, 67 MSPR 401, 404 (1995); see Dobert v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 148, 150 (1997) (same analysis as Cooke). The usual basis for an AWOL charge is absence from the work site. However, if the employer directs an employee to report to a site away from his usual post of duty (for example, a training program) and the employee refuses, reporting to his regular job site instead, AWOL is an appropriate charge and serves as a basis for removal. Trent v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 83-1317). Futrell-Rawls v. VA, DC-0752-10-0268-B-1 (NP 6/22/2011), noted:

In challenging the administrative judge's finding sustaining the AWOL charge, the appellant argues that she was not absent because, on some of the dates charged, she was "in the building." Presumably, she means to say that she was in the Pulmonary Section during some portion of the time charged. However, the appellant has offered no support for her claim that an AWOL charge can only be sustained when the employee is not in the building to which she is assigned, and we are aware of none. Cf. Howard v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 422, 424-25 (1996) (Board sustained agency's AWOL charge where the appellant was found asleep in his Postal vehicle). To prove a charge of AWOL, the agency must show that an employee was absent, and either that his absence was not authorized or that his request for leave was properly denied. Boscoe v. Department of Agriculture, 54 M.S.P.R. 315, 325 (1992). The appellant has not shown error in the administrative judge's findings that, on the dates charged, she was not in the Respiratory Section as she had been instructed, her presence in the Pulmonary Section on two specific dates was not authorized, and she had not requested leave for any of the dates cited. Therefore, the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge erred in sustaining the AWOL charge.

See Rodriguez v. USDA, 27 MSPR 79, 83 (1985) ("As a general proposition, an extension on reporting for permanent reassignment does not relieve an employee, during the period of the extension, from being temporarily detailed to another area."); Murray v. Dept. of Commerce, 22 MSPR 429, 431 (1984) (charge was refusal to accept a directed reassignment, but "[s]ince the appellant was not at his new duty station and not working, the agency could consider him in an absence without leave status."); Sweet v. DHHS, 14 MSPR 168, 172 (1982) ("[A]ppellant signed in and was physically present at his prior duty station on an almost daily basis even though he had been directed by his supervisors to report to the new work site. Under these circumstances, the agency cannot be faulted for placing appellant in an AWOL status."); cf. Carter v. DOD, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP) (if the agency establishes that an employee should have teleworked under agency continuity of operations regulations during the pandemic and did not, the resultant AWOL-based removal can be sustained); Rose v. DHHS, 721 F.2d 355 (Fed. Cir. 1983) ("For purposes of determining whether a suspension has occurred, we concluded that assignment to an inappropriate position may not be equated with deprivation of duties within the meaning of 5 USC 7501(2)."); Evans v. USPS, SF-0752-10-0966-I-2 (NP 6/19/2012) ("[A] fundamental requirement of every position is that an employee be where he is supposed to be when he is on duty."). But see Rivas v. USPS, 62 MSPR 480, 483 (1994) (removal for failure to report to the job site was reversed when the employee was actually constructively suspended at the time he was ordered to report to work).

Failure to abide by a rotation agreement and return to the United States to fill a position there warranted dismissal. Finnigan v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3339). AWOL also encompasses the conduct of employees who do not leave the agency premises, but instead take unauthorized trips to other locations for personal reasons, e.g., to visit the agency canteen. Meads v. VA, 36 MSPR 574, 579 (1988). Discipline was warranted for failure to accept travel assignments in SSA v. Boham, 38 MSPR 540, 546 (1988), an ALJ disciplinary case:

The respondent, however, does not have a right to be exempted from assignments which required travel since there is no inherent right for employees to be excused from inconvenient work assignments. Employees may seek and management may, in its discretion, grant such exceptions. For this Board to judge the reasonableness of such agency actions, as respondent urges, on hindsight would unduly limit an agency's right to assign work by unnecessarily impeding its ability to insure the performance of its mission.

DOD, CH-0752-12-0464-I-1 (NP 5/30/2013) ("The agency demoted the appellant when he failed to meet a condition of employment by refusing to deploy overseas in support of military operations. On appeal to the Board, the administrative judge affirmed the demotion. The Board's standard of review in cases involving an employee's failure to meet a condition of employment is fairly deferential. The Board has held that it defers to the agency's determination as to the requirements that must be fulfilled in order for an individual to qualify for appointment to and retention in a particular position, absent evidence of bad faith or patent unfairness.
