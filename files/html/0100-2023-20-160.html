22. Action Affecting Private Sector Employment Opportunity

Pasley v. Dept. of Treasury, 109 MSPR 105, 110-11 ¶ 10, 2008 MSPB 122 (2008), opined that a federal employer's reprisal against a former employee through pressure upon a subsequent private sector employer to terminate the employee was beyond the WPA's remedial scope:

The appellant asserts that the AJ erred in finding that the Board lacks jurisdiction over his allegation that he was terminated from his position with the Bank of America in reprisal for protected whistleblowing. However, we find no error, and affirm the AJ's determination on this issue. "Under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3), an employee, former employee, or applicant for employment may appeal to the Board from agency personnel actions alleged to have been threatened, proposed, taken, or not taken because of the appellant's whistleblowing activities." 5 C.F.R. § 1209.2(a) (emphasis added). "Personnel actions" for the purposes of an IRA appeal are codified at 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A). See Weber v. General Services Administration, 54 M.S.P.R. 444, 445-46 (1992), aff'd, 989 F.2d 1203 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). The listed "personnel actions" include the following: (1) An appointment; (2) a promotion; (3) an action under 5 U.S.C. Chapter 75 or other disciplinary or corrective action; (4) a detail, transfer, or reassignment; (5) a reinstatement; (6) a restoration; (7) a reemployment; (8) a performance evaluation under 5 U.S.C. Chapter 43; (9) a decision concerning pay, benefits, or awards, or concerning education or training if the education or training may reasonably be expected to lead to an appointment, promotion, performance evaluation; (10) a decision to order psychiatric testing or examination; and (11) any other significant change in duties, responsibilities, or working conditions. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A). This subsection further states that the listed actions must be "taken with respect to an employee in, or applicant for, a covered position in an agency, and in the case of an alleged prohibited personnel practice described in subsection (b)(8), an employee or applicant for employment in a Government corporation as defined in section 9101 of title 31." Id. Here, the appellant's termination would technically fall within the listed actions. However, because it was taken by the Bank of America, a private sector employer, it does not meet the definition of "personnel action" since it was not taken with respect to an employee in a covered position in an agency or a governmental corporation. 5 U.S.C. §§ 2105; 2302(a)(2)(B), (a)(2)(C); 31 U.S.C. § 9101. We, therefore, conclude that the AJ correctly dismissed this claim for lack of jurisdiction.

But cf. Mattil v. Dept. of State, 118 MSPR 662, 673-74 ¶ 23, 2012 MSPB 127 (2012) ("Although 'blacklisting' per se is not an enumerated personnel action under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A), construed broadly, it could constitute a failure to appoint, which would be properly before the Board in an IRA appeal if the appellant identifies particular employment opportunities that the agency denied as part of the alleged 'blacklisting.' 5 U.S.C. §§ 2302(a)(2)(A)(i), 2302(b)(8).").

Civil rights and labor relations statutes are generally construed to prevent reprisal in the form of a bad employment reference or interference with future employment. One might reasonably believe that a condition of employment is a reasonable reference (and avoidance of interference with future employment) after fully satisfactory government service. A "significant change" in "working conditions" would reasonably be expected to encompass interference with future employment. Until the Board changes its position or the Federal Circuit changes the Board's position, the result reached in Pasley will need to be addressed through a statutory amendment expanding the scope of actions deemed retaliatory under the WPA.
