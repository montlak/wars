c. Report, Investigation

If as part of the reply process the deciding official conducts a supplemental investigation, there is no due process error if the investigation results are shared with the appellant and the appellant has the opportunity to respond to the new information, Burks v. R.R. Retirement Bd., CH-0752-10-0197-I-1 (NP 6/2/2011):

The appellant objects to a "supplemental investigation" conducted by the deciding official. However, the record shows that the deciding official provided the appellant with additional information used to support the agency's proposal that was not originally made available to him and that the appellant responded. Procedural due process guarantees are not met if the employee has notice of only certain charges or portions of the evidence and the deciding official considers new and material information. Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1999). However, in this case the agency gave the appellant the additional information at issue. There is no evidence of an improper supplemental investigation by the deciding official.

Under decisions predating Stone, as to ex parte investigative reports provided to an agency deciding official, the employee would not prevail on an allegation that his ability to defend himself was impaired unless he showed that the deciding official was influenced by the new information in the decisional process, that the consideration of the new information likely had a harmful effect on the outcome before the agency, or that there was a violation of an OPM or agency regulation with harmful effect. Forrester v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 450, 455 (1985). It was not error for a deciding official to review documents not disclosed to the appellant if the action is not based on a consideration of those documents. Chappelle v. Dept. of Labor, 27 MSPR 352, 355 (1985); see Santos v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 694, 698 (1993) ("In light of the deciding official's belief that the serious nature of the appellant's misconduct alone merited removal...it is unlikely that the allegations contained in the second police report had any effect on the outcome of the removal action before the agency."). It was not harmful error for an agency deciding official to look at investigatory material that alluded to action not charged against the appellant if there was no proof that the deciding official relied on the information in reaching his decision. Book v. USPS, 6 MSPR 378, 381, 6 MSPB 322 (1981); see Daellenbach v. VA, CH-0752-15-0318-I-2 (NP 10/19/2022) ("in addition to the summary of investigation, the deciding official was aware of other information that was not included in the disciplinary file...the appellant has failed to produce any evidence to contradict the deciding official's testimony that she relied only on information in the disciplinary file in deciding his removal. Therefore, we find that he has failed to prove that the deciding official violated his due process rights by considering ex parte information. See Villareal v. Bureau of Prisons, 901 F.3d 1361, 1365-66 (Fed. Cir. 2018) (finding no due process violation, in part, because the deciding official did not rely on the ex parte information when making his decision on the removal).").
