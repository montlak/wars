8. Undue Hardship

Assuming the appellant's affirmative defense survives all the other tests identified by the Board, the agency may attempt to establish that the suggested accommodation presents an undue hardship upon agency operations. If this argument is accepted by the Board, the accommodation is then no longer reasonable and the affirmative defense is doomed. Because so few cases involving drug or alcohol abuse make it past the qualified disabled employee requirement, Board case law on undue hardship in these cases is scant.

Insufficient accommodation was extended to an electrician removed for lack of a driver's license. The license was suspended because the employee refused a breath test following an off-duty traffic accident. The appellant pleaded alcoholism and asked for an accommodation. Electricians worked in two-man teams and coworkers were willing to do the driving. Appellant asked that others be permitted to drive and that he be relieved of that responsibility during the license suspension. The Board decided that because coworkers could do the driving, the appellant's ability to drive was not critical to his job. The disability was subject to accommodation. Because other employees managed to get by without a license, the agency was unable to demonstrate any undue hardship from the accommodation. The appellant was pursuing rehabilitation; he had a good work history; and there was no indication that he drank at work or that his drinking problems otherwise interfered with his job. The removal was reversed. Korb v. Dept. of Army, 20 MSPR 338, 341-42 (1984). But see Malbouf v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 588, 592 (1990) (appellant was not a qualified disabled employee when he refused to take a breathalyzer test--there being no evidence that he was impaired when he refused the test and then lost the driver's license needed to perform his job).

The Board analyzed an agency's responsibility to demonstrate burden as to accommodation as to a nurse who was a drug addict and who used drugs on the agency premises. After entering a rehabilitation program and during the notice period, she was detailed to another area where she would not have access to drugs. There the employee performed satisfactorily through the date of her removal. The agency did not show that a continuation of that detail, a temporary assignment to another position, a modification of the work schedule or duties of the job, or placement of the appellant in approved leave status during the approved rehabilitation program, imposed a hardship on agency operations. The Board merged an analysis of risk and undue burden or hardship, Green v. Dept. of Air Force, 31 MSPR 152, 156-57 (1986):

[S]ince we find that appellant was a qualified handicapped individual, she was entitled to accommodation for her condition.[6] ...

[6] We note that other recent Board decisions have required the agency to accommodate handicapped employees in situations where potential threats to the safety of others were arguably present. See, e.g., Friel v. Department of the Navy, 29 MSPR 216 (1985) (where the Board rejected the agency's contention that the armed nature of appellant's position as a Police Officer precluded accommodation for his alcoholism); Marren v. Department of Justice, 29 MSPR 118 (1985) (where the Board held that the agency's failure to allow a Border Patrol Agent suffering from alcoholism to conclude his rehabilitation and return to work constituted handicap discrimination, notwithstanding the agency's claim that accommodation would impose an undue hardship on its program because of the armed nature of the employee's position).

...

The Board recently held in Averill v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 327 (1986), that the agency need not prove actual harm to substantiate its allegation of risk with regard to a handicapped employee. Instead, the agency is required to demonstrate only that its assertion of risk is made in good faith and is supported by evidence. In order to adequately support its assertion of risk, an agency must gather relevant information regarding the employee's work and medical history and independently assess whether the handicap could result in a reasonable probability of substantial harm to the handicapped employee or to others at the workplace. See Mantolete v. Bolger, 767 F.2d 1416, 1422 (9th Cir. 1985). We find that the agency has inadequately supported its assertion of risk in the instant case.

Although the use of drugs by an employee under a different set of circumstances may well constitute an intolerable risk justifying the agency's denial of accommodation, we are unable to find such a risk has been shown in this case. We find, instead, that appellant's particular drug addiction and the apparent lack of potentially hazardous outward manifestations associated with it did not pose a high risk to the patients under her care. The undisputed evidence of record reflects that appellant had safely and effectively performed the essential functions of her position during an extensive period while she was addicted to drugs.... Moreover, preponderant evidence also demonstrated that appellant had been drug-free for a period in excess of six months and had been a highly successful participant in the drug rehabilitation program. The presiding official concluded that the agency had failed to establish that accommodating appellant by allowing her to participate in and successfully complete an agency approved rehabilitation program would have involved an unacceptable risk either to appellant or those patients under her care....
