i. Reassignment

Failure to grant a requested reassignment qualifies as denial of a benefit under USERRA, Eberhart v. USPS, 88 MSPR 398, 400-02 ¶¶ 7-13 (2001):

The term "benefit of employment" is defined, for purposes of USERRA, as meaning "any advantage, profit, privilege, gain, status, account, or interest (other than wages or salary for work performed that accrues by reason of an employment contract or agreement or an employer policy, plan, or practice," including "rights and benefits under a pension plan, a health plan, an employee stock ownership plan, insurance coverage and awards, bonuses, severance pay, supplemental unemployment benefits, vacations, and the opportunity to select work hours or location of employment." 38 U.S.C. § 4303(2).

In concluding that the reassignment the appellant sought here was not a "benefit of employment," the administrative judge noted that the definition limited the term to benefits that "accrue[d]." He stated that the term "accrues" was similar in meaning to the terms "vest" and "vested"; that all three terms "denote[d] a present right"; and that the term "accrues" connoted "a sense of entitlement." Id. He then found that consideration for noncompetitive selection had not been shown to be a benefit to which the appellant was entitled; that the appellant also had not shown that any "employment contract or agreement," or any "employer policy, plan, or practice," entitled him to selection during the competitive process; that he therefore had not shown that he had "accrued" a right to reassignment; that any right he had to compete for the Freeland position had been afforded him; and that he accordingly had not shown that he had been denied a "benefit of employment" under USERRA.

Under some circumstances, the use of the term "accrue" could serve as an indication that the matters "accrued" are limited to matters to which the employee has a right. See Black's Law Dictionary (7th ed. 1999) (defining "accrue" as "[t]o come into existence as an enforceable claim or right. We note, however, that the Board has consistently construed the term "benefit of employment" as having an expansive meaning. It did so, as the appellant notes in his petition for review, in Petersen v. Department of the Interior, 71 MSPR 227, 236-38 (1996).... In that case, it indicated that language in a previous version of USERRA differed somewhat from the final version; that the previous version "defined 'benefit' as any aspect of the employment relationship provided by contract or employer practice or custom that offers 'advantage, profit, gain"'; and that this difference appeared to be "only editorial," rather than substantive. Petersen, 71 MSPR at 236 n.7. The Board also referred in that decision to legislative history indicating that "all attributes of the employment relationship" that were affected by absence due to military service were to be included among the benefits covered by USERRA. Id. at 236.

Our reviewing court has expressed approval of the broad construction adopted in Petersen, and it has indicated that a "benefit" covered by USERRA is "one that flows as a result of the person's employment...." Yates v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 145 F.3d 1480, 1484-85 (Fed. Cir. 1998); see Thomsen v. Department of the Treasury, 169 F.3d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1999).

Moreover, we note that the legislative history of USERRA shows an intent that case law applying predecessor legislation protecting the employment rights of members of the uniformed services would "remain[ ] in full force and effect in interpreting" USERRA's provisions, to the extent that it was consistent with those provisions. H.R. Rep. No. 103-158, at 19 (1993), reprinted in 1994 USCCAN 2449, 2452. The House report setting forth this legislative history noted that this was "particularly true of the basic principle established by the Supreme Court that the Act [was] to be 'liberally construed,"' and it then cited, inter alia, Fishgold v. Sullivan Drydock and Repair Corp., 328 U.S. 275, 285, 66 S. Ct. 11059 90 L. Ed. 1230 (1946). Id. In Fishgold, 328 U.S. at 285, 66 S. Ct. 1105, the U.S. Supreme Court indicated that it was responsible for construing earlier legislation protecting the employment rights of returning veterans "as parts of an organic whole," and for "giv[ing] each [part] as liberal a construction for the benefit of the veteran as a harmonious interplay of the separate provisions permits." In doing so, it followed "the familiar canon of statutory construction that remedial legislation should be construed broadly to effectuate its purpose." Tcherepnin v. Knight, 389 U.S. 332, 336, 88 S. Ct. 5489 19 L. Ed.2d 564 (1967).

In addition, we note that the inclusion of the phrase "the opportunity to select...location of employment" among the "benefit[s] of employment" covered by USERRA, see 38 U.S.C. § 4303(2), suggests an intention to include the benefit at issue in this case. For this reason, and in light of the legislative history and the Board and court decisions cited above, we hold that the Board's jurisdiction under USERRA includes the denial of a request for reassignment to a position in a different location, whether the employee can show that he has a right to such a reassignment or not.

Finally, we note that our holding here is consistent with our decision in Melvin v. United States Postal Service, 79 MSPR 372 (1998). In that case, the appellant was a transitional employee who claimed that the agency separated him without offering him a casual appointment while it retained and offered casual appointments to nonveteran transitional employees. Id. at 375-77. Although the administrative judge dismissed that USERRA appeal for lack of jurisdiction without holding a hearing, the Board stated that "[b]oth retention as a transitional employee and appointment to a casual position [could] be viewed as a 'benefit of employment' given the breadth of [the] definition" of that term. Id. at 376-77. Nothing in Melvin indicates that the appellant in that case had any entitlement to be offered or considered for a casual appointment.[2]
