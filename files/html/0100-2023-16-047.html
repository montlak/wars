13. Classification, Significance of Error

If there are multiple critical elements or multiple standards within the elements, one may argue that a given error should be classified to the element or standard most closely describing the error. The classification of an error becomes significant if a given element permits a set number of errors during a rating period. The Board allows agencies flexibility. The failure of a supervisor to instruct a subordinate properly as to a security clearance matter might arguably be properly pigeonholed in an element requiring the appellant to follow proper security procedures rather than in an element and standards calling for the appellant to exercise supervisory functions properly by telling employees what is expected of them. Parham v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 207, 212 (1989), without much discussion, the appears to conclude that the test for considering the significance of an error is not whether the error is more appropriately classified into another critical element or performance standard; instead, the test seems to be whether there is a relation between the charged error and the standard or element at issue. See Sherrell v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 534, 540 (1991) ("[A] performance standard is not impermissibly vague if a work assignment can be applied to more than one critical element.").

There may be a concern, however, about how a supervisor can determine that a performance classified as "met" or "exceeded" under one standard fails under another standard describing similar tasks with similar adjectives. Smith v. Dept. of Energy, 49 MSPR 110, 116 (1991), implied that if the same performance of the same tasks under similar standards but different elements results in different levels of appraisal, something is not right. Either the supervisor's testimony or other evidence concerning the performance does not rise to the level of substantial evidence or the standards are void for vagueness. In a concurring opinion, Chairman Levinson criticized this result, suggesting that the only analysis is of the element serving as the basis of the action.

Deference to the agency's evaluation of performance under standards was emphasized, Crider v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0432-14-0451-I-1 (NP 4/29/2016):

Although the appellant challenges the agency's assessment of the precise number of errors she made, she has neither alleged nor shown that she routinely met the standard of no more than two errors per document or report or that she did not repeat certain errors. And while, in her view, the errors were petty, constituting only minor discrepancies, the Board generally defers to managerial discretion in determining what agency employees must do to perform acceptably in their positions. See Jackson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 97 M.S.P.R. 13, ¶ 14 (2004). Bearing in mind that the agency's burden of proof in a performance action taken under chapter 43 is relatively low, substantial evidence, 5 U.S.C. § 4302(b), the appellant has not shown error in the administrative judge's finding that the specific and well-documented information regarding the appellant's numerous errors and omissions constitutes more than substantial evidence that she performed unacceptably during the PIP period in the five tasks under the "Technical" critical element.

Manning v. DHS, 104 MSPR 533, 539 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 23 (2007), rejected an attempt by an agency, having taken a Chapter 43 action, to recharacterize the deficiencies charged to the appellant at the PFR level or on remand of the case:

However, because the Board is required to review the agency's decision on an adverse action solely on the grounds invoked by the agency, we must reject the agency's suggestion that, upon remand, the AJ, if he finds that these incidents would not have resulted in a negative impact on project timelines or project cost, can consider the incidents charged under the first category of errors as incidents meeting the lower threshold of the second category of errors. See Gottlieb v. Veterans Administration, 39 M.S.P.R. 606, 609 (1989) (the Board may not substitute what it considers to be a more adequate or proper basis for the grounds charged by the agency)....
