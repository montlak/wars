D. BAD FAITH

The concept of agency bad faith was the wellspring supporting the early evolution of fee entitlement criteria when merits decisions made no finding of discrimination or prohibited personnel practices. The Allen criteria are really variations on a theme, involving a continuum from bad faith to gross negligence. It is difficult to draw distinctions between the standards of "bad faith," "wholly or clearly without merit," "knew or should have known," and "substantial innocence." To determine how a case can be characterized, all the sections discussing standards must be examined. The appellant who files a fee petition should state claims under as many different standards as appear applicable to require the AJ to distinguish those standards and improve the prospects for obtaining fees under one of them. Subdivide and conquer. We begin our discussion with agency bad faith.

Decisions reflect an evolution in counsel fee awards, from an analysis that punishes agency "bad faith," i.e., a myth that somehow evil managers will be chastened by taxpayer-paid fee awards and thereafter correct their ways, to an analysis that focuses more on compensating the appellant for the significant expense of prosecuting a Board appeal, regardless of whether the underlying agency action is motivated by the best or the worst intentions of agency managers. The transition in focus was principally the result of Federal Circuit rulings. The transition is still incomplete. The evolution of the law should be recognized as fee petitions, and oppositions to those petitions, are presented.

Bad faith is one criterion for fee recovery. The Board has not provided an encompassing definition of bad faith, preferring to concern itself with facts of individual appeals. A concept runs through civil service law, expressed by the Federal Circuit, that "it is well settled that in order to show bad faith on the part of a Government official, the petitioner must submit 'well-nigh irrefragable proof' to sustain the charge, as it is presumed that Government officials act in an appropriate and lawful manner in the discharge of their duties." McEachern v. OPM, 776 F.2d 1539 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Most do, but some do not, as the case reports indicate; there would be no Guide to write if all government officials acted appropriately and lawfully all the time. The Board has not adopted the phrasing of the Federal Circuit in its fee decisions, and it is just as well, for the burden placed on an appellant would be well-nigh insuperable.

The Board treats bad faith as the equivalent of arbitrary or malicious action, as good a description as any for so broad a test. The Board does not require that the bad faith needed to justify counsel fees equate to a prohibited personnel practice, Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 119, 121 (1987):

In assessing a claim that fees are warranted because of agency bad faith, the Board does not look to the statutory provisions with respect to prohibited personnel practices, 5 USC § 2302(b). Rather, it takes guidance from decisions of the federal courts in comparable situations. There, awards of fees have been found warranted when the losing party has acted in bad faith, vexatiously, wantonly, or for oppressive reasons. Allen, 2 MSPR at 433; see also Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society, 421 U.S. 240, 258-59, 95 S. Ct. 1612, 1622, 44 L. Ed.2d 141 (1975). The Board has noted, however, that its discretion to award fees under the Allen categories is considerably broader than the equitable discretion typically exercised by the courts. Allen, 2 MSPR at 434.[2]

Bad faith must ordinarily be attributable to the respondent against whom fees are sought. A fee award will not be assessed against OPM in a retirement case as a consequence of problems created for the appellant by his employer before retirement or as a result of the agency's assistance--by providing information--to OPM during the retirement appeal. The Board's authority is limited to requiring "the agency involved" in the appealed action to pay counsel fees to a prevailing appellant when the award is in the interest of justice. Gonczi v. OPM, 41 MSPR 671, 674 (1989).
