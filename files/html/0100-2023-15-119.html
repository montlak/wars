3. Proof of Participation

The agency's burden of proof of strike participation may be described as a burden of "persuasion." The agency bears the burden of persuasion from start to finish. Schapansky v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 735 F.2d 477 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Under Schapansky, proof that the employee has a specific intent to participate in an agreed strike plan is not required; proof of general intent is sufficient, demonstrated by unexplained absence during a strike of general knowledge. Picketing is not a necessary element of proof of strike participation. Picket line participation is, however, some evidence of striking. Under Johnson v. FAA, 13 MSPR 652, 655 n.2 (1982), that appellant's absence without leave during the strike and the agency's introduction of pictures showing him among picketers, unexplained by the appellant, established a prima facie case of his voluntary participation in the strike. Under Schapansky, once the agency has made a prima facie showing, the burden of going forward with the evidence to rebut that showing shifts to the employee, who is in the best position to present explanatory evidence. The burden on the employee is one of a burden of production of evidence, not of persuasion. The agency must ultimately establish the employee's participation in the strike. If the employee fails to rebut the evidence offered by the employer, the agency carries its burden of persuasion. The intent to strike under Schapansky is sufficiently proved by circumstantial evidence that may be more persuasive and conclusive than direct evidence. The circumstantial evidence was produced through an unrebutted prima facie case by the agency.

An employee who did not intend to strike could be expected to advise his employer of the true reasons for his absence at the earliest possible moment, lacking evidence of extraordinary circumstances preventing that communication. McPartland v. FAA, 14 MSPR 506, 508 (1983), interpreted Schapansky to mean that an agency could establish a prima facie case of strike participation by proving that an employee's absence during a strike was unauthorized. That conclusion was reaffirmed in Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 72, 90 n.7 (1983). To demonstrate the continuation of a strike, an agency need not present evidence proving that there existed on a certain date a specified number of coworkers, mutually withholding their services, with whom an appellant could affirmatively interact. Activity is concerted as long as the individual's activity contributed to a concerted effort. Noa v. FAA, 15 MSPR 126, 138 (1983). A plurality of actors is not a prerequisite to a finding of concerted activity. Johnstone v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 615, 618 n.11 (1984).

On a charge amounting to encouragement of a "sick-out," Wilson v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 287, 296-97 (1995), suggested that there should be evidence either that the appellant actively persuaded others to call in sick, or that others acted on the appellant's statements:

In the second removal action, the agency charged the appellant as follows:

Specifically, on May 21, 1993, while assigned to midnight shift, you contacted [Correctional Officer] Bernadette Simmons and you talked to her about calling in sick. You stated "We shouldn't take this, we should call in sick."

Additionally, on or about May 22, 1993, while working the morning watch shift, you contacted [Correctional Officer] David Quinn and attempted to convince him to call in sick the next day. Both Officers reported you were serious about your intent to have staff call in sick as response to recent administrative actions.

Based on your own admission, you spoke with others to include Officer Jenkins and Officer Muhammed and discussed calling in sick. You indicate none of the individuals with whom you spoke supported the idea. You also admit to calling [Correctional Officer] Patricia Harding regarding having staff call in sick.

In an August 9, 1993 decision notice, the agency initially characterized this conduct as "Coercing Staff To Call In Sick." However, the agency rescinded that notice and issued an August 24, 1993 decision notice in which it did not characterize the charge but merely stated that the conduct set forth in the notice of proposed removal was sustained. The agency has characterized its charge throughout the appeal as one of encouraging and counseling employees to call in sick.

Without making a finding as to whether the charged conduct actually occurred, the administrative judge found that the agency could not discipline the appellant because the charged conduct did not constitute actionable misconduct and was protected by the First Amendment. The administrative judge further found that the agency's action constituted reprisal for appellant's prior protected union and appeal activity. On review, the agency contends...that the administrative judge erred by finding that the appellant's conduct was not actionable and that the action constituted reprisal. OPM contends that the administrative judge erred by finding that the appellant's conduct was protected by the First Amendment and was not actionable, and that she erred by finding that the action constituted reprisal. The allegations presented by OPM and the agency are without merit.

Regardless of whether the charged misconduct actually occurred, we agree with the administrative judge's finding that the charged misconduct is not actionable. The notice of proposed removal states that the appellant spoke to other employees and discussed calling in sick and that nobody supported the idea. There is nothing in these circumstances to suggest that the appellant persuaded others to call in sick or that anyone acted on his statements. Simply put, the charged misconduct, even if true, does not rise to the level of "encouraging" illegal sick leave use. Because the charge does not set forth actionable misconduct, we need not reach the appellant's claim that his speech was protected by the First Amendment. Holland v. Department of the Air Force, 31 F.3d 1118, 1122 (Fed. Cir. 1994).
