7. Time for Initiating Enforcement

Under 5 CFR 1201.182(a) (2022), an enforcement petition should be brought "promptly," but within 30 days if the agency provides notice that it has complied with a settlement agreement:

Appellate jurisdiction. Any party may petition the Board for enforcement of a final decision or order issued under the Board's appellate jurisdiction, or for enforcement of the terms of a settlement agreement that has been entered into the record for the purpose of enforcement in an order or decision under the Board's appellate jurisdiction. The petition must be filed promptly with the regional or field office that issued the initial decision; a copy of it must be served on the other party or that party's representative; and it must describe specifically the reasons the petitioning party believes there is noncompliance. The petition also must include the date and results of any communications regarding compliance. Any petition for enforcement that is filed more than 30 days after the date of service of the agency's notice that it has complied must contain a statement and evidence showing good cause for the delay and a request for an extension of time for filing the petition.

The Board's regulation was traditionally applied to Board final orders adjudicating cases in favor of appellants. The agency was supposed to certify compliance with the Board order, and then the appellant was supposed to quickly raise with the Board compliance issues. Final Board orders accepting settlements into the Board record for purposes of enforcement were gradually brought within the scope of the Board regulation, and some agencies do get around to certifying compliance with settlements, but that is the exception. Most agencies do not send a letter or file a notice certifying compliance with a settlement, probably because the initial decision closing out a settled case simply references the acceptance of the settlement into the Board record for purpose of enforcement and dismisses the appeal as settled, but the initial decision does not order the agency to do anything (unlike a decision reversing an adverse action; then specific requirements are imposed on the agency).

Once an agency violates a settlement, the appellant is entitled to file an enforcement petition. See Whaley v. USPS, 61 MSPR 340, 347 (1994). Assuming no certification of compliance with a settlement, enforcement petitions must be filed in a reasonable time after the alleged breach of a settlement. See Poett v. USDA, 92 MSPR 44, 46 n.* (2002) (if no deadline was set by the settlement, the petition to enforce must be filed within a reasonable time after the alleged breach, taking into consideration the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach and the particular circumstances of the case); Patterson v. USPS, 71 MSPR 332, 334 (1996) (declining to excuse enforcement efforts brought years after the operative events); Shapira v. VA, 35 MSPR 136, 138-39 (1987) (rejecting, with little analysis, an enforcement action brought several years following the settlement agreement). [Refer to Chapter 1 concerning "Laches."]

There is a qualification concerning timing of an enforcement petition following a breach, and that is that the petition cannot be filed before the initial decision accepting the settlement becomes final, Jacobs v. Dept. of Air Force, DC-0752-15-0227-C-1 n.3 (NP 5/4/2016):

Although not raised by either party on review, when the appellant filed her petition for enforcement, the compliance initial decision dismissing her appeal as settled had not yet become final, and, accordingly, the administrative judge should have dismissed the petition for enforcement as premature. See Niday v. Department of the Army, 42 M.S.P.R. 673, 679 (1990) (finding that an appellant's contention that an agency failed to comply with a settlement agreement was premature when the initial decision dismissing the appeal as settled had not become final because the appellant had filed a petition for review); see also 5 C.F.R. § 1201.182(a) (providing that a party may petition the Board for enforcement of a final decision or order issued under the Board's appellate jurisdiction, or for enforcement of the terms of a settlement agreement that has been entered into the record for the purpose of enforcement in an order or decision under the Board's appellate jurisdiction). However, we find that this adjudicatory error does not form a basis for reversing the compliance initial decision because it did not affect either party's substantive rights. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984) (finding that an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision)....

The appellant seeking to enforce a settlement may await compliance, make a demand for compliance, and initiate proceedings with the Board within a reasonable time following further noncompliance. The appellant must act within a reasonable time following clear notice of repudiation of the bargain by the other party to the settlement. The Federal Circuit explained in Kasarsky v. MSPB, 296 F.3d 1331, 1334-38 (Fed. Cir. 2002), that an enforcement petition alleging a breach of a settlement agreement must be filed within a reasonable time from the date the petitioning party becomes aware of a breach of the agreement, and that the reasonableness of the time period depends on the circumstances:

This court has not addressed the time limit within which a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement must be filed. Thus, we are faced with a question of first impression. In reaching its decision, the Board looked for guidance to an analogous regulation pertaining to enforcement of a final decision or order issued under the Board's appellate jurisdiction. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.182(a) (2001). Under that regulation, a petition alleging a breach of a final decision "must be filed promptly" and "must describe specifically the reasons the petitioning party believes there is noncompliance." Id. The regulation further provides that "[a]ny petition for enforcement that is filed more than 30 days after the date of service of the agency's notice that it has complied must contain a statement and evidence showing good cause for the delay and a request for an extension of time for filing the petition." Id. Because an agency that enters into a settlement agreement is not required to serve a notice of compliance, these provisions are not directly applicable to petitions for enforcement of settlement agreements. See, e.g., Bostick v. Dep't of Health and Human Serv., 63 M.S.P.R. 399, 401 (1994).

While the above regulation does not directly apply to settlement agreements, the underlying rationale for the regulation is nevertheless instructive in the enforcement of settlement agreements. A party's obligation to abide by the terms of a settlement agreement is no less than its obligation to follow the Board's own orders. In the context of complying with an order from the Board, 5 C.F.R. § 1201.181(a) mandates that the parties "cooperate fully with each other so that compliance with the Board's orders and decisions can be accomplished promptly and in accordance with the laws, rules, and regulations that apply to individual cases." When an agency has complied with an order from the Board, the regulations require the agency to "inform the appellant promptly of the actions it takes to comply, and it must tell the appellant when it believes it has completed its compliance." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.181(b) (2001). The regulations also place a burden upon the appellant to "provide all necessary information that the agency requests in order to comply, and, if not otherwise notified, he or she should, from time to time, ask the agency about its progress." Id. The purpose of those provisions is to avoid unnecessary petitions for enforcement. Id.

The timeliness of a petition for enforcement of a final Board decision is measured against the date that the agency notifies the appellant of its compliance. Because the agency is not required to serve a notice of compliance in a settlement context, the timeliness of a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement must be measured against a different standard. The Board, in Adamcik v. United States Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 493 (1991), recognized this and adopted a rule governing this issue. In that case, the Board held that an enforcement petition alleging a breach of a settlement agreement which the Board retained the authority to enforce must be filed "within a reasonable amount of time of the date of the alleged breach of the agreement, taking into account the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach." Id. at 496. The Board further noted that "[t]he reasonableness of the time period depends on the particular circumstances of the case." Id. The Board, in Adamcik, based its reasoning, in part, on the underlying rationale found in the regulations relating to enforcement of the Board's orders. Id. Because the rule formulated in Adamcik draws reasonable parallels to and is consistent with the underlying rationale found in regulations relating to enforcement of Board orders, we find the reasoning expressed and the conclusions drawn by the Board in Adamcik to be sound.

The limitations period for filing a petition to enforce an order of the Board is triggered by the agency's notice of compliance. Because a party who settles with an agency is entitled to expect that the agency will comply with the terms of the agreement until the party has reason to believe otherwise, it is reasonable to conclude that the limitations period for filing a petition to enforce a settlement agreement should be triggered when the party alleging a breach of the settlement agreement first becomes aware of such breach. Thus, we hold that an enforcement petition alleging a breach of a settlement agreement must be filed within a reasonable amount of time of the date the petitioning party becomes aware of a breach of the agreement. The reasonableness of the time period depends on the circumstances of each case. A petition for enforcement is presumed to be timely and subject to the jurisdiction of the Board until challenged, either by the Board on its own initiative or by the agency. As a result of such a challenge, if on its face the petition is untimely, then the burden is on the petitioner to show facts establishing why the petition is not untimely.

Here, it is undisputed that the agency and Kasarsky reached a fee agreement whereby the agency would pay a fixed sum in exchange for dismissal of the fee petition. It is also undisputed that the agency did not pay Kasarsky the agreed amount. Under the rule set forth above, we must ascertain the following: Was there a breach of the settlement agreement? If there was a breach, when did the breach occur, and when did Kasarsky become aware of the breach? Finally, once Kasarsky became aware of the breach, did he thereafter file a petition for enforcement within a reasonable amount of time?

Disputes involving settlement agreements are governed by contract principles. See Tiburzi v. Dep't of Justice, 269 F.3d 1346, 1351 (Fed. Cir. 2001) ("It is 'axiomatic that a settlement agreement is a contract.'" (quoting Greco v. Dep't of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988)). The contract principles involved here relate to when a contract to pay money--with no date of performance specified--is breached. A breach of contract is simply the non-performance of a contractual duty. See Franconia Assocs. v. United States, 536 U.S. [129], 122 S. Ct. 1993, 2002 (2002) (citing Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 235(2) (1979) (hereinafter Restatement); J. Murray, Contracts § 206, p. 417 (2d rev. ed. 1974)); Wharf (Holdings) Ltd. v. United Int'l Holdings, Inc., 532 U.S. 588, 596-97 (2001) (describing a breach of contract as "a simple failure to perform"). When no time for performance is specified and one party performs, the non-performing party is not in breach of the contract until either (1) the performing party demands performance within a reasonable amount of time, and the other party still fails to perform within the time specified; or (2) the non-performing party repudiates the contract, and the performing party chooses to treat the repudiation as a breach. See Roehm v. Horst, 178 U.S. 1, 13, 20 S. Ct. 780, 44 L. Ed. 953 (1900) (repudiation gives the promisee the right of electing either to wait until the time for the promisor's performance has arrived or to act upon the renunciation and treat it as a final assertion by the promisor that he is no longer bound by the contract). Here, the parties agree that at some point, the agency's non-performance with respect to the fee agreement amounted to a breach. The dispute centers around when that breach occurred.

...

Certainly, the government is entitled to resolve disputes promptly and is not obligated to retain files open indefinitely. But the rule we adopt today does not leave the government subject to the whims or procrastinations of other parties to settlement agreements. The government is free to take steps to conclude open matters at any time by simply taking whatever action is called for in the agreement or by advising the contracting party that the government considers its obligations to have been satisfied. This is analogous to and no more burdensome than the obligation the government bears in the context of the enforcement of an order or decision to provide a notice of compliance before the duty to timely proceed with the filing of a petition for enforcement is triggered....

Kararsky was followed in Arnold v. MSPB, 572 Fed. Appx. 977, 981-82 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), determining that an enforcement petition is presumed to be timely filed and the burden is on the respondent to demonstrate its untimeliness:

Because "[a] petition for enforcement is presumed to be timely," we conclude that the initial burden to show untimeliness is on the party invoking the untimeliness defense--here, the Board or the agency. Kasarsky, 296 F.3d at 1335. However, here we need not decide whether the agency established that the PFE was untimely on its face, because, even if it was facially untimely, we find that the agency failed to fully consider whether Mr. Arnold's delay in filing his PFE was reasonable.

We begin with the proposition that "policy considerations counsel against dismissals for untimely filing and militate for providing employees with a hearing on the merits of their appeals" and that "broad equitable principles of justice and good conscience should be applied" when dealing with issues of timeliness. Walls v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 29 F.3d 1578, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (citing Alonzo v. Dep't of Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 183-84 (1980)) (internal quotation marks omitted). Moreover, "'if the employee gives a reasonable excuse for the delay, such excuse should be accepted by the presiding official, absent a showing of substantial prejudice to the agency caused by the delay in filing.'" Id. (quoting Alonzo, 4 M.S.P.R. at 184).

With these principles in mind, we conclude that the Board did not fully consider whether Mr. Arnold's ten month delay in filing his PFE was reasonable. We agree with the Board that delaying filing to save money for an attorney is not a reasonable excuse. But we hold that the Board failed to address the merits of Mr. Arnold's argument whether his reliance on the one-year time limit to challenge OWCP decisions, see 20 C.F.R. § 10.607(a) (2011), was reasonable. The AJ stated only that nothing in the Dismissal Order indicated that the time for filing a PFE would correspond to the time for seeking review of an OWCP decision. But that does not answer the question whether, in the absence of any instruction as to what "reasonable" means in this context, it was reasonable for Mr. Arnold to be guided by the OWCP deadline. Cf. Walls, 29 F.3d at 1583-84 (reversing and remanding where a notice stated that an appeal must be filed "within twenty (20) days" because it was unclear whether the notice meant calendar or working days). Here, Mr. Arnold's PFE was related to OWCP proceedings because the purpose of both was to obtain disability compensation. Furthermore, one-year time limits are common in a disability context, see, e.g., 5 U.S.C. § 8337(b) (2012); id. § 8453. The AJ's analysis, which relied on another Board case where a ten-month delay was found to be unreasonable, does not address Mr. Arnold's argument or explain why the OWCP deadline was unreasonable for Mr. Arnold to rely upon. It thus fails to follow our instruction to consider each case according to its unique facts and circumstances. See Kasarsky, 296 F.3d at 1335.

This case involves a pro se petitioner who was told only to file within a reasonable time. He had no guidance on what was reasonable. On remand, the Board must consider the facts and circumstances of this case: that Mr. Arnold was not given an indication of what a reasonable period would be; Mr. Arnold's pro se status; and Mr. Arnold's claim that he believed one year to be a reasonable period of time given that his OWCP claim provided a year in which to appeal. See id.; Walls, 29 F.3d at 1583; Pfaehler v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 783 F.2d 187, 189 (Fed. Cir. 1986); see also Mendoza v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 966 F.2d 650, 653-54 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (en banc). Because the facts of each case are different, the Board's prior determination that a ten-month delay was unreasonable does not answer the question whether Mr. Arnold had a reasonable excuse for his ten-month delay.[2]

The Board explained its position as to the timeliness of a petition to enforce a settlement in Adamcik v. USPS, 48 MSPR 493, 496-98 (1991), concluding that the petition must be filed within a reasonable time of the date of the alleged breach of the agreement, taking into consideration the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach, and offering some guidance concerning responses to enforcement submissions:

The Board's regulations provide that any petition for enforcement of a Board final decision that is filed more than 30 days after the date of service of the agency's notice of compliance must contain a statement and evidence showing good cause for the delay and a request for an extension of time for filing the petition. See 5 CFR § 1201.182(a). When the Board introduced this provision into its regulations, it explained that the parties to Board appeals have a responsibility to cooperate to assure that compliance is obtained promptly and completely. The Board also noted that the provision serves to improve the process, and shorten the time frame for the settlement of and adjudication of compliance disputes. See 51 Fed. Reg. 6729 (Feb. 26, 1986).

After public comment on this provision, the Board again stated that, although there is no time limit on the Board's authority to enforce compliance, the regulation's requirements encourage timely filing of requests for compliance in order to assure prompt resolution of compliance matters for the benefit of all concerned. See 51 Fed. Reg. 25146 (Jul. 10, 1986).

The underlying rationale for the regulation is equally applicable to enforcement of settlement agreements. Agencies that enter into settlement agreements are not required to serve notice of compliance. Thus, the time period for filing a petition for enforcement in a settled case cannot be identical to that of the regulation. The date of the alleged breach and/or the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach, can usually be identified, however. Also, in situations where a settlement agreement requires action by the agency, the Board has held that 30 days is a reasonable time in which to comply. See Graff v. Department of the Air Force, 39 MSPR 639 (1989).

While we do not here set a specific time limit for the filing of a petition for enforcement by a party alleging breach of a settlement agreement which the Board retained authority to enforce, we hold that such petition must be filed within a reasonable time of the date of the alleged breach of the agreement, taking into consideration the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach. Cf. Shapiro v. Veterans Administration, 35 MSPR 136, 138 (1987) (the administrative judge properly dismissed as untimely a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement filed two years after the date of the alleged breach). The reasonableness of the time period depends on the particular circumstances of the case.

In this case, the agency actions which form the basis of the appellant's allegations of breach of the settlement agreement occurred as follows: The agency returned the appellant to EAS 18, step 5, on August 12, 1989; the PS-50 that the appellant complains erroneously refers to her downgrade is dated December 21, 1989; and the agency reassigned the appellant on December 12, 1989. The appellant's petition for enforcement was filed on June 7, 1990, about 6 months after she was aware of the reassignment, the agency's last alleged breach of the agreement.

Additionally, the record establishes that, 4 months before the appellant filed the petition for enforcement, she viewed the reassignment as retaliation. On January 30, 1990, she filed an Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO) complaint alleging that the reassignment was retaliation for filing earlier EEO complaints.

The administrative judge's acknowledgment order did not give the appellant notice of the timeliness issue or afford her the opportunity to show good cause for the apparently delayed filing.... We therefore remand the case to the administrative judge for issuance of a show cause order and a determination as to whether the appellant filed within a reasonable time under the circumstances.

In addition, the Board finds that the administrative judge erred in failing to afford the appellant an opportunity to submit evidence and argument in response to the agency's final submission. The Board's regulations provide that the procedures for processing petitions for enforcement include: the initial petition; a reply to the petition by the allegedly noncomplying party with evidence of compliance, evidence of partial compliance and a reasonable schedule for full compliance, or a statement showing good cause for noncompliance; and the petitioner's response to the allegedly noncomplying party's evidence. See 5 CFR § 1201.183(a).

In this case, the administrative judge allowed the agency to file a rebuttal to the appellant's response to its evidence of compliance. Although such a filing is not specifically permitted by the Board's regulations, the administrative judge has wide discretion in directing the course of the appeal proceedings and we find that she did not abuse her discretion here in allowing the submission. See 5 CFR § 1201.41. See also Shelton v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 MSPR 280, 283 (1988). We find, however that the administrative judge erred in failing to afford the appellant an opportunity to respond to the additional evidence in the agency's rebuttal. See Lyon v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 MSPR 77 (1988). See also Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 32 MSPR 488 (1987), rev'd in part, vacated in part on other grounds, 850 F.2d 682 (Fed. Cir. 1988). As noted above, the Board's regulations prescribe that the petitioner will have an opportunity to respond to the allegedly non-complying party's evidentiary submissions. Where, as here, supplementary submissions are permitted, the right to respond also must be afforded.

Under the circumstances presented, Fitzpatrick v. Department of Justice, 91 M.S.P.R. 556, review dismissed, 52 Fed. Appx. 148 (Fed. Cir. 2002), is instructive. In that case, the appellant first learned that his employing agency had possibly breached the parties' settlement agreement in 1997 and took steps other than filing a petition for enforcement to pursue the matter. Fitzpatrick, 91 M.S.P.R. 556 ¶ 3. The appellant eventually filed a petition for enforcement with the Board in 1999. Id. Although the administrative judge found that the appellant believed he was pursuing a reasonable course of action to obtain proof of noncompliance from 1997 to 1999, he held that the delay in filing a petition for enforcement rendered it untimely. Id. The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit (Federal Circuit) disagreed and found that the appellant did not unreasonably delay filing his petition for enforcement because he pursued a reasonable course of action after he first received information indicating that the agency may have violated the settlement agreement and then filed his petition for enforcement within a reasonable time "after it became clear that the course he had taken would not resolve the question of whether a breach had occurred." Id. ¶ 7 (citing Fitzpatrick v. Department of Justice, No. 00-3385, 2001 WL 1313663, at *1, *3 (Fed. Cir. Oct. 19, 2001) (Table)).

Similarly, the appellant in this matter received the draft settlement agreement from his representative in March 2001, which coupled with the information that Letsky was revealing the terms of the settlement to others, may have led him to suspect that the agency had breached the confidentiality provision. The appellant asserts that he then made numerous attempts to contact his representative, the representative's supervisor, the agency, and the union to determine exactly what had occurred regarding the settlement of his case. When he did not receive the information he sought, he wrote to the administrative judge in July 2001.

Since the appellant sent letters to the administrative judge alleging noncompliance no more than 4 months after he first received the draft written settlement agreement, it is uncertain exactly when, after he received the draft, he realized that a settlement containing terms similar to those in the draft version had already occurred. It nonetheless appears that, between March 2001 and July 2001, he was reasonably attempting to ascertain what had transpired regarding the settlement of his appeal. Under these circumstances, the Board finds that the appellant filed his July 2001 allegations of noncompliance within a reasonable period of time. Because the petition for enforcement was timely with respect to the appellant's assertion that the agency breached the confidentiality provision of the agreement, remand for further adjudication of that allegation is appropriate.

Although he apparently received a written draft settlement agreement from his representative in March 2001, it appears that he did not immediately realize that a settlement had already occurred. It is also uncertain whether he knew that the terms set forth therein were the terms to which the parties had orally agreed on November 8, 2000. In fact, there were some significant differences between the oral settlement agreement in the record and the written draft the appellant received. For example, the written draft indicated that the appellant would submit a letter of resignation, though no date was given for doing so, and that the agency would pay the appellant $2000 within 6 weeks after the final signature was placed on the agreement. The oral settlement agreement, however, provided that the appellant would submit a letter of resignation with an effective date to be determined by the appellant prior to November 8, 2000. The oral agreement further provided that the agency would pay the appellant $2000 within 6 weeks of November 8, 2000. Therefore, if the appellant had only seen his representative's draft agreement, he would not have been aware that he was entitled to payment within 6 weeks after November 8, 2000. He may have been under the misapprehension that he had to sign the agreement to receive payment.

There are some similarities between this case and Kasarsky. In Kasarsky, the parties reached an agreement on the merits of the case and entered it into the record for enforcement purposes. 296 F.3d at 1332. The agency reduced the agreement to writing and sent the appellant a final, presumably signed, version in July 1997. Id. In October 1997, the parties entered into an agreement orally on the record regarding attorney fees, but no time was specified for the agency's payment of such fees. Id. at 1333. In December 1999, the appellant finally signed the written settlement agreement. Id. In January and February 2001, the appellant's attorney reminded the agency of its agreement regarding attorney fees and set a deadline for payment of March 5, 2001. Id. When the agency failed to pay, the appellant filed a petition for enforcement in April 2001, which the Board dismissed as untimely. Id. at 1333-34. The Federal Circuit reversed the Board's decision and held that the agency did not breach the agreement until the appellant made a demand for payment by a date certain and the agency failed to pay by that date. Id. at 1337-38. The Federal Circuit further found that the appellant could not be charged with actual knowledge of the breach until he set a firm deadline for the agency's performance and the agency failed to meet it. Id. From that point, the appellant had a reasonable time for filing a petition for enforcement. Id. at 1338-39.

In March 2001, after the appellant received the draft written version of the settlement agreement and had heard from a former co-worker that an agency supervisor was discussing details of his settlement, he should have had a suspicion that there was an agreement which contained a payment provision that may have been breached. However, as stated previously, it is unclear when, if ever, the appellant learned that the agency was supposed to pay him within 6 weeks of the November 8, 2000 settlement, as the written draft he received contains a payment time linked to the signing of the written draft, which never occurred.

While it appears that the appellant may have eventually received a copy of the audiotape containing the settlement agreement in this matter, it is not certain whether he did. A determination of when he received it or some other representation of the agreement's actual terms, if ever, is necessary to determine when he had actual knowledge of an agency breach of the payment provision. Therefore, the Board remands this issue for the administrative judge to give the parties an opportunity to submit evidence and argument regarding (1) whether the agency breached the agreement's payment provision, (2) if there was a breach, when the appellant became aware of it, which will in part be determined by when the appellant became aware of the terms of the actual oral settlement agreement, and (3) once the appellant became aware of the breach, whether he filed a petition for enforcement within a reasonable period of time thereafter. See Kasarsky, 296 F.3d at 1335-36. If the administrative judge finds that the appellant filed his petition for enforcement within a reasonable period of time after he had actual knowledge of a breach of the payment provision, the administrative judge shall adjudicate that issue on the merits.

Bostick v. DHHS, 63 MSPR 399, 401-02 (1994), provides another example of how the Board assesses timeliness of a petition seeking settlement enforcement:

Board regulations provide that a petition for enforcement of a final decision that is filed more than 30 days after the date of service of the agency's notice of compliance must contain a statement and evidence showing good cause for the delay and a request for an extension of time for filing the petition. 5 CFR § 1201.182(a). Because an agency that enters into a settlement agreement is not required to serve a notice of compliance, the time period for filing a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement cannot track the express requirements of these regulations. Rather than set a specific time limit for the filing of such a petition, the Board has stated that the petition must be filed "within a reasonable time of the date of the alleged breach of the agreement, taking into consideration the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach," and that the "reasonableness of the time period depends on the particular circumstances of the case." Adamcik v. United States Postal Service, 48 MSPR 493, 496 (1991).

In the present case, the appellant concedes that he was aware in December 1991 that the agency had upgraded the RMO position without upgrading his own Deputy RMO position, and that he waited 21 months before filing his petition for enforcement challenging this alleged breach. Moreover, although the appellant asserts that he did not realize there was a problem with the grading of his position until the subsequent delegation problems arose, he elsewhere notes that he actually suspected the agency of bad faith on this score even before it had completed the upgrade of the RMO position. Although the appellant did not receive notice of a specific time period for filing a petition to enforce compliance with the settlement agreement, he was notified of his general right to file such a petition, and his failure to do so for 21 months does not satisfy the standard of reasonableness set forth in Adamcik. Accordingly, we AFFIRM the part of the initial decision in which the administrative judge found that the appellant had not filed his petition for enforcement within a reasonable time after this alleged breach.

The appellant also concedes that he discovered the agency was withholding the authority he allegedly needed to perform the duties of his position in May 1993 and that he filed his petition for enforcement some four months later. He notes, however, that he filed a grievance concerning the matter in June 1993, and that he filed his petition for enforcement only 10 days after he received the agency's decision on that grievance. It is true that the pursuit of other avenues of redress, including the grievance process, generally does not constitute good cause for waiving the Board's filing deadline. See, e.g., Bachelor v. Department of the Army, 56 MSPR 108, 110 (1992). As we have noted above, however, the appellant in this case received only the more general information about petitions for enforcement that is provided in cases involving settlement agreements, and not the more specific information provided in other cases. Under these circumstances, we find that the appellant acted within a reasonable time of the date of the alleged breach....

See Davis v. MSPB, 609 Fed. Appx. 647 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP) (four years was unreasonable when there was no explanation for the delay); Nichols v. MSPB, 253 Fed. Appx. 961 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (no cause shown for the delay of ten months in the initiation of enforcement proceedings based on an allegation that the agency violated a settlement by failing to use its best efforts to assist the appellant in her disability retirement application); Arnold v. USPS, SF-0752-09-0965-C-1 (NP 12/27/2013) (ten months too long), rev'd and remanded, Arnold v. MSPB, 572 Fed. Appx. 977, 981-82 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), following remand, Arnold v. USPS, SF-0752-09-0965-M-1 (NP 1/16/2014) (enforcement petition timely)); Buono v. Dept. of Navy, PH-1221-07-0041-C-2 (NP 12/6/2010) (enforcement sought too late when the settlement agreement set a period of 30 days for an enforcement initiative and two years had passed since the order barring appellant from entering the workplace); Maddox v. GSA, 53 MSPR 288, 291 (1992) ("In Adamcik v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 493, 496 (1991), the Board found that a distinction exists between enforcement petitions of settlement agreements and enforcement petitions of other Board decisions because agencies that enter into settlement agreements are not required to serve notice of compliance on the appellant. The Board, therefore, held that the time period for filing a petition for enforcement in a settled case cannot be identical to that of the Board's regulation in 1201.182(a), and that a petition for enforcement in a settled case must be filed within a reasonable time of the date of the alleged breach of the agreement, taking into consideration the date of the petitioning party's knowledge of the alleged breach.").

Resort to OSC may not excuse an appellant's delay in seeking Board enforcement of a settlement, Chudson v. EPA, 71 MSPR 115, 118-19 (1996):

The appellant states on review that he discovered the agency's alleged breach upon receiving the Department of State's August 11, 1994 response to his FOIA request. He asserts that this response "demonstrated breach of the settlement agreement by [the agency] and Whistleblower Protection Act violations by the Department of State." Instead of filing a petition for enforcement concerning the agency's alleged breach, the appellant elected to file a whistleblower complaint with the Office of the Special Counsel (OSC) on August 31, 1994. Id. This appeal followed nearly 1 year later on August 21, 1995. We find that this explanation does not show good cause for the appellant's 1-year delay in filing once he learned of the agency's alleged breach. Bostick, 63 MSPR at 402.

The appellant is represented by counsel. He has filed a number of appeals with the Board, including two prior petitions for enforcement. Therefore, it cannot be said that he is unaware of the Board's procedures for filing a petition for enforcement. Moreover, in light of the appellant's familiarity with Board procedures and his tenacious pursuit of his appeal rights in the past, his decision to seek enforcement of the agency's alleged breach of the settlement agreement with OSC, rather than with the Board, does not reflect ordinary prudence or due diligence under the circumstances, particularly in light of the lengthy delay in this case. The Board has held that an employee's pursuit of his appeal rights in another forum does not constitute good cause for untimely filing. See, e.g., Waldon v. Department of the Army, 63 MSPR 478, 479 (1994), review dismissed, 36 F.3d 1112 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Shimmin v. Department of Justice, 63 MSPR 435, 438, aff'd, 43 F.3d 1486 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Lipsky v. US Postal Service, 58 MSPR 555, 560 (1993).

We find that the appellant has not demonstrated good cause for the untimeliness of his petition for enforcement. Accordingly, the appellant's petition for enforcement is dismissed. Although the appellant's petition for enforcement is dismissed as to this alleged breach of the settlement agreement, the agreement remains valid and its provisions continue to control any future actions of the parties.

See Patterson v. USPS, 71 MSPR 332, 336 (1996) (untimely petition to enforce a settlement: "The appellant's pursuit of his claims through other agencies similarly provides no basis for waiving the filing deadline.").

Nevertheless, the appellant need not rush to enforcement proceedings. He may wait until it is reasonably apparent that there has been a violation of the settlement, and he may seek some internal redress within an agency before invoking the Board's procedures, Fitzpatrick v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3385):

Ordinarily, a petition for enforcement of a final decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board must be filed within 30 days after the agency serves its notice of compliance with the decision. Any petition filed after that time must contain a statement and evidence showing good cause for the delay and a request for an extension of time for filing the petition. 5 CFR § 1201.182(a). Because an agency that enters into a settlement agreement is not required to serve a notice of compliance, there is no strict time limit on filing a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement; instead, the Board has held that such a petition must be filed within a reasonable time of the date of the alleged breach of the agreement. Adamcik v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 MSPR 493, 496 (1991). The reasonableness of the time period depends on when the petitioning party learned of the alleged breach and the particular circumstances of the case. Id.

The administrative judge in this case concluded that Mr. Fitzpatrick "was aware of the agency's alleged noncompliance approximately two years before he filed the petition for enforcement" and for that reason concluded that the delay of two years in filing the petition for enforcement was unreasonable. We disagree with the administrative judge's ruling. Although it is true that Mr. Fitzpatrick had information in 1997 that led him to suspect that the settlement agreement may have been breached, he did not have conclusive evidence of a violation. Indeed, the government acknowledged as much at oral argument, pointing out that Mr. Fitzpatrick's suspicion was based only on "second-or third-hand hearsay." Thus, we do not agree with the apparent conclusion of the administrative judge that Mr. Fitzpatrick had notice of a violation of the settlement agreement in September 1997. Nor do we agree with the government's suggestion that the time for filing a petition for enforcement ran from the time Mr. Fitzpatrick first had reason to suspect that the government may have breached the settlement agreement. Instead, it was reasonable for him to take other steps to attempt to confirm that a violation had occurred before filing a petition for enforcement. Although the course of action he pursued--raising the issue with the United States Attorney's office, which referred the matter to the Department of Justice's Office of Professional Responsibility--may not have been the most effective course to follow, it was a step that was reasonably designed to determine whether there had been an improper disclosure of matters that should not have been contained in his personnel file. When that inquiry neither confirmed his suspicions nor allayed his concerns, he sought confirmation by another means--requesting a copy of his personnel file from the FBI--but he received no satisfactory response to that request either. Shortly after requesting a copy of his personnel file and receiving no response, he filed his petition for enforcement.

If it had been clear in 1997 that the settlement agreement had been violated, it would have been reasonable for the Board to conclude that a two-year delay in filing the petition for enforcement rendered the petition untimely. In light of the fact that the evidence of a breach of the agreement that Mr. Fitzpatrick had in 1997 was inconclusive, however, he acted reasonably in taking measures designed to confirm his suspicions before filing a petition for enforcement. Mr. Fitzpatrick filed his petition within a reasonable period after it became clear that neither the OPR investigation nor Mr. Fitzpatrick's request for access to his official personnel file was going to give him the means to determine for sure whether there had been a violation of the settlement agreement. Under those circumstances, we conclude that the Board abused its discretion by concluding that Mr. Fitzpatrick should have filed his petition for enforcement at some earlier point in the process and that the petition he filed in August 1999 was untimely. We therefore remand the case for the Board to address whether the settlement agreement was or is being violated, and if so, what remedy may be appropriate.
