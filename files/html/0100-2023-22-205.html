a. Obligation of Judge to Ensure Due Process

The Board has not developed detailed regulations concerning the due process required during adjudication of counsel fee petitions. Treatment of fee petitions has been outside the mainstream of Board hearing and discovery procedures. The Board developed fairly elaborate rules for treatment of compliance disputes and enforcement proceedings. The same could be done for counsel fee adjudications. [Refer in this chapter to "Notice to Counsel of Defect in Fee Motion."]

Wilson v. DHHS, 834 F.2d 1011 (Fed. Cir. 1987), defines the minimum due process required for appellants and their counsel: they must have notice of defects in fee petitions and an opportunity to address those defects through oral argument, hearing, or written submissions, depending on the nature and complexity of the matters in dispute. See Diehl v. USPS, 88 MSPR 104, 109 ¶ 9 (2001) (appellant was not deprived of due process when counsel had an opportunity to respond to the agency objections to fees and those objections formed the basis of reductions in the AJ's decision); Blackman v. USPS, 67 MSPR 382, 385-86 (1995) ("[B]efore an administrative judge decides on her own to reject claims in an attorney fees application, she should communicate her concerns to the appellant and allow him an opportunity to correct the specific deficiencies identified"; there was no harmful error, however, because the appellant's counsel addressed the issues in his reply to the agency's response to the counsel fee motion); Madera v. OPM, 53 MSPR 310, 316 (1992) (AJ should have permitted OPM to respond to her determination, sua sponte, to consider fee entitlement under an "interest of justice" standard, but OPM's argument on the issue was considered by the Board on a PFR); McGuire v. USPS, 5 MSPR 54, 56, 5 MSPB 130 (1981) (remanding a case dismissed for a lack of prosecution when AJ's letters to counsel requesting additional information concerning the nature of services covered by the fee petition did not reach counsel and copies were not sent to appellant).

If the AJ fails to allow the appellant the opportunity to respond to deficiencies found by the AJ in the fee petition, the Board may, when possible, rectify the problem in its treatment of a PFR, Driscoll v. USPS, 116 MSPR 662, 669 ¶ 12, 2011 MSPB 80 (2011):

In Wilson v. Department of Health & Human Services, 834 F.2d 1011, 1012 (Fed. Cir. 1987), the Federal Circuit held that if an administrative judge has concerns about deficiencies in a motion for attorney fees, she should afford the party an opportunity to address the matter before rejecting the claims. Here, the administrative judge disallowed numerous claimed hours without first communicating her doubts to the appellant and providing her an opportunity to answer. Because the appellant's attorney has proffered explanations for some of these charges in her petition for review, we are able to make a determination regarding the reasonableness of the disputed fees without remanding the matter to the administrative judge. See Hart, 115 M.S.P.R. 10, ¶ 20.

The Board's regional offices often send out notices following the receipt of fee petitions establishing one date for additional submissions from the appellant or counsel to support the fee request, and a later date for the agency response. Typically the regional notices state that the record will close when the agency files its response. The Board's boilerplate is a denial of due process. As a matter of due process, the appellant--the party with the burden of proof--has the right to learn of and respond to the agency's objections, and the agency should have the opportunity to respond to new matters raised in the appellant's response to the agency's opposition to the fee petition. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Closing Record."] The Board has an interest in avoiding prolonged litigation over fee disputes, but it must also treat litigants fairly. The Board may recognize its responsibilities and conclude that an AJ improperly cut off an appellant's submissions on fees or that the AJ confused the parties concerning the date that the record would close. See Martinez v. USPS, 89 MSPR 152, 159 ¶ 16 (2001) (accepting a written fee contract on a PFR when AJ found deficient counsel's statement of the terms of fee agreement); Harris v. USDA, 48 MSPR 189, 195 (1991) (AJ improperly failed to inform counsel of when the record would close for submissions explaining certain hours billed by counsel). But see Brown v. OPM, 54 MSPR 415, 421-22 (1992) (when to close the record is within AJ's discretion, as long as the decision comports with basic requirements of notice and fairness; counsel had sufficient time to respond to agency's reply on fees and did not show harm when AJ received his submission but closed the record at date earlier than counsel requested).

Dismissal of a fee motion for failure to prosecute is improper if the appellant's address is in the record and the AJ fails to serve the appellant with inquiries concerning the fee motion after counsel of record fails to respond to the inquiries. The AJ should not, under those circumstances, have dismissed the fee petition without notifying the appellant of the need for additional evidence and providing an opportunity to respond. McGuire v. USPS, 5 MSPR 54, 56, 5 MSPB 130 (1981). If the record in counsel fee cases closes upon a conditional event, e.g., receipt of an agency submission that is not supposed to raise new issues, the AJ should avoid closing the record if new issues are raised by the agency. Bruning v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3005). If the agency's response is not served on appellant, the AJ's decision on fees will not be set aside if the agency submission adds nothing to the record bearing on the AJ's ability to consider appellant's entitlement to fees. See Kerr v. Nat'l Endowment for Arts, 14 MSPR 343, 345 (1983).
