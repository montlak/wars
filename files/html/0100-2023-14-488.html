broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 15: Pay, Premiums, and Allowances, Section Q: Distribution of Checks, Financial Documents

Q. DISTRIBUTION OF CHECKS, FINANCIAL DOCUMENTS

Back in the days of paychecks, the initial position of FLRA was that a proposal compelling the agency to continue a practice of delivering paychecks to employees at work rather than mailing the checks to employees' homes was nonnegotiable. In FLRA's opinion, the proposal related to the methods of performing agency work, making it a management right subject only to permissive negotiation. FLRA distinguished proposals that involved agency operations from those proposals that related primarily to employment conditions. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Mare Island Naval Shipyard, 16 FLRA 619 (1984), rev'd and remanded, FEMTC v. FLRA, 778 F.2d 1429, 1431 (9th Cir. 1985) ("Because any benefit to employees is a means of retaining a stable and committed workforce, the FLRA's reasoning would imply that all wages, hours, and working conditions are within the means and methods exception."). The case law in the check distribution cases was applied even though GAO regulations permitted several options as to check distribution. NAGE Locals R4-1, R4-97 and R4-103 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Weapons Station, Yorktown, 20 FLRA 186, 188 (1985). The method of distributing paychecks was nonnegotiable except at the option of the agency. Dept. of Navy and SEIU Local 556, 18 FLRA 638, 641 (1985). In one of the paycheck distribution cases, FLRA declined to find a ULP for lack of impact bargaining. The practice did not change for employees already in the unit when the change was made, but changed only as to newly hired employees, a modification that FLRA concluded would not have more than a de minimis effect on working conditions. Dept. of Navy, Office of Sec'y and FUSE, NAGE Local R1-144, 18 FLRA 802, 804 (1985). A proposal for an exemption to a mail-only policy for distribution of checks was negotiable when the agency apparently had no objection to the policy but refused to bargain about it for reasons relating to the structure of delegations of authority within the agency. AFGE Local 1409 and Army Adjutant Gen. Publications Ctr., 18 FLRA 508, 509 (1985).

The check distribution negotiability rulings were reconsidered, FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Mare Island Naval Shipyard, 25 FLRA 465, 466-67 (1987):

[O]n December 20, 1985, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, in a consolidated decision, reversed the Authority's determinations with respect to these proposals, and remanded the cases for further proceedings before the Authority if the Agency considered that it had other valid reasons for refusing to bargain. FEMTC v. FLRA, 778 F.2d 1429 (9th Cir. 1985). As for the reasons advanced by the Union and the Agency in support of their positions, the court stated that "[a]ll these reasons present the sort of questions collective bargaining is intended to resolve."...

We take this opportunity to reconsider the Authority's previous determination in these cases that the manner in which an agency delivers paychecks to its employees is a method and means of performing work within the meaning of section 7106(b)(1) of the Statute. We conclude that it is not. There may be aspects of the function of paying employees that would fall within the scope of section 7106(b)(1). However, the Agency has not established in these cases the required nexus between furthering the Agency's mission and the limited matter of how paychecks are delivered to employees.

In light of the decision of the Ninth Circuit, we conclude that paycheck delivery does not involve the methods and means of performing work within the meaning of section 7106(b)(1) of the Statute. Therefore, we overrule the Authority's previous holding that the manner of paycheck delivery is negotiable only at the election of the Agency, under section 7106(b)(1). To the extent that previous Authority decisions find that the manner of paycheck delivery is a section 7106(b)(1) matter, they are inconsistent with our decision here and will no longer be followed. However, we emphasize that in reaching this determination, we do not decide broader questions concerning whether and to what extent other matters which further an agency's mission fall within the scope of section 7106(b)(1).

FEMTC considered and rejected contentions by the employer that the proposal: did not relate to working conditions; was contrary to agency regulations supported by a compelling need (based on an argument relating to cost savings); interfered with the right of management to determine its budget; and violated the right of management to assign work, determine personnel, and assign employees. Added to the list of rejected arguments was one concerning the need of the agency to maintain its internal security practices concerning agency property and funds. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 25 FLRA 837, 840 (1987). FLRA required negotiations as to the employer's interest in changing the means of delivering wage and tax statements. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 30 FLRA 922 (1988).

The paycheck controversy sprang to life again in NTEU Local R14-89 and Army Air Defense Artillery Ctr., 32 FLRA 392, 398-402 (1988), involving a proposal that the agency deliver checks to employees within six days from the close of a pay period. The proposal was nonnegotiable because: it interfered with the right of the agency to determine the internal security practices needed to protect its payroll (balancing, verifying, reconciling, and auditing functions prior to distribution of checks); interfered with the agency's right to assign work since it required the agency to place employees on overtime to accomplish the object of the proposal and still meet its internal security needs; and interfered with the right of the agency to determine its means of performing work since it required reorganization of its finance office. NTEU Local 14-89 was distinguished, and a proposal was negotiable that maintained at six days the time between the end of a pay period and the day employees receive their paychecks, in AFGE Local 1698 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Aviation Supply Office, Philadelphia, 38 FLRA 1016 (1990) (proposal was consistent with GAO Policy and Procedures Manual for Guidance of Federal Agencies; proposal did not interfere with management rights because it caused agency to expend some additional resources to get job done; no violation of agency internal security practices). AFGE 1698 was followed in AFGE Local 48 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Submarine Base, Bangor, 38 FLRA 1359 (1991).

Negotiations are required over the methods of distributing leave and earnings statements. NFFE Local 738 and Dept. of Army, Army Eng'r Ctr., 37 FLRA 131 (1990); see Davis-Monthan AFB, Tucson and AFGE Local 2924, 42 FLRA 1267 (1991) (ALJ Decision). The content of accompanying documents may also be negotiable, as was a proposal that management bargain with the union concerning the content of statements accompanying employee's checks and statements of leave and earnings. Dept. of Labor and AFGE Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, 38 FLRA 1374, 1379 (1991). It was immaterial that the proposal affected nonunit employees.
