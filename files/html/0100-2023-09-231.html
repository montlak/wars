2. How Ex Parte Communications Are Treated

Ex parte communications are not supposed to occur. "The Board's administrative judges are required to use fair procedures that afford each party to an appeal an opportunity to respond to any other party's arguments and evidence." Fickie v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 525, 533 ¶ 20 (2000). An AJ should make every effort to avoid engaging in ex parte communications. If ex parte communications unavoidably occur, the AJ must make a complete memorandum of the conversation for the record and provide a copy to the parties. Golden v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 462, 464, 7 MSPB 326 (1981). Although the AJ in Golden, supra, did not make a complete memo because the parties were aware of what occurred by way of the conversation, there was no prejudice to the appellant. If the communication complained of is not truly ex parte under the Board's definition, e.g., a procedural matter, the regulatory requirement for memorialization of the communication may be inapplicable. Cf. Koch v. SEC, 48 Fed. Appx. 778, 785 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP) ("[T]he Board's regulations provide that 'interested parties may ask about such matters as the status of a case, when it will be heard, and methods of submitting evidence to the Board.' 5 C.F.R. § 1201.101(a). Because the record reflects that the contact between counsel for the SEC and the administrative judge related solely to the question whether the SEC's filing date was unchanged, it was not improper.").

Under the analysis of In re King, 1 MSPR 146, 154, 1 MSPB 144, 149 (1979), one remedy for an ex parte communication is to place the communication into the record and to allow the respondent an opportunity to comment. If the communication is verbal, it should be reduced to writing and placed into the record for comment. After King, the Board became less tolerant of ex parte communications. In In re Tariela, 1 MSPR 155, 1 MSPB 151 (1979), the Board criticized a VA official who sent letters to Board officials in the course of a Special Counsel case without serving OSC. The Board admonished the VA official that the Special Counsel was entitled to a current, not a belated, opportunity to respond to submissions. The Board advised the VA that it should "exert sufficient control over its employees to ensure that a fair and impartial adjudication is not imperiled by improper, intemperate and impulsive conduct of its officials." In an analogous situation, when an ex parte communication was apparently directed to the Board and the Board already issued its decision by the time the agency found out about the communication, the Board vacated its decision so that the agency could file a response to the communication. See Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 161, 162 (1982).

Should the appellant become aware of an ex parte communication between the AJ and the agency representative before the hearing, the communication must be immediately challenged. The appellant cannot stand by and await the result of the appeal before charging error in the adjudicatory procedures. Segal v. Coast Guard, 21 MSPR 705, 707 (1984). If the AJ engaged in ex parte communications, it will not do for the injured party to first object during a PFR from an adverse initial decision. If the appellant is to preserve for the Board's review his objection to the AJ's conduct, the objection to the communication should be on the record, at or before the hearing. See Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 621, 624-25 (1993) (although the appellant alleged the agency engaged in an ex parte request for an extension of time to file a prehearing submission, the appellant failed to show that he preserved his objection with the AJ and he failed to show prejudice to his substantive rights through the belated agency filing permitted by the AJ); Mitchell v. DOD, 46 MSPR 154, 160 (1990) ("The appellant was obligated to preserve for the Board's review his objection to the administrative judge's conduct below."); Miller v. OPM, 45 MSPR 122, 128 (1990) (as to an unspecified ex parte contact, the appellant could not wait until after the adjudication was complete to object for the first time to the AJ's conduct).

On occasion the AJ slips into discussion of the merits after a conversation begins on a procedural note. It is best for the party's representative to gently but immediately steer the AJ back to the procedural topic at hand, or conclude the conversation, by politely suggesting that they avoid potentially ex parte communications and engage the opposing party or counsel in a conference call to talk about what the AJ seeks to discuss.
