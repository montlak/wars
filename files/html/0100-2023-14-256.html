6. Equipment, Testing Methods and Personnel

Given the controlling nature of the DHHS Guidelines, no bar existed to the negotiability of a proposal that the employer use those Guidelines. Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 711-13 (1991) (Proposal 3); see AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 875, 878-84 (1991) (Proposal 5: clause was negotiable as arrangement that drug testing "be conducted in accordance with the HHS scientific and technical guidelines...methods and equipment used will meet the requirements as set forth in the guidelines"); AFGE Local 3457 and Dept. of Interior, Minerals Mgmt. Serv., 42 FLRA 567, 568, 572 (1991) (Proposal 1 negotiable: requiring employer to conduct testing in accordance with DHHS Guidelines and requiring agreement that methods and equipment used would meet the requirements of Guidelines).

A proposal that management use the most reliable testing methods and equipment in the implementation of its drug-testing program was nonnegotiable, NFFE Local 15 and Dept. of Army, Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 30 FLRA 1046, 1059-61 (1988):

...The proposal establishes a criterion governing management's selection of the methods and equipment to be used in any and all aspects of the testing program. It is broadly worded and does not distinguish between the particular parts or stages of the program or the purposes for which the tests and equipment would be used.... In short, management would be precluded from selecting equipment or methods which are reliable for a particular purpose if there were equipment and methods which were more reliable for that purpose.

By limiting the range of management's choices... Proposal 2 establishes a substantive criterion governing the exercise of management's determination of its internal security practices....

...

[B]y failing to distinguish between possible test uses and stages of testing and thus restricting management's ability to select a mix of tests... Proposal 2 excessively interferes with management's right to determine its internal security practices...and...is not an appropriate arrangement....

NFFE Local 15 determined Proposal 3, requiring tests to be performed by certified and qualified personnel to interfere with management rights, but the proposal was an appropriate arrangement for the exercise of those rights. The proposal ensured accuracy in the program. The burden on management was minimal. No compelling need was demonstrated to bar the proposal.

NFFE Local 15, 30 FLRA 1046, supra, was taken to court by the agency. The D.C. Circuit remanded the case to FLRA on the agency's motion for further consideration under the final DHHS Mandatory Guidelines for Federal Workplace Drug Testing (issued on April 11, 1988). In the NFFE Local 15 remand decision, reported at 33 FLRA 436, 439, 444 (1988), the proposal for safeguards to assure testing by qualified and certified operators and test personnel was consistent with the DHHS final Mandatory Guidelines of April 11, 1988. The Local 15 result was again considered by the D.C. Circuit in Dept. of Army, Aberdeen Proving Ground v. FLRA, 890 F.2d 467, 474 (D.C. Cir. 1989). At issue was the proposal that the employer agree to provide safeguards to assure that testing is not performed by unqualified or uncertified operators or test personnel. The proposal was inconsistent with DHHS Guidelines because those Guidelines established certification requirements for laboratories and their personnel. Although qualifications for individuals who performed tests were not set by the Guidelines, the laboratory officials were responsible for review and certification of all test results before they were forwarded to the employer. The court stated:

Requiring a laboratory to meet the additional standards envisioned by the proposal would be tantamount to saying that the Guidelines did not provide "exclusive" standards for qualifying or certifying test personnel. Any standards developed under the proposal would become particularly problematic if the testing personnel of a laboratory certified under the Guidelines failed to meet them.... The arbitrator would then face a dilemma between upholding the exclusivity of the Guidelines and enforcing the additional safeguards adopted pursuant to the proposal. The framework of management responsibility for laboratory staff that HHS laid out...mandating that employees meet the "outcome measure" of job performance, would be undermined to the extent that laboratory management would, additionally, have to hire only employees who meet the stricter standards generated pursuant to the proposal.

See NFFE Local 15 and Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 35 FLRA 936 (1990) (remand decision following court decision; proposal nonnegotiable because the Guidelines set the standards for certification of laboratories).

Provision 4, calling on management to agree that "[p]ersonnel involved in the collection/testing process shall be qualified to perform the assigned tasks," was nonnegotiable in NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, BATF, 41 FLRA 1106, 1115-116 (1991); see IFPTE Local 128 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, 39 FLRA 1500, 1508 (1991) (Proposal 4 nonnegotiable, inconsistent with Guidelines: "employees operating drug testing equipment be trained and certified by qualified instructors before any testing is started").

A negotiable proposal called on the agency to refer to the manufacturer's manual for information concerning how to set up drug tests. See IAM Local Lodge 2424 and Dept. of Army, Aberdeen Proving Ground, 31 FLRA 205 (1988) (Proposal 12(b)). On the technical side, NFFE Local 2015 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., 41 FLRA 1158, 1175, 1178-81 (1991), ruled negotiable Proposal 4, calling on the agency to agree "to use some form of disposable thermometers, such that each sample will be tested using a new bulb, to guard against the possibility of tainted samples." The proposal was not contrary to the final Guidelines, it did not interfere with the right of the agency to determine the technology of performing its work, and it did not excessively interfere with the agency's right to determine its internal security practices. AFGE Local 1808 and Dept. of Army, Sierra Army Depot, 42 FLRA 542, 552, 553-55 (1991), resolved as negotiable Proposal 6, requiring that "[t]he authorized collection agent will collect all drug testing specimens." See AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 875, 877-78 (1991) (Proposal 5F); AFGE Local 3457 and Dept. of Interior, Minerals Mgmt. Serv., 42 FLRA 567, 585-86 (1991) (Proposal 7: negotiable that authorized collection agency will collect all drug testing specimens; proposal did not prevent the agency from adhering to standards in Guidelines). IFPTE Local 1 and Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Shipyard, 49 FLRA 225, 250-52, 263-71 (1994), should be consulted for the discussion of Proposal 7, involving a variety of safeguards concerning testing procedures and equipment: cleaning and disposal of cuvettes, glassware; security of drug-testing laboratories; use of blind samples; labeling of drug standards; use of quality control specimens; storage requirements; and availability of documentation. The proposals were generally nonnegotiable to the extent that they mandated deviations from DHHS Guidelines. To the extent they proposed safeguards not covered by specific requirements in the Guidelines, the proposals were generally negotiable.
