c. Labor Arbitration Awards

Employees in bargaining units with collective bargaining contracts may elect to pursue through contract grievance processes actions otherwise appealable to the Board. For employees who are not in the Postal Service, the Board may review at the headquarters level the otherwise appealable action that resulted in an arbitration decision or award if discrimination is alleged before the arbitrator. For Postal Service employees who would otherwise have Board appeal rights, arbitration may be pursued under a collective bargaining contract; those employees may also have the right to appeal to the Board, that is, they are not forced to elect their remedy. By way of a request for Board review of an arbitration award for a Title 5 employee, or by way of a Board appeal by a Postal Service employee whose case was already arbitrated, the question is whether at the Board the arbitration award receives deference or has collateral estoppel effect. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards," "Postal Service Employees," and "Effect of Arbitration Award on Board Appeal."]

The Federal Circuit approved application of collateral estoppel to an arbitrator's decision during a later Board appeal by a Postal Service employee, Kroeger v. USPS, 865 F.2d 235, 238 (Fed. Cir. 1988):

As a preference eligible employee, Kroeger was entitled to appeal his removal to the Board.... On appeal to the Board, an appellant has a right to a hearing.... Kroger did appeal and did receive a hearing. Kroeger is simply wrong in equating his right to an appeal and a hearing with a right to a de novo review that would preclude application of collateral estoppel. Preference eligibility cannot justify relitigation of conclusively determined factual matters. The statute provides for an appeal but nowhere does it provide for de novo review and nowhere does it preclude use of collateral estoppel.

...

Whether the decision of a labor arbitrator can result in collateral estoppel before the board is a matter of first impression in this court. Other courts, however, have given an arbitration decision that meets the appropriate standards the same preclusive effect as any other decision.... We see no reason why an arbitrator's decision should not be given collateral estoppel effect before the board in the appropriate case, and no reason, in such cases, to force witnesses (if still available) to return for a repeat performance of their testimony.

In [Williams v. USPS, 35 MSPR 581 (1987)], after determining that Postal Service preference eligible employees should be treated no differently respecting the applicability of particular legal doctrines,...the board found it inappropriate to apply collateral estoppel in relation to arbitrators' decisions citing distinctive elements in the nature of an arbitral proceeding.... The board went on, however, to accord the arbitrator's decision "deference," saying "the board will, in most cases, only set them aside or modify them where the appellant shows that the arbitrator erred in interpreting civil service law, rules, or regulation."...

...The board's indication in Williams, that collateral estoppel may never be applied to preclude re-litigation of issues that had been submitted to binding arbitration, is incorrect. There are and will be cases in which application of collateral estoppel to the arbitrator's decision is justified.

Collateral estoppel is justified on the facts here. In [Thomas v. GSA, 794 F.2d 661 (Fed. Cir. 1986)], this court identified the criteria for application of collateral estoppel:

(i) the issue previously adjudicated is identical with that now presented, (ii) that issue was "actually litigated" in the prior case, (iii) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the end-decision then made, and (iv) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action.

[T]he board did not err in holding that the ALJ properly adopted the arbitrator's findings, and would not have erred if it had approved the ALJ's application of collateral estoppel in this case to bar Kroeger from relitigating facts found by the arbitrator.

Kroeger does not say he was denied opportunity to litigate the facts fully and fairly before the arbitrator. Nor does he set forth anything that was not presented to the arbitrator, but would be presented at a de novo hearing before the ALJ. Nor does he argue that the arbitration proceeding failed to meet any of the criteria set forth in Thomas. He simply labels the application of collateral estoppel "unfair." Some courts have cited fairness as a fifth criterion. See, e.g., Otherson, 711 F.2d at 272. Unfairness, however, is not an abstract concept supportable by mere assertions that procedures may in some cases differ before the ALJ and the arbitrator. Unfairness sufficient to bar application of collateral estoppel requires a specific showing that the precise dispute was not fairly litigated and resolved before the arbitrator. Kroeger has made no such showing.

Kirkpatrick v. USPS, 90 Fed. Appx. 392, 394-95 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP), reaffirmed application of collateral estoppel to merits and penalty determinations in Postal Service arbitration cases subsequently reviewed by the Board. In Kirkpatrick, the court analyzed several specific arguments raised by the petitioner in an attempt to demonstrate that, on the facts or on the law, the dispute was not fairly litigated and resolved before the arbitrator. The Federal Circuit suggested that the Board need not give preclusive effect to arbitration awards not directly involving a particular appellant, who is entitled to de novo review by the Board on the facts and law, including contract interpretation, affecting his appeal. See Horner v. Schuck, 843 F.2d 1368 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Res judicata did not prevent an agency from removing the appellant for lack of a security clearance after the arbitrator previously reinstated the appellant following an earlier removal based upon misconduct). Blagaich v. Dept. of Transp., 90 MSPR 619, 623-24 ¶ 11 (2001), aff'd, Blagaich v. Dept. of Transp., 63 Fed. Appx. 476 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (removal based on criminal conduct was not "the same cause of action" as a removal based on the failure of the employee to maintain the security clearance required for his position).

The Board distinguished Kroeger by declining to apply collateral estoppel to arbitrators' awards disposing of EEO allegations by Postal Service employees in Berkey v. USPS, 42 MSPR 24, 28 (1989); see Johnson v. USPS, 89 MSPR 373, 376 ¶ 7 (2001) ("The Board will not apply collateral estoppel to arbitrators' findings of prohibited discrimination in Postal Service cases and, instead, will resolve allegations of discrimination de novo."); cf. Collins v. Dept. of Transp., 89 MSPR 582, 588-89 ¶ 13 (2001) (res judicata not applied to IRA case that involved contract grievance withdrawn before it was adjudicated on merits). Geyer v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 682, 687 (1996), declined to apply collateral estoppel to an arbitrator's award in an IRA whistleblower reprisal appeal:

The Board may apply the doctrine of collateral estoppel if: (1) The issue previously adjudicated is identical to that now presented; (2) that issue was actually litigated in the prior case; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded by the doctrine was fully represented in the prior case. See Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 341 (1995). The Board, however, lacks the authority in an IRA appeal to adjudicate the merits of the underlying personnel action; rather, our jurisdiction is limited to adjudicating the whistleblower allegations. See Lewis v. Department of the Army, 63 MSPR 119, 125, 128-29 (1994), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1238 (Fed. Cir.) (Table), cert. denied, 116 S. Ct. 110 (1995). Thus, because the merits of the removal action are not before us in this IRA appeal, we find no basis on which to give collateral-estoppel effect to the arbitrator's findings regarding the merits of that action. See Peartree, 66 MSPR at 341. Nonetheless, as explained below, we will consider the arbitrator's decision as evidence showing that the agency would have taken the removal action absent the appellant's whistleblowing activities.

Yet res judicata was applied to bar a Board appeal through an IRA case of a removal that was earlier grieved under a negotiated agreement, even though the grievance proceeding did not appear to involve an assertion of whistleblowing reprisal, Johnson v. VA, 121 MSPR 695, 700 ¶¶ 9-10, 2014 MSPB 82 (2014):

A Board appeal that is not barred by section 7121(g) may, however, be barred under the doctrine of res judicata, Collins v. Department of Transportation, 89 M.S.P.R. 582, ¶ 13 (2001), which the agency raised as a defense in this case. Under that doctrine, a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action. Collins, 89 M.S.P.R. 582, ¶ 13. Thus, res judicata precludes parties from relitigating issues that were, or could have been, raised in the prior action, and is applicable if: (1) the prior judgment was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior judgment was a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases. Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 337 (1995).

In Giove v. Department of Transportation, 89 M.S.P.R. 560, ¶¶ 2, 4 (2001), aff'd, 50 Fed. Appx. 421 (Fed. Cir. 2002), the appellant filed a grievance regarding his removal. The grievance proceeded to arbitration, and the arbitrator held a hearing before issuing an award denying the grievance on the merits. Id., ¶¶ 2, 4. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit affirmed the arbitrator's award. Id., ¶ 4. Under these circumstances, the Board held that the appellant's Board appeal regarding his removal was barred by the doctrine of res judicata because it was a second action against the same agency based on the same set of facts giving rise to his right to seek relief from his removal by the agency, each party was afforded a full opportunity to present evidence and testimony before the arbitrator, and adjudicatory bodies of competent jurisdiction, specifically the arbitrator and the Federal Circuit, issued final judgments on the merits of the removal action. Id., ¶¶ 15-16.

The Board applied collateral estoppel to penalty review in USPS arbitration proceedings, other than as to EEO issues, involving preference eligible employees. See Farrelly v. USPS, 86 MSPR 230, 234-35 ¶¶ 10-12 (2000) (collateral estoppel was applied to a penalty determination when the arbitrator considered factors analogous to those itemized in Douglas); Farmer v. USPS, 80 MSPR 205, 210-11 ¶ 12 (1998) (collateral estoppel was applied to an arbitrator's determinations concerning the facts underlying a discharge and mitigation of the penalty); Newberry v. USPS, 49 MSPR 348, 353 n.6 (1991) (citing Berkey). But in Milligan v. USPS, 106 MSPR 414, 417-22 ¶¶ 7-19, 2007 MSPB 193 (2007), the appellant pursued a removal case to arbitration while he also filed an EEO complaint that challenged the removal. The arbitrator sustained the charges but mitigated the penalty to a suspension for "time-served," along with a reassignment. After the agency issued a final agency decision finding no reprisal or discrimination, the appellant, a preference-eligible employee, filed an appeal with the Board. The AJ applied principles of collateral estoppel and upheld the penalty. The Board then issued a decision stating that time-served suspensions are contrary to Douglas mitigation analysis, and legal errors, such as that involved in the penalty analysis, constitute an exception to the doctrine of collateral estoppel. The Board rejected application of res judicata to the preference eligible Postal Service employee's appeal because the arbitration award was contrary to Board precedent involving time-served suspensions:

As an initial matter, the AJ's discussion of the penalty is flawed as it relies on case law related to the standard of Board review of arbitrators' decisions under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d). Section 7121 does not apply to Postal Service cases. A preference-eligible Postal employee can file a grievance pursuant to a collective bargaining agreement (CBA) and a Board appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 7513 from the same adverse action. Mays v. U.S. Postal Service, 995 F.2d 1056, 1058 (Fed. Cir. 1993); Bacashihua v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 811 F.2d 1498, 1502 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Marjie v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 95, 98 (1996) (a Postal Service employee with Board appeal rights may file a grievance and a Board appeal from the same action, but such an employee does not have a right of Board review of an arbitration decision). An appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 7513 is a de novo proceeding. Morgan v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 607, 610-11 (1991). The burden and standards of proof in such a proceeding are governed by 5 U.S.C. § 7701, and do not include review of an arbitrator's findings under a deferential or any other standard.

Second, this case presents the question of whether collateral estoppel may be applied to the arbitrator's penalty selection. A party is collaterally estopped from relitigating an issue when (1) the issue is identical to one in a prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded by the doctrine had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action. McNeil v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 15 (2005). Normally, when a Postal grievance goes to arbitration and the criteria for collateral estoppel are met, neither the employee, nor the Postal Service, may relitigate the propriety of the penalty in a subsequent Board appeal. Farrelly v. U.S. Postal Service, 86 M.S.P.R. 230, ¶¶ 8-12 (2000), aff'd, 13 Fed. Appx. 910 (Fed. Cir. 2001); Farmer v. U.S. Postal Service, 80 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 13 (1998).

In the instant case, the criteria for collateral estoppel have been met. However, the Board has repeatedly held that, even where these criteria have been met, there are circumstances in which it is improper to apply collateral estoppel. E.g., Hay v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 167, ¶ 9 (2006) (the Board may refrain from applying collateral estoppel if its application would result in injustice or compromise public policy); Fulks v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 228, ¶¶ 22-27 (2005) (collateral estoppel should not be applied to an arbitration decision in which the penalty was determined by accident, i.e., by reference to the length of time taken by the appeal or administrative review process); Tanner v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 15 (2003) (the reviewing body may refrain from applying collateral estoppel if injustice or other public policy considerations would be compromised); Montalvo v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 48, 50-51 (1991) (collateral estoppel does not apply to pure questions of law or where the prior decision is facially incorrect under the Board's interpretation of civil service law). "For example, 'redetermination of issues may be warranted due to the different nature of one forum as compared to the other, or where there is reason to doubt the quality, extensiveness, or procedural or substantive fairness of the prior adjudication.'" Tanner, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 15. The Federal Circuit and Supreme Court have recognized the propriety of declining to apply collateral estoppel in such circumstances. See Haring v. Prosise, 462 U.S. 306, 313 n.7 (1983) (collateral estoppel is inappropriate when "controlling facts or legal principles have changed significantly" since the prior judgment, or when "special circumstances warrant an exception to the normal rules of preclusion"); Standefer v. United States, 447 U.S. 10, 23 n.18 (1980) (the estoppel doctrine is "premised upon an underlying confidence that the result achieved in the initial litigation was substantially correct"; "[i]n the absence of appellate review, or of similar procedures, such confidence is often unwarranted"); Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (where the requirements for collateral estoppel have been met, it would not be error for the Board to decline to apply that doctrine).

In this case, collateral estoppel may not be used to adopt a time-served suspension because doing so would be contrary to Board precedent. See Fulks, 100 M.S.P.R. 228, ¶¶ 22-27; Montalvo, 50 M.S.P.R. at 50-51. In the seminal case on this issue, Montalvo, the Board held that collateral estoppel effect cannot be given to an arbitrator's decision if the arbitrator mitigated the appellant's removal to a time-served suspension without pay. Montalvo, 50 M.S.P.R. at 50. The Board found that the doctrine of collateral estoppel does not apply to a pure question of law, such as whether the length of a suspension may be "determined by accident, rather than by a process of logical deliberation and decision." Id. Instead, arbitrators must apply the same substantive rules the Board must use when it reviews agency disciplinary actions, including application of Board precedent. Id. at 50-51.

With that in mind, the applicable Board case law requires the body reviewing a disciplinary action to consider the factors set forth in Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305-06 (1981). Returning to Montalvo, the Board in that case found that the arbitrator considered a number of factors in support of his mitigation decision, but he did not conduct a Douglas analysis or articulate any rationale for mitigating the penalty to a time-served suspension. Montalvo, 50 M.S.P.R. at 50. Thus, the Board held that the arbitrator's decision to mitigate Appellant Montalvo's penalty was contrary to prior court and Board rulings holding that suspension determinations made without articulation of a reasonable basis for the penalty selection are barred. Id. at 51. Using this rationale, the Board went on to make its own penalty determination based on the Douglas factors. Id.

Here, as in Montalvo, the arbitrator failed to conduct a Douglas factors analysis. Therefore, pursuant to Montalvo, the Board must make its own penalty determination in the present case.

Strengthening the argument that the Board must make its own penalty determination in this case is the recent Board decision of Fulks v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 228 (2005). Admittedly, Fulks is distinguishable from the instant appeal because it involved the Board's review of a non-Postal Service arbitration decision. However, Fulks is still relevant to the present case because in it the Board revisited and expanded its prior holding in Montalvo. Specifically, the Board held that an arbitrator's penalty selection of a time-served suspension cannot stand even if the arbitrator considered the relevant Douglas factors. As was the case in Montalvo, Fulks discussed the propriety of giving arbitration decisions collateral estoppel effect in situations where the arbitrator mitigated the agency's chosen penalty of removal to a time-served suspension. However, in Fulks, the Board further concluded that the arbitrator in Montalvo actually did consider at least some of the relevant Douglas factors. In support of this conclusion, the Board noted that the arbitrator in Montalvo had set forth a number of factors that he considered in mitigating the penalty. Consequently, the Board held that the law in Montalvo is clear; a time-served penalty is not improper simply because an arbitrator fails to consider the proper Douglas factors in deciding whether mitigation is appropriate. Rather, such a penalty is inappropriate if it is "determined by accident," i.e., by reference to the length of time taken by the grievance process (a time-served suspension). Fulks, 100 M.S.P.R. 228, ¶ 27. To put it more simply, the Board cannot sustain penalties that are arbitrary on their face. If an arbitrator imposes a time-served suspension penalty, the Board must hold that the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law pertaining to mitigation authority. Id., ¶ 29.

Accordingly, the Board may not apply collateral estoppel or otherwise impose the time-served suspension penalty selected by the arbitrator in this case. The proper course of action is to apply collateral estoppel to the arbitrator's decision with regard to the charged misconduct. Under Montalvo and Fulks, the Board is free to adopt the arbitrator's factual findings and then apply the Douglas factors to those findings to determine the appropriate penalty. Montalvo, 50 M.S.P.R. at 50-51; see Fulks, 100 M.S.P.R. 228, ¶¶ 28-31.

We note that it appears that under the CBA between the agency and its union, the agency may be required to comply with the arbitrator's decision to mitigate the removal, despite the impropriety of the time-served suspension penalty under Board law. Even so, as explained above, this is a de novo appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 7513, and the case law precludes us from imposing the arbitrator's time-served suspension penalty.

Our dissenting colleague disagrees and would give res judicata effect to the arbitrator's decision. We have found no case in which the Board has applied res judicata to an arbitration decision involving an employee of the Postal Service with Board appeal rights. Indeed, we believe that the application of res judicata in such cases would essentially prevent Postal Service employees with Board appeal rights from pursing both a grievance under their negotiated grievance procedure and an appeal to the Board. In defining res judicata, the Supreme Court has stated: "Under res judicata, a final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94 (1980). The Court has further noted "Because res judicata may govern grounds and defenses not previously litigated...it blockades unexplored paths that may lead to truth...It therefore is to be invoked only after careful inquiry." Brown v. Felsen, 442 U.S. 127, 132 (1979). The application of res judicata in the instant case is tantamount to requiring an election under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d). That section provides that an individual affected by a personnel action that is both appealable to the Board and covered by a negotiated grievance procedure may contest the action before the Board or via a grievance, but not both. This rule, however, does not apply to Postal Service employees who have the right to appeal actions directly to the Board. Such employees may file a grievance and a Board appeal from the same action. Marjie, 70 M.S.P.R. at 98. Giving res judicata effect to an arbitrator's decision in this context would make it effectively impossible to pursue both a Board appeal and a grievance because the resolution of the grievance through an arbitration decision would bar any litigation through a Board appeal. For these reasons, we find that it is inappropriate to apply the doctrine of res judicata to arbitration decisions involving Postal Service employees who have Board appeal rights.

This does not, however, mean that other principles of preclusion are inapplicable. The Board has long applied the doctrine of collateral estoppel to arbitration decisions involving Postal Service employees with Board appeal rights. See, e.g., Farrelly, 86 M.S.P.R. 230, ¶¶ 8-12. In contrast to res judicata, collateral estoppel bars the relitigation only of those issues of fact or law actually decided in the initial litigation. Allen, 449 U.S. at 94. In further contrast to res judicata, collateral estoppel, as discussed earlier, need not be applied when the result in the initial litigation is incorrect or would compromise public policy. Standefer, 447 U.S. at 23 n.18. Cf. Federated Department Stores v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 398 (1981) ("Res judicata consequences" of a final judgment in a prior action are not altered by the fact that "the judgment may have been wrong.")

Although application of collateral estoppel may result in a bar of further litigation, that will not always be the case. Thus, in Farrelly, 86 M.S.P.R. 230, the Board applied collateral estoppel to give preclusive effect to all of the findings and conclusions in the arbitrator's decision denying the appellant's grievance. Yet in Hay, 103 M.S.P.R. 167, the Board declined to apply the collateral estoppel doctrine because to do so would result in injustice or compromise public policy. Because the doctrine of collateral estoppel does not apply to issues or defenses not decided in the initial litigation and because it allows an exception for legally incorrect decisions, its application to arbitration decisions involving Postal Service employees with Board appeal rights does not result in an absolute bar to pursuit of a Board appeal. Instead, it provides a proper balance between the rights of such employees to pursue both a grievance and a Board appeal and the interest in conserving judicial resources that is served by the principles of preclusion.

For the reasons discussed earlier, we do not apply collateral estoppel to the arbitrator's penalty in this case. This would ordinarily require the Board to make the penalty determination. The Board, however, will not make that determination at this time because the litigation of this case raises an issue of fairness. In the prehearing conference, the administrative judge advised the parties that "the penalty that will be reviewed" in this appeal is the "time-served suspension and reassignment" imposed by the arbitrator. Again in her initial decision, the administrative judge indicated that she was reviewing the reasonableness of the time-served suspension. Thus, the appellant pursued his appeal with the objective of obtaining outright reversal of the disciplinary action taken against him, or alternatively, further mitigation beyond what the arbitrator ordered. The case was not litigated as a de novo appeal of the removal action and the appellant was not aware of the possibility that the removal action could be upheld. In such circumstances, we find that fairness dictates that this case be remanded for the parties to litigate the propriety of the agency's removal penalty.

USPS, 118 MSPR 10, 13 ¶ 10, 2012 MSPB 51 (2012) ("The Board has held that it is inappropriate to apply res judicata to arbitration decisions involving Postal Service employees who have Board appeal rights because such employees are permitted to file both a grievance and a Board appeal from the same adverse action. Milligan v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 414, ¶¶ 7, 16 (2007). As a preference eligible Postal Service employee, the appellant has Board appeal rights. 39 U.S.C. § 1005(a); 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B)(ii)."). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Method and Scope of MSPB Review," concerning procedures and standards governing review of arbitration awards.]

Collateral estoppel was properly applied to the factual findings of an arbitrator relating the merits of a proposed removal when the removal decision was appealed to the Board. Martin v. USPS, DA-0752-12-0270-I-1 (NP 9/27/2013) ("From our review of the record and the applicable law, the administrative judge correctly applied the doctrine of collateral estoppel, and therefore we discern no reason to disturb the administrative judge's finding that the appellant is estopped from re-litigating whether the agency proved the charged misconduct. See Ford v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 10, ¶ 11 (2012) (standard for applying collateral estoppel); Crosby v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 98, 106 (1997) (finding no reason to disturb the administrative judge's findings where the administrative judge considered the evidence as a whole, drew appropriate inferences, and made reasoned conclusions); Broughton v. Department of Health & Human Services, 33 M.S.P.R. 357, 359 (1987) (same).").

Res judicata applies to decisions of tribunals. See Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 89 MSPR 571, 576 ¶ 18 (2001) (res judicata did not apply to an agency grievance decision; "letters issued by the step 2 and step 3 grievance officials were not 'judgments' that trigger the application of the doctrine of res judicata"). In facts not clear from the opinion, the Board stated in dictum that if an appellant's case was in some fashion related to a grievance prosecuted by the appellant, and if the AJ independently had jurisdiction over and independently reviewed the facts of the case, the agency's ultimate disposition of the appellant's grievance would not serve as a ground for reversal of the AJ's disposition of the appeal. See Graham v. USPS, 4 MSPR 47, 49, 4 MSPB 137 (1980) (apparently a grievance under a labor agreement). This point was more clearly made in Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 184, 187 (1997), involving a grievance that was not pursued through arbitration:

The Board may apply collateral estoppel if: (1) The issue previously adjudicated is identical to that now presented; (2) that issue was actually litigated in the prior case; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded by the doctrine was fully represented in the prior case. See Kroeger v. USPS, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Peartree v. USPS, 66 MSPR 332, 341 (1995).

The "actual litigation" prerequisite for the application of collateral estoppel means that the issue must have been contested by the parties and submitted for determination by the court. See Raymond v. Dept. of the Army, 34 MSPR 476, 480 (1987) (citing Otherson v. Dept. of Justice, 711 F.2d 267, 273 (D.C. Cir. 1983)); see also Fisher v. Dept. of Defense, 64 MSPR 509, 514 (1994) (the "actual litigation" criterion requires that the issue must have been contested by the parties and resolved by the adjudicator). Implicit in this requirement is the notion that, when the parties "contest" an issue, the issue will be submitted for determination and resolved by a court or other neutral adjudicator. See, e.g., Fisher, 64 MSPR at 513-15 (applying collateral estoppel to a prior Board decision); Curry v. USPS, 52 MSPR 336, 339 (1992) (applying collateral estoppel to Postal Service arbitration proceedings); Raymond, 34 MSPR at 481 (applying collateral estoppel to a guilty plea conviction in a Federal district court).

Here, the Step 3 grievance, which accused the agency of violating various provisions of the Negotiated Agreement, was submitted by the appellant and his union for determination by an agency management official. In no sense can the issue of the voluntariness of the appellant's absence be deemed "actually litigated" when, in effect, the appellant presented his case to his opposing party and his opposing party ruled against him.
