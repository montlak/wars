b. Arrest, Conviction

According to Wright v. USPS, 84 MSPR 607, 611-12 ¶ 10 (1999), an arrest is not ordinarily probative as impeachment evidence; a conviction is of value in impeachment if the conviction implicates the truthfulness of the witness:

The Board has recognized the utility of Rule 609 of the Federal Rules of Evidence, which permits impeachment of a witness's credibility only with evidence of convictions, not arrests. Fouquet v. Department of Agriculture, 82 MSPR 548, 563 (1999), citing Green v. Bock Laundry Machine Company, 109 S. Ct. 1981, 1983 (1989). Under Rule 609, evidence only of an arrest is clearly inadmissible to impeach witness credibility. See, e.g., U.S. v. Jones, 965 F.2d 1507, 1513-14 (8th Cir. 1992), cert. denied sub. nom., Roulette v. U.S., 508 U.S. 941 (1993). Further, Rule 608 of the Federal Rules of Evidence, permitting extrinsic evidence of bad acts not involving felony convictions, would generally not allow the use of such impeaching evidence where the bad act alleged did not involve untruthfulness. See Fouquet, 82 MSPR at 563. For purposes of witness credibility impeachment, drug charges are not usually considered probative of honesty or truthfulness. See Wright & Gold, Federal Practice and Procedure, Evidence § 6118 at n.90. The evidence here, asserted for the purpose of impeaching the credibility of Vasquez, was only of an arrest; it is undisputed that the charge supporting the arrest was dismissed; and the charge supporting the arrest was not one clearly involving untruthfulness. We find that such evidence against Vasquez would clearly have been inadmissible below. Therefore, absent any evidence of a conviction of a charge involving untruthfulness, there is no reason to reopen this appeal to discuss the matter further.

Although the administrative judge allowed the appellant to present evidence regarding the taxpayer's arrest history, the Board has recognized the application of Federal Rule of Evidence 609, which permits impeachment of a witness's credibility only with evidence of convictions, not arrests. Wright v. U.S. Postal Service, 84 M.S.P.R. 607, ¶ 10 (1999); see Yanopoulos v. Department of the Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (determining that although the Federal Rules of Evidence are not applicable to Board proceedings, they are a helpful guide to proper hearing practice). Under Federal Rule of Evidence 608, the appellant could cross-examine the taxpayer regarding her prior arrests, but only to the extent that they involved charges probative of her character for truthfulness. See Wright, 84 M.S.P.R. 607, ¶ 10.

At least as to criminal conduct, the Supreme Court states that Rule 609(a)(1) of the Federal Rules of Evidence requires a judge to let a civil litigant impeach the credibility of a witness with evidence of prior felony convictions of that witness. Green v. Bock Laundry Machine Co., 109 S. Ct. 1981, 1993 (1989).

On inquiry into prior bad acts, the questioner is normally bound by the answer of the witness. If the witness denies the misconduct, it cannot be proved by other evidence. The purpose is to prevent the proliferation of collateral issues in the litigation. See Hawkins v. Smithsonian Inst., 73 MSPR 397, 405 (1997) (noting the scope of Rule 608). The adjudicator has discretion, however, to permit extrinsic proof if the proof is sufficiently probative of credibility. The exception, under Fed. R. Evid. 609, permits extrinsic proof of most crimes that are either felonies or involve dishonesty or false statement, regardless of punishment. One caution: an attorney is expected to have at least a good faith belief of the misconduct inquired into before questions are posed to a witness about past conduct. This is because of the prejudicial effect of the question alone in front of a jury. Although there are no juries in Board cases, ethically there is a requirement that lawyers conduct themselves before tribunals with due regard for legal custom and refrain from harassing tactics. The good faith belief requirement should be followed in Board hearings.
