1. Necessity for Impact and Implementation Bargaining

Although many management decisions may be nonnegotiable, for example, a determination to conduct a reduction in force, the immediate impact or reasonably foreseeable effects of those decisions may be negotiable, for example, the ability of employees who are downgraded to secure consideration for future vacancies at a higher grade. See, e.g., Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 35 FLRA 844, 855-56 (1990) (concerning negotiability of certain RIF procedures through impact bargaining). Similarly, once a past policy or practice matures into a term or condition of employment, it cannot be unilaterally changed. SSA and AFGE, 13 FLRA 112, 123 (1983) (ALJ Decision).

FLRA summarized the substantive aspects of impact bargaining, Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org. and Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, Nat'l Weather Serv., 37 FLRA 392, 395-96 (1990):

The duty to bargain under the Statute requires that, absent a clear and unmistakable waiver of bargaining rights, parties satisfy their mutual obligation to bargain before implementing changes in conditions of employment of unit employees.... With respect to management-initiated changes, a union must be provided with notice of the change and an opportunity to bargain, to the extent consistent with the Statute, over the change....

The extent to which an agency is required to bargain over changes in conditions of employment depends on the nature of the change. Specifically, a union may be entitled...to negotiate over the actual decision to implement the change.... An agency's decision to change a condition of employment may constitute an exercise of a management right under section 7106, however. If so, the agency only is obligated to bargain over the impact and implementation of the decision....

In addition, an agency is not obligated to bargain over the impact and implementation of a decision to change conditions of employment if the effect of the change on working conditions is de minimis.... Where a decision to make a change in conditions of employment is itself negotiable, however, the effect of the change on working conditions is not relevant....

Consistent with an agency's obligation to bargain, an agency may not implement changes in conditions of employment of unit employees, without agreement of the union, except in specific circumstances. In other words, with specific exceptions, an agency is required to maintain the status quo during the bargaining process.... Those circumstances include, among others, ones where the union has not timely requested bargaining or timely requested the services of the Federal Service Impasses Panel, or where implementation is consistent with the necessary functioning of the agency.... In addition, an agency's implementation of a change does not violate the Statute if "all" of a union's proposals regarding the change are nonnegotiable....

Fed. Bureau of Prisons, FCI Bastrop and AFGE Local 3828, 55 FLRA 848, 852 (1999), also described agency bargaining obligations when employment conditions are changed:

Prior to implementing a change in conditions of employment of bargaining unit employees, an agency is required to provide the exclusive representative with notice and an opportunity to bargain over those aspects of the change that are within the duty to bargain. See, e.g., U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, Memphis District, Memphis, Tennessee, 53 FLRA 79, 81 (1997). Absent a waiver of bargaining rights, parties must satisfy their mutual obligation to bargain before implementing changes in conditions of employment. See id.; National Weather Service Employees Organization and U.S. Department of Commerce, National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, National Weather Service, 37 FLRA 392, 395 (1990).

The extent to which an agency is required to bargain over changes in conditions of employment depends on the nature of the change. Specifically, a union may be entitled under the Statute to negotiate over the actual decision, or substance, of the change. See, e.g., Department of the Navy, Puget Sound Naval Shipyard, Bremerton, Washington, 35 FLRA 153, 155 (1990). An agency's decision to change a condition of employment may constitute an exercise of a management right under section 7106, however, and is not negotiable. Where the substance of the decision is not itself subject to negotiation, the agency is nonetheless obligated to bargain over the impact and implementation of that decision if the resulting changes have a more than de minimis effect on conditions of employment. See Department of Health and Human Services, Social Security Administration, 24 FLRA 403, 407-08 (1986). In circumstances where the effect of the change is more than de minimis, and the agency fails to provide adequate prior notice thereof to the exclusive representative, or rejects a timely request for negotiations pursuant to section 7106(b)(2) and (3) of the Statute, the agency will be found to have violated section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute. See, e.g., U.S. Government Printing Office, 13 FLRA 203 (1983); Department of the Air Force, Scott Air Force Base, Illinois, 5 FLRA 9 (1981).

In addition, where a union submits bargaining proposals and an agency refuses to bargain over them based on the contention that they are non-negotiable, the agency acts at its peril if it then implements the proposed change in conditions of employment. If the union's proposal is held to be negotiable, the agency will be found to have violated section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute by implementing the change without bargaining over the negotiable proposal. See U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Social Security Administration, Baltimore, Maryland, 39 FLRA 258, 262-63 (1991).

See DHS, Customs & Border Security and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 65 FLRA 88 (2010) (under analysis in Fed. Correctional Institution, 8 FLRA 604, 606 (1982) (FCI), arbitrator can order a status quo ante remedy if the agency exercises its right to implement a change but fails to bargain over impact).

The scope of impact negotiations includes both procedural and arrangements bargaining, GSA and AFGE Council 236, 62 FLRA 341, 343 (2008) (reviewing arbitration award):

The Arbitrator here found that, even though the subject of rotational assignments concerned the assignment of work under § 7106(a)(2)(B) of the Statute, the Agency was nevertheless obligated to bargain over procedures and appropriate arrangements pursuant to § 7106(b)(2) and (3) of the Statute. The Agency objects that bargaining under § 7106(b) is "permissive" and that the award appears to require substantive bargaining, in violation of § 7106(a) of the Statute. However, where an agency proposes to change the conditions of employment of unit employees pursuant to a management right under § 7106(a) or § 7106(b)(1), it is obligated to bargain over procedures, under § 7106(b)(2), and appropriate arrangements, under § 7106(b)(3), that address the particular change proposed. See United States Dep't of the Treasury, Customs Service, Washington, D.C., 59 FLRA 703, 709 (2004); Dep't of Health and Human Services, Social Security Admin., 44 FLRA 870, 881 (1992). As such, bargaining under § 7106(b)(2) and (3) does not violate the Agency's § 7106(a) rights and, therefore, the award is not deficient on this ground. Further, nothing in the award indicates that the Arbitrator ordered the Agency to bargain over substantive matters that are not negotiable. See Award at 12, 14 (indicating that the Agency is required to bargain over § 7106(b)(2) and (3) matters). As the award does not require bargaining that violates the Agency's rights under § 7106(a) of the Statute, we reject the Agency's argument that the award violates its management rights.

There is a caveat to the general obligation to bargain over impact: if the matter sought to be negotiated is already contained in the parties' agreement, and if there is no assertion that the procedures in the agreement were not followed properly, the agency has no further duty to bargain over the impact and implementation of that matter. See Naval Aviation Depot, Norfolk and IAM Local Lodge 39, 39 FLRA 1597, 1598 (1991) (ALJ Decision). [Refer in this chapter to "Midterm Bargaining Obligation and the 'Covered By' Doctrine: SSA."] There is a qualification on impact bargaining: the scope of negotiations is limited by the exercise of the management right having an impact on the unit. The D.C. Circuit declined to enforce an FLRA ULP decision that the employer improperly refused to bargain over space arrangements sought by the union in response to a reorganization. The decentralization of the activity and the space demands of the union were not related, except to the extent that the relocation would free up some space that the union sought to occupy. See FLRA v. DOJ, 994 F.2d 868, 873-74 (D.C. Cir. 1993) [discussed in Chapter 5, "Reasonably Foreseeable Adverse Effects; Speculative Adverse Effect."].

If an agency exercising a management right under § 7106 changes conditions of employment resulting in an impact or a reasonably foreseeable impact on unit employees, the agency is obligated under the Statute to provide adequate prior notice to the exclusive representative and, on request, bargain pursuant to §§ 7106(b)(2) and (3) concerning the procedures used to implement those changes or appropriate arrangements for unit employees adversely affected by those changes. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Jacksonville Dist. and NTEU, 15 FLRA 1014, 1026 (1984) (ALJ Decision) (establishment of specialized work groups). An agency may not unilaterally change a condition of employment established through past practice, even if the condition established by the practice differs from the express terms of the parties' collectively bargained agreement. PTO and POPA, 39 FLRA 1477, 1482-83 (1991). Past practice may be invoked as a defense to a charge of repudiation of an agreement: a past practice may modify or alter an agreement, and, if management abides by an established practice, it will not have committed a ULP by repudiating a contract provision altered by that practice. DOJ, INS, Border Patrol and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 41 FLRA 154, 169-70 (1991). [Refer later in this chapter to "Past Practice Varies From Contract Language."]

Without a change in a condition of employment, there is no duty to bargain impact or implementation. See VAMC, Leavenworth and AFGE Local 85, 40 FLRA 592, 612 (1991) (ALJ Decision) (no evidence that decision to discontinue sale of tobacco products in canteen used by patients constituted change in employment conditions). Assuming a change, if an agency exercises a management right but fails to provide adequate prior notice to the union or rejects a timely request for negotiations pursuant to §§ 7106(b)(2) and (3), the agency violates §§ 7116(a)(1) and (5). GPO and Joint Council of Unions, GPO, 13 FLRA 203, 228-29 (1983) (ALJ Decision). During the time that it completes implementation bargaining, the agency is required to maintain the status quo, except to the extent consistent with the necessary functioning of the agency. SSA and AFGE, 35 FLRA 296, 313 (1990) (ALJ Decision). For a good analysis of the duty to provide impact and implementation bargaining over changes otherwise within management's rights, review DOJ, INS, El Paso Dist. Office and AFGE Local 1210, 34 FLRA 1035 (1990). The bargaining obligation is not triggered by just the actual impact of a change; the test is one of reasonable foreseeability. Customs Serv. and NTEU, 29 FLRA 891, 899 (1987). It was reasonably foreseeable that the reorganization of a department would have an impact by reducing promotional opportunities and eliminating jobs. GPO and Joint Council of Unions, GPO, 13 FLRA 203, 226 (1983) (ALJ Decision). If impact continues over time, the obligation to bargain may also continue. If the parties bargain over a work evaluation or quality review program to be instituted through a pilot program, those negotiations do not waive subsequent impact bargaining concerning proposed changes in procedures and arrangements for employees affected by the new system based on experience gained during the pilot project. IRS and NTEU, 10 FLRA 182, 183-84 (1982).

Management may not unilaterally impose limitations on the continuation of past practices. Management may not, for example, tell the union that it intends to discontinue a practice in a month and, without the union's consent to the discontinuation, then refuse to bargain, Dept. of Labor and AFGE Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, Local 1748, 38 FLRA 899, 911-12 (1990):

Consistent with the overall obligation to bargain, one party cannot establish an agreement merely by "telling" the other party what the agreement is....

A waiver of bargaining rights must be clear and unmistakable. See, for example, Department of the Air Force, Scott Air Force Base, Illinois, 5 FLRA 9 (1981). See also, for example, United States Department of the Navy, United States Marine Corps and Marine Corps Logistics Base, 38 FLRA No. 59 (1990). The evidence before us does not establish that the Union clearly and unmistakably agreed that the practice of providing bottled water could be terminated without bargaining, provided the Union was given 30 days' notice....

Negotiations over the substance of a change in working conditions are limited by management rights. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Midwest Reg'l Office, Chicago and NTEU Chap. 95, 16 FLRA 141, 145 (1984). But management pursues a management rights defense at its risk. If an agency changes conditions of employment without providing the union with the opportunity to bargain, and if it is subsequently determined that the agency should have negotiated over the change, following a negotiability determination, the agency's failure to bargain violates §§ 7116(a)(1) and (5). If it is subsequently determined that the agency has no duty to bargain, the failure of the agency to have done so before making the change does not violate the Statute. See DHUD and AFGE Local 3701, 58 FLRA 33, 34 (2002).

It is not necessarily a violation of the Statute for management to change a working condition during the pendency of a negotiability appeal (as opposed to a case pending before FSIP). The underlying negotiability issue must be resolved before it can be determined if the agency's action violated its bargaining duty. Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 18 FLRA 731, 732 (1985). FLRA summarized matters in DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 39 FLRA 258, 262-63 (1991):

Where a bargaining obligation arises by virtue of an agency changing conditions of employment, the Agency is required to bargain only over negotiable proposals addressing those changes. See, for example, Department of Health and Human Services, Social Security Administration, Baltimore, 31 FLRA 651, 656 (1988). Where a union submits bargaining proposals and an agency refuses to bargain over them based on the contention that they are nonnegotiable, however, the agency acts at its peril if it then implements the proposed change in conditions of employment. If the union's proposal is held to be negotiable, the agency will be found to have violated section 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute by implementing the change without bargaining over the negotiable proposal.

Impact bargaining is distinct from substantive or decision bargaining. If an agency effectuates a change in working conditions by exercising one of its reserved rights, its duty to bargain is limited to the impact and implementation of that change. The degree of impact or reasonably foreseeable impact is relevant. But if the decision to make a change is negotiable, the question is whether the obligation to notify and negotiate was fulfilled, not the extent of impact of any unilateral change in conditions of employment on the unit employees. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 19 FLRA 1085, 1088 (1985); see Marine Corps Logistics Base, Barstow and AFGE Local 1482, 33 FLRA 196, 202 (1988) ("Where the decision to make a change in conditions of employment is itself negotiable, the extent of the impact of the change in conditions of employment on unit employees is not relevant. The question is whether the statutory obligation to notify and negotiate with the exclusive representative concerning the change was fulfilled."). The latter inquiry is appropriate when the bargaining is limited to procedures and appropriate arrangements under §§ 7106(b)(2) and (3). Army Reserve Components Personnel and Admin. Ctr. and AFGE Local 900, 19 FLRA 290, 293 (1985). Decisions are discussed below concerning specific areas of impact bargaining.

FLRA summarized the distinctions in approach toward a failure to bargain over impact and a decision in 92nd Bomb Wing, Fairchild AFB, Spokane and NFFE Local 11, 50 FLRA 701, 704-05 (1995) (involving failure to bargain sign-in procedure):

Where an agency institutes a change in a condition of employment and the change is itself negotiable, the extent of the impact of the change on unit employees is not relevant to whether an agency is obligated to bargain. E.g., Department of Health and Human Services and Social Security Administration, 30 FLRA 922, 926 (1988). In contrast, where an agency in exercising a management right under section 7106 of the Statute changes a condition of employment of bargaining unit employees, a statutory obligation to bargain concerning the impact of such change exists only if the change either results in more than a de minimis impact on unit employees or such impact is reasonably foreseeable. E.g., Department of Transportation, Federal Aviation Administration, 19 FLRA 472, 476 (1985). In agreement with the Judge, we find that the decision to institute the sign-out board was itself negotiable. Consequently, there is no need to analyze the extent of the impact of the change in conditions of employment on bargaining unit employees. We conclude that the Respondent was obligated to notify and negotiate with the exclusive representative concerning that change.

The line between decision (substantive) and impact bargaining is not always clear. Major aspects of an agency policy, e.g., a smoking prohibition, may be generally nonnegotiable if the policy becomes a method the agency uses to accomplish its mission, e.g., health care. But details of the policy may be negotiable. If the agency cuts off bargaining over the substance of the change in policy, it may effectively and improperly preclude impact bargaining over negotiable details of that policy. FLRA concluded in DHHS, Pub. Health Serv., Rapid City and NFFE Local 179, 37 FLRA 972, 980 (1990), that proposals need not be precisely labeled:

We are unwilling to interpret the Statute in a manner which would require a union, in this circumstance, to label its proposals in a particular way to preserve its right to bargain. To do so, in our view, would encourage the parties to engage in semantic disputes instead of collective bargaining and, as such, would not be consistent with the policies and purposes of the Statute.

We add a word or two concerning the element of intent. A ULP is not a criminal proceeding. Intent to violate the law is not a necessary element in a failure to bargain case. Marine Corps Logistics Base, Barstow and AFGE Local 1482, 33 FLRA 196, 202 (1988), determined that the state of mind of a manager or his inexperience has no bearing on the disposition of the ULP.
