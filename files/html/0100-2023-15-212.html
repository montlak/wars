a. Private Speech

First Amendment protections under Pickering do not protect private speech--speech that is only personal in nature. Private speech may adversely affect the service. Adverse action was justified when an agency had to undertake an extensive investigation to determine the veracity of an appellant's allegations that employees perjured themselves during an MSPB hearing; the appellant had reason and opportunity to know the falsity of those allegations. Barnes v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 243, 246-47 (1984) (rejecting an argument that the appellant's communications were union-related; that under NLRB v. Red Top, 455 F.2d 721 (8th Cir. 1972), the statements would be unprotected if union related), aff'd, Barnes v. Small, 840 F.2d 972 (D.C. Cir. 1988); see Brown v. FAA, 15 MSPR 224, 229 (1983) (discussing the Connick decision of the Supreme Court). The questioning of coworkers about the personal lives of others does not have the protection of the First Amendment. The speech does not concern a matter of public interest, a phrase fairly interpreted as relating to political, social, or community concerns. Hantzicostas v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-1466); see Means v. Dept. of Labor, 60 MSPR 108, 113-14 (1993) (reviewing an arbitrator's decision: the appellant did not argue, nor was there an indication, that his statements were protected under the First Amendment; no bona fide claim of "privacy" interest in "what he displays on his office wall"); Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 353 (1987) (substance of conversations with another concerning a shared interest in falconry did not rise to a level of "matters of public concern" and did not implicate First Amendment protections).

The line between comments by an employee that are of public interest but unprotected by the First Amendment, and comments that are of public concern and protected, is subjective, existing in the mind of the adjudicator, as demonstrated in Haddon v. Executive Residence at the White House, 313 F.3d 1352, 1360-61 (Fed. Cir. 2002):

Mr. Haddon alleges that some of the reasons given by the Executive Residence for discharging and then not rehiring him were not legitimate reasons because they violated his constitutional rights. In this regard, he points to statements he made about the First Family in the media and the lawsuits he filed against his co-workers. Mr. Haddon claims that the statements he made to the media (in interviews with The Washington Post and radio stations) were protected First Amendment speech for which he may not be terminated. The statements he made dealt with the First Family's food preferences, the staff's refusal to heed the President's food allergies, the President's lateness for dinner, and the poor service the staff was giving the First Family.

In order for a public employee's speech to qualify for First Amendment protection, this court has noted that:

The Supreme Court of the United States has established a two-part test to determine whether the speech of a public employee is protected under the First Amendment. The court must determine whether the speech addresses a matter of public concern and if so, whether the government's interest in the effective and efficient fulfillment of its responsibilities outweighs the employee's right to speak.

Henry v. Dep't of the Navy, 902 F.2d 949, 951 (Fed. Cir. 1990). These are both questions of law. Branton v. City of Dallas, 272 F.3d 730, 739 (5th Cir. 2001). An employee's speech addresses a matter of public concern if it can "be fairly construed as relating to any matter of political, social, or other concern to the community...." Connick v. Myers, 461 U.S. 138, 146, 103 S. Ct. 1684, 75 L. Ed. 2d 708 (1983). The inquiry depends on the "content, form, and context [of the speech], as revealed by the whole record." Id. at 147-48, 103 S. Ct. 1684. While at first glance Haddon's comments in the media may seem like matters of public concern, the answer is not that clear. We must keep in mind that just because the public may be interested in hearing about a subject, that subject does not automatically become a matter of public concern for purposes of the First Amendment. The Supreme Court has cautioned against being too quick to decide that events occurring in a government office are matters of public concern, stating that "[t]o presume that all matters which transpire within a government office are of public concern would mean that every remark--and certainly every criticism directed at a public official--would plant the seed of a constitutional case." Id. at 149, 103 S. Ct. 1684.

Mr. Haddon argues that "[i]ntentionally poor service by government employees, and serving foods which adversely affect the health of the President...are unquestionably matters of public concern." However, under this logic, nearly any private detail concerning the First Family could be turned into a matter of public concern. That cannot be the law. Further, Mr. Haddon's public statements went beyond simply expressing his concern for the President's health and the poor service given to the President by the staff. He also discussed the President's lateness for meals and related details of specific incidents that would seem unnecessary to a discussion of the President's health or the staff's poor service. We conclude that the statements Mr. Haddon made to the media are not related to matters of public concern and are thus not statements for which a public employee is entitled to protection under the First Amendment.

Even if we were to conclude that Mr. Haddon's statements related to matters of public concern, the balancing of interests required by the second part of the First Amendment test this court articulated in Henry favors the Executive Residence. If the "interest of the agency as employer in promoting the efficiency of the public service it performs...outweigh[s] [the employee's] interest in speaking on a public issue without fear of retaliatory dismissal," then the employee's speech is not protected. Brown v. Dep't of Transp., Fed. Aviation Admin., 735 F.2d 543, 546 (Fed. Cir. 1984). To state the balancing test another way:

Even if an employee's speech is on a matter of public concern, a government employer is entitled to restrict that speech if it can carry its burden of proving that the interest of the public employee as a citizen in commenting on the matter is outweighed by the interest of the state, as employer, in promoting effective and efficient public service.

Gustafson v. Jones, 290 F.3d 895, 909 (7th Cir. 2002) (citing Pickering v. Bd. of Educ., 391 U.S. 563, 568, 88 S. Ct. 1731, 20 L. Ed. 2d 811 (1968)). This is a fact-specific inquiry, involving factors such as "whether the speech would create problems in maintaining discipline and harmony among co-workers" and "whether the employment relationship is one in which personal loyalty and confidence are necessary." Id. The factors here favor the Executive Residence; clearly, it is important that White House staff can be trusted to keep private matters private.

The Board declined to provide constitutional protection for comments to a congressional office involving private concerns, SSA v. White, CB-7521-11-0004-T-1 (NP 4/22/2013):

The respondent contends that her conduct in Senator Landrieu's office was an exercise of her constitutional right to petition the government for redress of grievances and is therefore not subject to discipline. In support of her argument, she cites the Supreme Court's decision in Borough of Duryea v. Guarnieri, 131 S. Ct. 2488 (2011), for the proposition that a public employee has a right to petition the government on his own behalf or on behalf of another. However, in Guarnieri, the Court did not hold that the public employee's right to petition is absolute, but rather held that the framework for assessing public employee claims under the Speech Clause of the First Amendment applies with equal force to claims arising under the Petition Clause of that Amendment. Id. at 2500. Thus, as in speech cases, when a public employee "petitions as a citizen on a matter of public concern," her First Amendment interest "must be balanced against the countervailing interest of the government in the effective and efficient management of its internal affairs." Id. (citing Pickering v. Board of Education, 391 U.S. 563, 568 (1968)). By contrast, when a public employee petitions on a matter of "purely private concern," no balancing test is necessary, and the employee's First Amendment interest "must give way" to the government's interest. Id. (citing City of San Diego v. Roe, 543 U.S. 77, 82-83 (2004)).

Hence, the threshold inquiry in determining whether the respondent's First Amendment rights preclude disciplinary action is whether her petition to Senator Landrieu's office touched upon a matter of public concern. See Roe, 543 U.S. at 82-83. Whether a public employee's petition involves a matter of public concern depends on the "the content, form, and context" of the petition. Guarnieri, 131 S. Ct. at 2500 (quoting Connick v. Myers, 461 U.S. 138, 147-48 (1983)). Considering these factors, we agree with the ALJ that the respondent's effort on behalf of another individual to secure the Senator's intervention in order to ensure Medicare payment for a bone marrow transplant involved a purely private concern, and the respondent does not dispute this characterization on petition for review. Consequently, no balancing is necessary, and the respondent's First Amendment interest must give way to the government's interest. See Guarnieri, 131 S. Ct. at 2500; Roe, 543 U.S. at 82-83; Connick, 461 U.S. at 147.

We stress that a federal employee may be disciplined for Petition Clause activity, even on a matter of purely private concern, only if the applicable legal standard for discipline is satisfied. We have found that the applicable standard, i.e., "good cause" under 5 U.S.C. § 7521, is satisfied in this case. Accordingly, we conclude that the respondent may be disciplined for her behavior in Senator's Landrieu's office.
