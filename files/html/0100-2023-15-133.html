3. Failure to Follow Policy, Regulation

Failure to follow a policy or regulation may be the result of neglect or intent. The failing is actionable, although intent aggravates the situation and lack of knowledge of the policy or regulation negates the charge or mitigates the situation. Under Tucker v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-13-0483-I-1 (NP 7/24/2015):

To prove a charge of failure to follow policy or procedure, an agency must show that a proper policy or procedure existed and that the employee failed to follow it, without regard to whether the failure was intentional or unintentional. See, e.g., Hamilton v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 547, 555-57 (1996) (finding that an agency may establish a charge of failure to follow supervisory instructions by showing that proper instructions were given to an employee and that he failed to follow them, notwithstanding whether the failure was intentional or unintentional). Generally, in an adverse action appeal, the agency must prove its charge by a preponderance of the evidence. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B).

Insubordination, a charge distinct from a failure to follow a policy, may encompass intentional and willful disobedience of standing procedural or substantive policies. See Webster v. Dept. of Army, 911 F.2d 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990); Korman v. United States, 462 F.2d 1382, 1385 (Ct. Cl. 1972) ("No organization could continue to function adequately, or even survive, if its members could disobey with impunity the controlling rules."). The point was made in Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 500, 509 (1995), concerning a willful violation of a policy:

Despite being told by a supervisor that he could not store the personal items on government property and that he was to remove them, the appellant continued to store the items there for an additional 4 months. Thus, he willfully violated the policy, procedures and laws that he was responsible for enforcing, thereby conveying a message to his subordinates and peers that it was appropriate to essentially "play by one's own rules." Further, the appellant's flaunting of agency rules and regulations, both in misappropriating agency funds and in misusing government property, if not checked, would eventually result in disrespect and the undermining of agency goals and objectives, not to mention depletion and waste of the agency's resources. The agency cannot countenance individuals who ignore its mission in order to run their own programs.

Willfulness may be inferred by a knowledge of the policy through recent warnings and reminders prior to the adverse action. See Guthrie v. USPS, 41 MSPR 102, 106 (1989) (failure to wear safety shoes); Bonds v. Dept. of Army, 20 MSPR 349, 351 (1984) (removal of a store checker for a knowing rather than accidental or negligent violation of a policy governing the handling of customer refunds; the infraction involved a refund to a customer of $1; the policy was common knowledge). A suspension was justified for a VA nurse who failed to properly attend to a patient in violation of hospital regulations requiring a high level of monitoring for that class of patients. Steel v. VA, 21 MSPR 224, 226-27 (1984). Removal was justified for, among other offenses, countermanding a physician's order directing a particular treatment for a prison inmate. Listerman v. DOJ, 31 MSPR 179, 182-83 (1986) (physician's assistant). Discipline was imposed because a correctional officer failed to follow mandated policy of using reasonable force to subdue an inmate who had attacked a fellow officer. Cotton v. DOJ, 53 MSPR 397, 404-05 (1992); cf. Korowin v. DOJ, 10 MSPR 17, 20 (1982) (suspension, rather than removal, imposed against supervisory recreational specialist who did not properly respond to prison emergency). Although the agency has the burden of proving the appellant's failure to follow a policy, "the agency's burden of proving its charges by preponderant evidence does not require it to show that each and every exception to the rule did not apply for each date." Leonard v. VA, CH-0752-14-0301-I-3 (NP 1/3/2016).

Demotion to a nonsupervisory position was warranted for a foreman who failed to follow his supervisor's instructions and agency policies regarding overtime approval, leave, attendance, and time reports of subordinates. The continued maintenance of unofficial time records for subordinates after being told not to do so may constitute insubordination. Lawson v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 257, 258 (1981). The agency has a legitimate interest in keeping one set of complete and official records. Brantley v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-1349). Failure to adhere to postal policies concerning delivery of parcels furnished grounds for discipline in Riese v. USPS, 40 MSPR 666 (1989) (sustaining removal; there was past discipline for similar problems). Improper use of emergency lights and siren while on a nonemergency call, in violation of agency policies and resulting in an accident with property damage and an injury, was cause for removal of a federal protective officer. Yeager v. GSA, 39 MSPR 147, 150-51 (1988). Failure to adhere to a dress code was cause for removal. It was not a sufficient defense that the union filed a grievance concerning the dress code. Howard v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-2128). An employee could be disciplined for failing to abide by agency instructions concerning use of official time for EEO complaints. The agency had a right to control the time used for that purpose. Roseman v. Dept. of Treasury, 76 MSPR 334, 340 (1997). [Refer below to use of grievance procedures.]

Standard procedures are not always what they seem on paper. The Board acknowledged that the Postal Service condones the time-honored practice of letter carriers accepting Christmas gifts of cash from postal patrons; however, as to receipt of money during the rest of the year from individuals seeking favored treatment of their welfare checks, there was no condonation. The Board sustained removal of an appellant who accepted out-of-season gratuities over a long time. Familio v. USPS, 24 MSPR 47, 49 (1984); cf. Tudor v. Dept. of Treasury, 639 F.3d 1362 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (analysis of testimony concerning condonation of some incidents, involving agency law enforcement policies, relied on to discipline the appellant; remanding for further consideration other elements of the appeal).

A policy must be known to the employee to be violated. If the employee's conduct was previously permitted, a change of policy must be properly and expeditiously communicated to the employee. The agency cannot base discipline upon last-minute policy modifications informally transmitted to the employee. See Keeffe v. Library of Congress, 777 F.2d 1573 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (change in the policy concerning attendance at political conventions conveyed on the eve of departure through a telephone call to the child of the employee's housekeeper). The policy must be made known to the Board if the charge against the appellant is a violation of the policy. The regulation that defines the policy should be properly placed into the record. See Colston v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 158, 160 (1982). A policy should be well defined. If a regulation requires the reporting of assaults on employees that occur on duty and the supervisor is aware of an off-duty assault, no discipline should be taken for the failure to report even though reporting the off-duty assault may have been more prudent. The reporting requirement was not clearly defined. Batchelder v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 312, 315, 6 MSPB 266 (1981) ("While it may have been prudent to report the assault, in the absence of agency proof on the record of a clearly defined duty to report it, appellant's failure to do so cannot form the basis of an adverse action against him."); see Moncada v. Exec. Office of the President, 2022 MSPB 25, ¶ 27 (2022) ("Absent evidence that the appellant violated a procedure, the agency cannot prove its charge. See Myers v. Department of Agriculture, 88 M.S.P.R. 565, ¶¶ 24-25 (2001) (finding the agency did not prove that the appellant failed to follow agency procedures when it provided no evidence showing how the appellant's conduct failed to conform to those procedures), aff'd, 50 F. App'x 443 (Fed. Cir. 2002)."); Hayes v. USPS, 42 MSPR 651, 656 (1989) (failure to report a fight and threatened assault on an employee; there was a question of what reporting requirement the agency regulations imposed).

If the appellant asserts that he was unaware of a regulatory policy, the Board examines his duties and past warnings to see if he should have been aware of the policy. See Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 500, 506 (1995). Under Brown, if the employee is given yet violates a supervisor's order, the resulting charge of misconduct need not be based on a violation of an agency regulation. If the employee defends on the basis of the invalidity of the agency policy that he allegedly violated, consideration is given to the genesis of the policy and consistency of the policy with directives issued at higher levels of the organization. See Kelsey v. District of Columbia Gov't, 7 MSPR 389, 391 (1981).

A charge of failure to follow instructions does not require proof of insubordinate intent. Bashaw v. DOJ, 39 MSPR 670, 674 (1989), represented an attempt to clarify the offense of failure to follow regulations. The appellant, a border patrol agent, was accused of failing to follow an INS regulation calling upon him to report a claim for political asylum to a superior. Overturning the AJ's analysis that the appellant had not deliberately violated the policy, the Board emphasized that the offense need not be deliberate to be actionable:

[T]he charges against the appellants specifically concerned only "noncompliance with regulations issued by the service." The charges neither indicated nor implied that the appellants had "deliberately" violated OI 208.8 but, rather, merely stated that they had failed to follow the required agency procedure....

Actual knowledge (or lack thereof) of the regulation in question was not an element of the charges against the appellants. Just as ignorance of the law is no excuse in general, the appellants here may be found culpable of violating agency regulations that relate to the exercise of their duties. The INS Officers' Handbook...states...:

Keep well informed on changes in laws, rules and regulations....

Although the appellants claimed ignorance of OI 208.8 prior to the incident in question, they acknowledged having read and understood the Officers' Handbook.... Moreover, the appellants should have been aware of the notification requirement here at issue since they had received training in administering appropriate asylum procedures.

See Hamilton v. USPS, 71 MSPR 547, 555-56 (1996) (eliminating the element of intent from a charge of failing to follow instructions); cf. Greene v. OPM, NY-0752-11-0043-I-1 (NP 1/31/2012) ("The proposal and decision notices do not characterize the charge as requiring proof of intent to follow PII policy but instead simply note that the appellant was aware of the pertinent policy and did not follow it. Further, the decision notice makes clear that the agency did not consider the appellant's intent relevant to the charge. Although there is no case directly addressing this issue, we agree with the administrative judge's assessment of the charge as analogous to a failure to follow instructions. E.g., Hamilton v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 547, 555-57 (1996) (a charge of failure to follow instructions does not require proof of intent).").
