C. CONDUCT HARMING OTHERS; CRIMINAL VIOLATIONS

Many cases that wend their way to the Board involve activity that can be--often is--characterized as criminal. The agency removes the employee for the underlying conduct, for a criminal conviction, or both. If the employee is removed based only on a criminal conviction, and if that conviction is reversed on judicial appeal, the adverse action is subject to reversal. See Pawn v. USDA, 82 MSPR 284, 286 ¶ 5 (1999). [Refer to Res Judicata in Chapter 4, "Acquittal; Reversal of Criminal Conviction"; refer to Chapter 5, "Review of New Evidence."] If an employee is involved in criminal activity, the agency need not wait for him to be convicted before removing him. Cirocco v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3155). It is unclear whether an arrest, by itself, would furnish grounds for an adverse action. See Lawton v. VA, 53 MSPR 153, 158 (1992) (however, failure to report an arrest is a serious offense by an appellant who is a law enforcement officer).

One task of the AJ will be to analyze the elements of a crime charged against the appellant, Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 557-58 ¶¶ 25-26 (1999):

In identifying criminal charges in an adverse action case, the Board first examines the charge or charges and supporting specifications to ascertain whether the agency is charging an employee either directly or indirectly with criminal misconduct. See Lachance v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 147 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1998). To the extent that an agency has not made this issue clear in its proposal notice, the parties are encouraged to work with the administrative judge during the prehearing phase of the proceeding to reach stipulations on this threshold issue. Administrative Judges' Handbook, Chapter 9: Prehearing and Status Conferences. Barring an agreement on this matter through stipulation, the administrative judge will then construe the charge in a manner consistent with the specifications supporting the charge and with any record evidence or legal authority that may provide guidance toward reaching a logical construction of the charge. See King v. Nazelrod, 43 F.3d at 665.

Where a charge has been identified as a criminal offense, the elements of that offense must be identified and enumerated so that an evidentiary framework is established for proof of the charges. See id. To the extent that the elements of the criminal offense are not set forth, the administrative judge will look to the most closely analogous case precedent, or criminal law, rule or regulation as guidance. Finally, after the criminal offense and elements have been identified, the administrative judge will analyze the evidence of record to determine whether the agency has met its burden of proof on the charged misconduct. See id.

The appellant alleges that the agency had him arrested on a charge of theft, but charged him administratively with improper conduct to lower the burden of proof. To the extent that, by this argument, the appellant is claiming that the agency could not bring both a criminal charge against him and remove him from service based on the same set of facts, we disagree. In addition, the appellant was given the option of staying the Board proceeding pending a decision in the criminal proceeding, but he declined to do so. Even if the appellant were correct, and the agency influenced the criminal charge in the parallel proceeding, the agency was not required to bring an identical charge against him administratively. See Owens v. U.S. Postal Service, 57 M.S.P.R. 63, (1993) (finding that collateral estoppel did not apply when the appellant was convicted of a criminal charge that was substantially similar but not identical to the misconduct charged by the agency and arose from the same conduct).

Notwithstanding the above considerations, we have examined the appellant's version of the purported underlying circumstances surrounding her misdemeanor offense, in light of a further assertion contained in her petition for review. That is, the appellant reiterates an assertion made but not addressed below, i.e., that inasmuch as the appellant's off-duty offense was a pre-employment offense, the Board should be guided by the Office of Personnel Management's suitability guidelines. See 5 CFR § 731.201 et seq. The appellant cites no authority for the proposition that suitability criteria are applicable to this situation. Nevertheless, assuming arguendo that the administrative judge should have looked to suitability criteria for guidance in this matter, we find that her failure to do so did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights. Panter, 22 MSPR at 282.

Specifically, in a negative suitability determination, an agency must demonstrate either a pattern of conduct that is incompatible with the successful performance in the position at issue, or that would interfere with or prevent the employing agency from effectively performing its duties or responsibilities; if the determination is based on prior employment, the agency must consider the recency and the circumstances surrounding the conduct, as well as the presence or absence of any efforts toward rehabilitation. See Lewis v. General Services Administration, 50 MSPR 86, 88 (1991), modified on reconsideration, 54 MSPR 120 (1992).

In reviewing the question of the appellant's suitability for continued employment, it is permissible for the agency to consider the public trust in the agency in expressing its own level of trust and confidence in the appellant. The public will not, and should not be expected to, place trust and confidence in a government employee found guilty of a criminal misdemeanor, especially where the criminal activity relates to the role of the governmental agency. In this case, the RTC is entrusted to complete transactions involving holdings, including real estate properties similar to those at issue in the appellant's criminal case. The appellant's continued employment at the agency would undermine the public trust and effectiveness of the agency.
