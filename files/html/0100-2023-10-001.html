CHAPTER 3

HEARING

I. HEARING

"Order the trial, marshal, and begin." (Shakespeare, King Richard II, I, III). The hearing is the keystone right of employees who appeal actions to the Board. The right to be heard when one's interests are acutely affected by actions of an administrative agency is of cardinal importance. Inherent in the right to a hearing is the right to testify and present witnesses on one's own behalf. See Londoner v. Denver, 210 U.S. 373, 386 (1908); Sanislo v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 576, 578 (1983) (citing ICC v. Louisville & Nashville R. Co., 227 U.S. 88 (1913)). Integral to the right to a hearing is the prerogative to present every available defense. Long v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 384, 386 (1983).

The Board has the authority to provide hearings for any individual, regardless of employment status, who has standing to appeal. That authority is conferred by 5 USC 1204 and 5 USC 7701. Hein v. OPM, 1 MSPR 410, 1 MSPB 396, 414-16 (1980). It is immaterial whether the appeal to the Board arises under statutory or regulatory right, e.g., OPM RIF regulations; once the Board's appellate jurisdiction is properly invoked, the right to the hearing is statutory. Bommer v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 543, 550-51 (1987); see Spradlin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 279, 283 ¶ 9 (1999) (appellants in disability retirement cases are entitled to de novo hearing under 5 USC 7701(a)); Gowan-Clark v. OPM, 84 MSPR 116, 118-19 ¶ 5 (1999) ("appellants contesting OPM reconsideration decisions arising under the civil service retirement system...are entitled to a hearing even where there is no dispute of material facts"). Bommer, supra, also noted the right to a hearing even when there are no factual disputes.

The hearing furnishes the employee's only opportunity for a de novo trial before an impartial administrative factfinder. Schoenberg v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 89, 91 (1989) (citing Frampton v. Dept. of Interior, 811 F.2d 1486 (Fed. Cir. 1987)). Aside from submission of new evidence with a request to the Board to reopen a case, further review with the Board through PFR, or with EEOC or in the courts is then based on the administrative record compiled with the AJ, including the hearing record, exhibits, appeal documentation and appeal response, along with other materials filed during the course of the case while it was before the AJ. For the usual adverse action or performance-based action, the hearing follows the action, so that the employee fights the adverse action after its effective date. A post-termination hearing has been the norm in civil service litigation and it was judicially accepted under constitutional standards well before the Reform Act. See, e.g., Arnett v. Kennedy, 94 S. Ct. 1633 (1974) (interpreting the Lloyd-LaFollette Act); Bailey v. Richardson, 182 F.2d 46, 57 (D.C. Cir. 1950) (addressing the claim that a predecisional quasi-judicial hearing is required for a federal employee: "Never in our history has a Government administrative employee been entitled to a hearing of the quasi-judicial type upon his dismissal from Government service. That record of a hundred and sixty years of Government administration is the sort of history which speaks with great force. It is pertinent to repeat in this connection that the Lloyd-Lafollette Act, sponsored and enacted by advocates of a merit classified government service, expressly denies the right to such a hearing.").
