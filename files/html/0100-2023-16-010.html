3. Extent of OPM Review; Subordinate Organization Within Agency

OPM reviews the agency appraisal plan for general regulatory compliance. The theory is that if the plan meets OPM specifications, the elements and standards developed under the plan will probably meet the statutory requirements. The agency then develops critical elements and performance standards for positions. There is no statutory or regulatory requirement that OPM review the specific critical elements or performance standards. Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 833 n.6 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Adamsen, supra; Satlin v. VA, 60 MSPR 218, 222 (1993) (OPM need not approve changes in critical elements or performance standards). It is immaterial that OPM approval of the plan precedes issuance or changes in the appellant's performance standards. Rutz v. Dept. of Labor, 28 MSPR 677, 679 (1985). Whitney v. Dept. of Treasury, 28 MSPR 330, 333 (1985), noted:

We note that, as stated in OPM's letter, the appraisal system submitted by the agency was the "overall plan," and the agency was to ensure only that, in developing specific performance standards, its subcomponents complied with the requirements of the overall appraisal system. There is no statutory or regulatory requirement that OPM review the appraisal plan of the agency's subcomponents or review the specific critical elements or performance standards thereof. See Evans, supra, at 573. In fact, this Board has stated "that Congress purposefully did not specify how these standards and elements should be developed by the agencies because it had determined that within the statutory restrictions, this was a matter that should be left to agency discretion." Wells, supra, 1 MSPB at 231. Thus, it is sufficient to show that an appraisal plan by an agency's subcomponent was established under an OPM-approved agency performance appraisal system. See Evans, supra, at 573.

Further, there is also no statutory or regulatory requirement that an agency approve a subcomponent's appraisal plan developed pursuant to an OPM-approved agency appraisal system before the subcomponent takes a performance-based action under such a plan. Thus, we find that the presiding official properly determined that the performance-based action against appellant was taken pursuant to an OPM-approved performance appraisal system.

See Koo v. GPO, DC-0432-11-0069-I-1 (NP 9/30/2011) ("The appellant appears to be confusing the performance appraisal system with the job duties and performance requirements that are particular to her position. See 5 C.F.R. § 430.203 (definition of 'appraisal system'). OPM approval of a performance appraisal system does not involve approval of the performance standards and elements established for each particular position. Siegelman v. OPM's approval of an agency appraisal system may be by reference to OPM regulations, Salmon v. SSA, DC-0432-09-0732-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010) (aff'd, Salmon v. SSA, 663 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2011), post):

The appellant raises several arguments with respect to Office of Personnel Management (OPM) approval of the agency's performance appraisal system. First, she appears to argue that OPM's approval of the performance appraisal system submitted by the agency in 1995 was improper because OPM approved the system under its own regulations, rather than the applicable statute. However, as the administrative judge found, OPM is empowered to prescribe regulations to carry out the intent of the statute. See 5 U.S.C. § 4305. Therefore, OPM's reference to the regulations rather than the statute does not render its certification deficient. Moreover, to the extent that the requirements of the statute exceed those of OPM's regulations, the administrative judge correctly found that the agency's performance appraisal system met the statutory standard.

Under Salmon v. SSA, 663 F.3d 1378, 1383-85 (Fed. Cir. 2011), a plan approved within one agency may be adequate when, following organizational restructuring, the plan continues to be used by what amounts to a successor organization, the situation described for an SSA. Salmon also noted that under current OPM regulations an agency may obtain approval from OPM for a skeletal description of plans, what the court described as a "high-level" plan providing a "framework of policies and parameters," and the agency may later issue detailed operating performance appraisal plans without further OPM approval:

Finally, Ms. Salmon contends that SSA failed to demonstrate that its use of PACS had been properly endorsed by OPM. See 5 U.S.C. § 4304(b)(1) (2006) (requiring such approval for "each performance appraisal system developed by any agency").

In 1995, shortly after SSA was spun off from the Department of Health and Human Services, it submitted its performance appraisal system to OPM. OPM responded by letter, "We have reviewed the system and determined that it meets the requirements of 5 CFR part 430 subpart B. The system is approved." SSA began using PACS for employee performance appraisal ten years later, and Ms. Salmon contends that PACS was not covered by OPM's 1995 approval.

In Adamsen v. Department of Agriculture, 571 F.3d 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2009), modifying 563 F.3d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 2009), this court held: "If an agency significantly alters a previously-OPM-approved performance appraisal system, OPM review of the agency's modifications is necessary to achieve compliance with the basic purpose underlying the OPM-approval requirement." 571 F.3d at 1364. Ms. Salmon contends that PACS embodied such modifications; the government argues that PACS did not.

On the record before us, we agree with the Board that PACS did not change the obligations of SSA employees to such an extent that OPM re-review was necessary. The performance appraisal system description that SSA gave OPM in 1995 was a framework-type overview, not a detailed implementation. PACS further developed the details of SSA's performance appraisal system, in a manner consistent with the outline provided to OPM.

The regulations promulgated by OPM embrace this approach, wherein OPM approves a high-level plan and the agency fills in the details. See 5 C.F.R. § 430.203 (2011) (defining "appraisal system" as a "framework of policies and parameters" that must be presented to OPM for review, as opposed to an "appraisal program," which comprises "specific procedures and requirements established under...an agency appraisal system").

Ms. Salmon has shown no area in which PACS is materially inconsistent with the framework in SSA's 1995 submission to OPM. As far as we understand her, Ms. Salmon argues that PACS fails to satisfy the representations concerning employee participation that SSA made to OPM in 1995. As discussed supra, however, we do not adopt Ms. Salmon's view that "employee participation" requires that an employee participate in the development of her own performance standards. The 1995 submission stated, "While final authority for establishing performance plans rests with the appraising officials, employees and appraising officials should participate jointly in developing the plans." We thus conclude that SSA's negotiation of PACS with the employee union satisfied that commitment, as it satisfied the requirements of section 4302(a).

Ms. Salmon also repeats her arguments that PACS departs from the 1995 submission because PACS fails to apply "objective criteria," but we reject this argument for the reasons already discussed.

Finally, Ms. Salmon points to a separate proceeding in which an official who helped develop PACS testified that until PACS' adoption, "there was [sic] virtually no criteria governing performance awards." See also Kewley v. Dep't of Health & Human Servs., 153 F.3d 1357, 1364 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (recognizing the Board's discretion to admit and use hearsay evidence). The official stated that, prior to PACS, SSA used a "pass/fail" approach, in which the distribution of performance awards posed something of a puzzle for supervisors and employees, as compared to PACS' "multi-tiered" approach, in which it was easier and more clear. Ms. Salmon argues that this was a "significant change" in how performance evaluations were handled, and thus triggered a need for OPM review. We disagree. While PACS may have differed substantially from its predecessor program, we see nothing in the proffered testimony indicating that PACS was inconsistent with the 1995 submission to OPM. Indeed, the 1995 submission specifically states that implementing "appraisal programs" could use any of a variety of "summary levels" to assess employee performance, ranging from a binary "unacceptable/fully successful" metric to a five-tier system ranging from "unacceptable" to "outstanding."

We therefore find no basis on which to reverse the Board's conclusion that SSA was not required to resubmit PACS to OPM for approval.

Scillion v. DHHS, 45 MSPR 521, 525 (1990), decided that approval of an agencywide appraisal system by OPM is sufficient to cover agency subcomponents when the subcomponents' systems are developed in accordance with the agencywide system. See Satlin v. VA, 60 MSPR 218, 223 (1993); Van Patten-Steiger v. DHHS, 29 MSPR 324, 326 (1985) (OPM approval of the agency's overall appraisal system, not approval by the agency of its subcomponents' plans, is sufficient for a Chapter 43 action). If OPM approves a departmental appraisal plan, it is sufficient if OPM permits agency subcomponents to have their plans reviewed by higher level officials within the approved agency. Dunard v. VA, 31 MSPR 654, 655 n.* (1986); Grant v. Coast Guard, 24 MSPR 663, 666 (1984) ("The letter approving the DOT performance plan directed DOT to ensure that the implementation of the plan by its subcomponents met the overall requirements of the DOT plan. This agency thereafter submitted its plan to DOT which approved it."). OPM approval of an agency plan extends to the subcomponents of the agency if a subcomponent's plan was developed in accordance with the governing agency plan approved by OPM. Ketchum v. FAA, 28 MSPR 268, 270-71 (1985); see Whitney v. Dept. of Treasury, 28 MSPR 330, 333-34 (1985), supra. Under Whitney, there is no statutory or regulatory requirement that an agency approve a subcomponent's plan developed pursuant to an OPM-approved agency appraisal system before the subcomponent takes a performance-based action under the plan.

Not every performance-related policy issuance of an agency constitutes a performance appraisal system subject to OPM approval, Chambers v. SSA, PH-0432-10-0170-I-1 (NP 5/17/2011):

In his petition for review, the appellant asserts that there is no evidence that the Performance Assistance Communication System (PACS) Performance Plan was approved by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM). Aside from simply asserting that OPM did not approve the PACS Performance Plan, he has not provided evidence or argument suggesting that the PACS Performance Plan should be considered a performance appraisal system rather than a performance appraisal program under 5 C.F.R. § 430.203, apart from asserting that Doris Brookens' testimonial reference to PACS as a "system" demonstrates that it required OPM approval. Our review of the hearing testimony suggests that Ms. Brookens' reference to PACS as the agency's "new appraisal system" in no way indicated an appreciation of the legal distinction between a performance appraisal system and a performance appraisal program as set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 430.203. Thus, the appellant has provided no reason to find that the PACS Performance Plan constituted a significant change in the performance system such as to require additional OPM approval.
