2. Weight, Quality of Substantial Evidence

The substantial evidence test is imprecise. The administrative definition of the test is that an action will be affirmed if it is based on some evidence that a reasonable person might accept, despite contrary evidence, after reviewing the entire record. See 5 CFR 1201.4(p) (2022). The possibility of inconsistent conclusions from the evidence does not preclude an agency's findings from being supported by substantial evidence. Utnage v. Dept. of Army, 119 Fed. Appx. 269, 271 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (performance case; citing Consolo v. Fed. Maritime Comm'n, 86 S. Ct. 1018 (1966)); Hicks v. Dept. of Treasury, 107 Fed. Appx. 902, 905 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP). There remains the question of what should be considered evidence. [Chapter 4 of the Guide discusses Board case law concerning evidence.] We note here a few decisions that consider the weight or quality of evidence in Chapter 43 cases. [Refer to Chapter 17, "Substantial Evidence; Factual Dispute."]

One problem inherent in unacceptable performance cases results from the application of the hearsay rule. An agency need not prove a Chapter 43 case through documentary evidence, Cohen v. GSA, 53 MSPR 492, 498 (1992); Luscri v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 482, 492 (1989) (and Luscri, 32 MSPR 623 (1987)); testimony is sufficient. Sherrell v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 534, 541 (1991). Agencies are however, required to provide responses to appeals filed with the Board. Frequently these responses consist of statements, sworn and unsworn, supervisors' notes, and various documents pertaining to the charges. Most, if not all, of the documents constitute hearsay; they are statements made outside of the hearing setting without an opportunity for cross-examination at the time they were made. Because hearsay is admissible, it alone could support an agency case in an unacceptable performance action. [Refer to Chapter 4, "Hearsay."]

Affidavits may provide an alternative to original evidentiary sources. See Chaggaris v. GSA, 49 MSPR 249, 255-56 (1991). If documents are used, they need not be extensive. A memorandum from the appellant's former supervisor to the agency personnel office concerning the appellant's performance was sufficient when corroborated by a letter from the appellant responding to the proposed removal that referred generally to the appellant's "inability to perform properly." The Board acknowledged that the appellant's letter did not rise to the level of an admission of the charges, but it constituted a response to the proposed removal and constituted some evidence in support of the removal. Griffin v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 360, 362 (1985).

Fernand v. Dept. of Treasury, 100 MSPR 259, 265 ¶ 10 (2005), decided without a hearing, but with the benefit of affidavit and documentary evidence, noted the significance of documentary evidence in a Chapter 43 case:

An agency's burden of providing substantial evidence of an appellant's unacceptable performance can be met largely by submissions of documentation through the charges and the appellant's working papers. See Salter v. Department of the Treasury, 92 M.S.P.R. 355 ¶ 12 (2002), citing DePauw v. U.S. International Trade Commission, 782 F.2d 1564, 1566-67 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (specifications in a notice of proposed removal under chapter 43 that are sufficiently detailed to be disputed by the appellant, if he so desires, may be considered as evidence). A proposal notice can constitute valid proof of an agency's charges, where the notice is not merely conclusory, but sets forth in detail an employee's errors and deficiencies, and where the notice is corroborated by other evidence. Gill v. Department of the Navy, 34 M.S.P.R. 308, 311 (1987).

See Benton v. NRC, DC-0432-12-0751-I-2 (NP 12/23/2014) ("[A]nagency's burden of providing substantial evidence of an appellant's unacceptable performance can be met largely by submissions of documentation through the charges and the appellant's working papers. See Fernand v. Department of the Treasury, 100 M.S.P.R. 259, ¶ 10 (2005), aff'd, 210 F. App'x 992 (Fed. Cir. 2006); see also Salter v. Department of the Treasury, 92 M.S.P.R. 355, ¶ 12 (2002). A proposal notice can constitute valid proof of an agency's charges where, as here, the notice is not merely conclusory, but sets forth in detail the employee's errors and deficiencies. See Fernand, 100 M.S.P.R. 259, ¶ 10. In addition, the agency attached to the proposal notice all the documents it relied upon, including the revised drafts to assigned projects. Moreover, in addressing this issue, the administrative judge considered the testimony of the three agency officials who reviewed the appellant's work and found it deficient.").

Wallace v. Dept. of Air Force, 879 F.2d 829, 833 (Fed. Cir. 1989), commented on the amount of evidence needed to sustain a performance case:

Ms. Wallace also challenges the sufficiency of the evidence supporting the administrative judge's finding that the performance in two of the components of the critical element "Office Administration" was unacceptable. She contends that the administrative judge's findings on the "work flow" component of the office administration element were based solely upon "general testimonial evidence" that did not refer to any specific instances of unacceptable performance.

We know of no rule that precludes a finding of unacceptable performance based on general testimony. The issue before us is whether the findings of unacceptable performance are supported by substantial evidence, not whether that evidence related to specific instances of such performance.

...

The evidence with regard to the work flow component included the sample documents submitted by the Air Force. The administrative judge stated that "[s]uch examples, albeit limited, support the agency's contention that because the appellant's work required an excessive amount of review and correction the work flow was not smooth." In addition, Ms. Capps, whose testimony the administrative judge credited, stated that Ms. Wallace was unable to keep her work flowing smoothly, that "half of her work didn't get done," and that "it took Ms. Wallace a long time to do anything." Moreover, Mr. Lymburger, a member of the office where Ms. Wallace worked, stated in testimony taken at the EEOC hearing (which was introduced at the Board hearing) that "there were a lot of things (assigned to Ms. Wallace) that didn't get done when [he] wanted them," that "sometimes" work product was "[n]ot done correctly the first time or second [time] ," and that "close to one hundred percent" of complex documents initially done by Ms. Wallace were incorrect or had to be redone. The administrative judge's finding on the work flow component was supported by substantial evidence.

There are evidentiary limits. The agency appeal response, and sometimes the appeal itself, frequently contains much "evidence" that is neither explained nor self-explanatory. Supervisors' notes, memos to the record, photocopies of timesheets or work product may be unintelligible to the uninitiated. These materials, if unexplained at some point during the hearing process, will not constitute substantial evidence. The AJ will not be faulted for failing to attribute evidentiary weight to unassembled and unexplained documentation. Atkinson v. Dept. of Air Force, 18 MSPR 691, 693 (1984). If the agency evidence does not make sense, the AJ may order the agency to provide further explanation of its evidence, whether or not a hearing was requested. Stockley v. Dept. of Labor, 33 MSPR 514, 516 (1987). The Board will not, however, rescue an agency from a loss if the agency simply fails to provide the documentation or testimony necessary to support a charged performance deficiency. See Leonard v. DOD, 82 MSPR 597, 600-01 ¶¶ 9-11 (1999). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Hearsay," for the relation between substantial evidence and hearsay, use of supervisors' notes, and use of other types of documentation to support the substantial evidence requirement.]

Despite the difficulty of overcoming the agency's documentation under the substantial evidence test, a de novo hearing is guaranteed to the appellant who timely requests one. Even in a salary step-increase (WIGI) denial case, the Board rejected the notion that review was limited to examination of the agency reconsideration file, a procedure that would defeat the statutory right to a hearing. Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPR 505, 519-20, 1 MSPB 489 (1980). What meaning the hearing right will have, however, is another matter. Careful attention must be devoted to the Board's rulings involving hearsay to determine what evidence may be relied on by AJs, for hearsay can be significant in performance cases.
