broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 02: Procedures for Obtaining Fees, Section B: Compensable Services, Subsection 11: Related Proceeding, Part d: Unemployment Compensation
d. Unemployment Compensation

Lacking a connection to Board proceedings, time spent by counsel in unemployment compensation proceedings is noncompensable through the Board, King v. USPS, 20 MSPR 467, 471 (1984):

Although counsel's representation in the state unemployment compensation proceeding was a result of the agency's actions, appellant has not established that the unemployment compensation proceeding was in any way connected with or necessary to the successful prosecution of his appeal of the constructive suspension and reduction in grade actions before the Board....

An award of attorney fees for services rendered in a matter which is not connected with or necessary to the successful prosecution of an appeal before the Board, no matter how worthy the cause, would constitute an overreaching of our authority to award attorney fees under 5 USC 7701(g) as explicitly consented to by Congress. See Williams v. Office of Personnel Management, 718 F.2d 1553, 1557 (Fed. Cir. 1983). In the instant case, an award of attorney fees to counsel for his representation of appellant in the state unemployment compensation proceeding would constitute an overly tenuous reaching of our authority to award attorney fees under 5 USC § 7701(g).

Mannon v. Dept. of Army, 31 MSPR 503, 505 (1986), extended King with commentary concerning the necessary nexus for fees in an unemployment compensation proceeding and an EEO case that was determined not to be connected to the subsequent removal case:

The Board may not award fees for services rendered in a matter which is not connected with or necessary to the successful prosecution of an appeal before the Board. 5 USC 7701(g). In King v. U.S. Postal Service, 20 MSPR 467, 471 (1984), the Board held that an award of fees to counsel for his representation of appellant in state unemployment compensation proceedings would constitute an overreaching of the Board's authority to award attorney fees under 5 USC 7701(g). In Young v. Department of the Air Force, 29 MSPR 589, 592 (1986), the Board held that attorney fees are awardable for work done on an unsuccessful EEO complaint made before the agency and filed prior to a Board appeal, where: (1) the complaint arose from the common core of facts which were the basis of the Board appeal; (2) appellant prevailed on the appeal; and (3) attorney fees are warranted in the interest of justice.

Appellant filed an informal EEO complaint on May 29, 1985, two months before the agency proposed the removal action. In the fee motion, appellant has not claimed that the informal EEO complaint was related to the removal action, nor has she established that counsel's time spent in furtherance of the EEO complaint was in any way necessary or connected to the removal action which was the basis of her appeal to the Board. See Allen v. United States Postal Service, 2 MSPR 420 (1980) (appellant has the burden of establishing entitlement to an award of attorney fees). Consequently, the time spent by counsel on the EEO complaint and the unemployment compensation proceedings is not awardable. See Young, supra; King, supra....

But see Crawford v. Dept. of Treasury, 60 MSPR 614, 620 (1994) (apparently permitting time spent at an unemployment compensation hearing to defend against a presentation made by the agency representative that the agency would later attempt to use in the MSPB appeal).
