1. Timing of Hearing

The date of the hearing, within limits, is within the AJ's discretion. The Board's rule is 5 CFR 1201.51(a)-(c) (2022):

(a) The hearing will be scheduled not earlier than 15 days after the date of the hearing notice unless the parties agree to an earlier date. The agency, upon request of the judge, must provide appropriate hearing space.

(b) The judge may change the time, date, or place of the hearing, or suspend, adjourn, or continue the hearing. The change will not require the 15-day notice provided in paragraph (a) of this section.

(c) Either party may file a motion for postponement of the hearing. The motion must be made in writing and must either be accompanied by an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746. (See appendix IV.) The affidavit or sworn statement must describe the reasons for the request. The judge will grant the request for postponement only upon a showing of good cause.

Chapter 4 of the Judges' Handbook describes the process of noticing the hearing:

3. USE OF HEARING NOTICE. When a hearing is scheduled, the AJ must issue a written hearing notice and should use the standardized HotDocs hearing order (ON/15), which also provides for scheduling status and prehearing conferences. If the hearing is rescheduled, notice will usually be given to the parties in writing even if it is to confirm oral instructions, using the HotDocs, ON/20 template. If the AJ considers the case appropriate for a bench decision (discussed in chapter 12, section 5 of this Handbook), or it is a possibility, in order to comply with chapter 9, section 2e and chapter 12, section 5a, the AJ should notify the parties of this possibility by adding an appropriate paragraph to the hearing order.

4. ADVANCE NOTICE. The hearing may not be scheduled earlier than 15 days from the date of the notice unless the parties agree to an earlier date. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.51. Any such agreement must be documented. This requirement does not apply when a hearing is rescheduled. However, to give parties the opportunity to conduct discovery, prepare their cases, and discuss settlement, the AJ should usually provide 30 to 60 days' notice of the hearing date. Optimally, the hearing will be held within 75 days of the date of receipt of the appeal, although given case suspensions, lengthy discovery requests, drawn out settlement discussions, etc., this may not always be possible or advisable. As noted above, the parties should be provided advance notice of the possibility of a bench decision in the appeal.

The requirement of 15 days' advance notice of the hearing is not absolute, but if a shorter period of notice is allowed, it must be reasonable under the circumstances. The regulation allows the AJ to "change the time, date, or place of the hearing, or suspend, adjourn or continue the hearing," or reschedule the hearing, without a 15-day advance notice. The notice must be sufficient to allow the parties an opportunity to prepare for the reopened (or rescheduled) hearing. Muzzipapa v. VA, 53 MSPR 53, 58 (1992) (inadequate notice that amounted effectively to one day to prepare for a hearing; seven days notice was insufficient for a rescheduled hearing because it did not allow sufficient time to request, issue, and serve subpoenas necessary to obtain the presence of nonagency witnesses); Kennedy v. Military Sealift Command, 24 MSPR 96, 98-99 (1984) (AJ should have continued the hearing when the appellant had but two days to supply a witness list, his submission was one day late, the AJ declined to permit his witnesses to be called, and the AJ's instructions concerning the use of subpoenas were incomplete and inaccurate).

The Board may "stay" rather than continue its proceedings. Most commonly that occurs when the appellant who has appealed an adverse action is involved in related, concurrent criminal proceedings, a matter discussed below. An AJ may also stay an adverse action appeal if related, dispositive issues are pending in a PFR from a related appeal of that appellant. The AJ's use of a stay under those circumstances is preferable to a dismissal without prejudice, since a stay does not place upon the appellant the burden of subsequently refiling the appeal. See Hinton-Morgan v. Dept. of Army, 75 MSPR 382, 399-400 (1997). Under the Board's current regulations, codifying the dismissal without prejudice to refile an appeal, a DWOP will be employed rather than a "stay" to place the Board case on hold, and an AJ can, on request or on his own, provide for automatic refiling of an appeal in an initial decision dismissing an appeal on the basis of a DWOP. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')."]
