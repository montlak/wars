11. Passover Determination

The passover process can be briefly described. The Veterans Preference Act, as codified at 5 USC 3317(b) and 3318(b), provides that if an individual who has veterans preference status is placed on an OPM selection register, the selecting agency may not select an individual lower in rank on the register until an objection to the qualifications or suitability of the veteran is lodged with OPM. The veteran then has the right to protest the objection, known as a "passover," to OPM. That agency determines whether the agency objection is legitimate. OPM may reject the objection and preclude the agency from choosing another individual, forcing the agency to select the veteran or to forego the selection of an individual placed lower on the register. The regulatory scheme governing passovers is defined by 5 CFR 332.401-.406 (2022), and it is described in Abell v. Dept. of Navy, 343 F.3d 1378, 1380-81, 1385 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (noting the procedural distinctions in passovers of 5 and 10-point veterans; also noting that if an agency cancels a vacancy announcement and selects no one, passover procedures do not apply since there has been no passover). The passover process is of significance in VEOA appeals and in employment practice challenges. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Passover," "Manipulation of Appointments: Cancelation of Vacancy," for discussion concerning VEOA; refer later in this chapter to "Relation of Suitability and Passover Determinations" and "Failure to Provide Proper Veterans Preference."]

OPM regulations concerning the passover process do not create an appeal right to the Board from a passover decision (the appeal rights arises from VEOA, not OPM regulations). The regulation, 5 CFR 332.406 (2022), states:

Objections to eligibles.

(a) Delegated authority. Except as specified in paragraphs (a)(1) and (a)(2) of this section, OPM has delegated to agencies the authority to adjudicate objections to eligibles, including pass over requests.

(1) OPM retains exclusive authority to approve the sufficiency of an agency's request to pass over preference eligibles who are thirty percent (30%) or more compensably disabled. Such persons have the right, in accordance with 5 U.S.C. 3318, to respond to the pass over request before OPM makes a final decision.

(2) OPM also retains the exclusive authority to approve the sufficiency of an agency's reasons to medically disqualify or medically pass over a preference eligible or disabled veteran in certain circumstances, in accordance with part 339 of this chapter.

(3) An agency must refer any objection (including a pass over request) that is based on material, intentional false statement or deception or fraud in examination or appointment to OPM for a suitability action where warranted, under part 731 of this chapter.

(b) Standard for objections. An agency is not required to consider an individual for a position when an objection to (including a request to pass over) the particular individual is sustained or granted. An objection, including a pass over request, may be sustained only if it is based on a proper and adequate reason. The reasons set forth for disqualification by OPM in part 339 of this chapter constitute proper and adequate reasons to sustain an objection. Similarly, the criteria for making suitability determinations in part 731 of this chapter constitute proper and adequate reasons to sustain an objection. In addition, reasons published by OPM in the Delegated Examining Operations Handbook constitute proper and adequate reasons to sustain an objection.

(c) Sufficiency of the reasons for a pass over. Subject to the exception set forth in paragraph (e) of this section, an agency may not pass over a preference eligible to select a non-preference eligible unless OPM or an agency with delegated authority also makes a determination that the sufficiency of the reasons is supported by the evidence submitted for the pass over request.

(d) Agency's obligation while request for objection is pending. Subject to the exception set forth in paragraph (e) of this section, if an agency makes an objection against an applicant for a position (including seeking to pass over the applicant), and the individual that the agency wishes to select would be within reach of selection only if the objection is sustained, or the pass over granted, that agency may not make a selection for the position until a final ruling is made.

(e) Applicability of paragraphs (c) and (d). Paragraphs (c) and (d) of this section do not apply if the agency has more than one position to fill from the same certificate and holds open (in the event the objection is not sustained or the pass over request is denied) a position that could be filled by the individual against whom an objection or a pass over request has been filed.

(f) Procedures for objections and pass overs. Agencies must follow the procedures for objecting to or requesting to pass over an eligible that are published by OPM in the Delegated Examining Operations Handbook.

(g) No appeal rights to Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB). An individual may not appeal to the MSPB a decision by OPM or an agency with delegated authority to sustain an objection pursuant to this part, including a decision to grant a pass over request, irrespective of the reason for the decision.

Law before the regulation was issued in 2009 was not always so clear on MSPB jurisdiction. Lackhouse v. MSPB, 734 F.2d 1471 (Fed. Cir. 1984), considered whether an appeal right lies to MSPB from an unfavorable passover determination. The veteran argued that the Civil Service Commission adjudicated passover protests prior to the Reform Act and that the adjudicatory responsibility of the CSC was vested in the MSPB by Reorganization Plan 2 of 1978 (reprinted in 5 USC 1101, appendix). The court determined that with both OPM and the agency running the selection process, the IRS may have violated veterans preference statutes and implementing regulations. Lackhouse also argued that his passover discriminated against him on the basis of his age, in violation of 5 CFR 300.103(c), providing that an employment practice shall not discriminate on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, age, national origin, partisan political affiliation, or other nonmerit factor. Employment practices are defined by 5 CFR 300.101 (2022) to include the development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests, and other measurement instruments. 5 CFR 300.104(a) (2022) provides that a candidate who believes that an employment practice applied to him by OPM violates § 300.103 is entitled to appeal to the MSPB. The court concluded the record was inadequate to determine the substantive rights of Lackhouse and remanded the case to the Board for development of a record and an analysis of his legal rights. Over the dissent of Judge Nies, the majority stated in Lackhouse, 734 F.3d at 1474:

Similarly, while the pass over provisions, 5 U.S.C. §§ 3317(b) and 3318(b), were enacted by Congress, that alone does not prevent them from constituting employment practices. Lackhouse does not challenge the legality of those provisions but, rather, challenges their application to him in an allegedly discriminatory manner. The definition of "employment practices" in 5 C.F.R. § 300.101 is inclusive and does not exclude the pass over rule from constituting an employment practice. The rule is similar in function to the enumerated practices and it affects the ranking and selection of individuals. By analogy, the board's extension of section 3317(b) to non-preference applicants appears to constitute an employment practice. No argument is presented that persuades us that 5 U.S.C. § 3317(b) is not an employment practice. Hence, the procedures in sections 3317(b) and 3318(b) applicable to pass over would constitute employment practices that are applied by the OPM.

Judge Nies wrote a dissenting opinion, noting that the Reorganization Plan did not transfer all adjudicative authority from CSC to MSPB, but only designated responsibilities. Reasoning that because the MSPB lacked adjudicative authority, and because the Federal Circuit had jurisdiction over only those matters properly before the Board, Judge Nies suggested that only a district court had jurisdiction over the passover dispute.

On its return to the court, the circuit again determined that the Board had jurisdiction. No remedy was provided to Mr. Lackhouse, however, because the selection process, involving the venerable "rule of three" in promotion actions, was held to have been validly implemented and applied. Lackhouse v. MSPB, 773 F.2d 313 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

Without mention of Lackhouse, the Federal Circuit later determined that MSPB does not have jurisdiction over an appeal by an individual to an action of OPM sustaining the objection of an employing agency to certification of the individual as eligible for an open position. Malone v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3203) (distinguishing a suitability determination: "The fact is however (as the administrative judge held) that OPM did not make a suitability determination but, rather, sustained (under 5 C.F.R. Sec. 332.406) the objection of the employing agency (IRS) to certification of Malone as eligible for the open position. 5 C.F.R. Sec. 332.406 expressly authorized IRS and OPM to act just as they did with respect to petitioner. Moreover, there is no provision for appeal to the MSPB of an OPM decision sustaining an objection under 5 C.F.R. Sec. 332.406.").

King-Roberts v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3238), explained the jurisdictional limitations of Lackhouse:

King-Roberts points to no statute or regulation granting the board jurisdiction over an appeal from the Office of Personnel Management's (OPM) decision sustaining the Department of Veterans Affairs' (VA) objection to certifying her as eligible for the Medical Technologist position, and granting the VA's request for permission to "pass over" her in filling that post. Rather, King-Roberts argues that the board had jurisdiction over her appeal pursuant to Lackhouse v. MSPB, 734 F.2d 1471 (Fed. Cir. 1984), appeal after remand, 773 F.2d 313 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Generally, however, the board lacks jurisdiction to review an agency's refusal to select an applicant for a vacant position. More specifically, the board does not have jurisdiction to review an appeal of OPM's decision to sustain an agency's objection to certification of an employee's eligibility for hire.

Lackhouse cannot save King-Roberts' appeal from the same conclusion. There, we held that the board had jurisdiction under 5 CFR 300.104(a) to review the validity of 5 CFR 332.405, which permits an agency to drop from employment consideration an eligible who has been considered by the agency for three separate appointments for the same position. We held that the application of that regulation, the validity of which Lackhouse challenged, to deny him further consideration constituted an employment practice which allegedly discriminated against him on the basis of a nonmerit factor. We then held the regulation to be valid. Here, of course, neither the rule of three nor, more broadly, the validity of a regulation is an issue.

See Rosado v. MSPB, No. 2011-3003 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) ("Absent special circumstances, an agency's failure to select an individual for a position is not reviewable by the Board. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 7512, 7513. As noted by the Board, the statute governing agency procedures for passing over a preference eligible applicant does not provide for Board review of that decision, whether that determination is made by the agency alone or only after review by OPM. See 5 U.S.C. § 3318; 5 C.F.R. § 332.406(g); Lodge v. Dep't of the Treasury, 109 M.S.P.R. 614, 618 n.3 (2008). Because Mr. Linares Rosado has failed to show that the Board has jurisdiction over his non-selection, we affirm the Board's dismissal of his challenge to the Postal Service's medical unsuitability determination for lack of jurisdiction.").

Other Board decisions are clear. The Board lacks jurisdiction to entertain a direct challenge to a passover determination. Lodge v. Dept. of Treasury, 109 MSPR 614, 618 n.3, 2008 MSPB 175 (2008); see Gant v. DHS, DA-0731-13-1251-I-1 (NP 10/8/2014) ("Subsection (a) of 5 C.F.R. § 332.406 delegates to agencies the authority to adjudicate objections to eligibles. See Pecard v. Department of Agriculture, 115 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 10 (2010). In this context, an '[o]bjection' is 'an agency's request to remove a candidate from consideration on a particular certificate.' 5 C.F.R. § 332.102. Subsection (b) of 5 C.F.R. § 332.406 states that an objection may be sustained 'only if it is based on a proper and adequate reason,' which includes the criteria for making suitability determinations in 5 C.F.R. part 731. Subsection (g) states that an individual may not appeal to the Board a decision by an agency with delegated authority to sustain an objection. 5 C.F.R. § 332.406(g)."); Schoenrogge v. DOJ, DA-3330-09-0467-C-1 (NP 4/20/2011) ("OPM's regulations expressly provide, however, that '[a]n individual may not appeal to the MSPB a decision by OPM or an agency with delegated authority to sustain an objection pursuant to this part, including a decision to grant a pass over request, irrespective of the reason for the decision.' 5 C.F.R. § 332.406(g). Moreover, the Board has previously recognized that neither the statute providing for pass-over authority, 5 U.S.C. § 3318, nor OPM's regulations implementing that authority, provide for a right of appeal to the Board from an OPM pass-over decision. Lodge, 109 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 11 n.3. Thus, the Board has no authority to consider the appellant's contentions concerning the basis for the agency's pass-over determination."); Linares-Rosado v. USPS, NY-3443-08-0345-B-1 (NP 9/17/2010) ("the Board lacks jurisdiction to review an Office of Personnel Management pass over decision made under either statute. See 5 C.F.R. § 332.406(g); see also Lodge v. Department of Treasury, 109 M.S.P.R. 614, ¶ 11 n.3 (2008). Therefore, the administrative judge's error was harmless. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984) (an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision).")

The meaning and significance of Lackhouse have likely been eclipsed by the 1998 Veterans Employment Opportunities Act [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Veterans Employment Opportunities Act"], providing remedies for individuals whose veterans preference rights are infringed through federal employment actions or inactions.
