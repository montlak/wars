A. EXCLUSIONS

A union may not refuse to negotiate concerning the scope of a grievance procedure; it is not a permissive but rather a mandatory subject of negotiations. AFGE Local 225 and Army Armament Research and Dev. Command, 9 FLRA 758 (1982). The AFGE Local 225 case, and several other similar decisions, were reviewed in AFGE Local 225 v. FLRA, 712 F.2d 640, 641-42, 649 (D.C. Cir. 1983). The court provided a pellucid explanation of the negotiability problem:

The Act defines a "grievance" as a complaint by an employee, labor organization, or agency, concerning employment, the interpretation of a collectively bargained agreement, or the application of laws affecting conditions of employment. The legislation's broad, initial requirement that the parties negotiate procedures for settling grievances...is qualified by the specification that a collective bargaining agreement "may exclude any matter from the application of the grievance procedures." 5 U.S.C. § 7121(a)(2). Five subjects itemized in section 7121(c) of the Act must be excluded from the scope of the grievance procedure. [Matters relating to prohibited political activities; retirement, life insurance, or health insurance; suspension or removal under 5 USC 7532; any examination, certification, or appointment; or the classification of any position which does not result in the reduction in grade or pay of an employee.] 5 U.S.C. § 7121(c).

A collectively bargained agreement that excludes from the grievance procedure only the five subjects specified in section 7121(c) is termed a "broad scope" agreement; one that excludes other subjects as well is called a "limited scope" agreement. In the negotiations in question here the agencies sought, and the Union opposed, limited scope agreements, agreements that would have excluded from the grievance procedure such matters as reductions in force, uncoerced resignations, position classifications, and interpretations of agency regulations....

...

The FLRA has correctly recognized that section 7121(a) singles out grievance procedures for special attention. The first part of the section indicates a broad scope grievance procedure as the standard arrangement, but the second part permits the parties to negotiate limited scope agreements in the course of bargaining. The FLRA's reading of section 7121(a) is an acceptable attempt to formulate an approach consistent with the section's two-part structure, and with the House-Senate differences that appear to account for it. Under the Authority's construction, scope is a mandatory subject of bargaining, but if impasse is reached on this subject the Federal Service Impasses Panel is to impose a broad scope grievance procedure unless the limited scope proponent can persuade it to do otherwise. We would expect the Panel, in view of the FLRA's decision and the foregoing analysis, to rule against a proponent of a limited procedure who fails to establish convincingly that, in the particular setting, its position is the more reasonable one.

For additional discussion of the concept of a "broad scope" grievance procedure, refer to AFGE Local 3669 and VAMC, Minneapolis, 3 FLRA 311 (1980), discussed later in this chapter, "Exclusions" and the Department of Veterans Affairs. See NAGE Local R3-77 and PBGC, 59 FLRA 937, 943 (2004) (FSIP-authorized interest arbitrator could impose limited-scope grievance procedure), reconsideration denied, 60 FLRA 258 (2004). There was no valid objection to the negotiability of a grievance procedure that did not specifically exclude separation of probationers. AFGE Local 2442 and VA Reg'l Office, Newark, 4 FLRA 156, 157-58 (1980). It is not a ULP to bargain to impasse over the scope of a contractual grievance procedure, Vermont Air Nat'l Guard and ACT, 9 FLRA 737, 741-42 (1982):

...Under the "collective bargaining process" of the Statute, the parties have a mutual obligation to bargain in a good faith effort to reach agreement with respect to the matter, but the Statute "does not compel either party to agree to a proposal or to make a concession...." Where an impasse is reached with respect to a proposal by either party to narrow the scope of the negotiated grievance procedure, therefore, either party may request the assistance of the Panel to resolve the impasse under section 7119 of the Statute....
