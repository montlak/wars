d. Access To and Use of Documents and Information; Press Contacts

The protection provided to the filing of a grievance does not negate the confidentiality of taxpayer information mandated by the Internal Revenue Code, and discipline was not interference when taken as a result of improper disclosure of that confidential information by grievants to union attorneys. The scope of confidentiality and means of obtaining information without violation of law or regulation were known. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Wichita Dist. and NTEU, 17 FLRA 485, 508-09 (1985) (ALJ Decision). But as a result of a reversal in NTEU v. FLRA, 791 F.2d 183, 188-89 (D.C. Cir. 1986), FLRA reversed its position in the case of the employees who were accused of improperly disclosing tax information in the course of presenting their grievances. The employees, the court determined, did not have fair notice that their disclosures to their union lawyers were unlawful. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Wichita Dist. and NTEU, 23 FLRA 674, 675 (1986).

An agency gag rule that prohibited employees from releasing information to the press and required that all press inquiries be referred to the prison warden interfered with the right of an employee as union representative to state the union's views on matters concerning unit employees' terms and conditions of employment, when it resulted in a caution and an admonishment to a union official. Bureau of Prisons, FCI and AFGE Council of Prison Locals C-33, Local 1661, 17 FLRA 696, 697-98 (1985), cautioned:

This is not to say that the right of an employee acting in the capacity of a union representative to communicate information to the press, the general public, or other interested parties, is an unfettered one. See e.g., U.S. Forces Korea/Eighth Army, 11 FLRA 434, n.3 (1983); U.S. Forces Korea/Eighth Army, 17 FLRA No. 102 (1985). Thus, in reaching our conclusion herein, we have considered the Respondent's mission as a penal institution and its contention that in certain circumstances the release of information to outside parties could prejudice the maintenance of security and order within such an institution. The Authority notes in this regard that the information at issue in the instant case only served to confirm reports of anticipated temporary staff reductions which a newspaper reporter had already obtained from other sources. Moreover, there is no showing that the information was, on its face, so sensitive as to pose a threat to the continuing security or safety of the institution.
