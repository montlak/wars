b. Direct Appeal to MSPB

An interlocutory appeal must be initiated through a motion for certification filed with the AJ. The Board will dismiss an interlocutory appeal filed by a party who ignores the certification procedure. In re Royal, 1 MSPR 134, 134, 1 MSPB 131 (1979). The Board will not consider direct petitions for review or appeals of an AJ's denial of certification of an interlocutory appeal. Ragland v. IRS, 2 MSPR 238, 238, 2 MSPB 401 (1980) (denial of a request for a continuance of a hearing). But the Board will review allegations in a PFR from an initial decision relating to the AJ's denial of interlocutory certification. Green v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 637 (1984) (Table). Price v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 218, 223, 3 MSPB 314 (1980), left open a definition of the circumstances that might justify directing an AJ to certify an interlocutory appeal. The Board may, when a petition for review is pending, direct some type of intermediate relief prior to deciding the PFR on the merits. When an AJ issued an initial decision favoring the appellant in a removal appeal but denied interim relief to the appellant, both parties petitioned for review. The Board issued an "interlocutory order" reversing the AJ's denial of interim relief and granting that remedy pending consideration of the parties' petitions. Ellshoff v. Dept. of Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 62 (1997).

Graves v. Dept. of Navy, SF-3330-09-0786-I-1 (NP 6/3/2011), noted of an objection in a PFR to an AJ's refusal to grant an interlocutory appeal:

In a June 20, 2010 motion filed with the regional office and forwarded to the Board, the appellant moves for certification of an interlocutory appeal. He asserts that the administrative judge prematurely issued the June 17, 2010 initial decision without allowing him 10 days to file the motion concerning rulings that the administrative judge made in an order also issued on June 17, 2010. He apparently contends that his motion satisfies the certification criteria at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.92.

An administrative judge may permit an interlocutory appeal if he determines that the issue presented is of such importance to the proceeding that it requires the Board's immediate attention. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.91. Further, if an administrative judge denies a motion for an interlocutory appeal, the party may raise the matter in a petition for review. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.93(b).

The appellant has failed to show that the administrative judge abused his discretion in not issuing a preliminary ruling that would have been subject to a possible interlocutory appeal. The appellant has not shown that his allegations that the administrative judge denied him discovery, misapplied the law, and was biased against him are issues of such importance that they required the Board's immediate attention. Further, there was no delay in ruling that might harm him because we have considered his allegations in connection with his petition for review. Cf. Lee v. Environmental Protection Agency, 115 M.S.P.R. 533, ¶ 23 (2010) (the denial of the appellant's recusal motion does not involve an important question of law or policy, as the Board has already decided the standard it will apply in determining whether an administrative judge should be disqualified on grounds other than bias).
