2. Competitive Level

A proposal was nonnegotiable that the agency maintain certain positions in the same competitive levels during a period of reorganization. The proposal foreclosed changes in assigned duties to these positions that might alter their competitive level. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 17 FLRA 674, 677-79 (1985) (Proposal 4). The same result was reached as to a proposal that competitive level be defined so as to consist of all positions, occupied or vacant, in the same series and grade as the affected employee's current position or in a related series at his or her current grade. The proposal eliminated management's discretion to determine qualifications for positions; it directly interfered with management rights and was not considered to be an appropriate arrangement. CREA and Library of Congress, Congressional Research Serv., 25 FLRA 306, 318 (1987); see FEMTC of Charleston and Dept. of Navy, Charleston Naval Shipyard, 44 FLRA 683, 691-700 (1992) (Proposals 2 and 4, calling for nuclear and non-nuclear employees to be in same competitive level interfered with right of management to assign and select employees and to determine qualifications of employees for positions). Another proposal was nonnegotiable that defined a competitive level more narrowly than the definition contained in the OPM regulation. NTEU and DHHS, Region X, 25 FLRA 1041 (1987). A nonnegotiable proposal called on the agency to agree that employees with a certain seniority level met minimum qualifications for the performance of nuclear work, meaning that seniority or a past determination of qualification would be the sole criteria for performing the work, preventing the agency from determining whether each employee had current qualifications to perform the work. The benefit derived through the proposal for employees who could retain retention standings within their competitive levels was outweighed by the effect of the proposal on management's ability to assign employees. FEMTC of Charleston and Dept. of Navy, Charleston Naval Shipyard, 44 FLRA 683, 685, 691 (1992) (Proposal 1; also rejecting as nonnegotiable Proposals 2 and 4, nuclear and non-nuclear positions would be in same competitive level). A proposal was negotiable that did little more than restate OPM regulatory guidance concerning the establishment of competitive levels. NTEU and NRC, 31 FLRA 566, 588 (1988).

Nonnegotiable in AFGE Local 1547 and Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB, 65 FLRA 911, 915 (2011) (aff'd, Dept. of Air Force v. FLRA, 680 F.3d 826 (D.C. Cir. 2012)), was Proposal 9, precluding subgroups within competitive levels:

For the purposes to RIF, the [e]mployer will establish [c]ompetitive [l]evels based on the OPM identified [o]ccupational [s]eries (i.e. [j]ob [s]eries) and [p]ay [g]rade. Competitive [l]evels will not be broken down into any type of sub[ ]groups associated with locally created additional duties (i.e. multitasking, etc.) that may be identified in an employee's [c]ore [d]ocument, or as applicable, [p]osition [d]escription.

The proposal in AFGE Local 1547 was inconsistent with governing OPM RIF regulations, 65 FLRA at 916-17:

For the reasons that follow, we find that Proposal 9 is inconsistent with 5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(1) & (a)(2) and that it is therefore outside the duty to bargain under § 7117(a)(1) of the Statute.

Proposals that require that an agency act contrary to RIF regulations are inconsistent with government-wide regulations and are outside the duty to bargain. AFGE, Local 1547, 64 FLRA 813, 814-15 (2010); see also NAGE, Local R4-6, 52 FLRA 124, 127 (1996), and Laborers' Int'l Union of N. Am., AFL-CIO-CLC, Local 1267, 14 FLRA 686, 688 (1984).

By its terms, Proposal 9 affects the manner in which the Agency establishes the competitive levels of its employees. 5 C.F.R. § 351.403 dictates how competitive levels are established. Under § 351.403, competitive levels include:

all positions in a competitive area which are in the same grade (or occupational level) and classification series, and which are similar enough in duties, qualification requirements, pay schedules, and working conditions so that an agency may reassign the incumbent of one position to any of the other positions in the level without undue interruption.

5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(1).

In addition, "[c]ompetitive level determinations are based on each employee's official position, not the employee's personal qualifications." 5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(2).[10]

[10] The current version of 5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(2) was amended on August 11, 2008. 5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(2)(i) provides: "Except as provided in paragraph (a)(2)(ii) of this section for pay band positions, competitive level determinations are based on each employee's official position of record (including the official position description), not the employee's personal qualifications."

Proposal 9 is inconsistent with 5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(1) because it prevents the Agency from establishing competitive levels consistent with regulatory requirements. Specifically, Proposal 9 prevents the Agency from including in a competitive level positions which are "similar enough" to each other because they have similar position descriptions. See id. Proposal 9 is also inconsistent with 5 C.F.R. § 351.403(a)(2) because it contradicts the regulation's explicit direction that agencies base competitive level determinations on, among other things, official position descriptions. See id.

NTEU and NRC, 31 FLRA 566, 598 (1988) (Proposal 38.17), ruled negotiable a proposal that the employer not release a competing employee from a competitive level while retraining an employee with a limited temporary appointment or a limited temporary promotion. The proposal was not barred by OPM regulations and it was an appropriate arrangement. A proposal having the effect of preventing an agency from running a RIF to convert employees from full-time to seasonal status was negotiable in NFFE Local 341 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs, Wapato Irrigation Project, 39 FLRA 1272, 1273 (1991), because under § 704(a) of the CSRA agencies were required to continue to negotiate certain practices involving prevailing rate employees that were subject to negotiation prior to August 19, 1972.
