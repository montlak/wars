3. Service of Pleadings

It is essential to the fair conduct of litigation that each party know of the other's communications with the adjudicator. Other than the appeal that is served on the agency by the Board, the parties are to serve each other with pleadings, defined as written submissions setting forth claims, allegations, arguments, or evidence, including briefs, motions, petitions, attachments, and responses. 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2), 1201.4(b) (2022). The agency should serve pleadings and documents on both the appellant and his or her representative. 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2) (2022) ("Documents and pleadings must be served upon each party and each representative."). Service is by mail, fax, commercial delivery, personal delivery, or, most conveniently, by electronic filing. Each pleading filed with the Board (other than through e-filing) should be accompanied by a certificate of service stating how and when service was made. If a party has more than one representative, the Board will likely honor a request to permit service on only one of them. Dancer v. USPS, 38 MSPR 224, 229 n.9 (1988). If an agency representative fails to serve both an appellant and her representative, and that will occur with some frequency when service is just being made to the representative, the Board usually honors a request, if the parties cannot work this matter out among themselves, that simultaneous service be made upon the party and his attorney to avoid delays that result when one active participant in the appeal is not receiving direct mailings. The parties are expected to notify each other of changes of address. For letters to the Board, it should be sufficient to show that a copy was sent to the opposing party or representative by a standard business abbreviation, "cc": at the bottom of the letter showing to whom copies were sent, or the closing paragraph of the letter may state that the letter has been served (by a designated means) upon the opposing party or representative. For pleadings or other documents, it is recommended that the following statement be appended at the end of the document to be served, and signed by the person making the service:

I certify that a copy of the foregoing document was served upon [name of opposing counsel or unrepresented opponent] by mailing [faxing, etc.] it to her this ___ day of _________, 2023, at her address of record.

____________________

[Signature]

Service by e-filers involves a whole separate process, and the manner of service (electronic or by other means) depends on whether the other party or parties to the case are also e-filers. See 5 CFR 1201.14 (2022) ("Electronic Filing Procedures") and 5 CFR 1201.26 (2022) ("Number of pleadings, service, and response"). If everyone is an e-filer, the e-filing system takes care of service and shows who was served. The system generally works well. Its use is encouraged. Not only is service simplified, all electronic filings are lodged and appear in a "repository," making them instantly (more or less) accessible when you cannot find what you know you printed out a day or two ago.

It may occur that a party learns of documents submitted to the AJ when the opposing party files a PFR that cites to a document not received earlier. If, during the PFR stage, one wishes to raise the lack of service of a document considered by the AJ, the objection to Board consideration of the material should be by sworn statement. Assuming that a certificate of service accompanied the submission of the documents to the AJ, an unsworn protest at the PFR stage will not rebut the "presumption that letters sealed, properly addressed, and deposited in the U.S. Mail with postage prepaid were received." See Thomas v. DOD, 66 MSPR 546, 551 n.1 (1995). If service was not made properly, the Board assesses whether any harm has been done and how best to remedy the problem. See Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 721 F.2d 361, 365 n.10 (Fed. Cir. 1983) ("Petitioner's argument that he was denied due process because communications from the Board were sent only to his representative likewise fails. He cites no statutory or regulatory requirements at that time that copies were to be forwarded to him personally; having chosen a representative, communications to his representative are presumed to reach him."); Kukish v. USPS, 68 MSPR 360, 362 (1995) (no harm to the appellant who was served with a submission to the AJ but whose representative was not served).
