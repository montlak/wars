b. Clearly Erroneous

Rare are the decisions finding past discipline clearly erroneous. Eichner v. USPS, 83 MSPR 202, 210 ¶ 17 (1999), concluded that a prior suspension was clearly erroneous when the appellant was given a letter of warning for the same misconduct. The suspension could not be relied on by the agency to support enhancement of the penalty for a current offense.

If the appellant attempts to prove that the past discipline was clearly erroneous because it constituted disparate treatment, the appellant must show that others engaged in the same misconduct. Ally v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 680, 684 (1993) ("Although she alleges that other employees used obscene language that disrupted the workplace, she has not asserted that such language was used in a disrespectful manner toward a supervisor."); see Bess v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 583, 591 (1991) (if the appellant attempts to prove disparate treatment, the agency may offer a rebuttal concerning disciplinary actions against the appellant that were not referenced in the proposed removal). [Refer to Chapter 13, "Agency's Clear and Convincing Evidence; Carr Factors," and particularly to Cosgrove v. Dept. of Navy, for analysis of whistleblowers' appeals and the Board's analysis of past discipline and a current penalty.]

Buniff v. USDA, 79 MSPR 118, 123 n.4 (1998), explained how an appellant did not demonstrate that past discipline, a reprimand, was clearly erroneous when the appellant used discovery to obtain information concerning validity of the reprimand:

The appellant also argues that the AJ failed to "review" the letter of reprimand. He asserted below that the agency's bases for the reprimand were untrue and/or unproven, and that as a result the agency should not have been entitled to rely on the letter in assessing the penalty. Under Bolling v. Department of the Air Force, 9 MSPR 335, 339-40 (1981), the Board's review of a prior disciplinary action is limited to determining whether that action is clearly erroneous, if the employee was informed of the action in writing, the action is a matter of record, and the employee was permitted to dispute the charges before a higher level of authority than the one that imposed the discipline. Here, the letter of reprimand satisfies all of those prerequisites, and the appellant has not shown that it was clearly erroneous. In fact, pursuant to the appellant's discovery request, the agency sent him 80 pages of documentation on which it based the reprimand, and there is no indication in the record below that the appellant used any of those documents to challenge the validity of the letter of reprimand.

The Board disregarded past discipline it considered to be clearly unlawful in Edwards v. USPS, 13 MSPR 471, 474 (1982) (rejecting reliance on past discipline based on approved emergency annual leave and approved sick leave; noting, however, that the agency can rely on approved leave to show that the employee is of marginal value to the agency by reason of unreliable attendance), and Jenkins v. USPS, 12 MSPR 303, 306 (1982) ("Nor will the Board consider, as a relevant factor concerning the appropriateness of the penalty imposed, a prior disciplinary action based on approved leave.").

In Futrell-Rawls v. VA, DC-0752-10-0268-B-1 (NP 6/22/2011), the agency erroneously relied on past discipline in the form of suspensions that were never actually imposed and served:

...Where, as here, the appellant did not challenge the validity of his prior discipline before the administrative judge, the Board will look only at whether the prior discipline occurred. Holland v. Department of Defense, 83 M.S.P.R. 317, ¶¶ 5-8 (1999). Evidence in the record reflects that the reprimand was issued by decision dated August 4, 2009. No evidence supports the appellant's claim that the union successfully challenged the action. However, the agency has admitted that the 3-day suspension was never effected, explaining that, because the appellant continued to exhibit the same type of misconduct before a decision on the suspension was reached, those charges were folded into the proposed removal notice. Therefore, to the extent that the deciding official relied on the 3-day suspension as past record supporting an enhanced penalty, such reliance was improper because the suspension never occurred. Any such error was not harmful, however, since the appellant did have a past record, i.e., a reprimand, upon which the deciding official could properly rely to support an enhanced penalty. As noted, the administrative judge considered the appellant's "prior disciplinary record." Because we have found that the reprimand was prior discipline appropriate for consideration, even if the proposed 3-day suspension was not, the administrative judge's error, to the extent she made one, did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights. Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984).

The Board may avoid resolving a challenge to past discipline if the charges support the penalty under appeal without consideration of the prior discipline. See Starks v. Dept. of Army, 94 MSPR 95, 100 n.3 (2003) ("The appellant claims on review, however, that he was denied the opportunity to challenge the misconduct alleged in the agency's letter of reprimand....The 7-day suspension mentioned above, considered along with the current offenses, is sufficient to show that the penalty of removal is reasonable in this case."); cf. Bowen v. Dept. of Navy, 112 MSPR 607, 612 n.3, 2009 MSPB 220 (2009) (if the AJ incorrectly characterizes past discipline, e.g., a suspension rather than a reprimand, the error is harmless if the Board determines that the AJ properly concluded that the penalty is reasonable).
