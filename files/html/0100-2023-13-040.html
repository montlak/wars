g. Irrevocable Resignation; VSIP

Even an irrevocable resignation may be withdrawn. In Cook v. DOD, 63 MSPR 270, 273-74 (1994), the employee signed a form stating that he irrevocably resigned to obtain Voluntary Separation Incentive Pay (VSIP). The appellant then sought to withdraw the resignation before the effective date. When the agency declined to permit the resignation to be withdrawn, the employee appealed and the Board determined that the agency improperly declined to permit the appellant to withdraw the resignation:

We agree with the appellant...that the administrative judge erred in finding that the agency showed that it had a valid reason not to allow the appellant to withdraw his resignation before it became effective. The rule with respect to an employee's opportunity to withdraw a resignation is set forth at 5 CFR § 715.202(b), which provides:

An agency may permit an employee to withdraw his resignation at any time before it has become effective. An agency may decline a request to withdraw a resignation before its effective date only when the agency has a valid reason and explains that reason to the employee. A valid reason includes, but is not limited to, administrative disruption or the hiring or commitment to hire a replacement. Avoidance of adverse action proceedings is not a valid reason.

Because the information that leads to a refusal to allow the withdrawal of a resignation is within the agency's control, the agency bears the burden of proving the validity of its reasons under this regulation by a preponderance of the evidence. Browning v. Department of the Army, 61 MSPR 232, 236 (1994); Almon v. National Aeronautics & Space Administration, 16 MSPR 124, 128 (1983). In making the determination of whether the agency met its burden, the Board has held that the agency's discretion to refuse to accede to a withdrawal "is not unfettered, but rather is conditional," and "limited." Browning, 61 MSPR at 236 (quoting McBeen v. Department of the Interior, 27 MSPR 207, 210-11 (1985)).

We find that the agency has not met its burden of showing that it had a valid reason for declining the appellant's request to withdraw his resignation. The only reason that the agency offered and explained to the appellant was that the appellant's resignation under the VSIP [Voluntary Separation Incentive Pay] program was irrevocable. We find that under the circumstances of this appeal, this assertion alone does not establish by preponderant evidence a valid reason for denying the appellant's request to withdraw his resignation. See 5 CFR § 1201.56(c)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(q)]; McBeen, 27 MSPR at 211 (the adverse effect meant to be avoided by giving the agency limited discretion to refuse resignation withdrawals is the disruption in staffing or prejudice to the employment rights of others that may result from retaining the resigning employee on the rolls).

The agency has not alleged or shown that it hired or made a commitment to hire a replacement, see 5 CFR § 715.202(b), or that the appellant's position had been abolished, see Almon, 16 MSPR at 127. The agency has also failed to allege and present evidence showing that it is more likely true than untrue that administrative disruption would have occurred if it had accepted the appellant's withdrawal request before the effective date of his resignation. For example, it has not shown that it did not have enough resignations and retirements under its voluntary separation programs to avoid a RIF when the appellant requested a withdrawal of his resignation, or that permitting the appellant to withdraw his resignation would have adversely affected any employees whose jobs had been saved because of his resignation. See Knox v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 12 MSPB 58, 13 MSPR 479, 482 (1982).

Although the purpose of the VSIP program is to avoid or minimize the need for involuntary separations due to a RIF, base closure, reorganization, transfer of function, or other similar action, see 5 USC § 5597(b), the administrative judge's mere speculation that administrative disruption might have occurred is not enough to sustain the agency's burden of proving a valid reason by preponderant evidence, see Browning, 61 MSPR at 239-40 (the agency's desire to avoid a RIF that appeared to be at least 17 months away and might not occur at all did not constitute a sufficient showing of administrative disruption); see also Einstein v. Department of the Army, 26 MSPR 404, 408 (1985) (recruitment by an agency that does not reach the stage of actual hiring or commitment to hire a replacement did not constitute a valid reason such as "hiring or commitment to hire a replacement," or undue disruption absent special circumstances).

Another "irrevocable" resignation was revoked after the appellant accepted a voluntary separation incentive and then thought the better of it, Wisdom v. DOD, 63 MSPR 333, 334-36 (1994):

In the summer of 1993, the agency announced the availability of a Voluntary Separation Incentive Pay (VSIP) Program "in order to create placement opportunities for those employees impacted by [a] projected reduction-in-force." In that announcement, the agency stated: "VSIP is a one time cash incentive payable at the time of resignation."

The appellant submitted a VSIP application sometime before July 27, 1993 (requesting a proposed effective date of resignation "as soon as possible after July 27, 1993"). On August 2, 1993, the appellant had a one-on-one counseling session with Personnel Specialist Denise Jowers (Jowers), during which session he signed an SF-52, a Request for Personnel Action Form, requesting separation from Federal service pursuant to the Separation Incentive, effective August 3, 1993. On that document, immediately above the appellant's signature, appears a printed statement, as follows: "I understand this request for resignation is irrevocable."

On August 3, 1993, in the morning, the appellant called Jowers and asked, generally, about withdrawing resignations. Jowers explained to him that under the rules governing the incentive program, that was not possible. On August 3, 1993, in the afternoon, the appellant called Jowers again, attempted to withdraw his resignation, and was told that it was irrevocable....

The administrative judge made the following findings: (1) the agency had a valid reason not to allow the appellant to withdraw his resignation; (2) it was improbable that the appellant was not aware that his resignation was irrevocable at the time he signed the SF-52 on August 2, 1993; (3) the agency's refusal to allow the appellant to withdraw his resignation was not reprisal for his filing EEO complaints or for whistleblowing; and (4) the appellant's resignation was involuntary because he demonstrated that he was misled by the agency's statement that the cash incentive payment would be payable at the time of resignation.

The administrative judge erred in his first ruling, that the agency demonstrated that it had a valid reason--administrative disruption--not to allow the appellant to withdraw his resignation. An agency may permit an employee to withdraw his resignation at any time before it becomes effective, but may decline such a request only when it has a valid reason, and explains that reason to the employee.... A valid reason includes administrative disruption.... Here, the appellant's request to withdraw his resignation was made on August 3, 1993. His resignation did not become effective until midnight of that date. The administrative judge found that, since the VSIP Program was begun to avoid involuntary separations of employees when reductions had to be made, allowing employees to retract their resignations would mandate other, possibly more disruptive, steps to reduce manpower. The administrative judge therefore found that the agency had a valid reason for not permitting the appellant to withdraw his resignation, stating that "administrative disruption caused by the conducting of a RIF is a definite possibility."

The administrative judge's conclusion is, by its own terms, speculative. The agency has not shown that the appellant's request to withdraw his resignation, made less than 24 hours after he had formally requested separation, would have caused administrative disruption. The record does not reflect any facts which would lead to the conclusion that the agency set its staffing levels on August 2 or 3, based on its belief that the appellant had accepted the voluntary separation incentive. See Browning v. Department of the Army, 61 MSPR 232, 238-239 (1994) (concern that a RIF might result was speculative, and was thus an insufficient basis for denial of a request to withdraw resignation).

The agency's explanation of its reason for not accepting the appellant's withdrawal was that he could not revoke his request to resign because his request to resign was irrevocable. Such a statement does not constitute an "explanation of a valid reason" under 5 CFR § 715.202. Accordingly, the agency was required, under these circumstances, to allow the appellant to withdraw his resignation. Since the action which the agency took was a constructive removal, and since the appellant received none of the due process protections mandated under 5 USC § 7513, we reverse the agency action for lack of minimum due process....
