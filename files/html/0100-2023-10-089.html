14. Closing Argument, Brief

At the conclusion of a hearing, the AJ may permit the parties to make oral arguments or to submit posthearing no-reply briefs (or reply briefs) or written arguments. 5 CFR 1201.59(a) (2022). It is within the AJ's discretion to allow closing oral argument and the submission of post hearing briefs. Ford v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 495, 500 (1990); cf. Gaddis v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 42, 43 n.* (1985) (within an AJ's discretion to consider an untimely brief). If the appellant is dissatisfied with the AJ's failure to permit a closing argument, he should object to the AJ's conduct if the point is to be preserved for a PFR. See Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 621, 625 (1993); Hanks v. FEMA, 23 MSPR 567, 571 (1984) (filing of posthearing briefs within an AJ's sound discretion); Caviness v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 564, 568 (1982) (despite an allegation of error, the record did not reflect a request to the AJ for the submission of a posthearing brief); Miller v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 102, 105 (1981) ("The submission of post-hearing briefs is, under both regulatory and decisional law, a matter entrusted to the presiding official's broad discretion to enable him to elicit discussion of material facts and issues in the most efficient possible manner. 5 C.F.R. 1201.57(a) [now 5 CFR § 1201.58(a)] . See also, 5 U.S.C. § 556(c)(5). The filing of briefs in administrative proceedings is thus a discretionary privilege and not a mandatory right. Lee and Babcock Common Carrier Application (I.C.C.), 4 Ad.L2d (P&F) ¶ 882. Appellant has failed to show any prejudicial error denigrating his substantive rights resulting from the presiding official's denial of post-hearing briefs and, therefore, no abuse of discretion has been established. Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124 (1981); National Labor Relations Board v. Eclipse Lumber Co., Inc., 199 F.2d 684, 686 (9th Cir. 1952). Further, any possible disadvantage caused appellant has been compensated for in the Board's full consideration of his petition for review."); Petranek v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 419, 422, 4 MSPB 472 (1980) (no regulatory or statutory right to file briefs); cf. Bize v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 155, 165 (1980) (no error by the AJ in declining to consider the reply brief when the reply brief was not authorized during the prehearing proceedings).

Bowen v. Dept. of Navy, 112 MSPR 607, 620 n.6, 2009 MSPB 220 (2009), added as to the process of briefing and presentation of closing argument:

...The appellant further asserts that the AJ did not allow him to make a closing argument or file a closing brief, which limited his opportunity to fairly present his case. The appellant, however, has not cited to any law, rule, or regulation requiring that he be afforded an opportunity to make a closing argument or file a closing brief. In fact, closing arguments and the submission of post-hearing briefs are committed to the AJ's discretion, see Ford v. Department of the Navy, 43 M.S.P.R. 495, 500 (1990); MSPB Judge's Handbook, ch. 10, § 9(d), and the appellant has shown no abuse of discretion by the AJ in this regard.

The appellant must request the opportunity to brief the appeal if she is to raise on PFR the failure to permit her that opportunity. Even if she is improperly disallowed the opportunity to brief the case, there would be no reversible error without a showing of how her substantive rights were prejudiced. Redfearn v. Dept. of Labor, 58 MSPR 307, 310-11 (1993). An AJ who sets a briefing deadline after a hearing properly declines consideration of submission of briefs following the issuance of the initial decision if the party's representative provides no explanation for failing to adhere to the briefing schedule during the period before issuance of the decision. Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 72 (1983). If a brief is accepted after a deadline, the initial decision will not be vacated on the theory that the party filing the brief could reply to an earlier, timely submission of the other party without a showing of demonstrable prejudice. See Ackerly v. FAA, 16 MSPR 78, 82 (1983); cf. Holmes v. VA, CH-0752-14-0714-I-1 (NP 11/15/2022) ("We find that the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge prejudiced his substantive rights or abused her discretion by disallowing post-hearing briefs after affording the appellant a 3-day hearing to present his case on his appeal. It is within the administrative judge's discretion to keep the record open for a period of time after the hearing to allow the parties to submit additional evidence and argument. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.59(a).").

The Judges' Handbook advises, at Chapter 10, section 15:

WRITTEN SUBMISSIONS IN ADDITION TO HEARING.

(a) Briefs and Written Arguments. After the hearing ends, the AJ may order or permit the parties to submit post-hearing briefs or written arguments. 5 C.F.R. §§ 1201.41(b)(9) and .59(a). Parties sometimes ask to review the transcript or tape of the hearing before submitting a brief or written argument. It is within the AJ's discretion to grant or deny such a request. If the parties are allowed to submit briefs or written arguments, the date by which that material must be received must be specified in the record. Responsive briefs should rarely be permitted since the parties should already be aware of all factual and legal issues. The record is closed at the end of the hearing except for the submission of the requested written material.

In practice, the AJ ordinarily decides whether she wishes to receive closing arguments, posthearing briefs, or both, during the prehearing conference--if anyone raises the matter during the conference. Agency representatives seem to prefer posthearing briefs so that they can spend as much time as is necessary outlining what they think are the facts of significance and the controlling law. It is not uncommon for one party to decide that it wishes to use oral argument and the other party to decide that it wishes to submit a brief. That practice is acceptable to some AJs. When a posthearing brief is permitted, a representative may desire to also obtain any transcript of the Board hearing and to use transcript citations in the brief. The AJ will have to decide whether to permit sufficient delay in the briefing process to allow for the preparation and delivery of a transcript. If the matter of closing argument for briefing has not been sorted out during a prehearing conference, it should be considered at the hearing, during discussion of hearing preliminaries.

It is difficult to assess the value of briefs--the quality of the submissions varies. Some are no more than position statements resembling opening statements. Some are elaborate compendia of factual and legal argument. Some ramble. One problem in briefing a case can be the absence of a transcript. The transcript is produced after the hearing if it produced at all (someone has to purchase it or the AJ has to order its production at Board expense). The AJ may not be willing to delay the initial decision pending receipt of a brief following the delay entailed in production of a transcript. The transcript may be costly, another factor that may influence the decision to file a brief based on a transcript. Another problem encountered in briefing is the occasional difficulty in obtaining Board law, since it is so specialized that many law libraries do not carry the services that report Board decisions, although the decisions are carried in commercial electronic databases and on the Board's website. There are some good texts, likely known to our readers. Factual argument may be of assistance to the AJ. Although the AJ is supposed to perform necessary legal research, briefs demonstrate that arguments have been properly raised; in that way, arguments are not precluded during the course of subsequent appellate review. The brief creates a record of what a representative thought was significant about the appeal and the evidence at the time it was presented. That can be useful to others who take over the case or to the same representative who pursues or defends the appeal on PFR or beyond.

Whether the AJ receives posthearing briefs or closing arguments should have been sorted out in the prehearing conference. If the choice is closing argument, the AJ will ordinarily provide the parties a recess of 15-30 minutes at the close of the evidentiary portion of the hearing to gather their thoughts and prepare their arguments. The party with the burden of proof (the agency in disciplinary and performance cases) leads off, immediately followed by argument from the other side. There is no rebuttal argument. Unless something extraordinary occurs in closing argument, objections should not be made to something an opposing counsel or party says; an exception might be if someone uses closing argument to inject into the case a new claim or defense (even then the objection would probably best be made right after the closing argument has been completed).

Closing argument is a good time to mention Board or court (or EEOC) decisions of particular importance. If a decision was not already addressed in prehearing submissions (or even if it was mentioned), counsel may wish to bring to the hearing several copies of each significant decision. As it is being discussed in closing argument, provide a copy of the decision to the AJ and to opposing counsel for ease of later reference by the AJ. The AJ will not need a copy of well-known decisions, but the Board issues a lot of decisions, and the AJ will not remember them all.

Closing arguments can be used to advantage. They are useful because they are made while the case is fresh in one's mind and the salient facts fairly leap out for discussion. Time and again the opportunity for effective closing argument is wasted by representatives who read written statements aloud. They wrote their closing arguments before the hearing closed. When the time comes for eye contact with the AJ, the representative takes out a bunch of yellow sheets of paper and looks at them to read the argument verbatim. The AJ drifts off as the representative drones on. This is not advocacy; it is drudgery. Throw away the yellow sheets and speak from memory of the case and from knowledge of case law. There is no penalty for fragmented sentences or occasional pauses to collect one's neurons. The rewards of real communication with the AJ are considerable--so considerable that the AJ may even ask questions to obtain clarification of an argument or factual matter--just the sort of thing that happens as good oral argument is delivered to a good circuit court panel. Wit, occasional sarcasm, recitation of case law, and discussion of facts are proper in informative, persuasive argument. Argument should neither be waived nor wasted.

Closing arguments are not a matter of right. See Feld v. VA, DC-0752-10-0169-I-1 (NP 9/30/2011) ("Finally, the appellant argues that he was not allowed to offer a closing argument. The record shows that the agency was equally disadvantaged, and moreover that the appellant did not object to the administrative judge's decision at the time. At any rate, such a decision was within the administrative judge's sound discretion. See West v. Department of the Navy, 11 M.S.P.R. 28, 30 (1982) (a presiding official, by virtue of 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41, is given wide discretion to control the proceedings before him); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(6)."), aff'd, Feld v. VA, 484 Fed. Appx. 519 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP).
