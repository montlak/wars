a. Necessity for Application

Miller v. FDIC, DC-3330-13-0684-I-1 (NP 5/14/2014), suggested that a VEOA right-to-compete appeal requires an application for a position:

The appellant, a preference-eligible veteran currently employed with the agency, filed an initial appeal with the Board alleging that the agency denied him a right to compete for a vacancy announcement open to individuals outside of the agency's workforce. See 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1). Specifically, the appellant alleged that after he was not selected for two different positions, he emailed a staff member in the agency's human resources department and asked to be considered for "other Financial Economist or Senior Financial Economist positions or substantially similar positions in this or other geographies for a period of three years or for the length of time and manner prescribed by law." Days prior to this email request, the agency posted a vacancy announcement for a Financial Economist position in the Division of Depositor and Consumer Protection (vacancy numbers 2012-HQD-B0242 and 2012-HQ-B0239). The appellant, however, did not apply for this position. After learning that he was not selected for this position, the appellant filed a VEOA complaint with the Secretary of Labor alleging that he was denied his right to compete for this position under 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f), and he then filed the instant VEOA appeal with the Board alleging a similar violation of his right to compete.

The administrative judge found that the appellant established Board jurisdiction over his VEOA appeal but denied the appellant's request for corrective action. The administrative judge found that the appellant "is incapable of showing that he was denied the opportunity to compete" based on his failure to submit an application for the identified position. The administrative judge further rejected the appellant's claim that the agency was required to consider him for this position under 5 C.F.R. § 332.311(a), which requires agencies to accept "an application at any time for an examination for any position for which [the Office of Personnel Management (OPM)] maintains a register, for which a register is about to be established, or for which a nontemporary appointment was made in the preceding three years." The administrative judge concluded, however, that because the appellant did not file an application for the vacant position, "late or otherwise," 5 C.F.R. § 332.311(a) did not apply in this case. Finally, the administrative judge found that the appellant's reliance on OPM's VetGuide did not support his claim of a denial of a right to compete.

The appellant has filed a petition for review presenting two issues: whether the agency was required to consider him for a vacancy announcement for which he did not apply "when it already possesse[d] a completed application...for a substantially similar...position"; and whether a written request to be considered for future vacancies constitutes a valid application for a position that has been, or is about to be, posted....

...

We find, however, that the appellant has failed to identify any alleged errors with the initial decision warranting its reversal. We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant established the Board's jurisdiction over his VEOA appeal asserting a denial of the right to compete, Brewer v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 563, ¶ 7 (2009), 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1) under the facts of this case, and we further find that the appellant's failure to submit an application for the vacancy announcement in question demonstrates that, as a matter of law, he cannot demonstrate a violation of his veterans' preference rights under the facts of this case.

As a general matter, a preference-eligible employee must apply for a vacancy announcement in order to exercise his veterans' preference rights to compete and be considered for that position. See Letchworth v. Social Security Administration, 101 M.S.P.R. 269, ¶¶ 6, 7 & n.5 (2006); see also Abell v. Department of the Navy, 343 F.3d 1378, 1383 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (the VEOA ensures that a preference eligible veteran has the opportunity to apply for such vacancies). We thus reject the appellant's broad assertion that it was incumbent upon the agency to ensure that his prior application materials were submitted in response to future vacancy announcements for the same or "substantially similar positions" for which he had previously applied. See IAF, Tab 4 at 63; PFR File, Tab 1 at 13 ("The veteran submitting his application may or may not know of an existing vacancy, but the Agency is nonetheless required to consider him.").

Although the Board has found that in some circumstances a preference-eligible employee need not apply for a position in order to establish the Board's jurisdiction over a VEOA appeal raising a failure to compete claim, see Weed v. Social Security Administration, 112 M.S.P.R. 323, ¶ 15 (2009), the Board has limited such circumstances to where an appellant "allege[s] that he did not apply precisely because the agency violated his veterans preference rights...[by] effectively depriving him of notice of the vacancies," id.[3] Here, however, the agency publicized the Financial Economist vacancy announcement on OPM's USAJobs website and the appellant did not submit an application for this position. The Board's decision in Weed, therefore, does not establish a violation of the appellant's right to compete under 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1) under the facts of this case. See Weed, 112 M.S.P.R. 323, ¶ 15. Furthermore, we have considered, and reject, the appellant's argument that, as a preference-eligible veteran, his email request that he be considered for future vacancies constitutes an application for employment for the purposes of a right to compete claim. Cf. Ramsey v. Office of Personnel Management, 87 M.S.P.R. 98, ¶ 9 (2000) (the VEOA does not exempt veterans from the eligibility criteria that would be applicable to all candidates).

[3] In Weed, the Board found that the agency's reliance on the Federal Career Intern Program to fill certain competitive positions violated the appellant's right to compete for those positions under 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1). 112 M.S.P.R. 323, ¶ 16.

We also agree with the administrative judge that neither 5 C.F.R. § 332.311 nor OPM's VetGuide supports the appellant's theory of a failure to compete violation. Section 322.311(a) provides that an agency must accept a 10-point preference-eligible veteran's application for a position of employment "at any time" when there is a register, or when a register is "about to be established." 5 C.F.R. § 332.311(a). As explained above, however, the appellant did not submit an application for vacancy announcement 2012-HQD-B0242, and this section provides no basis for us to conclude that the agency obliged to submit one of the appellant's previous applications for employment to this particular vacancy announcement. We are likewise further persuaded by Graves v. Department of the Air Force, 494 F. App'x 68 (Fed. Cir. 2012) and agree that, under the VetGuide, an agency "is only required to retain an application in a special file when 'there is no immediate opening' for a position." Id. at 71. Similar to the facts of Graves, the instant appellant applied, and was considered, for several vacancies with the agency, and "the VetGuide provision did not require the agency to hold his application on file for future vacancies." Id. at 72. We thus find that the agency was not required to retain the appellant's previous employment applications, which were submitted and considered in connection with other positions, and apply them to future vacancies.

[4] We also note that although the Federal Circuit assumed for the purposes of its non-precedential decision that the VetGuide's provisions were enforceable, see 494 F. App'x at 71, the Board has found that an alleged violation of the VetGuide does not constitute "a non-frivolous allegation of a violation of a statute or regulation" under 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(1)(A), see Graves v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 117 M.S.P.R. 491, ¶ 9 (2012).
