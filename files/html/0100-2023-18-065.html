8. Mixing Chapters 43 and 75

With the caveat that decisions in Chapter 9 should be consulted, it is not harmful error for an agency to issue a proposal notice under Chapter 43 (unacceptable performance) and then proceed to take the action and defend the appeal under Chapter 75. Fikstad v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 425, 428, 6 MSPB 361 (1981). In some ways, Chapter 43 provides the employee more protection; in some ways, less. In a Chapter 75 action based on poor performance, the agency does not commit harmful error by failing to provide the employee a warning of substandard performance before it initiates adverse action. Dahl v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 182, 184, 5 MSPB 236 (1981); Taggart v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 59, 61, 5 MSPB 134 (1981) ("Under Chapter 75 there is no requirement that the agency provide an employee with a specific warning before it proposes an adverse action."). Assuming that no major deprivation of rights under Chapter 43 has occurred, a Chapter 75 action will not be invalidated if the two types of cases are mixed to some degree. As observed in Moten v. USPS, 229 Ct. Cl. 796 (1982):

Inclusion of some Chapter 43 terminology...does not detract from the conclusion that his dismissal could promote the efficiency of the service. There is considerable similarity and overlapping between removal of an employee for "unacceptable performance" and to "promote the efficiency of the service."

Drew v. Dept. of Navy, 672 F.2d 197 (D.C. Cir. 1982), and Darby v. IRS, 672 F.2d 192 (D.C. Cir. 1982), concluded that an agency committed no error or prohibited personnel practice by initiating a case under Chapter 43, by determining that the proceeding was invalid under that law because the agency lacked an approved performance appraisal system, and by continuing to press for and obtain approval of discharges based on unapproved performance criteria under Chapter 75 standards following Wells v. Harris, 1 MSPR 208, 214-15, 1 MSPB 199 (1979).

It was not harmful error in a performance-related action for the agency to fail to specify in the advance notice whether the action was taken under Chapter 43 or Chapter 75. Although the failure of the agency to clarify the nature of the proceeding at the request of the employee was considered deplorable, the court declined to set the action aside on that basis in Turnage v. USDA, 230 Ct. Cl. 799 (1982). When an agency confused an employee by making the proposal appear to stem from Chapter 43 rather than Chapter 75, and the employee claimed that mitigation would have been raised had the case been brought as a misconduct rather than a performance action, the Board examined the agency's actions relative to a mitigation defense to determine whether the employee could successfully have claimed that a mitigation defense would have been successful in reducing or avoiding the penalty. See Wright v. FAA, 40 MSPR 355, 363-64 (1989). There was no harmful error when, after the removal, the agency incorrectly informed the employee and the AJ that the action was taken under Chapter 43 rather than Chapter 75. McKenzie v. DHHS, 22 MSPR 75, 78 n.2 (1984). The question of whether an agency may proceed against an employee under both Chapters 43 and 75 was raised, but not definitively answered, in Combs v. DHS, DC-0432-18-0552-I-2, n.7 (NP 2/16/2023):

It is questionable whether the administrative judge should have adjudicated the performance-based charge under both chapter 43 and chapter 75 standards. It is well established that an agency may take a performance-based action under either chapter 43 or chapter 75, Lovshin v. Department of the Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 843 (Fed. Cir. 1985), and may convert the action from chapter 43 to chapter 75 if a hearing has not yet occurred, Ortiz v. U.S. Marine Corps, 37 M.S.P.R. 359, 363 (1988). It is also permissible for an agency to bring a hybrid action, charging an employee with unacceptable performance under chapter 43 procedures while bringing an additional or alternative charge, such as conduct unbecoming, under chapter 75 procedures. Lovshin, 767 F.2d at 843. However, we are unaware of any case in which the Board has considered allegations of unacceptable performance under both standards. Moreover, the Board has suggested in dicta that chapter 43 and chapter 75 procedures are, by statute, mutually exclusive with respect to the same charge. See Aguzie v. Office of Personnel Management, 112 M.S.P.R. 276, ¶ 4 n.2 (2009) (citing 5 U.S.C. § 7512(D)), reconsidered on other grounds, 116 M.S.P.R. 64 (2011)....
