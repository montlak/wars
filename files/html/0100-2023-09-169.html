i. Illegal or Punitive Term

The agency may insist, and the appellant may agree, on including in an LCA a clause that is illegal or contrary to public policy. When the agency enforces the agreement against the appellant, the appellant may claim the agreement is invalid and no bar to his appeal. The Board addressed the impact of illegal or unconscionable provisions in Butler v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 1, 3-4 (1993). The agreement included a provision that the appellant "waives all rights and claim to attorney fees and appeals costs, and agrees to pay the Agency's cost of any appeal filed as a result of breaking this agreement." The agency agreed that the provision calling for payment of the agency's costs of an appeal was unenforceable, but the agency disagreed with the AJ's conclusion that the agreement was void because of that one provision. The Board sided with the agency, adding in its decision a cautionary note concerning punitive or illegal provisions in LCAs:

A waiver of an employee's appeal rights to the Board contained in a last-chance settlement agreement is valid and enforceable if the terms of the agreement are comprehensive, fair, freely made, and are not the result of duress or bad faith negotiation on the agency's part. Ferby v. USPS, 26 MSPR 451, 456 (1985). The Board retains jurisdiction, however, to adjudicate whether an employee who has been removed for the alleged breach of the terms of a last chance agreement has in fact breached the agreement and whether the agreement is valid and enforceable. See Stewart v. USPS, 926 F.2d 1146, 1148-49 (Fed. Cir. 1991). It was therefore proper for the administrative judge to consider these two issues in the instant case.

The Board has not previously considered what effect the inclusion of a single term that is unconscionable or contrary to public policy should have on the enforceability of the remainder of a last-chance settlement agreement. We find that the Restatement (Second) of Contracts provides a useful framework of analysis. It states that when less than all of an agreement is unenforceable because of a term that is contrary to public policy, "a court may nevertheless enforce the rest of the agreement in favor of a party who did not engage in serious misconduct if the performance as to which the agreement is unenforceable is not an essential part of the agreed exchange." Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 184(1) (1979).[2] The Restatement similarly provides that where "a term rather than the entire contract is unconscionable, the appropriate remedy is ordinarily to deny effect to the unconscionable term." Id., § 208 comment g.

[2] We note that the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has cited section 184(1) for the proposition that, even if a waiver of a right to file EEOC charges contained in a last-chance agreement was void as against public policy, that fact would not affect the validity of the other portions of the agreement. McCall v. USPS, 839 F.2d 664, 666 n.* (Fed. Cir. 1988) (dictum).

By signing the last-chance agreement, the appellant agreed to waive his statutory right of appeal to the Board if he should be AWOL or fail to follow instructions during the one-year period covered by the agreement. In return, he avoided the substantial risk of forfeiting any right to employment and was given another opportunity to demonstrate satisfactory attendance and performance. This was the essential quid pro quo entailed in the agreement, and we find that the provision regarding the payment of costs in future appeals was not an "essential part of the agreed exchange."

We also find that the agency's inclusion of the offending provision in the last-chance agreement did not evidence bad faith or constitute "serious misconduct" that warrants abrogating the entire agreement. If an agency were to insist on including in its last-chance agreements provisions that the Board has previously found to be unconscionable or contrary to public policy, such action might evidence bad faith or "serious misconduct" sufficient to invalidate a last-chance agreement in its entirety. We do not believe, however, that the enforcement of the remainder of the settlement agreement in this case would encourage the agency to continue to insist upon the inclusion of the clause at issue in the hope of discouraging appellants from litigating whether a breach has occurred.

Accordingly, we find that, with the exception of the provision found by the administrative judge to be unconscionable, the last-chance agreement is valid and enforceable, including the waiver of appeal rights, and that the instant appeal must be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction.

An agency and an appellant may negotiate, without violating the principle of double jeopardy, an LCA that imposes a suspension in lieu of removal and reimposes the removal, with waiver of an appeal, if the appellant repeats conditions underlying the original adverse action. Cooper v. VA, 117 MSPR 611, 612-15 ¶¶ 2-9, 2012 MSPB 23 (2012), aff'd, Cooper v. VA, 515 Fed. Appx. 897 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP). Cooper was relied on in Bruhn v. USDA, 124 MSPR 1, 2016 MSPB 42, ¶¶ 19-22 (2016):

It appears that, in arguing that the LCA permits his double punishment for the same misconduct, the appellant is asserting that the LCA is unlawful. Outside the context of a settlement agreement, the Board has long held that an agency cannot impose a disciplinary or adverse action more than once for the same misconduct. Cooper v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 117 M.S.P.R. 611, ¶ 5 (2012), aff'd, 515 Fed. Appx. 897 (Fed. Cir. 2013). However, LCAs, like settlement agreements generally, serve the important public policy of avoiding unnecessary litigation and encouraging fair and speedy resolution of issues. Id., ¶¶ 7-8. The incorporation of some discipline into an LCA makes it more likely that an agency will consider entering into the agreement because the employee will not escape all punishment for the charged offense. Id., ¶ 8. Therefore, the Board has declined to invalidate an LCA that imposed a suspension for an appellant's misconduct, and also provided that future misconduct would lead to the reimposition of the removal that led to the LCA. Id., ¶¶ 2, 5, 8-9.

Here, the agency did not rescind the appellant's prior removal, as he claims, but rather held it in abeyance for 2 years pending his satisfactory completion of the terms of the LCA. The appellant agreed to serve a 45-day suspension for his misconduct and that the agency could effectuate his removal for the same misconduct at any time during the 2-year period if he again violated Federal law. Therefore, we agree with the administrative judge's finding that the appellant's removal for the same misconduct that served as the basis for a 45-day suspension was permissible.

As a corollary, the appellant asserts that his November 2015 removal was "a new personnel action" rather that the implementation of his prior removal. Thus, he argues that the agency deprived him of "minimum due process to appeal notice and reply rights."

In the November 2015 removal decision, the agency explained that, based on local law enforcement's discovery of marijuana on the appellant's personal property in May 2015, "the Agency is implementing your removal action as noted in the Letter of Decision dated November 14, 2014." Thus, although the basis for finding that the appellant breached the LCA was his subsequent misconduct, the agency did not issue a new personnel action. See Sullivan v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 196, 200 (finding that, although an agency referenced an appellant's subsequent misconduct in reimposing his earlier removal pursuant to the terms of a settlement agreement, only the merits of the earlier removal were before the Board), aff'd per curiam, 11 F.3d 1073 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). Because we find that the LCA is valid, and it contained a waiver of the appellant's right to appeal to the Board or otherwise contest his removal, we conclude that the agency did not violate his right to due process when it implemented his removal without providing a right to respond or notice of Board appeal rights. See Ferby v. U.S. Postal Service, 26 M.S.P.R. 451, 455-56 (1985) (finding that the Board may enforce an LCA pursuant to which the appellants knowingly and intentionally waived their right to appeal the reinstatement of their suspended penalties).

Mitigation of a penalty would not ordinarily be a consideration when an LCA is breached. See Whittaker v. VA, DA-0752-15-0157-I-1 (NP 1/3/2017) ("Mitigation is not an appropriate consideration here, given that the appellant's removal penalty was effected pursuant to an LCA in which she waived her Board appeal rights to challenge that penalty.").
