broida mspb Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices, Subchapter 02: Types of Prohibited Personnel Practices, Section A: Whistleblowing Reprisal, Subsection 06: Protected Disclosure: Reasonableness, Part a: "Reasonably Believes"; False Statement
a. "Reasonably Believes"; False Statement

The 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act amended 5 USC 2302(b) to more clearly define what is meant by the phrase "any disclosure of information by an employee or applicant which the employee or applicant reasonably believes evidences" a protected disclosure:

For purposes of paragraph (8)...(ii) a determination as to whether an employee or applicant reasonably believes that such employee or applicant has disclosed information that evidences any violation of law, rule, regulation, gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety shall be made by determining whether a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee or applicant could reasonably conclude that the actions of the Government evidence such violations, mismanagement, waste, abuse, or danger.

The WPEA also added to 5 USC 2302:

(f)(1) A disclosure shall not be excluded from subsection (b)(8) because--

...

(C)  of the employee's or applicant's motive for making the disclosure[.]

Addressing the retroactive effect of certain portions of the WPEA, Day v. DHS, 119 MSPR 589, 2013 MSPB 49 (2013) (Member Robbins concurring in part and dissenting in part), concluded that the expanded definition of the term "disclosure" as set forth in (f)(1) could properly be applied to cases pending at the time of the WPEA's enactment. [Refer to Chapter 17, "Retroactivity," for Day.]

Law postdating and predating the 2012 legislation is described below. Decisions that follow are modified by the legislation, but the legislation did not have the effect of overruling many of these decisions.

5 USC 2302(b)(8)(A) extends protection to individuals who reasonably believe that their disclosures encompass the statutorily described conditions. The personal motives of the whistleblower are immaterial. The statutory protections extend to employees who reasonably believe in their charges. See Johnson v. DOD, 87 MSPR 454, 459 ¶ 10 (2000) ("judge's judgment was clouded by his perception of the appellant as a 'troublemaker' rather than as a bona fide whistleblower"; "judge in part identified the appellant as a troublemaker for activity which, on its face, appears to be protected concerted activity"; "[W]here the employee has met the reasonable belief test, the motivation for her disclosures is irrelevant."), following remand, Johnson v. DOD, 95 MSPR 192 (2003) (dismissing appeal for lack or jurisdiction); Fickie v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 525, 530 ¶ 10 (2000) (disclosure may be protected even if an employee "is motivated by a desire to damage others' reputations"); Molinar v. VA, 80 MSPR 248, 251 ¶ 9 (1998) (as to a disclosure motivated by a poor relationship with the object of the disclosure: "Appellant's motive for making a disclosure...is irrelevant in determining whether the disclosure was protected."); Bump v. Dept. of Interior, 69 MSPR 354, 358 (1996) ("The WPA makes no provision for considering whether the employee's personal motivation rendered his belief not genuine."), followed in Pashun v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 374, 381 (1997); D'Elia v. Dept. of Treasury, 65 MSPR 540, 543 (1994) (AJ is expected to analyze fully the various circumstances that pertain to the reasonableness of the appellant's belief; if the Board is dissatisfied with the AJ's analysis, the Board may undertake its own examination of the facts of record); Special Counsel v. Starrett, 28 MSPR 60, 70 (1985); cf. BE & K Const. Co. v. NLRB, 122 S. Ct. 2390, 2041 (2002) ("Disputes between adverse parties may generate such ill will that recourse to the courts becomes the only legal and practical means to resolve the situation. But that does not mean such disputes are not genuine.").

The individual making the disclosure is protected based on his or her reasonable belief of the condition covered by the disclosure; the individual need not be correct in the analysis. See Edenfield v. VA, 54 F.4th 1357, 1361 (Fed Cir. 2022) ("[A]n employee's belief that a violation occurred can still be reasonable even if it is wrong. Drake v. Agency for Int'l Dev., 543 F.3d 1377, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 2008). Even if the agency's interpretation of the Handbook is a possible interpretation, it is not, as the Board erroneously held, the only plain interpretation...[T]he Board must look to the information that would have been available to or ascertainable by a disinterested observer at the time they made the disclosure....There is nothing in the record suggesting that a disinterested observer standing in Dr. Edenfield's shoes could have known or ascertained additional information before making the disclosure."); Parikh v. VA, 116 MSPR 197, 205 ¶ 17, 2011 MSPB 1 (2011) ("Although this evidence might tend to show that disclosure 8 did not actually evidence a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety, we still find that the appellant reasonably believed that disclosure 8 did evidence such a danger. See Drake v. Agency for International Development, 543 F.3d 1377, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (to establish that a disclosure was protected, an appellant need not show that the matter disclosed actually fell within one of the categories of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8); he need only show that a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by him could have reasonably concluded that it did); Fisher, 108 M.S.P.R. 296, ¶ 7 (same); see also Lachance v. White, 174 F.3d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1999)."); cf. Matias v. DHS, SF-1221-13-0178-W-2 (NP 4/14/2015) ("the test for a protected disclosure is whether the appellant had a reasonable belief that she was disclosing a violation of law, rule, or regulation when she made the disclosure, not in light of events or conversations occurring thereafter. See Webb, 122 M.S.P.R. 248, ¶ 13 (citing 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8))."); Aquino v. DHS, 121 MSPR 35, 44-45 ¶ 16, 2014 MSPB 21 (2014) ("the fact that other agency officials may have disagreed with the appellant's assessment does not show that the appellant's beliefs were unreasonable in light of the information available to him at the time of his disclosure").

Reasonable belief includes the possibility of error, Mithen v. VA, 122 MSPR 489, 500 ¶ 24, 2015 MSPB 38 (2015):

An individual making a disclosure may be protected from retaliation for whistleblowing based on his reasonable belief that his disclosure evidenced one or more of the categories of wrongdoing listed in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), even when his belief is mistaken. See Drake v. Agency for International Development, 543 F.3d 1377, 1382 (Fed. Cir. 2008). The test for determining whether an employee's belief regarding the disclosed matter is reasonable is whether a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee could reasonably conclude that the actions of the agency evidence the wrongdoing disclosed. Lachance v. White, 174 F.3d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1999)....

See Smith v. Dept. of Army, 2022 MSPB 4, ¶ 14 (2022) ("The appellant need not prove that the matter disclosed actually established one of the types of wrongdoing listed under section 2302(b)(8)(A); rather, the appellant must show that the matter disclosed was one which a reasonable person in her position would believe evidenced any of the situations specified in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8). Chavez v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 120 M.S.P.R. 285, ¶ 18 (2013).").

Delgado v. DOJ, 966 F.3d 556, 561 (7th Cir. 2020) (amended, without change to quoted portion, on petition for rehearing, Delgado v. DOJ, 977 F.3d 1295 (7th Cir. 2020)), noted of reasonable of belief rather than certainty:

As we explained in Delgado I [Delgado v. MSPB, 880 F.3d 913 (7th Cir. 2018)], the relevant language of the Act does not require a disclosure to assert a violation of law as definitively as the Board had required of Delgado. The Act protects a disclosure that the employee "reasonably believes evidences" a violation of the law. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8). Our decision squarely rejected the Board's rationale for finding that Delgado's disclosure was not protected. Under paragraph (b)(8), a whistleblower need not assert that he has definitive proof of a violation of law, such that he is confident that all innocent explanations can be refuted. 880 F.3d at 922.

Under the federal Act, whistleblowers are protected even if their disclosures fall short of a complete investigative report that leaves no room for disagreement or rebuttal. They are protected even if a more complete investigation or hearing ultimately shows that their suspicions were not correct. The Act leaves federal managers and supervisors with the power and responsibility to choose whether to investigate and ultimately to decide whether reported suspicions were correct. What the Act prohibits is retaliation--punishment--for employees who speak up about their reasonable suspicions of wrongdoing.

After all, managers in federal agencies are supposed to react to and investigate relevant information about their agencies even if--especially if--important facts are not yet known. The Office of Special Counsel itself is supposed to be in the business of conducting investigations. It should not merely wait for intrepid employees to conduct their own investigations, to prepare complete and definitive investigative reports, delivered to the Office tied up in ribbons....

If the reasonableness of an otherwise protected disclosure is disputed by agency evidence, the appeal should not be dismissed on jurisdictional grounds; it should go to hearing, Hessami v. MSPB, 979 F.3d 1362, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2020):

In finding that the substance of Dr. Hessami's allegations could not amount to misconduct, the Board assumed, based on the testimony of agency witnesses as cited in the opinion, that Dr. Nichols's prescribing choices were clinically justified, and that this should have been known to Dr. Hessami. That assumption was impermissible at the stage of the proceedings, where the question is the sufficiency of Dr. Hessami's allegations to invoke the Board's jurisdiction. Regardless of whether the agency may ultimately prove on the merits that Dr. Nichols's prescribing decisions were discernibly reasonable, Dr. Hessami has adequately alleged a reasonable belief that they were not. The issue should therefore be resolved at a hearing.

A reasonable belief is not made out by a false statement, Ryan v. DOD, 760 Fed. Appx. 990, 997 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP):

In his memorandum, Ryan asserted the existence of an illegal quid pro quo, but he has not provided any evidence of such a deal, either on appeal or previously. Therefore, he had no reasonable basis to believe that his memorandum revealed the type of misbehavior described in § 2302(b)(8)(A). See Lachance v. White, 174 F.3d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (holding that a belief is reasonable when "a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee [could] reasonably conclude that the actions of the government evidence [a violation]" and that the "purely subjective perspective of an employee is not sufficient"). The WPA does not give employees carte blanche to announce that their coworkers have committed serious legal violations based purely on speculation--let alone to ignore privacy laws and abuse their access to government records in doing so. Thus, Ryan has not shown that the AJ's finding that his disclosure was not protected by the WPA was unsupported by substantial evidence.

To demonstrate that the appellant reasonably believed in the assertions she made, the appellant must explain how she was aware of the condition she reported or other facts that would permit the Board to find that she nonfrivolously alleged that she reasonably believed that her disclosure was protected. King v. VA, 105 MSPR 21, 27-28 ¶ 15, 2007 MSPB 48 (2007)). To establish that she held a reasonable belief, the appellant need not prove that the condition disclosed actually constituted a violation of law or an abuse of authority, but she must show that a reasonable person, a disinterested observer, with knowledge of the facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee could reasonably conclude that there was a violation or an abuse of authority. See Reeves v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 153, 159 ¶ 13 (2005) ("The proper test for determining whether an employee had a reasonable belief that his disclosures revealed misconduct described in section 2302(b)(8) is whether a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee could reasonably conclude that the actions of the government evidence wrongdoing as defined by the WPA."); Applewhite v. EEOC, 94 MSPR 300, 306 ¶ 12 (2003) (stating "disinterested observer" test as to a disclosure concerning abuse of authority). The reasonableness of the appellant's belief as to a disclosure may be based upon the observations or reports of others who are in a position to assess the situation. See Elkassir v. GSA, 325 Fed. Appx. 909 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (appellant, who worked in an inventory management office, made a protected disclosure of a contracting violation based on facts supported by a report from a quality assurance specialist who inspected a product ordered by the agency).

Reasonableness of disclosures claimed to be protected may devolve to analysis of the plausibility of the assertions, and that examination will consider the content and context of the disclosures, as in Aubart v. MSPB, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP), involving an employee's complaints as to the accuracy or truthfulness of information pertaining to a duty station change:

Mr. Aubart argues on appeal that the Board's decision "failed to properly apply the non-frivolous standard at the jurisdictional stage." We disagree. A nonfrivolous allegation "is an assertion that, if proven, could establish the matter at issue" under the WPA requirements; it must be plausible on its face and more than conclusory. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(s); see Hessami, 979 F.3d at 1367-69; Yunus, 242 F.3d at 1371-72. As a result, the key question here is whether Mr. Aubart plausibly alleged (after the Hawaii district court ruling) that a disinterested observer, privy to all essential facts known and readily ascertainable to Mr. Aubart, would reasonably conclude that Ms. Orejana, Lt. Col. Siegrist, and Deputy Director Chilson "knowingly and willfully" made "materially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement[s] or representation[s]" in matters "within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of Government of the United States." 18 U.S.C. § 1001(a); see, e.g., United States v. Litvak, 808 F.3d 160, 170 (2d Cir. 2015). We agree with the Board that Mr. Aubart did not (and could not) plausibly allege that any disinterested observer, even a layperson, would reasonably believe that the named individuals violated § 1001.

First, no such reasonable belief is possible regarding Ms. Orejana and Lt. Col. Siegrist's statements in their CBCA submissions that the Siegrist Memorandum notified RCC-P employees that their "official duty station" had been changed--even though the Siegrist Memorandum did not itself use the term "official duty station" and did repeatedly use the word "temporary." The district court in Mr. Aubart's Hawaii litigation explained why the Siegrist Memorandum, properly read, treated Mr. Aubart's permanent duty station as changed, given the technical definitions set forth in the applicable law and regulations. Aubart, 2019 WL 3892408, at *5-6. As a result, no disinterested observer, aware of the district court litigation as Mr. Aubart certainly was (when he made the asserted whistleblowing disclosures), could reasonably believe that Ms. Orejana and Lt. Col. Siegrist's statements making essentially the same legal arguments that the district court ratified had knowingly and willfully made materially false statements in violation of § 1001(a). See id. (using "official duty station" and "permanent duty station" interchangeably); Informal Reply Br. 3 (Mr. Aubart recognizing that "permanent duty station" is synonymous with "official station").

Second, no disinterested observer could reasonably believe that Deputy Directory Chilson violated § 1001 by stating in his July 2019 declaration in district court that Lt. Col. Siegrist informed the RCC-P employees that their "duty station was being moved...because the office at Fort Shafter was no longer available." For the same reasons described above, there is no possible plausible allegation that anyone could view the first half of Deputy Directory Chilson's statement (i.e., that the duty station was moved) as materially false. Further, there is no possible plausible allegation that anyone could view the second half of the statement (i.e., that Fort Shafter was no longer available) as materially false simply because Deputy Directory Chilson did not additionally spell out the precise way (not available for regular work, but available for storage, etc.) or reason (for renovation) that Fort Shafter was no longer available.

However, "[t]he statute does not contain a requirement that a disclosure be made with such specificity as to enable the recipient of the disclosure to conduct an investigation without having to return to the employee for additional information." Keefer v. USDA, 82 MSPR 687, 692 ¶ 14 (1999) ("It appears that Congress intended to protect even partial disclosures, provided the information disclosed is sufficient to support a reasonable inference of wrongdoing despite the possibility that examination of all the facts would ultimately reveal that the actions were not improper."). The appellant is not required to confront alleged wrongdoers to establish that his belief was reasonable and the appellant need not prove an actual violation to establish that he had a reasonable belief that a disclosure met the statutory criteria. Conrad v. DOJ, 99 MSPR 636, 642-643 ¶¶ 13-14 (2005).

Ramos v. FAA, 4 MSPR 388, 392, 4 MSPB 446 (1980), distinguished a reasonable belief in the validity of whistleblowing allegations and proof of the accuracy of those allegations:

The "whistleblower" protection set forth at 5 USC § 2302(b)(8) affords relief to those against whom reprisals have been taken for the disclosure of information the employee or applicant "reasonably believes evidences" the conditions set forth. Although the employee must establish his defense of reprisal, 5 USC § 7701(c)(2)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.56(b)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(2)], he need not establish that the condition he reports demonstrates mismanagement, danger, or any of the situations set forth in the statute; he must only show that the reported condition was one which he reasonably believed to constitute one of the situations specified.[1] Such a showing may be made, of course, by establishing that mismanagement or danger existed and that such condition was known to and reported by the employee. The presiding official in this case, however, was incorrect in assuming that proof on appellant's part short of an actual demonstration of mismanagement or a dangerous condition could not be sufficient to meet the appellant's statutory burden.

[1] The original version of S. 2640, the Senate Bill which was to become the Civil Service Reform Act, protected the employee against reprisals taken by virtue of public disclosure of a violation of law, rule, or regulation so long as the disclosure itself was not violative of law, rule, or regulation. The Committee on Governmental Affairs specifically broadened the whistleblower protection by including, inter alia, more categories of disclosures which were protected and by making the standard the employee's "reasonable belief" that the information disclosed represents one of the categories specified. With reference to public health or safety related disclosures, the Committee specifically expressed its intent that the protection extended only to disclosures of dangers which were both "substantial and specific." The "reasonable belief" standard is also applied to the private sector reporting of suspected violation of EEO laws and reprisals taken against the employee for such disclosures. Berg v. While the employee who alleges reprisal for "whistleblowing" as a defense to an adverse action in an appeal to this Board need not establish with certainty the mismanagement or danger arising from agency actions, as this may well be extremely difficult for an employee to do, the employee must come forth with such proof, either in the form of testimony or documentary evidence, as will establish that the matter reported was one which a reasonable person in the employee's position would believe to evidence mismanagement, danger, or any of the situations specified in 5 USC § 2302(b)(8).

Similarly, an AJ erred by requiring proof of underlying events constituting reported wrongdoing rather than proof of a reasonable belief that reported matters constituted covered wrongdoing, Shannon v. VA, 121 MSPR 221, 232 ¶ 28, 2014 MSPB 41 (2014)

In assessing whether the appellant's disclosures were protected, we find the administrative judge applied an incorrect legal standard by requiring the appellant to prove that the alleged misconduct actually occurred. The test for protected status is not the truth of the matter disclosed but whether it was reasonably believed. See Special Counsel v. Spears, 75 M.S.P.R. 639, 654 (1997). Therefore, the appellant's failure to prove that the alleged misconduct described in her disclosures occurred is not a valid basis for finding that those disclosures are not protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8). Rather, to prove that her disclosures are protected, the appellant need only show that a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by her could reasonably conclude that: (1) the alleged misconduct occurred; and (2) the alleged misconduct evidences one of the categories of wrongdoing identified in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)(A).[4]

[4] The Senate report accompanying the WPEA expressly disapproves of requiring appellants asserting whistleblower reprisal claims to prove that the alleged misconduct occurred. See S. Rep. Attenuated hearsay or gossip does not establish a reasonable belief, Pedeleose v. DOD, 343 Fed. Appx. 605, 609 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP):

Addressing first Mr. Pedeleose's disclosure to the IG relating to Ms. VanDerbeck, we hold that substantial evidence supports the Board's decision that this was not a protected disclosure. In order to establish a protected disclosure, the whistleblower must have a reasonable belief that a law, rule, or regulation has been violated. See, e.g., Lachance, 174 F.3d at 1381 (requiring consideration of whether a "disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee" would reasonably conclude that a violation occurred); Horton v. Dep't of the Navy, 66 F.3d 279, 283 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (requiring "that the whistleblower had a reasonable belief that, for example, a rule or regulation had been violated"). Mr. Pedeleose's belief that a violation occurred--namely, that Ms. VanDerbeck was targeted for termination because of her whistleblowing about safety issues--was not reasonable, however. Rather, it was his purely subjective perspective, which he was even told was misguided. See Lachance, 174 F.3d at 1381 ("A purely subjective perspective of an employee is not sufficient even if shared by other employees.").

For example, Mr. Pedeleose learned of Ms. VanDerbeck's potential termination from a former DCMA employee, DeAngeles Davis. Ms. Davis did not learn of this information directly, but instead learned of it from another employee, Ms. Shelton-Thomas. Ms. Shelton-Thomas, in turn, merely heard about a meeting attended by Colonel Plourde and other employees where Ms. VanDerbeck's termination was allegedly discussed. Thus, the record evidence demonstrates that this information was nothing more than highly attenuated gossip. More significantly, Ms. Davis not only told Mr. Pedeleose about this entire "chain" of gossip, but also that any discussion of terminating Ms. VanDerbeck was due to her inappropriate conduct affecting DCMA's relationship with LMM employees, not due to her whistleblowing. In sum, there is substantial evidence that Mr. Pedeleose knew this information was highly suspect and that he was explicitly told that Ms. VanDerbeck's potential termination was not because of whistleblowing. Mr. Pedeleose's belief to the contrary was therefore not reasonable and, thus, the Board was correct to find that he did not make a protected disclosure concerning Ms. VanDerbeck's potential termination.

Moreover, at this jurisdictional stage, it is not necessary for the appellant to establish that his disclosure actually passes the reasonable belief test; rather, he need only make a nonfrivolous allegation that his disclosure was protected. At the jurisdictional stage in an IRA appeal, an appellant is not required to prove that his disclosure is protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8). Rice v. Because the Board has jurisdiction over this IRA appeal, we must examine whether the appellant established by preponderant evidence that he engaged in whistleblowing activity by making protected disclosures under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) and that such disclosure was a contributing factor in the personnel action in question; if so, the Board must order corrective action unless the agency established by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same personnel action in the absence of the disclosure. 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e), 2302(b)(8); Hood, 96 M.S.P.R. 438 ¶ 12 (2004). The Board may resolve these merits issues in any order it deems most efficient. Dick v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 290 F.3d 1356, 1363-64 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Hood, 96 M.S.P.R. 438 ¶ 12. We find it most efficient in this case to proceed directly to the issue of whether the appellant made a protected whistleblowing disclosure.

We find that the appellant failed to prove by preponderant evidence that he made a protected whistleblowing disclosure. In order to show entitlement to protection as a whistleblower, an appellant must establish that he disclosed information which he reasonably believed evidenced a situation set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), e.g., a violation of law, rule, or regulation or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)(A)(i),(ii). To establish that a belief was reasonable, an appellant need not prove that the matter disclosed actually established any of the situations set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8); rather he must show that a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable to the appellant could reasonably conclude that the disclosure evidenced wrongdoing. See Ray v. Department of the Army, 97 M.S.P.R. 101 ¶ 23 (2004). During the proceedings on the merits, the ultimate determination is made as to whether the appellant held a reasonable belief and the disclosures were protected. Dick, 290 F.3d at 1364.

The plain language of DOD Directive 2000.12, Army Regulation 190-58, DOD Instruction 2000.14, Army Material Command Regulation 525-13, and TRADOC Regulation 525-13 is not sufficient to show that the agency's action in training the depot's SRT at the DOE, rather than USAMPS, could reasonably be interpreted as a violation of these authorities. None of these authorities requires that the depot use USAMPS or any other Army training course to train its SRT. The only regulation, directive, or instruction identified that restricts the source of SRT training is TRADOC Regulation 525-13. TRADOC Regulation 525-13. This regulation states, in chapter 9 at 9-1, that personnel assigned to the SRT will satisfactorily complete training at "Special Reaction Team Training Course (Phase I) (7H-F17/830-F-12)." However, the TRADOC Regulations apply only to TRADOC subcommands, installations, and activities. "Applicability" section. The agency submitted a copy of an official Army webpage listing the 16 Army TRADOC installations (www.tradoc.army.mil/tradoc/install.htm). The depot is not included on this list. Thus, TRADOC Regulation 525-13 does not restrict the source of the depot's SRT training. Army Regulation 190-58, which is applicable to the depot's SRT, also does not restrict the source of SRT training. Army Regulation 190-58, chapters 4-1, 4-2.

Further, the appellant has not shown that he relied on anything other than the language of the authorities above in reaching his apparent conclusion that the agency had violated these authorities. He has not alleged nor shown that he made any inquiries or sought advice from supervisors or anyone else through agency channels concerning whether USAMPS training was required for the depot's SRT or whether the depot had obtained approval for the DOE training. Instead, he reported to the newspaper reporter his unsubstantiated belief that the SRT's training violated a DOD regulation and was unauthorized based solely on his experience as a Security Guard, his SRT training at USAMPS in 2000 (USAMPS diploma), and his misguided interpretation of agency regulations, directive, and instruction. Moreover, there is no evidence that the propriety of the DOE training course was the subject of an investigation, or that the agency took any other action in this regard, after the appellant made his disclosure.

Under all of these circumstances, we find that a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the appellant could not have reasonably concluded that the depot violated an agency law, rule, or regulation when it selected a DOE training course to train its SRT. Further, regardless of any concessions by the agency, "[t]he question of whether a disclosure is protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) is a matter of mixed fact and law and stipulations relating to such matters are not binding." Schneider v. Department of Homeland Security, 98 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 17 n.4 (2005) (citing Wojcicki v. Department of the Air Force, 72 M.S.P.R. 628, 634 (1996)). A disclosure made by the appellant in her OIG interview would be protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)(B) only if she reasonably believed that the information she disclosed evidenced a violation of law, rule, or regulation, or gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health and safety.

The proper test for determining whether an employee had a reasonable belief that her disclosures revealed misconduct prohibited under the WPA, is this: Could a disinterested observer with knowledge of the essential facts known to and readily ascertainable by the employee reasonably conclude that the actions of the government evidence wrongdoing as defined by the WPA? Lachance v. White, 174 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 1999), cert. denied, 528 U.S. 1153 (2000). In making a disclosure involving a violation of law, rule, or regulation, it is not necessary that the disclosure specify a particular kind of fraud, waste, or abuse that the WPA was intended to reach; the inquiry into whether a disclosure is protected ends upon a determination that the appellant disclosed a violation of law, rule, or regulation. Ganski v. Department of the Interior, 86 M.S.P.R. 32, 36, ¶ 11 (2000). However, the appellant has not provided sufficient evidence to establish that she reasonably believed that the information she disclosed to the OIG evidenced a violation of law, rule, or regulation.

The record also reflects that the appellant has not established that she reasonably believed her disclosure evidenced gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety. See 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)(B)(ii). Gross mismanagement is "a management action or inaction which creates a substantial risk of significant adverse impact on the agency's ability to accomplish its mission." Wood v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 133, ¶ 11 (2005). It is more than de minimis wrongdoing or negligence. Id. An examination of the OIG's memo of conversation recounting the appellant's interview reflects that, in her view, the subcontractor was not delivering the product required by her division. As a result, she refused to sign for the deliverables and thereafter utilized a different contractor to accomplish the work of her division. It is apparent that the appellant was unhappy with the subcontractor hired by the agency and advised Hoffman of the problems she was encountering. However, she has offered no evidence to show how the subcontractor's performance adversely impacted the agency's ability to accomplish its mission.

Gross waste of funds constitutes a more than debatable expenditure that is significantly out of proportion to the benefit reasonably expected to accrue to the government, Van Ee v. Environmental Protection Agency, 64 M.S.P.R. 693, 698 (1994), and the record is insufficient to establish that the appellant had a reasonable belief that this level of waste occurred. Although the appellant made clear to the OIG in her interview that she thought that the deliverables that Ms. Hoffman had insisted that the appellant accept were, in her opinion, not acceptable, there is no indication in the record of the scale of the expenditure involved or what, if any, funds were either involved in the project or would have been required to satisfy the appellant's concerns.

Nor did the appellant establish that she reasonably believed that Ms. Hoffman abused her authority. An abuse of authority occurs when there is an arbitrary or capricious exercise of power by a federal official or employee that adversely affects the rights of any person or that results in personal gain or advantage to himself or to preferred other persons. Wheeler v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 88 M.S.P.R. 236, 241, ¶ 13 (2001). Mr. O'Connor stated that the appellant alleged that she had been pressured by Ms. Hoffman to sign invoices that she felt were incorrect and perhaps fraudulent. Had Ms. Hoffman, in her role as a supervisor, actually prevailed on the appellant to sign for the deliverables despite the appellant's conviction that those deliverables were not acceptable, a disinterested observer might reasonably conclude that Ms. Hoffman's order was an arbitrary and capricious exercise of her power that adversely affected the appellant's rights as the COTR. See, e.g., Wheeler, 88 M.S.P.R. 236, ¶ 13. However, the record establishes that Ms. Hoffman did not prevail on the appellant to sign for the deliverables, because Ms. Hoffman signed for them herself.

Further, although Ms. Hoffman's signature itself might be considered an exercise of her power, the record does not establish that the appellant reasonably believed that this disclosure evidenced an abuse of Ms. Hoffman's authority. Finally, the appellant has not established that she reasonably believed that the information she disclosed to the OIG evidenced a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety. Therefore, because the appellant has failed to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that she made disclosures that she reasonably believed evidenced a violation of law, rule, or regulation, or gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to the public health or safety under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8), the appellant's request for corrective action is denied.

Whether an appellant's disclosures constitute protected whistleblowing is a matter of law. Notwithstanding the parties' stipulation that an appellant made protected disclosures, the Board must still resolve, for example, whether the appellant had a reasonable belief that a condition he reported established a substantial and specific threat to public health or safety. Wojcicki v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 628, 634, 640 (1996) (agency actions to remedy the appellant's safety concerns bolstered the conclusion that those concerns were based on a reasonable belief). But see Charest v. FEMA, 54 MSPR 436, 438 (1992) ("The administrative judge found that the agency stipulated that the appellant's reports of perceived security and leave violations constituted protected disclosures.").

If the appellant cannot establish that the reported condition established a situation described at 5 USC 2302(b)(8), the appellant must establish that the matter reported was one that a reasonable person in his position would have believed constituted one of the situations specified at 5 USC 2302(b)(8). Russell v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 337, 342 (1995) (concerning the alleged violation of agency rules governing inmate disciplinary proceedings); see Schlosser v. Dept. of Interior, 75 MSPR 15, 21-22 (1997) (employee may establish reasonable belief by showing that he was familiar with the alleged illegal operations and was in a position to form the belief, and that his belief was shared by similarly situated employees); Geyer v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 13, 17 (1994) (employee need not demonstrate gross mismanagement; rather, the employee must show that the matter reported was one that a "reasonable person in his position would believe evidenced mismanagement or any of the other situations specified in 5 USC 2302(b)(8)."); Special Counsel v. Hoban, 24 MSPR 154, 160 (1984) (employee's evidence must establish that the matter reported was one that a reasonable person in the employee's position would believe satisfied a statutory condition).

A prohibited personnel practice is made out if reprisal is taken against an individual who complains of what he reasonably believes is a violation of agency regulations. See, e.g., Hupka v. DOD, 74 MSPR 406, 410 (1997) (employee, who was not a lawyer, reasonably believed the agency violated the Privacy Act and made disclosures of those suspected violations to a congressional office); Special Counsel v. Eidmann, 49 MSPR 614, 628 (1991) (employee reported the violation of agency smoking regulations). The disclosure is not stripped of protection because it is voiced by the appellant "as a private citizen," nor does context seem to matter, e.g., a disclosure could be protected even if it was made in a settlement discussion. See Van Ee v. EPA, 64 MSPR 693, 696 (1994). But "[m]ere opinion cannot constitute a protected disclosure that supports a whistleblower claim." Allgood v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3390) ("[O]pinions...do not amount to a protected disclosure because they do not reveal, expose, or make known any facts or information.... Section 2302(b)(8)(A) requires a disclosure of information by an employee or applicant....").

Cases based on rumor, speculation, and unfounded opinions aside, to establish a defense of reprisal it is not necessary for the employee to show that the condition he reported demonstrated mismanagement or danger; he need only show that the condition was one that he reasonably believed to constitute one of the conditions covered by the statute. If the condition is actually demonstrated, that is sufficient, but it is not necessary. Ramos v. FAA, 4 MSPR 388, 392, 4 MSPB 446 (1980); see Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 237 (1995) ("The test for whether the appellant had a reasonable belief that his disclosure evidenced a violation of agency regulation is an objective one, and an actual regulatory violation does not need to be proved."). Under Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 61 MSPR 370, 376 n.5 (1994), the truth of a protected disclosure is of marginal importance in the analysis of a reprisal claim. But see Chianelli v. EPA, 86 MSPR 651, 657-58 ¶ 15 (2000) ("The OIG's audit of the pesticide program of South Dakota, another state in region 8, also undercuts the appellant's allegation that he made a protected disclosure when he asserted in his October 1, 1996 memorandum that the agency had misspent 35 million dollars in funding region 8 state programs and had virtually nothing to show for it.").

Opinion evidence is relevant to the reasonable belief test, but it is of little value unless the basis for the opinion is stated. See Special Counsel v. Spears, 75 MSPR 639, 658 n.10 (1997). Rumors alone will not likely form the basis of a reasonable belief in prohibited conduct. See Huffman v. At the outset, the appellant had obtained an opinion from the legal department stating that allowing the event to proceed (with videotaping and photography) would violate agency ethical regulations and possibly compromise the trade secrets of agency contractors. The appellant also relied upon a memorandum from the Deputy Secretary of the Army emphasizing the importance of avoiding these same ethical issues and the penalties for failing to do so. Moreover, the appellant's second-level supervisor also concluded that the event should not take place given the potential legal issues. This evidence more than suffices to provide a layman (like the appellant) with a reasonable belief that the project manager's proposed course of conduct could violate agency regulations. See Stiles v. Department of Homeland Security, 116 M.S.P.R. 263, ¶ 17 (2011) (an employee need not prove an actual violation to establish that he had a reasonable belief that his disclosure met the statutory criteria); Lane v. Department of Homeland Security, 115 M.S.P.R. 342, ¶ 27 (2010) (the appellant's reliance on the Contracting Officer's recommendation regarding the proper handling of an invoice suggested that he had a reasonable basis for believing his supervisor's conduct was contrary to law); Schnell, 114 M.S.P.R. 83, ¶ 20 (the reasonableness of the appellant's belief that the agency was violating the law was supported by a report concluding that the agency needed to improve its monitoring of contractor performance).

A supervisor's informed guidance and the appellant's knowledge of written expert guidance may negate the reasonableness of an allegedly protected disclosure, as occurred in Basil v. Dept. of Navy, DC-1221-21-0633-W-1 (NP 3/23/2023), involving an information specialist's disagreement about application of FOIA exemptions:

There is no indication in the record that the appellant is a lawyer. There are also several references in the record to the appellant being a "new hire" starting in approximately August 2020 and reflecting that she was in the process of learning how to respond to FOIA requests. These factors favor a finding that the reasonable person in her position could believe that the misapplication of FOIA exemptions violated the statute. See Mudd, 120 M.S.P.R. 365, ¶ 9 (2013) (considering an appellant's lack of expertise and experience in legal matters in finding that she made a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency violated various laws, rules, and regulations). However, the appellant indicated that, upon starting in her new position, she read the DOJ Guide for "roughly three weeks...with a fine highlighter." She then relied on the DOJ Guide both in her disclosures and in her pleadings as the basis for her belief that FOIA exemptions were being misapplied. Because the appellant both stated and demonstrated her familiarity with the DOJ Guide, we find that she failed to nonfrivolously allege that a reasonable person in her position could have believed the agency violated the law by exercising its discretion to elect which exemptions to cite.

Similarly, in O'Donnell, the court concluded that a Federal employee could not reasonably believe that his supervisor's determination that a landowner was not eligible for Government assistance was a violation of the Conservation Reserve Program law. O'Donnell, 561 F. App'x at 930. The court reasoned that the law granted discretionary authority to the agency, and the appellant's supervisor was in charge of exercising that discretion. Id. Here, as in the case of the law at issue in O'Donnell, agencies generally have discretion to apply FOIA exemptions, provided they do not withhold information they are required to release under FOIA. Further, as the appellant acknowledged, her supervisor had the authority to determine whether to exercise that discretion. Therefore, we find the appellant could not have reasonably believed that the agency's choice to rely on what she personally viewed as the incorrect FOIA exemption was a violation of law.

Lastly, the appellant's claim that in making her disclosure she primarily sought clarification from her supervisor about how the FOIA exemptions are applied further supports our finding that she did not reasonably believe that her disclosure evidenced the misconduct listed in section 2302(b)(8). Webb, 122 M.S.P.R. 248, ¶¶ 11-12 (finding that an appellant's emails to agency officials requesting clarification as to whether a proposed policy ran afoul of agency regulations did not constitute a disclosure of a violation of law, rule, or regulation). Thus, we find that the appellant failed to nonfrivolously allege that her communications about the agency's misapplication of FOIA exemptions were protected disclosures.
