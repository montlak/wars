e. Delay in Requesting Restoration Following Military Service

If the agency fails to provide proper notice of the time for requesting restoration following military service, reasonable delay is excused, Ward v. VA, 67 MSPR 425, 429-31 (1995):

Because we find that the agency's failure to notify the appellant of his restoration rights excuses the untimeliness of his request for restoration on August 8, 1991, we need not determine whether the appellant's phone call to Dr. Faling constituted a request for restoration.

The administrative judge rejected the appellant's reliance on Hetrick v. Department of the Interior, 39 M.S.P.R. 426 (1989), and found that she did not have the authority to waive the statutory time limit despite the appellant's due diligence or the agency's failure to notify him of his restoration rights. Because she found that Hetrick addressed the timeliness of a petition for appeal rather than the timeliness of a restoration request, the administrative judge refused to apply it here. We disagree.

Contrary to the administrative judge's account, Hetrick, in fact, addressed the timeliness of restoration requests. In that case, the Board held that where an agency fails to provide an employee with notice of restoration rights, good cause has been established to excuse the late filing of a request for restoration regardless of the employee's due diligence. See Hetrick, 39 M.S.P.R. at 429-30. Although the employee in Hetrick sought restoration following recovery from a compensable injury, we find it instructive here because both types of restoration are governed by the notice requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 353.106 (1986). This subsection expressly requires an agency to provide notice of restoration rights:

An agency shall notify an employee who is separated, furloughed, or given leave of absence because of military duty or injury, of his rights, obligations, and benefits relating to his Government employment.

5 C.F.R. § 353.106 (1986). Accordingly, we find that an agency's failure to notify an employee who left for military service prior to February 16, 1988, of restoration rights following such military service excuses the untimeliness of a request for restoration regardless of the employee's due diligence....

In this case, the evidence of record shows that the agency failed to provide the appellant with the notice required by 5 C.F.R. § 353.106. It is well settled that a general statement by an agency that an employee has an appeal right to the Board, but which does not inform the employee of the time limit for filing an appeal to the Board and lacks other information on where and how to file such an appeal, does not meet the requirements of the notice of appeal rights, and, therefore, is insufficient as such notice. See Shiflett v. U.S. Postal Service, 839 F.2d 669, 674 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Similarly, these form and manner requirements govern an agency's notice of restoration rights under 5 C.F.R. § 353.106 (1986). See Hetrick, 39 M.S.P.R. 429-30. We find, therefore, that the agency was required to inform the appellant, pursuant to subsection 353.106, of at least the time limit for filing a request for restoration and information on where and how to file such a request.

The only notice relating to restoration rights provided by the agency before 1993 was a memorandum in the appellant's possession before his departure in 1986 which stated that:

There are many benefits and rights which Mr. Ward is entitled due to his entry into military service and it is imperative that he be counseled by an Employee Relations Specialist. Please arrange an appointment for Mr. Ward by calling this office,...

The administrative judge found that the agency did not counsel the appellant of his restoration rights prior to his departure for military service. Indeed, the agency was unable to find any evidence that such counseling occurred. Therefore, we must determine whether the requirements of subsection 353.106 were satisfied by proper notice of restoration rights in the memorandum itself.

The memorandum does not set out notice of the time limits for requesting restoration, nor information on where and how to file such a request or how to compute the time within which a request must be filed. We thus conclude that the memorandum does not meet the requirements of the notice of restoration rights the agency was required to give the appellant, and, therefore, it is insufficient as such notice.

Accordingly, we find that the agency committed harmful procedural error when it failed to give the appellant notice of his restoration rights and that the agency's failure thus constituted good cause for the appellant's late filing of his restoration request....
