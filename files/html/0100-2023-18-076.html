a. Arrest, Warrant

Arrest, leading to the appellant being bound over for trial, established reasonable belief for a suspension during the notice period in Barber v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 229, 231, 7 MSPB 698 (1981). An arrest warrant plus other information was sufficient to shorten a notice period, Cox v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 336, 338 (1984):

The facts in this case are not in dispute. The shortened notice indefinite suspension was based on the fact that on September 28, 1982 a judge of the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Georgia issued a warrant for appellant's arrest to answer charges that he had violated the conditions of probation, imposed by that Court as a result of appellant's October 23, 1981 plea of guilty to a violation of the Internal Revenue Code and a criminal statute. Also taken into account by the agency was the "Petition for Warrant and Order to Show Cause Why Probation should not be Revoked," prepared by appellant's probation officer, wherein it was alleged that appellant had violated his probation by failing to file his 1979, 1980 and 1981 income tax returns.

In Martin [v. Department of the Treasury, 10 MSPB 568, 12 MSPR 12 (1982)], the Board found that while an investigation or an arrest may not alone be sufficient to establish "reasonable cause," an arrest or investigation "...may be accompanied by such circumstances as would give rise to a "reasonable cause," Martin at 573. The list of circumstances given by the Board was not intended to be all inclusive but merely to serve as guidance in considering all of the attendant facts and circumstances in determining "reasonable cause."

After consideration of all of the facts and circumstances of the instant case we find that the agency proved by preponderant evidence that it had reasonable cause to believe that appellant had committed a crime for which imprisonment might be imposed. The preponderant evidence here consists of the warrant and the supporting Petition prepared by appellant's probation officer, plus knowledge of appellant's plea of guilty and subsequent imprisonment in a prior case based on a similar charge.

But under the decision of the Federal Circuit in Dunnington v. DOJ, 956 F.2d 1151 (Fed. Cir. 1992) [discussed as to indefinite suspensions in Chapter 6, "Arrest"], an arrest (with or without a warrant) is not enough to invoke the crime exception unless the agency has more information, such as some type of investigative material, a criminal complaint, or other reliable information demonstrative of probable cause. See Barresi v. USPS, 65 MSPR 656, 662-63 (1994) (summarizing Dunnington). Land v. FAA, 12 MSPR 535, 537-38 (1982), explained as to shortened notice periods based on an "arrest plus" theory:

In Martin [v. Department of the Treasury, 10 MSPB 568, 12 MSPR 12 (1982)], the Board determined that an investigation in and of itself was insufficient to give rise to "reasonable cause" to invoke the crime provision, but that an investigation or arrest accompanied by other circumstances as would give rise to reasonable cause would be sufficient. Cited as examples of these accompanying circumstances are arrest coupled with a holding for further legal action by a magistrate; an investigation resulting in evidence supporting reasonable cause; and certain egregious acts detrimental to the accomplishment of the agency's mission, such as murder or national security offenses, brought to the agency's attention via the news media. Martin, supra, at 573, 12 MSPR at 19.

In the instant case, the agency argues that its action was based on more than the fact that appellant was arrested. The agency contends that its own investigation consisting of an interview with the arresting officer, review of the arrest records and an attempted interview with appellant coupled with appellant's arrest and his subsequent release on $8,000 bond gave it reasonable cause to believe that appellant had committed a crime. In addition to this information, there was media coverage and wide-spread notoriety of the incident within the agency. Considering all of this information collectively, we find that the agency had reasonable cause to invoke 5 USC § 7513(b) allowing for the abbreviated notice period.

Reasonable cause to invoke a shortened notice period existed in Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 145, 150 n.3 (1982):

The record in the instant case discloses that appellant's supervisor filed a formal complaint against her with the Citizens Complaint Bureau of the District of Columbia Superior Court and that an informal inquiry was made by an Assistant U.S. Attorney (AUSA). The AUSA concluded, as a result of the inquiry attended by appellant and her supervisor, that the facts alleged were sufficient so that an offense could be charged and probably successfully prosecuted for threatening bodily harm under § 22-507 of the D.C. Code, a violation of which is punishable by a fine of up to $500 and imprisonment of up to six months. However, the AUSA was reluctant to prosecute the charge for reasons unrelated to its validity. This information was known by the agency prior to proposing the emergency suspension action against appellant. The Board finds that the agency, presented with the above situation, had reasonable cause to believe that appellant had committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment could be imposed....
