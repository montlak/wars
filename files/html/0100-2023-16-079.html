3. Failure to Use Chapter 43

Under Wells v. Harris, 1 MSPR 208, 248-49, 1 MSPB 199 (1979), if an agency has no appraisal system in effect, or if the system is in use but the agency does not care to use it in an action against the employee, the agency must proceed, if at all, under adverse action procedures identified in Chapter 75 of Title 5 of the U.S. Code, requiring proof by a preponderance of evidence of service efficiency impairment. For a while the Board was remanding to agencies cases that were arguably deficient under Chapter 43 so that agencies could decide whether to pursue the case under Chapter 75. See, e.g., Nesson v. Dept. of Treasury, 11 MSPR 346, 347 (1982) (following an earlier remand of the case to the regional office to consider alleged deficiencies under Chapter 43, the agency successfully prosecuted a case against the appellant under Chapter 75); Young v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 73, 79, 7 MSPB 7 (1981) (rather than remand, the Board itself reconsidered a Chapter 43 case under Chapter 75 standards); Alton v. VA, 3 MSPR 539, 540 (1980) (noting in Wells that in "the absence of such a system, performance-based actions could still be taken pursuant to 5 U.S.C. Chapter 75, but that the higher preponderance of the evidence, burden of proof, and more difficult standard of demonstrating that the action promotes the 'efficiency of the service' would be applicable"); Coachman v. IRS, 2 MSPR 593, 593 (1980) (allowing the agency to elect to proceed under Chapter 75 when it could properly take action under Chapter 43). Then, assuming the agency elected to have the case considered as an adverse action, the Board would not reverse the agency action because of noncompliance with Chapter 43 if the action was in compliance with the requirements of Chapter 75 and if the agency met the higher burden of proof required for the adverse action. See Fikstad v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 425, 428, 6 MSPB 361 (1981). Improper invocation of Chapter 43 in the proposal notice was not harmful error. Darby v. Dept. of Treasury, 4 MSPR 58, 60 (1980); see Nesson v. Dept. of Treasury, 11 MSPR 346, 348 (1982) ("Any error involved in a removal based on unapproved performance standards and criteria may be, and here was, remedied by reconsideration under the procedural and substantive requirements of Chapter 75."); Garrett v. Dept. of Treasury, 7 MSPR 135, 139 (1981) (not harmful error for action to be initiated under Chapter 43 and assessed under Chapter 75 if the appellant cannot show how the result would have differed had the proposal been structured as a Chapter 75 case). [Refer to Chapter 8, "Performance" for performance-based Chapter 75 cases.]

The Board's approach under Wells was, for a time, reversed by Gende v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 604, 616 (1984), deciding that only in the exceptional case may an agency take a performance-based action against an individual under adverse action rather than unacceptable performance procedures. Then Gende was set aside, in Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826 (Fed. Cir. 1985), discussed below, so that an agency may now use either Chapter 43 or 75 procedures to remove an unacceptable performer. [Refer to Chapter 8, "Assigned Standard," concerning removals for poor performance under Chapter 75; refer to Chapter 11, "Mixing Chapters 43 and 75."]

A few citations trace the evolution of the law prior to the Federal Circuit's ruling in Lovshin that agencies may use either Chapters 43 or 75 to remove poor performers. Wells, supra, determining that failure of an agency to have in place a Chapter 43 appraisal system was no bar to an adverse action under Chapter 75 for poor performance, was reaffirmed in Williamson v. DHHS, 3 MSPR 18, 21, 3 MSPB 142 (1980). Lloyd v. SSA, 15 MSPR 217, 218 n.2 (1983), treated a removal as a Chapter 75 case, subject to the preponderant evidence standard, when the proposal notice made no reference to Chapter 43, and when there was no evidence that the agency established and implemented an appraisal system under Chapter 43. The courts agreed that performance cases could be prosecuted either under Chapter 43 or under Chapter 75. Hatcher v. Dept. of Air Force, 705 F.2d 1309 (11th Cir. 1983); Debose v. USDA, 700 F.2d 1262 (9th Cir. 1983); Kochanny v. BATF, 694 F.2d 698 (Fed. Cir. 1982), aff'g Kochanny v. Dept. of Treasury, 5 MSPR 33 (1981); Drew v. Dept. of Navy, 672 F.2d 197, 200 (D.C. Cir. 1982) ("Inclusion of some Chapter 43 terminology...does not detract from the conclusion that his dismissal could promote the efficiency of the service.").

Seeking to revive performance actions under Chapter 43, the Board reexamined Wells in Gende v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 604, 615 (1984), reconsideration denied, 25 MSPR 234 (1984), and concluded that performance problems should be addressed through Chapter 43 with exceptions for performance problems that could be fairly characterized as misconduct, e.g., insubordination, and for performance problems involving a substantial likelihood--proved by a preponderance of evidence--that providing the employee with an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance before proposing the action would result in injury, death, breach of security, or great monetary loss. If permanent employment depended on success in a training program, it was presumed that if the employee's success was based upon his ability to perform the regular job duties encompassed by the training, removal based on deficiencies during training was a performance-based action. Cortright v. Dept. of Transp., 27 MSPR 200, 202 (1985); Billings v. Dept. of Transp., 25 MSPR 201, 204 (1984) (vacated by 36 MSPR 421, 424 (1988) (removal for failure to complete a training program may proceed under Chapter 75)). Without regard to the Gende analysis, Chapter 75 could be properly applied to employees, for example, those of the Postal Service, not covered by Chapter 43. Maisto v. USPS, 27 MSPR 496 (1985) (Table). Gende was set aside in Lovshin, post.
