a. Documentation

Designation of an employee as a disabled preference eligible on an official agency record creates a presumption for the appellant that he is a preference eligible employee. Santiago v. USPS, 98 MSPR 179, 184 ¶ 10 (2005). In accordance with OPM guidance, a veteran's DD Form 214 showing the award of any Armed Forces Expeditionary Medal is acceptable proof of entitlement to veterans preference. The DD Form 214 need not show the name of the threat or country of service leading to the award of the medal. Hamilton v. USPS, 86 MSPR 215, 217-18 ¶ 7 (2000) (referencing an OPM website document "Appendix A: Wars, Campaigns and Expeditions of the Armed Forces since WWII Which Qualify for Veterans Preference"). [Refer to the OPM Vets Guide: https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/veterans-services/vet-guide-for-hr-professionals/.]

Sellers v. USPS, 98 MSPR 44, 48-49 ¶¶ 9-10 (2004), relied on a DD-214 form to establish preference eligibility:

Under 5 U.S.C. § 2108(1)(A) and § 2108(3), an appellant may establish that he is preference eligible by showing that he served on active duty in the armed forces during a war or in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized. These provisions require an appellant to prove both that he was on active duty in the armed forces during or at the time of the campaign, and that he actually served in the campaign or expedition for which the campaign badge was authorized. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 506 ¶ 6 (2002); Hamilton v. U.S. Postal Service, 86 M.S.P.R. 215 ¶ 5 (2000).

We find that the appellant has met this burden. The file includes a copy of the appellant's DD-214 form that shows that he received a Navy Expeditionary Medal during his active military service. Any Armed Forces Expeditionary Medal qualifies an employee for veterans preference. Jackson, 91 M.S.P.R. 506 ¶ 7; Hamilton, 86 M.S.P.R. 215 ¶ 7. An employee's DD-214 form showing receipt of any Armed Forces Expeditionary Medal is acceptable proof of entitlement to veterans preference, regardless of whether the DD-214 form specifies the name of the theater or country of service for which the medal was awarded. Id.; see also Office of Personnel Management's Veterans Information "Vet Guide," available at http://www.opm.gov/veterans/html/vetguide.asp [now at https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/veterans-services/vet-guide-for-hr-professionals/] ("Any Armed Forces Expeditionary medal or campaign badge...qualifies for [veterans] preference"); (agency veterans preference guidelines, which mirror the language on OPM's "Vet Guide"). Thus, notwithstanding the appellant's failure to respond to the jurisdictional order, the record below leaves no doubt that the appellant has appeal rights as a preference eligible employee.

The need to prove, and the means for proving, the statutory factors essential to preference eligible status were discussed in Anderson v. USPS, CH-0752-10-0335-I-1 (NP 8/23/2010):

In his petition for review, the appellant specifically asserts for the first time that he served in the Persian Gulf during February, March, and April 1987, and that that service entitles him to preference eligible status. Presumably, he believes that he is entitled to preference eligible status under 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3)(A) because the agency's evidence showed that a Navy Expeditionary Medal was awarded for service in the Persian Gulf from February 1 to July 23, 1987. (Vet Guide).

The appellant has failed to show that he is entitled to status as a preference eligible. The relevant part of 5 U.S.C. § 2108 defines a "preference eligible" as a "veteran" who "served on active duty in the armed forces during a war, [or] in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized." See 5 U.S.C. §§ 2108(1)(A), (3)(A). That provision requires an appellant to prove both that he was on active duty in the armed forces during, or at the time of, the campaign, and that he actually served in the campaign or expedition for which the campaign badge was authorized. See Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 506, ¶ 6 (2002); Hamilton v. U.S. Postal Service, 86 M.S.P.R. 215, ¶ 5 (2000). Here, however, the appellant's DD-214 does not show that he was awarded such a medal. Thus, the appellant has not sustained his burden of proving that he is a preference eligible entitled to appeal an adverse action to the Board. See, e.g., Alley v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 283, ¶ 7 (2005); cf. Jackson, 91 M.S.P.R. 506, ¶¶ 7-8 (the appellant met his burden of proving preference eligibility by submitting a copy of his amended DD-214 form which showed that he received a Navy Expeditionary Medal during his active military service); Hamilton, 86 M.S.P.R. 215, ¶¶ 6-8 (the appellant met his burden of proving preference eligibility by submitting copies of two DD-214 forms showing that he served on active duty in the Navy from July 6, 1978, through June 7, 1982, and that he received the Navy Expeditionary Medal during this tour of duty).

Badana v. Dept. of Air Force, 104 MSPR 182, 186-88 ¶¶ 11-14, 2006 MSPB 345 (2006), involving a selection action, considered documentation requirements for claims to preference eligibility:

However, we deny the appellant's request for corrective action for the following reasons. As noted above, with his application for the Technical Information Specialist position, the appellant submitted a 2002 DVA rating decision and a completed copy of the February 1990 version of the SF-15. With his application for the International Relations Specialist position, the appellant did not submit any documentation concerning his claimed entitlement to veterans' preference. The appellant contended in his appeal that, consistent with the documentation requirements contained on the face of the December 2004 version of the SF-15 his submission of the rating decision was sufficient to establish his preference eligibility before the agency.

Relying on the specific language of the 1990 version of the SF-15 that the appellant submitted to the agency with his application for the Technical Information Specialist position, both the agency and the administrative judge rejected the DVA rating decision because it was outdated. The 1990 version of the SF-15 lists the documentation required for establishing veterans' preference based on a compensable service-connected disability (such as that of the appellant), as follows:

1. An official statement, dated within the last 12 months, from the Department of Veterans Affairs or from a branch of the Armed Forces, certifying to the veteran's present receipt of compensation for service-connected disability or disability retired pay [;or]

2. An official statement, dated within the last 12 months, from the Department of Veterans Affairs or from a branch of the Armed Forces, certifying that the veteran has a service-connected disability of 10% or more [;or]

3. An official statement or retirement orders from a branch of the Armed Forces, showing that the retired serviceman was retired because of permanent service-connected disability or was transferred to the permanent disability retirement list. The statement of retirement orders must indicate that the disability is 10% or more.

Because the appellant's DVA decision was dated November 19, 2002, i.e., not within the 12 months prior to the date of his application, it was not considered timely.

However, on review, the appellant submits a December 2004 revised version of the SF-15, which contains revised criteria for establishing veterans' preference:

1. An official statement, dated 1991 or later, from the Department of Veterans Affairs or from a branch of the Armed Forces, certifying to the veteran's present receipt of compensation for service-connected disability or disability retired pay [;or]

2. An official statement, dated 1991 or later, from the Department of Veterans Affairs or from a branch of the Armed Forces, certifying that the veteran has a service-connected disability of 10% or more [; or]

3. An official statement or retirement orders from a branch of the Armed Forces, showing that the retired serviceman was retired because of permanent service-connected disability or was transferred to the permanent disability retirement list. The statement of retirement orders must indicate that the disability is 10% or more.

According to a press release issued by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), and submitted by the appellant on review, the form was revised to be consistent with DVA policy, which considers letters issued in 1991 or later as proof of a permanent disability, unless specifically stated otherwise.

As previously noted, the SF-15 was revised in December 2004. The appellant applied for the Technical Information Specialist position on January 23, 2004, well before the revised documentation requirements went into effect. The appellant has not provided any evidence showing that the agency erred by failing to consider the rating decision, as the agency was, at that time, permitted to consider only documentation dated within the last 12 months of the appellant's application for the position. Cf. Abell v. Department of the Navy, 343 F.3d 1378, 1380-82 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (noting that the Board had found that the agency properly credited the appellant with a 5-point preference rather than the 10-point preference sought because the appellant failed to provide a DVA rating decision dated within the last 12 months of his application for a position). As to the International Relations Specialist position, the appellant failed to submit the DVA rating decision or any other evidence concerning his entitlement to veterans' preference as a disabled veteran. The appellant has not explained why the evidence he submitted to the agency was sufficient to establish his preference-eligibility in accordance with the standards applicable at the time his job applications were under consideration. Consequently, we find that, while the appellant has established Board jurisdiction over his VEOA claim, he failed to provide the required proof of his preference-eligible status to the agency to support his application for either position. Absent such proof, the agency was not required to afford the appellant the claimed preference. Therefore, the appellant has not shown that the agency violated his rights under a statute or regulation related to veterans' preference....

The Board relies on military records to support claims of campaign badge awards. If the appellant asserts that he should have received, but did not receive, a badge, the Board disclaims jurisdiction to review "this purely military matter." Blakemore v. USPS, 53 MSPR 390, 393 (1992); cf. House v. USPS, DA-0752-13-4322-I-1 (NP 7/14/2014) ("Receipt of the Sea Service Deployment Ribbon alone is insufficient to establish preference eligibility. See Blakemore v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 390, 391-93 (1992).... To the extent that the appellant's argument may be construed as an assertion that the Navy improperly denied him a campaign badge based on his service, that is a purely military matter, and the Board lacks jurisdiction to review a purely military matter. See Siedschlag v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 M.S.P.R. 517, 520 (1988)."). Evidence of preference eligible status may not be credited if it is considered to be vague or ambiguous. See Dillion v. USPS, CH-0752-16-0127-I-1 (NP 12/2/2016).
