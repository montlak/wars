a. Paid Break Tied to Unpaid Break

FLRA went into greater detail in its examination of the limitations on negotiation of breaks in NAGE Local R1-109 and VAMC, Newington, 37 FLRA 448, 451-54 (1990). The question was whether paid breaks could be combined with unpaid breaks, as long as the workday was extended by the length of the unpaid break. The proposals were negotiable:

...5 U.S.C. § 6101(a)(3)(F) provides that, unless the head of an agency determines that the agency would be seriously handicapped in carrying out its functions or that costs would be substantially increased, the agency head shall provide that "breaks in working hours of more than 1 hour may not be scheduled in a basic workday." This statutory provision affords agencies the discretion to establish breaks in working hours, that is, nonwork or nonduty periods (noncompensated periods), during the workday, as long as those periods do not exceed 1 hour....

[W]here a break in working hours, such as a lunch period, is extended by a given amount of time, for example, 15 minutes, there must be an offsetting 15-minute extension of the workday. Otherwise, employees would not complete the 8 hours of work provided under 5 U.S.C. § 6101(a)(3)(D)....

[A]pplicable law and regulations permit an agency to establish rest periods during duty time--compensable "rest breaks"--that do not exceed 20 minutes.

In sum, except for statutory and regulatory exceptions that do not apply to this case...as long as an agency establishes a work schedule that includes five 8-hour workdays in a workweek and contains no breaks longer than 1 hour, the work schedule so established is consistent with law and regulation.

...

We note, in addition, that 5 U.S.C. § 6101(a)(3)(F) does not limit the number of breaks in a workday or the purpose of breaks in working hours. Because 5 U.S.C. § 6101(a)(3)(F) is stated in the plural--"breaks"--it would appear to permit more than one break in working hours or different types of breaks. The effect of that section, therefore, is not limited to the establishment of only lunch breaks....

We also find that because the proposal establishes periods of nonduty time, those periods of nonduty time are not compensated rest periods subject to the 20-minute limitation provided in 5 C.F.R. § 551.441(b) [which] neither precludes a nonduty rest period nor prevents the addition of a nonduty rest period to a compensated rest period. That section limits only the length of a compensated rest period.

...

The Agency claims that Proposal 2 is inconsistent with 5 U.S.C. § 6101(a) because it requires the Agency to extend the workday beyond 8 hours regardless of whether the Agency head has determined that the Agency would be seriously handicapped in carrying out its functions or costs would be substantially increased without such an extension.... The proposal does not require extensions of the workday beyond 8 hours. The proposal requires an extension of the workday so that employees will work a full 8 hours; the extension of the workday offsets the break in working hours provided under the proposal. In particular, the proposal is not inconsistent with 5 U.S.C. § 6101(a)(3) because that provision is concerned with changing the 5-day, 40-hour workweek requirement. The proposal does not prescribe the particular daily schedule that the Agency must adopt in order to implement the 5-day, 40-hour workweek. Under the proposal, the scheduling of the workweek remains within the Agency's discretion. Proposal 2 would affect only the length of the employee workday, not the length of time the employee is in a duty status.
