1. Endangerment of Employees

Removal is appropriate for a supervisor who, because of poor training of subordinates or inadequate supervision, contributed to an accident causing an employee's harm or death. See West v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 28, 30 (1982); cf. Artis v. TVA, AT-0752-14-0390-I-1 (NP 10/11/2022) (adverse action based on narrative charge that was characterized to include inadequate planning and execution of high hazard jobs); Doherty v. Dept. of Transp., 13 MSPR 274, 276 (1982) (suspension for negligent operation of a GOV with attendant property damage and "endangerment of life"); Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 11 MSPR 239, 241 (1982) (demotion from a supervisory position for failure to exercise supervisory responsibilities, resulting in damage to government aircraft and endangering offloading ramp personnel). Demotion to a nonsupervisory job was more appropriate than removal of an employee whose failure to carry out supervisory duties could have resulted in personal injury or property damage. Super v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 221, 223 (1984). Careless workmanship, suggesting a "pattern of behavior that presents a safety risk to other employees," supported removal in Kaminski v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 393, 397 (1993) (high voltage electrician). Regarding the potential for harm, if an employee is charged with endangering the safety of personnel during a training drill, there must be actual evidence of endangerment, not evidence of carelessness. Mistakes were expected during training drills. See Wyman v. Marine Corps, 29 MSPR 492, 494 (1985) (evidence from agency witnesses to the effect that no danger was created). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Respondeat Superior."]

Penalties for those involved in acts jeopardizing the safety of others were considered in Watkins v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 146, 147-48 (1985). The AJ initially sustained removal and the Board denied a PFR, but the appeal was then remanded by a Federal Circuit order for reconsideration of mitigation. On remand the Board reduced the penalty to a 60-day suspension:

Appellant was charged with exposing himself and a co-worker to excessive radiation in the course of taking x-ray pictures of pipes and fittings on a Navy ship. While the potential seriousness of the accident is indisputable, in reality the amount of radiation received was not "medically significant." Further the offense was inadvertent and technical, committed without any intent, malicious or otherwise. It was appellant's first offense on an otherwise spotless work and safety record. With regard to the effect of the offense on appellant's ability to perform, appellant had spent twenty-five years doing this type of work, five years with the agency, and it is unlikely that one incident of carelessness would significantly impact on his future performance. Although the agency asserted that appellant's supervisors had lost confidence in him, it is noteworthy that appellant's supervisor and assistant received only a reprimand and a five-day suspension respectively for their involvement in the incident. Considering the complete absence of any other violations of work or safety rules in appellant's record, it is clear that his potential for rehabilitation is good. While the record indicates that the agency considered alternatives to removal, including demotion, it did not take such action because of an alleged loss of confidence in appellant. As indicated above, the other personnel involved in the incident received relatively minor penalties; this fact considerably weakens the agency's assertion. However, the Board has acknowledged that a higher standard of care is demanded of employees in charge. See Antonucci v. Department of Justice, 8 MSPR 491, 493 (1981). Because appellant was in charge at the time the offense occurred, we find that a more substantial penalty than that received by the others is reasonable.

The harm that is caused or threatened may be to oneself. Failure to follow medical restrictions--a requirement that an employee walk with a cane--warranted discipline that was mitigated from removal to a 30-day suspension in Williams v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 387, 391 (1988) (considering as well a past disciplinary record). See Green v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 626, 636 (1994) (removal for the repeated failure to follow safety instructions requiring use of personal safety devices: "[F]ailure to comply with safety requirements where persons or property are endangered is also grounds for removal.").

Thompson v. USPS, 50 MSPR 41, 45-46 (1991), sustained removal for the failure of an employee to follow safety rules governing operation of a sorting machine:

[T]he appellant worked at a sack-sorting machine which was over 150 feet long and was elevated 16 feet above the ground. The machine had continuously running conveyer belts that operated at rapid speeds. Attached to the belts were trays that measured two feet by four feet, and carried sacks of mail weighing 70 or more pounds. Not only did the appellant work with this machine, he also worked with electricity and battery acid. The administrative judge found that the appellant's supervisor had instructed him on numerous occasions to wear his safety equipment while working.

The appellant...indicated in his submissions below that he wore his safety equipment when he determined that it was necessary for him to do so and that he had not had an accident in the performance of his work. The agency had a legitimate concern in ensuring that its employees are protected while working under dangerous conditions. It was not required to wait until the appellant had an accident before enforcing its safety rules.... [T]he appellant was required to obey his supervisor's lawful order and then grieve it later if he so desired. The appellant's failure to wear his safety hat and to have his safety equipment with him while working was willful and deliberate. Indeed, with respect to this particular charge, the evidence established that, even after the appellant was instructed to use his safety equipment, he still failed to put on his safety goggles. The appellant's persistent, defiant conduct demonstrated that he lacked the potential for rehabilitation. The agency should not be forced to tolerate such deliberate and blatant misconduct, especially since the appellant's misconduct could have resulted in serious injuries. The appellant's six years of service is insufficient to warrant mitigation under these circumstances.

Tisdell v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 44, 52-53 ¶¶ 18-19 (2003), sustained removal of a painter for failure to follow shop safety procedures and a past history of discipline. See Gragg v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 296, 11 MSPB 546 (1982), on subsequent appeal, Gragg v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 506, 510 (1984) (considering implications of violations of shop grooming policy as that policy was challenged through unfair labor practice proceedings before the FLRA), modified by Fleckenstein v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 470, 474 n.3 (1994). Caswell v. DHS, 257 Fed. Appx. 312 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), affirmed a Board decision sustaining removal of a border patrol agent charged with failure to respond to an agent requesting assistance.
