4. Cross-PFR

A party may obtain an initial decision that contains some defect or another, but the party is willing to accept the result. If the opposition decides to file a PFR, however, the party that would otherwise be satisfied with the result may desire to have the initial decision reviewed for defects otherwise considered unworthy of objection. Board regulations allow for cross-petitions for review to accomplish that result. See Barron v. Dept. of Transp., 68 MSPR 446, 448 (1995). The Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.114(c) (2022), states of a cross-petition:

Who may file. Any party to the proceeding, the Director of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), or the Special Counsel (under 5 U.S.C. 1212(c)) may file a petition or cross petition for review....

Under 5 CFR 1201.114(a) (2022):

(2) A cross petition for review has the same meaning as a petition for review but is used to describe a pleading that is filed by a party when another party has already filed a timely petition for review.

(3) A response to a petition for review and a cross petition for review may be contained in a single pleading.

5 CFR 1201.115 (2022) explains:

The Board normally will consider only issues raised in a timely filed petition or cross petition for review.

5 CFR 1201.114 (2022), states:

(e) ...A cross petition for review must be filed within 25 days of the date of service of the petition for review.

The cross-PFR must be filed within 25 days after service of the PFR, Mozqueda v. DOD, 54 MSPR 152, 156 (1992), and because filing of the cross-petition is governed by the "date of service" of the PFR, ordinarily the response to the PFR and cross-PFR must be filed within 25 days after the date that the envelope containing the service copy of the PFR was postmarked. 5 CFR 1201.114(e) (2022). The date for filing the response and cross-PFR is not calculated from the date that the PFR is received by the responding party. See Sargent v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 387, 396 (1992). An untimely cross-PFR will be adjudicated if the delay is excused under the Board's caselaw permitting waiver of a deadline or if the cross-PFR is based on evidence that "was not readily available before the close of the record below and is of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from that of the ID." Thornton v. OPM, 104 MSPR 471, 474 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 10 (2007) (also noting a brief period of internal delay in the transmittal of information received by OPM to the representative assigned to the case). Cargile v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-14-0056-I-2, n.4 (NP 10/3/2022), excused a late cross-PFR that would have been timely as a PFR:

On October 19, 2018, the appellant filed a motion to dismiss the cross petition for review as untimely filed without a showing of good cause. She correctly observes that the agency failed to file its cross petition within 25 days of the date of service of her petition for review, as required under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(e), and that the agency did not comply with the Clerk of the Board's instruction to provide a motion showing good cause for the filing delay, as required under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(g). We note, however, that the agency did file its cross petition before the June 20, 2016 finality date set forth in the initial decision. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(e). Thus, had the appellant not already filed a petition for review, the agency's June 16, 2016 pleading would have been timely. Instead, the swift filing of the appellant's petition for review contracted the regulatory time period for the agency to contest the initial decision. We find that it would be contrary to the spirit of 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(e) to dismiss the agency's cross petition under these circumstances. While we do not excuse the agency's failure to comply with the Clerk's instructions, we nonetheless exercise our discretion under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(g) to find good cause for the untimely filing.

A cross-PFR may be filed only if timely PFR was filed. If the other side's PFR is untimely, the cross-petition is dismissed. Gill v. Dept. of Treasury, 41 MSPR 267, 270-71 (1989); see Scali v. OPM, 106 MSPR 409, 412 ¶ 10, 2007 MSPB 194 (2007) (unusual result: the Board denied OPM's cross-petition calling for a remand to the AJ for a decision on the merits (the case had been dismissed for lack of jurisdiction) when the appellant's PFR was untimely); Castro v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 449, 453 (1995) ("The agency assumed the risk of having its cross-petition for review dismissed by not filing a timely petition for review on its own behalf in the first instance."). The same result appears to follow if the agency's PFR is dismissed because the agency fails to provide proper interim relief. See Guidry v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 453, 456 (1994). To avoid the problem of a cross-PFR that fails for lack of a justiciable PFR, a party should file a PFR rather than depend upon the cross-PFR process for raising material objections to the initial decision.

To effectively use the cross-PFR procedure, its purpose must be understood. One party answers the objections to an initial decision in another party's PFR by filing a reply to the PFR. The party replying to a PFR raises independent objections to an initial decision by filing a cross-PFR, assuming that the replying party did not initially file a PFR. These independent objections are answered, in turn, by a reply to the cross-PFR. The Board may treat a cross-petition as a response to the opposition's PFR if the cross-PFR addresses only the points raised in the opponent's PFR. Levy v. OPM, 41 MSPR 139, 140 n.1 (1989); see Pedeleose v. DOD, AT-1221-14-0260-W-1 (NP 1/22/2015) ("As noted above, the administrative judge found that the appellant proved by preponderant evidence that all of his asserted disclosures were protected. The agency has not filed a cross petition for review contesting this determination, and we see no reason to reexamine it."); Cross v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 62, 67 n.7 (2001) (Board declined to treat as a cross-PFR a pleading by the appellant that reiterated elements of his affirmative defenses but that did not make specific allegations of error as to the AJ's findings on those defenses or ask the Board to reverse the AJ's rulings); Adams v. SSA, 68 MSPR 690 (1995) (Table) ("Although the agency has filed a submission captioned 'cross petition for review,' it merely requests that the appellant's petition for review be denied; therefore, we have considered it only as a response to the petition for review."); Kimm v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 198, 207 n.3 (1994) (declining to treat as a cross-PFR the appellant's response to an agency PFR when the appellant sought to uphold a favorable initial decision but also put forth arguments of harmful error that were made to but not addressed by the AJ); Mycka v. OPM, 56 MSPR 675, 676 n.1 (1993) ("Because the appellant's submission responds to the agency's petition for review without any statement of objection to the initial decision, we have considered it as a response to the petition for review, rather than as a cross petition."); Huettner v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 472, 474 n.1 (1992) ("The appellant's document responds to the agency's arguments, does not disagree with the findings of the AJ, and does not seek further mitigation of the penalty from that already awarded by the administrative judge."); Nixon v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 624, 626 (1991) (disregarding the appellant's response to the agency reply to a PFR when the appellant incorrectly characterized the agency response as a cross-PFR; the agency submission was not a cross-PFR because it did not challenge the findings of the AJ leading to the dismissal of the appeal). The Board disregards a reply by a party to the cross-petition that the Board administratively converts to a reply to the PFR. See Swenson v. USPS, 45 MSPR 268, 269 n.* (1990); Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 186, 193 (1994) ("Because the agency's submission raises no allegations of errors by the administrative judge, it should not have been treated as a cross-petition for review and the appellants should not have been afforded the opportunity to respond to it.").

To preserve an issue for Board review, a PFR or cross-PFR must be filed. A new issue may not be raised through the response to a PFR. Sternberg v. DODDS, 41 MSPR 46, 49 n.3 (1989); cf. Crawford v. Dept. of Treasury, 60 MSPR 614, 618 (1994) (although the agency argued in its response to a cross-PFR some issues that the agency argued in its PFR, a motion to strike the response was denied because the agency raised no new issues); Sandland v. GSA, 28 MSPR 260, 360 n.* (1985) (without a cross-PFR, the appellant could not, in response to an agency PFR on a fee award, seek correction of the initial addendum decision to award an additional amount allegedly erroneously omitted by the AJ); Berggren v. Dept. of Transp., 25 MSPR 558, 559 n.* (1985) (appellant could not raise the disallowance of costs in a fee petition by reply to the agency's PFR challenging an underlying fee entitlement); Burner v. TVA, 20 MSPR 167, 169 n.1 (1984) (without a cross-PFR, appellant could not raise issues not addressed in agency's PFR).

The Board's rule that it will not consider objections to an initial decision unless the challenging party raised them in a timely petition or cross-PFR is a rule designed to facilitate the Board's consideration of the large number of PFRs that it receives by requiring specification of the issues the Board is asked to review. The rule also enables the parties to promptly know the aspects of the AJ's decision that are challenged. OPM may not avoid the result of the rule by a reconsideration request after the employer failed to make an argument through a PFR or cross-petition. The Board is not required to treat OPM more favorably than any other litigant. OPM may protect its interests through intervention at an earlier stage of the Board proceedings. Horner v. Burns, 783 F.2d 196 (Fed. Cir. 1986). [Refer later in this chapter to "OPM Reconsideration Requests."]

If a response raises a new issue, the response may be treated as a cross-petition, and the Board then ordinarily allows the party who filed the PFR generating the response to reply to the new issue raised in the response. See Holderness v. Defense Commissary Agency, 75 MSPR 401, 403 & n.1 (1997) (denying the agency a right to reply because the constructive cross-petition was denied). Generally, however, the rule requiring each party presenting objections to an initial decision to file its own PFR or cross-PFR is uniform in its application. Schoemer v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 363, 366 ¶ 5 (1999), noted:

Although the appellant disagrees with some of the administrative judge's findings and alleged failure to make findings, he has not filed a timely petition for review or cross-petition for review challenging the administrative judge's conclusions on the merits of the charges and on his affirmative defense. Thus, we will not address those issues on review....

Notwithstanding the niceties of the PFR process, the Board may accept a PFR from one party and, through its "reopening" authority, correct an error in favor of the party who did not file a separate PFR. In St. Clair v. VA, 59 MSPR 634 (1993), the AJ mitigated a 30-day suspension to a reprimand. The agency filed a PFR. The Board denied the agency PFR but reopened the case and tossed out the entire penalty. [Refer later in this chapter to "Authority to Reopen and Reconsider."]

Although the cross-PFR may be used to raise issues not raised in the appellant's PFR, it will not avoid the finality of an initial decision in an appeal involving more than one appellant if fewer than all the appellants file PFRs and if the agency does not file a PFR as to each appellant. In Felton v. EEOC, 31 MSPR 481, 483 (1986), there were apparently two appellants whose cases were decided in a consolidated initial decision. One appellant filed a PFR, the other did not. The PFR was filed a few days before the initial decision was to become final. The agency cross-petition was not filed until after the finality date of the decision. The Board declined to review issues raised by the agency cross-PFR concerning the appellant whose initial decision had become final: although a cross-PFR may raise issues not addressed in a timely PFR, it may not be used to skirt the timeliness requirement for filing a PFR. The lesson of Felton is that a PFR must be filed in each appeal presenting an issue for Board review. See Almond v. FAA, 12 MSPR 670, 671 (1982) (PFR submitted in companion case did not serve to obtain review in related but unconsolidated case).
