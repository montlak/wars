e. Request for Extension of Time to Appeal

With the exception of a request for an extension of the appellate deadline filed by parties interested in using preappeal mediation under 5 CFR 1201.22(b) (2022), the Board's regulation does not provide for an extension for filing an appeal, although the regulations permit an extension for filing a PFR under 5 CFR 1201.114(f) (2022). A request for an extension of the time to file an appeal is, however, a factor that may lead the Board to determine that reasonable diligence was exercised by an appellant whose appeal is late, Caldwell v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 674, 677 ¶ 8 (2000):

...The appellant's extension of time request was filed before the September 7, 1999 deadline for filing his petition for appeal. The appellant's evidence is credible and unrebutted by the agency, and he filed his appeal on September 28, 1999, within 30 days of his extension request. Based on these facts, coupled with his pro se status and his obvious confusion, we find that the appellant has established good cause for his untimely filing. Alonzo, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR at 184; see Quarezo v. Office of Personnel Management, 10 MSPB 448, 11 MSPR 522, 523 (1982) (the pro se appellant showed good cause for his untimely petition for appeal where he timely filed a motion with the regional office "to have his claim freezed," the regional office notified him that the Board's regulations did not contemplate or permit such request and that the time limit for filing an appeal was running, and he filed his petition for appeal 2 days following his receipt of this response). Further, the agency has neither asserted nor shown any substantial prejudice caused to it by the delay in filing. Alonzo, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR at 184.

Schuringa v. Dept. of Treasury, 106 MSPR 1, 5 ¶ 9, 2007 MSPB 116 (2007), reaffirmed the Board's approach to requests for extensions of time to file appeals:

In addition, the Board's regulations do not contemplate requests for an extension of time to file petitions for appeal and, during the proceedings on appeal, the appellant failed to specify when she contacted the Board or with whom she spoke concerning her appeal. In any event, that someone at the Board allegedly informed the appellant that the Board would not grant her request for an extension of time to file her appeal does not excuse her subsequent untimely filing. In fact, it supports a finding that she did not exercise due diligence under the circumstances. In sum, we cannot find, based on these facts, and even considering the appellant's pro se status and the minimal length of her filing delay, that she exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence concerning the filing of her appeal.

The Federal Circuit seemed to assume the existence of a request for an extension of time to file an appeal in Stout v. MSPB, 389 F.3d 1233, 1242 (Fed. Cir. 2004). Although an agency has no regulatory authority to extend the time for the appellant to appeal to the Board from an agency action, the Board will find good cause for a waiver of the appellate time limit when an appellant relies on an agency extension. See Hoagland v. Marine Corps, 20 MSPR 6, 6 (1984) (citing Baker v. OPM, 7 MSPR 379 (1981)); cf. Le Master v. VA, 79 MSPR 680, 682-83 nn.4, 5 (1998) (date of filing an extension request counted as the date of filing of an appeal that followed the request). But cf. Robinson v. MSPB, 366 Fed. Appx. 162 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (a request to the agency for an extension for filing the appeal did not extend the Board's regulatory filing period). A hearing should have been held on timeliness for an appellant who alleged he requested an extension of time from the Board to appeal and, although he did not have a copy of his extension request, he had a certified mail receipt. Quinones v. OPM, 33 MSPR 612, 614 (1987).
