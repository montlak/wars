1. Modification of Agency Policy, Facility

Reasonable accommodation includes a responsibility to permit an employee access to favorable parking arrangements and to permit an employee to use crutches despite a contrary agency policy that was based on unsubstantiated safety concerns. Vaughn v. USPS, 33 MSPR 496, 501 (1987). But if a parking arrangement is advanced as an accommodation, there must be a showing that the accommodation will allow the appellant to perform the essential duties of his job; if the connection is not made, the accommodation is not required. See West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 559 (1990).

Nanette v. Dept. of Treasury, 92 MSPR 127, 139-42 ¶¶ 41-48 (2002), declined to conclude that the agency discriminated against an individual whose sensitivity to airborne chemicals could only have been abated, if at all, through extensive modifications to facilities and, possibly, training of employees:

After considering all of the evidence discussed above, we find that the appellant's requested accommodations are, on their face, unreasonable. In fact, it is difficult to fathom an office environment wherein the articulated accommodations could be satisfied in total, let alone a federal office building that could be accessed via a chemical free portal extending from the appellant's parking space to her workspace. Indeed, the appellant's own physician, Dr. Alan Vinitsky, concluded that it was "unlikely" the agency could "find an appropriate safe place for her to work, given the degree of her dysfunction." The fact that the appellant testified that she is capable of working in an office environment where several of her requested accommodations have been provided does not prove that the laundry list of accommodations she has requested of the agency was reasonable.

...

In any event, even assuming the appellant's requested accommodations could, in fact, be implemented, the appellant has failed to demonstrate that the accommodations would provide sufficient protection such that she could "safely" perform the duties of her position as required by 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(a)(6). Cf. Chevron USA Inc. v. Echazabal, __ U.S. __, 122 S. Ct. 2045, 153 L. Ed.2d 82 (2002) (under the Americans with Disabilities Act, an employer may refuse to hire applicant where applicant's performance on job would endanger his own health, owing to a disability). Here, Drs. Ziem, Balbus, and Vinitsky, all agreed that there was no guarantee that the appellant could successfully avoid a debilitating chemical exposure at her workplace even where the agency complied with her requested accommodations. Indeed, the record demonstrates that the appellant's symptoms could be triggered by incitants that are undetectable to the senses or yet undiscovered by the appellant or her physicians.... Thus, although the appellant's willingness to work is admirable, we find that the consequences resulting from an accidental exposure could prove irreversibly catastrophic to her health.

Moreover, the fact that the appellant is vulnerable to chemicals emitted from adjacent buildings, parking structures, construction sites, retail shops and other areas which cannot be completely contained, monitored, or regulated by the agency, demonstrates her susceptibility to harmful exposure even where her accommodations are met.

Therefore, in light of the breadth of the appellant's requested accommodations, the difficulty in preventing an accidental exposure, the dire consequences associated with such an exposure, and the unpredictable nature of her working environment, we find that the appellant has failed to demonstrate that she can safely perform the essential functions of her position. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(a)(6).

Finally, the record demonstrates that the appellant failed to satisfy her "burden of proving that there was a position the agency would have found and could have assigned her to if it had looked." Jackson, 79 M.S.P.R. at 53. Here, in an effort to satisfy her burden under Jackson, the appellant introduced thousands of 1999 IRS job vacancy listings for various positions at or below her current grade and/or level. However, all of these positions appear to be located in an office building or office complex and, as discussed above, the appellant's disability prevents her from safely working in this kind of environment. Moreover, the appellant has failed to provide any evidence to suggest that she was professionally qualified for these positions or that these positions could be accomplished on a full-time basis from home.... Thus, the appellant's discrimination claim fails because she did not satisfy "her burden of ultimately showing, before the Board," that there was a vacant funded position at or below her grade level to which she could have been reassigned. Jackson, 79 M.S.P.R. at 53.

In her response to the PFR, the appellant suggests that her requests for accommodation were not unreasonable because she did not require a building environment "completely free" of harmful incitants. Instead, she argues, with reference to her hearing testimony, that she requires only a "workspace" free of incitants. However, the overwhelming evidence contained in the record contradicts her contention and reveals that the appellant does, in fact, require a chemical-free "building" environment. Indeed, the appellant specifically testified, and her physicians agreed, that her brief exposure to the agency's New Carrolton, Bailey's Crossroads, and Crystal City building environments triggered dangerous symptoms that resulted in prolonged illnesses. Furthermore, the appellant repeatedly demanded that her accommodations extend not only to her office space but to "the elevator, lobby and any other environment [she] must come in contact with from [her] car to the work area."

Moreover, the appellant's suggestion that she can simply "avoid" a harmful exposure in an office environment is unconvincing in light of the fact that many of the offensive incitants she has described are undetectable to the senses. In fact, the appellant's medical history relating to various exposures demonstrates that she is incapable of avoiding certain hazards because the onset of her symptoms ultimately provides the only notice that a noxious chemical is present. In sum, we find that the appellant's disability discrimination claim fails because the appellant has failed to demonstrate that she is a "qualified" individual with handicaps as required by 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(a)(6).
