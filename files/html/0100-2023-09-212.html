3. Delay in Appeal Response

Strict reading of 5 CFR 1201.22(b) (2022) is that the appeal response must be filed within 20 days from the date of the Board's acknowledgment order. Prompt submission of the agency file ensures "that an appellant sees the agency's core evidence well in advance of the hearing, so that he can conduct any discovery he deems necessary in preparation for the hearing and prepare his own list of requested witnesses." Geller v. USPS, 80 MSPR 597, 601 (1999) (from the dissenting opinion of Member Slavet). If the Board were to treat violations of the rule governing submission of the agency's appeal response as it does violations of the rule governing timely filing of the appeal, a lot of agencies would lose appeals at the onset of the appellate process. The Board does not ordinarily impose default for late responses to appeals. The rule on late responses to acknowledgment orders is that a sanction should be imposed only when a party fails to exercise basic due diligence or when a party exhibits negligence or bad faith. A party is not entitled to sanctions against an agency for the untimely filing of submissions without a showing of prejudice to that party. Hey v. USPS, 57 MSPR 443, 448-49 (1993) (involving a one-day delay in responding to the acknowledgment order); see Geller v. USPS, 80 MSPR 597, 598 (1999) ("Even if we were to assume that the administrative judge committed error by allowing the agency to submit its file late, that error would be harmless and would not require a remand.").

In practice, if the agency response is not sent in on time, the Board often sends the agency a reminder containing a warning of the possibility of sanctions. However, no agency representative should assume that an untimely response will be accepted. As will be noted in the discussion on sanctions, agencies can lose appeals for failure to comply with the rules governing timely responses to appeals. If there is a delay, the agency should explain it with a request for an extension, or the agency should at least provide an explanation for the delay when the response is submitted. If there is reasonable cause for the delay that is beyond the control of the agency, including delay caused by long-distance mails, there is good cause for accepting an agency's late response and submissions. Wall v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 252 (1984); see Karapinka v. Dept. of Energy, 6 MSPR 124, 127, 6 MSPB 114 (1981) (granting an extension because of the agency representative's health crisis). If the agency representative receives the appeal and acknowledgment order right before the response is due, or after it is due, a telephone call to the AJ explaining the situation (with notice of the call to opposing counsel, and a request for consent to an extension) will likely result in the AJ's agreement, confirmed in writing, to provide an extension for the agency response. [Refer to decisions on sanctions discussed later in this chapter, "Failure to Respond to Appeal" and note also the cases concerning late petitions for review and late responses in Chapter 5, "Timeliness of PFR."]

Agencies occasionally delay submitting a response because the Board does not send a copy of the appeal to the correct agency representative. Agencies are supposed to inform the Board's regional offices of the addressees to whom notice is to be sent concerning appeals launched by agency employees or former employees. Some large organizations have had difficulty with this requirement, resulting in the delay of the receipt and processing of appeals. Agency personnel offices will do well to keep the Board's regional and field offices informed of changes in the personnel responsible for receiving and responding to appeals.
