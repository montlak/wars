i. Hasty or Late Decision

"The presiding official's decision not to cite the brief, nonetheless, does not mean that he did not consider it." Diaz v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 265, 266 (1984); see Wilson v. USDA, 28 MSPR 472, 476 (1985), citing and distinguishing Burum v. OPM, 26 MSPR 617, 620 (1985) (prejudice resulted from the AJ's error in issuing a decision prior to the close of the record; the appellant had submitted timely new evidence). In Wilson, the appellant alleged that the AJ stated at the onset of the hearing that he had already outlined his decision. The initial decision was issued on the same date the briefs were received at the regional office. The Board's response in Wilson was that "even if the presiding official erred in failing to consider the briefs, the error did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights because appellant has failed to show that arguments made in the brief would have warranted a different outcome in his appeal." The same result occurred in another case. An AJ's decision will not be overturned because it is quickly issued. Haste to the point of failing to consider an appellant's arguments was not error after the appellant's submissions on a timeliness issue were delivered to the Board on one day and on the next day the AJ issued his decision against the appellant. Herrmann v. USPS, 29 MSPR 213, 215 n.3 (1985).

No reversal was warranted when the initial decision was issued on a date before the date set for receipt of the appellant's response on a jurisdictional issue; the appellant failed to show that the error caused him any harm. Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 350, 352 (1988), citing Jackson v. DOD, 28 MSPR 463 (1985); see McNair v. EEOC, AT-1221-10-0888-W-1 (NP 6/1/2011) ("an administrative judge's timing in issuing the initial decision, without more, does not evidence 'a deep-seated favoritism or antagonism that would make fair judgment impossible.'"); Tan v. VA, 89 MSPR 15, 18 (2001) (issuance of an initial decision before the date allowed by the AJ for a jurisdictional submission was error, but not harmful error; the appellant's submission would not have affected the outcome of the case); Gordon v. Dept. of Army, 83 MSPR 545, 547-48 (1999) (error for the AJ to issue an initial decision before the appellant had an opportunity to respond to the summary of the telephone conference, butt the Board lacked IRA jurisdiction because the appellant did not make protected disclosures; error was harmless since "no different outcome would otherwise have been warranted"); Earel v. USPS, 47 MSPR 165, 168-69 (1991) (bias was not shown through the AJ's premature issuance of an addendum initial decision); Gala v. USPS, 38 MSPR 474, 477 (1988) (after the AJ issued the initial decision, involving a jurisdictional issue, two days before the record closed, the Board noted the error of the AJ and admitted and considered the appellant's submission); cf. Deleon v. USPS, 7 MSPR 640, 643, 7 MSPB 471 (1981) (no error when the AJ issued a decision before he received the hearing transcript).

On the opposing side of the ledger, a decision that arrives too slowly to suit the parties does not demonstrate harmful error. The party lodging the objection must show how he was harmed by the timing of the decision. Bowen v. DOJ, 38 MSPR 332, 336 (1988); see Kelliher v. Veneman, 313 F.3d 1270, 1277 (11th Cir. 2002) (delay by the Board in rendering its decision was not shown to have prejudiced the appellant's rights or affected the outcome of his claims); Bal v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0442-I-1 (NP 9/12/2016) ("Regarding the administrative judge's delay in processing the appeal from hearing to issuance of the initial decision, it is clear that he had access to the complete record, including the hearing transcript. We find, therefore, that the appellant has not shown that any delay constituted an adjudicatory error that prejudiced his substantive rights."); Carson v. VA, AT-1221-11-0062-B-2 (NP 9/22/2015) ("Although the appellant contends that the administrative judge waited more than 15 months after the hearing to issue an initial decision, and that it was 'clear that the administrative judge's memory had faded by the time of the initial decision, the appellant acknowledged the complexity of this case, which involved a hearing that occurred over 3 days with multiple witnesses. In any event, the appellant's speculation that the administrative judge's memory had faded is not reflected in the detailed, well-reasoned initial decision, and the appellant has otherwise shown no prejudice to his substantive rights.") (remanded on other grounds, Bal v. Dept. of Navy, 729 Fed. Appx. 923 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) (Judge Mayer dissenting)); McCollum v. VA, 75 MSPR 449, 462 (1997) ("Although the administrative judge did not issue his initial decision in this IRA appeal within the timeframe of the Board's practice, and we understand that the appellant suffered personal hardship while awaiting a decision in his appeal, he has failed to show that the administrative judge was biased against him or committed reversible error in this regard. See Sanborn v. Department of the Navy, 15 MSPR 553, 554 (1983).").

Of delay in issuance of an initial decision, Bennett v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-12-0183-I-3 (NP 1/20/2023), remarked:

An administrative judge's delay in issuing a ruling, such as an initial decision, does not, without more, constitute reversible error. See Keefer v. Department of Agriculture, 92 M.S.P.R. 476, ¶ 6 (2002); Fouquet v. Department of Agriculture, 82 M.S.P.R. 548, ¶¶ 7-9 (1999) (finding that the appellant was not prejudiced by administrative judge's 1-year delay in issuing an initial decision when there was no evidence that the administrative judge could not recall the details of testimony so as to make accurate credibility determinations); Paclibare v. Veterans Administration, 22 M.S.P.R. 320, 323 (1984) (finding no prejudice to the appellant's substantive rights from the issuance of an initial decision 8 months after the hearing), aff'd, 785 F.2d 322 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table). Here, the administrative judge referenced the testimony of multiple witnesses and made specific findings as to their demeanor and credibility. The testimony is this case consists of several volumes of hearing transcripts, which were available to and referenced in detail by the administrative judge. The appellant's mere speculation that the administrative judge must have forgotten some unspecified testimony does not establish a basis for reversing the initial decision.
