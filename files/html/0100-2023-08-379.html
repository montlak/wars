5 USC 3304(f)(1) provides:

Preference eligibles or veterans who have been separated from the armed forces under honorable conditions after 3 years or more of active service may not be denied the opportunity to compete for vacant positions for which the agency making the announcement will accept applications from individuals outside its own workforce under merit promotion procedures.

The distinction in the application of veterans preference between competitive examination and merit promotion selection was noted in Price v. HUD, 612 Fed. Appx. 596, 597, 599 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP):

Federal agencies can use two types of selection processes to fill vacancies. Joseph v. FTC, 505 F.3d 1380, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2007). One process is competitive examination that is open to non-agency employees. Id. In a competitive examination, the selecting official chooses from a list of qualified candidates, each given a numerical rating. Id. As a preferential treatment, the numerical ratings for veterans in competitive examinations are increased. Id. (citing 5 U.S.C. § 3309). The other selection process is merit promotion, which is typically limited to agency employees and certain other federal employees. Id. VEOA created a new preferential treatment for veterans to compete through the merit promotion process that would have been otherwise closed to them. See 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1); 5 C.F.R. § 335.106 ("Special selection procedures for certain veterans under merit promotion."). Id. However, the veterans' rating bonus available in competitive examinations does not apply in merit promotions. Joseph, 505 F.3d at 1382; see 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(3).

...

Under VEOA, "[a]n agency's simultaneous use of the competitive process and the merit promotion process is not of itself a violation of veterans preference." Dean v. Consumer Prod. Safety Comm'n, 548 F.3d 1370, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2008). VEOA provides that eligible veterans "may not be denied the opportunity to compete for vacant positions for which the agency...will accept applications from individuals outside its own workforce under merit promotion procedures." 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1). We have explained that VEOA guarantees veterans "only a right to apply and an opportunity to compete" for certain merit promotion vacancies. Joseph, 505 F.3d at 1383. In competing for merit promotions, VEOA does not provide veterans a numerical rating bonus that may have been available in competitive examinations. Id. VEOA also says "nothing about the basis upon which the agency could make its selection." Id.

Summarizing decisions concerning the impact of VEOA on merit promotion, Oram v. DOD, DC-3330-17-0098-I-1 (NP 9/8/2022), explained:

Veterans preference is inapplicable to promotion and reassignment of employees within agencies, Brown v. VA, 247 F.3d 1222, 1223-24 (Fed. Cir. 2001):

Petitioners argue that the Board improperly interpreted 38 U.S.C. § 4214(a), by holding that veterans do not receive preference "when seeking promotions or transfers." This statutory provision reads:

It is, therefore, the policy of the United States and the purpose of this section to promote the maximum employment and job advancement opportunities within the Federal Government for disabled veterans....

38 U.S.C. § 4214(a)(1) (1994). Petitioners argue that Congress intended to give veterans preference in promotions because the statute calls for the "maximum employment and job advancement opportunities."...

There can be no dispute that the granting of preference to veterans in certain aspects of public employment is an established principle of law. Crowley v. United States, 208 Ct. Cl. 415, 527 F.2d 1176, 1182 (1975). Indeed, under the Veterans' Preference Act of 1944, 58 Stat. 387 ("VPA"), veterans may be accorded special point and service credit preferences. However, veterans are not accorded limitless rights and benefits. Crowley, 527 F.2d at 1182. In Crowley, the Court of Claims declined to accord veterans' preference under the VPA stating, "[t]here is perhaps no principle more settled in this area of the law than that promotion and non-promotion of employees within a department or agency of Government is a matter of supervisory discretion not ordinarily subject to judicial review." Id. at 1184. In Champion, the Board also declined to accord veterans' preference under the VPA, and determined that "promotion and non-promotion, transfer and non-transfer of employees within a department of government is a matter of supervisory discretion and not ordinarily subject to judicial review." 21 MSPR at 562.

Courts also have determined that, under the VPA, veterans' preference "does not apply to an employee's transfer or other intra-agency movement." Bates v. Kanyon, 97 F.3d 1464, 1464 (10th Cir. 1996); see also Glenn v. United States Postal Serv., 939 F.2d 1516, 1523 (11th Cir. 1991) ("[V]eterans' preference only applies to initial employment, not to movement of an incumbent employee from one job to another within an agency."); Stephens v. Coleman, 712 F. Supp. 1571, 1581-82 (N.D. Ga. 1989) ("[N]either the [VPA] nor the regulations promulgated thereunder accord veterans preferential treatment in promotions."). We affirm the proposition established in Crowley, namely, that veterans are not accorded any preference under the VPA when seeking promotion or intraagency transfers.

Moreover, reliance on 38 U.S.C. § 4214(a) does not salvage petitioners' argument. This statutory provision was enacted as part of the Vietnam Era Veterans' Readjustment Assistance Act of 1974, 88 Stat. 1593 ("VEVRAA"). Congress originally enacted VEVRAA to guarantee the rights of employees called into military service to return to their old jobs, and to require certain employers to take affirmative action to provide employment opportunities for veterans of the Vietnam War era. 38 U.S.C. § 4214. It is true that VEVRAA describes the policy of the United States as promoting veterans' "employment and job advancement opportunities within the federal government." 38 U.S.C. § 4214(a)(1). However, VEVRAA, like the VPA, accords veterans' preference only for initial employment. Congress specifically limited the preferences provided by this provision to "appointments." 38 U.S.C. § 4214(b)(1). Indeed, the agency regulations enacted pursuant to this section deal exclusively with "appointments." 5 CFR § 307. "The scope of veterans' preference cannot be enlarged by the fiction of treating within-agency movement as initial employment." Glenn, 939 F.2d at 1523. The general statement of purpose recited in 38 U.S.C. § 4214(a)(1) does not persuade us otherwise.

Neither the VPA nor VEVRAA accord preference for promotions and intra-agency transfers....

Cruz v. DHS, 98 MSPR 492, 495-96 ¶¶ 6-9 (2005), described the relation between veterans preference and merit promotion and reassignments:

[T]he appellant claimed below that the agency violated his rights to veterans' preference under VEOA when it failed to select him for promotion. An AJ may dismiss a VEOA claim for lack of jurisdiction only when an appellant fails to meet one or more of VEOA's jurisdictional requirements. See Waddell v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 411 ¶ 9 (2003). To establish Board jurisdiction over an appeal brought under the VEOA, an appellant must (1) show that he exhausted his remedy with the Department of Labor (DOL) and (2) make nonfrivolous allegations that (i) he is a preference eligible within the meaning of the VEOA, (ii) the actions at issue took place on or after the October 30, 1998 enactment date of the VEOA, and (iii) the agency violated his rights under a statute or regulation relating to veterans' preference. Abrahamsen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 94 M.S.P.R. 377 ¶¶ 6, 8 (2003); see 5 U.S.C. § 3330a. However, an appellant need not state a claim upon which relief can be granted for the Board to have jurisdiction over a VEOA claim. See Wooten v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 96 M.S.P.R. 671 ¶¶ 6, 11 (2004); Waddell, 94 M.S.P.R. 411 ¶ 9; Abell v. Department of the Navy, 92 M.S.P.R. 397 ¶ 5 (2002), aff'd, 343 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2003), and modified on other grounds, Abrahamsen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 94 M.S.P.R. 377 (2003).

In this case, it is undisputed that the appellant is a preference eligible who alleged that the agency violated his veterans' preference rights under VEOA by denying him a promotion after VEOA's enactment date of October 30, 1998, and that he had exhausted the DOL complaint procedure. The only remaining jurisdictional question is whether the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation that his statutory or regulatory veterans' preference rights were violated. In this inquiry, the appellant's claims of violation of his veterans' preference should be liberally construed. See Abrahamsen, 94 M.S.P.R. 377 ¶ 9; Young v. Federal Mediation Conciliation Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 99 ¶¶ 6-7 (2002), aff'd, 66 Fed. Appx. 858 (Fed. Cir. 2003).

In his response to the order to show cause, the appellant argued that the challenged promotion actions were subject to veterans' preference because the agency's vacancy announcements stated that veterans' preference would be considered in the selection. In particular, the appellant stated that the application for Maintenance Mechanic Leader asked if the applicant was in the military, cautioned that an inaccurate claim for veterans' preference could cause delay in processing the application, asked for the percentage points of preference that the veteran claimed, inquired if the preference was based on a campaign badge or expeditionary medal, and asked for the character of the veteran's discharge. Based on these assertions, we find that the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation that his veterans' preference rights were violated. Accordingly, the appellant met all of the jurisdictional requirements for raising a VEOA claim, and the AJ erred in dismissing this claim on jurisdictional grounds.

Nevertheless, we still dismiss the appellant's VEOA claim for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. In appraising the sufficiency of an appeal, the Board follows the accepted rule that an action should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the appellant can prove no set of facts in support of his claim which would entitle him to relief. Young, 93 M.S.P.R. 99 ¶ 5. In this case, the record establishes that the vacancy announcements at issue were merit promotions, not subject to veterans' preference, and the agency has presented undisputed evidence that the reference to veterans' preference in the announcements was inapplicable, surplus language. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Board have held that veterans usually are not entitled to any preference when seeking promotions or intra-agency transfers. See Brown v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 247 F.3d 1222, 1224-25 (Fed. Cir. 2001); Hamner v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 93 M.S.P.R. 84 ¶ 20 (2002). Furthermore, the Federal Circuit and the Board have found that when an appellant is not entitled to a veterans' preference, the appeals should be dismissed as a failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. Brown, 247 F.3d at 1225; Young, 93 M.S.P.R. 99 ¶ 8-9. Accordingly, we dismiss the appellant's VEOA claim for failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted.

Under Gingery v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-3330-11-0126-I-1 (NP 11/16/2011), VEOA does not apply when an individual is placed into a position through reinstatement:

On review, the appellant challenges the administrative judge's finding that the agency acted within its discretion by reinstating a former agency employee, who had been removed from her position as a GS-11 Internal Revenue Agent (IRA), to the GS-06 Examination Technician (ET) position at issue in this appeal pursuant to a Board mediated settlement agreement of the former IRA's removal appeal. He contends, as he did below, that the record does not establish that the former IRA's appointment to the ET position was lawful because there is no evidence that she "ever passed or was legitimately excepted from competitive examination as required by 5 U.S.C. §§ 3304(b) & 3302(1) for appointment to federal service."

We find this argument unpersuasive. As the administrative judge noted in the initial decision, under 5 C.F.R. § 330.101 an agency has the discretion to fill a vacant position by any properly authorized method. See Joseph v. Federal Trade Commission, 103 M.S.P.R. 684, ¶ 11 (2006), aff'd, 505 F.3d 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2007). One such method is reinstatement, which is defined in 5 C.F.R. § 210.102(b)(15) as the "noncompetitive reemployment" of someone formerly employed in the competitive service who "had a competitive status or was serving probation when he was separated from the service." The Board has held that an agency may fill a position through reinstatement rather than competitive examination without violating VEOA. Sherwood v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 88 M.S.P.R. 208, ¶ 10 (2001).

Under 5 C.F.R. § 315.401(a), an agency may appoint by reinstatement to a competitive service position a person who previously was employed under career or career-conditional appointment. At the time she was removed from her IRA position in February 2010, the individual who filled the ET position pursuant to the settlement agreement occupied a position in the competitive service under a career or career-conditional appointment, making her eligible for reinstatement. Thus, we agree with the administrative judge that the agency acted within its discretion and did not violate the appellant's veterans' preference rights when it cancelled the external announcement and appointed the former IRA to the vacant ET position pursuant to a settlement agreement.

The appellant further argues on review that the former IRA was appointed to the ET position by reassignment under 5 C.F.R. § 335.102, not by reinstatement under 5 C.F.R. § 315.401. We need not resolve whether the former IRA's appointment was by reassignment or reinstatement, however, as reassignment is also a properly authorized method of filling a vacant position, and the Board has held that an agency does not violate VEOA when it fills a vacant position by reassignment. See Jones v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 385, ¶ 12 (2010) (finding no VEOA violation where an agency cancelled a certificate of eligibles and filled a vacancy by lateral transfer of an agency employee whose name did not appear on the certificate); see also Villamarzo v. Environmental Protection Agency, 92 M.S.P.R. 159, ¶ 6 (2002) (finding no VEOA violation where the agency cancelled a vacancy announcement and filled the vacant position by reassignment through a non-competitive appointment when an agency employee accepted a voluntary demotion in grade to fill the position).

We also reject the appellant's apparent contention that he is entitled to corrective action because the agency failed to show that the former IRA's initial appointment to the competitive service was lawful. The propriety of the former IRA's initial appointment to the competitive service is not at issue in this appeal. As the Board explained in Villamarzo, the matter at issue in a VEOA appeal is not whether a particular agency action is proper and should be sustained. 92 M.S.P.R. 159, ¶ 5. VEOA gives the Board no authority to adjudicate the merits of any agency action. Id. Instead, VEOA authorizes the Board to determine only whether an agency has, in connection with the action that is the subject of an appeal, violated a statutory or regulatory provision relating to veterans' preference. Id. The appellant has not shown that any statute or regulation relating to veterans' preference prevented the agency from cancelling the vacancy announcements and filling the ET position by appointing the former IRA to the position pursuant to the settlement agreement.
