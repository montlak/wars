broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 11: Promotions, Vacancies, and Appointments, Section C: Ranking, Rating, and Qualifications, Subsection 06: General and Nonrestrictive Selection Criteria

6. General and Nonrestrictive Selection Criteria

Although FLRA has not embraced the negotiability of specific selection criteria in crediting plans or similar arrangements, it has permitted negotiation of general procedural criteria. In AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 26 FLRA 650, 651-52, 653 (1987), the proposal was that the selecting official review the candidates on the list he received and make a selection based on fairness and equity. The proposal was negotiable:

The Authority has consistently held that general provisions requiring management to exercise its statutory rights under section 7106 in compliance with law are within the duty to bargain.... [T]he disputed language in this proposal concerning "fairness and equity" is contained in 5 U.S.C. § 2301(b)(2), which sets forth the merit system principles that are to govern all applicants and employees for employment in the Federal work force.... [W]e find that the proposal constitutes a general provision which merely requires management to exercise its statutory rights under section 7106 in compliance with law. Further, whether the disputed language is necessary from the Agency's point of view is not relevant to whether the proposal is within the statutory duty to bargain.

...There is nothing in the express language of this proposal or in the record in this case which indicates that an arbitrator in reviewing a selection action claimed to be unfair or inequitable would be empowered to act in any manner that is inconsistent with applicable law....

New York State Nurses Ass'n and VA Bronx Med. Ctr., 30 FLRA 706, 762 (1987), approved as negotiable Proposal 9, that management consider a number of criteria concerning promotion and "special advancement." The criteria were not exclusive; the officials who would determine the promotions, a Nursing Professional Standards Board, could add criteria and consider as many of the identified factors as it desired in making its recommendations. There was no requirement that management give specific weight to the proposed factors. Those factors included: supervisory evaluations illustrating employee contributions to patient services, consistent and progressive professional or administrative growth, increasing professional responsibility, effective communications, demonstrated potential for future professional development.

Although general criteria may be negotiated, indirect procedures that restrict criteria are nonnegotiable, as was a portion of Proposal 2 in NFFE Local 1437 and Army Armament Research, Dev. and Eng'g Ctr., Picatinny Arsenal, 35 FLRA 1052, 1057 (1990), that prevented management from assigning to a panel the task of establishing criteria or qualifications unless the civilian personnel office first established procedures and guidelines. The provision placed a precondition on the ability of management to assign a specific task to the panel.
