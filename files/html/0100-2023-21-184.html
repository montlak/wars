N. OVERPAYMENTS

Many overpayments are not contested as to the existence of the error leading to the overpayment. The question is whether the overpayment can be waived, in whole or in part or, as to the debt that cannot be waived, whether an adjustment should be made in the repayment schedule set by OPM. If the appellant does not request OPM to waive an overpayment, the AJ should address the timeliness, rather than the substance, of the waiver argument. See Conyers v. OPM, 40 MSPR 339, 341 (1989). If an overpayment is successfully challenged, the Board may direct OPM to repay the portion of the overpayment already collected as well as the balance claimed to be due to OPM. See Fitzpatrick v. OPM, 42 MSPR 396, 398 (1989). The Board's authority to address overpayments applies as well to underpayments and actions by OPM to correct those underpayments. See Clouse v. OPM, PH-0841-20-0146-I-2 (NP 2/21/2023) (Board determines it has authority, though not specifically identified in statute, to address OPMs schedule of 99 monthly payments to liquidate an underpayment; OPM was directed to pay the underpayment in a lump sum).

Defined, Murphy Consolidation v. OPM, DA-0841-16-0524-I-1 (NP 3/29/2023):

...An overpayment debt under FERS occurs when an individual is paid benefits from the Civil Service Retirement Fund in the absence of entitlement or in excess of the amount to which that individual is properly entitled. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 845.102, 845.202, 845.203. Here, the appellants received benefits from the Civil Service Retirement Fund to which they were not entitled when OPM erroneously deposited annuity payments owed to another annuitant into their joint bank account. Consequently, they received an "overpayment" under FERS, and their arguments on review provide no basis to disturb the initial decision.

As with other claims, disputes over overpayments can be compromised between the object of OPM's attention and OPM. If there is an unambiguous offer from OPM to accept as complete payment for an alleged overpayment a sum less than originally demanded, or if a check is clearly offered as a compromise (with release language), and if that sum is paid, the MSPB will, on appeal from an overpayment claim, the Board will dismiss the appeal as moot. See Beasley v. OPM, 45 MSPR 333, 336 (1990).

The AJ should take an active role in overpayment cases to ensure the appellant understands what information needs to be produced. The AJ should request additional information whenever the information from the appellant concerning financial hardship is confusing or incomplete. Clinton v. OPM, 38 MSPR 221, 223 (1988). If OPM does not request clarifying information or information submitted by the appellant otherwise appears incorrect or unreasonable, then the AJ should request additional information from the appellant in order to make a finding on the existence of financial hardship or another OPM regulatory basis for waiver asserted by the appellant. Nixon v. OPM, 52 MSPR 672, 677 (1992); see Harris v. OPM, 55 MSPR 21, 23 (1992) ("A clear breakdown of OPM's payments to the appellant is necessary to complete a resolution of the appeal. In the absence of this information, a reasoned decision cannot be reached."). If the appellant's initial submission to the Board contains insufficient detail on the nature and extent of anticipated expenses, the AJ should give the individual an opportunity to supplement his evidence. Eaton v. OPM, 38 MSPR 216, 218 (1988). The Board may, or may not, remand a case to further develop overpayment information. See Woofter v. OPM, DE-0842-13-0191-I-1 (NP 7/11/2014) ("In some cases, where the appellant has been pro se, the Board has remanded the appeal to allow the appellant to produce more documentation regarding expenses. See, e.g., Starr, 81 M.S.P.R. 633, ¶ 7. Remand here is not appropriate. The appellant was represented throughout this appeal and had ample opportunity to place evidence in the record corroborating his claimed expenses. The appellant was on notice of the elements and burden of proof."). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Debts to Retirement Fund."]

The Board's jurisdiction extends to OPM efforts to collect overpayments to people receiving an annuity or benefit under the retirement system administered by OPM. The Board lacks jurisdiction to consider OPM recovery efforts as to a debt resulting from a payment made to an individual that appeared to be derived through an estate distribution. Whatever the status of the debt, collection was not against an annuitant or beneficiary. The debt could be resolved through other procedures for collection of money owed to the United States, but OPM's collection efforts were not subject to MSPB review. Miller v. OPM, 123 MSPR 68, 2015 MSPB 63 (2015).

Although the appellant's circumstances may not warrant waiver by OPM or the Board of an overpayment at the time of decision, circumstances of the appellant may change; and a later adjustment may be sought, Atchole v. OPM, DC-0845-16-0086-I-1 (NP 9/16/2016):

However, our decision in this matter does not preclude the appellant from exercising any right that he may have to make a mid-collection request to OPM for modification of the repayment schedule, compromise, suspension, or write-off, as provided for under any applicable law, rule, regulation, or OPM guideline. See 5 C.F.R. § 845.301 (stating that, when it has been determined that the recipient of an overpayment is ineligible for waiver, the individual nevertheless is entitled to an adjustment in the recovery schedule if he shows that it would cause him financial hardship to make payment at the rate scheduled); see also Hundley v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 632, ¶ 16 (1999) (finding that a change in an appellant's financial situation may be the basis for an application to OPM for a new determination regarding an adjustment in the repayment schedule); Martin v. Office of Personnel Management, 49 M.S.P.R. 134, 137 (1991) (finding that any effects of a future medical emergency could be addressed by a mid-collection request to OPM for lower payments, compromise, suspension, or write-off), aff'd, 960 F.2d 156 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

Alexander v. OPM, 114 MSPR 122, 124-26 ¶¶ 7-13, 2010 MSPB 104 (2010), decided that OPM may seek to recover from OWCP payment amounts that the appellant received as overpayments when the appellant was receiving both disability and workers compensation payments. See Martin v. OPM, 119 MSPR 188, 192 n.4, 2013 MSPB 13 (2013) ("We note that OPM's imposition of a repayment schedule, with deductions to be taken from the appellant's OWCP benefits, is a matter affecting the appellant's rights and interests under CSRS or FERS, as the case may be, and therefore lies within the Board's review authority. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8347(d)(1), 8461(e)(1); Alexander v. Office of Personnel Management, 114 M.S.P.R. 122, ¶¶ 11-12 (2010) (where appellant was receiving OWCP benefits from which his overpayment could be administratively offset, OPM's decision to set a repayment schedule was a matter affecting the appellant's rights or interests under CSRS).")
