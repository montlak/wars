d. Medical Certification, Credential, or Standard

If an employee's job depends on a medical certification and the employee is removed when the certification is revoked, the AJ should decide the propriety of the revocation. Cosby v. FAA, 30 MSPR 16, 19 (1986) (distinguishing Egan v. Dept. of Navy, 28 MSPR 509, 520-22 (1985) (security clearance)); see Paetow v. VA, 118 MSPR 462, 2012 MSPB 98 (2012) (evaluating conflicting evidence concerning fitness under the agency regulations of the appellant to occupy a position requiring a firearm); Justice v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 379, 390 (2001) (removal of an employee based on an agency determination that he was "permanently not fit for sea duty" due to a medical condition, citing Boulineau v. Dept. of Army, 57 MSPR 244, 247-48 (1993), for the proposition that agency may set forth in its regulations medical fitness standards for certain types of jobs, and may remove an employee for medical disqualification based on those regulations)); Baker v. FAA, 19 MSPR 370, 372-73 (1984) (failure to meet the medical requirements of an aviation safety inspector position led to removal from the position; the appellant failed to show that the vacancy he sought through the agency reassignment policy did not require a medical certificate). Inability to secure a psychological clearance justified an arbitrator's determination that the agency properly demoted a police officer whose ability to require a weapon--a condition of that position--depended on the clearance. Martin v. VA, 412 F.3d 1258 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

Siegert v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 684, 688-91 (1988) (also discussed in Chapter 4, "Collateral Estoppel"), preserved Board jurisdiction to review dismissal based on the failure of the appellant, a psychologist, to maintain clinical privileges reviewed by an agency credentials committee:

Agency regulations provide that clinical privileges are to be developed individually for a number of health care specialties, from physician to speech pathologist....

Standards for granting clinical privileges are developed by each clinical department or discipline. Further, individual clinical privileges may be granted and revoked biennially based on appraisals of clinical performance and professional conduct. Actions to deny clinical privileges are to be taken when a practitioner is found to be providing substandard care and when a practitioner's conduct requires action to protect the health or safety of patients, employees, or others....

Nor do we find the doctrine of collateral estoppel applicable here. The Board has identified three prerequisites for the application of the doctrine: (1) The issue must be identical to that involved in the prior action; and (2) the issue must have been actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination in the prior action must have been necessary to the resulting judgment. Payer v. Department of the Army, 19 MSPR 534 (1984).

...The Supreme Court has held that before a party can invoke the collateral estoppel doctrine, the legal matter raised in the subsequent proceeding must involve the same set of events or documents and the same "bundle of legal principles" that contributed to the rendering of the first judgment. Commissioner v. Sunnen, 333 U.S. 591, 599 (1948).

The Board finds that the doctrine of collateral estoppel cannot be applied in the appellant's situation. Even assuming that the matter raised before the Board invoked the same set of events or documents, the credentialing hearing committee was not applying the same "bundle of legal principles" as the Board. The hearing committee procedures allow only that the employee appearing before it has the right to consult counsel. This limited right to counsel changes the scope of the due process rights to present evidence, and to cross examine witnesses because of the limited role that counsel may play.

Further, there is no standard prescribed for the committee's ultimate determination. The Board adjudicates an adverse action...under the preponderance of evidence standard.... Although the hearing committee is encouraged to document its findings with reference to specifically identified incidents or situations, the regulations governing the committee do not prescribe that the committee weigh all of the evidence and assess it against a predetermined standard.

...

Additionally, the principles that operate to remove military determinations from Board review are not present here....

...

The rationale of the cases resulting in the finding that the Board lacks jurisdiction to review wholly military determinations is based on the military being a separate system from the civilian, with separate rules, regulations, procedures, and other considerations. This rationale is inapplicable in the appellant's case because determining the clinical privileges of civilians is not a wholly military matter. Further, the appellant was not subjected to the review of a military tribunal.

...

Further, an agency cannot through its own action confer or take away Board jurisdiction....
