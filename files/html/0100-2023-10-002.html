A. RIGHT TO HEARING

The right to a hearing exists even if the only issue is legal, Carew v. OPM, 34 MSPR 527, 528-29 (1987):

The Civil Service Reform Act (Act) and its legislative history indicate that Congress intended to grant appellants the right to a hearing even where there is no dispute of material facts.

[T]he Board [does] not have the authority to dispose of cases through summary judgment where an appellant has requested a hearing....

We note, however, that the right to a hearing is subject to the authority of the administrative judge to make evidentiary rulings under 5 CFR 1201.62(a), including the authority to exclude evidence determined to be irrelevant, immaterial, or unduly repetitious. Thus, where, as in this case, the administrative judge has determined that the dispositive issue is legal and that the facts sought to be presented by appellant are not material, appellant's right to a hearing would nevertheless entitle him to request a hearing for the purpose of presenting oral argument.

See Anderson v. OPM, 22 MSPR 201, 203 (1984) (right to hearing extends to a request that the right of recoupment be waived as to an annuity overpayment).

If there are no material facts in dispute, the appellant may find that he is limited to a hearing on the legal issues. Conway v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 502, 504-06 (1996), indirectly explained:

An appellant has a right to a hearing on the merits, upon his or her request, in a timely-filed appeal that is within the Board's jurisdiction. See 5 USC § 7701(a)(1); 5 CFR § 1201.24(d), (e). Whether the administrative judge must hold an evidentiary hearing, or may hold a hearing consisting of the presentation of legal argument, depends on whether there is a dispute of material fact. If the appellant does not show that there is a dispute of material fact after being given an opportunity to do so, the administrative judge may choose not to hold an evidentiary hearing. See Bommer v. Department of the Navy, 34 MSPR 543, 551-52 (1987); accord Beam v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 MSPR 54, 60 (1994). A dispute of fact is "material" if resolution of the dispute could affect the outcome of the case. Cf. Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, 477 U.S. 242, 248, 106 S. Ct. 2505, 2510, 91 L. Ed.2d 202 (1986) (discussing when a dispute of fact is "material" under Fed.R.Civ.P. 56); Murphy v. Department of Health & Human Services, 34 MSPR 534, 536 (1987) (previously unavailable evidence submitted for the first time on review is "material," and thus should be considered, if it warrants an outcome different from that reached in the initial decision).

Some cases do not present any factual disputes. For example, in an appeal from an unfavorable reconsideration decision on an application for a deferred annuity under 5 USC § 8338, the appellant bears the burden of proof. See 5 CFR § 1201.56(a)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)] . If in such a case the appellant does not dispute the findings of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) as to his type and dates of appointment(s), length of service, and other relevant facts, and disputes only OPM's conclusion that he is not entitled to benefits under the applicable law, then an administrative judge may appropriately decline to hold an evidentiary hearing, and entertain legal argument only.

In the present case, by contrast, from the outset the appellant has disputed the bona fides of the RIF as to him....

...

...The appellant alleged below that he "contest[ed] the bona fides of the RIF" because "neither the appellant's position nor other production shop planner positions have been eliminated and employees are still performing the duties of the positions." Three of the seven proposed witnesses identified in the appellant's prehearing submission...would have testified about planning work still being performed. The agency denied the appellant's allegations.

Plainly there is a dispute of fact. Moreover, the dispute is material. If, as the appellant alleged, he and others continued to perform the duties of their positions after those positions had been "abolished" in the RIF, then the RIF might not have been bona fide as to the appellant....

In canceling the hearing, the administrative judge found the appellant's evidence wanting, without having heard that evidence. We conclude that the administrative judge erred in denying the appellant an evidentiary hearing, because there is a dispute of material fact regarding the bona fides of the RIF as to the appellant....

An appellant is entitled to a hearing on each action appealed. An indefinite suspension and a removal relating to the same misconduct are separate actions. The appellant is entitled to separate hearings on each. See Moschner v. USPS, 7 MSPR 523, 527, 7 MSPB 375 (1981). If the actions are appealed at the same time, joinder is likely. If the appellant requests a hearing for an adverse action that is canceled and then reissued, the first hearing request will preserve the right to a hearing for the reissued adverse action if the appellant makes his intent reasonably apparent to the AJ, see Richardson v. TVA, 12 MSPR 319, 323 (1982), although it is better to file a separate, timely appeal from the reissued action to avoid possible litigation over whether a separate appeal should have been filed. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Consolidation"; Chapter 4, "Previous Determinations of Disputed Facts, Issues, Claims: Res Judicata, Collateral Estoppel, and Law of the Case--The Doctrines."]

The right to a hearing belongs to the appellant. See Hein v. OPM, 1 MSPR 410, 416, 1 MSPB 396 (1980) ("[I]t is the choice of [the appellant], rather than the agency involved, whether the case should be adjudicated on the documentary record or only after a hearing."). Dodd v. Dept. of Interior, 48 MSPR 582, 584 (1991), explained that:

Under 5 CFR 1201.45(b)(5), an administrative judge has the authority to order a hearing on his own initiative to: (1) resolve an important issue of credibility; (2) ensure that the record on significant issues is fully developed; or (3) otherwise ensure a fair and just adjudication of the case. The authority of an administrative judge to order a hearing, however, must be viewed in light of the congressional purpose underlying the right to a hearing. The hearing right exists for the benefit of the appellant.... A hearing with live witnesses should never be forced upon an employee who has forfeited or abandoned his right to a hearing....

See Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 87 MSPR 359, 366-67 ¶ 17 (2000) (the AJ "did not denigrate the agency's substantive rights by canceling the hearing because the agency has no right to request a hearing before the Board"); Grimes v. GSA, 84 MSPR 244, 248 ¶ 8 (1999) (even in a consolidated appeal that involves witnesses testifying in related cases, the appellant has the right to a decision that does not rely on hearing testimony); Kravitz v. OPM, 75 MSPR 44, 47 n.4 (1997) ("[A]n appellant may withdraw her statutory right to a hearing and an agency has no right to insist on a hearing."); Kirkpatrick v. Dept. of Interior, 49 MSPR 316, 318 (1991) (following Dodd, supra).

In Smisson v. Dept. of Air Force, 85 MSPR 427, 430 ¶ 7 (2000), without explaining the legal nuances of what occurred, the appellant, removed for excessive absences, appealed and requested a hearing. The AJ reversed the action without a hearing after determining from the record that the absences were excused by the agency and that the agency did not demonstrate that the absences were beyond the employee's control and extended beyond a reasonable time. The Board apparently determined that the agency's prehearing statement did not demonstrate that the witnesses identified by the agency would have made out the agency's case:

Regarding the agency's contention that it would have solicited hearing testimony from the appellant, an agency has no statutory right to a hearing. Hulett v. Department of the Navy, 27 MSPR 510, 512-13 (1985). Moreover, it is the agency's burden to establish the charge, and the agency may not require the appellant to submit evidence to prove its case. See Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 71 (1997) (the appellant was not required to submit medical evidence to assist the agency in proving its charge against her). The agency has not alleged or shown why it could not have submitted pertinent documentary evidence to establish its charge.

The result in Smisson was a judgment on the pleadings, a procedural device not explicitly approved by the Board. The decision serves to remind agency representatives of the need to carefully analyze elements of proof required and to ensure that the proof is supplied in the agency response to the appeal, as supplemented by the prehearing submissions or submissions that, if there is no hearing, are permitted before the close of the record.

For some time the MSPB was of the opinion that if an agency requested a hearing and the appellant did not, an AJ should exercise discretion in favor of a hearing because it normally provided a better basis for decision. Walker v. VA, 4 MSPR 78, 79 n.1, 4 MSPB 166 (1980). If the AJ denied an agency request for a hearing, that denial was to be reasoned, not the product of silence. Thompson v. Dept. of Transp., 9 MSPR 569, 573 (1982). Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (discussed at greater length in this chapter, "Adjudication After Waiver, Failure to Attend Hearing"), concluded that the hearing is the right of the appellant, not of the agency. If the appellant does not appear, the case is to be adjudicated on the basis of the record. The court left open the possibility that the agency could submit additional documents or affidavits if the opportunity to reply was given to the appellant. Whether a hearing could be granted to the agency under any circumstances was not treated by the court. Regulatory revisions in 1986 preserved the right of the agency to request a hearing, although the right was limited to a request. See 5 CFR 1201.25(b) (1986). The regulatory right of the agency to request a hearing was dropped in a 1989 regulatory revision. However, the AJ has discretion to order a hearing on his own initiative, and nothing prevents the agency from requesting the exercise of that initiative by a motion to the AJ. 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(5) (2022).
