2. Might Have/Would Have Analysis

Until 1986 the Board's regulation defined harmful error in terms of error that might have caused a different result. Board decisions, e.g., Parker and others, were often cast in terms of whether the absence of an error would have caused, or actually did cause, a different result. The Supreme Court addressed harmful error in a dispute over whether the rule applied to arbitrators in Cornelius v. Nutt, 105 S. Ct. 2882 (1985). After concluding that the Board's regulatory definition of the statutory harmful error rule was entitled to deference, the Court described the effect of the rule:

An employee who elects to appeal an agency disciplinary decision to the Board must prove that any procedural errors substantially prejudiced his rights by possibly affecting the agency's decision....

...

...If the agency violates those procedures with prejudice to the individual employee's rights, any resulting agency disciplinary decision will be reversed by the Board or by an arbitrator. Emphasis added.

The confusion among the "might have," "possibly," and "would have" interpretations of the previous regulatory rendition of the harmful error rule was eliminated--to a degree and for a time--in Mercer v. DHHS, 772 F.2d 856, 859-60 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (on remand from the court, 29 MSPR 387 (1985)). The employee, who worked for the Social Security Administration, was placed on enforced leave and then fired for making verbal threats toward his supervisor. The agency adverse action procedures called for an advisory arbitration process, meaning that at the employee's election, an arbitrator or grievance examiner could hear the dismissal charges before the agency made its final decision. This procedure is permitted under 5 USC 7513(c). The arbitrator or examiner would make recommendations after a hearing. These recommendations would be considered by the agency deciding official. SSA denied Mr. Mercer his pretermination hearing because it alleged that he was a threat to the government, one basis that the agency could invoke under its regulations to avoid the pretermination process. MSPB ruled that the agency erred by denying the pretermination hearing, but it also determined that the error was not harmful because Mercer could present to the agency deciding official any argument or evidence that might have been produced to an arbitrator or grievance examiner. The Board reasoned that Mercer could not show how the error "would have" affected the outcome of the agency decision, and there the battle lines were drawn:

While the burden is on Mercer to show that the failure to grant him the predecision hearing was harmful to his defense so that a different result might have been reached, the burden is not on him to prove that the outcome of such a hearing would have changed the result, an impossibility. He only needed to show that it was an error which could have changed the result.

...

While the agency was not constitutionally required to provide Mercer with an additional predecision procedure, it had done so.... The Board held that the agency had wrongfully deprived Mercer of this right, but that this error was harmless.

However, even in the circumstances of such a serious procedural error as the denial of a predecision hearing, a federal employee must prove that his rights were substantially prejudiced by the procedural error so that the outcome was possibly thereby affected. In this case, Mercer introduced undisputed evidence at the hearing that the official who proposed his initial thirty-five day suspension advised him that he would be allowed to return to his position after the suspension, if he were found fit for duty. Due to an agency organizational change occurring during Mercer's suspension period, a new official became the proposing official and issued the notice cancelling the proposed suspension and proposing Mercer's removal. This evidence of differing views within the agency as to the penalty to be imposed upon Mercer clearly demonstrates that, had Mercer been afforded a predecision hearing or an advisory arbitration, the outcome could have been different. Mercer was afforded neither benefit.

Although the government argues that Mercer's exercise of his right to an oral reply before the presiding official rendered harmless the denial of the additional predecision rights, we cannot agree. A person has a better and perhaps dispositive chance of successfully contesting termination of benefits before, not after, the benefits are terminated. See Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 254 (1970).

For these reasons, we reverse that part of the MSPB's decision holding that Mercer failed to show the procedural error harmed him.

This was too much for the Board. MSPB amended its harmful error definition on July 10, 1986, through Federal Register Notice, 51 FR 25146. The regulatory change, one of many changes in the Board regulations, was offered, according to the Federal Register summary, to integrate prior pilot programs and piecemeal changes, to improve organization and style, as well as to enhance public awareness of the Board's practices. The definition of harmful error at 5 CFR 1201.56 was changed, but the Federal Register comments did not offer a reason for the change. We can surmise, however, that the Board was not anxious to allow procedural error to vitiate agency decisions. The Board amended its regulations after Mercer. 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(3) (1983) defined harmful error, pre-Mercer:

Error by the agency in the application of its procedures which, in the absence or cure of the error, might have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different than the one reached. The burden is upon the appellant to show that based upon the record as a whole the error was harmful, i.e., caused substantial harm or prejudice to his/her rights.

The 1987 regulation redefined harmful error:

Error by the agency in the application of its procedures which, in the absence or cure of the error, would have [been] likely to cause the agency to reach a conclusion different than the one reached. The burden is upon the appellant to show the error was harmful, i.e., caused substantial harm or prejudice to his/her rights.

The harmful error rule was again changed on December 29, 1989, through notice at 54 FR 53500. The 1989 CFR defined harmful error, 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(3) (2014):

Error by the agency in the application of its procedures that is likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. The burden is upon the appellant to show that the error was harmful, i.e., that it caused substantial harm or prejudice to his or her rights.

That regulation is now found in the same form as in its 1989 version at 5 CFR 1201.4(r) (2022).

The Board made the change without explanation in its rulemaking notice, 54 FR 28632 (July 6, 1989), but MSPB received some comments on the change. In the final rulemaking notice, MSPB noted that it "did not adopt suggestions" for "revising the definition of harmful error "by changing 'would' to 'could.'" 59 FR at 53501.

The Board's definition altered the range of probability from the original (1979-80) "might have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different than the one reached," to the 1986 "would have [been] likely to cause the agency to reach a conclusion different than the one reached," to the current "is likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached."

The Federal Circuit set aside another removal in 1987 based on a Mercer analysis. See Boddie v. Dept. of Navy, 827 F.2d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1987) [discussed in this chapter, "Changes in Proposing, Deciding Official"]. In 1988, the Board ventured to state that "would have" is not the same as "would have been likely," but the Board did not offer its reasoning. Loveland v. Dept. of Air Force, 37 MSPR 554, 557-58 (1988). In 2000, the Board reverted to its earlier analysis when it announced in Turner v. USPS, 85 MSPR 565, 567 ¶ 5 (2000), that "[i]n order to show harmful error, an appellant must prove that any procedural error substantially prejudiced his rights by possibly affecting the agency's decision." Turner confused matters by using the "possibly affecting" language quoted in passing in Stephen v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 672, 681 (1991), a decision that relied on cases arising under the regulations in effect prior to the Board's effort to clarify, and toughen, the harmful error analysis. Cf. Baker v. DOD, SF-0752-16-0128-I-1 (NP 11/22/2022) ("Any speculation on the appellant's behalf as to how the result before the agency might have been different is insufficient to establish harm. Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 682-83 (1991) (noting that an appellant must provide proof of actual harm resulting from an agency procedural error, which requires more than the possibility that the same evidence presented to the Board might have been viewed differently by the agency)."). Tracing the semantic regulatory evolution gets one only so far. It is better to take the regulation as it now stands and consider its application.
