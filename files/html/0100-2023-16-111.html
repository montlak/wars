1. Proof of Poor Performance

Slama v. DHHS, SF-531D-15-0266-I-4 (NP 8/24/2022), summarized analysis of a WIGI appeal:

A permanent General Schedule employee such as the appellant generally is entitled to receive a WIGI in the rate of basic pay if he is performing at an ALOC at the end of the statutory waiting period. 5 U.S.C. § 5335(a); see Oulianova v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, 120 M.S.P.R. 22, ¶ 6 (2013) (explaining the requirements for a WIGI in greater detail). An employee is performing at an ALOC if he received a rating of level 3, or "‘[f]ully successful' or equivalent," on his most recent rating of record. 5 C.F.R. § 531.404(a). In the case of the agency's rating system, a level 3 rating means "achieved expected results." Therefore, the agency must prove by substantial evidence that the appellant's performance warranted a rating of less than "achieved expected results." Afifi v. Department of the Interior, 33 M.S.P.R. 282, 284-85 (1987); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(b)(1)(i).

The appellant prevails in a WIGI case if the agency does not demonstrate the failure of the employee to perform at an acceptable level of competence. The ALOC is defined at 5 CFR 531.404(a) (2022) to mean a fully successful performance of the employee's duties and responsibilities. This determination is made on the basis of a current or updated performance rating for an employee covered by an appraisal system. 5 CFR 531.409(b) (2022). It is not necessary for the employee to prove that he was performing at the acceptable level to win the appeal. The burden of proof is on the employer to show that the employee is not performing at an acceptable level of competence. See Carpenito v. VA, 19 MSPR 138, 140 (1984) (agency decision was reversed when agency evidence did not specifically address the applicable performance standard nor state how often the appellant failed to meet those standards); Zaph v. Federal Maritime Comm'n, 6 MSPR 619, 623 (1981) ("Contrary to the contention of the agency, the burden of proof with regard to a negative reconsideration decision appealed to the Board remains with the agency under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)."); Degani v. Dept. of Air Force, 1 MSPR 671, 676, 2 MSPB 81 (1980) ("[i]f the evidence does not support the agency's negative determination the Board may not sustain the action. To require the Board's presiding official to make an affirmative determination that the employee was performing at an acceptable level of competence...would violate the statutory requirements (5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)) that places the burden of proof on the agency."). Without the less than fully successful performance rating, the agency cannot deny the WIGI. See Ahuruonye v. Dept. of Interior, DC-531D-14-0587-B-1 (NP 7/15/2016) ("Similarly, the reason the agency's decision denying the appellant's WIGI to step 2 was reversed was not that his performance warranted a WIGI, but rather the administrative judge's finding that the agency failed to provide him a rating of record before the end of the appraisal period, a requirement for denying a WIGI. Ahuruonye v. Department of the Interior, MSPB Docket No. DC-531D-13-1273-I-1, Initial Decision (Feb. 28, 2014)."). But see Bowden v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 662, 666-69 (1993) (Board followed OPM guidance that placement of the appellant on a PIP demonstrated a lack of successful performance without regard to the prior rating at a successful level), post.

If proof demonstrates that the employee was performing at a minimally acceptable level, the WIGI is properly denied: the minimal performance level is sufficient only to retain employment; performance at the full performance level is required for a WIGI. Chesis v. Dept. of Treasury, 20 MSPR 383, 386-87 (1984); see 5 CFR 531.403 (2022). The AJ should consider all relevant evidence in an ALOC case, including claims of improper agency coercion and harassment. See Wells v. DHEW, 3 MSPR 520, 521 (1980); cf. Tripathi v. NASA, DC-0432-14-0612-I-1 (NP 7/28/2016) ("Here, the agency denied the appellant's WIGI on the same day that it issued the decision to remove him for unacceptable performance....Because the agency has proven that the appellant's performance was less than fully successful in this removal appeal, we find that it also has established by substantial evidence that the appellant was not entitled to receive a WIGI in his rate of basic pay, and we affirm the agency's action."). The AJ's review is not limited to the reconsideration record. Mattersdorf v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 456, 459 (1982) (citing Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPR 505, 518-20, 1 MSPB 489 (1980)).

An ALOC case requires proof of performance standards and proof of failure to meet those standards. There must be a correlation between the performance deficiencies and the standards used to evaluate the work. Carpenito v. VA, 19 MSPR 138, 140 (1984). If the WIGI is denied for less than an ALOC under fewer than all components of a performance standard for an element, the agency must present substantial evidence that performance was at less than the ALOC level for the element as a whole. Jones v. VA, 25 MSPR 328, 332 (1984). To meet its burden of showing that the appellant's overall performance was not at an acceptable level of competence, the agency need not prove each of the failures cited in its negative determination letter to sustain its action before the Board. Nordell v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 276, 285 (1983). If the ALOC decision depends on a measure of all of the work of the appellant, the failure of the agency to consider a substantial portion of the appellant's work will leave the ALOC decision unsupported by substantial evidence, Oulianova v. PBGC, 120 MSPR 22, 26-27 ¶¶ 9-10, 2013 MSPB 55 (2013):

PBGC Actuaries are rated on a 5-tier performance scale, with 5 being the highest and 1 being the lowest. This same scale applies at three levels of generality: the overall performance rating, the ratings on three objectives, and the rating on any specified activities that may be required to meet the objectives. The overall performance rating is derived from a weighted average of the employee's ratings on three objectives. The ratings on the objectives are, in turn, derived from an average of ratings on any applicable activities.

In this case, the PBGC assigned the appellant an overall rating of 2--"below expectations"--for FY 2010. PBGC derived this overall rating from ratings of 2, 3, and 3 on Objectives 1, 2, and 3 respectively. We find nothing incorrect in this calculation. However, we find no evidence that PBGC calculated the appellant's rating for Objective 1, Case Processing, based on numerical ratings for each of the five underlying activities, as it was required to do: (1) performs early trusteeship actuarial activities; (2) assists with valuation database construction; (3) completes actuarial case report; (4) prepares closeout package; and (5) participates in other actuarial duties. Rather, it appears that PBGC based its rating solely on activities 3 and 4 and only with respect to some of the cases that the appellant worked on during the appraisal period. We acknowledge that, if an employee's work plan does not project that she will complete a certain type of activity during the rating period, then that activity will not figure into the calculation. However, the work plans that PBGC submitted indicate that the appellant was assigned projects covering all five activities and covering more cases than PBGC discussed in its final performance review and its decisions to deny the appellant's WIGI. Moreover, PBGC has established a specific mathematical formula for rating its Actuaries on Objective 1. Nothing in the record suggests that PBGC even attempted to employ this formula in arriving at its rating much less ascertain the variables that are supposed to figure into it. Rather, PBGC appears to have based its rating for Objective 1 on a vague notion supported by anecdotal examples that the appellant needs to be more accurate and punctual with her work. Under the circumstances, we find that PBGC has failed to provide substantial evidence supporting its evaluation of the appellant's performance on Objective 1. See Carpentino v. Veterans Administration, 19 M.S.P.R. 138, 140-41 (1984) (the performance standards had specific objective numerical standards, and the agency failed to link the appellant's performance deficiencies to these numerical standards; it therefore failed to support its reconsideration decision by substantial evidence). Consequently, PBGC has failed to provide substantial evidence to support the appellant's overall performance rating for FY 2010 and its decision to deny her WIGI.
