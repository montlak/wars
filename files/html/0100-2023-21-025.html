2. Disability Incurred While in the Federal Service

To qualify for disability retirement, the disability must be incurred (or aggravated) during federal service, not necessarily in the last job occupied in the federal service. Davis v. OPM, 64 MSPR 1, 3-4 (1994) (CSRS), involved an individual who moved through several jobs as his medical difficulties became more disabling and as the agency attempted to accommodate him:

To qualify for disability retirement under the Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS), an employee must demonstrate that he has completed 5 years of civilian service, that he is unable to render useful and efficient service in his position because of disease or injury, and that he is not qualified for reassignment to a vacant position.[13] Under OPM's regulations, an appellant must show

(1) A deficiency in service with respect to performance, conduct or attendance...; (2) a medical condition which is defined as a disease or injury; (3) a relationship between the service deficiency and the medical condition such that the medical condition has caused the service deficiency; (4) the duration of the medical condition, past and expected, and a showing that the condition, in all probability, will continue for at least a year; (5) the appellant became disabled while serving under the CSRS; (6) the agency's inability to make reasonable accommodation to the employee's medical condition; and (7) the absence of another available position....

In the present case, it is undisputed that the appellant satisfies all of these conditions. In disallowing his application, OPM relies upon an additional, unenumerated condition for eligibility: The appellant must show he became disabled, not just while serving under the CSRS, but while serving in his current position under the CSRS. OPM offers no support for this criterion, and we have found none.

In adopting OPM's rationale, the administrative judge cites to Johnston v. Office of Personnel Management, 57 M.S.P.R. 590, 596 n.7 (1993), for the proposition that where an employee enters a position with a pre-existing illness, he can still qualify for disability upon a showing that his service deficiency was caused by the subsequent "progression" of the disease. Johnston, in turn, cites to the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM), which provides that an application for disability may be based on a disease existing

at the time of an employee's entry into the position from which retirement is sought, provided the employee was able to perform successfully at that time, and has subsequently become disabled as a result of progression of the disease.

Because there was no showing in the present case that the appellant's condition worsened after his appointment to the Electrician position on April 6, 1991, but only that the agency was no longer able to accommodate this condition, the administrative judge concluded that the appellant was ineligible for disability retirement.

Johnston, however, involved the question of whether an employee's medical condition had existed prior to the time he entered federal service. Johnston, 57 M.S.P.R. at 596. It is undisputed that the appellant here was injured while serving under the CSRS, and furthermore, that he has completed at least 5 years of civilian service as required under 5 U.S.C. § 8337(a). Accordingly, we conclude that the appellant was not required to show that he became disabled in the particular federal position from which he sought to retire. In this regard, we note that OPM's reasoning would lead to an absurd result in other cases. For example, if an agency attempted to accommodate a disabled employee through reassignment to another position and the employee was unable to perform the duties of the new position, we would have to conclude under OPM's reasoning that the employee was not eligible for a disability retirement annuity. We find no support in the statute for reaching such a conclusion.

The disability must occur during federal employment, Delgado v. OPM, 21 MSPR 453, 455-56 (1984):

In order to establish a prima facie case of disability, the appellant must present documentation that specifically demonstrates his inability to provide useful and efficient service in his position because of a medical condition. 5 C.F.R. § 831.502 (b). Here, the appellant was unable to show that his failure to provide such service, up to the time he was terminated on account of that failure, was caused by a medical condition.

The appellant may not be awarded disability retirement on the basis of a medical condition which disables him from performing the duties of a position from which he was separated for cause prior to the onset of the condition. As noted in our decision in Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPB 353, 354 n.27 (1981), in order to be eligible for a disability retirement annuity, an employee must, while employed subject to the retirement system, have become totally disabled for useful and efficient service in his position. Because the appellant did not suffer from a disabling medical condition while employed subject to the retirement system, he may not be awarded disability retirement benefits for a subsequently incurred disability. Further, while the appellant, in his petition for appeal, did not reassert his previous medical conditions as bases for his claimed disability, we have reviewed the remaining evidence of record and find no reason to disturb the agency's determination that it is insufficient to prove disability.

See Schwaier v. OPM, 61 MSPR 49, 52 (1994) (remanding to determine if the appellant was disabled when he was last employed by the agency: "The administrative judge also based his disability finding on medical conditions which were diagnosed after the appellant's disability application, without proof that they predated his removal, and on a surgery which was performed over a year afterward.").

Under FERS, a former employee who becomes disabled after separation from service is not entitled to a disability retirement benefit. Hardy v. OPM, 98 MSPR 323, 326 ¶ 11 (2005) (citing 5 CFR § 844.103(a)(2); Schwaier v. OPM, 61 MSPR 49, 52 (1994)); see Wall v. OPM, 111 MSPR 122, 128 ¶ 15, 2009 MSPB 57 (2009) ("The evidence does not substantiate the existence of a pre-termination disability that had a causal relationship to the appellant's AWOL or inability to master the duties of his job. Hardy, 98 M.S.P.R. 323, ¶ 15; Farragon v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 M.S.P.R. 63, 71 (1991).") (vacated and remanded on other grounds, Wall v. OPM, 348 Fed. Appx. 576 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), denial of disability application affirmed on remand, Wall v. OPM, 116 MSPR 188, 2010 MSPB 178 (2010), denial affirmed following remand, Wall v. OPM, No. 2011-3006 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP)).
