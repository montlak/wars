e. Return to Work With Changed Duties

If an agency banishes an employee from the workplace with an undue disruption notification, the agency may later place the employee into a duty status of its choice without an additional undue disruption notice, even if the assigned position differs from that occupied by the employee before the action appealed, Heining v. GSA, 61 MSPR 539, 545-46 (1994):

In DeLaughter v. USPS, 55 MSPR 380, 382 (1992), rev'd, 3 F.3d 1522 (Fed. Cir. 1993), the Board found that the agency's statement that it would either maintain the appellant on paid administrative leave or return him to duty while its petition for review was pending implied that it would make a finding of undue disruption if it did not return him to work. The Board concluded that an agency's failure to notify the appellant and the administrative judge that the appellant's return or presence would be unduly disruptive constituted harmless error. See 55 MSPR at 383. On review, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that the only way the agency could prevent the appellant's return to work under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A) was to determine that his presence would be unduly disruptive. See 3 F.3d at 1524. It found that the Board, by excusing the agency's failure to make an unduly disruptive finding as harmless error, had failed to enforce this statutory interim relief obligation. See id.

We conclude that this case is readily distinguished from the situation addressed by the court in DeLaughter. The agency here notified the appellant and the administrative judge (as required by 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(2)) that the appellant's return to JA-4 would be unduly disruptive to the work environment there; it then placed her in a nonduty status as a GS-12 Auditor. And, after stating that it would seek another position for her within the agency, it did assign her to a GS-12 position within the agency but outside JA-4. Nothing in the court's decision in DeLaughter precludes an agency from first determining that an employee's return to a position at her former duty station would be disruptive, and later detailing her to another position. The court in fact emphasized the many benefits, both tangible and intangible, that may accrue to an employee who is actually present and working, instead of being away from the work place while on administrative leave. See DeLaughter, 3 F.3d at 1524. Forcing an agency that made an initial finding of undue disruption to maintain an employee on administrative leave during the entire period that the petition for review is pending would thus be contrary to "the measures Congress instituted to enforce its stated preference for returning an employee to work." See id. We therefore find that the agency, by detailing the appellant to a position outside JA-4 after determining that her return to JA-4 would be unduly disruptive, complied with the interim relief order and did not run afoul of the court's decision in DeLaughter.

There was some suggestion that changed duties required notice and justification to the Board, but the decision, Haebe v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 167, 175-76 ¶¶ 14-15 (1999) (rev'd on other grounds, Haebe v. DOJ, 288 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2002)), is not likely reliable precedent following the Board's 1999 regulatory revisions. Cf. Patterson v. Dept. of Air Force, 77 MSPR 557, 561 (1998) ("The fact that the agency later determined that the appellant could be returned to the workplace does not affect the sufficiency of its original undue disruption determination."). [Haebe is discussed earlier in this chapter, "Implicit Disruption Determination; Strong Overriding Interest."]
