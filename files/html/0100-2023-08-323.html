m. Charge to Leave

Johnson v. USPS, 85 MSPR 1, 2-4 ¶¶ 2-8 (1999), determined that USERRA covered an AWOL charge (and a suspension):

By notice dated January 20, 1999, the agency informed the appellant of its decision to suspend him for 7 days, based on a charge of unsatisfactory attendance and absence without leave (AWOL) for 54 hours between November 14, 1998, and January 15, 1999. On April 26, 1999, the appellant filed this appeal, asserting that the agency improperly charged him with 16 hours of AWOL on October 16 and 17, 1998, and suspended him for 7 days. He requested a hearing.

The administrative judge (AJ) informed the appellant that the Board may not have jurisdiction over a suspension of 14 days or less, and afforded him an opportunity to file evidence and argument to establish the Board's jurisdiction. In response, the appellant contended that he "requested military leave pay for reserve duty" but "[i]nstead...was given AWOL's for the[ ] dates[ ] 10/17-18/98, 12/11/98, 11/14-15/98, 1/14-15/99 and a subsequent 7-day suspension that included most of these dates." The agency moved to dismiss the appeal as untimely filed and for lack of jurisdiction, contending, among other things, that the suspension was never served.

The AJ dismissed the appeal, without holding a hearing. She found that the alleged 7-day suspension and the 54 hours of AWOL, individually or combined, were not matters that were appealable to the Board. She further found that, because the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appeal, she was without authority to consider the appellant's allegations of discrimination based on age, race and gender. In view of her jurisdictional determination, the AJ found it unnecessary to adjudicate the timeliness of the appeal.

The appellant has filed a petition for review, contending that the AJ erred by failing to consider whether the agency violated the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994, Pub. L. No. 103-353, 1994 USCCAN (108 Stat.) 3149 (USERRA). The agency has responded in opposition to his petition.

The AJ correctly found, and the appellant does not dispute on review, that a charge of AWOL or a suspension for less than 15 days is not generally appealable to the Board. 5 USC § 7512(2); Rutberg v. Occupational Safety & Health Review Commission, 78 MSPR 130, 135 (1998). The only issue before the Board is whether they are appealable under USERRA. One of the purposes of USERRA is to "prohibit discrimination against persons because of their service in the uniformed services." 38 USC § 4301(a)(3). To that end, the statute provides:

A person who is a member of, applies to be a member of, performs, has performed, applies to perform, or has an obligation to perform service in a uniformed service shall not be denied initial employment, reemployment, retention in employment, promotion, or any benefit of employment by an employer on the basis of that membership, application for membership, performance of service, application for service, or obligation.

38 USC § 4311(a); Yates v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 145 F.3d 1480, 1483 (Fed. Cir. 1998).

For purposes of USERRA, service as a Reservist in the armed forces qualifies as "service in the uniformed services." 38 USC § 4303(13), (16); see Yates, 145 F.3d at 1483. In addition, with exceptions not relevant here, the term "employer" includes "any Federal executive agency," 38 USC § 4303(4)(A)(ii), (6), and the Postal Service is considered a "Federal executive agency," 38 USC § 4303(5).

Coverage of the statute extends to "any person employed by an employer." 38 USC § 4303(3). The term "benefit of employment" is broadly defined to include "any advantage, profit, privilege, gain, status, account, or interest (other than wages or salary for work performed) that accrues by reason of an employment contract or agreement or an employer policy, plan, or practice." 38 USC § 4303(2); Yates, 145 F.3d at 1483. Thus, the term "benefit of employment" encompasses the denial of leave and the suspension involved, here. It is undisputed that the suspension was a "paper suspension" which was recorded in the appellant's personnel file but was never served. Nevertheless, such a suspension involves a "benefit of employment" because it is disciplinary in nature and affects the employee's status or interest.
