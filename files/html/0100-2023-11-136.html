h. Comptroller General or GAO Decision

Under Apple v. Dept. of Transp., 38 MSPR 229, 232-36 (1988), decisions of the Comptroller General on pay matters are given deference by the Board, if not collateral estoppel effect, if the appellant participated in those proceedings:

The dispositive issue in the appeal, which we proceed to consider below, is whether the Board should apply collateral estoppel to decisions of the Comptroller General.

(1) Requirements for the Doctrine of Collateral Estoppel to Apply.

In Graybill v. United States Postal Service, 782 F.2d 1567, 1571 (Fed. Cir. 1986), cert. denied, 107 S. Ct. 462 (1986), the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit found that the Board could apply the judicially-developed doctrine of collateral estoppel in appropriate cases. See also Chisholm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 656 F.2d 42 (3rd Cir. 1981). For the doctrine to apply, the following requirements must be met: (1) The issue to be resolved must be identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue must have been litigated in the prior action; and (3) the determination of the issue in the prior action must have been necessary to the resulting judgment. See Williams v. United States Postal Service, 35 MSPR 581, 584 (1987). In Thomas v. General Services Administration, 794 F.2d 661, 664 (Fed. Cir. 1986), the court considered, as a fourth requirement for application of the doctrine, whether the party against whom the doctrine is applied was fully represented in the prior action.

Moreover, even if all of the requirements for issue preclusion are present, the Board may nevertheless choose to refrain from applying the doctrine of collateral estoppel in order to avoid an injustice or a compromise of public policy. See Payer v. Department of the Army, 19 MSPR 534, 538 (1984). As noted in Williams, the Board has declined to apply the doctrine to a grievance proceeding, because the interests of the employee who was demoted as a result of a grievance brought by another employee were not represented in and he was not a party to the grievance proceeding, and to decisions of state unemployment appeals boards, because the Board is a federal tribunal. See Williams at 589.

(2) Comptroller General Decisions as Persuasive Guidance.

There is no statute that expressly provides for judicial review of Comptroller General decisions or that makes presentation of a claim to the Comptroller General a prerequisite to bringing a civil action asserting the claim. The Tucker Act, 28 USC 1346(a) (2) and 1491, gives the courts jurisdiction over nontort money claims against the United States that is concurrent with the Comptroller General's jurisdiction to settle such claims pursuant to 31 USC 3702 and 3711. The court exercises original, not appellate, jurisdiction when it adjudicates a money claim brought under the Tucker Act, although it may look to a Comptroller General decision on the same claim for guidance. See United States v. Virginia Electric and Power Company, 412 F. Supp. 165, 169-70 (E.D.N.C. 1976) (even though his opinion might only be advisory, the Comptroller General has special expertise in the area of the legality of particular types of payment that would be a valuable contribution to the court's efforts to adjudicate the merits of the case). Similarly, the Board has recognized that the decisions of the Comptroller General, like those of the Attorney General, may constitute "persuasive guidance." See Travaglini v. Department of Education, 18 MSPR 127, 137 (1983) (decisions of the Comptroller General and the Attorney General constitute persuasive guidance with respect to the consequences resulting from the appointment of an individual that is contrary to law).

However, the courts do not have Tucker Act jurisdiction over a pay claim that is within the jurisdiction of the Board. See McClary v. United States, 775 F.2d 280 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Cf. Harris v. United States, 841 F.2d 1097 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (no Tucker Act jurisdiction over a pay claim that is grievable under a collective bargaining agreement). Thus, a judicial remedy is not available to the appellant under the Tucker Act. Nor could she obtain judicial relief under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), which applies, in the absence of a statutory review procedure, only where "there is no other adequate remedy in a court...." 5 USC 704. Such a remedy is provided by the Board's jurisdiction over the appellant's claims and the judicial review provisions of the Civil Service Reform Act (CSRA). Cf. Carducci v. Regan, 714 F.2d 171 (D.C. Cir. 1983) (personnel actions within the remedial scheme of the CSRA are not subject to review under the APA).

A decision of the Comptroller General is not conclusive on the court in a Tucker Act suit asserting the same claim. Here, where the Board's jurisdiction precludes a Tucker Act remedy, and in the absence of evidence that other quasi-judicial agencies give res judicata or collateral estoppel effect to Comptroller General decisions, we see no reason to depart from the Board's precedents under which Comptroller General decisions are treated as "persuasive guidance."[3] See Travaglini v. Department of Education, 18 MSPR at 137.[4]

[3] It is true that when an agency acts in a judicial capacity and the parties have had an adequate opportunity to litigate before it, the agency's factfinding may be given preclusive effect in a subsequent proceeding involving the same claim, see Kremer v. Chemical Construction Co., 456 U.S. 461, 484 (1982); United States v. Utah Construction Co., 384 U.S. 394, 421-22 (1966). However, this rule involves a determination of whether the agency is acting in a judicial, as opposed to an executive, capacity. This distinction, that depends on whether the agency's procedures provide the essential elements of adjudication, is often a difficult one to draw. See 4 K. Davis, Administrative Law, 21.3 (2d ed. 1983).

In our view, it is doubtful whether the Comptroller General issues decisions in a judicial capacity. No hearing is held, and the procedures do not otherwise specifically provide an opportunity to rebut the evidence and argument of the opposing party. See Nasem v. Brown, 595 F.2d 801, 807 (D.C. Cir. 1979) (where the credibility of witnesses is involved, procedures that did not provide a right to present live testimony and to cross-examine the opposing party's witnesses were insufficient to support giving collateral estoppel effect to a decision of an administrative agency); Restatement (Second) of Judgments 83 (essential elements of adjudication include opportunity to rebut opposing party's evidence and arguments). Moreover, in Wheelabrator Corp. v. Chafee, 455 F.2d 1306 (D.C. Cir. 1971), the court noted that the Comptroller General, in deciding a bid protest on the basis of his authority to settle claims, "does not claim that his action operates as a legal or judicial determination of the rights of the parties." Id. at 1313.

[4] Although the Comptroller General's decisions respecting money claims are binding on the Executive Branch of the Government, see Pettit v. United States, 488 F.2d 1026, 1031 (Ct. Cl. 1973), disapproved on other grounds, United States v. Testan, 424 U.S. 392, (1976), motion denied, 425 U.S. 957 (1976) (since the Comptroller General's decision rejecting a money claim is binding on the Executive Branch, where, rightly or wrongly, the Comptroller General has rejected a claim, it would be futile for the claimant to press the claim before Executive agencies); and Armstrong v. United States, 29 Ct. Cl. 148 (1894) (disallowance of claim under predecessor to 31 USC 3702 was res judicata on Executive Department), we believe that the rule applies to the agencies against which claims are filed, not to the Board's adjudications of matters over which the Board and Comptroller General have concurrent jurisdiction.

(5) Application and Conclusion.

In the present case, the record shows that the appellant presented the same issues of retroactive promotion and premium pay to the Comptroller General; the issues were litigated before the Comptroller General; the Comptroller General's decisions on these issues were necessary to the final adjudication by that office on the appellant's claims; and the appellant was fully represented in her case before the Comptroller General.

The regional director declined to consider the issues of retroactive promotion and premium pay because the appellant had brought those issues to the Comptroller General and obtained a decision on them from that office.

Even though the application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel is not appropriate to decisions of the Comptroller General, we find that the factual findings in the Comptroller General's decision in this case are entitled to deference for the following reasons: (1) The deferential standard articulated in Williams has been met[6]; (2) the Comptroller General is mandated by statute to issue decisions involving claims against the Government, such as those brought by the appellant[7]; (3) nothing in the Comptroller General's regulations indicates that the appellant could not obtain a fair adjudication of her claims;[8] (4) the arguments she raises in her petition for review fail to demonstrate that she did not receive a full and fair opportunity to prove her claims or that the Comptroller General's decision is contrary to law, rule, or regulation; and (5) deferring to the Comptroller General's mission and expertise in this case would neither result in an injustice or compromise any public policy. See Williams at 591-92. Nor has any misinterpretation of law, rule or regulation been shown.

[6] Consistent with judicial and congressional policy favoring arbitration, and to provide consistency in the way civil service law is applied in such decisions, the Board held in Williams that it would defer to Postal Service arbitration decisions and will set them aside or modify them where the appellant demonstrates that the arbitrator erred in interpreting civil service law, rules, or regulation. See Williams, 35 MSPR at 591-92.

[7] Under 31 USC 3702 and 3711, the Comptroller General is authorized to settle all claims of or against the United States Government, except where final authority rests with an Executive agency by statute.

The Comptroller General is required under 31 USC 3529 to issue a decision at the request of a disbursing or certifying official or the head of an agency on a question involving a payment to be made by the disbursing official or agency head or a voucher presented to a certifying official for certification.

The Comptroller General has discretion to relieve agency certifying officers from liability for payments made in good faith and for value received by the United States, unless payment is specifically prohibited by statute. Although the agency official is not required to seek a ruling from the Comptroller General before disbursing funds, the only complete protection he has from personal responsibility for an erroneous payment is to request and follow the Comptroller General's decision. Thus, an agency certifying officer's reliance on the advice or instruction of an agency administrative or legal officer, instead of obtaining a decision of the Comptroller General, may not relieve him of responsibility for making an erroneous payment. See B-184145, 55 Op. Comp. Gen. 297 (1975).

[8] Regulations published by the General Accounting Office at 4 CFR 31.7 provide as follows:

Claims (against the United States) are settled on the basis of the facts as established by the Government agency concerned and by evidence submitted by the claimant. Settlements are founded on a determination of the legal liability of the United States under the factual situation involved as established by the written record. The burden is on claimants to establish the liability of the United States, and the claimants' right to payment. The settlement of claims is based upon the written record only.

A review process, established at 4 CFR 32.1, provides as follows:

Settlements made pursuant to 31 USC 3702 will be reviewed: (a) In the discretion of the Comptroller General upon the written application of: (1) A claimant whose claim has been settled or (2) the head of the department or Government established [sic] to which the claim or account relates, or (b) upon motion of the Comptroller General at any time.

Cf. Yanko v. United States, 869 F.3d 1328, 1335 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (deferring to Comptroller decisions on pay and leave).

Bannister v. GSA, 42 MSPR 362, 367-68 (1989), distinguished deference to and the collateral estoppel effect of a GAO opinion:

[W]e find no merit to the agency's argument that the administrative judge erred by failing to defer to the GAO decision as persuasive guidance. In Apple v. Department of Transportation, 38 MSPR 229, 235 (1988), the Board held that a decision of the Comptroller General, although not entitled to collateral estoppel effect, may, nevertheless, be given deference by the Board, provided it meets the deferential standard articulated in Williams v. United States Postal Service, 35 MSPR 581, 584 (1987) (the Board found that deferral to an arbitration award was appropriate where: (1) The issue to be resolved was identical to the one in the prior action; (2) the issue must have been litigated in the prior action; and (3) the determination in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment). In Apple, the Board noted that the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit in Thomas v. General Services Administration, 794 F.2d 661, 664 (Fed. Cir. 1986), recognized a fourth requirement: Whether the party against whom the doctrine is applied was fully represented in the prior action. See Apple, 38 MSPR at 334.

In this case, the record shows that the GAO decision was based upon a presumed fact that was in error: That OPM informed the appellant that his service was not creditable before he retired, and that he retired without informing the agency of this OPM finding. The record also indicates that the appellant was not represented before the GAO, and that GAO stated that it had no authority to determine whether administrative error accounted for an employee's loss of pay. In contrast, the Board clearly has such authority.
