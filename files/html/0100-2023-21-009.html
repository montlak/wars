2. When-Actually-Employed; Seasonal Employee; Leave Without Pay; Part-Time

An appellant's placement on LWOP does not affect her status as an employee subject to the CSRS. Kimbrough v. OPM, 63 MSPR 263, 265 (1994). LWOP extended to Postal Service employees whose removals are being contested does not provide retirement service credit (assuming the employee is not retroactively reinstated). See O'Neill v. OPM, 76 F.3d 363, 366 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ("O'Neill neither settled, successfully appealed, nor successfully grieved his removal, his 31 January 1992 date of separation remained intact and became final. Under these circumstances, when a postal employee unsuccessfully challenges his removal, we hold that time in LWOP status is irrelevant for purposes of determining creditable service under 5 U.S.C. § 8332(b) when the LWOP period occurs after the effective removal date. ...To hold otherwise would encourage 'solidly removed' employees, i.e., employees who fully expect their removals to be upheld, to file meritless appeals and grievances merely to increase their creditable service and thus their retirement annuities. An employee may be able to reap the benefit of remaining on the active rolls of the agency while contesting his removal if ultimately successful. O'Neill was not successful and cannot receive credit after 31 January 1992."); cf. Daniel v. OPM, 43 MSPR 599, 603 (1990) (citing "Knipple v. Office of Personnel Management, 41 MSPR 7, 10 (1989) (time spent in leave-without-pay status after separation is not creditable for purposes of completing the 10-year requirement for a survivor annuity benefit).").

Post v. OPM, 49 MSPR 172, 173-76 (1991) (aff'd, Post v. OPM, 978 F.2d 1227 (Fed. Cir. 1992)), considered how periods of inactivity are credited for retirement purposes (FERS) as to seasonal IRS employees who worked on a "while actually employed" (WAE) basis for about seven or eight months a year full time and then were laid off for lack of work for the remainder for the year:

The appellants were employed by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) on a seasonal, "while actually employed" (WAE) basis. Appellant Post received her seasonal appointment on February 3, 1967, and retired under FERS on August 31, 1988. Appellant Johnson received her seasonal appointment on January 10, 1966, and retired under FERS on March 31, 1989. As seasonal employees, the appellants worked full-time schedules of forty hours a week for an average of seven to eight months of each year, and were laid-off for lack of work during the remaining periods of time.

The appellants challenged the accuracy of OPM's annuity calculations, contending that OPM had erred by failing to give them retirement credit for those periods of time prior to October 1, 1980, when they were laid off for lack of work.[1] In reconsideration decisions dated March 12, 1990, and February 12, 1990, OPM denied the appellants' respective requests for additional service credit, finding that they were not entitled to credit for the periods of time prior to October 1, 1980, when they were not actually in a work status.[2]

[1] In order to provide uniform standards and more equitable treatment for all seasonal employees, OPM issued a directive, effective prospectively from October 1, 1980, instructing agencies to treat non-work time for seasonal employees as leave-without-pay for purposes of service credit. See Federal Personnel Manual Letter 296-75 (July 10, 1980). The appellants thus were eligible to receive retirement credit for periods of time spent in a non-work status after October 1, 1980.

[2] Prior to October 1, 1980, IRS seasonal employees were only given retirement credit for actual time worked.

The appellants are not entitled to retirement credit for time spent in a non-work status prior to October 1, 1980

Federal employees are entitled to retirement credit of up to six months in any calendar year for time spent in a non-work status. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 8332(b) and (f), 8411(b)(3). To receive credit for administratively-authorized periods of inactivity, the employee must establish that her appointment had been contemplated as constituting full-time employment. See Dove v. United States, 161 Ct. Cl. 768, 770-71, 783-84, 786 (Ct. Cl. 1963); Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 18 M.S.P.R. 670, 672-73 (1984); Edwards v. Office of Personnel Management, 11 M.S.P.R. 562, 567-68.

"Full-time service" means any actual service in which the employee is scheduled to work the number of hours and days required by the administrative work week for his or her grade or class (normally 40 hours). 5 C.F.R. § 842.402. "Intermittent service" is non-full-time service without a prearranged regularly scheduled tour of duty; only the actual days in a pay status are creditable for retirement purposes. See Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Supp. 831-1, Subch. S3-4d. The seasonal employees at IRS, however, do not fit neatly into either category because they have a prescheduled tour of duty when they are employed.

Prior to October 1, 1980, the IRS treated seasonal employees in the same manner as intermittent employees for purposes of calculating retirement credit. See August 2, 1965, "Memorandum of Understanding" between the IRS, the General Accounting Office, and the Civil Service Commission. After October 1, 1980, however, "[i]n order to provide equitable treatment for benefits purposes and consistency in documentation of personnel actions," OPM directed agencies who used "seasonal employees" to treat them the same as other full-time and part-time employees, and give them up to six months of service credit per calendar year for time spent in a non-work status.

In finding that the nature and duration of the appellants' work made their seasonal positions tantamount to full-time, the administrative judge relied upon the holdings in Dove, 161 Ct. Cl. at 784-86, Brown, 18 M.S.P.R. at 673 and Edwards, 11 M.S.P.R. at 568. Based upon the facts that were presented in those cases, the court and the Board, respectively, looking to substance over form, held that, although each employee's service had been classified as WAE service, it was, in reality, full-time employment that was creditable for civil service retirement. ...We find, however, that significant factual differences exist between those cases and the appellants' situations here.

Specifically, the employee in Dove "was on the job 90 percent (or more) of the time over a period of better than 6 years."Dove, 161 Ct. Cl. at 785. In Edwards, the employee worked at least 95 percent of the potential workdays each month for a period of 9 years. See Edwards, 17 M.S.P.R. at 567-68. In Brown, the employee worked a regular schedule of 7:30 A.M. to 4:30 P.M., Monday through Friday, for one and one-half years, from January 5, 1980, through July 31, 1981. See Brown, 18 M.S.P.R. at 673. Additionally, in Brown, the Board noted the agency's Civilian Personnel Officer's statement that Mr. Brown "was a full-time employee assigned to an intermittent position for reasons for expediency and flexibility."Id.

By contrast, the evidence of record in the instant case shows that the appellants' service was intermittent in nature and that they worked, on average, a total of less than nine consecutive months each year over the course of their employment. Thus, under the "substance-over-form" analysis of Dove, the appellants have not shown that they were full-time employees. See Bain v. Office of Personnel Management, 49 M.S.P.R. 307, 310-12 (1991). Accordingly, because the appellants were not employed in reality in a prescheduled, full-time status, we find that they are not entitled to up to six months of service credit per calendar year for periods of administratively-authorized inactivity for purposes of calculating their retirement annuities.

Lloyd v. OPM, 52 MSPR 211, 214-15 (1992), also addressed service credit for "WAE" employees, noting that the status of the employees may require resolution of factual issues concerning the nature, duration, and continuity of work, with implications relative to calculation of service credit:

Federal employees generally receive one day of retirement credit for each day between the date of appointment and the date of separation. Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Supp. 831-1, Subch. S3-1. Full-time retirement credit is not given for employment while serving in an intermittent or WAE status. Id. Such employees receive retirement credit only for the time that they actually work.

Intermittent or WAE service is defined as non-full-time employment without a prearranged, regularly scheduled tour of duty. Id. at S3-4. We note that a regularly scheduled tour of duty does not have to include the same days or hours of work during each pay period, provided that the times are scheduled in advance. In the Matter of Kenneth L. Nash, 57 Comp. Gen. 82, 84 (1977). Thus, employees with a regularly scheduled tour of duty are entitled to full-time retirement credit, even if they work less than full-time.

The Board has held that an employee's benefits are based on the actual nature of his service, not the characterization of an employee's service on an SF 50 form. See Bain v. Office of Personnel Management, 49 M.S.P.R. 307, 311 (1991); Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 18 M.S.P.R. 670, 673 (1984);Edwards v. Office of Personnel Management, 11 M.S.P.R. 562, 568 (1982); see also In the Matter of Kenneth L. Nash, 57 Comp. Gen. 82, 84 (1977).

Here, although the appellant's SF 50s indicate that he served in a WAE status, the appellant has provided contrary statements from his former superiors and supervisors, who arranged his work schedules and who had direct knowledge of his actual service during the specific employment period in dispute. These credible statements corroborate that the appellant worked a regularly scheduled tour of duty during the 1957-1961 service period, and we so find.

Fleming v. OPM, 55 MSPR 369, 372 (1992), elaborated on the WAE service credit analysis:

Individuals performing intermittent service are entitled to credit for 1 year of service for every 260 days worked. FPM Supp. 831-1, subchapter S3-4(d)(1) (Mar. 29, 1985). Credit for intermittent service, however, may not exceed the actual time between the beginning and ending dates of the period during which the intermittent service was performed. FPM Supp. 831-1, subchapter S3-4(d)(4). For example, an individual who performed more than 260 days of intermittent service in a single year is entitled to only one year of service credit. Id. The administrative judge did not take this limitation into account when she calculated the appellant's credit for WAE service, and the appellant was thereby given more credit for WAE service than he was due.

Credit for part-time work is authorized by 5 USC 8339(p):

(p)(1) In computing an annuity under this subchapter for an employee whose service includes service that was performed on a part-time basis--

(A) the average pay of the employee, to the extent that it includes pay for service performed in any position on a part-time basis, shall be determined by using the annual rate of basic pay that would be payable for full-time service in the position; and

(B) the benefit so computed shall then be multiplied by a fraction equal to the ratio which the employee's actual service, as determined by prorating an employee's total service to reflect the service that was performed on a part-time basis, bears to the total service that would be creditable for the employee if all of the service had been performed on a full-time basis.

(2) For the purpose of this subsection, employment on a part-time basis shall not be considered to include employment on a temporary or intermittent basis.

(3) In the administration of paragraph (1)--

(A) subparagraph (A) of such paragraph shall apply with respect to service performed before, on, or after April 7, 1986; and

(B) subparagraph (B) of such paragraph--

(i) shall apply with respect to that portion of any annuity which is attributable to service performed on or after April 7, 1986; and
