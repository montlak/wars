4. Response to PFR

Most cases involve a PFR and a response from the opposing party. If no response is filed, the Board adjudicates the PFR without the benefit of the response. A party is not defaulted if it does not reply to a PFR. See Hayes v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 613, 615 (1997), followed in Schoenrogge v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 216, 218-19 n.1 (1997) ("The Board's regulations do not require that a party respond to a petition for review and the Board has no authority to issue a default judgment against the agency.").

The regulation relating to the timing and content of the response was earlier reprinted. Page limits apply to the response. Under 5 CFR 1201.114 (2022):

(h) Length limitations. A petition for review, a cross petition for review, or a response to a petition for review, whether computer generated, typed, or handwritten, is limited to 30 pages or 7500 words, whichever is less. A reply to a response to a petition for review is limited to 15 pages or 3750 words, whichever is less. Computer generated and typed pleadings must use no less than 12 point typeface and 1-inch margins and must be double spaced and only use one side of a page. The length limitation is exclusive of any table of contents, table of authorities, attachments, and certificate of service. A request for leave to file a pleading that exceeds the limitations prescribed in this paragraph must be received by the Clerk of the Board at least 3 days before the filing deadline. Such requests must give the reasons for a waiver as well as the desired length of the pleading and are granted only in exceptional circumstances. The page and word limits set forth above are maximum limits. Parties are not expected or required to submit pleadings of the maximum length. Typically, a well-written petition for review is between 5 and 10 pages long.

For paper filers in the past, the Board sent out an acknowledgment of a petition for review. But the acknowledgment is not regulatory. The failure of a party to receive the acknowledgment does not excuse the untimely filing of a response to the PFR, Vaughn v. Dept. of Treasury, 119 MSPR 605, 610 ¶ 8, 2013 MSPB 50 (2013):

...Although the Clerk of the Board customarily issues a letter to the parties acknowledging the receipt of a petition for review, this letter is not required by law, rule, or regulation. It is issued as a courtesy to the parties and to facilitate an orderly adjudication on review. Therefore, nonreceipt of such a letter does not, by itself, constitute good cause for an untimely filing. Cf. McCurn v. Department of Defense, 119 M.S.P.R. 226 (2013) (an agency's failure to provide notice of appeal rights as required by regulation constitutes good cause for an untimely appeal). Under the Board's regulations, the petition for review is the document that triggers the response filing period, 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(e), and the agency does not allege that it experienced any delay in receiving that document. Nor does the agency allege that its representatives were unaware of the regulatory filing deadline at issue in the absence of the letter that they were expecting. Because the agency did not attempt to determine the status of the petition for review for almost two months after receiving it, we find that it has not shown good cause for the filing delay, and we have not considered its response to the petition for review.

Assuming that a response to a PFR is filed, the response must be timely. Calculating the deadline for a response to a PFR can be tricky, Riley v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 683, 687 (1992):

A response to a petition for review must be filed within 25 days after the date of service of the petition. 5 CFR § 1201.114(d). The Board's regulations define "date of service" as the date on which documents are served on other parties, and "service" as "the process of furnishing a copy of any pleading to Board officials or other parties." Cf. 5 CFR § 1201.4(i)-(j).... A party has completed the process of furnishing a copy of a pleading to another party when he has deposited a copy of the pleading in the mail. Cf. 5 CFR § 1201.4(l) (the date of filing by mail is determined by the postmark date); Fed.R.Civ.P. 5(b) (service by mail is complete upon mailing); see generally 4 Charles A. Wright & Arthur R. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure sec. 1148 (1969). Accordingly, a response to a petition for review must be filed within 25 days after the date on which the envelope containing the service copy of the petition has been postmarked.

The same considerations govern the determination of the proper date to file a cross-PFR. This time is governed by the service, not the receipt, of the PFR. Purdy v. Dept. of Air Force, 53 MSPR 693, 697-98 (1992); see Mozqueda v. DOD, 54 MSPR 152, 156 (1992) (a cross-PFR: the date of service is the date the document is served; service is furnishing a copy to party; a party completes service by depositing a pleading in the mail; a cross-PFR is to be filed within 25 days "after the date on which the envelope containing the service copy of the petition has been postmarked"). If one is issued, the Board's acknowledgment order notifying the opposing party of a PFR should be carefully reviewed. This order identifies the date of service of the PFR, and it may also state the date of receipt of the PFR. The date of service controls the date for the response to a PFR. An error in reading the order does not excuse a belated response. See Zuniga v. USPS, 56 MSPR 572, 574 n.1 (1993). The filing time for the response is determined as of the date the response is filed with the Board. It is immaterial, as long as the response is timely filed, that the copy sent to the opposing party is postmarked after the last date allowed for the response. See Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 590, 595 n.1 (1995).

The factors that excuse a late PFR may govern disposition of a late response to a PFR. A late response was excused because the agency did not know until the Board issued a belated notice of receipt by the Clerk that the Board would accept as a PFR a document the appellant styled as a "Request to Reopen and Reconsider Decision & Motion for Certification as Interlocutory Appeal." Shamblen v. USPS, 54 MSPR 55, 57 n.3 (1992). A late response was not excused when the agency misfiled the PFR. Vickers v. Dept. of Labor, 20 MSPR 114, 116 n.2 (1984). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Timeliness of PFR."]

The response to a PFR is to respond to issues raised in the PFR; it is not to raise new allegations of error; new allegations of error would be raised by a timely PFR or cross-PFR. Because the regulations prior to 2012 contemplated that a PFR will be complete when filed, McMonagle v. BATF, 20 MSPR 618, 619 n.1 (1984), the Board's regulations did not contemplate, and the Board would not ordinarily allow, filings by an appellant subsequent to the PFR, unless the appellant presented good cause for the late submission of the supplementary material. Lipinski v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 186, 189 (1987); see Watson v. Dept. of Treasury, 49 MSPR 237, 241 (1991) (granting appellant's motion, based on intervening holiday, difficulty in obtaining transcript, and absences of representative, to permit filing of supplemental memorandum of points and authorities a short time following the filing of his PFR); Alshouse v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 181, 182 n.2 (1984) (citing Bize v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 155, 166, 3 MSPB 261 (1980)); Leonard v. USPS, 3 MSPR 532, 532, 4 MSPB 50 (1980) (declining to consider a PFR supplement filed long after the PFR due date). Counsel should submit all argument either with a PFR or in response to a PFR. McBeen v. Dept. of Interior, 27 MSPR 207, 209 (1985).

Although Board regulations permit the Board to request additional briefing after a PFR is accepted for review, 5 CFR 1201.117(a)(3) (2022), the procedure is rarely used. The Board does not consider a substantially amended PFR without good cause for delay in the submission. Brotherton v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 26, 26 n.1 (1984) (amendment was inappropriate when the submission was significantly longer, added case cites, other references, and documents); see Van Patten-Steiger v. DHHS, 29 MSPR 324, 326 n.1 (1985) (declining to consider an amended PFR without a showing that it presented new and material evidence). The Board determined in Kent v. GSA, 56 MSPR 536, 540 (1993), that a supplemental PFR could be filed as long as it was timely relative to the deadline for the underlying PFR. See Lohf v. USPS, 72 MSPR 597, 599 n.1 (1996) (accepting two submissions supplementing the PFR when the submissions were filed before the close of the record on review); Wood v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 24, 27 n.* (1989) (allowing a supplement that corrected typographical errors); cf. Scott v. Dept. of Commerce, 27 MSPR 5, 6 n.* (1985) (Board declined to grant an extension permitting the filing of supplement to the PFR: "There are no provisions in the Board's regulations for supplemental submissions."). Amendment of typographical and nonsubstantive errors may be allowed. See Cohen v. GSA, 48 MSPR 451, 456 (1991) (accepting a corrected PFR, after the original PFR was filed on time, because it was "far easier to read"); Neubeiser v. FERC, 41 MSPR 653, 654 n.2 (1989) (permitting amendment of PFR to correct typographical errors); Laranang v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 233, 235 n.3 (1989) (permitting a PFR supplement only to the extent of correcting typographical errors). But see Sanders v. SSA, 114 MSPR 487, 490 n.1, 2010 MSPB 155 (2010) (rejecting, on agency objections to more than one unauthorized filing, what appeared to be a second set of PFR grammatical corrections).
