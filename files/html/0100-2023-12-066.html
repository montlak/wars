a. Jurisdictional Issue

Reopening may be proper when the Board's jurisdiction is implicated. Collins v. USPS, 88 MSPR 551, 553 ¶ 6 (2001) (evidence of a veterans preference status, a jurisdictional issue, would not be considered when submitted for the first time with a PFR (because it was not shown to be unavailable when the record closed below, but the evidence was considered when the Board reopened the case on its own motion); see Jackson v. USPS, 91 MSPR 506, 509 ¶ 5 (2002) (reopening to consider evidence of the appellant's preference eligibility submitted quickly after appellant received the evidence from Navy); Gussie v. USPS, 69 MSPR 654, 658 (1996) (implying some tolerance for delay if the issue leading to reopening is one the Board had jurisdiction over in the appeal in first place).

The Board may reopen on a jurisdictional point, even though its decision to reopen was made several months after the underlying decision it then set aside, Gussie v. USPS, 69 MSPR 654, 658-59 (1996):

Pursuant to 5 USC §§ 7701(e) and 1204(a), the decision of the Board in this appeal is final. See Welber v. USPS, 62 MSPR 98, 101 (1994). Nonetheless, the Board may, within its discretion, reopen a final decision under certain circumstances pursuant to 5 USC § 7701(e) and 5 CFR § 1201.117, so long as it exercises its discretion within a reasonable period of time. Welber, 62 MSPR at 101-02; Anderson v. Department of Transportation, 46 MSPR 341, 348-49 (1990), aff'd, 949 F.2d 404 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). The Board may reopen an appeal under 5 CFR § 1201.117 to reconsider the fundamental issue of its jurisdiction to hear the appeal. Flaherty v. USPS, 68 MSPR 36, 39 (1995); Rysavy v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 28 MSPR 263, 264-65 (1985); McKay v. Veterans Administration, 23 MSPR 244, 245-46 (1984). The reason for this is that the Board may not act where it lacks the statutory or regulatory authority to do so. See O'Clery v. US Postal Service, 67 MSPR 300, 302 (1995); Shaw v. Department of the Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 587-89 (1989). Further, Board jurisdiction is always at issue and may be raised by either party or sua sponte by an administrative judge or the Board at any time during a Board proceeding. See, e.g., Williams v. EEOC, 64 MSPR 436, 438 (1994); Stephen v. Dept. of the Air Force, 47 MSPR 672, 678 (1991).

Although an agency has no right to reconsideration, the Board reopened an appeal at the agency's request to consider the fundamental issue of its jurisdiction to hear the appeal. McKay v. VA, 23 MSPR 244 (1984). The Board reopened an appeal when the appellant introduced with her PFR evidence of jurisdiction, concerning her veterans preference status--evidence that was not new but was material. The Board noted "strong equities" in the appellant's favor and a "strong policy consideration that an appellant be given a hearing on the merits of [her] case." Carey v. USPS, 50 MSPR 359, 361 (1991) (citing Gonczi v. OPM, 27 MSPR 445, 448 (1985)). But see Adair v. USPS, 69 MSPR 431, 435 (1996) (Board declined to reopen an appeal to sort out the preference status of an employee following allegedly incorrect agency advice on her status when the Board could have offered no meaningful relief on the merits of the case: "Where the only relief that can be granted would be a correction of paperwork involving a reorganization and reassignment, such relief is not meaningful or effective."); Morris v. Dept. of Labor, 13 MSPR 442, 443 (1982) (declining to reopen to consider whether the appellant was a nonprobationer; the Board examined evidence anyway and determined the appellant was terminated during probation).

The Board reopened to consider an agency's protest that the appellant lacked the veterans' status necessary to sustain a prior favorable Board decision, Flaherty v. USPS, 68 MSPR 36, 38-39 (1995):

In its motion to reopen, the agency contends that the appellant is not a preference eligible, and that the Board should thus reopen this case, based on lack of jurisdiction. In his Response, the appellant contends that the Board lacks authority to "reopen a final Order." The appellant, however, confuses the absence of a regulatory provision for the filing of requests for reconsideration of final Board decisions, with the obligation of the Board to insure that it has jurisdiction over the action before it.[4] See O'Clery v. USPS, 67 MSPR 300, 302 (1995) (the Board may not act where it lacks statutory or regulatory authority).

[4] We acknowledge that an agency's authority to request reopening must be exercised within a reasonable period of time. See Welber v. USPS, 62 MSPR 98, 103 (1994). That time period, for any party making such a request, is generally "measured in weeks, not years." See Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 46 MSPR 341, 349 (1990), aff'd, 949 F.2d 404 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). But cf. Rysavy v. DHUD, 28 MSPR 263, 264 (1985) (when the matter implicates the Board's appellate jurisdiction and the parties were given proper notice, reopening is proper). Although Rysavy did not discuss the length of time involved, we note that in Rysavy as in the case at hand the gap between the final Board decision on the merits and the reopening was approximately six months. In addition, since a compliance RIF is ongoing in this matter, there has been no long period of finality which the agency's request for reopening would disturb. Further, the overriding consideration remains that the Board has no power to act on an appeal in the absence of a regulatory or statutory grant of such authority. See, e.g., Shaw v. Dept. of the Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 588-89 (1989).

The Board may reopen an appeal under 5 CFR § 1201.117 to "reconsider the fundamental issue of our jurisdiction to hear the matter." McKay v. VA, 23 MSPR 244, 246 (1984). See also Parkinson v. USPS, 58 MSPR 393, 397 (1993) ("The Board has authority to reopen an appeal on its own motion in the interests of justice, particularly when the evidence is of such weight as to warrant a different outcome"), aff'd, 31 F.3d 1177 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Among the factors that can be relied upon to justify a reopening are the possibility of fraud or misrepresentation on the part of a party to an appeal, Baugh v. OPM, 49 MSPR 58, 62-63 (1991), and the possibility that the allegedly misleading information led to an unjust outcome. See Parkinson, 58 MSPR at 397. For the same reason, the appellant's argument that the doctrine of laches bars the agency's motion to reopen lacks merit.

As already noted, the appellant submitted, with his petition for appeal, two pieces of documentation that could support opposite conclusions as to his entitlement to appeal a RIF action. The Board proceeded to adjudicate the appeal without raising an issue as to jurisdiction. The Board must, however, apprise an appellant of what he must establish in order to invoke its jurisdiction.... The record does not reflect that any such order was sent to the appellant. We therefore find, in light of the evidence submitted by the agency, which raises a substantial jurisdictional issue, that this case should be remanded to afford the appellant an opportunity to establish Board jurisdiction over his appeal.

The Board reopened when, several years after the issuance of an initial decision, the appellant presented recently discovered evidence, not available when the appeal was being litigated before the AJ, bearing on the validity of a demonstration project that, in turn, controlled the nature of his appointment, tenure, and possible adverse action appeal rights. Humme v. Dept. of Interior, 109 MSPR 678, 2008 MSPB 205 (2008) (Southwestern Indian Polytechnic Institute demonstration project).
