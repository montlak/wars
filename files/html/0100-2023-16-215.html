2. Brief Conversations With Supervisors

Not every conversation between a supervisor and an employee is a formal discussion. A charge that alleges that formal meetings occurred with unit members and management, or that the employer bypassed the union through meetings with individual employees, should be supported by evidence permitting analysis of the following factors: (1) whether the individual who held the discussions was a first-level supervisor or was higher in the management hierarchy; (2) whether any other management representatives attended; (3) where the meetings took place (i.e., in the supervisor's office, at each employee's desk, or elsewhere); (4) how long the meetings lasted; (5) how the meetings were called (i.e., with formal advance written notice or more spontaneously and informally); (6) whether a formal agenda was established for the meetings; and (7) the manner that the meetings were conducted (i.e., whether the employee's identity and comments were noted or transcribed). As to a bypassing allegation, the proof should show the specific content of the communications at the meetings, so that FLRA can determine if any matters were discussed that the employer should have bargained with the union. DHHS, SSA, Bureau of Field Operations and AFGE Council of Social Security Dist. Office Locals, San Francisco Region, 10 FLRA 115, 118 (1982). FLRA would not determine whether an exit interview with departing employees constituted a formal meeting without evidence of: whether the individual who held the discussions was a first-level supervisor or was higher in the management hierarchy; how long the meeting lasted; how the meeting was called; whether a formal agenda was established for the meeting; or whether the employee's attendance at the meeting was mandatory. VA Med. and Reg'l Office Ctr., Cheyenne and AFGE Local 1014, 13 FLRA 415, 416 (1983).

A formal discussion should not be construed to include an impromptu and brief discussion concerning an employment matter between a supervisor and employee. Any other result would make it impossible for an agency and an employee to resolve any personnel problems or requests of an employee without the participation of the union, irrespective of the limited impact that the problem might have on unit working conditions. EPA and AFGE Local 3331, 8 FLRA 471, 478 (1982) (ALJ Decision). A meeting was not a formal discussion that lasted 15 minutes and involved a first-level supervisor who did not prepare an agenda before the meeting. Although attendance was mandatory, no attendance or minutes were taken. DLA, Defense Distrib. Region W. and Laborers' Int'l Union Local 1276, 48 FLRA 744, 753 (1993) (ALJ Decision). A short, impromptu meeting conducted at the request of two employees who desired to speak about a change in working conditions with a manager who happened to be at the worksite was not a formal meeting requiring notice to the union. Office of Program Operations, Field Operations, SSA and AFGE Council of Social Security Dist. Office Locals, 9 FLRA 48, 50 (1982). Brief discussions with management, initiated by an employee, concerning that employee's working conditions were not formal discussions if there was no attempt to bypass the union. VAMC, Bath and AFGE Local 491, 12 FLRA 552, 585 (1983) (ALJ Decision). But a brief meeting between a supervisor and employees was formal when it was conducted by the second-level supervisor of the employees present: the first-level supervisor was also present; the employees were required to attend; and the meeting was held away from their desks. The purpose and subject matter of the meeting were not limited to the discrete application of a personnel policy, but rather involved a general discussion of that policy and how it was working. The meeting involved both a discussion of a general rule affecting unit employees as well as a discussion concerning conditions of employees in the unit generally. GSA, Region 8 and AFGE Local 3275, 19 FLRA 20, 22 (1985).
