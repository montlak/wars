c. Use of the Privacy Act

Employees may claim that agencies willfully maintained false or inaccurate records on them and that, as a result, personnel decisions were made adversely affecting them. Individuals may claim that information from their records was willfully disclosed to unauthorized persons, resulting in embarrassment or other harm. The Act has also occasionally been used in an attempt to recover damages because an individual has suffered economic damages when the government has not first contacted him or her for information during an investigation but instead has investigated through contacts with indirect sources of information. See, e.g., Darst v. SSA, 172 F.3d 1065, 1068 (8th Cir. 1999) (agency did not violate the statute by examining its own files and making determinations about the conduct of an ALJ before attempting to obtain the information from the employee); Dong v. Smithsonian Inst., 878 F. Supp. 244 (D.D.C. 1996) (a potentially embarrassing investigation likely would have been foreshortened had the government first questioned the plaintiff about the operative events under investigation), rev'd on other grounds, 125 F.3d 877, 879 (D.C. Cir. 1997) (Smithsonian not subject to the Privacy Act). A suit to expunge records is not precluded by the Reform Act, under Abdelfattah v. DHS, 787 F.3d 524, 534 (D.C. Cir. 2015):

Our precedent does not foreclose, however, the equitable relief of expungement of government records for violations of the Constitution. We have repeatedly recognized a plaintiff may request expungement of agency records for both violations of the Privacy Act and the Constitution. See Doe v. U.S. Air Force, 812 F.2d 738, 741 (D.C. Cir. 1987); Smith v. Nixon, 807 F.2d 197, 204 (D.C. Cir. 1986); Hobson v. Wilson, 737 F.2d 1, 65 (D.C. Cir. 1984) (overruled in part on other grounds by Leatherman v. Tarrant Cnty. Narcotics Intelligence & Coordination Unit, 507 U.S. 163 (1993)). Such recognition is consistent with our conclusion in Spagnola v. Mathis, 859 F.2d 223, 229-230 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (per curiam). There we held the availability of a comprehensive remedial scheme in the Civil Service Reform Act ("CRSA") counseled against extending a Bivens cause of action for damages to compensate federal employees and job applicants for constitutional claims. Id. at 229. We nevertheless made clear that the CRSA did not preclude judicial review of such constitutional claims altogether. Civil servants and job applicants could still "seek equitable relief against their supervisors, and the agency itself, in vindication of their constitutional rights." Id. at 230. Abdelfattah seeks equitable relief for the Department's alleged violations of the Constitution, and Congress's provision of specific Privacy Act remedies does not bar his claims.

The courts distinguish decisions based on improperly maintained records and records that only reflect an arguably unwise decision. If the record causes the damage, there may be recovery. If the record only reflects a decision, there is no recovery based on the record. See Hubbard v. EPA, 809 F.2d 1 (D.C. Cir. 1986) (discussing cases). Knowledge of personnel actions by others does not violate the Act if the information was not disseminated from files but was known through gossip that did not have its source in disclosure of protected records. Pippinger v. Rubin, 129 F.3d 519, 530-31 (10th Cir. 1997).

Under the Privacy Act damages allegedly suffered as a result of improperly maintained records are difficult to recover. In Henderson v. SSA, 908 F.2d 559, 560 (10th Cir. 1990), plaintiffs resigned from SSA and then sought reinstatement, challenging the refusal of SSA to reemploy them. The plaintiffs brought a Privacy Act challenge that the district court dismissed, a decision affirmed on appeal:

Plaintiffs...filed this suit in federal district court alleging that the SSA had maintained a "black list" in violation of the Privacy Act. In their complaint, plaintiffs pointed to certain documents and asserted that they contained erroneous judgments by SSA officials. The district court found that the plaintiffs' allegations of errors in their records essentially sought review of the Social Security Administration's reinstatement determination and therefore review of an employment decision. The court held that review of employment decisions falls within the exclusive provisions of the Civil Service Reform Act and that the Privacy Act does not reach errors in judgment that occur during the course of an employment determination. Cf. Blevins v. Plummer, 613 F.2d 767, 768 (9th Cir. 1980) (per curiam). We agree with the district court that the Privacy Act does not vest the court with jurisdiction to review personnel decisions where the Civil Service Reform Act precludes such review.

See Orsay v. DOJ, 289 F.3d 1125, 1130 (9th Cir. 2002) ("Because Appellants' Privacy Act claims are in fact complaints about 'prohibited personnel practices' under the CSRA, we hold that the CSRA precludes consideration of the claims.").

Federal employees may seek to employ the Privacy Act provision that prohibits disclosure of information contained within agency records without the prior consent of the employee. 5 USC 552a(b). There are 12 exemptions from the disclosure rule, most notably disclosure to officers and employees of the agency who have a need to know the information in the performance of their duties. In general, the courts have been strict in their interpretation of the disclosure provisions. In order to prevail, a plaintiff must show that: the agency acted through one of its agents; information was disclosed to an unauthorized person; the disclosure was intentional or willful; the information came from an agency record; and the disclosure caused damage to the plaintiff. Fitzpatrick v. IRS, 665 F.2d 327 (11th Cir. 1982).

The Act itself does not define disclosure. The task of determining when disclosure occurred was left to the courts. The majority rule was first expressed in Savarese v. DHEW, 479 F. Supp. 304, 308 (N.D. Ga. 1979), aff'd sub. nom. Savarese v. Harris, 620 F.2d 298 (5th Cir. 1980), cert. den. 449 U.S. 1078. The court ruled that establishing disclosure of information from a record was insufficient. The plaintiff must establish that there has been a retrieval of information from the record that at some point was the source of the information. The so-called "source" or "retrieval" rule allows agencies to raise the defense that the official charged with making the disclosure obtained the information not from a record, but from some other source. As justification for the rule, Savarese noted:

Under plaintiff's view no government official could utter a single word concerning any person without first reviewing all systems of records within the agency to determine whether or not the information in question was contained therein.... It borders on the absurd to contend that all officials should have panascopic recall concerning every record within every system of records within the agency....

The District of Columbia Circuit rejected the source rule. The D.C. Circuit determined that when an official has a primary role in creating a record, retrieval of information from the record is not required to show a prohibited disclosure. Bartel v. FAA, 725 F.2d 1403, 1409 (D.C. Cir. 1984):

[W]e disagree with appellees that all information not "retrieved" from a record is "personal knowledge" falling outside of the Privacy Act's protection. Although they cite several cases from other circuits to support their position, none involved the peculiar set of circumstances present here: disclosure by an agency official of his investigation which generated a protected personnel record. For example, in Jackson, 503 F. Supp. 653, the court explicitly found that "the official alleged to have divulged information had knowledge of the fact independent of any record in the plaintiff's OPF [official personnel file] or elsewhere." Id. at 656 (emphasis supplied). More importantly, this case demonstrates that an absolute policy of limiting the Act's coverage to information physically retrieved from a record would make little sense in terms of its underlying purpose....

See Armstrong v. Geithner, 608 F.3d 854 (D.C. Cir. 2010) (Bartel inapplicable when evidence suggests that sources not relying on agency records account for the release of information, e.g., based on speculation, rumor, personal observation, or disclosures to others by the plaintiff himself); Pippinger v. Rubin, 129 F.3d 519 (10th Cir. 1997) (discussing decisions involving what constitutes the source of information under the Act).

Courts have been less stringent in determining what constitutes a record under the Act. See, e.g., May v. Dept. of Air Force, 800 F.2d 1402, 1403 (5th Cir. 1986) (disclosure under the Privacy Act cannot be avoided by putting a performance evaluation in handwritten rather than typewritten form); Chapman v. NASA, 682 F.2d 526, 529 (5th Cir. 1982) (supervisors' private notes must be treated in accordance with the Privacy Act when they reflect negatively on performance).

The Federal Circuit suggested in Rainone v. OPM, 249 Fed. Appx. 823 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), that the Privacy Act be used to obtain correction of an individual retirement record; the court determined that the MSPB lacked authority to direct OPM or an agency to make the correction in the course of an annuity appeal.
