4. Security Clearance Revocation; Sensitive Position

The federal employee who holds a security clearance has two concerns: maintaining the clearance and keeping his job. Often the job is dependent on the clearance. It frequently occurs that agencies revoke the clearance and then discharge the employee because the employee cannot perform the position without the clearance. Agencies may indefinitely suspend employees from their jobs while their clearances are suspended during investigation into their ability to hold those clearances. The concept of a security clearance has a traditional meaning in the sense of a secret, top secret, or SCI clearance. Security concerns, and job restrictions based on those concerns, can arise in analogous circumstances involving denial of access to buildings or areas within buildings, or denial of access to computers or networks or other types of sensitive information. Denial of access to classified information may not be characterized as denial or revocation of a clearance, but the effect can be the same for the employee who needs that access to perform his or her position responsibilities requiring that access. Matters are further complicated when employees are deemed ineligible to continue holding positions designated as sensitive or public trust. Ordinarily, when a clearance is revoked and the clearance is required for the job, the employee loses employment through adverse action under 5 USC 7512-13. At times an agency may invoke specific statutory authority under 5 USC 7532 to suspend and then remove a clearance and an employee. Several agencies have specific statutory authority pertaining to personnel security clearance programs, e.g., the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, and the Arms Control and Disarmament Agency. Agency programs for granting security clearances vary widely and the regulations of the specific agency, or subagency, should be consulted. In addition to Executive Order 10450, procedures before the NRC are governed by the Atomic Energy Act of 1954, as amended by the Energy Reorganization Act of 1974. The procedures are set forth in full at 10 CFR Part 10 (2022). Cf. Carlucci v. Doe, 109 S. Ct. 407 (1988) (describing statutory program then applicable to employees of the National Security Agency).

Because the term "national security" is given a narrow reading in personnel cases--being generally limited to cases involving the incumbents of sensitive positions--most of our discussion in this section relates to the retention and loss of security clearances. See Cole v. Young, 76 S. Ct. 861 (1956) (suggesting that national security should not be used as a justification to circumvent normal adverse action procedures unless the employee encumbers a sensitive position). Not every personnel action resulting from the loss of a clearance will result in an adverse action. Often, employees do not represent an imminent threat to national security when their security clearances are denied or revoked, or their need to access classified information is minimal. In the discretion of the agency, the employee can either be reassigned to a position or be assigned position functions not requiring access to classified information. Although the decisions discussed consider the problem of the employee who loses a clearance and is then fired for his inability to fill a job that requires the clearance, an agency may consider the loss of a security clearance as part of its determination to invoke an adverse action, even if that loss did not result in a charge that the employee could no longer maintain eligibility to hold his position. See Spotti v. Dept. of Air Force, 49 MSPR 27, 35 (1991); cf. Bishopp v. Dept. of Air Force, 75 MSPR 33, 41 (1997) ("The agency did not remove the appellant for not maintaining a security clearance. Thus, whether a clearance was required for her position and whether it has been revoked are not relevant to the penalty determination."). If an agency bases removal on revocation of a clearance and upon another charge, e.g., falsification of documents, the agency must still prove the facts and nexus of the more traditional adverse action charge. See, e.g., Benoist v. DOD, 40 MSPR 418, 426-27 (1989). Declassification of a position is not an action that can be challenged by an employee. Finley v. Hampton, 473 F.2d 180 (D.C. Cir. 1972).

An agency may take adverse action against an employee for failure to protect classified information, and the Board's review may be constrained as to the decision to classify the information entrusted to the employee, Croft v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 320, 322-24 (1989):

The appellant argues that the Board should use the standard set forth in the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). We reject that argument on the basis that the two statutes differ significantly in purpose and structure. FOIA specifies that agencies "shall make available" public information. 5 USC § 552(a). It also specifically gives the court a right to review records in camera. 5 USC § 552(a)(4)(B). Finally, by its terms, it limits the exemption from disclosure to matters that are authorized to be kept secret and "are in fact properly classified pursuant to...Executive order." 5 USC § 552(b)(1)(A), (B).

5 USC §§ 7513 and 7701 have a different purpose and provide no such specific authorizations with respect to matters of national security. The Supreme Court recognized this in Egan. See Egan, 108 S. Ct. at 825-26. Using the preponderance of the evidence test required by 5 USC § 7701(c)(1)(B), as in Egan, would "involve the Board in second-guessing the agency's national security determinations." Id. at 826. For these reasons, we find that FOIA provides no authority for administrative judges to review materials in camera or otherwise to determine that they are properly considered classified.

We hold that the administrative judge correctly found that the decision in Egan applies to an agency's determination that information is classified for reasons of national security. In Egan, the Court accepted the general proposition of administrative law that the absence of any statutory provision precluding appellate review creates a strong presumption in favor of appellate review.... The Court, however, found that this proposition "runs aground when it encounters concerns of national security."...

[U]nder the Court's analysis in Egan, the responsibility of determining whether information is classified for reasons of national security lies with the agency. The administrative judge properly determined that the information disclosed by the appellant was classified, and correctly found that the Board is without the authority to review the agency's classification determination of the information disclosed by the appellant. See Egan, 108 S. Ct. at 823-25.
