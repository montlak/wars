3. 5 USC 2302(b)(2): Investigative Statement

A provision of the Reform Act, 5 USC 2302(b)(2) (as revised in 1996), makes it a prohibited personnel practice for an agency to:

(b)(2) solicit or consider any recommendation or statement, oral or written, with respect to any individual who requests or is under consideration for any personnel action unless such recommendation is based on the personal knowledge or records of the person furnishing it and consists of--

(A) an evaluation of the work performance, ability, aptitude, or general qualifications of such individual; or

(B) an evaluation of the character, loyalty, or suitability of such individual[.]

The statute does not preclude hearsay evidence used in personnel actions, Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 351 (1987):

The legislative history of this provision indicates that it was designed to preclude an agency's reliance on statements or recommendations by outsiders and to specifically avoid partisan or political interference in effecting a personnel action. See Depte v. United States, 715 F.2d 1481, 1484 (Fed. Cir. 1983). However, this provision does not apply to situations where an agency takes statements from persons who are somehow involved in the incident giving rise to the disciplinary proceeding. Id. at 1484-85.

See Alexander v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3165) ("personal knowledge" requirement of § 2302 is directed not to forbidding hearsay but to forestalling political interference).

Lopez v. VA, 12 MSPR 187, 192 (1982), interpreted the statute as to portions of an agency internal investigation used in an appeal:

Appellant also contends that the agency committed a prohibited personnel practice in that the Special Investigation Board convened by the agency to investigate complaints against appellant by two patients included hearsay evidence in its report. Specifically, appellant alleges that this was a violation of 5 USC Section 2302(b)(2) which states that an employee empowered with the authority to undertake personnel decisions will not "solicit or consider any recommendation or statement, oral or written, with respect to any individual who requests or is under consideration for any personnel action unless such recommendation or statement is based on the personal knowledge or records of the person furnishing it...." Appellant's apparent argument is that the Report of the Special Investigation Board contained hearsay evidence and was therefore compiled in violation of the above cited prohibited personnel practice.

Under 5 USC Section 7701(c)(2)(B), the Board may not sustain a personnel action "based on" a prohibited personnel practice. In Gerlach v. Federal Trade Commission, 8 MSPB 599, 603, 9 MSPR 268, 274 (1981), the Board held that Section 7701(c)(2)(B) mandates reversal of the agency's action only where the Board has determined that a prohibited personnel practice was the 'motivating factor' or the 'real reason' for the agency action."

In this case, of the twenty-five affidavits appended to the agency's Report of the Special Investigation Board, four were favorable to the appellant. Of the remaining twenty-one, four concern hearsay evidence entirely, ten relate to personal knowledge of the declarants, and seven were comprised of a combination of the personal knowledge of the declarant and some hearsay. Thus, even if several of the affidavits represented a violation of 5 USC Section 2302(b)(2), we cannot find that those affidavits constitute the "motivating factor" or the "real reason" for appellant's removal.

Hammonds v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 222, 224 (1982), skirted direct analysis of § 2302(b)(2); instead, the Board examined collateral written substantiation of statements:

Appellant contends first in his petition for review that the agency's removal action constituted a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC § 2302 because it was based on uncorroborated oral allegations against her which were not properly documented in accordance with the agency's work assessment system and which were not brought to her attention through required written feedback reports. Under 5 USC § 2302(b)(2), an agency is forbidden to rely on oral recommendations or statements in effecting an adverse action whenever those recommendations or statements are not based on the personal knowledge or records of the person furnishing them.

In this case, the agency's deciding official based her decision, in part, upon recommendations by several managers and assistant managers who had supervisory responsibility to review the appellant's performance directly and who, therefore, based their recommendations upon personal knowledge. The oral recommendations of appellant's supervisors to the deciding official were substantiated by written summaries of performance interviews, specifying performance deficiencies. When appellant refused to participate in later scheduled work interviews, written evaluation memoranda were provided to her and entered into her personnel file. Further, appellant has not rebutted the agency's argument that its individual employee assessment system was designed to monitor the accuracy of a file clerk's performance of certain specific duties, not including timeliness, initiative and ability to follow instructions, in which appellant was found deficient. Nor has she discredited the agency's explanation that the assessment system was not used to evaluate trainees, such as appellant.

Therefore, we do not find the agency's failure to provide appellant with written feedback assessment slips to be error, particularly in light of the abundant evidence that she was continually advised, both orally and in writing, of her performance deficiencies and individual errors as they arose. Accordingly, we find no support in the record for appellant's claim that the removal action was based on a prohibited personnel practice or on unsubstantiated oral allegations.
