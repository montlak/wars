f. Appellant's Responsibility When Decision Notice or Information About Appeal Rights Is Not Received

"It is well settled that failure of an appellant to receive the required notice of appeal rights constitutes good cause for the late filing of a petition for appeal." Hamilton v. USPS, 84 MSPR 635, 640 ¶ 10 (1999) (referring to the decision letter containing that notice). See Shiflett v. USPS, 839 F.2d 669 (Fed. Cir. 1988) ("[T]he failure of the respondent to give notice to petitioner of her appeal rights constituted good cause for the late filing by petitioner of her appeal. An agency must follow all applicable procedures."). The critical focus is on the agency's failure to provide notice. An appeal from a noticeless action is not late because a union representative could have discovered appeal rights sooner by examining a negotiated agreement. Smith v. USPS, 40 MSPR 201, 204 (1989) (agency failed to give a removal notice to a tenured employee; a filing delay of almost two years was excused). Ambiguous references to an MSPB appeal will not serve to place a representative on notice of an appeal right. See Drose v. USPS, 85 MSPR 98, 102 ¶ 10 (2000) ("Where, as here, an agency has an obligation to provide notice of appeal rights, the obligation is not satisfied by a mere reference to Board appeal rights."); Parker v. USPS, 59 MSPR 603, 610 (1993) ("[E]ven assuming arguendo that Strawn had been the appellant's 'representative' in April 1991, the agency's bare April 26, 1991 statement to him that the appellant 'ha[d] not appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board' is insufficient to constitute compliance with an agency's obligation to provide specific appeal rights to an employee who is affected by an appealable action.").

Summarizing decisions, under Powell v. OPM, 114 MSPR 580, 584-85 ¶ 11, 2010 MSPB 171 (2010), on appeal from denial of a deferred annuity:

When an agency is required to notify an individual of his Board appeal rights but fails to do so, the agency's failure may constitute good cause for a filing delay. See Shiflett v. U.S. Postal Service, 839 F.2d 669, 674 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Mc Clendon v. Office of Personnel Management, 92 M.S.P.R. 250, ¶¶ 10-13 (2002). In such cases, an appellant need not show that he acted diligently in discovering his Board appeal rights; he need only show that he acted diligently in pursuing his Board appeal rights once he discovered them. Herring v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 438, 443 (1996). In this case, OPM's December 17, 2007 decision does not appear to include notice of Board appeal rights, and it is unclear whether or how long the appellant delayed in filing his Board appeal once he actually learned of his Board appeal rights. Because the timeliness issue will likely turn on these facts, and the parties were not previously ordered to address these specific issues, we find it appropriate to remand the appeal for further development of the record. See Bautista v. Office of Personnel Management, 74 M.S.P.R. 47, 50 (1997).

Powell does not oblige the appellant to seek out appeal rights when the agency should have provided notice of those rights. The Board's approach toward appeals initiated by appellants who should have, but were not, issued notices of appeal rights was also summed up by Turner v. USPS, 90 MSPR 385, 390 ¶ 10 (2001), stating that the filing time runs from when the employee became aware of the appeal right:

To establish good cause for the untimely filing of an appeal, a party must show that she exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980). We note that the removal decision did not inform the appellant of a right to appeal to the Board. An important factor in determining whether good cause exists is whether the appellant is entitled to notice of appeal rights. If the appellant here is preference eligible, then she is entitled to notice of her appeal rights. An agency's failure to apprise an employee of a right to appeal to the Board will justify waiver of the filing deadline, when the employee does not delay filing an appeal after she becomes aware of her appeal rights. Redus v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 193 ¶ 15 (2001)....

See McClendon v. OPM, 92 MSPR 250, 254-55 ¶¶ 10-13 (2002) (fifteen months of delay in filing an appeal were excused when notice was not provided); Munson v. Dept. of Commerce, 90 MSPR 583, 589 ¶ 14 (2002), rev'd on other grounds, Munson v. MSPB, 318 F.3d 1358 (Fed. Cir. 2003) ("...Where, as here, the agency failed to meet its obligation to provide a notice of appeal rights, the appellant is not required to show due diligence in discovering his appeal rights; rather, the appellant must show that he was diligent in filing an appeal after he learned that he could do so. O'Leary, 86 M.S.P.R. 87 ¶ 11; Besemer v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 260, 265 (1998)...."); Carroll v. USPS, 87 MSPR 570, 573-75 ¶¶ 6-11 (2001) (complete failure of an agency to provide notice of appeal rights to an employee justified an eight-year appellate delay). But cf. Gingery v. OPM, CH-300A-13-0240-I-1 (NP 2/14/2014) (employment practices appeal: "Although the regulations governing unemployment practices appeals do not explicitly state that the 30-day filing limit set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b)(1) applies to such appeals, we find that a person of ordinary prudence would not have believed that he could have waited indefinitely to file this appeal. At the very least, due diligence required that the appellant consult with the Board to ascertain whether his interpretation of the relevant Board regulations was correct. See Ford v. Office of Personnel Management, 59 M.S.P.R. 66, 69-70 (1993) (to the extent that the appellant was confused about the filing deadline, ordinary prudence requires that he inquire of the agency or the Board)."). Sellers v. USPS, 98 MSPR 44, 49-50 ¶¶ 12-13 (2004), reiterated the theme:

Because the agency's decision to remove the appellant was an appealable action, the agency was obliged to notify him of his Board appeal rights. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.21. The agency failed to do so. As a general matter, an agency's failure to notify an employee of his or her Board appeal rights under circumstances requiring it to do so will justify a waiver of the filing deadline. Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 583, 587 (1995). In such a case, the appellant is not required to show that he exercised due diligence in attempting to discover his appeal rights; the question is whether he was diligent in filing an appeal after he learned he could do so. Id. at 588.

The appellant's response to the show-cause order on timeliness does not answer this question, however, as the appellant has not explained when he first learned of his possible right of appeal to the Board or indicated the sufficiency of the notice of appeal right he received. Thus, whether the appellant was adequately informed of a possible right of appeal to the Board and whether he exercised due diligence in filing his appeal once he became aware of that right are issues that must be resolved on remand. See Fisher v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 M.S.P.R. 70, 76 (1995).

If the appellant did not receive notice of appeal rights, the appellant must supply to the AJ information responding to questions of when the appellant first learned of an appeal right and explaining any delay in filing the appeal, Wright v. VA, AT-3443-11-0198-I-1 (NP 6/28/2011):

The Board has held that, where an agency subjects an appellant to an appealable action without notifying her of her Board appeal rights, the appellant must demonstrate that she was diligent in exercising her appeal rights once learning of them, regardless of her diligence in discovering those rights, in order to show good cause for waiving the Board's filing deadline. See Smart v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶ 8 (2010). The appellant here claims that she was unaware of her Board appeal rights. The administrative judge, however, specifically informed the appellant of this basis for establishing good cause, including whether the appellant was notified of the time limit for filing an appeal, and directed her to provide a detailed explanation of her filing delay. The appellant did not provide this explanation, and the administrative judge appropriately concluded that the appellant did not meet her burden of proving that she timely filed her appeal or that good cause exists for the filing delay. See Sanchez v. U.S. Postal Service, 114 M.S.P.R. 345, ¶ 22 (2010).

The type of notice that an appellant must be given depends on the availability and timing of an appeal. Even the appellant's failure to receive a collateral document, e.g., a SF-50 evidencing a personnel action, does not negate the notice of appeal rights received. Reece v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 92-3350); see Paradiso v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 324, 327-28 (1994) ("in view of the clearly set forth twenty calendar day deadline of which the appellant was aware, she might have contacted the Board to inquire as to the necessity for having the SF-50 or to request an extension of time in which to file her appeal").

In an appeal of an annuity claim, OPM ordinarily issues a decision informing the appellant of appeal rights. If that decision contains no notice of appeal rights, the appellant must appeal with dispatch after learning of the right, Powell v. OPM, DC-0831-10-0408-B-1 (NP 5/11/2011):

It is undisputed that OPM failed to notify the appellant of his Board appeal rights. However, this fact alone does not establish good cause to waive the filing deadline in this case; the appellant must also establish that he acted promptly once he actually learned of his appeal rights. See Gordy v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 736 F.2d 1505, 1507-08 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Herring v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 438, 443 (1996). As the administrative judge explained in his show cause order, the appellant was required to establish that he filed his Board appeal within a reasonable amount of time after learning of his right to do so. The appellant did not identify the date that he learned of his right to appeal OPM's December 17, 2007 decision, and he did not otherwise account for the period of time between his receipt of the decision and the time that he filed his Board appeal on March 20, 2010.
