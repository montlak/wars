broida flra Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards, Subchapter 08: Grounds for Review, Section G: Contrary to Law; Award is Inconsistent with Law, Rule, or Regulation, Subsection 18: Management Right to Discipline; "Just Cause", Part j: Suspension
j. Suspension

Many suspension grievances are adjudicated under a contract's "just cause" clause. An arbitrator may determine whether all or part of a disciplinary action is not for just and sufficient cause and set aside or reduce an imposed penalty. The "just cause" determination may, in turn, relate to other formulations, e.g., FLRA pronouncements concerning burdens of proof as discipline arising out of protected union activity. See DOJ, INS and Nat'l Border Patrol Council, AFGE, 43 FLRA 939, 949 (1992). Some arbitrators will apply an "efficiency of the service" review standard--the same standard applied by MSPB in major adverse actions. See Dept. of Energy, Southwestern Power Admin. and IBEW Local 1002, 56 FLRA 624, 625 (2000) (requirements that discipline be for efficiency of the service are functionally identical to requirements that discipline be for just cause). The arbitrator need not, in deciding a disciplinary suspension grievance, go beyond determining whether the suspension meets the "efficiency of the service" standard of 5 USC 7503(a). The arbitrator need not accept and then decide based on an agency's own formulation of the statutory standard as it is applied to the challenged suspension. See VAMC, Ft. Wayne and AFGE Local 1384, 39 FLRA 717, 721 (1991) (upholding award setting aside suspension based on employee's protected right to refuse to attend meeting with supervisor without union representative present); DOJ, Fed. Prison Sys., El Reno FCI and AFGE Local 171, 35 FLRA 329, 335 (1990) (rejecting argument that an arbitrator had to decide a case involving the escape of an inmate under a theory of strict culpability) (citing INS and AFGE, 8 FLRA 248 (1982)).

An award meets the Back Pay Act "but for" test if a suspension is submitted to arbitration and the suspension is found not to be for just cause. By operation of the award the suspension is rendered an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action within the meaning of the Back Pay Act. When coupled with a direction by the arbitrator that the agency reimburse the grievant for all losses caused by the unwarranted suspension, the award meets the requirements of the Back Pay Act that, but for the unwarranted suspension, the grievant would not have suffered a withdrawal or reduction of his pay, allowances, or differentials. AFGE Local 1760 and SSA, Northeastern Program Serv. Ctr., 22 FLRA 195, 199 (1986). The award need not incant magic words. The finding by an arbitrator that a suspension was unsupported is a finding that the suspension was an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action within the meaning of the Act. AFGE Local 1915 and Dorn VA Hosp., Columbia, 32 FLRA 1223, 1226 (1988).

The back pay analysis for a suspension remedy is relatively straightforward, DHS, C&BP and NTEU Chap. 164, 65 FLRA 160, 163-64 (2010):

An award of backpay is authorized under the Back Pay Act only when an arbitrator finds that: (1) the aggrieved employee was affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action; and (2) the personnel action resulted in the withdrawal or the reduction of an employee's pay, allowances, or differentials. U.S. Dep't of the Air Force, Warner Robins Air Force Base, Ga., 56 FLRA 541, 543 (2000) (Warner Robins) (citing U.S. Dep't of Health & Human Servs., 54 FLRA 1210, 1218-19 (1998) (HHS)).

a. The award of backpay for the grievant's five-day suspension is not contrary to the Back Pay Act.

A violation of a collective bargaining agreement constitutes an unjustified and unwarranted personnel action under the Back Pay Act. See U.S. Dep't of Def., Dep't of Def. Dependents Schools, 54 FLRA 773, 785 (1998)). The Arbitrator found that the Agency violated Article 28, Section 1 of the parties' agreement. The Agency has not demonstrated that this finding is deficient. Accordingly, we find that the award satisfies the first requirement of the Back Pay Act.

The Arbitrator also found that the unwarranted and unjustified personnel action resulted in a reduction of the grievant's pay. Although the Arbitrator did not specify the amount of the loss incurred by the grievant, the Arbitrator did find that the action "resulted in [the grievant's] loss of pay and other benefits." Award at 6; see also id. at 5 (ordering that grievant be "made whole" for any loss of pay or benefits that were a direct result of the unjustified and unwarranted personnel action). This finding satisfies the second requirement of the Back Pay Act. See, e.g., U.S. Dep't of the Air Force, Tinker Air Force Base, Okla. City, Okla., 63 FLRA 59, 61 (2008) (arbitrator's finding that the unwarranted and unjustified personnel action resulted in grievant's loss of pay was sufficient to satisfy the requirement under the Back Pay Act).

Moreover, an employee is only entitled to receive compensation "equal to all or any part of the pay, allowances, or differentials, as applicable which the employee normally would have earned or received during the period if the personnel action had not occurred[.]" 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(A)(i). The award provides that the grievant should be "made whole" for the loss of any pay or benefits as a result of the suspension. Thus, the award does not provide that the grievant be compensated for any losses not actually sustained as a result of that action.

For the foregoing reasons, we find that the award of backpay in connection with the suspension does not violate the Back Pay Act, and we deny the exception. See HHS, 54 FLRA at 1218-19 (award of backpay is warranted where the arbitrator found that the unjustified and unwarranted personnel action resulted in the loss of pay for the grievant).

FLRA declined to disturb an award that mitigated a penalty from a suspension to a reprimand and forfeiture of the back pay covered by the period of the suspension. Dept. of Air Force, Kelly AFB and AFGE Local 1617, 32 FLRA 513, 516 (1988) (citing AFGE Local 2718 v. DOJ, 768 F.2d 348, 351-53 (Fed. Cir. 1985)); see DOJ, INS, Jacksonville and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council Local 3725, 36 FLRA 928, 932 (1990) ("Because this case involves a 5-day suspension, the Arbitrator was not bound to follow the same substantive standards as the MSPB. Accordingly, we reject the Agency's assertions that the award is deficient because the Arbitrator's findings regarding the issue of disparate treatment conflict with precedent of the Federal Circuit and the MSPB."). For the special case of indefinite suspensions, an arbitrator's authority to award back pay for the period between the onset of a suspension and an employee's acquittal, refer to Richardson v. Customs Serv., 47 F.3d 415, 418 (Fed. Cir. 1995).

FLRA reaffirmed that arbitrators considering disciplinary cases, e.g., short-term suspensions, are not governed by the MSPB and Federal Circuit case law that governs cases, e.g., adverse actions, determined under 5 USC 7512 and 5 USC 4303. DOJ, INS, Jacksonville and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council Local 3725, 36 FLRA 928, 932 (1990); see AFGE Local 1156 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Supply Business Ctr., 68 FLRA 531, 532-33 (2015) (Member Pizzella concurring) (MSPB law on nexus is not applicable to short suspension); SSA and AFGE Local 3239, 65 FLRA 286, 288 (2010) (arbitrator did not err as matter of law based on allegedly inappropriate application of Douglas factor to suspension of 14 days or less); Member Pizzella contended that nexus is requirement of any discipline; DOD, DCA and NAGE Local R4-45, 59 FLRA 490 n.* (2003) ("The Agency asserts that the award is contrary to a Federal Circuit decision concerning review of a decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board involving 5 U.S.C. § 7512. However, as this case does not involve an action covered by 5 U.S.C. § 7512, that decision does not provide a basis for finding the award contrary to law."); NAGE Local R1-109 and VA Connecticut Healthcare Sys., 58 FLRA 501, 504 (2003) ("The Authority has consistently held that arbitrators are bound by the same standards as MSPB only when resolving grievances concerning actions covered by 5 U.S.C. §§ 4303 and 7512."); AFGE Local 2172 and DOD, DCA, 57 FLRA 625, 629 (2001) (although arbitrators considering suspensions of less than 14 days may use and apply the legal principles established by the Federal Circuit for review of adverse actions under 5 USC 7703, "such use is not mandatory" and failure to do so does not constitute a violation of public policy); AFGE Local 2052 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Petersburg, 56 FLRA 604, 608 n.3 (2000) ("[T]he Douglas factors essentially constitute guidelines governing the appropriateness of penalties for adverse actions under 5 USC 7512."); AFGE Local 2612 and Dept. of Air Force, Rome Lab., 55 FLRA 483, 486 (1999) ("The Authority has consistently held that arbitrators are not required to consider the Douglas factors in cases, such as in the instant case, involving suspensions of 14 days or less."); AFGE Local 3947 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, Fed. Med. Ctr., Rochester, 47 FLRA 1364, 1370 (1993) (arbitrator need not apply the Douglas mitigation factors when deciding a grievance over a 14-day suspension); DOJ, Fed. Bureau of Prisons, Terre Haute and AFGE Local 720, 41 FLRA 237, 243 (1991) (substantive standards governing adverse actions adjudicated by MSPB and harmful error rule do not govern disciplinary cases). But see DHS C&IS and AFGE Local 1944, 71 FLRA 167, 168 n.9 (2019) (Member DuBester dissenting) (setting aside arbitrator's mitigation of a short suspension based on an error by the arbitrator in counting prior discipline under a penalty table, commenting "We note that our deference to the Agency here is consistent with that of the Merit Systems Protection Board. See Lachance v. Devall, 178 F.3d 1246, 1258 (Fed. We have instead repeatedly emphasized the agency's exclusive domain in disciplining its employees'); Parker v. U.S. Postal Serv., 819 F.2d 1113, 1116 (Fed. Cir. 1987) ('It is well established that the determination of the proper disciplinary action to be taken to promote the efficiency of the service is a matter peculiarly and necessarily within the discretion of the agency.'); Douglas v. Veterans Admin., 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 300-01 (1981) ('Management of the federal workforce and maintenance of discipline among its members is not the Board's function. Any margin of discretion available to the Board in reviewing penalties must be exercised with appropriate deference to the primary discretion which has been entrusted to agency management, not to the Board. Our role in this area, as in others, is principally to assure that managerial discretion has been legitimately invoked and properly exercised.')."); cf. SBA and AFGE Local 288, 71 FLRA 655, 657 (2020) (Member DuBester dissenting) (apparently declining to adopt MSPB standards for evaluation of past discipline as an aggravating factor in penalty analysis under Bolling v. Although nothing requires an arbitrator to apply the harmful error doctrine to a short suspension, nothing precludes its application. NFFE Local 1781 and USDA, Forest Serv., 42 FLRA 703, 709 (1991) (relying on DOJ, INS, Honolulu and AFGE Local 2886, Immigration and Naturalization Council, 41 FLRA 207 (1991)); cf. AFGE Local 1592 and Dept. of Air Force, Hill AFB, 45 FLRA 424, 427 (1992) (award sustaining assessment of levy against employee for property damage; arbitrators are not precluded from applying harmful error rule in cases that do not mandate its application). If the arbitrator is not required to apply statutory standards to a short-term suspension challenge, the alleged misapplication by the arbitrator of the statutory standard (involving protected activity under § 7102) does not provide a basis for setting the award aside. VA Maryland Healthcare Sys. and AFGE Local 331, 65 FLRA 619, 622 (2011). DHS, C&IS and AFGE Local 1616, 66 FLRA 693, 694 (2012), rejected the argument that the arbitrator could not impose a contractual "just cause" burden of proof on a suspension that the agency maintained was in part a performance-based action:

Where an arbitrator resolves a claim under a collective-bargaining agreement rather than a legal claim, "unless a specific burden of proof is required, an arbitrator may establish and apply whatever burden the arbitrator considers appropriate[.]" U.S. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, VA Md. Healthcare Sys., 65 FLRA 619, 621 (2011) (VA Md.) (quoting U.S. GSA, Ne. & Caribbean Region, N.Y.C., N.Y., 60 FLRA 864, 866 (2005)) (internal quotation marks omitted). In addition, where an arbitrator is not required to apply a particular legal standard, alleged misapplications of that standard do not provide a basis for finding the arbitrator's award deficient. E.g., Soc. Sec. Admin., 65 FLRA 286, 288 (2010) (SSA).

Here, the Arbitrator framed the issue as whether the Agency had "just cause" or "appropriate cause" to discipline the grievant as required by Article 29, Section B of the parties' agreement. He did not set forth as an issue, or resolve, whether the suspension violated Chapter 75. Because the issue before the Arbitrator was a purely contractual claim, the Arbitrator was not required to apply a particular legal standard, and the Arbitrator's alleged misapplication of Chapter 75 does not provide a basis for setting aside the award as contrary to law. See SSA, 65 FLRA at 288. Accordingly, we deny the Agency's contrary-to-law exception. Cf. VA Md., 65 FLRA at 621-22 (denying contrary-to-law exceptions challenging legal standard used by arbitrator, where arbitrator resolved grievance under parties' agreement on finding of no just cause for discipline).

Just as an arbitrator need not follow law developed on procedural or harmful error by the MSPB or its reviewing court, the Federal Circuit, during adjudication of challenges to short suspensions or lesser disciplinary actions, the arbitrator need not follow substantive MSPB law on the merits of short suspensions, AFGE Local 1770 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Bragg, 51 FLRA 1302, 1306 (1996):

In Metz [v. Dept. of Treasury, 780 F.2d 1001 (Fed. Cir. 1986)], the court prescribed evidentiary factors for the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) to consider in deciding whether an employee's statements constitute a threat to supervisors or co-workers. The Authority has repeatedly held that arbitrators are bound by the same substantive standards as the MSPB only when resolving grievances concerning actions covered by 5 U.S.C. §§ 4303 and 7512. See Naval Electronic Systems Engineering Center, 46 FLRA at 902; U.S. Department of the Army, III Corps and Fort Hood, Fort Hood, Texas and American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1920, 46 FLRA 609, 613 (1992). Specifically, suspensions of 14 days or less are not covered under 5 U.S.C. §§ 4303 or 7512. See U.S. Department of Justice, Immigration and Naturalization Service, Jacksonville, Florida and American Federation of Government Employees, National Border Patrol Council, Local 3725, 36 FLRA 928, 932 (1990) (Immigration and Naturalization Service). Therefore, because the grievant in this case was given a 10-day suspension, the Arbitrator was not bound to follow the same substantive standards as the MSPB. Consequently, the Union has not demonstrated that the award is inconsistent with Metz. Cf. Immigration and Naturalization Service, 36 FLRA at 932 (Authority rejected the agency's assertions that an award was deficient because the arbitrator's findings regarding the issue of disparate treatment conflicted with precedent of the Federal Circuit and the MSPB).

An arbitrator's review of procedural issues involved in suspensions need not comport with decisions of the MSPB and the Federal Circuit on appeals litigated before the Board. When an arbitrator found aspects of the processing of a grievance over a one-day suspension untimely, the union filed exceptions that claimed the agency was barred from raising the issue of timeliness before the arbitrator because the agency had not raised the issue during the grievance procedure. FLRA sustained the award finding the grievance untimely, Dept. of Army, Ft. Monroe and NAGE Local R4-11, 35 FLRA 1187, 1191-92 (1990):

...In [Gunn v. VAMC, Birmingham, 892 F.2d 1036 (Fed. Cir. 1990)], the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit reviewed the arbitrator's ruling on arbitrability in accordance with 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c), which is the same standard of review that it applies to final decisions of the Merit Systems Protection Board. The court in Gunn noted that, because its review was governed [by the statutory standard] set forth in John Wiley & Sons v. Livingston (that "procedural questions should be left to the arbitrator to decide") did not apply in the case before it....

In reviewing exceptions to arbitrators' awards under section 7122(a) of the Statute, the Authority does not apply the standard set forth in 5 U.S.C. 7703(c) but, as relevant here, is guided by decisions of "Federal courts in private sector labor-management relations[.]"... Consistent with the Supreme Court's decision in John Wiley & Sons v. Livingston and other decisions of Federal courts in private sector labor-management relations, we will deny exceptions that merely disagree with an arbitrator's determinations regarding the procedural arbitrability of a grievance. Accordingly, the decision in Gunn is inapplicable.

See SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 64 FLRA 516, 518 (2010) (arbitrator not required to apply Douglas factors to suspensions of less than 14 days); DHS, C&BP and AFGE Local 1929, 60 FLRA 883 (2005) (short suspension: arbitrator could set aside penalty on finding that agency did not act quickly enough to initiate discipline under contract calling for notice of proposed disciplinary action at earliest practicable date after alleged offense); NAGE Local R4-45 and DOD, DCA, 55 FLRA 784, 786 (1999) (constructive suspension of less than 14 days: arbitrator may use and apply legal precedents established by MSPB, but their use is not required); NATCA and Dept. of Transp., FAA, 52 FLRA 787, 792 (1996) (arbitrators may, but need not, apply to short term suspensions same substantive review rules used by MSPB).

Although arbitrators may not be bound in disciplinary cases to the case law and statutes governing adverse actions, FLRA expects arbitrators to enforce constitutional safeguards. If an award concerning disciplinary action is challenged for lack of constitutional due process, the law defining the protected property interest should be provided to FLRA. If due process arguments are made, for example, on the basis of provisions of agency regulations and a collective bargaining agreement, FLRA may dismiss the exception if "[w]ithout any reference to either the regulation or the agreement, there is no basis on which to find any constitutionally protected property interest or to determine what process was due to the grievant." Dept. of Air Force, Hill AFB and AFGE Local 1592, 47 FLRA 621, 624 (1993).

Constitutional issues that are indirectly involved in a grievance over discipline are not necessarily required to be correctly resolved, or resolved at all, if the arbitrator's determination is based on a contract article rather than the constitutional issue, DHS, C&BP, Border Patrol and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, Local 2554, 65 FLRA 752, 755 (2011):

The Agency alleges that the award is contrary to Garrity and the "obey now, grieve later" rule. When an exception involves an award's consistency with law, the Authority reviews any question of law raised by the exception and the award de novo. See NTEU, Chapter 24, 50 FLRA 330, 332 (1995) (citing U.S. Customs Serv. v. FLRA, 43 F.3d 682, 686-87 (D.C. Cir. 1994)). In applying the standard of de novo review, the Authority assesses whether an arbitrator's legal conclusions are consistent with the applicable standard of law. See U.S. Dep't of Def., Dep'ts of the Army & the Air Force, Ala. Nat'l Guard, Northport, Ala., 55 FLRA 37, 40 (1998). In making that assessment, the Authority defers to the arbitrator's underlying factual findings. See id.

Although the Agency alleges flaws in the Arbitrator's legal analyses concerning Garrity rights and the "obey now, grieve later" rule, e.g., the issue framed by the Arbitrator involved whether "disciplinary action in this case [was] taken for just cause and only for reasons that will promote the efficiency of the service[.]" Where an arbitrator resolves a claim under a collective bargaining agreement (CBA) rather than a legal claim, "unless a specific burden of proof is required, an arbitrator may establish and apply whatever burden the arbitrator considers appropriate[.]" U.S. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, VA Md. Healthcare Sys., 65 FLRA 619, 621 (2011) (VA Md.) (quoting U.S. GSA, Ne. & Caribbean Region, N.Y.C., N.Y., 60 FLRA 864, 866 (2005)) (internal quotation marks omitted). In addition, where an arbitrator is not required to apply a particular legal standard, alleged misapplications of that standard do not provide a basis for finding the arbitrator's award deficient. E.g., SSA, 65 FLRA 286, 288 (2010).

The Arbitrator found that the "Agency ha[d] the burden...to establish, by a preponderance of the evidence," that the grievant's suspension complied with the parties' agreement. For the reasons set forth supra Part II.B., the Arbitrator found that the "Agency [did] not...establish just cause for the [g]rievant's suspension[,] or that the suspension was only for reasons as will promote the efficiency of the Agency." Because the issue before the Arbitrator was a contractual claim, the Arbitrator was not required to apply a particular legal standard, and the Arbitrator's alleged misapplication of Garrity rights doctrine or the "obey now, grieve later" rule does not provide a basis for setting aside the award as contrary to law. See SSA, 65 FLRA at 288. Accordingly, we deny the contrary-to-law exceptions. Cf. VA Md., 65 FLRA at 621-22 (denying contrary-to-law exceptions challenging legal standard used by arbitrator, where arbitrator resolved grievance under parties' agreement on finding of no just cause for discipline).

Additionally, CBP claims that the Arbitrator's arbitrability determination is contrary to law because he relied on Hess [Hess v. IRS, 892 F.2d 1019 (Fed. Cir. 1989)]. In Hess, the court held that an employee's status "at the time adverse action [i]s taken" is determinative. 892 F.2d at 1020. Consistent with Hess and based on his interpretation of the CBA, the Arbitrator concluded that, as the grievant was a unit employee at the time CBP issued its suspension decision, the grievant was entitled to grieve the decision under the parties' negotiated grievance procedure. The Arbitrator's conclusion is consistent with Hess and there is no basis on which to conclude that the Arbitrator was precluded from applying Hess. Ctr., 11 FLRA 486, 487-88 (1983) (grievance arbitrable where grievant was a unit employee when dispute arose, grievance was filed, and arbitration was invoked, but was promoted to a supervisor before the hearing). See also Gen. Serv. Admin., Region 9, 44 FLRA 901 (1992) (Authority rejected union's claim that arbitrator erred by applying Hess in finding that grievant could not grieve adverse action because he was not a unit employee at the time the action was taken).
