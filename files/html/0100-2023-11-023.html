2. Medical Report, Records

The courts recognize, and the Board accepts, the reliability and probative value of written medical reports. See Brier v. FAA, 6 MSPR 148, 151, 6 MSPB 133 (1981) (Board accepted medical reports despite allegations by an appellant that physicians provided false information to an agency concerning a leave problem). Although a doctor's report is hearsay, it is admissible evidence and its probative value depends on the circumstances of the case. Sanders v. EEOC, 38 MSPR 309, 312, 315 (1988). The persuasiveness of medical reports stems from their explanation of how certain aspects of a particular condition render the employee unable to perform specific work assignments. Behar v. OPM, 28 MSPR 183, 184 (1985); see Whitmer v. OPM, 48 MSPR 312, 315 (1991) ("Although the appellant has pain and discomfort, the medical documentation does not specify any duties of the appellant's former position that she could not perform because of her medical conditions."); Royster v. OPM, 68 MSPR 655, 659 (1995) ("[A] physician's conclusion that an employee is disabled is persuasive only if the physician explains how the medical condition affects the employee's specific work requirements."); Johnson v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 386, 391-92 (1993) (weighing the unsworn statement of one physician who conducted one test against sworn, reasoned medical testimony concerning a diabetic condition).

Of the evidentiary significance of a medical report, Bahm v. Dept. of Air Force, 38 MSPR 627, 632 (1988):

...In assessing the probative weight of a medical opinion, the administrative judge must consider whether the opinion was based on a medical examination, whether the opinion provides a reasoned explanation for its findings as distinct from mere conclusory assertions, the qualifications of the expert rendering the opinion, and the extent and duration of the expert's familiarity with the treatment of the appellant. See Coffey v. Office of Personnel Management, 9 MSPB 504, 10 MSPR 259, 261 (1982).

See Bal v. Dept. of Navy, 729 Fed. Appx. 923, 928 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) (Judge Mayer dissenting) ("As the Board has recognized in other cases, '[w]here, as here, proximity in time, testimony, or other evidence provides the requisite link to the relevant period, the subsequent medical evidence can be very probative of a prior medical condition.' Bowman v. Small Bus. Admin., 122 M.S.P.R. 217, 225 (2015). Thus, the Board erred in discounting Mr. Bal's medical evidence based on its finding that the evidence was not from at or near the time period in question."); Anderson v. OPM, 96 MSPR 299, 304 ¶ 15 (2004) ("The probative value of the evidence from Dr. Dickenson and Dr. Abbott is further diminished by the fact that both of them have been treating the appellant for a relatively short period of time and did not begin treating the appellant until well after the time when she states her disability began and even after she was removed. Medical conclusions based on a long familiarity with a patient are of greater weight than those based on a brief association. Tan-Gatue, 90 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 11; see also Lassiter v. Department of Justice, 60 M.S.P.R. 138, 142 (1993) (one factor in assessing the probative weight of a medical opinion is the extent and duration of the physician's familiarity with the treatment of the appellant).").

"A determination of disability is based on objective clinical findings, diagnoses and expert medical opinions, subjective evidence of pain and disability, and evidence showing the effect of the appellant's condition upon his ability to perform in the grade or class of position last occupied." Treziok v. OPM, 89 MSPR 361, 370 ¶ 20 (2001); see Huffman v. OPM, 92 MSPR 651, 654 ¶ 6 (2002) (separate opinion of Member Slavet: summarizing cases and adding that the appellant's testimony of subjective pain and inability to work must be seriously considered when it is supported by competent medical evidence). But cf. Rainone v. OPM, 102 MSPR 88, ¶ 13, 2006 MSPB 130 (2006) (failure to submit medical reports cannot be the sole reason for denying a disability retirement application). Medical reports not containing reasoned explanations consistent with subjective evidence of pain and inability to perform essential functions of a position do not support a finding of disability. Tanious v. OPM, 34 MSPR 107, 111 (1987); see Gonzales v. OPM, 91 MSPR 46, 49 ¶ 5 (2002) ("[A] medical provider's conclusion that an individual is mentally incompetent is persuasive only if the medical provider explains how a mental illness renders the individual incompetent.").

A report of a physician qualified to give the report will not be discredited only because the physician works for the agency whose adverse action is appealed. See Merzweiler v. USPS, 69 MSPR 274, 280 (1996). Briefly stated clinical findings, generalized diagnoses, and the failure to explain how a condition causes a particular problem may diminish or entirely eliminate the value of a physician's report. Bernard v. OPM, 37 MSPR 486, 489-90 (1988); see Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 283-84 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (psychiatric report: "The terms should at least be defined, and the significance of the diagnosis and prognosis explained in reasonable detail and in a manner that relates both to the employee's current and projected capacity to perform his or her work satisfactorily."); Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 245, 251 (1994) ("Expert opinions that are simply conclusory statements based on an appellant's self-reports rather than on observations of the appellant's physical symptoms are entitled to little weight."). A psychiatric report may carry more weight if it is based on psychological testing and on information beyond what an employee tells her physician. Witt v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 31, 33 (1984). Compare Backus v. OPM, 22 MSPR 457, 461 (1984) (discounting a psychiatric report that did not explain the nature of the examination or the factors considered in reaching opinion), with Broadnax v. USPS, 24 MSPR 319, 324 (1984) (crediting a psychiatric statement when it was supported by a "long period of counseling and evaluation").

The Board tends to discount very recent medical diagnoses; it favors medical evaluations based on treatment of the appellant for a substantial period of time, including a period predating an adverse action. See Frye v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 242, 246 (1994); McKoy v. FCC, 10 MSPR 221, 224 (1982) (little probative value of a report from the psychiatrist who performed no psychological tests and based the diagnosis of a recurrent affective disorder on one interview and the appellant's history of psychiatric hospitalization). If medical reports could be expected to support a contention, e.g., that an individual had been assaulted, the absence of that evidence may detract from the underlying allegations. See Flores v. Dept. of Labor, 13 MSPR 281, 286 (1982) (rape).

Tan-Gatue v. OPM, 90 MSPR 116, 121-22 ¶¶ 11-12 (2001), commented on the relation between a physician's report and his familiarity with the appellant's condition:

The Board has consistently found that medical conclusions based on a long familiarity with a patient are of greater weight than those based on a brief association or single examination. See, e.g., Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPB 343, 6 M.S.P.R. 404, 423 (1981) (the probative value of medical opinion depends in part on "the extent and duration of the expert's familiarity with the treatment of the applicant's condition"). Further, the Board gives greater deference to medical opinions that are supported by reasoned explanations than it gives to mere conclusory assertions. Id. Thus, while the April 1996, note was written after a 6-month course of treatment and is entitled to some weight because of that fact, it is rendered nearly valueless as a result of its brevity and lack of explanation. Indeed, it fails even to state what "severe illness" the appellant suffered from. Conversely, although the October 1995, report is a much more thorough analysis of the appellant's mental status and is entitled to weight for that reason, the fact that it is based on a single visit diminishes its value. Moreover, because the report is based largely on the appellant's version of events, it appears to accept her claim that she had been the victim of harassment by her supervisor.

...We further note, in agreement with OPM, that there is at least some question as to the appellant's credibility, based on the August 26, 1995, report of Geoffrey Miller, M.D., who stated that the appellant "apparently is searching for some doctor who will conclude that she cannot work on a medical basis." OPM also correctly notes that the Board has considered an appellant's failure to apply for disability retirement until after a removal action as a factor discounting the force of that application. See Burckley v. Office of Personnel Management, 80 M.S.P.R. 617 ¶ 8 (1999) (probative weight of the appellant's affidavit is "reduced by the fact that he did not consider applying for disability retirement until after receiving the agency's notice of proposed removal, and also by the fact that there is no evidence from the agency of a service deficiency"), modified on other grounds by Sangenito v. Office of Personnel Management, 85 M.S.P.R. 211 (2000).

Rapp v. OPM, 108 MSPR 674, 680-81 ¶ 11, 2008 MSPB 107 (2008), added:

[P]robative value of medical opinions may depend in part upon such factors as the qualifications of the expert rendering the opinion, and the extent and duration of the expert's familiarity with or treatment of the applicant's condition. Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 M.S.P.R. 404, 423 (1981). Here, Dr. Adams testified, and OPM does not dispute, that she has been treating the appellant for several years. Medical conclusions based on a long familiarity with a patient are of greater weight than those based on a brief association. Tan-Gatue v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 116, ¶ 11 (2001), aff'd, 52 Fed. Appx. 511 (Fed. Cir. 2002). "While OPM and the MSPB may give only limited weight to seemingly strong medical evidence..., it typically does so only in the face of factors such as doubts about professional competence, contrary medical evidence, failure of the professional to consider relevant factors, [or] lack of particularity in relating diagnosis to nature and extent of disability." Vanieken-Ryals v. Office of Personnel Management, 508 F.3d 1034, 1042 (Fed. Cir. 2007); see, e.g., Heiter v. Office of Personnel Management, 107 M.S.P.R. 514, ¶ 7 (2007) (finding medical opinion testimony probative where such testimony is unrefuted by countervailing testimony). In the absence of such factors, the administrative judge incorrectly substituted his views as to what pattern of behavior could or could not result from the appellant's mental condition for those of Dr. Adams and Dr. Jimenez.
