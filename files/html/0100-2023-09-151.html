c. Procedures for Obtaining Dismissal; Refiling

The DWOP is usually initiated by the appellant's motion, although current regulations allow the DWOP at the initiative of either party or the AJ. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Motion Practice."] Under practice prior to the regulation, the dismissal could be at the initiative of the AJ, that is, sua sponte. Gidwani v. VA, 74 MSPR 509, 511 (1997). Nothing prevented an agency from suggesting to the AJ, with notice to the appellant, the desirability of the AJ initiating the DWOP. To secure a DWOP, the AJ's approval must be obtained in advance of the dismissal and everyone must be clear on the nature of the dismissal and the steps required to reinstitute the appeal. The DWOP motion is approved through an initial decision dismissing the appeal without prejudice, with notice of administrative (PFR) and judicial review rights. The initial decision closes the case out, unless there is a PFR or judicial review of the decision, until the appeal is refiled. The AJ may enter a DWOP as a result of a conference call if the parties are in agreement or good cause is shown for the DWOP; the discussion in the conference call is essentially an oral motion, allowed during a prehearing conference. 5 CFR 1201.55 (2022).

The refiling is readily accomplished by letter or pleading, addressed to the AJ originally assigned to the case, or e-filed, stating that the appeal is refiled, with service on the opposing party's representative. See Strausbaugh v. MSPB, 401 Fed. Appx. 524, 526 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (normal practice is to refile with the AJ originally assigned to the appeal). The appeal file for the original appeal is, according to MSPB guidance to regional directors of June 16, 1994, to be retained in the regional or field office so that it can be incorporated into any refiled appeal file. According to that policy guidance, and as reflected in the initial decisions issued in most cases dismissing appeals without prejudice to their later refiling, as well as in acknowledgment orders issued after appeals are refiled, documents submitted in the original appeal should not be resubmitted with the refiled appeal. E-filing simplifies the process (the appeal records are on the system).

A dismissal of the appeal without prejudice is error in this case because the record is not clear that the appellant intended to apply for a disability retirement or requested that his appeal be dismissed without prejudice. See generally McCarty v. Environmental Protection Agency, 108 M.S.P.R. 45, ¶ 15 (2008) (the administrative judge's failure to document the results of the prehearing conference meant none of his rulings were matters of record in the appeal). Moreover, the absence of a documented record of the matters discussed at the prehearing conference, in particular the appellant's purported agreement to dismiss his appeal, deprived the appellant of any opportunity to object to the administrative judge's conclusions from the prehearing conference. Id. Because the initial decision in the appellant's refiled appeal was based entirely on the initial decision in the first appeal, neither of the decisions is supported by the record.

Further, there is a question as to whether the appellant voluntarily agreed to the dismissal of his case. Shortly after the administrative judge issued the initial decision in the first appeal, the appellant filed a purported petition for review challenging it. Thereafter, in his response to the show cause order in the refiled appeal, he argued that the initial decision in his first appeal deprived him of his right to a hearing on the merits of his removal, and he expressed his desire to proceed with his case. These filings suggest two possibilities that the administrative judge did not consider in her initial decision in the refiled appeal--that the appellant did not actually voluntarily agree to dismiss his case or that he did not understand (or was not informed of) the consequences of agreeing to dismiss his case. Dismissing his case under such circumstances is contrary to the requirements of due process. See Gingery, 111 M.S.P.R. 134, ¶ 9. The Board has held that a dismissal without prejudice should not become a trap to deny an appellant the opportunity to have his case decided on the merits. Jaramillo v. Department of the Air Force, 106 M.S.P.R. 244, ¶ 6 (2007).

The AJ should monitor the parties' submissions to determine their intent. When the parties entered a "dismissal agreement" and submitted it to the Board, stating that the appellant withdrew his appeal and could refile it after a verdict in a criminal case was reached, their agreement was not a settlement of the appeal. Because the parties do not have the authority to waive timeliness requirements of Board regulations, the AJ should have considered the parties' dismissal agreement to be a request that the appeal be dismissed without prejudice to later refiling. See Rice v. Dept. of Treasury, 48 MSPR 288, 290 (1991). An AJ may err in treating a motion for DWOP as some other type of procedure. In Yacher v. DHHS, 80 MSPR 68 (1998), the agency filed a PFR from an initial decision that dismissed an appeal as settled. The agency demonstrated that the appeal was not settled; rather, it was to have been DWOP'd to permit the parties the opportunity to obtain additional evidence concerning the appellant's disability. When the error was brought to the Board's attention through the PFR in Yacher, the Board dismissed the appeal without prejudice, as the parties requested the AJ to do.
