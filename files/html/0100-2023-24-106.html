A. AUTHORITY TO REMAND

Remand authority is found at 28 USC 2106:

The Supreme Court or any other court of appellate jurisdiction may affirm, modify, vacate, set aside or reverse any judgment, decree, or order of a court lawfully brought before it for review, and may remand the cause and direct the entry of such appropriate judgment, decree, or order, or require such further proceedings to be had as may be just under the circumstances.

A circuit court does not ordinarily order a particular remedy. It will remand the case to the Board to enter the remedy suggested or directed, or remand to let the Board decide what to do with the appeal if the court has not directed something specific. The court also has the authority to remand a case to an arbitrator. See Pyles v. MSPB, 45 F.3d 411, 414 n.3 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (retirement benefits appeal: where the petitioner's delay in filing an MSPB appeal should have been excused by the Board, the court will reverse the Board's decision and remand the case for a hearing of the underlying appeal on its merits); Gunn v. VAMC, 892 F.2d 1036, 1040 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (in an arbitration case, delay did not justify arbitrator's refusal to consider grievance; the case was remanded).

The Supreme Court favors remands over appellate creativity, Florida Power & Light Co. v. Lorion, 470 U.S. 729, 744 (1985):

If the record before the agency does not support the agency action, if the agency has not considered all relevant factors, or if the reviewing court simply cannot evaluate the challenged agency action on the basis of the record before it, the proper course, except in rare circumstances, is to remand to the agency for additional investigation or explanation. The reviewing court is not generally empowered to conduct a de novo inquiry into the matter being reviewed and to reach its own conclusions based upon such an inquiry.

Caci, Inc.-Federal v. United States, ___F.4th___ (Fed. Cir. 2023), advises that:

In the context of appeals from other agency decisions, the Supreme Court has held that, "[g]enerally speaking, a [reviewing court] should remand a case to an agency for decision of a matter that statutes place primarily in agency hands." INS v. Orlando Ventura, 537 U.S. 12, 16 (2002) (per curiam). This is because "[t]he reviewing court is not generally empowered to conduct a de novo inquiry into the matter being reviewed and to reach its own conclusions based on such an inquiry." Fla. Power & Light Co. v. Lorion, 470 U.S. 729, 744 (1985).

See Colicelli v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP) (remanding on the apparent agreement of the parties that VA failed to supply in response to the appeal information necessary to adjudicate a claim to military leave).

The Board stated its role upon receiving a remand in Hoover v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 545, 552 (1993):

Further, the Board's reconsideration of the laches issue is barred by the application of the law of the case doctrine, specifically by the "mandate rule." Briefly stated, that rule means that an inferior tribunal is bound to honor the decisions of superior tribunals within a single judicial system. See generally 1B Moore's Federal Practice (1993 Supp.) § 0.404(10); 18 C. Wright, A. Miller & E. Cooper Federal Practice And Procedure: Law of the Case § 4478 (1981). As one court has cogently put it: "The 'mandate rule,' as it is known, is nothing more than a specific application of the 'law of the case' doctrine." Piambino v. Bailey, 757 F.2d 1112, 1120 (11th Cir. 1985), cert. denied sub nom., Hoffman v. Sylva, 476 U.S. 1169, 106 S. Ct. 2889, 90 L. Ed.2d 976 (1986). The Board is bound by the mandates of the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit because that is the judicial review authority for Board decisions, except those Board decisions involving discrimination issues. See 5 USC 7703(b)(1)-(2). Decisions of the Federal Circuit are controlling authority on the Board. Fairall v. Veterans Administration, 33 MSPR 33 (1987), aff'd, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

On remand, the Board decides the issue remanded, not other issue then raised by the appellant. See Melton v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-09-0448-M-1 (NP 4/21/2022) ("We will not consider the appellant's allegations of other improprieties, some of which previously were raised and rejected, because they all exceed the scope of the court's remand order. Melton, 664 F. App'x at 915; see, e.g., Zelenka v. Office of Personnel Management, 110 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 15 n.3 (2008) (refusing to address an appellant's arguments that exceeded the scope of the issues to be addressed on remand), rev'd on other grounds, 361 F. App'x 138 (Fed. Cir. 2010); Umshler v. Department of the Interior, 55 M.S.P.R. 593, 597 (1992) (finding that an administrative judge properly limited the scope of remand proceedings, consistent with the Federal Circuit's remand order), aff'd, 6 F.3d 788 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).).

The Federal Circuit does not always remand cases to the Board without explicit direction to the Board concerning the future course of the appeal. The circuit can and will direct a remedy if the case is clear. Horne v. MSPB, 684 F.2d 155, 158 n.4 (D.C. Cir. 1982); see Rogers v. OPM, 87 F.3d 471, 476 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (citing Meek v. Metropolitan Dade County, 985 F.2d 1471, 1478 (11th Cir. 1993), for the proposition that there is "no need to remand when the record clearly shows that only one result would satisfy the standard of review"). Sullivan v. Dept. of Navy, 720 F.2d 1266, 1276 n.3 (Fed. Cir. 1983), determined that the Federal Circuit had the authority to instruct the MSPB to order reinstatement of an employee with back pay and benefits. Hoska v. Dept. of Army, 677 F.2d 131 (D.C. Cir. 1982), concluded that dismissal was unsupported by substantial evidence. The court instructed the Board to award appropriate relief, including reissuance of the petitioner's security clearance, reinstatement, and back pay (the clearance restoration would not likely occur after the Supreme Court decision in the Egan case (discussed in Chapter 8, "Review of Agency Determination; Egan; Constitutional Due Process") . See Costello v. MSPB, 182 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (court reversed Board decision and directed cancelation of adverse action for lack of substantial evidence supporting the charges); Evans v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3174) (appeal from the MSPB, directing the Navy, on remand, to offer the petitioner retroactive restoration, back pay, and benefits).

When mitigation is at issue and the Board did not consider all the relevant factors, the Federal Circuit may size up the case and decide whether mitigation is warranted, remanding the case to the Board if necessary to accomplish the judicial will. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Penalty Determination."] The directions given to the Board on remand are binding. The court noted in a trademark case, In re Wella A.G., 858 F.2d 725, 728 (Fed. Cir. 1988):

An "inferior court has no power or authority to deviate from the mandate issued by an appellate court." Briggs v. Pennsylvania R.R., 334 U.S. 304, 306 (1948), quoted with approval in Northern Helex Co. v. United States, 225 Ct. Cl. 194, 634 F.2d 557 (Ct. Cl. 1980). The rule is equally applicable to the duty of an administrative agency, such as the Board, to comply with the mandate issued by a reviewing court. Federal Power Commission v. Pacific Co., 307 U.S. 156, 160 (1939).

Where an issue of Board jurisdiction is before the court, the court will make the jurisdictional determination when it can do so rather than remanding the case for a jurisdictional finding by the Board. See Yunus v. VA, 242 F.3d 1367, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 2001). But cf. McLaughlin v. MSPB, 853 Fed. Appx. 648 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) (Judge Newman concurring in part and dissenting in part) ("McLaughlin suggests that, rather than remand for the Board to correct its mistakes and consider the complete record again, we should assess the Board's jurisdiction on appeal. As a court of review, we decline to do so. It is for the Board, in the first instance, to consider whether McLaughlin has established by a preponderance of the evidence, in light of all correct legal standards, that the Board has jurisdiction over one or more of her claims.").

The court may remand to the Board as a result of some startling development during oral argument or briefing, allowing the Board to have the first opportunity to deal with the resulting tangle. See Zukowski v. Dept. of Commerce (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 90-3145) (remand after agency counsel disclosed, during the course of an appeal, that there may not have been a need for the appellant to fill out the financial disclosure statement when that failing led to the adverse action). The court returned an appeal to the Board when the government informed the court that, while the judicial appeal was pending, the Board reduced the penalty of a different appellant, Mr. Swan, who, the appellant asserted before the Board, was a comparator, Edwards v. USPS, 662 Fed. Appx. 951, 953-54 (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP):

We generally will not review extra-record evidence not presented to the MSPB. See Wilkes v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 644 Fed. Appx. 1015, 1019-20 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (Wallach, J., concurring); see also Oshiver v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 896 F.2d 540, 542 (Fed. Cir. 1990); Resp't's Br. 18 n.3 ("Although we recognize that this [c]ourt does not generally review evidence which was not presented to the [MSPB] ...we include as an addendum the MSPB's initial decision for Manager Swan...."). However, when factual information necessary to a final decision was unavailable during the administrative proceeding and later becomes available or changes while on appeal, we have found it appropriate to vacate and remand the administrative decision under review. See, e.g., Williams v. Soc. Sec. Admin., 586 F.3d 1365, 1368-69 (Fed. Cir. 2009) (vacating and remanding to the MSPB following Government counsel's admission of new evidence related to potential disparate treatment of a comparator that was not on record); Gregory v. U.S. Postal Serv., 30 Fed. Appx. 955, 957-58 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (remanding to the MSPB after a grievance pending at the time of the initial decision was decided in appellant's favor such that "further assessment of the correct penalty" was warranted).

A remand is appropriate in the instant appeal because the MSPB necessarily must consider Swan to ascertain the appropriateness of the penalty imposed upon Mr. Edwards. In Douglas, the MSPB set forth the relevant factors to consider when assessing the appropriateness of a penalty in an administrative proceeding related to adverse employment action. See 5 M.S.P.B. at 331-32. One of those factors asks the DO to consider the "consistency of the penalty with those imposed upon other employees for the same or similar offenses." Id. at 332. The MSPB has recognized that this factor "is of sufficient weight that it may warrant an outcome different from that of the initial decision." Lewis v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 2009 M.S.P.B. 96, ¶ 8 (2009). In particular, the MSPB has stated that, in the case of alleged disparate treatment between comparable employees, "the agency must prove a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment by a preponderance of the evidence." Id. We agree and therefore remand so that the MSPB may reassess Mr. Edwards's penalty in light of Swan.

Remand is not limited to reconsideration of a result. If the reasoning of the decision under review is unclear or incomplete, a remand may be ordered so that the decision can be rewritten. See Edwards v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP No. 02-3170) ("[W]e find the record in this case unduly sparse, and are left with an uneasy feeling about it.... [W]e believe the proper course is to vacate the underlying decisions of the Board in this matter, and remand the case to the Board for further proceedings, aimed at producing a more complete and coherent record."). If an arbitrator fails to resolve the charges against an employee, the court may remand the case for a more complete statement. AFGE and Purdie v. Defense Gen. Supply Ctr. (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3458); see Hardy v. USPS, 76 F.3d 366, 368 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (case was remanded because the court could not determine the basis for the Board's disposition of the appeal).

Remand may be required for the Board to make factual findings essential to resolve the timeliness of an appeal. See Ahuruonye v. Dept. of Interior, 690 Fed. Appx. 670, 675 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP) ("Where the Board has not made adequate findings on important components of the required analysis, we may vacate and remand for further findings. See Whitmore, 680 F.3d at 1372-75; Holderfield v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 326 F.3d 1207, 1209 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Whittington v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 80 F.3d 471, 476 (Fed. Cir. 1996)."); Whittington v. MSPB, 80 F.3d 471, 476 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ("We can neither reverse nor affirm the Board's decision because either action would require us to apply law the Board did not consider to facts the Board did not consider; such a complete de novo review is not our proper role and would deprive the parties of the process they are due."). The court may remand for further consideration of a procedural issue--the nature of the OPM reconsideration process--and the Board may then be called upon to determine whether the court's decision affects the underlying disposition of the appeal on the merits. See York v. USPS, 18 MSPR 517, 518 (1983) (remand did not affect merits disposition). The court may vacate the Board's decision and then remand the case to the Board. Should the Board's decision be vacated, the petition for review of the initial decision is once again before the Board. See Schmittling v. Dept. of Army, 92 MSPR 572, 585-86 ¶ 29 (2002).

Judicial review can bring unexpected results. In Abrams v. Dept. of Navy, 714 F.2d 1219 (3rd Cir. 1983), the employee was discharged for both violent conduct in an off-duty incident and unauthorized absence. The Board found no nexus between the off-duty conduct and service efficiency, but sustained the discharge for absenteeism. The employee appealed to the circuit court. No appeal or cross-appeal was filed by the Navy (or OPM). The court determined that the Board erred as to its finding of nexus, concluding that there was a presumption of nexus in the situation. The case was remanded to the Board to see if the petitioner had rebutted the presumption. Essentially the employee's appeal resulted in a remand to the Board to strengthen its decision against the employee, a result criticized in a dissenting opinion. On remand, the Board concluded there was nexus and reaffirmed the dismissal. The Federal Circuit later affirmed the Board's decision on remand. Abrams v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-733) [discussed in Chapter 7 under "Shooting; Killing"] . But see Bennett v. Dept. of Navy, 699 F.2d 1140 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (court would not consider enhancing a penalty without a cross-petition for judicial review by the government).

The MSPB may request a remand from the court if the Board believes that its decision needs more attention or if the Board decides the case will not survive judicial review without correction of fundamental errors. The Board may request a remand for further proceedings without intervening in the Federal Circuit litigation. Tompkins v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3605). A court may grant a remand request without the consent of the petitioner, but the petitioner's views will be considered. One concern is the amount of time the Board may take to consider the case on remand. An appellant petitioned the Federal Circuit to review denial of a counsel fee award in Woodall v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. Docket No. 86-1304). The Board then sought and received a remand. Although appellant's counsel requested that the court direct the Board to decide the case within four months, the court did not impose a time limitation. It took the Board about a year to decide the case, despite several letters from appellant's counsel to the Board's chairman urging prompter action. The appeal was already several years into the litigation process at the point that it went into court.

On occasion the court may avoid a remand and the court may itself correct an error in a Board decision when the correction may be of significance to an appellant but the correction does not alter the result of the appeal. See Lazaro v. MSPB, 306 Fed. Appx. 592 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (court noted in its decision affirming the Board that the AJ too narrowly described the scope of the VEOA appeal). But the court may decide that, despite a concession by the Board that it erred in some significant aspect of the decision, it will not decide the case, particularly if the facts are jurisdictional. That occurred in Benton v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP No. 2015-3004), a whistleblower case, intoning that "a court generally may review an agency's decision only on the grounds 'upon which the record discloses that its action was based.' Securities & Exchange Comm'n v. Chenery Corp., 318 U.S. 80, 87 (1943)." The court remanded to the Board for "further proceedings."
