d. Technical Constructions

Now for a few (more) technical constructions.

Unless otherwise provided by law, or in a case starting out through the EEO process, an appellant must file her appeal no later than the later of 30 days after the effective date of the action being appealed or 30 days after the date she receives the agency's decision. 5 CFR § 1201.22(b) (2022). [Refer later in this chapter to "Premature Appeals."]

If the time period is calculated from the effective date of an action, the effective date of an action, not the date of a decision letter, controls the appeal period. The Board does not have jurisdiction over an agency action that has not yet occurred. See Nunley v. USPS, 52 MSPR 442, 446 (1992). In Mathis v. Dept. of Treasury, 99 MSPR 231, 232 ¶ 5 (2005), the decision notice set July 9 as the effective date, but the personnel department told the appellant that the effective date was July 16, and the SF-50 bore the latter date. July 16 was the date that controlled the timing of the appeal. [Refer to Chapter 13, "Threatened Personnel Action," for appeals of threatened actions under the Whistleblower Protection Act.] The effective date of a reduction in pay is the date the appellant begins to be paid at a lower rate. McGinnis v. USPS, 49 MSPR 521, 524 (1991) (date the pay reduction begins is also the date used to calculate whether the employee met tenure requirements necessary to the Board's jurisdiction); Allen v. DHUD, 4 MSPR 264, 265, 4 MSPB 338 (1980) (intended effective date of the retroactive denial of a within grade increase was unclear in the agency's notice letter). The effective date of a voluntarily-initiated demotion, not the date of the notice advising the appellant of the demotion, controls the initiation of the filing period. Rivas v. USPS, 57 MSPR 489, 493 (1993). [Refer later in this chapter to "Constructive Adverse Action Without Notice of Employee's Protest to Agency."]

Croteau v. VA, PH-0752-13-5343-I-1 (NP 7/7/2014), explained that an amended adverse action runs from any later effective date of that action, as amended:

A Board appeal must be filed no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b). Here, the agency's original decision letter of June 20, 2013, set an effective date of June 30, 2013, for the appellant's reduction in grade. The due date for any appeal would ordinarily have been July 30, 2013. However, due to administrative error, the agency's subsequently-issued letter of July 16, 2013, was the equivalent of an amended decision as it was signed by an agency official and modified a fundamental feature of the June 20, 2013 decision letter, namely, the effective date of the appellant's reduction in grade. See McGarrity v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 610, ¶ 7 (2006); cf. Mathis v. Department of the Treasury, 99 M.S.P.R. 231, ¶ 5 (2005) (where the agency changed the effective date of the appellant's removal to a date later than the one specified in the removal decision, the latter effective date controlled for purposes of determining the Board's filing deadline); accord Suber v. Department of the Navy, 19 M.S.P.R. 498, 499 (1984).

It is undisputed that the agency changed the effective date of the appellant's reduction in grade to July 14, 2103. Therefore, the due date for filing an appeal would have been August 13, 2013. See Mathis, 99 M.S.P.R. 231, ¶ 5. However, it is also undisputed that the appellant did not receive notice of this change until July 18, 2013. The appellant filed her appeal on August 15, 2013, fewer than 30 days after she received the amended decision. The appeal was therefore timely filed. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b); see McGarrity, 103 M.S.P.R. 610, ¶ 8.

Whether an amendment of an adverse action notice extends the date for filing the appeal appears to depend on whether the amendment affects the effective date of the adverse action. Demps v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 90-3316), determined that if an adverse action decision states an effective date, the effective date controls the period for filing a Board appeal. Even if a later letter is issued containing additional information pertaining to the position to be assumed by appellant through demotion, as long as the letter does not alter the effective date of the demotion, the letter does not alter the period first established for filing the appeal. If the appellant wishes to timely challenge a RIF, the appeal should be filed within the regulatory period running from the date of the action, e.g., a demotion, caused by the RIF. The agency's later error concerning repromotion rights does not extend the time for challenging the underlying RIF. See Cochis v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3386).

The effective date of the action is that date, not the next day, as Washington v. GPO, DC-0752-10-0245-I-1 (NP 10/15/2010), explained at n.1:

During the proceeding below, the appellant's representative sought to excuse the untimely appeal by contending that he believed that because the termination was effected on a Saturday, the filing period would begin on the first business day thereafter, i.e., Monday, December 7, 2009. As fully explained in his initial decision, the administrative judge properly rejected this interpretation of the Board's filing regulations as "plainly wrong," and noted that the appellant "suggests no law, regulation, labor-management agreement language, agency policy or anything in the agency's decision letter and enclosures that might support his erroneous interpretation or could reasonably create confusion on his part." The Board has long recognized that a representative's negligence resulting in an untimely filing does not constitute good cause, even if the delay is minimal. See, e.g., Lands v. Department of the Air Force, 95 M.S.P.R. 593, ¶¶ 2-3, 7 (2004) (oversight by the appellant's attorney did not establish good cause for his one-day delay in filing the petition for review); McBurnett v. Department of the Army, 37 M.S.P.R. 395, 396-97 (1988) (appellant failed to show good cause for one-day delay in the filing of his appeal, based on attorney's misinterpretation of the appellant's effective date of removal).

As to holidays, 5 CFR 1201.23 (2022) allows the computation of time for appeal to exclude a holiday falling on the last day of the appeal period. However, the regulation includes only federal (U.S.) holidays, not holidays of another country. See Blevins v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 487, 488 (1984) (still forgiving the delay). But see Sanpedro v. OPM, 45 MSPR 503, 504 (1990) (late PFR was excused when the Board recognized and allowed for a foreign holiday in the Philippines). The regulation now provides that if the last day for filing falls on a weekend, the filing date is extended to Monday (unless it is a holiday). See Pope v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 756, 758 n.1 (1984); 5 CFR 1201.23 (2022). The regulation also applies if a Board order sets a filing date. Should the specified filing date fall on a weekend, Monday is the deadline. See Diarra v. USPS, 81 MSPR 630, 632 ¶ 4 (1999) (applying the rule to the refiling of an appeal that was dismissed without prejudice to refile).

Concerning appeals timed from the date of receipt of an agency decision, there is a presumption applied as to the date of receipt from a decision that is mailed, but the presumption is rebuttable, Fouchia v. OPM, 108 MSPR 271, 273-74 ¶¶ 6-7, 2008 MSPB 50 (2008):

In the absence of evidence to the contrary, an appellant is presumed to have received an agency's final decision 5 calendar days after the decision was issued. Williamson v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 7 (2007). However, when an appellant submits an appeal form which includes a certification that the statements therein are true, the allegations in the form may serve as evidence to rebut any presumption regarding the date that the appellant received a mailing from the agency. Geier v. Department of the Treasury, 90 M.S.P.R. 186, ¶ 8 (2001); Foust v. Department of the Treasury, 80 M.S.P.R. 477, ¶ 6 (1998), review dismissed, 194 F.3d 1332 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table).

On her appeal form, the contents of which she certified were true, the appellant alleged that she received OPM's final decision on June 3, 2007. OPM failed to rebut this allegation. Based on this evidence, we find that the appellant received OPM's final decision on June 3, 2007. The filing deadline in this case was therefore July 3, 2007, the 30th day after June 3, 2007. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.23 ("In computing the number of days allowed for filing a submission, the first day counted is the day after the event from which the time period begins to run.").

[Refer later in this chapter to "Significance of Postmark: Presumptive Filing Date"; "Significance of Actual Mailing Date; Mailstream Rule: Actual Filing Date."]

An appeal must be filed for each action challenged. If an individual is subjected to an appealable adverse action that is placed before the Board on a PFR, and then another appealable action is taken against the appellant, he must file a separate appeal rather than combine the two cases in the PFR. Mitchell v. Dept. of Air Force, 7 MSPR 395, 396 n.3, 7 MSPB 271 (1981); see Farrell v. DOJ, 56 MSPR 188, 191 (1993) (appellant should have filed a separate appeal from the alleged denial of restoration rights after a full recovery from a job-related injury, rather than seeking to litigate that issue through compliance proceedings concerning an appeal from the denial of restoration rights following a partial recovery). An appellant may not contest the termination of a later, temporary appointment to secure review of whether he was earlier properly terminated from a probationary position, if he previously had the opportunity to appeal the earlier termination and either did not do so or had that issue decided against him by the Board. Armed v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3212). Exceptions to the usual rule of one action, one appeal, occasionally arise. If there is a series of removal actions, necessitated by successive and successful challenges to prior actions, the Board may consider the latest removal action during remand proceedings rather than considering it as part of a separate appeal. Bruning v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3249). If the appellant reasonably believes that two actions have been accepted for adjudication, good cause may exist for the belated filing of a subsequent appeal when the regional office announces that the actions will not be treated as one. Bascom v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 295, 298 (1984). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Indefinite Suspension" and discussion later in this chapter, "Indefinite Suspension; Enforced Leave."]

In class actions, rare in Board annals, an individual has 35 days to file an appeal from the date of denial of a class action. 5 CFR 1201.27(b) (2022). Law predating the current regulations suggested that when an appeal is filed as a class action and the motion for class certification is denied, the time for filing appeals by individuals other than the class representatives (the named appellants) is tolled only to the extent of the amount of time that remained from the date the original appeal was filed to the last day that the appeal could have been filed. Allman v. DHUD, 21 MSPR 531 (1984). But see Carey v. Dept. of Interior, 103 MSPR 534, 2006 MSPB 295 (2006) (imposing what amounts to a constructive "opt-in" procedure for nonrepresentative persons encompassed within the class of an EEO complaint who wait out the EEO process and then seek to obtain hearings before the MSPB). [Refer to "Class Action" later in this chapter.]

To reiterate the regulation governing ordinary Board appeals: "an appeal must be filed no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of the appellant's receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later." 5 CFR 1201.22(b)(1) (2022). In Board practice, all "days" are calendar and not working days. 5 CFR 1201.4(h) (2022). [Decisions cited in this and other sections of the Guide referencing shorter filing periods were decided before filing periods were increased by changes in the Board's regulations in 1994.] The time period for filing an appeal, if the appellant previously filed a discrimination complaint on the appealable action with his agency, is 30 days from receipt of the final agency decision on the EEO case. If the final decision has not been issued within 120 days after the filing of the complaint, then the appeal must be any time after the 120th day up to 30 days from receipt of the final agency decision on the EEO complaint. 5 CFR 1201.154 (2022). [Refer to Chapter 12, "Timeliness of EEO Complaint," for more extensive discussion of EEO filing deadlines.] The 30-day (previously 20-day) filing period for appeal set by 5 CFR 1201.154 has the effect and force of law. Thalhamer v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 88-3287). Appeals of cases under 5 CFR 1201.155(b) (2022), involving an appeal to the Board from a grievance decision, must be lodged with the Clerk of the Board "within 35 days after the date of issuance of the decision or, if the appellant shows that he or she received the decision more than 5 days after the date of issuance, within 30 days after the date the appellant received the decision." It is immaterial to the timeliness of the Board appeal that the arbitrator's decision contained no statement of Board appeal rights. Churchill v. DCAA, 23 MSPR 699 (1984) (Table). But see Kirkland v. DHS, 119 MSPR 74, 78-81 ¶¶ 4-9, 2013 MSPB 3 (2013) (timeliness was waived when the agency failed to identify in its decision letter the time for seeking review of an arbitration award). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Governing Statute and Regulation," and "Time to Seek Review; Notice of Review Rights," for filing deadlines in cases challenging arbitration awards.]

Under earlier regulations, an appellant could file an appeal 35, sometimes 25, days from the date OPM issued a reconsideration decision. See Gerber v. OPM, 57 MSPR 572, 576 (1993) ("The appellant did not explain why she mistakenly believed that the 25-day filing period ran from the date that she received the reconsideration decision, rather from the November 19, 1992, date of the reconsideration decision. We note that OPM informed her in the reconsideration decision itself that the date tolling the 25-day deadline for filing with the Board was the date of the reconsideration decision."); Morton v. OPM, 46 MSPR 514, 515 (1990) (the statutory deadline was then 25 days). [Refer to appeals from OPM reconsideration decisions in this chapter, "Problem Caused by Adjudicators; Misdirected Appeal."]

These "simple" requirements governing the timeliness of appeals breed many problems and have spawned considerable case law concerning acceptance of untimely appeals.
