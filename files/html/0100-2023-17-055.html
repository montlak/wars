3. Conduct of Interest Arbitration; Ground Rules

FSIP employs interest arbitration to resolve negotiation impasses. The process of interest arbitration was described generally by Arvid Anderson in Presenting an Interest Arbitration Case: An Arbitrator's View, 3 Labor Lawyer 745 (ABA; Fall 1987):

Interest arbitration is different than grievance arbitration. Interest arbitration concerns the making of the contract, while grievance arbitration concerns the interpretation of the contract. Interest arbitration is essentially a legislative process, while grievance arbitration is essentially a judicial process. The making of a contract, i.e., the conditions of employment, by interest arbitration is a quite different function than interpreting a contract to determine whether the conditions of employment have been breached.

Most frequently, interest arbitration is imposed by FSIP. But the parties may agree among themselves to undertake interest arbitration. If they do so, they should obtain approval of the procedure from the Panel. Before exceptions to resulting awards are within the jurisdiction of FLRA, the FSIP must have approved the interest arbitration procedure used by the parties. AFGE, Nat'l Council of EEOC Locals, Local 216 and EEOC, 36 FLRA 9, 16 (1990) (noting also that if the awards are advisory rather than binding, there is no basis for FLRA to review exceptions).

The roles of the interest arbitrator and the FSIP concerning negotiability issues are discussed in Chapter 4. [Refer to Chapter 4, "Interest Arbitrators."] FLRA restated the essential principles governing interest arbitration, Commander, Carswell AFB and AFGE Local 1364, 31 FLRA 620, 622-23 (1988):

Interest arbitrators are not authorized to make negotiability rulings in order to resolve questions concerning the duty to bargain under the Statute.... In cases where the award is set aside because an interest arbitrator improperly asserted jurisdiction over and decided a duty to bargain question, we may require the parties to resume negotiations over the matter....

If a proposal raising a duty to bargain issue is presented to an interest arbitrator and that issue cannot be resolved on the basis of existing Authority case law, the arbitrator may not impose that proposal. If the arbitrator determines that he or she can resolve the duty to bargain issue and does so in an award, we will consider the following questions in order to determine whether the arbitrator applied existing Authority case law in resolving an impasse: (1) was the proposal raising the duty to bargain issue substantively identical to one which was previously addressed by the Authority? (2) were the parties' contentions before the arbitrator similar to ones addressed by the Authority in previous cases? (3) did the arbitrator cite and discuss applicable Authority case law and other relevant precedent? and (4) are there any other considerations which lead to a conclusion that the arbitrator correctly or incorrectly considered the duty to bargain issue?

We expect interest arbitrators, when presented with claims that matters are outside the duty to bargain, to consider these questions and to address them in their awards in order to provide the parties and the Authority with the basis on which they have resolved the claim. The parties likewise have an obligation to provide interest arbitrators with relevant precedent on particular duty to bargain issues. Consistent with our case law as set forth above, if exceptions to an interest arbitration award are filed with the Authority, we will examine the record to determine whether the arbitrator applied existing Authority precedent to resolve an impasse. If an examination of the award supports the conclusion that the arbitrator applied existing precedent, we will resolve the exceptions on the merits by determining whether the arbitrator correctly applied the precedent.

Interest arbitration imposed by the FSIP is subject to the significant jurisdictional limitation that the interest arbitrator should not entertain proposals from the union that have not earlier been bargained to impasse between the parties. See POPA v. FLRA, 26 F.3d 1148, 1155-56 (D.C. Cir. 1994) [discussed in Chapter 7, "Directed Interest Arbitration," on impasse proceedings].

Interest arbitration may be used to resolve disputes over ground rules. If an arbitrator issues an interest award setting ground rules, assuming retained jurisdiction, the arbitrator may then settle related disputes over matters including ratification procedures. An arbitrator interpreting a ground rules agreement could properly decide that the agreement constituted a waiver of the union's right to submit a contract to the membership for ratification. Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org., MEBA and Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, Nat'l Weather Serv., 32 FLRA 628, 631 (1988).
