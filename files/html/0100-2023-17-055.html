c. Loss of Productivity; Undue Interruption

Current regulations tie both competitive level and assignment right determinations to the concept of "undue interruption." 5 CFR 351.403(a)(1) (2022) (competitive level) (reprinted in this chapter, "Defined," under "Competitive Level"); 5 CFR 351.702(a)(4) (2022) (assignment rights). The definition of "undue interruption" appears at 5 CFR 351.203 (2022):

Undue interruption means a degree of interruption that would prevent the completion of required work by the employee 90 days after the employee has been placed in a different position under this part. The 90-day standard should be considered within the allowable limits of time and quality, taking into account the pressures of priorities, deadlines, and other demands. However, a work program would generally not be unduly interrupted even if an employee needed more than 90 days after the reduction in force to perform the optimum quality or quantity of work. The 90-day standard may be extended if placement is made under this part to a low priority program or to a vacant position.

Through 1986, considerations of "undue disruption" (a phrase described in 5 CFR 351.403 as loss of productivity and more recently regulatorily recoined "undue interruption") concerning establishment of a competitive level were the same as those involving the right to assignment. See Porter v. Dept. of Commerce, 13 MSPR 177, 179-80 (1982). Matters remained the same despite some revisions in OPM RIF regulations during 1986. Marcinowsky v. GSA, 35 MSPR 6, 7-10 (1987), involving a challenge to a competitive level definition, determined:

In arriving at his decision, the administrative judge properly cited the current regulation which states, at 5 CFR § 351.403(a), that:

Each agency shall establish competitive levels consisting of all positions in a competitive area which are in the same grade (or occupational level) and classification series and which are similar enough in duties, qualification requirements, pay schedules, and working conditions so that the incumbent of one position could successfully perform the critical elements of any other position upon entry into it, without any loss of productivity beyond that normally expected in the orientation of any new but fully qualified employee.

In interpreting this subsection, the administrative judge construed the "without any loss in productivity" standard as being synonymous with the older "undue interruption" test. The latter test derived from former section 351.403(a), which provided for the establishment of competitive levels consisting of positions which were sufficiently alike that assignments could be made between them without, inter alia, "unduly interrupting the work program." Under this test, otherwise comparable positions were properly placed in the same competitive level (and, by the same token, an employee was considered qualified for assignment to a position in another competitive level) if the incumbent of one position could perform satisfactorily in the other position without undue interruption to the activity. See 5 CFR §§ 351.403(a) and 351.701(a)(4) (1983). Depending on various factors, the ordinary work program was not considered to be unduly interrupted if optimum quality and quantity of work could be regained within ninety days of the RIF. See FPM Chapter 351, Section 2-3a (Inst. 263, July 7, 1981); Porter v. Department of Commerce, 11 MSPB 441, 13 MSPR 177, 179-80 (1982). Relying on this FPM provision, the administrative judge concluded that each of the positions in question required specific, technical subject-matter expertise which could not be readily acquired within ninety days of entry into the position.

However, on March 4, 1986, the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) transmitted a new FPM Chapter 351 on RIF, see FPM Letter 351-20, for use in conjunction with the RIF regulations that took effect on February 3, 1986. While the new FPM provisions still define "undue interruption" in terms of an employee's needing more than ninety days to successfully perform, see section 2-1(v), and while the phrase is still used in the discussion of assignment rights, it is absent from the discussion of competitive levels, see section 3-3, and from the corresponding OPM regulation. See 5 CFR § 351.403(a).

The question presented by this case is whether, as applied to the proper composition of competitive levels, the terms "without any loss in productivity" and "undue interruption," are substantively different. We find that they are not.

When the new regulations were first published as final rules on October 25, 1983, at 48 Fed. Reg. 49462, et seq., OPM stated, with respect to competitive levels, only that "improvements were proposed to incorporate more specific job-related criteria." OPM did not indicate that the current "without any loss in productivity standard was intended to have a different meaning than the former undue interruption" standard for determining when positions are to be placed in the same competitive level. Neither the proposed regulation, nor the comments, preamble or text of the final rule even suggested that OPM was proposing a substantive change in the standard. To hold otherwise would be to ascribe to OPM's rulemaking process a failure to inform interested parties of the subjects and issues involved for comment. Thus no change in meaning can be inferred from the final rule.

We also do not find that the March 4, 1986, FPM RIF provisions evidence a substantive change from the "undue interruption" standard. The new FPM provision states that the agency makes the "without any loss in productivity" determination by comparing for each position the qualifications required to perform the duties and responsibilities as stated in the official position description, the critical elements, and the standards for fully successful performance of these elements. See FPM Chapter 351, Subchapter 33(a)(1)(b). The duties and responsibilities of the official position description have been, and continue to be, an appropriate and necessary means by which to determine the composition of a competitive level. See Estrin v. Social Security Administration, 24 MSPR 303, 307 (1984); O'Donnell v. Department of the Army, 11 MSPB 378, 13 MSPR 104 (1982). The new FPM provision states that, in addition to the position description, it is appropriate to examine the critical elements and performance standards of the positions in question in order to determine whether they are similar enough so that the incumbent of one position could successfully perform in the other without any loss in productivity beyond that normally expected in the orientation of any new but fully qualified employee. The inclusion of these factors supports OPM's stated rulemaking objective of incorporating more specific job-related criteria for setting competitive levels, and reflects an awareness that critical elements and performance standards define the true nature of a position more completely than was possible prior to their inception, when the new performance appraisal system was developed.

Although an agency must consider work interruption as a factor in designating competitive levels, it is bound by regulation to designate competitive levels based on qualification requirements and job duties. Larson v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1362) (different nursing positions). An example of Board analysis involving comparison of job requirements, and disruption of positions located within the same competitive level, is Shultz v. Dept. of Interior, 12 MSPR 394, 396-97 (1982):

The agency contends that the two positions were properly placed in different competitive levels because soils analysis in the field is a significantly different activity than laboratory soils analysis, which was appellant's sole experience. The agency further contends that in order for appellant to perform the duties of a field analyst even marginally, he would need to undergo a minimum of two years' training. The agency claims that as a result of the extensive training which would be required for appellant to perform the duties of the field analysis position, the two positions are not interchangeable without unduly interrupting the agency's work program.

Appellant does not contest the agency's claim that he could not perform the duties of the field soils analysis position without training. He points out, however, that were he given the opportunity to hold the field soils scientist position he "could have learned quickly if the agency would chosen [sic] to train me."

The record discloses that, prior to the RIF, there were four soil scientist positions available at the agency's Water and Power Resources Service, where appellant worked. After the RIF, only one soil scientist position remained. Since it is the only soil scientist position in the State of North Dakota, the agency contends it requires an individual who could provide expert soil analysis in the field. The agency asserts that the differences in training between the laboratory and the field Supervisory Soil Scientist positions prevented these positions from being placed in the same competitive level. The Board finds merit in the agency's contention.

In addition, where an agency releases an employee from his competitive level, regulations provide that the employee may be separated, furloughed or assigned to another position for which the employee is qualified, with the employee's consent. 5 CFR 351.603. The agency contends in its petition for review in this appeal that appellant was not qualified for assignment to the retained Supervisory Soil Scientist position. This contention is based on the provisions of 5 CFR 351.701(a)(4) which state that an assignment during a RIF must not cause undue interruption of the activity to which the employee is being assigned. The agency contends that the same rationale which it articulated for not merging the competitive levels of the positions held by appellant and by the retained employee applies to its decision not to assign appellant to the retained position. We agree.

In summary, the Board finds that the agency properly defined appellant's competitive level. Redefining it along the lines indicated by the presiding official would require extensive retraining of appellant, and would thus result in an undue interruption of the agency's work program. Concomitantly, the Board finds that the agency properly declined to reassign appellant to the sole remaining Supervisory Soil Scientist position because he was not fully qualified. Accordingly, we conclude that appellant's separation by RIF was proper.
