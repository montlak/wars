3. 30-Day Advance Notice of Action

The requirement of 5 USC 7513(b)(1) that there be 30-days advance notice of an adverse action means that the action cannot be taken until at least 30 days after issuance of the proposal. The decision itself (stating a subsequent effective date) may be issued sooner than 30 days allowed. Rasheed v. Dept. of Air Force, 7 MSPR 585, 587, 7 MSPB 425 (1981); Maiers v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 383, 385 (1980) ("There is no statutory or regulatory requirement that a decision be delivered more than three days in advance of the date of the removal."). If an employee is given less than 30-days advance notice of dismissal, this is error, but only harmful error if it likely has a harmful effect on the outcome before the agency. Ladd v. USPS, 25 MSPR 506, 507 (1984). There is no harmful error if the employee avoids service of the proposal notice or unreasonably refuses its delivery by insisting on the right to have a union representative present when the notice was presented. See Pangarova v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 319, 325 (1989). There is no harmful error if the employee cannot be served with the decision notice personally, but the agency attempts to serve the employee through registered mail and the employee neglects to retrieve the letters from the post office. See McGrath v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 500, 506 (1981) ("The controlling regulations provide only that '[T]he agency shall deliver the notice of decision to the employee at or before the time the action will be effective.' 5 CFR 752.404(f) [(1981)]."); cf. Cole v. VA, 77 MSPR 434, 443 (1998) (no prejudice when the agency delivered a decision notice after the effective date of removal).

The Federal Circuit declined to set aside an adverse action when a proposal notice did not reach the appellant after the notice was properly mailed, Roach v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3375):

The AJ found that the Army timely mailed the notice of proposed removal to petitioner by certified and regular mail using the correct address to which petitioner stipulated. Because no evidence of circumstances that could have been expected to interfere with the delivery of two correctly addressed pieces of mail was presented and the Army had no other address for the appellant, the AJ concluded that the Army's efforts to provide the petitioner written notice of the proposed removal were fully intelligent and diligent.

This conclusion of the AJ is supported by substantial evidence. Even though the letter sent by certified mail was returned, the letter sent by regular mail was not. "If agencies could not rely on the due course of the mails, they could never safely act, for the employee could always wait till he is removed and the, before the board, claim late receipt of the Notice." Smith v. USPS, 789 F.2d 1540, 1544 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

A notice of adverse action 29 days in advance of the action rather than the statutorily required 30 days was not harmful error; there was no evidence of harm to the employee. Smith v. USPS, 26 MSPR 185, 187 (1985). Smith was reviewed by the Federal Circuit. Smith v. USPS, 789 F.2d 1540, 1545-46 (Fed. Cir. 1986). The court recognized the possibility that due to delay in the mails, Smith may have had less than his statutory 30-days advance notice. In fact, it may have occurred that he did not have seven days to furnish an oral reply, although a union representative who received Smith's notice furnished a solo oral reply. The court was left to determine whether deprivation of the full advance notice constituted harmful error. The government contended that because Smith had a hearing before the Board, any error was cured. The court declined to find harmful error. When Smith was notified of the proposed removal, albeit after the seven-day reply period, he could still have requested a reply. But the court also declined to accept the government's argument that a Board hearing cures lower-level error:

[W]e do not accept Smith's effective assertion of per se harmful error. In enacting 5 USC 7701, Congress did not intend that agencies be "punished" for nonprejudicial errors they may have committed while attempting to carry out Congress' mandate "to maintain 'an effective and efficient Government.'" Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms v. Federal Labor Relations Authority, 464 U.S. 89, 92 (1983).... "It is insufficient simply to show that a statutory procedure was not followed at the agency level. Harmful error must be shown." Handy v. United States Postal Service, 754 F.2d 335 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (denial of statutory right to oral reply).

Nor, as above indicated, can we accept the Government's assertion that proceedings before the board, solely because they are "de novo," must always be seen as having worked a per se cure of any procedural error that may have occurred at the agency level. Were that the case, judicial review of harmful error determinations would be a fruitless enterprise. As this court has stated, a person may have a "better and perhaps dispositive chance" of successfully contesting an adverse action at the agency level. Mercer [v. DHHS, 772 F.2d 856, at 860 (Fed. Cir. 1985)]. Though that practical observation does not lessen the employee's burden of showing harmful error, 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(r)], it appropriately focuses the attention of the board and the reviewing court on events and possibilities at the agency level.

...Smith has shown us no reason why, had all the evidence presented at the board hearing also been presented at the Step 1 hearing, identical minimal weight would not have been accorded the statements of Smith's witnesses, or why identical credibility determinations would not have been drawn respecting the testimony....

Smith, who as indicated bears the burden of establishing harmfulness, has not suggested anything that he might have said or done or presented at the predetermination hearing that might have caused a different agency decision. To say, as does Smith, only that the same evidence presented to the board might have been viewed differently by the agency would be to make every error harmful and to require reversal of every board determination that an error was harmless.

...

We are unable on this record to conclude that if the testimony and affidavit presented to the presiding official had been presented to the agency at the pretermination stage a different result might have been reached...or even that such testimony and affidavit could possibly have affected a change in the agency's decision.

Smith should be compared with Mercer v. DHHS, 772 F.2d 856 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Mercer was distinguished from Smith because there was a division in the agency about what disciplinary action was to be taken, and the court concluded that had Mercer received a pretermination hearing, as required under an agency regulation, his evidence might have swayed the deciding official. The Smith court ruled this possibility out for the appellant. Judge Newman offered a dissent in Smith, essentially urging that violation of the statutory advance notice and pretermination reply rights could not be considered under a harmful error analysis. She also pointed out sections of the record demonstrating that the evidence against Smith was not as strong as the AJ had suggested, meaning that agency officials might have been swayed by the contradictory evidence, even though the AJ was not. Judge Newman's analysis made as much sense as the majority opinion, but it will take a change in philosophy for the court and the Board to adopt the position that violation of a statutory right renders the action contrary to law and not just procedurally erroneous. The Federal Circuit earlier determined that the Board correctly applied the harmful error rule to a case involving a shortened adverse action notice period (normally 30 days): 5 USC 7701(c)(2)(A) provides that only harmful procedural error may vitiate an agency action; to be harmful, the error must substantially impair the employee's rights; there must be proof of impairment should a notice period be shortened. Schapansky v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 735 F.2d 477 (Fed. Cir. 1984); cf. Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 145, 149 n.1 (1982) ("Further, that an agency's notice of final decision, after receipt and consideration of the employee's answer, is issued prior to the expiration of the notice period does not of itself constitute harmful error warranting reversal of an adverse action. See Palmer v. United States, 121 Ct. Cl. 415, cert. denied, 344 U.S. 842 (1952); Cade v. U.S. Postal Service, 8 MSPB 362, 362 n.1 (1981). See also Gallego v. Department of the Navy, 11 MSPB 155, 156 (1982)."). [Refer later in this chapter to "Proof of Harm; Failure to Consider a Reply; Due Process."]

If there is no harmful error but the notice period is too short, the Board may direct the agency to correct its records to show a 30-day notice and provide the appropriate back pay to the appellant covering the additional notice period he should have received. Hawkins v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 501, 503-04 (1991) (agency improperly invoked a crime provision to shorten the notice period when it did not charge the appellant with a crime; no harmful error occurred since the appellant did receive notice and he did reply to the adverse action proposal; the agency was to amend its records retroactively to provide the appellant the full notice period with pay and benefits); see Callery v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 158, 162-63 (1991) (back pay analysis was inapplicable to a suspension action taken on shortened notice; the appellant lost no back pay because of the shortened notice period; the Board directed the records to be corrected to show a full 30-day notice period); Stephen v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 672, 689 (1991) ("Administrative judges in all future cases in which an agency has not afforded an employee with 30 days' advance notice of an action under the applicable provisions of the Reform Act must direct the agency to amend its records so as to retroactively afford the employee 30 days of back pay regardless of whether the appealed action is sustained."); Cade v. USPS, 8 MSPR 717, 718-19 n.1 (1981) ("The presiding official found that appellant replied to the charge against him within the tune which he did receive, and that appellant had adduced no evidence that a greater notice period would have allowed him to prepare a more persuasive reply. He found that appellant was prejudiced, however, in that he was entitled to be retained in a duty status with compensation for a full thirty days. See 38 Comp. Gen. 203 (1958). To effect this result, the presiding official ordered the agency to amend its records so as to retroactively afford appellant the full 30-day period. The Board finds this resolution reasonable, and within the presiding official's authority. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4."); cf. Hart v. United States, 284 F.2d 682, 687 (Ct. Cl. 1960) (plaintiff should receive pay for the period of an improper enforced leave imposed prior to removal, but the illegality of the suspension did not taint the otherwise procedurally correct removal).
