a. Payment to Counsel

Counsel fee checks go to counsel: counsel are then supposed to reimburse their clients for fees paid to counsel to prevent loss to the clients and to avoid double benefits to counsel. DiGiulio v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 345, 345-46 (1989); Blessin v. Dept. of Navy, 26 MSPR 615, 617 (1985) ("the award must go to counsel rather than the parties which pay their fees"). If the agency sends the fee check to the wrong party, e.g., the attorney's employer (a labor union), fees can be recovered for the time spent straightening out the error. See Hand v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 334, 339 (1993). The Federal Circuit determined that an exception exists: if fees are generated under the EAJA, fees go to the client. See FDL Technologies v. United States, 967 F.2d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (following Phillips v. GSA, 924 F.2d 1577 (Fed. Cir. 1991)). FDL Technologies reaches a poor result: fees are earned by attorneys and should be paid to them to avoid the problem of fee splitting with those who are not attorneys. There is another exception: a settlement agreement may stipulate reimbursement of fees directly from the agency to the appellant. See Jackson v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 242, 244 (1993). A retainer agreement could do so as well. And, in a whistleblower case, fees are awarded to the appellant (for time of counsel). See Rumsey v. DOJ, 123 MSPR 502, 508-09 ¶ 8, 2016 MSPB 28 (2016).

Guerrero v. VA, 2008 MSPB 100 ¶ 15 (2008), summed up law requiring that payments of fees be to counsel:

Finally, it was improper for the administrative judge to order the agency to pay Ms. Glucksman's fees directly to the appellant, regardless of whether the appellant had already made a payment to Ms. Glucksman. AID at 4-5; see Jensen v. Department of Transportation, 858 F.2d 721, 724 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (the appellant's counsel was entitled to receive the total amount of the attorney fee award even though he had already received partial payment from the appellant); Chin v. Department of the Treasury, 55 M.S.P.R. 84, 87 n.2 (1992) (the administrative judge improperly awarded fees directly to the appellant for services rendered by his attorneys); Wiatr v. Department of the Air Force, 50 M.S.P.R. 441, 450 (1991) (law firm's return of a portion of the retainer the appellant had initially paid the firm was irrelevant to the amount of reasonable attorney fees the agency was required to pay the firm), overruled in part on other grounds by Bonggat v. Department of the Navy, 59 M.S.P.R. 175, 178-79 (1993). Attorney fee awards must be paid directly to counsel and not to the parties who pay their fees. Bonggat, 59 M.S.P.R. at 180; Chin, 55 M.S.P.R. at 87 n.2. Ms. Glucksman's proper reimbursement of the appellant for fees he has already paid is an issue outside the province of the Board. See Jensen, 858 F.2d at 723-24.

Other than directing that counsel fee awards be paid to counsel filing the petition for fees, the Board does little to protect an attorney's interest in a fee. The Board lacks authority to resolve attorney fee lien claims against appellants, at least if the Board lost jurisdiction of the case following a settlement. McAlear v. MSPB, 806 F.2d 1016 (Fed. Cir. 1986); cf. Hanlin v. United States, 316 F.3d 1325, 1329-30, 1331 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (rejecting the theory that an implied-in-fact contract arose in favor of an attorney who advised the agency that he was authorized to receive a percentage of the recovery by his client, who successfully prosecuted a claim for veterans benefits; Judge Plager, concurring, raised the possibility of a Tucker Act claim). Knight v. United States, 982 F.2d 1573, 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1993), a commercial dispute involving attempts by counsel to secure fees after their successful recovery of a cost of living adjustment for their client, added:

Foster Pepper contends in the alternative that its fee arrangement created a partial assignment of its clients' claims against Interior, which is enforceable against the government under 5 USC 5525 (1988).[10] ...

[10] The statute provides that:

The head of each agency may establish procedures under which each employee of the agency is permitted to make allotments and assignments out of his pay for such purpose as the head of the agency considers appropriate.

...

...OPM's implementing regulations permit pay deductions for very limited purposes--labor dues, charity, income tax, alimony and child support, and savings. 5 CFR 550.311(a) (1992). Authorization for assignment to benefit attorneys (or creditors in general) is conspicuously absent.... Authority to permit other pay deductions is vested solely in the "head of an agency." 5 CFR 550.311(c) (1992). Even if such authority can be delegated, there is no indication that it has been delegated....

Settlements can create arrangements or conditions that the Board would not direct in the course of its own adjudication of a case. One enforceable settlement called for a payment of $4,500 in counsel fees--$3,000 to be paid in a lump sum by the agency, the remainder to be deducted from the appellant's salary at the rate of $300 per month for five months. Points v. USPS, 50 MSPR 383 (1991) (Table); see Gallagher v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 379, 383-84 (1993) (settlement approved a lump sum of $20,000: back pay would be deducted from that amount and the remainder would be counsel fees).
