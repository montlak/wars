e. Work Assignments; Reassignments; Details

A reassignment affecting two employees was more than de minimis. One employee was transferred to a location requiring more than an additional hour of commuting each way; for the other employee the commuting time was increased by about half an hour for each trip. Bargaining was required over impact, but a status quo ante remedy was not directed due to disruption of agency operations. Dept. of Transp., FAA and PASS, 20 FLRA 474, 477 (1985). In DHHS, SSA and AFGE Local 1164, 37 FLRA 278, 285 (1990), reassignment of one employee from a field representative to a claims representative position was more than de minimis and warranted an order directing restoration of the status quo and back pay (as a remedy for lost overtime) when the reassignment was effected without bargaining over impact and implementation and without bargaining over negotiable proposals advanced by the union following notice of the reassignment. See Customs Serv. and NTEU, 29 FLRA 891 (1987) (change involved where duties would be performed during period of renovation of facilities); FCI and AFGE Local 2052, 8 FLRA 604, 617 (1982) (ALJ Decision) (reassignment of employee involves a relocation of work performance and requires impact bargaining). But the right to assign work implies the right to determine the priority of assignments to be completed. If the work required remains the same, but priorities as to its completion are changed, there has been no change in working conditions requiring impact bargaining. DOJ, INS and AFGE Local 38, 17 FLRA 227, 233-35 (1985) (ALJ Decision); cf. GSA and AFGE Council 236, 62 FLRA 341, 343 (2008) (reviewing arbitration award: change in rotational schedule that could impact ability of employees to have opportunity to send their children to particular school was appropriate basis for arbitrator to conclude that effects of change were more than de minimis and required impact negotiations). Workload increases, by themselves, without affirmative management action, do not trigger impact bargaining requirements. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 66 FLRA 577, 579-80 (2012). The effect of reassignments of two employees to work at other than the housing units where they were ordinarily assigned at a military base, when their job descriptions called for them to perform maintenance and other duties at any of the base housing units, was not clearly de minimis. Further evaluation was required on remand to the ALJ of the impact of the reassignments, Dept. of Air Force, Tyndall AFB and AFGE Local 3240, 65 FLRA 877, 880-81 (2011):

The GC contends that the Judge erred in concluding that: (1) the Respondent did not change the employees' conditions of employment; and (2) even if there was a change in the employees' conditions of employment, such change was de minimis.

The Authority has held that, in order to determine whether an agency's action violated the Statute, there must first be a finding that the agency changed unit employees' conditions of employment. See, e.g., INS, Houston, 50 FLRA at 143. The determination of whether a change in conditions of employment has occurred involves a case-by-case analysis and an inquiry into the facts and circumstances regarding the agency's conduct and employees' conditions of employment. See id. at 144; 92 Bomb Wing, Fairchild Air Force Base, Spokane, Wash., 50 FLRA 701, 704 (1995). Also, in determining whether a judge's factual findings are supported, the Authority looks to the preponderance of the record evidence. See, e.g., U.S. Dep't of Transp., Fed. Aviation Admin., 64 FLRA 365, 368 (2009) (Member Beck concurring).

Moreover, an agency is obligated to bargain over the impact and implementation of a change only where that change has more than a de minimis effect on conditions of employment. See, e.g., PBGC, 59 FLRA at 50. In assessing whether the effect of a change in conditions of employment is more than de minimis, the Authority looks to the nature and extent of either the effect or the reasonably foreseeable effect of the change. Id. at 51.

Applying the above legal standards to the facts in this case, we find that the Judge failed to make the necessary factual findings for us to determine whether the Respondent's reassignments of Employees A and B constituted a change in their conditions of employment, and, if so, whether such change was more than de minimis.

With respect to the change in conditions of employment, the Judge found that, within the Sand Dollar Inn, there were "no fixed assignments and no permanent duty stations at particular locations" and that the Respondent's policy and practice "was to assign and alter which laborers would perform their duties at the various...locations...depending upon leave, availability, work load[,] and mission necessity." As further support for his conclusion, the Judge noted that the record showed that the work assignments at issue did not "result in a change to the substantive duties required of any laborer in the...unit."

The GC disputes the Judge's findings, asserting that the Judge, in so holding, "omitted" pertinent facts, specifically that there was "no history of routinely changing the permanent duty stations of employees" and that "past routine assignments to alternate work locations were only to cover for absences based on leave illness, scheduled days off or some specific overriding project." Moreover, the GC argues that the Judge omitted pertinent facts that would have shown that Wood Manor is substantially different from other locations, including that: (1) Wood Manor consists of fifty-two separate buildings that can house families; (2) the size of the units increases the amount of trash and outside work; (3) outside work has virtually no shade; (4) there is only one break area and the availability of water is limited; (5) the site is in a remote location, a few miles from the other locations; (6) there are no restaurants or food services within walking distance; and (7) employees must use personal vehicles to get to the site or go to lunch.

The Judge failed to discuss--and thus failed expressly to credit or discredit--much of the evidence presented regarding the employees' reassignments, including the duration and permanence of the employees' assignments, as well as the substantive duties required of each employee at the relevant locations. Because the Judge failed to make the necessary factual findings, we are unable to determine whether the Respondent's reassignment of Employees A and B constituted a change in their conditions of employment.

The Judge also found that, even if there had been a change in the employees' conditions of employment, the impact of the change was de minimis because the laborers were subject to performing substantially similar duties at the various lodging locations operated by the Respondent. As noted above, the Judge failed to discuss much of the evidence regarding the substantive duties required of Employee A and Employee B at the relevant work locations. As a result, we are unable to determine whether the change in the employees' conditions of employment, if such change occurred, was more than de minimis.

Accordingly, because we are unable to determine whether the Respondent's reassignment of Employees A and B constituted a change in their conditions of employment, and, if so, whether, such change was more than de minimis, we remand the case to the Judge for further consideration. On remand, consistent with this decision, the Judge should, in determining whether a violation of the Statute occurred, evaluate the contrary evidence and make the necessary factual findings regarding the reassignments of Employees A and B.

See Office of Program Operations, SSA, San Francisco and AFGE, Council of Social Security Dist. Office Locals, 20 FLRA 97, 99 (1985). FLRA rejected as de minimis a change that separated out a data entry function from an adjudication or authorization function of a claims examiner. The new management directive had no effect on the employees' grade qualification, work functions, or other working conditions. DHHS, SSA, Chicago Region and AFGE, 15 FLRA 922, 924 (1984). A reorganization's effect was no more than de minimis when the nature of the employee's duties remained the same although cases were reassigned. The reassignments of 10 employees to unclassified duties were temporary. The total effect of the reorganization was upon 10 employees in a consolidated national unit of 200 professional employees, or, in the alternative, in a group of 8,000 nonprofessional employees. DHUD and AFGE, 20 FLRA 374, 377 (1985) (result might be different under more recent decisions disavowing proportion of employees in unit affected as component of de minimis analysis). There was no duty to engage in impact bargaining over the institution of a rotational system for the assignment of typists when there was no adverse effect, e.g., no increase in overtime or other duties, resulting from the change. The effect was de minimis. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 16 FLRA 740, 741 (1984). But impact bargaining was required over a plan by management to rotate receptionist duties among a group of employees. DHUD and AFGE Local 3701, 58 FLRA 33, 34-35 (2002). Reassignment of some work to employees did not rise above de minimis impact, even though the duties were in addition to the employees' regular duties and were ordinarily performed by other employees. The nature of the duties remained the same as were previously performed. They did not require any measurable additional time to perform or require added skills or travel. The assignment of duties was temporary. Dept. of Transp., FAA and PASS, 20 FLRA 481, 484 (1985). Discontinuation of recertification training assignments for a small group of instructors was de minimis in its impact. The loss of the work was relatively small in comparison to the total duties assigned, and there was no indication that the trainers would be more vulnerable to a RIF. The agency announced a RIF, but there was no showing that the number of trainers would be reduced. The vulnerability of the trainers was speculative, not reasonably foreseeable. Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and IFPTE Local 4, 45 FLRA 574, 576-78 (1992).

A significant addition to the duties of an employee is a change in working conditions that warrants notice to the union and impact bargaining, if requested. DHHS, SSA, Atlanta and AFGE, 11 FLRA 419, 428 (1983) (ALJ Decision). There is an obligation to bargain over the impact and implementation of changes in job duties. DOD, Dept. of Army, Hq., Ft. Sam Houston and AFGE Local 2154, 8 FLRA 623, 638 (1982) (ALJ Decision). Modification of the forms used by case analysts required new, more complex, and time-consuming duties. Impact bargaining was required because of the duties and the effect of those duties on the ratings of the employees. SSA and AFGE, 16 FLRA 56, 70 (1984) (ALJ Decision). Scheduling appointments for claims representatives on Fridays was a change in past practice that violated implementation bargaining requirements. See SSA, Gilroy Branch and AFGE Local 3172, 53 FLRA 1358 (1998) (ALJ Decision). An increase in temporary supervisory responsibilities for guards was more than de minimis. Dept. of Air Force, Willow Grove ARS and NAGE Local R3-32, 57 FLRA 852, 857 (2002), reconsideration granted, result unchanged, 58 FLRA 277 (2003). A reduction in clinical privileges for two VA physicians was a change in duties sufficient to warrant impact bargaining. The physicians suffered an obvious limitation on their future assignments as well as on their retention standing. VAMC, Muskogee and AFGE Local 2250, 25 FLRA 875, 879 (1987). A change from one- or two-day voluntary details to 30-day mandatory details required impact bargaining. IRS, Fresno Serv. Ctr. and NTEU, 16 FLRA 98, 1125-27 (1984) (ALJ Decision). Assignment of employees to 120-day details, involving different supervisors but essentially the same work, required impact bargaining. The change involved the reasonably foreseeable effect of impact upon performance appraisal and promotional opportunities. DHHS, Family Support Admin. and NTEU, 30 FLRA 346 (1987) (ALJ Decision); see SSA Area IX and AFGE Council 147, 51 FLRA 357 (1995) (ALJ Decision) (impact bargaining should have occurred as to 120-day detail when effect on employee was not de minimis).

In Dept. of Air Force, Davis-Monthan AFB and AFGE Local 2924, 64 FLRA 85, 89-90 (2009), the employer committed a ULP by not providing notice and opportunity to the union to bargain to the extent permitted by law of the addition of one set of duties to a single employee:

It is well established that, prior to implementing a change in conditions of employment, an agency is required to provide the exclusive representative with notice of the change and an opportunity to bargain over those aspects of the change that are within the duty to bargain, if the change will have more than a de minimis effect on conditions of employment. See United States Dep't. of the Treasury, IRS, 56 FLRA 906, 913 (2000). In applying the de minimis doctrine, the Authority looks to the nature and extent of either the effect, or the reasonably foreseeable effect, of the change in bargaining unit employees' conditions of employment. Veterans Admin. Med. Ctr., Phoenix, Ariz., 47 FLRA 419, 423 (1993). In determining whether the reasonably foreseeable effects of a change are greater than de minimis, the Authority addresses what a respondent knew, or should have known, at time of the change. Id.

Asserting that § 7103(a)(13) of the Statute distinguishes between "conditions of employment" and "working conditions[,]" and that a change in the latter is outside the duty to bargain, the Respondent claims that it had no duty to bargain over the assignment of new duties to employee H because it only changed his working conditions.

The task of resolving a dispute over the meaning of a statutory provision "begins where all such inquiries must begin: with the language of the statute itself." 7th Infantry Div. (Light), Fort Ord, Cal., 47 FLRA 864, 868 (1993) (quoting United States v. Ron Pair Enterprises, 489 U.S. 235, 241 (1989)). In this regard, § 7103(a)(13) defines "conditions of employment" as "personnel policies, practices, and matters, whether established by rule, regulation, or otherwise, affecting working conditions[.]" (emphasis added). However, the Statute does not define "working conditions," and a majority of the Authority has previously declined to consider "working conditions" outside the context of "conditions of employment[.]" SSA, 55 FLRA 978, 980 n.7 (1999) (Member Cabaniss dissenting). Similarly, although courts have defined "working conditions" under other statutes, they have not done so within the context of the Statute. Compare, e.g., So. Ry. Co. v. Occupational Safety & Health Review Comm'n, 539 F.2d 335, 339 (4th Cir. 1976) (defining the term "working conditions[,]" as used under the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1974, as "the environmental area" in which an employee performs his or her daily tasks) with, e.g., Fort Stewart Schools v. FLRA, 495 U.S. 641, 646 (1990) (Fort Stewart) (although Court rejected definition of "working conditions" under § 7103(a)(13) of the Statute that related to physical conditions of the workplace, it did not define the term); EEOC v. FLRA, 744 F.2d 842, 850 n.18 (D.C. Cir. 1984) (same).

Although courts and the Authority have not defined "working conditions," when faced with issues involving "working conditions," they have accorded the term a broad interpretation that encapsulates a wide range of subjects that is effectively synonymous with "conditions of employment." See, e.g., Fort Stewart, 495 U.S. at 646: Antilles Consolidated Educ. Ass'n, 22 FLRA 235, 237 (1986) (stating that, in examining whether a bargaining proposal effects "working conditions" of employees, the Authority examines the "work situation or employment relationship" of employees). Moreover, to the extent that courts and the Authority have attempted to make distinctions among conditions of employment, such distinctions arose in the context of assessing whether a change in conditions of employment was or was not de minimis. See, e.g., AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council v. FLRA, 446 F.3d 162, 167 (D.C. Cir. 2006) (court found that a reduction in remedial training for employees was a change in conditions of employment that was not de minimis); United States Dep't. of the Treasury, IRS, 62 FLRA 411, 414 (2008) (Authority found that agency's decision to discontinue past practice of granting leave to employees to attend annual employee appreciation day event was not de minimis). The foregoing establishes that, under court and Authority precedent, there is no substantive difference between "conditions of employment" and "working conditions" as those terms are practically applied.

The Respondent has provided no other arguments challenging the Judge's finding that the Agency changed employee H's conditions of employment. Accordingly, we find that the Judge did not err by concluding that assignment changed Employee H's conditions of employment. Therefore, the Respondent was required to bargain over the impact and implementation of the changes to employee H's job duties so long as they had an effect that was more than de minimis.

The Respondent also challenges the Judge's conclusion that the assignment had an effect that was more than de minimis. However, the Respondent's assertion that, despite the security checks assignment, employee H continues to spend the majority of his time on pre-existing duties, namely "driving" and "filling out forms[,]" is unsupported by the record. In this regard, the Judge's undisputed findings establish that, in addition to employee H's "normal job responsibilities[,]" employee H now: inspects landscape and aircraft; drives over rougher terrain; must exercise "higher degrees of discretion and independent judgment" than he previously used; and must electronically prepare daily and monthly written reports on a permanent basis. The Respondent also does not dispute the Judge's findings that employee H had to receive training before he could perform the security checks, and that employee H required computer assistance for eight days before he could electronically prepare the reports by himself since he had no experience with computers or email. Further, the Respondent does not dispute the Judge's finding that, prior to the new duties, employee H only filled out forms when he discovered discrepancies with the van's fluid levels, rather than on a daily or monthly basis. The foregoing sufficiently establishes that employee H's new duties related to performing security checks encompass more than "driving" and "filling out forms." Accordingly, we find that the Judge did not err by concluding that the change had an effect that was more than de minimis.

Based on the foregoing, we find that the Judge did not err by concluding that the Respondent violated § 7116(a)(1) and (5) of the Statute.

A change in a program that required increased travel--welcomed by some employees, avoided by others--was a change resulting in more than de minimis effect on unit employees and required impact bargaining. IRS and NTEU, 16 FLRA 845, 860 (1984) (ALJ Decision). Management should have negotiated over the impact of a program allowing agency employees to accept temporary teaching assignments at local colleges. There was a reasonably foreseeable effect on the employees returning to their regular job after participation in the program, and there was a foreseeable impact on the employees remaining in the unit after the temporary relocation of the individuals participating in the teaching program. IRS and NTEU, 24 FLRA 999, 1012-13 (1986) (ALJ Decision). Management should have bargained over a decision to assign employees administrative duties for a month at a time on a rotating basis. Dept. of Labor, OSHA and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE Local 644, 24 FLRA 743, 746 (1986).

The reasonably foreseeable effects of program changes can be relatively small and still trigger an obligation to pursue impact bargaining. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 26 FLRA 344, 347 (1987), commented on the bargaining obligation arising from management's interest in instituting a Social Security Income Payment Review Redesign Study:

We find that the reasonably foreseeable impact of implementation of the study in this case on the affected employees elevated it to a level where bargaining was appropriate. The study was to last for at least one year and the possibility clearly existed that the changes would become permanent. Moreover, the QRAs were required to collect and record additional information in 10 percent of their cases. While the QRAs apparently would not be adversely affected directly by any errors made in the new data, QRAs are rated on the timeliness of their cases. It was therefore reasonably foreseeable that the additional time required by the study, compounded by such matters as the allegedly inadequate preliminary training and the varying number of study cases that might be assigned to any particular QRA, would affect their performance on other cases, their performance evaluations and related personnel actions. Those foreseeable effects gave rise to a duty to bargain with the Union concerning the procedures to be utilized in the study and appropriate arrangements for employees who might be adversely affected by implementation of the study.

Substantive negotiations are still limited by management rights. The agency need not negotiate on the amount of work that employees should be required to perform, even though there is an impact on the employees' ability to perform that work following a change in their working conditions, e.g., office layout or facilities. The right to assign work and determine its quantity is a management right. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Midwest Reg'l Office, Chicago and NTEU Chap. 95, 16 FLRA 141, 144-45 (1984). A proposal made during impact bargaining that essentially bars management from assigning new duties to employees interferes with the right to assign work and is not negotiable. NFFE Local 1214 and Dept. of Army, Hq., Army Training Ctr., 11 FLRA 580 (1983). Yet management should have bargained with the union over a unilateral increase in the performance standard of unit employees by 50% after other unit employees were assigned to duties that adversely affected their personal safety. INS and AFGE Local 1917, 24 FLRA 786, 793 (1986); see VARO, San Diego and AFGE Local 490, 44 FLRA 312, 320 (1992) (ALJ Decision) (management improperly changed productivity standards of file clerks unilaterally without bargaining over impact and implementation of change). A change in policy of allowing an employee "a reasonable opportunity" to improve performance before initiating a performance-based action to a policy limiting a performance improvement period to 30-90 days was more than de minimis. See EEOC and Nat'l Council of EEOC Locals No. 216, AFGE, 48 FLRA 306, 310 (1993). [Refer in Chapter 6 to "Changes in Standards."]

Not every change requires impact bargaining. The de minimis rule has some practical application, although, given evolution in legal standards, it can be difficult to distinguish the bargainable from nonnegotiable changes. The change of an assignment for one employee did not result in more than a de minimis impact on the unit. There was a change in schedule but no change in grade, pay, or the required number of hours. Although shift rotation and holiday work would be required as a result of the reassignment, employees worked but one holiday a year and spent 80% of their time on one shift. VAMC, Phoenix and AFGE Local 2382, 20 FLRA 399, 402 (1985); see FAA and PASS, 20 FLRA 430, 434 (1985) (increased travel time for one employee assigned to a new location: "impact or reasonably foreseeable impact of the assignment of new duties on the conditions of employment of the unit employees herein was no more than de minimis). The reassignment of an employee, a union representative, from one work station to another, a distance of less than a mile, was de minimis in impact. There was no change in grade or pay, and the employee was still able to obtain official time to represent employees. Dept. of Air Force, 63rd Civil Eng'r Squadron, Norton AFB and AFGE Local 3854, 22 FLRA 843, 864-65 (1986) (ALJ Decision). A reassignment involving no real inconvenience or changes in employee benefits was de minimis. Customs Serv. and NTEU, 29 FLRA 307, 326 (1987) (ALJ Decision); see GSA Region 9 and NFFE Local 81, 52 FLRA 1107, 1112 (1997) (temporary geographical office move for employee, at union's request, that involved no loss of pay or change of hours, and involved only slight inconvenience, did not trigger bargaining obligation because impact was de minimis; "the Statute does not require bargaining every time an employee is slightly inconvenienced").

The agency did not violate the Statute by unilaterally reassigning six unit employees from excepted to competitive service positions without notifying the union and allowing it an opportunity to engage in impact bargaining. The changes were no more than de minimis. Duties of the employees remained the same, the six employees were members of a unit of 1,900 employees, there was no past practice of bargaining concerning this type of change, and one employee experienced a temporary loss of pay but volunteered for the conversion. Nat'l Archives and AFGE Local 2578, 20 FLRA 129, 133-34 (1985). Reassignment of two employees of a unit of 8,000 from one position to another in an office had no more than a de minimis effect. Pay and grade were unaffected, and there was no change in promotion potential. Foreseeable adverse impact was speculative. DHUD, Columbia Area Office and AFGE Local 3654, 20 FLRA 233, 236 (1985). A different result may have been reached under more recent decisions disavowing the proportion of employees in unit affected as a component of de minimis analysis.
