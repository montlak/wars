a. Gambling Compulsion

The Board declined to extend the protections of laws relating to alcoholism and drug addiction to an appellant who claimed a gambling addiction. In Chow v. SSA, 4 MSPR 224, 226, 4 MSPB 300 (1980), the employee was removed for theft of three money orders that were agency property. The Board concluded that the appellant was not otherwise entitled to reasonable accommodation. He was not a qualified disabled person, one who was able to meet all of the program's requirements in spite of his disability. Even if the appellant was a qualified disabled individual, "reasonable accommodation would not require the agency to ignore his theft of money orders." Chow was followed in Johnson v. USPS, 12 MSPR 580, 582 (1982), and in Jacobs v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 76, 78 (1985) ("[C]ompulsive gambling is not a protected handicapping condition"). See Rezza v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 40, 44 (1987) ("appellant's compulsive gambling was outweighed by other factors, specifically the seriousness of the offenses and the appellant's position as a law enforcement officer"); Lampack v. USPS, 27 MSPR 468, 470 (1985) (mitigating the penalty for an employee with a gambling problem).

Removal was sustained for a letter carrier's mistreatment of the mail, but Ruiz v. USPS, 59 MSPR 76, 78-79, 81 (1993), approved the AJ's consideration of a gambling compulsion as a mitigating factor:

We find further that the administrative judge correctly determined that compulsive gambling is not a handicapping condition. See Jacobs v. Department of the Air Force, 29 MSPR 76, 78 (1985). The appellant suggests on petition for review that the Board avail itself of this opportunity to reverse its previous decisions on this subject. We decline to do so; compulsive gambling is not a qualifying handicap under the Rehabilitation Act. 29 USC § 701 et seq. See also, Chow v. Social Security Administration, 4 MSPR 224, 225-26 (1980).

The appellant offers Rezza v. Department of Justice, 698 F. Supp. 586 (E.D. Pa. 1988), in support of his claim. We note, first, that this case stands only for the proposition that an employee can bring a due process claim in addition to a claim for violation of the Rehabilitation Act; it did not reverse the Board's decision. Id. at 588. Second, the Board is not bound by the decisions of the district court. See Boulineau v. Department of the Army, 57 MSPR 244, 249 n.7 (1993). Third, the district court's unpublished earlier opinion in Rezza v. Department of Justice, 46 FEP Cases 1366, 1368 (E.D. Pa. 1988), cited by the appellant as "questioning" the Board's ruling on the issue, is of no precedential value. See McDonnell v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 MSPR 400, 402 & note (1990) (unpublished judicial opinions are nonprecedential and may only be cited to support a claim of res judicata, collateral estoppel, or law of the case). Moreover, the district court based its position on its finding that the American Psychiatric Association regards compulsive gambling as a "disorder." Id. Recognition of certain behavior as a disorder, however, does not equate with recognition of the behavior as a "handicap" under the Rehabilitation Act. Cf. Daley v. Koch, 892 F.2d 212 (2d Cir. 1989) (employee did not show that he was a qualified handicapped person, despite his acrophobia).

Moreover, even if it were a handicap, the appellant has not shown that his compulsion caused the misconduct; the evidence shows that he used a portion of the pilfered money to buy personal items, not to gamble. Mere speculation that the appellant may have intended to use part of the money for gambling purposes is insufficient to show causation. Cf. Lavalley v. U.S. Postal Service, 49 MSPR 129, 132 (1991) (employee's consumption of alcohol at lunch, immediately prior to the misconduct, is insufficient to show causation between alcoholism and the misconduct). Furthermore, reasonable accommodation of such a handicap would not require the agency to ignore tampering with the mail. See Chow, 4 MSPR at 226.
