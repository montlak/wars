A. PROCEDURES IN MIXED CASES

The Board must address an affirmative defense of discrimination raised as to an appealable matter properly within the Board's jurisdiction. Williams v. DOD, 76 MSPR 270, 273-74 (1997); see Mirza v. Dept. of Treasury, 875 F. Supp. 513 (N.D. Ill. 1995) ("[t]he MSPB lacks jurisdiction over claims of discrimination concerning promotions, performance evaluations, or any other personnel actions which are not themselves appealable to the MSPB."); Calhoon v. Dept. of Treasury, 90 MSPR 375, 378 ¶ 5 (2001) ("[A] discrimination claim, whether or not accompanied by a claim for compensatory damages, would not provide a basis for Board review in the absence of an otherwise appealable action."); Martinez v. Dept. of Army, DE-3443-14-0489-I-1 (NP 12/23/2014) ("Absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board lacks jurisdiction over discrimination and equal pay claims because 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b) is not an independent source of Board jurisdiction. See Morales v. Social Security Administration, 108 M.S.P.R. 583, ¶ 5 (2008) (citing Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980))."); Phillips v. Dept. of Air Force, 84 MSPR 580, 584 ¶ 8 (1999) (petition for enforcement: "The Board may only decide a claim of discrimination where the alleged wrongful act forms a basis for an agency action over which the Board otherwise has jurisdiction."); Smith v. USPS, 81 MSPR 92, 99 ¶ 11 (1999) ("Absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board does not have jurisdiction to consider the appellant's age and disability discrimination claims.").

The discrimination embraced by a mixed case is defined at 5 CFR 1201.151 (2022) to include allegations of discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, disability, age, national origin, reprisal, and "[a]ny rule, regulation or policy directive prescribed" under any of the laws defining prohibited discrimination (the 1964 Civil Rights Act, as it applies to federal employees (42 USC 2000e-16(a)); § 6(d) of the FLSA (29 USC 206(d)); § 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (29 USC 791); § 12, 15 of the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (29 USC 631, 633a)). The ADEA appears to apply only to employees and applicants for employment who are at least 40 years of age at the time of the allegedly discriminatory event. See Ramos v. Dept. of Army, 48 MSPR 399, 402 (1991); Rogers v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 33 MSPR 690, 696 (1987) ("Inasmuch as appellant was only thirty-six years old at the time of his removal, an allegation of age discrimination will not lie. See 39 C.F.R. § 1613.501(b)(4) (age discrimination provisions protect employees and applicants for employment who are at least 40 years of age at the time of the allegedly discriminatory event)."). Native American status is not a covered classification of discrimination under the foregoing statutes; it is considered a political rather than racial classification. Mullenberg v. United States, 857 F.2d 770 (Fed. Cir. 1988).

Discrimination against persons of any covered race is precluded; Caucasians are a "protected racial group." Title VII prohibits discrimination against white employees on the same standards applied to other races. Meyers v. EEOC, 45 MSPR 488, 492 n.4 (1990). Men may be the victims of discrimination if females are favored in personnel actions. See Portlock v. VA, 14 MSPR 359, 362 (1983), reaff'd on OPM petition for reconsideration, 16 MSPR 92, 93 (1983) (discriminatory to discipline only the male in an altercation with a female). Discrimination on the basis of hair length or marital status does not qualify as discrimination covered by Title VII (unless the allegation is that the employer treats unmarried members of one sex differently from unmarried members of the other sex). See Livingstone v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 36, 39 (1994).

As to sexual orientation, Kirsh v. Dept. of Transp., 66 MSPR 524 (1995) (Table), declined to issue mixed case appeal rights "[b]ecause the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) does not have jurisdiction over a claim of sexual orientation discrimination...." See Morales v. DOJ, 77 MSPR 482, 484 (1998) (Board lacked authority to review an arbitrator's award when the challenge was based on sexual orientation discrimination). Sexual orientation and age discrimination, in addition to race, color, religion, sex, national origin, and disability discrimination, are prohibited in government employment decisions by Executive Order 11478 (as amended on May 28, 1998). Genetic discrimination in the federal sector is prohibited by Executive Order 13145 (Feb. 8, 2000; 65 F.R. 6877). However in the 2015, decision in Baldwin v. Dept. of Transp., EEOC 0120133080 (July 15, 2015), EEOC decided that, for federal sector cases, discrimination against an individual because of that person's sexual orientation is discrimination because of sex and therefore prohibited under Title VII. [Refer to Chapter 1, "Marital Status," concerning allegations of marital status and pregnancy discrimination raised in appeals by probationary employees.]

The Board does not have jurisdiction over a claim of discrimination divorced from a personnel action otherwise within its jurisdiction. See, e.g., Lazarski v. OPM, 58 MSPR 475, 479 (1993) (citing Wren v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 1, 2, 2 MSPB 174 (1980)); see New v. VA, 72 MSPR 574, 595 (1996) ("Since the appellant has not established Board jurisdiction over any of her restoration claims, there is...no appealable matter before the Board. As such, the appellant's claim of disability discrimination is not within the Board's jurisdiction either."); Beltran v. USPS, 50 MSPR 425, 430 (1991) ("The Board lacks jurisdiction over affirmative defenses that are not raised in connection with an action that has been appealed to the Board."); Green v. USPS, 57 MSPR 23, 26 n.1 (1993) (when the Board lacked jurisdiction over an enforced leave appeal by an appellant who asserted that he should have been placed on light duty, the Board lacked jurisdiction over the disability discrimination claim). But if the action is within the Board's jurisdiction, including the an OPM suitability determination, the Board resolves a claim of discrimination made in the appeal. See Johnson v. OPM, 61 MSPR 293, 295 (1994); cf. Chavez v. USPS, 85 MSPR 481, 484, 488 ¶¶ 9, 20 (2000) (considering a claim that the appellant's allegedly improper restoration was due to his race, sex, age, national origin, and disability); Hundley v. OPM, 83 MSPR 632, 634-37 ¶¶ 5-10 (2000) (Board had authority to consider a marital status discrimination claim in an annuity overpayment recovery case; the discrimination issue was tied to the proper application of policies outlined in OPM's guidelines on annuity overpayments); Caldarone v. OPM, 36 MSPR 544, 546 (1988) (allegation of discrimination in a retirement appeal must be resolved by the AJ). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Related EEO Violation; Prohibited Personnel Practice Allegation," concerning EEO claims asserted in USERRA and VEOA appeals; refer to EEOC regulations at 29 CFR Part 1614 for federal sector EEO complaint procedures; refer to Chapter 13, "Collateral Jurisdiction Over EEO Claim Presented in IRA Appeal Not Involving Otherwise Appealable Action," concerning joinder of EEO affirmative defenses in Individual Right of Action (whistleblowing) cases.]

As to allegations of discrimination pleaded as an affirmative defense in an appeal properly before the Board, an MSPB finding of race, sex, age, or national origin discrimination is rare; there are more frequent findings of disability discrimination. Some discrimination findings involved in Board litigation (disability cases aside) result from EEOC determinations on appeal from MSPB decisions. Nonetheless, the Board regulations must be followed if an adverse action appeal is ultimately to be taken to EEOC or to the courts. The regulations defining the processing of mixed case appeals are at 5 CFR 1201.151-.162 (2022). EEOC regulations are at 29 CFR 1614.302 (2022) et seq. Operations of the "Special Panel," the entity [described later in this chapter, "Special Panel"] with the authority to resolve disputes between MSPB and EEOC on EEO issues, are governed by 5 CFR 1201.171-.174 (2022). Because the Panel meets rarely, even in a busy year, those regulations are not reprinted here. Judicial review is described at 5 CFR 1201.157, .161, .162, .175 (2022). These regulations, reviewed in the later discussion of judicial review rights, implement statutory provisions at 5 USC 7702. The statute, long and convoluted, is not reprinted here, but it should be read by anyone undertaking the mixed case appeal route. Section 7702 is not a part of discrimination law; rather, it is a procedural provision that distinguishes between discrimination law and civil service laws. The statute recognizes the respective expertise of EEOC in discrimination law and MSPB in civil service law. Kohfield v. Dept. of Navy, 75 MSPR 1, 7 (1997).

The discrimination defense is significant if it can be established. Under Sublette v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 82, 86 (1995) (reversing a finding of discrimination), the Board explained:

After finding that the agency discriminated against the appellant, the administrative judge then mitigated the penalty. This was in error, as the remedy for discrimination is to make the victim "whole." See Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405, 418-19, 95 S. Ct. 2362, 2372, 45 L. Ed.2d 280 (1975). In this case, having found discrimination, the administrative judge should have reversed the adverse action completely....

Hocker v. Dept. of Transp., 63 MSPR 497, 508 (1994), affirmed the Board's authority to award compensatory damages to appellants who demonstrate that they were discriminated against under the civil rights acts in proceedings pending on or after November 21, 1991. [Refer to Chapter 16, "Availability of Compensatory Damages," for Hocker.]

Discussion that follows involves Board regulations governing processing of mixed cases and burdens of proof established to evaluate EEO allegations. Apart from disability discrimination findings and those cases remanded to the Board from EEOC after the Board rejected, and EEOC directed, findings of discrimination, of the many thousands of reported Board decisions, but a handful have found racial, national origin, sexual or age-based discrimination. EEOC remands aside, the Board's principal final and published decisions finding discrimination or reprisal are: Rhee v. Dept. of Treasury, 117 MSPR 640, 2012 MSPB 26 (2012) (reprisal); Spahn v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 195 (2003) (sex discrimination); Arredondo v. USPS, 85 MSPR 113 (2000) (race or national origin); Di Pietro v. USPS, 65 MSPR 256 (1994) (age and reprisal); Creer v. USPS, 62 MSPR 656 (1994) (sex); Johnson v. DLA, 61 MSPR 601 (1994) (race); Madison v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 465 (1987) (reprisal); Wickramasekera v. VA, 21 MSPR 707 (1984) (race discrimination and reprisal); Howard v. Dept. of Commerce, 15 MSPR 23 (1983) (sex and age discrimination in a performance case); Moye v. VA, 10 MSPR 448 (1982) (reprisal); Cho v. Dwight David Eisenhower Army Med. Ctr., 8 MSPR 52, 7 MSPB 548 (1981) (race: removal and a within-grade increase appeal); and Mahurin v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 302, 4 MSPB 372 (1980) (age). Cf. Durden v. DHS, 108 MSPR 539, 2008 MSPB 88 (2008) (sex discrimination coupled with failure to provide medical accommodation); Sloan v. USPS, 77 MSPR 58 (1997) (an initial decision finding discrimination became final when no PFR was filed; the Board's decision addressed the appellant's entitlement to compensatory damages).

Even when the Board finds discrimination, it does not exercise the authority to correct it other than to cancel the offending adverse action and consider a claim for compensatory damages. The Board has not explained why its authority to remedy a discriminatory situation is less than EEOC, the counterpart agency enforcing the same civil rights laws. There is some case law involving enforcement of Board final decisions concerning accommodative requirements that are imposed on agencies. [Refer to Chapter 16, "Reinstatement with Accommodation for Disability."]

The Board's hearing process for a mixed case appeal is the same as for other Board appeals. 5 CFR 1201.152 (2022). There are two basic procedural differences between mixed case and other appeals. First, in the manner that an appeal is lodged with the Board, the mixed case may originate with an agency decision and be appealed directly to the Board, or the mixed case may be initiated, following the agency decision, after an EEO charge or complaint is processed through the counseling and investigatory procedures of the agency EEO complaint system and then appealed to the Board for a hearing. Second, the appeal route for the nonmixed case is from an initial decision, to the Board for a final decision, and then, ordinarily, on to the Federal Circuit. For the mixed case, review is more complicated. The appellant who desires to appeal a discrimination issue has a choice of seeking review of an initial decision by the Board, by EEOC, and in a federal district court. [These options are explored below in "Multiple Agency Review Processes" and in Chapter 17, "Discrimination Issue."]

One technical distinction between the mixed case and the ordinary appeal is the statutory expedition of the mixed case. 5 USC 7702(a). 5 CFR 1201.156 (2022) establishes time limits for processing these cases. The regulation provides:

(a) Issue raised in appeal. When an appellant alleges prohibited discrimination in the appeal, the judge will decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action within 120 days after the appeal is filed.

(b) Issue not raised in appeal. When an appellant has not alleged prohibited discrimination in the appeal, but has raised the issue later in the proceeding, the judge will decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action within 120 days after the issue is raised.

(c) Discrimination issue remanded to agency. When the judge remands an issue of discrimination to the agency, adjudication will be completed within 120 days after the agency completes its action and returns the case to the Board.

"While the statute imposes a time limit on the Board for deciding [mixed] cases, it does not contemplate any penalty or sanctions against the Board for failure to comply with this provision." Moye v. VA, 10 MSPR 448, 451 (1982) (agency argued in its PFR that initial decision was void for failure to comply with 120-day time limit). Current Board policy calls for all appeals to be decided at the regions (initial decision level) in less than 120 days, so the statutory or regulatory expedition of mixed case appeals is no longer of significance. Cf. Transpacific Steel LLC v. United States, 4 F.4th 1306, 1320 (Fed. Cir. 2021) ("The Supreme Court has recognized this linguistic point in the context of statutory commands to executive officers to take action within a specified time. It has made clear that such a command does not, without more, entail lack of authority, or of obligation, to take the action after that date has passed, even though the obligation to act by the specified time has been violated," citing Brock v. Pierce County, 476 U.S. 253, 262 (1986)).

Summarizing the complex process of the mixed case, and commenting on judicial review options, Punch v. Bridenstine, 945 F.3d 322, 324-325 (5th Cir. 2019), explained:

When a federal employee challenges an adverse employment action, such as termination, a "complicated tapestry" of statutes and regulations governs her claim. Butler v. West, 164 F.3d 634, 637 (D.C. Cir. 1999). We think the better metaphor is a road. But this trip is not for the easily carsick.

The Civil Service Reform Act ("CSRA") gives federal employees numerous job protections. One is the right to challenge certain "serious personnel actions." Perry v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 137 S. Ct. 1975, 1979 (2017). The employee "may merely allege that the agency had insufficient cause for taking the action under the CSRA." Kloeckner v. Solis, 568 U.S. 41, 44 (2012). The employee "may also or instead charge the agency with discrimination prohibited by another federal statute." Ibid. This second type of charge is called a "mixed case." Ibid. (citing 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302).

Under the CSRA, employees face a series of mutually exclusive paths to bring a "mixed case." Option 1 is an appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board ("MSPB"). The MSPB is "an independent adjudicator of federal employment disputes" that hears appeals from "particularly serious" actions, such as terminations of employment. Ibid.

After the MSPB decides a mixed case, the road forks again. The employee has three options for further review. First (Option 1.A), she can appeal to the Federal Circuit--but only if she waives her discrimination claims and limits her appeal to CSRA claims. 5 U.S.C. § 7703(b)(1). If she chooses Option 1.A, she must file her appeal within 60 days of the MSPB's decision. Id. § 7703(b)(1)(A).

Second (Option 1.B), the employee can instead keep pursuing her mixed case. But the road for review forks once again. The employee can immediately file suit in the appropriate federal district court (Option 1.B.i). Id. § 7703(b)(2). Or the employee can take a further administrative appeal to the EEOC (Option 1.B.ii). Id. § 7702(b)(1). And if she's unhappy with the EEOC's resolution, the employee can sue in district court. Id. §§ 7702(b)(3)(A), (b)(5)(A), (b)(5)(B), (c), 7703(b)(2); see Perry, 137 S. Ct. at 1980. Thus, both of these options (1.B.i and 1.B.ii) allow the employee to continue pursuing discrimination claims as part of her mixed case, and both eventually lead to litigation in district court. The employee's deadline to file a complaint in district court is 30 days from the final decision by the relevant administrative body--either the MSPB or the EEOC, depending on the route she chooses. See 5 U.S.C. § 7703(b)(2).

But the employee need not start with the MSPB--or take any of the roads running from it. There's another way. Call it Option 2. An employee can file a mixed case complaint with the agency's Equal Employment Opportunity ("EEO") office. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(b); see also Kloeckner, 568 U.S. at 45. If the agency's EEO office rules against the employee, she can appeal to the EEOC. When the EEOC's decision becomes final and reviewable, she can challenge it in district court. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.407. So Option 2, like Options 1.B.i and ii, eventually leads to district court.
