6. Voluntary Dismissal of Complaint

Having initiated a complaint against an ALJ, an agency may voluntarily seek dismissal of the case before adjudication. The issue is whether the respondent ALJ suffers prejudice through dismissal of the proceeding, as was explained in SSA v. Abell, 47 MSPR 98, 100-101 (1991), a furlough case resulting from budget shortfalls that were resolved through the appropriations process:

The agency's motion is in the nature of a plaintiff's motion for voluntary dismissal pursuant to Rule 41(a)(2) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.[3] Although the Federal Rules do not apply to the Board, the Board may look to them for guidance concerning the treatment of motions before it. Phillips v. Department of Health, Education and Welfare, 2 MSPB 479, 2 MSPR 315, 316 (1980) (applying Rule 41(a)(2) to a Board proceeding). In considering a motion for voluntary dismissal the Board must consider primarily the interests of the respondent. See McCants v. Ford Motor Company, Inc., 781 F.2d 855, 856 (11th Cir. 1986). A dismissal, however, should be granted unless the respondent will suffer a clear legal prejudice. McCants at 856-57; Conafay v. Wyeth Laboratories, 841 F.2d 417, 419 (D.C. Cir. 1988). Here the only prejudice alleged by respondents is that the procedural orders and recommended decisions issued in this case "could arguably be considered to have some minimal precedential value unless they are specifically vacated and withdrawn." They offer no support for this assertion.

[3] Rule 41(a)(2) provides for the voluntary dismissal of an action before a federal district court pursuant to a plaintiff's motion. Such a dismissal may only take effect upon an order from that court and in accordance with terms and conditions set out by the court.

It has long been held that initial decisions have no precedential value. Rockwell v. Department of Commerce, 39 MSPR 217, 222 (1988); Clark v. Department of the Navy, 12 MSPR 428, 429 (1982). In an action against an ALJ, the initial decision is issued in the form of a recommended decision. Social Security Administration v. Goodman, 28 MSPR 120, 122 (1985). Therefore, recommended decisions, in that they are not final decisions of the Board, lack precedential value. Similarly, procedural orders that have not been reviewed and upheld by the Board lack precedential value as well. As the Board has issued no precedential orders or decisions, respondents' concerns are meritless.
