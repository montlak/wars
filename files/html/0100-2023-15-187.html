g. Threat Against a Coworker

Removal or suspension is a reasonable penalty for threatening coworkers. Howard v. Dept. of Air Force, 114 MSPR 482, 485-86 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 154 (2010). Metz criteria were applied in Shaw v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 98, 111-12 (1998), involving threatening behavior toward coworkers:

In determining whether a charge of making threats or engaging in threatening behavior can be sustained, the Board will apply the reasonable person standard, considering the speaker's intent, the conditional nature of his statements, the listeners' reactions, their apprehension of harm, and the attendant circumstances. See Metz v. Department of the Treasury, 780 F.2d 1001, 1004 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Here, we find that the most significant criteria are the listeners' reactions and apprehension of harm, and the attendant circumstances.

LePore's testimony reflects that the appellant's repeated angry outbursts caused her to consider obtaining a restraining order against him, and to eventually file a statement with the security police. Similarly, both Arrington and Hignight testified that they were intimidated by the appellant's loud and aggressive reaction when asked to cease going through LePore's storage bins. As noted above, Arrington had to back up defensively as the appellant approached him in an aggressive and confrontational manner, while Hignight felt obliged to intervene between the appellant and Arrington because he believed that there would be an altercation. We find that these employees reacted in such a way as to evince an apprehension of harm. See, e.g., Coleman v. Department of Air Force, 66 MSPR 498, 504-05 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1165 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table); Peloquin v. U.S. Postal Service, 51 MSPR 435, 439 (1991). Further, we find that the appellant's prior intemperate and abusive remarks during the course of the SAV inspection constitute attendant circumstances that support a finding of threatening conduct. See Sims v. Department of Defense, 58 MSPR 131, 135-36 (1993). Moreover, although we note that the appellant was diagnosed as suffering from depression, there is no medical evidence that his threatening conduct was the result of his mental illness. See, e.g., Battle v. Department of Transportation, 63 MSPR 403, 406 (1994)....

Pope v. Dept. of Navy, 63 MSPR 51, 54 (1994), sustained a 90-day suspension for an otherwise exemplary and long-term employee who was accused, among other charges, of threatening a coworker:

Threatening to strike Kiel, as the appellant did, is serious, intentional misconduct that is destructive to the work environment. Also, being subjected to intimidation in the workplace in the form of a threat of physical violence is not something that any federal employee should have to tolerate, and thus, a severe sanction must be imposed for its deterrence value. Further, the appellant repeatedly and intentionally used abusive language toward her supervisor, a co-worker, and agency police officers. In addition, the appellant unjustifiably and intentionally refused to supply information to agency officials who were investigating her actions toward Kiel and Lee. Finally, the appellant did not follow agency rules requiring her to register her car with base security personnel. With respect to all of this misconduct, the appellant was defiant and showed no remorse.

A threat against an employee in a hospital, not in public view, warranted discharge in Hatton v. VA, 7 MSPR 103, 105, 7 MSPB 31 (1981). Off-duty threats by one employee to another did not warrant mitigation of removal because the other employee pulled out his pocket knife after the threats were made. The other employee's actions, although excessive, were provoked by the appellant's unprovoked threats. Brown v. USPS, 41 MSPR 485, 487 (1989); Bonsu v. VA, 3 MSPR 125, 127-28 (1980) (sustaining removal of a nursing assistant for disrespectful conduct and threatening a nurse). A discharge was upheld as to an employee who, with a pocket knife in hand, told a coworker that he would cut the buttons off her overalls. The case was analyzed under Metz factors. McGuire v. Dept. of Army, 333 Fed. Appx. 528 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP).
