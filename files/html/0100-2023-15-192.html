l. Mitigation

The question of the mitigating effect of psychological conditions on adverse action for a threat was considered but not persuasive, Lawson v. VA, NY-0752-10-0185-I-1 (NP 4/10/2014):

The agency removed the appellant, a GS-6 Teller (Typing), based on four charges: (1) rudeness and an unacceptable level of customer service to patients; (2) rudeness to a patient's family; (3) failure to complete a work assignment in a timely manner; and (4) making threatening remarks in the workplace. After a hearing, the administrative judge found that the agency proved charges 1 and 4, but not charges 2 and 3. The administrative judge found that the agency established a nexus between the proven misconduct and the efficiency of the service and further found that the penalty of removal was within the bounds of reasonableness under the circumstances for the sustained charges. The administrative judge found that the appellant's misconduct was attributable to her psychiatric condition, but that her condition was not a significant mitigating factor because she failed to prove that her condition could be controlled or remedied....

...

...The appellant argues, among other things, that she was not a threat because she did not actually own a gun and her coworkers did not genuinely feel threatened. She claims that she was merely an "eccentric" employee who posed no danger to agency personnel or veterans. She further argues that, even if, "in her ramblings and unconscious communications[ ] and expressions," she made some "inchoate threats," her actions were "due solely to the massive effects of the medications and hospitalizations that was visited upon her and that she was laboring under." We have considered the appellant's arguments, but for the following reasons, we do not disturb the administrative judge's explained finding that the agency proved its charge of making threatening remarks in the workplace.

...

We have considered the appellant's evidence and argument regarding her psychiatric condition as an "attendant circumstance" in the Metz criteria. See Battle v. Department of Transportation, 63 M.S.P.R. 403, 407 (1994) (considering an appellant's mental condition and evidence of bipolar disorder as an attendant circumstance under Metz). We find, however, that the evidence still supports a finding that the appellant made a threat. Although the medical evidence indicates that the appellant was diagnosed with bipolar disorder, the record does not establish that her statements were merely an unthreatening manifestation of her condition, see also Battle, 63 M.S.P.R. at 407 (finding that the appellant's evidence regarding his medical conditions was insufficient "to show that his statement was merely an outgrowth of his disease, and not a threat of physical harm").[4]

[4] We further consider the appellant's medical condition in the context of our analysis of the penalty as set forth below.

...

On review, the appellant argues in particular that her psychiatric condition should have been considered as a mitigating factor. Whether an employee's medical condition or mental impairment played a part in the charged misconduct is ordinarily entitled to considerable weight as a mitigating factor. Woebcke v. Department of Homeland Security, 114 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 15 (2010). A medical or mental impairment is not a significant mitigating factor, however, in the absence of evidence that the impairment can be remedied or controlled, that is, when the potential for rehabilitation is poor. Id. An appellant's medical condition can be considered as a mitigating factor even absent a showing that the condition rises to the level of a disability or that her actions were beyond her control. Id. Nor is the appellant required to show through medical evidence that such condition contributed to the misconduct at issue. Id. The record reflects that the administrative judge properly considered the appellant's evidence and arguments concerning her medical condition under the appropriate standards. We have considered the appellant's evidence and argument on review but conclude that it is insufficient to show error in the administrative judge's decision to uphold the agency's penalty. The appellant has not shown a sufficient reason to disturb the administrative judge's findings that the record did not show that the appellant's condition could be adequately controlled and thus that her condition outweighed the other factors supporting the agency's penalty, such as the serious nature of her proven misconduct. See Anderson v. Department of the Navy, 42 M.S.P.R. 16, 19 (1989); Murray v. Department of the Army, 40 M.S.P.R. 250, 258 (1989).

We also have considered the appellant's claims that her coworkers were harassing her and generally conspiring to have her removed. Harassment and unusual job tensions may serve as grounds for mitigation. Mosby v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 114 M.S.P.R. 674, ¶ 8 (2010); Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305 (1981) (potential factors in determining the reasonableness of a penalty include mitigating circumstances surrounding the offense such as unusual job tensions, personality problems, mental impairment, harassment, or bad faith, malice or provocation on the part of others involved in the matter). Having reviewed the appellant's claims, however, we find that the appellant has not provided a persuasive basis for disturbing the administrative judge's findings regarding the credibility of the coworkers' statements.
