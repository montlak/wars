h. Suspension, Removal Under 5 USC 7532

Under 5 USC 7532, an agency may take immediate action to suspend an employee without pay if the agency head considers that action necessary to the interests of national security. The employee has a right to a hearing, on request, and the agency must appoint a security hearing board, provided the employee is a citizen of the United States and a permanent or indefinite appointee. Some agencies provide for reassignment to unclassified duties in lieu of suspension. When reassignment is the agency's choice, rather than suspension without pay, nothing requires the agency to grant a hearing to the employee. Agencies may issue their own hearing regulations.

5 USC 7532 provides:

(a) Notwithstanding other statutes, the head of an agency may suspend without pay an employee of his agency when he considers that action necessary in the interests of national security. To the extent that the head of the agency determines that the interests of national security permit, the suspended employee shall be notified of the reasons for the suspension. Within 30 days after the notification, the suspended employee is entitled to submit to the official designated by the head of the agency statements or affidavits to show why he should be restored to duty.

(b) Subject to subsection (c) of this section, the head of an agency may remove an employee suspended under subsection (a) of this section when, after such investigation and review as he considers necessary, he determines that removal is necessary or advisable in the interests of national security. The determination of the head of the agency is final.

(c) An employee suspended under subsection (a) of this section who--

(1) has a permanent or indefinite appointment;

(2) has completed his probationary or trial period; and

(3) is a citizen of the United States;

is entitled, after suspension and before removal, to--

(A) a written statement of the charges against him within 30 days after suspension, which may be amended within 30 days thereafter and which shall be stated as specifically as security considerations permit;

(B) an opportunity within 30 days thereafter, plus an additional 30 days if the charges are amended, to answer the charges and submit affidavits;

(C) a hearing, at the request of the employee, by an agency authority duly constituted for this purpose;

(D) a review of his case by the head of the agency or his designee, before a decision adverse to the employee is made final; and

(E) a written statement of the decision of the head of the agency.

Section 7532 requires that within 30 days of the suspension without pay, the employee is entitled to a statement of the charges against him or her. The agency may amend these charges within another 30 days. The notification of charges must be as specific as possible in light of any appropriate security considerations. 5 USC 7532(c)(3)(A). The notice must be more than a conclusory statement that continued employment is not clearly consistent with the interests in national security. Coleman v. Brucker, 257 F.2d 661 (D.C. Cir. 1958). The notice should provide the employee with sufficient information regarding the charges to take advantage of the statutory response rights. Deak v. Pace, 185 F.2d 997 (D.C. Cir. 1950). An agency may not cure defects in the notice of charges by asking the employee questions at the hearing where a spontaneous response is required. A reviewing court will look at the hearing record to determine if the notice of charges was sufficiently specific. Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535, 545 (1959) (information developed during a security hearing was not first identified in the charges or notice to the employee: "Because the proceedings attendant upon petitioner's dismissal from government service on grounds of national security fell substantially short of the requirements of the applicable departmental regulations, we hold that such dismissal was illegal, and of no effect."). In addition to the specifics of the charges, the notice should also inform the employee of the rights to file an answer and obtain a hearing, to be represented by counsel, to testify on his or her own behalf, to present witnesses and evidence in rebuttal to the charges, and to cross-examine witnesses presented in support of the charges.

The hearing board must include at least three civilian employees or officers of the federal government. The hearing officers are selected by the agency from a roster maintained by OPM. An employee may challenge any member but must show good cause for disqualification. The hearing officers are required to be disinterested persons and, therefore, no employee of the agency involved may serve on the board. Most agencies appoint a legal counsel to assist the board. The legal counsel is not a prosecutor, but he is responsible for aiding the board in resolving prehearing disputes and issues of procedure, and in assuring that a complete hearing record is developed. Upon request, the legal counsel may also provide advice to the employee concerning his or her rights.

Agency regulations generally do not provide for formal discovery. Agency regulations may preclude the hearing board from considering evidence not previously furnished to the employee. Agencies may withhold the identity of confidential informants and may also withhold information from employees on national security grounds. However, courts have been particularly circumspect in scrutinizing an agency's failure to produce witnesses because of their confidential status, Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535 (1959), supra, and withholding information from employees on security grounds, Deak v. Pace, 185 F.2d 997 (D.C. Cir. 1950), supra. Because the agency is required to provide with the charges only the evidence it will rely upon at the hearing, employees and their representatives should not overlook the possibility that the background investigation has revealed information favorable to the employee's case not disclosed in the notice of charges. The full investigation report should be requested under the Privacy Act.

Adverse information may be developed against an employee on the basis of statements provided by confidential informants. This evidence will usually be introduced into the record by the agency through affidavits redacted to protect the informant's identity. The lack of opportunity to cross-examine a confidential informant is not a basis for objecting to the evidence submitted, but the board must take the lack of opportunity for cross-examination into account in assessing that evidence. Although the agency is not required to call confidential informants in support of its case, it must call any witnesses not classified as confidential if information provided by the witness is used in assessing the employee's status. Otherwise, the employee is effectively deprived of his right to cross-examination. Vitarelli v. Seaton, supra. There is no prohibition against an agency calling the employee as a witness.

The hearings present a shifting burden of proof. The ultimate burden is on the employee to demonstrate that granting a security clearance is clearly consistent with the interest in national security. At the same time, the agency bears some burden to demonstrate that the employee has engaged in the conduct alleged and that a sufficient nexus exists between the conduct and national security. See, e.g., Gayer v. Schlesinger, 490 F.2d 740 (D.C. Cir. 1973). Often, the only issues are whether the nexus between the conduct and national security is sufficient, and whether the employee can present mitigating factors showing that granting the clearance is clearly consistent with the interests in national security.

A hearing board does not make either an initial or a final decision on the issuance of a clearance. The board develops a record in the context of an adversarial proceeding and then makes findings of fact and a recommendation on retention of the employee to the agency head. The board's report, along with the hearing record and any confidential information made available to the board, are forwarded to the agency head. Although the agency may withhold information from the notice of charges on national security grounds, see 5 USC 7532(c)(3)(A), the agency is not precluded from relying on that information to revoke or deny a security clearance. The agency head, or his designee, is required to review the case prior to issuing a final decision. Unless there is a different regulatory requirement, a letter that informs the employee of a decision to terminate employment based on a review of the record is adequate. Cole v. Young, 226 F.2d 337 (D.C. Cir. 1955), rev'd on other grounds, 351 U.S. 536 (1956). The summary nature of proceedings under § 7532 are constitutionally valid. See Carlucci v. Doe, 109 S. Ct. 407 (1988).

Section 7532 neither specifically authorizes nor precludes judicial review of agency decisions regarding termination of employment in the interest of national security. It does, however, provide that the determinations by agency heads under the section are final. Courts have not interpreted this language to be a total preclusion of judicial review. Courts have refrained from reviewing factual determinations, but they have asserted jurisdiction over actions in the nature of mandamus and declaratory relief to review claims of regulatory, statutory, or constitutional violations. See, e.g., Vitarelli v. Seaton, 359 U.S. 535 (1959); Deak v. Pace, 185 F.2d 997 (D.C. Cir. 1950), supra. Hearings under § 7532 are not subject to the Administrative Procedure Act. Coleman v. Brucker, 156 F. Supp. 126 (D.D.C. 1957), rev'd on other grounds, 257 F.2d 661 (1958).

Although an agency may use 5 USC 7532 to take action against an employee, it need not do so and may follow another route, Mitrano v. Dept. of Air Force, 730 Fed. Appx. 900, 903 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP):

Mr. Mitrano asserts that he was entitled to a hearing under 5 U.S.C. § 7532(c)(3) and a decision by the head of the relevant agency before he was removed from his position. But Section 7532, which provides for removal without Board review, is not the only provision under which an employee may be removed for reasons relating in some way to national security. An agency may follow the normal removal path under 5 U.S.C. § 7513 instead, with its different mix of benefits and burdens, including Board review, in which case Section 7532 is inapplicable. See Biggers v. Dep't of the Navy, 745 F.3d 1360, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2014); see also Dep't of the Navy v. Egan, 484 U.S. 518, 522-26, 532-34 (1988). Here, the Air Force followed the Section 7513 path. Mr. Mitrano therefore has no basis for invoking Section 7532.
