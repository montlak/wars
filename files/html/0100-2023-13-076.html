c. Identity of Duties

To demonstrate Russell entitlement to a position at a higher grade following reassignment from the position and the upward reclassification of the former position, the employee must show a substantial identity of duties between the old and successor positions. That the duties of the new position include the duties of the former position, augmented with additional duties, does not demonstrate a constructive adverse action. The Board lacked jurisdiction over an appeal brought as a reduction in grade by an employee who was reassigned to a position of the same grade but whose initial position was upgraded a few weeks later because of enhanced duties. Caporale v. NASA, 4 MSPR 161, 162, 4 MSPB 244 (1980); see Fuller v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 53, 54 (1982) ("[A]ppellant had not met her burden of demonstrating that the duties of the GS-5 position she held prior to reassignment were essentially the same as the GS-6 position's duties."). The determinations concerning the duties of the positions are made by the AJ after reviewing testimony or other evidence concerning the nature of the two positions. Russell v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 765, 767 (1983). The existence of some differences between the position descriptions at issue does not preclude a constructive demotion claim. Bittner v. NCUA, 76 MSPR 380, 385 (1997) (citing Spicer v. DOD, 59 MSPR 359, 363-66 (1993): "the Board found a constructive demotion, notwithstanding differences in the position descriptions of the former position and the upgraded position, where a classification expert testified that the differences were not grade-controlling").

Ellis v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 102, 108 (1997), explained that in a Russell appeal, the essential analysis is on grade-controlling duties:

Although, as discussed above, there are differences between the position descriptions for the appellant's former GS-5 Supply Technician position and the upgraded GS-6 Supply Technician position, the existence of some differences between the position descriptions in question is not dispositive of the issue of whether a constructive demotion occurred. See Spicer, 59 MSPR at 363-66 (the Board found a constructive demotion notwithstanding differences between the former position and the upgraded position, where a classification expert testified that the differences were not grade-controlling).

Woolard v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 504, 506-07 (1982), compared position responsibilities after the appellant's reassignment and the subsequent abolition and recreation of the job allegedly previously held by the appellant:

As pointed out by the presiding official, the best evidence as to whether the duties of the two positions in question are the same is the respective position descriptions for appellant's former GS-322-4 Clerk-Typist position and the current GS-318-05 Secretary (Typing) position to which she claims entitlement. However, unlike the presiding official, we find that a comparison of the two position descriptions reveals that the current GS-318-05 job is one of greater responsibilities and consequently warrants a proportionately higher classification rating.

The positions differ primarily with regard to the greater degree of discretion afforded the incumbent of the GS-5 job. As an example, a major duty of that position is to answer some substantive telephone inquiries personally, whereas the incumbent of the GS-4 job is required merely to refer incoming calls to others and answer occasional routine or procedural questions. Another indicant of the greater responsibility and initiative required of one in the GS-5 position is the duty to schedule the supervisor's appointments and meetings, based upon personal knowledge of the supervisor's interests and commitments, and without the supervisor's prior clearance. That position also requires the incumbent to anticipate the supervisor's need for, and to obtain and present, background information and material for scheduled meetings. The incumbent of the GS-4 position, by contrast, is required merely to make appointments for staff members according to specific instructions.

The most significant difference between the two positions is the fact that the GS-5 incumbent personally serves the chief of an important sub-element of a major Army headquarters, while the GS-4 employee serves a general staff rather than a high ranking individual supervisor. While the level of a secretary's supervisor's administrative responsibilities is not a conclusive determinant of secretarial grade, it does have a direct relationship to the potential scope and nature of the secretary's duties and, as in this case, weighs heavily in favor of justifying the difference between a GS-4 and GS-5 grade rating. Here, the level of responsibility of the supervisor of the GS-5 incumbent is reflected in a commensurate level of initiative required of the holder of that position. While the GS-4 Clerk-Typist serves a staff based on general instructions and is required to have a clerical knowledge of the organization, the GS-5 Secretary (Typing) serves a higher-ranking individual, within the framework of that supervisor's desired results and personal preferences, and is required to have "sufficient knowledge of the substantive program under [the] supervisor's control," as well as of the supervisor's "duties, priorities, commitments, and program goals...."

Accordingly, the Board finds that the duties of the GS-318-05 Secretary (Typing) position to which appellant claims entitlement are significantly greater than those of her former GS-322-4 Clerk-Typist position, and that appellant has failed to demonstrate otherwise. Thus, appellant has not shown that her former GS-4 position was upgraded due to the issuance of a new classification standard by the Office of Personnel Management, or that it was upgraded due to the correction of a classification error by the agency. We conclude that appellant's former position was not upgraded, but was abolished, and that the new GS-5 position was established by the agency pursuant to a reorganization. Therefore, the evidence adduced in this case does not meet the criteria set forth in Russell....
