1. Closing Record

At some point, production of evidence ends and adjudication begins. For the Board, the presentation of evidence and argument to the AJ concludes when the record closes. 5 CFR 1201.59 (2022) establishes the basic rules governing closing of the record:

(a) When there is a hearing, the record ordinarily will close at the conclusion of the hearing. When the judge allows the parties to submit argument, briefs, or documents previously identified for introduction into evidence, however, the record will remain open for as much time as the judge grants for that purpose.

(b) If the appellant waives the right to a hearing, the record will close on the date the judge sets as the final date for the receipt or filing of submissions of the parties.

(c) Once the record closes, additional evidence or argument will ordinarily not be accepted unless:

(1) The party submitting it shows that the evidence or argument was not readily available before the record closed; or

(2) It is in rebuttal to new evidence or argument submitted by the other party just before the record closed.

(d) The judge will include in the record any supplemental citations received from the parties or approved corrections of the transcript, if one has been prepared.

Regulatory revisions in 2012 imposed some regulatory due process on last minute submissions. Preexisting case law addressing those submissions is included in the discussion that follows. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Adjudicated Without a Hearing; Respond to Evidentiary Submission."]

Control of record development is within the sound discretion of the AJ, Hathaway v. OPM, 118 MSPR 678, 680-81 ¶ 6, 2012 MSPB 128 (2012):

Administrative judges have broad authority in adjudicating their cases, and this broad authority includes the authority to sua sponte extend the record closing date to ensure that the record is fully developed and that the case is fairly and justly adjudicated. See Palmer v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 552, 555 (1991); Walton v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 48 M.S.P.R. 462, 468 (1991). The administrative judge did not abuse her discretion in reopening the record under the circumstances of this case.

Although AJs have discretion in determining when to close the record, the exercise of that discretion must comport with the basic requirements of fairness, notice, and an opportunity to respond to the submissions of the parties. See Nordhoff v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 45, 48 (1995). "The Board has long recognized that it is error for an AJ to issue an order or initial decision prior to the expiration of the time allowed for a party's response." Crumpton v. Dept. of Treasury, 98 MSPR 115, 118-19 ¶ 8 (2004); see Senyszyn v. Dept. of Treasury, 110 MSPR 437, 439-40 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 11 (2009) (citing Crumpton: confusing order from the AJ; the Board remedied the problem by considering the appellant's submission on PFR). AJs "are required to use fair procedures that afford each party to an appeal an opportunity to respond to any other party's arguments and evidence." Strauss v. OPM, 39 MSPR 132, 136 (1988); see Benson v. OPM, 83 MSPR 549, 553 (1999) (remanding because the AJ closed the record and adjudicated the case without holding a hearing or establishing a date for closing the record, with the result that the appellant did not get the hearing she requested and OPM was unable to obtain the AJ's review of medical reports); Blackmer v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 571, 574 (1992) ("Determining when to close the record is within the sound discretion of the administrative judge, but such discretion must comport with basic requirements of fairness and notice."); Strickler v. OPM, 51 MSPR 354, 357 (1991) (no error by AJ for closing the record at the conclusion of the hearing when OPM repeatedly failed to submit a file to the Board, failed to participate in either the prehearing conference or the hearing, and failed to submit the agency file when the initial decision was issued); Lamb v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 79, 84 n.1 (1989) (Board received agency affidavits at the PFR stage: "The appellant cannot, in all fairness, raise material issues just prior to the closing of the record and then attempt to preclude the agency from responding."); Schmidt v. USPS, 39 MSPR 188, 194 (1988) (AJ should have permitted the appellant to respond to the agency's submissions, including affidavits, pertaining to the jurisdictional issue of the appellant's duties); Groux v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 288, 290 (1983) (it was within the AJ's sound discretion to grant the first request for extension, then deny the second request and close the record); Sarich v. Dept. of Interior, 12 MSPR 144, 146 (1982) (AJ erred by closing the record before permitting the agency to respond to evidence from the agency EEO office, submitted directly to the AJ, tending to demonstrate the RIF was a sham); Armour v. OPM, 9 MSPR 243, 246 (1981) (AJ should have sought the appellant's response to an OPM evaluative memorandum; the memo, containing material not previously submitted, was relied on by the AJ in the initial decision); Garone v. OPM, 3 MSPR 478, 479 (1980) (remanding, following the offer of new evidence with a PFR, because the record did not contain evidence that the appellant received notice of an opportunity to submit evidence in support of an appeal or a notice of the closing of the record). But see Hillis v. OPM, 40 MSPR 592, 596 (1989) (no harm when the agency did not present new evidence or issues, but only argued its interpretation of evidence already in the record; the appellant did not request an opportunity to respond to the agency submission before the AJ, but first raised the issue in a PFR).

AJs should hesitate before excluding submissions of the parties if the submission is but slightly delayed and no prejudice results, as Goodale v. USDA, 5 MSPR 65, 67, 5 MSPB 139 (1981), considering exclusion of a brief, explained:

We note, for the future benefit of presiding officials, that when a submission is late, exclusion of the submission is a serious matter since it impinges on a party's right to be heard. When the issue of timeliness does not involve a jurisdictional question, a submission should be excluded when the tardiness affects the other party or the Board's orderly and timely processing of cases. When the tardiness is insubstantial, and the party has made a good faith effort to comply with the time requirements, exclusion is an inappropriate sanction.

A delayed submission may be excluded. In Dorrall v. Dept. of Army, 301 F.3d 1375, 1379-80 (Fed. Cir. 2002), the court affirmed the Board's rejection of a jurisdictional statement from the appellant in a constructive adverse action. The statement was four or five days after the date set by the AJ. The court observed that the appellant failed to show due diligence or the existence of circumstances beyond his control that preventing him from meeting the filing deadline.

Parties must take reasonable steps to determine the status of an appeal or hearing request if confusion results from the manner that the AJ handles a case. In Hughes v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 373, 375 (1983), the Board refused to reopen a RIF appeal based on the agency's claim that it did not know it was required to submit evidence to the AJ because it thought a hearing would be held. The AJ's notice indicated the closing of the record. The agency's receipt of evidence from the appellant should have put the agency on clear notice that no hearing would be held. One record closed without an agency submission because the notice of the record closing was sent by the AJ to the agency personnel officer rather than to the designated agency representative. There was no reversible error. The agency did not show that the representative was unaware of the notice. The agency failed to show why the personnel officer did not transmit the letter from the AJ to the agency representative. Queen v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 434, 435-36 (1984). Another AJ failed to issue an order closing the record, but the omission was harmless. The agency knew that there would be no hearing, that a decision was necessary within 120 days, and that the appellant submitted information to the AJ. The agency did not exercise due diligence and the agency did not clearly place the AJ on notice that it desired to introduce evidence. Finally, the agency failed to show in its PFR what evidence would have been submitted. Howard v. Dept. of Commerce, 22 MSPR 606, 609-10 (1984); cf. Fidler v. USPS, 53 MSPR 440, 444 (1992) (AJ erred by failing to notify the parties of the date that the record would close; the error was harmless because the appellant made no proffer of the relevance or materiality of evidence that she received during discovery); Gala v. USPS, 38 MSPR 474, 478 (1988) (record was closed too early but the appellant's substantive rights were not denigrated); Purser v. IRS, 22 MSPR 624, 627-28 (1984) (AJ's failure to notify the agency of record of the closing date; the error had no effect on the outcome); Alhouse v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 181, 183 (1984) (assuming that the AJ erred by closing the record at the end of the hearing rather than keeping the record open to receive affidavits from the appellant, the appellant failed to show how the error prejudiced his substantive rights). But see Ruffin v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 396, 398-99 (2001) ("When the administrative judge decides that a hearing is not necessary, he is responsible for setting a final date by which the parties must file their submissions"; the case was remanded to allow the appellant a hearing if the parties' submissions showed a factual dispute concerning the appellant's entitlement to VEOA relief).

An AJ errs if she fails to consider evidence or argument submitted before the date set for the closing of the record. The Board cures the error, if possible, through the PFR process by considering the submissions that should have been considered by the AJ. See Hudson v. DHUD, 55 MSPR 639, 641 (1992); Winslow v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 246, 249 (1990); McCloud v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 643, 646 (1987); Jackson v. DOD, 28 MSPR 463, 465 (1985); Burum v. OPM, 26 MSPR 617, 620 (1985); cf. Chudson v. EPA, 71 MSPR 115, 118 (1996) (Board considered evidence submitted with the appellant's PFR concerning a timeliness issue that the AJ raised without notice to the parties and without an opportunity to respond to the issue); Jones v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 241, 243 (1985) (Board considered submissions of the appellant not reviewed by the prior to the initial decision). But cf. Herrmann v. USPS, 29 MSPR 213, 215 (1985) (rejecting the allegation that the AJ did not consider the appellant's position because the initial decision was issued hastily following the AJ's receipt of the appellant's submission; the submission principally concerned a nondeterminative issue).

If an AJ states that she will keep the record open for receipt of affidavits, but instead closes the record at the conclusion of the hearing, although an error may exist in the adjudicatory procedure, there must be demonstrable prejudice to substantive rights before the result will be overturned and the case remanded. Alshouse v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 181, 183 (1984) (means of demonstrating prejudice were not discussed; presumably the appellant would submit the affidavits not considered by the AJ to the Board with a PFR arguing that they were material evidence). If an AJ fails to consider a party's closing argument, it is definitely an error, but not a harmful one if the appellant cannot show how the initial decision was affected by that failing. Surface v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 102, 103 (1986).

It was not harmful error that the appellant did not receive sufficient time to respond to the regional order to show cause why his appeal should not be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. No showing was made through the PFR that any error affected his substantive rights or that a different outcome would have resulted had he received an opportunity to submit information to the AJ. Holden v. OPM, 32 MSPR 367, 368 (1987); see James v. USPS, 64 MSPR 376, 379-80 (1994) (no harm when the AJ accepted and considered the agency's belated response to a compliance petition); White v. USPS, 64 MSPR 261, 267 (1994) (no harmful error when the appellant was not allowed to respond to a late agency submission: the appellant did not demonstrate in her PFR what response she would have made or how she could show Board jurisdiction; additionally, it was not clear that the appellant would have had a right to reply even if the agency submission was timely); Pierce v. DOD, 9 MSPR 556, 560 (1981) (no harmful error when the appellant was not served with a copy of the agency's closing argument, the argument was included in other agency submissions served on the appellant). But see Jordan v. OPM, 6 MSPR 389, 389 (1981) (remand for a new decision based on consideration of all the submissions of the parties: "Our presiding official advises the Board that certain evidence in this case was misplaced and that his initial decision was issued without consideration of that evidence.").
