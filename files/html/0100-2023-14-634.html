5. Training; Other Assistance to Employees

A nonnegotiable proposal required the agency to provide training to an employee as part of his performance improvement plan. AFGE, Nat'l Council of VA Locals and VA, 29 FLRA 515, 540-41 (1987) (Proposal 8). NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 38 FLRA 456, 474-75 (1990), resolved as nonnegotiable Proposal 6, seeking agreement that "[e]mployees who receive a rating of record below the fully successful level must receive sufficient assistance in the form of formal training, on-the-job training, counseling, and close supervision and other appropriate measures to enable them to perform at the Fully Successful level." FLRA recognized that the then-controlling regulation, 5 CFR 430.204(i) [somewhat similar to the 1999 regulation, 5 CFR 430.204(b)(1)(v)], called on agencies to develop appraisal systems that provide assistance to include training, counseling, and close supervision to employees who need assistance in improving their performance to a fully successful level:

...A proposal that paraphrases a Government-wide regulation is within the duty to bargain unless the proposal directly interferes with management's rights or is inconsistent with applicable regulations.... However, where incorporation of the terms of a Government-wide regulation directly into the contract would have the effect of imposing an independent, contractual requirement on an agency's discretion to exercise its management rights, a proposal doing so directly interferes with those rights....

In contrast to 5 C.F.R. § 430.204(i), which requires that agency appraisal systems provide for assistance, which may include training, to employees whose performance is below the Fully Successful level, the proposal requires that formal and on-the-job training be provided if necessary to enable employees to perform at the Fully Successful level. The Authority has consistently held that proposals requiring an Agency to provide job-related training during duty hours are outside the duty to bargain because the assignment of that type of training constitutes an assignment of work....

NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE, also considered Proposal 14, that the agency include on-the-job training and formalized training, as an inappropriate arrangement for employees whose performance was below fully satisfactory. The proposal was nonnegotiable, but on narrow grounds, 38 FLRA at 500-01:

[T]he potential impact on an employee who receives a less than fully successful performance rating can include denial of a within-grade increase, reduction in grade, and removal. It is axiomatic that these actions are among the most serious that can occur in an employee's Federal career. Thus, the nature and extent of the reasonably foreseeable impact on employees of a less than "Fully Successful" rating is severe.

While poor performance is often viewed as entirely within the control of employees, the "fault" is not necessarily entirely that of the employee. We cannot rule out the possibility that the nature and quality of supervision and training provided an employee can contribute to one degree or another to the level of employee performance.... However, we also recognize that employees generally bear the greater share of the responsibility for sustaining acceptable performance.

...While the agency may retain the discretion to determine the type, time and manner of the training, the proposal requires that some sort of training be provided. Thus, under the proposal, the Agency must provide training even in circumstances where training is not a reasonable or appropriate remedial action for the employee's particular performance deficiency.

...Such an unconditional requirement places an unnecessary burden on the Agency.

In our view, the negative impact on management's right to assign work caused by an absolute requirement that training be provided employees whose performance is less than Fully Successful, where that requirement does not allow for consideration of the merits of that particular choice of remedial action in the face of the circumstances involved, outweighs the benefits to be afforded employees. We conclude that the fifth sentence of Proposal 14 excessively interferes with management's right to assign work....

A contract provision for management assistance in improving performance of employees adversely affected by the exercise of the agency's right to appraise the employee's work was a properly negotiated arrangement. It attended to the immediate needs for remedial assistance and support for those employees, while it assured management's right to reassign employees for performance reasons after a reasonable opportunity to perform. An arbitrator's award directing the parties to attempt to determine, consistent with the provisions of the agreement pertaining to training and career development, what appropriate remedial training may be available for the grievant did not improperly impinge on management prerogatives in the assignment of training during the duty time of employees. The same reasoning made unobjectionable the directive of the arbitrator that the parties attempt to determine what necessary funds were available to provide appropriate remedial training. OPM and AFGE Local 32, 19 FLRA 39, 40-41 (1985). [Refer in this chapter to "Training" and "Opportunity to Improve Performance or Conduct."]

A negotiable proposal required the agency to provide its employees with the guidance and reference materials necessary to perform their jobs in a satisfactory manner. The requirement that performance standards be communicated to employees includes a requirement to supply employees with information necessary for the satisfactory achievement of those standards. The proposal was distinguished from those nonnegotiable proposals requiring training of employees, since the proposal did not call for an assignment of work to employees. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 28 FLRA 714, 719-21 (1987) (Proposal 4).

Nothing precluded negotiation of a clause that employees faced with possible performance-based actions have the opportunity to participate in the agency Employee Assistance Program (EAP) and that, "[b]ased on such consultation, management shall specify to the employee the conditions under which it will defer, modify or avoid an action based on less than satisfactory performance." As to the negotiability of that clause, Provision 44, POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 83-84 (1993), determined:

At the outset, we note that, contrary to the Agency's assertion, the disputed sentence in Provision 44 does not prevent management from taking disciplinary action. Rather, Provision 44 provides that management, after consulting with the EAP Coordinator, shall specify to an employee who is performing unacceptably the conditions "under which it will defer, modify or avoid" a proposed performance-based action. Moreover, management retains exclusive authority to determine the circumstances warranting changes to proposed discipline based on performance. By its terms, the provision does not require the establishment of conditions under which performance-based discipline will be mitigated. Rather, the provision only obligates management to reveal to affected employees the conditions, if any, it has determined, after consultation with the EAP Coordinator, would cause deferral, modification, or cancellation of an action.

[P]rovisions that require an agency to notify employees about matters relating to conditions of employment do not directly interfere with management's rights and are negotiable, provided they do not require the release of information which otherwise is protected.... The conditions under which the Agency would decide to defer, modify, or avoid a performance-based action unquestionably concern the employee's conditions of employment. Moreover, the obligation to advise an employee how to avoid, postpone, or mitigate a performance-based action does not bind management to any particular course of action. Accordingly, we conclude that the notice requirement in Provision 44 is negotiable.

FLRA declared nonnegotiable a proposal that the employer normally allow employees who have accepted assistance under the agency EAP a reasonable opportunity to improve work performance before being disciplined. The proposal precluded the agency from disciplining employees who, having been referred to the program for drug use, repeated that use while they were in the program. The protection sought through the proposal was contrary to § 5(d) of Exec. Order 12564. See AFGE Council of Prison Locals and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 47 FLRA 836, 845, 848-51 (1993) (Provision 3). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Drug Use and Drug Detection."]
