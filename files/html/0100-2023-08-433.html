a. SES Performance Appraisal Dispute

Charrow v. FRTIB, 102 MSPR 345, 346-50 ¶¶ 1-13, 2006 MSPB 171 (2006), determined that SES performance appraisals may not be challenged through regulatory implementation cases:

The Board is given jurisdiction by 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f) to review in its discretion final regulations issued by the Director of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM). When the Board exercises this authority, it may declare an OPM regulation invalid on its face if it determines that implementation of the regulation would require a prohibited personnel practice. In addition, the Board may declare an OPM regulation invalidly implemented by an agency if the Board determines that the implementation of the regulation has required a prohibited personnel practice. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f)(2). Burroughs v. Office of Personnel Management, 94 M.S.P.R. 315, ¶ 4 (2003), aff'd, 89 Fed. Appx. 699 (Fed. Cir. 2004).

The petitioner presents a challenge to her employing agency's implementation of OPM's regulations governing performance appraisals of members of the Senior Executive Service (SES). She contends that the appraisal of her performance in her SES position at the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board (the agency) resulted in a prohibited personnel practice in violation of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(12). For the reasons stated below, the Board denies petitioner's request for review.

The agency removed the petitioner from her career SES position of Director, Office of Communications, for Unsatisfactory performance. Pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 3592(a), petitioner exercised her right to present argument concerning the agency's action at an informal hearing before an administrative law judge (ALJ) designated by the Board. The ALJ expressed agreement with some of the arguments raised by petitioner, but he lacked authority to issue a decision that was binding on the agency, see id., and the agency chose not to modify the action. Finding that an independent review would be appropriate, the ALJ referred the record of the proceeding to OPM and the Office of Special Counsel. So far as the record shows, these agencies have taken no action concerning petitioner's case.

The petitioner then instituted this proceeding in which she requests that the Board review the agency's alleged "invalid implementation" of two OPM regulations that govern senior executive performance management. The first OPM regulation provides that each agency must devise a system for "appraising each senior executive's performance against requirements using measures that balance organizational results with customer and employee perspectives." 5 C.F.R. § 430.304(b)(4). The second regulation provides that each agency "must develop a method for deriving summary ratings from appraisals of performance against performance requirements." 5 C.F.R. § 430.304(c)(3). According to petitioner, the performance requirements under which she was rated Unsatisfactory are inconsistent with these regulations so that her appraisal and her removal based on it violated the statutes governing SES performance evaluations and thus constituted a prohibited personnel practice.

The agency urges the Board to deny petitioner's request for review since she has already exercised her rights to obtain an informal hearing and also may obtain an equivalent remedy through the discrimination complaint concerning the agency's actions that she has filed. The agency further contends, in the alternative, that its implementation of the relevant OPM regulations was a valid one. Petitioner argues that the Board should exercise its discretion to review her challenge because there is no direct appeal from the removal of an SES member for unacceptable performance and because the referral of her case to OPM and OSC has resulted in no action by either agency.

This Case Is Not An Appropriate One In Which To Exercise The Board's Regulation Review Authority.

Petitioner brings her claim under 5 U.S.C. § 1204, which provides in relevant part as follows:

(f)(1) At any time after the effective date of any rule or regulation issued by the Director of the Office of Personnel Management in carrying out functions under section 1103, the Board shall review any provision of such rule or regulation--

...

(B) on the granting by the Board, in its sole discretion, of any petition for such review filed with the Board by any interested person, after consideration of the petition by the Board;...

...

(2) In reviewing any provision of any rule or regulation pursuant to this subsection, the Board shall declare such provision--

...

(B) invalidly implemented by any agency, if the Board determines that such provision, as it has been implemented by the agency through any personnel action taken by the agency or through any policy adopted by the agency in conformity with such provision, has required any employee to violate section 2302(b).

Under the statute, the decision whether to grant review is a discretionary judgment for the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f)(1)(B); Johnson v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 265, ¶ 4 (2003). The most commonly-identified factors that the Board considers in deciding whether to grant review under section 1204(f)(1)(B) are "the likelihood that a given issue will be reached in a timely fashion through ordinary channels of appeal, the availability of other equivalent remedies, the extent of the regulation's application to the Federal service, and the strength of the arguments against the validity of its implementation." McDiarmid v. U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service, 19 M.S.P.R. 347, review dismissed, 23 M.S.P.R. 420 (1984). These factors should not be applied in a rigid or formulaic way, however, and in fact, as demonstrated below, the present case does not fit neatly into the McDiarmid framework.

Title IV of the Civil Service Reform Act (CSRA) of 1978, Pub. L. No. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111, created the SES to be "a corps of top management leaders." S. Rep. No. 95-969, 96th Cong. 1st Sess. 67 (1978). The SES was intended to represent "the most highly motivated and competent Federal service leadership possible." Id. Congress wanted to "make it possible for career employees to serve in top-level policy jobs," and to "make tenure as executives contingent upon successful performance." Id. at 68. It defined an SES member as an employee who "directs the work of an organization, is accountable for the success of specific programs, monitors organizational progress towards goals, supervises the work of employees other than personal assistants, or exercises other important policymaking or executive functions." Id. at 69. See 5 U.S.C. § 3132(a)(2).

Title II of the CSRA, which reconfigured the government's performance management system, contained separate provisions governing the newly-created SES that differ from those applicable to non-executive employees. Compare 5 U.S.C. §§ 4301-4305 with 5 U.S.C. §§ 4311-4315. Notably, Title II conferred on non-executive employees the right to appeal to the Board from actions based on unacceptable performance. See 5 U.S.C. § 4303(e)(3) (an employee "who has been reduced in grade or removed under this section is entitled to appeal the action to the Merit Systems Protection Board under section 7701"). In such an appeal, the agency bears the burden of proving its charge of unacceptable performance, and the Board is empowered to issue a binding decision reversing the action. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c).

Congress did not confer a similar right of appeal on senior executives removed for poor performance. Instead, the following provision was enacted:

(a) Except as provided in subsection (b) of this section, a career appointee may be removed from the Senior Executive Service to a civil service position outside of the Senior Executive Service--

(1) during the 1-year period of probation under section 3393(d) of this title, or

(2) at any time for less than fully successful executive performance as determined under subchapter II of chapter 43 of this title, except that in the case of a removal under paragraph (2) of this subsection the career appointee shall, at least 15 days before the removal, be entitled, upon request, to an informal hearing before an official designated by the Merit Systems Protection Board at which the career appointee may appear and present arguments, but such hearing shall not give the career appointee the right to initiate an action with the Board under section 7701 of this title, nor need the removal action be delayed as a result of the granting of such hearing.

5 U.S.C. § 3592 (emphasis supplied).

It is highly significant that Congress conferred on non-executives the right to appeal performance-based actions to the Board under section 7701, yet in the very same piece of legislation created a new corps of top managers, the SES, and chose not to allow them to appeal performance-based removals under section 7701. Petitioner in this case seeks to do an end-run around the clear legislative intent that there be no Board review resulting in a binding decision when a senior executive is removed for poor performance. Obviously, it is more than unlikely that the validity of the agency's implementation of 5 C.F.R. §§ 430.304(b)(4) & (c)(3) will be reached "through ordinary channels of appeal," McDiarmid, 19 M.S.P.R. at 349. However, this result is the product of Congress's deliberate choice not to permit a senior executive to appeal to the Board under section 7701 when he or she is removed for performance-based reasons.

Sections 1204(f) and 3592(a) must be read in harmony. The Board finds that it should not exercise its discretion under section 1204 to review the agency's implementation of OPM's regulations governing SES performance management. To do so, we believe, would thwart the clear legislative intent underlying section 3592 that there be no binding review by the Board of a removal from the SES for performance-based reasons. Congress envisioned a circumscribed role for the Board vis-à-vis the government's "corps of top management leaders," and the Board should not accept petitioner's invitation to expand that role. Although petitioner's filing comes dressed in the unusual legal garb of 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f)(2)(B), in terms of the arguments raised and the relief sought it is essentially a chapter 43 performance appeal, precisely what Congress intended not be available to a senior executive. See Regulation Review File, Tab 1 at 3, 5, 6 (petitioner contends that her performance standards were faulty and that her appraisal under them violated 5 U.S.C. § 4312, and seeks as relief reinstatement and back pay).

Finally, Oxley v. Department of Commerce, 24 M.S.P.R. 398 (1984), cited by petitioner, does not support granting her request for review. In that case, a career senior executive appealed his separation in a reduction in force (RIF). The Board reversed on the ground that the agency had not correctly determined the appellant's performance-weighted retention standing because of its improper implementation of the statutory provisions governing SES performance management. In Oxley, however, the Board did not review the agency's SES performance management system under section 1204(f). Rather, jurisdiction was predicated on 5 U.S.C. § 3595(c), which provides that a career senior executive is entitled to appeal to the Board when he or she is separated by RIF and claims that the agency misapplied the performance-weighting mechanism required by 5 U.S.C. § 3595(a). The Board's review there of the agency's SES performance rating was therefore within the scope of a RIF appeal that itself fell within the Board's statutory jurisdiction.
