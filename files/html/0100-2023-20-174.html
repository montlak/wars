3. MSPB Referral to OSC

In 1994 the Whistleblower Protection Act was amended to require the Board to refer to OSC certain matters that come to the attention of the Board during its adjudication of whistleblower cases. Section 4(c) of Pub. L. 103-424, codified at 5 USC 1221(f)(3), provides:

If, based on evidence presented to it under this section, the Merit Systems Protection Board determines that there is reason to believe that a current employee may have committed a prohibited personnel practice, the Board shall refer the matter to the Special Counsel to investigate and take appropriate action under section 1215.

1997 amendments to the Board's regulations provide, 5 CFR 1209.13 (2022):

§ 1209.13 Referral of findings to the Special Counsel.

When the Board determines in a proceeding under this part that there is reason to believe that a current Federal employee may have committed a prohibited personnel practice described at 5 U.S.C. 2302(b)(8) or (b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D), the Board will refer the matter to the Special Counsel to investigate and take appropriate action under 5 U.S.C. 1215.

MSPB does not direct an agency to take disciplinary action against a manager or a supervisor when an appellant proves whistleblower reprisal in an appeal or IRA. It refers the finding to OSC, and OSC decides how to proceed, e.g., by initiating a disciplinary complaint with MSPB. Stern v. VA, NY-1221-19-0193-C-1 (NP 4/20/2022), explained:

To the extent the appellant argues that the initial decision should have included an order to take disciplinary action, we disagree. The scope of the corrective action available in an IRA appeal is defined by statute. Specifically, under 5 U.S.C. § 1221, corrective action in an IRA appeal may include status quo ante relief, "back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages, and compensatory damages." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A). Attorney fees and costs are also included as part of corrective action in an IRA appeal. 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(B).

[4] As an alternative to an order to the agency regarding discipline, the appellant requests that the Board issue the notice to OSC contemplated under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(f)(3). Such notice is not part of the corrective action available in an IRA appeal and is therefore not properly the subject of a petition for enforcement. However, we note that the Clerk of the Board did provide such notice to OSC by letter dated June 11, 2020. The Clerk is directed to provide a copy of that letter to the appellant when issuing this decision.

The appellant correctly notes that 38 U.S.C. § 731 requires the Secretary of Veterans Affairs to take disciplinary action against employees whom the Board (among other entities) determines has committed certain prohibited personnel actions. However, the Board has no statutory role in imposing such discipline other than possibly making the underlying determination. 38 U.S.C. § 731(a)(1). We find no support for the proposition that the Board has jurisdiction to enforce section 731.

Statutory and regulatory requirements aside, over the years the Board has occasionally referred prohibited personnel practice allegations arising in the course of ordinary appeals to OSC. Although the Federal Circuit does not appear to believe that OSC referrals are required, Henry v. Dept. of Navy, 902 F.2d 949, 953 (Fed. Cir. 1990), a review of Board decisions indicates that referrals of prohibited personnel practice allegations are expressly made to OSC. See Rhee v. Dept. of Treasury, 117 MSPR 640, 651 n.11, 2012 MSPB 26 (2012) (referral to OSC based on finding of reprisal for EEO activity and a related prior Board appeal); Armstrong v. DOJ, 107 MSPR 375, 391 ¶ 36, 2007 MSPB 280 (2007) (overruled on other grounds, Edwards v. Dept. of Labor, 2022 MSPB 9 (2022)) (referral to OSC based on finding of whistleblowing reprisal); Miller v. VA, 92 MSPR 610, 623 ¶ 30 (2002) (referral to OSC based on a finding of whistleblowing reprisal in an adverse action appeal); Walton v. USDA, 78 MSPR 401, 403 (1998) (noting the statutory requirement; also noting that the statutory referral process is not mooted, following the assertion of a prohibited practice, when the agency rescinds a challenged action); Nurse v. OPM, 4 MSPR 116 (1980); Gonzales v. USPS, 3 MSPR 570, 4 MSPB 88 (1980) (referring to OSC allegations in an appeal based on a 14-day suspension with an allegation of physical disability discrimination); cf. Kirwan v. Dept. of Transp., 88 MSPR 445, 450 n.3 (2001) (because the appellant had an outstanding claim for corrective action in the form of disciplinary action against an official who might have retaliated against him, agency rescission of the proposed removal did not moot the IRA appeal). The Board also occasionally refused to make a referral and given the appellant the address of OSC. See Garcia v. EEOC, 35 MSPR 602, 603 n.2 (1987). The transmittal may be informal. There is nothing inappropriate about an AJ referring a copy of an initial decision to OSC, who may as a matter of right intervene in matters pending before the Board. Baxter v. OPM, 44 MSPR 125, 132 n.6 (1990). The Board or a regional office may provide OSC with a copy of an appeal raising a prohibited personnel practice defense. Informal Board guidance requires that copies of all initial decisions be sent to OSC, although OSC is not to be shown on the certificate of service.
