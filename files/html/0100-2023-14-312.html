broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 11: Promotions, Vacancies, and Appointments, Section B: Sources for Vacancies, Subsection 03: Prior Consideration of Inside Candidates; Justification for Nonselection, Part a: Decisions Before 1992
a. Decisions Before 1992

Decisions issued before the 1992 IAM Local Lodge 2297 decision [38 FLRA 1451, 1452 (1991)], supra, allowed priority consideration for unit employees. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA 422, 442 (1987) (Provision 1), determined to be negotiable a proposal that first consideration for selection be given to existing employees. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA at 437 (rejecting agency argument that proposal violated merit system principles at 5 USC 2301 requiring selection and advancement on basis of ability, knowledge, skills, and after fair and open competition assuring that all receive equal opportunity); see Dept. of Treasury v. FLRA, 837 F.2d 1163, 1167-68 (D.C. Cir. 1988) (no conflict between merit system principles and negotiability of proposal that management may not consider non-bargaining unit candidates for promotion until ten days after first considering candidates from within unit); AFGE Local 868 and Dept. of Interior, Chattanooga and Chickamauga Nat'l Military Park, 45 FLRA 224, 225 (1992) (proposal negotiable that management give first consideration to unit employees for an advertised unit position, but if there are fewer than two qualified applicants for a position the selecting official may concurrently consider unit applicant and other qualified candidates); AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, HCFA, Baltimore, 44 FLRA 1405, 1493 (1992) (negotiable proposal that minorities and women in identified pools of underrepresented internal applicants be given first consideration in filling bridge positions, i.e., entry-level administrative or professional jobs); UPTO and Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs, Walla Walla, 44 FLRA 1145, 1182, 1184 (1992) (Proposal 12 negotiable: "[t]he first list of best qualified candidates referred to the selecting official shall contain only bargaining unit employees. No additional candidates shall be referred to the selecting official for 10 days after the initial list is provided"; proposal did not prevent agency from implementing agency regulations providing preferences for selection of military spouses) (distinguishing AFGE Local 2429 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq. Space Sys. Div., Los Angeles, 38 FLRA 1469 (1991)); NTEU and NRC, 43 FLRA 1279, 1282-83 (1992) (Proposal 1 negotiable: management to give first consideration to agency employees when filling vacancies; agency may solicit outside candidates at same time that agency candidates are sought; management may, but need not, rate and rank outside candidates while internal candidates are considered--so that if selecting official does not wish to select an agency employee, a hybrid list of internal and outside candidates can be forwarded for consideration).

FLRA determined to be negotiable a proposal as to vacancies for nurses that "[p]reference will be given to qualified nurses presently employed when a vacancy is available." Rather than ruling on the language of the proposal, FLRA looked to the statement of intent by the union that the proposal operated as a means for employees to apply and be considered, apparently a priority consideration plan. Illinois Nurses' Ass'n and VAMC, Hines, 28 FLRA 212, 235, 236 (1987) (distinguishing Colorado Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, Ft. Lyons, Colo., 25 FLRA 803 (1987)). A proposal was negotiable that compelled prior consideration of unit employees for positions that included personal service contracts. The requirements of 10 USC 2304, concerning competition and solicitation of proposals, did not bar the proposal. The proposals established the order for all applicants to be considered for certain job openings. There was no preferential treatment established, nor were the proposals inconsistent with the ability of the agency to solicit from the maximum number of qualified sources and subsequently select from any appropriate source. Ft. Knox Teachers Ass'n and Ft. Knox Dependent Sch., 19 FLRA 878, 878-79 (1985) (Proposals 1 and 2).

In AFGE Local 331 and VA Hosp., Perry Point, 2 FLRA 428, 428 (1980), the proposal recited:

It is agreed that an employer will utilize, to the maximum extent possible, the skills, and talents of its employees. Therefore, consideration will be given in filling vacant positions, to employees within the bargaining unit. Management will not solicit applications from outside the minimum area of consideration or call for a Civil Service Register of candidates if three or more highly qualified candidates can be identified within the minimum area of consideration. This will not prevent applicants from other VA field units applying provided they specifically apply for the vacancy being filled, and that they are ranked and rated with the same merit promotion panel as local employees.

Perry Point determined that although Congress intended to preserve management's right to select in filling (unit) positions, it intended as well to permit the parties an opportunity to negotiate as to the standards, criteria and procedures used by management in making selections. Bargaining on these matters, however, could not prevent management from making the actual selection. The union proposal concerned only limiting the area of intensive search and did not prevent management from considering applicants outside the minimum area. The proposal did not prevent the employer from expanding the area of intensive search for eligibles or from making selections from any appropriate sources once the minimum area of consideration was considered and exhausted without finding a sufficient number of highly qualified candidates.

Negotiable was a proposal that the agency adhere to the principles and spirit of the late FPM Chapter 335 concerning merit promotion, that the agency use to the fullest extent skills and talents of its highly qualified employees before expanding the area of consideration in promotion, and that consideration for filling vacant positions be first given to employees of the office housing the unit. AFGE Local 1884 and DMA, Hydrographic Topographic Ctr., Providence, 4 FLRA 224, 224-25 (1980). The proposal, setting up first consideration for local employees, was considered controlled by the reasoning and result of Perry Point.

A negotiable proposal required management to consider applicants in a tiered arrangement including a first, second, and third area of consideration (all civilian technicians, all members of the National Guard, and all others). ACT, Del. Chap. and Nat'l Guard Bureau, Del. NG, 3 FLRA 57, 57 (1980). Relying on VA Hosp., Perry Point, supra, FLRA decided that the proposal did not prevent management from expanding its area of search and it did not require that any employee be selected from one of the designated areas of consideration. In a substantially identical case, ACT, Inc., Pa. State Council and Adjutant Gen., Dept. of Military Affairs, Pa., 4 FLRA 77, 80 (1980), the Delaware Chap. analysis was followed. FLRA added that the proposal, concerning recruitment and not selection (and then only as to excepted service positions), did not violate 5 USC 2301(b)(1), a merit system principle requiring that recruitment be from areas of qualified individuals so a workforce from all segments of society would be achieved, and that selection and advancement be on the basis of relative skills after fair and open competition to assure that all receive equal opportunity. See Dept. of Treasury v. FLRA, 837 F.2d 1163, 1167-68 (D.C. Cir. 1988), post.

A negotiable proposal required initial consideration for filling vacancies to be from the areas of the positions to be filled. The proposal was not objectionable, because it affected reassignments of individuals from outside the bargaining unit. The placement of outsiders into unit positions affected working conditions within the unit. The proposal identified initial procedures for consideration and did not preclude management from selecting individuals through other means once the process progressed through the initial steps negotiated with the union. A portion of the proposal that "[i]f the area of consideration does not yield three highly qualified candidates, the employer may consider applicants from other sources," was construed by FLRA not to be a requirement to select from the employees initially considered; instead, the proposal permitted the employer to choose from employees identified through other means once the initial consideration process had been completed. NTEU and IRS, 7 FLRA 275, 278, 280-81 (1981). Application of the "prior consideration" doctrine was extended to require management to negotiate over a proposal that it delay considering nonunit employees for 10 days following submission to management of a Best Qualified List (BQL) of unit employees. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, 24 FLRA 494, 494-95 (1986), aff'd, Dept. of Treasury v. FLRA, 837 F.2d 1163, 1168-69 (D.C. Cir. 1988). NTEU was cited in NAGE Local R5-165 and Tennessee Air Nat'l Guard, 35 FLRA 886, 889 (1990), for the proposition that "a proposal that would require an agency to give priority consideration to bargaining unit candidates, but would not prevent the concurrent solicitation of other candidates, does not violate management's right to select." But a provision was not negotiable that required management to show that an individual picked from outside the initial area of consideration is clearly better qualified than individuals who were considered from within the area of consideration. DOD, Dept. of Navy, Navy Ordnance Station, Louisville and IAM Local Lodge 830, 4 FLRA 760, 773 (1980) (ALJ Decision).

AFGE Local 1760 and DHHS, SSA, 28 FLRA 160, 162, 163 (1987), approved for bargaining a proposal that transferred employees be given written notice of vacancies in their present positions at least 15 days before the vacancies are filled. On written request of the employee, management was required to give the employee "every possible consideration for the vacancy save for an overriding exigency." If the transferred employee was not selected, management was required to provide a written explanation for nonselection. In Dept. of Treasury v. FLRA, 837 F.2d 1163, 1170 (D.C. Cir. 1988), the court observed as to the argument that the proposals placed individuals outside the bargaining unit at a disadvantage:

...Some arbitrariness...is inevitable in defining an area of consideration. Restricting consideration to employees of a given agency might exclude equally able employees who worked alongside them in the same building, or even in the same office, merely because they were employed by a different executive agency or department. The proposed provision, to be sure, might disadvantage any "confidential employees"...who were eligible for and desired a position that became vacant. But such employees are disadvantaged every day relative to bargaining-unit members because Congress decided to bar them from the benefits of collective bargaining.... That disadvantage may seem unfair, and the policy concerns that prompted it may be questionable, but the decision to create it, once taken by Congress, is not for this court to override.
