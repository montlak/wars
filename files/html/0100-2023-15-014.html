a. Leave Restriction Notice

Removal was warranted for violation of leave policies after the employee was warned through a leave restriction letter that his next violation of leave policy would result in his removal. Lindsey v. Dept. of Air Force, 8 MSPR 6, 9, 7 MSPB 507 (1981). Agencies may extend leave restriction letters unless there is some policy to the contrary. See Pope v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 289, 293 (1990) (poor attendance during the first year of restriction). If a restriction letter expires of its own terms, however, it will not be considered as continuing unless there is notice to the appellant. If the letter of restriction applies to but one type of leave, e.g., sick leave, a violation of procedures concerning the use of annual leave will not be considered a violation of the restriction letter. See Willis v. Dept. of Army, 52 MSPR 9, 11-12 (1991); Isom v. DLA, 22 MSPR 79, 81 (1984) (appellant was on notice that either a sick leave restriction letter was continued or that he was under new restriction letters). An unintentional violation of a leave restriction letter by an employee with some past discipline resulted in a penalty of a 30-day suspension, mitigated from a removal. Glover v. DHHS, 24 MSPR 636, 641 (1984). If the violations of reporting requirements occur because the appellant is suffering a mental disability and therefore cannot comply with the agency's leave policy, the Board will not sustain a charge relating to those requirements. Sanders v. EEOC, 38 MSPR 309, 313 (1988).

The Board requires agencies to avoid charging employees with AWOL when they are under leave restriction letters if the employees make good faith efforts to comply with the requirements of arguably ambiguous letters, as in Stoddard v. Dept. of Army, 109 MSPR 199, 202 ¶ 5, 2008 MSPB 137 (2008), involving a letter stating to the appellant:

Effective immediately, all leave requested for illness or injury, be it sick leave, leave without pay or annual leave and regardless of duration, must be supported by a medical certificate with the original signature of your physician or licensed practitioner. This certificate must state that you are incapacitated from work. The certificate must specifically state the period of time during which you are incapacitated or undergoing medical treatment. The required certificate or statement must be submitted to me as soon as possible upon your return to duty but no later than 15 calendar days from the date of your absence. Your absence will be carried as Absence without Leave (AWOL) until such time the documentation is furnished.

Stoddard concluded the employee met the requirements of the leave restriction letter, id. at 202-03 ¶ 7:

Removal for AWOL is improper if leave policies are ambiguous, if the appellant attempts to comply with them, and if there is no evidence that the absence of the employee impaired the agency's ability to carry out its mission. See Young v. Department of the Navy, 27 M.S.P.R. 274, 276 (1985). Here, the appellant made a good faith effort to comply with instructions that were at best ambiguous. The leave control letter provided that the certificate must include a specific statement that the appellant was incapacitated for work, but it did not indicate that this statement must include any particular combination of words. The appellant testified without contradiction that he was not orally informed of any such requirement. Given the lack of clarity in this matter, the RN's statement that the appellant's absences were "due to medical reasons" was arguably sufficient. Moreover, contrary to Humphrey's testimony, the leave control letter did not state that the certificate must be signed by a physician. Rather, it provided that the certificate must be signed by a "physician or licensed practitioner." Under Texas state law, the practice of nursing requires a license. See Tex. Occ. Code Ann., § 301.002 (2007). Additionally, the appellant testified that he was not advised that a certificate signed by an RN would be deemed not in compliance with the leave control letter. Finally, the agency has not asserted that the appellant's absences were disruptive. Under these circumstances, the AWOL charge should not have been sustained.[2]

[2] We further note that, while the July 9, 2007 certificate was untimely, it is evident that it otherwise complied with the requirements set forth in the leave control letter. Having received that certificate, the agency was obliged to grant the appellant available sick leave before removing him on AWOL charges. See Foreman v. U.S. Postal Service, 89 M.S.P.R. 328, ¶ 8 (2001); Atchley v. Department of the Army, 46 M.S.P.R. 297, 301 (1990). By the time of his removal, the appellant had accumulated 28 hours of sick leave, enough to cover more than half of the absences in question.

An agency regulation may specify that the failure to submit medical documentation in accordance with a letter of requirement will be a basis for denying leave but not an offense justifying disciplinary action, and that limitation will be respected by the Board in accordance with the principle that the agency is required to act in accordance with the procedures it adopts for itself. See Bowen v. Dept. of Navy, 112 MSPR 607, 611-12 n.2, 2009 MSPB 220 (2009). Other cases concerning documentation requirements are discussed in the sections that follow.
