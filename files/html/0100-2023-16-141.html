broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section G: Interference, Subsection 04: Interference With Witnesses, Union Representatives, Employees, Grievances, Evidence, Part c: Grievances
c. Grievances

A verbal complaint operates as a grievance for purposes of protection against reprisal, even if it is directed to the wrong forum or presented to the wrong management representative. VAMC, Buffalo and Bess, 13 FLRA 283, 292 (1983) (ALJ Decision). Discussions between a union steward and a personnel officer, not rising to the level of a grievance but constituting preliminary inquiries concerning an employee's problem, are representational efforts entitled to statutory protection. Chastisement by a supervisor of the steward for contacting the personnel office was interference. The supervisor's remarks were not protected and were made under coercive conditions. The recognition that candid comments and frank opinions are to be anticipated in labor relations discussions did not insulate the supervisor's improper remarks. Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 11 FLRA 290, 298-99 (1983) (ALJ Decision).

The employer violates § 7116(a)(1) by threatening to discipline an employee for conduct involving the filing and investigation of a grievance under the negotiated agreement. The employee is involved in protected activity when, before filing the grievance, he conducts his own investigation on his own time to stave off the disciplinary action leading to the filing of a grievance. The right to file and process a grievance also encompasses the right to gather evidence supporting that grievance or an investigation as to whether to file it. That right was infringed when management warned the employee that he would be disciplined if he had further contact with any witnesses involved in the investigation of his alleged misconduct before the official investigation was completed. DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Butner and AFGE Local 3696, 18 FLRA 831, 832-33 (1985); see DODDS, Mediterranean Region, Naples Am. High School and Schussel, 21 FLRA 849 (1986), supra.

No interference occurred when a manager loudly asked a union representative if he obtained the required authorization to speak with an employee concerning a possible grievance, and, when told that the union official had the authorization, the manager pursued the matter no further. Army Reserve Personnel Ctr., St. Louis and AFGE Local 900, 32 FLRA 665, 668 (1988). FLRA was less than clear, however, as to how an agency should treat a steward who makes a good faith attempt to represent (on official time) an employee whose unit status is disputed, FDA, Newark Dist. Office, W. Orange and AFGE Council 242, 47 FLRA 535, 536 n.2 (1993):

The General Counsel does not except to the Judge's finding that one of the employees whose unit status was disputed in this case, Crowley-Harris, is a confidential employee and, under section 7112(b)(2) of the Statute, may not be included in the bargaining unit represented by the Union. In this connection, we agree with the Judge that the Respondent's instructions that a Union steward cease representation of this employee did not violate section 7116(a)(1) and (2) of the Statute. We note, in this regard, that where a union seeks to represent an employee whose unit status is disputed, the union may file a representation petition and/or ULP charge to resolve the unit status issue. We also note the Judge's finding that, even in a situation such as the one involving Crowley-Harris, the Respondent could not lawfully take actions regarding the steward that would "unduly interfere with [the] exercise of rights protected by the Statute." Accordingly, although we agree with the Judge that, in this case, the Respondent's actions did not interfere with such rights, we find unclear, and do not adopt, the Judge's statement that union officials act at their peril when they seek to represent employees whose unit status is in dispute. Instead, the facts must be examined in each case to determine whether, even if an employee is found to be outside a bargaining unit, an agency's actions regarding a union's attempt to represent such employee violate the Statute.

Interference may be indirect. A union official could infer that there would be no point in filing a grievance in the hope of obtaining a particular remedy--priority consideration for a position--based on the statement of a supervisor that he would not select a union official for the position. Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 34 FLRA 956, 965-66 (1990).
