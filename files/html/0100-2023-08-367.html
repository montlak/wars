h. Geographic Extent of Competition

An agency may limit the scope of geographical competition for a vacancy without a VEOA violation, O'Brien v. OPM, 118 Fed. Appx. 484, 486-488 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP):

In his informal brief, O'Brien appears to argue all three of the issues raised before the Board. O'Brien first argues that OPM denied his right under 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1) to compete for the announced position. On that point, the Board concluded that 5 C.F.R. § 335.103(b)(2) provides OPM the right to geographically limit a vacancy announcement so long as the area of consideration is sufficiently broad to ensure the availability of high quality candidates. The Board also found that the vacancy announcement to which O'Brien responded was limited to the Washington, D.C. metro area. We agree with the Board that OPM has the authority to geographically limit a vacancy announcement so long as the regulation's criterion is met. Substantial evidence supports the Board's finding that OPM limited the vacancy announcement to which O'Brien responded to the Washington, D.C. metro area. O'Brien, residing in South Carolina, was thus properly excluded.

...

With respect to the remaining two issues raised by O'Brien before the Board, whether OPM issued a fraudulent announcement and whether it improperly handled O'Brien's inquiries, we agree with the Board that neither the Board nor the Department of Labor had jurisdiction over these issues. Jurisdiction for O'Brien's appeal to the Board was found in 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(d)(1). That section permits appeal of a Department of Labor investigation into alleged violations of veterans' preference statutes and regulations. Section 3330a(a)(1) permits the filing of a complaint with the Secretary of Labor by "[a] preference eligible who alleges that an agency has violated such individual's rights under any statute or regulation relating to veterans' preference." Under the statute, the Board's jurisdiction is limited to alleged violations of veterans' rights under any statute or regulation relating to veterans' preference. Any alleged improprieties by OPM in the announcement and selection process did not implicate and, thus, could not have violated the rights specified in section 3330a(a)(1).

See DeMontbreun v. Dept. of Navy, 296 Fed. Appx. 933, 934 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) ("[W]e are aware of no law, rule, or regulation--nor has Mr. DeMontbreun cited any--that required Navy to make its selections from a single nation-wide certificate or to wait until three or more candidates were in Category 1.").

But If an agency opens competition for a position, it may not impose a geographical or organizational limitation that precludes some but not all individuals who are not already agency employees from applying for the position if the applicants have veterans preference status. Jolley v. DHS, 105 MSPR 104, 106-112 ¶¶ 2-21, 2007 MSPB 51 (2007) (overruled as to VEOA right to compete claim for merit promotion positions sought by federal employees, Oram v. Dept. of Navy, 2022 MSPB 30 (2022)), post, is discussed in this chapter, "Competitive Appointment Procedures; Exceptions to Competitive Requirements; Expedited Hiring for Acquisitions Positions."
