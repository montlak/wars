2. Time for Initiating IRA Appeal With OSC and MSPB

The Board traditionally pays special attention to the timeliness of an appeal; an untimely appeal is one that may be dismissed in short order, without adjudication on the merits. IRA cases present special problems involving timeliness and the waiver of appeals that are filed beyond the time allowed by statute and regulation. The statutory and regulatory time limits for initiating an IRA case with the Board are outlined in earlier in this chapter, "Statutory and Regulatory Requirements and Limitations."

There is no statutory or regulatory time limit for initiating an IRA case with OSC, but once OSC closes the case out it advises the complainant of the right to file an IRA appeal with the Board, and Board appellate process is governed by a time defined by Board regulation, 5 CFR 1209.5 (2022), implementing 5 USC 1214(a)(3). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Statutory and Regulatory Requirements and Limitations" for the time limits for initiating a Board IRA appeal.]

As to initiation of the case with OSC, the Board has not created a regulatory time limitation. The Board noted its reasoning, somewhat obliquely, in Federal Register commentary of August 3, 1998, 63 FR 41181:

Comments were received from one Federal agency suggesting that the Board amend part 1209 to impose a time limit for bringing an action to the Special Counsel as a pre-condition for later bringing an individual right of action (IRA) appeal to the Board....

The Board has concluded that imposing such a time limit would not be a proper exercise of its regulatory authority. That authority is limited to matters within the Board's jurisdiction (5 USC 1204(h) and 7701(k), and 38 USC 4331).

Under 5 USC 7121(g)(2), an employee who has been affected by a prohibited personnel practice (other than discrimination) may elect among three specified remedies: (1) An appeal to MSPB under 5 USC 7701, (2) a grievance under a negotiated grievance procedure, or (3) an action under subchapters II and III of chapter 12 of title 5. Subchapter II concerns Special Counsel actions (which may lead to corrective action complaints before the Board), and subchapter III covers IRA appeals. Because the conjunctive is used with regard to the Special Counsel and IRA processes, it appears that Congress intended, without limits other than those specified, to allow complainants to go to the Special Counsel and bring IRA appeals to the Board on the same matter. By limiting the matters that can be brought to the Board under subchapter III to only "timely-raised" matters brought to OSC under subchapter II, as suggested by the commenter, the Board would be adding a limitation to the IRA appeal choice that is not contained in the statute.

There is an outermost limit on the initiation of the case with OSC, a practical limitation of time that was described as laches by the Federal Circuit, as it recognized that the ability of the agency to defend itself would be impaired by more than eight years of delay between the operative events and the initiation of the claim to OSC, Louie v. Dept. of Treasury, 211 Fed. Appx. 942, 946 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Also, the AJ's determination that the doctrine of laches bars his whistleblower reprisal claim relating to his non-selection for vacancy announcement No. LA-97-106 is not arbitrary or capricious. The AJ weighed Louie's assertion that he still had a copy of his application package and that relevant witnesses are available against the IRS's evidence that relevant documentation had been destroyed in the intervening years, that it would be prejudiced in its efforts to defend against the claim given the lack of documents reflecting its decision-making process, and the absence of a satisfactory explanation for Louie's delay in bringing this claim.[2] After an appellant shows that a disclosure occurred and that it was a protected disclosure under the WPA, the burden shifts to the agency to show by clear and convincing evidence that the appellant would not have been selected under vacancy announcement No. LA-97-106, even if the protected disclosure had not occurred. See Spencer v. Dep't of the Navy, 327 F.3d 1354, 1356 (Fed. Cir. 2003); 5 U.S.C. §§ 12 21(e), 2302(b)(8). Without the other applications that were submitted for this vacancy announcement or records reflecting how the applicants were rated and ranked, the agency is effectively unable to defend itself. The AJ's determination that this claim is barred under the doctrine of laches is not arbitrary or capricious and is supported by substantial evidence.

King v. DHHS, 71 MSPR 22, 28-30 (1996), explained the Board's regulatory construction of the time periods for filing IRA appeals:

[A] literal reading of the statute and regulation might support the appellant's contention that there is no time limit for filing an IRA appeal when OSC has not notified the employee that it will seek corrective action on the employee's behalf within 120 days. Subsection (a)(2) of 5 CFR § 1209.5 provides that an IRA appeal may be filed "at any time" after the expiration of 120 days after the appellant sought corrective action from OSC. Although subsection (a)(1) provides that an IRA appeal must be filed no later than 65 days after the issuance of OSC's termination notice, this restriction is not repeated in subsection (a)(2). Similarly, while subsection (A) of 5 USC § 1214(a)(3) provides that an employee must seek corrective action from the Board within 60 days after being provided with OSC's termination notice, this restriction is not repeated in subsection (B), which provides that an employee may seek corrective action from the Board 120 days after seeking corrective action from OSC.

In both the statute and the regulation, the two subsections are connected by the disjunctive "or," which ordinarily indicates an alternate relationship between the two subsections, i.e., that either subsection can be employed without the other. See 1A Norman J. Singer, Sutherland Statutory Construction § 21.14 (5th ed. 1993). If the non-restrictive language of 5 USC § 1214(a)(3)(B) and 5 CFR § 1209.5(a)(2) is applied literally, without regard to the restrictive language contained in 5 USC § 1214(a)(3)(A) and 5 CFR § 1209.5(a)(1), the appellant's contention that there is no time limit for filing an IRA when OSC has not notified the employee that it will seek corrective action on the employee's behalf within 120 days would be correct.

The principle that the word "or" should be read as indicating an alternate construction must be balanced against the principle that each section of a statute should be construed in connection with every other section so as to produce a harmonious whole. Sutherland Statutory Construction, supra, § 46.05. We further note that when a literal construction would lead to an absurd result, the literal construction will not be viewed as providing the "unambiguously expressed intent of Congress," and courts will look to other sources to discern congressional intent....

The Joint Explanatory Statement of the House and Senate which accompanied the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989 gives the following guidance regarding the time limits for filing an IRA appeal:

If the Special Counsel notifies the individual that the investigation has been terminated, the individual has 60 days in which to file an independent [sic] right of action. If the individual receives no notice of termination of the investigation within 120 days of filing the complaint, he or she may file an individual right of action at any time after the 120 day period has elapsed.

135 Cong. Rec. S2783-84, H749 (daily ed. Mar. 21, 1989).

Similar to the statutory and regulatory subsections discussed above, the second sentence of the Joint Explanatory Statement, considered in isolation from the first, provides for filing "any time" after the 120-day period has elapsed, without specifying a deadline after which an IRA appeal can no longer be filed. The first sentence states without exception, however, that when OSC has notified the individual that it has terminated its inspection, the individual must file an IRA appeal within 60 days. Reading the two sentences together, the most reasonable inference is that an appellant must give OSC 120 days to seek corrective action on the appellant's behalf before filing an IRA appeal but, once OSC has notified the appellant that it has terminated its investigation, the appellant has only 60 days in which to file an IRA appeal, regardless of whether OSC terminates its investigation before or after 120 days have elapsed.

...

Accordingly, we find that, once OSC has provided notice to an employee that it has terminated its investigation, the statute provides that the employee must file an IRA appeal with the Board within 60 days, regardless of the duration of OSC's investigation.

If 120 days have passed after the IRA complaint was filed with OSC, the case may be appealed to MSPB even if OSC is still investigating part or all of the allegations in the complaint. See Garrison v. DOD, 101 MSPR 229, 233-34 ¶ 7, 2006 MSPB 38 (2006). If OSC closes out a case by clearly stating that the whistleblower component of the matter may be appealed to MSPB, but retains oversight to examine possible other statutory or regulatory violations, the time to file the MSPB IRA appeal runs from the closeout letter, not from the completion by OSC of its inquiry into other, non-whistleblowing violations. See Inman v. VA, 115 MSPR 41, 45 ¶ 16, 2010 MSPB 184 (2010). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Individual Right of Action Appeals."]

An OSC closeout notice will ordinarily tell the appellant that she may file with the Board within 65 days (under 5 USC 1214(a)(3)(A)(ii) "no more than 60 days have elapsed since notification was provided to such employee, former employee, or applicant for employment that such investigation was terminated," interpreted as 65 days by the MSPB regulation, 5 CFR 1209.5(a)(1)). If there are several personnel actions complained of to OSC and fewer than all of those actions have been timely appealed to MSPB in an IRA case, the question may arise whether the appellant may, later than the 65 days following the OSC closeout letter, amend the timely appeal to add a personnel action that was referred to OSC but not initially appealed to the Board. Whiteman v. Dept. of Transp., DA-1221-09-0106-W-2 (NP 4/28/2011), appeared to say that an amendment could not be used under those circumstances:

On review, the appellant argues that the administrative judge erred in finding that the 2006 personnel actions she raised were untimely. Specifically, the contends that she first raised these actions in her March 13, 2009 response to the agency's March 3, 2009 motion to dismiss the appeal, not in her February 12, 2010 response to the administrative judge's July 20, 2009 jurisdictional order, as the administrative judge found. In addition, she claims that, even though she raised these personnel actions 87 days following the date of the notice of her MSPB appeal rights, i.e, 22 days after the deadline for filing an IRA appeal, see 5 C.F.R. § 1209.5(a)(1), good cause exists for the delay.

This argument is unavailing. The Board has no authority to waive the statutory time limit for filing an IRA appeal for good cause. See MacDonald v. Department of Justice, 105 M.S.P.R. 83, ¶ 11 (2007) (citing Wood v. Department of the Air Force, 54 M.S.P.R. 587, 592 (1992)) (the Whistleblower Protection Act does not make any provision for late filings or grant the Board the authority to waive the time limit for good cause shown). Therefore, regardless of whether the appellant first raised the 2006 personnel actions before the Board in her February 12, 2010 response to the administrative judge's July 20, 2009 jurisdictional order, as the administrative judge found, or in her March 13, 2009 response to the agency's March 3, 2009 motion to dismiss the appeal, as she claims, the portion of the appellant's IRA appeal related to the 2006 personnel actions is untimely because it was not filed within the statutory deadline.

If the 65th day following OSC closeout letter falls on a Sunday, under Board regulations the next business day is the IRA filing deadline. Reyes v. Dept. of Transp., AT-1221-21-0604-W-1 (NP 2/3/2023).
