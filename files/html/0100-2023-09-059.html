3. Application for Waiver

For late appeals, 5 CFR 1201.12 and 1201.22(c) (2022) allow an AJ to waive a Board regulation in an individual case for good cause shown if application of the regulation is not required by statute. Because the Board's regulatory time limit for filing most types of appeals is not required by statute, the "good cause" regulation serves as a basis for acceptance of late appeals. If the AJ recognizes that an appeal may be untimely, he normally will order the appellant to show cause why it should not be dismissed as untimely. The issue may also arise on an agency's motion to dismiss the appeal as untimely. Or the appellant may take the initiative and apply for a waiver before the AJ issues a show cause order or the agency files a motion to dismiss. Although the AJ should determine timeliness before the merits hearing, reversible error is not committed if the AJ fails to address the agency's motion to dismiss and adjudicates the timeliness issue at or after the hearing on the merits of the appeal. Myers v. OPM, 43 MSPR 323, 327 (1990). If, in addressing the agency's motion to dismiss an untimely appeal, the AJ denies that motion, the agency should object to the AJ's determination if the agency's position is to be preserved for full Board review through a PFR. See George v. USPS, 74 MSPR 71, 79 (1997).

Except in cases involving constructive adverse actions (when timeliness issues may involve credibility disputes) [discussed in Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action"], matters of timeliness are usually raised and adjudicated before a determination on the merits of an appeal. An early case, not now followed, Butler v. Dept. of Interior, 3 MSPR 465, 466, 3 MSPB 534 (1980), resolved that to avoid a sequential hearing--first on good cause for an untimely appeal, and then on the merits if the threshold issue is resolved in the appellant's favor--the AJ should conduct a hearing on both issues and base a decision on the merits only if he first concludes that appellant has shown good cause for the untimely appeal. In current practice, the AJ usually decides the timeliness issue based on written submissions and, if the appeal survives the timeliness challenge, the case proceeds to disposition on the merits, through a hearing, if necessary. [Refer later in this chapter to "Hearing Entitlement on Timeliness Issue."]

The AJ should specifically inform the appellant that the timeliness of his or her appeal is an issue. An acknowledgment order stating generally that the appellant may supply information on timeliness, e.g., "if there is a question as to whether your appeal is timely filed," is insufficient to place the appellant on notice of a timeliness issue. Notice is provided, however, in other ways: by an agency's motion to dismiss the appeal as untimely, by a conference call among the parties and the AJ discussing the issue, and by a subsequent notice providing the parties an opportunity to address the issue prior to the close of the record. Dotson v. USPS, 41 MSPR 412, 415 (1989).

Waiver applications should be supported by evidence. The requirement to establish good cause for a delayed appeal cannot be satisfied by argument alone. Perry v. MSPB, 397 Fed. Appx. 647, 648 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP). The statements in an application for waiver of a filing deadline (except for a late PFR) need not be sworn. In Gerber v. OPM, 57 MSPR 572, 575 (1993), the Board accepted an unsworn statement of the circumstances of filing a late appeal, but the Board did not accept the justification for the delay. The appellant asserted that she used an "overnight special delivery" service, but she did not submit proof of mailing, e.g., a receipt from the Postal Service or from a private express company. If facts need to be provided, a statement from the appellant or someone else with knowledge of the facts should suffice. The Board states that if an appellant provides a plausible and uncontradicted, but unsworn, reason for a delay in filing the appeal, it is entitled to weight by the AJ. See Carroll v. USPS, 6 MSPR 41, 43, 6 MSPB 47 (1981) ("The appellant's response to the show cause letter is consistent with the explanation he gave in the letter accompanying the petition for appeal. Although his statement is unsworn and unaccompanied by supporting evidence, it is entitled to weight because there is no contradictory evidence. The Board finds that the action of the appellant in checking with the field office concerning receipt of his appeal demonstrates a lack of negligence on his part."); see Moss v. Dept. of Army, 85 MSPR 478, 480 ¶ 6 (2000) (the Board credits an unsworn statement concerning the date the appellant received notice that OSC terminated an investigation of the appellant's IRA complaint); Pagel v. USPS, 84 MSPR 540, 541-42 ¶ 6 (1999) (unsworn statement by the appellant concerning the circumstances of a mailing is entitled to evidentiary weight in absence of an order by the AJ requiring the submission of an affidavit or sworn statement on the issue of timeliness; the lack of a legible postmark and the failure to use certified mail did not detract from the weight of the appellant's uncontested statement); Ford v. OPM, 69 MSPR 73, 75 (1995) (on consideration of a PFR from the dismissal of an untimely appeal: "The acknowledgment order did not specifically require the appellant to submit an affidavit or sworn statement to support her claims, and our regulations make no such specific requirement."); cf. Kowalczyk v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 396, 399 (1989) (considering both sworn and unsworn submissions on the issue of timeliness of the appeal); Perlman v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 591, 593 (1984) ("With respect to the initial attempts at filing as attested to in appellant's affidavit, the Board finds that these should have been credited by the presiding official since they were uncontested. Ceja v. United States, 710 F.2d 812 (Fed. Cir. 1983).").

One regulation governing appeals is 5 CFR 1201.22(c) (2022):

Timeliness of appeals. If a party does not submit an appeal within the time set by statute, regulation, or order of a judge, it will be dismissed as untimely filed unless a good reason for the delay is shown. The judge will provide the party an opportunity to show why the appeal should not be dismissed as untimely.

Contrast § 1201.22(c), involving late appeals, with 5 CFR 1201.114, involving late PFRs:

(g) Late filings. Any pleading described in paragraph (a) of this section that is filed late must be accompanied by a motion that shows good cause for the untimely filing, unless the Board has specifically granted an extension of time under paragraph (f) of this section, or unless a motion for extension is pending before the Board. The motion must be accompanied by an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746. (See Appendix IV.) The affidavit or sworn statement must include: The reasons for failing to request an extension before the deadline for the submission, and a specific and detailed description of the circumstances causing the late filing, accompanied by supporting documentation or other evidence. Any response to the motion may be included in the response to the petition for review, the cross petition for review, or the response to the cross petition for review. The response will not extend the time provided by paragraph (e) of this section to file a cross petition for review or to respond to the petition or cross petition. In the absence of a motion, the Board may, in its discretion, determine on the basis of the existing record whether there was good cause for the untimely filing, or it may provide the party that submitted the document with an opportunity to show why it should not be dismissed or excluded as untimely.

Why the difference in the quality of evidence needed to justify a late appeal and a late PFR? It is an historical anomaly. But there it is. Although the Board accepts unsworn statements in timeliness disputes involving appeals, the Board occasionally notes the value of sworn evidence, Cohen v. Dept. of Commerce, 56 MSPR 578, 582 n.1 (1993):

The fact that the appellant's representative's statements were unsworn did not detract from their evidentiary weight inasmuch as the Board's regulations do not require an affidavit or sworn statement to establish good cause for an untimely filing of an appeal. See 5 CFR 1201.4(1) and 1201.22(b); 5 CFR 1201.114(f) (an affidavit or sworn statement must accompany an untimely-filed petition for review). A judge may, however, require an affidavit or sworn statement to support an untimely-filed appeal where a party's explanation for an untimely filing provides insufficient evidence upon which to base a waiver determination. See Hunt v. USPS, 41 MSPR 381, 382-84 (1989).

Unsworn statements may be discounted for lack of an oath, particularly if the appellant is represented by counsel. Blue v. USPS, 65 MSPR 370, 375-76 (1994), pointed out that the AJ may require sworn evidence on timeliness. The Board discounted the appellant's unsworn and vague assertions concerning the circumstances of the filing of his appeal:

Because the appellant's contention on petition for review that he did not receive the administrative judge's timeliness show-cause order is not sworn, or in affidavit form, it is not evidence. See Gerber v. OPM, 57 MSPR 572, 575 (1993); Vincent v. Dept. of Justice, 32 MSPR 263, 268-69 (1987). Nor has the appellant provided any other evidence to support this contention. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575; Vincent, 32 MSPR at 269. We therefore find that the appellant has failed to rebut the presumption of due delivery and receipt. Nevertheless, we reopen this appeal to address the appellant's newly proffered arguments regarding the timeliness issue, assuming arguendo that he did not duly receive the administrative judge's timeliness show-cause notice below, as he contends. Cf. Scott v. Dept. of Veterans Affairs, 53 MSPR 572, 574 (1992) (where the administrative judge's timeliness show-cause notice was mailed to the appellant's representative but was incorrectly addressed, the Board considered on petition for review the appellant's evidence regarding the timeliness issue, even though the evidence was not timely submitted to the administrative judge).

The appellant's contentions on petition for review that he timely filed the appeal or, in the alternative, that there was good cause for his filing delay, are not sworn or in affidavit form. An appellant's explanations regarding the timeliness issue before an administrative judge need not be sworn, although the administrative judge may require sworn explanations if he or she deems it necessary under the circumstances. See Cohen v. Dept. of Commerce, 56 MSPR 578, 582 n.1 (1993). However, where, as here, the explanations are first proffered on petition for review by an appellant who is represented by an attorney, the fact that they are not sworn does detract from any evidentiary weight they might have. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575. In fact, as noted above, the general rule regarding unsworn statements in a party's pleading is that they do not constitute evidence. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575 n.3; Vincent, 32 MSPR at 268-69. Nor has the appellant provided any documentary evidence to support his assertions. See Gerber, 57 MSPR at 575; Vincent, 32 MSPR at 269.

Moreover, the appellant's explanations are substantively insufficient to establish that the appeal was timely filed or that there was good cause for the untimely filing. He contends that he "mailed" this appeal to the Board sometime ("exact date unknown") "prior to December 30, 1993." He speculates that the Board's delayed receipt of his timely mailed appeal may have been due to its having been mailed during December, a busy time of year for the Postal Service. We find that the appellant's vague contention that he mailed the appeal sometime before December 30, 1993, is insufficient to establish that he placed the appeal in the mail stream by the December 30, 1993 deadline. See Hudgies v. USPS, 55 MSPR 311, 315 (1992); cf. Williams v. USPS, 61 MSPR 213, 215-16 (1994) (the appellant's sworn, specific contention that he signed, dated, and placed the appeal in the mail on June 29, 1993 was found sufficient to establish timely filing on that date).

Even if an AJ does not insist on a sworn statement to evidence facts urged by an appellant to excuse or explain an untimely appeal, the sworn statement may be more convincing. Appellants should simply provide a statement of the circumstances that they believe justify a waiver and include a line preceding their signature with the declaration that, "in accordance with 28 USC 1746, I declare (or certify, verify, or state) under penalty of perjury under the laws of the United States of America that the foregoing is true and correct. Executed on (date). (Signature)." Statements that do not precisely mirror the statutory language pass muster, as long as they evidence the intent of the signer to be bound by the penalties for perjury. See Foust v. Dept. of Treasury, 80 MSPR 477, 479 n.* (1998) (appellant used the Board's "Sample Declaration Under 28 USC 1746" in the Appendix to 5 CFR 1201). Ceja v. United States, 710 F.2d 812, 813 n.* (Fed. Cir. 1983), noted that as to the requirement for an affidavit to support a showing of good cause for excusing a delay in filing an appeal, a written declaration made under the penalty of perjury and stating that the information contained is true and correct is equivalent to an affidavit under 28 USC 1746. [28 USC 1746 is reprinted in Chapter 3, "Postponement of Hearing."] With e-filing, the system asks the filer if the submission is made on personal knowledge and, if so, if it is sworn to be true.

Documentary evidence may be helpful when a waiver of a time limit is sought or when the evidence concerning timeliness is required by the AJ. Circumstantial evidence of timeliness, e.g., the dates of communications with the agency, could also establish the timeliness of an appeal. See Kowalczyk, supra (relying upon Lappin v. DOJ, 10 MSPR 528, 529 (1982)). A certified mail receipt will show that a mailing occurred. That the agency did not receive a copy does not alone establish that the appellant did not mail the submission to the AJ. Shavers v. USPS, 52 MSPR 187, 190 (1992). The certified mail receipt is conclusive proof of receipt of the document. See Pritchett v. OPM, 70 MSPR 504, 507 (1996).
