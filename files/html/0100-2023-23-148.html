l. Evidence Supplied After Decision of Judge

Without a showing of new and material evidence, the appellant may not wait until he files a PFR to provide new claims concerning compliance. Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 112, 117-18 (1986). But the Board accepts during the PFR stage evidence, often offered by agencies, demonstrating that compliance has been achieved. According to Nielson v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 324, 326-27 (1988), evidence of compliance can be supplied as compliance efforts continue:

...Voluntary efforts to comply in good faith with Board orders are favored, even if, as here, those efforts are ultimately found to fall short of full compliance. The objective of fostering prompt compliance with our orders would be ill-served were an agency required to limit its arguments to those previously made to the regional office despite the fact that the circumstances before the Board are different from those at the regional level due to the agency's additional actions to comply after the issuance of the Recommendation.

The agency's basis for not assigning appellant the full range of his former responsibilities is, in any event, not such a "new matter" as to force its rejection for failure to present it below. We find the internal directives relied on by the agency to be simply the formal administrative predicate for its previous argument that the agency's current structure and staffing arrangement is favored by management. As such, the internal directives charting or reciting the organization's structure and delegations add nothing to the agency's previous arguments. We note particularly that these internal directives, issued by the Naval District Washington, are not alleged to be a vehicle for implementing a non-discretionary mandate of law or government-wide rule or regulation, and they do not otherwise appear to provide a compelling basis for not restoring appellant to his former position and responsibilities.

As to appellants' concerns about serial failures of agency compliance, Jackson v. USPS, 45 MSPR 560, 563-64 (1990), informs us:

[A] final decision on an appellant's petition for enforcement does not preclude the appellant from filing additional petitions predicated on subsequent agency actions evidencing noncompliance.... Similarly, in filing exceptions to a recommended finding of noncompliance, the agency is not precluded from submitting evidence not presented to the administrative judge where the evidence reflects changed circumstances due to actions by the agency subsequent to the administrative judge's Recommendation. See Nielson v. Department of the Navy, 37 MSPR 324, 326 (1988).

However, when the administrative judge issues a decision resolving the issue presented by the petition for enforcement, the appellant is not entitled to present additional evidence on that issue in a petition for review absent a showing that the evidence is new and material and was not previously available.... We have also held that the agency cannot raise objections in its exception to the noncompliance recommendation which could have been, but were not, presented to the administrative judge. Eikenberry v. Department of Interior, 37 MSPR 438, 444 (1988). Here, the agency seeks to introduce evidence of a revision in one of its back pay regulations which had superseded the regulation that it presented to the administrative judge. However, the agency has not shown that the revised regulation was not available, with due diligence, at the time of its response to the petition for enforcement. The revision will therefore not be considered.

If the evidence referenced during the PFR on compliance is already in the record, no issue arises as to consideration of new evidence. See Doe v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 198, 203 n.3 (2003). The Board permitted what it considered to be new evidence in a reconsideration request relating to the appellant's entitlement to premium pay under a settlement. See Galatis v. USPS, 111 MSPR 484, 2009 MSPB 104 (2009); cf. Corum v. USPS, DC-0353-06-0728-C-1 (NP 9/6/2015) (accepting as new evidence on the appellant's PFR because the AJ did not inform the appellant of a right of discovery during enforcement procedures under revised Board regulations); Williams v. USPS, AT-0752-13-0127-C-1 n.2 (NP 5/8/2015) ("The agency's response to the petition for review was filed 3 days late. The appellant has not replied to the agency's response or contested it as untimely. Although the Board sets time limits for agencies to submit evidence of compliance, additional submissions may be accepted beyond the time limits if the circumstances warrant, such as when the evidence is new and material. See Gevaert v. Department of the Navy, 74 M.S.P.R. 303, 306 (1997). Thus, we will consider the agency's evidence of compliance because it directly rebuts the appellant's claims on review.").

At least as to arguments, there must be some finality, even in enforcement cases, and the parties will not be allowed to tangle matters endlessly by raising new issues and factual material that could have been presented earlier. Marshall v. GPO, 45 MSPR 62, 65 (1990), determined that "there is no regulatory right for a party to submit additional arguments after the Board issues a decision based on an administrative judge's recommendation that the agency be found in noncompliance." Hoover v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 545, 558-59 (1993), echoed these holdings when it noted that an agency cannot raise objections in its exceptions to a noncompliance recommendation if those objections could have been, but were not, presented to the AJ. The Board rejected a last minute proffer of evidence concerning basic enforcement issues, Bellows v. USPS, 53 MSPR 149, 152 (1992):

In the instant case, the agency argues that because of the amendment to section 436.425, the requirement that an appellant make a showing of efforts to obtain other employment applies regardless of the period between removal and reinstatement. However, the agency failed to present this revision of the regulation to the administrative judge, and it has not shown that with due diligence it could have done so. We therefore decline to consider it....

Before the change in regulations calling for initial decisions on enforcement petitions, if the agency wholly failed to respond to the AJ's compliance recommendations, the Board would likely accept the AJ's findings. See Melendez v. Puerto Rico Nat'l Guard, 68 MSPR 553, 555 (1995). To avoid undue protraction of enforcement matters, once the agency responded to a regional compliance recommendation by a submission to the Board, the appellant should either respond within the 20 days then permitted by regulation or obtain an extension for the response from the Board--otherwise the Board could deem the agency's evidence of compliance to be unrebutted. See Ajaye El v. USPS, 57 MSPR 112, 114 (1993) (relying upon 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(7)).

Board Headquarters may inquire by telephone to determine whether compliance issues involved in a PFR have been resolved; if the Board requests evidence concerning status, this evidence must be submitted in writing rather than by telephone. See Parrish-Fishman v. Dept. of Interior, 51 MSPR 20 (1991) (Table). Under current regulations, the AJ issues a decision on an enforcement petition, not a recommendation. See 5 CFR 1201.183 (2022).
