a. Rank Reduction

A reassignment is a change in position within an agency without diminution of grade or basic pay. Before the Reform Act, the concept of reduction in rank involved the loss in organizational status or control that followed a reassignment from a supervisory or managerial position. Because notions of bureaucratic hierarchy and authority were nebulous, rank reductions were difficult to define and adjudicate. The resultant confusion and administrative litigation over organizational shifts diminished the ability of agencies to manage their workforces. Section 204(a) of the Reform Act abolished the concept of reduction in rank, confining appealable actions to clear demotions (with reductions in pay), along with the other, more tangible, adverse actions, e.g., suspensions and removals. Without an IRA appeal, allegations of reassignment without change in grade or pay do not provide a basis for MSPB jurisdiction. See Pierce v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3039) (movement to another position at the same grade and pay is ordinarily not an adverse action within the Board's review authority under 5 USC 7512, 7701); Huber v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3061) (reduction in responsibilities without a concurrent cut in grade or pay is not appealable to the Board; a reduction in pay or grade must be ascertainable at the time of the reassignment, and not contingent on anticipated future developments); Manning v. MSPB, 742 F.2d 1424, 1427 n.5 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (although reduction in rank was an adverse action prior to enactment of the Civil Service Reform Act (CSRA) of 1978, the CSRA eliminated the concept of reduction in rank as an adverse action); cf. Thomas v. United States, 709 F.2d 48 (Fed. Cir. 1983) ("Thomas says he was transferred from a merit pay position to a general schedule position, that he was moved from a retention register with at least three non-veterans to a register with only one person who had greater tenure than himself, and that he was therefore more vulnerable in the event of a future RIF. However, assuming the truth of those allegations, they result from a lateral transfer and not from a type of agency action, e.g., removal, reduction-in-grade, etc., within the jurisdiction of the MSPB as specified in 5 C.F.R. Sec. 1201.3(a)..."); Harpster v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 43, 45 (1988) ("Although reassignment without a reduction in grade or pay was at one time an appealable action, it has not been appealable since the enactment of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978."); Russell v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 698, 704, 6 MSPB 585 (1981) (extensive discussion of a reduction in rank); Wakeland v. NTSB, 6 MSPR 37, 38, 6 MSPB 44 (1981) ("Reduction in rank is no longer an appealable action."). If a wholly new position is established at the same grade as the appellant's own grade and given a function the appellant previously held, no appellate jurisdiction exists because the appellant is not losing grade, actually or constructively, but losing a function. The claim is a reduction in rank outside the Board's jurisdiction. Cocca v. Dept. of Air Force, 8 MSPR 194, 195, 7 MSPB 668 (1981).

Tsungu v. MSPB, 513 Fed. Appx. 942, 945-46 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP), summarized the Board's lack of jurisdiction over assignments to new positions entailing no loss in earnings:

On appeal to the court, Mr. Tsungu contends that, notwithstanding the identical grade and pay, he suffered a reduction in rank and responsibility because the agency reassigned him from a supervisory to a non-supervisory position and because his future prospects may be affected. However, "a reduction in responsibility without a concurrent reduction in grade or pay...is not appealable to the Board." Wilson v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 807 F.2d 1577, 1580 (Fed. Cir. 1986). This is because the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 eliminated the old reduction in rank standard with the present reduction in grade or pay test in order to "increase the flexibility of agencies to assign employees to positions and duties where they are needed." Id. Thus, the Board does not have jurisdiction over Mr. Tsungu's reduction in rank or responsibility claim without him alleging that his grade or pay was reduced.
