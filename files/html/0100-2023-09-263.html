b. Law Enforcement Privilege

A valid privilege claim is that the information sought concerns an investigation into possible violations of criminal laws and that nondisclosure is necessary to protect the identity of government informants. The privilege requires balancing of the public interest in nondisclosure against the need of an individual seeking disclosure. This balance may be conducted through information provided by the parties or through in camera inspection. If the information provided by the party with the information seems adequate and if the appellant's need for the information appears minimal, in camera inspection is not required. Rooney v. USDA, 26 MSPR 142, 147-49 (1985), explained as to a claim of privilege concerning information from informants in law enforcement investigations:

...The agency has argued that the evidence is not relevant to the issues in this case; and that it is privileged because it concerns an investigation of possible violations of criminal laws, and because its nondisclosure is necessary to protect the identity of government informants.

It is clear that the courts have recognized the validity of the privileges described above. Roviaro v. United States, 353 U.S. 53, 77 S. Ct. 623, 1 L. Ed.2d 639 (1957); Denver Policeman's Protective Ass'n v. Lichtenstein, 660 F.2d 432 (10th Cir. 1981); Black v. Sheraton Corporation of America, 564 F.2d 550 (D.C. Cir. 1977); Brown v. Thompson, 430 F.2d 1214 (5th Cir. 1970); Peck v. United States, 88 F.R.D. 65 (S.D.N.Y. 1980); United States v. O'Neill, 81 F.R.D. 664 (E.D. Pa. 1979); United States v. Illinois Fair Plan Association, 67 F.R.D. 659 (N.D. Ill. 1975); Kinoy v. Mitchell, 67 F.R.D. 1 (S.D.N.Y. 1975); United States ex rel. Morris Jackson v. Petrilli, 63 F.R.D. 152 (N.D. Ill. 1974); Jabara v. Kelly, 62 F.R.D. 424 (E.D. Mich. 1974); Capitol Vending Co., Inc. v. Baker, 35 F.R.D. 510 (D.D.C. 1964). They also have held consistently, however, that the privilege is qualified, rather than absolute, and that it calls for balancing the public interest in nondisclosure against the rights and needs of the individual seeking disclosure. See Roviaro, 353 U.S. at 62, 77 S. Ct. at 628; Black, 564 F.2d at 1015; Illinois Fair Plan Ass'n, 67 F.R.D. at 661; Kinoy, 67 F.R.D. at 11-12; Jackson, 63 F.R.D. at 153; Jabara, 62 F.R.D. at 426.

...

There appears to be no evidence contradicting the INS's statement that the documents and information at issue concern the contents of investigatory files compiled for law enforcement purposes, and that they include information concerning individuals who have provided information and assistance to the INS. Numerous court decisions have recognized the importance of the need for confidentiality of similar information. See, e.g., Roviaro, 353 U.S. at 62, 77 S. Ct. at 628; Black, 564 F.2d at 555; Crawford v. Dominic, 469 F. Supp. 260, 264 (E.D. Pa. 1979); Jackson, 63 F.R.D. at 153. The agency's need to protect the confidentiality of its information therefore appears to be substantial.

With respect to the appellant's need for the information, it seems probable that its disclosure could clarify the nature of the INS activities at issue. We note, however, that information regarding INS activities of this nature prior to the date of the incident on which the appellant's removal is based seems unlikely to be of significant probative value in the adjudication of this case. The appellant's removal was based on his actions on that date, rather than on any involvement with Mr. Nugent or any other individual prior to that time; and the agency, with the cooperation of the INS, has presented a substantial amount of documentary and testimonial evidence concerning the purpose and nature of the INS's activities on the date in question. Furthermore, although the appellant has alleged that the investigative materials sought would be exculpatory, the arguments he has presented in support of this allegation indicate that the only exculpatory value the production of the investigatory materials could reasonably be expected to have would be their failure to corroborate evidence presented by the agency. The appellant's need for the materials he has requested therefore generally appears to be minimal. We find that the INS's interest in protecting the privileged evidence outweighs the appellant's need for that evidence....

Beamon v. Dept. of Labor, 35 MSPR 15, 19-20 (1987), offered guidance concerning "law enforcement evidentiary privilege":

The law enforcement evidentiary privilege protects investigative files.... While failure to comply with an order to properly assert a privilege may be a basis for the imposition of sanctions...sanctions are inappropriate where the privilege has been validly asserted.[1]

[1] ...Kinoy v. Mitchell, 67 FRD 1, 15 (SDNY 1975) (if documents are privileged, litigation must continue as best it can without them); Black v. Sheraton Corp. of America, 564 F.2d 550, 556 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (it is dubious that an adverse inference can be drawn from a valid assertion of a privilege since the imposition of such a burden might discourage the exercise of the privilege and undermine underlying policies);... Kinoy v. Mitchell, 67 FRD at 15, n.50 (sanctions are imposed only where a claimed privilege has been denied but the documents requested are still withheld).

...

The agency bears the burden of properly claiming the privilege for each document concerned.... However, where the agency has failed to assert the claimed privilege properly, the Board has permitted the agency an opportunity to satisfy the established requirements.... To assert a claim of law-enforcement evidentiary privilege, the head of an agency must submit an affidavit setting forth a formal claim of the privilege after personally considering the documents for which the privilege is claimed.... In such affidavit, the head of the agency must specifically designate and describe the documents for which the privilege is claimed and state the specific reasons for preserving their confidentiality.

The Board added to the discussion of law-enforcement privilege claims in Childs v. USPS, 67 MSPR 348, 352-54 (1995), explaining the privilege is qualified, subject to a balancing test:

The law-enforcement evidentiary privilege is qualified, rather than absolute, and calls for balancing the public interest in nondisclosure against the rights and needs of the individual seeking disclosure. Rooney v. Dept. of Agric., 26 MSPR 142, 147 (1985); see Beamon, 35 MSPR at 20. In determining whether to grant the privilege, the Board has a range of options, from simply accepting the formal claim of privilege where the showing of need is minimal, to an in camera inspection of the documents where the appellant's need is great and the government's claim is otherwise unsubstantiated. Rooney, 26 MSPR at 148. An in camera inspection is particularly appropriate for a qualified privilege such as the law-enforcement privilege. Beamon, 35 MSPR at 19.

The agency argues on review that the AJ "did not rule that the documents were protected by the law enforcement privilege but that they were clearly not relevant to the Appellant's case," i.e., that the AJ's ultimate ruling was based on general relevance grounds rather than privilege grounds. We disagree, for the following reasons. In previously granting the appellant's motion to compel with respect to the entire ISM, the AJ implicitly determined that the document was relevant for purposes of discovery, absent a valid claim of privilege.[3] See generally 5 CFR §§ 1201.72(b), 1201.73(c)(2). An in camera review would not have been required for the AJ to deny a motion to compel based on general relevance grounds. After the agency specifically raised the claim of privilege, the AJ conducted an in camera review to weigh the competing interests--the appellant's need for the information based on its relevance and materiality to his case versus the agency's need for confidentiality of the information--and ruled in effect that the ISM was not sufficiently relevant or material to outweigh the agency's legitimate interest in its confidentiality.[4] We therefore find that the AJ's ultimate ruling with respect to the ISM was based on the claim of privilege.

[3] In the context of discovery, relevance is defined to include "information that appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence." 5 CFR § 1201.72(a).

[4] The public interest in minimizing the disclosure of documents tending to reveal law enforcement investigative techniques has been recognized within the context of the law-enforcement evidentiary privilege. See Beamon, 35 MSPR at 19-20; see also Association for the Reduction of Violence v. Hall, 734 F.2d 63, 65 (1st Cir. 1984); Black v. Sheraton Corp. of America, 564 F.2d 531, 545-46 (D.C. Cir. 1977).

To assert a claim of law-enforcement evidentiary privilege, the head of the agency must submit an affidavit setting forth a formal claim of the privilege after personally considering the documents for which the privilege is claimed. The affidavit must specifically designate and describe the documents for which the privilege is claimed and provide specific reasons to justify their confidentiality. Beamon, 35 MSPR at 20; In re Subpoena, 18 MSPR 454, 457 (1983).

In Beamon, 35 MSPR at 20-21, the Board found that the AJ erred in granting the agency's claim of law-enforcement evidentiary privilege in the absence of the requisite affidavit. See also In re Subpena, 18 MSPR at 457. The Board further found in Beamon, 35 MSPR at 20, that the AJ's error in this regard might have prejudiced the appellant's substantive right to introduce evidence to support her discrimination claim, and remanded the case to afford the agency a specific opportunity to properly assert the privilege.

As the appellant argues on petition for review, the agency here failed to submit any affidavit by the agency head, and thus failed to properly assert its claim of law-enforcement evidentiary privilege. However, unlike the situation in Beamon where the Board found that the purportedly privileged documents were likely to be relevant and material to the issues involved, here the AJ reviewed the document in camera and found that it was not relevant or material to the issues involved, except possibly the portion already in the appellant's possession. The appellant has shown no basis to disturb this finding by the AJ. Although the appellant vaguely contends on petition for review that the ISM was "necessary...to review the procedures employed by the Postal Inspectors" who were his "chief...accusers" and "to challenge [their]testimony on cross-examination," he does not specify how the procedures used by the Inspectors are relevant to the issues in this case. Contrary to the appellant's apparent argument, whether the Inspectors complied with the ISM procedures has little relevance in this noncriminal case, particularly as the appellant has not even alleged that the agency's investigation of his misconduct was improperly conducted.

Because the appellant has failed to show that the entire ISM is likely to be relevant to the issues in this case, we find it unnecessary to remand this case to afford the agency a specific opportunity to properly assert the privilege. Cf. Beamon, 35 MSPR at 20. We further find that any error by the AJ in granting the privilege did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights and, thus, does not warrant reversal of the initial decision. Cf. O'Connor v. Small Business Admin., 60 MSPR 130, 132-33 (1993) (the Board found no reversible error in the AJ's discovery rulings where the matters sought by the appellant were not likely to be relevant to his case); Leftridge v. USPS, 56 MSPR 340, 346 (1993) (the Board found no error in the AJ's denial of the appellant's motion to compel where the appellant alleged merely that he needed the "policies and rules governing [Postal]investigations" to "identify any idiosyncrasies in the alleged investigation")....
