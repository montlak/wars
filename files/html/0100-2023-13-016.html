c. Judge to Inform Appellant of Jurisdictional Requirements

After the Board receives an appeal challenging a constructive adverse action, the AJ's usual course is to issue a show-cause order, an "affirmative defenses" order, or a directive in an acknowledgment order stating that the appellant must provide factual evidence supporting a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction. See Zervas v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 217, 218 (1985) (citing Burgess v. MSPB, 758 F.2d 641 (Fed. Cir. 1985), post). Agencies often file a motion to dismiss the appeal as beyond the Board's jurisdiction. Under decisions that follow, a distinction is drawn between evidence and nonfrivolous allegations. If the necessary showing is made under the applicable pleading standard, the Board grants a hearing; otherwise the appeal is dismissed with notice of appeal and judicial review rights. See Durden v. Dept. of Navy, 18 MSPR 373, 374 n.1 (1983).

The Board's show-cause order or its equivalent must explain to the appellant the nature of the allegations she must make to obtain a jurisdictional hearing in a constructive adverse action appeal. Correcting earlier Board practice requiring appellants to determine for themselves the nature of the allegations supporting Board jurisdiction, under Burgess v. MSPB, 758 F.2d 641, 642-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

The jurisdiction of the board is limited to those actions appealable to it "under any law, rule, or regulation." 5 USC 7701(a). The court in Rose v. Department of Health and Human Services, 721 F.2d 355, 357 (Fed. Cir. 1982), concluded that the section 7701(a)(1) hearing right arises only after an employee properly invokes jurisdiction. It is the burden of the appealing employee to do this....

...

...The right to a hearing arises after an employee makes a nonfrivolous allegation of the involuntariness of a retirement or resignation....

An involuntary resignation is a removal...and as such the issue of jurisdiction is intertwined with the merits of an action appealable pursuant to 5 USC 7512(1), 7513(d). It is this connection with the merits of an appeal which prompts the need for a hearing.

[W]hen the board fails to alert the employee to an issue on which jurisdiction depends, it has not given adequate notice as to how to respond....

...

...Nowhere does it order or even caution the employee that to establish jurisdiction it would be necessary to produce or allege facts or evidence on an appealable issue. We believe a contrary impression was conveyed since the order indicated nothing of the kind and said that a decision would be made on the basis of the petition and the responses of the parties. The order was misleading, incomplete, and latently ambiguous. It failed to tell Burgess that a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness was required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue, and that the production of factual evidence would be necessary to show that the board had jurisdiction and should grant a hearing. The government knows how to make this plain to employees if that is what it intends.

What is the AJ supposed to tell the appellant whose case may hinge on a jurisdictional point? On this, the Board is not too clear--perhaps because Burgess was not too clear. The Federal Circuit mixed the concepts of jurisdictional allegations and the production of evidence. It is one thing to require the pleading of nonfrivolous allegations to establish the right to a hearing on the interrelated issues of jurisdiction and the merits. It is another to require both allegations and evidence just to get to the point of an evidentiary hearing. Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 489, 491 (1989), emphasized the significance, for jurisdictional purposes, of an allegation as opposed to a preliminary production of evidence in a constructive adverse action appeal:

In Burgess...the court held that a jurisdictional show cause order must be reasonably calculated to apprise the appellant of what is required to establish Board jurisdiction. In this case, the show cause order made no mention of the contention that the agency assured the appellant that he would not lose any pay or have any adverse impact on his income level. We find, therefore, that the appellant has not had an adequate opportunity to make a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency provided him with inaccurate information which he relied on in accepting the lower grade position, thus making such acceptance involuntary.

Thus, this case must be remanded to the regional office to afford the appellant such opportunity to make a nonfrivolous assertion of Board jurisdiction of his loss of pay and if such assertion is made he shall be afforded a hearing on that jurisdictional issue.

However it is done, a jurisdictional issue may be raised through an AJ's order that fairly informs the appellant what must be alleged to establish the right to a jurisdictional hearing. The AJ's notice to the appellant must explicitly state what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue. Higgins v. USPS, 84 MSPR 64, 66-7 ¶¶ 4-5 (1999) (allegation of forced retirement and constructive suspension); see Romine v. USPS, 64 MSPR 68, 73 (1994) ("We will not dismiss an appeal for lack of jurisdiction without first issuing an order reasonably calculated to apprise the appellant of what is required to obtain a hearing, and allowing the appellant to submit argument and evidence in support of his or her claim.").

The appellant may also be informed by an agency motion to dismiss of what the appellant must allege for her case to survive dismissal and proceed to a jurisdictional hearing. But if the agency motion is to be relied on to provide notice to the appellant of what she must plead, the motion must provide a complete explanation of the various allegations that could be made to establish a basis for Board jurisdiction over the constructive action. See Pendarvis v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 492, 496 (1996) ("The agency's motion [to dismiss] ...was insufficient...because it did not put the appellant on notice that she could also establish entitlement to a jurisdictional hearing by alleging facts which, if proven, would establish that the agreement was unlawful, involuntary, or the result of fraud or mutual mistake."). The appellant may also obtain direction in the requisite development of jurisdictional information through the AJ's explanation at the onset of a hearing. See Lorenz v. USPS, 84 MSPR 670, 673 ¶ 6 (2000); Hutchins v. VA, 21 MSPR 770, 771 n.* (1984) (harmless error if the AJ does not advise the appellant of the requisite elements of jurisdiction over a claim of involuntary retirement when the hearing evidence addressed the voluntariness of retirement).
