1. Protective Order

When the AJ has jurisdiction over an appeal, he has the authority to enter orders protecting the appellant, witnesses, and other individuals from harassment. If the AJ does not have jurisdiction, e.g., after the initial decision was issued, the Board has jurisdiction to issue a protective order, as long as the case is pending before the Board.

The Board's authority to issue protective orders is statutory. 5 USC 1204(e)(1)(B) provides:

(i) The Merit Systems Protection Board may, during an investigation by the Office of Special Counsel or during the pendency of any proceeding before the Board, issue any order which may be necessary to protect a witness or other individual from harassment, except that an agency (other than the Office of Special Counsel) may not request any such order with regard to an investigation by the Office of Special Counsel from the Board during such investigation.

(ii) An order issued under this subparagraph may be enforced in the same manner as provided for under paragraph (2) with respect to any order under subsection (a)(2) [salary withholding provisions] .

The statutory authority extends to two distinct areas of concern: first, an investigation by OSC (ordinarily preceding Board litigation); second, the period of pendency of any proceeding (whether at the behest of OSC, an appellant, a witness or other individual) before the Board. The protection extends to two classes of individuals: first, witnesses; second, any "other individual."

The statute is implemented in several portions of the Board's regulations. First, to invoke the statute, one must file a motion. The protective order motion is defined, 5 CFR 1201.55(d) (2022):

A motion for an order under 5 U.S.C. 1204(e)(1)(B) to protect a witness or other individual from harassment must be filed as early in the proceeding as practicable. The party seeking a protective order must include a concise statement of reasons justifying the motion, together with any relevant documentary evidence. An agency, other than the Office of Special Counsel, may not request such an order with respect to an investigation by the Special Counsel during the Special Counsel's investigation. An order issued under this paragraph may be enforced in the same manner as provided under subpart F for Board final decisions and orders.

AJs also derive regulatory authority to issue protective orders under 5 CFR 1201.41 (2022):

(b) Authority. Judges will conduct fair and impartial hearings and will issue timely and clear decisions based on statutes and legal precedents. They will have all powers necessary to that end unless those powers are otherwise limited by law. Judges' powers include, but are not limited to, the authority to:

...

(14) Issue any order that may be necessary to protect a witness or other individual from harassment and provide for enforcement of such order in accordance with subpart F. ["Enforcement of Final Decisions and Orders"]

The Judges' Handbook, Chapter 7, adds:

7. PROTECTIVE ORDERS. During an investigation by the OSC or during the pendency of any proceeding before the Board, including nonwhistleblower cases, the AJ may issue an order to protect a witness or other individual from harassment. An agency (other than OSC) cannot request a protective order from the Board during the OSC investigation. See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(B)(i); 5 C.F.R. §; 1201.55(d), 1201.41(b)(14). Enforcement of a protective order is governed by 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(B)(ii) and subpart F of the Board's regulations.

A motion by OSC for a protective order should conform to 5 CFR 1201.146 (2022):

(a) Under 5 U.S.C. 1204(e)(1)(B), the Board may issue any order that may be necessary to protect a witness or other individual from harassment during an investigation by the Special Counsel or during the pendency of any proceeding before the Board, except that an agency, other than the Office of the Special Counsel, may not request a protective order with respect to an investigation by the Special Counsel during such investigation.

(b) Any motion by the Special Counsel requesting a protective order must include a concise statement of reasons justifying the motion, together with any relevant documentary evidence. Where the request is made in connection with a pending Special Counsel proceeding, the motion must be filed as early in the proceeding as practicable.

(c) Where there is a pending Special Counsel proceeding, a Special Counsel motion requesting a protective order must be filed with the judge conducting the proceeding, and the judge will rule on the motion. Where there is no pending Special Counsel proceeding, a Special Counsel motion requesting a protective order must be filed with the Clerk of the Board, and the Board will designate a judge, as defined at § 1201.4(a) of this part, to rule on the motion.

5 CFR 1201.147 (2022) requires that a motion for a protective order by an individual other than OSC conform to 5 CFR 1201.55(d), supra:

Requests for protective orders by persons other than the Special Counsel in connection with pending original jurisdiction proceedings are governed by § 1201.55(d) of this part.

Addressing, or not addressing, motions for protective orders by OSC, Special Counsel ex rel. Kunert v. Dept. of Army, CB-1208-12-0025-U-2 (NP 10/22/2012), declared:

Although there is some case law concerning protective orders sought by parties other than OSC, see, e.g., In re Uriarte, 93 M.S.P.R. 183, ¶ 8 (2002); Oates v. Department of Health & Human Services, 64 M.S.P.R. 349, 351 (1994), this is the first time that OSC has requested a protective order in conjunction with a stay request, and for the benefit of the alleged victims of whistleblower reprisal. As a legal matter, it is not clear that the protective order statute was intended to be used in this way. In this connection, we note that OSC does not allege that the agency's alleged harassment of the six individuals who used to work at ASAP is interfering with its investigation. Further, the evidentiary standard that OSC must meet to demonstrate that an order is "necessary" to protect a witness or individual from harassment is not set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(B)(i), nor is it established in any regulation or caselaw; this is therefore a question of first impression.

Under Cloonan v. USPS, 65 MSPR 1, 4 n.2 (1994), "A witness who seeks to enforce a protective order must file a motion to intervene in the appeal. 5 CFR 1201.182(c) (1993)."

According to 5 CFR 1201.148 (2022), protective orders are enforced through Subpart F of the Board's regulations. Section 1201.148 provides:

A protective order issued by a judge or the Board under this subpart may be enforced in the same manner as provided under subpart F of this part for Board final decisions and orders.

Subpart F of the Board's regulations includes 5 CFR 1201.182(c) (2022), describing a petition for enforcement:

(3) Under § 1201.37(e) of this part, a nonparty witness who has obtained an order requiring the payment of witness fees and travel costs may petition the Board for enforcement of the order.

(4) Under § 1201.55(d) of this part, a nonparty witness or other individual who has obtained a protective order from a judge during the course of a Board proceeding for protection from harassment may petition the Board for enforcement of the order.

(5) A petition for enforcement under paragraph...(c)(4) of this section must be filed promptly with the regional or field office that issued the order or, if the order was issued by the Board, with the Clerk of the Board. The petitioner must serve a copy of the petition on each party or the party's representative....

Regulations at 5 CFR 1201.147-48 (2022), governing original jurisdiction proceedings (normally OSC or ALJ cases), incorporate by reference 5 CFR 1201.55(d) and the Board's enforcement authority.

Board Order 1559.18 (9/27/90), describing the Board's Case Tracking System, provides indirect guidance on methods for protecting witnesses, at 3:

A request for a Protective Order is a motion filed by a witness or other individual seeking protection from harassment. This may be sought in any appeal, not just in those with allegations of reprisal for whistleblowing. This is not to be confused with a protective order issued to limit the scope of discovery under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. At the regional office level, a Protective Order may be issued only while an appeal is pending.... Headquarters review of an AJ's grant or denial of a protective order will be treated as an interlocutory appeal. An enforcement petition on a protective order will be treated as a compliance appeal....

Although the protective order is more regulated than deployed, there are a few decisions outlining the Board's approach to claims of harassment made during Board proceedings. Under Weber v. Dept. of Army, 47 MSPR 130, 134 (1991):

Pursuant to 5 USC 1204(e)(1)(B)(i), the Board "may, during an investigation by the Office of Special Counsel or during the pendency of any proceeding before the Board, issue any order which may be necessary to protect a witness or other individual from harassment...." The Board's regulations, at 5 CFR 1201.55(d), provide for the filing of such a motion, "as early in the proceeding as practicable." That section also states that "[T]he party seeking a corrective order must include a concise statement of reasons justifying the motion, together with any relevant documentary evidence."

Review of the record fails to reveal that the appellant requested that the administrative judge issue a protective order. While it is true, as he contends, that in both his request for a stay and one of its attachments, he used the word "harassment," those submissions fall far short of the showing required by the Board's regulations, and in no way constitute a request that a protective order be issued....

Weber noted, 47 MSPR at 135 n.5:

[A]ny motion for protective order may be filed by the Special Counsel during the pendency of her investigation, or the appellant may file such a motion on his own pursuant to 5 CFR 1201.55(d).

Weber determined that a request for a protective order should be made to the AJ and not raised for the first time in a PFR. Weber, an IRA case, specifically declined to determine "the extent, if any, to which the Board would have authority to grant a motion for a protective order in a case over which it lacked jurisdiction." Id. at 135 n.4.

The protective order is for witnesses, not documents, Miller v. Dept. of Army, CH-4324-09-0891-I-1 (NP 2/23/2011):

In its response to the petition for review, the agency seeks a protective order regarding certain information obtained through the discovery process in this case that the appellant has posted on the internet. The agency's motion fails to meet the requirements and purpose of the Board's regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.55(d) and therefore is denied. See Garst v. Department of the Army, 60 M.S.P.R. 514, 520 (1994) (the purpose of the Board's authority to issue protective orders is to protect a witness or other individual from harassment in order to aid the fact-finding process).

On occasion parties to Board litigation seek a protective order to ensure against disclosure of documents beyond the confines of the Board appeal. It may be that an AJ will approve the order and place it into the record. Miller suggests the AJ may lack that authority. See Lawson v. DHHS, 61 MSPR 313, 315-16 (1994), discussed in the next section.
