a. Withdrawal of or Failure to Support Nonnegotiability Allegation

A negotiability appeal will be dismissed if the agency withdraws its allegation of nonnegotiability. The parent agency may withdraw the allegation on behalf of its subordinate. FUSE, NAGE Local R1-144 and Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., 29 FLRA 1172, 1172 (1987); see NLRBU and NLRB, 73 FLRA 20, 20-21 (2022) ("The Union fails to present any evidence that the Agency is currently asserting that any of the proposals are contrary to law, rule, or regulation or permissively negotiable. Instead, the Union argues that the Agency previously declared the Union's proposals outside the duty to bargain before the Panel....To the extent that the Agency previously declared any of the Union's proposals nonnegotiable, it has withdrawn those challenges by withdrawing its allegations of nonnegotiability before the Authority. Consequently, there is no dispute between the Union and the Agency concerning the legality of the proposals. Therefore, we find that the conditions for a negotiability appeal before the Authority have not been met and we dismiss the petition, without prejudice to the Union's right to refile."); NFFE Local 1998 and Dept. of State, Passport Services, 71 FLRA 417, 417-18 (2019) (Member Abbott dissenting in part) ("But even if the Agency had made such an allegation during bargaining, it effectivity withdrew that allegation with regard to Proposals 1-4 and 6-7 by (1) abandoning it before us now and (2) not arguing that those proposals are contrary to any law, rule, or regulation. Therefore, there is no disagreement between the Union and the Agency over the negotiability of Proposals 1-4 and 6-7, and we dismiss the Union's petition as to those proposals, without prejudice to the right to refile, if the conditions governing review of negotiability issues are satisfied."); AFGE Nat'l Joint Council of Food Inspection Locals and USDA, 57 FLRA 764, 765 (2002) (once agency has withdrawn allegation of nonnegotiability concerning union's proposal, there is no longer an issue as to whether proposal is within duty to bargain; FLRA does not consider the proposal; it dismisses the petition). If the parties' submissions to FLRA demonstrate that there is no allegation of nonnegotiability by the agency, FLRA will not rule on the proposals. FLRA's dismissal of the union's petition is without prejudice to the union's right to file an appeal if the agency alleges that the proposals are outside the duty to bargain and the other conditions governing review are met. See POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 56 FLRA 69, 70 (2000); AFGE Local 948 and Dept. of Labor, Employment Standards Admin., 55 FLRA 582, 582 (1999) ("[W]e dismiss the petition...without prejudice to the Union's right to file an appeal if the conditions governing review are met and the Union chooses to file such an appeal."); AFGE Council 214 and Dept. of Air Force, Wright-Patterson AFB, 53 FLRA 131, 132 (1997), followed in POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 53 FLRA 625, 629-30 (1997) (agency did not provide nonnegotiability allegation in response to union request; when agency failed to object to some proposals embraced by negotiability appeal, FLRA dismissed the petition as to those proposals, subject to union's right to appeal if agency subsequently objected to their negotiability).

Under AFGE Local 2031 and VAMC Cincinnati, 56 FLRA 32, 32 n.3 (2000), the agency's failure to address a disputed proposal in its statement of position was "tantamount to a withdrawal of an allegation of nonnegotiability." If both parties fail to provide timely arguments in support of their positions concerning negotiability, the petition for review may be dismissed without prejudice to its refilling, AFGE Local 3584 and DOJ, FCI Dublin, 64 FLRA 316, 317 (2009):

Under § 2424.32 of the Authority's Regulations, the Union has the burden of "raising and supporting arguments that the proposal...is within the duty to bargain[.]" 5 C.F.R. § 2424.32(a). Likewise, the Agency has the burden of "raising and supporting arguments that the proposal...is outside the duty to bargain[.]" 5 C.F.R. § 2424.32(b). In this case, both parties have failed to meet these burdens.

With regard to Proposal 2, the Agency's statement of nonnegotiability fails to state which of management's rights is affected by the proposal or explain why the proposal is otherwise outside the duty to bargain. For its part, the Union addresses only the merits of the proposal. With regard to Proposal 6, the Agency makes an unsupported assertion that the proposal affects management's rights to assign employees and work; the Union argues that the proposal is an appropriate arrangement for employees adversely affected by the Agency's relocation. However, under Authority precedent, the geographical location where employees or organizational units conduct agency operations affects management's right to determine its organization, not its right to assign employees or work. See, e.g., NFFE, Local 7, 53 FLRA 1435, 1438-39 (1998).

As both parties have failed to satisfy their obligations, the Authority is unable to determine whether the proposals are within the duty to bargain. In previous, similar circumstances, the Authority has dismissed petitions for review without prejudice to the union's right to refile the petition if requirements governing refiling are satisfied. See Patent Office Prof'l Ass 'n, 56 FLRA 69, 99-101 (2000) (Chairman Wasserman and Member Cabaniss dissenting separately as to other matters). Consistent with this precedent, we dismiss the petition for review as to Proposal 2 and Proposal 6 without prejudice.

The agency response to the union's petition must articulate its position with more than a recitation of the statutory provisions concerning management rights, AFGE Council 170 and DCMA, 71 FLRA 1220, 1221 (2020) (Member DuBester concurring):

After an exclusive representative files its petition, the agency must file its statement, in which it must "inform the Authority and the exclusive representative why a proposal is not within the duty to bargain or contrary to law." [21] Further, an agency "has the burden of raising and supporting arguments that the proposal...is outside the duty to bargain or contrary to law." [22] The Authority has held that an agency fails to meet its regulatory burden when it merely cites a law or regulation without explaining how a particular proposal conflicts with that law or regulation.[23] Additionally, § 2424.32(c)(1) of the Authority's Regulations provides that "[f]ailure to raise and support an argument will...be deemed a waiver of such argument."
