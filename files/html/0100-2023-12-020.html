broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 01: Initial Decision, Section C: Credibility Findings, Subsection 01: Necessary Findings, Part a: Weight of Evidence Equally Balanced
a. Weight of Evidence Equally Balanced

The AJ has to decide the case. It is insufficient for an AJ to hold that an agency fails to meet its burden of proof because testimony on a key point conflicts, leaving the evidence in equipoise. The AJ must decide the underlying credibility issue. See Cook v. Dept. of Army, 105 MSPR 178, 186 ¶ 19, 2007 MSPB 76 (2007) ("We note further that, even if the agency's evidence were equally worthy of belief as that of the appellant, the appellant would be entitled to prevail. See Knudsen v. Department of Health & Human Services, 35 F.3d 543, 550 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (when the evidence is in 'equipoise,' the party with the burden of persuasion has failed to meet its burden)."); Jaramillo v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 469, 478 ¶ 20 (1999) (if the evidence is evenly balanced, the party with the burden of persuasion must lose); Facciponti v. USPS, 15 MSPR 183, 185 (1983) (issue for the AJ to resolve is not whether there is a substantial likelihood that the appellant will prevail on the merits, but whether the agency established its case against the appellant by preponderant evidence); Wright v. DHHS, 5 MSPR 178, 5 MSPB 232 (1981) (["T]he presiding official simply found that the contradictory testimony was in balance and that the agency had failed to meets its burden by default. The presiding official did not resolve the question of credibility raised by the contradictory testimony; his determination that the agency failed to meet its burden of proof has no reasoned basis."); cf. Hardin v. Dept. of Treasury, 95 MSPR 416 (2014) (Separate Opinion of Member Marshall) ("The evidence on this point is therefore, at best, in equipoise. See Knudsen v. Secretary of Health & Human Services, 35 F.3d 543, 550 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (where evidence is in equipoise, the party with the burden of persuasion has failed to meet that burden).").

The AJ may not credit both sides and attempt to reconcile some inconsistencies while omitting to reconcile major inconsistencies that require credibility resolutions. See Davis v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 24, 28-29 (1993). It is insufficient to avoid the critical factual issue by deciding that the testimony of opposing witnesses is "similarly forthright and credible." Teichmann v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 284, 285 (1986). But see Mitchell v. VA, 12 MSPR 256, 259 (1982) (involving patient abuse charges: the AJ could find that the agency failed to establish its charges by preponderant evidence when the "naked allegations of the patient" were met with an "unambiguous denial by appellant"). An initial decision will be sustained it resolves inconsistencies in testimony in a manner consistent with the record. See Wiecek v. DOJ, 3 MSPR 462, 464-65, 3 MSPB 532 (1980).
