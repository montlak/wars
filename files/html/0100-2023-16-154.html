broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section G: Interference, Subsection 06: Protected and Unprotected Statements, Writings, Part c: Management Officials
c. Management Officials

Despite the admonition of Learned Hand in NLRB v. Federbush Co., 121 F.2d 954, 957 (2d Cir. 1941), that "[w]hat to an outsider will be no more than the vigorous presentation of a conviction, to an employee may be the manifestation of a determination which it is not safe to thwart," generally FLRA does not find interference with protected rights if there is no more than an expression of opinion. Section 7116(e) protects the expression of personal views, arguments, or opinions by management, employees, or union representatives as long as the expression contains no threat of reprisal, force, or promise of benefit or was not made under coercive conditions. To determine whether statements made by management infringe § 7116(a)(1), the test is whether, under the circumstances, the statements tend to coerce or intimidate employees. Statements made by management personnel are coercive if, despite the intent of the employer, the individual employee could reasonably infer coercion. Objective, rather than subjective, standards guide those determinations. FLRA may look to the reaction of the employee to whom a communication is directed to assess whether the communication would be perceived as a threat by similarly situated employees. Marine Corps Logistics Base, Barstow and AFGE Local 1485, 33 FLRA 626, 637-39 (1988).

A statement by a supervisor, formerly active in union affairs, that the union did nothing for employees, made to an employee in a private discussion between the two individuals and in circumstances that did not follow or precede any coercive conditions, was not a ULP. The determination of whether the statement is made under coercive conditions must be an objective one, based on an assessment of the entire factual context. What is significant is not whether the employer intended or the employee perceived coercive effect, but whether the employer's actions would tend to coerce a reasonable employee. AAFES, Ft. Carson and AFGE Local 1345, 9 FLRA 620, 626-27 (1982) (ALJ Decision). 5 USC 7116(e) protected a supervisor who made statements to employees, such as "the Union isn't worth the paper it is printed on," "$11.00 a month isn't worth the money invested in it," "do you know your union dues are going up?," and "the Union has to represent you whether you are a member or not, dues are high and I hate to see you waste your money." At the time the statements were made,there was no representational election pending. The statements were not a ULP. They did not interfere with, restrain, or coerce employees in the exercise of their rights, and they were protected by § 7116(e). Oklahoma City Air Logistics Ctr., Tinker AFB and AFGE Local 916, 6 FLRA 159, 160 (1981). Expressions to employees by a manager that unions had no place in the agency and that they are a waste of government money, when not accompanied by a threat of penalty or reprisal, did not interfere with the right of employees to join labor organizations and they were not a ULP. 162nd Tactical Fighter Group, Arizona Air Nat'l Guard and AFGE Local 2924, 18 FLRA 583, 586-87 (1985).

Outside the context of representation elections, requiring management's neutrality, "section 7116(e) protects the expression of personal views, arguments, or opinions by management, employees, or union representatives as long as such expression contains no threat of reprisal or force or promise of benefit or is not made under coercive conditions." Remarks critical of a union during a radio program, though strongly expressed, were not under coercive conditions and contained no threats or promises. Dept. of Transp., FAA, Oakland Air Route Traffic Control Ctr. and W. Region, PATCO, 14 FLRA 201, 203 (1984). Speech was not coercive that did not convey a threat of economic reprisal taken solely on the employer's volition and when it had some basis in fact. FMCS and NAGE Local R3-118, 9 FLRA 199, 212 (1982) (ALJ Decision).

A manager has the freedom to react. A manager's statement to employees that he felt sorry for the children of the sick and demented people who wrote some articles in a union publication did not violate the Statute by disparaging the union. His comments were provoked by the abusive, vulgar, and disparaging nature of the article he answered. DOD, Dept. of Air Force, San Antonio Air Logistics Ctr., Kelly AFB and AFGE Local 1617, 13 FLRA 661, 674 (1984) (ALJ Decision). Protected was a suggestion from a manager to a union official not to send a letter to a congressman suggesting changes in agency operations because the changes might ultimately hurt the employees. There was no explicit or implicit threat or promise. The statement was no more than a prediction of possible adverse effects of the union letter. The action to be avoided was that of a third party, Congress. US Air Force Lowry AFB and AFGE Local 1974, 16 FLRA 952, 963-64 (1984) (ALJ Decision). Brief conversations between union organizers and activists and a manager with no history of prior union animus were not threats to those individuals or the union when the manager stated some concerns about the union organizing activity and requested that the union organizers be careful not to disclose confidential information. FMCS and NAGE Local R3-118, 9 FLRA 199, 209 (1982) (ALJ Decision).

An argument between a supervisor and a union official concerning the union's right to attend what the union believed to be a formal meeting was not conduct that interfered with protected rights. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 17 FLRA 594, 610 (1985) (ALJ Decision). "A grievance meeting is not so sterile that to ask, in effect, what are you grieving about, whether phrased 'What led up to this?' or 'Why did you go this route?' becomes an unfair labor practice." AAFES, Rocky Mountain Area Exch. No. 4125, Fort Carson and Palmer, 8 FLRA 432, 435 (1982) (ALJ Decision). There is no interference if a steward's conduct is criticized but no threats are made at a meeting with management called by the union in an attempt for the parties to discuss their concerns about the steward's handling of union matters. Dept. of Air Force, Air Logistics Command, Ogden Air Logistics Ctr., Hill AFB and AFGE Local 1592, 10 FLRA 88, 94 (1982) (ALJ Decision). Not amounting to interference was a statement by a supervisor to an employee that, given what was considered to have been a settlement of the grievance, the employee should think carefully about pursuing the grievance, and that the supervisor would fight the employee all the way on the grievance. A union representative was present at the meeting. The expression of the intent to defend the grievance was not a threat against the employee. Customs Serv. and NTEU, 19 FLRA 1141, 1148 (1985) (ALJ Decision).

If a manager makes an ambiguous statement, one that may or may not connote interference depending on how it is construed by a third party, he has no obligation to come forward with an explanation that will remove the unlawful thrust of the words used. But if at the time the statement is made the manager is made aware that the remark is perceived as threatening, the "ambiguity is removed and the latent threat is made explicit upon a failure to dispel the unlawful inference drawn and noted by the listener." Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and AFGE Local 2026, 6 FLRA 491, 496 (1981) (ALJ Decision).
