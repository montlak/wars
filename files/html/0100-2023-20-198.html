broida mspb Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices, Subchapter 05: Burden of Proof in Reprisal Case, Section A: Analysis Under Whistleblower Protection Act, Subsection 03: Contributing Factor Analysis, Part a: Order of Proof
a. Order of Proof

The 2012 WPEA prohibited the Board from altering ordinary burdens of proof. The appellant in an IRA case establishes a prima facie case first, the agency offers its defense, and the appellant rebuts the agency defense. The WEPA does that by placing the agency defense where it should be--after the appellant establishes that a protected disclosure was a contributing factor to the personnel action. 5 USC 1221 establishes the sequence for an appellant's IRA case:

(e)(1) Subject to the provisions of paragraph (2), in any case involving an alleged prohibited personnel practice as described under section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D), the Board shall order such corrective action as the Board considers appropriate if the employee, former employee, or applicant for employment has demonstrated that a disclosure described under section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D) was a contributing factor in the personnel action which was taken or is to be taken against such employee, former employee, or applicant. The employee may demonstrate that the disclosure or protected activity was a contributing factor in the personnel action through circumstantial evidence, such as evidence that--

(A)  the official taking the personnel action knew of the disclosure or protected activity; and

(B)  the personnel action occurred within a period of time such that a reasonable person could conclude that the disclosure or protected activity was a contributing factor in the personnel action.

(2) Corrective action under paragraph (1) may not be ordered if, after a finding that a protected disclosure was contributing factor, the agency demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same personnel action in the absence of such disclosure.

5 USC 1214 establishes the order of presentation for an OSC corrective action case:

(b)(4)(A) The Board shall order such corrective action as the Board considers appropriate, if the Board determines that the Special Counsel has demonstrated that a prohibited personnel practice, other than one described in section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D), has occurred, exists, or is to be taken.

(B)(i) Subject to the provisions of clause (ii), in any case involving an alleged prohibited personnel practice as described under section 2302(b)(8)or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D), the Board shall order such corrective action as the Board considers appropriate if the Special Counsel has demonstrated that a disclosure or protected activity described under section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D) was a contributing factor in the personnel action which was taken or is to be taken against the individual.

(ii)  Corrective action under clause (i) may not be ordered if, after a finding that a protected disclosure was a contributing factor, the agency demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same personnel action in the absence of such disclosure.

The analytical requirements apply both to IRA cases and appeals that raise whistleblower reprisal as an affirmative defense, as Pelcher-Herring v. FMCS, DC-0752-13-0152-I-1 (NP 1/23/2014), explained as to defenses under 5 USC 2302(b)(9) and (b)(8), but not to a (b)(12) defense:

We note that a provision of the WPEA may affect the manner in which the administrative judge analyzes the appellant's affirmative defense under section 2302(b)(9)(B). Under the WPEA, "[c]orrective action...may not be ordered if, after a finding that a protected disclosure was a contributing factor, the agency demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same personnel action in the absence of such disclosure." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(2) (emphasis added).[9] The WPEA further provides that this sequence of analysis "shall apply in any proceeding brought under section 7513(d)"--as in the instant appeal--"if, or to the extent that, a prohibited personnel practice as defined in section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D) is alleged." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(i). Thus, because there has been no finding whether the appellant has established by preponderant evidence that she engaged in a protected activity under section 2302(b)(9)(B), or that such protected activity was a contributing factor in her removal, we cannot reach the issue of whether the agency has presented clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the personnel action even in the absence of the appellant's alleged protected activity. See Ryan v. Department of the Air Force, 117 M.S.P.R. 362, ¶ 13 (2012) (pre-WPEA decision recognizing that, "[b]ecause the [Whistleblower Protection Act] does not mandate any particular sequence for trying the elements of a whistleblower case, in appropriate cases, the Board has historically first addressed the agency's affirmative defense").

[9] This sequence of analysis does not apply to a claim of a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b)(12). See 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(1) (mandating the sequence of analysis only in claims "under section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D)"). On remand, therefore, depending on the administrative judge's resolution of the appellant's subsection (b)(9) affirmative defense, the administrative judge may be able to reach the ultimate issue of "whether, weighing all of the evidence, the appellant has met her burden of proving the agency's removal action violated a law, rule, or regulation implementing, or directly concerning, the merit systems principles contained in 5 U.S.C. § 2301" in violation of section 2302(b)(12). Jenkins, 118 M.S.P.R. 161, ¶ 19; see id. ("The Board has not previously established the precise elements for proving a violation of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(12).").

The Board is not precluded from making preliminary and possibly dispositive jurisdictional determinations, for example, whether disclosures are protected or whether there is a nonfrivolous allegation that an official knew of a protected disclosure, Reed v. VA, 122 MSPR 165, 174-75 n.8, 2015 MSPB 2 (2015) (replacing Reed v. VA, 122 MSPR 24, n.7, 2014 MSPB 85 (2014)):

We note that the WPEA instructs that a denial of a request for corrective action on the basis that the agency established its affirmative defense may only be made "after a finding that a protected disclosure was a contributing factor." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(2); see Clarke v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 121 M.S.P.R. 154, ¶ 19 n.10 (2014); Belyakov v. Department of Health & Human Services, 120 M.S.P.R. 326, ¶ 7 n.3 (2013). Nothing in the WPEA, however, precludes the Board from considering, at the jurisdictional stage, whether the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation that the disclosure was a contributing factor in the agency's decision to take or fail to take a personnel action, even in the absence of a finding that she made a nonfrivolous allegation that the disclosure was protected.

Before the WPEA, the WPA did not provide a particular analytical sequence in IRA appeals. Yunus v. VA, 84 MSPR 78, 82 ¶ 8 (1999) (determining first that the agency demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken action despite the protected activity), aff'd, Yunus v. VA, 242 F.3d 1367 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (noting, however, that the jurisdiction of the Board was apparent from the facts of record). The Board could invert the burdens of proof by examining first whether the agency's case was strong enough to show by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action, even if reprisal were assumed to be a contributing factor. See Clark v. Dept. of Army, 997 F.2d 1466, 1470 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("There is no reason why an administrative judge may not assume for purposes of analysis that a petitioner has established the first step, and address the second step first. If the AJ concludes that the petitioner does not have a whistleblower defense because of a failure of proof at the second step, then it is unnecessary, and probably wasteful, to require him also to address step one."); Schmittling v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 225, 228 ¶ 18 (1999) (permitting AJ to assume a protected disclosure and to first determine that the agency would have taken the action against the appellant absent that disclosure); cf. Geyer v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 682, 688 (1996) (WPA does not mandate a particular analytical sequence; the Board considered the appellant's conduct so egregious that it assumed, for IRA analysis, that whistleblowing was a contributing factor, then determined that the agency demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that the appellant would have been removed had there not been whistleblowing); Mausser v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 41, 44 (1994) (follows Clark); Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 397, 403-04 n.4 (1994) ("[W]e agree with the administrative judge's finding that, even assuming the appellant's whistleblowing status, the agency proved by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the same actions in the absence of his disclosures.").

If the ordinary order of proof was varied, the AJ was to ensure that he maintained flexibility to develop issues that arise and to permit full exploration of pivotal points such as the motive of management officials to retaliate and the involvement of managers who retaliatory motives in the decisional processes of officials who might otherwise appear to be neutral. See Mangano v. VA, 109 MSPR 658, 669-71 ¶¶ 26, 28, 30, 2008 MSPB 202 (2008). Even following the WPEA, the Board still permits the AJ to make some assumptions in his analysis, Kennington v. Dept. of Treasury, DE-1221-12-0426-W-1 n.4 (NP 12/17/2013):

The Board previously permitted administrative judges to consider an agency's affirmative defense before giving the appellant the opportunity to present his evidence. See, e.g., Azbill v. Department of Homeland Security, 105 M.S.P.R. 363, ¶ 16 (2007) (the Board may determine the merits of an IRA appeal in any order it deems most efficient). However, the Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act, enacted on November 27, 2012, prohibits this approach. See Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act of 2012, Pub. L. No. 112-199, § 114, 126 Stat. 1465, 1472; see also 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(2). We do not believe that this prohibition precludes an administrative judge from assuming, without finding, that an appellant's disclosures were protected and then proceeding to determine whether the appellant has nonfrivolously alleged that his protected disclosures were a contributing factor in the personnel actions at issue.

If the ordinary order of proof was varied, the AJ was to be clear how and when proof will be presented. Diefenderfer v. Dept. of Transp., 108 MSPR 651, 661 ¶¶ 25-26, 2008 MSPB 105 (2008), remanded because the AJ failed to identify how proof was to proceed:

We find further that the administrative judge's reliance on the limited nature of the evidence the appellant had presented concerning the nonselections is misplaced. In his order describing the scope of the hearing, the administrative judge stated that he would assume, "[a]t this juncture of the appeal,...that all of the personnel actions that [he had] not excluded [were] 'covered,' and that all of the appellant's disclosures [were] protected." He then indicated that the agency was to "present its case first," that the appellant would "have a chance to rebut that case," and that, if the agency could not "prove its case by clear and convincing evidence, then [the administrative judge would] require the appellant to establish her case on the merits by preponderant evidence."

We see nothing suggesting that the administrative judge ever advised the parties that the agency had failed to carry the burden of proof described in the order. Instead, the record shows that, at the conclusion of the hearing, the parties submitted briefs addressing the issue of whether the agency had presented clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the actions at issue in the absence of any protected disclosures. Under these circumstances, the appellant cannot be faulted for failing to present more evidence in connection with her nonselections for the APOI and AAPM positions. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (an appellant must receive explicit information on what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue).
