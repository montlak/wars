4. Mitigating Factors; Choice of Penalty

A Chapter 43 performance case permits no mitigation if the charges are sustained. Chapter 43, however, requires a performance improvement period as a precondition to removal or demotion for substandard performance. Through a Chapter 43 case, the employee is essentially given a last chance before serious job action is taken against her. In a Chapter 75 performance case, there is no requirement of a performance improvement or warning period. See Dahl v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 182, 184, 5 MSPB 236 (1981) ("Under Chapter 75 there is no requirement that the agency provide an employee with a specific warning before it proposes an adverse action."). Once adverse action is considered, however, the agency (and the Board) must consider mitigation of a penalty, Brewster v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 650, 652-53, 6 MSPB 547 (1981) [vacated for lack of jurisdiction, Brewster v. Dept. of Navy, 727 F.2d 1119 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (Table) (NP), dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, Brewster v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 68, 68 (1984)]:

In a Chapter 75 case whether the issue involves the adequacy of the appellant's performance, the burden is on the agency to prove by a preponderance of the evidence not only that the appellant has failed to perform the duties of his position in a satisfactory manner, but also, that the appellant's removal will promote the efficiency of the service. However, in determining whether the appellants removal will promote the efficiency of the service, the Board is, in effect, deciding whether the penalty of removal is reasonable under the circumstances. Thus, the same criteria that are applied to determine the reasonableness of the penalty in conduct-related cases such as Douglas...would appropriately apply to the reasonableness of the penalty in performance-related cases.

The extent of the Board's review of the agency-imposed penalty is enunciated in Douglas, supra, 5 MSPB 332, 5 MSPR 305. There, after noting that in selecting an appropriate penalty the agency is required to responsibly balance the relevant factors involved in the particular case, the Board held that the purpose of its review of the selected penalty is essentially to assure that the agency conscientiously considered such factors and struck such a balance "within tolerable limits of reasonableness." In the instant case, the most relevant factors to be considered are: deficiencies in the appellant's work performance; the appellant's previous apparent satisfactory performance; and his length of service.

In the present case the record indicates that the appellant was counselled several times concerning his work deficiencies within the year preceding his removal and that his performance nevertheless declined. Yet, there is no evidence to contradict the appellant's assertion that his performance prior to this period was satisfactory. Given the agency's failure to present evidence to indicate that the appellant's earlier performance on the job was deficient, "he is entitled to the presumption that his performance has been satisfactory." Douglas v. Veterans Administration, supra, 334, 5 MSPR at 308.

Moreover, the appellant had over thirty-three and a half years of government service. There is no evidence in the record that the agency considered such service when it decided upon the removal penalty. Because of appellant's period of service, the bulk of which was satisfactory, except for the last year, the agency should have considered whether there were other positions requiring less responsibility, at the same or lower grade, which the appellant could satisfactorily fill. Evidence of such consideration is totally lacking from the record in this case.

The ultimate issue is whether service efficiency will be enhanced through the agency's penalty selection. See Parton v. FCC, 7 MSPR 236, 7 MSPB 142 (1981), supra; cf. Littledyke v. USDA, 6 MSPR 430, 433, 6 MSPB 365 (1981) (considering adequacy of proposal notice). Parton, supra, observed, 7 MSPR at 239-40:

The appellant urged in his petition that his removal was unduly harsh because, inter alia, the agency never assisted the appellant to improve his performance in contravention of the Merit Systems Principles. Principle 6 provides at 5 USC § 2301(b)(6) that "[e]mployees should be retained on the basis of the adequacy of their performance, inadequate performance should be corrected, and employees should be separated who cannot or will not improve their performance to meet required standards." That statutory language evolved from the following Senate and House proposals:

Employees are to be retained on the basis of their performance, and those who cannot or do not meet the required standards should be separated. Training is to be provided to employees to improve organizational and individual performance.

S. Rep. No. 95-969, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 19 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1978, pp. 2723, 2741.

Employees whose performance is inadequate should be encouraged to improve, and employees who cannot and will not improve their performance to meet established standards should be separated from the Federal service.

H. R. Rep. No. 95-1403, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 16 (1978).

We conclude that, in light of the legislative history of Merit Systems Principle 6 as reflected in the House and Senate Reports, the Principle encourages agencies to inform their employees of any deficient performance and afford them an opportunity to improve before initiating a removal action, as opposed to another disciplinary action. We held in Wells v. Harris, 1 MSPB 199, 203, 1 MSPR 208, 214 (1979), that the Merit Systems Principles are not self-executing in that they cannot be the basis of an action unless they are implemented by a law, rule, or regulation, but according to the legislators' statement of purposes in enacting them, the Principles are "expressly stated to furnish guidance to Federal agencies in carrying out their responsibilities in administering the public business." H. R. Conf. Rep. No. 95-1717, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 3 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1978, p. 2860.

As we held in Douglas, 5 MSPB 331-32, 5 MSPR 304-05, one relevant factor in determining the appropriateness of a penalty is "the clarity with which the employee was on notice of any rules that were violated in committing the offense, or had been warned about the conduct in question...." Analogously, the appellant in the case now before us presented uncontroverted evidence that the agency never assisted him to improve his performance, or even informed him of any deficiency, after it discovered his high error rate in grading examinations. We find that, because the agency did not provide the appellant an opportunity to improve his performance, the appellant's removal was a clearly excessive penalty. Demotion might have been a viable alternative, but since the appellant's high error rate in using a template scoring sheet indicates carelessness rather than inability on his part, we believe that the most appropriate penalty is a thirty (30) day suspension without pay to impress upon him the seriousness of his deficient performance.

Relevant mitigating factors in a performance case prosecuted as an adverse action include the length of service, the deficiencies leading to the action, and the record of past satisfactory service. When an agency had not considered a record of 33 years of service, with only the last being unsatisfactory, the agency should have considered whether there were other positions requiring less responsibility, at the same or lower grade, that the appellant could satisfactorily fill. Because no consideration was given to those factors, the agency failed to show that its selection of the penalty was reasonable. The case was remanded for consideration of whether there was another position the appellant could have satisfactorily filled. Brewster v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 650, 653, 6 MSPB 547 (1981), on remand, 14 MSPR 209, 212 (1982) (sustaining removal: "[T]he Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 does not require agencies to perform the type of extensive outplacement service for employees who are removed for unsatisfactory performance...especially in view of the fact that the agency provides no such service to employees who are performing satisfactorily."), vacated for lack of jurisdiction, Brewster v. Dept. of Navy, 727 F.2d 1119 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (Table) (NP), dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, Brewster v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 68, 68 (1984); see Person v. SSA, 12 MSPR 175, 178-79 (1982) (removal in a Chapter 75 performance case but considering mitigating factors: the seriousness of deficiencies; the effect of the deficiencies on the confidence of a supervisor in the appellant's ability to perform duties; the employee's past work and disciplinary records; the clarity of past warnings of performance deficiencies; the adequacy of alternative sanctions); Miller v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 102, 108 (1981) (remanding a performance-based removal for additional evidence on whether a lower-grade position was available, within the appellant's qualifications, that the appellant could be expected to perform satisfactorily).

The scope of Board cases suggesting the propriety of mitigation factors in Chapter 75 performance-based cases may have been attenuated by Gober v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 354, 358 (1983), relying on Van Gilder v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 337, 340-41 (1982). There the Board stated that ordinary mitigating factors of the employee's past disciplinary record and past work record are not equally applicable to performance cases. Nor is a table of penalties dispositive in a performance case, particularly if the table contemplates a single instance of careless workmanship rather than the continuing inability to perform the duties of a position. If counseling was given and yet poor performance continued for months with an adverse impact upon the agency, removal would be justified. But see Tennyson v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 123, 127 (1982) (despite several work errors, removal was mitigated to a 10-day suspension because of the appellant's excellent record and because the agency's penalty table called for a penalty of a reprimand for a first offense of careless workmanship or negligence to a 10-day suspension as the minimum penalty for a third offense).

The limitations of Gober, supra, were reaffirmed or disregarded--depending on how it is read--in another decision concluding that an employee's past disciplinary record, past work record, and the adequacy and effectiveness of alternative sanctions are not mitigating factors fully applicable to performance cases. Relevant factors include the nature and seriousness of the deficiencies in performance, previous performance, length of service, the effect of deficiencies on the supervisor's confidence in the appellant's ability to perform assigned duties, and mitigating circumstances surrounding the case. Gregory v. Dept. of Educ., 16 MSPR 144, 146 (1983); see Madison v. DLA, 48 MSPR 234, 239 (1991) (other factors to consider included the employee's receipt of notice that deficient performance might be a basis for adverse action, the failure by the employee to avail himself of the opportunity to improve performance and reluctance to carry out assigned tasks). A factor relevant to determining whether the employee should be removed is whether his deficient performance could adversely affect his performance in a different position. McCoy v. DOD, 20 MSPR 284, 287 (1984). A record of 11 years of good performance was a sufficient mitigating factor for a district court reviewing EEO allegations to direct the Board to mitigate a removal based on poor performance under Chapter 75, a result that led the Board to impose a 60-day suspension. Healy v. Dept. of Navy, 32 MSPR 28, 30-31 (1986). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Performance Action," concerning mitigation of penalties.]
