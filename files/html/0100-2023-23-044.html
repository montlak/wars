f. Time Pressure

McGowan v. DHUD, PH-0752-10-0572-I-1 (NP 4/1/2011), concluded that there was no coercion of an appellant who was given a short deadline by government counsel to consider and accept a settlement:

Nor can the appellant reasonably claim that he agreed to the settlement under duress. We note that the appellant specifically represented to the administrative judge that his decision to settle was voluntary. Further, that the agency's representative was insistent and imposed time pressure on the appellant to agree did not render his decision to settle involuntary. See Parrott v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 519 F.3d 1328, 1335 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (an appellant's choice is not coerced or involuntary merely because he faces an inherently unpleasant situation or is limited to two unpleasant options). In similar circumstances, the Federal Circuit has held that giving an appellant approximately an hour to decide whether to resign or face discipline did not make the decision coerced because the agency was not obligated to make the offer at all. Id. In addition, the appellant here reiterated his agreement to the terms, and represented he did so voluntarily, at the settlement conference approximately 2 weeks after the initial agreement. Simply put, the appellant's choice was not coerced and he always had the option of proceeding with his Board appeal challenging the agency action.

See Lowery v. USPS, CH-0752-14-0839-I-1 (NP 6/22/2015) ("The record also shows that the appellant tentatively agreed to the offer on December 1, 2014, and that the agency emailed both the appellant and his representative copies of the final agreement on December 18, 2014. Under these circumstances, we find that the appellant was not personally excluded from settlement negotiations nor was he subjected to time pressure in making his decision."); cf. Diaz v. USPS, SF-0752-15-0497-I-1 (NP 1/29/2016) ("Likewise, although the appellant now claims that the settlement agreement's July 10, 2015 deadline for submitting his disability retirement application was coercive and caused him duress, he has not shown that he involuntarily accepted this term, that circumstances permitted no other alternative, and that such circumstances were the result of the agency's coercive act. See Hinton, 119 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 4. Moreover, the appellant received an extension of the deadline within the 7-day revocation period under the OWBPA, at which time he could have revoked the settlement agreement without the pressure of any imminent deadline but failed to do so.").

The appellant's belief that those present were pressing for a quick end to a mediation did not suffice to vacate a settlement in Barnes v. Dept. of Navy, AT-0752-16-0686-I-1 (NP 3/21/2023):

The appellant asserts that, since entering into the settlement agreement, her medical conditions have deteriorated. However, she does not claim that her medical conditions caused her to sign the agreement or to not understand what the settlement provided when she signed the agreement. Instead, she argues that she felt "pressured, almost forced" to sign the agreement because the mediator stated that he needed to get to the airport, the agency attorney said he needed a "final answer then," and her husband wanted her to begin the healing process. We do not find that these are allegations that the agency representative engaged in coercive acts or of circumstances that left the appellant with no alternative but to accept the agreement as presented by the agency. Furthermore, the agreement specifically states that it is "not based on any harassment, threats, coercion, or intimidation and [the parties] acknowledge that they enter into this Agreement knowingly, voluntarily and of their own free will." To the extent the appellant may be trying to assert that her medical conditions prevented her from understanding the terms of the agreement, the agreement also explicitly states that she "does not suffer from any mental disease or defect that impairs her ability to think, analyze, and understand the terms and conditions of this agreement." Moreover, the record reflects that the appellant was represented during the mediation process by her husband, who signed the settlement agreement as her representative. Thus, we find that the appellant has not, by her challenges to the validity of the agreement, met her burden of proving that the settlement agreement was coerced or that she did not freely enter into the agreement.

Time pressure did not invalidate an agreement for a represented appellant with an OWBPA rescission clause, Bautz v. Dept. of Treasury, AT-1221-15-0577-W-1 (NP 4/18/2016):

On review, the appellant renews her argument that the settlement agreement is invalid. She alleges that she accepted the terms because of the coercive acts of the agency representative and was allowed no other alternative except to accept and sign the settlement that day, even though the agreement itself provided that she had 21 days to consider it and 7 days to revoke it. She contends that the agency representative did not explain the time frames regarding the settlement and that her attorney stated simply that the language is "boilerplate." She also argues that she received no additional consideration in exchange for the waiver. Finally, the appellant argues that her attorney pressured her into signing the agreement by stating that, if she decided to revoke it, he would no longer represent her. With her petition for review, the appellant has submitted copies of emails exchanged between her and her attorney during the days following her signing of the agreement.
