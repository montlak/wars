b. Entry of Interlocutory or Interim Relief on Remand

The topic of "interim relief," that is, the restoration of the appellant to duty or pay status after a successful initial decision and while pending an agency PFR, is discussed earlier in this chapter, "Interim Relief." On occasion the Board decides an appeal in favor of the appellant, e.g., it cancels a removal, but remands the appeal to the region for development of a decision on another aspect of the case, e.g., an allegation of discrimination. The Board may then direct the agency to take specific action, such as canceling the removal and restoring of the appellant to his former position, leaving the AJ to consider the EEO issue on remand. See Gannon v. USPS, 61 MSPR 41 (1994) (agency did not challenge the mitigation of removal to a reprimand ordered by the AJ; appellant filed a PFR on his EEO claim). Ginocchi v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 62, 73 (1992), directing the agency to cancel a demotion that occurred without due process while the Board remanded the appeal to the AJ to determine whether the agency committed a prohibited personnel practice, commented:

The relief that the Board grants during remand is distinguishable from the interim relief that has been provided to the appellant under Section 6 of the Whistleblower Protection Act, since the relief of Section 6 is prospective only. Further, the final relief granted during remand is derived from the Board's general authority under 5 USC 1205, "Powers and functions of the Merit Systems Protection Board."

Immediate relief was ordered for an appellant, coupled with a remand to consider EEO issues, Edwards v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 404, 408 (2000):

Despite the absence of a final decision on the appellant's discrimination and reprisal allegations, under the circumstances of this case, we find it appropriate that the agency now cancel its removal action and restore the appellant to his District Adjudications Officer position with INS. See 5 USC § 1204(a)(1) and (2). The continued processing of, and ultimate decision on, the discrimination and reprisal claims can have no effect on the appellant's entitlement to return to duty under our holding under this Opinion and Order. See McLain, 82 MSPR 526 ¶ 11.

See Cowart v. USPS, 117 MSPR 572, 574 ¶ 7, 2012 MSPB 43 (2012) (initial decision directing reinstatement became final; appellant filed a PFR on EEO defenses and claims; Board remanded for consideration of the EEO claims, while directing agency to reinstate appellant); Hutchison v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 302, 308 (1989) (implying that, in a counsel fee dispute, some fees may be awarded following a decision on PFR, leaving resolution of the remaining fee dispute for remand); cf. McLain v. USPS, 82 MSPR 526, 529-30 ¶¶ 9-11 (1999) (directing cancelation of a constructive suspension that the Board determined on PFR was without due process, leaving resolution of discrimination and reprisal issues for remand: "Despite the absence of a final decision on the appellant's discrimination and reprisal allegations, under the circumstances of this case, we find it appropriate that the agency now cancel its suspension action and restore the appellant to his position. See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(1) and (2). The continued processing of, and ultimate decision on, the discrimination and reprisal issues can have no effect on the appellant's entitlement to return to duty under our holding in this Opinion and Order. Tyler, 62 M.S.P.R. at 513."); cf. Ellshoff v. Dept. of Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 62 (1997) (issuing an "interlocutory order" reversing the AJ's denial of interim relief and granting that remedy during the pendency of its consideration of the parties' PFRs). But cf. Garcia v. OPM, CH-844E-19-0212-I-1 (NP 10/5/2022) ("The Board's regulations do not provide for an award of interim relief at the petition for review level. To the extent the appellant is asserting that the administrative judge erred in declining to award interim relief below, the Board reviews such a claim under an abuse of discretion standard.").

The Board may decide that, rather than issue a decision that awards some relief, while leaving other aspects of the appeal for development in a later proceeding, it will vacate the initial decision and allow all issues to be subsequently resolved, Thoin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 322, 323 ¶ 4 (1999):

Normally when the Board adjudicates a petition for review without remanding the appeal to the administrative judge, it issues a final decision and provides the appellant with notice of his right to seek judicial review. However, in this case, as explained above, not all of the issues surrounding the overpayment the appellant received have been resolved. Rather than issuing a final decision that addresses only some of the issues (existence and amount of the overpayment), raising the possibility that the appellant will be forced to split his claim by going to court and appearing before OPM simultaneously, we have vacated the initial decision but are not issuing a final judicially reviewable decision. After OPM has issued a reconsideration decision on the waiver and/or adjustment issues (as more fully set out below), the appellant may refile his appeal. The administrative judge should then issue a decision addressing any challenge the appellant may raise to OPM's determination on the issues remanded to OPM; the administrative judge's decision should also fully set out, again, his findings and conclusions on the existence and amount of the overpayment, and advise the appellant of his right of Board and judicial review.
