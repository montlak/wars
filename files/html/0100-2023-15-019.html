6. Evidence of Illness First Submitted to MSPB

Just as nothing in law or regulation restricts an agency to relying on its documentary administrative record or withholds from the agency a full and fair opportunity to cross-examine or otherwise rebut an appellant's evidence presented for the first time at the Board's hearing, circumstances of an AWOL appeal may justify appellant's submission of determinative medical evidence of illness for the first time at the MSPB hearing. The AWOL charge may still be sustained, despite medical evidence, if the appellant did not comply with procedures governing leave requests to justify a grant of sick leave in lieu of the AWOL charged against the employee. In Zeiss v. VA, 8 MSPR 15, 17-19, 7 MSPB 516 (1981) (clarified in Nash v. USPS, Keesler v. Dept. of Transp., and Young v. USPS, post), the only charge was AWOL, coupled with a past disciplinary record. The employee produced evidence of incapacitation for the first time at the Board hearing and the Board sustained the dismissal on what appeared to be an implied charge of failing to follow procedures governing leave requests:

The Board subsequently reopened the case on its own motion, pursuant to 5 CFR § 1201.117, to address the issue of "whether, in an AWOL based adverse action, an employee on appeal to the Board may rely upon medical evidence of illness not submitted to the agency as a defense to the action."...

The agency argues that pursuant to OPM regulations and the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM), an agency is granted the authority and responsibility of determining an employee's entitlement to sick leave. Therefore, it contends that allowing an appellant to rely on evidence not previously submitted to the agency would frustrate the intent of the regulations and put the Board in the position of determining the propriety of leave requests. OPM contends that the proffered evidence is relevant only to the question of the propriety of the penalty chosen by the agency. It also argues that appellant's evidence, consciously withheld from the agency, cannot properly be considered by the Board.

This Board has consistently rejected the notion that its scope of review is limited to that of appellate courts or to consideration of the administrative record established before the agency. Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPB 343, 349-50, 6 MSPR 404, 412-14 (1981); Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313, 315-18, 5 MSPR 280, 286-90 (1981); Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 MSPB 489, 497-99, 1 MSPR 505, 518-19 (1980); Toppi v. Office of Personnel Management, 2 MSPB 360, 2 MSPR 193 (1980). This Board is mandated by 5 USC § 7701(a) and (b) to conduct a hearing if requested by appellant, and to consider de novo all the relevant evidence presented by both parties, whether offered at a hearing or transmitted as part of the administrative record. Nothing in the law or in our regulations restricts an agency to reliance upon its documentary, administrative record, or withholds from that agency a full and fair opportunity to cross examine or otherwise rebut an appellant's evidence presented for the first time at the Board's hearing. See Chavez, supra, 6 MSPB 350-51, 6 MSPR 414. We therefore find that an employee in an AWOL based adverse action, as in any other adverse action, may rely upon medical evidence of illness not previously submitted to the agency as a defense to the action.

The Board will now examine the relevant evidence to determine if the presiding official erred in holding that the evidence presented by appellant for the first time at the hearing did not warrant reversal or reconsideration of the agency decision.

On May 9, 1979, appellant informed the agency the he was sick. He did not contact the agency again during the relevant period. Upon returning to work on May 30, 1979, he informed the agency that he had been ill. Appellant was told to bring in a certificate from a doctor to substantiate the illness. He was also given a notice of proposed removal but the personnel office notified him that the proposal would be rescinded upon production of a doctor's certificate. By the date of the removal decision appellant had neither furnished the required medical evidence nor replied to the notice of proposed removal. For the first time at the hearing before the presiding official, appellant claimed that his absence was due to drug addiction withdrawal symptoms. His psychiatrist testified in support of this contention and stated that appellant was unable to work during the period of his absence. The presiding official sustained the agency action finding that, in light of the advice and opportunity given to appellant to submit medical evidence, the late submission of such evidence at the hearing did not warrant reconsideration of the agency decision.

Under 5 CFR § 630.401 an agency is required to grant sick leave in certain situations, including where, as here, an employee is incapacitated for the performance of his duties due to sickness. However, section 630.402 requires that an employee apply for sick leave within the time limits set by the agency, and section 630.403 provides that the agency may grant a request for sick leave only when such request is supported by administratively acceptable evidence. Jackson v. Department of the Navy, 5 MSPB 456, 5 MSPR 45 (1981). The latter section also requires that if an absence lasts longer than three days, the agency may require, if it determines necessary, that an employee provide a medical certificate substantiating the reason for the absence.

The record reveals that appellant was notified numerous times between the date of his return and the date of the removal decision that in order to be granted sick leave and have the notice of proposed removal rescinded, it was necessary for him to produce a medical certificate explaining his absence. Appellant did not provide the agency with documentation of his illness by the date his removal decision was issued. The agency's charge of AWOL for 96 hours was a proper application of its procedures. See also Garrison v. U.S. Postal Service, 4 MSPB 173, 4 MSPR 87 (1980).

Although there may be circumstances where in an AWOL based adverse action submission of medical evidence of illness for the first time on appeal may be determinative of the outcome, we find that, in the instant case, the presiding official did not err in determining that the evidence presented by appellant did not warrant reversal or reconsideration of the agency decision.

Nash v. USPS, 8 MSPR 307, 310 n.10 (1981), helped to clarify the distinction between disciplinary action sustained even though medical evidence of sickness is received at the Board hearing and a case that should instead be based upon failure to comply with rules or regulations governing the submission of documentation. The distinction in Nash appeared to be drawn between the employee who fails to produce evidence to the agency prior to its decision on the adverse action and the individual who provides the information, belatedly, but before the agency decision is made:

Cf. Zeiss v. Veterans Administration, 7 MSPB 516, 8 MSPR 15 (1981). In that case the Board held that although there may be circumstances where in an AWOL based adverse action the submission of medical evidence of illness for the first time on appeal may be determinative of the outcome, the presiding official did not err in determining that medical evidence presented by the appellant in support of a sick leave request for the first time on appeal did not warrant a reversal or reconsideration of the agency's decision to remove him for AWOL. The Board based its holding in Zeiss on findings that: (1) the agency told the appellant to bring in a certificate from a doctor to substantiate his alleged illness for 88 hours upon his return to work; (2) the appellant was also given a notice of proposed removal which the personnel office informed him would be rescinded upon production of a doctor's certificate, and (3) by the date of the removal decision appellant had neither furnished the required medical evidence nor replied to the notice of proposed removal.

In the instant case, although appellant failed to submit the doctor's certificate in support of his illness promptly upon his return to duty (and in violation of the agency's sick leave procedures), he did submit the medical evidence prior to the agency's decision to remove him and the agency erred in refusing to consider it. If the agency desired to discipline the appellant for his untimely submission, the proper procedure would not have been to propose[ ] his removal for AWOL on the dates he was ill, but to charge him with disobeying agency directives and propose disciplinary action on that ground.

Keesler v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 27, 28 n.1 (1983), qualifying Zeiss, further clarified the right of an appellant to present medical evidence for the first time to the Board in response to an AWOL-related adverse action:

Although the agency contends that the presiding official erred by considering the appellant's testimony and medical evidence of illness submitted for the first time on appeal, the Board has determined that an employee in an adverse action based on unauthorized absence, as in any other adverse action, may rely upon medical evidence of illness not previously submitted to the agency as a defense to the action. See Bell v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 15 MSPR 491 at 493 (1983); Nunes v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 12 MSPB 20 at 21 (1982); Zeiss v. Veterans Administration, 7 MSPB 516, 517 (1981). Appellant failed to present his evidence of illness at the agency level in response to the removal proposal, but his properly admissible evidence established at the hearing that he was incapacitated for duty and timely requested sick leave on the date on which he was charged with strike participation. Appellant's evidence rebutted the prima facie case of strike participation. See Bangerter v. Department of Transportation, 16 MSPR 670 at 695 (1983). Unlike the situation in Zeiss, 7 MSPB at 518, the medical evidence of illness submitted for the first time to the presiding official in the instant case is of sufficient weight to be determinative of the outcome....

Young v. USPS, 79 MSPR 25, 32 (1998), sustaining removal for some AWOL, reiterated the law concerning presentation of evidence of incapacitation:

A charge of AWOL will not be sustained if the appellant presents evidence to the Board that was not previously presented to the agency showing that she was incapacitated for duty during the relevant time period. See, e.g., Bishopp v. Department of the Air Force, 75 MSPR 33, 39 (1997). If an employee has sufficient sick leave to cover the period in question, the agency must grant the leave request when the employee has provided administratively acceptable evidence of incapacitation because of illness or injury, regardless of whether the employee has complied with applicable leave procedures. See, e.g., Patterson v. Department of the Air Force, 74 MSPR 648, 652 (1997).

See Thom v. Dept. of Army, 114 MSPR 169, 172 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 110 (2010) ("Even if the appellant failed to provide the September 16, 2005 medical certificate to the agency, we may properly consider it on review. See Wesley v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 277, ¶ 18 (2003) ('A charge of AWOL will not be sustained if the appellant presents evidence to the Board that was not previously presented to the agency showing that he was incapacitated for duty during the relevant time period.')"; still sustaining removal when the documentation did not adequately justify the absence).

If the appellant was warned in a leave restriction letter that he should first have submitted evidence justifying sick leave to the agency but he did not produce that evidence until the Board hearing, the discipline for AWOL is likely to be sustained. Morris v. Dept. of Air Force, 30 MSPR 343, 345-46 (1986) (treating the leave restriction as a warning demonstrating under Zeiss that the AWOL charge was reasonable). Kulinski v. USPS, 37 MSPR 574, 577-78 (1988), accepted medical evidence offered during the hearing to explain AWOL but found it insufficient to justify the absence:

If, at the time in question, the appellant's mental condition was such that it adversely affected his ability to comply with the agency's instructions, then the AWOL charge was not properly sustained. See Villanueva v. United States Postal Service, 26 M.S.P.R. 534, 536-37 (1985) (agency did not prove its charge of absence without leave where employee presented unrebutted medical evidence that his attendance problems were directly caused by a post-traumatic stress condition). On review of the meager medical evidence in this case, we find that it is insufficient to establish that, during the period he was charged AWOL, the appellant's mental condition prevented him from securing the medical examination that would have constituted compliance with the agency's instructions. Without evidence to show the nature and extent of the appellant's illness at the time he was charged AWOL, and how it affected him, we cannot find that he was unable, by virtue of his mental condition, to comply with the agency's instructions. See Mayers v. Government of the District of Columbia, 21 M.S.P.R. 144, 146 (1984) (failure of medical evidence proffered by employee to cover all dates of his absence would, without more, support decision to sustain employee's removal for absence without leave). We find, therefore, that the agency's charge of AWOL was proper.

See Grubb v. Bowman v. Bowman v. SBA, 122 MSPR 217, 222-25 ¶¶ 10-11, 13-14, 2015 MSPB 18 (2015) (Member Robbins dissenting), reaffirmed the significance of medical evidence first offered to the Board in an appeal involving AWOL and failure to following leave reporting procedures:

There is some support in the cases relied on by the administrative judge for the proposition that mitigating factors based on an appellant's medical condition will be considered only if they were made known to the agency before the adverse action was effected. For example, in Roseman, 76 M.S.P.R. at 345, the Board stated that evidence that an employee's medical condition or mental impairment played a part in the charged conduct is ordinarily entitled to considerable weight as a mitigating factor. The Board held that, even where the condition does not rise to the level of a disability, "if the agency knew about it before taking the action at issue, we may consider the condition in determining the appropriate penalty." Id. (emphasis added). The Board reiterated this statement in Woebcke, 114 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 15.

Despite the above-quoted language, any doubt about the relevance of the appellant's testimony regarding the connection between his mental condition and the absences which formed the basis for the charges in this matter was eliminated by our reviewing court in Norris v. Securities & Exchange Commission, 675 F.3d 1349 (Fed. Cir. 2012). In that case, the court held that an arbitrator erred by stating that the issue before him was whether the penalty was within tolerable limits of reasonableness "based upon the facts and circumstances known to [the deciding official] at the time." Id. at 1352, 1357 (emphasis in original). Emphasizing the de novo nature of Board proceedings, the court held that where new evidence supporting mitigation of the penalty is presented to the Board, the evidence must be considered in determining whether the agency's penalty was reasonable. Id. at 1355-57. Accordingly, we find that the administrative judge properly considered the appellant's hearing testimony that his depression played a part in his absences prior to his hospitalization on December 15, 2012.

...As the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit observed in Reilly v. Office of Personnel Management, 571 F.3d 1372, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2009), "[t]he field of forensic medicine abounds with examples of subsequent medical documentation relevant to a prior condition. As a classic example, inferences about prior intoxication can be drawn from blood alcohol tests conducted at a later time." Id. Other courts have reached the same conclusion in analogous contexts. See Pollard v. Halter, 377 F.3d 183, 193-94 (2d Cir. 2004) (finding in the context of a case involving Social Security disability benefits that "evidence bearing upon an applicant's condition subsequent to the date upon which the earning requirement...was last met is pertinent evidence in that it may disclose the severity and continuity of impairments existing before the earning requirement date") (internal quotation omitted); Ivy v. Sullivan, 898 F.2d 1045, 1049 (5th Cir. 1990) (recognizing that "noncontemporaneous medical records are relevant to the determination of whether onset occurred on the date alleged by the claimant" in the context of Social Security disability benefits); Smith v. Bowen, 849 F.2d 1222, 1225 (9th Cir. 1988) (noting that "[i]t is obvious that medical reports are inevitably rendered retrospectively and should not be disregarded solely on that basis"); Basinger v. Heckler, 725 F.2d 1166, 1169 (8th Cir. 1984) (finding that "medical evidence of a claimant's condition subsequent to the expiration of the claimant's insured status is relevant evidence because it may bear upon the severity of the claimant's condition before the expiration of his or her insured status"); Beckham v. United States, 392 F.2d 619, 625 (Ct. Cl. 1968) (determining that "we must look to manifestations of the disease or ailment appearing both before and after the point in time of separation" to determine fitness at the time of separation).

Where, as here, proximity in time, testimony, or other evidence provides the requisite link to the relevant period, the subsequent medical evidence can be very probative of a prior medical condition. See Reilly, 571 F.3d at 1382. Here, the medical evidence in the record corroborates the appellant's testimony that his mental illness played a significant part in his absence. See Allen v. Department of the Army, 76 M.S.P.R. 564, 568 (1997) (accepting evidence from a clinical psychologist supporting a psychological illness and related absence).

Evidence submitted to the Board must be persuasive.
