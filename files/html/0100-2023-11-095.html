d. Jurisdictional Issue

Board law becomes confused or impenetrable in the application of res judicata and collateral estoppel to jurisdictional issues. Res judicata is supposed to apply to cases previously adjudicated on the merits. Because the Board, contrary to the guidance of the Federal Circuit, occasionally confuses the inability to prove a claim with the failure to invoke Board jurisdiction--particularly in cases involving constructive adverse actions and IRA appeals--analysis of res judicata becomes muddled as the Board attempts to avoid relitigation of cases that it dismisses on jurisdictional grounds but in effect were dismissed because the appellant failed to prove a claim.

Res judicata and collateral estoppel are not bases for dismissing an appeal for lack of jurisdiction; rather, they are bases for dismissing an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction. Mycka v. OPM, 56 MSPR 675, 679 (1993); see Merzweiler v. OPM, 100 MSPR 442, 445 ¶ 7 (2005) (res judicata and collateral estoppel are bases to dismiss an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction, but with limited exceptions those doctrines are not appropriate bases for dismissal of an appeal when jurisdiction is lacking) Martinez v. USPS, 88 MSPR 356, 358-59 ¶ 6 (2001) (stating rule); Besemer v. USPS, 77 MSPR 260, 263 (1998) (stating rule); cf. Roesel v. Peace Corps, 111 MSPR 366, 372 ¶ 15, 2009 MSPB 90 (2009) ("res judicata is not a basis to dismiss an appeal for lack of jurisdiction"); Hicks v. USPS, 83 MSPR 599, 604 ¶ 12 (1999) (when a prior appeal was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, there was no examination of the merits of the agency's action). Res judicata is not a jurisdictional bar to repetitive litigation; if not timely raised it is waived. Castle v. Dept. of Treasury, 68 MSPR 417, 423 n.6 (1995); cf. Alford v. OPM, DC-844E-13-0283-I-1 (NP 2/4/2014) ("Collateral estoppel may be grounds for dismissing an appeal for lack of jurisdiction only where the decision being given preclusive effect was itself based on a lack of jurisdiction. Noble v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 693, ¶ 11 (2003).").

If a prior appeal is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, it does not serve to bar a subsequent appeal through res judicata, Abou-Hussein v. Dept. of Navy, AT-1221-11-0850-W-1 (NP 8/1/2013):

Additionally, the administrative judge erred in determining that the doctrine of res judicata applies and bars the appellant from re-litigating his claims that the agency made death threats, targeted his livelihood and employability, and made false allegations accusing him of espionage, terrorist connections, substance abuse, and having a mental disorder. Under the doctrine of res judicata (also known as "claim preclusion"), a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action. Encarnado v. Office of Personnel Management, 116 M.S.P.R. 301, ¶ 10 (2011). Res judicata precludes parties from re-litigating issues that were, or could have been, raised in the prior action and is applicable if: (1) the prior judgment was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior judgment was a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases. Id.

Contrary to the administrative judge's findings, the Board has not issued a final judgment on the merits regarding the aforementioned claims. The administrative judge who presided over the 2009 IRA appeal dismissed the claims for lack of jurisdiction, and the Board affirmed those findings in its April 4, 2012 Nonprecedential Final Order. ...A dismissal for lack of jurisdiction is not a judgment on the merits.

Cf. Kruise v. Dept. of Army, 721 Fed. Appx. 985, 986-87 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) ("We have recognized that res judicata should not bar a later claim when the forum in the prior action lacked jurisdiction to grant the requested relief. Cunningham v. United States, 748 F.3d 1172, 1179-81 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (holding res judicata did not bar a breach of contract claim for damages where that remedy was not available in prior action).").

But as to a prior finding of lack of jurisdiction, collateral estoppel may prevent relitigation of the claim, Armas v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 244, 248-49 (1996) (IRA appeal):

The administrative judge found that the appellant's claims that his transfer and his restoration to duty were otherwise appealable actions were precluded by res judicata. The decision on which he relied was one in which he had dismissed the appellant's appeal for lack of jurisdiction. (Docket No. SF-1221-95-0411-W-1). The Board has found that res judicata is not applicable to a prior decision that dismissed an appeal for lack of jurisdiction. See Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 338 (1995). Nevertheless, the doctrine of collateral estoppel may preclude further consideration of an issue if: (1) The issue previously adjudicated is identical to that now presented; (2) that issue was actually litigated in the prior case; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded by the doctrine was fully represented in the prior case. See id. at 341.

Here, these four criteria are met with respect to the administrative judge's determination that the appellant's transfer and his restoration to duty are not otherwise appealable actions. Accordingly, although the basis for the administrative judge's decision should have been collateral estoppel rather than res judicata, his error in this regard did not affect the appellant's substantive rights. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984) (an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision).

Kmiecik v. USDA, CH-0752-10-0055-I-1 (NP 6/10/2010), summarized the preclusive effect of collateral estoppel as to prior jurisdictional determinations:

The appellant argues on review that the second collateral estoppel factor is not present because "[T]he initial decision was not fully litigated as the only question addressed [in the previous appeal] was jurisdictional." Unlike res judicata, however, a final decision on the merits is not necessary for a finding of collateral estoppel. Compare Carson v. Department of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (res judicata only applies when there was a prior decision on the merits), with Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 341 (1995) (a decision that is not on the merits may have collateral estoppel effect). Prior litigation of a jurisdictional issue has collateral estoppel effect. See, e.g., Coats, 111 M.S.P.R. 268, ¶ 8; Sessions v. Department of the Army, 74 M.S.P.R. 343, 347-48 (1997). A review of the initial decision in the previous appeal shows that the voluntariness issue was fully litigated, i.e., the administrative judge made a finding that the appellant's resignation was voluntary. Thus, the administrative judge in the instant appeal correctly found that the second collateral estoppel factor is present.

See Sanders v. Dept. of Treasury, SF-315H-14-0540-I-1 (NP 1/23/2015) ("Under certain circumstances, such as those presented here, an appellant who has failed to establish Board jurisdiction in one appeal may be collaterally estopped from establishing jurisdiction in a subsequent appeal based on the same underlying cause of action. See Davis v. U.S. Postal Service, 119 M.S.P.R. 22, ¶ 16 (2012); see also Metallo v. Department of Defense, 110 M.S.P.R. 229, ¶ 12 (2008) (finding that the appellant was collaterally estopped from relitigating the Board's jurisdiction over her restoration appeal because the Board's jurisdiction over that claim was actually litigated in a prior appeal, which was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction); Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 338, 341 (1995) (a decision that is not on the merits could be given collateral estoppel effect)."); Coats v. USPS, 111 MSPR 268, 273 ¶ 8, 2009 MSPB 82 (2009) ("A dismissal for lack of jurisdiction would generally preclude a second action in the same forum under the doctrine of collateral estoppel, which would preclude relitigation of the same jurisdictional issue. Peartree, 66 M.S.P.R. at 338."); Campbell v. USPS, 88 MSPR 546, 549 ¶ 6 (2001) ("A jurisdictional determination may also be afforded collateral estoppel effect, precluding relitigation of the same jurisdictional issue."); cf. Blees v. USPS, 96 MSPR 628, 629-37 (2004) (Separate Opinions of Acting Chairman McPhie and Member Marshall consider when jurisdictional determinations have preclusive effect through collateral estoppel or res judicata), on compliance recommendation, Blees v. USPS, 97 MSPR 657 (2004). But cf. Harris v. EEOC, DC-0752-15-0733-I-1 (NP 2/29/2016) (WIGI denial: "Collateral estoppel may be grounds for dismissing an appeal for lack of jurisdiction if a jurisdictional determination in a prior decision is afforded collateral estoppel effect and the appellant provides no other valid basis of Board jurisdiction. Noble v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 693, ¶ 11 (2003). Here, the issue that was actually litigated in the prior appeal was whether the Board had jurisdiction over the appellant's WIGI appeal while his reconsideration request was still pending before the agency. However, the agency subsequently issued a reconsideration decision. Accordingly, we find that collateral estoppel does not apply to this case. See Coats v. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 268, ¶ 8 (2009) (finding that collateral estoppel did not apply to bar jurisdiction over the appellant's Veterans Employment Opportunities Act of 1998 claim where the issue actually previously litigated regarding jurisdiction in the previous appeal was whether his prior complaint was timely filed with the Department of Labor (DOL) and the current appeal involved a subsequently filed DOL complaint).").

The Federal Circuit explained the intersection of the two doctrines of preclusion in appeals involving jurisdictional issues, Jackson v. VA, 97 Fed. Appx. 297, 299-300 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP):

"Under [the doctrine of] res judicata, a final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action." Allen v. McCurry, 449 U.S. 90, 94, 101 S. Ct. 411, 66 L. Ed.2d 308 (1980). Because Jackson's prior involuntary resignation and job restoration claims were not adjudicated on the merits, but were dismissed in the prior appeals for lack of jurisdiction, those dismissals may not constitute a basis for precluding all claims that could have been raised in those appeals under res judicata. See Fed.R.Civ.P. 41(b) (an involuntary dismissal for lack of jurisdiction is not an "adjudication on the merits"). However, the error is harmless because Jackson cannot attempt once again to revisit the jurisdiction issue simply by urging the same arguments, merely reworded. The Board previously dismissed Jackson's involuntary resignation and job restoration claims for lack of jurisdiction based on Jackson's failure to show that her retirement was involuntary or that she suffered a compensable injury. Jackson v. Dept. of Veterans Affairs, No. SF-0752-98-0070-I-2, slip op. at 5-6 (M.S.P.B. July 9, 1998) (involuntary resignation claim); Jackson v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, No. SF-0353-01-0167-I-1, slip op. at 2-3 (M.S.P.B. Apr. 23, 2001) (job restoration claim). These determinations are entitled to preclusive effect under the doctrine of collateral estoppel. "Under collateral estoppel, once a court has decided an issue of fact or law necessary to its judgment, that decision may preclude relitigation of the issue in a suit on a different cause of action involving a party to the first case." Allen, 449 U.S. at 94. Although the Board used the term "res judicata" in characterizing its dismissal of Jackson's involuntary resignation and job restoration claims, rather than employing the more precise term "collateral estoppel," the Board's dismissal was nonetheless made on the correct ground. The term "res judicata" is often generically used to encompass both issue preclusion (or collateral estoppel) and claim preclusion. Migra v. Warren City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Educ., 465 U.S. 75, 77 n.1, 104 S. Ct. 892, 79 L. Ed.2d 56 (1984) (noting that in its generic sense, "res judicata" is a broad term that encompasses both issue preclusion, sometimes referred to as collateral estoppel, and claim preclusion, sometimes referred to as res judicata). The Board properly applied the doctrine of issue preclusion, recognizing that this doctrine precludes Jackson's attempted relitigation of these claims following the Board's prior decisions on the underlying issues.

Dooley v. VA, 306 Fed. Appx. 594, 596 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP), strongly implied that if the Board lacks jurisdiction over an appeal, it may not dismiss a subsequent appeal through res judicata:

Notwithstanding this error by the Board, the DVA asks this court to affirm the Board's dismissal. The DVA correctly points out that the Board not only would have lacked jurisdiction over Dooley's First Amendment claim if he had brought it in his earlier appeals, but likewise lacked jurisdiction over Dooley's First Amendment claim in this case. Because Dooley's employment was temporary, he was not an "employee" for purposes of 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a). The Board therefore lacks jurisdiction to hear any appeal concerning Dooley's termination, other than an IRA whistleblower claim. See, e.g., Anderson v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 12 F.3d 1069, 1070-72 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (holding that Board lacks jurisdiction over appeals by temporary employees challenging termination). Both in the initial case and in this case, the Board lacked jurisdiction to render any judgment on Dooley's First Amendment claim--including a judgment dismissing that claim on res judicata grounds. "If it lacks jurisdiction, the Board is without authority to decide the issues presented by a petitioner. The [Board] has only the jurisdiction conferred on it by Congress. This jurisdiction, provided by statute, creates the power of the [Board] to hear and decide a case, i.e., the Board's subject matter jurisdiction. Without jurisdiction, the Board's decision on the merits of a petition is a nullity." Schmittling v. Dep't of Army, 219 F.3d 1332, 1337 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (citations and internal quotation marks omitted).

We decline to affirm a judgment that the Board lacked jurisdiction to enter, and instead we vacate the Board's judgment so that the Board can dismiss Dooley's petition for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. See, e.g., Pittman v. Dep't of Justice, 486 F.3d 1276, 1282 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (vacating and remanding with instructions to dismiss claim when Board acted without subject matter jurisdiction). We therefore vacate and remand with instructions that the Board dismiss for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.

If, however, there was a prior jurisdictional decision, that decision is res judicata as to a case involving that same jurisdictional issue--or at least that is what the Board appeared to suggest in Caple v. USPS, 52 MSPR 262, 265-66 (1992):

The doctrine of res judicata provides that matters that were settled in a prior appeal in which there has been a final decision are precluded from being brought before the Board in a subsequent appeal. West v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 MSPR 551, 561 (1990); Van Sant v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 MSPR 408, 412, review dismissed, 889 F.2d 1099 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table). The appellant argues that res judicata cannot apply in this case because there has never been a final decision on the merits of his reprisal claim. We disagree. The Supreme Court has repeatedly stated that the principle of res judicata applies to questions of jurisdiction. See, e.g., Underwriters National Assurance v. North Carolina Life and Accident and Health Insurance Guaranty Association, 455 U.S. 691, 706, 102 S.C. 1357, 1366, 71 L. Ed.2d 558 (1982); Durfee v. Duke, 375 U.S. 106, 111, 84 S.C. 242, 244, 11 L. Ed.2d 186 (1963); American Surety v. Baldwin, 287 U.S. 156, 166, 53 S.C. 98, 101, 77 LED. 231 (1932).

The Board has also applied the doctrine of res judicata to questions of jurisdiction. See Schoening v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 MSPR 6 (1989). Because the need for finality of decisions applies with equal force to jurisdictional decisions as it does to decisions on the merits, there is no reason not to apply the doctrine of res judicata to jurisdictional findings by the Board. See Underwriters National Assurance, 455 U.S. at 706 n.13, 102 S.C. at 1366 n.13.

Here, the Board issued its final decision on the question of whether the agency's action in the fall of 1990 was taken in reprisal for the appellant's prior EEO complaint. MSPB Docket No. DC07529110148 (Initial Decision Feb. 6, 1991), petition for review denied, 49 MSPR 420 (1991) (Table). The Board found that it was not and that, because of the previous settlement agreement, the appeal was outside the Board's jurisdiction. Id. The appellant now seeks to have the Board reexamine that decision. The doctrine of res judicata precludes us from doing so.

The agency's decision to process the appellant's claim of reprisal as a non-mixed case does not alter this finding. Regardless of how the claim is processed, the Board has issued its final decision on the appellant's allegation of reprisal, and we will not reexamine the matter. Although the agency's labor relations manager stated in a letter to the appellant's representative that the agency's decision should render the appeal before the Board moot, the Board must independently determine the decision's effect. Here we find that the doctrine of res judicata barred the Board from hearing the appellant's appeal, and, thus, the appeal was properly dismissed as outside the Board's jurisdiction.

Batiste v. USPS, 100 MSPR 369, 373-74 ¶¶ 11-13 (2005), applied res judicata and collateral estoppel to prevent litigation of a jurisdictional issue that was actually litigated in an earlier appeal:

...Where a first action was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, as it was here, the doctrine of res judicata will not normally preclude the appellant from bringing the same action again. A second action in the same forum, however, might be barred by the doctrine of collateral estoppel, which precludes relitigation of the same jurisdictional issue. Peartree, 66 M.S.P.R. at 338; see also Tanner v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 10 (2003). When he amended his petition for appeal, it appears that the appellant was indeed seeking to relitigate the jurisdictional issue. We thus examine whether collateral estoppel applies here.

Collateral estoppel is appropriate when (1) an issue is identical to that involved in the prior action, (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action, (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment, and (4) the party to be precluded was fully represented in the prior action. Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988). The issue of constructive suspension meets this definition. First, the issue here is identical to the one involved in the prior action. In the earlier appeal, the appellant had asked the Board to deem the entire period between January 2003 and August 2004 to be a constructive suspension. Batiste I at 2. In the instant appeal, the appellant sought below to raise constructive suspension as one of several reasons for the Board to recognize jurisdiction over his restoration appeal. On PFR, however, his focus shifts, and now he is simply arguing that the Board should deem his period of voluntary unemployment between January 22 and March 19, 2003, to be a constructive suspension. The time periods for the alleged constructive suspension fully overlap in the two claims. In our view, the appellant has presented in his PFR "the same set of events or documents and the same 'bundle of legal principles' that contributed to the rendering of the first judgment," Tanner, 94 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 11, and thus, the issue is identical.

Second, the jurisdictional issue was actually litigated in the earlier appeal. The appellant requested a jurisdictional hearing, which was held, and the AJ rendered a decision. Batiste I; see Fisher v. Department of Defense, 64 M.S.P.R. 509, 514 (1994) (the "actually litigated" criterion requires that the issue be contested by the parties and resolved by an adjudicator). Third, the Board's determination that it lacked jurisdiction in the earlier appeal was its sole justification for dismissing the first appeal, i.e., it was necessary to the final judgment. Batiste I. Fourth, the appellant, though pro se, was fully represented in the earlier appeal. See Noble v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 693, ¶ 9 (2003) (citing Fisher, 64 M.S.P.R. at 515) ("a party's pro se status does not preclude the application of collateral estoppel; the 'fully represented' requirement is satisfied when the party to whom collateral estoppel is applied has had a full and fair chance to litigate the issue in question"). For these reasons, the Board finds that the appellant's claim of constructive suspension is barred by collateral estoppel....

The Federal Circuit declined to become involved in formal distinctions between "merits" and "jurisdictional" adjudications when res judicata was raised as a bar to an IRA case, essentially holding in Vanderford v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3278), that a "jurisdictional" dismissal that is based on factual determinations is really a "merits" determination that precludes relitigation:

This case has been here before. Mr. Vanderford previously brought an individual right of action appeal to the Board, arguing that his ten-day suspension was an illegal reprisal for an alleged protected disclosure. In response, the Board held that Mr. Vanderford did not have a reasonable belief that his disclosure was protected, see Vanderford v. Department of the Navy, No. SF-1221-95-0758-W-1 (Sept. 18, 1995), and dismissed his appeal. Mr. Vanderford petitioned for review in this court. We affirmed the Board's dismissal. See Vanderford v. Department of the Navy, 86 F.3d 1178, 1996 WL 252838 (Fed. Cir. 1996).

Mr. Vanderford thereafter filed another complaint with the Office of Special Counsel, arguing that his ten-day suspension was an unlawful reprisal for the disclosures previously held not to be protected. Not surprisingly, the Office of Special Counsel closed that complaint on May 5, 1997, stating that Mr. Vanderford's complaint had already been adjudicated by the Board and this court. Mr. Vanderford again repaired to the Board, duplicating his previous appeal. The agency moved to dismiss the second appeal to the Board on the ground of res judicata because the parties and issues were the same as in the previously decided case. The Board granted the agency's motion, and Mr. Vanderford returned to this court with his petition for review.

Res judicata prevents a party from litigating again against a same party issues that previously were or could have been litigated in a proceeding that has come to final judgment. See Federated Dep't Stores, Inc. v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 398 (1981). The doctrine applies to administrative decisions, see Astoria Fed. Say. & Loan Ass'n v. Solimino, 501 U.S. 104, 107-08 (1990), and the Board enforces the doctrine in its decisions. See, e.g., Peartree v. United States Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 337 (1995).

Notwithstanding the fact that this appeal raises precisely the same issues as previously adjudicated finally against Mr. Vanderford, Mr. Vanderford argues that the Board has the authority to reopen his first appeal of the ten-day suspension, and that the Board abused its authority in this case by refusing to reopen the first case and by dismissing the second case on res judicata grounds.

We have considered and reject Mr. Vanderford's arguments that res judicata does not bar his second appeal to the Board from his ten-day suspension. We note that in Mr. Vanderford's first appeal to the Board, the Board stated that it dismissed Mr. Vanderford's appeal for lack of jurisdiction. However, the Board reached its decision by determining that Mr. Vanderford had made no protected disclosures and as such the Board's first decision was a determination upon the merits, rather than a jurisdictional ruling. See Barton v. Department of Justice, 985 F.2d 547, 548-49 (Fed. Cir. 1993). In any event, "dismissals for lack of jurisdiction may be given res judicata effect as to the jurisdictional issue." Amgen Inc. v. United States International Trade Comm'n, 902 F.2d 1532, 1536 n.5 (Fed. Cir. 1990). Thus, whether the Board's first decision is characterized as on the merits or jurisdictional, the result is the same. The Board's decision to dismiss Mr. Vanderford's petition for review is correct.

See Cobb v. VA, 27 MSPR 187, 188 (1985) (unappealed finding of a district court that the Board had jurisdiction over an appeal from improper restoration to duty following military service was res judicata and immune from collateral attack during further Board consideration of the case). But see Tisdale v. Dept. of Treasury, 44 MSPR 390, 393-94 (1990) (res judicata applies to cases involving the same parties and issues; the doctrine did not bar a second decision on the merits of a restoration appeal when the first appeal on the matter was dismissed as premature; the merits determination differed from the timeliness determination).
