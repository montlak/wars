broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section H: Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals, Subsection 04: Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ("DWOP"), Part a: Reasons for DWOP
a. Reasons for DWOP

Examples of DWOPs abound.

Appeals are dismissed without prejudice to permit the parties to pursue discovery. See Tierney v. DOJ, 89 MSPR 354, 355 ¶ 3 (2001); Christofili v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 384, 387 ¶ 3 (1999); Sheehan v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 204, 206, 207-08 (1990); cf. Leonard v. USPS, AT-0752-13-0196-I-1 (NP 4/17/2014) (appeal should have been DWOP'd rather than dismissed for lack of jurisdiction when the appellant was seeking information from the military concerning his serve record to demonstrate that he was a preference eligible entitled to appeal rights). An appeal was dismissed without prejudice to permit an agency to obtain the testimony of a "key witness" and to complete discovery. Roth v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 395, 396 n.3 (1990) (not stating whether the dismissal was at the agency's request or sua sponte by the AJ after being informed of the agency's concerns).

An AJ used the procedure to permit the parties to explore settlement. See Shelby v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 279, 281 (1991). The appellant's imminent incarceration justified a DWOP in Williams v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 427, 430, 4 MSPB 479 (1980) (agency made an insufficient showing of the unavailability of witnesses or evidence to demonstrate that it would be prejudiced by the dismissal). Over the appellant's objection, an AJ sua sponte dismissed a case without prejudice to permit the time to analyze new Board decisions that the AJ felt were of significance in the IRA appeal. King v. Dept. of Army, 84 MSPR 235, 237 ¶¶ 4-6 (1999). Although the "study time" dismissal in King did not result in recusal of the AJ, as the appellant requested, the Board's acceptance of the AJ's action suggests that the Board will accept nearly any reason for a DWOP.

An AJ properly employed the dismissal during an appellant's lengthy convalescence. Toma v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 408, 410, 5 MSPB 420 (1981) (AJ set out a timetable for review of the appellant's medical ability to proceed with the appeal); see Ayers v. NASA, 80 MSPR 550, 552 ¶ 3 (1999) (appeal dismissed without prejudice based on appellant's continuing medical treatments); Zell v. Dept. of Army, 57 MSPR 86, 88 (1993) (medical condition hindered the appellant's ability to proceed with the appeal; the AJ's order concerning jurisdictional issues was vacated until the appellant could refile and reargue them); Rodriguez v. DOD, 38 MSPR 202, 203 (1988) (appellant's medical condition interfered with his ability to attend the hearing and assist counsel with preparation of the appeal); Padilla v. Dept. of Air Force, 58 MSPR 561, 563 (1993) (dismissal for six months for the appellant to recover from a medical disability (citing Biberstine v. DODDS, 37 MSPR 248, 254 (1988), for the proposition that when a party requests a continuance that is both lengthy and indefinite, the AJ may dismiss the appeal without prejudice to refiling)); Keene v. Dept. of Interior, 47 MSPR 41, 44 (1991) (DWOP resulting from the medical inability of the appellant to proceed immediately with the appeal "is a procedural option committed to the sound discretion of the administrative judge"); Poudrier v. OPM, 18 MSPR 646, 647 (1984) (AJ correctly dismissed the appeal without prejudice to refiling rather than continuing the case for eight weeks; the appellant's medical condition created uncertainty as to when he could proceed with the appeal).

The DWOP delays adjudication while a related litigated or nonlitigated matter proceeds. An AJ used the procedure to take appellant's case off the Board's docket while EEOC considered the appeal of a decision that would result in appellant's retroactive promotion, with an impact upon the WIG appeal. Reitan v. Architectural & Transp. Barriers Bd, 18 MSPR 585, 586 (1984); See Delaney v. AID, 87 MSPR 688, 691-92 ¶ 7 (2001) (DWOP properly used to permit the parties to resolve issues pertaining to restoration of the appellant to status quo; objection by the appellant and delays in the resolution of restoration issues led the Board to remand the case for a hearing on the merits unless there was a settlement of outstanding matters). The DWOP was used to allow an appellant to await an OWCP decision before proceeding with her appeal in Sullivan-Brooks v. VA, 53 MSPR 405, 406 (1992). A DWOP delayed adjudication until the Board decided a legal issue in a pending case that would influence the analysis of the appellant's case. See Pham v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-13-0147-I-1 (NP 2/3/2014). A DWOP, over the appellant's objection, allowed time for OPM to rule on an agency-initiated passover request. Gingery v. Dept. of Treasury, 111 MSPR 134, 138 ¶ 9, 2009 MSPB 59 (2009). A DWOP was entered to permit completion of litigation of related reprisal allegations in another forum, the Department of Labor, in Jenkins v. EPA, DC-0752-11-0348-B-6 (NP 1/5/2016). An appeal was dismissed without prejudice to avoid a lengthy continuance while the Board's decision on PFR in another appeal would potentially dispose of issues common to the two cases. Hoback v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 315, 317 ¶ 5 (2001); cf. Moss v. Dept. of Air Force, 82 MSPR 309, 313 ¶ 11 (1999) (DWOP used to await result from the Federal Circuit in a related case); Satterfield v. USPS, 80 MSPR 132, 135-36 ¶ 6 (1998) (suggesting the desirability of a DWOP the OWCP considered a claim covering the period of absence leading to appeal); Hall v. OPM, 27 MSPR 176, 177 (1985) (DWOP proper in an annuity overpayment appeal to permit OPM to determine whether to grant a waiver of recovery).

A DWOP may condition refiling on events that could preclude refiling. The DWOP then effectively becomes a final settlement of the appeal. In Hardy v. USPS, 72 MSPR 71, 75 (1996), the appellant agreed his appeal would be dismissed without prejudice, that he would pursue disability retirement or a worker's compensation award, and that he would not pursue his Board appeal if he received one of those benefits. When he received an OWCP award, he was precluded from pursuing his affirmative defense of discrimination before the Board. But cf. Mojarro v. USPS, 115 MSPR 433, 435-37 ¶¶ 6-9, 2010 MSPB 225 (2010) (reopening an appeal that was DWOP'd pending action on a disability retirement application when the appellant determined he could not support the application and sought to pursue his appeal).

A DWOP may also be used with collateral components of an appeal, e.g., a counsel fee petition. See Gidwani v. VA, 74 MSPR 509, 511 (1997). A DWOP was used in an enforcement proceeding to permit the parties to further define and resolve outstanding issues. Paxton v. USPS, 82 MSPR 291, 293 ¶ 6 (1999) (citing Espinoza v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 679, 682 (1996), post, dismissing a petition to enforce settlement without prejudice to refiling to permit the parties to consult about and resolve outstanding issues without assistance from the AJ).
