1. Same or Equivalent Position

If the individual who has a right to priority consideration for reemployment is denied that right, under McKoy v. Dept. of Army, 18 MSPR 636, 640 (1984), the remedy is "to reinstate appellant to her former or an equivalent position, with all attendant benefits, retroactive to the date on which the agency first had an appropriate vacancy after appellant requested reemployment." If the employee receives disability retirement benefits and then shifts to workers compensation, at the terminus of the workers compensation, the employee has restoration rights to the same or an equivalent position under Metsopulos v. USPS, 33 MSPR 536, 540 (1987):

Throughout this appeal, the agency has maintained, for various reasons, that it had no obligation to give the appellant priority consideration for the position he once held or an equivalent one. Most telling is the agency's response to the appellant's request for restoration, in which the agency asserted its belief that it had no obligation to consider the appellant for a managerial position. This assertion was repeated in responding to the appellant's petition for appeal to the Board.

Reinstatement of the appellant in a distribution clerk position patently fails to satisfy the agency's obligation under 5 U.S.C. § 8151. In giving effect to an employee's right to priority consideration, the agency must make reasonable efforts to place the appellant in the same or equivalent position. Because of the agency's failure to consider the appellant for supervisory positions, we find that the agency failed to show that it made all reasonable efforts to place and afford appellant priority consideration in accordance with 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(2). See Phillips v. Department of Transportation, 22 MSPR 223 (1984) in a case where an appellant had occupied a supervisory position when he was separated due to a compensable injury, the agency's refusal to consider the appellant, whose compensation benefits had been terminated, for a supervisory position violated his priority consideration rights).

Identity of positions is not required. There needs to be some substance to the assertion that a position held through restoration after an injury by a fully recovered employee is not equivalent of the job held before the injury, Burgess v. Dept. of Interior, 95 MSPR 134, 139-40 ¶¶ 12-13, 15 (2003):

The appellant argues that the two positions at issue are not equivalent because the Frederick Douglass facility operates all year, while Anacostia is seasonal, and that the Frederick Douglass facility entails greater supervisory responsibility. These assertions fail to provide a basis for disturbing the administrative judge's explained conclusion that the Anacostia position was equivalent to the Frederick Douglass Home position.

The administrative judge relied upon the testimonies of several agency witnesses who indicated that both of the supervisory positions in question had the same grade-level, responsibilities, major duties, working hours and were similarly located. The administrative judge noted the appellant's claims regarding how the positions allegedly differed, including the appellant's contention that the Anacostia position was essentially seasonal. The administrative judge found, however, that the appellant lacked any evidence for these assertions, and that he failed to prove, and the record evidence contradicted, his contention that the positions were not essentially equivalent. Further, the appellant's unsworn contention that the positions are not equivalent because they involve supervising different numbers and types of employees is raised for the first time on review without a showing that it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite his due diligence, see Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980), and is inconsistent with the applicable position descriptions,

The appellant has also submitted as alleged new evidence an agency document apparently dated September 1991 and purportedly relevant to the issue of whether he could have been promoted, had he remained the incumbent at the Frederick Douglass Home position. B. Entitlement to promotion, however, is not a basis for finding that the positions were not equivalent under the regulatory definition of "equivalent position" set forth above. Veterans Administration, 3 M.S.P.R. 345, 349 (1980), nor has he shown that it is new by explaining why it was unavailable below, despite his due diligence, see Avansino, 3 M.S.P.R. at 214.
