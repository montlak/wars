8. Settlement Reached During Board Appeal

Most settlements reached during Board appeals include a release of further Board appeal rights, at least as to the matter settled and likely as to all matters that arose or could have arisen through the date of the settlement. Settlements and their interpretation and application are discussed in Chapter 16. Release clauses often specifically reference Board appeal rights, but they need not be that precise to bar future Board appeals if the intent of the parties appears sufficiently clear to the Board. Vaughan v. USPS, 607 Fed. Appx. 977, 978 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP), said of the preclusive nature of a settlement reached during a Board appeal substituting a resignation for a removal:

The Board ordinarily does not have jurisdiction over an action subject to a voluntarily executed settlement agreement except pursuant to an enforcement petition. See Mays v. United States Postal Serv., 995 F.2d 1056, 1059-60 (Fed. Cir. 1993); McCall v. United States Postal Serv., 839 F.2d 664, 665, 669 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (affirming dismissal for lack of jurisdiction where settlement agreement resolving prior MSPB appeal waived right to appeal). But a party may nevertheless establish jurisdiction if the party can show that the agreement was not voluntarily executed. Asberry v. United States Postal Serv., 692 F.2d 1378, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 1982).

Brandt v. Dept. of Navy, AT-1221-12-0521-W-1 (NP 4/26/2013), illustrates the Board's interpretation of a broad release involving Board appeal rights:

Pursuant to the terms of the agreement, the agency agreed to cancel the removal action and allow the appellant to resign, expunge the appellant's personnel file, not pursue collection of the appellant's indebtedness (including 240 hours of advance sick leave), and not challenge the appellant's unemployment and disability retirement claims. In return, the appellant agreed to withdraw his appeal and "not to institute any other actions, charges, appeals, class actions, or complaints concerning this appeal." The appellant further agreed as follows:

As material inducement to the Agency to enter into this Settlement Agreement, Appellant hereby irrevocably and unconditionally releases, acquits, and forever discharges the [agency's] officers, employees, and representatives collectively from any and all charges, appeals, claims, liabilities, obligations, promises, agreements, controversies, damages, actions, suits, rights, demands, costs, losses, debts, and expenses including attorney fees and costs actually incurred related to this appeal.

Id. We agree with the administrative judge that this language constitutes a waiver of the appellant's Board appeal rights. See Lee v. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 551, ¶ 7 (2009) (an agreement to "release" the agency "from all claims" constituted a waiver of Board appeal rights).

The appellant contends that the waiver is not enforceable. A waiver of appeal rights in a settlement agreement is enforceable and not against public policy if the terms of the waiver are comprehensive, freely made, and fair, and the execution of the waiver was not the result of duress or bad faith on the part of the agency. Lawrence v. Office of Personnel Management, 108 M.S.P.R. 325, ¶ 6 (2008), aff'd, 318 F. App'x 895 (2008). An appellant may establish that the waiver is unenforceable if he can establish that the settlement agreement was invalid due to fraud, duress, coercion, or misrepresentation by the agency. See id.

Here, the appellant asserts that he signed the agreement because the agency informed him that, if he did not sign the agreement, he would have to pay back the advanced sick leave. The Federal Circuit has long rejected economic duress as a basis for invalidating a settlement agreement. See Asberry v. U.S. Postal Service, 692 F.2d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1982). We find, therefore, that the appellant has not shown that the waiver of appeal rights in unenforceable, and the administrative judge correctly dismissed the appeal. We have considered the appellant's remaining arguments on review and find that they are not relevant to the dispositive issue in this case.

A settlement in a Board case may have the effect of withdrawing the Board appeal but be unenforceable by the Board if the agreement is not provided to and approved by the AJ for entry into the record for purposes of enforcement. See Pucek v. DOD, CH-315H-14-0147-I-1 (NP 10/20/2014) (probationer's Board appeal withdrawn through settlement: "Although the Board can consider a settlement agreement reached outside of a Board proceeding to determine its effect on the Board appeal and any waiver of appeal rights, it cannot enforce such an agreement. Lee, 111 M.S.P.R. 551, ¶ 4 & n.2. The appellant's request that we find that the agency has breached the terms of the settlement agreement is tantamount to a request for enforcement of the agreement. See Lopez v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 461, 463 (1996). Because the agreement was not entered into the record for enforcement purposes, the Board does not have the authority to act on the appellant's request. Id."); cf. Rios v. DHS, AT-0752-16-0619-I-1 (NP 1/3/2017) (when it became clear that the parties did not agree that a settlement would be entered into the Board's record for enforcement, the appeal should have been dismissed as withdrawn rather than dismissed as settled).
