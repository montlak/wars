2. Inadequate Initial Inquiry Into Facts

An action was clearly without merit and wholly unfounded after the agency removed employees whom the agency should have known were absent because of jury duty or sick leave rather than strike participation. It was immaterial that massive confusion resulted from the strike of the air traffic controllers; although the agency error may have been understandable, the action was still wholly unfounded. See Coleman v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 21 MSPR 596, 602-03 (1984). Should the deciding official ignore clear evidence that an employee was on leave during time that he was charged AWOL, fees are appropriate. The action is clearly without merit and the agency should have known that it would not prevail. Erdman v. Dept. of Labor, 6 MSPR 51, 53, 6 MSPB 54 (1981). However, as to individuals who were absent because of coercion but who failed to raise a coercion defense to a striking charge during the agency reply stage, it could not be said that the agency's case was clearly without merit or wholly unfounded or that the agency knew it could not prevail. Clay v. Dept. of Transp., 22 MSPR 602, 604 (1984) (also denying fees under other criteria, including "knew or should have known"); see Perez v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 52, 54, 4 MSPB 142 (1980), post.

A less than optimal investigation does not establish that the action was clearly without merit if there was credible, probative evidence supporting the agency case, Hilliard v. USPS, 403 Fed. Appx. 504, 507 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP):

...While the Board's analysis suggests that the Postal Service should have conducted a more thorough investigation of the circumstances surrounding the charge and the specification that were dismissed during or after the hearing, the Board nonetheless found as a factual matter that the agency had credible, probative evidence supporting the charges against Mr. Hilliard and that the case against him was not clearly without merit. In light of the great deference we give to the Board in fee determinations under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g), see Grubka v. Dep't of Treasury, 924 F.2d 1039, 1041 (Fed. Cir. 1991), we sustain the Board's order denying attorney fees.
