broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 02: Procedures for Obtaining Fees, Section C: Reasonableness of Fees, Subsection 01: Hourly Rate, Part e: Travel Time
e. Travel Time

On the theory that lawyers do not work--or do not work hard--when they travel, Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 611, 618 (1985), set a $20 per hour limit on travel time. Lizut was followed, Hoyt v. Dept. of Transp., 28 MSPR 275, 279 (1985), until Crumbaker v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 191 (Fed. Cir. 1986), dispatched the Board's restrictive approach to compensation for lawyers' travel time, after noting that there were arguments and cases for and against the Board's position:

We are persuaded that the analysis in Henry v. Webermeier, 738 F.2d 188 (7th Cir. 1984), is the better reasoned approach:

Probably [the appropriate billing rate] is the same billing rate as would be appropriate for the other time the lawyers put in on the case. When a lawyer travels for one client he incurs an opportunity cost that is equal to the fee he would have charged that or another client if he had not been traveling. That is why lawyers invariably charge their clients for travel time, and usually at the same rate they charge for other time, except when they are able to bill another client for part of the travel time (a lawyer might do work for client A while flying on an airplane to a meeting with client B). And if they charge their paying clients for travel time they are entitled to charge the defendants for that time in a case such as this where the plaintiffs have shown a statutory right to reasonable attorneys' fees. Of course, if the travel is unnecessary the time spent in travel should be subtracted out.... If, though reasonable in terms of the amount of time consumed, the travel is unnecessarily luxurious, the court should not reimburse the plaintiffs for the entire out-of-pocket expenses of travel; but that is a completely separate issue from the hourly billing rate.... The presumption, which the defendants have not attempted to rebut, should be that a reasonable attorney's fee includes reasonable travel time billed at the same hourly rate as the lawyer's normal working time.

...This approach is consistent with the purpose of 5 USC 7701(g)(1), which has "a general goal of removing impediments to the litigation of a meritorious claim."...

In addressing the presiding official's reduction of the claimed rate, the Board noted that the billing rates charged to Crumbaker "were charged to all clients for whom they worked," and the Board found that "the actual rates claimed by counsel are reasonable and in accord with prevailing community standards...." Thus, the Board's reduction in the rate for travel time was not because that rate was other than the prevailing rate. Rather, it was because the Board regarded compensation for counsel's travel time at the same rate as other legal work "inappropriate."... That decision is not in accordance with law and we reverse it.

The Board now joins the circuit in allowing full fees for travel time. SSA v. Balaban, 33 MSPR 309, 320 (1987).

A countermeasure to a fee demand that includes a claim for travel time is an assertion that the travel was unnecessary because skilled counsel were available without importing someone else. The Board tangentially addressed this defense, rejecting it in one appeal, noting that the agency had not established, at the level of the AJ, facts showing either that local counsel was available or that local counsel were as skilled as the attorney chosen by the appellant. Hoover v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 641, 643 (1986). More directly, under Garstkiewicz v. USPS, 50 MSPR 476, 478-79 (1991), modifying Hoover:

The administrative judge found that reasonable attorney fees did not include travel time and travel costs because the agency established that qualified local counsel existed for the appellant. We disagree.

In Hoover v. Department of the Navy, 30 MSPR 641, 643 (1986), the Board stated that out-of-town counsel is entitled to travel "time" at the same hourly rate as the attorney's normal working time. 30 MSPR at 643. The Board has also held that, under the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit's decision in Bennett v. Department of the Navy, 699 F.2d 1140 (Fed. Cir. 1983), the Board has authority to award expenses, other than those set forth at 28 USC 1920. See Russell v. Department of the Navy, 43 MSPR 157, 163 (1989). Such expenses include those connected with reasonable travel.

...

[T]he availability of a local attorney with the same specialized expertise as the chosen representative is not a bar to the reimbursement of travel-related fees and expenses. An appellant may choose any representative as long as that person is willing and available to serve. To the extent that prior decisions, such as Hoover or Russell may be contrary to the conclusions reached in this decision, we hereby modify them.
