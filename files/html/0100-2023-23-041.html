c. Disappointment; Confusion

Allegations of duress must be measured by objective rather than subjective evidence. Washington v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3017). Post-settlement remorse, reflected in a statement that the appellant, represented by counsel, did not understand the full implications of a settlement agreement, is insufficient to set the agreement aside. Bohan v. USPS, 31 MSPR 601, 603 (1986) (waiver of counsel fees); see Jordan v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3536) ("That there were collateral consequences to his actions, whether anticipated or not, has no bearing on...whether the settlement regarding that violation was freely entered into."); Henson v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 221, 224 ¶ 10 (2000) ("An appellant's post-settlement remorse or change of heart cannot serve as a basis for setting aside a valid settlement agreement."); Ali v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 563, 569 (1991) ("The fact that the appellant had a change of heart and refused to sign the agreement after it was reduced to writing does not affect the validity of the [oral] agreement."); Chiarella v. USPS, 41 MSPR 16, 19 (1989) (failure to appreciate the consequences of retirement bargained for in a settlement did not warrant setting aside the agreement).

The Board rejected claims that a settlement agreement was coerced because the appellant did not understand its full impact on his career. See Smith v. USPS, PH-0752-13-1102-I-1 (NP 4/8/2014) ("The fact that the appellant may have been unaware of the ramifications of the settlement agreement does not invalidate it. Thompson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 52 M.S.P.R. 233, 237 (1992). The appellant was given 12 days from the date of the Step B settlement agreement to consider the terms of the agreement, weigh his options, and decide whether to resign or be removed."); Frizzell v. Dept. of Air Force, 53 MSPR 413, 415 (1992) ("[S]ettlement was the result of negotiations which took place, with the appellant's active participation, over a period of approximately one month[.]"). The failure of an employee to fully understand the implications of the agreement was an insufficient basis to set it aside. Virgil v. USPS, 75 MSPR 109, 113-14 (1997) (Board would not set aside a settlement barring future employment from a specified geographic region when the appellant was unaware of the boundaries of that region); Thompson v. VA, 52 MSPR 233, 237 (1992) ("Having been represented, her alleged failure to realize the full implications of a settlement agreement is insufficient to set the agreement aside. See Laranang v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 233, 236-37 (1989). Likewise, the appellant's change of heart does not provide a reason to set it aside. See Ali v. Department of the Army, [MSPB Docket No. AT07528910494, slip op. at 9 (Oct. 18, 1991).]"); cf. Special Counsel v. Jennings, 44 MSPR 1, 4 (1990) (declining to set aside an agreement that may have been the product of an unpleasant choice, but that was not negotiated under duress of time, that was entered by an authorized representative, that was not the product of fraud, misrepresentation, or bad faith negotiations, and that did not result from intimidation of a witness; failure of employee to fully understand the implications of an agreement was not a sufficient basis to set it aside).

Komiskey v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1997 NP No. 96-3300), provides an example of a settlement that did not meet the expectations of the appellant, but that was nonetheless valid:

The Department of the Army and Komiskey signed a settlement agreement dismissing any claims against the agency in exchange for, inter alia, the agency's agreement to make "all efforts necessary to process [her termination] action in such a way that [she] will end up with a clean record." Both parties acknowledged that the Office of Personnel Management was unlikely to grant Komiskey an exception from the rules governing discontinued service retirements.

Komiskey's desire for an unblemished record, no matter how often repeated, does not amount to a mutual understanding that the settlement agreement would be invalid if OPM processed her personnel action with a "retirement in lieu of a performance removal." Similarly, the fact that the agency agreed to attempt to clean her record, with knowledge that it was not likely to achieve this result, does not constitute bad faith or mutual mistake. There has been no breach of the clear and unambiguous language of the settlement agreement.

Had Komiskey wished to prepare a contingency plan, she could have done so explicitly in the settlement agreement. Because she did not, she is not now entitled to avoid the terms of her agreement.

Allegations of lack of sophistication or unequal bargaining knowledge were insufficient to void a settlement, Wade v. VA, 61 MSPR 580, 583-84 (1994):

The appellant does not explain how conducting the settlement negotiations over the telephone caused his acceptance of the agreement to be involuntary. His allegation that the settlement agreement should be set aside because the negotiations were conducted over the telephone lacks specific support and does not show that he did not willfully and knowingly enter into the agreement. See Clark v. Department of the Treasury, 48 MSPR 330, 333 (1991).

The appellant also claims that the settlement agreement should be set aside because he had only a high school education and was unrepresented during the telephonic settlement conference, while the agency's representative during the conference was an attorney. The appellant apparently believes that he was somehow disadvantaged during the proceedings before the Board because the agency's representative was an attorney. The Board's regulations, however, allow parties wide discretion in choosing their representatives, subject only to disqualification for conflict of interest or position. See 5 CFR § 1201.31; Walton v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 48 MSPR 462, 469 (1991). The appellant appears to have elected not to have his representative present during the telephonic conference, and must now accept any consequences of that election.

Moreover, parties to a settlement agreement are presumed to have full legal capacity to contract unless they are under guardianship, an infant, mentally ill or defective, or intoxicated. See Ray v. Department of Health & Human Services, 57 MSPR 16, 20 (1993). The appellant did not assert below, and he does not now assert, that he lacked full legal capacity to enter into the settlement agreement. He has submitted no evidence showing that he was unable to understand the nature of the settlement agreement or that his decision was not an informed one. See Riley v. Department of the Army, 55 MSPR 671, 677 (1992).

The appellant also does not explain why he neglected to request the presence of his representative during the settlement conference. Even assuming that the appellant's representative had been unable to participate in the settlement conference despite such a request by the appellant, the appellant is responsible for the actions or inactions of his chosen representative and cannot avoid the consequences of his choice. See Walton, 48 MSPR at 469.

Disappointment with a settlement did not serve to set it aside in Diaz v. USPS, SF-0752-15-0497-I-1 (NP 1/29/2016), noting that the appellant could have invoked an OWBPA revocation clause:

Here, the fact that the appellant now believes that the settlement agreement is a "bad deal" for him does not render the agreement invalid. The record reflects that the appellant was represented during the settlement negotiations and that he and his attorney signed the agreement. He has not submitted any evidence showing that he was unable to understand the nature of the settlement agreement or that his decision was not an informed one. See Wade v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 580, 584 (1994). As such, the appellant's dissatisfaction with the terms he agreed to is insufficient to warrant setting aside the settlement agreement. See Hinton, 119 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 4; Henson, 86 M.S.P.R. 221, ¶ 10. Likewise, although the appellant now claims that the settlement agreement's July 10, 2015 deadline for submitting his disability retirement application was coercive and caused him duress, he has not shown that he involuntarily accepted this term, that circumstances permitted no other alternative, and that such circumstances were the result of the agency's coercive act. See Hinton, 119 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 4. Moreover, the appellant received an extension of the deadline within the 7-day revocation period under the OWBPA, at which time he could have revoked the settlement agreement without the pressure of any imminent deadline but failed to do so.
