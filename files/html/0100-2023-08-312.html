b. Constructive Adverse Action

Randall v. DOJ, 105 MSPR 524, 529-30 ¶¶ 11-12, 2007 MSPB 119 (2007), explained that an otherwise tenured employee complaining of a constructive adverse action may bring the case as an adverse action appeal under Chapter 75, as a claim under USERRA, or both:

We further note that the appellant's allegation that she involuntarily resigned from her civilian position, may be the basis for Board jurisdiction under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75, see Spiegel v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 140, 141 (1980) (an involuntary resignation is tantamount to a removal). While we find here that the appellant has established jurisdiction over her USERRA claim, USERRA does not supercede or otherwise limit the appellant's right to bring a constructive removal appeal. See 38 U.S.C. § 4302(a) ("Nothing in [USERRA] shall supercede, nullify or diminish any Federal...law...that establishes a right or benefit that is more beneficial to, or is in addition to, a right or benefit provided for such person in [USERRA] "). An employee-initiated action, such as a transfer between agencies, resignation, or retirement, is presumed to be voluntary unless the appellant presents sufficient evidence to establish that the action was obtained through duress or coercion or shows that a reasonable person would have been misled by the agency. Talley v. Department of the Army, 50 M.S.P.R. 261, 263 (1991). On remand, the administrative judge shall notify the appellant of her burden of proof to establish that her resignation was involuntary and therefore an action within the Board's adverse action jurisdiction. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The administrative judge shall also notify the appellant of her burden of proof to establish the timeliness of her chapter 75 involuntary resignation appeal. See Wright v. Department of Transportation, 99 M.S.P.R. 112, ¶ 12 (2005).

Whether this appeal proceeds under the Board's USERRA jurisdiction, chapter 75 of Title 5, or both, if the appellant prevails, she may be entitled to back pay or other compensation. Upon finding that an agency violated a provision of USERRA, "the Board shall enter an order requiring" the agency "to comply with such provision [ ] and to compensate [the appellant] for any [resulting] lost wages or benefits." 38 U.S.C. § 4324(c)(2). If the appellant proves on remand that the agency violated USERRA and that she lost wages or benefits because of that violation, she may be entitled to compensation. If she proves that she resigned from her civilian position because of the agency's harassment based on her obligation to perform military service or her Reservist status, she may be entitled to compensation under USERRA for lost wages and benefits as a result. 38 U.S.C. § 4324(c)(2); see Dombrowski v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 102 M.S.P.R. 160, ¶ 12 (2006). Moreover, if the appellant establishes jurisdiction over a constructive removal appeal under chapter 75, she may be entitled to reinstatement and back pay as status quo ante relief. 5 U.S.C. § 5596; see Porter v. Department of Defense, 98 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶¶ 21-24 (2005).

See Jolley v. MSPB, 636 Fed. Appx. 567 (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP) (Board should have adjudicated a claim in a forced retirement appeal that depended on a USERRA violation); Martir v. Dept. of Navy, 81 MSPR 421, 424-26 ¶¶ 7-12 (1999) (Board had jurisdiction over claim that appellant resigned to avoid separation through RIF that resulted when his veterans status allegedly resulted in failure to hire him for positions filled with intent of protecting nonveterans against RIF). When a hostile environment is alleged as the cause of a resignation, the bottom line is that the Board is to consider the totality of the circumstances, Lentz v. MSPB, 876 F.3d 1380, 1386 (Fed. Cir. 2017):

...The appropriate standard is whether the totality of events, on all of the evidence, produced a working environment sufficiently hostile as to lead to involuntary resignation. See Kline v. Dep't of Transp., FAA, 808 F.2d 43, 46 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (vacating and remanding to the Board where "the record does not demonstrate that the presiding official identified, balanced and then considered" the relevant evidence); DeLaughter v. U.S. Postal Serv., 3 F.3d 1522, 1524 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (remanding to the Board where "it is abundantly clear that the Postal Service failed to follow the appellate review procedure" before the Board), abrogated on other grounds by Guillebeau v. Dep't of Navy, 362 F.3d 1329 (Fed. Cir. 2004).

A remand order from the Board determined, Lentz v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-15-0363-R-1, n.7 (NP 6/30/2022):

...Given the court's reasoning in Lentz, 876 F.3d at 1386, that the proper question in that case was whether the totality of the evidence, including both the evidence of alleged USERRA violations and the evidence of other coercive agency actions, rendered the appellant's resignation involuntary, we similarly find that, in determining whether the agency constructively suspended the appellant, the totality of the evidence, including evidence of alleged USERRA violations and evidence of other alleged coercive actions, must be considered. See Thomas v. Department of the Navy, 123 M.S.P.R. 628, ¶ 9 (2016) (although various fact patterns may give rise to an appealable constructive suspension, all such claims are premised on the proposition that an absence that appears to be voluntary actually is not because the employee lacked a meaningful choice in the matter and it was the agency's wrongful actions that deprived her of that choice)....

Lentz continued:

[W]e also note that, when allegations of discrimination or reprisal are alleged in connection with a determination of voluntariness, such as the issue of the voluntariness of the appellant's resignation in these cases, such evidence of discrimination or reprisal may be addressed insofar as it relates to the issue of voluntariness and not whether the evidence would establish discrimination or reprisal as an affirmative defense. O'Brien, 91 M.S.P.R. 139, ¶ 6.
