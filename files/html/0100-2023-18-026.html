4. Agency Predecisional Hearing

The Reform Act does not provide for a pretermination hearing and one is not constitutionally required. Ramon v. EEOC, 30 MSPR 278, 280 (1986). Under 5 USC 7513(c) an agency may provide for a hearing by regulation in lieu of or in addition to the oral and written reply session. An interesting case concerning the application of the harmful error rule to the right to a predecision hearing was Mercer v. DHHS, 24 MSPR 368 (1984). The employee received a proposal of dismissal for AWOL, threats, and failures to perform work. The agency (Social Security Administration) regulations required that after the proposal and before the final agency decision, the employee would, on request, have the opportunity to obtain third-party review of the allegations. The review was to be conducted by an impartial arbitrator, who would receive and review evidence and make nonbinding recommendations to the deciding official, who in turn would review the recommendations and issue a decision as to what, if any, penalty to impose. The internal agency regulation that established the review procedure allowed the procedure to be avoided by the agency in certain circumstances, including the situation of an employee whose continued presence posed a threat to the government. Mr. Mercer's removal was proposed, and he was placed on enforced leave. He requested advisory arbitration. He was then taken off enforced leave and returned to the job. Although he was returned to the job, the agency denied him advisory arbitration because the agency considered him to pose a threat to the government. The Board concluded that the agency improperly denied advisory arbitration, but then the Board concluded that the error was harmless because the employee failed to show how the outcome would have been different if he had the third-party review. On appeal, the Federal Circuit reversed the Board, Mercer v. DHHS, 772 F.2d 856, 859-60 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

While the burden is on Mercer to show that the failure to grant him the predecision hearing was harmful to his defense so that a different result might have been reached, the burden is not on him to prove that the outcome of such a hearing would have changed the result, an impossibility. He only needs to show that it was an error which could have changed the result.

...

While the agency was not constitutionally required to provide Mercer with an additional predecision procedure, it had done so.... The Board held that the agency had wrongfully deprived Mercer of this right, but that this error was harmless.

However, even in the circumstances of such a serious procedural error as the denial of a predecision hearing, a federal employee must prove that his rights were substantially prejudiced by the procedural error so that the outcome was possibly thereby affected. In this case, Mercer introduced undisputed evidence at the hearing that the official who proposed his initial thirty-five day suspension advised him that he would be allowed to return to his position after the suspension, if he were found fit for duty. Due to an agency organizational change occurring during Mercer's suspension period, a new official became the proposing official and issued the notice cancelling the proposed suspension and proposing Mercer's removal. This evidence of differing views within the agency as to the penalty to be imposed upon Mercer clearly demonstrates that, had Mercer been afforded a predecision hearing or an advisory arbitration, the outcome could have been different. Mercer was afforded neither benefit.

Although the government argues that Mercer's exercise of his right to an oral reply before the presiding official rendered harmless the denial of the additional predecision rights, we cannot agree. A person has a better and perhaps dispositive chance of successfully contesting termination of benefits before, not after, the benefits are terminated. See Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 264 (1970).

For these reasons, we reverse that part of the MSPB's decision holding that Mercer failed to show the procedural error harmed him.

After Mercer was decided, the Board increased the burden imposed under the harmful error rule by modifying its regulation.

As to the failure of the Postal Service to use a predisciplinary interview in a misconduct case, Bethune v. USPS, PH-0752-13-0109-I-1 (NP 1/22/2015), discerned no constitutional due process right to the interview and no harmful error in its absence:

...To the extent that the appellant suggests that the agency's policy of providing a PDI is another constitutional due process requirement, he is mistaken. See Miller v. U.S. Postal Service, 110 M.S.P.R. 550, ¶ 6 (2009) (the agency did not violate the appellant's due process rights by not providing him with a PDI prior to proposing his removal).

Where an agency meets the minimum requirements of due process, the Board may still reverse an adverse action based upon harmful error for a failure to comply with statutory, regulatory, or agency procedures, if the error likely had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency. Stephen, 47 M.S.P.R. at 681, 685. However, the burden of showing harmful error lies with the appellant and cannot be presumed. Id.; see 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(A); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(b)(1), (c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(1), 1201.4(r)]. In order to show harmful error, an appellant must prove that the procedural error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Stephen, 47 M.S.P.R. at 685.

Here, even if the agency had a policy of providing a PDI and failed to provide that PDI to the appellant, he has presented no argument or evidence that the procedural error was harmful. See generally Miller, 110 M.S.P.R. 550, ¶ 6 (the agency did not commit harmful error by not providing the appellant with a PDI prior to proposing his removal). He was able to respond to the charge after the proposed removal, but before its effectuation, and he has presented nothing to demonstrate that the outcome was likely to have been different if he had also been given the PDI he claims was lacking. Accordingly, even if the agency deprived the appellant of the right to respond to the allegations in a PDI, prior to the issuance of the notice of proposed removal, this was neither a due process violation, nor a harmful error.
