5. Supervisory Practices; Remarks

Differences in treatment of employees do not alone establish disparate treatment. Proof of discriminatory motive is significant. Lee v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 3, 5 (1985) (relying on Cross v. VA, 21 MSPR 415, 419 (1984), and Butler v. USPS, 10 MSPR 45, 51-54 (1982), distinguishing Francis v. AT&T, 55 FRD 202 (D.D.C. 1972), and Mead v. U.S. Fidelity & Guaranty Co., 442 F. Supp. 114 (D. Minn. 1977)). Under Lee, no discrimination was found as a result of the supervisor's practice of keeping notes concerning the appellant's performance. The supervisor did not keep notes on other employees because he did not receive complaints about them and their performance was satisfactory. Butler v. USPS, 10 MSPR 45, 51 (1982), discussed an affirmative defense based on disparate treatment:

As the Court stated in Burdine, relative to a showing of pretext "[t]he plaintiff retains the burden of persuasion." Id. 450 U.S. at 256, 101 S. Ct. at 1095. Burdine held that an employee may meet this burden by showing that a discriminatory reason more likely than not motivated the employer or that the employer's explanation was unworthy of credence.

The presiding official in considering that the agency explanation for its actions, i.e., that the penalties were levied through different organizational channels, and that the appellant's offense was more serious, erred in allocating the burden of persuasion to the agency. Thus it was up to the appellant to show that some discriminatory motive lurked behind these agency explanations. Even if, as the presiding official concluded, the penalty against the white supervisor was common knowledge throughout the agency, the appellant has not shown that those persons who recommended or took action against him acted out of a discriminatory motive or intent. Neither has he shown that it was discriminatory on the part of the agency to conclude that his offense was more serious than that of the white supervisor whose sexual coercion of his subordinates did not involve attempted or actual physical assault.

Reprisal was found on the part of supervisors who threatened to remove the appellant if another EEO complaint was filed, who misused fitness for duty examinations to harass the appellant, and who grossly overreacted to the incident that led to the removal proceedings. Madison v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 465, 474 (1987). But a supervisor's insistence on having tasks done the way he wants is not evidence of discrimination. Davies v. USDA, 5 MSPR 251, 256, 5 MSPB 291 (1981).

Of evidence offered of religious discrimination, Dorsey v. Dept. of Air Force, 78 MSPR 439, 448-49 (1998), involving removal for misrepresentation, failure to comply with an order, falsification of a document, and AWOL, noted:

The appellant also attempts to prove his claim of religious discrimination based on a "disparate treatment" theory. Under this theory, an employee must demonstrate through the use of direct or circumstantial evidence that the employer treated him differently than other employees because of his religious beliefs; the evidentiary burdens placed on the employee mirror those placed on employees alleging discrimination based on race or sex. Reed, 76 MSPR at 130-31.

The appellant alleges that there is direct evidence of religious discrimination in the form of a statement made by his first-line supervisor. Effective July 12, 1995, the appellant received a "fully successful" performance appraisal for the period July 1, 1994, to June 30, 1995. The appellants testified that, when he asked his supervisor about a "bad" performance" appraisal in late 1995, his supervisor told him that "the reason why I'm giving you low performance appraisals is because...you don't want to work on your Sabbath." The appellant testified that the supervisor "said something about that you're one of our...best schedulers...and it has...nothing to do with your performance. It's that you just won't work on the Sabbath. That's why I'm giving these...low marks."

Even assuming that this testimony by the appellant is credible, it is not direct evidence of discrimination because it does not bear directly on the specific employment decision in question, namely the appellant's removal. See, e.g., Randle v. LaSalle Telecommunications, Inc., 876 F.2d 563, 569-70 (7th Cir. 1989); see also George v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 MSPR 71, 80 (1997) (direct evidence of discrimination may be any written or verbal policy or statement made by employer that both reflects directly the alleged discrimination and bears directly on the contested employment decision).

The appellant may establish a prima facie case of discrimination based on circumstantial evidence by showing that he engaged in prohibited conduct similar to that of a person outside his protected class, and that disciplinary measures enforced against him were more severe than those enforced against the other person. Reed, 76 MSPR at 134. The burden of proof would then shift to the agency to articulate some legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions; if the agency meets that burden, the appellant bears the ultimate burden of showing that the reasons offered by the agency were not the true reasons for its actions, but merely pretext for discrimination. Id.

The appellant has not identified any agency employee who was not a Seventh-day Adventist and who engaged in similar misconduct but was not removed. In fact, the agency's deciding official testified that there was "no precedent" on the Air Force base for the type of misconduct committed by the appellant....

As to derogatory remarks, Wynne v. Dept. of Army, 46 MSPR 320, 325 (1990), affirmed an arbitrator's decision that an agency properly dismissed an employee who struck her supervisor:

...When the arbitrator referred to the appellant's unsuccessful attempt to characterize the proposing official as a racist, he implicitly found that the testimony of the appellant's witness that the proposing official said, "[W]e're going to get her black a**" in reference to the appellant immediately after the assault, was insufficient in itself to establish race discrimination....

...The appellant tried to present evidence through her own testimony that she was treated differently than other employees with regard to breaks, overtime and performance evaluations. The arbitrator discounted all of the appellant's testimony, however. Assuming that the remark was made, it apparently was the only remark of that kind that was ever made by this proposing official, as no evidence was introduced to establish this was a pattern. It immediately followed his discovery that the appellant's supervisor was in the dispensary after the incident with the appellant, and he had previous disciplinary problems with the appellant. Under the circumstances, we do not find that this single remark, assuming it was made, is sufficient evidence to establish pretext. See Young v. City of Houston, 906 F.2d 177 (5th Cir. 1990) (black supervisor's reference to a white employee as a "white token" and a "white faggot" do not constitute direct evidence of racial discrimination, citing Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228 (1989) (O'Connor, concurring) ("[s]tray remarks in the workplace while perhaps probative...cannot justify requiring the employer to prove that its...decisions were based on legitimate criteria.").

Rios v. Rossotti, 252 F.3d 375, 379 (5th Cir. 2001), said of the probative significance of discriminatory remarks:

...The Supreme Court has assessed the value of discriminatory remarks by examining whether the remarks indicated invidious animus and whether the speaker of the remarks was "principally responsible" for the adverse employment action. See Reeves, 120 S. Ct. at 2110; see also Russell v. McKinney Hosp. Venture, 235 F.3d 219, 225 (5th Cir. 2000) (stating that "[t]he value of [discriminatory] remarks is dependent upon the content of the remarks and the speaker" (citing Reeves))....

See Tyner v. USDA, DC-0752-16-0098-I-1 (NP 9/7/2016) ("Here, although the appellant alleged that various coworkers and contractors, including J.L., made racial comments to him during the course of his employment with the agency, we agree with the administrative judge that the appellant failed to prove that any agency managers or officials involved in the suspension action at issue made remarks reflecting a discriminatory attitude."); Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 623 n.8 (2001) ("Although Sealander's comment might be construed as directly reflecting the allegedly discriminatory attitude toward members of the appellant's gender, we find that it does not constitute direct evidence of discrimination because it did not directly bear on the contested employment decision, i.e., the decision to remove the appellant from federal service.").

Padilla v. VA, 6 MSPR 494, 498, 6 MSPB 416 (1981), determined that evidence of a lack of minorities in supervisory jobs could indicate discrimination in promotions, but the evidence was insufficient to establish that the agency's reasons for removing the appellant were pretextual. Evidence that the appellant's supervisors uttered one or two derogatory remarks did not support a finding of pretext, considering appellant's falsification of records. A supervisor's arguably discriminatory actions toward an employee did not demonstrate discrimination in an employment action for misconduct against the employee if the manager who made the employment decision was not influenced by the supervisor. See Holbrook v. Reno, 196 F.3d 255, 260-61 (D.C. Cir. 1999). But see Rios v. Rossotti, 252 F.3d 375 (5th Cir. 2001), supra (considering "rubber stamp" theory in promotion case).
