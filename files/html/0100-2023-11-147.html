a. Proposing Official

The Board reviews an agency decision. What difference does the testimony of the proposing official make? The proposal could have been discriminatory or retaliatory, a matter treated in Chapters 12 and 13. Perhaps the proposing official was selected by proponents of the adverse action to ensure a higher level of discipline was proposed than might have been proposed by an individual who would ordinarily serve as the proposing official, a matter discussed in Chapter 11. Of the exclusion of the proposing official as a witness in an arbitration case, Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 (NP 5/2/2013), stated:

The appellant argues that the agency should have been required to produce the proposing official as a witness. He argues that her non-appearance at the arbitration hearing deprived him of due process and his right to confront his accuser and that, absent the proposing official's testimony, the agency was unable to provide sufficient evidence to support its charge. The appellant further argues that the arbitrator should have drawn an adverse inference against the agency by resolving in the appellant's favor any ambiguity caused by the proposing official's non-appearance.

We disagree. The agency called the proposing official as a witness, but she did not appear. The agency asserts that it was unable to compel the proposing official's testimony because at the time of the arbitration hearing she was no longer an agency employee. Although the Federal Arbitration Act gives an arbitrator in a commercial dispute the authority to subpoena a witness, 9 U.S.C. § 7, it does not appear that such statutory authority applies in an arbitration conducted pursuant to a collective bargaining agreement between a federal agency and a union.[3] Cf. National Treasury Employees Union and Department of the Treasury, 67 F.L.R.A. 24, 26-27 (2012) (suggesting that the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute, 5 U.S.C. §§ 7101-7135, and not the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. §§ 1-16, governs the conduct of an arbitration concerning a federal workplace dispute). Even assuming that the agency could have compelled the proposing official to testify, however, the agency was under no obligation to produce the proposing official for the benefit of the appellant. Dubiel v. U.S. Postal Service, 54 M.S.P.R. 428, 432 (1992). If the appellant believed that the proposing official was a key witness to his own case, he should have used whatever means were available in the arbitration to call her as a witness himself. In short, the fact that the proposing official did not testify does not indicate that the arbitrator committed error.

[3] We note that the record reflects that the appellant did not seek to subpoena the witness.

Furthermore, the proposing official's non-appearance did not violate the appellant's "right to confrontation" because this is not a criminal matter and the Sixth Amendment does not apply. See Austin v. United States, 509 U.S. 602, 608 (1993). The agency also did not violate the appellant's due process rights as set forth in Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532, 546 (1985). What Loudermill requires is meaningful notice and opportunity to respond prior to the removal. 470 U.S. at 546; see Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1375-76 (Fed. Cir. 1999). The appellant's supposed inability to examine the proposing official at the arbitration hearing did not impair his ability to respond to the notice of proposed removal.

In addition, it was not necessary for the agency to present the testimony of the proposing official in order to meet its burden of proving its charges by preponderant evidence. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B). First, the proposing official's "rationale" for bringing the charges is perfectly clear. It is contained in the notice of proposed removal, which speaks for itself. Second, as explained below, we find that there is no dispute of material fact regarding the agency's charge. Therefore, there was no additional evidence that the agency needed to adduce from the proposing official or anyone else in order to prove its charge.[4] For this same reason, we find that, even if any ambiguity caused by the proposing official's nonappearance should be resolved against the agency, the appellant has not identified any such ambiguity that could be material to the outcome of the appeal. Moreover, to the extent that the appellant is requesting that the Board re-weigh evidence on factual matters already decided by the arbitrator, the Board will not do so. See Hilton-Boy v. Department of Health & Human Services, 56 M.S.P.R. 176, 179 (1992).

[4] The appellant also argues that the deciding official's decision not to sustain all of the AWOL specifications shows that the proposing official is not "credible." We find that the proposing official's credibility is not at issue because she was not, and was not required to be, a witness at the hearing. The appellant also alleges that the proposing official proposed his removal out of animus toward his religion. However, the appellant has not identified any evidence to support this conjecture.

See Joshua v. SSA, 2 MSPR 280, 282, 2 MSPB 444 (1980) (appellant's immediate supervisor and the proposing and deciding officials are ordinarily unobjectionable as witnesses). [Refer to Chapter 3, "Decisionmaker."]
