B. NEGOTIABILITY FRAMEWORK

Agency objections to proposals seeking to modify or contain drug testing programs rest on the needs asserted by agencies to establish internal security practices, assign work, impose discipline, maintain employees' privacy, and to avoid tampering with government-wide regulations and the Executive Order establishing the drug testing system.

The negotiability of proposals concerning drug testing programs received review in NFFE Local 15 and Dept. of Army, Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 30 FLRA 1046 (1988), on remand (from unpublished order of May 25, 1988 by the D.C. Circuit), 33 FLRA 436 (1988). In the original NFFE Local 15 decision, FLRA noted the background of its drug testing program and the general constraints upon proposals seeking to modify the program, 30 FLRA at 1049-50:

On September 15, 1986, President Reagan issued Executive Order 12564.... Section 3...directs the head of each Executive agency to establish mandatory and voluntary drug testing programs for agency employees and applicants in sensitive positions. Section 4(d) authorizes the Secretary of Health and Human Services (HHS) to promulgate scientific and technical guidelines for drug testing programs, and requires agencies to conduct their drug testing programs in accordance with these guidelines.... Section 6(a)(1) states that the Director of...[OPM] shall issue "government-wide guidance to agencies on the implementation of the terms of [the] Order[.]" Section 6(b) provides that "[t]he Attorney General shall render legal advice regarding the implementation of this Order...."

On November 28, 1986, OPM issued Federal Personnel Manual Letter 792-16, "Establishing a Drug-Free Federal Workplace." Section 2(c) of the letter states: "Agencies shall ensure that drug testing programs in existence as of September 15, 1986, are brought into conformance with E.O. 12564."...

...

On February 13, 1987, HHS issued "Scientific and Technical Guidelines for Drug Testing Programs."...

As to the internal security implications of a proposal determining the frequency of drug testing, FLRA determined, 30 FLRA at 1055-56:

[B]y limiting management's ability to conduct random testing for employee use of illegal drugs, Proposal 1 directly interferes with management's internal security practices. As the Agency indicated...one purpose for instituting the drug testing program is to identify "individuals whose drug abuse could cause disruption in operations, destruction of property, threats to safety for themselves and others, or the potential for unwarranted disclosure of classified information through drug-related blackmail."...

...Unannounced random testing has a deterrent effect on drug users and makes it difficult for drug users to take action to cover up their use or otherwise evade the tests....

We will not review the Agency's determination that the establishment of a drug testing program involving random tests for the positions which it has identified as sensitive positions is necessary to protect the security of the installations.... Where a link has been established between an agency's action--in this case random drug testing--and its expressed security concerns, we will not review the merits of that action....

Although most of the proposals that follow are sufficiently general that the resolution of related negotiability disputes will have government-wide application, FLRA decides a negotiability appeal involving proposals specifically tailored to a particular agency regulation, an example being IFPTE Local 1 and Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Shipyard, 49 FLRA 225, 232-40 (1994), principally involving negotiated modifications to OPNAVINST 5355.
