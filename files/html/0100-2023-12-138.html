c. Disparate Treatment

Mitigation may be required if the appellant, through a PFR, presents evidence that the agency took less severe action against another employee similarly situated following the initial decision in the appellant's case. The evidence qualifies as new and material evidence warranting an outcome different than that ordered by the AJ. Shuff v. Panama Canal Comm'n, 14 MSPR 295, 296 (1983); Beckham v. VA, PH-0752-10-0303-I-1 (NP 5/13/2011) ("We grant the appellant's petition for review for the limited purpose of considering his new evidence regarding the disciplinary history of another employee as it relates to his claim of disparate penalty. We find, however, that this new evidence does not change the outcome of the appeal and that the agency has shown a legitimate reason for the difference in penalties. See Lewis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶ 6 (2010).") (overruled, Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022), except to the extent the decision is consistent with Williams v. SSA, 586 F.3d 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2009), and Facer v. Dept. of Air Force, 836 F2d 535 (Fed. Cir. 1988)); Lewis v. VA, 111 MSPR 388, 390-91 ¶ 5, 2009 MSPB 96 (2009) (remanding for consideration of disparity of penalty in comparison to an employee who received a proposal before, and a decision after, the appellant's MSPB hearing), following remand, Lewis v. VA, 113 MSPR 657, 2010 MSPB 98 (2010) (mitigating removal to suspension based on a disparate penalty analysis); Berkey v. USPS, 38 MSPR 55, 58 (1988) (remanding to determine whether a different result was required in a removal case when, after the initial decision was issued, the agency issued a lesser penalty to a comparable employee whose misconduct was admitted by the employee on the day before the appellant's hearing); cf. Miskill v. SSA, 863 F.3d 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (arbitrator should have considered comparators as to whom information was developed after the adverse action and before the arbitrator's award); Edwards v. USPS, 662 Fed. Appx. 951 (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP) (Federal Circuit remanded a case to the Board when information on a comparator was presented during the judicial appeal).

New evidence concerning the disparity in penalties must be brought to the attention of the Board with reasonable dispatch following its discovery. See Mille v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 248, 251 (1985) (disparate penalty was not issued until several days after the close of the record; the evidence was new and material; an AWOL removal was mitigated to a suspension); Berkey, supra. To be material, the penalty imposed on the other employee must not be superficially similar. There must be uniformity based on consideration of the underlying factors in a given case. A single charge may reasonably provide a range of penalties, each appropriate in a particular set of circumstances. See Furlick v. EEOC, 20 MSPR 71, 74 (1984). As to argument concerning disparity in penalties that could have been raised before the AJ, the Board will not consider the argument without a showing that it is based on new evidence that, despite the party's due diligence, was unavailable before the regional record closed. See Spates v. USPS, 68 MSPR 9, 12 (1995) (appellant failed to use discovery to obtain information concerning possible disparate penalties); Majors v. USPS, 3 MSPR 146, 148-49, 3 MSPB 254 (1980) (disparity defense should not have been raised for first time on PFR when evidence could have been discovered before record closing). The Board declined to apply the law of the case doctrine to bar new evidence concerning treatment of a comparator, Boucher v. USPS, 118 MSPR 640, 646-47 ¶¶ 16-19, 2012 MSPB 126 (2012) (overruled, Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022), to the extent that the disparate penalty analysis should extend beyond the same or similar offenses):

...Under the law of the case doctrine, a decision on an issue of law made at one stage of a proceeding becomes a binding precedent to be followed in successive stages of the same litigation. Pawn v. Department of Agriculture, 90 M.S.P.R. 473, ¶ 15 (2001). There are three recognized exceptions to the doctrine: (1) the availability of new and substantially different evidence; (2) a contrary decision of law by controlling authority that is applicable to the question at issue; or (3) a showing that the prior decision in the same appeal was clearly erroneous and would work a manifest injustice. MacLean v. Department of Homeland Security, 116 M.S.P.R. 562, ¶ 16 (2011); Hoover v. Department of the Navy, 57 M.S.P.R. 545, 553 (1993).

The administrative judge found that two exceptions to the law of the case doctrine are applicable here. Specifically, she found that: (1) there is new evidence in this case that the agency did not submit during the initial adjudication of the appellant's removal appeal, i.e., that Mr. McCainey was convicted of a drug-related offense but was not subsequently disciplined; and (2) notwithstanding the Board's remand instructions that she restate her findings regarding the reasonableness of the penalty in the event that she found the appellant failed to prove her affirmative defense of sex discrimination, a manifest injustice would result from a failure to reconsider the appropriateness of the penalty.

...

We also agree with the administrative judge's determination that a manifest injustice would result if the reasonableness of the penalty were not reexamined in light of this new information. If the Board were to refuse to consider this information, it would in effect be rewarding the agency for incorrectly stating that Mr. McCainey was not a suitable comparator because he had not been convicted, and for failing to produce evidence that is plainly material to the reasonableness of the penalty until late in the proceedings. The Board should not apply the law of the case doctrine under these circumstances.
