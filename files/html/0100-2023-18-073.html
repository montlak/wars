D. SHORTENED NOTICE PERIOD; CRIME PROVISION

Agencies may be faced with the problem of an employee believed to be too dangerous to keep in duty status during the notice period required for an adverse action. If adverse action is undertaken, 5 USC 7513 provides:

(b) An employee against whom an action is proposed is entitled to--

(1) At least 30 days' advance written notice, unless there is reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment may be imposed, stating the specific reasons for the proposed action;

(2) a reasonable time, but not less than 7 days, to answer orally and in writing and to furnish affidavits and other documentary evidence in support of the answer.

An OPM regulation implements the statutory shortened notice provisions, 5 CFR 752.404(d) (2022):

(d) Exceptions.

(1) Section 7513(b) of title 5, U.S. Code, authorizes an exception to the 30 days' advance written notice when the agency has reasonable cause to believe that the employee has committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment may be imposed and is proposing a removal or suspension, including indefinite suspension. This notice exception is commonly referred to as the "crime provision." This provision may be invoked even in the absence of judicial action.

(2) The advance written notice and opportunity to answer are not required for furlough without pay due to unforeseeable circumstances, such as sudden breakdowns in equipment, acts of God, or sudden emergencies requiring immediate curtailment of activities.

The statute creates two notice periods. The employee has the right to 30 days of notice prior to the effective date of an adverse action. The employee also has seven days to respond to an adverse action proposal. The 30-day notice period may be eliminated if the employer has reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime subject to a penalty of imprisonment. The seven-day period provided for the response to the adverse action may not be shortened, although the statute does not preclude the agency from placing the employee on paid administrative leave status during the period allowed for the reply. The distinction in treatment of the two notice periods was explained in Lockwood v. VA, 725 Fed. Appx. 938, 941 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP):

The notice of proposed indefinite suspension was issued pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7513. Under that statute, an agency may impose a serious penalty, such as indefinite suspension, "only for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service." Id. § 7513(a). When a disciplinary action is proposed, an employee is normally entitled to 30 days' advance written notice "stating the specific reasons for the proposed action." Id. § 7513(b)(1). However, a proviso commonly referred to as the "crime provision" permits the agency to shorten that notice period when "there is reasonable cause to believe the employee has committed a crime for which a sentence of imprisonment may be imposed." Id.; see 5 C.F.R. § 752.404(d)(1). In this case, the VA invoked the crime provision and reduced the length of the written notice period from the normal 30-day period and provided him with the statutory minimum of seven days to respond to the charges. See 5 U.S.C. § 7513(b)(2).

The agency has a choice. The employee can be placed on administrative leave during the notice period (depending on authority of an agency to use administrative, investigative, or notice leave under 5 USC 6329a-b, or the employee may, under carefully defined circumstances, be taken off the payroll entirely, except for the seven-day reply period. The employee may characterize and challenge the shortened notice period as a constructive adverse action if it is imposed in violation of the statute. A resulting appeal would still need to meet Board jurisdictional requirements. See Jones v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 93-3509) (if the notice period was shortened but the appellant was ordered back to work, the Board would lack jurisdiction over a constructive suspension of less than 14 days). The shortened notice period may be used to invoke an indefinite suspension. See Dunnington v. DOJ, 956 F.2d 1151 (Fed. Cir. 1992), post; Gonzales v. Dept. of Treasury, 37 MSPR 589, 592 (1988), post; cf. Perez v. DOJ, 480 F.3d 1309 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (opinion affirming but not requiring the use of a shortened notice period used in an indefinite suspension that was affirmed by a labor arbitrator), request for rehearing and rehearing en banc denied, Perez v. DOJ, 508 F.3d 1019 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (denial was accompanied by concurring and dissenting opinions that expressed confusion or division as to the issues in the case and the relation of the shortened notice period and the burden of proof necessary to sustain an indefinite suspension); Dawson v. USDA, 121 MSPR 495, 2014 MSPB 67 (2014) (Member Robbins concurring) (another opaque opinion involving what appeared to be a combined indefinite suspension and "emergency suspension" (a suspension during the adverse action notice period) based on alleged criminal misconduct).

There is one other requirement coupled with the "reasonable cause" condition: according to Dunnington v. DOJ, 956 F.2d 1151, 1155 (Fed. Cir. 1992), the agency must establish a nexus between the "alleged acts of misconduct and the efficiency of the service."
