a. Consideration of Mitigating Factors

The Board's authority is to review an agency's selection of a penalty to determine if the agency reasonably exercised its judgment by considering all relevant circumstances and, if necessary, adjusting the penalty. If the agency failed to engage in proper weighing of mitigating factors, the Board imposes the maximum reasonable penalty. Swann v. TVA, 18 MSPR 290, 293 (1983); see Saiz v. Dept. of Navy, 122 MSPR 521, 524 ¶ 5, 2015 MSPB 40 (2015) ("The deciding official need not show that he considered all the mitigating factors, and the Board will independently weigh the relevant factors only if the deciding official failed to demonstrate that he considered any specific, relevant mitigating factors before deciding on a penalty. Balouris, 107 M.S.P.R. 574, ¶ 6; Batten, 101 M.S.P.R. 222, ¶ 11."); Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 183 ¶ 13 (2002) ("Board will modify a penalty only when it finds that the agency failed to weigh the relevant factors or that the penalty the agency imposed clearly exceeded the bounds of reasonableness"); Cantu v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 253, 256-57 ¶¶ 4, 9 (2001) (penalty is adjusted to the maximum reasonable penalty if the agency fails to weigh relevant factors or the agency's judgment clearly exceeds the limits of reasonableness; "penalty of removal is within the outermost boundary of reasonable penalties for the sustained charges").

Of the Board's responsibility to consider mitigating factors, McCray v. VA, AT-0752-14-0810-I-1 (NP 2/9/2015), observed:

In addition, the appellant argues that the deciding official failed to consider all of the Douglas factors. However, it is well settled that not all of the factors are pertinent in every case. See Luna v. Social Security Administration, 85 M.S.P.R. 301, ¶ 16 (2000). Further, an agency need not demonstrate that it considered all mitigating factors in determining the penalty; similarly, the administrative judge need not contemplate mitigating factors not identified by the appellant as significant. See Yeschick v. Department of Transportation, 801 F.2d 383, 385 (Fed. Cir. 1986)....

The Douglas factors are generally relevant in selecting a penalty, but they are not a checklist that the agency must apply mechanically. O'Connell v. DOD, 374 Fed. Appx. 30 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP). Not every mitigating factor need be considered to sustain the penalty. Cf. Koester v. U.S. Park Police, 758 Fed. Appx. 925, 931 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP) ("The arbitrator is not required to consider each and every Douglas factor, but only those that are relevant. Malloy v. U.S. Postal Serv., 578 F.3d 1351, 1357 & n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2009)."). "The Board will independently weigh the relevant factors only if the deciding official failed to demonstrate that he considered any specific, relevant mitigating factors before deciding upon a penalty." Batten v. USPS, 101 MSPR 222, 227 ¶ 11, 2006 MSPB 35 (2006). The Board has determined, despite the admonition in Douglas that the agency should consider alternative sanctions, that there is "no requirement that the agency consider alternative penalties." Rather, "[t]he agency is required to show only that the penalty selected was reasonable." Thias v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 46, 49 (1986). The Thias holding is gibberish if the Board continues to maintain that Douglas has not been modified. If the agency considers alternative sanctions, the agency's position concerning mitigation is enhanced. If the proposed removal is reduced to a demotion in the agency decision letter, it does not diminish the seriousness of each individual charge but shows willingness of the agency to consider the appellant's previously unblemished record. Lindsey v. DOJ, 16 MSPR 310, 312 (1983); see Brewer v. American Battle Monuments Comm'n, 23 MSPR 554, 557 (1984) ("The agency's act of reconsidering the penalty is in and of itself some evidence that the agency officials conscientiously considered the relevant factors and attempted to strike a responsible balance within the limits of reasonableness."); Bradbie v. EEOC, 705 F.2d 1331 (Fed. Cir. 1983) ("The fact that she and Holmes, together and individually, may have considered other penalties prior to the final decision does not persuade us that the final decision was unreasonable. Rather, it indicates that the officials acted prudently and with care."). Among the errors that will lead the Board to abandon deference to an agency's penalty determination is consideration by the deciding official as aggravating factors that should have been considered in mitigation. See Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 185 ¶ 17 (2002)). [Refer to Chapter 17, "Penalty Determination," for court decisions reviewing MSPB penalty assessments.]
