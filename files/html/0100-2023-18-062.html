5. Citation of Standards of Conduct

A notice should be specific; yet it need not include a volume of law. OPM declined to compile a list of causes for removal that promote the efficiency of the service because the term has been interpreted by many appellate bodies over the years. The failure of a removal proposal or notice to specify a cause, published in some fashion, promoting the efficiency of the service is not a basis for setting the action aside. Penaloza v. DHHS, 4 MSPR 322, 327-28, 4 MSPB 389 (1980). An agency may describe the appellant's misconduct, prove it, and establish that the adverse action promotes service efficiency. Levick v. Dept. of Treasury, 75 MSPR 84, 90 (1997) (agency neither charged nor found the appellant guilty of violating any statute or agency regulation). An agency need not describe in detail all potentially proscribed employee conduct and resulting discipline. Brown v. FAA, 15 MSPR 224, 233 (1983). The Board upholds general standards of conduct as valid bases for the imposition of adverse action, rejecting the argument that the standards are void for vagueness as long as they permit an employee to obtain guidance from agency legal officials about particular actions that the employee may desire to take. Wild v. DHUD, 7 MSPR 595, 600 (1981). See Collazo v. SSA, AT-0752-11-0676-I-1 (NP 7/26/2012) ("Furthermore, under the precedent of our reviewing court, an agency is not required to specifically prohibit every type of misconduct. See, e.g., Brown v. Department of the Navy, 229 F.3d 1356, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (even though civilian employee had not received "formal notice" that he could be subject to removal for engaging in adulterous affair with Marine's spouse, employee's "common sense" should have forewarned him), cert. denied, 533 U.S. 949 (2001). Federal employees are expected to exercise good judgment, notwithstanding the lack of literal guidance from any agency rule, regulation, or other agency policy statement. Boyer v. Department of the Navy, 56 F.3d 84 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table) (NP)."); cf. Bidnick v. DOJ, AT-0752-14-0060-I-1 (NP 4/15/2015) ("However, we are aware of no law, rule, or regulation, requiring agencies to have a separate established protocol for addressing every conceivable category of misconduct.").

Of a "conduct unbecoming an officer" charge, in Meehan v. Macy, 392 F.2d 822 (D.C. Cir. 1968), remanded on other grounds, 425 F.2d 469 (1968), and 425 F.2d 472 (1969) (en banc), predating the Reform Act, the court observed, in its initial opinion:

[I]t is not feasible or necessary for the Government to spell out in detail all the conduct which will result in retaliation. The most conscientious of codes that define prohibited conduct of employee includes "catchall" clauses prohibiting employee "misconduct," "immorality," or "conduct unbecoming."

See Lowe v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 73, 76 (1994) (agency fleshed out a charge of unbecoming conduct in a proposal notice by describing the conduct and characterizing it as a violation of a sexual harassment prevention program). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Service Efficiency."]
