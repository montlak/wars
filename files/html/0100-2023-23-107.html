F. REEMPLOYMENT RIGHT

The correct remedy for violation of reemployment priority rights is for the agency to either separate the improperly hired employees and fill the positions correctly or hire those on the reemployment priority list who are qualified for the positions claimed. The remedy is retroactive to the time of the violation of the reemployment priority rights. Banaszek v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 98, 99 (1984); see Delalat v. Dept. of Navy, 86 MSPR 455, 463 ¶ 21 (2000) ("Where an agency has not placed an appellant on an RPL at the earliest date to which he was entitled to such placement, the Board may order the agency to retroactively place him on the RPL, consider him for positions that became vacant during the period of the retroactive placement, and appoint him to a position which he would have received if he had been properly placed on the RPL."). [Refer to Chapter 10, "Reemployment Rights," and Chapter 14, "Restoration Rights Upon Recovery From Occupational Injury or Illness."]

Discussion of reemployment rights, associated back pay obligations, and the need for the appellant to demonstrate a readiness to work for the period covered by the back pay obligation, was provided in King v. Dept. of Navy, 100 MSPR 116, 120-23 ¶¶ 9-16 (2005), aff'd, King v. Dept. of Navy, 167 Fed. Appx. 191 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP):

As discussed above, the agency was ordered to reconstruct its selections for GS-11 security specialists and equivalent positions in the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard commuting area for the period from June 7, 2000, to June 6, 2002, and to give the appellant RPL consideration for positions filled during that period. The agency determined that the first GS-11 security specialist or equivalent position filled during the relevant time period was filled on September 10, 2001. See King, 98 M.S.P.R. 547 ¶¶ 11-17. Accordingly, the agency maintains that any back pay obligation begins on that date. The appellant, in contrast, asserts that the agency's back pay obligation begins on June 7, 2000. We agree with the agency.

An employee, who fully recovers from a compensable injury within one year from the date eligibility for compensation began, is entitled to immediate and unconditional restoration to her former position or an equivalent one. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(a); Hall v. Department of the Navy, 94 M.S.P.R. 262 ¶ 17 (2003). In contrast, an employee, who fully recovers from a compensable injury more than one year from the date eligibility for compensation begins, is only entitled to priority consideration for restoration to her former position or an equivalent one. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(b); Hall, 94 M.S.P.R. 262 ¶ 17. Stated differently, in the second instance, the employee has no entitlement to a position until one is found through the priority consideration process.

It is undisputed that the appellant recovered more than a year after her eligibility for OWCP benefits began. Accordingly, she was not entitled to immediate restoration to her former position or an equivalent one, but only to priority consideration for such a position. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 353.301(a) & (b). The record shows that the agency reconstructed its hiring activity for the relevant time period and that, had the appellant been placed on the RPL as she should have been, the first position that she would have been entitled to was filled on September 10, 2001. See King v. Department of the Navy, 98 M.S.P.R. 547 ¶¶ 11-17. Thus, it is that date on which the agency violated the appellant's restoration rights, and it is from that date that the agency's back pay obligation begins. Nothing that we are aware of entitles an individual on the RPL to pay for the period prior to her selection from the RPL and, therefore, nothing entitles the appellant to back pay for the period prior to her retroactive selection.

The appellant was not ready, willing, and able to return to work with the agency.

When the Board finds that an employee has been the victim of an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action, the goal is to place the employee in the circumstances she would have been in had the personnel action never taken place. Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984); Bartel v. Federal Aviation Administration, 24 M.S.P.R. 560, 564-65 (1984). Consistent with that goal, the Office of Personnel Management's regulations and the Board's case law provide that an individual is not entitled to back pay for any period of time during which she was not "ready, willing, and able" to perform her duties because of an incapacitating illness or injury, or for reasons unrelated to or not caused by the unjustified or unwarranted personnel action. 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(c)(1); Lyle v. Department of the Treasury, 85 M.S.P.R. 324 ¶ 6 (2000); Bullock v. Department of the Air Force, 80 M.S.P.R. 361 ¶ 13 (1998); see Bartel, 24 M.S.P.R. at 565. These rules apply, not only where, for example, the Board reverses an adverse action, but also where an individual has recovered from a compensable injury and successfully appeals a denial of restoration rights. See Detherage v. Department of the Air Force, 33 M.S.P.R. 470, 473 (1987).

The agency bears the initial burden of proving that it has provided an appellant the appropriate amount of back pay. See, e.g., Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361 ¶ 11. Where, however, the agency produces "concrete and positive evidence, as opposed to a mere theoretical argument" that the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to work during all or part of the period during which back pay is claimed, the burden of proof shifts to the appellant to show her entitlement to back pay. See Bullock, 80 M.S.P.R. 361 ¶ 11; Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 60 M.S.P.R. 498, 501-502 (1994), quoting Piccone v. United States, 186 Ct. Cl. 752, 407 F.2d 866, 876 (1969).

Like all civilian positions at Puget Sound Naval Shipyard, the GS-11 security specialist position offered to the appellant required a security clearance. The appellant, however, refused to answer two questions on the clearance questionnaire, and, therefore, could not be granted an interim clearance necessary for her to report for duty. In our May 18, 2005 decision, we discussed the appellant's failure to answer the questions at length and found nothing improper in the agency's requiring the appellant to complete the security clearance form prior to reporting for duty. See King v. Department of the Navy, 98 M.S.P.R. 547 ¶¶ 18-23. We also found that we could not discern why answering the two questions was difficult for the appellant, a former security specialist and now an attorney. Id. ¶ 21. Finally, we found that the agency had not acted in bad faith and that its actions did not constitute noncompliance with the Board's order. Id.

As stated above, an agency need not pay back pay for the periods of time that an individual was not ready, willing, and able to report for duty because of reasons unrelated to or not caused by the unjustified or unwarranted personnel action. Here, the appellant was not ready, willing, and able to report to the position offered by the agency because she lacked the security clearance required to work at the agency facility. Her lack of a clearance was unrelated to the agency's violation of her restoration rights. Accordingly, at the time the appellant failed to complete the security clearance form in the summer of 2004, she was not ready, willing, and able to work at the agency facility.

Finally, we must determine whether there was any period between the date of the wrongful personnel action, September 10, 2001, and the summer of 2004 when the appellant was ready, willing, and able to report for work with the agency. The essence of the question is whether there was a point in time at which the appellant would have completed the security clearance process. The agency asserts that the best indicator of what the appellant would have done earlier is what she did do in 2004. The appellant offers nothing to contest that reasoning, and, in the absence of other evidence, we find it reasonable to conclude that at no point between September 10, 2001, and the summer of 2004 would the appellant have been ready, willing, and able to report for duty.
