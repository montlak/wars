B. EXAMINATION OF WITNESSES

The Judges' Handbook explains the initial procedures for witness examination in Chapter 10, section 12(b):

b. Administering the Oath. Each witness must be sworn in by the AJ or court reporter but because certain individuals may object to taking an oath based on religious grounds, before swearing in each witness the AJ should ask if he or she has an objection to taking an oath. If not, then the oath or affirmation may be worded: "Do you solemnly swear (or affirm) that the testimony you give in this proceeding will be the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth so help you God (or so help you)"? If recalled, a witness need not be resworn, but should be reminded that he or she is still under oath or affirmation....

The Hearings Procedures Guide explains how the Board views the function of witness examination during a hearing. Although the Hearings Procedures Guide has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook, it remains an excellent source for guidance and is still used for training and orientation. The Guide states, at 15:

D. PROCEDURE FOR EXAMINING WITNESSES

1. Oath or Affirmation

The AJ has the authority to administer oaths and affirmations. 5 CFR Sec. 1201.41(b)(1).

Every witness before testifying is required to declare that the witness will testify truthfully, by oath or affirmation administered in a form calculated to awaken the witness' conscience and impress the witness' mind with the duty to do so. Fed. R. Evid. 603.

...

Privilege of testifying cannot be denied solely because the witness will not take an oath or affirmation. Gordon v. State of Idaho, 778 F.2d 1397, 1401 (9th Cir. 1985); United States v. Looper, 419 F.2d 1405, 1407 (4th Cir. 1969).

If a witness refuses to take an oath/affirmation, the least restrictive means of assuring that the witness will testify truthfully should be used. The Federal Courts have held that the following language suffices to satisfy the Fed. R. Evid. 603: "I understand that I must tell the truth"; "I agree to testify under penalty of perjury"; "I understand that if I testify falsely I may be subject to criminal prosecution"; "I understand that I must accurately state the facts." See Gordon v. State of Idaho at 778 F.2d 1397, 1401 (9th Cir. 1985).

2. Right to Confront and Examine Witnesses

Although the agency has the burden of proving the charges it brings, both parties have a right to call witnesses and participate in direct and cross-examination. McKenzie v. Dept. of Interior, 16 MSPR 397, modified, 18 MSPR 12, vacated in part and affirmed in part, 18 MSPR 377 (1983).

The Hearings Procedures Guide provides further explanation about the method of examination of witnesses, at 16-17:

5. Method and Scope of Examination

a. Kinds of Examination by Parties

Testimony of a witness should normally be adduced in response to specific questions. McCormick on Evid., 3rd Ed. HB-Sec. 5. However, when a pro se appellant is testifying, the AJ may allow narrative testimony.

Testimony can be in person or via the telephone.

b. Administrative Judge's Control Over Examination of Witness

The AJ is responsible for convening the hearing and regulating the course of the proceeding. 5 CFR Sec. 1201.41(b)(6).

The AJ is responsible for exercising reasonable control over the mode and order of interrogating witnesses so as to:

(1) Make the interrogation and presentation of evidence effective for ascertaining the truth;

(2) avoid needless consumption of time; and

(3) protect witnesses from harassment or undue embarrassment. Alford v. United States, 282 U.S. 687 (1931).

c. Order of Examination; Re-examination

(1) The AJ has discretion in arranging the order of examination and cross-examination. Franks v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 387 (1983).

d. Scope of Cross-examination

(1) Cross-examination is matter of judicial discretion. The AJ's limitation on scope of the examination is not improper even though not prescribed by the Federal Rules of Evidence. Behensky v. Dept. of Transp. 27 MSPR 690, 696 (1985).

(2) Fed. R. Evid. 611(b), however, limits cross-examination to:

(a) Matters brought out on direct examination and inferences naturally drawn from those matters; and

(b) matters affecting the credibility of the witnesses.
