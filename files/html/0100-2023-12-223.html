1. Determining Whether a Remand Is Necessary

When the Board determines that the AJ's initial decision contained factual or legal errors, or both, the Board determines whether to send the appeal back to the AJ for another initial decision, with proper analysis, with or without further hearing. The remand does not provide an opportunity to raise issues that could have been, but were not, raised in the appeal the first time around, New v. VA, 99 MSPR 404, 414 ¶ 21 (2005):

As discussed above, the appellant and her attorney representative could have but did not raise the April 1987 settlement agreement as a defense to the 1988 poor overall attendance charge in New I. Where an argument could have been raised on an initial appeal, it is inappropriate to consider that argument on a second appeal following remand. Hoover v. Department of the Navy, 57 M.S.P.R. 545, 554 n.4 (1993). "This widely accepted rule furthers the important value of procedural efficiency, 18 C. Wright and A. Miller, FEDERAL PRACTICE AND PROCEDURE § 4478 (1981), and prevents the bizarre result that a party who has chosen not to argue a point on a first appeal should stand better as regards the law of the case than one who argued and lost." Id. (internal quotation omitted); see 18B Wright, Miller & Cooper, id., § 4478 (2002) ("Evidence that could have been presented earlier commonly is not considered, in keeping with the general rules that discourage slovenly or ill-considered approaches to the first trial"). Therefore, we find that the law of the case doctrine precludes the Board from addressing now whether the settlement agreement is a defense to any of the charged absences for purposes of the appellant's priority restoration claim....

Many Board decisions correct errors of the AJ and sustain, reverse, or modify the result reached by the AJ without a remand. If the record before the AJ, and before the Board on PFR, is sufficiently well developed, the Board may perform its own factual analysis, particularly if there are no credibility issues, Slater v. DHS, 108 MSPR 419, 426 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 73 (2008):

We find it unnecessary to remand this case for application of the correct legal standard because we can resolve it based upon the existing record. Generally, remand is unnecessary where the existing record is sufficient for meaningful review. See Smith v. Office of Personnel Management, 100 M.S.P.R. 500, ¶ 6 (2005). Remand is unnecessary here because both parties addressed the relevant factual dispute, the appellant's medical status, because the record is complete with regard to this medical question and there is no need for additional development of the record to resolve it, and because we need not base any findings on witnesses' demeanor. See Smedley v. Office of Personnel Management, 108 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 13 (2008); Wiley v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 535 ¶ 11 n.4 (2006), aff'd, 218 Fed. Appx. 1001 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (Table); Hagan v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 10 (2005). Furthermore, notwithstanding the AJ's legal error, we find it unnecessary to remand this case for further proceedings because, applying the correct, lower legal standard we find that the agency has not shown that the AJ erred in not upholding the removal. See Valenzuela v. Department of the Army, 107 M.S.P.R. 549, ¶ 13 (2007).

Similarly, Fisher v. EPA, 108 MSPR 296, 305-06 ¶ 14, 2008 MSPB 51 (2008), noted:

...Although the administrative judge did not resolve the merits of the IRA appeal because of the jurisdictional dismissal, we find it appropriate to adjudicate the merits of the appellant's claims on review considering that: (1) the appellant was notified of the requirements for establishing his claims; (2) he was afforded a full opportunity to submit evidence and argument in that regard and to rebut the agency's evidence; (3) the record is completely developed; and (4) the appellant neither requested a hearing before the administrative judge nor contests on petition for review the administrative judge's decision to adjudicate the case based on the written record. [S]ee Sutton v. Department of Justice, 94 M.S.P.R. 4, ¶ 16 (2003), aff'd, 97 Fed. Appx. 322 (Fed. Cir. 2004)....

See Justice v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 379, 393 ¶ 28 (2001) (although the appellant was not specifically notified by the AJ of what was required to establish a disability discrimination claim, remand was unnecessary because undisputed record evidence showed he failed to establish the claim); Rosales v. OPM, 41 MSPR 590, 592-93 (1989) (reversing a determination that the appellant's appeal and PFR were untimely, but affirming, based on the evidentiary record and on the lack of a hearing request, OPM's denial of retirement benefits).

In Schrodt v. USPS, 79 MSPR 609, 617 (1998), a deficient initial decision was set aside in a final decision that included factual analysis without a remand:

As the record is complete and a hearing has been held, the Board can proceed directly to the ultimate issue of whether appellant has shown by a preponderance of the evidence that his termination was motivated by discrimination. See Pierce v. Social Security Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 01961642, slip op. at 2 (Feb. 20, 1998); see also Jeffries v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01962760, slip op. at 2 (Mar. 10, 1998) ("where the record is fully developed...the factual inquiry can proceed directly to...,") citing United States Postal Service Board of Governors v. Aikens, 460 U.S. 711, 103 S. Ct. 1478, 75 L. Ed.2d 403 (1983), and St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, 509 U.S. 502, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 125 L. Ed.2d 407 (1993). See also Kutyna v. National Aeronautics & Space Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 01944637, slip op. at 3 (Dec. 4, 1995), and cases cited therein.

In OPM annuity appeals, the Board may issue a nonfinal decision (that is not judicially reviewable) correcting an initial decision by vacating it and remanding the case directly to OPM for further processing. The procedure avoids the possibility of forcing an appellant to seek judicial review of a final Board decision while the appellant is also required to litigate related issues before OPM. See Thoin v. OPM, 84 MSPR 322, 323-24 ¶ 4 (1999). On remand to OPM, the Board may set a time for OPM to process the remanded case. See Brown v. OPM, CH-844E-16-0273-I-1 (NP 6/13/2022) ("OPM shall issue the new reconsideration decision within 60 calendar days from the date of this Remand Order and shall advise the appellant of her right to file an appeal with the Board's Central Regional Office if she disagrees with that new decision. See Litzenberger v. Office of Personnel Management, 88 M.S.P.R. 419, 424 (2001).").

At the PFR level, a remand may become unnecessary if there is a pending appeal raising issues that would otherwise be subject to the remand. See Lentz v. Dept. of Interior, SF-1221-15-0688-W-1 (NP 6/30/2022) ("Courts have the inherent authority, in the interest of judicial efficiency, to dismiss an action because of the pendency of another action, so long as an identity of issues exists and the controlling issues in the dismissed action will be determined in another lawsuit. Kinler v. General Services Administration, 44 M.S.P.R. 262, 263 (1990). In most cases of judicial efficiency, we would dismiss the subsequent appeal and the prior appeal would be adjudicated. Id.; O'Leary v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 124, ¶ 7 (2001).").

The remand process is followed when an appeal is returned to the Board from the Federal Circuit for additional factual findings. See, e.g., Perske v. OPM, 56 MSPR 442, 445 n.4 (1993). The Board's remand order is a nonfinal order, meaning that it would not ordinarily be a Board decision subject to judicial review. See Wildberger v. SBA, 65 MSPR 673, 675-76 (1994). Under Perske, supra, when an appeal is remanded for additional factual findings, the Board will not reconsider issues already raised and resolved through a party's PFR. Board decisions remanded to the regional offices should be expedited. (Memo of February 27, 1980, from Deputy Managing Director to Chief Appeals Officers). In practice, the Board's regional offices adjudicate remands under the same 120-day guidelines that govern processing of the rest of their cases.
