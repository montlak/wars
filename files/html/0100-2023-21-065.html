j. Absence; Use of Leave

Performance and attendance are factors considered in a disability retirement claim, Rozar v. OPM, 61 MSPR 136, 142-43 (1994):

The record shows in this regard that shortly before the appellant resigned, her performance in a critical element of her position was unacceptable and that she had been given a summary rating of unacceptable. The record further reveals that the illness associated with her fainting episodes forced her to miss an immense amount of work time from September 1989 until her resignation.

Under Tucker v. OPM, 19 MSPR 58, 58 n.* (1984), absence alone does not explain why a condition renders the appellant disabled, but in Ryan v. OPM, 20 MSPR 6, 7-8 (1984):

Appellant filed a timely petition for review contending that the presiding official, in not finding a service deficiency based on his absence of sixteen (16) days in one month, ignored an earlier Board decision, Meighen v. Office of Personnel Management, 7 MSPB 82 (1981), which held that the ability to perform forty-five (45) percent of the normal work period does not constitute useful and efficient service. He further contends that the presiding official made conclusions contrary to the medical evidence in the record and erroneously relied on inapplicable cases in not finding that appellant was totally disabled.

In Meighen, supra, appellant was absent 144 days out of 241 days in 1979, and 95% of the absences were due to illness. In the instant case, appellant was absent only 30 days in two years because of illness, and these absences do not establish a deficiency in service with respect to attendance.

See Bogumill v. OPM, DE-844E-13-0207-I-1 (NP 2/14/2014) ("Thus, because the record fails to establish the extent to which the appellant's claimed condition caused her absences, we agree with the administrative judge that the appellant's absences are insufficient to establish her entitlement to a disability annuity."); Fisher v. OPM, 85 MSPR 30, 33 ¶ 6 (1999) ("It is well settled that absences alone are insufficient to show that an employee suffers from a disabling condition."); Hupp v. OPM, 64 MSPR 273, 277 (1994) ("While absences from work do not conclusively establish that an employee is incapable of rendering useful and efficient service, they are nonetheless a factor worthy of consideration in judging disability. Coleman v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 M.S.P.R. 570, 574 (1990)."); Brownscombe v. OPM, 37 MSPR 382, 387 (1988) (AJ properly allowed OPM to reference a criminal conviction explaining the basis for a portion of appellant's absence); Devaney v. OPM, 30 MSPR 261, 265 (1986) ("Because the evidence establishes that appellant's ailments impaired his work performance, his absence is worthy of consideration in establishing his entitlement to disability."); McCoy v. OPM, 28 MSPR 185, 188 n.4 (1985) ("The presiding official discounted the importance of the 300 days absence, stating that 'absence alone, does not establish disability for purposes of this type of appeal.' However, proof of absence is a factor worthy of consideration in judging disability when corroborating evidence has been introduced establishing impaired performance of one's duties. See Trainor v. Office of Personnel Management, 12 MSPB 280, 281-82 (1982)."); Tebay v. OPM, 24 MSPR 92, 94-95 (1984) ("While we recognize that the proof of excessive approved absences provides some support for appellant's disability claim, the standard form submitted by appellant's superior does not disclose observance of any physical problems at work, stating only that appellant's performance was inadequate due to excessive absenteeism. Further, a report of a follow-up phone conversation between the supervisor and an undisclosed OPM investigator corroborates and expands upon the supervisor's initial statement by confirming that the supervisor did not witness any physical problems and that appellant's service deficiency was strictly related to absenteeism.").

The Board may look to both ability to perform in light duty positions as well as extensive leave in evaluating disability retirement. See Thomas v. OPM, 9 MSPR 377, 379 (1982) ("A letter submitted by a psychiatrist states that appellant suffers from an occupational problem and that she is unable to continue in her job assignment. However, as we note above, appellant has performed her duties in the light duty position and has not requested leave for prolonged periods because of her alleged disability.").
