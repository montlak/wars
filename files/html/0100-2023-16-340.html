a. Interest

Back pay awards under 5 USC 5596 carry interest to the extent provided through the December 22, 1987, amendments to 5 USC 5596. Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation and AFGE Local 1978, 33 FLRA 671, 680 (1988) (referencing 1988 USCCAN 1329-1, 1329-428-429 and Pub. L 100-202); see USDA, Forest Serv., Chattahoochee-Oconee Nat'l Forests, Gainesville and NFFE Local 1329, 45 FLRA 1310, 1311 (1992). [Refer to Chapter 9, "Damages, Penalties, Expenses, Interest," concerning the details of the interest statute.]

The award of interest is available in a ULP case in addition to other monetary remedies provided by statutes, e.g., the Fair Labor Standards Act. In SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 55 FLRA 246, 250-51 (1999), employees received back pay and liquidated damages for FLSA overtime awarded by an arbitrator. FLRA awarded interest to remedy the ULP of failure by the agency to timely implement the award:

The Respondent's sole defense is that because the FLSA provides the basis for the monetary payments awarded by the Arbitrator, any determination about entitlement to interest due to delay in making those payments also must be based on the FLSA, which makes no provision for interest.

As correctly argued by the Respondent, an allowance of interest on a claim against the United States requires an explicit waiver of sovereign immunity by Congress. See Zumerling v. Marsh, 783 F.2d 1032, 1034 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Zumerling). See also Library of Congress v. Shaw. The court in Zumerling held that the FLSA contained no such explicit waiver, and found no other federal statute that would permit the payment of post-judgment interest in that case. The Respondent also relies on Doyle v. United States, supra. However, neither Doyle nor Zumerling considered whether the Back Pay Act, which was amended in 1987, provides a waiver of sovereign immunity. It is well established that the requisite express waiver of sovereign immunity not contained in a given statute can be supplied by a separate statute. Brown v. Secretary of Army, 918 F.2d 214, 216 (D.C. Cir. 1990), cert. denied, sub nom. Brown v. Stone, 502 U.S. 810 (1991), citing Loeffler v. Frank, 486 U.S. 549 (1988).

As noted by the Charging Party, the Back Pay Act explicitly provides that unfair labor practice remedies shall be payable with interest. 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1) and (2)(A). This operates as an explicit waiver of sovereign immunity, which has been held to operate in cases covered by the Back Pay Act. Ward v. Brown, 22 F.3d 516, 520 (2nd Cir. 1994). At least one court has indicated that interest is payable on federal FLSA claims, as long as it does not duplicate the payment of liquidated damages. Parker v. Burnley, 703 F. Supp. 925, 927 (N.D. Ga. 1988).[12]

[12] The payment of interest in this situation does not duplicate the payment of liquidated damages and is not contrary to the general rule that employee overtime pay is computed under either the FLSA or the title 5 overtime provisions, but not both. See American Federation of Government Employees, Local 2022 and U.S. Department of the Army, Headquarters, 101st Airborne Division, Fort Campbell, Kentucky, 40 FLRA 371, 375 (1991). Interest is payable here because of the Respondent's violation of section 7116(a), not section 5545. Cf. Lanehart v. Horner, 818 F.2d 1574, 1576 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (ordering employee overtime pay rate higher than mandated by FLSA or section 5545(c) because of operation of leave statutes).

The amount of recovery due the employees as a result of their incorrect classification by the Respondent as FLSA-exempt employees was based on the FLSA. The Respondent has conceded its incorrect classification. When the Respondent failed to make the proper payments under the Vaughn award, which has herein been determined to be an unfair labor practice, the employees suffered further monetary losses because they were deprived of the use of that money. The remedy for this new violation by the Respondent, the withholding of the payment awarded by the Arbitrator to remedy the Respondent's incorrect classification of unit employees, is determined independent of the FLSA. The Back Pay Act, including its interest provision, is the appropriate basis to remedy the violation.[13]

[13] Because this interest is based on the Respondent's unfair labor practice, and not on a violation of the FLSA, interest begins to accrue at the time that the Respondent was obligated to pay the backpay and liquidated damages, rather than the time it committed the FLSA violation. The Respondent was obligated to pay the backpay and liquidated damages when the award became final and binding. Under section 7122(b) of the Statute, if, as in the Vaughn award, no exceptions are filed to an award during the 30-day period after the award is served on the parties, it becomes final and binding.
