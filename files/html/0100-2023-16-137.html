3. Surveillance

Attendance by managers--school principals--at union organization meetings for teachers, constituted surveillance. It was not necessary to show that employees were affected by the presence of the managers. The test is whether the action by the supervisors tended to have a chilling effect on the exercise of employees' protected rights. It was reasonable to infer, given the awareness of the employees of the presence of their supervisors, that they might have felt inhibited by their supervisors' presence and that they might have been reluctant to participate in the meeting and ask questions. The presence of the supervisors violated § 7116(a)(1). Dept. of Army, Ft. Bragg Sch. and North Carolina Fed'n of Teachers, 3 FLRA 364, 375-76 (1980) (ALJ Decision). A supervisor's unnecessary presence in a union office while representation functions were being conducted had a threatening and chilling effect on unit employees. SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 7 FLRA 823, 830 (1982) (ALJ Decision). A supervisor's unnecessary observation of a steward, coupled with statements that his ULP charges were worthless, that there would be repercussions, and indirect statements that the steward should not conduct union business, along with improper charges of AWOL against the steward, interfered with protected rights. Naval Supply Ctr., San Diego and AFGE Local 1399, 21 FLRA 792, 806-09 (1986) (ALJ Decision). It was a ULP for a supervisor to leave a union official with the impression that his work and conduct were under greater surveillance because a ULP was filed. GSA, Nat'l Capital Region and NFFE, 4 FLRA 502, 509 (1980) (ALJ Decision). A manager's statement to employees after an election that they would be closely watched because they were supporters of the union violated § 7116(a)(1). 162nd Tactical Fighter Group, Arizona Air Nat'l Guard and AFGE Local 2924, 18 FLRA 583, 586 (1985). A grievance was arbitrable that management was improperly surveilling union officers. 5 USC 7103(a)(9)(C)(ii) defines a grievance as any complaint by a labor organization concerning a violation of law, and the union alleged a violation of 5 USC 7116(a)(1) through management's surveillance. NLRB and NLRBPA, 71 FLRA 1149 (2020) (Chairman Kiko concurring; Member Abbott dissenting).

Management did not involve itself in unlawful restraint when its labor relations officer took notes and observed a union demonstration outside an activity building. The demonstration was public, meant to be seen. There is a distinction between surveillance of a union meeting or the private meetings of employees, and public gatherings. Bureau of Engraving & Printing and NTEU, 15 FLRA 977, 982 (1984) (ALJ Decision). The agency committed no ULP when its security officer observed some informational picketing for 10 minutes and took notes of what the signs said. There was no indication that the officer could or did identify any of the picketing employees, Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Base and AFGE Local 2225, 15 FLRA 987, 989 (1984):

Informational picketing by its very nature is public and intended to be observed. It is designed to draw attention to the union's message and to give employees a chance to publicly demonstrate their support for the position stated on the picket signs. Thus, observation by management is to be expected and, unless under the circumstances management's actions accompanying its observation would reasonably tend to interfere with, restrain or coerce employees...there can be no finding that the Statue has been violated....

Surveillance can be through a two-way lens. A union official had a statutory right to photograph a worksite that he thought was operated in a manner that violated labor contract safety provisions. Management's observation of the official, and questioning of him, was not, however, a ULP. The photos were taken on a military installation and may have been subject to restrictions on photography of high-security areas. The agency's actions were not unreasonable, given the security considerations. Defense Property Disposal Region, Ogden and Leavitt, 24 FLRA 653, 657 (1986).
