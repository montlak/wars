h. Obstruction, Theft, or Mistreatment of Mail

The Postal Service is responsible for efficient delivery of all classes of mail. Womack v. USPS, 22 MSPR 196, 200 (1984) (mitigating a supervisor's removal to a suspension for the violation of postal procedures, involving the changing of color codes, resulting in the delay of mails); see Carter v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3467) (removal of a mail carrier for delaying mail delivery); Spruill v. USPS, 84 MSPR 36, 42 ¶ 12 (1999) (not sustaining a charge of delaying mail when the agency did not show when the letters came into the appellant's office; the agency must prove that the appellant possessed the letters and delayed in processing them); Walsh v. USPS, 74 MSPR 627, 640 (1997) (removal of a city carrier charged with discarding 1,100 pieces of deliverable mail); Edwards v. USPS, 26 MSPR 85, 86-87 (1985) (removal of a city carrier for misdelivery of mail and wrongfully receiving money); Littlejohn v. USPS, 25 MSPR 478, 480 (1984) (removal of a city carrier for delaying mail by placing deliverable mail into the "waste section" so that it would be destroyed and he would not have to deliver it); Robinson v. USPS, 14 MSPR 259, 260 (1982) (removal for the failure to deliver mail after an inquiry determined that snow conditions did not prevent its delivery); Laughlin v. USPS, 12 MSPR 183, 186 (1982) (removal for, among other charges, failing to protect mail properly); Burton v. USPS, 11 MSPR 467, 468-69 (1982) (removal of a city carrier for delaying the mail); cf. Dunmars v. USPS, 78 Fed. Appx. 114, 116 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (removal for several charges, including mishandling an accountable item and delaying or curtailing mail).

Obstruction of the mails was cause for dismissal. Jackson v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-598). Obstruction of mails by storing undelivered mail in a personal locker resulted in removal mitigated to a 45-day suspension as a result of the employee's 20 years of otherwise satisfactory service, a heavy volume of mail delivery, physical problems of the appellant, a demonstration of remorse, and potential for rehabilitation. Robinson v. USPS, 28 MSPR 681, 688 (1985); see Toth v. USPS, 76 MSPR 36, 40-41 (1997) (mitigating removal to a 30-day suspension for the mistreatment and delay of mail; the appellant had a good past record and his potential for rehabilitation was excellent). Unauthorized opening or tampering with the mail justified removal because it violates a fundamental duty of Postal Service employees, regardless of their positions. Daniels v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-704). The Federal Circuit determined that firing a letter carrier who discarded excess "junk" mail was not an excessive penalty. Postal regulations must be obeyed. Robinson v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2788). But see Oberman v. USPS, 13 MSPR 218, 223 (1982) (mitigating removal to a suspension of a security police officer who removed some magazines, destined for destruction, from the dead letter branch; the appellant's position required great integrity, but, his record was long and distinguished and the incident would not have a lasting effect on his ability to perform). The misrouting (to expedite delivery) of a piece of mail, for no personal gain but as a favor to another person, constituted a violation of postal procedures but did not warrant removal of an employee with an otherwise good and long service record. The penalty was mitigated to a suspension and demotion to a nonsupervisory position. Halper v. USPS, 91 MSPR 170, 173-74 ¶¶ 8-11 (2002).

Obstruction of or misuse of "test mail" may result in removal. Test mail has the same protection by law as any other mail, and it has been routinely used by the Postal Service in investigations of employee conduct. McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR 192, 207 n.11 (1992); see Reynolds v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3371) (test mail was entitled to same the protection as other mail); Thompson v. USPS, 596 F. Supp. 628, 634 (W.D. Va. 1984) (discharge in a mixed case appeal for the theft of items from test mail: "A violation of this fiduciary duty by an agent or employee of the Postal Service is treated harshly, and theft is considered among the most serious offenses."); Leftridge v. USPS, 56 MSPR 340, 347 (1993) (adverse action based on surveillance of the treatment of test mail).

If the employee is entrusted with custody of the mails, the improper treatment of mail of relatively small value may warrant removal, as in Ruiz v. USPS, 59 MSPR 76, 80-81 (1993), affirming removal of a letter carrier:

Finally, the appellant argues that the misconduct is of a de minimis nature, as it involved a theft of $25, and urges mitigation of the penalty on this basis. We find that the appellant's conduct, opening mail and removing its contents for his personal use, strikes at the heart of the agency's mission and breaches the trust placed in the appellant by reason of his position. As a letter-carrier, the appellant occupied a position that placed him in control of and with custody over the items at issue. Removal has been deemed an appropriate penalty where an employee takes unauthorized personal possession of property entrusted to his care, regardless of the monetary value of the stolen property. See DeWitt v. Department of the Navy, 747 F.2d 1442, 1445 (Fed. Cir. 1984)...Underwood v. Department of Defense, 53 MSPR 355, 359-61, aff'd, 980 F.2d 744 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). Smith v. US Postal Service, 31 MSPR 508 (1986), cited by the appellant, involved tampering with mail that had already been disposed of as trash because of its undeliverability, and the appellant was a custodian, not a letter carrier. Id. at 510. Thus, the items were not within the employee's control and custody, and the misconduct did not directly breach the duties and responsibilities of his position. See also Kelly v. Department of Health and Human Services, 46 MSPR 358, 363 (1990) (mitigation warranted when stolen items were not placed within the employee's control because of her position with the agency).

Therefore, we find that the penalty is reasonable based upon the charge sustained by the administrative judge. The appellant's misconduct is serious and directly detrimental to the agency's mission, mail delivery, and the appellant's duties. The appellant's lack of a prior disciplinary record and his twenty-two years of service are insufficient under these circumstances to warrant mitigation of the penalty.

See Gandia v. USPS, 556 Fed. Appx. 945 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) (removal of letter carrier for removal of gift cards from undeliverable mail); Mosley v. USPS, 501 Fed. Appx. 960, 964 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP) ("Mr. Mosley points out that the gift card had a value of only $25, that he had served for more than 25 years in the Postal Service with a largely unblemished record, and that he is a veteran. Mr. Mosley cannot benefit from the small value of the card, however, because he admits that he did not know the card's value until he used it in January. The administrative judge noted the length of Mr. Mosley's career in the Postal Service but determined that the threat his conduct posed to the integrity of the mail, along with his lack of remorse and the Postal Service's loss of trust in him, justified removal. Under these circumstances, we conclude that the Board did not abuse its discretion in finding that this misconduct reasonably warranted separation."); Vancura v. USPS, 119 Fed. Appx. 272, 274 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (removal of a mail handler with a long record of good service who, despite warnings, entered a facility used to store undeliverable mail and removed 42 compact discs; "[w]e agree with the Postal Service that those entrusted with the mail must meet rigorous standards of honesty and credibility, and that the remedy imposed, after several warnings is within the discretionary scope contemplated by the Douglas factors"); Rizor v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3780) (removal of a window clerk for the theft of promotional coupons of small value from the mail: "Theft of mail, whether deliverable or undeliverable is considered a serious matter by the Postal Service, and the Postal Service provides clear notice to employees that theft could result in removal." (distinguishing Ciulla v. USPS, 37 MSPR 627, 631-32 (1988), post ("[N]ot only did [Ciulla] readily admit his conduct but he did not present any 'incredible' or 'obviously fabricated' exculpatory testimony."))); cf. Eagleheart v. USPS, SF-0752-06-0167-B-2 (NP 3/28/2011) ("[B]ecause the agency charged the appellant with mistreatment of mail matter and not unauthorized possession of the missing CDs, it was not required to establish that the appellant had any CDs in his possession. Furthermore, the agency's inability to locate the CDs is not probative of the charge of mistreating the mail. See Leftridge v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 M.S.P.R. 340, 344 (1993) (the agency's failure to search for or locate missing mail is not probative of the charge of mishandling mail); see also Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 420, 425 (1996) (circumstantial evidence can constitute proof by preponderant evidence).").

"Violating the sanctity of the mail is an egregious offense that goes directly to the heart of an employee's relationship with the USPS." Reynolds v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3371) (applying the principle to test mail). Misappropriation of the contents of three letters warranted removal of a postmaster in Henscheid v. USPS, 14 MSPR 422, 425 (1983) ("Appellant's offense was unacceptable for a postmaster in a one-person post office and in a prominent management position."); see Butler v. USPS, 37 MSPR 457, 463 (1988) (removal of an inexpensive magazine from the office by a custodian who was not responsible for the custody of mails warranted a 60-day suspension rather than removal); cf. Lavalley v. USPS, 49 MSPR 129, 132 (1991) (removal of a postal worker who "opened and examined five pieces of sealed mail, and showed the contents of two of the envelopes to his coworkers; such conduct strikes at the very heart of his duties and the agency's mission"). Diversion of the shipment of some merchandise of substantial value warranted removal in Rockhill v. USPS, 39 MSPR 467, 469 (1989) (a television set worth about $250).

An employee was properly removed for the theft of a novelette, Barnes v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3312):

Barnes correctly recognizes that the value of the novelette which he allegedly stole--$4.95--is, like the soap in Miguel, de minimis. However, that is the extent of the similarities between this case and Miguel. The theft committed by Barnes was not a lone black mark on an otherwise unblemished career, but rather was the third de minimis theft committed by Barnes in his relatively short seven year career. More important, perhaps, is the fact that Barnes was a mail handler, a public position which demands scrupulously trustworthy employees because there are few accounting safeguards to protect the public (here, inmates) from theft. Stated simply, the government has a right to expect mail handlers who can perform their duties without over-the-shoulder supervision.

See England v. USPS, 117 MSPR 255, 2012 MSPB 7 (2012) (removal of mail handler for removal and use of $25 gift card; remand to consider affirmative defenses).

Removal by a custodian of undeliverable items of small value from a waste bin warranted a 60-day suspension, mitigated from a removal as a result of the small value of the items, the cooperation of the employee in the investigation, and the otherwise good service of 18 years. Smith v. USPS, 31 MSPR 508, 510 (1986); see Jefferson v. USPS, 73 MSPR 376, 382 (1997) (mitigation to a suspension of a custodian [Appellant Dean] with a good record who was removed for taking items of small value from waste mail; agency protestations of a loss of trust and confidence were given no weight because they were unsupported by the evidence and constituted a "mere conclusory statement"; no evidence of the notoriety of the offense was presented; the Board considered other cases involving the taking of undeliverable mail or property of de minimis value); Bonacchi v. USPS, 40 MSPR 364, 368 (1989) (removal of a supervisor as a result of a direction to a subordinate to discard undeliverable mail was mitigated to a demotion; no delay or obstruction of deliverable mail occurred and there was no tangible benefit from the disposal).

Dismissal of a distribution clerk was mitigated to a 90-day suspension following his removal of two magazines and a calculator of de minimis value from a collection of undeliverable mail. The Board discounted conclusory supervisory disclaimers of continued trust in the appellant, who had a 19-year career with the Postal Service and no prior discipline. Ciulla v. USPS, 37 MSPR 627, 631 (1988); see Jefferson v. USPS, 73 MSPR 376, 383-84 (1997) (official erred by treating all theft alike and apparently believing that any theft warranted removal, failing to responsibly determine the penalty; removal was mitigated to suspension of a distribution clerk [Appellant Jefferson] who removed materials from waste mail; it was the first offense of an exceptionally reliable employee during a 19 year career).

Although the problem is essentially one involving performance rather than misconduct, an adverse action may be based on improper handling of mail. The misdispatch of registered mail warranted an adverse action of suspension, reduced through mitigation from removal because of the apparent disparate imposition of discipline and consideration of the appellant's past record. Lomax v. USPS, 8 MSPR 395, 397 (1981); see Duncan v. USPS, 9 MSPR 318, 319-20 (1981) (failure to provide reliable, efficient service to postal customers); Banks v. USPS, 8 MSPR 442, 444 (1981) ("curtailment of mail").
