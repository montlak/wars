a. Effect of EEO Allegation or EEO Complaint

The Board's guidance on its jurisdiction over arbitration decisions involving Postal Service employees who raise EEO issues in their grievances was explained in Jackson v. USPS, 57 MSPR 57, 58-59 (1993), determining that the Postal Service employee must invoke the Board processes within the 20 [now 30] days then allowed from the effective date of an adverse action irrespective of his initiation or pursuit of a grievance, and irrespective of an allegation of discrimination:

The agency removed the appellant from his Mailhandler position on February 25, 1992, for unsatisfactory attendance and failure to follow rules and regulations regarding leave requests. He then filed a grievance through the agency's negotiated collective bargaining procedure, ultimately taking his case to arbitration. On September 29, 1992, the arbitrator issued a decision denying the appellant's grievance. On October 16, 1992, the appellant filed an appeal of the removal action to the Board, and requested Board review of the arbitrator's decision.

The administrative judge found that the appellant had failed to show good cause for his seven-month filing delay and dismissed his appeal as untimely. The administrative judge further held that the Board had no authority under 5 CFR 1201.154(d) to review the arbitrator's decision because "there was no allegation of discrimination."

...

...In response to the Board's acknowledgment order, which provided him an opportunity to explain his delay, the appellant stated only that his filing fell within the regulatory time limit for requesting Board review of arbitration decisions pursuant to 5 CFR 1201.154(d). As we discuss below, however, this regulation is inapplicable to Postal Service employees and thus does not provide the appellant with an additional filing period. We therefore find that, under 5 CFR 1201.22(b), his filing period expired 20 days from the effective date of his removal. We further find that the appellant's pursuit of his grievance does not constitute good cause for his untimely filing. See Lewis v. US Postal Service, 47 MSPR 228, 232 (1991).

Even if the appellant alleged prohibited discrimination the administrative judge properly dismissed the appeal of the arbitrator's decision.

The administrative judge found that he had no authority to review the arbitration decision under 5 CFR 1201.154(d) because the appellant did not raise an allegation of discrimination. Section 1201.154(d) implements 5 USC 7121(d) which requires aggrieved employees alleging unlawful discrimination to elect between appealing to the Board and pursuing negotiated grievance procedures; it further provides for Board review of arbitration decisions where such discrimination is alleged. The Board has held, however, that 5 USC 7121(d) does not apply to Postal Service employees with Board appeal rights who may file both a grievance and a Board appeal at the same time. Lucas v. US Postal Service, 39 MSPR 459, 461 (1989); Hall v. US Postal Service, 26 MSPR 233, 236 (1985). Correspondingly, the Board may only review Postal Service arbitration decisions within the context of a timely appeal of the agency action. Lucas, 39 MSPR at 461. Thus, since the appellant's petition for appeal of his removal was untimely filed and he had failed to show good cause for this delay, we conclude that the administrative judge properly dismissed the appeal.

It may be that an appeal to the Board, untimely if the only triggering event were the underlying personnel action, may become timely if an EEO complaint was also filed with the Postal Service, even if the filing of the EEO complaint postdates unsuccessful use of the grievance procedure. See McGuire v. USPS, 82 MSPR 247, 250 ¶ 9 (1999) (resolving the timeliness of a Board appeal filed by an employee who, in addition to filing a grievance that was unsuccessfully pursued through arbitration, challenged removal through an EEO complaint; the time for appeal was calculated from agency's issuance of final agency decision on EEO complaint); Hill v. USPS, 38 MSPR 339, 342 (1988) (accepting evidence at the PFR level of the status of the appellant's EEO complaint).
