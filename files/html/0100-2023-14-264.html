14. Information for Employees; Union Representatives

FLRA blessed some proposals obliging management to provide information concerning its drug testing program to employees and to the union. In AFGE, Dept. of Educ. Council of AFGE Locals and Dept. of Educ., 38 FLRA 1068, 1088 (1990), enforcement denied as to other proposals, Dept. of Interior, Minerals Mgmt. Serv. v. FLRA, 969 F.2d 1158, 1161-63 (D.C. Cir. 1992), a portion of Proposal 4 could be negotiated requiring management to inform employees that they would have the opportunity to submit supplemental medical documentation to demonstrate that they legitimately use a specific (prescription) drug. AFGE, Dept. of Educ. Council of AFGE Locals also permitted the negotiation of a proposal that management inform employees that drug abuse counseling and referral services would be available for employees through the Employee Assistance Program.

AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 1661 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Danbury, 31 FLRA 95, 97-100 (1988), declared negotiable a requirement that the agency inform the employee of the exact drug or class of drugs tested prior to the submission of a sample. The proposal did not require the employer to inform the employee at a particular time. The agency could inform the employee at the time of testing. AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 866-69 (1991), resolved to be negotiable a portion of Proposal 4F, that the employer provide employees, on a quarterly basis, with a list of medications and substances that could result in false positive tests; nonnegotiable was a counterpart provision effectively precluding drug testing unless the list were also given to the employee a day ahead of a test. NTEU and Dept. of Energy, 41 FLRA 1241, 1245-48 (1991), approved a provision for negotiation that provided notice of the right to union representation. See AFGE, Dept. of Educ. Council of AFGE Locals and Dept. of Educ., 38 FLRA 1068, 1084, 1088-89 (1990) (Proposal 4, negotiable: management to inform employees they have right to union representation at any point in drug testing process, whether "before, during, or after the test is conducted"), enforcement denied as to other proposals, Dept. of Interior, Minerals Mgmt. Serv. v. FLRA, 969 F.2d 1158, 1161-63 (D.C. Cir. 1992). [Refer in this chapter to "Union Representation; Observer; Lab Inspections," "Explanation of Reasons for Testing."]

AFGE Local 3407 and DOD, DMA, 39 FLRA 557, 558-568 (1991), declared negotiable Proposal 2, requiring the employer to provide classes to employees to inform them about the drug testing program. A related case, NFFE Local 2015 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., 41 FLRA 1158, 1160, 1163-64 (1991), approved for negotiations Provision 1, compelling agency briefings on the drug program with union participation in the briefings:

The disputed portion of Proposal 1 requires the Agency to provide briefings to employees occupying sensitive positions who are subject to random drug testing, and to allow the Union up to 10 minutes to speak at the briefings. We conclude that the proposal does not directly interfere with the Agency's right to assign work and is within the duty to bargain.

The proposal here is to the same effect as Proposal 2 in American Federation of Government Employees, Local 3407 and U.S. Department of Defense Mapping Agency, Hydrographic-Topographic, Washington D.C., 39 FLRA 557 (1991) (Defense Mapping Agency), in which the Authority found negotiable a proposal that required the agency to provide "classes" for all unit employees for the purpose of explaining various aspects of the agency's drug testing program. The Authority addressed and rejected the same arguments raised by the Agency in this case that the disputed proposal directly interfered with the right to assign work because it required the agency to train employees. The Authority distinguished proposals that establish a mechanism for conveying information, such as classes, from proposals that involve instruction concerning employees' duties and responsibilities. The former were held not to involve the assignment of work and to be negotiable as long as the proposals otherwise concerned conditions of employment and did not require the release of protected information. The Authority found that matters concerning an agency's drug testing program "unquestionably concern conditions of employment." Id. at 563.

Subsequent to the decision in Defense Mapping Agency, the Authority found two other provisions requiring an agency to provide (1) training to union officials concerning drug testing and related employee assistance programs and (2) briefings for all employees regarding implementation of a drug testing program to be negotiable because they did not directly interfere with the agency's right to assign work. National Treasury Employees Union and U.S. Department of the Treasury, Internal Revenue Service, Washington, D.C., 39 FLRA 1532 (1991) (IRS). We reach the same result here.... [W]e conclude that Proposal 1 does not directly interfere with the Agency's right to assign work. Moreover, the proposal concerns a condition of employment and it is neither alleged nor apparent that the proposal would require the release of information in violation of applicable law or regulation. The Agency's reliance on F.E. Warren and Bureau of Land Management is misplaced. Unlike the provision in F.E. Warren, which involved instruction to new employees on elements of their duties, the proposal here does not require instruction to employees concerning any facets of their duties. Additionally, in Defense Mapping Agency, we stated that we would no longer follow Bureau of Land Management to the extent that it indicated that all proposals requiring agencies to conduct sessions intended to convey information to employees would be analyzed as training proposals. Defense Mapping Agency, 39 FLRA at 567.

Based on the foregoing, we conclude that Proposal 1 does not directly interfere with the Agency's right to assign work. As no other basis for finding the proposal nonnegotiable was asserted, we conclude that Proposal 1 is within the duty to bargain. In reaching this conclusion, we note that the Agency made no arguments with regard to the portion of the proposal entitling the Union up to 10 minutes to speak at the briefings. In the absence of any such arguments, and noting the Union's statement that the proposal was intended solely to provide the Union with the opportunity to describe the regulatory and contractual protections available to unit employees undergoing drug testing, we find no basis on which to conclude that this portion of the proposal is outside the duty to bargain.

Proposals ancillary to the testing program, e. g., for training and briefing of union stewards, on official time, concerning the Drug Free Workplace Program and the Employee Assistance Program, were negotiable procedures. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 39 FLRA 1532, 1533-34 (1991) (Provision 1); see NAGE, FUSE, Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 42 FLRA 730, 772-73 (1991) (negotiable, because they were not assignments of work, were Proposals 10 and 11: union officials and stewards will be given same training as supervisors; employees will be given biannual training sessions on ills of substance abuse); AFGE Local 3407 and DOD, DMA, 39 FLRA 557, 558-68 (1991), supra. Another training proposal for union representatives was approved in Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 713 (1991) (Proposal 11). NTEU Chapters 243 and 245 and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 45 FLRA 270, 274-75 (1992), determined negotiable Proposal 2, that management provide supervisory training manuals concerning drug testing to "each bargaining unit" if the manuals are different from drug testing manuals given to unit employees. See AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 859, 864-65, 901, 902 (1991) (Proposal 4, requesting briefings in advance of testing; approving Proposal 9B, that "[a]ll union representatives and bargaining unit employees will receive employer-provided training on the entire drug testing program.").

Although FLRA usually blocks the negotiability of proposals that establish the qualifications of agency officials engaged in various functions, negotiable in IFPTE Local 89 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, Grand Coulee Project Office, 48 FLRA 516, 533-34 (1993), was Proposal V.A.1, that training on the drug testing program for employees "be prepared by an official trained and qualified to provide such training":

...Nothing in the plain wording of Proposal V.A.1 precludes the Agency from determining the qualifications necessary to provide the required training. Rather, the proposal simply requires that the Agency ensure that those employees responsible for providing the training are qualified to do so....
