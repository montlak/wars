broida mspb Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices Chapter 13: Prohibited Personnel Practices, Subchapter 03: Individual Right of Action, Section A: Statutory and Regulatory Requirements and Limitations, Subsection 02: Time for Initiating IRA Appeal With OSC and MSPB, Part a: Laches in Filing OSC Complaint
a. Laches in Filing OSC Complaint

Unlike the statutory time limit for taking a case to the Board after closure by OSC, no statute or regulation sets a time limit on the initiation of an IRA complaint with OSC. However, undue delay in presentation of a matter to OSC may result in a bar to its later Board adjudication based on the doctrine of laches. In Mercer v. DHHS, 82 MSPR 211, 216-17 ¶¶ 10-15 (1999), the Board entertained an IRA appeal initiated with OSC a few years after the operative events and filed with the Board some years later, when OSC had completed its investigation. The agency, the Board decided, offered insufficient proof that its defense was prejudiced through delay between the underlying removal and the Board appeal:

The equitable defense of laches bars an action when an unreasonable delay in bringing the action has prejudiced the party against whom the action is taken. See, e.g., Social Security Administration v. Carr, 78 MSPR 313, 330 (1998). The party asserting laches must prove both unreasonable delay and material prejudice. Id.; see Nuss v. Office of Personnel Management, 974 F.2d 1316, 1318 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Hoover v. Department of the Navy, 957 F.2d 861, 863 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Pepper v. United States, 794 F.2d 1571, 1573 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

Here, the AJ found that the filing delay, for purposes of applying laches, was more than 7 years, the time between the appellant's termination and her filing of this IRA appeal. We find, however, that the pertinent filing delay was fewer than 4 years because the appellant was terminated from her position in December 1990, and she filed her OSC complaint in September 1994. Although, as the AJ noted, the appellant could have filed this appeal within 120 days after filing her OSC complaint, she was entitled to wait until the OSC terminated its investigation before filing this Board appeal. 5 USC § 1214(a)(3). We find that waiting to file this appeal until the OSC terminated its investigation, as the appellant was entitled to do, did not constitute a delay subject to the doctrine of laches. See Acree v. Department of the Treasury, 74 MSPR 119, 127 (1997) (the doctrine of laches did not apply where the appellant timely refiled the appeal 4 years after it was dismissed without prejudice to refiling). Thus, the agency had the burden of proving both that the appellant's filing delay of fewer than 4 years was unreasonable and that the filing delay materially prejudiced the agency.

We will address the issue of material prejudice first. We note, preliminarily, that it has been held that a filing delay of more than 6 years raises a presumption of prejudice. See Pepper, 794 F.2d at 1575. We find, however, that the appellant's filing delay of fewer than 4 years is not so lengthy as to raise a presumption of prejudice. See Hoover, 957 F.2d at 862-64 (laches was not applied despite a 5-year delay in filing a petition for enforcement, where the agency failed to submit sufficient specific evidence to support its assertion of prejudice); cf. Carr, 78 MSPR at 330 (a 3-1/2-year delay between the alleged misconduct and the removal complaint was not unreasonable where the agency waited to see whether a pattern of misconduct developed before initiating the action); Special Counsel v. Santella, 65 MSPR 452, 465-66 (1994) (a 3-year delay from the time the Special Counsel learned of possible prohibited personnel practices and the initiation of actions against the petitioners was not unreasonable).

Regarding the issue of prejudice, the agency representative contended as follows:

[T]wo of the four primary management officials directly involved, by the appellant's own assertion, in the instant case are no longer employed by the agency and in contacting these individuals they have relayed to myself that they have very little to no recollection of the specific events surrounding this case. One of the other four management officials identified by the appellant does continue to work for the agency in a different geographic region. In contacting this individual, he too has little to no recollection of specific events surrounding this case. The other party identified by the appellant, which [sic] still works for the agency in the same location, has been the only person contacted that has any recollection of the specific events surrounding the events of this case.

He submitted additional documents which were purportedly retrieved from the National Archives and Records Administration, and vaguely contended that, in the absence of unspecified additional documents, "the agency's defense must, by necessity, be based upon the fading memory of its witnesses, without corroborating documentation."

The agency did not identify the witnesses in question or provide further details as to their availability or ability to recollect specific events. See Nuss, 974 F.2d at 1319 (a party's claim that witnesses have retired does not establish that they were actually unavailable); Hoover, 957 F.2d at 864 (similar finding). In fact, no evidence was submitted to support the unsworn assertion by the agency representative. See Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 MSPR 163, 68 (1995) (the statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence); cf. Truitt v. Department of the Navy, 45 MSPR 344, 347 (1990) (sworn statements that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters asserted therein). Because the agency failed to submit any evidence to support its assertion of prejudice, we find that it failed to meet its burden in this regard. See Hoover, 957 F.2d at 862-64.

In light of our finding that the agency failed to meet its burden of proving it was materially prejudiced by the filing delay, we need not examine whether the filing delay was unreasonable under the circumstances. See id. at 864. Accordingly, we conclude that the agency failed to establish the defense of laches.

Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 88 MSPR 22, 24-26 ¶¶ 5-9 (2001), dismissed an IRA appeal under the doctrine of laches because the agency could not defend against events that occurred 7-10 years before the appeal:

The Board has held that the equitable defense of laches bars an action when an unreasonable delay in bringing the action has prejudiced the party against whom the action is taken. Mercer v. Department of Health & Human Services, 82 MSPR 211, 1(10 (1999). Further, relying on the Federal Circuit's decision in Pepper v. United States, 794 F.2d 1571, 1575 (Fed. Cir. 1986), the Board has suggested that a filing delay of more than 6 years raises a presumption of prejudice in individual right of action appeals. Mercer, 82 MSPR 211 ¶ 12. In Pepper, the court applied the presumption of laches to a 6-year delay in filing an action from a military discharge. Pepper, 794 F.2d at 1575. The administrative judge relied on Mercer, and, in turn, on Pepper, to support the presumption of prejudice in this whistleblower appeal.

However, subsequent to issuing the decision in Pepper, the Federal Circuit reversed itself, and "reject[ed] the notion that the government can rely on a presumption of prejudice, or shift the burden to plaintiff to show lack of prejudice if the delay is long, to support the affirmative defense of laches" in a military discharge case. Cornetta v. United States, 851 F.2d 1372, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Further, courts are loath to apply a presumption of laches in any case where the statutory period for filing has not expired. See, e.g., TAG/ICIB Services, Inc. v. Pan American Grain Co., Inc., 215 F.3d 172, 175 (1st Cir. 2000). There is no statutory time limit for filing a request for corrective action with OSC. Augustine v. Department of Justice, 50 MSPR 648, 652 (1991).

In light of the foregoing, we find that the administrative judge improperly applied the presumption of laches under the circumstances of this appeal. Nonetheless, the administrative judge's error did not harm the appellant's substantive rights and provides no basis to reverse the initial decision because he still correctly dismissed the appeal under the doctrine of laches. Punter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984). As explained below and as the administrative judge went on to find, even absent the presumption of laches, the agency established that it was prejudiced by the appellant's unreasonable delay in requesting corrective action in that it would be unable to mount a defense to the action. See, e.g., Travelers Indemnity Co. v. United States, 16 Cl. Ct. 142, 157 (1988) (one type of prejudice that may stem from delay in filing suit is that the government may be unable to mount a defense; this prejudice may include loss of records, destruction of evidence, fading memories, or unavailability of witnesses).

The agency submitted a declaration from an Employee Relations Specialist in the personnel office at Robins Air Force Base, where the appellant was an employee. She stated that, because of the passage of time, under the rules of the Office of Personnel Management, many documents relevant to the appellant's employment, which had been maintained in his Official Personnel File, had been destroyed. She stated further that these documents included performance appraisals, requests for personnel actions, reprimands (if any), and position descriptions. She also stated that she has been unable to locate any other files that supervisors might have kept and that might contain copies of these documents. Additionally, she stated that three of the employees crucially involved in the appellant's removal action are no longer employed by Robins Air Force Base, and employees in the agency who were in the appellant's chain of command have vague or no memory of him. She further stated that she has been unable to determine who supervised the appellant during the time period in question. The administrative judge properly relied on this declaration. See Truitt v. Department of the Navy, 45 MSPR 344, 347 (1990) (sworn statements that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matter asserted therein). Moreover, the appellant appears to agree with the agency. He stated in his response to the order to file evidence and argument on the laches issue that "many if not all [of the agency's staff involved in the alleged retaliation] have moved on or retired."

Finally, because of the length of time that elapsed between the alleged retaliatory personnel actions, because of the loss to the agency of both documents and personnel that could give the agency information regarding these personnel actions, because of the fading recollections of agency personnel about the appellant, and because the appellant has no satisfactory explanation for the delay in filing, this case differs from other cases where the Board has refused to apply the doctrine of laches. Cf. Mercer, 82 MSPR 211 ¶¶ 14-15 (laches will not be applied where there was a delay of less than 4 years in filing a request for corrective action and some personnel familiar with the allegedly retaliatory personnel actions were unavailable); Eikenberry v. Department of the Interior, 37 MSPR 438, 447 (1988) (laches will not be applied where appellant has a satisfactory explanation for the delay and the agency fails to show prejudice).

Twenty-seven years precluded Board jurisdiction over an IRA appeal, Gibbs v. MSPB, 615 Fed. Appx. 685, 686 (Fed. Cir. 2015 NP):

In order to prevail on its defense of laches, the government must show "(1) unreasonable and unexcused delay in bringing the claim, and (2) material prejudice to the defendant as a result of the delay." Advanced Cardiovascular Sys., Inc. v. Scimed Life Sys., 988 F.2d 1157, 1161 (1993) (citing A.C. Aukerman Co. v. R.L. Chaides Construction Co., 960 F.2d 1020, 1028 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (en banc)). We review the Board's dismissal de novo. See Cambridge v. United States, 558 F.3d 1331, 1335 (Fed. Cir. 2009).

The Board did not err in its determination that laches bars Mr. Gibbs' action. Mr. Gibbs concedes that he delayed filing by 27 years. He also does not dispute the government's claims that this 27-year delay materially prejudiced its ability to defend against his allegations. His sole argument that laches does not apply is that this significant period of delay was reasonable or excused because of his mental illness and because his psychiatrist advised him against filing. Even assuming--as we must on a motion to dismiss--that Mr. Gibbs will be able to substantiate these claims, they cannot excuse a 27-year delay.
