broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 08: Disciplinary Procedures and Standards, Section H: Standards of Conduct, Subsection 01: Disclosure Statements and Arrangements Concerning Real or Apparent Conflicting Interests

1. Disclosure Statements and Arrangements Concerning Real or Apparent Conflicting Interests

OPM regulations implementing Executive Order No. 11222, requiring financial disclosure statements from employees at Grade 13 and above who are responsible for procurement, administering grants or subsidies, or regulating or auditing private enterprise, precluded negotiation of a proposal that limited the disclosure to those at GS-16 and above. AFGE Local 3488 and FDIC, 12 FLRA 532, 532-33 (1983). A proposal was nonnegotiable that stipulated employees may be required to furnish information concerning conflict of interest situations but that no employee would be required to give a statement under oath, except as required by law, AFGE, Nat'l INS Council and DOJ, INS, 8 FLRA 347, 362 (1982):

...The Authority has determined that an agency's right to determine internal security practices under the Statute extends to establishment of rules to prevent disruption of its operations, unwarranted disclosure of privileged information, and destruction of its property. In the opinion of the Authority, the right to determine internal security practices also extends to the establishment of rules applicable to internal investigations relating to the integrity of an agency's operations..., of requiring oaths to ensure obtaining truthful and reliable information in such circumstances. Thus, the Agency's decision to require oaths to ensure its obtaining truthful and reliable information in conducting investigations, which are the subject of this disputed proposal, is an internal security practice under section 7106(a)(1). Accordingly, since the proposal would prevent the Agency from administering oaths in such investigations except where it must do so under law, the proposal is not within the Agency's duty to bargain. Of course, pursuant to the provisions of section 7106(b)(2) of the Statute, the procedures which management officials of the Agency will observe in exercising their authority to determine internal security practices, including the procedures to be followed in connection with the use of oaths in specific situations, would be within the duty to bargain.

POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 53 FLRA 625 (1997), addressed a large number and variety of proposals concerned with financial disclosure and adjustments of work for patent examiners whose financial interests were required to be disclosed to the agency in accordance with government-wide regulations. FLRA compared the proposals to the regulations issued by the Office of Government Ethics, codified at 5 CFR Parts 2634 and 2635 (1997). The decision should be reviewed for the reasoning supplied to support the negotiability of the proposals noted in the following paragraph.

In POPA, Proposal 1, defining the financial interests an examiner and his family are prevented from acquiring and retaining, was not considered to infringe on the agency's right to assign work because the agency could direct divestiture of the investments. Proposals 2, 3, and 7, setting the procedures for employees to follow to attempt to avoid conflicting investment interests, were within the duty to bargain. Proposal 8, establishing a procedure for appeals and substitution of employees when a directed reassignment of an examiner to a new industry group would create financial conflicts, was negotiable as an arrangement. Proposal 23, requiring accounting for time spent on financial disclosure and related responsibilities, was an appropriate arrangement to permit a defense to a charge of nonperformance of other work. Proposal 6, establishing a joint committee and adjudicatory standards to determine whether investments were properly classified by management to constitute a conflict, was not inconsistent with the provisions of 5 CFR 2635.403 (1997), guiding the issuance of agency regulations that prohibit the acquisition or holding of conflicting financial interests. Proposals 18-19, setting standards for waiver of financial conflicts, were inconsistent with provisions of 5 CFR 2635.402-403 (1997), requiring evaluation of waivers on a multifactor, case-by-case basis. Proposal 9, governing assignments of an atypical or temporary nature, required the agency to order either waiver of the conflict or recusal of the examiner; the proposal, allowing case-by-case review, was not inconsistent with the standards of conduct regulations. Proposal 10, allowing a flexible period of divestiture depending on the nature of an investment, was consistent with the requirement of 5 CFR 2635.403(d) (1997), setting a standard of a "reasonable period of time." Negotiable as an arrangement was Proposal 17, that an examiner will not be removed due to a conflict with a financial interest that existed prior to employment, or due to an agency-initiated change of work assignment unless the conflict was knowingly concealed by the examiner during an agency inquiry. FLRA concluded as to Proposal 17 that "the benefit to examiners of being assured that they will not be removed in a situation where they engaged in no wrongdoing, and in which the Agency is free to take a full range of other actions that remedy or eliminate the conflict of interest, outweighs the burden on the Agency's right to discipline employees." Negotiable, within the agency's authority under 5 CFR 2634.905 (1997) to exempt from disclosure requirements a class of individuals whose duties or level of supervision made conflicts either remote or inconsequential, was Proposal 21, stipulating that the duties of positions below GS-13 did not warrant the submission of financial disclosure statements. Another negotiable provision was Proposal 25, requiring the agency to seek approval from the Office of Government Ethics for an alternative financial disclosure procedure that would limit financial interests to be reported to those specified in the proposal, and that would exempt from disclosure interests in "widely diversified excepted investment funds." Finally, ruled negotiable was Proposal 22, seeking agreement that the parties establish a joint labor-management committee to address conflict of interest issues created when employees seek outside employment.

In POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 59 FLRA 331 (2003), considering another group of proposals concerning ethics standards for unit employees, disputes over some of the proposals involved conflicts between the proposals and government-wide ethical standards contained in OGE standards of conduct. The deference shown by FLRA to OGE regulations included a determination, based on extensive statutory analysis, that grants of discretion by OGE to agency officials were nonnegotiable, i.e., if OGE granted authority to an agency to make a determination affecting ethical responsibilities, the substantive range of authority granted to the agency was nonnegotiable. Nonnegotiable were: Provision 1 (establishing circumstances justifying the extension of filing dates for confidential financial disclosure forms); Provision 2 (requiring that if the agency ethics official determines that a unit employee has a conflict of interest, the ethics official will first attempt to counsel the employee and provide possible remedies to the situation); Provision 3 (establishing criteria governing the agency's exercise of its discretion under OGE regulations to determine those employees who will not be required to file confidential financial disclosure forms; the Authority honored OGE's interpretation of its regulations as affording agencies sole and exclusive discretion to make the determinations under those regulations); Provision 5 (establishing a process and a set of criteria to be used by a joint labor-management committee for reviewing and making recommendations, impacting on possible conflicts of interest, concerning the agency's determination of the technological area of an employee's official duties); Provision 6 (establishing a process and criteria to be applied when management changes an employee's work assignment resulting in conflicts of interest of specified degrees of seriousness); Provision 7 (prescribing the actions management will take when a conflict interest results from specific work assignments: management was required to either accept an employee's recusal or grant a waiver of the disqualification or, in some circumstances, the employees may be required to divest themselves of the conflicting financial interest); Provision 8 (when an employee is determined to have an "intractable conflict of interest," as defined by the provision, the agency "normally" will allow the employee to recuse himself or herself or will waive the ethics requirements pertaining to the conflict); Provision 9 (when an employee has a conflict of interest resulting from the current employment of the employee's spouse or minor child, the agency "normally" will allow the employee to recuse himself or herself or will waive the pertinent ethics requirements); Provision 10 (requiring the designated agency ethics officer to contact an employee and verify the technological area of the employee's work before determining that the employee has a conflict and ordering the employee to divest the conflicting financial interest); Provision 11 (an employee who is asked to divest shall be instructed on how to apply for a certificate of divestiture (relating to taxability of gains on the sale) and shall be given the opportunity to be granted the certificate prior to any final requirement to divest); Provision 12 (allowing 30 days for divestiture after a certificate of divestiture has been granted or denied). Negotiable was Provision 4 (describing the scope of a written request for guidance from a designated agency ethics officer; the language in the proposal was not materially different than the language of a regulation describing the scope of a request for guidance).
