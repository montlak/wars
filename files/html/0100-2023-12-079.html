broida flra Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 06: Mootness; Changed Circumstances, Section A: Intervening or Existing Agreement

A. INTERVENING OR EXISTING AGREEMENT

If the parties come to agreement about the subject matter embodied in a proposal during the pendency of a negotiability petition, the petition is moot and will be dismissed. FEMTC and Mare Island Naval Shipyard, 7 FLRA 701, 701-02 (1982); see Int'l Org. of Masters, Mates and Pilots and Panama Canal Comm'n., 52 FLRA 251, 254 (1996) (negotiability appeal is moot if valid, binding agreement disposing of subject matter in dispute has been reached by parties; it is immaterial that agreement has not become part of parties' collective bargaining agreement); AFGE Local 900 and Dept. of Army, Army Reserve Personnel Ctr., St. Louis, 46 FLRA 1494, 1499 (1993) (when renegotiation produced agreement, negotiability appeal as to supplanted, previously disputed, proposals was moot). Once a valid and binding agreement is reached concerning a proposal before FLRA in a negotiability appeal, the proposal is moot. AFGE Council 214 and Wright-Patterson AFB, 21 FLRA 575, 577-78 (1986); see AFGE Local 85 and VAMC, Leavenworth, 32 FLRA 210, 211-12 (1988) (bargaining order serves no purpose as to moot proposal). But see AFGE Local 1770 and Dept. of Army Hq., XVII Airborne Corps and Ft. Bragg, 34 FLRA 903, 904 (1990) (petition was not moot when parties were in dispute over effect of counterproposals discussed following declaration of the nonnegotiability of proposal).

A dispute over a ground rules agreement once amended and replaced becomes moot. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, 33 FLRA 879, 880 (1989). If a contract becomes effective through operation of law, as when the agency fails to timely disapprove certain negotiated provisions, there is no real negotiability dispute, and a negotiability appeal will be dismissed. AFGE Local 1858 and Army Missile Command, Redstone Arsenal, 4 FLRA 361, 362 (1980); see Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org. and Dept. of Commerce, Nat'l Weather Serv., 40 FLRA 890, 891 (1991) ("As neither party responded to the Authority's Order with evidence that the Agency's disapproval of the local parties agreement was served on the Union within 30 days after the agreement was executed, the entire agreement, as negotiated and executed, became effective and binding.... Therefore, the Union's petition for review does not raise a dispute concerning an effective and binding agreement that is cognizable under section 7117 of the Statute."). But not all intervening agreements moot a negotiability appeal. Although a proposal before FLRA is superseded by a similar proposal imposed on the parties by an interest arbitrator, the dispute is not moot if the agency insists that the provision imposed by the arbitrator is nonnegotiable. See NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., Washington, 40 FLRA 570, 573 (1991).

If after the union submits a negotiability appeal regarding a proposal, the parties continue to negotiate and reach agreement on a modified proposal on the same subject matter, the negotiability appeal on the proposal is moot, unless the parties have decided to permit it to be preserved for FLRA through explicit agreement. The agreement to preserve the issue for FLRA review should be written. Once the modified proposal is in the contract, FLRA will presume that the only means the parties will employ to change the proposal is through a contract reopener clause. New York State Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, Brooklyn, 14 FLRA 386, 388 (1984). FLRA determined that, if the parties determined that a proposal declared negotiable by FLRA would be resubmitted for negotiation under a reopener clause of an existing contract, FLRA dismissed the negotiability appeal as premature. A ruling would be advisory. Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 7 FLRA 610, 611 (1982). But see AFGE Local 1808 and Dept. of Army, Sierra Army Depot, 42 FLRA 542, 544-45 (1991) (when agency declared proposals nonnegotiable, union could pursue negotiability appeal despite agency's argument that it had no duty to bargain because agreement was by then concluded, a conclusion apparently challenged by the union).

Mootness is established by an intervening agreement, not by an intervening change in work practices, NAGE Local R1-109 and VA Connecticut Healthcare Sys., 61 FLRA 593, 595-96 (2006):

The Agency claims it has taken actions that render proposals 2, 4, and 11 moot. In this regard, as noted above, the Agency detailed an employee from West Haven to Newington to assist the Newington employee on the evening shift. According to the Agency, the detailed West Haven employee provides coverage for the Newington employee when she is on leave and the Newington employee has been assigned an established tour of duty to accomplish the task of sterilizing the West Haven instruments. To support its claim that the proposals are moot, the Agency relies on Authority precedent holding that a petition for review becomes moot if the parties come to an agreement about the subject matter of the proposals within the petition. See Statement of Position at 7 (citing Int'l Organization of Masters, Mates and Pilots, Marine Div., Panama Canal Pilots Branch, 52 FLRA 251, 254 (1996) (Masters, Mates, and Pilots) and Fed. Employees Metal Trades Council, AFL-CIO, 7 FLRA 701, 701-02 (1982) (Metal Trades Council)).

...

Section 2429.10 of the Regulations states that the Authority will not issue advisory opinions. Thus, where the issues that led to the filing of a negotiability petition for review have been resolved, or where there is no longer a dispute between the parties, the Authority will dismiss the petition for review as moot. See AFGE, Nat'l Veterans Admin. Council, 41 FLRA 73, 74 (1991) (citing AFGE, Local 85, 32 FLRA 210, 211-12 (1988)). The Authority will also dismiss petitions for review as moot in cases where a proposal requires some action on a date that has passed and there is no explanation in the record as to how the proposal could be implemented. See id. (citing NTEU, 38 FLRA 263 (1990) (Proposal 1)).

Here, the Agency claims it has already taken actions consistent with proposals 2, 4, and 11, thus mooting all of the Union's proposals. Relying on Masters, Mates, and Pilots and Metal Trades Council, the Agency claims that its actions, in effect, constitute an agreement between the parties over the subject matter of the proposals. For the reasons explained below, we reject the Agency's claim and find that the Union's proposals are not moot.

In Masters, Mates, and Pilots, the Authority found the proposals moot because the parties had met and reached a valid, binding agreement disposing of the subject matter in the disputed proposal, as evidenced by a written memorandum of understanding and an appendix, which resolved the matter in dispute. In Metal Trades Council, the Authority found that the union's proposal was moot because the agency claimed, and the union did not dispute, that subsequent to the negotiability appeal, the parties continued bargaining and eventually reached agreement on a proposal relating to the subject matter at issue in the union's negotiability appeal. Here, the Agency does not allege, and there is no indication in the record, that the parties subsequently met over the Union's proposals or reached an agreement resolving the issues raised in the Union's proposals. Furthermore, unlike the union in Metal Trades Council, the Union in this case specifically disputes the Agency's claim that an agreement has been reached. Consequently, the Agency has not demonstrated that the Union's proposals are moot.

It may occur that FLRA takes so long to decide a negotiability appeal that the parties litigate the appeal long after they conclude a contract on other issues. The expiration of the contract as the parties await the FLRA determination does not moot the negotiability appeal, FLRA v. OPM, 778 F.2d 844, 847-48 (D.C. Cir. 1985):

...The OPM does not cite, and we could not find, a single example of a FLRA negotiability decision whose resolution was based in whole or in part on the "bargaining context." Instead, the meaning of the union proposal and its consistency with applicable law is the sole focus of the negotiability decision.... Once the FLRA holds that a proposal is negotiable, it is an unfair labor practice for another agency to refuse to negotiate on that proposal at a time when the proposal is properly subject to collective bargaining.

...OPM's mootness argument would produce absurd results: while all other agencies would be bound by a negotiability determination regardless of the life of any particular collective bargaining agreement, the agency to which a negotiability order was directed would be bound for the life of one such agreement....

Furthermore, at least under present circumstances with long case delays at the FLRA, the acceptance of OPM's theory would substantially erode employee bargaining rights. There is already a narrow scope of bargaining with federal agencies and these agencies can easily refuse to bargain over proposals that the FLRA ultimately holds negotiable.... If negotiability appeals become moot once a collective bargaining agreement expires, the union's right to bargain would be rendered meaningless in many cases because negotiability appeals often extend beyond the term of an agreement, and because the FLRA recently has held that unions may not negotiate over new subjects during the term of an agreement absent a specific contract reopener.
