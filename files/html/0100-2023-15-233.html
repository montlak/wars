2. Employment Application; Security Questionnaire; Lack of Candor

Employment applications provide a fertile source of falsification cases. If falsification of an employment application is demonstrated, the misconduct goes to the appellant's reliability, veracity, trustworthiness, and ethical conduct. It affects the efficiency of the service. Removal for lack of candor or false statement in an employment application can come during or after a probationary period. Freeland v. DHS, 825 Fed. Appx. 750 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) ("Mr. Freeland's employment was still subject to a background investigation."). Freeland noted that the charge can be lack of candor, incorporating some element of intent and subject to a Douglas mitigation analysis for the tenured employee:

Mr. Freeland argues that the Board failed to acknowledge that the Agency, in its Removal Notice, accused Mr. Freeland of intentionality. As noted in the Board's decision, a lack of candor does not require "intentionality or an intent to deceive." Ludlum, 278 F.3d at 1284-85 (stating that lack of candor involves "a failure to disclose something that, in the circumstances, should have been disclosed to make the statement accurate and complete"). The Board's finding that Mr. Freeland's failure to disclose the unfavorable circumstances regarding his prior employment was enough to support the charge of lack of candor is supported by substantial evidence, regardless of any "intentionality." To the extent Mr. Freeland is arguing that the deciding official failed to analyze the first Douglas factor, "the nature and seriousness of the offense, and its relation to the employee's duties, position, and responsibilities, including whether the offense was intentional or technical or inadvertent, or was committed maliciously or for gain or was frequently repeated," we find the Board's determination that the deciding official did consider such a factor supported by substantial evidence. See Douglas v. Veterans Admin., 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 305-06 (1981). The record contains the deciding official's analysis of each Douglas factor and the Board heard testimony from the deciding official and credited such testimony.

Mr. Freeland also contends that his incorrect answer on the SF-85Ps was not done for "personal gain." However, a finding of lack of candor does not require a finding of personal gain. Additionally, although the Board states that a finding of personal gain would be a "commonsense inference," it notes that the charge was not based on a finding of personal gain. One of the Douglas factors is personal gain. To the extent Mr. Freeland is arguing the deciding official inappropriately found personal gain in deciding the penalty, we find the Board appropriately weighed the testimony from the deciding official and Mr. Freeland. We see no reason to overturn the Board's credibility determination.

Removal for falsification of government documents promotes service efficiency because acts of falsification raise serious doubts regarding the employee's honesty and fitness for employment. See Hamilton v. Dept. of Air Force, 52 MSPR 45, 47 (1991). Delessio v. USPS, 33 MSPR 517, 521 (1987), declared:

[R]emoval is a reasonable penalty for employment application falsification.... This...has been held to be the case in the private sector, especially where, as in this case, there is an explicit proscription of such falsification. See National Labor Relations Board v. Florida Steel Corporation, 586 F.2d 436, 450 (5th Cir. 1978); see also Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. v. National Labor Relations Board, 539 F.2d 1335, 1336 n.4 (4th Cir. 1976).

See Hawes v. OPM, 122 MSPR 341, 350 ¶ 21, 2015 MSPB 29 (2015) (suitability appeal: "A falsification charge includes an element of private material gain. See Boo v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 13 (2014). Securing employment is private material gain that will support a falsification charge. Id.").

Concealment of information called for on the SF-171 has a "serious deleterious effect on the process through which persons are selected for federal employment." Smith v. Pennsylvania Ave. Dev. Corp., 8 MSPR 410, 413 (1981). Willis v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 82, 84 (1982), said of removal of a voucher examiner for falsification of an SF-171:

...Appellant's misconduct in completing his own SF-171 would obviously cause his supervisor to doubt appellant's ability to perform his job accurately and with the necessary degree of integrity. This doubt would certainly breach the employer-employee relationship and necessitate the supervisor continually monitoring appellant's work product. This duplication of effort would certainly not promote the efficiency of the service. Therefore, we conclude that appellant's misconduct does affect his own ability to perform the job and consequently the efficiency of the service.

A material false statement of employment qualifications constitutes a species of fraud. See United States v. Marzani, 71 F. Supp. 615, 620 (D.D.C. 1947); Widdifield v. USPS, 8 MSPR 115, 117, 7 MSPB 600 (1981) (noting that in Rodriguez v. Seamans, 463 F.2d 837 (D.C. Cir. 1972), the court upheld an employee's removal for false answers on an employment form, regardless of the constitutionality of the questions posed, because the making of false answers called into question his trustworthiness and reliability). One summary of the burden of proof in an appeal involving an employment application, Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 224 (1995), states:

To sustain a charge of falsification, the agency must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the appellant knowingly supplied incorrect information with the specific intent of defrauding, deceiving, or misleading the agency. See, e.g., Walcott v. US Postal Service, 52 MSPR 277, 282, aff'd, 980 F.2d 744 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). The requisite intent can be established either directly or by circumstantial evidence. See Forma v. Department of Justice, 57 MSPR 97, 103, aff'd, 11 F.3d 1071 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).

Under Scott, 69 MSPR at 226:

...An incorrect statement, coupled with a lack of any credible explanation or contrary action by the employee, can constitute circumstantial evidence of an intent to deceive. See Stein v. US Postal Service, 57 MSPR 434, 439 (1993); Hernandez v. Department of Education, 42 MSPR 61, 69 (1989) (plausible explanations are to be considered in determining whether a falsification is intentional)....

See Jacobs v. DOJ, 35 F.3d 1543, 1546-48 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (considering circumstantial and direct evidence as to a charge that the employee falsified her past job history in response to Question 22 of the "Questionnaire for Sensitive Positions"); Hanker v. Dept. of Treasury, 73 MSPR 159, 164 (1997) (as to a suitability appeal involving failure to list some past employment and the reasons for separation from those jobs: "[I]n the absence of any plausible explanation for his failure to include [the disability] on his SF-86, we infer an intent to deceive under the circumstances."); McKenna v. INS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3366) (citing Hanker for the proposition that "[w]here plausible explanations for an employee's failure to disclose information are absent, intent to deceive may be inferred.").

A case involving questions in application forms seeking to obtain information about past employment history is the suitability appeal of McFalls v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-3167), sustaining removal for the employee's misstatements concerning the basis of his departure from his prior employer:

Mr. McFalls completed an employment application for an auditor position with the USAAA on April 14, 1999. In the application, he indicated that he was employed as an auditor at the Department of Health and Human Services ("HHS"), even though he had submitted his resignation to HHS one day earlier. Although his resignation was not effective until May 8, 1999, and therefore he was technically still working for HHS, we believe that Mr. McFalls had an affirmative obligation to notify the USAAA, at least after May 8, 1999, that he no longer was employed by HHS. However, Mr. McFalls failed to inform the USAAA of his resignation even during the interview with the USAAA on June 1, 1999. In fact, he told the USAAA that he had resigned from HHS only when a personnel management specialist with the USAAA confronted him on June 2, 1999. Substantial evidence supports the Board's finding that Mr. Mcfalls intended to deceive the USAAA.

Mr. McFalls contends that the Board incorrectly decided that he provided false information on Optional Form ("OF") 306, "Declaration for Federal Employment," and Standard Form ("SF") 86, "Questionnaire for National Security Positions," during the appointment process for his position with DFAS. We again disagree; substantial evidence supports the Board's finding.

The false information on these forms concerned Mr. McFalls' employment history.... With respect to his employment history, Mr. McFalls' supervisor at HHS informed him that his performance was poor and that a letter of removal was being drafted at the agency's headquarters. Mr. McFalls agreed to resign rather than face a possible performance-based termination. He failed to disclose this resignation information on the OF-306 and SF-86 forms, however. Specifically, question 11 on form OF-306 asked: "During the last 5 years, were you fired from any job for any reason, did you quit after being told that you would be fired, did you leave any job by mutual agreement because of specific problems. In response, Mr. McFalls answered "No." Similarly, question 22 on form SF-86 asked whether during the last seven years, the applicant had "[l]eft a job by mutual agreement following allegations of unsatisfactory performance." In response, Mr. McFalls did not provide any information regarding his resignation from HHS. In light of these facts, we conclude that substantial evidence supports the Board's finding that by not disclosing information about his resignation from HHS in response to question 11 on OF-306 and question 22 on SF-86, Mr. McFalls provided false information on these forms.

In Harrison v. USDA, 411 Fed. Appx. 312, 313-15 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), one of a number of the sustained charges in a removal case concerned a response to an inquiry about prior employment:

The second charge was supported by four specifications. All four specifications involved answers Ms. Harrison provided on Optional Form 306 ("OF-306") as part of her employment application with the FSA and on the "electronic questionnaire for investigations processing" form ("e-QIP") that Ms. Harrison filled out during the investigation of the first charge.

Before joining the FSA, Ms. Harrison worked in a similar position with the Federal Emergency Management Agency ("FEMA") until she was terminated in November of 2004. Following her termination, she appealed to the Board and eventually entered into a settlement agreement with FEMA. The first two specifications relate to her failure to disclose the existence of the settlement agreement. When Ms. Harrison filled out OF-306 on her first day of work with the FSA, she answered "no" to question 12:

During the last 5 years, have you been fired from any job for any reason, did you quit after being told that you would be fired, did you leave any job by mutual agreement because of specific problems, or were you debarred from Federal employment by the Office of Personnel Management or any other Federal agency?

Ms. Harrison also responded "no" to a similar question on the e-QIP. Because Ms. Harrison had resigned from her position with FEMA by mutual agreement, the administrative judge concluded that her answers were false and therefore sustained the first two specifications.

...

With respect to the second charge, Ms. Harrison claims that she did not knowingly and intentionally falsify information regarding her employment with FEMA. To prevail on that charge, the agency was required to prove that the statement was false, that it was material, and that the employee acted with the intent to deceive the agency for her private gain. Leatherbury v. Dep't of the Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1301-02 (Fed. Cir. 2008). The settlement agreement between FEMA and Ms. Harrison permitted her to acknowledge that she left her position by mutual agreement. Although FEMA has amended her SF-50 to indicate that she was not removed but left by agreement, that change did not alter her obligation to respond truthfully to the questions relating to separation by mutual agreement. Falsification of federal employment applications is a type of misconduct as to which materiality is presumed. See Kissner v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 792 F.2d 133, 134 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Moreover, an intent to defraud can be inferred from circumstantial evidence, such as an applicant's desire not to disclose information that could adversely affect the likelihood of her being hired. Here, the administrative judge considered the record as a whole and Ms. Harrison's explanations as to why she answered the questions the way she did and found that she acted with an intent to deceive the FSA. That finding is supported by substantial evidence; we therefore sustain the Board's decision with respect to the first and second specifications of the falsification charge.

...The appellant was a Correctional Officer in a Federal prison who worked face to face with inmates every day. His honesty must be beyond question because any breach of integrity could compromise the safety of the entire institution....

A charge of falsification of employment documents can be sustained only on a finding of intent to deceive or mislead. Cadena v. DOJ, 3 MSPR 304, 308, 3 MSPB 390 (1980); see Naekel, supra, 782 F.2d 975 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Hathaway v. DOJ, 384 F.3d 1342, 1351-52 (Fed. Cir. 2004) (disclosures to agency investigators of information that the appellant was charged with withholding in later questions during an interview belied the suggestion that the appellant intentionally withheld information during an agency inquiry); Thias v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 46, 48 (1986) ("[t]he agency did not charge appellant with violating that regulation, only with falsifying an official document.... To prove falsification, the agency is required to show that appellant possessed the specific intent to falsify").

Implicit in the charge of falsification of an SF-171 Form is the allegation that the misstatements were made intentionally and with the purpose of defrauding the government. The intent may be shown by circumstantial evidence and it may also be shown if there has been a misrepresentation made with reckless disregard for the truth or with the conscious purpose of avoiding knowledge of the truth. See Riggin v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 50, 55 (1982) (employee was removed for one charge of falsification despite 18 years of service; the charge went to his reliability and trustworthiness in his position as an investigation clerk), following remand from Riggin v. DHHS, 6 MSPR 24, 28 (1981) (immaterial that the form falsified was not mandated by regulations); cf. Pappas v. OPM, 76 MSPR 152, 158-59 (1997) (suitability appeal: the employee failed to properly respond to an OPM question as to whether the appellant left a job because of unfavorable circumstances).

Deliberate misrepresentation on employment or promotion applications may be inferred when the appellant could have or should have provided clarification on an application and failed to do so. See Bryant v. DOJ, 39 MSPR 632, 636 (1989) (appellant failed to request clarification of a question on a promotion application concerning his educational achievements); cf. McCreary v. OPM, 27 MSPR 459, 462 (1985) (suitability case: "Specific intent to make a false or fraudulent statement may be inferred when the alleged misrepresentation is made with reckless disregard for truth"; rejecting the argument that the appellant admitted the most serious of his misdemeanor conviction and would have no motive for concealing two lesser convictions that he knew would be available to OPM investigators with access to his complete criminal record).

The application for federal employment, SF-171, carries the warning of the possibility of removal as a penalty for falsification. Payne v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 693, 697, 6 MSPB 581 (1981). Removal for intentional falsification of an employment application has consistently been found to promote the efficiency of the service. Falsification of an SF-171 permits a presumption of nexus between the dismissed employee's misconduct and the efficiency of the service. Delessio v. USPS, 33 MSPR 517, 521 (1987); see Kissner v. OPM, 792 F.2d 133 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("Falsification of records, such as the SF-171, is a type of misconduct from which a nexus between the dismissed employee's misconduct and the efficiency of the service is presumed."); Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., 782 F.2d 975 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (distinguishing between incorrect and fraudulent answers to questions on an SF-171); Haack v. USPS, 68 MSPR 275, 283 (1995) (sustaining dismissal for providing false answers to eight questions on two PS-2485 Certificate of Medical Examination forms that requested information about the appellant's medical history); Howard v. OPM, 31 MSPR 617, 621 (1986) (OPM-directed suitability removals); Wilcox v. USPS, 9 MSPR 493, 495 (1982) (appellant failed to produce any evidence to substantiate his claim that he had reasons to believe that his first criminal conviction had been or should have been removed from the court records); cf. Betts v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 37, 39 (1984) (discounting reasons provided by the appellant for omitting a few months of his employment history on a SF-171). But see McClain v. OPM, 76 MSPR 230, 238 (1997) (charge in a suitability case of the falsification of a response to an inquiry about a criminal record was not sustained when the appellant believed that a "deferred prosecution" meant the record of the guilty finding would be expunged and any conviction set aside). It appears to be immaterial that the false application does not achieve the desired result. See Bryant v. DOJ, 39 MSPR 632, 638 (1989) (an incorrect application for promotion did not result in promotion).

Employees may be charged with intentional deception through employment applications that do not fairly respond to broad questions about whether the appellant departed a prior job under unfavorable circumstances or through mutual agreement as a result of specific problems, as Pappas v. OPM, 76 MSPR 152, 156-60 (1997), a suitability appeal, explained:

In analyzing the suitability determination here, the administrative judge found that the appellant's superior with the NYSP (Lt. Thomas Wood) had informed the appellant that his performance was unacceptable and that Lt. Wood thus planned to recommend his termination; she further found that Lt. Wood had suggested to the appellant that, for his own good and that of the NYSP, he should consider resigning. She concluded that when confronted with his poor performance results and Lt. Wood's anticipated recommendation, the appellant chose to resign rather than pursue any disciplinary matter through. the NYSP's internal review process.

The administrative judge nonetheless found that the appellant had not made an intentionally false or deceptive statement or engaged in criminal or dishonest conduct by failing to disclose the circumstances of his resignation in answering the questions quoted above on the SF-86 and the SF-171. She determined that he had not been fired by the NYSP and had not been told that he would be fired (merely that Lt. Wood had recommended his termination). The administrative judge further found that the appellant had not left his job with the NYSP by mutual agreement because of specific problems or allegations of misconduct or unsatisfactory performance because the record did not show "an exchange of benefits between the appellant and the NY State Police that would constitute a mutual agreement, particularly in light of the fact that the appellant was later foreclosed from receiving unemployment benefits because he resigned voluntarily." Finally, she concluded that the question on the SF-86 asking whether the appellant had left a job under "unfavorable circumstances" was "inappropriate" because that phrase was "so broad and vague as to be subject to wide interpretation." She therefore found that his "exclusion" of a response to that question was reasonable. The administrative judge thus determined that none of the questions quoted above applied to the appellant's resignation from the NYSP and that they did not require him to describe the circumstances of his resignation.

OPM argues that the administrative judge erred in finding that the appellant had not made an intentionally false or deceptive statement or engaged in criminal or dishonest conduct with respect to this matter. We agree. As noted above, the administrative judge's own undisputed factual findings show that the appellant took advantage of Lt. Wood's suggestion and resigned rather than contest Lt. Wood's presumptive recommendation that he be terminated for unsatisfactory performance; Capt. Wood testified that he made clear to the appellant his opinion that resignation was in the "best interest" of both the appellant and the NYSP. Given this background, it seems clear that the appellant did leave his job because of "specific problems" and "following allegations of unsatisfactory performance," and that the appellant and the NYSP mutually agreed that resignation was his best option under those circumstances. The appellant thus should have disclosed those circumstances in response to questions on the SF-171 and the SF-86, and his failure to do so raises an inference of an intent to deceive. See Naekel, 782 F.2d at 978; Devitto, 61 MSPR at 302.

As noted above, however, the administrative judge found that the appellant had not resigned by "mutual agreement" because he did not obtain unemployment benefits as a result of that resignation and thus had not received the tangible "exchange of benefits" required to support a "mutual agreement." OPM rightly points out, that this reasoning strongly suggests that the administrative judge interpreted "mutual agreement" to mean a contract; under the law of contracts, an enforceable contract does not exist unless it is based on a bargained-for exchange known as "consideration." See J.M. Perillo & H.H. Bender, Corbin on Contracts, § 5.1 at 2-7 (Rev. ed. 1995). Consideration under the law of contracts, however, is not measured in dollars and cents alone, and courts are not usually disposed to enter upon "nice calculation" of whether consideration is adequate. See Corbin on Contracts, § 5.14 at 63-65. A very small consideration (the proverbial "peppercorn") can support an enforceable contract; convenience, avoidance of troublesome details and efforts, and forbearance to institute proceedings against the other party, for example, can be proper elements of consideration. See id. at 65-71. Even under the law of contracts, then, the agreement between the appellant and the NYSP that he should resign might be an enforceable contract supported by sufficient consideration; the NYSP obtained the appellant's departure without termination proceedings, and the appellant avoided the unpleasant prospect of contesting such proceedings.

It is unnecessary, however, to decide whether or not this agreement was binding under the law of contracts. Nothing on the face of the SF-86 or the SF-171 stated or implied that the phrase "mutual agreement" should be interpreted in that way.[7] As support for her interpretation, the administrative judge cited Forma v. Department of Justice, 57 MSPR 97, 100, aff'd, 11 F.3d 1071 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table), as an example of resignation by mutual agreement, where the agency agreed to show that the employee had resigned involuntarily (so that he could receive unemployment benefits) in exchange for his resignation. Review of Forma, however, does not disclose that the Board required (either directly or implicitly) an agreement between an employee and his former employing agency regarding a resignation to be supported by consideration under the law of contracts in order to constitute a "mutual agreement." It merely found in that case that an agreement between the employee and the agency existed and that it resulted in his resignation following allegations of unsatisfactory performance. Under those circumstances, the Board held, the appellant should have disclosed on an SF-86 and an SF-171 that he had left his job with that agency by mutual agreement because of a specific problem and following allegations of unsatisfactory performance; it therefore found that the appellant had intentionally given false answers by failing to disclose those facts. See Forma, 57 MSPR at 103-05.

[7] As OPM rightly observes, it is not otherwise reasonable to assume that a wide variety of federal job applicants, responding to simple, general questions on employment forms in widespread use, should have a familiarity with the black-letter law of contracts in order to respond to them.

Based on essentially the same facts, we reach the same result. The lack of a promise of unemployment benefits supporting the mutual agreement between the NYSP and the appellant that he should resign after allegations of unsatisfactory performance did not discharge him from his duty to tell the truth and to reveal the circumstances of his resignation. Our precedent simply does not direct us to absolve him from that responsibility, and we decline to do so.

As noted above, the administrative judge also found that it was reasonable for the appellant not to disclose these matters in response to a question on the SF-86 asking whether he had left a job under "unfavorable circumstances" because that phrase was too broad and subject to wide interpretation. OPM argues that this finding is mistaken, and we agree. We do not dispute that the phrase is general and that, in a particular case, an employee might be justifiably uncertain about whether it applied to the specific facts of his employment history. This is not that case. As discussed above, the appellant left his job with the NYSP rather than face possible termination proceedings for unsatisfactory performance. Given those facts, the appellant reasonably should have known that he had left his NYSP job under unfavorable circumstances, and it was not reasonable for him to "exclude" a response on that issue. The argument that the phrase was too broad for him to respond accurately to the question thus is not plausible here, and an inference of an intent to deceive arises from his failure to submit a true response. See Hanker, 73 MSPR at 164 (a failure to disclose information in response to a question on an SF-86, coupled with the lack of a plausible explanation for that failure, warranted an inference of an intent to deceive). For the reasons discussed above, then, the record shows that the appellant did give intentionally false or deceptive answers and engaged in dishonest conduct in completing the SF-86 and the SF-171.

See Forma v. DOJ, 57 MSPR 97, 103 (1993) ("Because the appellant himself asserted that he left by mutual agreement, and he did not deny that there had been allegations of unsatisfactory performance, the only accurate answer to the question '[have you] left a job by mutual agreement following allegations of unsatisfactory performance,' was 'yes.' Similarly, a 'yes' response was the only accurate answer to the question 'did you leave by mutual agreement because of specific problems,' because either allegations of poor performance or the existence of a 'mismatch' between the appellant's interests and strengths and the requirements of his position would have constituted a 'specific problem.'").

If the charge is lack of candor in responses to a pre-employment inquiry, the issue is the appellant's motivation, rather than the agency's conjecture, as to the responses. In Acosta v. DHS, AT-0752-08-0677-I-3 (NP 4/6/2011), the question was what prompted the employee to resign his prior job, when the evidence suggested two possible reasons. The Board concluded that the appellant truthfully responded when he gave the reason that caused his prior separation:

Effective May 30, 2008, the agency removed the appellant from his Federal Air Marshal (FAM) position with the Transportation Security Administration based on three specifications of lack of candor. Specifications 2 and 3 arose from the appellant's answering "no" to Question 22 on his Standard Form (SF) 86 and Question 20 on his Security Clearance Application concerning whether, in the previous 7 years, he had "[l]eft a job for other reasons under unfavorable circumstances." Specification 4 arose from the appellant's answer in response to a question during an administrative inquiry regarding why he resigned from his position with the United States Customs Service (USCS); he stated that he resigned in anticipation of his appointment to the agency and because of the USCS's refusal to reassign him to a different program. The deciding official determined that the appellant lacked candor because when the appellant resigned from his position with USCS on July 15, 2001, he was aware that USCS had proposed a 14-day suspension action on or about April 20, 2001.

After holding a hearing, the administrative judge issued an initial decision that affirmed the removal action. He found that the agency proved the three specifications of lack of candor, and that the appellant failed to prove his affirmative defense. Further, the administrative judge found that the removal action promoted the efficiency of the service. In a timely filed petition for review, the appellant reiterates his arguments on appeal below. The agency did not respond.

In sustaining the lack of candor charge, the administrative judge found that the proposed 14-day suspension action "was clearly relevant and should have been disclosed," even if the appellant's reason for leaving USCS was completely unrelated to the proposed suspension action. In so finding, he essentially concluded that the existence of the proposed disciplinary action ipso facto mandated its disclosure. However, such an approach bypasses the critical question of whether the appellant left USCS as a result of receiving the proposed 14-day suspension action, or if there were other circumstances that motivated his departure. See Jacobs v. Department of Justice, 35 F.3d 1543, 1547 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (interpreting the "unfavorable circumstances" language as meaning that the circumstances caused an individual's departure from a job). The administrative judge should have weighed the conflicting evidence in this regard, but he made no findings on this issue. We therefore weigh the evidence on review. See Cummings v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 17, 21 (1991), aff'd, 980 F.2d 745 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).

The appellant's July 27, 2001 resignation letter sets forth the alleged harassment to which he was subjected, articulates his frustration over USCS management's alleged reluctance or incapability to reassign him, and states that he was resigning "to stop [his] personal and professional turmoil since these 'managers' have made it a point to continue to perpetrate their harassment." He then states that "[i]t is sad commentary about how Customs really treats its employees. I am notifying your office that I am resigning from the [USCS] effective the date of this memorandum." This is consistent with the "reason for resignation" contained in the SF-50 documenting the appellant's resignation, which states, "incompetent management within polygraph operations investigation services division, and office of investigation, USCS," and with the appellant's oral and written statements to the agency and to the Board.

The agency, however, relied heavily upon the portion of the SF-50 documenting the appellant's resignation, which states: "Agency Finding: resigned after receiving written notice on 04/20/01 of proposal to suspend for 14 days." The record evidence and the inherent inconsistencies in the SF-50 itself cast doubt upon whether the "Agency Finding" in SF-50 accurately described the motivation behind the appellant's resignation. In contrast, the "reason for resignation" section of the SF-50 is consistent with the reasons set forth in the appellant's July 2001 resignation letter and the other record evidence.

We also note that at the hearing and at his deposition, the appellant testified that following his resignation, USCS issued an SF-50 that did not include an "agency finding," and later issued a second one that included an "agency finding." This is consistent with the appellant's May 2008 written response to an agency official regarding his reasons for resigning from the USCS. The agency did not submit any evidence or argument on this issue.

It is also significant to our analysis that USCS's proposed disciplinary action had been pending against the appellant for approximately 3 months without apparent action when he decided to resign. There is no indication that USCS had decided to take or effect the action at the time of the appellant's resignation. Additionally, the proposed action was only a 14-day suspension, which further supports that it was not the motivation behind the appellant's resignation.

Lack of candor is a broader and more flexible concept than falsification, and as such, its contours and elements depend on the particular context and conduct involved. Little v. Department of Transportation, 112 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 18 (2009). It may involve a failure to disclose something that, under the circumstances, should have been disclosed to make a statement accurate and complete. Id.; see Ludlum v. Department of Justice, 278 F.3d 1280, 1284 (Fed. Cir. 2002). As discussed below, under the circumstances of this appeal, we find that the facts do not support a lack of candor charge.

Neither Question 22 in the SF-86 nor Question 20 in the Security Clearance Application specifically elicited whether the appellant left a previous job as a result of proposed or effected disciplinary actions. Regardless of what the agency and USCS believe was the reason for the appellant's resignation, the questions on these employment application forms are posed to the applicant for employment and, thus, the answers to these questions necessarily concern the applicant's reason for leaving a former job. Thus, the appellant was only obligated to inform the agency of the proposed disciplinary action in response to these questions if he left the USCS because of the proposed 14-day suspension action. See Jacobs, 35 F.3d at 1547. As discussed above, there is little evidence to support the view that the appellant resigned from the USCS because of the proposed 14-day suspension, and much evidence supporting that the appellant resigned from the USCS for reasons unrelated to the proposed 14-day suspension. Consequently, he was not required to disclose the proposed disciplinary action in response to Question 22 on the SF-86 or Question 20 on the Security Clearance Application. We therefore find that the appellant did not demonstrate a lack of candor in responding to the questions at issue, and that the agency failed to prove Specifications 2 and 3. See Jacobs, 35 F.3d at 1547.

The final specification of lack of candor alleges that the appellant's responses during the December 2007 administrative inquiry regarding why he resigned from the USCS were "less then fully forthcoming" because the appellant first stated that he resigned from USCS in anticipation of his appointment to the agency, and then stated that he resigned following the denial of his transfer request. In support of this specification, the agency presented a memorandum summarizing the administrative inquiry prepared by an agency investigator on May 12, 2008. First, as noted above, the agency investigator drafted the memorandum on May 12, 2008, 5 months after the December 13 and 14, 2007 interviews. The record does not contain contemporaneous notes of the administrative inquiry, and the May 12, 2008 memorandum does not state that it was based on notes taken during the meeting with the appellant. Thus, there is reason to doubt the accuracy of the May 12, 2008 memorandum. Moreover, to the extent that the agency contends that the appellant failed to disclose the proposed disciplinary action, there is no indication that he was asked about such matters during the investigative interview. Finally, and most significantly, the appellant's purported responses during the administrative inquiry are consistent with the reasons he has repeatedly provided for his resignation from the USCS. Thus, we conclude that the agency failed to show that the appellant lacked candor during the December 2007 administrative inquiry.
