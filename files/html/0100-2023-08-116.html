c. Marital Status

Essential to an allegation of marital status discrimination is different treatment of married and unmarried employees. See Dvortsin v. DHS, 314 Fed. Appx. 307, 309 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) ("In order to allege marital status discrimination, Ms. Dvortsin must assert facts, which if proven, demonstrate that unmarried employees were treated differently from married employees."); Collins v. MSPB, 65 Fed. Appx. 297 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (marital status claim was not made out by a contention that the appellant was terminated, as the spouse of an enlisted man, while another woman in a comparable situation, who was the spouse of an officer, was not terminated); Lipniarski v. MSPB, 26 Fed. Appx. 919 (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP) ("Because Lipniarski failed to present any evidence that the INS allowed single employees to take lengthy leaves of absence or to fail to report for duty on account of family obligations, we conclude that the Board correctly found that he failed to set forth a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, and that the Board therefore lacked jurisdiction to hear his appeal."), followed, LaFond v. DOD, SF-315H-13-0884-I-1 (NP 4/1/2014) ("Although the appellant alleged that Ms. H. and others may have gossiped about him, the appellant did not allege facts that, if proven, would show that the agency would have treated a single employee differently under the same circumstances. Cf., e.g., Lipniarski v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 26 F. App'x 919, 922-923 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (probationary employee failed to nonfrivolously allege marital status discrimination because he did not present any evidence that the agency treated single employees more favorably than married employees with respect to extended leave for family obligations). He also did not allege facts that would show that the agency exhibited a keen interest in his marital status, as the Board found in the other cases he cited."); Chase-Baker v. DOJ, 198 F.3d 843, 845 (Fed. Cir. 1999) ("The statute is...concerned...only with any difference in treatment of married and unmarried employees."); Goklaney v. Dept. of Navy, PH-315H-11-0355-I-1 (NP 11/28/2011) ("We agree with the administrative judge that the statements of the appellant's managers regarding the appellant's perceived preference to spend time with his family rather than travel for work do not go to the essence of the appellant's status as a married individual; rather, they go to his status as an individual with familial responsibilities."); Miller v. DOJ, 41 MSPR 353, 356 (1989) (no disparity was apparent from an allegation that an employee's supervisor told him that he could have problems because of frequent calls made by the employee's wife to the facility where he worked).

If a probationer alleges that he was the victim of adverse action because he was married to a person of another race, his complaint is one of racial rather than marital status discrimination. Shah v. GSA, 7 MSPR 626, 628, 7 MSPB 460 (1981); see Fort v. Dept. of Navy, DC-315H-10-0756-I-1 (NP 9/20/2011) ("Specifically, she alleges that she was discriminated against because she is an "Officer's wife." We agree with the administrative judge, however, that the appellant did not allege facts that, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over her appeal. The appellant has not alleged that she was treated differently than an unmarried person; she merely argues that she was treated differently, and that people were jealous of her, because she was married to an Officer. These arguments do not go to the essence of her status as a married person. See Smirne v. Department of the Army, 115 M.S.P.R. 51, ¶ 8 (2010)."); Smith v. Dept. of Energy, 89 MSPR 430, 434 ¶ 9 (2001) (marital status claim was not made out by assertion that the appellant's RIF was tainted by "illegal favoritism" of a personnel specialist's husband), following remand aff'd on other grounds, Smith v. Dept. of Energy, 73 Fed. Appx. 398 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP); cf. Gramling v. Dept. of Interior, DC-315H-15-0375-I-1 (NP 6/9/2015) ("We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant has not alleged that he was treated differently due to his marital status because there was no indication that the appellant would not have been terminated for making inappropriate comments if he had been married.").

For the employee to obtain a Board hearing, his allegation should be supported by detailed facts identifying the discrimination. See Goss v. Dept. of Air Force, 131 Fed. Appx. 721, 724 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP) (marital status discrimination: to assert a nonfrivolous claim an employee must at a minimum make factual allegations that support the elements of the claim, citing Hayes v. USPS, 390 F.3d 1373, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2004)); Herring v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3443) (bare allegation of marital status discrimination, unaccompanied by any supporting factual allegations, is insufficient to support jurisdiction); Vandewall v. Dept. of Transp., 52 MSPR 150, 157 (1991) (appellant did not present specific nonfrivolous allegations that married persons were treated less harshly for similar misconduct). There must be a connection alleged between marital status and the termination. See Kennington v. MSPB, 385 Fed. Appx. 983, 986 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) ("His allegations that his supervisor and President Obama are able to marry while he is not able to marry someone of the same sex are devoid of any hint of causation"). The claim of marital status discrimination should not be raised for the first time in a petition for review, unless there is new and material evidence. Macko v. Dept. of Army, DA-315H-13-4722-I-1 (NP 5/6/2014).

Jurisdiction is not established by unsworn and unsupported allegations amounting to conjecture about the motivation of the action against the appellant. See Bedynek-Stumm v. USDA, 57 MSPR 176, 179 (1993) ("assertions he made to support his contention all regard disputes over work-related matters, and are completely unrelated to his marital status except for his conjectures that this coworker sought to dominate him because of her negative feelings toward him as an unmarried male"); cf. Goodwin v. PBGC, DC-315I-22-0138-I-1 (NP 7/11/2022) ("She also claims on review that the agency's Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO) office had told her that it would be hard for her to prove that she was harassed based on her 'race and sex' because both her and her alleged harasser were 'black females,' that she could not file an age discrimination complaint because she was under 40 years old, and that District of Columbia law did not apply to her as a Federal employee. These arguments undercut her allegation of marital status discrimination and suggest that she raised them only after first claiming discrimination and retaliation on other grounds."); Smith v. SSA, SF-315H-16-0544-I-1 (NP 7/6/2022) ("If an appellant's claim is limited to conjecture about animus toward him as an unmarried male, such a claim does not amount to a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction."..."Here, the appellant asserted below that his supervisor 'played favoritism' with a married coworker. Because such a pro forma allegation is insufficient, we agree with the administrative judge that the appellant failed to nonfrivolously allege jurisdiction."); Batchelder v. MSPB, 71 Fed. Appx. 67 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (allegations pertaining to the appellant's status as single and as a conservative dresser did not rise to the level of nonfrivolous allegations of marital status discrimination in a probationary termination appeal).

Under Edem v. Dept. of Commerce, 64 MSPR 501, 503-06 (1994), involving alleged marital status discrimination, the probationer's allegations need not be sworn to advance to a jurisdictional hearing, although the Board's current regulation defining a nonfrivolous allegation suggests that allegations should be in sworn form. 5 CFR 1201.4(s) (2022). Assuming a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, the Board applies the analytical model of McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973): the employee has the initial burden of establishing a prima facie case; the agency may rebut that case by articulating a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for the termination; and the employee may then attempt to establish that the "legitimate reason" was pretextual. See Carmichael v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 135, 138 (1982); Craighead v. USDA, 6 MSPR 159, 162, 6 MSPB 142 (1981) (applying McDonnell Douglas to a marital status discrimination claim). A probationer alleging marital status discrimination has the same burden of proof as an individual alleging discrimination under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act [discussed in Chapter 12, "Probationer"] . See McClintock v. VA, 6 MSPR 475, 478, 6 MSPB 402 (1981) (describing shifting burden of proof).

Ney v. Dept. of Commerce, 115 MSPR 204, 208-09 ¶ 11, 2010 MSPB 219 (2010), synopsized circumstances warranting a hearing on a marital status discrimination allegation:

In particular, the appellant specifically alleged that (1) she had good performance reviews and no one explained her termination; (2) her supervisor regularly raised questions about her marital status; (3) she was assigned menial tasks not assigned to single employees; (4) married employees were subjected to harsher standards; (5) married employees were objects of ridicule; (6) her responsibilities were given to an inexperienced single employee; and (7) single employees received better treatment and preferences for telework. The Board has found similar allegations sufficient to entitle an appellant to a jurisdictional hearing. See, e.g., Smirne v. Department of the Army, 115 M.S.P.R. 51, ¶ 11 (2010) (the appellant alleged that her purported performance problems were fabrications, she was the only single, pregnant, newly hired secretary, and she was the only new secretary terminated); Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 8 (the appellant alleged that his supervisor made derogatory remarks about his marital status, told him he would not be having "problems" if he was married, and excluded his fiancée and young child from an official function); Kiser v. Department of Education, 66 M.S.P.R. 372, 379-81 (1995) (the appellant alleged that his supervisor made repeated comments about his marriage and social life, he was terminated despite a superior performance review, and single employees were treated less harshly for the same conduct); Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 M.S.P.R. 501, 504-505 (1994) (the appellant alleged that her supervisor questioned her three times about her relationship with her husband (from whom she was separated) and children); Paine v. Department of Health & Human Services, 32 M.S.P.R. 135, 137 (1987) (the appellant alleged that the agency overlooked more serious acts of misconduct and performance deficiencies for married employees than single employees). Thus, the appellant presented facially nonfrivolous allegations regarding her marital status discrimination claim, and the administrative judge erred in dismissing her case without holding a jurisdictional hearing. See Stokes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 761 F.2d 682, 686 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (if a probationary employee presents a facially nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, she is entitled to a hearing).

Pommert v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 99, 101-02 (1982), involving a claim that an employee was fired because his child care responsibilities as a single parent caused him to leave early, described the contours of marital status discrimination:

It is clear from appellant's contentions of appeal that his allegation of marital status discrimination is not based on his status as single but rather on his contention that he was entitled to leave early, despite the absence of accrued leave, because of family obligations. Appellant's need to leave early did not arise from the fact that he was single or even that he had been divorced but rather from the fact that he was entrusted with the care of his children. In this respect, appellant is no different from other parents regardless of their marital status. Thus, we cannot agree with his contention that the failure to grant his request constitutes marital discrimination.

This is consistent with prior Board decisions concerning marital discrimination claims. In Shah v. GSA, 7 MSPB 460, 7 MSPR 626 (1981), we held that in order to make an allegation of marital status discrimination which was not frivolous in character, appellant's allegations must go to the essence of the marital status of the appellant. There, the Board found that a foreign-born appellant who alleged that he was discriminated against because he was married to a white American woman would not establish a claim of marital status discrimination, even if his allegations were true, since there was no showing "that the actions against him were taken as a result of marital status per se." Id. at 460, 7 MSPR at 628. Similarly, in Hilden v. U.S. Department of Agriculture, 8 MSPB 3, 8 MSPR 300 (1981), we held that a claim that appellant's termination was discriminatory since it put her in a desperate financial situation because of her single status was frivolous since her financial situation, not her marital status as single, was the basis for her allegation. There, the Board found that the presiding official should have dismissed her appeal without making a finding as to the merits of her termination. See also Yakupzack v. Department of Agriculture, 9 MSPB 435, 10 MSPR 180 (1982).

In the present case, the presiding official should have also dismissed appellant's appeal as frivolous, since, on its face, his allegation of discrimination was not based on his status as single but on his familial obligations. Inasmuch as Col. Baker's denial of his request to leave early did not evidence a discriminatory motive towards single individuals, contrary to the presiding official's finding, there was no basis for inferring a discriminatory motive to his subsequent decision to terminate appellant for insubordination. Therefore, the presiding official erred in finding that appellant established a prima facie case of discrimination in light of the nature of appellant's allegations.

Family or child care responsibilities were insufficient to invoke marital status discrimination jurisdiction over a probationer's discharge in Merritt v. Dept. of Navy, PH-315H-10-0264-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010), considering whether the appellant's supervisor expressed "a keen interest in the appellant's marital status, including the way in which his or her marital status might affect employment," and determining that racially-oriented remarks about the nature of the appellant's marriage may have demonstrated racial or national origin prejudice but not marital status discrimination:

In his affidavit, the appellant indicated that the reason that he requested telework to Georgia was "in support of [his] family" and "to attend to [his] family." However, the fact that he has family responsibilities does not constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination. For instance, in Lipniarksi v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 26 F. App'x 919 (Fed. Cir. 2001), the appellant alleged before the court that, "had he not been married, he would not have been called away to assist his wife with her father's illness, and thus the [employing agency] would not have terminated him." Id. at 922. The Federal Circuit rejected this argument, concluding that "[marital] responsibilities do not excuse married employees from having to comply with orders to report for duty any more than they excuse similarly situated single employees having comparable family obligations," and it affirmed the Board's decision. Id. at 922-23. Although Lipniarski is an unpublished decision, the Board may rely on unpublished Federal Circuit decisions if it finds the court's reasoning persuasive. E.g., Herring v. Department of the Navy, 90 M.S.P.R. 165, ¶ 13 n.* (2001), review dismissed, 35 F. App'x 887 (Fed. Cir. 2002). We believe that the appellant's allegations regarding his family obligations are similar to the allegations in Lipniarski; thus, the court's reasoning is persuasive. Similarly, the Board has held that "childcare responsibilities are not dependent upon an individual's marital status" and such allegations "[do] not go to the essence of [the appellant's] marital status." Awa v. Department of the Navy, 41 M.S.P.R. 318, 322 (1989); see Pommert v. Department of the Army, 14 M.S.P.R. 99, 101-02 (1982) (an allegation of marital status discrimination was frivolous when it was based on the appellant's childcare obligations, not his marital status). Therefore, any allegation based on the fact that he desired to support his family and/or care for his children does not support his claim of marital status discrimination.

Child care responsibilities, to the extent they contributed to a probationer's discharge, did not equate to marital status discrimination, Williams v. DOD, PH-315H-14-0435-I-1 (NP 7/21/2014):

In determining whether an appellant has established jurisdiction under 5 C.F.R. § 315.806(b), the Board follows a two-step process. Marynowski v. Department of the Navy, 118 M.S.P.R. 321, ¶ 5 (2012). First, the appellant must make nonfrivolous claims of jurisdiction, i.e., factual allegations that, if proven, would establish that her termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status. Id. An appellant who makes such nonfrivolous claims is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which she must then prove the basis for jurisdiction, i.e., that her termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status, by a preponderance of the evidence. Id. To make a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, an appellant must allege facts which, taken as true, would show that she was treated differently because of her marital status or facts that go to the essence of her status as married, single, or divorced. Id., ¶ 9. In Marynowski, for instance, the appellant alleged that the supervisor who terminated her was motivated by jealousy of her marriage because she had repeatedly spoken about it. Id., ¶ 8. The supervisor, for example, would mention the appellant's mode of dress and grooming habits and speculate aloud as to whether these things pleased her husband. Id. The Board concluded that the appellant's allegation, taken as true, indicated that the supervisor displayed a "keen interest in the state of the probationer's marital status." Id., ¶ 9.

Although the appellant in the instant appeal alleged that her supervisor was not understanding of her need to take leave to care for her children and that her supervisor disapproved of her previous interracial marriage with her ex-husband, the appellant did not allege facts that, if proven, would show that the agency would have treated a single or married, rather than divorced, employee differently under the same circumstances, cf., e.g., Lipniarski v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 26 F. App'x 919, 922-23 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (nonprecedential) (probationary employee failed to nonfrivolously allege marital status discrimination because he did not present any evidence that the agency treated single employees more favorably than married employees regarding extended leave for family obligations). She also did not allege facts that would show that the agency exhibited a keen interest in her marital status, as the Board has found in other cases. See, e.g., Marynowski, 118 M.S.P.R. 321, ¶ 9; Strausbaugh v. Government Printing Office, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 8 (2009); Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 M.S.P.R. 501, 504 (1994). In these cases, the appellant's supervisor expressed a keen interest in the appellant's marital status, including the way in which his or her marital status might affect employment. Marynowski, 118 M.S.P.R. 321, ¶¶ 8-9; Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶¶ 3, 8; Edem, 64 M.S.P.R. at 503-05; see Green-Brown v. Department of Defense, 118 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 6 (2012) (the appellant alleged that her supervisor threatened her employment standing while referencing her marital and parental status).

In contrast, the appellant here did not allege that the supervisor expressed a keen interest in the appellant's status as divorced. Rather, the appellant claimed that her supervisor was not understanding of her need to take leave to care for her children. However, childcare responsibilities per se are not dependent on an individual's marital status and do not go to the essence of marital status. Green-Brown, 118 M.S.P.R. 327, ¶ 7 n.2; see Awa v. Department of the Navy, 41 M.S.P.R. 318, 322 (1989); Pommert v. Department of the Army, 14 M.S.P.R. 99, 101-02 (1982) (probationary employee, a divorced parent with custody of children, failed to nonfrivolously allege marital status discrimination because his allegation was based on familial obligations, not marital status). The appellant also claimed that her supervisor disapproved of her previous interracial marriage, even though she admitted that the supervisor said that she personally does not have a problem with interracial relationships. The appellant's evidence does not suggest that the action against her was taken as a result of marital status per se but rather that it was allegedly taken as a result of racial and national origin prejudice. See Shah v. General Services Administration, 7 M.S.P.R. 626, 628 (1981) (unkind remarks about the appellant's interracial marriage failed to suggest that action against him was taken as a result of marital status per se).

A woman who claimed that she was terminated because of her child care responsibilities did not make out a claim of marital status discrimination; the allegations of discrimination must go to the essence of the appellant's marital status. Awa v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 318, 322 (1989); see Ciecwesz v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3312) (marital status discrimination was lacking in the probationer's claim that dismissal for failure to meet tax obligations resulted from financial pressures caused by support obligations following divorce: "While it cannot be said that Ciecwesz's child support payments are entirely independent of his marital status, such payments are an obligation that can be incurred regardless of marital status."); cf. Boucher v. VA, 27 MSPR 320, 321 n.* (1985) ("Appellant's claim of discrimination based on marital status does not go to the essence of his status as a single person and thus his claim is not within the Board's purview.").

Although child care responsibilities did not support a probationer's claim of marital status discrimination, allegations of disparate treatment of single and married employees supported jurisdiction, Green-Brown v. DOD, 118 MSPR 327, 328-31 ¶¶ 2-8, 2012 MSPB 84 (2012):

The agency terminated the appellant during her probationary period from her Program Analyst position based on "repeated time and attendance problems." On appeal, she challenged the merits of the action and, referring to herself as a "single black mother," argued that her supervisor had violated her rights and treated her unfairly based on her "marital status, race, and parental status." She requested a hearing. The administrative judge issued an order setting forth the legal standard for establishing Board jurisdiction over probationary terminations. In response, the appellant, among other things, repeated her claim that her supervisor used her status as a single black mother to create limitations, threaten her employment standing, and isolate her in the workplace....

In an initial decision based on the written record, the administrative judge dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction. She found that, even accepting the appellant's allegations as true, the appellant failed to assert a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination because she did not provide any description of how married employees were treated more favorably and cited to no specific examples of when she was treated disparately or denied a benefit of employment based on her marital status. The administrative judge further found that the appellant's allegation that she desired to, or was required to, care for her child did not support her claim of marital status discrimination because such allegations do not go to the essence of an individual's marital status.

In determining whether an appellant has established jurisdiction under 5 C.F.R. § 315.806(b), the Board follows a two-step process. Marynowski v. Department of the Navy, 2012 MSPB 82, ¶ 5; see Burton v. Department of the Air Force, 2012 MSPB 73, ¶¶ 10-11 (citing Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322, 1344 (Fed. Cir. 2006)). First, the appellant must make nonfrivolous claims of jurisdiction, i.e., factual allegations that, if proven, would establish that her termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status. Marynowski, 2012 MSPB 82, ¶ 5. An appellant who makes such nonfrivolous claims is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which she must then prove the basis for jurisdiction, i.e., that her termination was based on partisan political reasons or marital status, by a preponderance of the evidence. See id.

Here, the appellant alleged that on numerous occasions her supervisor threatened her employment standing while referencing her marital and parental status in an effort to get her to switch teams and/or organizations, to return to her previous employer, or to quit. The appellant further asserted that, when those attempts failed, he moved to terminate her employment based "on bogus claims" in order to "cover up" his retaliatory actions. In this regard, she challenged the incidents on which her termination was based, noting that, after they occurred, she received a performance bonus. In addition, the appellant described an incident that happened when she was working at home. According to the appellant, her supervisor asked where her daughter was, she replied that her uncle was also at home, and her supervisor said that it was only "okay" for her to work at home with her daughter present if someone was there to assist her. According to the appellant, married employees, including some who had minor or disabled dependents, were not subject to this policy.

We find the appellant's statement challenging as unsupported the time and attendance problems asserted by the agency, taken together with her statements alleging that, by his remarks, her supervisor targeted her as a single mother are sufficient to constitute nonfrivolous allegations that her termination was based on her marital status.[2] See Marynowski, 2012 MSPB 82, ¶ 8 (appellant's allegations that her supervisor showed a keen interest in her marital status, and was jealous that she had a husband, and that her fixation on the appellant's marital status caused her to lose training opportunities, constituted a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination); Strausbaugh v. Government Printing Office, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 8 (2009) (appellant's allegations that his supervisor showed a keen interest in the state of his marital status, and that he was terminated for bringing his fiancée to an official agency function in violation of rules which allegedly limited participation to immediate family members, constituted a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination); Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 M.S.P.R. 501, 504-05 (1994) (appellant's allegations that her supervisor showed a keen interest in the state of her marriage, particularly on how marital difficulties might affect her children, and that she believed her supervisor's disapproval of her separation played a substantial role in her termination, constituted a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination).

[2] We agree with the administrative judge's finding that childcare responsibilities per se are not dependent on an individual's marital status and do not go to the essence of marital status. See Awa v. Department of the Navy, 41 M.S.P.R. 318, 322 (1989). However, we also note the appellant's statement in her petition for review that she did not provide any statements in her correspondence to the Board to suggest, as the administrative judge did, that "she desired to or was required to care for her child."

Because she made a nonfrivolous allegation, the appellant is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which she must establish by a preponderance of the evidence that marital status discrimination was the basis for her termination. Marynowski, 2012 MSPB 82, ¶ 8.

An appellant argued that the agency terminated her because she was married. Her allegation was that the agency was concerned that married female employees were more likely to become pregnant and quit than were unmarried female employees. The Board determined that the marital status involved was closely intertwined with the Pregnancy Discrimination Act, making it a violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act for an employer to discriminate because of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions. Uriarte v. USDA, 6 MSPR 393, 395, 6 MSPB 334 (1981) (remanding for adjudication: "In this particular case the allegations of marital status discrimination and sex discrimination are so intertwined that no meaningful finding as to one can be made without consideration of the other."); see Wade v. Dept. of Navy, 45 MSPR 595, 599 (1990) ("The Pregnancy Discrimination Act, Pub. L. No. 95-555 (codified at 42 U.S.C. 2000-e(k)), expands the definition of unlawful sex discrimination to include discrimination 'because of or on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions.'"). In Brown v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 451, 454 (1986), jurisdiction was established by an allegation that the appellant was discharged because she was pregnant and unwed. In Herring v. VA, 72 MSPR 96, 101 (1996), an allegation that unmarried pregnant women were treated more harshly than married pregnant women could constitute a nonfrivolous allegation entitling the appellant to a hearing. But see Van Daele v. USPS, 1 MSPR 601, 603, 2 MSPB 16 (1980) (Board lacked jurisdiction over probationer's allegation that she was discriminated against based on pregnancy).

A hearing was directed in Smirne v. Dept. of Army, 115 MSPR 51, 53-55 ¶¶ 8-12, 2010 MSPB 188 (2010), involving an appellant who asserted that allegations of poor performance were untrue and that she was the only one of ten newly hired secretaries who was unmarried and pregnant while she worked at the facility:

To show that the Board has jurisdiction because of discrimination based on marital status or partisan political reasons, an appellant must make a nonfrivolous allegation that her termination was based on one of those factors. See Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 6. Nonfrivolous allegations are allegations of fact which, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter at issue. Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 325, 329 (1994). To make a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, a person may allege facts to show that she was treated differently because of her marital status or that go to the essence of her status as a married, single or divorced person. See Qatsha v. Department of Defense, 86 M.S.P.R. 121, ¶ 7 (2000). An appellant's allegations regarding marital status or partisan political discrimination must be "more than mere conjecture." Ellis v. Department of the Treasury, 81 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 13 (1999). An appellant must provide supporting facts to show that the allegation is not merely a pro forma pleading. Stokes, 761 F.2d at 686; Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 6; see Qatsha, 86 M.S.P.R. 121, ¶ 7. In determining whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction entitling her to a hearing, the administrative judge may consider the agency's documentary submissions; however, to the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions of the parties and the agency's evidence may not be dispositive. Ferdon, 60 M.S.P.R. at 329. If the appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation of discrimination based on marital status, she is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing. See Stokes, 761 F.2d at 686; Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 6.

The appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation that her termination was due to marital status discrimination.

In response to the administrative judge's Order to Show Cause on jurisdiction, the appellant submitted a sworn statement that: (1) the agency's assertions of poor performance were untrue; (2) that she was the only one of 10 newly hired secretaries who was unmarried and pregnant during the time she was employed there and that she was the only new secretary terminated; and (3) that her supervisor's failure to counsel her regarding her performance, promptly credit her with donated leave, or congratulate her on the birth of her son are corroboration that her termination was based on her marital status. The agency submitted an affidavit from the appellant's supervisor that stated the decision to terminate the appellant was based on performance deficiencies and that no other secretary who had worked for him had similar deficiencies. The supervisor also stated that other single parents had worked for him successfully. Id.

Turning first to the appellant's contentions regarding her supervisor's behavior, we do not believe that the conduct she describes falls within the ambit of factual allegations that the Board has found constitute nonfrivolous allegations of marital status discrimination. Cf. Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 8 (finding that comments allegedly made by a supervisor that showed a "keen interest in the state of the probationer's marital status" were sufficient to constitute nonfrivolous allegations (citing Gribben v. Department of Justice, 55 M.S.P.R. 257, 259 (1992)). The appellant in this appeal has not made allegations about statements or conduct by her supervisor that reflect any animus or concern about her status as an unmarried person or single mother.

However, the appellant correctly asserts that the administrative judge improperly weighed the evidence and concluded that she failed to nonfrivolously allege jurisdiction based on marital status discrimination. Ferdon, 60 M.S.P.R. at 329. We find the appellant's sworn statement that she did not have the performance problems asserted by the agency, taken together with her statements that she was the only single, pregnant, newly hired secretary and the only new secretary terminated are sufficient to constitute nonfrivolous allegations that her termination was based on her marital status. If proven, these facts could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter at issue. Id.; see also Brown v. Department of Health and Human Services, 31 M.S.P.R. 451, 453-54 (1986) (an appellant's assertion that she was terminated because she was pregnant and the agency believed her to be "just another unwed mother" conferred jurisdiction, as it constituted a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination).

Because she has made a nonfrivolous allegation, the appellant is entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which she must support her allegation with facts that would, if not controverted, require a finding that marital status discrimination was the basis for her termination. See Stokes, 761 F.2d at 686; Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 6.

In Stokes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 761 F.2d 682 (Fed. Cir. 1985), the court explained that a probationary employee faces a two-step process in establishing Board jurisdiction over an appeal such as this one. He must first make an allegation of marital discrimination supported by factual assertions indicating that the allegation is not a pro forma pleading. Id. at 686. If the appellant makes such an allegation, he has a right to a hearing at which he must support his allegation with a showing of facts which would, if not controverted, require a finding that marital discrimination was the basis for the agency action. Id. If the appellant meets this second step requirement, dismissal for lack of jurisdiction is inappropriate, and the Board proceeds to determine the merits of the case, i.e., whether the agency has articulated and supported a nondiscriminatory reason for its action, and whether the probationary employee has shown that reason to be mere pretext. The administrative judge misapplied the above standards in at least three important respects. First, she erred in stating that the appellant would have the burden, at the jurisdictional hearing, to "show that the facts he set forth in his claim of marital status discrimination are true." The appellant's burden was instead to support his claim with evidence which would, if not controverted, require a finding that marital status discrimination was the basis for the agency action. Stokes, 761 F.2d at 686.

Second, the administrative judge erred in limiting the inquiry at the second step of the jurisdictional analysis to determining whether Van Riper made the comments attributed to her about the appellant's marital status and whether she treated an unmarried female differently than the appellant. The appellant was entitled to introduce any and all evidence that would, if not controverted, establish marital discrimination. As detailed below, such evidence would include, inter alia, evidence tending to show that the appellant's performance was not deficient, that Van Riper did not raise concerns about the appellant's performance to his first and second-level supervisors, that she did express concern on numerous occasions about his alleged sexual liaisons, and that she did not follow ordinary agency procedures in terminating the appellant's appointment to the supervisory position.

Third, because the appellant's burden at the second step of the Stokes process is to show facts which, "if not controverted," would require a finding of marital discrimination, 761 F.2d at 686 (emphasis added), the administrative judge erred in considering the affidavits and other evidence submitted by the agency in resolving controverted factual issues relating to jurisdiction.[3] See also Ferdon v. United States Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994) (in ruling on jurisdiction, an administrative judge may not consider the agency's evidence to resolve conflicting factual assertions). To the extent that the administrative judge weighed the evidence and made credibility findings in order to determine that the agency articulated and supported a nondiscriminatory reason for its action, she impermissibly went beyond the jurisdictional issue and ruled on the merits of the case. See Stokes, 761 F.2d at 687.

Whether an appellant elects to present evidence at a hearing, or instead elects to rely on his written submissions, would not affect the burden of proof on the jurisdictional issue.

In Gribben v. Department of Justice, 55 MSPR 257, 259 (1992), the Board found that the administrative judge had erroneously required the appellant to satisfy the second step of the process before granting her a hearing. Gribben had alleged that the agency removed her because of an extramarital relationship with another agency employee. Among the factual assertions she made in support of this claim were the following: (1) When her supervisor learned of her extramarital relationship, he spoke to her in an abusive manner, and commented about her morals, her relationship with her children and the scandal that had been created; (2) the supervisor commented about the appellant's husband's virtuous nature, the innocence of her children, her married status, and the effect of the affair on her husband; and (3) the supervisor began to give her the "silent treatment," and made it clear that the affair had jeopardized her employment with the agency. The assertions made by the appellant in the instant case are quite similar to those made in Gribben. In each, the supervisor allegedly showed a keen interest in the state of the probationer's marriage, and particularly on how any marital difficulties might affect the couple's children. The only significant difference is that the supervisor in Gribben allegedly "made it clear" to the probationer that her extramarital relationship had jeopardized her employment with the agency, 55 MSPR at 259, while the appellant in the instant case said she "believe[d] that Ms. Spink disapproved of my separation and living arrangements and these facts played a substantial role in the decision to terminate my employment." Although the alleged causal connection between the probationer's marital status and the decision to terminate employment is not stated as explicitly in the instant case as it was in Gribben, the appellant stated a basis for her belief--the content of three alleged office conversations with Ms. Spinks. We find that the appellant made a non-frivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, not a pro forma pleading.

We further find that the administrative judge erred in two respects regarding the evaluation of the parties' evidentiary submissions. First, the administrative judge's reliance on Bedynek-Stumm for the proposition that unsworn statements are insufficient to establish an entitlement to a jurisdictional hearing is inapposite. Although the Board stated in Bedynek-Stumm that the particular "unsupported and unsworn assertions" made in that case were insufficient to establish a right to a jurisdictional hearing, 57 MSPR at 179, it did not hold that sworn statements are essential to establishing a right to a hearing, and no such requirement exists.[4] Second, the administrative judge erred in crediting the agency's version of events over that of the appellant because the agency submitted declarations made under penalty of perjury while the appellant's statements were unsworn.[5] An administrative judge may consider an agency's documentary submissions in determining whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction, but to the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions. Ferdon v. USPS, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994).

To make a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, an appellant must allege facts which, taken as true, would show that she was treated differently because of her marital status or facts that go to the essence of her status as married, single, or divorced. See Qatsha v. Department of Defense, 86 M.S.P.R. 121, ¶ 7 (2000). The appellant's allegations, taken as true, indicate that Ms. Nwuzor took a keen interest in the state of the probationer's marital status. See Strausbaugh v. Government Printing Office, 111 M.S.P.R. 305, ¶ 8 (2009); Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 M.S.P.R. 501, 504 (1994). Furthermore, by alleging that Ms. Nwuzor, who recommended the appellant's termination, was jealous that the appellant had a husband, and that her fixation on the appellant's marital status caused her to lose training opportunities, the appellant stated a basis for her belief that there was a causal connection between her marital status and her termination. See Strausbaugh, 111 M.S.P.R. 304, ¶ 8 (appellant alleged he was terminated for bringing his fiancée to an official agency function in violation of rules which allegedly limited participation to immediate family members); Edem, 64 M.S.P.R. at 505 (appellant alleged that she believed her supervisor's disapproval of her separation and living arrangements played a substantial role in her termination). Accordingly, we conclude that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction, and is therefore entitled to a jurisdictional hearing at which she must prove her claim of marital status discrimination by a preponderance of the evidence. See Burton, 2012 MSPB 73, ¶¶ 10-11.

DISSENTING OPINION OF MEMBER MARK A. ROBBINS

I agree with my colleagues' discussion and application of Burton v. Department of the Air Force, 2012 MSPB 73. I also agree that the appellant did not non-frivolously allege that the termination of her appointment during her probationary period constituted discrimination for partisan political reasons. I do not agree, however, that the appellant has non-frivolously alleged marital status discrimination.

The agency terminated the appellant's appointment during her probationary period on the ground that she had: Missed seven scheduled meetings that she was expected to attend; recommended discharging a "severely handicapped student" without following proper procedures; wanted to discharge another student who was "in great need" of her services; was tardy in completing her "CAF file"; failed to complete her "sponsorship" and her "HIPPA training"; and had been the subject of "complaints from schools regarding lack of communication, lack of seeing [her] students and the quality of [her occupational therapy] services." As noted by the administrative judge, the appellant does not deny the allegations in the termination notice. She also does not contend that the agency has retained non-married probationers with performance deficiencies similar to hers, nor does she make any other allegation that, if proven, would permit an inference that the agency's reasons for terminating her appointment were a pretext for marital status discrimination. Although the appellant alleges generally that she performed well and that the families she worked with liked her, these are conclusory statements that do not contradict any of the legitimate non-discriminatory reasons in the termination notice. Likewise, the appellant's allegation that her supervisor did not observe enough of her work to criticize her performance, does not contradict the legitimate non-discriminatory reasons in the termination notice.

The appellant's supervisor's alleged comments about the appellant's husband were inappropriate. Nevertheless, such comments are at best weak circumstantial evidence of a discriminatory attitude, and standing alone they do not support an inference that the agency's numerous, unrebutted non-discriminatory reasons for terminating the appellant's appointment were a pretext for marital status discrimination. See McClintock v. Veterans Administration, 6 M.S.P.R. 475, 478 (1981) (a probationer's appeal was properly dismissed because she did not present sufficient evidence from which it could be inferred that the agency's legitimate non-discriminatory reasons for terminating her appointment were a pretext for marital status discrimination). The appellant's supervisor's alleged comments are not direct evidence of discrimination making this a mixed motive case, as the alleged comments do not "bear directly on the contested employment decision." George v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 71, 80 (1997).

Accordingly, I would not remand this appeal for further proceedings but instead would affirm the administrative judge's dismissal for lack of jurisdiction.

GPO, AT-315H-09-0034-B-3 (NP 3/5/2012) (concluding there was no jurisdiction)), resulted in a remand based on a nonfrivolous assertion that a supervisor's concerns about the appellant's marital status led to his probationary termination:

The administrative judge thereafter dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction without conducting a hearing, finding that the appellant presented virtually "no evidence...of a bias based on marital status," that the appellant's exclusion from the official function "evidence[d] only that the appellant's supervisor enforced...what he considered to be a rule," that fiancées were not family members "for most governmental purposes," that there was "seemingly no connection between the [official function] and the appellant's termination," and that the appellant therefore had not made a nonfrivolous claim of marital status discrimination.

A probationary employee has no statutory right of appeal to the Board. 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511-7514; Stokes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 761 F.2d 682, 684 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The Office of Personnel Management (OPM) has, however, granted limited appeal rights to career or career-conditional employees who are terminated during their first year of employment. Specifically, OPM's regulations provide, inter alia, that "[a]n employee may appeal...a termination not required by statute which he or she alleges was based on...marital status." 5 C.F.R. § 315.806(b) (emphasis added); see Stokes, 761 F.2d at 684-85. In Stokes, the court explained that

[t]he Board would...have no jurisdiction to consider an appeal filed by a probationary employee who does not "allege" marital discrimination...and may simply dismiss such an appeal on sight. The regulation, however, establishes a probationary employee's right to appeal, and thus the Board's initial jurisdiction to act, i.e., to consider that appeal, when the probationary employee does so allege.

761 F.2d at 685. A probationary employee faces a two-step process in establishing Board jurisdiction over an appeal such as this one. First, the employee must allege marital status discrimination and support the allegation with factual assertions indicating that it is not a pro forma pleading. Id. at 686. If the appellant meets this requirement by making a facially nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination, he has a right to a hearing. Id. Second, at the hearing, he must support his allegation with a showing of facts which would, if not controverted, require a finding that marital status discrimination was the basis for his discharge. Id.

In Gribben v. Department of Justice, 55 M.S.P.R. 257, 259 (1992), the Board found that the administrative judge had erroneously required the appellant to satisfy the second step of the process before granting her a hearing. Gribben had alleged that the agency removed her because of an extramarital relationship with another agency employee. Among the factual assertions she made in support of this claim were the following: (1) When her supervisor learned of her extramarital relationship, he spoke to her in an abusive manner, and commented about her morals, her relationship with her children and the scandal that had been created; (2) the supervisor commented about the appellant's husband's virtuous nature, the innocence of her children, her married status, and the effect of the affair on her husband; and (3) the supervisor began to give her the "silent treatment," and made it clear that the affair had jeopardized her employment with the agency. Id. The Board found that these assertions constituted a non-frivolous allegation of marital status discrimination entitling her to a jurisdictional hearing. Id. at 259-60.

The assertions made by the appellant in the instant case are comparable to those made in Gribben. In each, the supervisor allegedly showed a keen interest in the state of the probationer's marital status. The supervisor in Gribben allegedly "made it clear" to the probationer that her extramarital relationship had jeopardized her employment with the agency, 55 M.S.P.R. at 259, while the appellant in the instant case alleged that his supervisor, Spiers, told him that "[i]f you were married to your fiancée instead of living with her you would not be having these problems," which he claims "turned out to be a reference to losing his job." Further, the appellant alleged that Spiers made "[d]erogatory statements...regarding [his] marital status"; that "[w]hen [he] asked for family leave to take care of his 3 year old child, he was told...that 'if you were married she could do that'"; that "management officials asked questions regarding [his] living with a woman and having a baby but not being married to her"; and that Spiers, "after telling [him] that he could bring his spouse and 3 year old child with him to [an official function] .... fired him for bringing his companion (the mother of his child with whom he had been living for the past 13 years)...." In addition, the appellant asserts that his termination notice includes only one reason for his separation, i.e., that he violated rules which allegedly limited participation in the official function to "immediate family members"; and he alleges that his "fiancée was the real object of the termination action because she did not fit the description of spouse." Thus, the appellant has stated a basis for his belief that there was a causal connection between his marital status and the decision to terminate his employment. See also Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 M.S.P.R. 501, 504-505 (1994) (the appellant's assertion that conversations in which her supervisor allegedly showed a keen interest in the state of her marriage, and in the manner in which any marital difficulties might affect her children, constituted a nonfrivolous allegation of marital status discrimination). The appellant's allegations constitute nonfrivolous allegations of marital status discrimination, rather than a mere pro forma pleading. See id. at 505; Gribben, 55 M.S.P.R. at 259-60.

We find further that the administrative judge misapplied the above standards by dismissing this appeal for lack of evidence that the appellant's termination was the result of marital status discrimination. As stated above, proof is not necessary at this stage of the proceedings, where the appellant need only make a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction to obtain a jurisdictional hearing. See Stokes, 761 F.2d at 686.

Ellis v. Dept. of Treasury, 81 MSPR 6, 9-11 ¶¶ 9-14 (1999), suggested that the Board may consider the appeal of a probationer (or first year appointee under the Veterans Readjustment Act) who asserts that his termination resulted from sexual discrimination or sexual harassment (reprisal for rejection of sexual advances) that was linked to the appellant's marital status (a single, unattached male):

Where "allegations of marital status discrimination and sex discrimination are so intertwined that no meaningful finding as to one can be made without consideration of the other," the Board's scope of review must not be narrowed solely to the marital status discrimination claim. Uriarte v. Department of Agriculture, 6 MSPB 334, 6 MSPR 393, 395 (1981). Rather, in such circumstances, the "allegations that the action had been based upon [the appellant's] marital status and upon sex discrimination must be examined together." Id. In the present case, we find that the appellant's allegations present claims of marital status discrimination and sex discrimination so intertwined that a meaningful finding on one cannot be made without consideration of the other, and that accordingly, the appeal must be remanded for a hearing.

In Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 MSPR 501, 504 (1994), the Board found that the appellant had made nonfrivolous allegations of marital status discrimination entitling her to a hearing because her allegations, if proven, could have supported the inference that her termination was linked to her supervisor's "keen interest in the state of [her] marriage." See also Gribben v. Department of Justice, 55 MSPR 257, 259 (1992). So-called "quid pro quo" sexual harassment, which is a form of sex discrimination, occurs when "sexual demands are made by a supervisor to a subordinate in exchange for career advantages or under threats of adverse job consequences." Downes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 775 F.2d 288, 290 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

Here, the appellant's allegations contain closely-related elements of marital status discrimination and quid pro quo sexual harassment. He alleges that his supervisor took an interest in his single status, that she subjected him to sexually suggestive touches, looks, and gestures, that she implied that he could advance his career if he began a sexual relationship with her, that she did not scrutinize the behavior of married employees the way she scrutinized his behavior, and that she paid particularly close attention to his interactions with other women because of his unmarried and "available" status. It was those interactions with female passengers and airline representatives that allegedly led to Ms. Thornton's reports about the appellant's behavior. While the appellant may have behaved improperly with female passengers and airline representatives, the appellant may be able to show, as he asserts, that his interactions with female passengers and airline representatives were proper and that Thornton singled him out for "observation" and interpreted his actions as improper because he was single. As the appellant contends, it may be that Thornton viewed what may have been entirely proper interactions with other females as improper because of her alleged "sexual interest" in him as an unmarried man, and that she may have reported those contacts as improper because of her alleged vindictiveness toward him after he refused her purported sexual overtures. On this point, we note that although the appellant was appointed to his position on March 31, 1997, the termination notice mentions no instances of improper behavior by the appellant between that date and late November 1997 when Thornton became his supervisor.

The administrative judge found that the appellants allegations were similar to the ones at issue in Flores v. Farmers Home Administration, 10 MSPB 808, 12 MSPR 286, 287 (1982), where the appellant claimed that "she was fired because her employer expected 'some type of social relationship' with her and that though she wasn't married she had a boyfriend and refused." The Board in Flores summarily found no right to a jurisdictional hearing because "[s]ocial relationships can occur between employees and supervisors regardless of their marital status," and the appellant did not allege sufficient facts to make nonfrivolous allegations that the purported expectations of her supervisor were based on her marital status. 10 MSPB 808, 12 MSPR at 287 (emphasis added). The Board in Flores did not discuss a situation such as the one presented here, where there are detailed, interrelated allegations of marital status discrimination and sexual harassment. Thus, this case is distinguishable from Flores.

The facts alleged here are also distinguishable from the facts alleged in Bedynek-Stumm v. Department of Agriculture, 57 MSPR 176 (1993), another case on which the administrative judge relied. In Bedynek-Stumm the appellant made unsworn and unsupported statements that "a female coworker disliked him, and therefore harassed him, because he [was] single." 57 MSPR at 179. The Board found that the appellant was not entitled to a hearing because "the factual assertions he made to support his contention all regard disputes over work-related matters, and [were] completely unrelated to his marital status except for his conjectures that this coworker sought to dominate him because of her negative feelings toward him as an unmarried male." 57 MSPR at 179. Here, the appellant has provided details regarding the alleged interest in him by Thornton, including specific assertions that Inspector Watson also observed Thornton singling the appellant out for attention and knew of Thornton's interest in the appellant as an unmarried man. We find that, unlike the contentions in Bedynek-Stumm, the appellant's assertions are more than mere conjecture. Cf. Paine v. Department of Health & Human Services, 32 MSPR 135, 137 (1987) (the appellant was entitled to a hearing on the issue of marital status discrimination where she premised her discrimination allegation on the assertion that the agency treated her differently because she was a divorced single parent, when it overlooked more serious acts of misconduct and performance deficiencies by its married employees).

In sum, we cannot say, on this record, that the appellant's termination was based on his marital status. We find that under Uriarte, the appellant has made nonfrivolous, intertwined allegations of marital status discrimination and quid pro quo sexual harassment entitling him to a hearing. On remand, the appellant shall be allowed to introduce such argument and evidence on the issue of sex discrimination as may be relevant to his claim of marital status discrimination. The ultimate factual issues that the AJ must resolve are 1) whether the agency terminated the appellant "based on...[his] marital status," 5 CFR § 315.806(b), that is, whether he was "exposed to disadvantageous terms or conditions of employment to which [married employees] were not exposed," cf. Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, 523 U.S. 75, __, 118 S. Ct. 998, 1002, 140 L. Ed.2d 201 (1998) (the ultimate question in a sex discrimination case is whether the claimant was "exposed to disadvantageous terms or conditions of employment to which members of the other sex are not exposed"), and 2) whether any discrimination that is found to have occurred goes to the basic nature of the appellant's marital status.

The Federal Circuit interpreted Ellis, supra, to mean that "where allegations of marital status discrimination and sex discrimination are so intertwined that no meaningful finding as to one can be made without consideration of the other, the Board must review both." Garcia v. VA, 173 Fed. Appx. 830, 831 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP).

The appellant bases his claim that his termination was due to his marital status or for partisan political reasons on two narrow allegations: (1) That his supervisor "did not believe that any homosexual person should ever be a Republican"; and (2) that his supervisor "assumed" he was single because he was gay, but she also knew he was not romantically available to her because he was gay.

The appellant's allegation that the Acting Manager became aware that she was single or divorced in October 2016 is insufficient to constitute a nonfrivolous allegation that another agency official imposed her termination 4 months later on the basis of marital status discrimination. See Smirne, 115 M.S.P.R. 51, ¶ 8."); Johnson v. MSPB, 495 Fed. Appx. 68, 71 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP) ("Even if the Board had accepted Johnson's belated argument that his supervisors made comments about his single status, Johnson did not assert facts which, if proven, would demonstrate that married employees were treated differently than unmarried employees. Chase-Baker v. U.S. Dep't of Justice, 198 F.3d 843, 845 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (a non-frivolous allegation of marital status discrimination requires factual assertions of disparate treatment between married and unmarried employees); Stokes v. Fed. Aviation Admin., 761 F.2d 682, 685 (Fed. Cir. 1985).").

The Board had jurisdiction over a probationer's claim that his removal was discriminatory because married employees who engaged in specific misconduct were not disciplined. Ward v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 320, 322 (1985) (appellant provided details of the disparate treatment). Nonetheless, the proof of disparate treatment may be difficult. On review, Ward's allegations that he was removed as a probationer, while married employees with employment difficulties were retained, were insufficient to reverse the removal. The deciding official was unaware of the marital status of the employee. Other evidence showed that employees were not immune from discipline because of their marital status. Ward v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3047). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Disparate, Comparable Penalties," concerning disparate treatment defenses and Chapter 12 concerning discrimination cases.]

Marital status discrimination is not implicated by an employee's allegation that she was fired during her probationary period because her supervisor disapproved of her fiancés' visit to her at agency-located housing quarters. Yakupzack v. USDA, 10 MSPR 180, 181 (1982); see Curry v. SSA, DA-315I-17-0203-I-1 (NP 5/10/2022) ("The appellant alleges that the agency returned her to a nonsupervisory position due to the actions of her husband, who is employed as an Administrative Law Judge in the same office where the appellant works. Even if taken as true, these allegations do not show that the agency treated the appellant differently due to her marital status nor do they relate to the essence of her status as married."); Kukula v. USDA, 13 MSPR 543, 545 (1982) (appellant did not make a marital status claim by alleging that he was fired because his supervisor's advances were rejected by the employee's wife and mother-in-law; a matter concerning conduct or performance does not rise to an allegation of a prima facie case of marital discrimination); Flores v. Farmers Home Admin., 12 MSPR 286, 287 (1982) (no jurisdiction when the appellant alleged she was fired for not engaging in a social relationship with her boss: "Social relationships can occur between employees and supervisors regardless of their marital status."). A comment that an individual who received frequent phone calls from his wife ought to straighten out his married life did not establish a prima facie case of marital status discrimination against a terminated probationer. The supervisor's alleged comment did not go "to the essence" of the appellant's status as a married person. James v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 124, 127 (1992); see Miller v. DOJ, 41 MSPR 353, 356 (1989) (too many spousal calls); Hilden v. Dept. of Agriculture, 8 MSPR 300, 302 (1981) (denying jurisdiction invoked on the theory that if the appellant were married, her financial situation would have been better and she would not have become involved in difficulties leading to termination); Wild v. DHUD, 7 MSPR 595, 598 (1981) (marital status was not implicated when the employee was removed for engaging in business practices with his wife that violated agency standards of conduct).

An allegation of sexual discrimination will not trigger an appeal right for a probationer; marital status and sexual discrimination cannot be interchangeably pleaded. Pierce v. GPO, 70 F.3d 106 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (sexual harassment allegation is insufficient by itself to warrant Board review of the merits of a probationer's termination); Hurst v. GSA, 2 MSPR 497, 500, 3 MSPB 21 (1980) ("we decline to hold that allegations of sex discrimination and marital status discrimination are so similar that they may be made interchangeably. Where the question is one of jurisdiction, special importance attaches to the precise nature of the allegations comprising the appeal"). But see Ellis v. Dept. of Treasury, 81 MSPR 6 (1999), supra.

The marital status discrimination jurisdiction of the Board is limited to probationary competitive service appointees, Lee v. OPM, DC-315H-10-0080-I-1 (NP 8/10/2010) (involving an FCIP employee):

The appellant argues on petition for review that the Board has jurisdiction over her appeal based on her claim of marital status discrimination. However, as the administrative judge correctly explained in his initial decision, only individuals in the competitive service have the right to appeal under 5 C.F.R. § 315.806(b) based on allegations of marital status discrimination. [S] ee Allen v. Department of the Navy, 102 M.S.P.R. 302, ¶ 6 (2006). The appellant has no right of appeal under that section because she was in the excepted service--not the competitive service. Therefore, the only way for the appellant to establish jurisdiction over her appeal would be to show that she is an "employee" within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a). See Allen, 102 M.S.P.R. 302, ¶ 6. Because the appellant is not an "employee" within the meaning of that section, the administrative judge correctly dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction.
