broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 08: Disciplinary Procedures and Standards, Section H: Standards of Conduct

H. STANDARDS OF CONDUCT

The employer may be required to negotiate standards of conduct that are not the exclusive means or reasons for taking disciplinary action against the employee, as long as the standards are consistent with governing laws or regulations that also define the conduct proscribed. Negotiation of those standards is consistent with the discretion given to the agencies by OPM under Executive Order 11222, requiring that employees adhere to certain standards of conduct. Under the implementing regulations, 5 CFR 735, OPM required agencies to prepare and submit to OPM for approval regulations implementing standards of ethical and other conduct. [Ethics regulations are now issued by the Office of Government Ethics; see 5 CFR Part 2635 (2022).] Standards of conduct for employees are matters affecting conditions of employment, and, to the extent those matters are discretionary, they are within the duty to bargain, DLA and NAGE, 12 FLRA 412, 416-17 (1983), as long as the proposals do not otherwise conflict with management rights or other limitations on negotiability. AFGE Nat'l Joint Council of Food Inspection Locals and USDA, Food Safety and Quality Serv., 9 FLRA 663, 666 (1982). To the extent that an agency's ability to formulate those standards is discretionary, it is negotiable. Negotiable standards included association with other persons, stock ownership, acceptance of free lunches, responsibility for financial obligations, use of intoxicants, participation in legal gambling, and acceptance of compensation for union-related work. NTEU and IRS, 6 FLRA 522, 531-37 (1981).

A proposal that makes an employee's private life his own business is negotiable if it recognizes the primacy of the agency's standards of conduct and governing laws relating to conflicts. NFFE Local 1798 and VAMC, Martinsburg, W.Va., 27 FLRA 239, 241-42 (1987). FLRA disallowed a proposal that "[t]he private life of an employee is his/her own affair." The difficulty was the provision's inconsistency with 5 CFR 2635.809 (2022) and 5 CFR 735.203 (2022) [formerly 735.207 and 735.209], requiring that employees pay their financial obligations in a proper and timely manner, and that employees not engage in criminal, infamous, dishonest, immoral, or notoriously disgraceful conduct or other conduct prejudicial to the government. DLA Council of AFGE Locals and DOD, DLA, 24 FLRA 367, 367-69 (1986). Nonnegotiable was a proposal that essentially defined permissible employment or other activities of family members as to whether those activities would create a direct conflict of interest. The proposal restricted management's right to impose restrictions on employees concerning apparent conflicts as defined by Executive Order 11222 and 5 CFR Part 735. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 31 FLRA 181, 186-88 (1988). [Refer to 5 CFR Part 2635 (2022) for current standards of conduct regulations issued by the Office of Government Ethics; refer in this chapter to "Outside Employment, Business Relationships."]

ACT and DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau, Rhode Island NG, 38 FLRA 1005, 1010, 1013-14 (1990), declined to require negotiations over a portion of Proposal 2, restricting the agency's choice of disciplinary systems. The union proposed to preclude the agency from disciplining civilian technicians under the Uniform Code of Military Justice for improper wearing of military uniforms. FLRA intoned that a proposal "which precludes discipline for infractions regarding the wearing of the military uniform directly interferes with the Agency's right to take disciplinary action under section 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute." The proposal was not considered under arrangements tests. In the context of a choice between a misconduct and performance-based action based on violations of agency principles of "values and ethics," the union could not compel negotiation over a proposal excluding the agency performance evaluation system as a means of enforcing ethical requirements. The proposal interfered with the right to assign work, and it did not constitute an appropriate arrangement. NFFE Local 1214 and Dept. of Army, Hq., Army Training Ctr., 49 FLRA 215, 217, 220-22 (1994) (Provision 1).
