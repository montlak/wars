A. BASIS FOR APPEAL

For those foraging in the WIGI and acceptable level of competence (ALOC) field for the first time, the step increase procedure seems complex, but it is briefly described. Within salary grades there are salary steps know as within-grade or step increases (WIGIs). Individuals who perform fully satisfactorily or at an acceptable level of competence advance from step to step after waiting periods designated by OPM regulations of one, two, and three years, depending on the step. If a person's performance is not fully satisfactory during the waiting period, as judged by supervisors using performance appraisal systems or some other means of objective review known to the employee, at the end of the waiting period the employee is notified that his performance is not at an ALOC and the step increase is withheld. The employee is also told what he should do to improve his performance and earn the step increase at some later date. Employees who have not been told what performance standards govern their step increases by the end of the waiting period should be given that information with an opportunity to meet those standards. Employees dissatisfied with a negative ALOC determination, with its effect of precluding a step increase, may appeal that determination through an internal agency process called reconsideration. The reconsideration official considers the employee's case and decides whether to sustain the ALOC negative determination. If the determination is sustained, meaning the step increase remains denied, the employee may appeal to the MSPB.

5 USC 5335(c) provides for Board appeals from negative ALOC determinations following an agency reconsideration review. It is the ALOC decision, not the reconsideration file, that is reviewed; the reconsideration process is an administrative exhaustion requirement. Gary v. USDA, 4 MSPR 203, 205, 4 MSPB 282 (1980). No appeal lies with the Board if, after a step increase is granted as a result of administrative error, the error is quickly corrected and no timely appeal is taken from any subsequent denial of the step increase. See Batchelder v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 529, 531 (1982) (nine-week delay in correction). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Correction of Pay, Grade," concerning Board jurisdiction over appeals challenging corrections in pay rates.] Assuming, however, a substantive denial of the step increase, if the Board reverses an agency decision as to level of competence, its ruling is retroactive to the date the step increase was due to the employee. Degani v. Dept. of Air Force, 1 MSPR 671, 675, 2 MSPB 81 (1980). Victory for the appellant should also result in amendment of the ALOC determination and the underlying performance rating. Guy v. Dept. of Energy, 37 MSPR 230, 232 (1988); see Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 72 MSPR 378, 381 n.1 (1996) (suggesting that in a performance-based action under Chapter 43, as well as in a WIGI appeal, the underlying rating should be raised to the level deemed by the AJ to reflect the evidence of successful performance in the course of the successful appeal); Shaishaa v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 450, 453 (1992) (to cancel a WIGI denial and to moot an appeal, the agency must return the appellant to the status quo by canceling the action, paying him retroactively for the period of the denial, removing all references to the action from the personnel record, and amending the ALOC determination and the underlying performance evaluation); cf. Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 30, 32 (1997) (after the agency properly reevaluated the appellant's performance and raised the critical element only to the level of minimally satisfactory, the appellant could not require the agency to grant him a WIGI). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Purging of Records; Modification of Documentation, Performance Appraisal," for requirements concerning modification of documentation and appraisals following a successful Board appeal.]
