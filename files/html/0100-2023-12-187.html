broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part p: Inability to Obtain Representation
p. Inability to Obtain Representation

The Board believes that the instructions accompanying initial decisions clearly explain how to request Board review through PFRs; by implication, assistance of counsel is not necessary for a PFR. Tidwell v. OPM, 53 MSPR 464, 467 (1992). Late filing of a PFR is not excused by the inability to obtain legal or union assistance. Waldon v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 478 (1994); see Brum v. VA, 109 MSPR 129, 131 ¶ 5, 2008 MSPB 126 (2008) (late PFR was not excused because the appellant expected the Board to appoint a lawyer for her in an adverse action appeal); Acierto v. OPM, 83 MSPR 179, 180 ¶ 4 (1999) (inability to obtain representation does not constitute good cause for an untimely filing); Simpson v. USPS, 80 MSPR 416, 418-19 ¶¶ 6-7 (1998) (delay was not excused by delay in obtaining both the transcript and counsel); Wright v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 76 MSPR 330, 332 (1997) (a claim that the appellant had financial difficulties and could not afford an attorney did not provide good cause for an untimely PFR); McFalls v. OPM, 72 MSPR 252, 259 (1996) ("Neither a lack of legal expertise...nor an inability to obtain legal counsel, is sufficient to justify an untimely filing."); Rodriguez v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 396, 398 (1996) (although pro se status is a factor weighing in favor of excusing a delayed PFR, despite the inability to hire an attorney, other factors (including the failure to request an extension and the length of the delay--18 days--weighed against excusing the delay); Choco v. OPM, 69 MSPR 638, 641 (1996) (lengthy delay in filing a PFR was not excused because the appellant "was unable to find a responsible person to assist him with his retirement claim"); Bianco v. Dept. of Air Force, 68 MSPR 51, 52-53 (1995) (inability to obtain a representative did not excuse a lengthy delay in filing a PFR; good cause was not established by the appellant's assertion that he did not discover a basis for Board's jurisdiction until he obtained representation); Barber v. USDA, 63 MSPR 32, 35 (1994) (inability to find or afford representation was not sufficient to excuse a delay); DeGraaff v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 343, 346 (1992) ("[A]ppellant's explanation that his attorney withdrew from his case, that he was broke, and that he spent months looking unsuccessfully for a new representative, is insufficient to establish good cause for the over 1 year and 3 months in filing his petition for review."); Blanchard v. VA, 45 MSPR 569, 570 (1990) (rejecting unspecific allegations of physical incapacitation and a lack of funds); Smith v. OPM, 45 MSPR 508, 511 (1990) (rejecting unsubstantiated allegations of mental incapacity, stress and the inability to afford counsel); DeTugade v. OPM, 45 MSPR 129, 132 (1990) (confusion and the resulting delay while the appellant sought advice on how to refile the PFR was not excused; the implication was that the appellant could have sought advice from the MSPB concerning procedural requirements); Daniels v. VA, 42 MSPR 437, 438 (1989) (seeking counsel does not provide good cause to extend the time for filing a PFR); Block v. DLA, 40 MSPR 198, 200 (1989) (inability to obtain legal counsel does not establish good cause for waiver of the time limit for filing a PFR); Miller v. USPS, 39 MSPR 18, 20 (1988) (inability to afford counsel and lack of confidence in self-representation did not excuse a late PFR); Staples v. Dept. of State, 37 MSPR 398, 400 (1988) (seeking legal assistance does not establish good cause to extend the time limitation for filing a PFR); McLaughlin v. USPS, 35 MSPR 255, 256 (1987) (appellant has the right to counsel, but the appellant, not the Board, is responsible for securing counsel); Robinson v. VA, 33 MSPR 483, 486 (1987) (Board is not required to appoint counsel for the appellant); Cormier v. USPS, 22 MSPR 112, 114 n.1 (1984) (attempts to secure counsel did not justify extending the time limit for the submission of additional information following the filing of a PFR), aff'd, 809 F.2d 792 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Moses v. OPM, 11 MSPR 68, 70 (1982) (belated PFR: "the Board has found that an appellant's argument that he was seeking union and legal assistance was insufficient to excuse the untimely filing of an appeal").

A late petition is not excused when an individual submits his case file to an attorney who will not accept responsibility for the case because the appellant cannot pay a fee. Goree v. USPS, 20 MSPR 93, 94 (1984); see Baker v. USPS, 80 MSPR 333, 336 ¶ 5 (1998) ("Further, shortly after the initial decision was issued, the appellant's then-attorney sent her a letter, enclosing a copy of the initial decision, in which he told her that he would not pursue her petition for review without a substantial payment towards the fee owed to him, and that if she wanted to pursue a petition for review she would have to file one with the Clerk of the Board by May 19, 1995."). In an analogous case the Board refused to permit a 30-day delay in filing a PFR by an appellant who claimed to be unable to afford counsel fees. Woods v. Dept. of Treasury, 21 MSPR 5, 6 (1984). Perforce, the deadline is not excused because the appellant did not give the initial decision to his counsel until after the PFR filing date expired. Walton v. USPS, 54 MSPR 510 (1992). The Board declined to excuse a PFR delayed by seven weeks when appellant waited for "family and friends who knew people with a legal education." Lambright v. OPM, 114 MSPR 507, 509 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 158 (2010).

Hodge v. USPS, 88 MSPR 50, 52 ¶ 6 (2001), summed up the Board's approach to lengthy delays caused by financial inability to retain counsel:

Notwithstanding the appellant's pro se status, we find that he has not shown good cause for the nearly one-month delay in filing his petition for review, which was not a minimal delay and does not provide a basis for waiving the filing deadline. See, e.g., Robinson v. Office of Personnel Management, 85 MSPR 589 ¶ 6 (an approximately thirty-day delay in filing a petition for review was not minimal and did not provide a basis for waiving the filing deadline), review dismissed, No. 00-3336 (Fed. Cir. July 12, 2000) (Table); Colon v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 MSPR 514, 519 (1996) (an overall filing delay of twenty-seven days was not minimal), appeal dismissed, 132 F.3d 52 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table); cf. Parker v. Office of Personnel Management, 80 MSPR 277 ¶ 3 (1998) (a one-month delay in filing a petition for review is significant). The appellant's contention that his delay was due to his lack of funds and inability to afford an attorney does not constitute good cause for an untimely-filed petition for review. See Robinson, 85 MSPR 589 ¶ 5; Wright v. Railroad Retirement Board, 76 MSPR 330, 332 (1997). Explicit filing instructions were given to him in the initial decision in this case, and the appellant has not shown that he otherwise exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case.
