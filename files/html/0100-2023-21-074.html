s. Agency Medical Disqualification

In O'Brien v. OPM, 20 MSPR 395, 397-98 (1984), medical disqualification for hypertension led to approval of disability retirement:

Appellant alleges that he is disabled for his position because his blood pressure is higher than that permitted by FAA and OPM regulations for air traffic controllers and that although his blood pressure is controllable by medication, the antihypertensive minipress (prazosin hydrochloride), the use of antihypertensives is proscribed by both FAA and OPM regulations. In support of his allegations, appellant submitted medical reports by Dr. Teich, as well as Drs. Thomas Elmquist and Jeffrey Nichol, diagnosing his condition as hypertension. In a report dated February 16, 1981, Dr. Teich noted that appellant's blood pressure, which was initially 170 systolic over 120 diastolic, could be controlled only by medication. He therefore concluded that appellant was disabled for his position as a controller.

Further, appellant refers to FAA Order 7210.E3, Facility Operation and Administrative Manual (71180), which, he states, prohibits the use of "[a]ny drugs such as, but not limited to, antihypertensive agents" within 24 hours of assuming duty. Additionally, appellant quotes from OPM regulations governing air traffic controllers, OPM Qualifications Standard X-118, Series GS-2152, which, he states, requires as follows: "Blood Pressure--must demonstrate blood pressure levels no greater than (systolic 150 mm. Hg; Diastolic 90 mm. Hg). Must require no medication for control of blood pressure."

Based on the evidence presented, we find that both OPM and FAA regulations medically disqualify appellant from performing air traffic control duties based on his hypertension. Also, appellant's supervisor, in the Superior Officer's Statement (Standard Form 2801-A) submitted with appellant's application for disability retirement, indicated that appellant's physical condition precluded reassignment or a trial detail. Further, appellant alleges, and there is no evidence to the contrary, that because of his medical condition he was absent from his position from January 1980 until his removal on August 29, 1981. Indeed, appellant's supervisor also stated in the Superior Officer's Statement that appellant had not performed any of the duties of his position since January 1980. We therefore find that based on the evidence of record, appellant has sustained his burden under Chavez, supra, in establishing that he is disabled for his last occupied position of air traffic control specialist.

See Yencho v. OPM, 20 MSPR 483 (1984) (hypertension leading to disability annuity for air traffic controller).

The appellant's release from a National Guard civilian technician position because he failed to meet weight requirements did not, without evidence that a medical problem was at issue, establish entitlement to a disability annuity. Gibler v. OPM, 39 MSPR 417, 421 (1988).
