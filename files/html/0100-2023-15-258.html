2. Burden of Proof

If the agency charges the appellant only with theft, lesser or other offenses may not be considered by the Board. Major v. Dept. of Army, 31 MSPR 283, 285 n.1 (1986). If the agency charges the employee with conduct constituting a criminal offense, i.e., theft, the charge requires the agency to prove the elements of criminal conduct. Peck v. DOD, 75 MSPR 244, 248 (1997):

[W]ith regard to the element of "intent," it is hornbook law that a criminal intent or mens rea is necessary to sustain a charge of theft or larceny. "Under the general rule, if the existence of a state of mind is incompatible with the intent to steal it will preclude a conviction of larceny." 52A C.J.S. Larceny § 25a., p. 447. As a corollary, a person who takes an object believing it to be his own when in fact that object belongs to someone else has not committed theft. A taking under a mistake of fact is not larceny. See 52A C.J.S. Larceny § 25b., p. 448.

Theft on a particular date is not established by proof of theft on a different date. See Boyd v. USPS, 41 MSPR 552, 555 (1989). If an employee is charged with theft, the agency must prove every element of the crime, including intent. See, e.g., Martinez v. DLA, 4 MSPR 477, 479, 5 MSPB 16 (1981) (the appellant asserted that items allegedly stolen were retrieved from a trash bin); cf. Cooper v. USPS, 42 MSPR 174, 177 (1989) (shoplifting charge: to prove theft the agency had to prove that the appellant was in possession of goods not paid for and that the appellant had the intent not to pay). If an individual is accused of conspiring with another to steal government property, the agency must show the criminal intent to steal. Worden v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 407, 409 (1984) ("Other than transporting the fireplace insert after it was built by Mr. Durner, there is no evidence regarding appellant's intent to steal.").

A theft case is established by preponderant evidence. Banks v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 268, 271, 4 MSPB 342 (1980). Should there be conflicting testimony on intent, the AJ must resolve the credibility conflicts through a detailed analysis of why he or she found the chosen version more credible, for "if the existence of a state of mind is incompatible with the intent to steal it will preclude a conviction of larceny." Peck v. DOD, 75 MSPR 244, 249 (1997) (citing Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453, 458 (1987)).

Preponderant evidence of theft may be established by testimony or by physical evidence, Banez v. DOD, 69 MSPR 642, 646-47 (1996):

In his petition for review, the appellant again reiterates his denial of the charge. He alleges that there is no direct physical evidence that he committed the alleged offense; namely, no Tide boxes with tape, no cash register receipts, and allegedly no evidence that he, in fact, purchased the Tide at any, no less at a marked down price. However, an agency must prove a charge by preponderant evidence and while such direct or physical evidence may be desirable, it is not necessary in all cases. Here, in light of the eye witness testimony, and other evidence as described in the initial decision, the absence of physical evidence does not establish a lack of preponderant evidence that the appellant committed the offense alleged. See Wildeman v. Department of the Air Force, 23 MSPR 313, 319 (1984) (to support a charge of theft, an agency must prove by preponderant evidence that the appellant is the person who actually took the property; eye witness testimony is sufficient to show that an accused individual is the person who actually took the property; only in the absence of such testimony is other evidence needed to infer that the stolen property was in the alleged thief's "exclusive possession"). Thus, here there is no need for direct evidence to establish the theft in this case; i.e., that the appellant is the individual who took the Tide and to infer that it was in his "exclusive possession." Further, there is no dispute that in committing the acts alleged, the appellant intended to permanently deprive the agency of the $13.99, representing the alleged theft amount. King v. Nazelrod, 43 F.3d 663, 667 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

Under Franklin v. Dept. of Navy, 10 MSPR 83, 85-86 (1982), an agency proves theft through evidence of the employee's intent to permanently deprive the agency of its property:

Although, the presiding official did not explicitly so state, he presumably defined the charge of attempted theft as incorporating the intent to steal. Stealing implies the common law crime of larceny, which generally requires a showing of an intent to permanently deprive the owner of his property. See United States v. Trinder, 1 F. Supp. 659 (D.C. Mont. 1932); United States v. Maloney, 607 F.2d 222 (9th Cir. 1979). The agency cites the case of Pennsylvania Indemnity Fire Corporation v. Aldridge, 117 F.2d 774, 777 (D.C. Cir. 1941), for the proposition that in cases of larceny one must show only the intent of the taker "to appropriate the stolen property to a use inconsistent with the property rights of the person from whom it is taken," a showing of "felonious intent." As noted by this case and Maloney, supra, cases dealing with common law larceny have defined the term in various fashions without reaching a consensus. However, without attempting to reconcile this dilemma, we find the presiding official's evaluation of the facts in this case to be consistent with guidance given by the Supreme Court in Morissette v. United States, 342 U.S. 246, 72 S. Ct. 240, 96 L. Ed.2d 288 (1952), when the Court examined an instance of theft of government property under 18 USC § 641. In that case, an individual took discarded casings from government property for his own use. The court, noting that the defendant admitted consciously and intentionally removing the casing, stated, however, that this isolated fact was not "an adequate basis on which the jury should find the criminal intent to steal or knowingly convert, that is, wrongfully to deprive another of possession of property." Morissette, supra, at 256, 72 S. Ct. at 246. "Whether that intent existed, the jury must determine, not only from the act of taking, but from that together with defendant's testimony and all of the surrounding circumstances." Id.

The presiding official, the fact-finder in the instant case, considered appellant's testimony that he was only taking the fan for a few days so that his sick wife might gain some relief from the heat and found this statement to be credible. The presiding official found this credible statement coupled with the circumstances under which appellant took the fan, making no attempt to conceal his actions, defeated the necessary intent element of the crime of attempted theft. Under the circumstances of this case, we find no error in the presiding official's evaluation. Therefore, this contention does not warrant reversal of the initial decision.
