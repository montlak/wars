g. Agencies With Independent Statutory Personnel Authority

To suit their special needs, and to avoid (some of) the complexities of the civil service system, some agencies have obtained statutes providing them independent personnel authority. Hargrove v. DOD, 77 MSPR 266, 269-71 (1998), considered whether Department of Defense Domestic Elementary and Secondary Schools statutory hiring authority excluded teachers from the right to appeal to the Board from personnel actions resulting from RIFs. The Board concluded the employees retained their appellate rights:

In 1965, Congress amended 20 USC § 241(a), a statute that itself amended earlier legislation directing that schools be provided for dependent children living on Federal installations when no local educational agency was able to provide suitable comparable free public education for such children. See Pub. L. No. 81-815, 64 Stat. Ch. 995 (1950), repealed by Pub. L. No. 103-382 § 331, 108 Stat. 3519 (1994). Section 241(a), as amended, allowed local installations to employ personnel:

whose compensation, tenure, leave, hours of work, and other incidents of the employment relationship may be fixed without regard to the Civil Service Act and rules and the following...(4) sections 3501 to 3504...of Title 5.

See Pub. L. No. 89-77, 79 Stat. 243, codified at 20 USC § 241(a).[*] Sections 3501 through 3504 of title 5 govern retention preference during RIFs. Section 3502(a) instructs the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to "prescribe regulations for the release of competing employees in a [RIF]." OPM's regulations regarding RIF retention are set forth at 5 CFR Part 351. Thus, under 20 USC § 241(a), the agency had the option of using an appointment process which granted its employees the RIF appeal rights set forth at 5 CFR § 351.901, or, alternatively, using an appointment process that did not provide its employees with such rights. See Todd v. Department of Defense, 63 MSPR 4, 7 (1994), aff'd, 55 F.3d 1574 (Fed. Cir. 1995); cf. 58 Comp. Gen. 430 (1979) (exception from civil service laws in 20 USC § 241 is permissive, not mandatory).

[*] Legislative history reflects that the amendment was intended to provide such schools with the flexibility to make the personnel practices and compensation for school employees comparable to the labor practices generally found in public schools rather than in the Federal civil service. See S. Rep. No. 311, 89th Cong., 1st. Sess. (1965), reprinted in 1965 USCCAN 1910, 1912-14.

In October 1994, Congress repealed 20 USC § 241(a). See Pub. L. No. 103-382 § 331(b), 108 Stat. 3965 (1994). The current statute provides that:

[The agency]...may without regard to the provisions of any other law relating to the number, classification, or compensation of employees (A) establish positions for civilian employees...(B) appoint individuals to such positions; and (C) fix the compensation of such individuals for service in such positions.

See Pub. L. No. 103-337 § 351(b), 108 Stat. 2663 (1994), codified at 10 USC § 2164(e)(2). The agency appointed the appellant to her Safety and Occupational Health Specialist position after the current version of the statute was enacted. The administrative judge determined that the Board has jurisdiction to consider this appeal based on his finding that the Board has jurisdiction generally to consider RIF appeals from employees hired under this new provision. We agree.

The starting point for every case involving statutory construction must be the language of the statute itself. See Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 MSPR 380, 382 (1994). Where the statutory language is clear, it must control, absent a clearly expressed legislative intent to the contrary. See Todd, 63 MSPR at 7. In the present case, there is nothing in the plain language of the appointing statute that could be read as giving the agency the discretion to disregard the provisions of 5 USC §§ 3501-3504 relating to RIFs. As the text of 10 USC § 2164(e)(2) makes clear, Congress gave the agency the option of disregarding only those statutes relating to the "number, classification, or compensation of employees." To interpret this statute as giving the agency the option to disregard additional, unenumerated provisions would run afoul of the maxim "expressio unius est exclusio alterius." Cf. King v. Briggs, 83 F.3d 1384, 1388 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (jurisdiction found over adverse action appeal where the appointing statute, 29 USC § 783(a)(1), allowed agency to disregard only those provisions of Title 5 governing appointments to the competitive service, classification, and General Schedule pay rates in its appointment process); 2A Sutherland Stat. Const. § 47.23 (the expression of one thing excludes another).

We examined the legislative history of the National Defense Authorization Act of 1995, Pub. L. No. 103-382, which contains the amended language. None of the materials, including the House and Senate reports, the floor debates, and the different versions of the bills that were introduced prior to enactment of the final version, provide an explanation for the change in the language of the appointing statute. See, e.g., H.R. Conf. Rep. No. 103-701; H.R. Rep. No. 103-499; S. Rep. No. 103-282; 140 Cong. Rec. S8321-01 to -02 (daily ed. July 1, 1994); id. at S7148 (daily ed. July 1, 1994); S. 2208, S. 2211, S.2059, & S. 2182, 103d Cong. (1994). Nor do any of the legislative materials discuss the Board's jurisdiction over appeals from DDESS employees. Thus, the legislative history indicates no congressional intent contrary to the plain language of the statute.

The agency argues that, despite the change in the legislation, it may continue to exclude its employees from the procedural protections of the RIF statutes and regulations because RIF actions fall within the § 2164(e)(2) exclusion for laws "relating to the number...of employees." The RIF statutes and regulations, however, are not directed toward an agency's authority to establish or eliminate positions; rather, they address the manner in which employees shall be released after the agency decides to eliminate positions. See 5 USC §§ 3501-3504; 5 CFR § 351.901. Therefore, we find that this contention lacks merit.
