3. Qualifications; Assignment Right

"The agency bears the burden of proof on the issue of whether the agency has afforded the appellant his assignment rights." Greathouse v. USPS, 76 MSPR 534, 537 (1997); see Windon v. Dept. of Air Force, 16 MSPR 98, 100-01 (1983) ("Our review of the record indicates that the agency did not introduce evidence pertaining to any of the criteria or in any way show that appellant was unqualified. A careful review of the testimony by Mr. James reveals that he did not express any opinion as to appellant's qualifications, as both appellant and the agency agree. Consequently, the agency did not meet its burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that appellant was not qualified for the position. Losure v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 2 MSPB 361 (1980)."). The appellant who claims that he was denied displacement rights because the agency did not properly consider his qualifications has an initial responsibility to identify the positions he qualified for through the RIF process. It is then the agency's burden to prove that the employee was not qualified for those positions. The AJ may, if there is no challenge to their accuracy, rely on position descriptions to determine the qualifications of the appellant. Aronson v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 310, 314, 4 MSPB 379 (1980). An employee must identify the agency's alleged error in denying him assignment to other positions under 5 CFR 351.701 (2022). The Board may limit its inquiry to the position identified at the hearing. Keating v. SBA (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 83-1390).

If position descriptions are unavailable, the agency may use secondary evidence to establish the relative qualifications of employees. Certain Former CSA Employees v. DHHS, 762 F.2d 978 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The qualification criteria are established at 5 CFR 351.702 (2022). Windon v. Dept. of Air Force, 16 MSPR 98, 100-01 (1983) (the agency had the burden to prove that the appellant was unqualified for a job sought through assignment rights; the agency failed to offer testimony concerning the appellant's qualifications). If an employee claims assignment rights to a job and provides testimony that he is qualified for the job and that the position description and classification of the current job and the job sought are the same, the agency is required to provide a specific rebuttal, not broad generalities concerning lack of qualifications. See Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 73 MSPR 476, 493-94 (1997) ("[a]n official position description of record is the best evidence as to what KSAs the agency requires the incumbent of a position to possess"; testimony and documentation were appropriately considered by the AJ to determine the appellant's qualifications; although not determinative, a relevant factor was the appellant's rating as qualified through merit promotion procedures for a position he sought through RIF assignment rights); Knab v. NASA, 13 MSPR 509, 513-14 (1982) (evaluating testimony concerning the duties and responsibilities of positions sought through the exercise of assignment rights). Once the employee identifies positions that she should have acquired through bumping, the RIF is reversed if the employer fails to produce evidence to rebut the rights asserted. The PFR is not the place for the agency to submit documentation to establish its case. Willson v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 167, 171 n.3 (1984).

Although an agency may determine the qualifications required for a position claimed through RIF assignment rights, the agency is not given deference as to the appellant's ability to meet those qualifications. The agency must prove its contentions as to the appellant's abilities (or lack of ability) through preponderant evidence, Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 73 MSPR 476, 492-93 (1997):

[T]he agency's argument that its qualifications determination is entitled to deference is not supported by precedent.

In a RIF appeal, the Board conducts a de novo review to determine whether the agency has proven by a preponderance of the evidence that it properly applied the RIF regulations. The proper application of RIF regulations is a substantive right, not a mere procedural entitlement. See, e.g., Markham v. Department of the Navy, 66 MSPR 559, 563 (1995); Schroeder v. Department of Transportation, 60 MSPR 566, 577 (1994). An agency's burden of proving that it properly applied the RIF regulations includes a showing that it afforded the appellant his proper assignment rights. See Schmidt v. Department of Transportation, 18 MSPR 613, 614 (1984). This showing in turn requires the agency to show that the appellant is not qualified for purposes of assignment rights under 5 CFR § 351.702(a).

Applying all of these principles in concert and based on a reading of the plain language of the disputed regulation, we find that, in a RIF appeal, the Board (and therefore, an administrative judge) must conduct a de novo review to determine whether the agency has shown by a preponderance of the evidence that:

[T]he employee...[c]learly demonstrates on the basis of overall background, including recency of experience, a positive ability to successfully perform all critical elements of the specific position upon entry into it, without undue interruption to that activity and without any loss of productivity beyond that normally expected in the orientation of any new but fully qualified employee.

See 5 CFR § 351.702(a)(4) (1994). The clear language of the regulation does not require the Board to afford the agency's determination deference and, even assuming that it did, such deference would be inconsistent with the statutory preponderance of the evidence standard of review that the Board is obligated to apply in RIF appeals. Therefore, the administrative judge correctly declined to afford the agency's qualifications determination deference.

Under Buckler, the AJ may consider evidence of qualifications that includes the appellant's current and prior position descriptions, performance standards, and performance appraisals with the position description, vacancy announcement, and performance standards for the job sought through assignment rights. 73 MSPR at 494. See Smith v. Dept. of Army, 86 MSPR 282, 285-86 ¶ 6 (2000) (close examination of appellant's qualifications showed he had assignment rights to a material handler position); Treese v. USPS, 77 MSPR 187, 191 (1998) ("The determination of an employee's qualification for assignment under section 351.702(a) is within the agency's discretion, subject to review by the Board under the preponderance-of-the-evidence standard.").
