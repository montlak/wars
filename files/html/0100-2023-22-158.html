d. Expertise and Diligence of Counsel; Likely Outcome

Moving on to decisions of the Board that evaluate the time spent on a case, the Board's approach toward the evaluation of time invested is sometimes unclear. In one appeal counsel claimed expertise in labor law. The experience was penalized: the Board reduced compensable time charged for research from 24 hours to 10 hours. Emelio v. USPS, 27 MSPR 233, 237 (1985); cf. Melanson v. VA, 19 MSPR 228, 231 (1984) (reducing research time as investment in future cases), post. That was an odd result: it penalized counsel for experience in Board cases. It is not likely that the Board will adopt the corollary, that inexperienced counsel should be able to bill the government for their education in civil service law. It would have been better for the Board to state its objections to specific elements of the time charged rather than inject the factor of relative expertise into the fee equation.

The Board attempted to provide some guidance as to time spent on a case in Melanson v. VA, 19 MSPR 228 (1984), involving a removal found to be a prohibited personnel practice. The AJ awarded $16,256 in fees, challenged by the agency as unreasonable. The AJ reduced some research hours as an "investment recoverable from handling future cases," an approach the Board found reasonable, even though it is questionable how many attorneys will handle more than one or two Board cases in their careers. The reduction for research time seems to be a spinoff from Kling v. DOJ, 2 MSPR 464, 471 n.6, 2 MSPB 620 (1980), supra, summarizing factors taken from Johnson v. Georgia Highway Express, Inc., 488 F.2d 714 (5th Cir. 1974): "A case of first impression requires more research, which deserves compensation, but time merely devoted to learning a new field of law is an investment for future cases." Given the 31 instances of charged deficient performance and the need for a detailed reply to the proposal to remove, Melanson considered to be reasonable 11.5 hours of conference between counsel and client. Conferences of 7.75 hours with the Special Counsel's office were also found reasonable. The Board approved charges for time spent discussing the case at lunch, a practice the Board permitted--with the lunches held during hearing breaks--but cautioned such charges would warrant "close scrutiny." Also subject to close scrutiny was the use of two attorneys to attend four depositions: the theory is that two attorneys should not be paid when one will do. Yet the Board held that more than one attorney may be appropriate and that there is no reason to presume that such representational decisions will be made frivolously. It is up to the AJ to evaluate whether the second counsel is active in the hearing process and whether the claim for his time is proper.

Counsel are expected by their clients (and state disciplinary boards) to pursue their cases with unflagging zeal. Although the Board does not recognize this expectation in its fee decisions, it allows counsel to put a reasonable amount of time into a case even though it appears likely that the appellant will prevail. In one appeal, the AJ let it be known in advance of the hearing that the agency's position would not likely prevail. Agency counsel sought to distinguish the cases cited by the AJ and pressed on to a hearing. Appellant's counsel naturally litigated on, but the AJ cut the subsequent award of fees back by 35 billable hours for excessive time spent on the case after the prehearing conference when, it was asserted, appellant's counsel should have understood that his client would win. The Board set aside the reduction, recognizing implicitly that the attorney had the right to continue his preparation and fully present the case at the hearing. Kennard v. DOD, 33 MSPR 231, 234-35 (1987); see Crumbaker v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 191 (Fed. Cir. 1986).
