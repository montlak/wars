broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 05: Remedies, Section A: Interference, Restraint, and Coercion, Subsection 04: Disciplinary Cases, Part b: Effect of Failure to Bargain Over Changes in Rules, Procedures
b. Effect of Failure to Bargain Over Changes in Rules, Procedures

Is an employee insulated from discipline following the allegedly insubordinate refusal to obey an order that implements an unbargained change in work rules? It depends. Employees who could have otherwise been disciplined, whether for insubordination or other misconduct, are not entitled to have those disciplinary actions rescinded to the extent that the discipline would have been appropriate and lawful despite an agency's unilateral change. FLRA, however, may order rescission of disciplinary actions taken as a result of an employee's failure to abide by changed and unbargained requirements. See DOJ, INS, El Paso Dist. Office and AFGE Local 1210, 39 FLRA 1431, 1438-39 (1991) (on reconsideration motion) (relying on VA W. Los Angeles Med. Ctr. and AFGE Local 1061, 23 FLRA 278 (1986)).

In AAFES, Oakland Army Base and AFGE Local 1157, 25 FLRA 740, 745 (1987), the agency failed to negotiate over a change in procedures leading to increased scrutiny of driving records of individuals whose positions required them to drive agency vehicles. The program led to the discovery of vehicular infractions for several employees and resulted in adverse actions. Had there been no change in the agency program for monitoring its drivers, the violations would not have been uncovered and the employees would not have been disciplined. FLRA concluded that the discipline was validly imposed and should not be set aside despite the ULP committed as a result of the failure to bargain over the impact of the enhanced monitoring program:

...While their misconduct might not have been discovered except for the new policy, that fact does not serve to excuse the misconduct or warrant cancellation of the disciplinary actions. See, for example, Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 24 FLRA No. 82 (1986) (where, in a mixed motive removal action, the Authority concluded that even in the absence of protected activity, the employee would have been removed because of the misconduct described). We conclude that the remedy sought by the General Counsel would not effectuate the purposes and policies of the Statute.

From the Merit Systems Protection Board, Gragg v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 506 (1984), presented an interesting set of circumstances. Mr. Gragg, an equipment cleaner, was removed because he failed to follow a directive requiring that he be clean shaven (so that he would wear a respirator). Two years after the removal, FLRA decided that the agency's action changing the policy, without allowing the union the opportunity to negotiate, was a ULP, to be remedied by revocation of the policy. Condensing the procedural history, the Board determined that the appellant was not insubordinate because, as a result of the FLRA determination, the appellant was not guilty of insubordination (since he had not disregarded a proper order). The removal was reversed.
