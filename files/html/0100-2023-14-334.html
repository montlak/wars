12. National Guard Positions; Military and Civilian Compatibility Requirements

Matters concerning the military aspects of civilian technician employment are not negotiable. They do not concern conditions of employment within the meaning of the Statute. NFFE Local 1655 and Adjutant Gen. of Ill., 20 FLRA 829, 829-30 (1985). A proposal seeking to avoid compatibility requirements for National Guard technicians in nonmobilization units was outside the duty to bargain. It was inconsistent with the Technicians Act requiring compatibility. NAGE and Dept. of Army and Air Force, Nat'l Guard Bureau, 17 FLRA 546, 546-48 (1985). Another provision concerning compatibility of civilian and military positions was rejected as inconsistent with controlling regulations in NAGE Local R14-87 and Kansas NG, Topeka, 18 FLRA 736, 738 (1985); see NFFE Local 1623 and S. Carolina NG, Columbia, 28 FLRA 633, 641-42 (1987), aff'd NFFE Local 1623 v. FLRA, 852 F.2d 1349, 1352-53 (D.C. Cir. 1988). The court observed in NFFE Local 1623:

...The Technicians Act makes clear that the entire question of compatibility is a military issue. The decisions about what criteria should govern compatibility and what precise positions should be compatible are left entirely to the Secretary of the Army or of the Air Force. 32 U.S.C. § 709(b) (1982). The statute further specifies that compatibility is in no way optional or negotiable; exemptions from the compatibility requirements are possible only "as prescribed by the Secretary." Finally, the statute requires that incompatibility be remedied in one swift way: a technician who is no longer compatible "shall be promptly separated from his...employment." 32 U.S.C. § 709(e)(1).

...

...The provisions of the Technicians Act governing compatibility do not specifically countermand the Local's proposal, and thus the union argues that the proposal is not proscribed. We think the Local fails to appreciate the special way in which military matters are excluded from bargaining. This is not a situation in which a statute supplies a basic framework for carrying out a federal mission, whose interstices can then be filled by employee-management agreements. As we have said, the military side of the National Guard lies wholly outside of the collective bargaining realm. It is for this reason that we interpret the Technicians Act's outline of the compatibility requirement, with its delegation of responsibility to the Army and Air Force Secretaries, as an exclusive grant of authority. The Local cannot negotiate in derogation of that authority.

Not negotiable was a demand that the employer agree that all job-related requirements affecting a technician in his technician employment are automatically renewable unless the technician is separated for physical requirements or for just cause. Under law, a prerequisite to employment as a civilian technician of the Guard is enlistment as a military member of the Guard. When the term of enlistment expires, the technician, to maintain civilian employment, must reenlist in, and be accepted for reenlistment in, the National Guard. The proposal would, essentially, require the Guard to reenlist the employee, a military matter affecting the "uniformed services," whose conditions of employment are, through definition in § 7103, outside the coverage of the Statute. Membership in the Guard is a matter specifically provided for by federal statute and is outside the duty to bargain under § 7103(a)(14)(C). The proposal was inconsistent with 10 USC 976, precluding labor organizations from bargaining on behalf of members of the armed services over terms and conditions of military service. ACT, Mont. Air Chap. and Montana Air Nat'l Guard, Hq. 120th Fighter Interceptor Group, 20 FLRA 717, 717 (1985), on remand from ACT, Montana Air Chap. v. FLRA, 756 F.2d 172, 179-81 (D.C. Cir. 1985).

A proposal was nonnegotiable that effectively required the agency to negotiate as to the military combat status of reserve components of the National Guard. The proposal did not directly concern employment conditions, although it would have had an effect on the ability of these units to employ women who otherwise would have been excluded from service in combat-designated units. NFFE Local 1724 and Utah NG, Salt Lake City, 7 FLRA 732, 734 (1982). A nonnegotiable proposal set conditions on filling of military positions. It did not affect conditions of employment of unit employees. ACT, N.Y. State Council and New York, Div. of Military and Naval Affairs, 11 FLRA 475, 478 (1983). Another proposal was nonnegotiable that concerned the manner technicians would meet position requirements through military reserve training experience. NAGE and Michigan NG, 29 FLRA 1231, 1234 (1987); see ACT, New York State Council and DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau, N.Y., Div. of Military and Naval Affairs, 45 FLRA 17, 17-18 (1992) (nonnegotiable: a proposal that allowed only certain military experience in the Guard to be counted toward meeting the experience requirements for positions). A proposal was nonnegotiable that for certain technician positions, "all [military and civilian] applicants will be evaluated using identical criteria"; the proposal "directly affects and substantively limits the Agency's discretion to establish qualifications for advancement of military technicians and the criteria for promotion or placement of military technicians." AFGE Local 3013 and DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau, Maine Air Nat'l Guard, Augusta, 40 FLRA 203, 206, 207-10 (1991) (Proposal 3). The union could not compel negotiations on a proposal that essentially compelled the employer to decide in advance whether it would fill a position from a particular source, military or civilian, and then limit selections to applicants from that source. (Proposal 6). But not every promotion proposal advanced by a civilian unit within the Guard is nonnegotiable. Some are so innocuous that they survive scrutiny. There was nothing inappropriate about Proposal 4 in AFGE Local 3013 that "[i]t is recognized that in the interest of effective and efficient government operations that the most highly qualified individuals should be selected for employment." The proposal was a statement of general policy but, FLRA added, "[t]he proposal does not...require the Agency to select the most highly qualified individuals."
