e. Reassignment Without Regard to RIF

A reassignment without a change in pay, grade, or competitive level is not an adverse action. Reassignments may be used to move employees to locations where their work is needed without application of RIF procedures. See Tiltti v. Weise, 155 F.3d 596, 601 (2d Cir. 1998) ("The reassignments were not designed to reduce the number of Customs' employees, but to transfer employees to locations where Customs concluded they were more needed--as Customs had done ten years earlier in reassigning other Patrol Officers from other locations to the Southwest Border. The Patrol Officers were not subject to a reduction in force. See Krizman v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 77 F.3d 434, 439 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ('[t]he reorganization as a whole was not a [reduction in force]; only those employees who were assigned to lower-graded positions as a result of the reorganization were subjected to [reduction in force] actions.')."); Buckheit v. USPS, 107 MSPR 52, 58 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 224 (2007) (reassignment, when a RIF might have occurred, did not become a RIF when the only loss in pay was premium pay and no displacement of another employee occurred).

Assuming the reassignment does not require displacement of another employee under RIF regulations, an agency may choose to use RIF procedures when reassigning an employee, but the reassignment is not appealable without a demotion, that is, reassignment to a position with lower grade or rate of pay under Myers v. Dept. of Army, 87 MSPR 77, 78-79 ¶¶ 4-6 (2000):

An agency is required to use RIF procedures when it releases a competing employee from her competitive level by furlough of more than 30 days, separation, demotion, or reassignment requiring displacement when the release is required due to a reorganization. 5 C.F.R. § 351.201(a)(2); Dixon v. U.S. Postal Service, 64 M.S.P.R. 445, 450 (1994), aff'd sub nom. Scorcia v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 F.3d 503 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). Here, although the appellant was not released from her competitive level, and the agency was therefore not required to use RIF procedures under 5 C.F.R. § 351.201(a)(2), the agency applied such procedures to reassign the appellant to another position within her competitive level.

However, a RIF reassignment is not in itself an appealable action under the regulations applicable in this case. Bissett v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 631, 637 (1995). An employee reassigned during a RIF can only appeal to the Board if the reassignment was a RIF demotion. Carita v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 277, 279 (1995). A RIF demotion does not occur unless, among other things, the employee is reassigned to a position carrying a lower grade or rate of pay. 5 C.F.R. § 351.901; Mueller v. U.S. Postal Service, 75 M.S.P.R. 424, 426, (Vice Chair Slavet, concurring), aff'd, 135 F.3d 774 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table).

The AJ cited 5 C.F.R. § 351.901 as the basis for the Board's jurisdiction over this appeal. This regulation provides that an employee who has been furloughed for more than 30 days, separated, or demoted by a RIF may appeal to the Board. Id. The appellant, however, was not furloughed, separated, or demoted. Rather, the Notification of Personnel Action shows that the appellant was assigned to a position with the same pay and grade. The only change in her position was the department in which she was assigned. Thus, even if she had been released from her competitive level the appellant's reassignment would not be an appealable RIF action. Carita v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. at 279 (1995).

If no displacement is involved, a reassignment is not a reduction in force. Musone v. USDA, 31 MSPR 85 (1986); see Elmore v. Hampton, 373 F. Supp. 360 (E.D. Tenn. 1973) (agency need not follow RIF procedures and may instead use a reassignment unless the employee is released from his competitive level and there is a reassignment requiring displacement); Vercelli v. USPS, 70 MSPR 322, 325 (1996) (change from EAS-15 to EAS-18 was not a change to a lower-graded position or any other action constituting a RIF); Gregory v. OPM, 66 MSPR 691, 700 (1995) ("An agency is not obligated to follow RIF procedures where an employee is reassigned and another employee is not displaced.") following Harpster v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 43, 45 (1988) ("[b]ecause another employee was not displaced when the agency assigned the appellant to a newly-created position, the agency was not required to follow RIF procedures."); Tankesley v. TVA, 54 MSPR 147, 151 (1992) (position change did not involve removal from a competitive level); Ditmore v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 31 (1986) (immaterial that the reassignment was outside the competitive area); Aho v. USDA, 25 MSPR 569, 571 n.* (1985), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1065 (Fed. Cir. 1985); MacMurdo v. USDA, 24 MSPR 388, 394 (1984) ("As the position to which he was reassigned was vacant, however, the agency chose this method of avoiding a RIF. Thus, inasmuch as there was no reassignment requiring displacement, the agency was not obligated to invoke the RIF regulations."); Sharp v. SBA, 21 MSPR 585, 586 n.* (1984) ("Appellant was assigned to a vacant position and no displacement of another employee resulted. Appellant's movement from his current position does not constitute a reassignment requiring displacement."); Lopez v. DHUD, 5 MSPR 52, 53, 5 MSPB 128 (1981) ("The appellant...was merely transferred from one organizational unit to another without any change in grade or competitive level. A reassignment from one organizational unit to another without a change of grade, pay or competitive level is not an adverse action appealable under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.3. ...No release, demotion, nor displacement was effected. Thus, the agency was not required to follow reduction-in-force procedures...."); Smith v. DHEW, 2 MSPR 479, 3 MSPB 3 (1980) (reassignment from one GS-5 position to another was not an appealable event).

Reassignment in the same grade and pay, although in a different competitive level, does not require use of RIF procedures. Larson v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1301). It is immaterial that the reassignment is to a position in a different competitive level, as long as it is at the same pay and grade and no displacement is required. Brunjes v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 189, 190, 2 MSPB 356 (1980), followed, Kimsey v. Dept. of Interior, 18 MSPR 685 n.* (1984); see Chance v. FAA, 5 MSPR 277, 279 (1981) ("[R]eassignment of an employee to a vacant position in a different competitive level does not require the implementation of RIF procedures if the employee retains the same grade and pay."). Wear v. USDA, 22 MSPR 597, 598-99 (1984), summarized the late FPM:

Citing 5 CFR 351.201(b), appellant argues in his petition for review that because a shortage of funds is a legitimate management reason for a reduction in force, the agency had to fill the Anchorage vacancy by reduction in force even if no employee was displaced. Under 5 CFR 351.201(b), an agency is required to follow reduction-in-force procedures when, in its discretion, it chooses to fill a vacancy in another competitive level with an employee who has been reached for release from his competitive level. But under 5 CFR 351.602, an employee can be released from his competitive level only by application of reduction-in-force procedures, specifically, those governing the first round of competition. Since the agency chose not to invoke reduction-in-force procedures but to reassign appellant under 5 CFR 335.102, we find, under the circumstances, that appellant was not released from his competitive level so as to require invocation of RIF procedures, despite having been reassigned, apparently, to a separate competitive level.

Moreover, we reject appellant's assertion that the agency had no option other than to conduct a reduction in force because a legitimate reason for conducting one existed. In Ketterer, supra, at 462, we held that the reasons that would justify a reduction in force would also justify a directed reassignment. See also Camhi v. Department of Energy, 12 MSPB 47 (1982) (holding that lack of work, which is a legitimate reason for a reduction in force, is a proper reason for directing an employee's reassignment).

The Federal Personnel Manual confirms that an agency has the option to reassign an employee or to conduct a reduction in force as circumstances dictate. Federal Personnel Manual Chapter 351, subchapter 3-6, advises that "If the agency elects to reassign an employee to the vacant position rather than affecting him or her by reduction in force, it need not use reduction-in-force procedures. The reassignment can be made regardless of whether the employee is within reach on the retention register."[*]

[*] See also Elmore v. Hampton, 373 F. Supp. 360, 362 (E.D. Tenn. 1973), holding that 5 CFR 351.201(a) does not require the invocation of RIF procedures where the employee is reassigned without displacement, even where the reason for the reassignment is overstaffing, which would constitute a valid reason for RIF under that section. To hold otherwise would be to construe 5 CFR 351.202 so as to conflict with 5 CFR 351.201 by requiring use of RIF procedures which are not mandatory under the circumstances, and would be contrary to both Board and court precedent, as well as interpretations of the Civil Service Commission and the Office of Personnel Management. We therefore reject appellant's contention that the agency had no choice but to effect his reassignment by RIF.

That the reassignment is from one organizational unit to another is immaterial. Hayes v. NASA, 2 MSPR 477, 3 MSPB 1 (1980). Regarding an employee reassigned in grade to a vacant position, there is no RIF, and therefore no appealable action to the MSPB, even though the move is from a merit pay position to a General Schedule position, and even though the employee's new retention register may make him more vulnerable in a future RIF. Thomas v. United States, 709 F.2d 48 (Fed. Cir. 1983); see Crain v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3015) (reassignment without a loss of grade or pay does not qualify as a RIF; under 5 CFR 351.201(a)(2) (2022), a RIF occurs when the agency releases a competitive employee from his competitive level by demotion, defined by 5 CFR 210.102 (2022) as a change to a lower grade or lower rate of pay). Sharma v. Dept. of Navy, DA-0351-15-0179-I-1 (NP 9/21/2016), noted:

We agree with the administrative judge that, even though the agency gave the appellant notice that his position would be eliminated, because the appellant accepted the agency's offer of a permanent position as a Microbiologist, GS-09, Step 7, at the Brooke Army Medical Center, a position with the same pay, grade, and benefits on the same military installation as the one he previously encumbered, the appellant was not subjected to a RIF. E.g., Kohfield v. Department of the Navy, 75 M.S.P.R. 1, 4 (1997). A lateral transfer with no loss in grade or pay is not an appealable RIF action under 5 C.F.R. part 351. Cf. Perisho v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 55, 57 (1995) (finding that a lateral reassignment is not an appealable RIF action); Harpster v. Department of the Army, 39 M.S.P.R. 43, 45 (1988) (finding that a lateral reassignment is not a basis for Board jurisdiction).

We also agree with the administrative judge that, absent a loss of grade or pay, the appellant failed to identify any other source of Board jurisdiction over his appeal. Absent an otherwise appealable action, the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appellant's discrimination claims. E.g., Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980) (observing that prohibited personnel practices under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b) are not an independent source of Board jurisdiction), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982)....

The reassignment may properly be used to relieve a staff imbalance in different locations. There is no requirement to use a RIF under those circumstances. Comisky v. OPM, 20 MSPR 78, 80-81 (1984). If individuals are assigned to unclassified light duty because of physical inability or medical disqualification to perform work, and if they are later returned to their original positions and terminated for inability to perform their work, no RIF occurred. There was no release due to shortage of funds for their permanent positions. Had the employees been able to perform their work, they would have been retained. D'Leo v. Dept. of Navy, 53 MSPR 44, 48 (1992) ("Nor does the Board have jurisdiction over matters concerning an agency's authority to make decisions to establish or abolish positions, assign duties, and take personnel actions.").

One decision suggests that the government may not use forced reassignments to achieve reductions in force. Comisky v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1302) (citing Motto v. GSA, 335 F. Supp. 694 (E.D. La. 1971)), aff'd, 502 F.2d 1165 (5th Cir. 1974), cert. denied, 420 U.S. 927 (1975). If the agency knows that the reassignment is to a position that will soon be downgraded, the employee may be in a position to assert successfully that the reassignment was a sham, a violation of his RIF rights to competition for retention at his original grade level. See McClure v. FEMA, 32 MSPR 672 (1987) (agency improperly circumvented RIF procedures when it reassigned the employee from an abolished GS-13 position because of a shortage of funds to a position the agency knew did not warrant a GS-13 grade and classification level).
