1. Probative Value

The statement of a party-opponent is an admission entitled to evidentiary weight. An admission that is not explained, rebutted, or contradicted may properly be considered. Perez v. USPS, 48 MSPR 354, 356 (1991); see Ludlum v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 56, 65 ¶ 18 (2000) (extrajudicial statement is not conclusive but it may receive weight if not adequately explained, rebutted, or contradicted); Beck v. DOJ, 67 MSPR 219, 223 (1995) ("The appellant was free to attempt to rebut his own statement...but he did not present any evidence in rebuttal."); Reniere v. USDA, 62 MSPR 648, 653 (1994) ("The agency's response is also in the nature of an admission and is entitled to evidentiary weight. Perez v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 MSPR 354, 356 (1991)."). An admission is of particular weight if the declarant has expertise as to the subject matter of the admission. See Leibowitz v. DOJ, 88 MSPR 635, 638 ¶ 10 (2001) (admission by the appellant concerning the immigration status of a household member was entitled to weight when the appellant had expertise in immigration law). An admission of marijuana use was sufficient, without regard to the sufficiency of a chain of custody for a drug test, to sustain the charge of marijuana use, Cole v. Dept. of Air Force, 120 MSPR 640, 645 ¶ 9, 2014 MSPB 16 (2014):

There is no dispute that the appellant admitted that he had used marijuana in response to the MRO's question whether there was any legitimate medical explanation for the drug test result. Further, as noted above, the appellant "freely admit[ted]" in his written response to the proposal notice that he had used marijuana, and, further, he stated that he had "admitted guilt and shown remorse" for engaging in the charged misconduct. An agency may rely on an appellant's admissions in support of its charge, Leaton v. Department of the Interior, 65 M.S.P.R. 331, 337 (1994), aff'd, 64 F.3d 678 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), and an appellant's admission to a charge can suffice as proof of the charge without additional proof from the agency, see Wells v. Department of Defense, 53 M.S.P.R. 637, 643-44 (1992) (the appellant's admission that he engaged in alleged conduct in violation of a regulation is sufficient proof to sustain the charge of disregarding a regulation or directive); Mascol v. Department of the Navy, 7 M.S.P.R. 565, 567 (1981) (the appellant's admission was sufficient to sustain the charge). ...Further, the appellant did not attempt to recant his admissions; he did not claim that they were coerced or otherwise involuntary; and he failed to rebut the agency's testimonial evidence concerning those admissions at the hearing. Under the circumstances, we infer that he does not dispute that evidence. See Leaton, 65 M.S.P.R. at 337 (the Board inferred from the appellant's failure to testify that he did not dispute that he admitted to engaging in charged misconduct). We therefore find that the appellant's numerous unrecanted admissions that he used marijuana constitute preponderant evidence that he used an illegal drug, as charged.

A written statement of wrongdoing by an appellant was characterized as a declaration against interest (it is an admission), is properly used as proof in support of a preponderance of evidence. Fields v. VA, 20 MSPR 1, 2 (1984); see Eldridge v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0597-I-1 (NP 4/5/2016) ("We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant's stipulations and admissions were sufficient to prove that the appellant engaged in the conduct alleged in the charge. Id.; see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.63 (the parties may stipulate to any matter of fact, and the stipulation will satisfy a party's burden of proving the fact alleged); see also Cole v. Department of the Air Force, 120 M.S.P.R. 640, ¶ 9 (2014) (finding that an appellant's admission was sufficient to prove a charge)."). Sworn statements offered as part of a plea bargain agreement may constitute nonhearsay if there are other circumstantial guarantees of trustworthiness. Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 562 ¶ 37 (1999). An appellant's assertion that his statement is unworthy of weight because it was obtained by "questionable methods," e.g., an investigator implied that the statement would not be used in a disciplinary procedure, was belied by a clause in the statement that "[n]o threats or promises of any kind have been made to induce...this statement." Beck, supra, 67 MSPR at 222-23.

Can an admission alone support a case? Apparently it cannot if the charge involves tangible injury to person or property. There must be some extrinsic evidence, something more tangible than an admission. Extrajudicial statements (those made outside a hearing setting) may not satisfy even a substantial evidence standard, despite being made voluntarily and in a noncustodial setting. In part, this is because the psychological motivation prompting a confession after the fact is an unknown factor. See Wohlwend v. DHHS, 16 MSPR 458, 461 (1983), post; Roberts v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 674, 679, 8 MSPB 323 (1981) ("Regardless of what may have motivated the appellant here to admittedly make statements which are highly incriminating, those statements, repudiated by the appellant under oath and standing alone, cannot be found to establish as more likely true than not that the appellant used and sold narcotics.").

A removal was reversed when an employee was charged with negligently endangering the life of a patient because, other than the appellant's recanted admission, there was no evidence of injury to the patient, no witnesses to the event, no notation of the incident on the patient's chart, and the only witnesses who provided medical evidence said that the patient suffered no injury. "Due process demands that there be a corpus delicti in cases where a tangible injury to person or property is alleged," Wohlwend v. DHHS, 16 MSPR 458, 461 (1983):

This Board, while recognizing that the degree of corroborative evidence necessary to support an extrajudicial confession for the purpose of a criminal conviction is not required to establish appellant's misconduct in a civil or administrative proceeding, has nevertheless noted that the due process concerns underlying the criminal rule are present. In Roberts v. Department of the Treasury, 8 MSPB 323 (1981), we held that, standing alone, unsworn extrajudicial statements made to a security officer in a custodial situation, and which were repudiated by the appellant under oath, could not support an agency removal action by preponderant evidence.

The alleged extrajudicial statements in this case were made in a non-custodial interview, and their voluntary nature is not seriously at issue. However, the due process guarantee is not concerned solely with whether or not an accused has been coerced into implicating himself/herself. As noted in Roberts, without corroborating evidence, extrajudicial statements may not even satisfy a substantial evidence standard, in part because the psychological motivation prompting a confession after the fact is an unknown factor. Id. at 326. Due process demands that there be a corpus delicti in cases where a tangible injury to person or property is alleged. See Opper v. United States, 348 U.S. 84, 89-91, 75 S. Ct. 158, 162-163, 99 L. Ed.2d 101 (1954). By introducing no independent evidence establishing that the patient was taken off of the respirator or had in fact suffered some injury, the agency has failed to support the removal action by preponderant evidence. 5 CFR § 1201.56(a) and (c)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b) and 1201.4(q)]. We find that the presiding official erred, therefore, in considering witness credibility a determinative factor.

The requirement of a corpus delicti does not mean that prior statements have no value. Although prior statements may not have sufficient reliability in the face of contradictory or, at least, inconsistent sworn testimony to establish a preponderance of evidence, the reliability of the appellant's prior statements may be bolstered by corroborative record evidence. Bixby v. USDA, 24 MSPR 13, 18-19 (1984). Admissions made by the appellant during the course of an agency investigation will support a charge, particularly if the appellant does not attempt to rebut the investigator's report at the hearing. The Board will infer from the appellant's failure to testify that he does not dispute the report. See Leaton v. Dept. of Interior, 65 MSPR 331, 337 (1994). A guilty plea may serve as an admission of misconduct; the AJ need not rely upon a conviction. Sleboda v. DOJ, 54 MSPR 386, 389-90 (1992) (although the appellant withdrew his guilty plea, the parties stipulated that he pled guilty and the appellant did not recant the stipulation throughout the hearing). [Refer in this chapter to "Previous Determinations of Disputed Facts, Issues, Claims: Res Judicata, Collateral Estoppel, and Law of the Case--The Doctrines."]

The Board cautioned in Garibay v. VA, 31 MSPR 258, 262, 263 (1986), that oral admissions should be received with caution, "owing to their liability of being misunderstood and misreported." According to the Board, "an admission is nothing more than an item in the mass of evidence. It is not, in any sense, final or conclusive. The opponent has the right to proceed with his proof in denial of its correctness." Ambiguous admissions are insufficient for factual determinations; a hearing should be held to resolve the ambiguities or inconsistencies presented by the record. Garibay, 31 MSPR at 262 n.6 (citing Wigmore & Chadbourn, Evidence in Trials at Common Law, § 2094); see Garibay v. VA, 35 MSPR 327, 333 (1987) (following remand) ("[a]n extrajudicial admission is not conclusive on the party who made it or to whom it is attributable; in the absence of the elements of estoppel, such an admission may be explained, rebutted or contradicted. 31 C.J.S. Evidence § 380 (1974).").
