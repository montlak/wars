broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 18: Reductions in Force, Section C: Retention Standing; Retreat Rights, Subsection 05: National Guard; Civilian Technicians

5. National Guard; Civilian Technicians

If an agency, e.g., the National Guard, is exempt from OPM regulations governing retention standing, nothing precludes negotiation over a proposal that determines standing on the basis of appraisal ratings if management retains the right to establish point ranges for appraisals. ACT, Pa. State Council and Adjutant Gen., Dept. of Military Affairs, Pa., 3 FLRA 50, 51 (1980). But any proposal is nonnegotiable that concerns the RIF of National Guard technicians, who must also hold military status, that does not consider both civilian and military ratings. There is a compelling need under agency regulations for consideration of military performance. ACT and Dept. of Air Force, Montana Air Nat'l Guard, Hq. 120th Fighter Interceptor Group, 11 FLRA 505, 508 (1983), aff'd, ACT, Montana Air Chap. v. FLRA, 756 F.2d 172, 179-80 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (relying on AFGE Local 2953 v. FLRA, 730 F.2d 1534, 1545-47 (D.C. Cir. 1984), post).

A proposal was nonnegotiable that required the agency to establish a RIF register ranking National Guard technicians according to a number of factors related to their civilian technician service, including their civilian appraisal scores, but without reference to the appraisals of their military supervisors. The proposal, by barring any consideration of military performance, conflicted with the mandate of the National Guard Technicians Act of 1968 that both civilian and related military performance be considered as part of a technician's overall evaluation. It also conflicted with the National Guard Bureau regulation that implemented the statute in an essentially nondiscretionary fashion. The proposal was barred due to compelling need under § 7117(a)(2). AFGE Local 2953 and Nat'l Guard Bureau, Office of Adjutant Gen., Nebraska, 7 FLRA 87, 88-89 (1981) (distinguishing ACT, Pennsylvania State Council and Adjutant Gen., Dept. of Military Affairs, Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, 3 FLRA 50 (1980)).

The D.C. Circuit reviewed and affirmed the Local 2953 decision in AFGE Local 2953 v. FLRA, 730 F.2d 1534, 1536-37, 1541, 1544, 1546 (D.C. Cir. 1984). Explaining the nature of the employment of the National Guard technician within the civil service system, the court determined:

The National Guard Technician Act of 1968...is a special act of Congress enacted for the limited purpose of making fringe and retirement benefits of federal employees and coverage under the Federal Tort Claims Act...available to National Guard technician employees of the various states. It accomplishes this by creating a dual status for such technicians. They are to be considered as federal employees for some purposes but also to be members of the respective state National Guard units where they serve.... [T]he separation of technicians from their employment for failure to meet military security standards, or for other cause is to be effected by the state adjutant general...and the adjutant general also is to accomplish a "reduction-in-force, removal, adverse action involving discharge from technician employment, suspension, furlough without pay, or reduction in rank or compensation."... In addition a right of appeal from any action taken...does "not extend beyond the adjutant general."...

...

[U]nder the Union's proposal the Guard's retention register would not consider military rating. This is no mere procedural tinkering. It is, rather, a substantive proposal that would work a considerable change on the retention rank charts employed by the Guard during RIFs. If, for example, Technician A had received an evaluation score of 90 in the appraisal of his civilian skills, and an evaluation of 10 regarding his military capabilities, for a total of 100, he would be retained in the technician's job over Technician B who received respective evaluation scores of 89 and 90 (total 179) because Technician B was marginally less qualified than A with regard to civilian skills though overwhelmingly more qualified in the military and overall evaluations. The implications of the proposal are therefore overwhelming.

...

...The "compelling need" for TPM 351 is obvious once the fundamental assumptions of the Technician Act are brought to light....

[E]nactment of the Technician Act was essential to assure that the military mission of the Guard would be carried out effectively and efficiently. This intent is evident in the provisions which provide for the continuance of the authority of state adjutants general to exercise command authority over technicians--as they do over other guardsmen....

...

[I]t follows quite clearly that since military preparedness is the sine qua non of the Technician Act, TPM 351 conforms to the mandate of the Act and serves a "compelling need" when it demands that both military evaluation and technician efficiency be considered when the Guard formulates retention registers. Any standard that subordinated or eliminated military proficiency in the ratings and evaluations for the RIF process would leave open the possibility that the "best" technicians--in military terms--would be retired during a RIF while the worst would remain....

Such result is incompatible with sound defense planning of our military preparedness which must necessarily seek the highest combination of technician ability and military effectiveness. Accordingly, we hold that the primary mandate of the Act is to insure that the military capability of the guard is effective and efficient, and that the Nebraska National Guard was not required to engage in collective bargaining negotiations over the union's proposal that completely eliminated the military rating and evaluations from the RIF process....

Negotiable despite claims of either compelling need based on TPR 300 (351) or legal requirements of 32 USC 709(f) was a proposal that required placement on retention registers of employees in one of three tenure groups (nonprobationary permanent employees, probationary permanent employees, and indefinite employees) and, within each group, listing of employees in descending order based on their length of service, augmented by up to 30 years of service based on their last three performance appraisals. ACT Montana Air Chap. 29 and DOD, Nat'l Guard Bureau, 56 FLRA 674, 674, 676-78 (2000).

A proposal was nonnegotiable that during a RIF the agency vacate positions held by employees other than career or permanent technicians before removing career or permanent technicians. It interfered with management's right to separate employees. ACT, N.Y. State Council and New York, Div. of Military and Naval Affairs, 11 FLRA 475, 479-81 (1983) (Proposal 3). Any proposal that compelled management to retain National Guard technicians irrespective of their military status was inconsistent with federal law, 32 USC 709(e)(1), requiring separation from civilian employment of a technician who is separated from the Guard or who ceases to hold the military grade required for the position. ACT and Dept. of Air Force, Montana Air Nat'l Guard, Hq. 120th Fighter Interceptor Group, 11 FLRA 505, 511-12 (1983) (Proposal 7), aff'd, ACT, Montana Air Chap. v. FLRA, 756 F.2d 172, 179-80 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (relying on AFGE Local 2953 v. FLRA, 730 F.2d 1534, 1545-47 (D.C. Cir. 1984)). A proposal relating to RIFs of National Guard technicians that factored into retention standing at least a portion of the technicians' military service was inconsistent with the Technicians Act. ACT, Pennsylvania State Council and Pennsylvania Army and Air Nat'l Guard, 14 FLRA 38, 43 (1984) (Proposal 4). National Guard technicians could not negotiate a proposal for reemployment rights on the basis of retention standing alone, for the proposal could allow rehiring without the requisite military standing required by the Technicians Act. ACT, Pennsylvania State Council and Pennsylvania Army and Air Nat'l Guard, 14 FLRA 38, 42 (1984). A proposal that entitled National Guard technicians to reemployment rights when released from active duty in the Guard was already covered by statute; the proposal was nonnegotiable. ACT and Delaware NG, 15 FLRA 330, 331 (1984).
