1. Parker

A significant decision defining harmful error was Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPR 505, 1 MSPB 489 (1980), involving denial of a within-grade step increase under 5 USC 5335, a statute permitting the increase only if an employee's work is at an acceptable level of competence. The AJ in Parker found several errors: first, the negative ALOC determination preceded the conclusion of the statutory waiting period for the WIGI by a few weeks, meaning that the employee had not had his performance evaluated for the entire period; second, the statements of the employee during the reconsideration process were not adequately summarized; third, the reconsideration decision did not adequately address the appellant's reply. The AJ did not consider the evidence offered by the agency during the hearing, but instead reversed the agency's negative determination for failure to establish that the determination was supported by sufficient evidence. On PFR the Board noted that 5 USC 7701(c)(2) provides for reversal on appeal of an agency decision if there is harmful error in the application of agency procedures used in arriving at the decision. The statutory language was adopted to avoid reversal of agency actions because of technical procedural oversights that have not substantially prejudiced or impaired the employee's rights. The statute placed on the appellant the burden of establishing, as an affirmative defense, that the agency committed procedural error and that the error was harmful.

Under Parker, application of the harmful error standard may be relatively simple if subsequent events make obvious the error's actual effect or absence of effect. Two inconsequential errors were noted in Parker: the failure of the reconsideration file to advise the appellant of the right to appeal, cured through a timely appeal; and the failure of the agency to provide the appellant with a copy of the reconsideration file, not harmful because a copy was given to the appellant prior to the hearing on the appeal. However, Parker determined that if subsequent events do not reveal whether the error was actually prejudicial, the probable effect of the error must be determined in light of all the evidence and the circumstances of the particular case, 1 MSPR at 514:

Although the inquiry is necessarily one addressed to the imponderables of what might have been, the mere theoretical possibility of prejudice cannot suffice as a basis for inferring actual prejudice. Indulging in such an easy substitute for reasoned judgment would create a presumption engulfing the statutory rule and would be inconsistent with the burden placed by statute upon the appellant to show harmful error. Unless it is likely that an alleged error affected the result, its occurrence cannot have been prejudicial.... Stated another way, the question is whether it was within the range of appreciable probability that the error had a harmful effect upon the outcome before the agency. However stated, the decisive factors are the closeness of the agency's decision, the centrality of the issue affected by the error, and any steps taken to mitigate the effect of the error....[6]

[6] As with the prejudicial error rule under the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 USC 706, caution must be exercised in applying the harmful error doctrine when basic rights to procedural due process are implicated.... Also, there may be a greater probability that an error affected the result when a heavier evidentiary burden must be satisfied to sustain that result, e.g., preponderant evidence, than when a lesser evidentiary standard is applicable, e.g., substantial evidence.

Parker abrogated a degree of adjudicative autonomy in the review of personnel actions, 1 MSPR at 517:

A further word is warranted concerning the consideration by presiding officials of agency procedural errors that have not been raised by appellants. Ordinarily a procedural error which is not alleged by the appellant to have been harmful should be assumed not to have been so, consistent with the appellant's statutory burden to show harmful error as an affirmative defense.[9] In this case the appellant, an articulate supervisory employee who was represented by counsel at the hearing, did not raise either of the errors found harmful by the presiding official, and neither of those purported errors was of a grave or substantial character affecting appellant's basic procedural rights. In these circumstances it was inappropriate for the presiding official to take cognizance of such procedural errors.

[9] It may be appropriate for a presiding official to address on his or her own initiative an error that implicates an employee's basic procedural rights, when cognizance of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice.... Slightly greater leeway may be justified in this regard where the appellant is particularly unsophisticated, unassisted by counsel or other qualified representative, and unlikely to have recognized the procedural error and its prejudicial effect.

To summarize: first, the appellant has the burden of proof on harmful error, Huston v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 260, 263, 6 MSPB 223 (1981), an affirmative defense; second, procedural error warrants reversal only if the procedures followed substantially impaired the employee's rights. Doyle v. VA, 229 Ct. Cl. 261 (1982) (following Brewer v. USPS, 647 F.2d 1093 (Ct. Cl. 1981)); Helman v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 164, 167 (1984) ("A claim of procedural error is an affirmative defense over which an appellant has the burden of proof. An appellant must establish not only that the agency erred in the application of its procedures but also that he/she has been substantially harmed or prejudiced by the agency's error."). The theoretical possibility of prejudice is an insufficient basis for inferring actual prejudice. "Unless it is likely that the alleged error affected the result, its occurrence cannot have been prejudicial." Reed v. Dept. of Transp., 76 MSPR 126, 134 (1997) ("A mere conjectural possibility of prejudice...cannot suffice as a basis for inferring actual prejudice and does not establish harmful error."); Mortensen v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 624, 626 (1983) (citing Parker v. DLA, 1 MSPR 505, 1 MSPB 489 (1980)); Garrett v. Dept. of Treasury, 7 MSPR 135, 138 (1981) (AJ erred by basing harmful error finding on "pure speculation"); cf. Vilca v. TSA, DE-0752-20-0272-I-1 (NP 2/17/2023) ("Our analysis of the due process and harmful error issues presented is generally the same regardless of whether we apply 5 U.S.C. chapter 75 or the agency's policies.").
