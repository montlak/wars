C. CLEAR AND PATENT BREACHES OF NEGOTIATED AGREEMENTS

As stated by Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 51 FLRA 858 (1996), discussed below, a breach of contract is an unfair labor practice if the breach constitutes a repudiation of the contract when measured by two elements: the nature and scope of the alleged breach of an agreement (i.e., was the breach clear and patent?); and the nature of the agreement provision allegedly breached (i.e., did the provision go to the heart of the parties' agreement?). Scott AFB clarified some earlier law.

Alleged violations of a negotiated agreement concerning differing and arguable interpretations of the agreement, as distinguished from alleged actions constituting clear, unilateral breaches of the agreement, are remedied within the grievance and arbitration procedures of the negotiated agreement rather than through the unfair labor practice procedures. Marine Corps Logistics Base, Barstow and AFGE Local 1485, 33 FLRA 626, 642 (1988); Div. of Military and Naval Affairs, New York and New York State Council of ACT, 8 FLRA 307, 322 (1982) (ALJ Decision) (stating rule); Dept. of Transp., FAA, Int'l Airport, Brownsville and PATCO, 5 FLRA 565, 575-76 n.2 (1981) (ALJ Decision) (not every contract breach constitutes ULP; clear, flagrant and persistent breach of contract may rise to seriousness of unilateral change in contract and ULP, but without flagrant or patent breach constituting unilateral change, differing and arguable interpretations of agreement are remedied within framework of grievance machinery of contract rather than through ULP process). If a contract dispute as to arbitrability is not clear from the face of the contract, FLRA will reject a ULP charge and determine that arbitration is the appropriate means to resolve the dispute. Harry S. Truman Memorial Veterans Hosp. and AFGE Local 3399, 11 FLRA 516, 519 (1982). Before 1996, the ULP test was stated somewhat differently: actions of management involving differing and arguable interpretations of a negotiated agreement, as distinguished from alleged actions constituting clear and patent breaches of an agreement, were not ULPs. DLA, Defense Depot Tracy and Laborers' Int'l Union Local 1276, 16 FLRA 1083, 1091 (1984) (ALJ Decision).

Assuming a clear breach, the form of the agreement breached is immaterial. A ULP may be committed through the breach of either a written or an oral agreement. It is a ULP for an activity to fail to adhere to an oral agreement concerning a negotiable matter. The existence of the agreement may be determined through credibility findings on contrary versions of what occurred and was said at bargaining sessions. VA Outpatient Clinic, Los Angeles and AFGE Local 2297, 22 FLRA 399, 416 (1986) (ALJ Decision). If no oral agreement results from general discussions concerning employee benefits, no ULP is committed should management not adhere to what the union incorrectly concludes is an agreement; there must be a meeting of the minds. Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Indian Affairs and IBEW Local 768, 31 FLRA 267, 275 (1987). There must be a collective bargaining agreement (or the equivalent) for management to commit a ULP by breaching the accord. A "partnership agreement" that was not explicitly or impliedly incorporated into the parties' negotiated agreement did not necessarily achieve the same binding effect as a negotiated agreement. See AFGE Local 3529 and DOD, DCAA, 52 FLRA 1313, 1317 (1997) (reviewing exceptions to arbitration award). But see Dept. of Transp., FAA, Standiford ATCT and NATCA Local SDF, 53 FLRA 312, 319 (1997) ("The fact that the sessions were conducted in a partnership atmosphere, as opposed to 'traditional' collective bargaining, does not preclude a conclusion that the sessions constituted collective bargaining within the meaning of the Statute.").

Several principles govern resolution of contract issues in the ULP forum. First, a matter does not rest on contract interpretation if the contract is silent on the issue and there is no language subject to differing interpretations. IRS, W. Region, San Francisco and NTEU, 11 FLRA 655, 666 (1983) (ALJ Decision). Second, if there is a ULP claim validly presented, FLRA will decide it; FLRA specifically declined to adopt private-sector doctrines of deferral to negotiated arbitration procedure announced by the NLRB in Collyer Insulated Wire, 192 NLRB 837 (1971). Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., Region VIII and NTEU, 18 FLRA 377, 390 (1985) (ALJ Decision). FLRA examines claimed statutory violations in the context of the contract and past practice to determine whether the asserted violation was allowed by the contract. IRS and NTEU, 47 FLRA 1091, 1110-11 (1993). Although an issue may be within the ambit of a grievance procedure, when basic employee rights, e.g., the ability to join or seek assistance from a union without reprisal, are in question, it is not appropriate to defer to contract grievance-arbitration processes, even though the alleged conduct also involves a contract violation. Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and Portsmouth FEMTC, 7 FLRA 766, 776 (1982) (ALJ Decision). [Refer to Chapter 9, "Statutory Violation; Bargaining Failures."]

A party to a negotiated agreement assumes some risk when it interprets and applies an agreement. If a respondent's interpretation of the agreement or memo of understanding results in a clear and patent breach of the agreement, the interpretation rises to the level of a ULP. But if the respondent's interpretation is arguably within the terms of the negotiated agreement, the interpretation is a matter of contract interpretation for resolution through contract grievance and arbitration procedures. Oklahoma City Air Logistics Ctr., Tinker AFB and AFGE Local 916, 3 FLRA 512, 521 (1980) (ALJ Decision); see PBGC and IUPEDJ, 73 FLRA 386, 387 (2022) ("We agree with the Judge that these memorandum references are ambiguous and could reasonably be interpreted to support either of the contrary views offered in this case. Because the Respondent acted in accordance with a reasonable interpretation of the memorandum, the Judge correctly concluded that the Respondent did not unlawfully repudiate the memorandum."). Several examples illustrate the principle. It is an unfair labor practice for an agency to unilaterally suspend its collective bargaining agreement with the union, to refuse to process grievances, and to refuse to process cases through to arbitration. VA Hosp., Danville and AFGE Local 1963, 4 FLRA 432, 444 (1980) (ALJ Decision). It is appropriate to resolve through ULP procedures the repudiation of provisions of an agreement concerning travel and per diem. DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 21 FLRA 1092, 1094 (1986) ("[W]e are here presented with an alleged repudiation of the agreement rather than with differing and arguable interpretations of that agreement. It is entirely appropriate to resolve this issue in an unfair labor practice proceeding. See, for example, American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1923, AFL-CIO, 20 FLRA No. 88 (1985)."); see Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Chicago and NTEU Chap. 10, 33 FLRA 147, 154 (1988) (dispute over travel reimbursement for union officials was not ULP given various unclear provisions of contract on travel reimbursement). In a conflict over whether a local manager had authority to negotiate a local agreement, there was no ULP because of a patent breach or lack of good faith when reasonable persons could disagree as to whether the master agreement permitted local negotiations. Food Safety and Quality Serv., USDA and AFGE Local 2490, 7 FLRA 665, 672-73 (1982) (ALJ Decision); see US Penitentiary, Florence and AFGE Local 1301, 54 FLRA 30, 31 (1998) (interpreting Scott AFB, 51 FLRA 858, 862 (1996), "a party that is acting in accordance with a reasonable interpretation of its agreement has not committed a clear and patent breach of the agreement"); cf. NWSEA and Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, 71 FLRA 275, 277 n.32 (2019) (Member DuBester dissenting) (reviewing arbitration award), amended, 71 FLRA 380 (2019) ("reasonable interpretation" analysis); NAGE Local R3-32 and Dept. of Air Force, Air Reserve Station Willow Grove, 61 FLRA 127, 133 (2005) (reviewing arbitration award: union did not commit ULP by failing to respond to a management grievance when union's actions constituted reasonable interpretation of its contract). A dispute as to the representation rights expressed in a contract, given ambiguous contract language, was to be resolved through arbitration procedures rather than through ULP litigation. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 22 FLRA 91, 114 (1986) (ALJ Decision). The question of whether a union should have directed requests for information to a labor relations officer rather than someone else, as it had agreed pursuant to a memorandum of understanding that the union later canceled, would be resolved through contract rather than ULP procedures. 22nd Combat Support Group, March AFB and AFGE Local 3854, 30 FLRA 331, 333-34 (1987).

ULP allegations were dismissed concerning free follow-up physical examinations for employees under a contract provision that required those examinations to be scheduled in accordance with good medical practice. The employer discontinued routine follow-up exams because its studies indicated that routine examinations were not consistent with good medical practice. The interpretations of the union and employer of the contract were both plausible and the employer demonstrated an attempt to improve rather than eliminate the physical exam program. The parties' differences should have been resolved through grievance procedures. SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 15 FLRA 614, 622-24 (1984) (ALJ Decision). A dispute should have been resolved under the grievance procedure rather than through a ULP proceeding that involved interpretation of a contract clause (viewed by the agency as required as a condition precedent to beginning negotiations on ground rules) that the union submit substantive proposals concerning the impact and implementation of the change in working conditions proposed by management. The refusal to bargain charge, relating to negotiations over ground rules, hinged on the interpretation of the contract clause and differing and arguable interpretations of that clause; the case was not appropriately decided through the ULP process. DHHS, SSA, New York Region and AFGE, 23 FLRA 422, 433-34 (1986) (ALJ Decision).
