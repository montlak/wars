4. Timing of Request; Arbitration Award

The Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.119(b) (2022), calls for the Director of OPM to file a petition for reconsideration "within 35 days after the date of service of the Board's final order." Delays will be excused for good cause, e.g., misdirection by the Board of the administrative record to an incorrect office location of OPM. See Morgan v. USPS, 48 MSPR 607, 609 n.1 (1991); Allen v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 419, 420 (1984) (describing, for purposes of calculating the time for OPM to request reconsideration, the Board's prior practice of grouping decisions and sending them periodically to OPM in a single mailing); Allen v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 269, 270 (1984) ("Application of the statutory time limit to all challenges to Board final decisions serves an important [c]ongressional purpose underlying the Civil Service Reform Act, to reduce substantially the amount of time consumed by the process for review of federal personnel actions under prior law."). The time limit runs from issuance of the entire Board decision, including any dissent. See York v. MSPB, 711 F.2d 401, 403-04 (D.C. Cir. 1983). If the OPM request is untimely, the Board may reopen the case on its own motion, stay corrective action, and then treat an issue of importance raised by OPM. See Fitzgerald v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 609, 610 (1984).

It was the law under Johnson v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 593 (1984), that an OPM reconsideration petition must be filed within 30 days from OPM's receipt of a notice of a Board decision; otherwise the petition was denied as untimely should OPM fail to demonstrate good cause for the untimely filing. It was the receipt of the decision by OPM that triggered the period for requesting reconsideration. Thomas v. Dept. of Treasury, 25 MSPR 217, 219 n.1 (1984); see Devine v. Sutermeister, 724 F.2d 1558, 1562 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (the 30-day period for OPM to seek reconsideration ran from the date OPM received notice of the decision of the MSPB or of an arbitrator); Thomas v. Dept. of Treasury, 22 MSPR 396, 397 (1984) (the employing agency, or respondent, was not the "agent" of OPM for purposes of calculating the date of receipt of a Board order). OPM was to provide proof, e.g., a date stamp, of the date that it received the final decision of the Board if the timeliness of its reconsideration request was disputed. Schaefer v. INS, 28 MSPR 566, 568 (1985). The date of receipt of the decision diminished in significance with the holding in Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 531, 534-36 (1988). The Board there decided that it would suspend operation of its regulation and held that "[a]ny OPM petition for reconsideration which is filed after the date of [Hammond] will be considered timely if it is filed within 35 days after the date of service of the Board's final order." The modification was made to allow OPM a reasonable time to receive copies of orders routinely mailed to OPM by the Board. Hammond traced the evolution of prior rulings concerning the timing of OPM reconsideration petitions.

As to arbitration awards issued in cases that could have gone to the MSPB and that are judicially reviewable in the Federal Circuit, OPM must first petition the arbitrator for reconsideration before OPM may pursue the case in court. Under 5 USC 7121(f), MSPB rules govern OPM as to judicial review of arbitration awards. OPM must seek reconsideration from the arbitrator within 35 days (pursuant to 5 CFR 1201.119(b) (2022) from the date the arbitrator's award was served on the union and the agency. If OPM does not meet the timeliness requirement, the arbitrator may not reopen his award on the basis of an untimely request from OPM. Bloomer v. DHHS, 966 F.2d 1436 (Fed. Cir. 1992).
