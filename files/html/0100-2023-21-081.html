b. Basic Pay; Cost of Living Adjustment

Retirement is calculated on receipt of basic pay, a definition provided by 5 CFR 831.1202 (2022) (CSRS):

Basic pay means the pay an employee receives that is subject to civil service retirement deductions. The definition is the same as the definition of "basic pay" under 5 U.S.C. 8331(3).

For FERS, 5 CFR 844.102 (2022) provides:

Basic pay means the pay an employee receives that is subject to deductions under FERS.

Under Famulary v. OPM, 13 MSPR 624, 624-29 (1982), a cost of living adjustment granted under a USPS collective bargaining contract was to be included in basic pay for purposes of determining recovery of earnings sufficient to disqualify continuation of disability retirement benefits:

Appellant, Philip S. Famulary, initiated this appeal from an Office of Personnel Management (OPM) reconsideration decision finding that he was not entitled to continuation of his disability retirement annuity beyond December 31, 1979. OPM's decision was based upon its determination that appellant had been restored to his earning capacity within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d). The presiding official reversed the OPM decision finding that OPM had erred in discontinuing appellant's disability annuity payments because appellant had not been restored to the required earning capacity.

OPM filed a timely petition for review in which it contends that the presiding official erred in interpreting the applicable statutory provision, which provides, in pertinent part that:

If an annuitant receiving disability retirement annuity from the Fund, before becoming 60 years of age, is restored to an earning capacity fairly comparable to the current rate of pay of the position occupied at the time of retirement, payment of the annuity terminates on reemployment by the Government or one year after the end of the calendar year in which earning capacity is so restored, whichever is earlier. Earning capacity is deemed restored if in each two succeeding calendar years the income of the annuitant from wages or self-employment or both equals at least 80 percent of the current rate of pay of the position occupied immediately before retirement. [Emphasis added.]

5 U.S.C. § 8337(d)

OPM contends that the presiding official erred because he interpreted the words "current rate of pay" as including the postal cost-of-living adjustment (COLA), in addition to the "base salary," in determining whether appellant's income for calendar years 1977 and 1978 exceeded the 80% requirements. OPM posits that based on the specific requirement of the collective bargaining agreement between the United States Postal Service and its employees, it has always excluded the COLA from the determination of current rate of pay. The petition for review raises a significant question of statutory interpretation, and is therefore GRANTED.

...

"Basic pay" is defined for retirement purposes at 5 U.S.C. § 8331(3) as not including "bonuses, allowances, overtime pay...pay given in addition to the base pay of the position as fixed by law or regulation."

Although the pay schedule of a Postal Service employee is not fixed by law or regulation, but rather by collective bargaining agreement entered into by the Postal Service and its employees, this Board has consistently stated that, where any agency enters into a collective bargaining agreement, the Board will treat the provisions of that agreement in the same manner as it would the provisions of the agency's regulations. Giesler v. Department of Transportation, 3 MSPB 367 (1980).

Article IX of the collective bargaining agreement between the Postal Service and its employees covering the period July 21, 1975-July 20, 1978, which is entitled Salaries and Wages, provides, in pertinent part, that:

D. The cost of living adjustment shall be taken into account only in computing base rates...

E. The cost of living adjustment shall not become a fixed part of the Postal Service Base Salary Schedules. [Emphasis added.]

Paragraph E was amended in the collective bargaining agreement covering the period from July 21, 1978-July 20, 1981 to provide that:

E. The cost of living adjustment shall not become a fixed part of the Postal Service Basic Salary Schedules. [Emphasis added.]

Although based on these paragraphs there appears to be an ambiguity as to whether the COLA should be included in determining the "basic pay" of a postal worker under 5 U.S.C. § 8331(3), this Board finds that the only reasonable interpretation of the statute requires the inclusion of the COLA for purposes of determining the "current rate of pay" for purposes of 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d). In analogizing appellant's position to those in the competitive and excepted service, the Board notes that the COLA, which is applicable to all similarly situated employees, is not among those itemized allowances, special rates of pay and differentials which are specifically excluded from basic pay by various statutes and regulations. 5 U.S.C. § 8331(c); 5 C.F.R. Parts 531, 550, 591.

Further, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 5332, the salaries set by schedule for General Schedule employees are "basis pay" for their respective positions. By authority granted in 5 U.S.C. § 5305, the President may adjust the rate of pay in "statutory pay systems." Under 5 U.S.C. § 5301, the General Schedule pay is a statutory pay system. Therefore, the President has the authority to grant, and in fact has granted, federal employees comparability pay adjustments. These comparability adjustments are then added to and become part of "basic pay" for the respective position as of the date they become effective.

The Board therefore finds that it is more reasonable to interpret the Postal Service collective bargaining agreement to provide that the across-the-board cost-of-living adjustment, paid to postal workers, similarly becomes part of their base pay upon the date it becomes effective.

The OPM "Restoration to Earning Capacity Worksheet" which is used to determine whether annuitant has earned more than 80% of current rate of pay states that "current salary" is the annual base pay of the position on December 31 of the year for which income is reported. Pursuant to those instructions OPM must consider the postal COLA for the year where the COLA has in fact become part of the base pay.

OPM also argues that under the doctrine set forth in Udall v. Tallman, 380 U.S. 1, 16 (1965) this Board must give deference to its interpretation of 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d) because it is a reasonable construction made by the administrative body charged with the administration of the statute, and is not an arbitrary or capricious construction. However, where there are compelling indications that the interpretation is wrong, the agency's interpretation need not be followed. Cf. Kenneth v. Schmoll, 482 F.2d 491 (10th Cir). As shown above, comparability increases to the base pay for General Schedule employees become part of base pay and are not considered additional pay under 5 U.S.C. § 8331(3). OPM has not presented any legal authority for its arbitrary determination that postal workers should be treated differently by excluding COLA from "base pay" and through the analogy to General Schedule employees, there are compelling indications that the interpretation is incorrect.

Appellant's earnings in 1977 did not constitute 80% of the current rate of pay, the statutory limit set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d). The presiding official correctly found that appellant's earnings did not equal or exceed 80% of the rate of pay from which he retired for two successive years and his annuity should not have been discontinued. The initial decision is hereby AFFIRMED as MODIFIED herein.
