broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 11: Promotions, Vacancies, and Appointments, Section C: Ranking, Rating, and Qualifications, Subsection 01: Merit Promotion Plan

1. Merit Promotion Plan

It is said that a merit promotion plan is generally negotiable. INS and AFGE, Nat'l INS Council, 16 FLRA 80, 87 (1984). A proposal was negotiable for unit positions that merit promotion procedures be based on evaluations of employees by supervisors, performance evaluation, oral interviews, and academic achievement with specific directions and ratings as to each subelement of the merit promotion evaluation process; however, it was negotiable only at the election of the agency as to promotion procedures for nonbargaining unit supervisory positions. Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters, Local F-61 and Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, 3 FLRA 438, 441-45 (1980). The decision was interesting because of the breadth of the proposals. Evaluation methods determining skill levels of those on the list of most highly qualified candidates were to include supervisors' evaluation, performance evaluation, and oral interview (each 30% of the total weight), and academic achievement (10%). As to supervisory evaluations, the evaluation could not cover a period of more than the past two and a half years, the evaluator (unless there were exceptional circumstances) had to have been the employee's supervisor for at least three months, and no evaluation would ordinarily be submitted by supervisors lower than GS-6. Applicants could require evaluations to be discussed with them before submission to the personnel office; a formula was determined for establishing relative weight of evaluations; every effort would be made to secure two evaluations; and, no employee would be rated by a former supervisor who left the department. As to performance evaluation, the evaluation was to be "observed" by a union representative; grades were to be determined by a panel using a numerical scoring system; and, the union president would be consulted concerning the type of evaluation to be used. Negotiable criteria for oral interviews included that they be conducted by three but not more than five supervisors; that they ask the same "amount" of job related questions; that the applicant's score be totaled and averaged; that appropriate measures be taken to ensure that those interviewed did not speak with those individuals waiting to be interviewed; and that the interview be observed by a union representative. A formula was also established for weighing or scoring academic credits.

A proposal was unobjectionable that required management to follow its merit promotion plan and to use a negotiated procedure for rating, ranking, and evaluating candidates for referral for defined transfers, promotions, reassignments, long details, and appointments with known promotion potential. The agency was not stripped of discretion to use other means of appointment or selection for positions. Because the merit promotion plan excluded certain appointment actions, it allowed for consideration of candidates from other appropriate sources outside the minimum area of consideration established by the plan. AFGE Local 909 and Army Hq. Military Traffic Mgmt. Command, Washington, 6 FLRA 502, 502-03 (1981). But see AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council and INS, 51 FLRA 1308, 1320-22 (1996) (Provision 2 nonnegotiable: that merit promotion plan or reassignment be used to fill positions outside the continental United States: the proposal limited the right of management to select from any source; proposal was not appropriate arrangement because record did not show that proposal addressed an adverse effect on employees attributable to exercise of management right). A proposal was negotiable that higher-level duties and responsibilities not be assigned to employees on a continuing basis if not assigned in accordance with merit promotion procedures. The proposal noted that the "assignments create the impression of favoritism and pre-selection and impair employee confidence in the integrity of the promotion program." DLA Council of AFGE Locals and DOD, DLA, 24 FLRA 367, 370, 372 (1986).
