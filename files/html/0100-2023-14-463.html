broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 15: Pay, Premiums, and Allowances, Section J: Insurance Coverage, Health Care, Subsection 02: NAF Employees

2. NAF Employees

AFGE Local 1897 and Dept. of Air Force, Eglin AFB, 24 FLRA 377, 378-84 (1986), declared negotiable the level of health insurance contributions for NAF employees. FLRA, over the dissent of Chairman Calhoun, determined the proposal negotiable because the contribution level was not set by statute and was not excluded by definition from the scope of negotiable conditions of employment. The proposal did not determine the conditions of employment of nonunit employees. It did not interfere with management's right to determine its budget; the agency did not demonstrate a conflict between the proposal and agency regulations. Negotiability of provisions for dental and group insurance for NAF employees was reaffirmed in SEIU Local 556 and Dept. of Navy, Navy Exch., Pearl Harbor, 25 FLRA 796, 797-801 (1987), and in SEIU Local 556 and Dept. of Navy, Navy Exch., Pearl Harbor, 37 FLRA 320, 333-43 (1990) (following remand from court of appeals, FLRA found that no compelling need supported a regulation arguably blocking negotiability). SEIU Local 556 was upheld in Dept. of Army v. FLRA, 914 F.2d 1291, 1292 (9th Cir. 1990), although bargaining over any coverage that would have duplicated workers' compensation protection was nonnegotiable because those benefits were "specifically provided for by Federal statute," precluding them from negotiation under 5 USC 7103(a)(14)(C).

The ability to negotiate levels of the employer's contribution for NAF employees' health insurance coverage was reconfirmed in AFGE Local 1857 and Dept. of Air Force, Air Logistics Ctr., Sacramento, 36 FLRA 894, 894-95, 899-911 (1990), announcing that the proposals did not interfere with the right of the agency to determine its budget and that the proposals did not conflict with an agency regulation supported by a compelling need. Another decision determined to be negotiable an increase in the employer's contribution from 50% to 70% of the cost of health insurance premiums. The agency was not required to establish a specific program or operation; an indirect effect on the agency's budget was insufficient to interfere with the right of the agency to determine its budget. The cost of the increase in benefits, about $7.1 million, was insignificant relative to total agency (AAFES) sales of $5.9 billion and employee compensation costs of $900 million. The contribution sought by the union "vitally" affected conditions of employment of unit employees. AFGE and DOD, AAFES, Dallas, 38 FLRA 282, 293-94 (1990); see NAGE Local R7-72 and Dept. of Army, Rock Island Arsenal, 38 FLRA 1223, 1224, 1228-31 (1990) (proposals concerning agency-paid percentage of NAF employee health benefits that were not blocked by management right to set budget or by an agency regulation supported by a compelling need relative to cost savings or comparability of benefits relative to military personnel); cf. FDIC v. FLRA, 977 F.2d 1493, 1497-99 (D.C. Cir. 1992) (affirming ULP finding of unilateral change in working conditions as to health insurance contribution rate that was within agency's discretion).

NAGE Local R4-26 and Dept. of Air Force, Langley AFB, 40 FLRA 118, 123, 128-42 (1991) (Proposal 2), raised no bar to the negotiability of a proposal concerning optional participation of NAF employees in the Air Force Insurance Plan or union plan, with a provision establishing comparable rates of contribution. See IAM Lodge 2135 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 50 FLRA 677, 678 (1995), supra, modifying Langley. NAGE Local R4-26 rejected the argument that proposals concerning insurance benefits were nonnegotiable because § 7112(c)(2) of the Statute excludes from grievance procedures grievances involving "retirement, life insurance, and health insurance." The scope of a grievance procedure is not necessarily coextensive with the scope of the duty to bargain. NAF employees may negotiate for coverage of a temporary disability insurance plan at no cost to employees. Pay and benefits for the NAF employees are governed by agency regulations rather than federal law. Those regulations did not conflict with the proposal. SEIU Local 556 and Dept. of Army, Army Support Command, Ft. Schafter, 29 FLRA 1553, 1563-65 (1987) (Proposal 3).

AFGE Local 3240 and Dept. of Air Force, Tyndall AFB, 58 FLRA 696, 697-98 (2003), determined that the agency could avoid negotiating over levels of agency contributions toward health insurance for NAF employees because of the need for uniformity in contribution levels for agencies subject to § 349 of the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal year 1995, Pub. L. 103-337, 108 Stat. 2727 (Oct. 5, 1994):

We find that the proposals are contrary to law because they are inconsistent with the statutory mandate for uniformity under § 349 of the Authorization Act, and are therefore nonnegotiable.

Section 349 of the Authorization Act required DoD, no later than October 1, 1995, to take such steps as may be necessary to provide "a uniform health benefits program for employees of the Department of Defense assigned to a nonappropriated fund instrumentality of the Department." In the legislative history of the Act, Congress emphasized that this provision would require DoD "to provide for a single uniform program" for NAF employees. House Rpt. 103-499. The House Report also noted that DoD components had offered comprehensive health insurance coverage to NAF employees in the past and that such plans have had "major differences in provisions and premium contribution rates...[which are] inconsistent with the national effort toward universal health care coverage." Id.

The Authorization Act and its legislative history demonstrate that Congress intended DoD to establish one health benefits program with uniform provisions, including premium contribution rates, for all DoD NAF entities. Section 349 does not provide discretion to the heads of any of the six DoD components and their subordinate agencies to establish separate health benefit programs.

We agree with the Agency that Proposal 1 "would make it impossible to provide a 'single uniform health benefit program' for all DoD [NAF] employees[ ]" because "[i]f one local union could negotiate a different percentage of premiums to be paid by the Agency, then other unions could also do so." Such bargaining would be inconsistent with the clear Congressional mandate of uniformity that was intended to address the differences in premium contribution rates throughout DoD for NAF employees. Similarly, if Proposal 2 were found negotiable, the uniformity mandated by § 349 would be negated because other unions could propose the addition of different plans.

This case differs from NFFE, Local 29, 32 FLRA 721, 729-731 (1988). In that case, the Authority found that a different statute that required uniformity "insofar as practicable" for regulations issued by the Secretaries of military departments did not require the establishment of uniform procedures for civil service employees and members of the Armed Services. In so finding, the Authority noted that had Congress intended to mandate uniformity, it would not have expressly authorized agencies to exercise their discretion with regard to considerations of practicability. In contrast, in the legislation applicable to the instant case, Congress has expressed its intent to mandate uniformity.

Similarly, this case differs from Dep't of the Navy v. FLRA, 952 F.2d 1434, 1443 (D.C. Cir. 1992). There, the court held that proposals that sought to regulate conditions of employment of employees in other bargaining units were nonnegotiable. In so holding, the court noted that, generally, the fact that two or more different bargaining units seek to negotiate over a matter that falls within a mandatory subject of bargaining does not render the matter non-negotiable. The court noted that "multi-unit (or "coordinated") bargaining may well be the appropriate solution on those rare occasions...when more than one bargaining unit legitimately can claim bargaining rights over a particular subject." Id. at 1443. However, the court found that "in the absence of such coordinated bargaining, however, each union may lay claim to a right to negotiate over matters within the compass of mandatory subjects of bargaining for employees within its designated bargaining unit." Id.

That case did not involve a Congressional mandate that required uniformity on a department-wide basis. As noted above, if the proposals in the case before us were found to be negotiable, other proposals by other unions setting different employer contribution levels and PPO coverage would necessarily be negotiable, contrary to the Congressional mandate for uniformity. While it may be possible to formulate proposals that would be consistent with the department-wide uniformity in NAF health benefits programs mandated by Congress, the proposals before us do not achieve that goal.

Consequently, we agree with the Agency that to sanction collective bargaining on these proposals would run counter to the nondiscretionary mandate for DoD-wide uniformity that the Authorization Act requires because it could lead to different agreements among the six DoD components and their subordinate agencies on premium contribution rates and PPO coverage.
