b. Motion to Dismiss Filed During Hearing

Used as a stratagem for the appellant, a motion to dismiss is similar to a motion for summary judgment except that it is made after the agency rests its case; the motion to dismiss cannot supplement the record. The motion is made during the hearing, after the agency rests and before the appellant commences. The motion will seek a decision favorable to the appellant on all or part of the case against him. As a practical matter in Board proceedings, the motion is the appellant's. The agency cannot foreclose the appellant's statutory right to a hearing. Under McKenzie v. Dept. of Interior, 16 MSPR 397, 401-02 n.3 (1983), the AJ has the authority to rule on an appellant's motion for dismissal following the presentation of an agency's case-in-chief, provided the agency is given the opportunity to object to the motion. McKenzie relied on the provisions of then FRCP 41(b), now replaced by FRCP 52(c):

If a party has been fully heard on an issue during a nonjury trial and the court finds against the party on that issue, the court may enter judgment against the party on a claim or defense that, under the controlling law, can be maintained or defeated only with a favorable finding on that issue. The court may, however, decline to render any judgment until the close of the evidence. A judgment on partial findings must be supported by findings of fact and conclusions of law as required by Rule 52(a).

In McKenzie, when the AJ dismissed the agency's unacceptable performance case against the appellant, the Board analyzed the use of the Rule 41 (now Rule 52(c)) procedure and said that, in making a determination on the evidence before it, the adjudicator must evaluate all the evidence without making special inferences favoring the agency or the appellant. Glencoe v. SBA, 29 MSPR 537, 538-39 (1985). The AJ must carefully consider the weight and credibility of the agency's evidence and must consider whether the agency has established an unimpeached prima facie case in its case-in-chief; if the agency has, the motion must be denied. If the motion is granted, the AJ is required to prepare an initial decision containing findings of fact and conclusions, as well as the reasons or bases for the conclusions, on all material factual and legal issues presented by the record, just as in any other appeal. The Board equates the appellant's position in an appeal to that of a defendant in a civil action, for the agency must establish the essential elements in its case against an appellant:

In analyzing F.R.CIV.P. 41(b), courts have traditionally held that when a defendant[3] moves for dismissal following presentation of the plaintiff's case-in-chief, the trial court must use its full judgment on the weight and credibility of the plaintiff's evidence and must make its decision on the motion based on all the evidence before the court. Woods v. North American Rockwell Co., 480 F.2d 644 (10th Cir. 1972); Emerson Electric Co. v. Farmer, 427 F.2d 1082 (5th Cir. 1970). See also 5 Moore, Federal Practice ¶ 41.13[4] (2d ed. 1969) and cases cited therein. The cases have also held that the trial court should evaluate the evidence without making special inferences in the plaintiff's favor. Emerson Electric Co., supra, at 1086; Ellis v. Carter, 328 F.2d 573, 577 (9th Cir. 1964). The Board adopts this standard for its presiding officials when considering the motion for judgment. Where an appellant moves for judgment at the close of the agency's presentation of its case-in-chief, the presiding official shall decide whether or not to grant the motion based on whether the action is supported by the requisite degree of proof under 5 USC § 7701(c). In all cases, the presiding official shall carefully consider the weight and credibility of the agency's evidence in making his or her decision. In making that determination, the presiding official must consider whether the agency has established an unimpeached prima facie case in its case-in-chief. If the agency has, the appellant's motion must be denied.

[3] An appellant in an adverse action before the Board occupies, in essence, the same position as a defendant in a civil action because it is the agency which must establish the essential elements in its case against an appellant.

Finally, the Board notes that even if the presiding official does grant an appellant's motion for dismissal, he or she is still required to prepare an initial decision under 5 CFR § 1201.111, which contains "findings of fact and conclusions, as well as the reasons or bases therefor, upon all material issues of fact and law resented on the record." As stated in Spithaler v. Office of Personnel Management, 2 MSPB 2 (1980), the initial decision must still:

[I]dentify all material issues of fact, summarize the evidence on each such issue sufficiently to disclose the evidentiary basis for the presiding official's findings of fact, set forth those findings clearly and explain how any issues of credibility were resolved and why, describe the application of burdens of proof, and address all material legal issues in a fashion that reveals the presiding official's conclusions of law, legal reasoning and the authorities on which the reasoning rests.

McKenzie determined that the agency case should not have been resolved on the point raised by the appellant: proof of OPM approval of a performance appraisal system. The appeal was remanded to permit the appellant the opportunity to present evidence of the substantive performance issues presented in the Chapter 43 removal.

The Board reaffirmed the use of a motion to dismiss. The AJ may grant an appellant's motion for judgment following the agency's case-in-chief; the appellant is under no burden to disprove the charges against him. Gibson v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 384, 386 (1983) ("If the agency failed to establish its prima facie case, there is no basis for requiring the appellant to prove harmful error." (citing McKenzie)); see Denney v. USPS, 66 MSPR 191, 193 (1995) (AJ may "grant an appellant's motion to dismiss an appeal after the agency's presentation of its case in chief," but the AJ may not grant the motion if the agency presented an unrebutted or unimpeached prima facie case, and the AJ may not make special inferences in the appellant's favor; the AJ incorrectly granted the motion after reaching special inferences that essentially gave the appellant the benefit of the doubt in a falsification case). But cf. Larsen v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-13-2150-I-1 (NP 9/29/2015) ("The day before the hearing, the appellant moved for a decision to be made in favor of the appellant without a hearing because of the administrative judge's failure to adjudicate the appeal within 120 days and ex parte communications. He also moved for the administrative judge to take "judicial notice" of these two "facts." The administrative judge did not rule on these motions. The Board has recognized that it generally lacks the authority to grant summary judgment. Johnson v. Department of Justice, 104 M.S.P.R. 624, ¶ 30 (2007). The appellant has not shown that this matter presents circumstances in which summary judgment is appropriate, and thus we discern no prejudicial error.").

The denial of a motion to dismiss amounts to a refusal to enter judgment at that time. It is a tentative and inconclusive ruling on the quantum of proof at that point and does not alone mean that a prima facie case was established, Special Counsel v. Brown, 28 MSPR 133, 140-41 (1985):

In McKenzie, we held that presiding officials, including ALJs, may rule on motions to dismiss at the close of an agency's case in chief and, in ruling on such motions, shall consider the weight and credibility of the agency's evidence and must consider whether the agency has established a prima facie case-in-chief. "If the agency has, the appellant's motion must be denied." Id. at 402.

...

In McKenzie, we restated a policy of interpreting the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure as instructive, although not binding. A review of the decisions of the federal courts leads the Board to conclude that the position of the Special Counsel is without merit. The federal courts have not focused on whether a prima facie case has been made out, but rather have centered on how the evidence preponderates at the time the motion is made. See Ellis v. Carter, 328 F.2d 573 (9th Cir. 1964); Albright v. United States, 558 F. Supp. 260, 264 (D.D.C. 1982); United States v. AT & T, 524 F. Supp. 1336, 1343 (D.D.C. 1981). As a result, it cannot be inferred from a denial alone that a prima facie case has been established. A denial amounts to nothing more than a refusal to enter judgment at that time. At most, it constitutes a tentative and inconclusive ruling on the quantum of plaintiff's proof." Sanders v. GSA, 707 F.2d 969, 972 (7th Cir. 1983) (citing Armour Research Foundation of Illinois Institute of Technology v. Chicago, Rock Island and Pacific Railroad, 311 F.2d 493, 494 (7th Cir. 1963), cert. denied, 372 U.S. 966, 83 S. Ct. 1091, 10 L. Ed.2d 129 (1963)).

The Federal Circuit suggested that AJs have discretion in deciding how to treat motions to dismiss charges, Tompkins v. USPS, 415 Fed. Appx. 226, 228 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP):

...On a related matter, we also see no error in the AJ's declining to rule on the issue of the agency's charge prior to the hearing. An AJ is given broad discretion in procedural matters. Turner v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 805 F.2d 241, 245 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

If an AJ mistakenly decides that an agency failed to meet its burden of proof after the conclusion of its case, the appeal must be remanded for rehearing so that the appellant can present his case and so that the agency can present rebuttal evidence. See Hansen v. DLA, 10 MSPR 444, 448 (1982) (error to adjourn after the conclusion of an agency case when there would have been more evidence concerning the reasonableness of penalty). Although the AJ may rule on a motion to dismiss at the close of the agency's case-in-chief, and the motion may be granted if the agency has not by then established an unimpeached prima facie case, McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR 192, 204 (1992), as a practical matter, few motions to dismiss are granted. The AJ will not dismiss an agency case if there is a risk that the Board will fault him on his judgment and remand for completion of the hearing. If the appellant moves to dismiss at the end of the agency's case, it is suggested that the motion (likely an oral motion) be coupled with the statement that if the motion is denied, the appellant reserves his right to a hearing on his defenses. Agency counsel should follow the same process if the agency moves at the end of the appellant's case to dismiss an appeal that must be proved by the appellant (e.g., an Individual Right of Action whistleblower case or a constructive adverse action).

Comparing discussion in the next section on summary judgment and the discussion in this section on motions to dismiss, any attempt to obtain summary disposition adverse to the other party must be carefully considered and presented to avoid loss of a hearing right if the motion is denied.

As a technique for agencies responding to Board appeals, motions to dismiss are discussed in this section as they pertain to hearings. Jurisdictional issues are often raised prior to the onset of the hearing: the appellant may not be covered under the Board's appeals authority or his appeal may be late.
