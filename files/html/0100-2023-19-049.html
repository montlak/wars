7. Retirement Cases: Application by OPM of Statutory Requirements

Discrimination claims may be raised in the course of retirement appeals, Kleinknecht v. OPM, 96 MSPR 198, 202 ¶ 8 (2004) (claim was not properly raised for the first time on PFR), but OPM will not likely be subject to liability for a discrimination claim in a retirement appeal if all that is disputed is nondiscretionary application of statutory requirements, as was explained in Thompson v. OPM, 81 MSPR 677, 679-80 ¶¶ 6-8 (1999), involving a challenge to an annuity computation based on race and disability discrimination:

While the administrative judge's apparent failure to address the appellant's discrimination claims is error, it is harmless error. Once the appellant became 62 years old, he was entitled to a deferred annuity because of his length of service in the civil service. See 5 USC § 8338(a). Computation of this annuity is controlled by 5 USC § 8339. 5 USC § 8338(d). OPM is constrained by law to follow the annuity computation formulas passed by Congress. See Office of Personnel Management v. Richmond, 496 U.S. 414, 110 S. Ct. 2465, 110 L. Ed.2d 387 (1990). Thus, OPM has no discretion to deviate from these computation formulas. The appellant has failed to show that OPM did not correctly apply the laws regarding the calculation of deferred retirement annuities. Because OPM applied the correct laws to the appellant's case and calculated the appellant's annuity based on those laws, there is no act on the part of OPM to which the appellant can point and claim that OPM acted in a discriminatory manner.

An analogy can be drawn to cases involving the discretionary/ministerial distinction for determining governmental immunity. The Supreme Court has held that for purposes of the Federal Tort Claims Act, the government is immune from suits brought when a statute, regulation, or policy mandates a particular conduct and the employee follows that conduct. United States v. Gaubert, 499 U.S. 315, 322-25, 111 S. Ct. 1267, 113 L. Ed.2d 335 (1991). In other words, if an employee correctly carries out the ministerial functions of the agency, the agency is not liable. Conversely, the agency is liable if the employee fails to follow the conduct required by the statute, regulation, or policy because "there is no room for choice and the action will be contrary to policy." Gaubert, 499 U.S. at 324, 111 S. Ct. 1267.

In the present case, 5 USC § 8339 leaves no room for choice. The OPM employee who calculated the appellant's deferred annuity had no discretion to decide the amount of the annuity. Because that employee correctly applied the annuity computation formulas to the appellant's situation, he or she correctly carried out the ministerial functions of OPM. Thus, we find that as a matter of law, the computation of the appellant's annuity allows no discretion to OPM and in the absence of discretion, there can be no improper discrimination. Therefore, the appellant's claims of discrimination based on race and disability must fail.

The Board reiterated the limited application in retirement cases of affirmative defenses asserting discrimination, Jordan v. OPM, 108 MSPR 119, 122-23 ¶¶ 8-9, 2008 MSPB 31 (2008):

Congress has expressly mandated at 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1) that the Board render a decision on allegations of discrimination raised in conjunction with otherwise appealable actions. Marchese v. Department of the Navy, 32 M.S.P.R. 461, 464, petition dismissed, 35 M.S.P.R. 296 (1987) (Table). Here, the appellant is seeking Board review of OPM's final decision denying him a discontinued service retirement annuity under 5 U.S.C. § 8336, and 5 U.S.C. § 8347(d)(1) provides that "an administrative action or order affecting the rights or interests of an individual...under this [subchapter III--Civil Service Retirement] may be appealed to the...Board...." See Nebblett v. Office of Personnel Management, 73 M.S.P.R. 342, 345 (1997), aff'd, 152 F.3d 948 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table). Thus, because the appellant has a right under 5 U.S.C. chapter 83 to appeal OPM's final decision denying his application for a discontinued service retirement annuity to the Board, the administrative judge erred by informing him that the Board lacks jurisdiction to consider his discrimination claims and by not adjudicating his claims. However, for the reasons explained below, we find that the administrative judge's error did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984).

The Board has found that, as a matter of law, the retirement statutes allow for no discretion on the part of OPM in determining an individual's entitlement to an annuity and in following the annuity computation formulas enacted by Congress, and, in the absence of discretion, there can be no improper discrimination. Hundley v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 632, ¶ 9 (1999); Thompson v. Office of Personnel Management, 81 M.S.P.R. 677, ¶¶ 6-8 (1999), aff'd in part, vacated in part on other grounds, and remanded, 230 F.3d 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table). In Thompson, the Board found that OPM was constrained by law to follow the annuity computation formulas enacted by Congress, that it has no discretion to deviate from those formulas, and that, if it acted in compliance with the formulas established by statute, there was no act on OPM's part that could be shown to be discriminatory. Thompson, 81 M.S.P.R. 677, ¶¶ 6, 8. In this case, the applicable retirement statute is 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d), which sets forth the criteria for establishing an employee's entitlement to a discontinued service retirement annuity. Thus, if OPM correctly applied the criteria set forth under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d) in this appeal, then there can be no improper discrimination. See Hundley, 83 M.S.P.R. 632, ¶ 9; Thompson, 81 M.S.P.R. 677, ¶¶ 6, 8.

See Delos Reyes v. OPM, 381 Fed. Appx. 996, 998 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) ("OPM carried out its ministerial functions without discretion and thus could not have improperly discriminated against Delos Reyes."); Muyco v. OPM, 114 MSPR 694, 699 ¶ 15, 2010 MSPB 199 (2010) ("because the appellant's entitlement to make a deposit is a matter of law that does not permit OPM to exercise any discretion, there can be no discriminatory act by OPM"); cf. Oliver v. OPM, NY-0831-16-0059-I-1 (NP 4/13/2022) (dispute over payment to obtain credit for post-1956 military service: MSPB has no authority to award punitive damages or compensatory damages for age discrimination claims; no back or front pay from OPM). Although MSPB has jurisdiction over retirement appeals by CIA employees, it does not have jurisdiction over an associated disability discrimination claim. See Cash v. CIA, AT-844E-16-0508-I-1 (NP 4/14/2022).

If discrimination claims are raised in the course of annuity appeals, they should be raised when the appeal is pending before the AJ and not for the first time on PFR unless the appellant can explain in his PFR how and when he learned of the alleged discrimination and why he did not earlier raise the issue. Harlston v. OPM, 109 MSPR 148, 151-52 ¶ 5, 2008 MSPB 132 (2008); see Kleinknecht v. OPM, 96 MSPR 198, 202 ¶ 8 (2004), supra.
