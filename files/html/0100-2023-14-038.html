d. Exaggeration of Charge

Punishment for an offense greater than the offense charged is contrary to proper determination of a penalty. If the appellant is charged with unauthorized possession of government property, the deciding official of the agency errs by treating the charge as one of theft. The penalty chosen by the agency as a result of the mischaracterization of the charges is then subject to mitigation by the Board. See Culley v. DLA, 60 MSPR 204, 214-15 (1993) (relying on Dubiel v. USPS, 54 MSPR 428, 434 (1992)). Obliquely stated, the Board may mitigate a penalty if the agency "viewed the appellant's misconduct supporting the charge and the charge itself to be more serious than the actual circumstances surrounding the charge and the charge itself." Shelly v. Dept. of Treasury, 75 MSPR 677, 683 (1997); cf. Khan v. DHS, SF-0752-15-0830-I-1 (NP 11/21/2016) ("The facts of this case differ from those in which an employee is on notice of one charge, but a deciding official treats the charge as something altogether different in determining the appropriate penalty. Cf. Jenkins v. Environmental Protection Agency, 118 M.S.P.R. 161, ¶¶ 9, 12 (2012) (Ward/Stone violation when the deciding official relied on portions of a table of penalties concerning a charge other than the one brought against the employee and referenced in the notice of proposed removal); Culley v. Defense Logistics Agency, 60 M.S.P.R. 204, 214-15 (1993) (deciding official erred by treating an 'unauthorized possession of government property' charge as theft in determining an appropriate penalty); Dubiel v. U.S. Postal Service, 54 M.S.P.R. 428, 431-32 (1992) (finding that the deciding official erred by treating a specific charge of improperly addressing a subordinate as if it were a charge of sexual harassment while assessing the appropriate penalty)."); Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 n.9 (NP 5/2/2013) ("The appellant argues that the agency 'piled on' the AWOL specifications in an effort to have him removed. Although the Board would frown upon an agency sandbagging one of its own employees in such a way, we find that that is not what happened here. The record shows that the appellant's supervisor warned him at least once during this time period that he was accruing significant amounts of AWOL. Yet the appellant continued to accrue more AWOL, thus piling the incidents on himself. Moreover, we find that the specifications refer to discrete acts of misconduct and are not duplicative in any way. Cf. Southers v. Veterans Administration, 813 F.2d 1223, 1225-26 (Fed. Cir. 1987)."). If the deciding official uses a penalty table, but relies on the wrong classification of offense, a due process issue may be presented. See Jenkins v. EPA, 118 MSPR 161, 164-67 ¶¶ 2, 9-12, 2012 MSPB 70 (2012) (discussed in Chapter 11, "Information Pertaining to Aggravation or Mitigation of Penalty; Ward").
