F. ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS

The agency must identify a specific agency-wide regulation; show that there is a conflict between its regulation and the proposal; and demonstrate that its regulation is supported by a compelling need with reference to § 2424.11 of the FLRA regulations. AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, Office of Sec'y, Hq., Office of Gen. Counsel, Social Security Div., 21 FLRA 178, 180 (1986). It is no bar to negotiability that the agency intends to retain jurisdiction to issue regulations on an agency-wide level. The only bar to negotiability under § 7117 is that the proposal is inconsistent with law, government-wide rule or regulation, or an agency regulation supported by a compelling need. AFGE Local 3656 and FTC, Boston Reg'l Office, 4 FLRA 702, 703 (1980). The compelling need defense did not meet FLRA regulatory criteria in NAIL, Local 5 and DLA, Red River, Texarkana, 67 FLRA 85, 90 (2012) (Proposal 2):

However, with regard to the third requirement, the Agency does not address the criteria for determining compelling need under § 2424.50 of the Authority's Regulations. See PASS, 64 FLRA at 479 (taking into account the fact that the agency did not address the criteria for determining compelling need under § 2424.50 in finding that the agency failed to establish that a proposal was contrary to an agency regulation for which there was a compelling need). Because the Agency does not address such criteria, the Agency has failed to show that there is a compelling need for the Instruction. See U.S. DOD, Office of Dependents Sch., 40 FLRA 425, 443 (1991) (considering the fact that the agency failed to identify the Authority's compelling need criteria on which it relied and left for the Authority to guess how many of the arguments that the agency made related to any or all of those criteria in finding that the agency failed to establish a compelling need for its regulation); cf. Ass'n of Civilian Technicians, Mont. Air Chapter No. 29, 56 FLRA 674, 676 (2000) (concluding that the agency failed to establish a compelling need for its regulation, which based retention standing during a RIF primarily on performance ratings rather than length of service, because, in part, the agency did not assert that its more senior employees were less skilled or that they had received, or were more likely to receive, lower performance appraisals than more junior employees). Consequently, we find that the Agency has failed to establish that Proposal 2 is inconsistent with an Agency regulation for which there is a compelling need.
